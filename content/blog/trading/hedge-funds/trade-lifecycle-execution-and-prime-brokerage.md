> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A trade does not end when you click buy; it runs through order routing, execution under a best-execution obligation, a fair allocation across every fund you manage, and a financed, stock-lent, T+1 settlement that depends entirely on your prime broker.
>
> - **Routing and execution** turn an order into fills across many venues, and the law holds you to a **best-execution** obligation that weighs price, speed, likelihood of fill, cost, size, and venue — not the single best printed price.
> - **Allocation** is where the founder's integrity is tested in plain sight: when a partial fill has to be split across your fund and a managed account, it must be split **fairly** — pro-rata at one blended price — or it is a breach you can be barred for.
> - The **prime broker** is the single most important counterparty your fund has. It finances your book against collateral, holds your assets in custody, lends you stock so you can short, clears and settles your trades, and introduces you to capital.
> - The one number to remember: financing a **\$100M** book at 2x leverage means borrowing **\$100M**, and at a 5.5% all-in financing rate that costs roughly **\$5.5M a year** — a cost that eats your management fee before you have made a dollar of alpha.

Maya thought the hard part was the trade. She had spent fourteen years learning to find a mispriced stock, size the position, and hold it through the noise. So on the first real trading morning of the Meridian Fund — her own long/short equity fund, two weeks live, USD 60 million under management — she did what she had done ten thousand times before. She formed a view on a mid-cap industrials name, decided to buy 50,000 shares, and told her trader, "Buy 50,000 of the industrials name, work it through the morning, don't chase."

What surprised her was everything that happened after those words. The order did not go to "the market" the way she had always pictured it. It went into the order management system, which sliced it and handed the pieces to a smart order router that scattered them across three exchanges and two dark pools, hunting for liquidity without telegraphing the size. The fills trickled back over forty minutes. Then her operations contact asked a question she had never had to answer as an employee: "This is going across the main fund and the separately managed account for the family office, right? How do you want it allocated?" Meanwhile, because Meridian ran at roughly 1.8x gross, the prime broker had quietly financed most of the purchase against the rest of the book as collateral. And two of the names she also wanted to short that morning required a **locate** — the trader had to confirm the prime broker could actually borrow the shares before she was allowed to sell them. The trade she thought was one decision was, in fact, the trigger for a routing engine, a financing line, a stock-loan desk, a fairness rule, and a settlement chain that would not finish for another day.

This post is about that chain. A hedge fund is a business that sells an aligned promise — to compound capital with skill, inside a structure that protects the investor — and a startling amount of that promise lives not in the alpha but in the plumbing between "buy" and "settled, financed, correctly-allocated position." Get the plumbing wrong and you can have a great trade and still breach a fairness rule, fail to borrow the stock you sold, or wake up to a margin call that forces you to sell into the move you were trying to ride. Figure 1 is the whole journey on one page: a decision becomes an order, gets routed and executed, allocated, confirmed, financed, and finally settled. We will walk every step, and then spend the back half of the post on the counterparty that sits underneath all of it — the prime broker.

## Foundations: how a trade actually settles

Before any of the nuance, you need a working vocabulary. If you have traded for a living, the *price* side of this is second nature; it is the *operational* side — what happens to the order after it is filled, and who is financing and holding the position — that is usually a blank page for a new founder. Let us define every term from zero.

**An order** is an instruction to buy or sell a quantity of an instrument, with constraints (a limit price, a time horizon, a participation rate). "Buy 50,000 shares, work it, do not chase" is an order with a size, a style, and an implicit urgency. The order is not yet a trade; it is an intention waiting to meet a willing counterparty.

**Order routing** is the process of deciding *where* to send each piece of an order to get it filled. Modern equity markets are fragmented across many venues — multiple exchanges, plus off-exchange **dark pools** and market-makers. A **smart order router (SOR)** is software that splits a parent order into child orders and sends them to the venues most likely to fill them at the best terms, often slicing a large order into many small ones over time so it does not move the price against you.

**Execution** is the act of the order actually trading — a fill — against a counterparty at a venue. A large order is rarely one fill; it is dozens or hundreds of partial fills at slightly different prices, which blend into one **average execution price**. Execution is where your intention becomes a real economic event.

**Best execution** is a legal and fiduciary obligation: when you trade on behalf of clients, you must seek the most favorable terms *reasonably available under the circumstances*. Critically, this does not mean "always get the single lowest price on the screen." It is a multi-factor, process-based duty — we will spend a whole section on it — that weighs price, speed, likelihood of execution and settlement, total cost, order size, and the nature of the venue.

**Allocation** is the splitting of an executed order across the multiple accounts a manager runs. Most founders do not run one pool of money; they run a main fund plus, often, one or more **separately managed accounts (SMAs)** for large investors who want their money in their own name. When you trade a block intended for several of those accounts, you must allocate the fills among them — and you must do it *fairly*, by a pre-disclosed methodology, so that no account is systematically favored.

**The prime broker (PB)** is the bank that sits between your fund and the market and bundles the services a fund cannot run without. It **finances** your positions, holds your assets in **custody**, lends you securities so you can **short**, clears and settles your trades, and runs a **capital-introduction** team. The big prime brokers are large investment banks — Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, J.P. Morgan, and a handful of others dominate; smaller funds use a **mini-prime** or **introducing broker** that resells a big bank's custody behind a more attentive service layer. We cover the PB as one node in the wider provider stack in [the service-provider stack: prime broker, administrator, auditor](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor); here we go deep on the trading relationship itself.

**Financing** is the prime broker lending you money against your portfolio so you can hold more than your cash alone could buy. If your fund has USD 60 million of equity and buys USD 100 million of stock, the PB has financed the USD 40 million gap, taking your portfolio as collateral. The borrowed balance is a **debit balance**, and you pay interest on it — the financing cost that, as the TL;DR warned, can quietly consume your management fee.

**Margin** is the collateral you must post to support a financed or short position. The PB sets a **margin requirement** for each position — the share of its value you must fund yourself — and applies a **haircut**, a discount to an asset's collateral value to protect the PB against a price move. A **margin call** is the PB's demand for more collateral when your equity falls below the required level; if you cannot meet it, the PB can liquidate your positions.

**Securities lending (stock loan)** is how a short sale is even possible. To **short** a stock — sell it now in the hope of buying it back cheaper — you must first *borrow* the shares to deliver to the buyer, because you do not own them. The PB's securities-lending desk sources the shares and charges you a **borrow fee** (an annualized rate on the value borrowed). For an easy-to-borrow large cap the fee is trivial; for a hard-to-borrow name it can be enormous, and the loan can be **recalled** at any time.

**Rehypothecation** is the practice by which the prime broker re-uses the assets you have pledged to it as collateral — lending them out, or pledging them onward, to fund its own activities. It is normal, it is in your prime brokerage agreement, and it is exactly what turns a failing prime broker into your problem: assets that have been rehypothecated can be tangled up in the PB's own bankruptcy and frozen, which is the single reason serious funds spread their assets across more than one prime.

**T+1 settlement** is the timetable on which a trade becomes final. **Settlement** is the moment cash and securities actually change hands — the buyer's cash is delivered against the seller's shares. In US equities the standard settlement cycle moved from two business days after the trade (T+2) to **one business day (T+1) in May 2024**. Until settlement, a trade is agreed but not done; the operational machinery between execution and settlement — confirmation, affirmation, clearing — exists to make sure the two sides agree on exactly what happened so the exchange of cash and stock goes through cleanly.

Here is the through-line that connects all of these. Your investors are trusting you with two distinct promises: that you will pick well, and that the *machinery around the picking* — routing, fair allocation, financing, custody, settlement — will not lose, misprice, or mis-split their money. The first promise is alpha. The second is operations, and the prime broker is the beating heart of it. A founder who masters the first and ignores the second is one bad locate, one unfair allocation, or one frozen prime away from a fund-ending problem. The rest of this post is about not being that founder.

## Order routing and execution

Start with the gap between how a trader expects a market to work and how it actually works. The naive mental model is a single exchange where you post "buy 50,000" and it fills against whoever is selling. The real structure is a fragmented landscape: in US equities alone there are over a dozen registered exchanges and dozens of off-exchange venues, including dark pools that do not display quotes. Liquidity is scattered, and a large visible order is an invitation for others to trade ahead of you and push the price away. Routing exists to solve that problem.

When Maya's trader takes "buy 50,000, work it, do not chase," the order first lands in the **order management system (OMS)** — the fund's system of record for orders and positions. From there it is handed to an **execution management system (EMS)** or a broker's algorithm, which decides on a *strategy*: a VWAP algorithm to track the volume-weighted average price through the day, a participation algo that takes a fixed percentage of market volume, or an implementation-shortfall algo that trades more aggressively up front to minimize the risk of the price running away. The chosen strategy then drives the **smart order router**, which slices the 50,000-share parent into a stream of small child orders and sprays them across venues, sweeping displayed liquidity and probing dark pools, all while trying not to reveal the full size.

The economic stakes here are not abstract. Two costs are always in tension. The first is **market impact** — the price moves you cause by demanding liquidity; trade too fast and you push the stock up as you buy. The second is **timing risk** — the chance that the price runs away from you while you trade patiently; trade too slowly and you miss the move you were trying to capture. Good execution is the art of trading the parent order off against both. "Do not chase" is Maya telling the desk to favor low impact over speed; an urgent "get me in now" tells it to accept impact to cut timing risk.

It helps to put numbers on the stakes. Suppose Maya's 50,000-share order is in a name that trades a million shares a day, so her order is 5% of the day's volume — large enough that pushing it through the open market in a single sweep would visibly move the price. A reasonable rule of thumb is that crossing the spread and demanding immediate liquidity on an order this size might cost tens of basis points of market impact; on a \$2M position, 20 basis points of impact is \$4,000 of value handed to the other side. A patient algorithm that participates in, say, 10% of volume and trades over a couple of hours might cut that impact to a handful of basis points — but it accepts the risk that the stock drifts up \$0.40 while it waits, which on 50,000 shares is \$20,000 of timing risk. There is no free choice here; the trader is buying down one cost by taking on more of the other, and the right balance depends entirely on how urgent the alpha is. A signal that decays in a day demands speed; a slow-moving value thesis can afford patience.

For a new fund, a subtle and important point: you usually do *not* build this routing technology yourself. You rent it. Your prime broker and your executing brokers provide the algorithms and the SOR; you provide the OMS/EMS that talks to them and records the result. We discuss the systems layer in depth in [the technology stack: OMS, risk, and data](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-technology-stack-oms-risk-data). The founder's job is not to write a matching engine; it is to choose good brokers, choose the right algorithm for each order, and — above all — to be able to *demonstrate* afterward that the execution sought favorable terms. Which brings us to the obligation that governs all of it.

## Best execution

The phrase sounds like a guarantee of the best price. It is not. **Best execution** is a process-based fiduciary and regulatory duty: when you trade for clients, you must seek terms that are the most favorable *reasonably available under the circumstances*, judged across several factors and over time, not on a single trade in hindsight. An adviser's best-execution duty flows from its fiduciary obligation under the Investment Advisers Act; broker-dealers carry a parallel duty under FINRA Rule 5310. The common thread is that it is about *diligence and process*, not a clairvoyant promise.

Figure 7 lays out the factors that go into the judgment. Price is one of them — and net of costs it is usually the most important — but it shares the table with five others.

![A two by three grid of the six best-execution factors: price, speed, likelihood of fill, cost, order size, and venue and counterparty](/imgs/blogs/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage-7.png)

- **Price** — the best available price, *net of all costs*, not the headline quote.
- **Speed** — how quickly the order can be filled; for some orders immediacy is worth more than a fraction of a cent.
- **Likelihood of execution and settlement** — whether the venue will actually fill the order and settle it cleanly; a great quote you cannot reliably hit is worthless.
- **Total cost** — explicit commissions and fees plus implicit costs: the spread you cross and the market impact you cause.
- **Order size and market conditions** — a 50,000-share order in a thin name is a different problem from 500 shares in a mega-cap; the right venue and strategy depend on both.
- **The nature of the venue and counterparty** — a lit exchange, a dark pool, or a single dealer each carry different fill, information-leakage, and counterparty profiles.

The practical obligation on a founder is twofold. First, you must have a **best-execution policy**: a written description of how you select brokers and venues, how you choose execution strategies, and how you review the results. Second, you must actually *do* the review — periodically (commonly quarterly) examine your executions against benchmarks, document that you considered the factors, and keep records. The point a regulator checks is not whether any single trade got the lowest possible price; it is whether you had a reasonable process and followed it.

One specific trap deserves a callout because it routinely catches new managers: **soft dollars**. A broker may bundle research or services into the commission you pay — you direct trades there partly to receive research. Soft dollars are permitted within a safe harbor (Section 28(e) of the Securities Exchange Act), but they create an obvious conflict: you are spending your *clients'* commission dollars partly for your *own* benefit, and you must be able to show the arrangement is consistent with best execution and properly disclosed. If you route order flow to a broker because they give you a Bloomberg-style data terminal rather than because they execute well, you have quietly let your operating costs ride on your investors' best-execution rights. Allocators' operational reviewers know to ask about this; we treat that review in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto).

#### Worked example: judging an execution against the obligation

Maya's 50,000-share buy fills as a blend at a volume-weighted average price of \$40.06. The arrival price — the midpoint when she released the order — was \$40.00. So her **implementation shortfall** is 6 cents a share, or 0.15% of notional: on 50,000 shares that is \$3,000 of slippage on a \$2,000,000 position. Was that best execution? On price alone it looks like she "paid up" 6 cents. But the VWAP for the name over the trading window was \$40.07, so her algorithm actually *beat* the market's average by a cent, and she captured the position she wanted without spooking the stock — the close was \$40.30, well above her fill. A naive "did you get the lowest tick?" test would flag the 6-cent shortfall as a cost. The best-execution test asks the right question: given a 50,000-share order in this name, on this day, did the process seek favorable terms across price, impact, and likelihood of fill? Beating VWAP by a cent while moving the stock minimally is a defensible *yes*, and Maya's quarterly best-execution review will document exactly that comparison.

*Best execution is graded on the quality of the process and the blend of factors, not on whether a single fill hit the day's low.*

## Allocation

This is the section that turns a trading topic into a *founder integrity* topic, and it is where new managers most often stumble into a genuine regulatory breach without meaning to. The setup: most managers do not run a single pot of money. The Meridian Fund is the main vehicle, but Maya also manages a **separately managed account (SMA)** for the family office that seeded her — money held in the family office's own name, run pari passu with the fund. When she trades the industrials name, she is trading it for *both* accounts. The question operations asked her on day one — "how do you want it allocated?" — is the question.

If every order filled completely and instantly, allocation would be trivial: give each account its target share at the one price. The problem is the **partial fill**. When Maya wants 30,000 shares for the fund and the SMA combined but only 20,000 fill before the price runs away, somebody gets less than they wanted — and the order in which you assign the fills, and the prices you assign, decides who eats the worse executions. That decision is where favoritism, conscious or not, creeps in.

The rule is **fair allocation**, and the cleanest way to satisfy it is **pro-rata at a single blended price**. You compute the average execution price across all the fills, and you give every account its share of the *block* — typically pro-rata to the size each account wanted — at that one blended price. Nobody gets the cheap early fills and nobody gets stuck with the expensive late ones, because everyone gets the same average. Figure 5 contrasts the fair and unfair handling of exactly this situation.

![A before-and-after comparison of allocating a partial fill: the unfair side cherry-picks the cheap fills to one fund while the fair side gives every account the same blended price](/imgs/blogs/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage-5.png)

Why is the cherry-picking version not just unfair but illegal? Because as an investment adviser you owe a fiduciary duty to *every* client, and systematically steering the good fills to one account — say, the fund where you earn a performance fee, or your own personal account — is a fraud on the disadvantaged clients. The SEC has brought numerous **cherry-picking** cases against advisers who allocated profitable trades to favored accounts and losing trades to others, often disguised by delaying the allocation decision until *after* they saw which way the trade went. The defense is structural: **allocate before you know the outcome**, by a written, pre-disclosed methodology, and document the allocation at or near the time of the trade.

This is also why the **timing** of allocation matters as much as the method. Best practice is to record the intended allocation *when you place the order* (or immediately on execution), not at end of day after you have watched the position. An allocation decided after the fact is presumptively suspect, because the manager could have chosen who wins. Your administrator and your compliance program both check this; we cover the books-and-records side in [NAV, accounting, and the administrator](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator).

#### Worked example: allocating a partial fill pro-rata across two funds

Maya wants to buy the industrials name for two accounts: 20,000 shares for the Meridian Fund and 10,000 for the family-office SMA — a combined order of 30,000 shares, so the fund's target weight is two-thirds and the SMA's is one-third. The market only gives her **20,000 shares** before she stops chasing, filled in three tranches: 8,000 at \$99.50, 7,000 at \$100.00, and 5,000 at \$100.80. The blended average price is

> (8,000 × \$99.50 + 7,000 × \$100.00 + 5,000 × \$100.80) ÷ 20,000 = \$2,000,000 ÷ 20,000 = **\$100.00 per share**.

Pro-rata, the fund gets two-thirds of the 20,000 filled — **13,333 shares** — and the SMA gets one-third — **6,667 shares**, *both at the same \$100.00 blended price*. Neither account got the cheap \$99.50 tranche to itself; neither was stuck with the \$100.80 tranche. The unfair alternative — handing the 8,000 cheap shares to the fund (where Maya earns 20% of the profits) and the expensive ones to the SMA — would have saved the fund \$0.50 a share on 8,000 shares, roughly \$4,000, transferred straight out of the family office's pocket into Maya's incentive fee. Done once it is a few thousand dollars; done as a pattern it is a cherry-picking enforcement case and a barred adviser.

*Fair allocation means every account gets the same blended price on a partial fill, decided before anyone knows how the trade turned out.*

## The prime-broker relationship

Now we arrive at the counterparty this whole post has been circling. **The prime broker is the most important relationship a hedge fund has** — more important than any single investor, because an investor can redeem and hurt you, but a prime broker can *freeze your entire book*. The PB is not a place you send orders; it is the institution that finances your positions, holds your assets, lends you the stock you short, settles your trades, and opens doors to capital. Figure 2 shows the bundle.

![A graph showing the fund connected to one prime broker hub, which fans out to five services: financing, custody, stock loan, clearing, and capital introduction](/imgs/blogs/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage-2.png)

Walk the five services, because each one shapes what your fund can actually do.

**Financing and leverage.** When your fund holds more than its cash can buy, the PB has lent you the difference against your portfolio as collateral. This is the engine that lets a long/short equity book run gross exposure of 150–300% of capital. The PB sets the margin requirement and charges a financing spread on the borrowed balance. This is also where most of the PB's revenue from you comes from — not a negotiable flat fee but the financing spread and the borrow fees, which scale with how much you trade and how much leverage you use. It is why no prime treats financing as a commodity: the more you borrow and short, the more you are worth to them.

**Custody.** The PB holds your fund's cash and securities. This sounds clerical and is in fact one of the two most important controls in the whole structure: because the assets sit at a regulated institution rather than in an account the manager personally controls, an outsider — the administrator, the auditor, an allocator's operational reviewer — can independently confirm what the fund owns by going straight to the custodian. Self-custody, where the manager holds the assets directly, is the brightest red flag in operational due diligence and is exactly the gap that let Madoff fabricate statements for decades.

**Securities lending.** The PB's stock-loan desk sources the shares you need to short and charges you a borrow fee. If you run any short book at all, this capability is a hard constraint on which trades you can express — a short thesis you cannot borrow into is just an opinion. We give it its own section below.

**Clearing and settlement.** The PB clears and settles your trades — taking each execution from confirmation through to the actual exchange of cash and securities on T+1 — and reconciles the positions and balances. This is the back-office machinery that turns a pile of fills into a clean, agreed, settled book. It is worth slowing down on the steps, because they are where trades quietly *fail* if anyone is careless. Right after execution, the details of the trade — security, side, quantity, price, account — are sent to the executing broker as a **confirmation**, and your side **affirms** that the details match. Mismatches are caught here, while there is still time to fix them, rather than on settlement day when a break means a failed delivery. The affirmed trade then **clears** — in US equities through the National Securities Clearing Corporation, which nets each member's obligations down to a single daily payment and delivery per security so that thousands of offsetting trades collapse into a handful of net movements. On **settlement day** (T+1), the net cash and net securities actually change hands at the depository, and the position is finally *settled*: real, owned, and reflected in the books. The compression to T+1 in 2024 cut the time between trade and settlement in half, which is good for systemic risk but unforgiving operationally — there is now barely a day to catch and fix a break, which is exactly why the confirmation-and-affirmation step has to be tight. We cover the cash and reconciliation side in [cash, treasury, and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk).

**Capital introduction.** Most large primes run a "cap intro" team that introduces their hedge-fund clients to allocators at conferences and meetings. It is genuinely useful for a new fund with a thin Rolodex, but be clear-eyed: the PB introduces you because keeping you alive and trading is good for *their* financing revenue. A cap-intro meeting is a warm door, not a sale; the allocator still runs full investment and operational diligence.

Choosing a prime broker is therefore one of the highest-stakes decisions a founder makes before launch, and price is only one input. A small fund quickly learns that the big banks have **minimum-revenue thresholds**: a prime broker earns from your financing spread, your borrow fees, and your trading, and if your expected balances are too small to clear its minimum, a top-tier bank simply will not take you as a direct client. That is the gap **mini-primes and introducing brokers** fill — they aggregate many small funds behind a single big-bank custody arrangement, giving a sub-\$100M launch institutional-grade custody and clearing with far more attentive service than it could ever command on its own. As the fund grows, it can graduate to a direct relationship with a bulge-bracket prime and negotiate harder terms. The questions a founder should actually weigh are the strength and credit quality of the institution (because its failure is your tail risk), the depth and pricing of its stock-loan inventory in the names your strategy needs, the generosity of its financing and haircuts on your particular book, the quality of its operations and reporting, and only then the headline financing spread. An allocator's operational reviewer will ask which primes you use and why, and "they were the cheapest" is not the answer that builds confidence.

The founder's takeaway is that the PB is a *partner whose incentives are aligned with your survival and your activity* — and a *single point of failure* you must manage. A great PB relationship gets you generous financing, deep stock-loan inventory, attentive service, and warm introductions. A strained one can tighten your margin terms, recall your borrows at the worst moment, and — in the extreme — go down and take your assets with it. That tension is why the rest of this post is about managing the PB: pricing the financing, understanding the stock loan, and not betting your fund on a single counterparty.

## Financing and margin

Financing is where the cost of being a leveraged fund hides in plain sight, and where a sleepy founder gets a brutal wake-up call. Three numbers govern it: how much you borrow, what it costs, and how much collateral you must post against it.

Start with the borrowed balance. If Meridian has USD 60 million of equity and runs at, say, 1.8x gross long exposure, it holds about USD 108 million of stock and has borrowed roughly USD 48 million from the PB — a **debit balance** of \$48M. The PB charges interest on that debit balance at an **all-in financing rate**: a reference short-term rate plus a spread the PB negotiates with you based on your size, your activity, and how much your overall relationship is worth to them. In a higher-rate environment that all-in rate might be in the mid-single digits; the spread alone might be a fraction of a percent for a valued client and more for a small or low-activity one.

Then come the controls. The PB applies a **haircut** to each asset class when deciding how much collateral value your positions provide — a liquid large-cap long might get a small haircut and finance cheaply, while an illiquid or volatile position gets a punishing haircut, ties up far more of your capital, and may not be financeable at all. The PB also sets a **maintenance margin** requirement: a floor on your equity relative to your positions. If the book falls and your equity dips below that floor, the PB issues a **margin call** — a demand for more collateral, usually due same-day. Meet it and you carry on; fail to meet it and the PB has the contractual right to *liquidate your positions* to bring you back into line, selling into exactly the weakness that caused the call.

This is the mechanism that turns leverage from a friend into an executioner. Leverage magnifies your gains, but it also magnifies your losses *and* hands the timing of your forced selling to your prime broker's risk model rather than your own conviction. The discipline of running inside your financing — keeping a buffer above the maintenance margin, knowing your haircuts, stress-testing the book against a sharp move — is exactly the survival skill we frame in [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up). Figure 6 makes the cost of leverage concrete: every turn you add increases the borrowed balance, and the annual financing bill rises with it.

![A bar chart of annual financing cost rising with gross leverage on a one hundred million dollar book at a five point five percent financing rate, with the two times case highlighted at five point five million dollars per year](/imgs/blogs/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage-6.png)

#### Worked example: financing cost on a \$100M book at 2x leverage

Take a cleaner round case to see the cost. Suppose Meridian has grown to **\$100M** of equity capital and runs at **2x gross leverage**, so it holds **\$200M** of stock. The financed (borrowed) portion is the gross book minus the capital: \$200M − \$100M = **\$100M** of debit balance. At an all-in financing rate of **5.5%** a year, the annual financing cost is

> \$100M × 5.5% = **\$5.5M per year**.

Now compare that to the revenue line. On \$100M of AUM, a 1.5% management fee is \$1.5M a year — *less than a third* of the financing bill. The financing cost is not paid out of the management fee, of course; it is a cost borne by the fund and shows up as a drag on the fund's returns. But it reframes how a founder should think about leverage. Running at 2x does not just double your exposure to your alpha; it commits the fund to a \$5.5M annual hurdle that your strategy must clear *before* the leverage is accretive. If your unlevered edge is thin, leverage can turn a marginally profitable strategy into a money-loser once financing is paid. And if you push to 3x — \$200M borrowed — the financing bill jumps to \$11M a year. The chart's amber bars are not decoration; each one is a higher fixed hurdle you have volunteered to clear.

*Leverage borrows return from the future at a fixed price; at 5.5%, a 2x book on \$100M owes \$5.5M a year before it earns a dollar of alpha.*

#### Worked example: a margin call when the book drops

Now watch the executioner work. Meridian holds \$200M of stock against \$100M of equity, financed with \$100M of debit. Suppose the PB requires the fund to maintain equity of at least **30%** of the long market value. At the start, equity is \$100M against \$200M of stock — 50%, comfortably above the floor. Then the market drops **15%** across the book. The stock is now worth \$170M, but the \$100M debit balance does not shrink — debt does not fall when your assets do. So the equity is now \$170M − \$100M = **\$70M**, and as a share of the \$170M long value that is **41%** — still above the 30% floor, so no call yet. Push the drop to **30%**: the stock is worth \$140M, equity is \$140M − \$100M = **\$40M**, which is **29%** of \$140M — *below* the 30% floor. The PB issues a margin call for enough collateral to restore the 30% minimum, due that afternoon. Maya's choices are grim: wire in fresh cash she may not have, or sell positions into a falling market to pay down the debit — locking in losses and shrinking the book at the worst possible moment. This is precisely the dynamic that destroyed LTCM and, decades later, Archegos: leverage plus a sharp move equals forced selling on someone else's timetable.

*A margin call converts a paper loss into a forced sale, because your debt stays fixed while your collateral falls — and the prime broker, not you, decides when the clock runs out.*

## Securities lending

Here is the mechanic almost every new founder underestimates: you literally cannot sell a stock you do not own without first borrowing it. Shorting is not "negative buying"; it is a borrow-sell-buyback-return cycle, and every step depends on your prime broker's stock-loan desk. Figure 4 walks the whole sequence.

![A pipeline of the short-sale lifecycle: locate the shares, borrow them with a daily fee, sell short, hold the short while paying the fee, buy back to cover, return the borrowed shares, and realize the profit or loss](/imgs/blogs/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage-4.png)

Walk it step by step. First, the **locate**: before you are even allowed to short, your broker must confirm it can borrow the shares for you to deliver — US rules (Regulation SHO) require a reasonable basis to believe the security can be borrowed and delivered, precisely to prevent "naked" shorting where the seller never delivers. This is why Maya's trader had to clear the locate on her two short ideas *before* she could sell them; a short thesis with no available borrow is dead on arrival. Second, the **borrow**: the stock-loan desk lends you the shares from its own inventory or another client's, and a **borrow fee** begins to accrue daily, quoted as an annualized rate on the value borrowed. Third, you **sell short** — deliver the borrowed shares to the buyer and take in the sale proceeds. Fourth, you **hold the short**, posting margin against it and paying the borrow fee every day the position is open. Fifth, when you want out, you **buy back** the shares in the open market to **cover**. Sixth, you **return** the borrowed shares to the lender, closing the loan. Your profit or loss is the price you sold at minus the price you bought back at, *minus the borrow fee you paid the whole time*.

Two features of stock loan can wreck an otherwise correct short. The first is the **borrow fee itself**, which for **hard-to-borrow** names — heavily shorted stocks, small floats, meme names — can run from a few percent to, in extreme squeezes, tens or even hundreds of percent annualized. A short that is right on direction can still lose money if the borrow fee eats the move. The second is the **recall**: the lender can demand its shares back at any time, forcing you to either find another borrow or buy back the position immediately. In a short squeeze, recalls cascade exactly when the price is spiking, mechanically forcing shorts to cover into the rally — the **buy-in** — which is part of what makes squeezes so violent. The founder's lesson is that your PB's stock-loan inventory and pricing are a hard, dynamic constraint on your short book, not a back-office detail.

#### Worked example: a short sale via stock loan with a borrow fee

Maya is convinced a richly valued software name, trading at \$50, is heading lower. She shorts **10,000 shares**. The locate clears, the PB lends her the shares at a borrow fee of **8% annualized** (a moderately hard-to-borrow rate), and she sells them for **\$500,000** in proceeds. She holds the short for **90 days** — a quarter of a year — and the thesis works: the stock falls to **\$42**. She buys back 10,000 shares for **\$420,000** and returns them to close the loan.

Her gross gain is the \$500,000 sale minus the \$420,000 buy-back = **\$80,000**. But she paid a borrow fee the whole time. On \$500,000 of borrowed value at 8% a year, a quarter-year (90 days ≈ one-quarter) costs roughly

> \$500,000 × 8% × (90 ÷ 365) ≈ **\$9,863**.

So her net profit is about \$80,000 − \$9,863 = **\$70,137**. The 8% borrow shaved roughly 12% off her gross gain. Now run the same trade in a name with a **40%** borrow fee — a genuine special: the quarter's borrow cost would be about \$500,000 × 40% × (90 ÷ 365) ≈ **\$49,315**, eating well over half the \$80,000 move. And if the lender had recalled the shares on day 30 while the stock was briefly *up* at \$53, Maya might have been bought in at a loss before her thesis ever played out. The direction was right; the *financing of the short* decides whether being right pays.

*A short's profit is the price move minus the borrow fee minus the risk of recall — get the borrow wrong and a correct call still loses money.*

## Multi-prime

For most of its history the hedge-fund industry ran on a single prime broker per fund. One PB is simpler: one financing relationship, one custodian, one set of margin terms, one reconciliation, one feed into your books. For a sub-\$100M launch it is often the only practical choice, because primes have minimum-revenue thresholds and a tiny fund is not worth a big bank's full attention — which is why mini-primes and introducing brokers exist to serve the smallest funds. So why would any fund take on the operational pain of running several?

The answer has a date: **September 2008**, when Lehman Brothers failed. Lehman was a major prime broker, and hedge-fund clients who had concentrated their assets there discovered the brutal meaning of **rehypothecation**. Assets the funds had pledged to Lehman as collateral had been re-used — lent out and pledged onward by Lehman — and when Lehman went into bankruptcy, especially through its London entity, those rehypothecated assets were tangled in the estate and **frozen**. Funds that were solvent and right on every trade could not access their own positions for months or years because their single prime broker had failed. The lesson burned into the industry was that a prime broker is not just a service provider; it is a **counterparty whose own failure can take you down**, and concentrating everything at one is a survival risk independent of your trading. We treat the broader counterparty-risk discipline in [cash, treasury, and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk).

So serious funds went **multi-prime**: spreading assets, financing, and stock loan across two, three, or more prime brokers so that no single failure can freeze the whole book. The trade-offs are real and run both ways. Figure 3 lays them out.

![A matrix comparing single-prime and multi-prime across counterparty risk, operations, financing terms, and which funds each suits best](/imgs/blogs/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage-3.png)

On **counterparty risk**, multi-prime is decisively better: if one PB fails, only the slice of your assets there is at risk, and the rest of the book keeps running. On **operations**, multi-prime is harder and more expensive: you now reconcile two or three sets of positions and balances, aggregate them into one view of the fund, and manage collateral across firms — which is one reason multi-prime funds lean on their administrator and middle office to consolidate, the machinery we describe in [the operating model: front, middle, and back office](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office). On **financing terms**, multi-prime can be *better*: when two primes know they are competing for your balance, they sharpen their financing spreads and stock-loan rates, and you can route a given trade to whichever prime offers the best terms for that name. The honest summary is that single-prime is the right answer for the smallest funds because the operational simplicity and lower cost outweigh a counterparty risk they cannot yet afford to mitigate, while multi-prime becomes the standard as a fund grows past the point where one PB's failure would be an extinction event.

A practical wrinkle for short sellers: stock-loan inventory is not the same across primes. One PB might have deep, cheap borrow in a name your thesis needs while another cannot source it at all. Multi-prime funds therefore gain not just counterparty diversification but a *better menu of borrows*, routing each short to the prime with the inventory — yet another way the PB relationship, plural, shapes which trades the fund can actually express.

## Common misconceptions

**"Best execution means I have to get the lowest price."** No. Best execution is a process obligation across price, speed, likelihood of fill, cost, size, and venue, judged over time. A fill that "paid up" a few cents while beating VWAP and avoiding impact can be perfect best execution; a fill that grabbed the lowest tick by leaking your size and moving the stock can be terrible. What a regulator checks is whether you had a reasonable process and followed it — not whether you were clairvoyant on a single trade.

**"Allocation is an operational detail I can sort out at the end of the day."** This is the misconception that ends careers. Deciding *after* you have watched a trade who gets the good fills is the textbook pattern of cherry-picking enforcement cases. Allocation must be fair, pre-disclosed, and recorded at or near the time of the trade — pro-rata at a blended price is the clean default. The fairness of your allocation is a direct measure of whether you are honoring your fiduciary duty to every client, and it is examined.

**"The prime broker is just where I send orders — a commodity I pick on price."** The PB is the most important counterparty your fund has. It finances you, holds your assets, lends you stock to short, settles your trades, and can freeze your entire book if it fails. Its financing terms and stock-loan inventory directly determine what strategy you can run. Choosing a PB on the financing spread alone, ignoring the strength of the institution and the depth of its borrow, is choosing your fund's biggest single point of failure carelessly.

**"Shorting is just selling — I click sell and I'm short."** You cannot deliver shares you do not own. A short is a borrow-sell-buyback-return cycle that begins with a **locate** and runs on a daily **borrow fee**, and the lender can **recall** the shares at the worst possible moment. A short thesis you cannot borrow into is not a position; it is an opinion. The financing of the short, not just the direction, decides whether you make money.

**"Multi-prime is over-engineering for a small fund."** For the smallest launches, single-prime really is the right call — the operational cost and simplicity outweigh a counterparty risk you cannot yet afford to spread. But the instinct that multi-prime is *never* worth it is exactly the complacency Lehman punished. As you grow, concentrating every asset at one prime becomes an extinction risk independent of your trading, and the move to multi-prime is not gold-plating; it is survival insurance plus better financing competition.

**"Leverage is free money that multiplies my returns."** Leverage is borrowed return at a fixed price, and the price is the financing cost — \$5.5M a year on a \$100M book at 2x and 5.5%. It magnifies losses as well as gains, and it hands the timing of your forced selling to the prime broker's margin model. A thin unlevered edge can become a money-loser once financing is paid, and a sharp drop can trigger a margin call that forces you to sell into the very move you were positioned for.

## How it plays out in the real world

The mechanics above are not academic; they are the exact forces behind the most famous blow-ups in the business, and the daily reality of every operating fund.

**Lehman and the birth of multi-prime (2008).** When Lehman Brothers collapsed in September 2008, hedge funds that had concentrated their prime brokerage there found that assets pledged as collateral and **rehypothecated** by Lehman — especially through its UK entity, where rehypothecation rules were looser — were trapped in the bankruptcy estate. Solvent, well-run funds were locked out of their own positions for an extended period through no fault of their trading. The episode is why multi-prime went from a luxury to a standard for any fund large enough to run it, and why "where do my assets actually sit, and can they be re-used?" is now a first-order question in every prime-brokerage negotiation and every operational due-diligence review.

**Archegos and hidden multi-prime leverage (2021).** The multi-prime structure that protects a fund from one PB's failure can also *hide* a fund's true leverage from each PB — and that cuts the other way. Bill Hwang's family office, Archegos, built enormous concentrated positions through **total-return swaps** spread across several prime brokers, so that no single PB saw the whole picture or the total leverage. When the underlying stocks fell, the margin calls hit all the primes at once; Archegos could not meet them, and the forced unwind cost the prime brokers more than **USD 10 billion** in losses — Credit Suisse alone lost roughly **USD 5.5 billion**, plus Nomura, Morgan Stanley, and UBS. Hwang was later convicted of fraud. The lesson is the dark mirror of the Lehman lesson: multi-prime protects *you* from a single counterparty, but it can blind *each* counterparty to your total risk, which is why prime brokers' risk models and regulators' reporting (the systemic-risk reporting we cover in [registration and regulation: SEC, CFTC, and Reg D](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d)) now work so hard to see leverage across the whole system.

**The everyday version.** Strip away the disasters and the same mechanics run quietly every day at every fund. A long/short manager checks her available borrow before building a short basket, because half her ideas are in hard-to-borrow names and the borrow fees will determine which ones are worth expressing. A multi-strategy fund routes a block to whichever of its three primes offers the best financing on that asset class that week. An operations team reconciles three sets of prime-broker positions every morning before the administrator strikes a preliminary NAV. A compliance officer pulls a sample of partial fills each quarter to confirm they were allocated pro-rata and recorded at trade time. And a CFO watches the maintenance-margin buffer like a hawk, knowing that a 30% drop in a 2x book triggers a same-day margin call and that the difference between meeting it with cash and meeting it with forced sales is the difference between a bad quarter and a dead fund.

There is one more real-world pattern worth naming, because it is the most common and the least dramatic: the trade that goes *almost* right. A position fills cleanly, allocates pro-rata, and settles on time — but the financing on it is 80 basis points wider than a competing prime would have charged, because the founder never put the relationship out to a second bid. Over a year on a \$100M debit balance, those 80 basis points are \$800,000 of return quietly leaked to the prime broker, a sum that rivals the entire management fee on a small fund. No headline, no enforcement action, no blow-up — just a fund that compounds a little slower than it should because the founder treated the back half of the trade lifecycle as someone else's problem. The disasters teach the dramatic lessons; this is the lesson that costs ordinary funds money every single day.

The thread through all of it is the spine of this whole series: a fund is a business that must keep its promise *credible*, its structure *sound*, and its capital *sticky*. The trade lifecycle is where soundness lives. A founder can pick beautifully and still be undone by an unfair allocation that draws an enforcement action, a short she could not borrow, a financing cost that quietly ate her edge, or a single prime broker whose failure froze her book. Mastering the path from order to settled position is not back-office trivia delegated to someone else; it is a core part of keeping the promise the fund was built to sell.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you stop being an employee whose only job is the trade and become a founder responsible for the *whole* path from decision to settled position. Before you place your first order as a fund, you need a best-execution policy you can defend, a written and pre-disclosed allocation methodology, a prime-brokerage agreement you have actually read (especially the rehypothecation and margin clauses), and a clear-eyed plan for whether one prime is enough. Once you are live, this is the machinery your operations team runs every day and the machinery an allocator's operational reviewer will probe hardest — because great returns sitting on a sloppy trade lifecycle is exactly the fund they have learned to decline.
