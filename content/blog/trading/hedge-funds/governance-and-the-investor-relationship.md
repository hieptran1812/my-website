> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Governance is a control that decides who can move the money and who can rewrite the rules, and keeping those two powers in different hands is what keeps capital from running in a crisis.
>
> - The offshore board of directors oversees the fund and signs off on the actions that hurt investors most — gates, suspensions, valuation, fee changes — so a captive board (the manager's lawyer and two friends) is worse than no board at all.
> - The limited-partner advisory committee (LPAC) is the investors' voice on conflicts, valuation policy, and terms; it has real power over the manager precisely where the manager's interest and the investors' interest split.
> - A side letter that quietly gives one investor better terms — a fee break, first-out liquidity, position-level transparency — becomes a fund-wide entitlement the moment another investor holds a most-favored-nation (MFN) clause, and it becomes a trust crisis the moment the others find out.
> - The number to remember: a 0.5% fee break to one `\$50M` anchor LP, cascaded through MFN clauses, cost the Meridian Fund about `\$1.0M` a year on `\$300M` of assets — and the reputational cost dwarfed the cash.

Maya runs the Meridian Fund. It is a long/short equity fund, three years old, a little over `\$300M` in assets, and until last Tuesday she would have told you her investors trusted her. Then a `\$40M` foundation submitted a redemption notice during a rough quarter, and Meridian's administrator, processing the request, noticed something. The fund's largest investor — a `\$50M` family office that had anchored the launch — had a side letter granting it *first-out* liquidity: in any gating event, that one investor got paid before anyone else. None of the other limited partners knew. The foundation's investment officer found out the way investors always find out about these things — during the scramble, at the worst possible moment, from someone who let it slip.

The foundation did not redeem because Meridian's returns were bad. They were fine. The foundation redeemed because it learned that in a crisis it was standing behind a hidden investor it had never been told about, and it no longer trusted the structure it was sitting inside. Two other LPs followed within a month. The fund did not blow up on a trade. It bled out on a governance failure — a secret term that broke trust, and trust, once it goes, takes the capital with it.

This post is about the machinery that was supposed to prevent that: the board of directors, the LP advisory committee, genuine transparency, and the side-letter and most-favored-nation problem. The thesis is simple and it runs through everything below. **Governance is a control, not a formality.** It exists to separate the power to move money from the power to write the rules, so that no single party — least of all the manager — holds both. When that separation is real, investors stay through a drawdown. When it is theater, they run at the first sign of trouble. Figure 1 is the wiring diagram for the whole post.

## Foundations: the governance of a fund

Before the mechanics, the vocabulary. A reader who can run a book may never have seen how the *business* of a fund is governed, so we build every term from zero.

**Governance** is the system of bodies, rights, and rules that decides who can authorize what inside the fund. It is distinct from *management* (running the money) and from *administration* (calculating the books). Governance answers a single question: when the manager wants to do something that helps the manager and hurts the investors, who can say no?

**The fund** is the legal vehicle the investors' money lives in — typically a Cayman company for the offshore feeder, a Delaware limited partnership for the onshore feeder, both pooling into a master fund. The investors are **limited partners (LPs)** in the onshore vehicle and **shareholders** in the offshore one; for this post we will call them all LPs or investors. The **manager** — the management company, acting as general partner and as the investment adviser — runs the money and earns the fees. The split between the fund (where the money is) and the manager (who runs it) is covered in [the fund structure](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund) and [domestic vs offshore](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder); here we care about the bodies that sit between them.

**The board of directors** is the governing body of the offshore fund — a Cayman company has directors the way any company does. The board's job is to oversee the fund on behalf of its shareholders, approve the things that matter most (the appointment of service providers, the audited accounts, and crucially the *discretionary* actions like gating or suspending redemptions), and check the manager. An **independent director** is one with no employment, ownership, or family tie to the manager. Offshore boards are usually a mix of one manager representative and two or more independent directors supplied by professional directorship firms in the Cayman Islands.

**The limited-partner advisory committee (LPAC)** is a committee of a handful of the fund's larger or earliest investors, formed to represent the LP base on matters where the manager has a conflict of interest — valuation of hard-to-price assets, related-party transactions, conflicts waivers, and sometimes amendments to terms. The LPAC is not a board and usually cannot fire the manager, but on the specific conflict matters the fund documents assign to it, its consent is required. It is the institutionalized voice of the investors.

**A side letter** is a private agreement between the manager and one investor that modifies that investor's terms — outside the standard subscription documents every other investor signs. Side letters are legal and common. They are also the single most dangerous governance instrument in a fund, because they create *unequal* investors inside a vehicle whose investors believe they are equal.

**The most-favored-nation (MFN) clause** is a term — itself often granted in a side letter — that entitles an investor to receive any better term the manager grants to any other investor of the same or smaller size. It is the investors' defense against secret side letters: if you have an MFN, a favor to someone else becomes a favor to you. It is also the mechanism that turns one side letter into a fund-wide cost, as we will see.

**Fiduciary duty** is the legal obligation the manager owes the fund and its investors to act in their interest, not the manager's own, with care and loyalty. It is the backdrop against which every governance question is judged. **Transparency** is the degree to which investors can see what the fund actually holds, how it is priced, and what risk it carries. **The key-person provision** is a term giving investors rights (often the right to redeem or to suspend new investment) if a named person — usually the founder — stops running the fund. Each of these is a governance control. Figure 2 lays out which body polices which.

![Matrix showing the offshore board, LPAC, administrator, auditor, and manager, each with who they answer to and what they police](/imgs/blogs/governance-and-the-investor-relationship-2.png)

The structure is deliberately fragmented. The board oversees the fund. The LPAC speaks for the investors on conflicts. The administrator (independent) owns the NAV. The auditor (independent) verifies the assets exist. The manager runs the money and is the party every other body is built to check. No single party can both move the money and rewrite the rules. That fragmentation is not bureaucratic accident — it *is* the control.

## Why governance is a control

Markets people are trained to think about controls. A stop-loss is a control. A position limit is a control. A control is a thing that constrains your future self when your future self is tempted, frightened, or simply wrong. Governance is exactly that, applied to the manager rather than to the book.

The manager has a built-in conflict with the investors, and it is not a moral failing — it is structural. The manager earns a management fee on assets, so the manager wants assets to stay high, which means the manager wants to *delay* paying out redemptions in a stress. The manager earns an incentive fee on gains, so the manager is tempted to mark illiquid positions optimistically near a crystallization date. The manager's reputation rides on the fund surviving, so the manager will be tempted to gate, suspend, or side-pocket to stop a run — actions that protect the manager's franchise but trap the investors' money. Every one of these temptations is strongest exactly when the fund is in trouble, which is exactly when the investors most need protection. The fee mechanics behind these temptations are in [fees and terms](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark); the liquidity tools the manager is tempted to reach for are in [liquidity terms](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets).

Governance is the answer to those temptations. The board sits above the manager and must approve the discretionary actions. The LPAC sits beside the investors and must consent to the conflict decisions. The administrator owns the NAV so the manager cannot mark its own book. The auditor checks once a year that the assets are real. Each body removes one of the manager's temptations from the manager's sole control.

And the payoff of all this is a single word: **trust**. Trust is not sentiment. Trust is the technical reason capital is sticky. An investor who trusts the structure will sit through a 20% drawdown because she believes the manager cannot quietly disadvantage her while she waits. An investor who does not trust the structure redeems at the first bad quarter, because the only protection she has is to get her money out before someone else does. Governance converts a fund's investor base from a herd that stampedes into a coalition that holds. Figure 3 shows the same fund, the same drawdown, under weak and strong governance.

![Before-and-after comparison of a fund in a 20 percent drawdown under weak governance versus strong governance, where weak governance leads to capital running and strong governance keeps it](/imgs/blogs/governance-and-the-investor-relationship-3.png)

Notice what is identical across the two columns: the strategy, the returns, the drawdown. The only difference is whether anyone but the manager has a vote. In the left column the manager acts alone, the hidden side letter surfaces, and the capital runs. In the right column an independent board and an LPAC stand between the manager and the panic, the terms are equal, and the capital stays. The drawdown did not kill the fund. The absence of governance did.

This is why an allocator's operational due diligence team — the [ODD veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto) — spends as much time on governance as on the trading. A fund with a brilliant strategy and a captive board is, to an ODD analyst, a fund that will disadvantage its investors the day it gets scared. They have seen it before. They will pass.

There is a deeper point here that markets people often miss because they spend their careers on the asset side. The reason a hedge fund can charge two-and-twenty at all is that it sells an *aligned promise*: the manager will compound the investor's capital with skill, inside a structure that protects the investor when the manager's interest and the investor's interest diverge. The performance is the credible part of the promise — it is what the manager controls and what the track record demonstrates. The structure is the sound part — it is what governance delivers. An investor is not only buying Maya's stock-picking; she is buying the assurance that Maya cannot, when frightened, gate a liquid fund, mark an illiquid book up before a fee crystallizes, or let a secret investor jump the redemption queue. Strip the governance away and the promise loses half its content: the investor is now buying naked manager discretion, which is worth far less and commands far lower fees, if it can be sold at all. Governance is not a tax on the promise; it is the half of the promise the manager cannot make with her returns alone.

This reframes the cost question entirely. A launching manager underwater on the high-water mark, counting every dollar of fixed cost, looks at independent directors and an audited NAV process and an LPAC and sees overhead eating into a thin management fee. What she is actually looking at is the price of being able to charge institutional fees and raise institutional capital. A fund without credible governance is not a cheaper version of a fund with it; it is a different, smaller, more fragile business that tops out at the friends-and-family capital that does not run ODD. The governance spend is the entry ticket to the part of the market that has the money.

## The offshore board

Start with the board, because most launching managers treat it as a checkbox and most allocators treat it as a litmus test.

The offshore feeder — the Cayman company most non-US and tax-exempt US investors use — is a company, and a company has directors. Those directors owe their duties to the *fund and its shareholders*, not to the manager. In Cayman practice, the board is typically two or three directors, usually drawn from professional directorship firms (the well-known ones supply directors to thousands of funds), plus sometimes one representative of the manager. The professional directors are independent: they have no economic stake in the manager's fees, and they sit on the boards of many unrelated funds, so their reputation depends on not rubber-stamping bad behavior at any one of them.

What does the board actually do? On a quiet day, very little that is visible: it meets quarterly, reviews the financials, confirms the service providers are in place, and approves the audited accounts. The board earns its existence on the *non-quiet* day. The fund's articles and the offering documents reserve certain actions to the board's approval — and they are precisely the actions that hurt investors:

- **Suspending the calculation of NAV or the payment of redemptions.** A manager cannot, in a well-governed fund, simply stop honoring redemptions. The board must approve a suspension, and the board must be satisfied it is in the interest of *all* shareholders, not just the manager's desire to stop a run.
- **Imposing a gate.** Limiting redemptions to a percentage of the fund is a discretionary action; in many structures the board signs off.
- **Approving the valuation policy and, for hard-to-value assets, the actual marks** — often on advice from the LPAC, the administrator, and the auditor.
- **Appointing and removing service providers** — the administrator, the auditor, the prime brokers — so the manager cannot fire the watchdog that is watching it.

The board is, in other words, the body that stands between the manager and the investors at the exact moments the manager is most conflicted. An independent board that takes its duties seriously will sometimes tell the manager no. A captive board — the manager's lawyer, the manager's friend, and the manager's brother-in-law — will say yes to anything, which is worse than no board, because it gives investors the false comfort of governance without the substance.

The legal weight behind the board is worth understanding, because it is what makes the directors more than figureheads. A Cayman director owes fiduciary duties to the company — the fund — and through it to the shareholders. The duties are the standard corporate ones: to act in good faith in what the director considers the best interests of the company, to exercise the care, skill, and diligence a reasonably diligent person would, and to avoid conflicts. A director who rubber-stamps a manager's self-serving gate is not merely lax; he is potentially in breach of duty, and because the professional directors sit on dozens of boards and carry insurance and reputations, that exposure is real to them in a way it is not to a friend doing the manager a favor. This is the structural reason an *independent* director behaves differently from a captive one: the independent director has something to lose by saying yes when he should say no.

A recurring debate among launching managers is how many directors to appoint and whether to put a manager representative on the board at all. The institutional norm has moved toward boards with a clear independent majority — two or three independent professional directors, and at most one manager representative — precisely so the manager cannot carry a vote on the discretionary actions. There has also been a long-running concern in the industry about "jumbo" directors who sit on hundreds of fund boards and cannot plausibly give each one real attention; allocators increasingly ask how many boards a proposed director serves and whether the directorship firm has the capacity to engage when it matters. For a launching manager, the practical move is to hire a reputable directorship firm, insist on directors who will actually convene and deliberate in a stress, and resist the temptation to stack the board with allies — because the allocator's ODD team will ask exactly these questions, and the wrong answers read as a manager who wants the appearance of governance without the constraint.

#### Worked example: the board vetoes the gate the manager wanted

Meridian hits a bad stretch. A crowded short squeezes, the fund is down 14% on the quarter, and `\$45M` of redemption requests land for the quarter-end — about 15% of the fund's `\$300M`. Maya is frightened. If she pays all `\$45M`, she is a forced seller into a market that is already against her, and selling her most liquid names to fund redemptions leaves the remaining investors holding a *less* liquid, more concentrated book. Her instinct is to gate: invoke the 10% fund-level gate in the documents, pay out `\$30M` (10% of `\$300M`), and roll the remaining `\$15M` to next quarter.

Under Meridian's documents, a gate requires the offshore board's approval. Maya brings it to the board. The independent directors ask the questions she did not want to ask herself. *Is the book genuinely illiquid, or is gating just convenient?* The administrator confirms the fund's positions are 90% liquidatable within five trading days. *Will gating protect remaining investors, or simply protect the management fee on assets that would otherwise leave?* The board concludes the liquidity is there, that gating a liquid book would signal panic and *accelerate* redemptions next quarter, and that the only party gating clearly protects is the manager's fee base. The board declines to approve the gate. Maya pays the full `\$45M`, takes the pain, and — because she did not gate a liquid fund — keeps the rest of her investors, who saw a manager honor her terms under stress.

*A captive board would have rubber-stamped the gate, protected `\$15M` of fees this quarter, and triggered a run the next; the independent board cost Maya a hard quarter and saved her the fund.*

Two practical notes for a launching manager. First, independent directors cost money — a few tens of thousands of dollars a year for a small fund — and they are worth it many times over, both because allocators demand them and because the day you most want someone to stop you is the day you are least able to stop yourself. Second, the board is only as independent as its composition: a board where the manager controls the majority of votes is independent in name only, and a sophisticated allocator will count the votes.

## The LPAC

If the board is the investors' protection at the fund level, the LP advisory committee is the investors' *voice* at the conflict level.

The LPAC is more common in private-equity and credit funds than in liquid hedge funds, but it appears in hedge funds with meaningful illiquid holdings, side pockets, or large institutional investors who demand it. It is a committee of a handful of investors — usually the largest, or a representative cross-section — convened to advise on and, on specified matters, *consent to* decisions where the manager has a conflict of interest. The fund documents define exactly which matters require LPAC consent, because an open-ended advisory committee with no defined powers is just a mailing list.

The matters typically reserved to the LPAC are the conflict matters:

- **Valuation of hard-to-value assets.** When a position has no clean market price — a private placement, a stressed bond, a side-pocketed name — the manager's mark drives the NAV, which drives the fees. The LPAC reviews and, in some structures, must approve the valuation methodology or the specific marks, so the manager is not grading its own homework. This conflict is the subject of its own deep dive, [valuation and conflicts](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/valuation-and-conflicts-governance).
- **Related-party transactions.** If the manager wants the fund to transact with an affiliate, the LPAC's consent removes the suspicion that the manager is moving value to itself.
- **Conflict waivers generally.** When a conflict arises that the documents did not anticipate, the LPAC can consent on behalf of the LP base, which protects the manager (it has cover) and the investors (a disinterested body looked at it).
- **Term amendments and, critically, the existence and content of side letters** — in funds where the LPAC is given visibility into them.

The LPAC's power is narrow but sharp. It cannot direct the investments. It usually cannot fire the manager. But on the specific conflict questions the documents hand it, the manager genuinely needs its consent, and a manager who runs roughshod over the LPAC has told every large investor in the room that their voice does not matter — which is how large investors decide to leave.

There is a tension worth naming. The LPAC is usually composed of the *largest* investors, and the largest investors are precisely the ones most likely to hold their own side letters. A committee meant to protect all investors that is staffed by the investors most likely to have cut private deals is a governance structure with a conflict baked into it. A well-run fund manages this by (a) giving the LPAC a duty to the whole LP base, not to its members' own positions, and (b) — the strong-governance move — disclosing all side letters to the LPAC, so the committee that polices conflicts at least knows where the conflicts are.

It is worth being precise about what the LPAC's consent does and does not buy the manager. When the documents require LPAC consent for a conflicted action and the manager obtains it, the manager gains a powerful defense: a disinterested body of sophisticated investors, advised by counsel, looked at the conflict and approved it. That is close to the gold standard for a private fund, short of going to court. But the consent is only as good as the disclosure behind it. An LPAC that approves a valuation methodology without being shown the actual marks, or consents to a related-party transaction without seeing the terms, has not really discharged its function — it has provided cover without scrutiny, which is the LPAC version of a captive board. The manager who wants the protection the LPAC's consent provides has to give the LPAC enough information to refuse, because a consent that could never have been a refusal protects no one and impresses no allocator.

#### Worked example: the LPAC checks a year-end valuation

Meridian holds one illiquid position — a `\$15M` stake in a private placement that has not traded in months. It is December, the incentive fee crystallizes on December 31, and Maya's model supports marking the position anywhere from `\$15M` to `\$19M`. The difference is not small for her: the fund is up 9% on the year before this position, and on `\$300M` of assets the gap between a `\$15M` mark and a `\$19M` mark is `\$4M` of NAV, which at a 20% incentive fee is roughly `\$800,000` of fee to the manager — payable now, on a mark that is her own judgment. Under Meridian's documents, any valuation of a Level-3 position above the independent administrator's default methodology requires LPAC review. Maya brings her `\$19M` mark to the LPAC. The committee, advised by the administrator and a third-party valuation agent, finds the supporting comparables stale and the discount-rate assumption aggressive, and consents only to a `\$16M` mark. The `\$3M` difference comes back out of the NAV, and roughly `\$600,000` of incentive fee Maya could have booked on an aggressive mark simply does not crystallize.

*The LPAC did not accuse Maya of anything; it simply ensured that the one person who profits from a high mark was not the only person who got to set it — which is the entire reason the committee exists.*

## Transparency and reporting

Transparency is the governance control investors experience every month, and it is the one most managers under-deliver and most allocators over-demand. The two are connected: transparency is negotiated, not standard, and what you get depends on who you are.

At the floor, every investor gets a **monthly statement**: their capital balance, the month's net return, and the fund's NAV per share. They get **audited annual financial statements** — prepared by an independent auditor, the canonical check that the assets the manager claims exist actually exist (the absence of this is the heart of the Madoff fraud, of which more below). Most funds add a **monthly or quarterly letter** with commentary, gross and net exposure by some bucketing (long/short, sector, region), and top-level risk numbers.

Above the floor, transparency becomes a negotiation. A large institutional investor — a `\$100M` pension allocation — will demand far more than a `\$2M` individual: position-level holdings (sometimes through a third-party aggregator so the manager does not hand its book to a potential leaker), the full risk file (VaR, stress tests, concentration, leverage), regular calls with the portfolio manager and the risk officer, and the right to do an annual on-site operational review. A small investor gets the tear-sheet. The difference is not arbitrary; it tracks the size of the ticket and the sophistication of the allocator, and much of it is written into a side letter. Figure 7 is the ladder.

![Stack diagram of the transparency ladder from the monthly tear-sheet that every LP gets down to full position-level access available only by side letter](/imgs/blogs/governance-and-the-investor-relationship-7.png)

There are honest reasons transparency is tiered. Full position-level transparency to every investor would let a redeeming investor front-run the fund's liquidation, or let a competitor reverse-engineer the strategy. The manager protects the fund — and the *remaining* investors — by not broadcasting the book. That is legitimate. The governance problem is not that transparency is tiered; it is when the *existence* of the higher tiers is hidden, so an investor on Tier 2 does not know that another investor is quietly sitting on Tier 4 with information she cannot get and would price into her own decision to stay or go.

There is also a *cadence* to transparency that governs the relationship as much as the content does, and a launching manager underestimates it at her peril. Investors do not only want to see the numbers; they want to see them *on time, consistently, and without surprises*. A fund that delivers its monthly statement on the fifth business day every month, its audited annuals by the contractual deadline, and its risk file when promised has, through sheer reliability, told its investors that the back office is sound — and a sound back office is what an investor cannot verify directly and most fears is broken. Conversely, a late NAV, a restated number, or a missed reporting deadline is a five-alarm signal to an institutional investor, because operational sloppiness is correlated with operational fraud and the investor cannot tell the two apart from the outside. The discipline of reliable reporting is itself a governance control: it is the manager continuously demonstrating that the machinery works. This is why the mechanics of the NAV process and the independent administrator — covered in [NAV, accounting, and the administrator](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator) — are inseparable from the investor relationship; the administrator is not just a bookkeeper, it is the independent party whose monthly output is the proof of soundness the investor is paying for.

The regulatory backdrop matters here too. Whatever performance figures the manager *advertises* are governed by the SEC's Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1), which since its late-2022 compliance date requires, among much else, that net-of-fee performance be shown alongside gross — a transparency floor the regulator imposes regardless of what any side letter says. A manager cannot privately promise one investor a rosier performance presentation than the rule allows. Beyond the rule, the voluntary standard institutions look for is GIPS — the Global Investment Performance Standards — a discipline for calculating and presenting a track record in a way that is comparable across managers and resistant to cherry-picking. A fund that maintains GIPS-compliant performance has, again, converted transparency into a credibility asset: it is telling the allocator that its numbers are not just disclosed but disclosed under a standard the allocator trusts.

#### Worked example: what a `\$100M` pension demands versus a small LP

A public pension is considering a `\$100M` allocation to Meridian — a third of the fund, and a transformational ticket. Its consultant runs the operational due diligence, and the transparency demands are non-negotiable: monthly position-level holdings delivered through a risk aggregator within ten business days; the full risk file monthly (gross `\$X`, net `\$Y`, 99% one-day VaR, the top-ten concentrations, the leverage); a quarterly call with Maya and a separate quarterly call with the risk officer; an annual on-site; and the right to redeem if the reporting lapses. Meridian's smallest investor, a `\$2M` individual, gets the monthly tear-sheet and the audited annuals — and nothing else, because that is what `\$2M` buys and because the fund will not hand its book to fifty small accounts.

The pension's demands cost Maya real operational effort — a dedicated investor-relations and risk-reporting capability — but they are also a gift: a fund that can satisfy a pension's transparency regime is a fund that has institutional-grade reporting for *everyone*, and that is a selling point to the next allocator. The asymmetry is fine and expected. What is *not* fine is if the small LP is never told that the pension can see the whole book in near-real time while she sees a one-line return a month later — because in a stress, information is the difference between getting out first and getting out last.

*Transparency is a control because asymmetric information is a weapon; the governance failure is not that big LPs see more, but that small LPs are not told the asymmetry exists.*

## Side letters and the MFN problem

Now the hard part, the part that broke Meridian in the hook. A side letter is a private contract between the manager and one investor that changes that investor's terms. It is legal, ubiquitous, and — handled badly — corrosive.

Why do side letters exist? Because investors are not interchangeable. A `\$50M` anchor investor who commits before launch is taking a risk the `\$2M` investor in year three is not, and wants to be paid for it — usually with a fee break (a founders' share class, 1.5% / 15% or 1% / 10% instead of 2 and 20). A large institution has policy requirements — a regulatory carve-out, a confirmation it will get certain notices, a most-favored-nation clause to protect itself — that the documents do not provide for. A sovereign wealth fund will not invest without reserved capacity. None of these is sinister on its own. The terms investors negotiate cluster into four buckets, shown in Figure 5.

![Grid of the four kinds of side-letter term that investors negotiate: fee breaks, liquidity, transparency, and capacity, with examples in each](/imgs/blogs/governance-and-the-investor-relationship-5.png)

The four buckets differ in *who* pays for them. A **fee break** costs the *manager* — it is the manager's revenue the investor is taking. A **transparency** grant or a **capacity** reservation also mostly costs the manager (effort, foregone flexibility). But a **liquidity** preference — a shorter lock-up, or worst of all a *first-out* priority in a gate — costs the *other investors*, because liquidity is a shared and finite resource: every dollar one investor gets out first in a stress is a dollar of liquidity not available to the rest. A fee break is the manager's business to give. A liquidity preference is a redistribution among investors who believe they are equal, and that is why it is the side-letter term that detonates trust.

This is where the **most-favored-nation clause** enters. An MFN says: *if you grant any other investor of my size or smaller a better term, I get it too.* Sophisticated investors insist on an MFN precisely because they cannot see the other side letters; the MFN is their blind defense against being quietly disadvantaged. And the MFN is what turns one private favor into a fund-wide cascade. The moment the manager grants a better term to investor A, every MFN-holding investor of equal or smaller size is contractually entitled to the same term. Figure 4 traces the chain.

![Pipeline showing how a side letter granting one investor a better term combines with another investor's most-favored-nation clause to spread the term to all MFN holders](/imgs/blogs/governance-and-the-investor-relationship-4.png)

The cascade is the reason side letters cannot be treated as harmless one-off accommodations. A manager who hands out fee breaks one investor at a time, thinking each is a contained private deal, can wake up to find the breaks have propagated across the entire MFN-holding base — and that the fund's *effective* fee schedule is the lowest one it ever signed, applied to everyone who thought to ask for an MFN.

#### Worked example: an MFN fee break cascades across the LP base

Meridian's standard management fee is 1.5%. To anchor the launch, Maya gives the `\$50M` family office a 0.5% break — 1.0% instead of 1.5% — in a side letter. She thinks of it as a `\$250,000`-a-year cost (0.5% of `\$50M`), a fair price for `\$50M` of day-one capital. But three other investors hold MFN clauses: an `\$80M` fund-of-funds, a `\$40M` endowment, and a `\$30M` family office. Each is the same size or larger or smaller in the band the MFN covers, and each is entitled to the better 1.0% rate. The break cascades:

| Investor | AUM | Fee given up at 1.0% vs 1.5% |
|---|---|---|
| Anchor family office | `\$50M` | `\$250,000` |
| MFN fund-of-funds | `\$80M` | `\$400,000` |
| MFN endowment | `\$40M` | `\$200,000` |
| MFN family office | `\$30M` | `\$150,000` |
| **Total cascade** | `\$200M` | **`\$1,000,000`** |

What Maya scoped as a `\$250,000` accommodation became a `\$1.0M`-a-year reduction in management-fee revenue once the MFN cascade ran — four times the cost she planned, on `\$200M` of the fund's `\$300M`. Figure 6 shows the spread investor by investor.

![Bar chart comparing each investor's management-fee revenue at the standard 1.5 percent versus the 1.0 percent rate that cascades through most-favored-nation clauses, totaling about one million dollars a year](/imgs/blogs/governance-and-the-investor-relationship-6.png)

*A side letter is never a deal with one investor; through the MFN clause it is a quiet repricing of everyone who had the foresight to ask for protection.*

#### Worked example: the first-out liquidity preference and its effect on the others

Now the term that broke the fund. The `\$50M` anchor's side letter contained not just the fee break but a *first-out* liquidity preference: in any gating or suspension event, the anchor is paid in full before any gate applies to anyone else. Consider a stress in which `\$90M` of redemptions arrive against a `\$300M` fund and Maya gates at 10% — `\$30M` of liquidity available this quarter. Without the side letter, the `\$30M` is shared pro-rata: every redeeming investor gets the same fraction. With the side letter, the anchor's `\$50M` is paid *first* — but the gate only released `\$30M`, so the anchor consumes the entire quarter's liquidity and the other redeeming investors get *nothing* this quarter, rolling to next quarter behind an investor they did not know existed.

The cash math is bad enough: the other LPs' liquidity, in the stress, is not 10% — it is zero, because a hidden investor stands in front of them. But the trust math is fatal. When the foundation's officer discovers the first-out term during the redemption scramble, the discovery does not merely cost the foundation its place in line. It tells the foundation that the fund's *stated* terms — pari passu, everyone equal in a gate — were a fiction, and that there may be other fictions it has not yet found. The foundation redeems what it can, and so does everyone else who learns, because the only protection against an unknown number of hidden preferences is to leave before the next one surfaces.

*A first-out liquidity side letter does not just reorder the queue; it converts every other investor's stated terms into terms they can no longer trust, which is the one thing a fund cannot survive.*

This is why strong-governance funds handle side letters in a specific way: every side letter is disclosed to the offshore board and the LPAC; liquidity preferences that disadvantage other investors are simply refused (fee breaks, which cost only the manager, are fine; first-out liquidity, which costs other investors, is not); and an MFN-election mechanism is built so that every MFN holder is *offered* every applicable better term in writing, turning the cascade from a hidden landmine into a managed, disclosed process. The cost of doing it right is real — `\$1.0M` of cascaded fees in Maya's case. The cost of doing it wrong is the fund.

The disciplined MFN-election process deserves a closer look, because it is the single mechanism that converts the side-letter problem from a hidden risk into a managed one, and a launching manager who builds it correctly never has a Meridian moment. The mechanics are unglamorous and they work. The manager maintains a **side-letter register** — a single document listing every side letter, the investor, and each non-standard term, scoped by the size band it applies to. Whenever a new better term is granted, the manager runs the register and identifies every MFN holder entitled to elect it. Each entitled investor is sent the term in writing and given a window — typically thirty days — to elect it or decline. The terms that have *cost* implications are tiered by size, so that a term offered to a `\$50M` investor is available to investors of `\$50M` and below, but a `\$200M` sovereign's bespoke capacity right is not handed to a `\$2M` individual. Done this way, the cascade still happens — Maya still gives up her `\$1.0M` — but it happens *openly*, on the manager's schedule, with every investor seeing that the fund treats equals equally. The trust survives precisely because nothing is hidden. Compare that with the Meridian failure: the same `\$1.0M` of economics, but discovered by accident, in a crisis, by an investor who concluded the whole structure was a fiction. Identical cash, opposite outcome — and the only variable was disclosure.

There is one more discipline worth stating plainly, because launching managers get it wrong under the pressure of closing capital: the time to refuse a corrosive term is *before* you sign it, not after the cascade. A first-out liquidity preference, a side letter that lets one investor veto a strategy change, a transparency grant that hands one LP your live book — these are terms that disadvantage your other investors or your own flexibility, and the moment you sign one you have created an asymmetry that an MFN cannot fully cure and that disclosure can only mitigate. The strong-governance answer is to have a list, before you ever sit across from an anchor investor, of the terms you will grant (fee breaks within a stated founders'-class band, reasonable regulatory carve-outs, an MFN itself) and the terms you will not (first-out liquidity, anything that subordinates one investor to another in a stress). Then, when the `\$50M` family office asks for first-out liquidity and threatens to walk, you can say no and mean it — and an investor who hears a manager refuse a term *because it would disadvantage other investors* has just learned the single most reassuring thing about you, which is that you will refuse to disadvantage *them* the same way later.

## What the board and LPAC police

Pull the governance bodies together and look at what, concretely, they are positioned to catch — because a control you cannot describe in terms of the specific harm it prevents is a control you do not really have.

**The board polices the discretionary, investor-harming actions.** A gate. A suspension. A side pocket that traps an investor's capital in an illiquid bucket. A change to the valuation policy. The firing of an auditor or administrator who is asking inconvenient questions. Each of these is an action the manager might take to protect itself at the investors' expense, and each requires the board's sign-off, so the manager cannot do it alone.

**The LPAC polices the conflicts.** A mark on a hard-to-value position that conveniently lifts the NAV before a fee crystallization. A transaction with an affiliate. A side letter whose terms disadvantage the rest of the LP base. A conflict the documents never anticipated. The LPAC's consent — or its refusal — is the disinterested check on the manager's self-interest.

**Together they police the gap between what the fund says and what the fund does.** The offering documents promise pari-passu treatment, an independent valuation process, disciplined use of liquidity tools. Governance is the machinery that makes those promises *enforceable* rather than aspirational. Without it, the documents are marketing; with it, they are a contract someone is watching.

It helps to see the division as a deliberate separation of powers, the same idea that runs through any well-designed institution. The manager has the executive power — it runs the money day to day. The board has an oversight power over the fund — it can block the discretionary actions that hurt shareholders. The LPAC has a consent power over the conflicts — it can refuse the self-dealing the executive is tempted toward. The administrator and auditor have an independent verification power — they produce the numbers and confirm the assets, so the executive cannot manufacture its own reality. No single branch can do everything, and each can check the one most likely to harm the investors. A fund where the manager quietly controls the board, staffs the LPAC with its own beneficiaries, and leans on a captive administrator has collapsed all the branches into one, and an investor in that fund is relying entirely on the manager's character — which is exactly the bet governance exists to let her avoid having to make.

The reason this separation has to be *built*, and built before the storm, is that you cannot construct a credible check in the middle of the event it is meant to check. The day Maya wants to gate is not the day to go find independent directors; the quarter a position needs an honest mark is not the quarter to stand up an LPAC. The bodies have to be in place, populated with people who are genuinely independent and genuinely engaged, with their powers written into documents that were drafted when no one was under pressure. Governance built in calm is a control; governance assembled in a crisis is theater, and investors can tell the difference instantly because the second kind always conveniently approves whatever the manager wanted.

What neither body does is run the money or guarantee performance. Governance does not make Maya a better stock-picker. It makes Meridian a fund whose investors believe that when Maya is frightened, she cannot quietly disadvantage them — and that belief is what keeps their capital in the fund long enough for her stock-picking to compound. That is the whole game: a fund is an aligned promise to compound capital inside a structure that protects the investor, and governance is the part of the structure that does the protecting.

## Common misconceptions

**"The board is a rubber stamp."** Sometimes it is — and that is the failure mode, not the design. A captive board staffed by the manager's affiliates rubber-stamps everything and is worse than no board, because it sells investors false comfort. An independent board with professional directors who sit on dozens of unrelated funds has reputational skin in the game and will, on the actions that matter, tell the manager no. The Meridian gate example is exactly the board doing its job. Allocators know the difference and count the independent votes; a board the manager controls is not governance, it is decor.

**"Side letters are harmless — they're just accommodations."** A fee break to one investor is the manager's own revenue to give and harms no one else. But a *liquidity* preference is a redistribution among investors who believe they are equal, and an *undisclosed* side letter of any kind, combined with the MFN clauses other investors hold, is a fund-wide entitlement and a trust crisis waiting for its trigger. Side letters are not harmless; they are a governance instrument that is benign when disclosed and policed, and corrosive when hidden.

**"An MFN clause is just legal boilerplate."** An MFN clause is the most consequential sentence in many side letters. It converts every private favor into a fund-wide cost — the `\$250,000` accommodation that became a `\$1.0M` cascade. A manager who grants MFNs without tracking which terms trigger them is a manager who has lost control of the fund's own fee schedule and liquidity terms. Boilerplate does not cost you a million dollars a year.

**"Transparency is optional for a private fund."** It is true that a private fund is not a public company and does not file public disclosures, and that full position-level transparency to every investor would be reckless. But transparency *to the bodies that govern the fund* is not optional — the administrator must see the positions to strike the NAV, the auditor must verify the assets, the board and LPAC must see the conflicts they police, and institutional investors will not allocate without an audited track record and a real risk file. Optional transparency to small LPs is a choice; opacity to the governing bodies is a fraud risk, which is the Madoff lesson.

**"Governance is overhead that hurts returns."** Governance costs money — independent directors, audits, an LPAC process, a real reporting capability — and a launching manager underwater on the high-water mark feels every dollar of it. But governance is what makes capital *sticky*, and sticky capital is what lets a strategy compound through a drawdown instead of being liquidated into it. A fund that saves `\$50,000` on independent directors and then suffers a redemption cascade in its first bad quarter has not improved its returns; it has ended them. The cost-vs-survival framing runs through [building a hedge fund](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/building-a-hedge-fund-the-founders-map).

**"If my returns are good, governance takes care of itself."** Returns and governance are independent failure modes. Amaranth had spectacular returns until the week it lost about `\$6.6B` on a concentrated bet; Madoff *reported* smooth returns for years on a portfolio that did not exist. Good returns can coexist with a governance time bomb — a captive board, hidden side letters, no independent valuation — and the bomb goes off on its own schedule, usually in the stress the returns were supposed to prevent. Allocators separate investment due diligence from operational due diligence for exactly this reason.

## How it plays out in the real world

Governance failures are not theoretical; they are the named disasters every allocator carries in their head, and they are the reason operational due diligence wields a veto.

**Madoff is the canonical governance failure.** Bernard Madoff's fund — revealed as a Ponzi scheme in December 2008, with about `\$65B` in fabricated statement value and roughly `\$17.5B` of principal lost — failed every governance control simultaneously. There was no independent administrator; Madoff's own operation produced the statements. The auditor was a tiny, essentially unknown three-person firm, not a credible independent check. Assets were self-custodied rather than held at an independent prime broker. The returns were impossibly smooth, a red flag a real risk process would have flagged. Every single one of these — independent administration, a credible auditor, third-party custody, an independent risk view — is a governance control, and every single one was absent. Madoff is why "independent administrator, independent auditor, third-party custody" is the first thing an ODD team checks and the reason a great-returns fund can be rejected on operations alone. The lesson a launching manager should draw is not that investors are paranoid; it is that the controls Madoff lacked are cheap relative to what they protect, and that a manager who resists them — who wants to self-administer, who picks a tiny unknown auditor to save a few thousand dollars, who keeps assets off an independent prime — is sending the one signal that guarantees no serious allocator will ever fund the fund.

**Archegos is a governance failure dressed as a trading loss.** Bill Hwang's family office took roughly `\$10B+` out of its prime brokers in 2021 — Credit Suisse alone lost about `\$5.5B` — through concentrated total-return swaps with hidden leverage spread across multiple banks, none of which could see the others' exposure. As a family office it faced lighter governance and disclosure requirements than a fund managing outside capital, and that opacity was the enabling condition: no single counterparty had transparency into the whole position, so no single counterparty could apply the brake. It is the transparency problem at the counterparty level, and it ends with Hwang convicted of fraud in 2024. The cash-and-counterparty side of this lives in [cash, treasury, and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk).

**Most governance failures never make the news**, and they are the ones a launching manager should fear, because they are survivable-but-fatal: the secret first-out side letter discovered during a redemption scramble, like Meridian's; the captive board that gated a liquid fund and triggered the run it was trying to prevent; the manager who marked an illiquid book up before a fee crystallization with no LPAC to check it, and lost every institutional investor when the marks reversed. These do not produce `\$60B` headlines. They produce a fund that quietly liquidates in year three, one of the large fraction of launches that do not survive three to five years — and the cause of death on the certificate reads "redemptions," when the real cause was a governance control that was theater.

The practical takeaway for a founder is to treat governance the way you treat risk management: as a first-class control you build *before* you need it, not a formality you assemble after an allocator asks. Hire independent directors who are genuinely independent. Stand up an LPAC and give it real, defined consent rights. Disclose every side letter to the board and the LPAC, refuse the liquidity preferences that disadvantage other investors, and run a disciplined MFN-election process so the cascade is managed rather than hidden. Deliver the transparency your investors are entitled to, and do not let an information asymmetry become a secret. Do this and your investors will believe — correctly — that you cannot quietly disadvantage them when you are scared. That belief is the asset. It is what keeps the capital from running, and capital that does not run is capital that compounds.

## When this matters / Further reading

Governance matters most at two moments and they are the moments that decide a fund's life. The first is at launch, when you are negotiating with anchor investors and tempted to give a `\$50M` family office whatever it asks for to close the day-one capital — and a single first-out liquidity term or an untracked MFN clause signed that week becomes the thing that breaks the fund three years later. The second is in the crisis, when you are frightened and want to gate, suspend, or mark your way out of trouble, and the only thing standing between your fear and your investors' capital is a board and an LPAC that can tell you no. Build them before you need them, because you cannot build them in the storm.
