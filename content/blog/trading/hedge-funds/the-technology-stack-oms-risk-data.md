> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A fund's technology stack is a build-versus-buy decision at every layer, and the right answer at launch is "buy almost everything," because the cost curve is brutal as a share of a small fund's revenue and trivial at scale.
>
> - The stack has four layers wired in sequence: **market data** feeds the **OMS/EMS** (orders and fills), which feeds the **portfolio and risk system** (positions, P&L, limits), which feeds the **administrator and prime broker** through the **data plumbing** and the daily **reconciliation**.
> - At launch you **buy** every layer from established vendors. The build case opens up only at the extremes — sub-millisecond execution for a latency strategy, or a bespoke data pipeline for a systematic shop at scale — and even then you build the one piece that *is* your edge and buy the rest.
> - The stack costs a small fund roughly **\$110k–\$280k a year** depending on strategy, and that bill rises far slower than AUM does — so it eats a punishing share of revenue at launch and a rounding error at a billion.
> - The one number to remember: at **\$25M** of AUM, a discretionary fund's technology bill can consume close to **30%** of its 1.5% management-fee revenue; a data-hungry systematic fund's bill can eat **nearly half**. Overbuild here and you starve the J-curve before you ever reach scale.

Maya had finally found the wall she could not trade her way through. The Meridian Fund's strategy worked — she had eleven months of clean, audited numbers from a small pilot and a research process she trusted. But the strategy she actually wanted to run at launch was systematic: a few hundred names, rebalanced daily, driven by a factor model she fed with point-in-time fundamentals, intraday prices, and a corporate-actions feed she could not afford to get wrong. When she priced out the data and technology that strategy genuinely needed — the market-data feeds, a portfolio and risk system that could hold the whole book and compute her exposures by the close, an order system that could route a basket without fat-fingering it — the number came back at roughly **\$200,000 a year**. At her planned launch AUM of **\$40 million**, her management fee at 1.5% would gross **\$600,000**. The technology stack alone would eat a third of her entire revenue before she paid for the administrator, the audit, the lawyers, or herself.

Her first instinct was the one almost every technical founder has: *I'll build it.* She could code. She had built her own backtester. Why pay a vendor **\$80,000** a year for an order-management system when she could write something in a few months? Her old head of technology, whom she took to coffee, talked her out of it in twenty minutes. "You won't build it in a few months. You'll build a prototype in a few months, spend two years finding the edge cases, and the whole time you'll be a software shop instead of a fund. And the day an operational-due-diligence analyst asks you who maintains your OMS, your answer will be 'me, in my spare time,' and you will fail the diligence. Buy the boring parts. Build the one thing that is actually your edge, if anything. Spend your scarce engineering on alpha, not on a worse version of software that already exists."

This post is about that wall and the decisions on the far side of it. A fund's technology stack is not one purchase; it is a set of build-versus-buy decisions, one at every layer, and the right answer changes with your strategy and bends sharply with your AUM. Get it wrong in the expensive direction — overbuild a gold-plated stack you cannot pay for — and you burn the runway you need to survive the [J-curve to break-even](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/surviving-the-j-curve-break-even-aum). Get it wrong in the cheap direction — run the fund on a spreadsheet and a chat window — and you cannot scale, you make the kind of manual error that loses investor trust, and you fail the operational due diligence that decides whether an institution will ever fund you. Figure 1 is the whole stack on one page: the four layers, wired in the order a trade actually flows through them. We will spend the rest of the post earning the right to read it, then price every layer to the dollar.

## Foundations: the systems a fund runs on

Before any of the build-versus-buy nuance, you need a clear mental model of the layers and a handful of definitions firmly in place. If you trade for a living, you have *used* most of these systems without ever having to *choose, pay for, or maintain* them — they were just there, provided by an employer who had a technology team. Running your own fund means the stack becomes your problem, your line item, and your operational-due-diligence exposure. So let us define each layer from zero.

The model is a chain. A trade is born as an idea, gets routed to the market, comes back as a fill, updates a position, gets checked against a risk limit, and finally lands in the official books that the administrator strikes the NAV from. Each link in that chain is a piece of software (or a service), and each handoff between links is a place data has to move correctly. The whole post is a tour of that chain and the decision — buy a vendor's link or build your own — at each one.

**The order-management system (OMS).** The system of record for orders and positions. When the portfolio manager decides to buy 50,000 shares of a stock, the order is *staged* in the OMS, sent to the market, and the resulting *fills* are *booked* back into it. The OMS holds the firm's current positions, tracks what is working versus filled, enforces pre-trade compliance checks (you are not allowed to short a restricted name, you cannot exceed a position limit), and is the source the rest of the stack reads from. The OMS answers the question *what do we own and what have we ordered?*

**The execution-management system (EMS).** The trader-facing system that actually works orders in the market — slicing a large order into pieces, routing to different venues and brokers, running execution algorithms, showing live market depth. Where the OMS is the book of record, the EMS is the cockpit. For a discretionary fund the line between them blurs, and many vendors sell a combined **OMS/EMS** (often written "OEMS") so the order, the working slices, and the booked fills all live in one system. For a high-frequency or low-latency strategy, the EMS is specialized and latency-sensitive enough that it becomes the one thing a fund might build itself.

**The portfolio-management system (PMS).** The system that holds the whole book — every position, its cost basis, its current mark, the accrued P&L, the exposures by sector, factor, currency, and counterparty. Where the OMS is about *orders*, the PMS is about *the portfolio as a whole*: it is what the PM looks at to understand the fund's state, and what feeds the risk system and the shadow accounting. At a small fund the PMS and OMS are often the same product or tightly bundled; even so they answer two different questions — one about the orders, the other about the book those orders build — so it is worth naming them separately.

**The risk system.** The software that measures the portfolio's risk and enforces the fund's limits. It computes exposures (gross, net, by sector or factor), value-at-risk (VaR — an estimate of the loss the portfolio could suffer over a horizon at a given confidence), stress scenarios (what happens to the book if a 2008 or a 2020 repeats), and it flags or blocks breaches of the limits the fund has committed to. The risk system is where the fund's stated risk discipline becomes mechanical rather than aspirational, which is why allocators care so much that it exists and runs independently of the PM's gut. The math behind VaR, covariance, and factor risk lives in the math series; here the risk system is the *software that runs that math every day.*

**Market data.** The feeds of prices and reference information the whole stack runs on: live and historical prices, quotes, corporate actions (splits, dividends, mergers that change what a position means), security reference data (the master record of every instrument's identifiers and terms), and any specialized data the strategy needs (fundamentals, estimates, alternative data). Market data is the fuel. It is also, as we will see, the layer where costs are most ferocious for a data-hungry strategy and where the answer to "should I build this?" is a flat no — you license data, you do not manufacture it.

**FIX connectivity.** The plumbing standard. FIX (the Financial Information eXchange protocol) is the messaging language that order-management and execution systems use to talk to brokers, exchanges, and prime brokers — sending orders, receiving fills, confirming trades. When a fund "connects to a broker," in practice it is establishing a FIX session. FIX is the lingua franca that lets a bought OMS talk to any broker without bespoke integration for each one, which is a large part of *why* buying beats building: the vendor already speaks FIX to hundreds of counterparties.

**Reconciliation.** The daily (and intra-day) process of confirming that the fund's own records agree with the outside world's. Every morning the prime broker sends a file of the positions and cash it thinks the fund holds; the fund's own books say what it thinks it holds; reconciliation is the process of confirming the two match and chasing down every break (a difference) until it is explained. Reconciliation across the OMS, the PMS, the prime broker, and the administrator is the operational heartbeat of a fund, and the place a manual, spreadsheet-driven shop quietly accumulates the errors that eventually blow up.

**The administrator and the prime broker.** The two outside parties the stack ultimately feeds. The **administrator** is the independent firm that strikes the official NAV and keeps the investor register; the **prime broker (PB)** finances the fund, holds its assets in custody, and lends it stock to short. We covered both in depth in [the service-provider stack](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor); here they are the endpoints of the data plumbing — the systems your stack sends data *to* and receives files *from*, every single day.

**Build versus buy.** The decision, at each layer, between licensing a vendor's product and writing your own. "Buy" means paying a vendor an annual fee for software they build, host, support, and keep connected to the market. "Build" means your own engineers write and maintain it. The default at launch is buy, for reasons we will make concrete; the build case is narrow and specific, reserved for the one layer (if any) where an off-the-shelf product genuinely cannot express your edge.

Here is the through-line that connects all of these. A hedge fund sells a promise — *trust me with your capital and I will compound it inside a structure that protects you* — and the technology stack is part of how the founder keeps the second half of that promise true. The OMS and EMS make sure the trades you intend are the trades that happen. The risk system makes sure the fund stays inside the boundaries you promised. The data plumbing and reconciliation make sure the number the administrator strikes is the number that is real. A fund with a sound stack is a fund whose promise is *operationally credible*; a fund running on heroics and spreadsheets is one whose promise depends on no one making a typo, which is not a promise an institution will fund. The stack is plumbing, and plumbing is invisible right up until it floods the house.

## The OMS and EMS

The order side of the stack is where most traders' intuition is strongest, because it is closest to the screens they already live on. But the choices here set the tone for everything downstream, so let us be precise about what these systems do and where the build-versus-buy line actually falls.

**What the OMS does for you.** The order-management system is the funnel every trade passes through, and it does four jobs at once. First, it is the **book of record for orders and positions** — the single authoritative answer to "what do we own and what is working in the market right now?" Second, it runs **pre-trade compliance**: before an order goes out, the OMS checks it against the rules the fund has committed to (restricted lists, position limits, concentration caps, the prohibition on shorting a name you do not have a borrow for) and blocks the ones that breach. Third, it handles **allocation** — when a single order is filled across multiple accounts or sub-funds, the OMS splits the fill fairly and records who got what. Fourth, it feeds the rest of the stack: the portfolio system, the risk system, the shadow books, and the reconciliation all read positions and trades from the OMS.

**What the EMS does for you.** The execution side is the cockpit the trader works orders from. It shows live market data, lets the trader slice a large order into child orders, route them to different venues and brokers, run execution algorithms (VWAP, TWAP, implementation-shortfall strategies that try to minimize the market impact of a big trade), and watch the fills come back. For a discretionary equity fund trading liquid names a few times a day, the EMS can be modest — even the broker's own front end may suffice at the very start. For a fund whose edge depends on *how* it trades (a high-turnover strategy, a market-maker, anything latency-sensitive), the EMS is where the real engineering goes.

**Why most funds buy a combined OMS/EMS.** The mature vendor market sells integrated order-and-execution platforms that bundle the book of record, the compliance checks, the execution cockpit, and — critically — FIX connectivity to hundreds of brokers and venues already built and maintained. That last point is the quiet decider. A bought OMS comes pre-connected: the day you sign with a new prime broker or executing broker, the vendor turns on a FIX session that already works, because they maintain that integration for all their clients. Build your own, and every single broker connection is a project you own, test, and maintain forever. You are not in the business of maintaining FIX sessions; you are in the business of finding alpha. The connectivity alone is usually enough to settle the decision.

**The cost shape.** Bought OMS/EMS platforms are typically priced per user (per "seat") plus a platform fee, sometimes with usage-based components. For a small fund, an institutional-grade OMS/EMS runs on the order of **\$30,000 to \$120,000 a year**, depending on the vendor, the number of seats, and how much execution functionality you need. A cheaper tier of cloud-based order systems aimed squarely at emerging managers can come in lower; the premium tier aimed at multi-strategy platforms runs much higher. The key feature of the cost is that it scales with *seats and complexity*, not with AUM — which is exactly why the same system is a heavy burden on a \$40M fund and an afterthought on a \$1B one.

**The narrow build case.** When does a fund build its own order or execution system? Almost only when *the execution itself is the edge.* A low-latency or high-frequency strategy lives or dies on microseconds; no general-purpose vendor EMS is fast enough, so those firms build their own execution stack down to the network card. A systematic fund running a high-turnover basket strategy might build a custom order router that expresses its specific rebalancing logic better than a generic algo. But notice the pattern: in both cases the firm builds the *one component that is its competitive advantage* and buys everything around it. Nobody serious builds their own OMS to save a vendor fee. They build it because the vendor's version would blunt the very thing they are good at — and they have the engineering budget to do it well and maintain it forever.

#### Worked example: the OMS and data cost at \$50M versus \$500M AUM

Maya launches the Meridian Fund as a discretionary long/short equity book at **\$50 million** of AUM. Her stack: a bought OMS/EMS at **\$60,000** a year (two seats plus the platform fee), a portfolio-and-risk system at **\$70,000**, market data — terminals, an exchange feed, reference and corporate-actions data — at **\$95,000**, and reconciliation and operations tooling bundled with her administrator at **\$25,000**. Round it: **\$250,000** a year, give or take, for the full technology layer. Against her 1.5% management fee, her gross management revenue is **\$750,000**. The stack is eating **one dollar in three** of her management fee before she has paid the auditor, the lawyer, or herself.

Now run the same fund forward to **\$500 million**. The OMS/EMS rises — more seats, more functionality — but not tenfold; call it **\$130,000**. The portfolio-and-risk system climbs to perhaps **\$160,000**. Market data, the most usage-driven line, grows the most as she adds analysts and feeds — say **\$180,000**. Operations and reconciliation rise to **\$50,000**. Total: roughly **\$520,000** — the stack roughly doubled while AUM grew tenfold. But her management revenue at 1.5% is now **\$7,500,000**, so the same kind of stack that ate a third of revenue at launch now eats **under 7%**. *The technology bill in dollars barely keeps pace with headcount; as a share of revenue it collapses, which is the entire reason overbuilding at launch is a self-inflicted wound and the patient, bought-everything stack is the survivable choice.*

## The portfolio and risk system

If the OMS/EMS is about the orders going out, the portfolio-and-risk system is about understanding the book that results — and about staying inside the boundaries you have promised investors and yourself. This layer is where the fund's risk discipline stops being a paragraph in the pitch book and becomes a number that turns red on a screen.

**The portfolio side.** The portfolio-management system holds the whole book and computes everything you need to understand its state: positions and their cost bases, current marks, accrued P&L (daily, month-to-date, year-to-date), and exposures sliced every way that matters — long versus short, gross versus net, by sector, by factor, by currency, by single-name concentration, by counterparty. A discretionary PM lives in this system; it is where she sees, in one place, that her book is 150% gross and 30% net, that she is overweight one sector, that a single name has grown to an uncomfortable share of capital. It also feeds the **shadow NAV** — the fund's own internal valuation of the book that gets reconciled against the administrator's official strike each month, a control we covered in the [service-provider stack](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor).

**The risk side.** The risk system takes the portfolio and turns it into risk measures and limit checks. It computes **VaR** (the loss the book might suffer over, say, a day at 95% or 99% confidence), runs **stress tests** (re-pricing the portfolio under historical or hypothetical shocks), decomposes risk into factors (how much of the fund's risk is just market beta versus genuine stock selection), and — most operationally important — it **monitors limits**. Every fund commits to limits: a maximum gross exposure, a single-name cap, a sector cap, a stop-loss, a drawdown trigger. The risk system is what makes those limits mechanical. When a position drifts toward a limit, the system flags it; when it breaches, the system escalates. This is the difference between "we have risk limits" (a sentence) and "our risk limits are enforced by a system that the PM cannot quietly override" (a control an allocator will actually believe).

**Why this layer is an operational-due-diligence focus.** When an allocator's operational-due-diligence team examines a fund, they want to see that risk is monitored *independently of the person taking the risk.* A fund where the PM eyeballs his own exposures in a spreadsheet and decides for himself whether he is within limits has no real risk control — he is marking his own homework, the same failure mode that sinks a self-administered fund on the NAV. A fund with a real risk system, ideally watched by someone other than the PM, can answer the diligence question credibly. This is also a recurring theme in the careers series' treatment of [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up): the institutions that survive are the ones where risk is a system, not a mood.

**The cost shape and the build line.** A bought portfolio-and-risk platform for a small fund runs roughly **\$40,000 to \$150,000 a year**, and a more sophisticated risk-analytics platform with deep factor models and stress libraries can run higher. The build case here is narrower than people expect. The temptation is strong — a quant founder *can* compute VaR and exposures in a notebook — but a notebook is not a system: it is not auditable, not independent, not robust to the founder being on holiday, and not something an ODD analyst will accept. The genuine build case is when a fund's risk *is its edge* — a fund whose entire strategy is a proprietary factor model so specific that no vendor's risk decomposition describes it correctly. Even then the pattern repeats: build the proprietary risk model, but buy the surrounding plumbing that delivers it, stores it, and presents it.

#### Worked example: the build-versus-buy NPV for a risk system

Maya is tempted to build her own risk system. She models it honestly. **Buy:** a vendor platform at **\$80,000** a year, rising at maybe 5% a year as the fund grows. Over five years that is roughly **\$80k + \$84k + \$88k + \$93k + \$97k = \$442,000** of cash cost, with zero engineering distraction and an ODD-passable answer from day one.

**Build:** she would need to hire a quant developer at a fully-loaded cost of about **\$250,000** a year, who would spend the first year building the system and the next four maintaining and extending it. Five years of that one engineer is **\$1.25 million** in salary alone — before infrastructure, before the opportunity cost of that person not working on alpha, and before the risk that the home-grown system has a bug that an institutional vendor's battle-tested code would not. Even discounting future costs at, say, 8% a year, the build path's present value is on the order of **\$1.0 million**, more than double the buy path's roughly **\$385,000** present value. And the build path *adds* risk (an untested system) and ODD friction (a single key-person dependency), where the buy path *removes* both.

The only thing that flips this calculation is if the risk model itself is the edge — if buying a generic system would mean *not running the strategy at all.* For Maya, running standard long/short equity, it is not. She buys. *When the thing you would build is plumbing rather than alpha, the build NPV is almost always negative once you price in the engineer's salary, the maintenance tail, and the diligence penalty for a key-person-dependent black box.*

## Market data

Market data is the fuel the entire stack burns, and it is the layer where the build-versus-buy question has the clearest answer — you never build data — and where the cost can be the most savage, especially for a systematic strategy. Figure 2 lays the whole stack out as a build-versus-buy grid; market data is the row where the build column reads, flatly, *never*.

![A matrix showing each layer of the technology stack as a row with columns for the default decision, the narrow build case, and the annual cost, with buy as the green default for every layer and market data marked never-build](/imgs/blogs/the-technology-stack-oms-risk-data-2.png)

**What "market data" actually contains.** It is not one feed; it is a bundle of distinct data types, each separately licensed. **Live prices and quotes** — the real-time stream of where instruments are trading, used by the EMS and the risk system. **Historical prices** — the time series the research and risk processes run on. **Reference data** — the master record of every instrument: its identifiers, its terms, what it actually is. **Corporate actions** — the feed of splits, dividends, mergers, spin-offs, and symbol changes that alter what a position means overnight, and that, if you get them wrong, silently corrupt your P&L and your risk. **Strategy-specific data** — the fundamentals, estimates, ownership data, or alternative datasets a particular strategy needs as its raw alpha input. A discretionary equity fund needs the first four and a little of the fifth; a systematic fund can need enormous quantities of the fifth, cleaned and point-in-time-correct, which is where the cost explodes.

**Why you license, never manufacture.** Market data is produced by exchanges, data vendors, and specialized providers who have spent decades and fortunes collecting, cleaning, and distributing it. You cannot build the New York Stock Exchange's price feed; you license it. You cannot reconstruct twenty years of clean, survivorship-bias-free fundamentals; you license it from a vendor who maintains it. The "build" instinct that is sometimes defensible for software is never defensible for data — what a fund might build is the *pipeline that ingests, cleans, and stores* licensed data, not the data itself. And even the pipeline is a buy-first decision unless the data engineering is genuinely part of the edge.

**Why systematic strategies pay so much more.** The single biggest driver of the difference between two funds' technology bills is usually not the OMS or the risk system; it is data. A discretionary long/short equity fund and a systematic factor fund can run identical-size books and have wildly different data costs, because the systematic fund consumes vastly more data, needs it cleaner and more historical, and often pays for specialized or alternative datasets the discretionary fund never touches. The discretionary PM reads a research report and a few terminals; the systematic engine ingests every name's fundamentals, point-in-time, going back decades, plus intraday prices, plus whatever alternative data feeds the signal. That is the difference between a **\$95,000** data bill and a **\$200,000+** one at the same AUM.

**The licensing traps.** Market data carries contractual landmines a founder must know about. Data is licensed for *specific uses* — display versus non-display (feeding an automated system), per-user versus enterprise, redistribution restrictions. A systematic fund feeding prices into an automated model is buying *non-display* licenses, which are priced very differently (and often far higher) than the per-screen terminal licenses a discretionary desk pays. Vendors audit usage and bill for breaches. Exchange data carries its own per-feed fees layered on top of the vendor's charge. The practical lesson: market-data contracts are negotiated, audited, and easy to under-budget, and a founder who quotes herself the screen price for what is actually a non-display systematic use will be unpleasantly surprised at the first audit.

#### Worked example: data cost for a systematic versus a discretionary strategy

Maya prices her data bill two ways for the same **\$50 million** fund. As a **discretionary** long/short equity manager, she needs two terminal seats, a consolidated price feed, security reference data, and a corporate-actions feed. Bundle it and she is at roughly **\$95,000** a year — meaningful, but a line she can carry.

As a **systematic** factor manager running the same \$50M, the picture changes. She needs the same prices and reference data, *plus* non-display licenses to feed her automated model (priced well above the per-screen rate), *plus* a deep, clean, point-in-time fundamentals dataset going back two decades, *plus* an estimates feed, *plus* possibly one alternative dataset that sharpens the signal. That bundle comes in closer to **\$200,000** a year — more than double the discretionary bill on an identical-size fund. On \$50M, the discretionary data bill of \$95,000 is about **0.19%** of AUM; the systematic bill of \$200,000 is about **0.40%**. *The strategy, not the AUM, sets the data bill — which is why a systematic founder must price her data before she sets her launch AUM, not after, or the math of the J-curve will not close.*

## The data plumbing

The plumbing is the unglamorous layer that connects everything: the feeds and files that move data between the fund's systems and its prime broker and administrator, and the FIX sessions that move orders and fills between the fund and the market. It is invisible when it works and catastrophic when it does not, because a broken pipe does not announce itself — it just quietly produces a wrong number that flows downstream into the NAV. Figure 3 traces a single trade through the whole stack so you can see where the plumbing sits.

![A pipeline diagram tracing one trade from the live market-data feed through the execution and order systems, the portfolio system, the risk check, the prime-broker file, the reconciliation, and finally to the administrator striking the NAV](/imgs/blogs/the-technology-stack-oms-risk-data-3.png)

**FIX, the order plumbing.** As we defined it, FIX is the messaging protocol that carries orders out to brokers and fills back. The practical reason it matters for build-versus-buy is that a bought OMS/EMS *already speaks FIX to your counterparties.* Establishing a new broker or prime-broker relationship is, on the technology side, mostly a matter of the vendor turning on a FIX session they already maintain. A fund that built its own order system owns every one of those sessions — building, testing, certifying, and maintaining a separate integration for each broker, each of which can change its FIX dialect or its endpoints. This is the most concrete single reason "buy the OMS" wins: the connectivity is a maintenance burden that compounds with every counterparty you add, and the vendor amortizes it across all their clients.

**The prime-broker and administrator feeds.** Every morning (and often intra-day), the prime broker sends the fund a **start-of-day file**: its record of the fund's positions, cash balances, and the prior day's activity. The administrator likewise exchanges files with the fund and the PB to produce the official books. These feeds are the raw material of reconciliation. They arrive on a schedule, in formats the fund's systems must ingest and parse correctly, and they are the outside world's version of the truth that the fund's own books must agree with. The data plumbing is the set of automated jobs that pulls these files in, loads them, and presents the differences. When this plumbing is automated and reliable, reconciliation is a quick morning check; when it is manual — someone downloading a file and pasting it into a spreadsheet — it is a daily opportunity for a typo to become a NAV error.

**Reconciliation, the operational heartbeat.** Reconciliation is the process of confirming that the fund's own records (its OMS and PMS) agree with the prime broker's file and the administrator's books, and chasing every **break** until it is explained. Positions must match. Cash must match. Trades must match. A break can be benign (a timing difference that clears the next day) or a real error (a trade booked wrong, a fee mis-accrued, a corporate action missed). A well-run fund reconciles every day, automatically, and treats unexplained breaks as a stop-the-line event. A fund that reconciles monthly, by hand, discovers its errors a month late, by which point they have already flowed into a published NAV and an investor statement. We go deeper on the daily mechanics in [the trade lifecycle, execution, and prime brokerage](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage) and on the broader machine in [the operating model: front, middle, and back office](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office); here the point is that reconciliation is a tooling decision, and the tooling is something you buy or get bundled with your administrator, not something you should be running in Excel at any meaningful size.

#### Worked example: the cost of a manual-reconciliation error

The Meridian Fund, in its lean early days, reconciles by hand. One month a corporate action — a 2-for-1 stock split in a mid-cap long — is missed in the fund's own books because the corporate-actions feed flagged it but nobody loaded it before the month-end strike. The fund's internal records show the position at the pre-split price and half the share count; the administrator, working from the prime broker's file, has it right. The shadow NAV and the official NAV diverge.

Caught at reconciliation, this is a five-minute fix. Missed — as it is, because the reconciliation is a hurried manual paste — it flows into the published NAV. The position is mismarked by, say, **\$400,000** on a **\$50 million** fund. That is **0.8%** of NAV — the difference between reporting the month up **1.2%** and up **2.0%**. When the error surfaces at the next audit, the fund must **restate** the NAV. Restating a NAV is not a clerical event; it means every subscription and redemption struck at the wrong price was wrong, the incentive-fee accrual was wrong, and every investor who transacted that month was charged or paid the wrong amount. The administrator and auditor must unwind and re-strike. The direct cost in professional fees and the founder's time can run into the tens of thousands of dollars, **\$30,000 or more**, but the real cost is the email the fund must send its investors explaining that a number they were told was wrong — the single fastest way to lose the institutional trust the whole business depends on.

A bought reconciliation tool, or reconciliation bundled with the administrator, would have flagged the break automatically the next morning. The tool costs perhaps **\$20,000 a year**. The error it prevents cost more than that in one month, and the trust it preserves is priceless. *Manual reconciliation is not a cost saving; it is an uninsured liability that pays out, eventually, in exactly the currency a fund cannot afford to spend — investor confidence.*

## Build vs buy

Now we can state the build-versus-buy framework cleanly, because every layer has taught the same lesson from a different angle. The default is **buy.** The build case is narrow, specific, and reserved for the one layer where an off-the-shelf product would blunt your actual edge. Figure 5 contrasts the two worlds the decision produces: the spreadsheet-and-email shop versus the integrated stack.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting a spreadsheet-and-email fund where every step is manual and error-prone against an integrated stack where connected systems book trades, hold positions, enforce risk limits, and reconcile automatically](/imgs/blogs/the-technology-stack-oms-risk-data-5.png)

**Why buy is the default.** Four reasons, each of which we have now seen in the layers above. First, **time to launch**: a bought stack is live in weeks, where a built one is a multi-year project, and every month you spend building is a month of runway burned before the fund earns a dollar. Second, **connectivity**: vendors already speak FIX to your brokers and exchange files with your prime and administrator, a maintenance burden that compounds with every counterparty. Third, **operational due diligence**: a bought, vendor-supported, independently-maintained system passes ODD; a home-grown one maintained by the founder in his spare time is a key-person dependency and a single point of failure that ODD treats as a red flag. Fourth, **focus**: every engineer-hour spent building plumbing is an hour not spent on alpha, which is the only thing the fund is actually paid for. A fund that becomes a software company has misallocated its scarcest resource.

**When build is right.** Build the one thing that *is* your edge, and only that. A latency strategy builds its execution stack because microseconds are the alpha. A systematic shop at scale might build its data pipeline because cleaning and assembling its specific dataset, point-in-time-correct, is part of what it is good at — and at scale the cost arithmetic flips, because a billion-dollar fund can afford an engineering team and amortizes it over a far larger revenue base. A fund with a genuinely proprietary risk model builds that model. The discipline is to be ruthlessly honest about whether the thing you want to build is *alpha* or *plumbing.* If it is plumbing, buy it. If it is alpha, build it well, maintain it forever, and buy everything around it.

**The hybrid reality.** In practice almost every fund is a hybrid: it buys the OMS/EMS, the portfolio-and-risk system, the market data, and the reconciliation tooling, and — if it has any build at all — builds the single component that carries its edge, gluing it to the bought stack through the vendors' APIs. The bought systems expose interfaces precisely so a fund can plug its proprietary piece in. The art is in the gluing and in choosing vendors whose APIs are open enough to let your one built thing talk to everything else. This is why "build versus buy" is slightly the wrong framing; the real question is *which single layer, if any, is worth owning, and how do I buy the rest so it connects cleanly to that one piece.*

**The decision against the spine.** Run every layer's decision against the fund's promise. Does building this make the promise more *credible* (better performance) — or does it just make the founder feel clever while burning runway? Does it make the structure *sounder* — or does it introduce a key-person black box that fails ODD? Does it help keep capital *sticky* — or does it risk the operational error that loses it? For all but the one edge-bearing layer, buying makes the promise more credible (you launch sooner with capital intact), the structure sounder (vendor-supported, ODD-passable), and the capital stickier (fewer operational errors). The build instinct feels like control; for plumbing, it is usually a way to spend money and time you do not have on a worse version of something you could have rented.

## The cost curve by AUM and strategy

Everything above resolves into one picture, and it is the most important thing a founder must internalize before setting launch AUM: the technology stack's cost rises with AUM, but far slower than AUM rises — and as a *share of revenue* it bends down sharply. Figure 4 shows the dollar cost of the stack against AUM for a discretionary and a systematic fund.

![A line chart of annual technology-stack cost in dollars against assets under management on a log scale, with a lower discretionary line and a higher systematic line, both rising far more slowly than AUM grows](/imgs/blogs/the-technology-stack-oms-risk-data-4.png)

**The dollar curve is sub-linear.** Read Figure 4 carefully. A discretionary fund's stack costs roughly **\$110,000** at **\$25M** of AUM, **\$150,000** at **\$50M**, **\$220,000** at **\$100M**, and about **\$360,000** at **\$250M**. The AUM grew tenfold from \$25M to \$250M; the technology bill roughly tripled. The systematic line sits above it everywhere — about **\$280,000** at \$50M, roughly twice the discretionary bill at the same size — because of the data cost we examined, but it has the same shape: it rises much more slowly than AUM. The reason is structural. Technology cost is driven by *seats, feeds, and complexity*, none of which scale one-for-one with assets. Adding a billion dollars of capital to an existing strategy adds a few analysts and a bit more data, not ten times the systems.

**The revenue-share curve bends down.** Now the picture that actually matters for survival. Figure 7 takes the same costs and divides them by management-fee revenue (modeled at the compressed-average **1.5%** rate), giving technology cost as a percentage of the management fee.

![A line chart of technology-stack cost as a percentage of management-fee revenue against AUM on a log scale, falling steeply from a punishing share at small AUM toward a small fraction at large AUM, with a reference line at one hundred percent](/imgs/blogs/the-technology-stack-oms-risk-data-7.png)

The curve is brutal at the left and gentle at the right. At **\$25M** of AUM, a discretionary fund's **\$110,000** stack eats close to **30%** of its **\$375,000** of management-fee revenue. By **\$250M**, the same kind of stack — now about \$360,000 — eats under **10%** of \$3.75M of revenue. The systematic fund's curve sits higher: at **\$50M**, its **\$280,000** stack consumes roughly **37%** of \$750,000 of revenue, and at the very small end a data-heavy systematic launch can flirt with the reference line where technology alone would consume the *entire* management fee. This is the whole reason the build-versus-buy default is "buy and keep it lean": at launch AUM, every avoidable dollar of technology spend comes straight out of the runway you need to survive to scale, and the cost as a share of revenue only becomes comfortable once you are well past the break-even AUM we examine in [surviving the J-curve](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/surviving-the-j-curve-break-even-aum).

**Strategy is the second axis.** The two lines in each chart are not a detail; they are the second half of the lesson. Your strategy sets your stack's cost as much as your AUM does, and the founder must choose a strategy whose technology bill her launch AUM can actually carry. We made exactly this point in [choosing your strategy: the operating lens](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens) — a strategy is not just an alpha; it is an operating cost structure. A systematic strategy that needs **\$200,000** of data is a fine strategy at **\$300M** of launch AUM and an impossible one at **\$30M**, because at \$30M the data bill alone is two-thirds of the management fee. The honest founder prices the stack *before* committing to the strategy at a given launch size, not after, because the cost curve, not the alpha, is what decides whether the business survives long enough for the alpha to matter.

**The vendor landscape.** Because buying is the default, the practical question becomes *who to buy from*, and the market is mature enough that every layer has an established premium tier and a lighter, cheaper tier built for emerging managers. Figure 6 lays out that landscape by layer.

![A grid showing the vendor landscape by layer, with an established premium tier and a lighter outsourced tier for the order systems, the portfolio and risk system, market data, and operations and reconciliation](/imgs/blogs/the-technology-stack-oms-risk-data-6.png)

The premium tier is the integrated platforms the large multi-strategy funds run — full-featured, expensive, and overkill for a launch. The lighter tier is the set of cloud-based order systems, admin-bundled portfolio tools, vendor data APIs, and outsourced middle-office providers built specifically for small and emerging funds, which let a launch buy an institutional-grade-enough stack at a fraction of the premium price. The founder's job is to buy the lighter tier that passes ODD, not the premium tier that impresses no one and starves the runway — and to know that the day she scales, the upgrade path to the premium tier exists and is well-trodden.

## Common misconceptions

**"Excel is fine — I'll upgrade when I'm bigger."** This is the single most expensive belief a founder can hold, because it confuses *cost* with *liability.* A spreadsheet has no licensing fee, so it feels free; what it actually is is an uninsured liability that pays out in NAV errors, missed corporate actions, and the manual reconciliation breaks we priced at tens of thousands of dollars and immeasurable trust. Worse, an Excel-and-email shop *fails operational due diligence* — an allocator's ODD team will see manual position-keeping and hand-reconciliation and decline, regardless of returns, because there is no segregation of duties and no system enforcing anything. Excel is not a launch stack you upgrade later; it is a stack that prevents you from ever getting the institutional money that would let you grow.

**"Build is cheaper than buy — I'm an engineer, I'll just write it."** Build is almost always *more* expensive once you price it honestly, as Maya's risk-system NPV showed. The vendor fee you are avoiding is a few tens of thousands of dollars a year; the engineer you must hire to build and forever maintain the system is **\$250,000** a year, plus the opportunity cost of that person not working on alpha, plus the diligence penalty for a key-person-dependent black box. Build is only cheaper in the narrow case where the thing built *is* the edge and would otherwise mean not running the strategy at all. For plumbing, "I'll just write it" is how funds become understaffed software companies that never find time to trade.

**"Technology is a back-office afterthought — I'll sort it out after I raise."** The causality runs the other way. You cannot raise institutional capital *without* a credible operational stack, because operational due diligence — which has a veto over the investment decision and which we treat in its own right under the [service-provider stack](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor) — examines exactly this. The stack is not what you build after the money arrives; it is part of what convinces the money to arrive. Treating technology as an afterthought is treating the thing an ODD analyst checks first as the thing you will get to last.

**"More expensive tools mean better returns."** There is no relationship between the premium-tier price of your OMS and your alpha. The premium platforms exist to serve the operational complexity of multi-strategy giants, not to generate returns. A small fund that buys the premium tier to look serious has merely starved its runway to impress no one — allocators care that your stack *passes ODD and works*, not that it is expensive. The right spend is the lightest stack that is institutional-grade enough to pass diligence and support the strategy, and not a dollar more.

**"Market data is a commodity — I'll just get the cheapest feed."** Market data is a contractual minefield, not a commodity. The same prices carry wildly different prices depending on display versus non-display use, per-user versus enterprise licensing, and redistribution rights, and vendors audit usage and bill for breaches. A systematic founder who budgets the cheap per-screen rate for what is actually a non-display, model-feeding use will be audited and back-billed. Getting market-data licensing wrong is not a small mistake; it can be a five- or six-figure surprise and a compliance problem.

**"Once I've bought the stack, I'm done — it runs itself."** The stack is a set of relationships and processes, not a one-time purchase. Vendors raise prices, change features, and must be managed; data licenses must be kept compliant; reconciliation must be run and its breaks chased every single day; the FIX sessions must be monitored. The cost in Figure 4 is an *annual run-rate*, not a setup fee, and the operational discipline of running the stack — reconciling daily, monitoring limits, keeping licenses clean — is the part that actually keeps the promise to investors. Buying the systems is the easy half; operating them with discipline is the half that passes the next ODD review.

## How it plays out in the real world

The clearest real-world lesson about the stack is the one that runs through the named failures, and it is not about the systems funds *had* — it is about the controls they *lacked.* Recall **Madoff**, the canonical operational-due-diligence failure: there was no independent administrator striking the NAV, a tiny unknown auditor, and self-custody. The fraud was possible precisely because the technology and control stack that produces an independent, verifiable number did not exist — Madoff's "statements" were fabricated because no independent system constrained them. The lesson the industry took is exactly the one this post argues: the stack that produces an independent, reconciled, externally-verifiable number is not optional overhead; it is the thing that makes a fund investable at all. An honest operational review checks for the stack's *existence and independence*, and its absence is disqualifying regardless of the returns on the page.

The leverage-and-risk disasters teach the complementary lesson. **LTCM** in 1998 and **Amaranth** in 2006 both had real operations and real systems — and both still lost billions, LTCM roughly **\$4.6 billion** in months on convergence trades at 25-to-30-times leverage, Amaranth about **\$6.6 billion** in roughly a week on concentrated natural-gas spreads. A risk system is not a force field; it measures and enforces the limits the fund chooses, and if the fund chooses to run enormous leverage and concentration, a perfectly functioning risk system will faithfully report the fund into the ground. The stack makes risk *visible and enforceable*; it does not make a bad risk decision good. This is why the risk system's value to an allocator is not that it exists but that it is watched by someone empowered to act on it — a theme the careers series develops in [decision-making under uncertainty](/blog/trading/quant-careers/decision-making-under-uncertainty-the-seniors-edge). **Archegos** in 2021 sharpens the point from the counterparty side: a family office hid roughly **\$10 billion+** of losses behind total-return swaps spread across multiple prime brokers, each of whom had an incomplete picture — a failure of the *plumbing's* visibility across counterparties, exactly the reconciliation-and-aggregation problem the data layer exists to solve.

On the mundane, daily level — the level most founders actually live at — the stack's real-world payoff is the *absence* of stories. The funds that quietly survive are the ones where the reconciliation runs every morning and the breaks are chased before the strike, where the risk limits turn a screen red before a position becomes a headline, where the administrator's file and the fund's books agree to the penny, and where the ODD analyst's checklist of "who strikes the NAV, who monitors risk, who reconciles, who maintains the systems" gets a clean answer at every line. None of that makes a war story. It makes a fund that is still alive in year five, which is the only war story that matters. The cost curve in Figures 4 and 7 is the price of admission to that quiet survival, and the founder's discipline is to pay exactly that price — buy the lean institutional-grade stack, operate it rigorously — and not a dollar more or a control less.

There is one more real-world pattern worth naming: the **upgrade path.** A fund does not buy its final stack on day one. It launches on the lighter, emerging-manager tier — a cloud OMS, an admin-bundled portfolio tool, a sensible data package, outsourced reconciliation — because that is what the launch AUM can afford and it passes ODD. Then, as it scales past the break-even AUM and the cost-as-share-of-revenue curve bends down, it upgrades layer by layer toward the premium tier the larger allocators expect, adding the multi-prime reconciliation, the deeper risk analytics, the dedicated data engineering. The mistake is to buy the destination stack at the starting line; the discipline is to buy the right stack for *this* AUM and to know the road to the next one. The technology stack, like the fund itself, is a thing you scale into — and the founders who survive are the ones who match the stack to the J-curve rather than fighting it.

## When this matters / Further reading

The technology stack matters most at exactly two moments: when you are setting your launch AUM and choosing your strategy, and when an allocator's operational-due-diligence team opens its checklist. At the first moment, the cost curve decides whether the strategy you want to run is affordable at the size you can launch — a data-hungry systematic book needs a launch AUM that can carry a \$200,000 data bill, and pretending otherwise is how a fund starves before it scales. At the second moment, the stack decides whether your great returns are even investable — a spreadsheet-and-email shop fails ODD no matter how good the Sharpe. Between those two moments, the stack is the quiet plumbing that keeps your promise to investors true: that the trades you intend are the trades that happen, that the fund stays inside the limits you committed to, and that the number the administrator strikes is the number that is real. Buy almost everything, build only your edge, operate it with daily discipline, and scale the stack into the J-curve rather than ahead of it.
