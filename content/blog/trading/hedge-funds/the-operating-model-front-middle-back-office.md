> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A hedge fund is a machine that turns an investment idea into a settled, reconciled, reported position, and the front, middle, and back offices are the three sections of that machine.
>
> - The **front office** takes risk (PMs and traders pick trades and send orders); the **middle office** controls it (risk, trade support, compliance monitoring); the **back office** records it (settlement, reconciliation, NAV support). A single trade passes through all three before it is real.
> - The wall between those offices is **segregation of duties**: the person who trades the book must never also be the person who values it, and the person who authorizes a payment must never also be the person who releases the cash. That wall is exactly what operational due diligence and the auditor are there to test.
> - At a four-person launch one founder wears every hat — and a few controls (independent NAV, dual cash authorization, an outside auditor) you still cannot skip, no matter how small you are. The **administrator** is how a small fund buys most of a back office without hiring one.
> - The one fact to remember: a fund dies from an operational break far more often than from a bad trade, and the operating model exists to catch the break before it becomes a loss.

Maya could pick stocks. Eleven months into the Meridian Fund she was up 14% net, her drawdowns had been shallow, and her old colleagues had started asking, only half joking, whether she was hiring. What she could not do, she discovered one Tuesday afternoon, was answer a question that had nothing to do with stock picking at all. Her prime broker's operations contact had emailed: *we show a 2,000-share difference on your position in one name versus what your books say settled yesterday — can you confirm which is right?* Maya stared at the email. She did not actually know which was right. She had sent the order, watched it fill on her screen, and moved on to the next idea. Where the trade went after the fill — who confirmed it, who told the custodian to pay for it, who wrote it down, and who checked that everyone's version of the day matched — was a part of the business she had simply never thought about.

It is the part nobody teaches a trader, because a trader never has to think about it. On a bank desk or inside an established fund, an entire apparatus sits behind the PM, silently turning each click into a settled, paid-for, recorded position. When you launch your own fund, that apparatus is suddenly *yours to build*, and the first thing you learn is that running money and running the operation of running money are two completely different jobs. One is about being right on the market. The other is about a trade being correctly captured, allocated, confirmed, settled, reconciled, and valued — every single time, with no person able to fake any step on their own.

This post is about that second job: the **operating model**, the way a fund organizes itself into a **front office**, a **middle office**, and a **back office**, and the segregation of duties between them that an allocator's operational reviewer and your auditor will both demand to see. The cleanest way to understand the whole machine is to do exactly what Maya never did: follow one trade from the moment the idea forms to the moment it lands in the official net asset value. Figure 1 is that journey on one page — the trade flowing left to right through the three offices, changing hands at every step. We will spend the rest of the post walking it slowly, then pricing out what it costs to build, and what a four-person shop can and cannot skip.

## Foundations: how a fund's operation is organized

Before any nuance, get one mental model and a short stack of definitions firmly in place. The model is this: a fund is a small factory, and like any factory it has a line. Raw material (an investment idea) enters at one end; a finished, recorded product (a settled position reflected in the fund's value) comes out the other. The line is divided into three stations, and the entire discipline of fund operations is making sure the product cannot skip a station, and that no single worker controls the whole line.

Let us define every term this post uses, from zero. If you trade for a living the market mechanics are familiar; it is this *operational* vocabulary that is usually a blank page.

**The front office.** The risk-takers. These are the portfolio managers (PMs) who form the investment views and the traders who turn those views into orders and work them in the market. The front office *generates* the fund's positions and its profit and loss. It is the part of the fund a trader already understands, because it is the only part most traders ever see. Everything the front office does creates work for the other two offices.

**The middle office.** The controllers. The middle office sits between the people taking risk and the people recording it, and its job is to *check the front office without trading itself*. It contains risk management (measuring and limiting the fund's exposures), trade support or "trade ops" (capturing each trade correctly, chasing confirmations, resolving breaks), and compliance monitoring (watching that trades stay inside legal and mandate limits). The middle office is the office founders most often forget exists, because on a small desk its jobs are invisible until something goes wrong.

**The back office.** The recorders. The back office handles everything *after* the trade is agreed: settlement (the actual exchange of cash for securities), reconciliation (confirming the fund's records match everyone else's), and support for the net asset value process (feeding clean data to whoever strikes the official fund value). The back office has **no authority to trade** — that separation is deliberate and load-bearing, and we will return to it. At a small fund, most of the back office is not done in-house at all; it is outsourced to the **administrator**.

**The trade lifecycle.** The full path a single trade travels: idea, order, execution, allocation, confirmation, settlement, reconciliation, and finally its appearance in the NAV. Each step hands the trade to a different function and a different control. The lifecycle is the spine of this entire post, because every office is defined by which steps of the lifecycle it owns.

**Net asset value (NAV).** The official value of the fund per unit of ownership: total assets minus liabilities, divided by the number of shares outstanding. If the fund is worth \$52,000,000 net of liabilities and there are 50,000 shares, the NAV per share is \$1,040. Every fee, subscription, and redemption is calculated against the NAV. The whole operating model exists, in the end, to make this one number trustworthy. We go deep on how it is struck in [NAV, accounting, and the administrator](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator).

**The administrator (the "admin").** The independent outside firm that keeps the fund's books, strikes the NAV, holds the investor register, and processes subscriptions and redemptions. For a launch, the administrator is the back office you rent instead of build. We cover the full provider relationship in [the service-provider stack: prime broker, administrator, auditor](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor).

**The prime broker (PB).** The bank that finances the fund's trades, holds its assets in custody, lends it stock to short, and clears and settles its trades. The PB's records are one of the independent sources the back office reconciles against every day. We cover its full role in [the trade lifecycle, execution, and prime brokerage](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage).

**Segregation of duties.** The principle that no single person should control a transaction from start to finish. The one who trades must not also value; the one who authorizes a payment must not also release it; the one who keeps the records must not also audit them. Segregation of duties is the central control of the entire operating model and the thing operational due diligence is most determined to verify.

**Straight-through processing (STP).** The ideal where a trade flows automatically from execution to settlement without anyone re-keying it by hand. Every manual re-entry is a place an error can be introduced, so the more of the lifecycle that is straight-through, the fewer breaks the back office has to clear. Small funds have less STP and more manual steps, which is precisely why they have more breaks.

**Reconciliation (the "recon").** The daily process of confirming that the fund's own records of its positions, cash, and trades match the independent records of its prime broker and its administrator. Reconciliation is how the operation *finds out it is wrong* before the error reaches the NAV or the investor.

**A break.** A mismatch found during reconciliation: the fund's books say one thing, the prime broker (or the administrator) says another. A break is not yet a loss; it is a signal that something — a missed trade, a fat-fingered quantity, a fee booked twice, a corporate action handled in one place but not the other — needs investigating and clearing. The back office lives and dies on how fast it clears breaks. An uncleared break is how a small error quietly compounds into a real one.

**Operational due diligence (ODD).** The allocator's separate, veto-wielding review of whether the fund's *operations* are sound — distinct from the investment case. ODD checks segregation of duties, independent administration, cash-movement controls, valuation policy, counterparty setup, and business continuity. A fund with great returns can be, and routinely is, declined on ODD alone. We give ODD its own treatment in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto); for now, hold that the operating model in this post largely exists *to pass ODD*.

Here is the through-line that ties those definitions together. A hedge fund sells a promise: *trust me with your capital and I will compound it inside a structure that protects you.* The operating model is the structure half of that sentence made physical. The front office makes the bet; the middle office checks that the bet is inside the rules and the risk budget; the back office proves the bet actually happened, was paid for, and is worth what the statement says. Strip away the controls between those offices and the promise becomes unverifiable — which, to an institutional investor, means uninvestable. Maya's 14% was real. The 2,000-share break in her inbox was the operating model gently informing her that "real" is not the same as "correctly recorded," and that the gap between the two is exactly the business she had not yet built.

## The trade lifecycle

The fastest way to understand the three offices is to stop thinking about them as departments and start thinking about them as *stages a trade passes through*. A trade is not an event; it is a process with eight steps, and each step changes whose hands the trade is in. Walk the steps once, carefully, and the rest of the post is just detail.

**1. Idea.** A PM forms a view — a stock is mispriced, a spread should converge, a sector is about to re-rate. The idea lives in the front office. It carries no operational footprint yet; nothing has happened that anyone outside the desk needs to record.

**2. Order.** The PM (or the trader on her behalf) decides to act and stages an **order**: buy 100,000 shares of a name, with a price limit and a time horizon. The order is still front-office, but the moment it exists, the rest of the machine is on notice. Before it goes out, a well-run fund runs a **pre-trade check** — does this order breach a position limit, a concentration rule, a mandate restriction, a regulatory threshold? At a large fund that check is automated in the order management system; at a small fund it is a discipline the founder has to impose on herself.

**3. Execution.** The trader works the order in the market and it **fills** — wholly or in pieces, at various prices. Execution is the last purely front-office step. The instant a fill prints, an obligation is born: the fund now *owes* cash and is *owed* shares (for a buy), and that obligation has to be confirmed, settled, and recorded whether or not the PM ever looks at it again.

**4. Allocation.** If the fund runs more than one account or vehicle — a domestic feeder and an offshore feeder, or several separately managed accounts alongside the main fund — the fill has to be **allocated** across them, fairly and by a pre-set rule, so no account is favored with the better fills. Allocation is a middle-office and trade-support function, and it is a genuine compliance pressure point: cherry-picking good fills into a favored account is a classic abuse regulators look for. For a single-fund launch this step is trivial, but the moment Maya adds a managed account it becomes real.

**5. Confirmation.** The fund and its broker independently record the trade, and the two records are **matched**: same security, same quantity, same price, same settlement date. A mismatch here is the earliest, cheapest place to catch an error, which is why confirmation happens within hours of the fill, not days. This is trade support — middle office — talking to the broker's back office. Affirmation through an electronic matching utility is the straight-through version; a phone call and an email is the manual version that small funds often start with.

**6. Settlement.** On the settlement date — for US equities, one business day after the trade under the **T+1** standard adopted in 2024 — the actual exchange happens: cash leaves the fund's account and the securities arrive (for a buy), through the custodian and the prime broker. Settlement is back-office and it is where a payment is actually authorized and released — which makes it the single most cash-sensitive step in the lifecycle and the one most fiercely governed by segregation of duties.

**7. Reconciliation.** The next morning, the back office (or the administrator) **reconciles**: the fund's record of yesterday's trades, positions, and cash is lined up against the prime broker's record and the administrator's record. They should agree to the share and the cent. Where they do not, a **break** is logged and chased down until it clears. Reconciliation is the safety net that catches everything the earlier steps missed.

**8. NAV.** Periodically — monthly for most hedge funds — the reconciled positions are priced at independent marks, fees and expenses are accrued, and the administrator strikes the official **NAV**. The trade that started as a flicker of conviction in the PM's head is now a line in the fund's audited value, a number an investor is asked to trust. The lifecycle is complete.

Notice what just happened across those eight steps: the trade was handed off four times, from the front office (steps 1–3) to the middle office (4–5) to the back office (6–7) to the administrator (8), and at every handoff a different person with a different incentive checked it. That choreography of handoffs *is* the operating model. The three offices are just names for who is holding the trade at each stage.

#### Worked example: a 2 million dollar trade walked through every step

Maya decides to buy a position in a mid-cap industrial. The Meridian Fund runs \$50,000,000, and she wants a 4% position, so she stages an order for \$2,000,000 of the stock at roughly \$40 a share — about 50,000 shares. Walk it:

- **Idea and order.** Maya forms the view and stages the order. Her pre-trade check confirms a \$2,000,000 position is 4% of NAV, inside her 8% single-name limit and her concentration policy. The order is cleared to go.
- **Execution.** The trader works it over the morning and fills 50,000 shares at an average \$40.10, a total of \$2,005,000 plus, say, \$200 of commission. The fill prints. The fund now owes \$2,005,200.
- **Allocation.** Meridian has one fund, so the whole fill allocates to it — no split needed today. (When Maya adds an offshore feeder next year, this step splits the fill pro rata between the two.)
- **Confirmation.** By early afternoon, trade support matches the fund's record against the executing broker's: 50,000 shares, \$40.10, settling T+1. The records agree. No break at confirmation.
- **Settlement.** The next business day, on the T+1 cycle, the prime broker pays \$2,005,200 out of the fund's cash and the 50,000 shares land in custody. A payment of that size is *authorized* by one person in operations and *released* by a second — never the same hand.
- **Reconciliation.** The following morning the back office reconciles. The PB shows 50,000 shares and \$2,005,200 of cash out; the fund's books show the same; the administrator's feed agrees. The trade reconciles clean.
- **NAV.** At month-end the administrator prices the 50,000 shares at the closing mark, includes the position in total assets, accrues the fees, and strikes the NAV. Maya's idea is now part of the fund's official value.

*One trade touched seven functions and was checked by four independent parties before it became a number an investor could see — and Maya, the person with the idea, controlled exactly one of those seven steps.*

## The front office

The front office is the part of the fund that looks like the job a trader signed up for. It is the PMs forming views, the analysts doing the research that feeds those views, and the traders working orders in the market. Its product is **risk and return**: positions that, if the thesis is right, make money. Everything glamorous about a hedge fund — the call that was right, the trade that printed, the year that beat the index — happens here.

But the front office has a second nature that founders underrate: it is the source of all the work the rest of the fund does. Every order the front office sends becomes an allocation to compute, a confirmation to match, a settlement to fund, a reconciliation line to clear, a position to value. A high-turnover strategy that fires hundreds of trades a day generates a torrent of operational work; a low-turnover concentrated book generates a trickle. When Maya chose her strategy in [choosing your strategy: the operating lens](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens), she was, whether she realized it or not, choosing how heavy her operation would have to be. The front office sets the *volume* every other office must process.

The front office also generates the data the middle and back offices depend on, which means front-office sloppiness propagates downstream. A trader who books a trade to the wrong account, fat-fingers a quantity, or forgets to capture an off-system trade has not just made a front-office error — he has planted a break that the back office will spend the next morning hunting. This is why even a small fund needs a disciplined **trade-capture** habit: every trade recorded, immediately, in one system of record, with no trades living only in someone's head or on a sticky note. The cheapest break to fix is the one that never happens because the trade was captured cleanly the first time.

One more front-office truth worth stating plainly: the front office should *take* risk, not *measure its own*. The PM who is long a position is the worst-placed person to judge dispassionately how much that position could lose, because she is the one who believes in it. That is not a character flaw; it is human, and it is exactly why the middle office exists as a separate function. The front office's job is to be right; the middle office's job is to make sure that being wrong cannot kill the fund. Hold that distinction — it is the seam along which the entire operating model is built.

## The middle office

The middle office is the office nobody outside the industry has heard of and the one a founder most underestimates. It sits between the risk-takers and the recorders, and its mandate is a single sentence: *check the front office, independently, without trading.* It does three jobs.

**Risk management.** The risk function measures the fund's exposures — gross and net, by name, sector, factor, and scenario — and watches them against pre-set limits. It computes the metrics that tell the founder how much the fund could lose under a normal bad day and under a stress, and it raises a hand when a limit is breached. The discipline of risk management — and why it is the thing that most often separates funds that survive from funds that blow up — is covered in [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up). For the operating model the point is structural: risk must be **independent of the desk**. A risk officer who reports to the PM and depends on the PM's good year for his bonus is not a control; he is a rubber stamp. The whole value of the function is that it can say "cut this position" to the person who put it on.

**Trade support (trade operations).** Trade support owns the middle steps of the lifecycle: capturing each trade correctly into the system of record, allocating fills across accounts, chasing and matching confirmations with brokers, and being the first responder when a break appears. When the prime broker emailed Maya about a 2,000-share difference, trade support is the function that should have caught it before the PB did — and the function whose absence meant the PB caught it first. Trade support is the connective tissue of the lifecycle; it is the office that makes sure a trade does not fall through a crack between execution and settlement.

**Compliance monitoring.** Compliance in the operating sense is the day-to-day watch that trades and behavior stay inside the legal and mandate boundaries: position limits, restricted lists, holding-period rules, the prohibitions on the founder's personal trading, the regulatory thresholds that trigger a filing. This is the operational arm of the broader compliance program; the registration and rulebook side lives in [registration and regulation: SEC, CFTC, Reg D](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d). The monitoring is middle-office because it has to be independent of the desk it monitors — the same logic as risk.

What unites all three middle-office jobs is **independence from the front office**. Each one exists to catch a front-office mistake or excess, and each one is worthless if it answers to the person it is supposed to check. At a large fund this is enforced by org chart and reporting lines. At a four-person launch it has to be enforced by *process and outside parties*, because Maya cannot hire a risk officer who is independent of Maya. We will see how a small fund squares that circle when we get to small-AUM controls — the short version is that the administrator and the auditor become the independence the headcount cannot supply.

#### Worked example: a reconciliation break worth catching

A month after the industrial trade, Meridian's back office runs its morning reconciliation and finds a break. The fund's books show a cash balance of \$3,180,000; the prime broker shows \$3,165,000 — a difference of \$15,000. Fifteen thousand dollars is small against a \$50,000,000 fund, but a break is a break, and an uninvestigated one is a crack you do not understand.

Trade support pulls the day's activity apart. The cause: the stock paid a quarterly dividend of \$0.30 a share on the 50,000-share position — \$15,000 — that the prime broker credited to the cash account on the pay date, but that the fund's internal books, which had not been updated for the **corporate action**, never recorded. The fund's cash was *understated* by exactly the missed dividend. The break is real money the fund was owed and had received, but had not written down.

The fix is to book the dividend into the internal records; the two sides then agree at \$3,180,000, and the break clears. Had no one reconciled, the error would have flowed into the month-end NAV — the fund would have reported a value \$15,000 too low, every investor's statement would have been a hair wrong, and the fee accrual computed against that NAV would have been off too. *The break was not a loss; it was the operating model catching a missing entry before it poisoned the official value — which is the entire reason reconciliation exists.*

## The back office

The back office is where the trade becomes *settled, paid-for, and recorded* — the unglamorous machinery that turns an agreed trade into a fact. It owns the last operational steps of the lifecycle and it has one defining constraint: **the back office has no authority to trade.** The people who settle, reconcile, and account must be structurally separate from the people who put on the risk. That separation is not bureaucratic caution; it is the wall that stops a single bad actor — or a single honest mistake by an overworked person doing two jobs — from running unchecked from trade to cash to records.

The back office does three things.

**Settlement.** On the settlement date the back office (working through the prime broker and custodian) ensures the cash goes out and the securities come in, for a buy — and the reverse for a sell. Settlement is where money actually moves, which makes it the most dangerous step in the lifecycle from a controls standpoint. A wire is irreversible. So settlement is governed by the tightest segregation of duties in the whole fund: the **four-eyes** principle, where a payment must be set up by one person and approved by a second before it can be released. No single individual can move the fund's cash alone. This is the control that, more than any other, separates a fund that can be defrauded from one that cannot.

**Reconciliation.** We have met it twice already, because it is the heart of the back office. Each day the back office confirms that the fund's record of its world matches the independent records held by others — the prime broker for positions and cash, the administrator for the official books. Three sources, one truth; where they disagree, a break is born and chased. A clean daily reconciliation is the single best evidence that a fund's operation is sound, which is why ODD analysts ask to see the reconciliation process and its break log. A fund that reconciles monthly instead of daily, or that lets breaks age for weeks, is telling an ODD analyst that it finds out it is wrong slowly. That is a finding.

**NAV support.** The back office does not, in a well-run fund, strike the official NAV itself — that is the administrator's job, and its independence is the point (we will see why under segregation of duties). But the back office *feeds* the NAV: it provides the clean, reconciled positions and cash, resolves the breaks before the strike, supplies the data for fee accruals, and reconciles the fund's own **shadow NAV** against the administrator's official one. The shadow NAV is the back office's check on the checker — the fund computes its own value independently and compares it to the administrator's, and a clean match between the two is itself a control. Two independent computations that agree are far harder to fake than one.

The back office also owns the unglamorous job of chasing **failed trades**. A trade fails to settle when, on the settlement date, the cash or the securities are not where they should be — the counterparty could not deliver the shares, a settlement instruction was wrong, or the fund's cash was short. A failed trade is not free: the fund can owe interest on the unsettled balance, can be **bought-in** (forced to buy the shares in the market at a worse price to deliver them), and can have its standing with the prime broker quietly dinged. Tracking the settlement status of every open trade, and resolving the fails before they cost money, is back-office work that never makes the pitch book but absolutely makes the difference between a clean operation and a leaky one.

The reason the back office cannot trade is the same reason a bank teller cannot also approve the bank's own audit: the person who records and settles transactions must not be able to *create* them, or the record stops being independent of the action. When you read about a fund that was defrauded from the inside, look for the place where someone controlled both the trade and the record of it. That is always where the rot started, and it is precisely the configuration the back-office separation is designed to make impossible.

## Segregation of duties

If you remember one principle from this entire post, make it this one, because it is the principle the auditor and the ODD analyst care about above all others: **no single person should control a transaction from beginning to end.** Segregation of duties is the rule that the offices we have just described must be staffed — and authorized — so that the dangerous combinations are structurally impossible.

There are three combinations that matter most, and they map exactly onto the front-middle-back split.

**Trading and valuing must be separate.** The person who puts on the position must not be the person who decides what it is worth. If they are the same person, that person can mark a losing or illiquid position at a flattering price and inflate the fund's value — and therefore the fees they earn on it. This is why valuation lives with the independent administrator, not the desk. The PM is the worst-placed and most-conflicted person to value her own book, and segregation removes the temptation by removing the ability.

**Authorizing and releasing cash must be separate.** The person who initiates a payment must not be the person who releases it. One sets up the wire; a second, independent person approves it. This four-eyes control on cash is the single most important fraud defense in the operating model, because cash is the thing that, once gone, does not come back. Madoff's fund — which we will return to — had no such separation; the manager controlled the cash, the records, and the story, and the absence of any independent hand on the money is exactly what let a fabrication run for decades.

**Recording and auditing must be separate.** The person who keeps the books must not be the person who attests they are correct. That is why the auditor is an outside firm with its own liability on the line, not an internal function. An internal "audit" of records the same shop produced is not an audit; it is the shop checking its own homework.

Figure 6 lays these out as a grid: in each row, the person who does the first task on the left must *not* also do the forbidden task in the middle, and the right column names who does it instead. Trade the book, but do not also value it — the administrator values. Authorize a payment, but do not also release the cash — a second person releases. Keep the records, but do not also audit them — an outside auditor signs the year.

![A three by three grid showing segregation of duties: the person who trades must not also value, the person who authorizes a payment must not also release the cash, and the person who keeps records must not also audit them, with an independent party owning each forbidden task](/imgs/blogs/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office-6.png)

The reason segregation of duties is the thing ODD checks hardest is that it is the difference between a fund that can be defrauded and one that cannot — and an allocator handing over a beneficiary's retirement money cares far more about "can this fund be stolen from" than about whether last year's return was 14% or 12%. A great-returns fund with broken segregation is a fund where the great returns might not be real, or might be siphoned, and an ODD analyst has seen enough Madoffs to know that the controls, not the numbers, are what protect the investor. The operating model's whole purpose, viewed from the outside, is to make the founder's promise *verifiable by someone who does not trust the founder.* Segregation of duties is how that verifiability is built.

#### Worked example: segregation of duties in a four-person shop

This is the hard case, because segregation seems to require headcount Maya does not have. The Meridian Fund is four people: Maya (founder/PM), one analyst, one trader, and one operations person, call her Dana. There is no risk department, no compliance department, no internal audit. How does a four-person shop segregate duties?

The answer is that segregation is achieved by *outside parties and process*, not internal headcount:

- **Trading vs valuing.** Maya and the trader put on the risk. Neither values the book — the **independent administrator** strikes the NAV from prices it sources itself. The trading-vs-valuing wall is satisfied not by hiring a valuation team but by hiring the administrator. This is the single most important control a small fund buys, and it is non-negotiable.
- **Authorizing vs releasing cash.** A payment is set up by Dana and approved by Maya (or vice versa) before the bank or prime broker will release it — four-eyes, enforced by the bank's own dual-authorization settings, not by trust. Two people, even in a four-person shop. No wire moves on one signature.
- **Recording vs auditing.** Dana and the administrator keep the books; an **outside auditor** signs the annual financials. The recording-vs-auditing wall is the auditor, an outside firm.

So a four-person shop *can* segregate the three dangerous combinations — but only by leaning on the administrator (for valuation), the bank's dual-authorization (for cash), and the auditor (for attestation). The headcount Maya lacks is supplied by providers whose entire business is independence. *A small fund does not skip segregation of duties; it rents it — and the founder who tries to save money by self-administering or self-authorizing has not saved money, she has made her fund uninvestable.*

## Build vs outsource

The most consequential operating decision a founder makes is not which office to staff first; it is how much of the operation to **build in-house** versus **outsource** to providers. And the dominant answer, for nearly every launch, is that the **administrator absorbs most of the back office**.

Think about what the administrator does, from the definitions: it keeps the fund's books, strikes the NAV, holds the investor register, processes subscriptions and redemptions, accrues the fees, and reconciles against the prime broker. That list is, almost verbatim, the job description of a back office. Hiring an administrator is how a four-person fund operates with a back office it never had to build — and, critically, with the *independence* that an internal back office reporting to the founder could never provide. The administrator is not just cheaper than building; it is *better*, because its independence is the control ODD demands.

So the realistic build-vs-outsource map for a launch looks like this:

- **Front office: always in-house.** The PMs, traders, and analysts are the fund — you do not outsource the alpha.
- **Middle office: mostly in-house, partly outsourced.** Trade support and the day-to-day risk and compliance monitoring usually sit with the founder and the ops person, but the *compliance program* is typically built and run by an outside **compliance consultant** or outsourced chief compliance officer, and risk analytics often come from a vendor system rather than a hand-built model. The middle office is the most blended of the three.
- **Back office: mostly outsourced to the administrator.** Settlement runs through the prime broker; the books, NAV, register, and investor servicing run through the administrator. The internal "back office" at a small fund is often one operations person who supervises the providers, reconciles the fund's shadow records against theirs, and handles the exceptions the providers escalate.

The trade-off is the usual one. Building in-house gives you control and, eventually at scale, lower marginal cost; outsourcing gives you independence, a credible provider name in your pitch book, and a fixed cost you can afford on day one when you cannot afford a team. For a launch, outsourcing wins on every axis that matters — especially the one where the administrator's independence is itself the control. The build-it-yourself temptation is strongest exactly where you must resist it: the back office, where in-house means the founder marking her own book.

#### Worked example: the cost of outsourcing the back office to the administrator

Maya prices it out. To build an in-house back office capable of the administrator's job — daily books, NAV, register, subscriptions, redemptions, fee accruals, reconciliation — she would need at least one experienced fund accountant and supporting systems. A capable fund accountant in a major financial center is not cheap; loaded with benefits, systems access, and the data feeds the role needs, call it \$200,000 a year all-in for one hire who would still be a single point of failure and, fatally, *not independent of Maya.*

The administrator, by contrast, quotes a floor of roughly \$60,000 a year at Meridian's \$50,000,000 of AUM — about 12 basis points of assets — for a whole firm's worth of fund accounting, an independent NAV, the register, and investor servicing, delivered by people whose names go in her pitch book and whose independence passes ODD. The administrator is both *cheaper* (\$60,000 vs \$200,000+) and *more credible* (independent vs in-house) than the hire — and it scales: at \$250,000,000 the admin fee might rise to \$110,000, which is just 4.4 basis points, while a second in-house accountant would have cost another \$200,000.

*Outsourcing the back office to the administrator is the rare decision where the cheaper option is also the more credible one — you pay a fraction of an in-house team's cost and get the independence the in-house team could never provide.*

## The operating model at small AUM

Now the reality almost every founder actually lives: launch is small. The kit's honest numbers say the median launch is tens of millions, not hundreds, and break-even on an institutional-grade infrastructure is around \$80,000,000 to \$100,000,000 of AUM. At \$50,000,000 with four people, the three-office model does not disappear — but it gets *compressed onto fewer humans*, and the danger is that the compression quietly collapses the controls that make the offices worth having.

At small AUM, **one person wears several hats.** Maya is the front office and, informally, the head of risk and compliance. Dana is the entire internal middle and back office — trade capture, reconciliation, provider supervision, cash setup. The org chart that a large fund spreads across dozens of people is folded onto two. Figure 5 makes the contrast vivid: a day-one shop where the founder trades, checks her own risk, pulls her own positions, and marks her own book, versus a scaled operation where PMs run the book, an independent risk officer checks the desk, the administrator strikes the NAV, and operations reconciles independently.

![A before-and-after comparison showing a day-one shop where the founder does everything from trading to marking the book, versus a scaled operation where separate people and the administrator handle trading, risk, valuation, and reconciliation](/imgs/blogs/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office-5.png)

The temptation at small AUM is to read that left column as acceptable because the fund is small and the founder is honest. It is not acceptable, and here is the discipline: **some controls do not scale with size, and you cannot skip them no matter how small you are.** Three in particular survive the compression intact:

**You cannot skip the independent administrator.** No matter how small, the NAV must be struck by an independent outside party. A founder who self-administers to save \$60,000 has not saved \$60,000; she has rendered her fund uninvestable to every professional allocator, because the trading-vs-valuing wall is breached at its most important point. This is the lesson Maya learned the hard way with the fund-of-funds in [the service-provider stack](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor).

**You cannot skip dual cash authorization.** No matter how small, no wire moves on one signature. Four-eyes on cash costs nothing — it is a setting at the bank and the prime broker — and it is the control that makes the fund un-defraudable from the inside. A four-person shop that lets the founder move cash alone has a hole an ODD analyst will find and a structure a future bad day could exploit.

**You cannot skip the outside auditor.** No matter how small, the annual financials are signed by an independent, *recognized* auditor — not a one-or-two-person shop nobody has heard of, which is the Madoff configuration. The recording-vs-auditing wall is the auditor, and it survives at every size.

Everything else at small AUM is a matter of doing more with manual process and provider supervision: more hand-keying (and therefore more breaks to clear), more reliance on the administrator and the compliance consultant, more discipline imposed by the founder on herself in place of an org chart. That is fine — it is how almost every fund starts. What is not fine is letting "we're small" become the excuse that breaches the three walls that protect the investor. Size dictates *how many people* run the offices; it never dictates *whether the dangerous combinations are separated.* The controls are the same at \$50,000,000 and \$5,000,000,000; only the headcount that delivers them changes.

Figure 7 shows that headcount story: internal staff by office as AUM grows from a \$50,000,000 launch to \$250,000,000 to \$1,000,000,000. The front and middle offices grow as the fund grows, but the internal back office stays strikingly thin at every size — because the administrator, not internal headcount, is doing the back-office work.

## Common misconceptions

**"Operations is just back-office paperwork — the real work is the trading."** This is the misconception that kills funds. A fund dies from an operational break, a redemption it could not process, a cash-control failure, or a compliance blow-up far more often than from a single bad trade. Operations is not paperwork that follows the real work; it is the machinery that makes the real work *real*. Maya's 14% meant nothing the day a fund-of-funds learned she struck her own NAV. The trading earns the return; the operation lets you keep and report it.

**"At a small fund, segregation of duties is impossible, so we skip it."** A four-person shop cannot hire a risk department, but it absolutely can — and must — segregate the three dangerous combinations, by renting the independence: the administrator values, the bank's dual-authorization splits cash, the auditor attests. Segregation is achieved by providers and process, not by headcount. The founder who treats "we're small" as a license to combine trading and valuing has not adapted the model to her size; she has broken it.

**"The administrator is an overhead I can do myself until I'm bigger."** Self-administration does not save money; it destroys investability. The administrator's *independence* is the control — it is the answer to "who says your NAV is real besides you?" A fund that self-administers to save a five-figure fee has converted a small cost into a fatal flaw, because no professional allocator will invest where the manager marks his own book. You outsource the back office not when you can afford to, but from day one, precisely because you cannot afford the alternative.

**"Reconciliation is a formality — the prime broker's numbers and mine always match."** They do not always match, and the day they do not is the day reconciliation earns its keep. A missed dividend, a corporate action booked on one side only, a fee charged twice, a trade captured to the wrong account, a settlement that failed — these are routine, and reconciliation is how you find them before they reach the NAV. A fund that treats reconciliation as a rubber stamp is a fund that finds out it is wrong from its investors instead of from its own controls.

**"A break is a loss."** A break is a *mismatch*, not a loss. Most breaks are timing differences, missing entries, or data errors that net to zero once cleared — like Maya's \$15,000 dividend, which was money the fund had, not money it lost. The danger of a break is not the break itself but leaving it uninvestigated, because an uncleared break hides the one break that *is* a real loss. The discipline is to clear every break, every day, so the real one cannot hide among the harmless ones.

**"Straight-through processing means I don't need an operations person."** Automation reduces the manual re-keying that creates breaks, but it does not remove the need for someone to supervise the providers, investigate the breaks that automation flags, handle the exceptions no system anticipated, and own the relationship with the prime broker and administrator. STP makes the operations person more leveraged; it does not make them optional. The exceptions are exactly where the operational risk concentrates, and exceptions need a human.

## How it plays out in the real world

The clearest real-world lessons in the operating model come, as usual, from the funds that got it wrong — because a working operation is invisible and a broken one is a case study.

**Madoff is the canonical operating-model failure.** Bernard Madoff's fund reported impossibly smooth returns for years and was, in the end, a Ponzi scheme that fabricated roughly \$65,000,000,000 of statement value with around \$17,500,000,000 of principal actually lost. The operational tell was not the returns; it was the structure. Madoff had **no independent administrator** — he produced the statements himself. He had a **tiny, unknown auditor** — a three-person shop nobody had heard of, in clear breach of the recording-vs-auditing wall. He used **self-custody** — the assets were not independently held where an outsider could confirm them. Every one of those is a segregation-of-duties failure, and any honest operational due diligence would have caught the missing controls long before the math was checked. Madoff is why independent administration moved from best practice to table stakes after 2008: the missing operating model, not the fake numbers, is what an ODD analyst is trained to find.

**Archegos shows the cost of broken middle-office controls — at the counterparty.** When Bill Hwang's family office collapsed in 2021, it cost its prime brokers more than \$10,000,000,000, with Credit Suisse alone losing around \$5,500,000,000. Hwang had built enormous concentrated positions through total-return swaps spread across multiple primes, each of which could see only its slice and none of which saw the whole. The failure was a middle-office failure of *risk visibility* — at the banks, not just at Archegos: the prime brokers' risk functions could not see the total leverage because it was fragmented across counterparties, and the controls that should have aggregated and limited the exposure did not fire in time. The lesson for a founder is that the middle office's job — seeing the true, aggregated risk and limiting it — is exactly the job whose absence turns a concentrated position into a multi-billion-dollar hole.

**LTCM and Amaranth show what the operating model does *not* fix.** Long-Term Capital Management lost about \$4,600,000,000 in 1998 on leveraged convergence trades; Amaranth lost roughly \$6,600,000,000 — about 65% of its assets — in a week on concentrated natural-gas bets in 2006. Both had real operations: administrators, auditors, settlement, reconciliation. The operating model did its job — the trades were correctly captured, settled, and recorded — and the funds still blew up, because the operating model is a control against *fraud and mispricing*, not against *bad risk*. This is exactly why ODD and investment due diligence are separate processes: a clean operation is necessary for an institution to invest, and nowhere near sufficient. The operating model gets your fund *believed*; it does not get the strategy *right*. You still have to not lose the money — a discipline covered in [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up).

**The everyday version is the failed trade and the aged break.** Most operational damage is not a Madoff; it is mundane. A trade fails to settle because the shares could not be delivered, and the fund pays interest or is bought-in. A break sits uninvestigated for three weeks and turns out to be a fee booked twice that overstated the NAV, so a redeeming investor was paid too much and the remaining investors quietly subsidized him. A corporate action — a split, a merger, a special dividend — is handled by the prime broker but missed in the fund's books, and the position is mis-stated until the next reconciliation catches it. None of these is dramatic. All of them are exactly what the front-middle-back operating model and the daily reconciliation exist to catch, and all of them are what an ODD analyst is probing when she asks Maya, in that quiet voice, "Walk me through what happens after the trade fills." The right answer — *seven functions, four independent parties, a daily reconciliation, three walls that cannot be crossed* — is the operating model. The wrong answer is the sound of a fund that has not built one yet.

## When this matters / Further reading

The operating model matters the moment you stop trading someone else's capital inside someone else's apparatus and start running your own fund — because the apparatus that was invisible behind you becomes yours to build, and the gap between "I was right on the trade" and "the trade is correctly settled, reconciled, and reported" is now your responsibility. It matters most acutely on the day an allocator's operational reviewer sits across from you and asks who confirms, who settles, who reconciles, and who values — because the quality of your answer, not the quality of your returns, decides whether their beneficiaries' money is allowed to find you.

Build the model around one discipline: a trade must pass through three independent offices — front takes the risk, middle controls it, back records it — and the three dangerous combinations (trading and valuing, authorizing and releasing cash, recording and auditing) must be separated by providers and process even at four people. Rent the independence you cannot staff: the administrator for valuation, the bank's dual-authorization for cash, the auditor for attestation. Reconcile daily, clear every break, and never let "we're small" become the excuse that crosses a wall. A fund dies from an operational break far more often than from a bad trade, and the operating model is the machine that catches the break before it becomes a loss.
