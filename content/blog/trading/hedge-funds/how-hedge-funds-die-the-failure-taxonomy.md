> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Hedge funds do not die in a thousand creative ways; they die in about seven recognizable ones, and each has a specific defense a founder can build before launch.
>
> - The fast deaths are the **blow-up** (leverage + concentration + a correlated tail: LTCM, Amaranth, Archegos) and **fraud** (Madoff and the operational-due-diligence failure that should have caught it).
> - The slow deaths are **style drift**, the **redemption death-spiral**, **key-person loss**, and the most common ending of all — **slow death by fee compression and mediocrity** while costs grind past a sub-scale management fee.
> - Roughly **7–11% of hedge funds liquidate every year** (spiking in 2008 and 2020), and a large fraction of launches never see year five. Survival is engineered, not lucky.
> - The whole point of a taxonomy is leverage for the defender: name the mode, name the root cause, build the one defense that closes it.

Two long/short-equity funds launch in the same quarter. Both are run by genuinely talented PMs out of a top platform. Over their first three years, both compound at roughly the same rate — call it low-double-digit annualized, gross. On a returns chart, you could not tell them apart. One of them is a healthy `\$600M` business at year ten, paying its founders well and turning down capital. The other is a footnote: gated, then wound down at year three, with LPs recovering cents on the dollar after a forced sale.

The difference was never the returns. It was everything around the returns. The fund that died ran its book at gross exposure no risk committee ever capped, priced its own illiquid positions, offered monthly liquidity on assets that took six months to sell, and was — in every meaningful sense — one person. When that person had a bad quarter and an LP got nervous, the structure had no shock absorbers. The redemptions came, the fire sale started, the marks fell, and the spiral closed in eighteen months. The survivor had built, deliberately and boringly, a defense against each way a fund can die — and so when the same kind of bad quarter arrived, it absorbed the hit and kept compounding.

This is the post about how funds actually end. Not the heroic version where a brilliant trade goes wrong, but the real version: an ops failure, a redemption cascade, a compliance blow-up, a slow fade into mediocrity. The encouraging news is that the list of ways to die is short and repeats across decades. Figure 1 is the whole map — the failure taxonomy, with each mode's root cause and the defense that closes it. The rest of this post walks each row, with the named cases that made it famous and the survival math a founder should run before signing a single subscription document.

## Foundations: the ways a fund ends

Before the cases, the vocabulary. A hedge fund is a business that sells an aligned promise — to compound capital with skill, inside a structure that protects the investor. A fund "dies" when one of three things breaks so badly the promise can no longer be kept: the **performance** stops being credible, the **structure** stops being sound, or the **capital** stops being sticky. Every failure mode below is one of those three breaking, and most deaths involve two of them feeding on each other.

Let me define the terms this post leans on, from zero.

**AUM** (assets under management) is the total capital the fund manages. **NAV** (net asset value) is the fund's net worth — assets minus liabilities — and **NAV per share** is NAV divided by the number of shares, the price at which investors buy and redeem. **Leverage** is borrowed exposure: if a fund has `\$100M` of capital and holds `\$300M` of positions, it runs 3× gross leverage, and a 10% move in the book is a 30% move in the equity. **Gross exposure** is the sum of long and short positions; **net exposure** is longs minus shorts. A market-neutral fund can be 8× gross and near 0× net — small directional bet, large amount of stuff that can go wrong.

**Concentration** is how much of the risk sits in one position, one theme, or one trader's book. The opposite of diversification, and — as we will see — the silent partner in almost every blow-up. A **correlated tail** is the event everyone forgets to model: the day all the things you thought were independent move together, so the diversification you counted on evaporates exactly when you need it. (We have a whole sibling piece on [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis); it is the mechanical heart of the blow-up.)

On the capital side: a **lock-up** is a period (often a year) during which an investor cannot redeem. **Redemption frequency** is how often they can ask for their money back afterward — quarterly is common, monthly is generous, annual is conservative. A **notice period** (30–90 days) is how far ahead they must warn you. A **gate** caps how much can be redeemed at once — an investor-level gate near 25%, or a fund-level gate, stops a stampede from forcing a fire sale. A **side pocket** quarantines an illiquid position so a redeeming investor's share of it stays put until it can be sold properly. **Liquidity mismatch** — the difference between how fast you have promised to return cash and how fast you can actually sell the book — is the structural flaw behind the redemption spiral.

On the structure side: the **administrator** is an independent firm that strikes the NAV and verifies positions; the **custodian** holds the assets; the **prime broker** (PB) provides financing, leverage, and securities lending; the **auditor** signs off on the financials once a year. The reason these are *independent* third parties is the entire defense against fraud — a manager who self-administers, self-custodies, and uses a tiny unknown auditor has removed every check on whether the reported NAV is real. **Operational due diligence (ODD)** is the allocator's separate, veto-wielding review of exactly those controls (covered in depth in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto)). A fund with spectacular returns can be killed at the ODD stage and never see a dollar.

The **high-water mark** is the previous peak NAV per share above which the manager can charge a performance fee. After a drawdown, no incentive fee accrues until NAV climbs back above that peak. This sounds like investor protection, and it is — but it is also a failure mechanism, because the manager's incentive fee is an option that pays zero when the fund is underwater, and a long stretch underwater can starve the management company until it gives up. We have a dedicated treatment of that trap in [the high-water mark trap in a drawdown](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-high-water-mark-trap-in-a-drawdown).

With that vocabulary, the taxonomy. There are six modes worth designing against, and most real deaths are a combination:

1. **The blow-up** — a sudden, large loss from leverage stacked on concentration when a correlated tail arrives. Fast. LTCM, Amaranth, Archegos.
2. **Fraud** — the reported NAV is fiction, sustained by missing independent controls until it collapses. Madoff.
3. **Style drift** — the fund quietly trades away from its mandate, often to dig out of a drawdown, until it is taking risks its investors never signed up for.
4. **The redemption death-spiral** — a drawdown triggers redemptions that force sales that print lower marks that trigger more redemptions, a self-reinforcing loop.
5. **Key-person loss** — the fund *is* a person (or a tiny team), and when that person leaves, retires, dies, or is poached, the franchise unwinds.
6. **Slow death** — no single catastrophe; just years of mediocre returns, fees compressing, costs grinding past a sub-scale management fee, until the founders close it themselves. **This is the most common ending.**

Underneath those six sits a seventh, which is less a mode than a finale: **liquidation and wind-down** — the actual mechanics of closing, returning capital, and dealing with the illiquid dregs. Every mode above ends there if the defense fails. The wind-down itself has a quality scale. A *good* wind-down is orderly: the manager announces a closure, sells the book at fair prices over a reasonable window, returns capital pro-rata, and the last investors get close to the last reported NAV. A *bad* wind-down is the tail end of a spiral: the liquid assets are already gone, the remaining book is illiquid and mismarked, redemptions are gated, and the final investors recover a fraction of what their statements said. The difference between the two is set long before the wind-down begins — by whether the fund had gates, side pockets, honest marks, and liquidity terms that matched its assets. A founder cannot always avoid closing; a founder can almost always control *how* they close, and that determines whether their reputation survives for the next launch.

Roughly 7–11% of hedge funds liquidate per year (HFR, with spikes in 2008 and 2020), and a large fraction of launches never reach year five. Funds dying is not the exception; it is the base rate. The job is to not be in the modal cohort — and, failing that, to die in a way that returns investors their money and leaves the founder's name clean.

#### Worked example: the two funds with the same returns

Picture the two funds from the hook with numbers. Both launch with `\$100M` and compound gross at 12% a year for three years, so both grow the book to roughly `\$140M` before fees and flows. Fund A — the survivor — runs gross exposure capped at 2.5×, uses an independent administrator and a Big-4 auditor, offers a one-year lock-up with quarterly redemptions behind a 25% gate, and has three portfolio managers and a COO. Fund B — the casualty — runs gross at 8×, prices its own thinly-traded names, offers monthly liquidity, and is one PM with an assistant.

In year three, both take a 15% drawdown on the book. For Fund A, that is a `\$21M` mark-down on a `\$140M` book, painful but survivable, and the gate ensures redemptions trickle rather than flood. For Fund B, 8× gross turns a 15% book move into a much larger equity hit, the monthly liquidity lets nervous LPs head for the door immediately, and self-priced marks crater as the fire sale begins. Same headline 12% for three years; one fund is alive and one is gone.

*The returns were identical — the structure decided who survived.*

## The blow-up

The blow-up is the death that makes the front page, and it is the most over-studied and under-learned of the modes. Over-studied because LTCM, Amaranth, and Archegos are taught in every risk seminar. Under-learned because founders watch the movie and conclude "I would never make *that* trade," missing that the trade is almost never the cause. The cause is a recipe with three ingredients, and the trade is just the match.

The recipe is **leverage + concentration + a correlated tail**. Leverage turns a moderate loss into a fatal one. Concentration means the loss is not diversified away. The correlated tail is the event where the positions you believed were independent all move against you at once, so the leverage and the concentration compound instead of cancel. Remove any one ingredient and the blow-up usually does not happen. A concentrated book with no leverage bleeds but survives. A levered book that is genuinely diversified can absorb a single position going wrong. It is the combination — and specifically the false belief that the book is diversified when a tail will reveal it is not — that kills.

**LTCM (1998)** is the textbook. Long-Term Capital Management was run by John Meriwether with Nobel laureates Robert Merton and Myron Scholes on the masthead — about as much intellectual firepower as a fund has ever assembled. Their convergence trades (betting that small price gaps between related instruments would close) had thin margins, so they ran balance-sheet leverage around 25–30× and held roughly `\$1.25T` in derivatives notional on a capital base that was a tiny fraction of that. Each individual trade looked low-risk and diversified. Then Russia defaulted in August 1998, and the correlated tail arrived: every convergence trade that was supposed to be independent moved the same way at once, because in a flight to quality, the thing that links them all — the demand for liquidity and safety — overwhelms their idiosyncratic differences. LTCM lost about `\$4.6B` in a matter of months. The losses were so large and so entangled with the banking system's books that the New York Fed organized a `\$3.6B` recapitalization with fourteen banks to wind it down in an orderly way. The lesson the founders missed was not "do not do convergence trades" — it was "leverage plus the belief that your positions are diversified is a bet that no correlated tail will ever arrive."

**Amaranth Advisors (2006)** is the concentration lesson without the genius-trade complexity. Amaranth was a multi-strategy fund of roughly `\$9B+`, but a single trader, Brian Hunter, had built an enormous concentrated position in natural-gas calendar spreads — bets on the price difference between gas delivered in different months. The position was so large relative to the market that there was no graceful way out: when the spread moved against him in roughly a week, the fund could not exit without crushing the price further. Amaranth lost about `\$6.6B` — roughly 65% of its assets — in days, and collapsed. There was no esoteric model failure here. There was one book, in one market, too big to sell. The lesson: concentration is not just position size, it is *position size relative to how fast you can exit*, and a single trader's single book can be the whole fund's tail.

**Archegos Capital Management (2021)** is the modern, sneakiest version, and it is worth dwelling on because it shows how the blow-up hides. Archegos was a *family office* — Bill Hwang's personal capital, which meant it faced lighter disclosure than a fund taking outside money. Hwang took concentrated, highly levered positions in a handful of stocks, but he did it through **total-return swaps** with multiple prime brokers rather than by buying the shares outright. A total-return swap lets you get the economic exposure of a stock without showing up as the owner — the bank holds the shares and you pay or receive the return. Spread across several primes, none of whom could see the others' books, the *hidden leverage* was enormous and invisible. When the underlying stocks fell, the margin calls hit all at once, the primes raced to liquidate the same concentrated positions into each other, and the losses to the banks exceeded `\$10B` — Credit Suisse alone took roughly `\$5.5B`, with Nomura, Morgan Stanley, and UBS also hit. Hwang was later convicted of fraud (2024). The lesson stacks on the first two: concentration and leverage again, but now with a counterparty blind spot, where no single prime broker had the full picture of the exposure they were collectively financing.

There is a fourth, quieter ingredient that all three blow-ups shared and that founders systematically underweight: **capacity illusion** — the false belief that a position can be exited near its marked value. LTCM's convergence trades, Amaranth's gas spreads, and Archegos's swap exposures all *looked* liquid on a normal day and all became un-exitable on the day that mattered, because the fund's own size was a large fraction of the market it needed to sell into. The screen price assumes you are a small participant; the fire-sale price is what you actually get when you are the market. This is why a position-size limit expressed only as a percentage of NAV is insufficient — the real limit is position size relative to *daily liquidity*, so that you can exit a meaningful fraction of the book inside the redemption notice period without moving the price against yourself. A fund that ignores this will mark its book at prices it can never realize, and the gap between the mark and the realizable value is the hidden loss that surfaces the instant it must sell.

Three funds, three decades, the same recipe. Notice what the defense has to be. You cannot promise never to be wrong about a trade. You *can* cap gross exposure so leverage can never turn a moderate loss into a fatal one. You *can* limit single-name and single-theme concentration so no one book is the whole fund. You *can* stress-test against the correlated tail — model the day everything moves together, not the day each thing moves alone — and you *can* avoid hidden leverage by keeping your total economic exposure transparent to yourself and your risk function. None of these are exotic. They are the unglamorous content of [risk management as a business function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function), which is exactly why under-resourced funds skip them and blow up.

#### Worked example: how leverage turns a small loss into a blow-up

Take a fund with `\$1B` of investor capital running an LTCM-style book at 25× balance-sheet leverage, so it controls about `\$25B` of positions. The trades are convergence bets, each expected to make a tiny spread, and on a normal day the book moves a fraction of a percent. Now the correlated tail arrives and the book moves against the fund by just **2%**. Two percent of `\$25B` is `\$500M` — half the capital — gone in the move. The fund is now running `\$500M` of capital against the same `\$25B` of positions, so its effective leverage has jumped to 50×, and every further adverse tick is twice as damaging. Another 2% move wipes the rest. A position set that looked safe because each leg "only" risked a fraction of a percent has, through leverage and correlation, destroyed the fund. Scale this up and you arrive at the real LTCM number: about `\$4.6B` lost from a book that, position by position, looked conservative.

*A 2% move in the assets became a 50% move in the equity — that multiplier is the whole story of the blow-up.*

## Fraud

Fraud is a different kind of death. The blow-up is a real loss that arrives suddenly; fraud is a *fictional* gain that has been sustained for years until reality catches up. And the defining feature of the canonical fraud is that the standard operational controls every legitimate fund uses would have caught it — which is why fraud belongs in a founder's taxonomy not as "I would never," but as "here is exactly which controls existence depends on."

**Madoff (revealed December 2008)** is the largest Ponzi scheme in history and the canonical operational-due-diligence failure. Bernard Madoff reported steady, smooth, almost-too-good returns for years across a purported options strategy. The reported value of client statements reached roughly `\$65B`, of which around `\$17.5B` was actual principal that investors lost. There was no strategy. New investor money paid old investor "returns," and the statements were fabricated. What is instructive is *how it was possible*, because every enabling condition is something a legitimate fund deliberately removes:

- **No independent administrator.** Madoff's operation effectively struck its own NAV. There was no third party verifying that the positions on the statements existed or that the returns were real. An independent administrator is the single most important fraud control, and it was missing.
- **A tiny, unknown auditor.** A multi-billion-dollar operation was audited by a three-person accounting firm operating out of a strip mall — a glaring mismatch between the assets and the auditor's capacity. Legitimate institutional funds use Big-4 or recognized specialist auditors precisely so that the audit is a real check.
- **Self-custody.** The assets were not held at an independent custodian where their existence could be confirmed. The fund could claim to hold whatever it wanted.
- **"Too smooth" returns.** The reported track record had a volatility profile that no real strategy produces — gains nearly every month, almost no drawdowns. To a trained ODD analyst, an implausibly smooth return stream is a red flag, not a selling point.

The reason this is in a founder's taxonomy is twofold. First, as a founder you must build the very controls whose *absence* enabled Madoff — independent administrator, independent custody, a credible auditor, third-party pricing — not because you are tempted to commit fraud, but because **allocators will not give you money until those controls exist**, and ODD will veto you if they do not. The controls that prevent fraud are the same controls that make a clean fund investable. Second, you must understand that a single fraud anywhere in your structure — a rogue employee, a mispriced book, a fabricated mark — can end the fund even if your strategy is sound, because the moment trust breaks, capital leaves. The defense is the boring, expensive, non-negotiable operational stack covered in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto) and [cash, treasury, and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk): segregation of duties, independent administration and pricing, controlled cash movement, and an auditor whose name an LP recognizes.

A subtle point worth naming: fraud does not always start as fraud. It sometimes *becomes* fraud when a manager, deep in a drawdown and facing redemptions, fudges a mark to avoid showing the loss — "just this quarter, until the position recovers." That small lie, in a structure with weak independent pricing, is the doorway. Independent administration and pricing exist precisely so that the manager *cannot* fudge the mark even under pressure, which protects the LP and, frankly, protects the founder from their own worst moment.

## Style drift

Style drift is the quietest killer because it does not announce itself. A fund raises capital on a clear mandate — say, US large-cap long/short equity with low net exposure. Investors allocate *to that mandate*, sized within their own portfolios as a low-net equity exposure. Then, slowly, the fund starts doing other things. A few illiquid private positions creep in. The net exposure drifts up because the market is rallying and the manager hates missing it. After a drawdown, the manager reaches into a new market — credit, or crypto, or macro — to "diversify" or to dig out. None of these moves is individually crazy. Together, they mean the fund is now taking risks its investors never signed up for, sized as if it were still the original strategy.

Style drift kills in two ways. The first is direct: the new, off-mandate risk is one the manager is *less skilled at*, so the drift itself produces losses — the long/short PM who wanders into macro and gets run over. The second is indirect and more insidious: even if the off-mandate bets work, the fund has broken its promise. When investors discover (often in a drawdown, when they look closely) that their "low-net equity" fund was actually running a levered macro book, they redeem — not because of the loss, but because of the *surprise*. The fund has stopped being the thing they allocated to, and an allocator's whole job is to control what they are exposed to. A fund that cannot be trusted to stay inside its mandate is uninvestable regardless of returns.

There is a tight, dangerous link between style drift and the high-water mark. A fund that has been underwater for a year earns no incentive fee — the manager's option is worthless until NAV recovers. That creates a powerful temptation to swing for the fences: take bigger, off-mandate risk to climb back above the high-water mark faster, because the management company is bleeding and the founder is watching their best people consider leaving. This is the mechanism by which a drawdown turns into style drift turns into a blow-up. It is also why the high-water mark, which looks like pure investor protection, has a dark side worth understanding in full — see [the high-water mark trap in a drawdown](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-high-water-mark-trap-in-a-drawdown).

The defense against style drift is structural and cultural. Structurally: a **written mandate** with explicit limits (instrument types, markets, gross and net exposure caps, liquidity buckets), enforced by a **risk committee** or an independent risk officer who can flag and block off-mandate positions, with **drift alarms** that fire when exposures wander outside the agreed bands. Culturally: a founder who treats the mandate as a promise rather than a guideline, and who would rather show a flat year inside the mandate than a recovered year that broke it. Investors can forgive a drawdown they were warned about; they rarely forgive a strategy they did not buy.

#### Worked example: style drift in a drawdown

The Meridian Fund, Maya's `\$200M` low-net US equity long/short fund, has a flat-to-down year and finishes 8% below its high-water mark. No incentive fee accrued; the management company netted only the 1.5% management fee — `\$3M` of gross revenue — against fixed costs near `\$1.8M`, so the year was lean and two analysts are quietly interviewing elsewhere. Maya faces a choice. To climb the 8% back to the high-water mark on her mandate, she needs a normal good year. To climb it *fast*, she could lever up, push net exposure from 30% toward 70%, and add a concentrated position in a hot sector she does not normally trade.

If she drifts, three things happen. Her risk profile silently doubles, so a bad quarter that used to cost 5% now costs 12%. Her investors, sized for a low-net fund, are now carrying a high-net fund without knowing it. And if she is wrong, she has converted a recoverable 8% drawdown into a 20% drawdown that triggers redemptions. The disciplined path — stay inside the mandate, recover at the mandate's pace, communicate honestly that the year was hard — is slower and less heroic, and it is the one that keeps the fund alive.

*Style drift is most tempting exactly when it is most dangerous: at the bottom of a drawdown, with the fee option worthless and the team restless.*

## The redemption death-spiral

The redemption death-spiral is where the *capital* leg of the promise breaks, and it is the failure mode that turns a survivable drawdown into a fatal one. It is worth understanding as a mechanism rather than an event, because each step is individually reasonable and the whole is a doom loop. Figure 2 traces it.

![Pipeline diagram of the redemption death-spiral from drawdown through forced sales back to more redemptions](/imgs/blogs/how-hedge-funds-die-the-failure-taxonomy-2.png)

The loop runs like this. A drawdown shakes investor confidence. Some investors submit **redemption requests** — they want their capital back. To pay them, the fund must **raise cash**, which means selling positions. Under pressure and on a clock (the notice period is short, the redemption date is fixed), the fund sells what it *can* sell — the liquid, high-quality positions — first, because the illiquid ones cannot be moved fast. This is the **fire sale**, and it has two ugly consequences. First, selling into the redemption forces prices down, so the fund realizes worse marks than the screen showed. Second, and worse, the fund is now left holding a book that is *more illiquid and lower-quality* than before, because the good stuff is what sold. The remaining NAV gets **marked lower**, both from the fire-sale losses and because the residual book is riskier. Remaining investors see the lower mark and the deteriorating quality, get scared, and submit *their* redemptions — and now the loop closes: lower marks feed the next redemption wave, which forces more selling, which prints still-lower marks. The fund spirals down until it gates, suspends, and winds down, often leaving the last investors holding illiquid dregs worth far less than the last reported NAV.

The root cause is almost always **liquidity mismatch**: the fund promised faster liquidity than its assets could honestly support. A fund that offers monthly redemptions but holds positions that take six months to sell has built the spiral into its own terms. When everyone wants out at once, the promise is unkeepable, and the act of trying to keep it (the fire sale) is what destroys value. This is the same dynamic that takes down open-ended property funds and credit funds in every crisis: the liability side (redemptions) is faster than the asset side (sales).

The defenses are all about controlling the liability side and matching it to the assets. **Lock-ups** stop redemptions for an initial period, giving the fund time. **Notice periods** give the fund warning so it can sell in an orderly way rather than a panic. **Gates** — an investor-level gate near 25%, or a fund-level gate — cap how much can leave at once, breaking the stampede so the fund can sell gradually. **Side pockets** quarantine illiquid positions so a redeeming investor's claim on them stays put until they can be sold properly, rather than forcing a fire sale of the liquid book to cash them out. And the master defense, underlying all of these: **match the redemption terms to the actual liquidity of the assets.** A fund holding illiquid credit should offer annual (not monthly) liquidity with a long notice period; a fund holding liquid large-caps can offer more frequent redemptions safely. The crisis-tested version of this playbook lives in [the crisis playbook: 2008 and 2020](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-crisis-playbook-2008-and-2020), where the funds that survived were the ones whose terms matched their books and the funds that died were the ones whose liquidity promises wrote a check the assets could not cash.

#### Worked example: a redemption death-spiral

The Meridian Fund holds `\$200M`, of which `\$120M` is liquid large-caps that can be sold in days and `\$80M` is less-liquid mid-caps and special situations that take months to exit cleanly. Maya offered quarterly liquidity with a 45-day notice period and — critically — *no gate*. A 10% drawdown spooks the LP base, and at the next redemption date she receives requests for `\$60M`, 30% of the fund.

With no gate, she must honor all `\$60M`. She cannot touch the illiquid `\$80M` in time, so she sells `\$60M` out of the `\$120M` liquid sleeve. Now the fund is `\$140M`, but its composition has flipped: `\$60M` liquid and `\$80M` illiquid — the book is now 57% illiquid, far riskier than before, and the forced selling pushed her realized prices below the marks, deepening the loss. The remaining LPs see a smaller, more illiquid, lower-marked fund and conclude they should get out before the rest of the liquid sleeve is gone. The next quarter brings `\$50M` of requests against `\$60M` of remaining liquidity, and the fund is now selling the last of the good stuff to pay them. Within a year Maya is left with the `\$80M` illiquid residue, a tiny investor base, a management fee that no longer covers costs, and no choice but to gate the illiquid book and wind down. Had she set a 25% gate, the first quarter's redemptions would have been capped at `\$35M`, the liquid sleeve would have survived, and the spiral would have broken before it started.

*The fund did not die of the drawdown — it died of a liquidity promise it could not keep, and the one missing line in the terms sheet (a gate) was the difference.*

## Key-person loss

A hedge fund is unusually concentrated in human capital. The edge — the research process, the relationships, the judgment, the track record investors are actually buying — often lives in one person's head, or a handful of people's heads. That makes **key-person loss** a genuine failure mode, and a strange one, because the fund can be performing perfectly well right up until the person leaves.

The triggers are mundane: the founder retires, gets sick, dies, has a falling-out with a partner, or — most common — the star PM who actually generates the returns gets poached by a platform offering a richer deal. The moment that person is gone, two things happen. First, the **performance engine** may stop, because the edge left with them; the remaining team cannot reproduce what the departed person did. Second, and faster, the **capital leaves**, because allocators have key-person provisions and because the whole investment thesis ("I am backing this person's skill") is now void. Many LP agreements contain a **key-person clause** that *automatically* triggers redemption rights or a fund suspension if a named individual ceases to be involved — so the departure of one person can, by contract, force the wind-down of the entire fund regardless of how the book is performing.

This mode interacts viciously with the high-water mark and the slow death. A fund that is underwater pays no incentive fee, so the star PM's compensation collapses exactly when a competitor's offer looks most attractive. The founder, watching their best person consider leaving, may then drift the style to recover faster (to restore the bonus pool), which raises blow-up risk. So a drawdown can set off key-person loss, which sets off either a redemption spiral (if the LPs leave) or a blow-up (if the founder swings for the fences to keep the team). The modes are not independent; they cascade.

There is a second, subtler form of key-person loss that founders rarely plan for: the *founder* becoming the bottleneck rather than the asset. As a fund grows from `\$50M` to `\$500M`, the skills that built it — being a great trader, having the conviction to size a position — are not the skills that run it: hiring, delegating, building a risk function, managing investors, overseeing operations. A founder who cannot make that transition becomes a single point of failure in a different sense, the person every decision routes through, so that the firm cannot scale beyond their personal bandwidth and stalls into mediocrity (which is its own death, the slow one). The funds that compound for decades are usually the ones where the founder deliberately worked themselves out of the day-to-day, building an institution that does not need them in the room for every trade. That is the opposite instinct from the one that made them a great trader, and the failure to make the switch quietly ends more promising funds than any market move.

The defenses are about reducing the fund's dependence on any single person and aligning the people who matter. **Deepen the team** so the edge is institutional, not personal — multiple PMs, a documented research process, a real second-in-command. **Vest the key people** with equity in the management company on a multi-year schedule, so leaving is expensive. **Build a succession plan** that allocators can see, so a departure is a transition rather than a catastrophe. And handle the **key-person clause** thoughtfully: it protects LPs, but a founder should understand which names are listed and what triggers it, because a poorly-drafted clause can hand investors a redemption right at the worst possible moment. The broader point — that a fund is a business whose most important assets walk out the door every evening — is why the people and incentive structure is as load-bearing as the strategy, a theme that runs through [risk management as a business function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function).

#### Worked example: a key-person event triggering the wind-down

The Meridian Fund has grown to `\$400M`, but the returns are really generated by Maya's co-founder and head of research, Daniel, who built the signal library. Daniel is named in the key-person clause: if he ceases to be actively involved, LPs holding a majority of the capital gain an immediate redemption right. A platform offers Daniel a guaranteed `\$3M`-a-year package plus a P&L share — far more than his current stake pays while the fund is only modestly above its high-water mark. He leaves.

The key-person clause fires. Within the notice window, LPs holding `\$280M` of the `\$400M` exercise their redemption right — not because the fund just lost money (it did not), but because the person they were backing is gone and their contracts let them act. Maya now has to liquidate most of the book to return `\$280M`, the management fee on the remaining `\$120M` no longer covers the fund's `\$2M` cost base, and without Daniel the research engine is hobbled. A profitable, well-run fund unwinds in months because its edge and its capital were both tied to one person who was not locked in. Had Daniel held vesting equity in the management company worth more than the platform's offer, the calculus might have been different — and that vesting is the cheapest insurance the fund never bought.

*The book was healthy; the fund still died, because both the alpha and the capital were hostage to one un-vested person.*

## Slow death: compression and mediocrity

The deaths that make the news are the blow-up and the fraud. The death that actually claims the *most* funds is the one nobody films: **slow death by fee compression and mediocrity.** No scandal, no spectacular loss, no headline — just a fund that never quite reaches scale, posts unremarkable returns, watches its fees grind down, and is eventually closed by founders who have done the math and concluded it is not worth continuing.

The mechanics are arithmetic, not drama. A hedge fund's management company has fixed costs — administrator, audit, legal, compliance, technology, market data, an OMS, salaries, office, insurance — that for an institutional-grade small fund commonly run `\$0.5M–2M+` a year. Those costs are paid out of the **management fee** (a percentage of AUM), because the **incentive fee** is unreliable: it is an option that pays zero when the fund is underwater. So the management company's survival depends on AUM being large enough that the management fee covers the fixed costs. Below that **break-even AUM**, the founders are subsidizing the fund out of their own pockets, which they will do for a while on hope, but not forever.

Three forces grind a sub-scale fund toward death. First, **fee compression**: the classic "2 and 20" has compressed industry-wide to roughly 1.3–1.5% management and 15–17% performance (HFR fee data), and a small fund desperate for capital often grants founders-share-class discounts (1% / 10%) and side-letter concessions that pull its *effective* fee even lower — so the management fee per dollar of AUM keeps shrinking. Second, **the high-water mark**: a stretch of mediocre or negative returns means no incentive fee for an extended period, so the management fee is the *only* revenue, and if that does not cover costs, the fund bleeds. Third, **the cost of being investable**: to attract institutional capital you need institutional-grade operations, which raises your fixed costs — but you cannot afford institutional-grade operations until you have institutional capital. That chicken-and-egg trap strands many funds in a sub-scale limbo: too small to afford the infrastructure that would let them raise, too under-built to raise it.

The result is a fund that limps along below break-even, the founders growing tired, the best people leaving for platforms (key-person risk), the LPs slowly redeeming out of boredom rather than fear, until one day the founders close it. This is not failure in the dramatic sense — it is the median outcome for a launch that never found scale or a durable edge. The honest framing, which lawyers and "fund-in-a-box" vendors will not give you, is that **most launches are small and break-even is brutal**: you commonly need `\$80M–100M+` of AUM just to cover an institutional-grade cost base, and many allocators will not even look at you below `\$250M–500M`.

The defenses are the least glamorous and the most decisive. **Reach break-even AUM** before you launch, or have a credible, funded plan and runway to get there — do not launch into a structure you cannot afford. **Defend the edge**: a fund that is merely mediocre has no reason to exist when an index fund is cheaper, so the strategy must produce returns an allocator cannot get elsewhere, net of your fees. **Control costs** ruthlessly in the early years — use a mini-prime or introducing broker if you are sub-`\$100M`, keep the team lean, and do not buy infrastructure you do not yet need. And **size the fee to survive**: granting too many discounts to win the first dollar can leave you below break-even on the dollars you do have. The full survival math — break-even AUM, the J-curve of early cash flow, the cost stack by AUM — is the spine of the operating sections of this series; the failure-mode framing here is just the reminder that mediocrity is a way to die, not merely a way to underperform.

#### Worked example: the fee-compression slow death

Maya launches the Meridian Fund at `\$40M` of day-one AUM with a 1.5% management fee, after granting founders-class LPs a discount that pulls her *effective* management fee to about 1.2%. Her institutional-grade fixed costs — administrator, audit, compliance, two analysts, market data, an OMS — run `\$1.2M` a year. Her management-fee revenue is `\$40M × 1.2% = \$480,000`. She is `\$720,000` a year in the hole, covering the gap from her own savings and the hope that performance will bring the incentive fee.

Year one, the fund is up a respectable 9%, but a 9% gain on `\$40M` generates only `\$720,000` of gross profit, and the 15% incentive fee on that is about `\$108,000` — nowhere near closing the `\$720,000` gap. Year two, the fund is flat, so there is *zero* incentive fee, the management fee is still only `\$480,000`, and Maya is another `\$720,000` underwater. She has now burned `\$1.4M+` of her own capital, her best analyst has left for a platform, and the AUM has crept to only `\$55M` because raising as a sub-scale, unremarkable fund is brutal. To break even she needs roughly `\$1.2M ÷ 1.5% = \$80M` of AUM at full fee, or more given her discounts — and she is not close. There is no blow-up, no fraud, no scandal. There is just arithmetic, and in year three Maya returns the capital and closes the fund.

*Most funds do not die of a bad trade — they die because the management fee never covered the cost of staying in business.*

## Designing against each failure mode

The reason to build the taxonomy is that it converts a vague fear ("my fund might fail") into a specific, finite checklist ("here are the six ways, here is the defense for each"). A founder who designs against each mode before launch is not buying certainty — funds still die — but is removing the *unforced* deaths, the ones that come from a structure that left a mode wide open. Figure 3 contrasts the two funds from the hook on exactly these defenses.

![Before-and-after comparison of an undefended fund that died at three years versus a defended fund alive at ten years](/imgs/blogs/how-hedge-funds-die-the-failure-taxonomy-3.png)

Walk the modes in order:

- **Against the blow-up:** cap gross exposure so leverage cannot turn a moderate loss into a fatal one; limit single-name and single-theme concentration; stress-test against the *correlated tail*, not the average day; and keep your total economic exposure (including swaps and other off-balance-sheet leverage) transparent to yourself. This is the content of a real risk function — see [risk management as a business function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function).
- **Against fraud:** build the independent operational stack that makes fraud structurally impossible and your fund investable — independent administrator, independent custodian, third-party pricing, a recognized auditor, segregation of duties, and controlled cash movement. This is also exactly what survives [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto).
- **Against style drift:** put the mandate in writing with explicit instrument, market, and exposure limits; enforce it with a risk committee or independent risk officer; and set drift alarms that fire when exposures leave the agreed bands. Treat the mandate as a promise.
- **Against the redemption spiral:** match your redemption terms to the *actual* liquidity of your assets, and arm the terms sheet with a lock-up, a meaningful notice period, a gate near 25%, and side pockets for illiquid positions. The single most common omission — and the one that killed the Meridian Fund in the worked example — is the missing gate.
- **Against key-person loss:** deepen the team so the edge is institutional, vest the key people on a multi-year schedule, build a visible succession plan, and understand exactly what your key-person clause triggers.
- **Against slow death:** reach break-even AUM (or launch with the runway to get there), defend a genuine edge, control costs in the early years, and size the fee so the dollars you have actually cover the cost of being in business.

Notice that the defenses are mostly *structural and pre-commitment* — decisions made before launch, written into documents, hard to undo. That is deliberate. The failure modes all get *more* tempting under stress (drift when underwater, over-leverage to recover, weak liquidity terms to win the first dollar), so the defense has to be locked in beforehand, when the founder is calm, rather than improvised in the drawdown, when they are not. The structure you build on a good day is the thing that saves you on a bad one. Figure 6 turns this around to the *detection* side: the warning sign each mode broadcasts before the obituary.

## Common misconceptions

**"Funds die from bad trades."** Mostly false. The dramatic blow-ups (LTCM, Amaranth, Archegos) make trades look like the cause, but the *trade* is just the trigger — the cause is leverage plus concentration plus a correlated tail, a structural condition the founder controls. And the modal death is not a bad trade at all; it is the slow fade of a sub-scale fund whose management fee never covered its costs. Far more funds die of operations, redemptions, and arithmetic than of a single losing position.

**"Great returns keep you alive."** False, and the hook proves it: two funds with identical returns, one alive at ten years and one dead at three. Returns are necessary but nowhere near sufficient. A fund with great returns can be killed at ODD over a cash-control gap, drained by a redemption spiral, ended by a key-person departure, or unwound by fraud somewhere in its structure. The structure and the capital base decide survival at least as much as the P&L.

**"Leverage is the problem — just don't use it."** Too simple. Leverage is one of three ingredients in the blow-up, and many legitimate, durable strategies (market-neutral, relative-value) require meaningful gross leverage to produce a return at all. The defense is not zero leverage; it is *capped, stress-tested, transparent* leverage — leverage sized so that a correlated tail cannot turn a moderate loss into a fatal one, and never hidden from your own risk function the way Archegos hid it from its prime brokers.

**"A redemption spiral only hits bad funds."** False. A redemption spiral is a function of *liquidity mismatch and weak terms*, not of investment quality. A perfectly good fund that offered monthly liquidity on a six-month book can be spiraled to death by a wave of redemptions, while a mediocre fund with a one-year lock-up and a 25% gate rides out the same wave. The spiral is in the terms sheet, not the track record.

**"Fraud is something other people do — it's not in my taxonomy."** Wrong frame. Fraud is in your taxonomy not because you intend it, but because (a) the controls that *prevent* fraud are exactly the controls allocators demand before they invest, so building them is mandatory regardless, and (b) fraud sometimes starts as a single fudged mark by an honest manager under drawdown pressure — which is why independent pricing exists to make that impossible. You design against fraud to be investable and to protect yourself from your own worst moment.

**"Key-person risk is just an HR issue."** False — it is a contractual, capital, and alpha risk all at once. A key-person clause can force a wind-down by contract when one named person leaves, even if the fund is performing. The departure can take the edge (alpha) and the capital (LPs exercising redemption rights) simultaneously. Treating it as "we'll hire a replacement" misses that the fund's entire investment thesis can be voided overnight.

## How it plays out in the real world

Put numbers and named cases to the framework. Roughly 7–11% of hedge funds liquidate every year (HFR), and the rate spikes in correlated crises — Figure 4 shows the pattern, with the kill years (2008's global financial crisis, 2020's COVID liquidity shock) standing out as multiples of the normal-year baseline. In those years, the modes do not arrive one at a time; they cascade. A blow-up at one fund forces deleveraging that moves prices, which marks down a *second* fund, which triggers redemptions, which forces *its* fire sale — the correlated tail and the redemption spiral arriving together across the industry. That is why crisis years are not just "a bit worse" but multiples worse: the failure modes are correlated with each other, not independent.

![Bar chart of hedge fund liquidations by year showing large spikes in the 2008 and 2020 crisis years](/imgs/blogs/how-hedge-funds-die-the-failure-taxonomy-4.png)

The named cases each isolate one ingredient cleanly, which is why they are taught. **LTCM (1998)** is leverage and the correlated tail in their purest form — Nobel-grade modeling defeated by the simple fact that "independent" convergence trades all move together in a flight to quality, and 25–30× leverage turned that into a `\$4.6B` loss and a Fed-organized rescue. **Amaranth (2006)** is concentration without exotic complexity — one trader, one natural-gas book, too big to exit, `\$6.6B` and about 65% of the fund gone in roughly a week. **Archegos (2021)** is hidden leverage and the counterparty blind spot — total-return swaps spread across multiple primes who could not see each other's exposure, `\$10B+` in losses to the banks, and a fraud conviction for Bill Hwang. **Madoff (2008)** is the fraud and ODD failure — no independent administrator, a strip-mall auditor, self-custody, and implausibly smooth returns, sustained until `\$65B` of fabricated statement value collapsed with `\$17.5B` of real principal lost.

But the cases that *don't* make the news are the more useful study for a founder, because they are the ones you are statistically likely to become. They are the funds that launched at `\$30–50M`, ran respectable-but-unremarkable numbers, never reached the `\$80–100M` break-even, watched their founders-class discounts and side letters compress an already-thin fee, lost their best analyst to a platform during a flat year, and quietly returned capital at year three or four. There is no Wikipedia page for these funds. They are the bulk of the 7–11%, and they died of the slow death, the most preventable and least glamorous mode. The lesson a founder should take from the famous blow-ups is real — cap leverage, limit concentration, stress the tail — but the lesson from the silent majority is the one that actually decides whether *your* fund is in next year's liquidation count: reach scale, defend a genuine edge, build the independent operations that make you investable, write a terms sheet whose liquidity promise matches your assets, and lock in the key people. The funds that do all of that are not immortal — but they have closed off every unforced way to die, which is the most a founder can do, and far more than most launches ever bother to.

## When this matters / Further reading

This taxonomy matters most at two moments. The first is **before launch**, when you are drafting the documents and choosing the structure — because nearly every defense above is a pre-commitment written into the LPA, the terms sheet, the risk policy, and the service-provider contracts, and almost impossible to retrofit once the fund is running and stressed. The second is **in a drawdown**, when every failure mode gets more tempting at once — when style drift looks like a recovery plan, weak liquidity terms look like a way to keep capital, and over-leverage looks like a path back above the high-water mark. The taxonomy is a pre-written answer to "what are the ways this could go wrong, and did I close them?" — calm-day thinking you can lean on when the bad day comes.

The deeper you go on any one mode, the more it connects to the rest of the founder's playbook. The blow-up is a risk-function story: [risk management as a business function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function). Fraud and style drift are operational-controls stories that live or die at the allocator's veto: [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto). The redemption spiral is a crisis story with a tested playbook: [the crisis playbook: 2008 and 2020](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-crisis-playbook-2008-and-2020), and a counterparty story too: [cash, treasury, and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk). And the slow death plus key-person loss are both downstream of the fee structure and the manager's worthless option in a drawdown: [the high-water mark trap in a drawdown](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-high-water-mark-trap-in-a-drawdown).

For the market mechanics behind the blow-up — why correlations snap to one and diversification evaporates in a crisis — see the cross-asset sibling on [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis) and the discipline of [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up). The taxonomy is short on purpose. A founder who can name all six modes, name the root cause of each, and point to the one defense that closes it has done the single most valuable piece of survival planning there is — because in this business, not dying is most of winning.
