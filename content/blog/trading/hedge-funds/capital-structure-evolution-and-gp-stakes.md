> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — As a fund matures, its capital structure evolves from one commingled vehicle into a platform of share classes, separately managed accounts, and permanent capital — and the management company that sits above all of it becomes a valuable asset that can be sold at a multiple of its fee revenue.
>
> - A fund does not stay a single pool forever: it adds **founders and standard share classes**, opens **separately managed accounts** for big institutions, raises **permanent capital** that cannot redeem, and may eventually sell a **GP stake** — a minority slice of the management company — for liquidity, growth capital, or succession.
> - The thing being sold in a GP-stake deal is not the fund and not the strategy; it is a passive cut of the **management company's fee economics**. Buyers like Goldman Sachs Petershill, Blackstone, and Dyal price it on **fee-related earnings** — management fee minus operating costs — because that line is recurring and contractual, while the incentive fee is a volatile option.
> - A GP-stake sale of **20% for \$60M** implies the whole management company is worth roughly **\$300M**; if that firm earns **\$15M** of fee revenue a year, the deal is being struck at about **20 times** revenue, or a high-single-digit-to-low-double-digit multiple of fee-related earnings.
> - The one number to remember: the cheque is **one-time**, the give-up **compounds** — sell 20% of the fees and, on a growing book, the cumulative revenue you surrender passes the \$60M proceeds in roughly **12 years**, and keeps paying after that. The management company, not the fund, is the asset you are ultimately building.

Two years after the Meridian Fund crossed a billion dollars in assets, Maya was sitting across a conference table from a managing director at a GP-stakes firm, looking at a term sheet that offered her \$60 million for 20% of her management company. Not the fund — the firm. The entity that collects the fees, pays the salaries, and signs her name. The buyer wanted no say in a single trade, no seat on the investment committee, no vote on hiring her next portfolio manager. It wanted a passive, permanent cut of the fee revenue, and it was willing to wire \$60 million for one fifth of it.

Maya did the arithmetic that every founder eventually does, except this time the arithmetic ran in the other direction. When she had taken a seed deal at launch, she was selling a slice of a business worth almost nothing to get to the start line. Now someone was telling her that one fifth of her firm was worth \$60 million — which meant the whole thing was worth around \$300 million. Against fee revenue of about \$15 million a year, that was a multiple of roughly 20 times revenue. The fund she had built to compound other people's money had, somewhere along the way, become an asset of its own — one with a market price, a buyer, and a multiple. She was no longer just a portfolio manager. She was the owner of a company someone wanted to buy a piece of.

This post is about that transformation: how a fund's capital structure evolves as it matures, and how the management company sitting above it turns into a valuable, sellable asset. We will follow Meridian from a single commingled fund through the full menu a maturing manager works through — new funds and share classes, separately managed accounts, permanent capital — and arrive at the GP-stake sale and the question it forces: what is a management company actually worth, and what are you really selling when you sell a piece of it? Figure 1 is the whole evolution on one timeline; the rest of the post earns the right to read it.

This is the natural sequel to two earlier posts. At launch, Maya sold a slice of a business worth almost nothing to a seeder to reach viability — see [seed and acceleration capital](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/seed-and-acceleration-capital). Hold both of those: the fund is the vehicle that holds investor money; the management company is the business you own. This post is about how that business grows up and what it becomes worth.

## Foundations: the pieces of a mature manager

Before the economics, the vocabulary. Almost everyone who can run money is fluent in the *markets* layer and blank on the *capital-formation* layer — the structures, vehicles, and ownership claims that turn a strategy into a sellable enterprise. Define these eight terms cold and the rest of the post assembles itself.

**Commingled fund.** The default starting vehicle: a single pooled fund in which every investor buys the same shares, pays the same fee schedule (within their class), and shares the same returns, gains, and losses pro rata. One pool, one NAV per share, one set of positions. Almost every fund launches as a single commingled vehicle because it is the simplest thing to run and audit. The Meridian Fund began life this way.

**Share class.** A subdivision of the *same* fund with different economic terms but the same underlying portfolio. All classes own the same positions; they differ in fee schedule, currency, liquidity, or lock-up. A **founders class** offers a fee break (say 1.5% and 15% instead of 2% and 20%) to early or large investors; a **currency-hedged class** strips out FX risk for a foreign investor; an **institutional class** carries lower fees and a longer lock-up for a big ticket. Adding a class does not change the strategy — it repackages the same fund for a different buyer.

**Separately managed account (SMA).** A portfolio the manager runs *for a single investor*, in an account that investor owns directly, rather than in the commingled fund. The investor's assets sit in their own custody account; the manager trades them under a discretionary mandate. The investor gets transparency, control, custom risk limits, and the ability to pull capital without affecting anyone else. The manager gets a large, sticky mandate — but takes on extra operational overhead and usually a fee discount. An SMA is the same strategy, run alongside the fund, for one big client who wants it on their own terms.

**Permanent capital.** Capital that *cannot be redeemed* on the investor's demand — money locked in for the long term or indefinitely, typically through a closed-end or listed vehicle, a long-dated lock-up structure, or capital from a strategic partner. Permanent capital removes the manager's single most dangerous risk — a redemption run that forces selling into a falling market. Open-ended hedge funds offer periodic liquidity; permanent capital offers none, which is precisely what makes it so valuable to the manager and so hard to raise.

**GP stake.** A minority equity interest in the *general partner* or the *management company* — the business that manages the fund, not the fund itself. A buyer of a GP stake owns a percentage of the firm's economics: a cut of its fee revenue and its enterprise value, with whatever (usually limited) governance and information rights the contract specifies. Selling a GP stake is selling a piece of *yourself as a business*, distinct from any single fund. This is the centerpiece of the post.

**Management-company valuation.** The market value of the firm that manages the funds — what someone would pay to own all of it. Because a management company's cash flows are mostly recurring fee revenue, it is valued the way any cash-flow business is: as a *multiple* of its earnings. The multiple depends on how stable, diversified, and sticky those earnings are. A fragile single-fund manager trades at a low multiple; a diversified platform with permanent capital trades at a high one.

**Fee-related earnings (FRE).** The portion of a manager's profit that comes from the *management fee* after subtracting operating costs — salaries, rent, technology, audit, administration, compliance. FRE excludes the incentive fee. It is the stable, contractual, recurring slice of the firm's economics, and it is the line that GP-stake buyers and acquirers care about most, because they can forecast it. The incentive fee is upside; FRE is the floor.

**Monetization.** The act of converting an ownership stake in the management company into cash — selling a GP stake, selling the whole firm, or listing it. Monetization is how a founder turns years of compounded enterprise value into liquidity, whether to diversify their personal wealth, fund the firm's growth, or solve succession. It is the endgame this whole post points toward.

With those eight in hand — commingled fund, share class, SMA, permanent capital, GP stake, management-company valuation, fee-related earnings, and monetization — we can follow the evolution.

## Why the capital structure evolves

A founder rarely sits down on day one and designs a multi-vehicle platform. The structure evolves because the fund's needs change as it grows, and each new need is best solved by a new piece of structure rather than by bending the original commingled fund. Three forces drive the evolution, and they pull in the same direction: toward more vehicles, stickier capital, and a management company that is worth more.

The first force is **investor heterogeneity**. At launch, Maya's investors were a fairly uniform group — friends, family, former colleagues, a seeder, and a handful of fund-of-funds, all happy to buy the same shares of one commingled fund. As Meridian grew, the investors stopped being uniform. A European family office wanted a euro-hedged class. A US pension wanted a separately managed account it could custody itself and inspect line by line. A sovereign wealth fund wanted a multi-year lock-up in exchange for a fee break. Each of these is a different buyer with a different need, and you cannot serve all of them with a single share class of a single fund. The capital structure fragments because the capital base fragments.

The second force is **capital quality**, by which a mature founder means *stickiness*. As covered in [liquidity terms: lock-ups, gates, and side pockets](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets), the deadliest risk to a fund is not a bad trade but a redemption cascade — a wave of investors all asking for their money back at once, forcing the manager to sell good positions into a falling market to raise cash. A founder who has lived through one drawdown spends the rest of their career trying to make capital stickier: longer lock-ups, share classes that reward patience, and ultimately permanent capital that cannot leave at all. Every move toward stickier capital is a move toward a more durable business — and a more valuable one.

The third force is **the founder's own balance sheet**. Early on, a founder's wealth is almost entirely tied up in their own firm — an undiversified, illiquid bet on a single business that can be destroyed by one bad year or one key-person event. As the firm matures and becomes valuable, the founder faces a personal-finance problem that has nothing to do with markets: nearly all their net worth sits in one private company they cannot sell shares of on an exchange. A GP-stake sale solves this. It lets the founder take some chips off the table — converting illiquid enterprise value into cash they can diversify — without giving up control or selling the whole firm. The capital structure evolves, in the end, partly to serve the people who built it.

Figure 2 lays out the menu. Each structure serves a different investor and gives the manager a different kind of value, at a different cost.

![A matrix with four structure options as rows and three columns showing who each one serves, what it gives the manager, and what it costs, from share classes through SMAs and permanent capital to a GP stake](/imgs/blogs/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes-2.png)

The progression is not strictly sequential — a fund might raise permanent capital before it opens an SMA, or never sell a GP stake at all — but the logical order holds: you repackage the existing fund first (share classes), then you build vehicles alongside it (SMAs, permanent capital), and only at maturity does the management company itself become the thing on the table (a GP stake). Let us walk each one.

## New funds and share classes

The first and cheapest evolution is to subdivide the fund you already have. Share classes let you offer different terms to different investors *without changing the portfolio* — every class owns the same positions and earns the same gross return; they differ only in the fee, currency, liquidity, or lock-up wrapped around that return.

The most common is the **founders class**: a fee discount for early or large investors. As discussed in [how a hedge fund makes money](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-a-hedge-fund-makes-money), the standard fee is "2 and 20," now compressed in practice to roughly 1.3% to 1.5% management and 15% to 17% incentive. A founders class might offer 1.5% and 15% — or, for a true anchor investor, 1% and 10% — usually capped by a time window (the class closes after the first year) or an AUM ceiling (the class closes once it reaches a set size). The discount is the price of early credibility: a founders class lets you tell the next prospect that serious money committed at launch.

Other classes solve other problems. A **currency-hedged class** strips FX risk out of the return for a foreign investor, so a euro-based family office is not betting on the dollar on top of betting on Maya. A **long-lock class** offers a fee break in exchange for a two- or three-year lock-up, turning a fee discount into stickier capital. A **gated institutional class** carries lower fees but tighter redemption terms for a pension that wants size at a discount and is willing to give up liquidity for it. Each class is the same fund seen through a different contractual lens.

There is also the option of launching a genuinely *new fund* — a second strategy, a more concentrated version of the flagship, a long-only sleeve, or a vehicle in a different jurisdiction. A new fund is a bigger step than a class: it has its own portfolio, its own NAV, its own documents, and its own audit. It expands the franchise rather than repackaging it, and it is how a single-strategy manager becomes a multi-strategy firm. But it also multiplies the operational and compliance load, so most managers add classes long before they add funds.

The reason classes come first is that they are nearly free to operate. A new share class is a line in the fund's offering documents and a different fee calculation in the administrator's NAV process; it owns the same positions, files under the same audit, and reports through the same infrastructure. A new *fund* is a second business: a separate legal entity, a separate set of subscription documents, a separate prime-brokerage relationship in many cases, a separate audit engagement, and a separate compliance footprint with the regulator. The marginal cost of a class is measured in legal fees and administrator setup; the marginal cost of a fund is measured in the entire fixed run-rate of a small firm repeated. A founder who adds a class is repackaging; a founder who adds a fund is, in effect, launching again.

What makes the new-fund step worthwhile is *franchise value*. A single-strategy manager lives and dies by one strategy's edge — when that edge decays, the firm decays with it. A multi-strategy or multi-product firm can lose one strategy and survive on the others, which is exactly the diversification a GP-stake buyer pays a higher multiple for. The same logic that makes a multi-vehicle platform more valuable than a single fund makes a multi-fund franchise more valuable than a single fund: the firm's fee-related earnings no longer rest on one book. The trap is launching a second fund the founder cannot actually run — chasing assets into a strategy where the firm has no edge, which dilutes the brand and the returns at once. The disciplined version is to add a fund only where the firm has a genuine, separable edge, and to staff it before launching it.

#### Worked example: a founders class versus a standard class

The Meridian Fund launches with two classes. The **standard class** charges 2% management and 20% incentive. The **founders class**, open only for the first \$50M and the first twelve months, charges 1.5% and 15%. A seeder puts \$30M into the founders class; later investors put \$70M into the standard class, for \$100M total.

On the management fee alone: the founders class generates 1.5% of \$30M, which is \$450,000 a year. The standard class generates 2% of \$70M, which is \$1.4M a year. Combined management-fee revenue is \$1.85M. Had Maya charged everyone the standard 2%, she would have collected 2% of \$100M, which is \$2M — so the founders discount costs her \$150,000 a year in management fee. In exchange, she got \$30M of anchor capital committed early, when credibility was scarcest and \$30M was the difference between a viable launch and a stalled one.

*A founders class trades a small, permanent fee discount for early, credible capital — cheap if it tips you over the line to viability, expensive if you would have raised the money anyway.*

## Separately managed accounts

A share class still lives inside the commingled fund. A **separately managed account** lives outside it. In an SMA, a single investor — almost always a large institution — gives the manager a discretionary mandate to trade *their own* account, held in *their own* custody, rather than buying shares of the pooled fund. The strategy is the same; the wrapper is entirely different.

Institutions want SMAs for reasons that have little to do with returns and everything to do with control. **Transparency**: in an SMA, the investor can see every position, every day, in their own account — no waiting for a monthly statement, no commingling with strangers whose redemptions could move the portfolio. **Custody and control**: the assets never leave the investor's custodian, which sidesteps a whole category of operational and fraud risk — the very risk that destroyed Madoff's investors, who had handed him both the trading and the custody. **Customization**: an SMA can carry bespoke risk limits, exclusion lists, leverage caps, or liquidity terms that the commingled fund cannot offer without disadvantaging its other investors. **Fee leverage**: a big enough SMA ticket buys a fee discount, the same way a large block trade gets a better price.

For the manager, an SMA is a large, sticky mandate — often \$100M or more from one client — but it is not free. It adds operational overhead: a second (or fifth, or tenth) account to reconcile, separate reporting, separate risk monitoring, and the constant discipline of trading the SMA *pari passu* with the fund so no account is favored. It usually carries a fee discount. And it concentrates business risk in one relationship: lose the client, lose the mandate overnight, with none of the gates or notice periods that protect the commingled fund. An SMA is the same strategy run for one demanding client who pays in size and demands in control.

The discipline that an SMA forces on a firm is *trade allocation*, and it is one of the places a compliance program earns its keep. When the manager wants to buy a stock, that buy has to be split fairly across the commingled fund and every SMA running the strategy — usually pro rata to each account's size, at the same average price, with no account systematically getting the better fills. A manager who let the fund front-run the SMA, or steered the best fills to the largest fee-payer, would be breaching their fiduciary duty and inviting a regulatory enforcement action. So the firm needs a documented allocation policy, a system that splits orders automatically, and an audit trail proving every account was treated *pari passu*. The operational burden of an SMA is not just a second account to reconcile; it is a second set of fiduciary duties to discharge provably, every trading day.

There is a further wrinkle when an SMA imposes constraints the fund does not share — a leverage cap, an exclusion list, a liquidity limit. Now the SMA and the fund hold *different* portfolios, and the manager has to run two slightly divergent books from one set of ideas, monitoring each against its own limits. This is manageable, and large firms do it across dozens of mandates, but it is a real tax on the front office and the risk function. It is why a founder weighs an SMA mandate not only on its fee but on whether the firm's operating model — the systems, the middle office, the compliance program — can actually absorb another account without dropping the ball on the ones it already has. A blown trade allocation or a breached SMA limit is exactly the kind of operational failure that ends funds, far more reliably than a bad trade.

#### Worked example: an SMA versus the commingled fund for a \$200M mandate

A large US pension wants exposure to Meridian's strategy and offers \$200M — but only through an SMA it custodies itself, with daily transparency and a hard 1.5× leverage cap, at a discounted 1% management and 15% incentive fee.

Through the **commingled fund** at standard 2% and 20%, that \$200M would generate 2% of \$200M, which is \$4M a year in management fee. Through the **SMA** at 1% and 15%, it generates 1% of \$200M, which is \$2M a year — half the management-fee revenue. Maya gives up \$2M a year in management fee and 5 points of incentive fee for the privilege of taking the mandate.

Is it worth it? \$200M is twice Meridian's launch AUM. Even at the discounted 1%, the SMA's \$2M a year roughly covers Meridian's entire fixed run-rate of about \$1.2M (see [the operating model: front, middle, and back office](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office) for what that run-rate buys). The SMA is sticky institutional money that validates the firm to every future allocator's operational due diligence — and, critically, it is fee revenue that flows to the same management company whose value we are about to compute. The discount is the toll for size and credibility.

*An SMA is a smaller fee per dollar in exchange for a larger, stickier, more credible dollar — worth it when the mandate's size and the client's stamp of approval outweigh the discount.*

## Permanent capital

Open-ended hedge funds offer periodic liquidity — quarterly redemptions, say, with 60 days' notice. That liquidity is a feature for the investor and a liability for the manager: it means a chunk of the asset base can walk out the door at the worst possible moment. **Permanent capital** removes that liability by removing the redemption right. It is money the investor cannot pull on demand — committed for a long fixed term, indefinitely, or only through selling shares to another buyer rather than redeeming from the fund.

Permanent capital takes several forms. A **closed-end or listed vehicle** raises a fixed pool that trades on an exchange: an investor who wants out sells their shares to another investor, and not a dollar leaves the fund. A **long-dated lock-up structure** commits capital for five, seven, or ten years, the way a private-equity fund does, in exchange for a return premium. A **strategic partner** — a large institution or a GP-stakes firm — provides capital with no redemption right as part of a broader relationship. In every case, the manager gets the same prize: an asset base that cannot run.

Why does the manager value this so highly? Because permanent capital changes what the manager can *do*. With redeemable capital, the manager must always hold enough liquidity to meet a redemption, which forces shorter holding periods, more liquid positions, and a constant fear of the next gate. With permanent capital, the manager can hold illiquid positions to maturity, ride out a drawdown without being forced to sell, and take a genuinely long-term view — and, just as importantly, the manager's fee revenue becomes predictable, because the AUM cannot shrink overnight. Predictable AUM means predictable fee-related earnings, and predictable fee-related earnings are exactly what makes a management company valuable. Permanent capital is hard to raise — investors charge a premium in terms for giving up their liquidity — but every dollar of it raises both the safety and the worth of the firm.

The listed-vehicle route is worth understanding because it is the purest form. When a manager floats a closed-end fund on an exchange, it raises a fixed pool of cash that is permanently locked into the strategy. An investor who wants out does not redeem from the fund — they sell their shares to another investor on the exchange, exactly as they would sell a stock. Not a dollar of the fund's capital moves; ownership simply changes hands in the secondary market. The cost is that the shares can trade at a *discount to NAV*: if sentiment toward the manager sours, the shares might trade below the per-share value of the assets, which is frustrating for shareholders and a reputational drag on the manager, but it does not force any selling inside the fund. The manager has converted redemption risk into discount-to-NAV risk — a far more survivable problem, because a discount embarrasses you while a redemption run can destroy you.

There is a subtler reason mature managers chase permanent capital: it is the cleanest path to the kind of fee-related earnings a GP-stake buyer will pay the highest multiple for. A buyer underwriting a stake models the firm's future fee revenue and discounts it for the risk that the AUM shrinks. Redeemable capital carries a large such discount — a crisis can take a quarter of it in a single redemption window. Permanent capital carries almost none. So permanent capital does double duty: it protects the firm operationally *and* it lifts the multiple a buyer will pay for the management company, because it converts a volatile, redeemable revenue base into a contractual, durable one. A founder building toward a stake sale or an eventual exit is, whether they frame it this way or not, building toward permanent capital.

#### Worked example: permanent-capital economics versus open-ended

Meridian raises \$150M into a closed-end, listed permanent-capital vehicle alongside its open-ended fund, locking it in for the long term against the open-ended fund's quarterly liquidity.

The *fee* arithmetic looks similar — 1.5% on \$150M is \$2.25M a year either way. The difference is in *durability*. In a sharp drawdown, the open-ended fund might see 25% of its capital file for redemption, so \$150M of open-ended AUM could become \$112M almost overnight, dragging that \$2.25M of management fee down to about \$1.7M and forcing Maya to sell positions into the decline to fund the exits. The permanent-capital vehicle cannot redeem: its \$150M — and its \$2.25M of fee — stays put through the drawdown, and Maya holds her positions to recovery instead of dumping them.

Now value the difference. A management company is worth a multiple of its fee-related earnings, and the multiple rises with the *stability* of those earnings. If open-ended fee revenue is worth, say, 8 times FRE because it can run in a crisis, permanent-capital fee revenue might be worth 12 times because it cannot. The same \$2.25M of fee revenue is worth roughly 50% more to a buyer when it sits on capital that cannot flee.

*Permanent capital does not pay a higher fee; it makes the same fee worth more, because a buyer pays a higher multiple for revenue that cannot redeem in a panic.*

## GP-stake sales

Everything so far has expanded or stabilized the *fund's* capital. A **GP-stake sale** does something categorically different: it sells a piece of the *management company itself*. The buyer is not an investor in the fund. The buyer is a part-owner of the business that runs the fund — entitled to a slice of its fee revenue and its enterprise value, in perpetuity, for a cheque paid today.

This is worth dwelling on, because it is the moment the asset being traded flips. In a seed deal at launch, a founder sells a slice of a business worth almost nothing to get capital *into the fund*. In a GP-stake sale at maturity, a founder sells a slice of a business worth a great deal to get capital *out of the firm* — into the founder's own pocket, or onto the firm's balance sheet for growth. Same kind of instrument, opposite stage, opposite purpose. Figure 4 shows how the stake plugs in.

![A graph showing a stake buyer paying sixty million dollars for twenty percent of a management company, the fund paying fees into that management company, and the economics splitting eighty percent to the founders and twenty percent to the buyer, with the buyer also providing growth capital and introductions but no day-to-day vote](/imgs/blogs/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes-4.png)

A founder sells a GP stake for one of three reasons, and a good buyer asks which it is. **Liquidity**: the founder's wealth is trapped in an illiquid private firm, and a stake sale lets them diversify by taking cash off the table without selling the whole business or losing control. **Growth capital**: the proceeds (when they go to the firm rather than the founder) fund expansion — seeding new strategies, hiring a sales team, buying technology, or providing the firm's own balance-sheet capital to anchor a new fund. **Succession**: an aging founder uses a staged stake sale to transition ownership to the next generation of partners or to a strategic buyer, turning a one-person firm into an institution that outlives them.

The buyers are a specialized industry. **Goldman Sachs Petershill** is the best-known dedicated GP-stakes vehicle, buying minority interests in alternative-asset managers. **Blackstone**, through its strategic-capital arm, and **Dyal Capital Partners** (now part of Blue Owl) built large portfolios of minority stakes in hedge funds and private-equity firms. Others — **Investcorp**, **Affiliated Managers Group (AMG)**, and various sovereign and institutional buyers — play in the same space. What they share is a model: buy passive minority stakes in many managers, take a diversified cut of fee revenue across all of them, and rely on the winners to carry the portfolio. It is the seeder's call-option logic from [seed and acceleration capital](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/seed-and-acceleration-capital), but bought later, at a far higher price, on a proven business.

The crucial term is what the buyer *gets* and what it *does not*. A GP-stake buyer typically gets a fixed percentage of the management company's economics — fee revenue, often both management and incentive — plus information rights and a share of any future sale of the firm. What it usually does *not* get is operational control: no vote on individual trades, no seat on the investment committee, no ability to fire the founder. The whole point of the asset, from the buyer's side, is that it is *passive* — a diversified income stream from managers who keep running their own shops. From the founder's side, that passivity is the appeal: you can sell a fifth of the economics and still run the firm exactly as before.

A GP-stake transaction runs like a private-company M&A process, and a founder unprepared for that will be at a disadvantage against a buyer who does these for a living. The buyer conducts extensive due diligence on the *business*, not the strategy: the durability of the AUM, the concentration of the investor base, the length of the lock-ups, the strength of the operating infrastructure, the depth of the team beyond the founder, and the key-person risk that the whole firm walks out the door if the founder does. The negotiation centers on three numbers — the percentage sold, the price, and what the economics include — and on a thicket of terms around them: whether the stake is in the management company or also the general partner, whether it shares in the incentive fee or only the management fee, what information the buyer receives, what happens on a sale of the firm, and what (limited) consent rights the buyer has over major decisions like a change of control. The founder's leverage in that negotiation is the firm's quality and the existence of competing bidders; the buyer's leverage is that it has done this a hundred times and the founder is doing it once.

A sophisticated founder also thinks about *staging*. Selling 20% in one transaction maximizes today's cheque but surrenders the most economics; selling 10% now and reserving the right to sell another 10% later — ideally at a higher valuation once the firm has grown — lets the founder take some chips off the table while keeping upside in the stake itself. Staging also matters for succession: a founder planning to retire over a decade can use a sequence of stake sales to transfer ownership gradually to the next generation of partners or to a strategic buyer, smoothing the transition and spreading the tax. The single 20%-for-\$60M deal is the simplest case; the real-world version is often a multi-step program negotiated with one eye on the firm's growth trajectory and the other on the founder's personal timeline.

#### Worked example: a GP-stake sale of 20% for \$60M and the implied valuation

A GP-stakes firm offers Meridian \$60M for 20% of the management company. The implied valuation is immediate: if 20% is worth \$60M, then 100% is worth \$60M divided by 0.20, which is **\$300M**.

Now sanity-check that against the firm's economics. Meridian manages about \$1B and earns roughly \$15M a year in fee revenue. At a \$300M valuation, the deal is being struck at \$300M divided by \$15M, which is **20 times fee revenue**. If Meridian's fee-related earnings — fee revenue minus its operating costs — run at about \$9M a year, the deal is being struck at \$300M divided by \$9M, or roughly **33 times FRE**. (Reported hedge-fund GP-stake deals have spanned a wide range of FRE multiples; treat these as illustrative.) Whether that multiple is rich or cheap depends entirely on how stable and growable the buyer judges Meridian's earnings to be — which is why the rest of the negotiation is about FRE durability, not about the strategy's last quarter.

For the \$60M, Maya gives up 20% of every future fee dollar, forever. She keeps 80% of the economics, full operational control, and the buyer's growth capital and allocator introductions. Whether that is a good trade is the subject of the valuation section below.

*A GP-stake price is a multiple in disguise: divide the cheque by the stake to get the implied firm value, then divide that by fee-related earnings to see the multiple you are selling at.*

## Valuing the management company

The question underneath every GP-stake sale is the one Maya asked at the conference table: *what is the management company actually worth?* The answer is the same as for any cash-flow business — it is worth a multiple of its earnings — but a fund management company has two very different earnings streams, and a buyer treats them very differently. Figure 7 stacks them.

![A vertical stack showing the revenue streams of a mature manager, with the volatile incentive fee at the top, the management fee below it, operating costs subtracted, and the stable fee-related earnings as the base that a stake sale is priced on](/imgs/blogs/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes-7.png)

The **management fee** is the bottom, recurring layer: roughly 1.5% of AUM, billed whether or not the fund makes money. Subtract the firm's operating costs — salaries, rent, technology, audit, administration, compliance — and what remains is **fee-related earnings (FRE)**: the stable, contractual profit the firm earns just for managing the assets. FRE is predictable. A buyer can forecast it, because it depends mostly on AUM, which moves slowly, and on a fee rate fixed by contract. This is the line a GP-stake buyer values most highly, and it is why buyers obsess over capital stickiness — permanent capital and long locks make FRE more predictable, which lifts the multiple.

The **incentive fee** is the volatile top layer: roughly 20% of profits above the high-water mark, and zero in any down year (see [fees and terms: the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark)). The incentive fee can be enormous in a great year and nothing in a bad one. Because it is an option — it pays only when the fund is above its prior peak — a buyer values it at a *lower* multiple than FRE, if it credits it at all. A firm whose value rests mostly on incentive fees is a firm whose value swings with markets; a firm whose value rests on FRE is a firm with a floor.

So the valuation is: take fee-related earnings, apply a multiple set by how stable, diversified, and growable those earnings are, and add a smaller credit for the incentive-fee stream. A fragile single-fund manager dependent on one strategy's good year trades at a low multiple — a buyer fears the earnings could evaporate. A diversified platform with multiple strategies, institutional SMAs, permanent capital, and a long track record trades at a high multiple — its earnings are durable. Figure 3 shows how the same fee earnings command a higher multiple as the business de-risks.

![A bar chart showing management-company value rising from twelve million dollars at four times fee earnings in a fragile first year to thirty-nine million dollars at thirteen times in year seven, holding fee-related earnings flat at three million dollars to isolate the rising multiple](/imgs/blogs/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes-3.png)

The figure holds fee-related earnings *flat* at \$3M to make the point clean: the value rises from \$12M to \$39M not because the firm earns more, but because the *same* earnings become more reliable as the firm matures. Diversification, sticky capital, a long track record, and an institutional operation are the levers that move the multiple — and every structure in this post (share classes, SMAs, permanent capital) is, viewed from the buyer's seat, a multiple-expansion exercise. This is the deepest reason the capital structure evolves: each step makes the management company worth more.

It is worth naming the specific levers, because a founder who knows what moves the multiple can deliberately build toward a higher one. **Capital stickiness** is the biggest: a buyer pays far more for revenue sitting on permanent capital and long locks than for revenue sitting on quarterly-redeemable money, because the former cannot evaporate in a panic. **Strategy diversification** is next: a firm running three uncorrelated strategies can lose one and survive, so its earnings carry a smaller discount than a single-strategy shop whose entire value rests on one edge that may decay. **Investor-base diversification** matters for the same reason — a firm whose AUM is spread across fifty institutions is safer than one where a single investor is half the book and could leave overnight. **Track-record length and consistency** lower the buyer's uncertainty about future earnings: ten years of steady results are worth a higher multiple than two spectacular years that may not repeat. **Operational and team depth** address key-person risk: a firm that can run without the founder is worth more than one that *is* the founder, because the buyer is not underwriting a single human being's continued presence.

Read the other way, those levers are a punch list. A founder who wants to sell the firm — or a stake in it — for the best price spends the years before the sale lengthening lock-ups, adding a second strategy, diversifying the investor base away from any single dominant ticket, building an institutional operation, and deepening the team beyond themselves. None of those moves changes this quarter's returns; all of them raise the multiple a buyer will pay for the firm's earnings. The management company is an asset whose value the founder can actively engineer, and the capital-structure evolution in this post is, at bottom, that engineering.

#### Worked example: valuing a management company at a multiple of fee revenue

Apply the framework to Meridian at maturity. The firm manages \$1B, charges a blended 1.5% management fee, and earns 1.5% of \$1B, which is \$15M a year in management-fee revenue. Its operating costs — a team of a dozen, an institutional technology and service-provider stack, offices, insurance — run about \$6M a year. Fee-related earnings are therefore \$15M minus \$6M, which is **\$9M**.

A buyer judges Meridian a diversified, sticky, institutional platform and applies a multiple of, say, 11 times FRE. The FRE-based value is 11 times \$9M, which is **\$99M**. The buyer adds a modest credit for the incentive-fee stream — discounted heavily because it is volatile and option-like — of perhaps another \$30M to \$60M, depending on how the buyer treats the upside. That lands the total management-company value somewhere around \$130M to \$160M on the conservative FRE-anchored view.

Notice that this is *lower* than the \$300M implied by the 20%-for-\$60M term sheet in the earlier example. That is the negotiation: a *strategic* buyer paying for growth potential and capacity rights might price the whole firm at 20 times *revenue*, while a *conservative* FRE-anchored valuation lands far lower. The gap between those two numbers — the strategic premium — is exactly what Maya and the buyer are really arguing about.

*A management company is worth a high multiple of its stable fee-related earnings plus a low multiple of its volatile incentive fee — so the way to raise the firm's value is to make its earnings more reliable, not just larger.*

## Common misconceptions

Smart markets people who have never run the business of a fund carry a predictable set of wrong mental models into the capital-structure conversation. Six of them do real damage.

**"The fund is the asset I own."** This is the most expensive misconception, and it is the heart of the post. You do not own the fund — the *investors* own the fund; their capital, their gains, their losses. What you own is the **management company**: the business that earns the fees. When a buyer wires \$60M for "20% of your firm," they are buying 20% of the management company's fee economics, not 20% of the fund's NAV. A founder who confuses the two cannot value their own life's work. The fund is the engine; the management company is the car you own and can sell.

**"A GP stake means losing control."** It almost never does. A standard GP stake is a *minority*, *passive* interest — the buyer gets a cut of the economics and information rights, but no vote on trades, no investment-committee seat, and no ability to fire the founder. The entire business model of a GP-stakes buyer depends on the manager continuing to run their own shop; control would defeat the purpose. The real governance risk is not control but *conflict*: a buyer whose economics reward AUM growth may quietly favor asset-gathering over performance — so you keep the stake minority, passive, and clear on governance, and you keep that misalignment in view (see [valuation and conflicts: governance](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/valuation-and-conflicts-governance)).

**"SMAs are just smaller funds."** An SMA is not a small fund — it is a *different structure entirely*. The fund pools many investors into one vehicle the manager controls; an SMA is one investor's own account that the manager merely trades under mandate. The investor owns the assets, controls the custody, sees every position, and can pull the mandate without a gate or notice period. An SMA gives away transparency and control that a commingled fund deliberately withholds — which is exactly why institutions want it and why it carries extra operational burden and a fee discount.

**"Permanent capital just means a long lock-up."** A long lock-up still ends; the investor still has a redemption right, merely deferred. Permanent capital means there is *no redemption right at all* — the investor exits, if ever, by selling their shares to another investor, not by pulling money from the fund. That distinction is the whole value: a fund full of three-year locks still faces a wall of redemptions when the locks roll off, while a permanent-capital vehicle never does. Deferred liquidity is not the same as no liquidity.

**"A high incentive fee is what makes my firm valuable."** A buyer values your firm mostly on its *fee-related earnings* — the stable management-fee profit — and discounts the incentive fee heavily because it is an option that pays zero in a down year. A firm whose value rests on incentive fees is a firm whose value collapses in a drawdown. The way to a high valuation is durable, recurring FRE — diversified strategies, sticky capital, institutional operations — not a spectacular performance-fee year that may not repeat.

**"Selling a stake is a sign of weakness."** Sometimes a stake sale signals trouble — a founder cashing out before a decline. Far more often it is a sign of *maturity*: the founder is diversifying personal wealth that is dangerously concentrated in one private firm, raising growth capital, or planning succession. A GP-stakes firm buying 20% of a billion-dollar manager is making a considered bet on a durable business, not bailing out a sinking one. The presence of a respected stake buyer on your cap table is, if anything, a credibility signal to allocators — much as a respected seeder's name was at launch.

**"A bigger multiple just means I waited long enough."** Maturity helps, but the multiple is earned, not granted by the calendar. Two firms the same age can trade at wildly different multiples: the one with permanent capital, three strategies, fifty institutional investors, and a deep bench commands a high multiple; the one that is still a single redeemable fund dominated by one investor and dependent on one founder commands a low one, no matter how long it has existed. Time gives you the *opportunity* to build durability into the firm; it does not build it for you. A founder who spends a decade running the same fragile single-fund structure has a decade-old fragile firm, and a buyer will price it as such.

## How it plays out in the real world

The GP-stakes industry is real, large, and named. **Goldman Sachs Petershill** has built a multi-billion-dollar business buying minority stakes in alternative-asset managers and even listed a vehicle (Petershill Partners) on the London Stock Exchange to give public investors exposure to a portfolio of those stakes — a striking illustration of the management company *as the asset*: the thing being securitized and traded is not any fund's returns but a basket of managers' fee economics. **Dyal Capital Partners**, which raised a series of large funds to buy minority interests in hedge-fund and private-equity managers, merged into **Blue Owl Capital**, which itself went public — turning a portfolio of GP stakes into a publicly traded firm. **Blackstone's** strategic-capital arm and **Investcorp** play the same game. The existence of these buyers, and their willingness to pay double-digit multiples of fee-related earnings, is the market evidence that a management company is a valuable, tradeable asset.

The structures stack in the real world exactly as Figure 5 contrasts them. A young fund is a single commingled vehicle: one revenue line, one redemption calendar, one bad quarter that hits every fee. A mature platform is a constellation — flagship fund, founders and institutional classes, several large SMAs, a permanent-capital vehicle, perhaps a second strategy — and its fee revenue is diversified across all of them, with redemptions staggered and a chunk of capital that cannot run at all.

![A before-and-after comparison contrasting a single commingled fund with one pool, one redemption calendar, and value resting on one vehicle, against a multi-vehicle platform with a fund plus classes plus separately managed accounts plus permanent capital, staggered locks, and diversified fee-related earnings a buyer will pay up for](/imgs/blogs/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes-5.png)

That diversification is not vanity; it is what lets the management company command a high multiple, because it is what makes the fee-related earnings durable. A buyer pays 11 times FRE for a platform and 4 times for a fragile single fund precisely because the platform's earnings survive the loss of any one vehicle.

The risk to watch is **misalignment**, the chronic problem that runs through this whole series. A GP-stake buyer's economics scale with AUM, so the buyer wants the firm to gather assets — even past the point where the strategy's edge can absorb them. As covered in [scaling capacity vs alpha and the platform](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/scaling-capacity-vs-alpha-and-the-platform), many strategies decay as they grow, so a founder who lets a stake buyer push them toward asset-gathering can quietly trade performance for size. The founder bears the performance drag; the buyer still collects on the larger management fee. This is why a careful founder keeps the stake minority and passive, caps any capacity rights, and treats the buyer's growth ambitions with the same skepticism they would bring to any investor whose incentives are not perfectly their own. Figure 6 makes the long-run cost of the stake concrete.

![A line chart showing that selling twenty percent of the management company for sixty million dollars delivers a one-time cheque, while the cumulative twenty percent of fee revenue given up compounds and passes the sixty million dollars of proceeds in about year twelve, then keeps growing](/imgs/blogs/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes-6.png)

That crossover — about year twelve on a fee book growing at a modest pace — is the founder's version of the seeder's permanent revenue share, just bought back at a far higher price for a far more valuable firm. The \$60M solves a real problem today: it diversifies Maya's wealth, or funds Meridian's next strategy. But the 20% follows the firm for as long as it exists. As at launch, the question is never "is \$60M a lot of money?" — it always is — but "is what I am buying with it worth a permanent fifth of everything I will build from here?"

The broader market trend is unmistakable, and it is the final piece of evidence for the post's thesis. Over the past decade, GP-stake transactions have moved from a niche curiosity to a multi-billion-dollar specialty, with dedicated funds raised specifically to buy minority interests in alternative-asset managers, and with some of those stake portfolios — Petershill Partners, Blue Owl — listed on public exchanges so that ordinary investors can own a slice of a basket of fund managers' fee economics. Think about what that means: the management company has become so clearly an asset that there is now a *public market* for diversified portfolios of them. A founder launching today is not just building a fund; they are building a private company that sits inside an established market for private companies of exactly its kind, with known buyers, known structures, and observable (if wide-ranging) multiples. That market did not exist for an earlier generation of founders, who could monetize only by selling the whole firm or by collecting fees until they retired. The modern founder has a third option — sell a minority slice, keep control, keep compounding — precisely because the management company is now understood, by a sophisticated set of buyers, as the valuable asset it always quietly was.

## When this matters / Further reading

The capital-structure evolution matters the moment a founder stops thinking like a portfolio manager and starts thinking like the owner of a business. Early on, all that matters is performance and survival; the fund is one pool, the structure is simple, and the only asset on anyone's mind is the track record. But as the firm matures — as the AUM grows, the investor base fragments, and the founder's wealth concentrates dangerously in one illiquid company — the *structure itself* becomes a lever, and the management company becomes an asset with a market price.

The practical takeaways: think of every structural move (a founders class, an SMA, a permanent-capital vehicle) not just as a way to serve an investor but as a way to make your fee-related earnings more durable, because durable FRE is what a buyer pays a high multiple for. Understand that you own the management company, not the fund, and learn to value it the way a buyer will — a multiple of stable FRE plus a discounted credit for the volatile incentive fee. And when a GP-stakes buyer offers you a cheque for a slice of the firm, divide the cheque by the stake to see the implied valuation, divide that by FRE to see the multiple, and ask the seeder's eternal question one last time: is this one-time cash worth a permanent share of everything I build from here? The cheque is finite; the give-up compounds.
