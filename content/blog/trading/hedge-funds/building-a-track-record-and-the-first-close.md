> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A first-time manager cannot raise from institutions without an auditable, fundable track record, and there is no way to skip straight to one. You build it on a bridge — a separately managed account, an incubator fund, or a carefully ported prior record — run it for one to two years, get it audited and presented to a recognized standard, and only then is it the evidence allocators actually fund.
>
> - **Allocators fund evidence, not assertions.** A backtest, a self-reported spreadsheet, or "trust me, I ran the book" is not a track record. An independent auditor, an administrator-struck NAV, and a net-of-fee, GIPS-compliant presentation are what convert your returns into something an institution can pass through diligence.
> - **The bridges differ in cost, credibility, and what you can claim.** A separately managed account is cheap and produces a clean, fee-bearing, third-party-administered record. An incubator fund gives you fund-structure P&L. A ported prior record is cheap but legally restricted — you cannot freely claim your old desk's numbers.
> - **The anchor investor de-risks the launch.** A `\$25M` anchor on day one signals quality to the next allocator and pulls in follow-on capital, so the AUM ramp with an anchor crosses the `\$100M` institutional-viability floor in months rather than years.
> - **The one milestone to remember:** the **first close** — the day the subscription documents are countersigned and the money wires. It is not the end of raising; it begins a **rolling close** where each subsequent close stacks new capital on the last.

Maya had run a long/short equity book for eight years at a multi-manager platform, and her sleeve had compounded at a net rate that would make most allocators salivate. She had the strategy, the risk discipline, and a thick file of investors who said they would "love to see something" when she went out on her own. So she did. She incorporated a Delaware management company, paid a lawyer `\$90,000` for a set of fund documents, and called the first allocator on her list — a fund-of-funds that had told her, twice, that they wanted to back her.

The call lasted four minutes. The allocator asked one question: "Where's the audited track?" Maya started to describe her platform returns — the Sharpe, the drawdown, the attribution. The allocator cut her off gently. "I believe you. But I can't write a memo on numbers I can't verify, that belong to a fund I can't see, computed by a process I can't inspect. Bring me a year of live, audited, net-of-fee performance in your own vehicle and we'll have a real conversation." Maya hung up and realized she had built the legal shell of a fund and forgotten the one asset the shell exists to hold: a credible promise, backed by evidence. She had no track record she could actually use. And there was no way to conjure one overnight.

This post is about how Maya — and you — get from that four-minute phone call to a fundable fund. It turns out there are **bridges**: ways to build an auditable track record before you have a fund, and a sequence of milestones that turns the first dollar into a self-reinforcing flow. The spine of the whole journey is in Figure 1.

![A seven-stage pipeline showing the path from having no fund, through picking a bridge such as a separately managed account, incubator, or ported record, running live capital for about two years, getting audited and GIPS-compliant numbers, securing an anchor investor, reaching the first close, and re-opening as a rolling close.](/imgs/blogs/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close-1.png)

The hedge-fund business is, at root, the business of selling an aligned promise — to compound capital with skill inside a structure that protects the investor. Everything earlier in this series built the structure: the [management company versus the fund](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund), the [domestic-versus-offshore master-feeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder), the [fund documents](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription). This post is about making the promise *credible* before anyone has seen you keep it. That credibility has a name and a shape: an auditable track record, and the first close that it unlocks.

## Foundations: what a fundable track record is

Before the bridges make sense, you need the vocabulary precisely. Allocators use these words with a precision that founders, fresh from the trading desk, often underestimate. A loose word here costs you a meeting.

**Track record.** A track record is the live, time-stamped, verifiable history of your investment results. The operative words are *live* and *verifiable*. A backtest is not a track record — it is a simulation of decisions you never actually had to make with real money on the line. Paper trading is not a track record. A spreadsheet you keep is not a track record, because no independent party stands behind it. A track record is the realized, dated series of returns produced by deploying real capital under real constraints, where someone other than you can confirm the numbers are true.

**AUM and NAV.** *Assets under management* (AUM) is the total capital you run. *Net asset value* (NAV) is what one unit of the fund is worth: total assets minus liabilities, divided by units outstanding. The NAV per share is the spine of a track record — your return for a period is the percentage change in NAV per share, after fees. The crucial detail: in a credible fund, the NAV is *struck by an independent administrator*, not by you. A self-priced NAV is the single biggest red flag in the business — it is the thing that let [Madoff](#how-it-plays-out-in-the-real-world) fabricate returns for decades.

**Separately managed account (SMA).** An SMA is an investment account owned by a single client, in the client's name, that you manage under an investment management agreement (IMA). You never take custody of the client's money; you have *trading authority* over their account. The client can see every position and can fire you at will. An SMA is the simplest legal way to run outside money: no fund, no offering documents, no commingling. Its returns, audited and properly presented, can become a track record.

**Incubator fund.** An incubator fund is a small, real fund structure — a fund entity, a management company, an administrator, sometimes a "fund-in-a-box" lightweight setup — that you launch with a tiny amount of capital (often your own and friends-and-family money) specifically to build a *fund-structure* track record. It produces an audited NAV per share, the way a full fund does, but at a fraction of the cost and complexity, so you can later show "here is one to two years of live fund returns" without having raised institutional capital first.

**Audited track.** An audited track record is one where an independent accounting firm has examined the books and *attested* that the reported returns are accurate, computed under recognized accounting standards (US GAAP for most US funds). The auditor checks that positions are real, prices are right, fees are correctly accrued, and the NAV per share that produced your return is correct. An audit is an annual event; the audited financial statements are the document allocators ask for first.

**GIPS.** The *Global Investment Performance Standards* are a voluntary, globally recognized framework for how investment performance is calculated and *presented*. GIPS forces consistency: you must show all your accounts in a composite (no cherry-picking the winners), compute returns a standard way, present net-of-fee results, and disclose your methodology. A fund can also undergo *GIPS verification*, where an independent firm confirms the firm-wide claim of compliance. Institutions look for GIPS because it removes the most common forms of performance manipulation. Audit and GIPS are different things: audit attests the *numbers are true*; GIPS attests the *presentation is honest and standardized*.

**Anchor investor.** An anchor (sometimes "cornerstone") investor is a credible, sizeable LP who commits early — often before or at the first close — and whose commitment is visible to everyone else you talk to. The anchor's dollars matter, but the *signal* matters more: a respected family office or fund-of-funds putting in `\$25M` tells the next allocator "someone who does this for a living already did the work and said yes." An anchor is not the same as a seeder; a [seeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-capital-raising-reality-chicken-and-egg) takes a slice of your management-company economics in exchange for capital, whereas an anchor is usually just a (possibly fee-advantaged) LP in the fund.

**First close.** A fund raise is not a single event; it is a series of *closes*. The first close is the date on which the first batch of committed investors sign their subscription documents and wire their money, and the fund begins trading. It is the moment the fund goes live. Crucially, you do not need every committed dollar to do a first close — you set a minimum viable size, close the investors you have, and start.

**Rolling close.** After the first close, an open-ended hedge fund typically re-opens for new subscriptions at regular intervals — usually monthly. This is the *rolling close*: each subsequent close adds new capital to the running fund at the then-current NAV. This is unlike a closed-end private-equity fund, which has a fixed fundraising window. A hedge fund's rolling close means raising never really stops — and it means the audited track you accumulate after the first close is what raises the *next* tranche.

**Soft circle.** A soft circle is a verbal, non-binding indication from an investor: "we're interested in about `\$10M` if you launch by year-end." It is not a commitment — soft circles famously evaporate — but it is how a manager gauges whether a first close is achievable. A first close is built by hardening soft circles into signed subscription documents.

**Porting a track record.** Porting is the act of carrying your prior investment results from a former employer into your new fund's marketing. It is legally and ethically fraught: the returns were earned with the prior firm's capital, resources, and often a team, and there are rules — and the prior firm's permission — governing what you can claim. We devote a whole section to what carries over and what does not.

With the vocabulary set, the rest of the post is about how these pieces fit together: why you need the audited track at all, the three bridges to build one, the legal minefield of porting, how an anchor de-risks the raise, the mechanics of the first close, and the rolling close that follows.

## Why you need an audited track

It is worth dwelling on *why* the audited track is non-negotiable, because founders coming off a great run often feel insulted by the demand. "My numbers are my numbers," the reasoning goes. "Why should an institution doubt me?"

The answer has nothing to do with you personally. An allocator's job is to deploy other people's money — a pension's retirees, an endowment's scholarships, a family's wealth — and then to *defend that decision* to an investment committee, an auditor, and possibly a regulator or a lawsuit. They cannot defend a decision built on numbers they cannot verify. The audited track is not a hurdle to prove you are honest; it is the infrastructure that lets a busy, fiduciary-bound allocator say "yes" without taking your word for it.

Figure 5 makes the contrast concrete: the same returns look completely different to an allocator depending on whether they are a self-reported claim or an audited, GIPS-compliant track.

![A before-and-after comparison. The left column, labeled no audited track, shows a self-reported spreadsheet, gross hand-picked dates, no third-party NAV, and an allocator verdict of cannot diligence this. The right column, labeled audited GIPS-compliant track, shows auditor-signed financials, net-of-fee full-period returns, an administrator-struck NAV, and an allocator verdict of fundable proceed to diligence.](/imgs/blogs/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close-5.png)

Consider what the audited track defends against, each a real failure mode the allocator has seen:

- **Cherry-picking.** Without a standard, a manager shows the months and accounts that flatter. GIPS forces a complete composite over the full period.
- **Survivorship.** A manager who ran ten accounts shows the three that survived. A composite includes the dead ones.
- **Gross-versus-net games.** Gross returns omit the fees the investor actually pays. The [SEC Marketing Rule](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule) requires net-of-fee performance shown alongside any gross figure, precisely because gross flatters.
- **Self-pricing.** A manager who marks their own illiquid positions can smooth returns or hide losses. An administrator-struck NAV, audited annually, removes that discretion.
- **Outright fabrication.** The extreme case is a Ponzi, where the returns are invented entirely. Independent administration, custody, and audit are the three controls that make fabrication nearly impossible.

The audited track is the answer to all five at once. It is why the allocator's first email after a good meeting is, invariably, "can you send the audited financials and a GIPS-compliant performance presentation?" If your answer is "I don't have those yet," you are not in the game — you are in the queue to *get into* the game, which is exactly what the bridges are for.

There is a related cost that founders feel acutely and rarely plan for: the **track-record gap**. The day you leave your seat, your clock resets. The prior firm's track is theirs, not yours (more on that below), so even a star who managed billions arrives at the new fund with, effectively, zero *portable, fundable* record of their own. The one-to-two-year minimum that allocators want is not arbitrary cruelty — a single good year can be luck, and they need enough of a series to see a Sharpe, a drawdown, and a recovery. So the founder faces a gap: a period, often two years, where they are running real money on a bridge, paying for audit and administration, and *not yet* able to raise institutional capital. Planning for that gap — its duration, its burn, and its funding — is as much a part of the launch as the legal structure. A founder who launches expecting to raise on day one, with no plan for the gap, runs out of runway in the gap. A founder who treats the gap as the *cost of manufacturing the asset* — and funds it deliberately with savings, an SMA fee stream, or friends-and-family capital — comes through it with the one thing that opens every door.

#### Worked example: the cost of an annual audit and GIPS verification

Maya wants to understand what "getting audited" actually costs, because the demand is abstract until it has a price. She prices it out with her service providers for the Meridian Fund.

The **annual fund audit** — an independent accounting firm examining the fund's financial statements under US GAAP — runs `\$30,000` to `\$60,000` per year for a small, single-strategy fund (more for complex or illiquid books). Maya budgets `\$45,000`. The **fund administrator** that strikes the monthly NAV — the independent party whose numbers the auditor relies on — runs another `\$30,000` to `\$60,000` a year for a small fund; Maya budgets `\$40,000`. To present a **GIPS-compliant** track she needs to compute returns to the standard, and to claim *GIPS verification* she pays an independent verifier perhaps `\$10,000` to `\$25,000` for the first verification; Maya budgets `\$15,000` for the initial verification and a smaller annual fee thereafter.

So the credibility infrastructure — the thing that converts her returns into a fundable asset — costs roughly `\$45,000` (audit) plus `\$40,000` (admin) plus `\$15,000` (GIPS verification) ≈ `\$100,000` in the first year. On a `\$10M` SMA, that is a punishing **1.0%** of assets just for credibility, before she has paid a lawyer, bought market data, or paid herself a dollar. On a `\$40M` fund, the same infrastructure is **0.25%** of assets — still real, but bearable.

*The audited track is not free, and its cost-per-dollar is brutal when you are small — which is precisely why you build it on the cheapest bridge that produces a credible record, then scale onto it.*

## SMAs as a bridge

The separately managed account is the cleanest, cheapest bridge to a track record, and for most first-time managers it is the right first move.

Here is the mechanics. You sign an **investment management agreement** with a single client — often a wealthy individual, a family office, or a friend-and-family backer who believes in you. The account is opened at a custodian *in the client's name*; the client's money never touches your accounts. You are granted **limited power of attorney** to trade the account — you can buy and sell, but you cannot withdraw cash. You charge a management fee, and usually a performance fee, debited directly from the account. A third-party administrator or the custodian computes the account's monthly value, and at year-end an auditor can examine the account's results.

What makes the SMA a *credible* bridge — as opposed to your own trading, which proves nothing — is that the money is real and external, the fees are real (so your incentives are aligned and tested), and the record is third-party-verified. An allocator looking at a two-year audited SMA sees: this person made live decisions with someone else's money, charged a fee, and produced a verifiable net-of-fee return. That is a track record, even though there was never a "fund."

The trade-offs are real. An SMA's returns are the returns of *one* account with *one* client's constraints — if the client demanded extra liquidity or imposed position limits, the record reflects that, not your unconstrained strategy. SMAs do not commingle, so you cannot show "the fund returned X"; you show "the strategy, in a representative account, returned X." And an SMA does not scale the way a fund does — running fifty SMAs is an operational nightmare, which is exactly the problem a fund structure solves. But as a *bridge* — a way to build one to two years of audited, fee-bearing, third-party-administered performance before you have a fund — the SMA is hard to beat.

The comparison of all four bridges is laid out in Figure 2.

![A four-row matrix comparing SMA, incubator, proprietary, and ported bridges across three columns: setup cost, credibility, and claimability. SMA scores low cost, high credibility, clean claimability. Incubator scores medium cost, high credibility, clean claimability. Proprietary scores low cost, low credibility, weak claimability. Ported scores low cost, medium credibility, restricted claimability.](/imgs/blogs/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close-2.png)

Notice in Figure 2 that the SMA dominates on all three dimensions for a first-timer: it is cheap to stand up, an allocator trusts it because it is real fee-bearing client money with independent administration, and the returns are cleanly yours because *you* made the decisions for an *outside* investor. The only bridge that matches its credibility is the incubator, which costs more.

#### Worked example: a `\$10M` SMA run for two years becomes a fundable track

Maya takes the SMA route. A family office she has known for years agrees to seed a `\$10M` separately managed account. She signs an IMA charging **1.5%** management and **15%** performance — a founders-friendly rate she will later carry into the fund. She opens the account at a prime broker in the family office's name, with limited trading authority. From day one she also hires an administrator (`\$40,000`/year) to strike a monthly value and an auditor (`\$45,000`/year) to examine the account annually.

Over two years the strategy compounds. Net of her fees, the account returns **+11%** in year one and **+14%** in year two — a net NAV path of `\$10M` → `\$11.1M` → `\$12.65M`. Critically, every monthly value was struck by the administrator, and each year-end was audited. At the end of year two Maya has something she did not have on the day of that four-minute phone call: a **two-year, audited, net-of-fee track record** with a verifiable monthly return series, presentable to GIPS standards.

When she calls the fund-of-funds back, the question is no longer "where's the audited track?" She emails the audited financials and a GIPS-compliant presentation. Now the conversation is about strategy and terms — the conversation she wanted to have two years earlier.

*An SMA turns the chicken-and-egg of [the raising trap](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-capital-raising-reality-chicken-and-egg) into a sequence: you cannot raise without a track, so you build the track on one client's account first, then raise on it.*

The two years did cost Maya something beyond money: time, and the opportunity cost of running `\$10M` instead of `\$100M`. That is the price of the bridge. The alternative — launching a fund with no track and hoping — is how most first-time funds end up among the [funds that don't survive three years](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-founders-readiness-when-to-launch).

## Incubator funds

The second bridge is the incubator fund. Where an SMA builds a track in a single client's account, an incubator builds it inside an actual fund structure — which matters because some allocators discount SMA returns ("that's one account, not a fund") and prefer to see fund-structure P&L: a real NAV per share, struck monthly, audited annually, in a vehicle that looks like the fund you intend to scale.

An incubator fund is a deliberately minimal version of the real thing. You form the fund entity (often a single domestic vehicle, skipping the [offshore master-feeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder) you would add later) and the management company, hire a lightweight administrator, and seed it with a small amount — your own capital plus friends-and-family money, often `\$1M` to `\$5M`. You manage it exactly as you would a full fund. The output is a genuine fund track record: "the Meridian Fund returned X, net, over these two years, audited, NAV struck by an administrator."

The advantage over the SMA is purely about *form*: it is fund-structure P&L, which some institutional allocators weight more heavily, and it lets you test your real operating model — the administrator relationship, the NAV process, the audit — at small scale before institutional capital arrives. The disadvantage is cost: you are paying for a fund's infrastructure (formation, admin, audit) on a tiny asset base, so the cost-per-dollar is even worse than the SMA. Forming even a minimal fund runs `\$30,000` to `\$80,000`, and the annual admin and audit are the same `\$70,000`-ish you would pay anyway.

There is a subtle but important benefit to the incubator that founders miss: **continuity**. If you incubate the *actual* fund — the same legal entity you intend to scale — then your two years of incubator track *is* the fund's track. You do not "port" anything; the audited history belongs to the Meridian Fund because it *was* the Meridian Fund, just smaller. An allocator coming in at the first close is buying into a vehicle with a real, continuous, audited two-year history. That is the strongest possible position for a first-time manager, which is why a well-advised founder who can afford it often runs the incubator as the real fund from day one, then simply does a larger close once the track is built.

#### Worked example: incubating the Meridian Fund versus the SMA

Maya compares the two bridges side by side with real numbers, because the choice shapes the next two years.

**SMA path.** `\$10M` in a single family-office account. Annual cost: administrator `\$40,000` + audit `\$45,000` ≈ `\$85,000`, or **0.85%** of assets. Output: a strategy track in a representative account. Some allocators will say "that's an SMA, not a fund."

**Incubator path.** She forms the actual Meridian Fund as a single Delaware vehicle, seeds it with `\$1M` of her own money and `\$3M` from three friends-and-family LPs — `\$4M` total. One-time formation: `\$50,000`. Annual cost: administrator `\$40,000` + audit `\$45,000` ≈ `\$85,000`, which on `\$4M` is a savage **2.1%** of assets. But the output is fund-structure P&L in the *exact entity* she will scale, with no porting question.

The incubator costs more per dollar and locks up her own capital, but it produces the cleaner asset: when she opens the Meridian Fund's first close to institutions, the fund already has a continuous, audited two-year history. The SMA is cheaper and gives her a bigger live track (`\$10M` vs `\$4M`), but leaves her to either convert the SMA into the fund (a porting question) or argue that an SMA track should count.

*An incubator is more expensive per dollar but produces fund-structure P&L in the very vehicle you will scale — so the track you build is the track you keep, with no porting argument to win.*

## Porting a prior track record

The third bridge is the one founders most want to use and most often get wrong: carrying the track record from a prior employer into the new fund's marketing. It is the cheapest bridge — your old returns already exist — but it is hemmed in by law, by the prior firm's permission, and by the [SEC Marketing Rule](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule). Get it wrong and you have not built credibility; you have created a compliance liability and possibly defamed your own integrity.

The core principle: **a track record belongs to the firm where it was earned, not to the person who earned it.** When Maya ran her sleeve at the platform, the capital was the platform's, the risk system was the platform's, the leverage and financing were the platform's, and — critically — there may have been a *team*. Her returns were produced by a machine she was part of, not a machine she owned. The law and industry practice draw a careful line around what she may now claim as evidence of *her* skill.

Figure 7 maps the split: what carries over and what you cannot claim.

![A branching graph titled porting a prior track record. The prior desk P and L at the top splits into two branches. The defensible branch leads to port it with written consent and clean attribution, and to caveat it as a prior-firm result not earned in the Meridian Fund. The not-yours-to-claim branch leads to three nodes: other portfolio managers P and L is not your record, results that leaned on the firms leverage capacity or data, and any number the prior firm will not let you present.](/imgs/blogs/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close-7.png)

What you generally *can* present, with care:

- **The sleeve you personally and demonstrably managed.** If you were the decision-maker for an identifiable book — your trades, your sizing, your risk calls — that *portfolio's* results can often be presented, with disclosures, as a record of your decisions. This is "extracted performance" or a "predecessor track record" under the Marketing Rule, and it carries strict conditions.
- **With written consent.** You almost always need the prior firm's written permission to use *any* of its performance data. Without it you risk breaching confidentiality, employment, or IP terms — and you cannot get the underlying records audited.
- **With honest attribution and a caveat.** You must label it clearly: these returns were earned at a prior firm, with that firm's resources, and are *not* the returns of the Meridian Fund. You cannot present prior-firm results as if they were your new fund's results.

What you generally *cannot* claim:

- **Other people's P&L.** If a team produced the returns, you cannot present the team's results — or the whole firm's results — as your own. Attribution must be honest and specific to your decisions.
- **Results that depended on the firm's resources.** If your returns relied on the firm's leverage, financing rates, data, technology, or capacity that you will not have at the new fund, presenting them as portable is misleading.
- **Anything without consent.** No permission, no presentation. This is both a legal and a practical constraint: an auditor will not verify records the prior firm will not release.

The Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1, compliance date November 2022) wraps all of this. It governs "extracted performance" (carving out a subset of a portfolio) and "predecessor performance" (carrying a track from a prior firm), and it requires, among other things, that the person responsible for the prior performance be the same, that the accounts be sufficiently similar, that all relevant accounts be shown (no cherry-picking), and that net-of-fee performance be presented. The honest summary: porting is *possible*, but it is a supplement to a real track, not a substitute for one, and it is governed tightly enough that you must run it past a securities lawyer before a single number goes in a deck.

#### Worked example: what Maya can and cannot port

Maya managed a `\$120M` long/short sleeve at her platform for the last three years, net returns of roughly **+9%**, **+13%**, **+10%** after the platform's internal fee allocation. She wants to use this in her deck. She walks through the test with her lawyer.

**Can she claim the `\$120M` sleeve's returns?** Possibly, as *predecessor performance* — she was the identifiable decision-maker, the records exist, and the strategy is the same one the Meridian Fund will run. But only if (a) the platform gives written consent to use the data, (b) she shows *all* of her relevant accounts, not just the good years, (c) she presents it net of a fee structure and labels it clearly as prior-firm performance, and (d) the strategy at Meridian is genuinely similar (same universe, similar constraints).

**What must she strip out?** Her sleeve at the platform ran at the platform's financing rates and used the platform's centralized risk and execution. If a meaningful slice of her return came from financing or leverage she will not have at a `\$40M` startup, she must disclose that the new fund will run differently — and she cannot imply the prior return is what Meridian will produce. She also cannot include the two other PMs whose books she informally helped; those returns are not hers.

**The practical outcome.** The platform, after legal review, grants limited consent to present her sleeve's audited returns *as prior-firm performance, clearly caveated*. So her deck shows two things side by side: a labeled, prior-firm predecessor track for context, and — the asset that actually closes the deal — her two-year audited SMA track in her own vehicle. The ported record is the supporting actor; the audited bridge track is the lead.

*Porting is real but restricted: you can present, with consent and caveats, the sleeve you personally ran — but you cannot claim your old desk's machine as your own, and a ported track supplements a live audited track rather than replacing it.*

This is also why "my backtest is a track record" is the most dangerous misconception a founder can hold — a backtest has none of the properties (live, audited, third-party-verified, attributable) that make a track record fundable. See [the honest test for whether you have an edge](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test) and the quant-research discipline of [backtesting done right](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/backtesting-done-right-quant-research) for why a simulation, however rigorous, is the start of the journey, not the proof.

## The anchor investor

With an audited track in hand, the next lever is the anchor investor — and it is the single highest-leverage relationship in a first raise. An anchor is a credible, sizeable LP who commits at or before the first close and whose commitment is visible to everyone else you talk to.

The anchor solves a coordination problem. Every allocator you pitch is asking, silently, "who else is in?" Nobody wants to be the first money into a new fund — being first means there is no one to share the diligence burden or the reputational risk. This is a classic standoff: everyone waits for someone else to go first, so nobody goes. The anchor breaks the standoff. When a respected family office or fund-of-funds commits `\$25M`, every subsequent conversation changes: "we have a `\$25M` anchor from [a name the allocator respects], closing next month." The anchor's dollars are useful; the anchor's *signal* is decisive.

Why would an anchor go first? Because going first is worth something, and a smart founder pays for it — not in management-company equity (that is what a [seeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-capital-raising-reality-chicken-and-egg) takes) but in *terms*. An anchor typically gets a **founders share class**: a fee discount in exchange for early, sizeable commitment. Instead of the standard 2-and-20, the anchor might get 1.5-and-15 or 1-and-10, sometimes capped to a time window or an AUM ceiling on the class. The anchor also often gets capacity rights (the right to add more as the fund grows) and most-favored-nation terms (a guarantee that no later investor gets better terms). The founder gives up some fee revenue on the anchor's slice; in return the founder gets day-one credibility and the capital that makes the first close viable.

The effect on the raise is dramatic, and Figure 3 shows it. With an anchor, the fund launches at a credible size, the signal pulls in follow-on capital, and the AUM ramp crosses the `\$100M` institutional-viability floor in months. Without an anchor, the fund launches small, every conversation restarts the "who else is in?" standoff, and the ramp crawls.

![A line chart titled an anchor de-risks the raise. The green line, a fund with a 25 million dollar anchor and a 40 million dollar first close, rises steeply and crosses the 100 million dollar institutional-viability floor at about month nine, reaching 250 million dollars by month eighteen. The orange dashed line, a fund with no anchor and a 12 million dollar first close, rises slowly and stays below 100 million dollars at month eighteen.](/imgs/blogs/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close-3.png)

The gap in Figure 3 is not because the anchored fund is run better — by assumption the strategy is identical. The gap is pure *coordination*: the anchor converts a cold standoff into a warm momentum. The numbers are illustrative, but the shape is real and well understood by every allocator: a fund with a credible anchor raises faster because the anchor has done the social work of going first.

#### Worked example: a `\$25M` anchor unlocking follow-on capital

Maya lines up an anchor. A multi-family office she has built a relationship with over years agrees to commit `\$25M` to the Meridian Fund's first close, in exchange for a founders share class at **1.5% / 15%** (versus her standard **2% / 20%**), with most-favored-nation terms and capacity rights to add up to another `\$25M` in the first year.

What does the discount cost her? On `\$25M`, the founders class pays **1.5%** management = `\$375,000`/year versus the standard **2%** = `\$500,000` — she forgoes `\$125,000` of annual management fee, plus a slice of performance fee. Over a few years that is real money.

What does it buy? First, `\$25M` of day-one AUM — more than half of her `\$40M` target first close. Second, and worth far more, the *signal*: she can now tell every other prospect "[respected family office] is anchoring with `\$25M`." In the three months after the anchor commits, two more allocators who had been "watching" convert to soft circles, then to commitments, adding `\$15M`. The anchor's `\$25M` directly catalyzed the other `\$15M` that completes the `\$40M` first close. Within a year, follow-on capital — drawn by the anchor's presence and the now-live audited track — takes the fund past `\$100M`, exactly the ramp in Figure 3.

*The anchor's fee discount is the price of going first, and it is cheap: forgoing `\$125,000` a year in fees to catalyze a `\$40M` launch that crosses viability in months is one of the best trades a founder makes.*

A caution: the anchor must be a *credible* name, not just a big check. `\$25M` from an unknown source, or from a backer with a poor reputation, can signal *negatively* — "why did only that person say yes?" The signal is only as good as the signer. And founders must avoid over-concentration: if a single anchor is 60% of the fund, the fund is hostage to one redemption. The art is an anchor large enough to signal, small enough that the fund is not captive to it.

There is also a structural question founders should settle with the anchor up front: what happens when the anchor wants out? An anchor who takes a founders-class discount has, in effect, been paid to take launch risk — and a fair deal often gives the anchor a lock-up (one to two years) so the fund is not destabilized by an early redemption, while giving the founder the comfort that the day-one capital is sticky. The most damaging version of an anchor is one with a short notice period who can pull `\$25M` at the first wobble, taking the fund below break-even and signaling to everyone else that the cornerstone walked. Negotiate the anchor's lock-up and notice period with the same care as the fee discount; the *stickiness* of the anchor's capital is part of what you are buying with the discount, and it belongs in the side letter alongside the fee terms and the [liquidity provisions](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets) that govern every other investor.

## The first-close mechanics

The first close is the milestone the whole bridge-building has been pointing at. It is the day the fund goes live. But "the close" is not a single signature — it is the culmination of a dated, multi-step sequence that turns non-binding interest into wired capital. Figure 4 lays out the calendar.

![A timeline of the first-close calendar with six events. T minus 90 days, soft circle, a verbal non-binding indication. T minus 60 days, send the documents, the offering memorandum, partnership agreement, and subscription agreement go to each committed investor. T minus 30 days, KYC and AML, the administrator runs identity and source-of-funds checks. T equals zero, close date, subscriptions are countersigned and investors wire to the fund. T plus one day, capital is live and the fund starts trading. T plus thirty days, rolling close, the fund re-opens for new subscriptions.](/imgs/blogs/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close-4.png)

Walk through the sequence:

**Soft circles harden (T minus ~90 days).** You have a handful of soft circles — verbal "we're in for about X." None is binding. The first job is to convert them. You set a **target first-close size** (the minimum viable AUM that makes the fund worth launching — for Maya, `\$40M`) and a **close date**, and you tell each soft-circled investor: "we're closing on [date] at [target]; to be in the first close, we need your subscription documents and wire by then." A deadline converts soft circles into decisions.

**Documents go out (T minus ~60 days).** Each committing investor receives the offering package: the [private placement memorandum (PPM), the limited partnership agreement (LPA), and the subscription agreement](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription). The subscription agreement is the operative document — it is where the investor represents that they are an accredited investor (or qualified purchaser), states the amount they are subscribing, and agrees to the fund's terms. Investors and their counsel review; there is usually negotiation, especially with the anchor (side letters, MFN terms).

**KYC and AML clear (T minus ~30 days).** Before money can be accepted, the administrator runs know-your-customer and anti-money-laundering checks on each investor: identity verification, source-of-funds, sanctions screening. This is not optional and it takes time — a large institutional investor's KYC can take weeks. Founders routinely underestimate this and nearly miss their close date because an investor's paperwork is stuck in compliance.

**The close (T = 0).** On the close date, subscription agreements are countersigned by the general partner, and the committed investors wire their capital to the fund's account at the custodian. The administrator confirms receipt and records each investor's capital account at the opening NAV per share (typically `\$1,000` or `\$100` per unit on day one). The fund is now a real, funded vehicle.

**Trading begins (T plus 1 day).** The capital is at the prime broker. Maya can deploy. The promise the fund sold — to compound capital with skill — is now live, and from this day forward she is building the fund's *own* audited track.

#### Worked example: the first close at `\$40M` and its economics

Maya's first close lands. The `\$25M` anchor (founders class, 1.5/15), plus `\$15M` from three other LPs at standard 2/20, totals `\$40M` of day-one AUM.

What does `\$40M` generate? The management fee, blended across the two classes: `\$25M` × 1.5% = `\$375,000` plus `\$15M` × 2% = `\$300,000` = **`\$675,000`** of annual management-fee revenue. Against the fund's fixed run-rate — administrator `\$40,000`, audit `\$45,000`, legal/compliance `\$60,000`, market data and tech `\$80,000`, plus Maya's modest salary and an analyst — call it `\$0.9M` to `\$1.0M` a year, the management fee *does not yet cover costs*. Maya is running at a loss on the management fee alone, subsidized by her own capital and betting on performance fees and growth.

This is the brutal arithmetic of a sub-scale launch and exactly why the [break-even AUM is roughly `\$80M`](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-a-hedge-fund-makes-money) at a 1.5% fee. At `\$40M` Maya is below break-even. The first close is not a victory lap; it is the starting line of a race to `\$100M` before her runway runs out — a race the anchor's signal and the now-accumulating audited track are designed to win.

*A `\$40M` first close is a launch, not a business: the management fee on it does not cover an institutional-grade cost base, so the founder is racing to the rolling close and the break-even AUM before the runway is gone.*

## After the first close: the rolling close

The first close is the beginning, not the end, of raising. An open-ended hedge fund re-opens for subscriptions at regular intervals — usually monthly — and each of these is a **close**. This rolling-close structure is what makes the first close so consequential: it is the seed from which all subsequent capital grows, because now you have two things you did not have before — *live capital* and a *growing audited track in your own fund*.

The mechanics of a rolling close are simple. At each monthly subscription date, new investors (and existing investors adding capital) subscribe at the current NAV per share. The administrator processes their KYC, accepts the wire, and issues units at that month's NAV. There is no new fund formation, no new documents beyond each subscriber's agreement — the fund is already live, so adding capital is routine. The hard part is not the mechanics; it is generating the demand to fill each close.

This is where the bridges pay their final dividend. After the first close, every month that the Meridian Fund trades adds another month to its *own* audited track. The fund-of-funds that wanted "a year of live audited performance in your own vehicle" can now watch that year accumulate. The allocators who were "watching" have a concrete reason to convert: the track is real, growing, and the anchor is already in. Each successful close also makes the next one easier — capital begets capital, because every new credible name reduces the "who else is in?" friction for the next prospect. Figure 6 shows the compounding.

![A stacked bar chart titled the rolling close. Five bars show successive closes. The first close adds 40 million dollars. The second adds 15 million for a running total of 55 million. The third adds 23 million for 78 million. The fourth adds 32 million and crosses the 100 million dollar viability line at 110 million. The fifth adds 40 million for a running total of 150 million dollars over a year.](/imgs/blogs/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close-6.png)

Notice the *acceleration* in Figure 6: the increments grow (`\$15M`, then `\$23M`, then `\$32M`, then `\$40M`) even though the strategy is unchanged. That is the flywheel — a longer audited track, a larger AUM, and more credible LPs already in the fund each make the next close easier to fill. By the fourth close the fund crosses the `\$100M` institutional-viability floor; by the end of year one it is at `\$150M`, finally above break-even and into the range where larger, slower institutional allocators (pensions, endowments) will even take a meeting.

The rolling close also imposes a discipline. Because new investors come in at the current NAV, you must be scrupulous about the [high-water mark and fee mechanics](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark) so that early and late investors are treated fairly — a new investor should not pay a performance fee on gains that accrued before they subscribed. This is why fund-structure accounting (per-series or equalization) exists, and why the administrator's role is load-bearing. The rolling close is the engine of growth, but it only works if the NAV is clean, the fees are fair, and the audited track keeps accumulating without a blow-up.

#### Worked example: the flywheel from `\$40M` to `\$150M`

Maya's first year of rolling closes, tied to Figure 6:

- **Month 0 (first close):** `\$40M`. Anchor `\$25M` + `\$15M`. Below break-even.
- **Month 3:** the audited track now has three live months in the fund itself. Two prospects who were watching convert: `+\$15M` → `\$55M`.
- **Month 6:** six months of live, in-fund performance plus a clean audit trail. A fund-of-funds writes a ticket: `+\$23M` → `\$78M`.
- **Month 9:** a small endowment, gated by a consultant who needed to see the operation, allocates: `+\$32M` → `\$110M`. The fund crosses `\$100M` — now SEC RIA registration territory, and a credible institutional size.
- **Month 12:** with a one-year audited in-fund track and `\$100M`+, a pension's consultant clears the fund; `+\$40M` → `\$150M`.

At `\$150M`, the blended management fee (roughly **1.7%** across the founders and standard classes) generates about `\$2.5M` a year — comfortably above the `\$1M` cost base. The Meridian Fund is now a *business*, not a bet. And none of it happened without the bridge: the two-year audited SMA track that let Maya make the first credible call, the anchor that broke the standoff, and the first close that started the flywheel turning.

*The first close is the hardest dollar; the rolling close is where the audited track and the anchor compound the rest — each close easier than the last, until the fund clears break-even and institutions engage.*

## Common misconceptions

Founders carry a set of beliefs about track records and raising that are not just wrong but actively dangerous, because each one leads to a launch that allocators will not fund. Here are the six that cost the most.

**"My backtest is a track record."** A backtest is a simulation. It has none of the four properties that make a record fundable: it is not *live* (no real decisions under real constraints), not *audited* (no independent attestation), not *third-party-verified* (you computed it), and it is trivially *overfit* (you tuned the strategy on the same data). Allocators have seen ten thousand beautiful backtests; they fund the live, audited track behind one. A backtest is the *start* of building an edge — see [the honest test for an edge](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test) — not the proof of one.

**"I can claim my old desk's P&L freely."** You cannot. The returns were earned with the prior firm's capital and resources, often by a team, and you need the firm's written consent and honest, decision-specific attribution to present any of it. Claiming the whole desk's or firm's P&L as your own is misleading under the Marketing Rule and can expose you to legal action from the former employer. Port the sleeve you demonstrably ran, with consent and caveats — and only as a supplement to a real live track.

**"I just need good returns and the money will come."** Good returns are necessary and nowhere near sufficient. A great-returning fund with a self-priced NAV, no auditor, and no administrator is *unfundable* — it will fail operational due diligence before anyone looks at the Sharpe. Credibility infrastructure (audit, admin, GIPS) is not a tax on good returns; it is what makes good returns *legible* to a fiduciary allocator.

**"I'll raise the whole fund at once."** First-time funds almost never do a single big close. You set a minimum viable first close, close the investors you have, start trading, and grow through the rolling close as your in-fund audited track accumulates. Waiting for "everyone" before launching usually means launching never — the soft circles evaporate while you wait.

**"An SMA isn't a real track because it's not a fund."** Some allocators prefer fund-structure P&L, but a two-year audited, fee-bearing, third-party-administered SMA *is* a track record of your decisions with outside money. It is a perfectly fundable bridge — and for many first-timers the best one, because it is cheap and clean. The "it's not a fund" objection is addressed by either converting the SMA into the fund or, better, by incubating the actual fund from the start.

**"The anchor is just early capital."** The anchor's dollars matter less than the anchor's *signal*. A credible anchor going first breaks the coordination standoff that keeps every other allocator on the sidelines. That is why founders pay for it with a founders-class fee discount — the discount buys the signal, and the signal is worth multiples of the forgone fee.

## How it plays out in the real world

The principles above are enforced, daily, by how real allocators behave and by the cautionary tales of funds that skipped the steps. A few concrete realities.

**The audited track is the literal first ask.** Walk into any allocator meeting as a first-time manager and the diligence request list — the [due-diligence questionnaire and data room](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-pitch-book-ddq-and-the-data-room) — leads with "audited financial statements for all available periods" and "a GIPS-compliant performance presentation." There is no path around this. Funds-of-funds, pensions, endowments, and most family offices will not proceed without it. The bridges exist precisely because this ask is non-negotiable and a brand-new fund cannot satisfy it on day one.

**Madoff is why the controls are not optional.** Bernie Madoff's fund reported smooth, positive returns for years and attracted tens of billions of dollars — `\$65B` of fabricated statement value, roughly `\$17.5B` of actual principal lost when it collapsed in December 2008. The fund had *no independent administrator*, a tiny unknown auditor, and self-custody. Every control the audited track imposes — independent administration, independent audit, independent custody — is a direct response to how Madoff fabricated returns. When an allocator insists your NAV be struck by a real administrator and audited by a recognized firm, they are not doubting you specifically; they are refusing to be the next victim of the absence of those controls.

**Net-of-fee is the law now.** Before the SEC Marketing Rule's 2022 compliance date, managers had more latitude to lead with gross returns. Now, any performance advertising must show net-of-fee results, and the rules around hypothetical and extracted performance are explicit. A first-time manager who shows only gross, or who ports a prior track without the proper attribution and net presentation, is not just unpersuasive — they are out of compliance. Build the track net from day one.

**Most launches are small, and most below `\$100M` struggle to raise.** The honest data: the median hedge-fund launch is in the tens of millions of AUM, not hundreds; funds managing over `\$1B` control the large majority — roughly **90%** — of industry assets; and many institutional allocators and their consultants will not look below `\$100M` to `\$250M` of AUM and one to three years of audited track. A `\$40M` first close is *normal* and *below* most institutional minimums — which is exactly why the rolling close, powered by the accumulating audited track, is the whole game. The fund that survives is the one that gets from the first close to `\$100M`+ before the runway expires; a large fraction of launches do not survive three to five years, and running out of runway before crossing break-even is a leading cause.

**Incubating the real fund is the cleanest path when affordable.** The strongest first-timers, especially those who left a platform with their own capital, do not run an SMA *and* a separate fund. They incubate the actual fund — the same legal entity they will scale — with their own and friends-and-family money, run it for one to two years, accumulate a continuous audited track *in the fund itself*, and then do a larger anchored close onto a vehicle that already has a real history. It costs the most per dollar early, but it sidesteps every porting and "is an SMA a fund?" argument, and it presents the allocator with the ideal object: a live fund with a continuous, audited, multi-year track and an anchor already committed.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you decide to leave a seat where the firm's track record is not yours and build one that is. The sequence is not optional and it cannot be rushed: pick a bridge (SMA, incubator, or — as a supplement — a ported record), run live capital for one to two years, get it audited and GIPS-presentable, line up a credible anchor, do a minimum-viable first close, and grow through the rolling close as your own in-fund track accumulates. Every step exists because allocators fund verifiable evidence, not assertions — and the founder's job is to manufacture that evidence on the cheapest credible bridge, then compound onto it.

The deeper point connects to the spine of this whole series: a hedge fund is a business that sells an aligned promise, and the track record is how the founder makes that promise *credible* before anyone has watched it be kept. The structure (covered earlier) protects the investor; the audited track proves the skill; the anchor and the first close convert proof into capital. Get the track wrong and the soundest structure in the world has nothing fundable inside it.
