> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — To raise money you have to know your buyer, and the buyers are not interchangeable. Each allocator type — funds-of-funds, pensions, endowments, family offices, RIAs, sovereign wealth funds — has a different ticket size, time horizon, risk appetite, and gatekeeper, and most of the raising battle is matching your fund's *stage* to an allocator who can actually write the check you need.
>
> - The allocators sort into three buckets: **institutions** that own the assets (pensions, endowments, sovereign funds, insurers), **intermediaries** that pool other people's money (funds-of-funds, RIAs, private banks), and **private wealth** (family offices, accredited and qualified individuals).
> - Ticket size scales with the allocator: a family office might write `\$1M`, a fund-of-funds `\$5M`–`\$50M`, a pension `\$50M`–`\$300M`, a sovereign fund `\$100M` to over `\$1B`. A pension's *minimum* check can be larger than a small fund's *entire AUM*.
> - **Investment consultants are the gatekeepers** for most institutional money. They run a buy list, and a fund below their AUM and track-record floor never reaches the committee, no matter how good the returns.
> - The single number to remember: most institutions will not look below roughly **`\$250M`–`\$500M` AUM and a 1–3 year audited record**, so a `\$40M` first fund's realistic buyers are seeders, family offices, and early-stage funds-of-funds — not pensions.

Maya has a beautiful pitch. Her long/short equity strategy returned a strong, clean number in two years of running her own capital, her drawdowns were shallow, and she has a real, defensible reason her edge exists. She is launching the Meridian Fund with `\$50M` of capacity she wants to fill, and she has just landed a meeting with a `\$30B` public pension's hedge fund team. She walks in, presents flawlessly, and the room is warm. Then the head of the program says, gently: "This is excellent. But our minimum allocation is `\$100M`, and our policy is not to be more than 10% of any manager's assets. So you'd need to be at least a `\$1B` fund before we could even start. Come back when you're there."

Maya leaves confused. She came to sell `\$50M` of capacity and was told the smallest check the buyer can write is twice her entire fund. Nothing was wrong with her strategy. She was pitching the wrong buyer.

This is the most common and most expensive mistake a founder makes in raising: treating "allocators" as one undifferentiated pool of money and pitching whoever will take a meeting. The reality is that the people who fund hedge funds form a structured landscape — call it the LP landscape — and each type sits in a different place along three axes: how big a check they write, how long they hold, and who decides. Figure 1 is the map; the rest of this post is how to read it and use it.

Knowing this map is most of the raising battle, because it tells you which doors are worth knocking on at your size — and which are a polite waste of a meeting. Before we get to the matching, we have to define the players. This post leans on the mechanics covered in [the capital-raising reality](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-capital-raising-reality-chicken-and-egg) (the chicken-and-egg problem of raising) and the documents you'll need from [the pitch book, DDQ, and data room](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-pitch-book-ddq-and-the-data-room); here we focus purely on *who* the buyers are.

## Foundations: who the buyers are

Every term you need to read the LP landscape, defined from zero. If you already run money but have never raised it, this is the vocabulary the people on the other side of the table use.

**LP (limited partner).** The investor in a fund. In a US fund organized as a Delaware limited partnership, the manager is the *general partner* (GP) and the investors are *limited partners* — their liability is limited to the capital they commit, and they have no role in running the fund. "LP" is used loosely across the industry to mean "the investor," even when the legal structure is different. When someone says "I'm out raising from LPs," they mean the allocators.

**Allocator.** The person or institution that decides how to deploy a pool of capital across managers and asset classes. A pension's chief investment officer is an allocator; so is the analyst at a fund-of-funds who recommends which managers make the buy list. "Allocator" and "LP" are often used interchangeably, but allocator emphasizes the *decision* — these are the people you have to persuade.

**Ticket size.** The dollar amount of a single allocation. A `\$25M` ticket is one allocator putting `\$25M` into your fund. Ticket sizes vary enormously by allocator type, and — crucially — many allocators have a *minimum* ticket below which they cannot or will not invest, because a check too small to matter isn't worth the diligence cost.

**Mandate.** The set of rules an allocator must follow: what asset classes they can invest in, what risk they can take, what minimum size and liquidity a manager must offer, what diversification limits apply. A pension's mandate might forbid being more than 10% of a manager's AUM or investing in a fund younger than three years. Mandates are why a warm, willing allocator still can't say yes — the rules won't let them.

**Fund-of-funds (FoF).** An intermediary that raises money from its own investors and allocates it across a portfolio of hedge funds. It charges its investors a fee for the selection and diligence work, on top of the underlying funds' fees (the "fee-on-fee" problem). FoFs are important to founders because they are often the most willing to back an early-stage manager — selecting emerging talent is their product.

**Endowment.** The permanent investment pool of a university, hospital, or museum, spending a few percent a year to fund operations and preserving the rest forever. Endowments — especially the large university ones — pioneered heavy allocations to hedge funds and private assets. They have very long horizons and sophisticated internal teams.

**Foundation.** A charitable institution's investment pool, similar to an endowment but funding grants rather than an operating budget. Endowments and foundations are usually grouped together as "E&F."

**Pension.** A pool that funds retirement benefits — *public* (government employees) or *corporate* (a company's plan). Pensions are among the largest sources of institutional capital, but they are slow, heavily governed, and almost always advised by an investment consultant. A *defined-benefit* plan promises a fixed payout and so must hit a return target; that target is why pensions allocate to hedge funds at all.

**Family office.** The entity that manages a wealthy family's money. A *single-family office* serves one family; a *multi-family office* serves several. Family offices are the most flexible allocators — fast decisions, relationship-driven, willing to back a manager they trust — but they vary wildly in size and sophistication.

**Sovereign wealth fund (SWF).** A state-owned investment fund, often built from commodity revenue or trade surpluses. SWFs write the largest tickets of any allocator and have the longest horizons, but they want *capacity* — the ability to put a lot of money to work — which a small fund cannot offer.

**RIA (registered investment adviser) and private bank.** Intermediaries that manage money for individual clients. An RIA advises high-net-worth individuals and may allocate a slice of client portfolios to hedge funds; a private bank does the same for its wealthy clients, often through a curated platform. They aggregate many small clients into meaningful tickets but run their own due-diligence desk.

**Insurer.** An insurance company investing its premium float. Insurers are large, slow, and mandate-bound, with strict rules on liquidity and credit quality driven by regulation and their own liabilities.

**Investment consultant.** The advisory firm a pension, endowment, or foundation hires to recommend managers and set policy. The consultant maintains a "buy list" or "approved list" of funds it has vetted, and the institution typically allocates only to funds on that list. This makes consultants the **gatekeepers** of most institutional capital. Big names include Cambridge Associates, Aon, Mercer, Albourne, and Wilshire. If a consultant has not approved you, the institution's committee usually never sees your pitch.

**AUM (assets under management).** The total capital your fund manages. It is the single most-watched number in raising, because nearly every gate is keyed to it: minimum tickets, consultant floors, concentration caps, and the break-even math that decides whether the fund can survive. When an allocator asks "how big are you?" they are really asking how durable, how institutional, and how investable you are.

**Track record.** The audited history of your fund's returns. Length matters as much as level: a one-year record, however strong, is statistically thin and reads as luck to a careful allocator, while a three-year audited record through different market conditions reads as repeatable skill. Most institutions want at least 1–3 years, and the cleaner and longer the record, the more doors open.

**Lock-up and redemption terms.** The rules governing when an LP can get its money back — the lock-up period before any redemption is allowed, the redemption frequency (monthly, quarterly, annually) afterward, and the notice period required. These terms are a negotiation point with every allocator, because they trade the manager's need for stable capital against the LP's need for liquidity, and different allocators weigh that trade-off very differently.

**Operational due diligence (ODD).** A separate, veto-wielding review of a fund's *operations* — not its returns. ODD checks whether your administrator is independent, whether you control cash movements properly, whether your valuation policy is sound, whether you have the right service-provider stack. A fund with great returns can be rejected on ODD alone. We cover it in depth in [operational due diligence, the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto); here it matters as one of the gates institutional money passes through.

With the vocabulary in place, the rest of the landscape comes into focus.

## The allocator taxonomy

The cleanest way to hold the landscape in your head is to group every allocator by *whose money it is* — because that determines how the allocator behaves. There are three buckets. Figure 2 lays them out.

![A tree diagram grouping allocators into institutions, intermediaries, and private wealth, with each bucket branching into its specific allocator types](/imgs/blogs/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates-2.png)

**Institutions** own the assets directly. A pension's money belongs to its beneficiaries; an endowment's belongs to the institution in perpetuity; a sovereign fund's belongs to the state. These allocators are large, slow, governed by committees and policies, and almost always advised — they answer to a board, a set of beneficiaries, and a regulator. They write big checks but move at the speed of governance. Pensions, endowments, foundations, sovereign wealth funds, and insurers live here.

**Intermediaries** pool other people's money and allocate it for them, charging a fee for the service. A fund-of-funds raises from its own LPs and picks underlying managers; an RIA aggregates individual clients; a private bank curates a platform for its wealthy customers. Because their *product is selection*, intermediaries are often more willing to take a chance on a new or small manager than a direct institution would be — finding tomorrow's winners early is how they justify their fee. They answer to their own investors.

**Private wealth** is individuals and families investing their own money. Family offices, plus accredited investors and qualified purchasers investing directly, live here. They are the most flexible and the fastest — a principal can decide over dinner — but the most idiosyncratic, because each one has its own preferences, biases, and tolerance.

The reason this grouping is load-bearing: **the bucket predicts the behavior.** An institution will be slow, consultant-gated, and mandate-bound; an intermediary will be selection-driven and willing to go early; private wealth will be fast, relationship-driven, and unpredictable. When you know which bucket you're pitching, you know what to expect before you walk in.

It also helps to step back and ask *why any of these allocate to hedge funds at all*, because the answer shapes what they want from you. A pension with a return target it must hit to fund its liabilities uses hedge funds for *uncorrelated return* — a stream that doesn't move with its big equity and bond books, smoothing the ride toward the target. An endowment uses them for *return per unit of risk* and access to edge it can't get in public markets. A family office may use them for *capital preservation with upside* — a place to compound wealth without taking pure equity beta. None of these buyers wants "high returns" in the abstract; each wants a *specific role* filled in a larger portfolio. The founder who understands the role the allocator is trying to fill can position the fund as the answer to that need rather than as a generic bet on skill. A long/short equity fund pitched as "uncorrelated, low-beta return that complements your equity book" speaks to the pension's actual problem; the same fund pitched as "I'm a great stock-picker" does not.

Notice what the taxonomy does *not* tell you, though: it doesn't tell you which of these can actually write a check at *your* size today. For that we need the next two axes — what each wants, and how big a ticket each writes.

## What each allocator wants

Every allocator is solving its own problem, and your fund is only interesting insofar as it solves that problem. Pitch the same fund to a fund-of-funds and a pension and you are selling two different products, even though it's the same strategy in the same vehicle.

**Funds-of-funds want a differentiated edge they can package — and founder-friendly terms.** An FoF's product is a curated portfolio of managers, so it is hunting for *something its other managers don't already give it*: a distinct strategy, a new market, an edge it can explain to its own investors. FoFs are among the most willing to back an emerging manager precisely because backing tomorrow's stars early is their value proposition. In exchange, they negotiate hard — fee discounts, a founders-class share, capacity rights, sometimes a most-favored-nation clause guaranteeing they get any better terms you later offer.

**Pensions want size and a track record above all.** A public pension is governed by fiduciaries who must defend every decision to a board and, ultimately, to the public. Their first questions are not about your edge; they are about your *durability*: How much do you manage? How long is your audited record? Are your operations institutional? Can we put a meaningful amount to work without becoming too large a share of your fund? They want to minimize the chance of a headline that says "pension lost retirees' money on an unproven manager." Size and a clean multi-year record are how you de-risk that headline.

**Endowments and foundations want sophistication and access.** The large endowment teams are among the most sophisticated allocators alive; they can evaluate a complex strategy on its merits and have very long horizons. What they prize is *access* — the ability to invest with managers others can't reach — and genuine intellectual edge. They are more willing than a pension to back something unusual, but they have small teams and can only track so many relationships.

**Family offices want relationship and trust.** A family office is investing a real person's wealth, often the wealth of someone who built a business and understands risk viscerally. They back people they trust and strategies they understand, and they decide fast. The flip side: a family office's loyalty is personal, and a single bad year — or a single bad interaction — can end the relationship.

**RIAs and private banks want clean operations and liquidity.** Because they answer to many individual clients who may need their money, RIAs and private banks weight liquidity heavily and run their own due-diligence desks. They want a fund that fits cleanly on a platform: solid operations, reasonable lock-ups, clear reporting. They are not chasing the most exotic edge; they want something defensible they can put many clients into.

**Sovereign wealth funds want capacity at scale.** An SWF has so much money that a fund it can only put `\$25M` into isn't worth the relationship. They want managers who can absorb `\$100M`, `\$500M`, sometimes more, and run it for years. For a small fund, an SWF is almost never a day-one buyer.

**Insurers want predictable liquidity and credit-quality fit.** An insurer is investing premium float against future claims, under regulatory capital rules that penalize illiquid and volatile holdings. So an insurer's first questions are about how your fund affects *its* balance sheet: how liquid is the position, how is it valued, how does it score under the insurer's capital framework. Insurers are slow, mandate-bound, and conservative — they're a late-stage allocator, not early money, and they care more about the fund fitting cleanly into their regulatory and liability picture than about squeezing out the last basis point of alpha.

The deeper point is that **each allocator type has a different definition of "risk."** To a pension fiduciary, the dominant risk is *career and headline risk* — the chance of having to explain to a board why retirees' money went to an unproven manager who then blew up. To a fund-of-funds, the risk is *selection risk* — picking a manager whose edge decays or who turns out to be a closet-index in disguise — balanced against the upside of catching a star early. To a family office, the risk is *trust risk* — backing a person who turns out not to be who they seemed. To an insurer, the risk is *balance-sheet and regulatory risk*. When you pitch, you are not really selling returns; you are selling *reassurance against that allocator's specific fear*. The founder who understands which fear is in the room tailors the pitch to it: to the pension, lead with operational soundness and the depth of your record; to the FoF, lead with what makes your edge durable and different; to the family office, lead with alignment and how much of your own money is in the fund.

This is also why the *same* feature reads differently to different buyers. A one-year hard lock-up looks like a feature to a pension (sticky capital, no redemption surprises) and a bug to an RIA (whose clients may need liquidity). A concentrated, high-conviction book looks like edge to a sophisticated endowment and like uncontrolled risk to a conservative insurer. There is no universally "good" set of terms — there is only fit between your terms and a specific allocator's mandate and fear.

The pattern: institutions want to minimize regret (size, record, ops), intermediaries want to find edge they can package, and private wealth wants trust. Sell each one the thing it is actually buying.

#### Worked example: the same fund, two different asks

Maya's Meridian Fund is the same strategy whether she pitches a fund-of-funds or a pension. But the *ask* is completely different, and confusing them costs her meetings.

To a fund-of-funds, the ask is: *"Back me early. I have a differentiated long/short edge in an under-covered corner of the market, my two-year record is clean, and I'll offer you a founders-class fee of 1.5 and 15 plus capacity rights."* The FoF can write `\$10M` today against Meridian's current `\$40M`, because backing emerging managers is its business.

To a pension, the same fund requires a different ask: *"Here is a `\$100M`+ allocation opportunity in a proven, institutional-grade strategy with a three-year audited record and a full service-provider stack."* But Meridian is `\$40M` with a two-year record — it cannot honestly make that pitch yet. The pension's minimum check is `\$100M` and its mandate caps it at 10% of any manager, so it needs Meridian to be a `\$1B` fund. The right move is not to pitch the pension now; it's to put the pension on a multi-year list and pitch the FoF today.

*Same strategy, same vehicle — but the buyer determines the story, the ticket, and whether "yes" is even possible.*

## Ticket sizes and minimums

The axis that ends more conversations than any other is dollars. Each allocator type writes checks in a characteristic range, and — just as important — many have a *minimum* below which they cannot engage. Figure 3 shows the typical bands.

![A bar chart on a log scale showing typical ticket-size ranges by allocator type, from a family office at one million dollars to a sovereign fund above one billion](/imgs/blogs/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates-3.png)

Reading from smallest to largest, with illustrative ranges (these vary widely by allocator and year):

- **RIAs and private banks:** `\$1M`–`\$25M`, often aggregated from many small clients. The minimum to even appear on their platform may itself be a hurdle.
- **Family offices:** `\$1M`–`\$50M`. Enormously variable — a small single-family office might write `\$1M`; a large one writes tens of millions.
- **Funds-of-funds:** `\$5M`–`\$50M`. The early-stage friendly check. An FoF can take a meaningful early position without needing you to be large.
- **Endowments and foundations:** `\$25M`–`\$150M`. They want a position big enough to matter to their portfolio but are constrained by their own size and team bandwidth.
- **Public and corporate pensions:** `\$50M`–`\$300M`. Big checks, but with big minimums — frequently `\$50M`–`\$100M` floors — and concentration caps that require you to already be large.
- **Sovereign wealth funds:** `\$100M` to over `\$1B`. The largest checks, but they need the capacity to deploy them.

The brutal asymmetry hiding in this chart is that **a large allocator's minimum check can exceed a small fund's entire AUM.** A pension that can't write less than `\$100M` literally cannot invest in a `\$40M` fund — there isn't enough fund to hold the check. This is why "go where the big money is" is exactly backwards for a small fund: the big money's minimums are the problem.

There's also a self-imposed floor on the manager's side, set by regulation and by terms. Most hedge funds require a per-investor *minimum investment* of `\$1M` or more, partly to keep investors at the *accredited investor* or *qualified purchaser* level the exemptions require — covered in [registration and regulation](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d). An accredited investor needs income over `\$200,000` (or `\$300,000` joint) or net worth over `\$1M` excluding their home; a qualified purchaser needs at least `\$5M` in investments. So even the smallest LP in your fund is, by law, wealthy.

There's a subtler reason minimums exist on the *allocator's* side, and it's worth understanding because it explains so much of the landscape's behavior. Every allocation carries a fixed cost: the diligence, the legal review, the ongoing monitoring, the line on a committee report. That cost is roughly the same whether the check is `\$5M` or `\$50M`. So an allocator with a `\$50M` minimum isn't being arbitrary — it's saying that below `\$50M`, the diligence cost isn't worth the impact on its portfolio. A pension managing `\$30B` would need to make six hundred separate `\$50M` allocations to deploy its whole book; it physically cannot diligence and monitor thousands of tiny positions, so it sets a floor that keeps its manager count manageable. The bigger the allocator, the bigger the minimum, for the simple reason that a giant pool can only track so many relationships. This is the structural force behind Figure 3's staircase: ticket sizes scale with allocator size not because big allocators are greedy, but because they're capacity-constrained on *attention*, not just dollars.

That same fixed-cost logic explains why the gap between a `\$40M` fund and the institutions feels uncrossable. It isn't that institutions dislike small funds; it's that the math of their own scale forbids it. A `\$100M` minimum into a `\$40M` fund is impossible, and a `\$25M` minimum into a fund where you'd then be 60% of the assets violates every concentration rule the institution has. The institution's size, which makes it a tempting target, is the very thing that locks you out until you grow.

#### Worked example: a `\$20M` FoF ticket versus a `\$100M` pension ticket, and the AUM each requires

Suppose two allocators love Meridian equally. A fund-of-funds offers `\$20M`; a pension offers `\$100M`. The pension's check is five times larger — surely take the pension?

Look at what each requires of Meridian's AUM. The pension's mandate caps it at 10% of any manager's assets, so a `\$100M` ticket requires Meridian to be at least a `\$1B` fund. At `\$40M` today, that's a non-starter — the check is mathematically impossible regardless of how much the pension likes the strategy. The FoF's `\$20M`, by contrast, takes Meridian from `\$40M` to `\$60M`, a 50% jump, and the FoF is comfortable being a third of a small fund because backing emerging managers is its product.

So the "smaller" `\$20M` ticket is the one Maya can actually get, and it does more for her business — it's the difference between a `\$40M` fund and a `\$60M` fund, a step toward the `\$80M`–`\$100M` viability threshold from [the high-water mark and fee math](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark). The `\$100M` pension ticket is real money that is simply unreachable at her stage.

*The bigger check is worthless if the allocator's own rules make it impossible to write; the right ticket is the largest one a willing allocator can actually deploy at your current size.*

## The consultants: the gatekeepers

For most institutional money, you do not pitch the institution. You pitch its consultant — and if the consultant doesn't approve you, the committee never hears your name.

Pensions, endowments, and foundations rarely select managers unaided. They hire an investment consultant — Cambridge Associates, Aon, Mercer, Albourne, Wilshire, and others — to set asset-allocation policy and, critically, to *vet and recommend managers*. The consultant maintains a **buy list** (also called an approved or recommended list): the set of funds it has researched, met, diligenced, and decided are institutional-quality. The institution's committee then allocates almost exclusively to funds on that list. The consultant is, in effect, a permission layer between you and the capital.

This changes the raising problem completely. Getting an institutional allocation is a *two-step* sale: first you must get the consultant to add you to its buy list, then the consultant has to recommend you to a specific client whose mandate fits. Both steps have their own gates, and the first one — getting researched at all — is where small funds die, because consultants prioritize their research time on managers their clients can actually invest in, which means managers above a meaningful AUM and track-record floor.

Consultants screen on the same durability signals pensions care about: AUM (commonly they won't spend research time below roughly `\$250M`–`\$500M`), length of audited track record (1–3 years minimum, often more), and institutional operations (an independent administrator, a real auditor, clean ODD). A fund that fails the screen is not "rejected" so much as *never considered* — it simply doesn't make it into the research pipeline.

It helps to understand the consultant's own business model, because it explains every behavior you'll encounter. A consultant is paid by the institution — usually a retainer or a fee based on assets advised — to make recommendations the institution can defend. The consultant's product is *defensibility*: when a pension's allocation underperforms, the board's first question is "did we follow our process?" and the consultant's research file is the answer. This means the consultant is structurally conservative. Recommending an established `\$2B` manager who then has a bad year is survivable — "we followed a rigorous process and the manager was well-regarded." Recommending a `\$40M` first-time manager who blows up is a reputational disaster — "why did we put retirees' money with an untested fund?" The asymmetry is enormous: the consultant captures little upside from finding the next star early but bears the full downside if an early bet goes wrong. Rationally, the consultant waits until a manager is proven, large, and well-staffed before risking its name on a recommendation. The emerging-manager problem is baked into the consultant's incentives, not a bias you can charm away.

There are exceptions worth knowing. Some institutions run dedicated *emerging-manager programs* — carve-outs explicitly designed to seed smaller, newer, or diverse-owned managers, sometimes with a lower AUM floor and a mandate to take day-one or early-stage risk. A handful of consultants specialize in or staff these programs. If your fund qualifies, an emerging-manager program can be a genuine institutional door at a stage when the main institutional channel is firmly shut. But these programs are a small slice of total institutional capital, they're competitive, and they still demand institutional-grade operations — the relaxation is on AUM and track length, not on whether your administrator is independent or your cash controls are real.

The flow matters, so Figure 4 traces how a dollar of pension money actually reaches your fund.

![A pipeline showing institutional capital flowing through a consultant screen, shortlist, investment due diligence, operational due diligence, an investment committee vote, legal close, and finally funding](/imgs/blogs/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates-4.png)

Each stage is a filter. The consultant screen knocks out anyone below the AUM and record floor. Investment due diligence interrogates the strategy and returns. Operational due diligence — a separate veto we cover in [operational due diligence, the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto) — can kill a great-returns fund over a cash-control gap. The committee votes. Legal negotiates the subscription documents and any side letter. Only then does capital wire. From first consultant meeting to funded allocation can take a year or more.

The strategic implication for a founder: **if your fund is too small for the consultants, institutional money is not on the table this year, no matter how good your returns are.** Chasing pensions before you've cleared the consultant floor is chasing a door that is locked from the inside. The money flowing through this gate is mostly institutional, as Figure 6 shows.

![A horizontal bar chart showing hedge fund capital by source, with pensions and funds-of-funds together supplying roughly half and private wealth a smaller slice](/imgs/blogs/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates-6.png)

Because institutions and intermediaries dominate the capital base, the consultant-gated channel is where the *bulk* of the industry's money lives. But that channel is closed to a sub-scale fund — which is exactly why the early-stage allocators, who don't use consultants, are the ones a new fund must court first.

#### Worked example: the consultant gate, or why a `\$40M` fund never reaches the committee

Maya gets introduced to a consultant whose clients include several pensions she'd love to reach. The meeting goes well. Then nothing happens. Why?

The consultant's research team covers a few hundred managers, and its clients' mandates almost universally require at least `\$250M` AUM and a three-year record before allocating. Meridian is `\$40M` with two years. Spending a senior analyst's weeks diligencing a fund that *no current client could invest in* is, for the consultant, a waste — there's no path to a recommendation. So Meridian is logged as "emerging, revisit later" and shelved. It never enters formal research, never reaches a buy list, and so the pension committees that trust this consultant never see Maya's name.

Nothing was wrong with the fund. The gate isn't a judgment on the strategy; it's a triage of the consultant's scarce research time toward managers their clients can actually buy. Maya's mistake was spending a quarter courting consultants instead of the allocators who don't use them.

*A consultant's buy list is a research-time triage, not a quality verdict — being too small to be worth researching feels identical to being rejected, but the fix is scale, not a better pitch.*

#### Worked example: the two-step sale, and why institutional money is a multi-year project

Say Meridian has grown to `\$300M` with a three-year audited record — finally above the consultant floor. Now the institutional channel opens, but it opens *slowly*, and the dollar timeline is worth walking through.

Step one is getting researched. A consultant assigns an analyst, who spends weeks or months on the strategy review, an on-site visit, an ODD pass, and a written research report. If Meridian clears it, it lands on the buy list — but a buy list is a menu, not an order. Step two is the consultant matching Meridian to a *specific* client whose mandate fits and who happens to be allocating to long/short equity *right now*. A `\$10B` pension might rebalance into hedge funds once a year; if Meridian gets onto the list in month three but the pension's next allocation window is month fourteen, the `\$100M` check is eleven months away even after approval. Then the committee has to vote, legal has to negotiate the subscription documents and any side letter, and only then does capital wire.

Add it up: from the first consultant meeting to a funded `\$100M` allocation can easily be 12–24 months, even when everything goes right. The lesson for sequencing is stark — institutional money is not something you raise *this year*; it's a pipeline you have to start building a year or two before you need it. The founders who land big institutional checks at `\$300M` are usually the ones who started the consultant relationships at `\$100M`, planting them so the research was already underway when they crossed the floor.

*Institutional capital arrives on a 12-to-24-month lag, so the consultant relationships you need at scale must be seeded long before you qualify — by the time you're "ready," it's too late to start.*

## Matching your stage to the right LP

Now the payoff. The whole point of the landscape is that **your fund's stage dictates which allocators are reachable**, and matching stage to allocator is most of the raising battle. Pitch above your stage and you collect polite no's; pitch at your stage and you actually fill capacity.

A rough stage-to-allocator map:

- **Pre-launch / day one (no fund yet, or `\$0`–`\$25M`):** your buyers are *seeders* and *acceleration capital* (covered in [seed and acceleration capital](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/seed-and-acceleration-capital)), your own network, and a few high-conviction family offices. Seeders give you day-one AUM in exchange for a slice of your management-company economics. Nobody institutional is reachable.
- **Early stage (`\$25M`–`\$100M`, 1–2 year record):** add *early-stage funds-of-funds* (the ones that specialize in emerging managers), more family offices, and the most aggressive RIAs. You're still below the consultant floor, so direct institutional money is largely out.
- **Institutional threshold (`\$100M`–`\$250M`, 2–3 year audited record, full ops):** you can start getting *researched* by consultants and pitching the smaller endowments and the more flexible pensions. This is the grind to get onto buy lists.
- **Scale (`\$250M`–`\$1B`+, 3+ year record):** the full institutional landscape opens — large pensions, big endowments, and eventually sovereign wealth funds. The minimums that were impossible at `\$40M` are now writable.

![A grid contrasting allocators who take early-stage funds under fifty million against those who require scale above two hundred fifty million](/imgs/blogs/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates-5.png)

The mistake Maya made at the start — pitching a `\$30B` pension with a `\$50M` fund — is the universal first-timer error. The fund was good; the *match* was impossible. The discipline is to build a target list ordered by reachability: who can write a check at your size *today*, who is a 12-month target as you grow, and who is a multi-year aspiration you should warm up but not chase.

There's a second dimension to matching beyond pure stage: *strategy fit and liquidity fit*. An allocator doesn't just ask "is this fund big enough?" — it asks "does this strategy fit a hole in my portfolio, and does its liquidity match what I need?" A long/short equity fund competes for a different slot than a global-macro fund or a credit fund; an allocator already heavy in equity long/short may pass on Meridian not because it's too small but because it doesn't need *more* of that exposure. Liquidity matters just as much: an RIA serving clients who might redeem won't put them in a fund with a two-year hard lock, however good the returns, while a pension with a thirty-year horizon happily accepts illiquidity in exchange for stickier terms. So the target list isn't one-dimensional. The right buyer is the intersection of *can write a check at my size*, *needs my kind of strategy right now*, and *can live with my liquidity terms* — and at any given moment that intersection is a much shorter list than the full allocator universe.

A practical way to run this is to keep a living spreadsheet of named allocators, each tagged with its type, its likely ticket range, its stage requirement, its strategy appetite, and the relationship status (cold, warm, in diligence, committed). Sort it by reachability and work top-down. The discipline this enforces is *not wasting your scarcest resource* — your own time and credibility — on doors that are structurally closed. A founder who spends six months chasing three pensions and a sovereign fund has burned half a year for zero capital; the same six months spent on eight family offices and three emerging-manager FoFs might have closed `\$30M`. Raising is a numbers game played against a structured field, and the founders who win it are the ones who play the reachable part of the field hard rather than the prestigious part of the field hopelessly.

#### Worked example: matching a `\$40M` fund to its realistic buyers

Maya sits down and builds Meridian's actual target list, instead of a wish list. Meridian is `\$40M`, two-year record, clean ops, founders-class fees on offer.

Reachable today: a *seeder* who already passed (would have been day-one, now a relationship to revisit); three *early-stage funds-of-funds* that back emerging managers and can write `\$5M`–`\$15M` each; a handful of *family offices* in her network, `\$1M`–`\$10M` tickets; one aggressive *RIA* that platforms emerging managers for `\$2M`–`\$5M`. A realistic 12-month raise from this group might be `\$20M`–`\$40M`, roughly doubling the fund.

Twelve-month targets: as Meridian crosses `\$100M`, a couple of *smaller endowments* and one *flexible corporate pension* become approachable — and she should start the consultant relationships *now* so the research is underway when she clears the floor.

Multi-year aspirations: the `\$30B` public pension and any *sovereign wealth fund*. She keeps them warm with periodic updates but spends zero raising energy chasing a check they can't write yet.

*The target list is sorted by reachability, not by desirability — the biggest, most prestigious allocators go last because their minimums and consultant gates make them unreachable until you've already raised from everyone below them.*

## The early-stage allocators

If the institutions are unreachable until you scale, then a new fund lives or dies on the allocators who take day-one and early-stage risk. These are worth knowing in detail, because they are your first capital.

**Seeders and acceleration capital.** A seeder provides day-one AUM — historically `\$25M`–`\$150M`+ — in exchange for a share of your *management-company economics* (often `\$~15`–25% of revenue, sometimes a GP equity stake), usually with a multi-year lock-up and most-favored-nation fee terms. Named historical players include Blackstone's BAAM/Strategic Capital, Goldman's Petershill (GP stakes), Investcorp, and Reservoir. A seeder is expensive — you give up a permanent slice of your business — but it solves the chicken-and-egg problem of needing AUM to attract AUM. Acceleration capital is the later-stage, smaller-give-up cousin. The full economics are in [seed and acceleration capital](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/seed-and-acceleration-capital).

**Early-stage funds-of-funds.** A subset of FoFs explicitly specialize in emerging managers — they run "emerging manager" or "first-loss" programs and *want* the alpha and terms that come from backing talent before the crowd. These are often the single best target for a fund in the `\$25M`–`\$100M` range. They'll write `\$5M`–`\$25M`, they'll go early, and they understand a short track record. The trade is in the terms: expect fee pressure, capacity rights, and sometimes a piece of your economics.

**Family offices.** The most flexible early money. A family office can decide in a week, isn't consultant-gated, and will back a manager on conviction and relationship. The catch is that family offices are idiosyncratic — each has its own preferences and biases — and the relationship is personal, so it can also end personally. They are excellent first capital but rarely large enough to fill a fund alone.

**Friends, family, and your own capital.** The truest day-one money is the founder's own and a small circle who back the *person*. It is real AUM and it signals conviction to later allocators — an LP's first question is often "how much of your own money is in this?" But it is finite and concentrated, which leads directly to the risk that defines small funds.

**Platforms and emerging-manager programs.** A growing category sits between seeding and direct allocation: multi-strategy *platforms* and *emerging-manager programs* that allocate to newer managers, sometimes inside a managed account they control rather than your commingled fund. A platform may give you `\$25M`–`\$100M` to run, but on *its* infrastructure and risk limits, and it can pull the capital fast if you breach them. For a founder this is a trade-off — fast scale and instant credibility against tight constraints and concentration in one source — but it's a real path to early AUM that doesn't require clearing the consultant floor.

A useful mental model for sequencing all of this: early-stage allocators are *betting on you*, while institutions are *buying a proven product*. A seeder, an emerging-manager FoF, or a conviction family office allocates because they believe in the person and the edge before there's a long record to prove it — they're paid for that risk with cheaper terms or a slice of your economics. A pension or an insurer only shows up once the bet has been de-risked into a track record and an institutional operation. You raise from the people betting on you first; you sell a product to the institutions later. Confusing the two — pitching a "proven product" story to an institution before it's true, or asking a conservative institution to "bet on you" — is the structural mismatch that wastes a founder's first year.

#### Worked example: the concentration risk of one LP being 40% of a small fund

A large family office offers Maya `\$16M` into the `\$40M` Meridian Fund — a fantastic, fund-doubling check. But run the arithmetic on what it does to concentration. After the allocation, Meridian is `\$56M` and that single LP is `\$16M`, or roughly **29%** of the fund. If Maya had instead been at `\$24M` and taken the same `\$16M` check, that one LP would be **40%** of the fund.

Now the risk: if that family office redeems — because of a family event, a strategy disagreement, or simply a portfolio rebalance — Maya doesn't lose 40% of her fund's value, she loses 40% of her *capital base* at once. To meet the redemption she may have to sell positions into a bad market, crystallizing losses for the *remaining* LPs and shrinking the fund below its break-even AUM. One LP's exit can trigger a death spiral. This is exactly why allocators ask about concentration in diligence, and why the standard pension mandate caps any single manager at 10% of *its* portfolio — the same logic, viewed from the other side. The principle of not letting one position dominate is the same one in [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up).

*A single LP who is 40% of your fund is a single point of failure for the whole business — diversifying your investor base is risk management, not just fundraising.*

## Common misconceptions

The LP landscape is dense with folklore. Six beliefs that get founders into trouble:

**"If the returns are good enough, the money will find me."** No. The consultant gate, the AUM minimums, and the mandate rules are *structural* — they sit between your returns and the capital regardless of how good the numbers are. A `\$40M` fund with a stellar record is invisible to a consultant whose clients can't invest below `\$250M`. Performance is necessary; it is nowhere near sufficient.

**"Go where the big money is — pitch the pensions and sovereign funds."** Exactly backwards for a small fund. The biggest allocators have the biggest *minimums* and the strictest gates, which makes them the *least* reachable at launch. The big money is the last money you raise, not the first.

**"A bigger ticket is always better."** Not when it comes from one LP. A `\$16M` check that makes a single investor 40% of your fund creates redemption-concentration risk that can sink the business. Allocator diversity is worth more than raw size for a small fund.

**"Funds-of-funds are dead — they're just fee-on-fee."** Reports of the FoF's death are exaggerated, and for a *founder* they remain one of the most important allocator types: the early-stage and emerging-manager FoFs are often the first institutional-flavored capital a new fund can get. Whatever you think of fee-on-fee as an investor, as a manager you want these allocators on your list.

**"The consultant works for me — I just need to impress them."** The consultant works for the *institution*, not for you, and is paid to be skeptical on its client's behalf. Getting onto a buy list is not about charm; it's about clearing an objective floor (AUM, record, ops) so the consultant's research time can plausibly lead to a client recommendation. Below the floor, no amount of impressing helps.

**"Family-office money is easy money."** It's *fast* money, not easy money — and it comes with its own risks. Family offices are idiosyncratic, the relationship is personal, and a single one can be a dangerously large slice of a small fund. Easy to get is not the same as safe to hold.

## How it plays out in the real world

The numbers behind the landscape are well documented, even if they're imprecise. The global hedge fund industry manages roughly `\$4.5`–`\$5.0` trillion as of 2026 (HFR, Preqin, BarclayHedge), across an estimated 8,000–10,000 funds. But the distribution is brutally skewed: funds managing over `\$1B` — the "billion-dollar club" — control roughly **90%** of industry assets, while the *median* fund is far smaller and most managers run under `\$100M`. The capital is concentrated at the top, and the consultant-gated institutional channel is the road to that capital.

That capital is overwhelmingly institutional. Industry investor-base surveys (AIMA, Preqin) consistently show pensions, endowments, foundations, sovereign funds, and insurers — plus the funds-of-funds that pool capital on their behalf — supplying the large majority of hedge fund money, with direct private-wealth capital a smaller slice (Figure 6). For a founder this is a double-edged fact: the money is real and large, but it sits behind the gates a small fund can't yet clear, which is precisely why the early-stage allocators matter so disproportionately at launch.

The institutionalization of hedge fund capital was itself a deliberate shift. Through the 1990s and 2000s, as pensions and endowments piled into hedge funds chasing return targets, they brought their governance with them — consultants, ODD, mandate rules, GIPS-standard track records. That's why a modern launch faces a far higher operational bar than a 1990s launch did: the dominant buyers demand it. The cautionary tales reinforce the point. The Madoff fraud, revealed in 2008, was the canonical *operational* failure — no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, self-custody, suspiciously smooth returns — and it hardened ODD across the industry into the veto it is today. After Madoff, the question "who is your independent administrator?" stopped being a formality and became a gate, which is why a small fund's ops now matter as much to an allocator as its returns.

The "endowment model" deserves its own mention, because it shaped the appetite of an entire generation of allocators. Pioneered most famously at large university endowments in the 1980s and 1990s, the model leaned heavily into illiquid and alternative assets — hedge funds, private equity, venture, real assets — on the theory that a perpetual pool of capital can harvest the illiquidity premium that institutions with shorter horizons can't. It was wildly influential: pensions, foundations, and family offices copied it, which is a large part of *why* hedge funds attracted trillions of institutional dollars in the first place. For a founder, the legacy is double-edged. The endowment model is the reason sophisticated long-horizon capital exists and is willing to lock up for years — but it also means those allocators have seen every strategy, employ sharp internal teams, and are hard to impress. They are a genuine target for a sophisticated, differentiated fund once you've cleared the size bar, and they're more willing than a pension to back something unusual, but they are not a soft touch.

It's also worth being honest about how the landscape is shifting. The classic standalone fund-of-funds has lost ground over the last decade as large institutions built internal teams to allocate directly and cut out the fee-on-fee layer. But the *function* of the FoF — finding and backing emerging managers — didn't disappear; it migrated into emerging-manager programs, seeding platforms, and specialist allocators. For a founder, the practical takeaway is that the *channel* matters less than the *behavior*: whatever it's called this decade, you're looking for the allocator whose business is taking early-stage manager risk, because that's the one who can say yes before you're proven. Meanwhile the very largest funds have pulled in an outsized share of new flows — institutional capital concentrates with the managers that already cleared every gate — which is precisely why the early years, when you're courting the allocators willing to bet on you, are the hardest and most decisive part of the journey.

One more real-world dynamic to internalize: raising is *reflexive*. Allocators watch each other. A respected early-stage fund-of-funds writing a `\$15M` check into Meridian is not just `\$15M` of capital — it's a signal that a professional, full-time diligence shop examined the fund closely and said yes, and that signal makes the next allocator's decision easier. This is why the *order* in which you raise matters as much as the total: the first credible institutional-flavored LP is worth more than its dollars, because it de-risks every subsequent conversation. A founder who lands a recognized emerging-manager FoF early can often parlay that single name into a cascade of family-office and RIA checks that would have been far harder to get cold. The flip side is just as real: a fund that has been "out raising" for two years with nothing to show develops a stink that allocators smell, because they assume someone smarter already passed. Momentum is an asset and stagnation is a liability, which is another reason to start where you can actually close.

The practical upshot for Maya is unglamorous but liberating: she stops mourning the pension that couldn't write her a check and builds the reachable list. She closes two early-stage funds-of-funds and three family offices over a year, growing Meridian from `\$40M` toward `\$100M` while keeping no single LP above a sane share of the fund. The first FoF's `\$15M` becomes the reference that warms up the next three conversations. As she approaches the institutional threshold, the consultant relationships she planted early start to mature into actual research coverage. The path to the big money runs *through* the small money — there is no shortcut around the landscape. The relationship-management discipline that keeps those early LPs is the subject of [governance and the investor relationship](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/governance-and-the-investor-relationship).

Know your buyer, match your stage, and most of the raising battle is already won.
