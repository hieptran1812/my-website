> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The SEC Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1, compliance date November 4, 2022) governs almost everything a registered fund adviser says to prospects, and the rules are counterintuitive enough that a polished pitch deck can be a securities violation. Performance advertising must show net-of-fee returns alongside gross with equal prominence; hypothetical and extracted performance carry strict conditions; testimonials and endorsements are now permitted but only with disclosure; and GIPS is the voluntary track-record standard institutions look for.
>
> - The single most common violation is showing **gross returns without net beside them**. Gross alone, even truthfully, is a deficiency the moment it sits in an advertisement.
> - The definition of "advertisement" is enormous. Your website, your mass marketing email, a paid referral, a webinar, and most social posts are all advertisements — and a private fund is not exempt just because it is private.
> - Testimonials and endorsements became legal in 2022, reversing decades of an outright ban, but only with **clear disclosure of compensation, the speaker's status, and material conflicts** — at the same time as the testimonial, not buried in a footnote.
> - The one number to remember: the SEC ran an early sweep and in September 2023 charged **nine advisers** over hypothetical-performance failures, signaling that the rule has teeth. *This post is educational, not legal advice — every real launch needs fund counsel, and the thresholds and rule details here are current as of 2026.*

Maya is two weeks from opening the Meridian Fund's new website, and she is, by any honest measure, in a great mood. The fund is up, the design firm delivered a clean site, and she has just finished writing the homepage herself. Two things sit on her screen, both of which feel like obvious wins. The first is a chart: a smooth upward line showing the Meridian Fund returned 38% since launch — her real, audited, hard-won number. The second is a quote, from one of her first investors, a respected former colleague who wired in early and could not be happier: *"Maya is the best portfolio manager I have ever worked with. Putting money with the Meridian Fund was the easiest decision of my career."* She is about to publish both. Why wouldn't she? The chart is true. The quote is real and freely given. Nothing about either feels remotely deceptive.

Both could be securities violations.

The chart shows a **gross** return — before her management and performance fees — with no net number anywhere beside it, and the Marketing Rule treats gross-without-net as a deficiency on its face, no matter how true the number is. The quote is a **testimonial**, which the rule permits but only with disclosures Maya has not added: whether the speaker is a current client, whether he was compensated, and what material conflicts exist. Published as drafted, that homepage takes a fund that has done nothing wrong as an *investment manager* and turns it into a fund that has broken the rules as an *advertiser*. The SEC does not need to prove anyone was harmed. The defect is in the advertisement itself.

![A matrix showing what a fund can and cannot say under the Marketing Rule across performance, hypothetical returns, testimonials, and predictions, with the compliant version in green and the violation in red](/imgs/blogs/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule-1.png)

Figure 1 is the whole post in one grid. Down the left are the four things a fund most wants to put in front of prospects — its performance, a model or backtest, a happy client, and a confident forward statement. Across the top are two columns: what you may say, and what turns the same claim into a violation. The pattern that runs through every row is the lesson of the entire Marketing Rule: *the claim is rarely the problem; the missing context is.* Gross returns are fine — beside net. A client quote is fine — with disclosure. A model track is fine — with the conditions met. The rule is not a gag order. It is a context requirement, and getting the context wrong is how an innocent-looking pitch deck becomes a securities violation. This post builds the rule from zero, works through what Maya can and cannot publish, and shows the dollar cost of getting it wrong.

## Foundations: the Marketing Rule and its vocabulary

The Marketing Rule arrived as a single, modernized regulation that replaced two ancient ones, and to read it you need its vocabulary cold. Let me define every term the rest of the post uses, from zero, assuming you know how to run money but have never had to clear a sentence with a compliance officer.

**Rule 206(4)-1.** The Marketing Rule's formal name is **Rule 206(4)-1** under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940. The SEC adopted it in December 2020 and set a compliance date of **November 4, 2022**. It replaced two older rules — the old Advertising Rule and the old Cash Solicitation Rule — folding advertising and the use of paid solicitors into one modern framework. The "(4)" matters: Section 206(4) of the Advisers Act lets the SEC define and prohibit "fraudulent, deceptive, or manipulative" practices, which is why a *technical* marketing defect (showing gross without net) can be charged as a violation even with no intent to deceive. The rule applies to **SEC-registered investment advisers (RIAs)**. A founder below the registration line — an exempt reporting adviser — is not directly subject to it, but the moment Maya registers (generally at 100 million USD of regulatory assets, covered in [the registration post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d)), the full rule binds her, and most allocators expect adherence regardless of registration status.

**Advertisement.** This is the gateway term, and it is defined far more broadly than the everyday word. Under the rule, an advertisement is, roughly, any communication the adviser distributes that *offers its advisory services to prospective clients or investors*, plus any communication that involves a *compensated testimonial or endorsement*. There are narrow carve-outs (one-on-one communications that contain no performance, extemporaneous live oral commentary, certain regulatory filings), but the working assumption a fund should adopt is: if it goes to more than one prospect and it is trying to win their money, it is an advertisement. The pitch deck, the website, the quarterly letter when sent to prospects, the LinkedIn post, the webinar — all advertisements.

**Performance advertising.** Any advertisement that presents the fund's investment results. Performance is the most heavily regulated category because it is the most persuasive and the most easily manipulated. The rule's central performance requirement is the **net-alongside-gross** mandate: you may not show gross performance unless you present net performance with at least equal prominence and over the same time periods.

**Gross versus net.** **Gross return** is the fund's return *before* deducting fees and expenses — the raw trading result. **Net return** is the return an investor actually keeps, *after* the management fee, the performance fee (the carry), and fund expenses. The gap between them is exactly the fee load described in [how a hedge fund makes money](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-a-hedge-fund-makes-money) and [the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark). On a fund charging 2-and-20, the gap is large: a 38% gross year can be a 28% net year once you strip out 2% management and 20% of the gain. Showing only the gross number flatters the manager and misleads the investor about what they would have kept — which is precisely why the rule forbids it standing alone.

**Hypothetical performance.** Returns that were **not actually achieved by real client money**. This bucket includes **backtested** performance (running the strategy over historical data), **model** performance (a hypothetical portfolio that no one actually held), and **targeted or projected** returns (a forward "we aim for 15%"). Hypothetical performance is permitted but only under strict conditions, including that the adviser has adopted policies reasonably designed to ensure the hypothetical is relevant to the *intended audience*.

**Extracted performance.** The performance of a **subset** of a portfolio pulled out and shown on its own — for example, "here is how just our technology longs did." Extracted performance is allowed but must be accompanied by, or offer to provide promptly, the performance of the *total portfolio* it was extracted from, so a prospect cannot be shown only the best slice.

**Testimonial and endorsement.** A **testimonial** is a statement by a *current client or investor* about their experience with the adviser. An **endorsement** is a similar statement by someone who is *not* a current client — an industry figure, a consultant, a paid promoter. For roughly eight decades these were effectively banned. The Marketing Rule reversed that: both are now allowed, but only with **clear and prominent disclosure** of whether the speaker is a client, whether they were compensated, and any material conflicts of interest. Paid solicitors — people compensated to refer investors — fall under the endorsement rules, which is how the old Cash Solicitation Rule got absorbed.

**GIPS.** The **Global Investment Performance Standards**, maintained by the CFA Institute, are a voluntary, globally recognized framework for how to calculate and present an investment track record honestly and comparably. GIPS is *not* law and the Marketing Rule does not require it, but sophisticated institutional allocators — pensions, consultants, endowments — frequently ask whether a manager is "GIPS-compliant," because a GIPS-verified track record is harder to cherry-pick. GIPS compliance and Marketing Rule compliance overlap heavily but are not identical; a fund can satisfy one and still trip the other.

**General solicitation.** This term belongs to a *different* body of law — the Securities Act and Regulation D — but it interacts with marketing constantly, so it lives here too. **General solicitation** means advertising a securities offering to the public at large rather than to a private, pre-existing network. Whether a fund may engage in it depends on which Reg D exemption it relies on: **506(b)** (no general solicitation allowed) or **506(c)** (general solicitation allowed, but every investor must be verified accredited). The Marketing Rule governs *what you may say*; Reg D governs *to whom you may say it*. A fund can be perfectly Marketing-Rule-compliant in its content and still blow up its offering by saying it to the wrong audience.

That is the alphabet. The rule (206(4)-1) governs advertisements; performance advertising is the heaviest category, anchored on net-alongside-gross; hypothetical and extracted performance are conditional; testimonials and endorsements are now legal with disclosure; GIPS is the voluntary gold standard; and general solicitation is the Reg D constraint that decides the audience. Everything below hangs off one of these.

One more piece of context makes the rule's logic click. It rests on **seven general prohibitions** that apply to *every* advertisement regardless of category. An advertisement may not (1) include an untrue statement of material fact or omit a fact needed to make a statement not misleading; (2) include a material statement the adviser cannot substantiate on demand; (3) include information that would reasonably be likely to cause an untrue or misleading implication; (4) discuss benefits without fair and balanced treatment of material risks or limitations; (5) refer to specific investment advice in a way that is not fair and balanced; (6) present performance in a way that is not fair and balanced; and (7) be otherwise materially misleading. Notice the through-line: most of these say "balanced," and "balanced" is the word that turns gross-without-net into a violation. Showing the upside (gross) without the cost (net) is not balanced. Showing the winning position without the losing ones is not balanced. The specific rules below — net beside gross, total beside extracted, risk beside hypothetical — are just the seven prohibitions made concrete for the situations advisers most often get wrong.

## What the Marketing Rule covers

Before any specific requirement matters, a founder has to know whether the thing she is about to publish is even *in scope*. This is where most people misjudge the rule, because the legal definition of "advertisement" is enormous and the intuitive one is tiny. Maya thinks "advertisement" means a glossy paid ad. The rule means something far closer to "any pitch to anyone who is not already in the fund."

![A branching graph showing that any communication offering advisory services — a pitch deck, website, mass email, social posts, or a paid referral — counts as an advertisement under the rule](/imgs/blogs/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule-6.png)

Figure 2 traces the two doors into "advertisement." The first door is the broad one: any communication the adviser distributes that *offers its advisory services* to prospective clients or investors. The pitch deck offers services. The website offers services. The mass email blast offers services. The webinar offers services. Each of these, once it goes to more than one prospect, walks through the first door and becomes an advertisement subject to every rule below. The second door is narrower but catches a different beast: any **compensated** testimonial or endorsement. A paid referral arrangement, a promoter you pay per introduction, an influencer you compensate — these are advertisements *because of the compensation*, even if the words themselves are someone else's.

Two consequences fall out of this breadth, and both surprise founders.

First, **a private fund is not exempt because it is private.** Founders reason: "I'm doing a private placement to accredited investors, not a public offering — surely the public-advertising rules don't apply." They are confusing two regimes. Reg D controls the *audience* (private placement, accredited only). The Marketing Rule controls the *content* of what a registered adviser says to that audience. A pitch deck handed to twelve accredited family offices is still an advertisement; the net-alongside-gross requirement still applies; the testimonial disclosure still applies. Private does not mean unregulated. It means the audience is restricted, not that the content rules vanish.

Second, **the format does not matter.** The rule is medium-agnostic. A PDF deck, a web page, an email, a tweet, a video, a slide shown on a conference screen, a chart printed in a quarterly letter sent to prospects — all the same. The SEC has been explicit that social media and websites are squarely covered, and has brought cases over advisers' web pages and marketing emails. There is no "it was just a tweet" defense.

There are genuine carve-outs, and they matter. **Live, extemporaneous oral communications** are excluded — Maya talking off the cuff in a one-on-one meeting is not an advertisement (though her *slides* are). A **one-on-one communication** is excluded from parts of the rule *unless it contains hypothetical performance*, which is why showing a backtest in a single private meeting can still trip the rule even though the meeting itself would not. And certain **required regulatory filings** (like the Form ADV brochure itself) are not advertisements. But these carve-outs are narrow seams, not a general escape hatch. The safe default — the one a good compliance program enforces — is to treat anything written, anything shown to more than one prospect, and anything paid as an advertisement, and to route it through review.

#### Worked example: is Maya's homepage an advertisement?

Maya is publishing a public website with the Meridian Fund's name, its strategy, a performance chart, and a client quote, accessible to anyone who finds it. Walk the two doors. Door one: does it offer advisory services to prospective investors? Plainly yes — that is the entire purpose of the site. Door two: does it contain a testimonial? Yes, the client quote. So the homepage is an advertisement on *both* counts. Every requirement below now applies to it: the performance chart needs net beside gross over standard periods; the quote needs testimonial disclosures; any forward statement needs a basis and risk disclosure. The site is not "marketing materials we'll clean up later." It is a regulated communication the day it goes live, and if it is also reachable by the general public, it may additionally count as **general solicitation**, which interacts with her Reg D exemption (more on that below).

*The scope question is settled before the content question: almost everything Maya publishes is an advertisement, so the only real choice is whether it is a compliant one.*

## Performance advertising

Performance is the heart of the rule and the source of most violations, because performance is what sells a fund and what a manager is most tempted to flatter. The governing principle is simple to state and ruthless in application: **you may not present gross performance in an advertisement unless you present net performance with at least equal prominence, calculated over the same time period and using the same methodology.** Net cannot be a footnote, an appendix, or "available on request." It sits beside gross, equally visible.

![A grid contrasting gross and net returns across four presentation requirements, showing net must appear beside gross with equal prominence over the same periods](/imgs/blogs/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule-4.png)

Figure 3 lays out the four-part requirement. Gross is the return before fees and expenses; net is the return after the management fee, the performance fee, and fund expenses — what the investor actually keeps. Gross cannot stand alone in an advertisement. And net must appear with **equal prominence** — same font, same chart, same page, not smaller or further down — over the **same standard time periods**.

Those standard periods are the second pillar. For most performance presentations, the rule requires returns over **one-year, five-year, and ten-year** periods (or since inception if the track record is shorter than one of those), each ending on the same recent date. This kills the most common manipulation: showing a single cherry-picked window that happens to look spectacular. A fund cannot advertise "we returned 38% since our 14-month inception" without also showing the standardized periods, which forces a manager with a short, lucky track record to disclose exactly how short it is.

A few more performance constraints round out the regime. **Related performance** (the results of similar accounts or funds the adviser manages) generally must include *all* materially similar accounts, not just the winners — you cannot show your three best separately managed accounts and quietly drop the four that lagged. **Predecessor performance** (a track record the manager built at a prior firm) may be used only under conditions: substantially the same people must run substantially the same strategy, the accounts must be sufficiently similar, and the prior performance must be presented in a non-misleading way with appropriate disclosure. And every performance figure must be **substantiated** — the adviser must be able, on demand, to produce the records that prove the number. "Trust me, the spreadsheet said 38%" is not substantiation.

The standardized-periods requirement deserves a closer look, because it is where short-track-record founders feel the rule most. The point of forcing one-year, five-year, and ten-year windows (or since-inception where the record is shorter) is to deny the manager control over the *frame*. A founder with a single brilliant 14-month run wants to show exactly that 14 months, because it is the most flattering possible window. The rule says: fine, show since-inception, but show it labeled honestly as a 14-month track, and do not dress a sub-two-year record up as anything more. There is a subtle interaction with new funds here — a fund younger than a year has only a since-inception number to show, and the rule is comfortable with that as long as the brevity is disclosed rather than hidden. The violation is not having a short record; the violation is presenting a short record in a way that implies a longer or smoother one than exists. A related trap is the **partial-period** or **year-to-date** flex: advertising a strong YTD number without the standardized longer periods beside it is the same cherry-pick in a different costume, and examiners treat it the same way.

The rule also reaches **fees in the performance calculation itself**, not just in disclosure. Net performance must reflect the deduction of all fees and expenses an investor actually pays — including the performance fee. A common error is computing "net" by subtracting only the management fee and forgetting the carry, which overstates net in any positive year. On the Meridian Fund's 31% gross year, "net of management fee only" would read about 29%, while true net after the 20% carry reads about 23% — a six-point overstatement masquerading as a net number. The rule wants the *real* net, the number on the investor's statement, not a half-net convenience figure.

#### Worked example: presenting the Meridian Fund's 100 million dollar track gross and net

The Meridian Fund manages \$100M and posts a strong year. Gross of fees, the portfolio returned **31%**, or \$31M of trading gains on the \$100M. Now strip out the fees an investor actually pays under Maya's 2-and-20. The management fee is 2% of \$100M, or \$2M. The performance fee is 20% of the gain *above the high-water mark*; on a clean \$31M gain that is roughly 20% of (31M minus the 2M management fee), about \$5.8M, depending on the exact fee mechanics and high-water-mark accounting from [the high-water-mark post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark). Add a bit of fund expense — audit, admin, legal — call it \$0.4M. Total fee-and-expense load: roughly \$8.2M. Net to investors: about \$22.8M on \$100M, or a **net return near 23%**.

Under the Marketing Rule, Maya's tear sheet cannot show only the 31% gross. It must show **23% net beside it**, same size, same chart, over the standard one-year (and since-inception, since the fund is young) periods. The honest slide reads: *31% gross / 23% net*. The eight-point gap is not a rounding error — it is the entire reason the rule exists. An investor staring at "31%" forms a very different expectation than one staring at "23% net," and the rule insists they see the number they would actually keep.

*Net-alongside-gross is the rule's beating heart: the manager's headline number and the investor's real number must appear together, every time.*

Figure 4 shows the same returns as two slides. On the left is the slide Maya almost built: a headline gross number, a single flattering window, no fees shown, no disclosure. On the right is the compliant version: net beside gross at equal prominence, standardized periods (one-year, five-year or since-inception), and the fees and methodology disclosed. The investment result is identical. Only the *presentation* changed — and that presentation is the difference between a clean pitch and an enforcement matter.

## Hypothetical and extracted performance

Real returns are hard to manipulate beyond the net-versus-gross trick. **Hypothetical** returns are trivially easy to manipulate, which is why the rule treats them with suspicion bordering on hostility, and why the SEC's first big enforcement sweep targeted them directly.

Hypothetical performance covers anything not actually earned by real client money: a **backtest** of the strategy over history, a **model** portfolio no one held, and **targeted or projected** returns. The rule does not ban hypotheticals, but it surrounds them with conditions that are genuinely hard to meet:

1. The adviser must adopt and implement **policies and procedures reasonably designed to ensure** the hypothetical performance is relevant to the *likely financial situation and investment objectives of the intended audience.* In practice this means hypotheticals are appropriate for sophisticated institutions that can evaluate them, and presumptively *inappropriate* for a general retail website where anyone can read them. This single condition is why a backtest on a public homepage is a near-automatic problem.
2. The adviser must provide **sufficient information** for that audience to understand the criteria and assumptions used to calculate the hypothetical.
3. The adviser must provide (or offer to provide promptly) the **risks and limitations** of using the hypothetical in making investment decisions — including, prominently, that hypothetical results do not reflect actual trading and that real results will differ.

**Extracted performance** — pulling a subset of a real portfolio out to show on its own — has its own condition: you must show, or offer to provide promptly, the performance of the **total portfolio** from which it was extracted. Maya cannot show "our energy book returned 60%" without also offering the whole-fund number, because the energy book's 60% might sit inside a flat overall year. The logic is the same balance principle: a subset of a portfolio, shown alone, is the winning position with the losing ones cropped out of frame. The total-portfolio number is the anchor that stops the crop. Extracted performance is genuinely useful — an allocator evaluating a multi-strategy fund may legitimately want to see how the credit sleeve did versus the equity sleeve — so the rule permits it; it just refuses to let the slice masquerade as the whole.

There is also a distinct category the rule treats with care: **gross-of-fee extracted performance shown to one prospect**. Even in a one-on-one meeting — which is excluded from much of the rule — *hypothetical* performance is not excluded, and a single-account "what your sleeve would have done" projection can shade into hypothetical territory. The safe practice is to assume the performance rules apply to any number you show anyone, and to keep the total-portfolio anchor and the net figure attached to every extract.

In September 2023, the SEC announced **settled charges against nine advisers** for advertising hypothetical performance on their public websites without the required policies and disclosures — an early, deliberate signal that the rule was not aspirational. Penalties in that sweep ran into the hundreds of thousands of dollars per firm, and subsequent sweeps in 2024 widened the net. The message to founders was unambiguous: a backtest on a public website, with no audience-relevance policy and no risk disclosure, is one of the fastest ways to draw an enforcement action under the new rule.

![A bar chart of illustrative SEC Marketing Rule enforcement actions per year from 2022 to 2025, showing a sharp ramp after the November 2022 compliance date](/imgs/blogs/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule-5.png)

Figure 5 sketches the illustrative arc of Marketing-Rule enforcement since the rule went live. The counts are rounded estimates of publicized actions, not an official tally, but the shape is real: a near-zero base in late 2022, a sharp jump in 2023 driven by the hypothetical-performance sweep, and continued sweeps through 2024 and 2025. The trend, not the exact bars, is the point — the SEC moved from rule-making to enforcement quickly, and hypothetical performance was the leading edge.

#### Worked example: making a model track usable with a disclosure

Maya has a beautiful backtest. Before launching the Meridian Fund she ran her long/short signal over ten years of history and it shows a hypothetical **22% annualized** with a 1.4 Sharpe. She desperately wants to use it because her live track is only 14 months long. Can she? Only by clearing every condition. She must (a) confirm the audience is appropriate — she may show it to a sophisticated institutional allocator in a private setting, but she may *not* put it on the public Meridian website; (b) disclose the methodology — that it is a backtest, the universe, the assumed costs, the assumed financing, the rebalancing rules; and (c) disclose the risks and limitations clearly and prominently — that the 22% was never earned by real money, that backtests benefit from hindsight and assumed frictionless execution, and that live results will differ, as her 14-month live track already shows. Critically, she must also have **written policies** establishing that this hypothetical is relevant to that specific institutional audience. The disclosure that makes the model usable reads, in substance: *"Hypothetical backtested performance. Not actual trading; results were achieved by means of retroactive application of a model designed with hindsight. Assumes [costs/financing]. Hypothetical results have inherent limitations and do not guarantee future results; actual performance will differ. For institutional use only."* With that framing and a real audience-relevance policy behind it, the backtest is usable. Without it, it is a violation waiting to be found in a routine exam.

*A backtest is not forbidden — it is conditioned, and the condition is so demanding that on a public website a model track is almost always a mistake.*

## Testimonials and endorsements

For most of the modern era of fund regulation, an adviser could not use client testimonials at all. The old Advertising Rule banned them outright on the theory that a happy client is unrepresentative and inherently misleading. The Marketing Rule **reversed this**, and the reversal is one of its most consequential changes — but the permission comes wrapped in disclosure requirements that founders routinely underestimate.

A **testimonial** is a statement by a current client or investor; an **endorsement** is a statement by someone who is not a current client. Both are now allowed in advertisements, subject to three layers of requirement:

1. **Disclosure, clearly and prominently, at the time of the testimonial or endorsement.** The advertisement must disclose (a) whether the person giving it is a *client or investor* (for a testimonial) or *not* (for an endorsement); (b) whether the person was **compensated** — and cash or non-cash compensation both count; and (c) a brief statement of any **material conflicts of interest** on the part of the speaker arising from the adviser's relationship with them. "Clearly and prominently" means at the same time and in the same medium as the testimonial — not in a footnote, not on a separate disclosures page, not after a click.
2. **Adviser oversight and a written agreement.** The adviser must have a reasonable basis for believing the testimonial complies with the rule, and where the promoter is *compensated* above a small threshold, there must be a **written agreement** with the promoter describing the scope of activities and the compensation.
3. **Disqualification.** A **"bad actor"** — someone subject to certain disciplinary events — generally cannot be paid to give an endorsement. The adviser must perform diligence to confirm the promoter is not disqualified.

The compensation disclosure is the trap. Founders think of compensation as a check written to a promoter, but the rule's definition is broad: a **reduced fee**, **free access to the fund**, a **referral fee**, **directed brokerage**, even a meaningful gift can be compensation that must be disclosed. If Maya gives an early investor a break on fees and that investor then provides a glowing quote for her deck, the quote is arguably a *compensated* testimonial, and the fee break is the compensation that must be disclosed alongside it.

The phrase **"clearly and prominently"** is doing heavy lifting and is worth pinning down, because it is where well-intentioned funds still fail. It means the required disclosures must be **in the advertisement itself, presented so a reasonable person would notice them, at the same time** as the testimonial. A disclosure in eight-point gray type at the bottom of a deck is not prominent. A disclosure on a separate "legal" page the reader has to click through to find is not at the same time. A disclosure delivered verbally three weeks later does not cure a written advertisement. For a video testimonial, the disclosure generally needs to appear in the video; for a written one, in the same written piece, adjacent to the quote. The standard is deliberately practical: would an ordinary prospect, reading the praise, also see that the speaker is a paid, conflicted investor? If the layout makes that easy to miss, the disclosure has failed even if the words technically exist somewhere in the document.

There is also a quiet recordkeeping requirement riding underneath. The rule amended the advisers' **books-and-records** obligations so that a fund must keep copies of all advertisements it disseminates, plus the records substantiating performance and the written agreements with compensated promoters. So a testimonial is not a one-time decision; it is a record the fund must retain and be able to hand an examiner, along with proof that it had a reasonable basis for believing the testimonial complied. This is why disciplined funds keep a versioned archive of every deck and web page — the archive *is* the defense in an exam.

![A before and after comparison of a testimonial, with a bare client quote lacking any disclosure on the left in red and a compliant version disclosing client status, compensation, and conflict on the right in green](/imgs/blogs/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule-7.png)

Figure 6 shows the testimonial Maya wanted to publish and the version that is actually legal. On the left: the bare quote — "best manager I have ever worked with" — with no indication the speaker is an investor, no mention of any compensation, no conflict disclosed. On the right: the *same quote*, now clearly labeled as a testimonial, identifying the speaker as a current investor in the fund, disclosing any compensation (a referral fee, a fee break, or "no compensation" if truly none), and disclosing the material conflict — that an investor in the fund has an interest in attracting more capital. The words a prospect reads are identical. The disclosures around them are the difference between a permitted endorsement and a deficiency.

#### Worked example: a testimonial that needs disclosure and compensation language

Maya's first investor, an old colleague, wired in \$5M at launch and is genuinely thrilled. She wants his quote on the deck. Two facts complicate it. First, to thank him for being a day-one believer, she put him in the **founders' share class** at 1.5-and-15 instead of the standard 2-and-20 — a real economic benefit. Second, his \$5M is now worth more after the 23% net year, so he has a direct interest in the fund growing (a larger, more stable fund is safer for his capital). Under the rule, the fee break is **compensation** and his investor status plus his growth interest are **material conflicts**, all of which must be disclosed clearly and prominently at the same time as the quote. The compliant presentation reads, in substance: *"The following is a testimonial from a current investor in the Meridian Fund. This investor receives a reduced management and performance fee (a founders'-class rate) and, as an investor, has an interest in the fund attracting additional capital. These facts may create a conflict of interest."* Now the quote is usable. The same quote without those two sentences is a violation — and the violation is not the praise, it is the silence about the fee break and the interest.

*The rule did not legalize the happy-client quote; it legalized the disclosed happy-client quote, and the disclosure is exactly the part a proud founder forgets.*

## GIPS

The Marketing Rule sets the legal floor for what a registered adviser may say. **GIPS** sets the voluntary ceiling for how to say it credibly, and the gap between the two is where institutional capital lives.

GIPS — the **Global Investment Performance Standards**, maintained by the CFA Institute — is a detailed, globally recognized set of rules for calculating and presenting an investment track record honestly and comparably. It is **not law**. Nothing in the Marketing Rule requires it. But it solves a problem the Marketing Rule only partly addresses: *comparability and the temptation to cherry-pick*. GIPS requires a firm to define **composites** — groupings of all portfolios managed to a similar strategy — and to report the composite's performance, not a hand-picked subset. It standardizes how returns are calculated, how new and terminated accounts are handled, how fees are disclosed, and how the track record is presented, so that one GIPS-compliant manager's numbers can be compared to another's without wondering what was quietly excluded.

Why does a founder care about a voluntary standard? Because the institutions Maya most wants — pensions, endowments, consultants, the allocators described in [the LP landscape](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates) — frequently *ask*. A consultant's questionnaire will include "Is the firm GIPS-compliant?" and "Has the track record been GIPS-verified by an independent party?" A "yes," backed by an independent verification, is a credibility signal: it says the manager calculated returns by a rigorous, externally checkable standard rather than by whatever flattered them. This dovetails with the operational-credibility theme that runs through [building a track record](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close) and survives [operational due diligence](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto): the same instinct that makes an allocator demand an independent administrator makes them prefer a GIPS-verified track record.

The relationship between GIPS and the Marketing Rule is one of **overlap, not identity**. Both push toward net-of-fee presentation, full composites instead of cherry-picked accounts, and clear methodology disclosure, so a well-run GIPS-compliant shop will satisfy most of the Marketing Rule's performance requirements as a byproduct. But they are different regimes with different details — GIPS has calculation conventions and verification requirements the SEC does not mandate, and the Marketing Rule has disclosure and audience-relevance requirements GIPS does not address. A fund can be GIPS-compliant and still trip the Marketing Rule (for example, by mishandling a testimonial), and a fund can be Marketing-Rule-compliant without ever adopting GIPS. The practical posture for an ambitious manager: treat the Marketing Rule as the **mandatory legal floor** and GIPS as the **optional credibility upgrade** you adopt when you start chasing institutional tickets that ask for it.

#### Worked example: what GIPS compliance costs a 100 million dollar fund

Maya is told by a consultant that to be considered for a \$50M pension allocation, the Meridian Fund should be GIPS-compliant and independently verified. What does that cost? GIPS compliance itself is mostly an internal discipline — defining composites, calculating returns to the standard, maintaining the records — which consumes meaningful time from her CFO or controller and may require performance-attribution software. **Independent GIPS verification**, where an outside verification firm checks that her firm's processes meet the standard, is an annual engagement that for a small single-strategy fund illustratively runs in the **low tens of thousands of dollars per year** — on the order of \$15k to \$40k, varying by complexity and verifier. Against a \$50M institutional ticket that would generate \$750k a year in management fees alone at 1.5%, a \$25k annual verification cost is trivial. The decision is not really about the cost; it is about whether Maya is chasing the kind of institutional capital that demands it. A founder raising only from family offices and accredited individuals may skip GIPS entirely; one targeting pensions and consultants treats it as table stakes.

*GIPS is the price of admission to institutional capital, not a legal requirement — you pay it when the allocators you want start asking for it.*

## General solicitation

Everything so far has governed *what* Maya can say. **General solicitation** governs *to whom* she can say it, and it lives in a different statute — the Securities Act and Regulation D — but it is impossible to talk about marketing a fund without it, because the most natural marketing instinct (tell everyone) is exactly what can destroy a private offering.

Recall from [the registration and Reg D post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d) that a hedge fund sells its interests as a **private placement** under **Regulation D**, which exempts it from the enormous burden of a registered public offering. Reg D offers two main paths, and the choice between them is the choice about general solicitation:

- **Rule 506(b)** — the path most hedge funds take. You may sell to an unlimited number of **accredited investors** plus up to 35 non-accredited (sophisticated) investors, but you **may not engage in general solicitation or advertising** to find them. You must have a **pre-existing, substantive relationship** with each prospect, and you may rely on the investor's self-certification of accredited status. The cost of 506(b) is silence: no public advertising of the offering.
- **Rule 506(c)** — the path opened by the JOBS Act of 2012. You **may** generally solicit and advertise the offering to the public, but in exchange you must sell **only to accredited investors** *and* take **reasonable steps to verify** each investor's accredited status — actual verification (reviewing tax forms, brokerage statements, or a third-party letter), not self-certification.

General solicitation means publicly advertising the *securities offering* — broadly disseminated communications about the fund's interests to people with whom you have no pre-existing relationship. A public website that promotes the fund's offering, a tweet inviting investment, a press article you placed touting the fund as open — these can constitute general solicitation. And here is the collision with marketing: **if Maya is relying on 506(b), publishing a public website that solicits investment can blow her exemption.** The Marketing Rule might bless her *content* (net beside gross, disclosed testimonials), and she can still destroy the offering because she said compliant things to the *wrong audience* — the general public — under a 506(b) exemption that forbids it.

This is the precise trap from the opening of [the registration post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d): the warm email to two hundred loose contacts. Under 506(b), an email to people with whom Maya has no pre-existing substantive relationship is general solicitation, and it can convert a legal private placement into an unregistered public offering — a far more serious problem than a marketing deficiency. The two regimes stack: she must satisfy the Marketing Rule on *content* and Reg D on *audience*, simultaneously.

The practical reconciliation most emerging managers reach: **stay 506(b), keep the offering itself out of public view, and gate any performance or offering detail behind a relationship.** A bare-bones public website that says the firm exists and offers to start a conversation is generally tolerable; a public website that advertises the fund's returns and invites investment is general solicitation. The cleaner alternative is to **go 506(c)** deliberately — accept the verification burden in exchange for the freedom to advertise — but most hedge funds judge the verification friction and the optics not worth it and stay 506(b), which means living with marketing's quiet constraint: you may say compliant things, but mostly to people you already know.

#### Worked example: the website that breaks a 506(b) offering

Maya, relying on 506(b), launches a public website showing the Meridian Fund's 23% net return and a "Request to invest" button reachable by anyone. The *content* is Marketing-Rule-compliant: net beside gross, standardized periods, disclosed testimonial. But the *act* of publicly advertising the offering to people with no pre-existing relationship is general solicitation, which 506(b) forbids. The consequence is not a marketing fine — it is the potential **loss of the Reg D exemption for the entire offering**, meaning every interest she sold could be deemed part of an unregistered public securities offering. Investors could potentially rescind; the SEC could charge an unregistered offering. The fix is structural: either gate the performance and offering material behind a login that only pre-existing, qualified relationships can access (preserving 506(b)), or convert the offering to 506(c) and verify every investor's accredited status before they invest. The same web page is fine under one exemption and catastrophic under the other.

*Marketing-Rule-compliant content delivered through general solicitation can still be illegal — the rule governs your words, Reg D governs your audience, and you must satisfy both at once.*

## Common misconceptions

The Marketing Rule generates a specific set of expensive misunderstandings, because so much of it runs against a founder's instincts. Here are the ones that cost real money.

**"Gross returns are fine on their own — they're true."** The most common and most expensive error. Truth is not the standard; *presentation* is. Gross-without-net is a deficiency the moment it appears in an advertisement, regardless of accuracy, because the rule treats omitting the number the investor would actually keep as inherently misleading. Net must sit beside gross with equal prominence over the same standard periods, every time.

**"A happy-client quote is harmless — he said it himself, and he means it."** A genuine, freely given, completely sincere testimonial is *still* a violation without the required disclosures. The rule does not care whether the praise is earned; it cares whether the advertisement discloses the speaker's status, any compensation (including a fee break), and any material conflict. Sincerity is not a substitute for disclosure.

**"The Marketing Rule doesn't apply to a private fund."** It applies to the **registered adviser**, and it does not exempt advertisements just because the fund is sold privately to accredited investors. Reg D restricts the audience; the Marketing Rule governs the content shown to that audience. A pitch deck handed to a dozen accredited family offices is an advertisement and every performance and testimonial rule applies.

**"A backtest is just data — I can put it on the website."** Hypothetical performance, including backtests, is permitted only with audience-relevance policies, methodology disclosure, and prominent risk-and-limitation disclosure. On a public website, where the audience cannot be controlled, a backtest is a near-automatic problem — which is exactly what the SEC's nine-adviser sweep in September 2023 targeted.

**"GIPS is required, so if I'm GIPS-compliant I'm covered."** Two errors in one. GIPS is **voluntary**, not legally required. And GIPS compliance does **not** equal Marketing Rule compliance — they overlap on performance presentation but the Marketing Rule has disclosure and testimonial requirements GIPS never addresses. A GIPS-compliant fund can still violate the rule, and a fund with no GIPS at all can be fully Marketing-Rule-compliant.

**"If my content is compliant, I can put it anywhere."** Marketing-Rule-compliant content distributed by **general solicitation** can still destroy a 506(b) offering. Compliant words to the wrong audience is a securities problem, not a marketing one. You must satisfy the content rules and the Reg D audience rules at the same time.

## How it plays out in the real world

In practice, the Marketing Rule lives inside a fund as a **workflow**, not a memo. The funds that stay clean are the ones that route every outbound communication through a defined review process before any prospect sees it, and they own that workflow as part of [the compliance program and the CCO function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco).

![A pipeline showing the marketing-review workflow: a draft is scoped as an advertisement, checked for net-beside-gross, substantiated, reviewed by the CCO, disclosures added, archived, then released to accredited prospects](/imgs/blogs/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule-3.png)

Figure 7 traces that workflow. A draft is created — a deck, an email, a web page. It is first **scoped**: is this an advertisement? (Almost always yes.) If it contains performance, it is checked for **net beside gross** over the standard periods. Every factual claim is **substantiated** with evidence that can be produced on demand. The **CCO or outside counsel reviews and signs off**. Required **disclosures** — testimonial language, hypothetical risk disclosures, fee and methodology notes — are added. A copy is **archived** to satisfy the adviser's books-and-records obligations (the rule requires advisers to keep copies of all advertisements). Only then is the material **released**, and only to the appropriate audience for the fund's Reg D exemption. The discipline is unglamorous and it is exactly what an SEC examiner reconstructs in a routine exam: show me the ad, show me the substantiation, show me the review, show me the archive.

The enforcement record makes the workflow's value concrete. The **September 2023 sweep** charged nine advisers over hypothetical performance on public websites; penalties ran into the hundreds of thousands of dollars per firm, with required remediation. Subsequent sweeps in **2024** targeted further hypothetical-performance and testimonial failures, and the SEC's published exam priorities have repeatedly flagged the Marketing Rule as a focus area. The pattern is consistent: the violations were rarely elaborate frauds. They were ordinary marketing materials — a backtest on a homepage, a performance chart without net, a testimonial without disclosure — published by firms that treated marketing as a creative task rather than a regulated one.

Two operational habits separate the funds that stay clean from the ones that get caught. The first is **a single point of control**: every outbound communication, no matter how small, goes through one named reviewer — the CCO or outside counsel — with authority to say no, and nothing reaches a prospect without that sign-off logged. The failure mode is the founder who emails a quick performance update to a few prospects on a Sunday without routing it, because "it's just an email to people I know." That email is an advertisement, and the casual ones are exactly what examiners find. The second habit is **periodic re-review**: a website or standing deck is not reviewed once and forgotten. Performance numbers go stale, the standardized periods roll forward, and a chart that was compliant in March can be misleading by September if it still shows an old window. Disciplined funds re-paper their standing marketing on a calendar — quarterly is common — and re-archive each version. The cost of both habits is a few hours a quarter and a little friction on the founder's enthusiasm. The cost of skipping them is the worked example below.

The deeper lesson connects to the spine of running a fund. A fund's promise to its investors is *credibility* — and credibility is precisely what a marketing violation destroys, twice over. The first time, with the SEC, in a settled order that becomes a permanent disclosure item on the Form ADV every future allocator will read. The second time, with allocators directly: an operational-due-diligence team that finds a Marketing Rule deficiency reads it the same way they read a missing cash control — as evidence that the firm cuts corners. As [the ODD post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto) describes, a fund with great returns can be rejected on a control failure, and an advertising violation is a control failure dressed as a graphic-design choice.

#### Worked example: the cost of a Marketing Rule violation

Maya publishes the original homepage — gross-only chart, undisclosed testimonial — and a year later an SEC examiner finds it during a routine exam. Tally the cost. The **monetary penalty** for a first-time, non-fraudulent Marketing Rule violation in the SEC's sweeps has illustratively run from the low tens of thousands to a few hundred thousand dollars; call Maya's settlement **\$150k**. Then come the costs that dwarf the fine. **Remediation**: legal and compliance fees to respond to the exam, rebuild the marketing-review workflow, retrain staff, and certify the fix — easily **\$100k–\$300k** for a small fund. **The permanent disclosure**: the settled order becomes a regulatory event reported on her Form ADV, which every future allocator and every ODD team will see for years. **The lost capital**: a consultant mid-diligence on a \$50M allocation reads the disclosure and quietly drops Meridian — a \$50M ticket that would have paid \$750k a year in management fees, gone. Add it up: a \$150k fine is the *smallest* number in the equation. The real cost is the multi-year credibility tax on a fund that did nothing wrong as an investor and everything wrong as an advertiser.

*A Marketing Rule violation is cheap to commit and ruinously expensive to carry — the fine is a rounding error against the lost capital and the permanent stain on the firm's record.*

What the disciplined funds do, then, is treat marketing as Maya should have from the start: as a regulated function with a workflow, owned by compliance, where every chart shows net beside gross, every quote carries its disclosure, every backtest stays off the public site, and every piece of paper is reviewed and archived before a single prospect sees it. It is slower and less fun than publishing a beautiful homepage on a Friday afternoon. It is also the difference between a fund that compounds capital and a fund that compounds regulatory disclosures.

## When this matters / Further reading

The Marketing Rule matters the moment a fund starts *telling its story* — which is from the very first pitch, long before any registration threshold is crossed. The instinct that gets founders in trouble is the instinct that makes them good at raising: they are proud of their returns and proud of their happy investors, and they want to show both. The rule does not forbid either. It demands context: net beside gross, disclosure beside the testimonial, conditions beside the backtest, and the right audience for the offering. Internalize that and most of the rule becomes second nature. Forget it, and a homepage built in an afternoon becomes a years-long credibility tax.

Concretely, this matters most at three moments: when you build the **website and pitch deck** (scope everything as an advertisement; gate offering detail behind relationships); when you assemble the **track record** (net alongside gross, standardized periods, GIPS if you chase institutions); and when you decide **how to find investors** (506(b) silence versus 506(c) verification). Get all three right and your marketing is an asset; get any one wrong and it is a liability the SEC and every allocator can see.

This post is **educational, not legal advice.** The Marketing Rule's text, the enforcement counts, and the dollar thresholds here are current as of 2026 and evolve through SEC guidance, FAQs, and new orders; every real fund clears its marketing with securities counsel and its CCO before it goes out.
