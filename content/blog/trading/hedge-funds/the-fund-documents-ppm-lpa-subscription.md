> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A hedge fund is not a portfolio with a marketing deck on top; it is a stack of four legal documents, and every promise the fund makes lives or dies inside their clauses.
>
> - The **PPM** (private placement memorandum) discloses what investors are buying and every risk that buying it carries; its job is to make the manager's downside *defensible*, not to sell.
> - The **LPA / operating agreement** is the binding rulebook — fees, the high-water mark, liquidity, key-person, indemnification, and the threshold to amend any of it. It governs the fund for its whole life.
> - The **subscription agreement** is how an investor commits capital and represents under penalty that they qualify; the **IMA** grants the manager the authority to trade and the right to the fee.
> - The number to remember: a clean document set is a one-time legal bill of roughly **\$50k–150k**, paid before you manage a single dollar — and a single mis-drafted liquidity or key-person clause can cost far more than that for the rest of the fund's life.

Maya had been running the Meridian Fund for two years, and the numbers were good: up a cumulative 34%, a Sharpe north of one, no down quarter worse than 4%. She had stopped reading her own fund documents the week after launch, the way most founders do — they are dense, lawyer-drafted, and the lawyer assured her they were "standard." Then, in a thin, jumpy market, her largest investor — a fund-of-funds holding \$40M of the fund's \$110M — sent a redemption notice for the full amount.

Maya pulled up the LPA, the limited partnership agreement, for the first time in twenty-four months and read the liquidity section line by line. Monthly redemptions. Thirty days' notice. No lock-up. No gate. She had skimmed that clause during a frantic pre-launch week and assumed it said what she wanted it to say. It did not. Her single largest investor could pull more than a third of the fund's assets in thirty days, and she had to honor it — at par NAV, regardless of what selling \$40M of her less-liquid positions into a jumpy tape would do to the other investors who stayed. The clause she skimmed was now the most important fact about her business.

That is the lesson this post is built around, and it is worth stating plainly before anything else: **a fund IS its documents.** The strategy is real, the returns are real, the team is real — but legally, contractually, and operationally, the fund is the set of agreements that say who can do what, who is owed what, who can leave when, and who eats the loss when something goes wrong. Figure 1 is the map of those documents and how they reference one another. Everything else in this post is a tour of that map, clause by clause, with the ones that bite called out by name.

![A graph showing the fund's core documents and how the PPM, LPA, subscription agreement, IMA, and side letter reference each other](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription-1.png)

This post sits downstream of [the fund structure: management company vs the fund](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund), which explains the *entities* — the management company, the general partner, the fund itself. Here we move from the entities to the paper that binds them. If the entity diagram is the skeleton, the documents are the nervous system: the rules that make the skeleton actually move.

## Foundations: the document stack

Before the clauses, the vocabulary. A hedge fund launch produces a thick binder, but almost all of it is one of a handful of document types, each with a job that does not overlap the others. Get the jobs straight and the binder stops being intimidating.

**The offering.** When a fund "offers" interests to investors, it is selling securities — a limited partnership interest or LLC membership interest is a security under US law. Because the fund does not register that security with the SEC (registration is for public mutual funds and ETFs), it relies on a **private placement** exemption, which is why the headline disclosure document is called a *private placement memorandum*. "Private placement" means the fund is sold privately, to a limited set of qualifying investors, rather than advertised to the public. The whole document stack exists to make that private offering legal and defensible.

**The PPM (private placement memorandum).** Sometimes called the offering memorandum or OM. This is the **disclosure** document: it describes the fund, the strategy, the people, the terms, the service providers, and — at great length — every risk. The PPM does not bind anyone to anything. Nobody signs it. It is an *offer*: "here is what we are selling and everything that could go wrong; if you still want in, here is how." Its legal purpose is to give investors full and fair disclosure so that they cannot later claim they were misled. The PPM is the manager's liability shield.

**The LPA (limited partnership agreement) or operating agreement.** If the fund is a Delaware limited partnership, its constitutional document is the LPA; if it is an LLC, it is the operating agreement; the function is identical, so this post uses "LPA" for both. This is the **binding contract** — the rulebook. It defines the relationship between the general partner (the GP, who runs the fund and bears unlimited liability) and the limited partners (the LPs, the investors, whose liability is capped at their investment). The LPA sets the management fee, the incentive fee, the high-water mark, the liquidity terms, the GP's powers, what happens if a key person dies, how the manager is indemnified, and the vote needed to change any of it. When investors and managers fight, they fight over the LPA.

**The subscription agreement (the "sub doc").** This is how an investor actually *commits* capital and gets admitted to the fund. The investor fills it out, makes a long list of legal representations (chiefly: "I am an accredited investor / qualified purchaser, I understand the risks, I am not buying to immediately resell, my money is clean"), and signs. The subscription agreement is the investor's signed promise that they qualify and understand what they are doing. It is also where anti-money-laundering and know-your-customer information is collected.

**The IMA (investment management agreement).** This is the contract between the fund and the **management company** (the entity Maya owns and runs) that grants the management company the authority to manage the fund's assets and the right to be paid the management fee. In a simple GP-as-manager structure the management terms can live inside the LPA, but most institutional setups use a separate IMA so the manager's authority and economics are cleanly documented. The IMA is what turns Maya's firm into the fund's legal investment manager.

**The side letter.** A bespoke, privately negotiated agreement between the fund and a single large investor that modifies the standard terms *for that investor only* — a fee discount, better liquidity, a most-favored-nation clause, extra reporting, a capacity right. Side letters are how big LPs extract concessions without rewriting the LPA for everyone. They are also a quiet source of risk, because the fund must honor every side letter it signs, and a generous one can undercut the standard terms it spent so much to set.

A few more terms this post leans on, defined from zero: **AUM** is assets under management, the total capital the fund runs; **NAV** is net asset value, the fund's assets minus liabilities, the number that determines what each investor's stake is worth; the **GP** is the general partner (the manager's controlling entity); the **LP** is a limited partner (an investor); the **administrator** is the independent firm that strikes the NAV and processes subscriptions and redemptions; a **lock-up** is a period during which an investor cannot redeem; a **gate** caps how much can be redeemed at once; and the **high-water mark** is the prior peak value above which the incentive fee resumes. Figure 2 lays the four core documents side by side — what each does, who signs it, and what it governs.

![A matrix with rows for the PPM, LPA, subscription agreement, and IMA and columns for primary job, who signs, and what each governs](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription-2.png)

The single most useful mental model is the one in the figure: **disclose, bind, commit, empower.** The PPM discloses, the LPA binds, the subscription agreement commits, the IMA empowers. When something goes wrong and you need to know which document controls, ask which of those four jobs the dispute touches. A fight about whether a risk was disclosed is a PPM question. A fight about fees or liquidity is an LPA question. A fight about whether an investor qualified is a subscription-agreement question. A fight about the manager's authority or termination is an IMA question. Maya's redemption crisis was an LPA question, which is exactly why she should have read the LPA two years earlier.

## The PPM

Start with the document that does the most legal work while binding the fewest people. The PPM's entire reason for existing is **disclosure**, and disclosure is the manager's protection, not the investor's. That sounds backwards until you see the logic.

Under the US securities laws, an investor who loses money can sue the manager for fraud or misrepresentation if the manager failed to disclose a material risk or made a misleading statement in connection with the sale. The defense against that suit is a document that disclosed the risk in advance. If the PPM said, in plain language, "this strategy uses leverage of up to four times, can lose more than 50% in a stressed market, may suspend redemptions, and depends heavily on the continued involvement of the portfolio manager," then an investor who suffers exactly that outcome cannot credibly say they were not warned. **The PPM converts a risk into a disclosed risk, and a disclosed risk is far harder to sue over.** This is why PPM risk-factor sections are exhaustingly long: every risk the lawyers can foresee goes in, because each disclosed risk is one fewer thing the manager can be ambushed with later.

The PPM is assembled in a fairly standard order, shown in the stack below. It opens with cover-page legends — the legally required notices that this is a private offering not registered with the SEC, not for general distribution, and so on. Then a **summary of terms**: a one-or-two-page table of the fee schedule, minimum investment, lock-up, redemption frequency, notice period, and the fund's domicile. Then the **manager and key personnel** — who runs the money and their background. Then the **investment strategy and objective** — what the fund does, in enough detail to be honest but not so much that it gives away the alpha. Then the long pole of the document, the **risk factors**, followed by **fees and expenses** in full, **conflicts of interest**, **tax and ERISA considerations**, and finally the **service providers and legal terms**.

![A vertical stack showing the sections of a PPM in order from cover legends down to service providers and legal terms](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription-4.png)

Two sections deserve special attention because founders consistently underweight them. The first is the **risk factors**. A good risk-factor section is fund-specific, not boilerplate. A long/short equity fund must disclose the specific risks of short selling (theoretically unlimited loss, short-squeeze risk, borrow availability, recall risk), of leverage, of concentration, of liquidity in the names it trades, and of the strategy failing if a factor regime changes. A founder who lets the lawyer paste a generic risk section is exposed precisely where the fund is actually risky. When the strategy blows up in the specific way the manager *knew* it could, the absence of a specific disclosure is what turns a bad year into a lawsuit.

The second is **conflicts of interest**, which is where the structural truth of the business gets disclosed: the manager is paid a percentage of assets and a percentage of gains, which incentivizes both gathering assets and taking risk; the manager may run other funds or accounts that compete for the same trades; the manager allocates expenses between the fund and the management company; affiliated service providers may be used. Disclosing a conflict does not eliminate it — it makes acting within it defensible. An undisclosed conflict that surfaces later is a regulatory and reputational grenade.

The conflicts section is also where the **expense allocation** policy lives, and it is more contentious than founders expect. Some costs are clearly the fund's (audit, administration, brokerage, the cost of the data feeds used to trade); some are clearly the management company's (the founder's salary, the office, general overhead); and a large grey zone sits between them. Who pays for the research subscription that informs trades but also builds the firm's reputation? Who pays for the compliance consultant retained to keep the *manager* registered? A PPM that discloses a broad right to charge "research, technology, and operational expenses" to the fund gives the manager latitude — and gives a future investor or regulator a target if the manager abuses it. The SEC has brought enforcement actions against advisers who charged the fund for expenses the disclosure did not clearly cover. The lesson is that the expense-allocation language is not housekeeping; it is a clause that determines, every year, how much of the fund's return is quietly redirected to the management company, and an investor who reads carefully will ask exactly where the line sits.

A third underweighted section is the **strategy description** itself, which sits in a genuine tension. Disclose too little and the PPM fails its disclosure job — an investor cannot evaluate a strategy described as "opportunistic investing across asset classes," and a regulator may view such vagueness as evasive. Disclose too much and the manager hands competitors the alpha. The craft is to describe the strategy's *character* and *risks* fully — the markets it trades, the typical leverage, the concentration limits, the holding period, the factors it is exposed to — without disclosing the specific signals or models that generate the trades. The PPM should let an investor understand what could go wrong without teaching a rival how the fund makes money. A strategy section that is all marketing adjectives and no risk character is a section that will not protect the manager when the strategy behaves exactly as a more honest disclosure would have warned.

The terms summary inside the PPM must agree, word for word in substance, with the LPA. The PPM *summarizes* the binding terms; the LPA *is* the binding terms. When the two disagree, you have a document that promises one thing and a contract that delivers another, and that gap is litigation waiting to happen. One of the most common — and most embarrassing — formation errors is a PPM that describes a 90-day notice period while the LPA says 30, because the documents were drafted by different people at different times and never reconciled. Reconciling the PPM and the LPA is not optional polish; it is the difference between a coherent fund and a contradiction.

The PPM also matters because of *how* the fund is allowed to use it. Under Regulation D, most hedge funds rely on **Rule 506(b)**, which permits an offering to an unlimited number of accredited investors (plus up to 35 non-accredited ones) but **forbids general solicitation** — no advertising, no public website touting returns, no cold outreach to strangers. The alternative, **Rule 506(c)**, permits general solicitation but requires the manager to *verify* (not merely accept the investor's word) that every investor is accredited. Most funds choose 506(b) and keep the PPM strictly private, distributing it only to prospects with whom they have a pre-existing relationship. The mechanics of these exemptions are covered in [registration and regulation: the SEC, the CFTC, and Reg D](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d); what matters here is that the PPM's distribution is itself regulated, and mishandling it can blow the private-placement exemption the whole fund depends on.

#### Worked example: the cost of the document set

Maya's lawyer quoted her a formation package. For a lean, domestic-only structure — a single Delaware limited partnership, a Delaware GP entity, a PPM, an LPA, a subscription agreement, and an IMA — the all-in legal cost came to roughly \$50,000. When she later added an offshore Cayman feeder to take in non-US and tax-exempt investors (a master-feeder arrangement covered in [domestic vs offshore: the master-feeder structure](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder)), the document set roughly tripled, to around \$150,000, because the offshore entity, its own offering memorandum, and the master-feeder mechanics all needed drafting and coordinating across two jurisdictions.

Break that \$150,000 down the way Figure 7 does: entity setup runs perhaps \$20,000, the PPM drafting around \$40,000, the LPA around \$45,000, the subscription agreement plus IMA around \$20,000, and the Cayman feeder add-on around \$25,000. None of these is recoverable; the document set is a sunk one-time cost paid before the fund manages a single dollar. Against a fund that hopes to reach \$100M of AUM at a 1.5% management fee — \$1.5M of annual revenue — a \$150,000 document set is a tenth of one year's gross. Against a fund that launches at \$15M and earns \$225,000 a year, it is most of a year's revenue.

*The legal bill is the cheapest part of the fund's whole life; the expensive part is a clause inside those documents that you got wrong and live with for a decade.*

## The LPA / operating agreement

If the PPM is the document you hand to investors, the LPA is the document you live inside. It is the binding contract that governs the fund for its entire life, and almost every consequential fight a fund has is a fight about an LPA clause. Read it as a list of clauses, each of which protects somebody from something specific — that is the lens Figure 6 uses, and it is the only way to read an LPA without your eyes glazing over.

![A grid pairing six key LPA clauses with the specific party and failure mode each one protects against](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription-6.png)

**The fee clauses.** The LPA defines the **management fee** (a percentage of AUM, charged regardless of performance — historically 2%, now commonly 1.3–1.5%) and the **incentive fee** (a percentage of profits, historically 20%, now often 15–17%). The management fee funds the business: it pays the team, the rent, the data, the service providers. The incentive fee is the manager's upside — and it is structurally an option. The manager pays nothing if the fund loses money; the manager captures a slice of every gain. That asymmetry is the entire economic point of the structure, and the LPA is where its exact terms live: the percentages, when the incentive fee crystallizes (usually annually), and whether there is a hurdle rate (a return the fund must beat before any incentive fee applies). The full economics of these fees are dissected in [fees and terms: the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark); here the point is narrower — the fee terms are an LPA clause, and the LPA controls.

**The high-water mark clause.** This is the clause that protects investors from paying twice for the same gains. The incentive fee is charged only on gains above the prior peak NAV per share. If the fund makes 20% (an investor pays an incentive fee on that 20%), then loses 20% (no incentive fee, and the NAV is now below its prior peak), the manager earns *no* incentive fee on the climb back to that prior peak — only on gains above it. Without this clause, an investor would pay an incentive fee on the same dollars over and over as the fund yo-yoed. The high-water-mark clause is short, but it shapes the manager's incentives profoundly: a manager deep underwater earns no incentive fee until they recover, which is both a discipline (don't lose money) and a danger (a desperate manager underwater has an incentive to swing for the fences).

#### Worked example: the high-water mark in action

The Meridian Fund ends year one at a NAV of \$1.20 per share, up from \$1.00. On that \$0.20 of gain, Maya's management company earns its 20% incentive fee — \$0.04 per share — and the high-water mark is set at \$1.20. In year two the fund has a hard year and the NAV falls to \$0.96, a 20% drawdown from the \$1.20 peak. Maya earns no incentive fee that year. In year three the fund climbs 25%, from \$0.96 back to \$1.20. That is a strong year by any measure — and Maya still earns *zero* incentive fee, because \$1.20 only returns the fund to its prior high-water mark. Not until the NAV climbs above \$1.20 does the incentive fee resume, and then only on the gains above \$1.20.

The math is unforgiving: a 20% loss requires a 25% gain to recover (because \$0.96 × 1.25 = \$1.20), and that entire 25% recovery is incentive-fee-free. A manager who has a bad year is not just down on paper — they have signed away a year or more of their incentive economics, locked in by a one-paragraph clause in the LPA.

*The high-water mark is why the second-best year a fund can have is the recovery year: great for the investor, zero incentive fee for the manager.*

**The liquidity clauses.** This is the cluster that nearly sank Maya. The LPA defines the **lock-up** (a period — often one year — during which an investor cannot redeem at all, or can only redeem with a penalty), the **redemption frequency** (how often investors can pull capital — monthly, quarterly, or annually), the **notice period** (how far in advance — 30 to 90 days — an investor must give notice), the **gate** (a cap, often around 25%, on how much can be redeemed in a single period, at either the investor level or the fund level), and the **suspension** right (the manager's power to halt redemptions entirely in extreme conditions). The whole purpose of this cluster is to manage the **liquidity mismatch** — the danger of having promised investors faster access to their money than the manager can actually liquidate the portfolio without crushing it. A fund that offers monthly liquidity while holding positions that take a quarter to exit without market impact has written a contract it cannot honor in a crisis. The deeper mechanics of lock-ups, gates, and side pockets are in [liquidity terms: lock-ups, gates, and side pockets](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets); the point here is that all of it is LPA text, and the text Maya signed gave her none of these protections.

The before-and-after figure below makes the consequence concrete by showing the same redemption request hitting two funds with two different liquidity clauses.

![A before-after comparison of a sloppy fund liquidity clause and a tight one, showing the same redemption request producing a fire sale versus an orderly exit](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription-5.png)

**The key-person clause.** This protects investors against the fund losing the one or two people whose judgment is the actual product. A typical key-person clause names the principals (Maya, in Meridian's case) and specifies what happens if a key person dies, becomes incapacitated, or ceases to be substantially involved in managing the fund: investors may be granted a special redemption right (the ability to pull capital outside the normal liquidity terms, including during a lock-up), or the fund may be required to wind down, or a vote may be triggered. The clause exists because investors are not really buying a strategy — they are buying *Maya's* execution of it, and they want a contractual exit if Maya is no longer at the wheel.

#### Worked example: the key-person clause triggers

Eighteen months in, Maya is in a serious cycling accident and is hospitalized for six weeks, unable to manage the portfolio. The Meridian LPA's key-person clause defines a triggering event as Maya "ceasing to devote substantially all of her business time to the fund for a continuous period exceeding 30 days." At day 31, the clause triggers automatically. Under its terms, every limited partner is granted a 30-day special redemption right at the next month-end NAV, exercisable *regardless* of the one-year lock-up that would otherwise apply.

Investors holding \$60M of the fund's \$110M elect to redeem under the special right. Because the key-person clause overrides the lock-up, Maya's deputy — running the book in her absence — cannot invoke the lock-up to slow the outflow. The clause that exists to protect investors has, in a stroke, exposed the fund to losing more than half its assets while its founder is in a hospital bed. The lesson is not that key-person clauses are bad — they are standard and investors demand them — but that the *trigger definition* (30 days? 60? "substantially all" versus "primary" involvement?) and the *consequence* (special redemption versus a vote versus wind-down) are negotiable, and the founder who does not negotiate them carefully has written a fragility into the fund's constitution.

*A key-person clause turns the founder's health into a liquidity event; the only question the LPA decides is how violent that event is.*

**The indemnification clause.** This protects the *manager*. It provides that the fund will indemnify the GP, the management company, and their personnel — covering legal costs and liabilities — for actions taken in good faith on the fund's behalf, typically excluding fraud, gross negligence, and willful misconduct. The clause matters enormously: without it, a manager sued by a disgruntled investor over a legitimate, good-faith investment decision would pay their own defense costs and could be personally liable for an honest loss. With it, the fund's assets stand behind the manager for ordinary business judgment, while still leaving the manager personally exposed for genuine misconduct. The boundary — what counts as "gross negligence" versus an honest mistake — is one of the most heavily negotiated phrases in the entire LPA.

#### Worked example: indemnification covers the manager

In year two, an investor who redeemed at a loss sues Maya personally, alleging that a concentrated position that hurt the fund was reckless. The position was disclosed in the PPM's strategy section, sized within the fund's stated risk limits, and entered in good faith based on Maya's research. The Meridian LPA's indemnification clause provides that the fund will advance Maya's legal fees and indemnify her against any judgment for acts taken in good faith and within her authority, excluding fraud and gross negligence.

Maya's legal defense runs to \$300,000 over a year. Under the indemnification clause, the fund advances those fees from fund assets rather than Maya paying out of pocket, and because a court ultimately finds her conduct was a good-faith business judgment well within the disclosed strategy, the indemnification holds and she bears none of the cost personally. Had the LPA's indemnification standard been drafted narrowly — say, requiring Maya to prove the decision was "prudent" rather than merely "in good faith" — she might have funded that defense herself. The clause did exactly its job: it let Maya make a defensible decision that lost money without that loss becoming a personal financial catastrophe.

*Indemnification is the clause that lets a manager take real risk on the fund's behalf without betting their house on every trade; its exact wording is the size of that protection.*

**The amendment clause.** This is the meta-clause: the rule for changing all the other rules. It specifies the consent required to amend the LPA — commonly a "majority in interest," meaning LPs holding more than half the fund's capital, though some amendments (especially those that disadvantage LPs, like raising fees or worsening liquidity) require a higher supermajority or unanimous consent. The amendment clause is what stops the GP from quietly rewriting the deal to its own advantage; it is also what can trap a fund that needs to fix a genuinely broken clause but cannot assemble the votes. A well-drafted amendment clause balances stability (the rules can't shift under investors' feet) against flexibility (the fund can adapt). Most LPAs also reserve to the GP a right to make non-material or administrative amendments unilaterally, with material amendments requiring the LP vote.

#### Worked example: an amendment needs a majority in interest

Maya wants to add a fund-level gate to the LPA after her redemption scare — a protection she should have had from day one. The Meridian LPA's amendment clause requires the consent of a "majority in interest," defined as limited partners holding more than 50% of the fund's aggregate capital. The fund has \$110M of AUM. To pass the amendment, Maya needs the affirmative consent of LPs holding more than \$55M.

She also discovers the catch: because adding a gate *worsens* liquidity for existing investors, the LPA requires not a simple majority but a two-thirds supermajority for any amendment that "materially adversely affects" LP redemption rights — so she actually needs holders of more than \$73.3M (two-thirds of \$110M) to consent. The fund-of-funds holding \$40M, still smarting from its blocked redemption desire, refuses. Without it, Maya can muster only \$58M of consents — a simple majority, but short of the supermajority the clause demands. The protection she needs most is the one her own amendment clause makes hardest to add. She is left to apply the gate only to *new* investors via a new share class, leaving the existing book ungated.

*The amendment clause decides whether a fund can fix its own mistakes; a founder who drafts it too rigidly is locking in today's errors against tomorrow's wisdom.*

## The subscription agreement

The subscription agreement is the document an investor signs to commit capital, and its center of gravity is **representations** — legally binding statements the investor makes about themselves, under penalty of having lied in a securities transaction. The fund relies on these representations to stay inside its regulatory exemptions, so they are not boilerplate to be skimmed; they are the load-bearing facts that keep the offering legal.

The first and most important representation is about **qualification**. Depending on which Investment Company Act exemption the fund uses, the investor must represent that they are either an **accredited investor** or a **qualified purchaser** (and, separately, a **qualified client** if the fund charges an incentive fee). These are precise legal thresholds, and the subscription agreement is where the investor swears they meet them:

- **Accredited investor** — individual income above \$200,000 (or \$300,000 jointly) for the past two years with the expectation of the same this year, *or* net worth above \$1M excluding the primary residence. Certain license holders (Series 7, 65, or 82) also qualify. This is the baseline threshold for a Rule 506 offering.
- **Qualified purchaser** — an individual with at least \$5M in investments, or an entity with at least \$25M. A fund relying on the 3(c)(7) exemption (which allows unlimited investors) requires *every* investor to be a qualified purchaser.
- **Qualified client** — at least \$1.1M managed by the adviser or net worth above \$2.2M (thresholds indexed for inflation). Under Advisers Act Rule 205-3, a registered adviser may charge a performance fee *only* to qualified clients. This is why a fund's true investor floor is often higher than the headline minimum: to charge the incentive fee legally, every investor must clear the qualified-client bar.

The subscription agreement also collects the investor's **anti-money-laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC)** information: identity verification, the source of funds, beneficial-ownership disclosure for entities, and screening against sanctions lists. The administrator — not the manager — typically runs this check, which is both a regulatory requirement and an operational-due-diligence checkbox that allocators look for. A fund that admits capital without proper KYC is exposed to admitting tainted money, and to a regulator's wrath if it does. The subscription agreement is also where the investor represents that they are buying for their own account and not with a view to immediate resale (preserving the private-placement exemption), that they have received and read the PPM, that they can bear the total loss of the investment, and that they are not relying on any representation outside the PPM itself — a clause that ties the investor back to the disclosure document and away from any verbal sales pitch.

The pipeline below traces the full subscription flow: an investor reads the PPM, reviews the LPA and terms, signs the subscription agreement and makes their representations, proves their qualification, clears KYC and AML, has the administrator verify and counter-sign, wires the capital, and is finally admitted to the fund with a capital account struck at the prevailing NAV. Every step is a gate, and the administrator can stop the line at any of them.

![A pipeline showing the subscription flow from reading the PPM through KYC and AML checks to admission with a NAV capital account](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription-3.png)

The reason the representations matter so much is that the fund's regulatory exemptions depend on the investors *actually* being what they represent. If a fund relying on 3(c)(7) admits an investor who is not really a qualified purchaser, the exemption can be jeopardized for the *entire* fund — one bad admission can taint everyone. This is why funds increasingly require documentation rather than a mere checkbox, and why the administrator's verification step exists. The subscription agreement is the legal instrument that places the burden of qualification squarely on the investor's signed word, with the administrator as the backstop.

The subscription agreement also gives the fund a remedy when a representation turns out to be false. It typically includes the investor's agreement to **indemnify the fund** for any loss caused by a misrepresentation — so if an investor falsely claimed to be a qualified purchaser and that false claim later jeopardizes the fund's exemption, the cost falls back on the investor who lied, not on the fund or the other investors. It also gives the fund the right to **compulsorily redeem** an investor whose continued participation would breach the fund's terms or threaten its exemptions — the eject button the fund uses to remove an investor who turns out not to qualify, or whose AML profile deteriorates, or who becomes a regulatory problem. Founders rarely think about the compulsory-redemption right at launch, but it is the clause that lets the fund protect the *other* investors from a single bad admission, and a fund without it is stuck with whoever it let in. The representations and these remedies, taken together, are why the subscription agreement is far more than a check-the-box form: it is the contract that keeps the whole offering inside the law, with consequences attached for the investor who breaks it.

## The IMA

The investment management agreement is the contract between the fund and the management company, and it does three things: it **grants authority**, it **sets the fee**, and it **defines termination**.

The **grant of authority** is the heart of it. The IMA delegates to the management company the discretionary power to manage the fund's assets — to buy and sell securities, enter derivatives, use leverage, and direct the prime broker — within the limits the fund's documents set. This is what makes Maya's firm the fund's legal investment manager rather than a third party with opinions. Counterparties (the prime broker, the administrator, the executing brokers) rely on the IMA to know who is authorized to act for the fund. Without a clean IMA, the chain of authority is muddy, and a prime broker cannot be sure whose instructions to honor.

The **fee provision** in the IMA (or, in simpler structures, in the LPA) specifies the management fee the management company is paid for providing these services. This is where the management fee — the percentage of AUM that funds the business — is contractually owed to the management company. The incentive fee, by contrast, usually flows through the GP's allocation in the LPA rather than the IMA, because it is structured as a special profit allocation to the general partner rather than a fee for services (a distinction that has tax consequences for the manager). The exact plumbing varies, but the IMA is where the management company's right to be paid for managing is documented.

The **termination provision** is the one founders ignore at their peril. It defines how and when the fund can fire its manager, and how and when the manager can resign. A fund's board or its investors typically have the right to terminate the IMA "for cause" (fraud, gross negligence, a regulatory bar, a material breach) and sometimes "without cause" on notice. For an offshore fund with an independent board, the termination clause is a real check on the manager; for a typical onshore GP-controlled fund, the manager is harder to dislodge because the GP controls the entity. A founder negotiating an IMA — especially with a seed investor who may want the right to replace the manager — should treat the termination clause as one of the most important sentences in the document set, because it determines whether the founder actually controls the business they built.

The IMA also typically specifies the **standard of care** the manager owes the fund, the manager's right to delegate (to a sub-adviser, for instance), the manager's obligation to maintain its own regulatory registrations, and the allocation of expenses between the management fee and the fund — the same expense question the PPM discloses, now made contractual. And it sets the management fee's *mechanics*: charged monthly or quarterly, in advance or in arrears, on which measure of assets. These mechanics, dull as they sound, are real cash flow to the management company, and a founder who lets them be drafted carelessly may find the fund's first months of fees arriving later or smaller than the business plan assumed.

#### Worked example: the management fee the IMA delivers

The Meridian Fund launches at \$15M of AUM. Maya's IMA sets a 1.5% annual management fee, charged quarterly in advance on the AUM at the start of each quarter. In the first quarter, the management company is owed 1.5% ÷ 4 = 0.375% of \$15M, or about \$56,250, paid up front — the cash that covers the administrator, the audit retainer, the data feeds, and a thin salary. Over the full first year, if assets hold near \$15M, the management fee delivers roughly \$225,000 of revenue.

Set that against the fund's fixed costs. A lean institutional-grade setup runs on the order of \$0.5M a year once the administrator, audit, outside compliance, and basic technology are paid. At \$15M of AUM, the \$225,000 management fee covers less than half of that — Maya is funding the gap out of pocket or out of her incentive fee, and her break-even AUM, the assets she needs just to cover those fixed costs from the 1.5% fee, is roughly \$0.5M ÷ 0.015 = \$33M. The IMA's fee clause is what turns AUM into the revenue that keeps the lights on, and at launch scale that revenue is thin enough that the *mechanics* — in advance versus in arrears, on gross versus net assets — are the difference between making payroll and not.

*The management fee is not the founder's paycheck; at launch it is barely the fund's electricity bill, and the IMA is the clause that decides when that trickle of cash actually arrives.*

## How they fit together

Step back and look at the four documents as a single machine, the way Figure 1 lays them out. They are not redundant; they are a relay. The **PPM** describes and discloses — it is the public face of the offering, the document an investor reads to decide. It *summarizes* the LPA's terms and *attaches* the subscription agreement. The **LPA** is the binding reality behind the PPM's summary — it governs the fund, it *authorizes* the IMA, and it is the document the side letter *amends*. The **subscription agreement** is the on-ramp: it commits the investor and feeds the representations the PPM and the exemptions rely on. The **IMA** is the engine mount — it bolts the management company onto the fund and gives it the authority to act.

The critical property of this machine is **internal consistency**. Every term that appears in more than one document must say the same thing in all of them. The fee in the PPM summary must equal the fee in the LPA must equal the fee in the IMA. The liquidity terms in the PPM must equal the liquidity terms in the LPA. The qualification standard in the subscription agreement must match the exemption the PPM claims. When these drift apart — usually because documents were drafted at different times or by different hands — the fund has a latent contradiction that surfaces at the worst possible moment, in a dispute, when an investor points at the document that favors them and the manager points at the one that favors them, and a court has to decide which controls. Reconciling the document set is the single most valuable thing a founder can do before launch and the thing most likely to be rushed in the final pre-launch week.

The relationship to the fund's *entities* is worth restating. The documents and the entities are two views of the same structure: the entities (covered in [the fund structure: management company vs the fund](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund)) are the legal *persons* — the fund, the GP, the management company; the documents are the *contracts* between those persons. The LPA is the contract between the GP and the LPs; the IMA is the contract between the fund and the management company; the subscription agreement is the contract between the investor and the fund. You cannot understand one view without the other. A founder who understands the entity diagram but not the documents knows the skeleton but not the joints.

## The clauses that bite

Some clauses are dangerous not because they are exotic but because they are easy to get subtly wrong, and the cost of the error compounds over the fund's life. Here are the ones that most often come back to bite.

**The liquidity clause** is first because it is the one that nearly took Maya down, and it is the most common founder error. The temptation at launch is to offer generous liquidity — monthly redemptions, a short notice period, no lock-up — because generous liquidity is easier to sell to investors who fear being trapped. But generous liquidity creates a liquidity mismatch: the fund promises faster access than it can deliver in a crisis. The right liquidity terms match the *liquidity of the underlying portfolio*. A fund trading large-cap equities can offer monthly liquidity honestly; a fund holding distressed credit or small-cap names cannot, and should offer quarterly redemptions with a 90-day notice, a one-year lock-up, and a gate. The clause that is easiest to sell is often the one that kills the fund.

**The high-water-mark reset clause.** A subtle variant: what happens to the high-water mark when an investor redeems part of their stake, or when a *new* investor comes in at a different NAV? Funds that issue interests as a single commingled pool face the "equalization" problem — a new investor entering after the fund has gains either unfairly benefits from or is unfairly charged for incentive fees attributable to a period before they invested. The standard fix is a **series accounting** or **equalization** methodology, and getting it wrong means the fund either over-charges or under-charges incentive fees, both of which are administrator headaches and potential disputes. This clause is invisible until the fund has both gains and ongoing subscriptions — exactly when it is hardest to fix.

**The key-person trigger definition.** As Maya's accident showed, the *definition* of the triggering event matters as much as the consequence. "Substantially all of her business time" is a stricter standard than "primary involvement," and a 30-day trigger is far more sensitive than a 90-day one. A trigger that is too sensitive turns a temporary absence into a liquidity event; a trigger that is too loose fails to protect investors when the founder genuinely checks out. The negotiation is about where to set that dial.

**The indemnification standard.** The phrase that defines what conduct loses the manager's indemnification — "gross negligence," "willful misconduct," "fraud," "bad faith," "reckless disregard" — is heavily negotiated because it draws the line between an honest mistake (covered) and culpable conduct (not covered). A manager wants the broadest possible protection; investors want the manager personally exposed for real misconduct. The exact words determine, years later, whether the fund or the founder pays for a lawsuit.

**The fee-calculation clause.** Whether the management fee is charged on gross AUM or net assets, whether it is charged in advance or in arrears, how it handles subscriptions and redemptions mid-period, and exactly when the incentive fee crystallizes — these mechanical details, buried in the LPA's definitions, determine the actual dollars that flow to the manager. A clause that crystallizes the incentive fee monthly rather than annually, for instance, lets the manager lock in fees on a good month even if the year ends down — a term investors increasingly reject. Small wording differences here are real money.

**The side-letter / most-favored-nation interaction.** A side letter that grants one investor a fee discount with an MFN clause ("if you give anyone better terms, I get them too") can cascade: a later, larger investor's better terms automatically flow back to the MFN holder, eroding the fund's economics in a way the founder did not foresee when signing the first side letter. Side letters must be tracked and their interactions modeled, or the fund's effective fee schedule drifts below what the LPA says.

## Common misconceptions

**"The PPM is a marketing document."** It is the opposite. The PPM's job is *disclosure*, and the better it does that job, the *less* it reads like marketing — it spends most of its length cataloging risks and conflicts. The marketing happens in a separate pitch deck and one-pager (which themselves must comply with the SEC Marketing Rule, including showing net-of-fee performance). Treating the PPM as a sales document is how managers end up with risk sections too thin to protect them. The PPM exists to make the manager's downside defensible, not to make the fund look good.

**"The documents are standard, so I don't need to read them."** They are standard in *form* — every PPM has risk factors, every LPA has a liquidity clause — but the *contents* of those clauses are where every fund-specific decision lives, and those decisions are negotiable and consequential. "Standard" describes the table of contents, not the terms. Maya's LPA was perfectly standard in form and catastrophic in substance, because the liquidity clause within the standard structure was wrong for her strategy.

**"The lawyer handles the documents, so they're the lawyer's responsibility."** The lawyer drafts; the founder *decides*. A lawyer can tell Maya what a 30-day notice period means and what a one-year lock-up means, but the lawyer cannot know that Maya's strategy holds positions that take a quarter to exit — only Maya knows that, and only Maya can decide that the liquidity terms must match. The business judgments embedded in the documents are the founder's, and a founder who delegates them to the lawyer has delegated the fund's fate to someone who does not run the portfolio.

**"Offshore documents mean something shady."** A Cayman feeder and its offering memorandum are not a red flag — they are the standard, legitimate way a US fund accepts non-US and US-tax-exempt investors (like endowments and pensions) without creating adverse tax consequences for them. The structure is disclosed, audited, and regulated. Conflating an offshore feeder with secrecy confuses tax efficiency with tax evasion; they are not the same thing, and the documents make the structure fully transparent to investors and regulators.

**"Once the documents are signed, they're fixed."** The LPA contains an amendment clause precisely because the rules can change — with the required consent. But as Maya discovered, the amendment threshold can make a needed fix nearly impossible to enact, which is why the *initial* drafting matters so much: the easiest time to get a clause right is before anyone has signed it, because after launch you need a majority — or a supermajority — to change it.

**"The fund is the business I own."** A founder often conflates the *fund* (the pool of investor capital, governed by the LPA, in which investors hold interests) with the *management company* (the business the founder actually owns, which earns the fees). The documents draw this line sharply: the founder owns the management company and the GP; the investors own the fund. The IMA is the bridge between them. Confusing the two is how founders make decisions that benefit the fund at the management company's expense, or vice versa, without realizing they are trading one pocket for another.

## How it plays out in the real world

The starkest real-world lesson about fund documents is **Madoff**, revealed in December 2008 as the largest Ponzi scheme in history — roughly \$65B in fabricated statement value, with around \$17.5B of actual principal lost. Madoff's documents existed, but the *structure they described* was the fraud: there was no independent administrator striking an honest NAV, no real custody of assets, and the auditor was a tiny, unknown firm. The documents promised oversight that the structure did not deliver. Every operational-due-diligence checklist written since is, in part, a reaction to Madoff: an allocator now reads the documents *and* verifies that the independent administrator, the real auditor, and the genuine custody the documents describe actually exist. Documents that describe controls the fund does not have are worse than no documents — they are a false promise.

The **liquidity-clause** lesson played out across the industry in 2008. Funds that had offered generous liquidity on illiquid portfolios faced redemption requests they could not honor without crushing their remaining investors. Many invoked **gates** and **suspensions** — clauses buried in their LPAs that most investors had never focused on — and discovered that those clauses, dormant for years, were suddenly the most important terms in the fund. Funds with well-drafted gates and side-pocket provisions could ring-fence illiquid positions and honor redemptions in an orderly way; funds without them either broke their own terms or fire-sold their books. The clause Maya skimmed is the same clause that decided which 2008 funds survived intact and which ones imploded under their own redemption promises.

The **key-person** lesson appears whenever a fund's founder leaves, dies, or is incapacitated. Investors who negotiated a tight key-person clause got their contractual exit; investors who did not were stranded in a fund run by people they had never underwritten. And the **indemnification** lesson appears in the litigation that follows any major loss — managers who drafted a broad good-faith indemnification standard had the fund's assets behind their defense; managers who accepted a narrow "prudence" standard found themselves personally funding their lawyers. None of these are exotic edge cases. They are the ordinary ways that funds are tested, and in every one of them, the outcome was decided not by the trade but by a clause.

Maya's own story ends better than it might have. The \$40M redemption was honored — painfully, with weeks of careful selling that cost the remaining investors a few tenths of a percent — but the fund survived. Maya spent the next quarter doing what she should have done before launch: she read every clause of her own documents, line by line, with her lawyer beside her translating, and she built a new share class for incoming capital with a one-year lock-up, a 90-day notice period, quarterly redemptions, and a 25% gate. She could not retroactively impose those terms on her existing investors — the amendment clause made that nearly impossible — but she could make sure that every dollar that entered the Meridian Fund from that day forward came in under terms that matched her strategy. The expensive lesson was not the legal bill. It was the two years she spent not reading the contract that was, in every sense that mattered, her business.

## When this matters / Further reading

These four documents matter most at exactly three moments: **before launch**, when every clause is still negotiable and getting them right is cheap; **when raising capital**, when sophisticated allocators read the LPA more carefully than the founder did and ask why the liquidity terms do not match the strategy; and **in a crisis**, when a dormant clause — a gate, a key-person trigger, an indemnification standard — suddenly becomes the most important fact about the fund. The founder who treats the document set as a one-time legal chore to be delegated and forgotten is the founder most likely to discover, two years in, that a clause they skimmed decides their fate. Read the documents. They are the fund.
