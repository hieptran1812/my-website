> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Raising capital is not a returns contest; it is a credibility audit, and the toolkit that wins it is a tight pitch book, a defensible track-record presentation, a complete due-diligence questionnaire, and a clean data room.
>
> - The **pitch book** leads with the firm, the people, and the edge; the returns slide sits in the *middle*, because allocators buy a repeatable process run by people they trust, not a backtest.
> - The **track record** must show **net beside gross** (the SEC Marketing Rule requires it), name the right benchmark, and break the return down with **attribution** — gross-only numbers read as a red flag, not a strength.
> - The **DDQ** standardizes you into six comparable sections, and the **data room** is where every claim in the pitch book gets verified against a document; what does not reconcile sinks the raise.
> - Allocators score the famous **five Ps** — **people, philosophy/process, performance, portfolio, operations** — and the number to remember is that a great-returns fund is routinely **vetoed on operations alone**, so credibility and process carry at least as much weight as performance.

Maya had rehearsed the pitch for the Meridian Fund a dozen times, and the slide she was proudest of was the track record: a clean equity curve, up a cumulative 41% over two years, a Sharpe a hair above one, drawn against a flat line for the market. She opened with it. The allocator across the table — a senior diligence officer at a USD 6B fund-of-funds — let her get three sentences in, then did something Maya did not expect. He flipped *past* the returns slide. Past the next one too. He stopped on slide nine, the one Maya had thrown together the night before, and asked: "Who strikes your NAV, and can they move cash without your sign-off?"

That question had nothing to do with returns and everything to do with whether he would ever recommend writing Maya a cheque. He was not impressed by 41%; he had seen a hundred decks with a 41% on the front. He was testing whether the *operation* behind the 41% was real — whether the number was independently produced, whether her cash was controlled, whether the fund could survive her getting hit by a bus. He spent forty of the meeting's forty-five minutes on the team, the service providers, and the controls, and almost none on the trades. Maya left thinking the meeting had gone badly. In fact it had gone exactly as a real diligence meeting goes, and she had no slide ready for the part that mattered most.

That is the lesson this post is built around, and it is worth stating plainly before anything else: **allocators evaluate people and process at least as much as performance.** The returns slide is the price of admission, not the sale. The sale is made — or lost — on whether an experienced allocator believes the edge is real and repeatable, the team is stable and aligned, and the operation is sound enough that they will never have to explain to *their* investors why they put money into a fund that blew up on a control gap. The toolkit that answers those questions is the subject of this post: the pitch book, the track-record presentation, the due-diligence questionnaire (the DDQ), and the data room. Figure 1 is the anatomy of the first of them — the slides of a pitch book, in the order a good one puts them.

This post sits downstream of [the LP landscape: who allocates](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates), which maps the investor types you are pitching to — funds-of-funds, pensions, endowments, family offices. Here we move from *who* you are pitching to *what* you put in front of them and how they take it apart. And it sits upstream of the one process that can kill a raise after everything else has gone right: [operational due diligence, the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto). Keep that veto in mind throughout — every document in this post is read, in part, as evidence for or against it.

## Foundations: the documents of a raise

Before the craft of each document, the vocabulary. A capital raise produces a small, specific set of artifacts, each with a distinct job, and conflating them is the first mistake founders make. Get the jobs straight and the raise stops being a mystery.

**The pitch book (the deck).** A short presentation — typically fifteen to thirty slides — that tells the fund's story: who runs it, what the edge is, how the strategy works, what it has returned, how it is positioned, what the terms are, and how the operation is built. Its job is to get a "yes, let's go to diligence." It is the *opening argument*, not the evidence. A pitch book persuades; it does not prove. The proof comes later, in the data room.

**The track-record presentation.** The performance section of the raise — the equity curve, the monthly returns, the statistics — done to a professional standard. The two words that govern it are **gross** and **net**. *Gross* returns are what the strategy earned before fees; *net* returns are what an investor actually pockets after the management fee and the incentive fee are taken out. An allocator cares almost entirely about net, because net is what they will earn, and showing gross-only is one of the fastest ways to look amateur or evasive.

**The management fee and the incentive fee.** The two fees that create the gap between gross and net. The **management fee** is an annual percentage of assets under management (AUM) — historically 2%, now averaging closer to 1.3–1.5% (HFR fee data, 2024–2025) — charged whether the fund makes money or not. The **incentive fee** (or performance fee) is a share of the profits — historically 20%, now averaging roughly 15–17% — charged only on gains, and typically only on gains above the prior peak (the **high-water mark**). The mechanics of these fees and the high-water mark are the subject of [fees and terms: the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark); here they matter because they are the wedge between the gross number a founder is proud of and the net number an allocator buys.

**Attribution.** Breaking a return down into *where it came from* — which positions, which bets, which factors. A fund up 18% has a number; a fund that can say "9% from long stock selection, 2% from shorts, 3% from a factor tilt, and 4% from one concentrated winner" has a *story*, and the story is what tells an allocator whether the edge is repeatable skill or a single lucky leg.

**GIPS.** The Global Investment Performance Standards — a voluntary, globally recognized standard for how investment performance is calculated and presented. A GIPS-compliant track record follows strict rules about how returns are computed, how composites are formed, and what must be disclosed. Institutions look for GIPS compliance because it removes the manager's discretion to flatter the numbers; it signals "you can trust how this was calculated."

**The SEC Marketing Rule.** Rule 206(4)-1 under the Investment Advisers Act (compliance date November 2022) governs how a US investment adviser advertises performance. The rule that bites hardest here: if you show gross performance, you must show **net performance** with at least equal prominence, computed consistently. The marketing of the fund — and the limits this rule places on it — is the subject of [marketing and the SEC marketing rule](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule); for now the point is that net-beside-gross is not a stylistic choice, it is a regulatory requirement, and a deck that shows gross-only is also a compliance problem.

**The DDQ (due-diligence questionnaire).** A standardized questionnaire an allocator sends a fund, asking the same battery of questions of every manager — strategy, team, operations, compliance, terms, references. Its purpose is to make funds *comparable*: by forcing every manager into the same template, an allocator can lay ten funds side by side and score them on the same axes. A good DDQ answer is complete, specific, and consistent with everything else the fund has said.

**The data room.** A secure document repository — historically a literal locked room of paper, now a permissioned cloud folder — where the allocator's diligence team finds the *evidence* behind every claim. Audited financials, the fund documents, the service-provider contracts, the compliance manual, the team's background checks. The pitch book makes claims; the data room is where those claims are verified.

**The five Ps.** The mental model allocators use to organize their evaluation: **People, Philosophy** (often expanded to philosophy *and* process), **Performance, Portfolio, Operations.** Different shops phrase the five Ps slightly differently — some say "process" instead of "philosophy," some add "price" (the terms) as a sixth — but the structure is consistent: performance is *one* of five dimensions, and the other four are about whether the performance is real, repeatable, and safely produced. Figure 2 lays the five Ps out against what an allocator looks for in each and what fails the column.

![A matrix with five rows for people, philosophy and process, performance, portfolio, and operations and columns for what allocators ask and what kills the score](/imgs/blogs/the-pitch-book-ddq-and-the-data-room-2.png)

The single most useful thing to internalize from the figure is the asymmetry. Performance is a single column, and it is *necessary* — a fund that has lost money for two years will not raise regardless of how good its operation is. But performance is not *sufficient*, and the other four columns are where most raises are actually won or lost. A fund can have a brilliant track record and be rejected because the founder is the only person who understands the strategy (a People failure), or because the edge cannot be articulated (a Process failure), or because the book is one concentrated bet (a Portfolio failure), or because the administrator is not independent (an Operations failure — the ODD veto). The founder who spends 80% of the raise polishing the Performance column and 20% on the other four has the weighting exactly backwards.

## The pitch book

Start with the deck, because it is the first thing an allocator sees and the document that decides whether there is a second meeting. The cardinal error founders make is treating the pitch book like a *returns advertisement* — front-loading the equity curve, making it big and green, and burying everything else. That ordering reflects what the founder is proud of, not what the allocator is buying.

A good pitch book is ordered to answer the allocator's questions in the order they ask them, and an experienced allocator's first questions are not about returns. They are: *Who are you? Why should I believe you have an edge? Is that edge repeatable?* Only then: *What has it returned?* And then immediately: *How is it positioned, what does it cost, and is the operation real?* The deck in Figure 1 follows exactly that order.

**Slide one: the firm and the ask.** Who the firm is, what it manages (or is targeting), and what you are raising — the strategy, the target AUM, the capacity, the share classes on offer. This slide sets the frame. An allocator who runs a billion-dollar mandate and sees you are raising USD 20M into a strategy with USD 50M of capacity now knows, in ten seconds, whether you fit their book at all. Be honest about size; pretending to be bigger than you are is caught instantly and poisons everything that follows.

**The team.** Who runs the money, their pedigree, their tenure together, and — critically — their *alignment*. Allocators want to see that the founder has meaningful personal capital in the fund ("skin in the game"), that there is a team and not just one irreplaceable person, and that the key people are locked in rather than likely to leave. The team slide is where the **People** P is argued, and it is the slide a returns-first deck tragically under-builds.

**The edge and the process.** What the fund does, *why it makes money*, and *how* the team finds and sizes positions. This is the **Philosophy/Process** P, and it is the hardest slide to do well because it requires articulating an edge specifically enough to be credible without giving away the alpha. "We invest opportunistically across asset classes with high conviction" is not an edge; it is a sentence that survives any market and therefore says nothing. "We exploit the post-earnings-announcement drift in mid-cap industrials, holding 30–50 names for three to six weeks, sized by signal strength and capped at 4% gross per name" is an edge — it can be evaluated, stress-tested, and either believed or not. The craft of building and evaluating a signal lives in [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research); the pitch-book job is to describe the edge's *character* honestly while protecting the secret sauce.

**The track record.** Now, in the middle of the deck, the performance. Gross and net side by side, the right benchmark, the key statistics (annualized return, Sharpe, worst drawdown, percentage of positive months), and attribution. This is the **Performance** P. The next section covers exactly how to present it.

**The portfolio.** Current positioning, gross and net exposure, sector and factor concentration, the largest positions, the liquidity profile, and the risk limits. This is the **Portfolio** P. The allocator is checking whether the portfolio matches the story (a "diversified long/short" fund whose book is three names is lying somewhere) and whether the liquidity of the positions matches the liquidity offered to investors. A liquidity *mismatch* — offering monthly redemptions on assets that take months to sell — is a classic fund-killer, covered in [liquidity terms: lock-ups, gates, side pockets](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets).

**Risk and operations.** The risk framework (how the fund limits and monitors risk) and the service-provider stack — the administrator, the prime broker(s), the auditor, the law firm, the compliance setup. This is the **Operations** P, and a strong deck treats it as a *selling point*, not a footnote. Naming a recognized administrator, a Big Four auditor, and multiple prime brokers is itself an argument: it says "an experienced diligence team will not find a control gap here."

**The terms.** Fees, the high-water mark, the lock-up, the redemption frequency and notice period, gates, the minimum investment, and any founders share class. This is the *deal*. A **founders share class** — a fee discount for early or large investors, e.g. 1.5%/15% or 1%/10% versus the standard 2/20, often capped by a time window or an AUM ceiling — is a standard tool for rewarding the investors who take launch risk.

Two craft points separate a professional deck from an amateur one. The first is **length and discipline**: a strong pitch book is *short*. Fifteen to twenty-five slides, each making one claim cleanly, with the dense detail pushed to an appendix the allocator can mine if they want it. A founder's instinct is to add — another performance cut, another case study, another page of biography — until the deck is sixty slides and the argument is buried. Allocators read dozens of decks a month; the one that respects their time with a tight, well-ordered story signals a manager who can prioritize, which is itself a People-and-Process signal. The detail belongs in the DDQ and the data room, where the diligence team goes looking for it; the deck's job is to earn the right to be asked for that detail.

The second is knowing **what not to put in**. Do not put the secret sauce — the specific signals, the model parameters, the exact trades — in a deck that will circulate. Do not put forward-looking return *promises* ("we target 15% annually"); allocators read targets as either naive or a compliance problem, and the SEC Marketing Rule treats projected performance with particular suspicion. Do not put a flattering cherry-picked period as the headline track ("since our best quarter"). And do not put in a benchmark chosen to flatter. Each of these is a tell that an experienced reader catches instantly, and each converts a credibility-building document into a credibility-destroying one. The deck that omits the secret sauce, makes no promises, shows the full honest track, and uses the right benchmark is the deck that survives the scrutiny the rest of this post describes.

#### Worked example: the meeting-to-allocation conversion math

Maya's advisor told her to think of fundraising as a funnel with brutal conversion at every stage, and to do the arithmetic before she set her expectations. Suppose Maya runs **100** first meetings over an 18-month raise. A realistic — and still optimistic — funnel for an unknown first-time manager: roughly **1 in 4** first meetings produce enough interest to advance to a DDQ and a deeper look, so **25** funds reach the questionnaire stage. Of those, maybe **40%** survive the DDQ and the onsite to enter formal investment diligence — **10** funds. Investment diligence and the separate operational diligence each kill some: say **half** clear both vetoes and reach an investment committee — **5** funds. And of those committees, perhaps **half** vote yes and actually wire — **2 to 3** allocations.

So 100 first meetings convert to roughly **2 to 3 cheques**. If Maya's average ticket is \$8M, that is \$16M–24M raised from 100 meetings — and 100 quality first meetings is itself most of a year of full-time travel and introductions. Run the funnel the other way: if Maya needs \$80M to clear her break-even AUM, she needs roughly **10 cheques** at \$8M each, which at a 2–3% meeting-to-cheque rate means she needs to source **300 to 500 quality first meetings.** That is why raising takes years, not months, and why warm introductions (which convert far better than cold ones) are worth so much.

*The raise is a numbers game with a savage funnel; the document toolkit exists to raise the conversion rate at every stage, not to skip the funnel.*

## The track-record presentation

The performance section is where founders most often shoot themselves in the foot, because the instinct is to make the number look as big as possible, and every instinct toward flattering it reads to an allocator as a reason to distrust it. The discipline of a credible track record runs in the opposite direction: show the number *honestly*, *net of fees*, against the *right comparison*, broken down into its *sources*. Each of those four is a place a weak presentation fails.

**Gross versus net.** This is the first and most important distinction. Gross is what the strategy returned; net is what the investor keeps after the management fee and the incentive fee. Allocators care about net, full stop, and the SEC Marketing Rule requires that any gross figure you advertise be shown with net of equal prominence. A deck that leads with a gross number — or worse, shows only gross — signals either that the founder does not understand what investors buy or that the net number is materially less impressive and is being hidden. Neither is a good signal.

#### Worked example: a gross-to-net presentation of a \$100M track over a year

Take the Meridian Fund at \$100M of AUM, running a 1.5% management fee and a 17% incentive fee over the prior peak. In its best year the strategy was up a **gross 18.0%** — a \$18M gain on \$100M. Now walk it down to net, the way Figure 3 does.

First, the **management fee**: 1.5% of \$100M is \$1.5M, charged through the year regardless of performance. That drops the gross 18.0% to **16.5%** net of management fee. Then the **incentive fee**: 17% of the gain *after* the management fee — 17% of that 16.5%, which on \$100M is 17% of \$16.5M, or about **\$2.8M**. Subtract that, and the investor's **net return is 13.7%** (\$13.7M of \$100M). The total fee load is **4.3% of NAV** — \$1.5M of management fee plus \$2.8M of incentive fee — which is the wedge between the founder's proud 18.0% and the investor's 13.7%.

That 4.3% wedge is not hidden; it is the deal. But an allocator who is shown "up 18%" and later discovers the net was 13.7% will not feel they got a deal — they will feel they were misled, and they will doubt every other number in the deck. Showing 13.7% net beside the 18.0% gross from the start *builds* trust; hiding it destroys trust the moment it is found, which is always.

*Gross is the founder's story and net is the investor's reality; presenting net first is the cheapest credibility you will ever buy.*

![A line chart showing a fund's gross cumulative return reaching eighteen percent over a year against its net return of about fourteen percent with the fee drag shaded between the two curves](/imgs/blogs/the-pitch-book-ddq-and-the-data-room-3.png)

**The right benchmark.** A return means nothing without a comparison. A long/short equity fund up 13.7% net in a year the market was up 20% had a *bad* year despite the positive number; the same fund up 13.7% in a year the market fell 15% had a spectacular one. Allocators want to see the fund against a benchmark that reflects what it actually does — an equity long/short fund against an equity index *and* against an HFR long/short equity sub-index, a market-neutral fund against cash plus a spread, a macro fund against a macro peer group. Choosing a flattering, irrelevant benchmark (or none) is a tell. The discipline of measuring a strategy against the right yardstick — Sharpe, information coefficient, turnover, the metrics that separate real edge from beta — is the subject of [evaluating alpha signals: IC, Sharpe, turnover](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research), and allocators apply exactly that lens to a track record.

**Attribution.** The single most powerful thing a founder can do to make a track record credible is to *explain it*. A number is a claim; attribution is the evidence that the claim reflects skill rather than luck. Allocators want to know whether the returns came from many independent bets (repeatable) or one concentrated winner (a coin flip that landed heads), from genuine stock selection (skill) or from a factor tilt that any cheap index could have captured (beta dressed as alpha).

#### Worked example: attribution of a \$12M gain across sources

The Meridian Fund netted investors \$12M on its \$100M book in a year (after the \$2M of fees and costs that turned a \$14M gross gain into \$12M net). Maya's attribution, shown in Figure 6, broke the \$14M gross gain into four sources: **\$6.5M from long stock selection** (excluding her single biggest name), **\$1.5M from short selection**, **\$2.0M from a deliberate factor and beta tilt**, and **\$4.0M from one concentrated winner** — a single position that worked spectacularly.

The allocator's eye goes straight to that \$4M name. It drove roughly **a third of the gross gain from one position**, and an experienced diligence officer will mentally *strip it out* to ask: what did the rest of the book do? Stripped of the lucky name, the strategy made \$10M gross — still good, still positive across longs, shorts, and the tilt — which tells the allocator the edge is real even without the home run. Had the answer been the reverse — \$11M of the \$14M from one name and the rest of the book flat or negative — the allocator would conclude the track record is one lucky bet wearing a strategy's clothes, and the "edge" is unproven. Attribution is what lets them tell the two apart, which is why a founder who *volunteers* a clean attribution is far more persuasive than one who only shows the topline.

*A topline return is a claim; attribution is the testimony, and allocators trust the manager who breaks down their own returns more than the one who only shows the sum.*

![A bar chart showing the sources of a twelve million dollar net gain on a hundred million dollar book including long selection, short selection, a factor tilt, one large winner, and the fees that reduce gross to net](/imgs/blogs/the-pitch-book-ddq-and-the-data-room-6.png)

**Survivorship and portability.** Two quieter traps lurk in a track record, and allocators probe both. The first is *survivorship*: a manager who shows the record of their winning fund while quietly omitting an earlier fund that closed, or a strategy sleeve that was shut down, is presenting a survivor-biased track that flatters the real skill. Allocators ask "is this every dollar you have ever managed in this style, or only the ones that worked?" — and a reference call to a former employer or administrator often surfaces the answer. The second is *portability*: a manager whose track record was earned at a prior firm may not be legally entitled to claim it. Performance built on an employer's capital, systems, and team is often the employer's record, not the individual's, and presenting it as your own without the right to do so is both a Marketing Rule problem and a credibility grenade if the prior firm disputes it. A first-time founder's honest answer is frequently "I ran this book at my prior firm and have a letter permitting me to reference the performance, attributed appropriately" — and having that letter in the data room is worth far more than a longer but unportable record.

**GIPS and verification.** For an institutional raise, the gold standard is a **GIPS-compliant** track record, ideally **verified** by an independent firm. GIPS compliance means the returns were calculated and presented according to a strict global standard that removes the manager's discretion to cherry-pick periods, exclude bad accounts, or compute returns in a self-flattering way. Many large institutions and their consultants will not seriously consider a manager whose track record is not GIPS-compliant, because GIPS is how they trust the numbers without re-auditing them. A first-time manager rarely launches GIPS-compliant on day one, but knowing that institutions expect it — and building toward it — is part of taking the **Performance** P seriously. The mechanics of building a track record that survives this scrutiny from the first day are covered in [building a track record and the first close](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/building-a-track-record-and-the-first-close).

## The DDQ

After a promising first meeting, the allocator sends the **due-diligence questionnaire** — the DDQ. It is a long, standardized document, often dozens of pages, asking the same battery of questions every serious allocator asks every manager. The standardization is the point: it lets the allocator compare funds on identical axes and feed each section to the right internal team. Figure 5 lays out its six sections.

![A grid of six DDQ sections covering strategy, team, operations, compliance, terms, and references with what each section asks and which diligence team it feeds](/imgs/blogs/the-pitch-book-ddq-and-the-data-room-5.png)

**Strategy.** What the fund does, in detail: the universe it trades, the typical leverage and exposures, the holding period, the capacity, the risk limits, how positions are sized, and how the strategy is expected to behave in different market regimes. This section feeds the **investment** diligence team, who will compare your answers to your track record and your portfolio and look for inconsistencies. A strategy described as "low volatility, market-neutral" alongside a track record with a 30% drawdown does not reconcile, and the mismatch is a flag.

**Team.** Full biographies, ownership of the management company, who is responsible for what, the depth of the team behind the founder, employee turnover history, and key-person arrangements. This section feeds investment diligence and bears directly on the **People** P. Allocators are hunting for key-person risk (does the whole fund depend on one person?), for hidden instability (have three analysts left in a year?), and for alignment (does the founder have personal capital in the fund?).

**Operations.** The administrator, the prime broker(s) and custodians, how the NAV is struck, how cash moves and who can authorize it, the trade and reconciliation process, the technology stack, and business continuity and cyber arrangements. This section feeds the **operational** diligence team — the ODD team — who hold a *separate veto*. The operations section of the DDQ is, in effect, the opening exam for [operational due diligence, the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto), and a thin or evasive answer here guarantees a hard ODD process.

**Compliance.** The fund's regulatory registrations (SEC, CFTC/NFA where applicable), the chief compliance officer (the CCO) and whether that role is independent or worn by the founder, the Form ADV, the valuation policy, the conflicts-of-interest policy, the code of ethics, and the personal-trading rules. This section also feeds ODD. The mechanics of these registrations live in [registration and regulation: the SEC, the CFTC, and Reg D](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d); the DDQ checks that you have actually done them and can document it.

**Terms.** The full economic and liquidity terms — fees, the high-water mark, any hurdle, the lock-up, redemption frequency and notice period, gates, side-pocket rights, the minimum investment, and any side letters or founders class. This is the *deal on offer*, and the allocator's terms specialists will negotiate from here.

**References.** Service-provider references (so the allocator can call your administrator and auditor directly), existing-investor references (so they can ask other LPs how you have behaved), and prior-employer references. This is the *back-channel* check, and it is more powerful than founders expect. Allocators talk to each other and to service providers constantly, and a single bad reference call — an administrator who is lukewarm, a former colleague who is evasive, an existing investor who quietly mentions a missed redemption — can end a raise that the documents had passed.

#### Worked example: a DDQ red flag that sinks the raise

Maya's Meridian Fund was deep in diligence with a USD 2B endowment when the DDQ surfaced the thing that ended it. The operations section asked, routinely: "Who has authority to authorize the movement of cash from the fund's accounts, and what controls govern such movements?" Maya's honest answer was that, to keep things lean at launch, *she* could initiate and authorize wires from the fund's prime-brokerage account herself, with no second signatory and no independent administrator confirmation before the cash moved.

To Maya this felt like efficiency. To the endowment's ODD analyst it was a fatal control gap — the single most important control in the entire operational checklist, the one whose absence enabled the Madoff fraud, where investor cash could leave the fund on one person's signature. It did not matter that Maya was honest, talented, and up 41%. The ODD team has a *veto*, and "the founder can move client cash unilaterally" is an automatic veto in most institutional ODD processes. The endowment passed, and they did not soften it: they told Maya's placement agent the cash-control gap was disqualifying and that they would revisit only after she put an independent administrator and a dual-authorization process in place. Maya fixed it within a quarter — but the allocation, and the months of diligence, were gone.

*The DDQ is where a control gap you considered a minor convenience becomes the one fact that ends the raise; answer it honestly, then fix what the honest answer reveals.*

The deepest lesson of the DDQ is that its real function is *consistency-checking*. The allocator already has your pitch book, your track record, and your verbal story from the meeting. The DDQ, the data room, and the reference calls exist to check that all of those agree with each other and with the documents. A fund whose pitch book says "30-day liquidity," whose DDQ says "quarterly with 60 days' notice," and whose LPA says something different again has not committed three small errors — it has demonstrated that nobody is minding the details, which is exactly the People-and-Operations failure that allocators are screening for.

## The data room

If the pitch book makes claims and the DDQ asks for them in writing, the **data room** is where every claim gets verified against a primary document. Historically a literal locked room of paper that diligence teams visited; today a permissioned cloud folder (or a dedicated virtual-data-room service) the allocator's team is given access to. The data room is where the raise stops being a story and becomes an audit.

A complete data room for a hedge fund raise contains, at minimum: the **fund documents** (the PPM, the LPA or operating agreement, the subscription documents, the IMA — the stack covered in [the fund documents: the PPM, the LPA, the subscription agreement](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription)); the **audited financial statements** for every year of the fund's life; the **service-provider agreements** (the administration agreement, the prime-brokerage agreement, the audit engagement letter); the **compliance manual**, the code of ethics, and the valuation policy; the firm's **Form ADV**; the team's **background-check and reference documentation**; and detailed **performance data** — monthly returns, the GIPS-compliant presentation, and the attribution behind the headline numbers.

Two things make the data room decisive. First, **it is where claims are checked against evidence.** A pitch book that says "audited by a Big Four firm" is checked against the actual audit report in the data room. A track record that claims 41% is checked against the administrator's monthly NAV statements. A "USD 100M AUM" is checked against the fund's financial statements. When the data room confirms the claims, trust deepens; when it contradicts them — even slightly — the contradiction does more damage than the original claim ever did good, because it converts the whole deck from "things I am told" into "things I must now independently verify."

Second, **the quality of the data room is itself a signal of the operation.** A complete, well-organized, professionally maintained data room tells the allocator the fund's back office is real and disciplined. A data room that is missing the audit, has financials that do not tie to the reported returns, or arrives as a chaotic dump of mislabeled PDFs tells the allocator the opposite — that the operation is thin, regardless of what the operations slide claimed. Diligence teams have seen hundreds of data rooms, and they form an impression of the fund's operational maturity from the data room's *organization* before they have read a single document inside it.

A third, practical consideration is **access control and staging.** A modern virtual data room is permissioned: the founder controls who sees which documents and when, and the system logs every view and download. That control is useful in two directions. It lets the founder *stage* disclosure — sharing the top-level documents early and the more sensitive material (detailed positions, the full investor register, the most commercially sensitive contracts) only once an allocator is seriously advanced and has signed appropriate confidentiality terms. And the access log gives the founder a signal of allocator engagement: a diligence team that has opened the audit, the administration agreement, and the compliance manual three times is serious; one that glanced at the deck and never entered the data room is not. The discipline is to have everything ready and organized *before* the raise, then release it in a controlled sequence — not to scramble to assemble documents while a serious allocator waits, which itself signals an unprepared operation.

The deepest principle of the data room is **reconciliation.** Every number an allocator cares about should reconcile across the documents: the AUM in the pitch book equals the AUM in the financials equals the AUM in the Form ADV; the returns in the track record equal the returns derivable from the administrator's NAV statements; the terms in the deck equal the terms in the LPA equal the terms in the DDQ. A founder who has personally reconciled their own data room before opening it — checked that every number agrees with every other number — has done the single most valuable piece of raise preparation, because the alternative is for the allocator's team to find the discrepancy, and a discrepancy found by the allocator is far more expensive than one found by the founder. The reconciliation is not a one-time task either: every month the NAV is struck, the financials are updated, and a new investor subscribes, the numbers move, so the data room is a living artifact that must be kept current and consistent for as long as the raise is open.

## The first meeting

Everything above is the toolkit; the first meeting is where it is first put to use, and where many raises quietly end before any document is exchanged. Forty-five minutes, sometimes less, often with a junior or mid-level person rather than the decision-maker. The founder's instinct is to use the time to walk the deck slide by slide. The allocator's reality is that they decide, within the first ten minutes, whether this is a fund worth diligence — and they decide it on *people and clarity*, not on the equity curve.

What an experienced allocator is actually doing in a first meeting is testing three things. **Can this person articulate their edge clearly and specifically?** A founder who cannot explain, in plain language, why their strategy makes money and when it will not is a founder whose edge the allocator cannot evaluate or trust. **Is this person honest about risk and about their losing periods?** A founder who has a crisp, unflinching answer to "tell me about your worst month and what you did" is far more credible than one who deflects or claims they never really have bad months. **Does the operation behind the returns sound real?** Maya's allocator flipping to slide nine to ask about cash controls was running exactly this test — and her lack of a ready answer was a worse signal than a slightly lower return would have been.

The practical implications are sharp. Lead with the team and the edge, not the returns. Have a *better* answer ready for "what's your worst drawdown and why" than for "what's your best year." Be able to name your administrator, your auditor, your prime brokers, and your compliance setup without flipping through the deck. And read the room: if the allocator keeps steering to operations and team, that is not them being difficult — that is them telling you what they actually evaluate, which is the subject of the next section.

There is also a relationship dimension to the first meeting that founders, especially trader-founders used to transactional markets, consistently undervalue. Institutional allocation is a *relationship* business with a long memory. The senior person across the table may not allocate to you this cycle — you may be too small, too new, or in a strategy they are not adding to right now — but they will remember how you handled their hard questions, whether you were honest about your weaknesses, and whether you followed up professionally. Many real allocations come on the *second* or *third* meeting, sometimes years later, after the manager has built a longer track record and the allocator has watched them behave consistently in the interim. A founder who treats a first meeting as a one-shot sales pitch and pushes too hard for a quick yes can damage a relationship that, played patiently, would have produced a much larger cheque down the road. The goal of the first meeting is rarely a wire; it is to earn the *next* meeting and to plant the manager firmly, and favorably, in the allocator's memory. The funnel math from earlier — two to three cheques per hundred meetings — only works if the ninety-seven meetings that did not convert this cycle were handled well enough to convert later.

## What allocators actually evaluate

Pull the whole post together into the framework allocators actually use, the **five Ps**, and the asymmetry at its heart: performance is one P of five, and the other four decide most raises. Figure 2 is the reference; here is what each P means in practice and why a founder must build for all five.

**People.** Who runs the money, and can the allocator trust them with capital? This covers pedigree and track record, but more importantly *stability* and *alignment*: is there a team or a single point of failure, are the key people locked in, does the founder have meaningful personal capital in the fund, has the team been stable? Allocators are acutely aware that they are not buying a backtest, they are betting on people behaving well — under pressure, in a drawdown, when offered a bigger payday elsewhere. A fund with a great track record and a destabilizing People problem (the founder is the only person who understands the strategy; two senior analysts just left; the founder has no skin in the game) routinely loses to a fund with a slightly worse record and a stable, aligned, deep team. The careers-side view of how managers and teams form is in [from senior to PM or starting your own](/blog/trading/quant-careers/from-senior-to-pm-or-starting-your-own).

**Philosophy and process.** Is the edge *real* and *repeatable*, and is there a disciplined process that generates it? Allocators distinguish sharply between a manager who got lucky and a manager with a repeatable engine. They probe the philosophy ("why does this inefficiency exist and why does it persist?") and the process ("walk me through exactly how a position enters and exits the book"). A manager who cannot articulate a repeatable process — whose returns seem to come from intuition that cannot be described or transferred — is uninvestable for an institution, because the institution cannot underwrite intuition. The discipline of *not* fooling yourself about an edge is exactly the discipline allocators are testing, and it connects directly to [overfitting, purged CV, and the deflated Sharpe](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/overfitting-purged-cv-deflated-sharpe-quant-research).

**Performance.** The track record — net of fees, against the right benchmark, with attribution, ideally GIPS-compliant. Necessary but not sufficient. Performance is the column founders over-invest in because it is the one they can most directly control and are most proud of. But an allocator treats it as a *filter*, not a *decision*: bad performance screens you out, but good performance only gets you to the other four columns, where the decision is made.

**Portfolio.** What does the book actually look like right now, and does it match the story? Gross and net exposure, concentration, sector and factor tilts, the largest positions, and — critically — the *liquidity match* between the assets held and the redemption terms offered. A "diversified" fund with three positions, a "market-neutral" fund with a large net long, or a fund offering monthly liquidity on quarterly-to-sell assets all fail the Portfolio test, because the portfolio contradicts the story or carries a hidden risk the terms do not protect against.

**Operations.** Is the back office real, independent, and controlled? Independent administrator striking the NAV, segregation of duties, controlled cash movement, a real auditor, a real compliance function, business continuity and cyber arrangements. This is the **ODD veto**, and it is the column that most cleanly demonstrates the asymmetry: a fund with a brilliant track record and a control gap is *rejected*, period, because no allocator will explain to their own investors that they put money into a fund that blew up on a control failure they could have caught. Operations is where the famous failures — Madoff above all — were catchable in advance and were caught by the allocators who did real ODD.

The founder's takeaway is the weighting. The five Ps are not a scorecard with performance at 60% and the rest splitting 40%. Performance is a *gate* you must clear, and then the weight spreads roughly evenly across people, process, portfolio, and operations — with operations holding an outright *veto*. Build the raise toolkit to argue all five, and build the operation so the fifth one cannot sink you.

## Common misconceptions

**"The returns slide sells the fund."** It does not; it gets you in the room. The fund is sold on people, process, and operations — the four Ps that decide whether an allocator believes the returns are real, repeatable, and safely produced. A founder who optimizes the deck around the equity curve and neglects the team, edge, portfolio, and operations slides has built a deck that opens strong and dies in diligence. Maya's allocator flipping *past* the returns slide is the literal embodiment of this myth being false.

**"A longer track record is always better."** Length helps — institutions want to see how a manager behaved across at least one full cycle, and many will not look below a one-to-three-year audited track. But length is not strictly better. A long track record that is mostly *beta* (the manager rode the market up and called it skill), or that includes a near-blowup, or that was earned at a previous firm and may not be legally portable, can be *worse* than a shorter, cleaner, genuinely-alpha record with honest attribution. Allocators care about the *quality* and *attribution* of the track, not just its length; three years of demonstrable, repeatable, well-attributed alpha beats seven years of disguised beta.

**"Gross returns are fine to lead with."** No. The SEC Marketing Rule requires net beside gross with equal prominence, and beyond the rule, leading with gross is the single fastest way to look like you do not understand what investors buy or are hiding the net. Allocators care about net, because net is what they earn. Show net first, gross beside it, and the fee wedge explained — that is credibility, not a weakness to be hidden.

**"The DDQ and data room are just paperwork the lawyers handle."** They are the *evidence* phase of the raise, and they are where most raises that survive the first meeting actually die — on an inconsistency, a control gap, a missing audit, or a bad reference call. A founder who delegates the DDQ and data room entirely and never personally reconciles their own numbers is handing the allocator's diligence team the discrepancies that will sink the raise. Reconcile your own data room before you open it.

**"A great track record means I'll pass operational due diligence."** This is the most expensive myth of all, because it gets the asymmetry exactly backwards. ODD is a *separate process with a separate veto*, and it does not care how good your returns are — it cares whether your cash is controlled, your NAV is independent, and your operation can survive a shock. The best track record in the world does not survive a Madoff-shaped control gap. Treat operations as a first-class part of the raise, not an afterthought you bolt on once you have investors interested.

**"Raising is easy if you can trade."** Trading skill and raising skill are nearly orthogonal. The funnel is savage — on the order of two to three cheques per hundred first meetings for an unknown manager — and the cheques go to the funds that argue all five Ps credibly, not to the best traders. Many genuinely skilled managers fail to raise because they treated the raise as a returns advertisement rather than a credibility audit, and many merely-good managers raise well because they built the toolkit and the operation that allocators reward. The fund is a *business*, and raising is the business skill the trading desk never taught.

## How it plays out in the real world

The single most instructive case is **Bernie Madoff**, because it is the failure that built the modern allocator's evaluation framework. Madoff ran what was revealed in December 2008 to be the largest Ponzi scheme in history — roughly USD 65B in fabricated statement value, with about USD 17.5B of actual principal lost. For decades, Madoff passed the test that this post's myth-busting warns against: he had a *track record* that looked extraordinary — steady, smooth, positive month after month, almost never a down month. The Performance column was, on its face, perfect.

Every other column was a screaming failure that real diligence would have caught, and some allocators *did* catch it and walked away. There was **no independent administrator** — Madoff's own firm produced the statements. There was **no real custody segregation** — the assets were self-custodied. The **auditor** was a tiny, unknown three-person firm auditing a multi-billion-dollar operation, an immediate operational red flag. And the returns were **too smooth** — a strategy that supposedly traded equities and options simply cannot produce returns that consistent, which any process-literate diligence officer should have found impossible to attribute. The allocators who ran real operational due diligence on Madoff — who asked "who strikes the NAV, who has custody, who audits this, and how can the returns be this smooth?" — declined to invest. The ones who looked only at the performance column wired the money. Madoff is *why* the Operations P holds a veto, and why "a great track record means I'll pass ODD" is a fatal misconception.

The broader pattern shows up across the named blowups. **Amaranth Advisors** (2006) lost roughly USD 6.6B — about 65% of its assets — in roughly a week on a concentrated natural-gas position; a real read of the Portfolio P (concentration, liquidity, the size of one book relative to the market) would have flagged the risk before the loss. **Archegos** (2021) lost prime brokers over USD 10B on hidden leverage spread across multiple counterparties through total-return swaps; a real read of the Portfolio and Operations Ps — actual gross exposure, actual leverage, counterparty arrangements — was exactly the diligence the prime brokers failed to do on their own client. In each case the *performance* looked fine until the moment it did not, and the warning lived in one of the other four Ps.

On the constructive side, this is why the institutionalization of fundraising tools has been so thorough. The DDQ exists in standardized templates (the AIMA illustrative DDQ is an industry reference) precisely so allocators do not have to reinvent the question battery for each fund. The GIPS standards exist so performance cannot be quietly flattered. The SEC Marketing Rule exists so net-beside-gross is not optional. The entire apparatus — pitch book conventions, the DDQ, the data room, ODD with its veto — is the industry's accumulated, codified answer to "how do we tell a real, durable fund from a great-looking one that will blow up or turn out to be fake." A founder who understands the apparatus as a *credibility-verification machine*, and builds to satisfy it honestly, raises capital. A founder who treats it as paperwork standing between them and the money they obviously deserve does not.

The difference is entirely in whether the toolkit was built to argue *credibility* or to advertise *performance*, and that difference is the difference between a closed allocation and a polite pass.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you decide to raise outside capital, which for almost every founder is the moment the fund becomes a real business rather than a personal account. The instinct to lead with returns is natural and wrong; the discipline to build a pitch book that argues people and process first, a track record presented net-of-fees with attribution, a DDQ answered honestly, and a data room that reconciles to the last dollar — that discipline is what separates the founders who raise from the equally-talented ones who do not. Allocators evaluate the five Ps, performance is only one of them, and operations holds a veto. Build for all five.
