> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A hedge fund is not one company. It is at least *three* legal entities, and the one you actually own is not the one with the money in it.
>
> - **The fund** is a limited partnership that pools the investors' capital and holds the positions. It earns *returns for the investors* — it does not earn fees; it *pays* them.
> - **The management company** (the investment manager / adviser) is the operating business you own. It signs the contracts, employs the staff, and collects the **management fee**. It is the only entity with enterprise value — the thing you could one day sell or take seed capital against.
> - **The general partner** is the entity that legally controls the fund, bears the liability the investors are shielded from, and receives the **incentive allocation** (the 20%). At a small launch the GP and the management company are usually the same people wearing two hats.
> - The one fact to remember: **the fund holds the assets, the management company earns the management fee, and the GP carries the legal risk and earns the incentive.** Three boxes, three jobs — and a claim that lands on one of them should never reach the founder's house.

Maya had spent nine years running a long/short equity book, and she was certain she understood what she was building. "I'm starting a fund," she told her future administrator over the phone, and in her head there was one thing — *the fund* — a single company with her name effectively on the door, into which investors would wire money and out of which she would, eventually, pay herself. She had a name picked out, the Meridian Fund, and she had been saying "my fund" for so long that it had calcified into a single object.

Then her formation lawyer drew three boxes on a whiteboard. One box for the fund. A second, completely separate box for the company that would *manage* the fund. A third box for the entity that would *control* the fund and carry its legal liability. Three boxes, three sets of formation documents, three names on three sets of bank-account paperwork, and — the part that genuinely surprised her — only *two* of them would she own outright, and neither of them was the one with the investors' money in it. The thing she had been calling "my fund" for eighteen months was the one box she would own the *least* of.

She is not unusual. Almost every first-time founder pictures one company and discovers a federation of entities. And the confusion is not harmless. If you do not know which box holds the capital, which box earns which fee, and which box a lawsuit lands on, you cannot answer the most basic questions an allocator's operational due diligence team will ask you, you cannot read your own fund documents, and you cannot understand the asset you are spending the next decade of your life building. The structure is not paperwork you hand to a lawyer and forget. It *is* the business.

This post draws those three boxes properly and follows every dollar between them. By the end you will be able to look at any hedge fund and name its parts: where the capital sits, where the fees go, where the liability stops, and which of the entities is the one the founder actually owns. Figure 1 is the whole skeleton on one page; we will spend the rest of the post earning the right to read it.

## Foundations: the building blocks of a fund's legal skeleton

Before any of the structure makes sense, you need a small vocabulary firmly in place. If you trade for a living, the *markets* side of a fund is second nature to you — it is this *legal-entity* layer that is usually a complete blank, because nobody trades it and nobody teaches it. Every box, every fee flow, and every misconception later in this post is a recombination of the eight ideas below. Take them slowly.

**An entity.** A legal "person" that the law recognizes as separate from the human beings behind it. An entity can own property, sign contracts, sue, be sued, owe taxes, and go bankrupt — all on its own, distinct from its owners. The entire structure of a hedge fund is an exercise in creating *several* such legal persons and assigning each one a narrow job, so that the law treats them as separate. When you grasp that a company is a separate legal person from the human who owns it, the three-box structure stops being mysterious.

**A limited liability company (LLC).** A flexible US entity whose owners (called **members**) are generally *not* personally liable for the entity's debts — their downside is capped at what they put in. An LLC is the default wrapper for the operating entities of a fund: the management company is almost always an LLC, and the general partner is usually a separate LLC. The "limited liability" is the whole point: it means a creditor of the LLC cannot, in the normal case, reach the personal assets of the people who own it.

**A limited partnership (LP).** A partnership with two classes of partner. The **general partner (GP)** runs the partnership, makes the decisions, and bears *unlimited* liability for the partnership's obligations. The **limited partners (LPs)** are passive investors: they contribute capital, share in the profits and losses, and — crucially — their liability is *limited* to what they invested, *provided* they stay passive and do not take part in management. The US hedge fund itself is almost always structured as a limited partnership: the investors are the LPs, and a GP entity sits on top to control it. This single structure is why an investor can lose their whole investment but never owe more.

**The general partner (GP).** In a fund's limited partnership, the GP is the entity (usually a small LLC) that legally controls the fund and absorbs the unlimited liability the LPs are protected from. Because that unlimited liability is dangerous, founders almost never make themselves the GP *as individuals* — they interpose an LLC as the GP, so the unlimited liability lands on an entity with few assets rather than on a human being. The GP is also, by long convention, the entity that receives the **incentive allocation** — the 20%.

**Limited liability, generally.** The legal principle, running through both the LLC and the LP, that an owner's loss is capped at their investment and creditors cannot pierce through the entity to the owner's personal wealth. It is the reason the structure has walls at all. Every box you are about to meet exists, in part, to put a wall between one kind of risk and another, so that a problem in one box does not flood into the next. We return to this idea — and to how it can fail — in the liability section.

**Assets under management (AUM).** The total pool of capital the manager is responsible for: the money the investors have entrusted to the fund. AUM is the base the management fee is charged against, which makes it the single most important number in the *business* of running a fund. Two managers can run an identical strategy with identical returns; if one has USD 40M of AUM and the other has USD 400M, their *businesses* are nothing alike, because the fee revenue differs more than tenfold. We treat the fee economics in depth in [how a hedge fund makes money](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-a-hedge-fund-makes-money); here you only need AUM as the thing the management fee bites on.

**Net asset value (NAV).** The value of the fund, struck on a schedule (monthly for most hedge funds). Per investor, it is usually expressed as NAV *per share* or per partnership interest: if you own one interest and the NAV per interest rises from \$1,000 to \$1,050 over the year, your stake is up 5% before fees. The fund's administrator computes the NAV; every subscription, redemption, and fee is calculated against it. Keep two numbers separate in your head: NAV-per-interest is the *price* of the fund, and AUM is roughly that price times the number of interests outstanding — the *size* of the fund.

**"The fund" versus "the manager."** This is the distinction the whole post turns on, so meet it now in one sentence and we will spend the rest of the post unpacking it: *the fund is the pool of investors' money; the manager is the business that runs that pool and gets paid for it.* They are different legal entities, with different owners, different bank accounts, different books, and different jobs. When a headline says "the hedge fund earned its managers a fortune," it is quietly eliding two entities — the fund earned *returns for its investors*, and a *separate* entity, the manager, earned *fees*. Hold that gap; it is the source of nearly every misconception we correct later.

With those eight ideas in hand — entity, LLC, LP, GP, limited liability, AUM, NAV, and the fund-versus-manager split — we can build the skeleton.

## The three-entity skeleton

Here is the structure in its simplest honest form. When you launch a US hedge fund for US taxable investors, you are not creating one company. You are creating, at minimum, **three** legal entities, and they have three completely different jobs.

The **fund** is the investment vehicle — the limited partnership that pools the investors' capital and holds the positions the strategy puts on. The **management company** (the investment manager or adviser) is the operating business that *runs* the fund and collects the management fee. The **general partner** is the entity that *controls* the fund, carries its liability, and receives the incentive allocation. Three boxes. The fund holds the money; the management company earns the fee; the GP carries the risk and earns the 20%.

The reason this trips up every newcomer is that the founder's mental picture collapses all three into one — "my fund" — when in reality the founder's *ownership* attaches to the management company and the GP, *not* to the fund. The investors own the fund (they are its limited partners). The founder owns the *business that manages* the fund. Those are different objects with different value, and confusing them is the single most expensive misunderstanding a first-time founder can carry into a launch.

Figure 1 above lays the skeleton out as a layered structure: the founder at the top owns the investment manager and the general partner; those two entities, in turn, manage and control the fund; and the investors subscribe into the fund from the side. Follow the arrows and you can read the whole arrangement: *ownership flows down from the founder into the management entities, control and management flow from those entities into the fund, and capital flows into the fund from the investors.* The founder never owns the fund. The investors never own the management company. That asymmetry is the structure.

Why bother with three boxes instead of one? Three reasons, each of which we develop later: **liability isolation** (a problem in one entity should not reach the others, or the founder personally), **clean economics** (the fund's books should show only investments, while the management company's books show the operating business you would one day sell), and **tax and partnership mechanics** (structuring the 20% as an *allocation* to the GP rather than a *fee* can change its tax character, and keeping the entities separate makes that clean). For now, internalize the shape: three entities, three jobs, money moving between them in specific directions.

A note on scope before we go deeper. Everything in this post is the *domestic* US structure — a Delaware limited partnership for US taxable investors, managed by a US adviser. The moment you take money from non-US or US tax-exempt investors, a *fourth* and *fifth* entity appear: an offshore feeder (usually a Cayman company) and a master fund that the feeders invest through. That machinery — the master-feeder — is its own subject, and we cover it in [domestic versus offshore: the master-feeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder). Get the three-box domestic skeleton solid first; the offshore boxes bolt onto it without changing the core logic.

## The management company and the general partner

Let us start with the two boxes the founder actually owns, because they are the ones founders understand least and care about most. These are the entities where the *business* lives — and the business, not the fund, is the asset the founder is building.

The **management company** — the "ManCo," formally the investment adviser — is the operating entity. It is the company that has employees, that signs the lease and the Bloomberg contract and the prime-brokerage agreement, that hires the analysts and pays their salaries, and that holds the firm's name and reputation. It is, in the ordinary case, an LLC owned by the founder (and any partners). And it is the entity that *receives the management fee*. When the fund pays its 1.5% annual fee, that money flows out of the fund and into the management company's bank account, and out of *that* account come the salaries, the rent, the data bills, and — whatever is left — the founder's draw and profit.

The crucial property of the management company is that it is **the only entity in the structure with enterprise value.** The fund has assets, but those assets belong to the investors, not the founder. The management company has a revenue stream — the fees — and a brand, a track record, a team, and a strategy. That bundle is what an acquirer or a GP-stakes buyer pays for. When you read that a firm like Petershill or Blackstone's Strategic Capital "bought a piece of a hedge fund," they almost always bought a slice of the *management company's revenue*, not a slice of the fund's assets. We develop that valuation logic in [capital structure, evolution, and GP stakes](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes); the fact to hold now is that the management company is the thing with a sale price.

The **general partner** is the second entity the founder owns, and it does two jobs the management company does not. First, it **legally controls the fund** — the GP is, by the limited partnership structure, the partner with authority to make decisions and bind the partnership. Second, it **carries the unlimited liability** that the limited partners are shielded from, which is exactly why the GP is structured as a *separate LLC* rather than as the founder personally: if catastrophe strikes the fund and the GP's unlimited liability is triggered, that liability lands on an entity with few assets, not on the founder's house. (We push on the limits of that protection in the liability section.)

The GP's *economic* job is to receive the **incentive allocation** — the 20% of profits above a high-water mark. By convention and for tax reasons, the performance compensation is usually structured not as a *fee* charged by the manager but as a *special allocation of the fund's profits to the general partner*. This is a genuinely important distinction that we will quantify in the worked examples: the management fee is a *fee* paid to the *management company*, while the incentive is an *allocation* of profit to the *general partner*. Two different entities, two different mechanisms, two different tax treatments. At a large multi-strategy platform these may sit in separate, carefully designed entities; at a small first launch, the management company and the GP are frequently owned by the same people and run by the same hands — the GP exists mostly to isolate liability and to be the clean recipient of the incentive allocation.

Why does the founder own *two* entities here rather than folding the GP into the management company? Partly liability (the GP's unlimited liability is quarantined in its own thin entity), partly tax (the incentive allocation flows cleanly to a dedicated GP), and partly optionality (a future GP-stakes deal or a partner buy-in is cleaner when control and economics sit in their own boxes). For your first launch, the practical reality is two LLCs that you own, doing two jobs — running the business and controlling the fund — and earning two different revenue lines.

A detail that surprises founders: the GP is deliberately kept *thin*. It holds little beyond its right to control the fund and receive the incentive allocation, and it passes most of that allocation through to its owners rather than accumulating assets. The thinness is the point. The GP carries the fund's *unlimited* liability, so you do not want it to be a fat target — you want it to be a control entity with few assets, so that if its unlimited liability is ever triggered, a creditor reaching it finds little, and the value of the franchise sits safely in the management company and in the founder's hands rather than in the entity most exposed to the fund's risk. The management company, by contrast, is the entity you *do* let accumulate value: it holds the team, the brand, the systems, and the fee revenue, and it is the box you would sell. The asymmetry — a thin GP carrying liability and a substantial management company carrying value — is intentional structural design, not an accident of formation.

How the *ownership* of these two entities is held also matters as the firm grows. At a one-person launch, the founder simply owns 100% of both the management company and the GP. As partners join, the founder typically grants them equity in the management company (a share of the fee revenue and any sale proceeds) and a participation in the GP's incentive allocation (a share of the carry), often on a vesting schedule so that a partner who leaves early forfeits the unvested portion. Keeping the management company and the GP as separate entities makes this clean: a partner can be given, say, 10% of the management company's profit and 15% of the GP's carry as two distinct, separately negotiated stakes. Fold the two roles into one entity and every partner negotiation becomes a tangle. The structure that contains liability also turns out to be the structure that makes building a partnership tractable.

#### Worked example: the value of the management company as the asset Maya owns

Maya's Meridian Fund has grown to USD 200M of AUM. Her management company charges a 1.5% management fee, so it grosses \$3,000,000 a year. After her fixed cost stack of roughly \$1,200,000 — administrator, audit, compliance, data, salaries, office — the management company nets about \$1,800,000 a year of operating profit before the incentive allocation. Now suppose, in a steady year, the GP's incentive allocation adds another \$2,000,000 of distributable profit on top.

| Line | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Management fee revenue (ManCo) | \$200,000,000 × 1.5% | \$3,000,000 |
| Less fixed operating costs | (\$1,200,000) | \$1,800,000 |
| Plus GP incentive allocation (illustrative year) | + \$2,000,000 | \$3,800,000 |
| Combined annual profit to the founder's entities | — | **\$3,800,000** |

A buyer valuing the *business* is not buying the fund's USD 200M — that belongs to the investors. The buyer is buying the *management company and GP's* roughly \$3,800,000 of annual profit and the franchise that produces it. At even a modest multiple, that is a multi-million-dollar enterprise — and it is the asset Maya owns. *The fund is the investors' money; the management company and the GP are the business, and the business is what the founder is really building.*

## The fund: the limited partnership

Now the box the founder owns the least of, and the one with all the money in it. The **fund** is the investment vehicle — the limited partnership that exists for one purpose: to pool the investors' capital and deploy it in the strategy. Everything about the fund's structure follows from that one job.

The investors are the **limited partners (LPs)**. They subscribe capital into the fund, they receive partnership interests in return, they share pro-rata in the fund's gains and losses, and their liability is limited to what they invested. An LP who puts in \$5,000,000 can lose that \$5,000,000 if the strategy blows up — but they can never be asked for more, and a creditor of the fund cannot reach their other assets. That limitation is the whole reason sophisticated investors are willing to place capital in a blind pool run by someone else: the downside is bounded and known.

The fund does **not earn fees.** This is the sentence that, once it clicks, reorganizes everything. The fund's job is to *compound the LPs' capital*; the fees are something the fund *pays out* to the manager and the GP. On the fund's books you will see investments, cash, and the fees it owes — never fee *revenue*, because the fund is the payer, not the payee. When people say "the hedge fund made USD 50 million," they almost always mean the fund generated USD 50M of returns *for its investors*; the *manager* then took its slice of that as fees. Two different numbers, two different entities, and conflating them is the root of the "2-and-20 makes you rich on day one" myth we dismantle later.

Mechanically, the fund holds the prime-brokerage account, the cash, and the positions. When Maya buys 100,000 shares of a stock, she does so *in the fund's name*, with the fund's money, through the fund's prime broker. The positions are the fund's assets; the LPs own those assets pro-rata through their partnership interests; and the GP controls the whole thing while the management company executes the strategy under contract. The fund is, in a sense, the simplest box: it is a container for capital and positions, governed by the limited partnership agreement, with a clear single purpose.

One subtlety that matters for the rest of the series: the fund's **net asset value** is the number against which everything is measured. Each month the administrator strikes the NAV — the fund's total assets minus its liabilities (including accrued but unpaid fees) — and allocates it across the partnership interests. Subscriptions come in at NAV, redemptions go out at NAV, the management fee accrues against NAV, and the incentive allocation is computed on the gain in NAV above the high-water mark. The fund is where the NAV lives; the manager and GP simply get paid amounts *derived from* it.

#### Worked example: the fee flow on a USD 200M fund up 10%

Maya's Meridian Fund holds USD 200M and returns 10% gross over the year, so it generates \$20,000,000 of gains for the limited partners before fees. Watch the money move between the three boxes. The **management fee** (a fee paid to the management company) is 1.5% of AUM. The **incentive allocation** (an allocation of profit to the GP) is 20% of the gain above the high-water mark; assume the fund is at a fresh high-water mark, so the full gain counts.

| Flow | From → To | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|---|
| Management fee | Fund → Management Company | \$200,000,000 × 1.5% | \$3,000,000 |
| Incentive allocation | Fund → General Partner | 20% × \$20,000,000 | \$4,000,000 |
| Net to the limited partners | stays in the Fund | \$20,000,000 − \$3,000,000 − \$4,000,000 | \$13,000,000 |

The fund generated \$20,000,000; the management company collected \$3,000,000; the GP took \$4,000,000; and the LPs kept \$13,000,000 of gains (a 6.5% net return on their USD 200M). Notice that *no single entity* got the whole \$20,000,000 — it split across the three boxes by three different mechanisms. *The fund makes the money; the management company and the GP each take a defined slice through a defined channel, and what is left compounds for the investors.*

## The investment manager and the IMA

We have the three core entities. Now the contract that wires the management company to the fund, because without it the manager has no legal right to trade the fund's money or collect a fee.

The **investment manager** is, in most domestic structures, the management company itself acting in its capacity as the fund's adviser. (In larger or offshore structures the "investment manager" and the "management company" can be separate entities, but for the domestic three-box launch, treat them as the same entity wearing its "adviser" hat.) The manager is the entity *contracted to trade the fund* — to make the investment decisions, place the orders, and run the strategy — in exchange for the management fee.

The contract that creates this relationship is the **investment management agreement (IMA)**, sometimes called the advisory agreement. It is one of the most important documents in the whole structure, and it does a handful of specific things. It **appoints** the management company as the fund's investment manager. It **grants** the manager discretionary authority to trade the fund's assets within the strategy's mandate. It **sets the management fee** — the rate, the AUM base, the accrual and payment schedule. It **defines the standard of care** the manager owes the fund and allocates liability between them. And it **specifies termination** — how and when the relationship can be ended. The IMA is what turns "Maya runs the strategy" from an informal intention into a binding legal arrangement with a defined fee and defined duties.

This is the moment to be precise about a distinction the prose has been building toward. The management company earns the **management fee** *under the IMA* — it is a contractual fee for services. The general partner earns the **incentive allocation** *under the limited partnership agreement* (the LPA, the fund's governing document) — it is a special allocation of the fund's profits to the GP, not a fee for services. Two documents, two entities, two compensation mechanisms:

- The **management fee** is a *fee*, paid to the *management company*, governed by the *IMA*, charged on *AUM*, predictable, and owed regardless of performance.
- The **incentive allocation** is an *allocation of profit*, made to the *general partner*, governed by the *LPA*, charged on *gains above the high-water mark*, variable, and zero when the fund is underwater.

Why split the compensation across two mechanisms and two entities instead of just charging one big fee? Tax is a large part of the answer. Structuring the performance compensation as an *allocation* of the fund's gains to the GP — rather than as a *fee* — can let the GP inherit the *character* of those gains (for instance, long-term capital gains keep their favorable character flowing through to the GP partners, rather than being recharacterized as ordinary fee income). The deductibility and timing also differ. You do not need to master the tax code to launch — your fund counsel and tax adviser handle the design — but you do need to understand *that the two pieces of your compensation arrive through two different legal channels into two different entities*, because that fact shapes the whole structure and shows up on every set of books you will ever read.

The IMA also matters for an unglamorous reason that an allocator's operational due diligence team cares about intensely: it is where the **standard of care, the indemnification, and the termination rights** live. A sloppy or overly self-serving IMA — one that indemnifies the manager for almost anything, or that makes the manager nearly impossible to remove — is a red flag in operational due diligence, the separate veto-wielding review process we discuss across the operations track. The IMA is not boilerplate to skim; it is the spine of the manager-fund relationship, and it gets read closely by exactly the people whose money you want.

#### Worked example: the GP's incentive allocation versus the IM's management fee in dollars

Take Meridian again at USD 200M, up 10% (so \$20,000,000 of gains), at a fresh high-water mark. Separate the two compensation lines by which entity earns them and through which document:

| Compensation line | Entity | Document | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Management fee | Management Company | IMA | \$200,000,000 × 1.5% | \$3,000,000 |
| Incentive allocation | General Partner | LPA | 20% × \$20,000,000 | \$4,000,000 |

In a *good* year like this one, the GP's incentive allocation (\$4,000,000) exceeds the IM's management fee (\$3,000,000) — the performance line is where the real money is. But now run a *flat* year: the fund returns 0%, so the gain is zero and the incentive allocation is **\$0**, while the management fee is still \$3,000,000. The two lines have completely different shapes: the management fee is steady and pays the bills; the incentive allocation is a performance option that can pay nothing for a long stretch. *The management fee keeps the manager alive; the incentive allocation makes the founder wealthy — and they arrive through two different entities by two different legal mechanisms.*

## Who pays whom

We have met every box and the contract that wires them together. Now trace the cash, because the *direction* of every flow is what most people get backwards. Figure 2 lays the flow out as a sequence; follow it left to right and top to bottom.

![A six-step flow showing money moving from investors into the fund, then out of the fund to the manager as a management fee and to the general partner as an incentive allocation, with operating costs paid out and the founder profit left at the end](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund-2.png)

The flow has a clear, one-directional logic:

1. **Investors → the fund.** The limited partners wire their capital into the fund and receive partnership interests in return. This is the only inflow into the structure from the outside world.
2. **The fund → the investment manager.** The fund pays the **management fee** (1.5% of AUM, accrued monthly, deducted from NAV) to the management company under the IMA. This is the management company's steady revenue.
3. **The fund → the general partner.** The fund allocates the **incentive allocation** (20% of gains above the high-water mark) to the GP under the LPA. This is the performance-linked compensation.
4. **The management company → operating costs.** Out of its fee revenue, the management company pays the administrator, the auditor, compliance, market data, technology, salaries, rent, and insurance. These are the costs of being a credible, institutional-grade business.
5. **The management company / GP → the founder.** Whatever is left after costs — the management company's operating profit plus the GP's incentive allocation — is the profit distributed to the founder and any partners.

Read it as a single sentence: *capital flows in from investors to the fund; fees and the incentive allocation flow out of the fund to the manager and the GP; costs flow out of the manager; and the residual is the founder's profit.* The fund is a way-station the capital sits in and the source the fees are drawn from — it is never the destination of the fees. The manager and the GP are the destinations.

The *timing* of these flows is worth a line, because it shapes the founder's cash flow. The management fee typically **accrues monthly** against the fund's NAV — at a 1.5% annual rate, roughly 0.125% a month — and is paid out to the management company monthly or quarterly. That steady drip is what lets the management company meet payroll and pay the data bills on a predictable schedule. The incentive allocation, by contrast, is usually **crystallized** only once a year (commonly at year-end), meaning the GP's claim on the 20% is computed and locked in just once annually, after the high-water mark and any hurdle are applied. Until crystallization, the incentive is an *accrual* on the books — a number that can still shrink if the fund gives back gains before year-end. This is why a founder can have a strong year on paper for ten months and still see the incentive allocation evaporate if November and December are bad: nothing is locked until it crystallizes. The management fee keeps the lights on month to month; the incentive allocation is a lumpy, once-a-year event that may or may not arrive — and the gap between those two rhythms is a large part of why under-scaled funds run out of cash even in a decent year.

The most common error is to assume the fees flow *into* the fund, or that the founder is paid *by* the fund directly as a salary. Neither happens. The fund pays the management *company*, and the company pays the people. The fund allocates profit to the *GP*, and the GP distributes to its owners. The founder is paid as an *owner of the management company and the GP*, out of *their* profits — never as an employee of the fund, and never directly out of the fund's assets. Getting this direction right is what lets you read a fund's financial statements without confusion: the fund's statements show fees as *expenses*; the management company's statements show those same fees as *revenue*.

#### Worked example: a liability scenario — a fund-level claim that stops before the founder's assets

Suppose a trading counterparty sues the Meridian Fund, claiming the fund failed to deliver on a derivatives contract, and wins a judgment for \$8,000,000. Where does that \$8,000,000 come from? Trace it through the entities, assuming the structure was set up and operated correctly.

| Step | What happens | Whose money |
|---|---|---|
| Judgment lands on the fund | The claim is against the *fund* (the LP), which was the contracting party | The fund's assets |
| Fund pays from its assets | The fund satisfies the judgment from its NAV | Investors' capital (pro-rata) |
| Limited partners' exposure | Each LP's loss is capped at their committed capital; they owe nothing more | Limited to investment |
| General partner's exposure | The GP's unlimited liability is triggered only if the fund's assets are exhausted | The thin GP LLC's assets |
| Founder's personal assets | Protected by the LLC walls around the GP and ManCo, absent fraud or a piercing event | **Untouched** |

The \$8,000,000 comes out of the fund's assets first — which is the investors' capital, the risk they knowingly took. The limited partners cannot be asked for more than they invested. The GP's unlimited liability is a backstop, but it is an LLC with few assets, so a creditor reaching it finds little. And the founder's *personal* wealth — her house, her savings — sits behind the LLC walls and is not reachable, absent fraud, a failure to respect the entity separations, or a court "piercing the veil." *The structure is a series of walls precisely so that a claim landing on one box stops at that box and does not flood through to the founder personally.*

## Why three entities: liability and tax

We have asserted the "three boxes" structure; now justify it. Why not just run everything through one company? Because a single company would mix three kinds of risk and three kinds of money that *must* be kept apart, and the separation buys the founder two things that are worth more than the cost of three sets of formation documents: **liability containment** and **clean tax and economics**.

Start with **liability**. The fund takes *market and counterparty* risk — it can lose money on trades, it can default on a derivatives contract, it can be sued by a counterparty. The management company takes *operating* risk — it can be sued by an employee, breach a vendor contract, or face a regulatory action. The general partner takes the fund's *unlimited* liability as the controlling partner. If you ran all of this through one entity, a claim arising from *any* of those risks could reach *all* of the assets — the investors' capital, the firm's revenue, and potentially the founder personally. By splitting the structure into separate entities, each with limited liability, you build walls: a trading loss in the fund does not threaten the management company's revenue; an employment lawsuit against the management company does not reach the fund's assets; and the GP's unlimited liability is quarantined in a thin LLC rather than sitting on the founder's personal balance sheet.

Figure 7 shows that containment as a set of walls. A claim lands on the fund; the fund's assets absorb it; the LP wall means the limited partners lose only their committed capital and nothing more; the GP/management-company wall means the liability hits an *entity*, not the founder as a *person*; and the founder's house sits protected behind both walls.

![A layered diagram showing a claim hitting the fund, the fund assets absorbing the loss, two entity walls labeled limited-partner and general-partner stopping the claim, and the founder's personal assets protected behind them](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund-7.png)

A critical caveat, because this is where founders get lazy and allocators get nervous: **the walls only hold if you respect the entities.** Limited liability is not automatic and not unconditional. If you commingle the fund's money with the management company's money, sign the fund's contracts in your personal name, skip the corporate formalities (separate bank accounts, separate books, board or member resolutions where required), or — worst of all — commit fraud, a court can "pierce the corporate veil" and reach through the entity to you personally. The structure protects you *only* to the extent you treat the boxes as genuinely separate. This is one of the deeper reasons operational due diligence teams care so much about clean operations: the separations they verify are the same separations that keep the founder's liability contained. Sloppy operations do not just risk an error; they can dissolve the legal protection the whole structure exists to provide.

Now **tax and economics**. Keeping the entities separate makes two things clean. First, the **incentive allocation as an allocation of profit to the GP** (rather than a fee charged by the manager) lets the performance compensation flow through the partnership with its tax character intact — a structure that is far cleaner when the GP is its own entity governed by the LPA. Second, the **management company's books** show *only* the operating business: fee revenue in, costs out, profit to the owners. That clean separation is exactly what a GP-stakes buyer or an acquirer needs in order to value the business, and exactly what the founder needs in order to understand what they own. Fold the fund and the management company together and you would have a single set of books mixing investors' capital with the firm's operating profit — impossible to value, impossible to sell, and a tax nightmare. The three-box structure is, in part, a machine for keeping the *investors' money* and the *founder's business* legibly apart.

There is a US-specific tax point worth naming, even if you leave the mechanics to counsel: the fund as a partnership is generally a **pass-through** entity — it does not pay entity-level tax; instead, its gains, losses, and income flow through to the partners (the LPs and the GP), who pay tax on their shares. This pass-through treatment is part of why the limited partnership is the dominant US fund structure, and it is why the incentive allocation's *character* (capital gain versus ordinary income) matters and is preserved by allocating it to the GP. The offshore feeder, by contrast, is usually a corporation precisely to *block* the pass-through for non-US and tax-exempt investors who do not want US-taxable income flowing to them — but that, again, is the master-feeder story we treat in [domestic versus offshore: the master-feeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder).

## Common misconceptions

The three-box structure generates a predictable set of misunderstandings. Naming and correcting them is the fastest way to make sure the structure has actually clicked.

**"The fund is the business I own."** No — this is the central error, and the reason the post exists. The fund belongs to the *investors* (the LPs); the founder owns the *management company* and the *general partner*. The fund is the investors' money; the business is the manager. When you sell a hedge fund firm, you sell the management company and the GP economics, never the fund's assets — those go home to the investors. The asset you are building with a decade of your life is the management company, not the fund.

**"The fund earns the fees."** No — the fund *pays* the fees. The fund's job is to compound the LPs' capital; the management fee and the incentive allocation flow *out* of the fund and *into* the manager and the GP. On the fund's books, fees are expenses. On the management company's books, those same fees are revenue. The fund is the payer; the manager and GP are the payees.

**"The management company and the GP are the same thing."** Not legally, even when they are owned by the same people. The management company is the *operating business* that earns the *management fee* under the *IMA*; the GP is the *control entity* that earns the *incentive allocation* under the *LPA* and carries the fund's *unlimited liability*. At a small launch they are run by the same hands and the distinction can feel academic — but it is real, it shows up in the documents and the tax treatment, and it is exactly why founders interpose an LLC as the GP rather than serving as GP personally.

**"Limited liability is automatic, so my personal assets are always safe."** No — limited liability is *conditional* on respecting the entities. Commingle funds, sign in your personal name, skip the formalities, or commit fraud, and a court can pierce the veil and reach you personally. The walls hold only if you treat the boxes as genuinely separate, which is one more reason clean operations are not optional.

**"More entities just means more legal fees for no real benefit."** The separation is not lawyer make-work. It contains liability (a claim on one box stops at that box), it keeps the economics clean (the business is legible and sellable), and it makes the tax treatment work (the incentive allocation keeps its character; the fund passes through). The few extra sets of formation documents are cheap insurance against the much larger cost of mixing investor capital, operating risk, and the founder's personal wealth in one pot.

**"The offshore feeder is shady — onshore-only is cleaner."** No — the offshore feeder exists for a mundane and legitimate tax reason: non-US and US tax-exempt investors (like pensions and endowments) need a *blocker* corporation so that US-taxable income does not flow through to them. It is standard, expected, and entirely above-board. We treat it fully in [domestic versus offshore: the master-feeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder); for now, do not mistake "offshore" for "improper."

## How it plays out in the real world

The three-box structure is not a textbook idealization — it is what you will find inside essentially every US hedge fund, from a USD 30M first launch to a USD 50B multi-strategy platform. What *changes* with scale is not the boxes but their elaboration.

At a **small first launch** — Maya's Meridian Fund at USD 40–200M — the structure is at its leanest. There is one fund (the Delaware LP), one management company (an LLC that is also the investment adviser), and one GP (a thin LLC). The management company and the GP are owned by the same one or two people; the GP exists mostly to isolate liability and to be the clean recipient of the incentive allocation. The whole structure might be formed for somewhere in the range of USD 50k–150k of one-time legal cost, and it can be run by a founder, a junior analyst, and a stack of outsourced service providers. The structure is simple, but it is *complete* — every box is there, because operational due diligence will check for every box, and a fund missing the separations will be rejected regardless of its returns.

At a **large platform** — the multi-strategy or multi-fund firms — the same three roles are spread across many more entities. There may be multiple funds (one per strategy or per fee class), a master fund with several feeders (domestic LP, offshore corporation), multiple management entities across jurisdictions, a separate GP for each fund, and intermediate holding companies that own the management entities. Figure 4 contrasts the founder's naive single-company picture with the correct separated structure; at scale, the "after" side simply has more boxes, but the *logic* — capital in the funds, fees to the managers, control and incentive in the GPs, liability contained — is unchanged.

![A side-by-side comparison contrasting a single mixed company on the left with a separated structure on the right where the fund, the management company, and the general partner each occupy their own box](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund-4.png)

The history of fund *failures* underlines why the separations matter. Consider the canonical operational disaster: **Madoff**. The fraud that destroyed roughly USD 17.5B of investor principal (against fabricated statement values near USD 65B) was, structurally, a collapse of exactly the separations this post describes. There was no genuinely independent administrator striking the NAV, the custody of assets was not independent, and the auditor was a tiny unknown firm — so the entity that was *supposed* to be a passive container for the investors' capital was in fact controlled end-to-end by the manager, with no independent check on whether the assets or the returns were real. The three-box structure, *operated honestly with independent service providers*, is precisely the arrangement that makes a Madoff hard: an independent administrator computing the fund's NAV, independent custody of the fund's assets, and a real auditor are the checks that confirm the fund actually holds what it claims. The boxes are not just legal containers; operated correctly, they are a control system.

On the *liability* side, the catastrophic blow-ups — **LTCM** in 1998 (roughly USD 4.6B lost on leveraged convergence trades), **Amaranth** in 2006 (roughly USD 6.6B lost in a week on concentrated natural-gas bets), **Archegos** in 2021 (over USD 10B of losses to prime brokers through hidden, concentrated total-return swaps) — are mostly stories about *risk inside the fund*, not about the entity structure failing. But they illustrate the boundary the structure draws: the losses landed on the funds and their counterparties (and in Archegos's case, on the prime brokers who extended the leverage), and the limited partners lost their committed capital. The structure does not stop a fund from losing money — nothing does — but it defines *who bears the loss*: the investors who took the risk, capped at their capital, with the founder's personal wealth contained behind the entity walls, absent fraud.

#### Worked example: the management company's take versus the founder's personal balance sheet

Maya's Meridian Fund is at USD 200M, up 10%, at a fresh high-water mark. Her management company earns \$3,000,000 in fees; her GP earns \$4,000,000 in incentive allocation; after \$1,200,000 of costs, the founder's entities throw off about \$5,800,000 of profit for the year. Now a fund-level counterparty claim of \$8,000,000 lands. Where does each pool of money stand?

| Pool | Amount | Exposed to the fund-level claim? |
|---|---|---|
| Fund assets (investors' capital) | \$200,000,000 | Yes — the claim is paid from here first |
| GP entity assets | thin (few assets) | Yes, as a backstop — but little to reach |
| Management company profit | \$1,800,000 (fee less costs) | No — separate entity, separate claim required |
| Founder's personal assets | her own | No — protected by the entity walls, absent fraud |

The fund's \$8,000,000 judgment is absorbed by the fund's assets (the investors' capital, the risk they took). The founder's *business* profit and *personal* wealth sit in separate boxes, behind walls the claim does not cross — provided the entities were respected. *The same structure that lets the founder build and keep an enterprise also defines the bright line between the investors' risk and the founder's own.*

The ownership picture is worth fixing in one last image. Figure 5 shows the two ownership chains running in parallel: the founder owns the management company and the GP (the business); the limited partners own interests in the fund (a pro-rata slice of the NAV) but hold *no* equity in the management company — they do not own the business, only a claim on the fund's value. Two chains, two kinds of owner, meeting only at the fund, which the founder *runs* but does not *own*.

![A two-branch ownership tree showing the founder owning the management company and the general partner on one side, and the limited partners owning fund interests but no management-company equity on the other](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund-5.png)

To consolidate, Figure 3 lays the three entities against the four questions that define each one — what it is, what it owns, what it earns, and what it risks. Read across each row and you have the whole structure in a grid: the fund is a limited partnership that owns the investors' capital, earns returns for the LPs, and risks trading losses; the management company is an operating LLC that owns the staff and contracts, earns the management fee, and risks going broke if it stays sub-scale; the general partner is a control LLC that owns legal control of the fund, earns the incentive allocation, and carries the fund's unlimited liability.

![A three-row comparison grid where each row is an entity and the columns describe what each is, owns, earns, and risks](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund-3.png)

That grid is the post in one figure. Three entities, three jobs, three revenue-or-risk profiles — and only two of them owned by the founder. Figure 6 closes the loop on the *economics* of the entity the founder cares about most: it shows how the management company's fee revenue only becomes profit once it clears the fixed cost stack, at three AUM scales.

![A bar chart showing fixed operating costs against management-fee revenue at forty, eighty, and two hundred fifty million dollars of assets, with a shortfall at the smallest size and a partners' take emerging once the fee clears costs](/imgs/blogs/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund-6.png)

At USD 40M, the 1.5% fee (\$600,000) does not even cover the \$1,200,000 cost stack — a \$600,000 shortfall the founder funds from reserve. At USD 80M, the fee (\$1,200,000) exactly clears costs — break-even, where the management company's profit first turns positive. At USD 250M, the fee (\$3,750,000) leaves roughly \$2,550,000 of partners' take above costs. The management company is the business the founder owns; this chart is the shape of that business as it scales. ## When this matters / Further reading

The three-entity structure matters the day you stop saying "my fund" and start drawing boxes. It matters when you read your own fund documents and need to know which entity each clause binds. It matters when an allocator's operational due diligence team asks who controls the fund, who computes the NAV, and who would bear a loss — and your ability to answer cleanly is itself a signal that you understand the business you are running. It matters when a seed investor or a GP-stakes buyer offers to "buy a piece of your fund," and you need to know they mean a slice of the *management company*, not the fund's assets. And it matters, quietly and constantly, every time the structure does its real job: keeping a claim that lands on one box from flooding through to the investors who did not take that risk, or to the founder personally.

The single sentence to carry forward is the one we opened with: *the fund holds the assets, the management company earns the management fee, and the general partner carries the legal risk and earns the incentive allocation.* Three boxes, three jobs — and the one you actually own is the business, not the money. Everything else in fund formation, from the documents to the fees to the offshore structure to the compliance regime, is built on top of this skeleton.
