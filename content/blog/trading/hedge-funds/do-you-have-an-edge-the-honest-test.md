> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Before you spend a dollar on lawyers, prove four things to yourself, in writing, the way an allocator's analyst would: that your track record is **real** (audited, net of fees, not cherry-picked, against the right benchmark), that your edge is **repeatable** (skill you can distinguish from luck with a real sample), that it is **capacious** (it survives at \$100M or \$500M of AUM, not just at your personal size), and that you are **fundable** (you have capital, the temperament, and a story an allocator buys). Self-deception at this gate is the single most expensive mistake in this entire series.
>
> - Two good years are not a track record. With **zero** true edge, pure luck still hands roughly **14%** of managers a return above 10% a year over two years. A short, lucky stretch in a bull market is indistinguishable from skill until you have enough data to tell them apart.
> - Your "great" track shrinks fast under honesty. A **15% gross** return becomes about **10.4% net** of a 2-and-20 fee, and then has to survive an audit, a benchmark adjustment, and survivorship correction before an allocator will believe it.
> - The edge that works on your **\$2M** personal book may not exist at **\$200M**: market impact, turnover, and the depth of the names you trade set a hard capacity ceiling. Most strategies decay; a few are gone entirely by the time you reach institutional scale.
> - Most launches do not survive. On industry estimates, roughly **half** of new funds are gone within about three years, and a large share never see year five. "Just launch and figure it out" is not a plan; the honest founders fail this test on purpose and stay where they are.

Maya runs a long/short equity book inside a mid-sized firm. The last two years have been exceptional: up 24% in year one, up 19% in year two, while her benchmark did roughly half that. Her PnL is the talk of the floor. Two of her old classmates have launched their own funds. A prime broker's capital-introduction team has already taken her to lunch twice, and a "fund-in-a-box" vendor has emailed her a slide deck titled *From PM to Founder in 90 Days*. Everyone she talks to assumes the only real question is *when*, not *whether*. Her own internal monologue has quietly slid from "could I run my own fund?" to "how soon can I?"

Here is the problem, and it is the same problem that quietly sinks most aspiring founders. Maya has not yet asked the one question that decides everything: **does she actually have an edge, or did she just have two good years inside a strong market?** Her book was net long the whole time. The market was up sharply both years. A naive long-only index, levered to match her volatility, would have produced numbers that look a lot like hers. She has never seen her returns net of the fees she would have to charge, never had them audited, never separated the part that was skill from the part that was a rising tide lifting a long book. She is, in the most literal sense, about to bet her savings and the next several years of her life on a number she has not checked.

This post is the honest test you run *before* you call a lawyer, before you wire a retainer to a fund administrator, before you tell your spouse the plan. It is four questions — is the track **real**, is the edge **repeatable**, is it **capacious**, are you **fundable** — and it is deliberately unkind, because every later post in this series costs real money and real years, and they are all wasted if you fail this one. Figure 1 is the scorecard; we will spend the rest of the post earning the right to fill it in honestly.

## Foundations: what "edge" really means

Before we can test for an edge, we have to define one without the comfortable vagueness most traders allow themselves. An **edge** is a *repeatable, identifiable reason* your returns beat a fair, risk-matched alternative, *net of all costs*, *after you have honestly accounted for the role of luck*. Every word in that sentence is doing work, and the rest of this section defines the terms it leans on. The reader who can run money but has never run the *business* of running money needs all of them, because allocators speak this language fluently and they use it to reject you.

**Returns: gross versus net.** Your **gross return** is what the strategy made before fees and most expenses. Your **net return** is what an investor actually keeps after the management fee, the performance fee, and fund-level expenses come out. The gap is large and it is the gap that matters, because *nobody invests in your gross return* — they invest in the net. A management company sells a net number; a backtest and a personal PnL are gross numbers. Confusing the two is the most common self-flattery in this business.

**The fees that create the gap.** A classic hedge fund charges **"2 and 20"**: a **2% annual management fee** on assets, charged whether you make money or not, plus a **20% performance (or incentive) fee** on the profits. Industry averages have compressed to roughly **1.3% to 1.5% management** and **15% to 17% performance** as of 2025 (HFR fee data), but 2-and-20 remains the mental anchor. The management fee funds the business; the performance fee is the upside. Crucially, the performance fee is an **option**: it pays you a slice of gains and *zero* when you are flat or down. That asymmetry matters later.

**AUM and NAV.** **AUM** (assets under management) is the total capital you run. **NAV** (net asset value) is what one share or unit of the fund is worth — the fund's assets minus its liabilities, divided by units outstanding. Performance fees are usually charged on the rise in NAV per share, and only above the previous peak. That peak rule is the **high-water mark**: if the fund drops, you collect no incentive fee until NAV climbs back above its prior high. We cover the full mechanics in [fees and terms](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark); here it is enough to know the incentive fee is conditional and the management fee is not.

**The audited track record.** A **track record** is the time series of your fund's net returns. It becomes *credible* only when an independent **auditor** has signed off on the numbers and an independent **administrator** has struck the NAV each period. A self-reported spreadsheet is not a track record to an allocator; it is a claim. The difference between a claim and an audited track is the difference between "trust me" and "here is a number a Big Four firm and a third-party administrator will both stand behind."

**The right benchmark.** An edge is *relative*. Making 20% in a year the market made 25% is not skill; it is underperformance dressed as a big number. The **benchmark** is the fair, risk-matched alternative an investor could have bought instead — often an index, scaled to your volatility, plus the right factor exposures (market beta, size, value, momentum). Your edge is what is left *after* you subtract what a cheap, passive, risk-matched portfolio would have delivered. This is **alpha**: return that is not explained by exposure to things investors can buy cheaply. The math of measuring it lives in [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research); the discipline of not fooling yourself lives here.

**Sharpe ratio.** The **Sharpe ratio** is your excess return (over the risk-free rate) divided by the volatility of those returns. It measures return *per unit of risk*. A Sharpe of 1.0 means you earn one unit of excess return for each unit of volatility you take — respectable for a discretionary fund. The trouble is that Sharpe is *estimated* from a sample, and a short sample produces a noisy, often flattering estimate. Distinguishing a real Sharpe of 1.0 from a lucky run of zero-edge trades is a sample-size problem, which is Test 2.

**Drawdown.** A **drawdown** is the peak-to-trough decline in NAV. The maximum drawdown is the worst such fall in your history. Allocators care intensely about drawdowns because they reveal what *actually* happens to your capital under stress, and because investors redeem at the bottom. A track record with a 35% drawdown and a track record with an 8% drawdown can show the same annualized return and be completely different products.

**Capacity.** **Capacity** is the amount of capital your strategy can run *before the act of running it destroys the edge*. A signal that earns 3% a year on a \$10M book might earn 1% on \$200M and nothing on \$1B, because your own trading moves the prices you are trying to capture. Capacity is the most-ignored term on this list and the one that quietly kills the most launches, because it does not show up in a backtest run at small size.

**Survivorship bias.** When you look at the funds that exist *today* and conclude "hedge funds make great returns," you are looking only at the *survivors*. The funds that blew up and closed are not in the data, so the average looks far better than reality. The same bias infects your own thinking: you remember your winning trades more vividly than your losers, and you compare yourself to the famous managers, not to the thousands who launched, lost money, and quietly wound down. An honest assessment corrects for the bodies you cannot see.

**Repeatability, skill, and luck.** Finally, the heart of it: an edge is **repeatable** only if it is **skill** — a process that produces a positive expected return *because of a real, persistent reason* (an information advantage, a structural inefficiency, a behavioral bias you exploit, a risk premium you harvest with discipline) — rather than **luck**, which is the same outcome produced by chance over a sample too small to tell the difference. The entire honest test is, at bottom, a fight against confusing the two.

One more framing before the tests, because it is the spine of this whole series and it explains *why* the test is so unforgiving. A hedge fund is a business that sells an **aligned promise**: to compound a stranger's capital with skill, inside a structure that protects them. The four tests are simply asking, in advance, whether you can keep that promise. *Real* and *repeatable* together decide whether the **skill** in the promise exists. *Capacious* decides whether the skill survives at the size the business requires. *Fundable* decides whether anyone will trust you with the capital and whether you can stay solvent long enough to deliver. Every later post in this series — formation, raising, operations, compliance, survival math — is about keeping the *structure* sound and the *capital* sticky. But none of it matters if the promise itself was never credible, and that is exactly what this post tests.

With the vocabulary in place, the four tests follow. Each one is a gate. You do not "mostly pass." A fund that fails any single gate should not launch, and the founder who pretends otherwise is the one who funds the lawyers, the administrator, and the office lease out of savings for eighteen months before discovering, the hard way, what an allocator's analyst would have told them for free.

## Test 1 — is the track real?

The first test is the most concrete and the most commonly failed by self-deception rather than fraud. Most aspiring founders genuinely believe their track record, because the version they carry in their head is the *most flattering* version: gross, self-reported, focused on the best book, measured against no benchmark, over the period that happens to look best. The "real" version — net, audited, complete, benchmarked — is almost always a smaller number, and sometimes a different number entirely.

There are four ways a track that feels great fails to be real, and you have to clear all four.

**Gross versus net.** This is the first and biggest haircut. The number you trade to is gross; the number you sell is net. Subtract the management fee, subtract the performance fee, subtract fund expenses, and the headline shrinks. Figure 4 shows the full schedule; the worked example below makes it concrete. The discipline is simple: *never quote yourself your gross return again.* From the moment you decide to launch, the only number that exists is net, because that is the only number an investor will ever receive.

**Audited versus self-reported.** Inside a firm, your PnL is struck by the firm's middle office and prime broker; you do not get to mark your own book. The moment you plan to run your own fund, ask: *who strikes my NAV, and who audits it?* An allocator will not take your spreadsheet. They want an **independent administrator** computing the NAV and an **independent auditor** signing the annual financials. The Madoff fraud — roughly \$65B of fabricated statement value, about \$17.5B of principal lost, revealed December 2008 — is the canonical reminder of why: he self-custodied, used a tiny unknown auditor, and reported returns too smooth to be real. The lesson institutional allocators took from it is that *independent administration and audit are not optional*, and a track record without them is, to them, an unverified claim. Figure 3 contrasts the two side by side.

![A before-and-after comparison of two tracks with the same headline return, one rejected in due diligence for being gross and self-reported, one advancing because it is audited net of fees](/imgs/blogs/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test-3.png)

**The right benchmark, honestly chosen.** Maya's two years looked spectacular against cash. Against a volatility-matched long equity index in a bull market, they may look ordinary. The honest benchmark is the cheapest risk-matched alternative an investor could have bought *instead of you*. If your book is net long and the market rose, a large part of your return is **beta** — market exposure anyone can buy for a few basis points — not **alpha**. Subtract the beta. What remains is the part you can charge 2-and-20 for. Most aspiring founders have never done this subtraction, and a meaningful fraction discover that after it, very little is left.

**No cherry-picking.** The final way a track is unreal is selection. Showing only your best sub-book, only the period since your worst year, only the strategy that worked, only the months you want — each of these inflates the number and each is precisely what due diligence is designed to catch. The **SEC Marketing Rule** (Rule 206(4)-1, in force since November 2022) governs exactly this for registered advisers: performance advertising must show **net-of-fee** numbers alongside gross, and constrains hypothetical and extracted performance. The honest version is the *complete* version: every month, every book, including the drawdowns. The voluntary gold standard institutions look for is **GIPS** (Global Investment Performance Standards), which exists precisely to make track records comparable and cherry-pick-proof.

**Survivorship-adjusted, against your own history.** There is a fifth, subtler dimension, and it is the one in the bottom row of Figure 6. Even an audited net track measured against the right benchmark can flatter you if you forget the funds — and the strategies, and the personal accounts — that *did not survive* to be measured. Industry return data is famously survivorship-biased: the dead funds drop out, so the surviving average overstates the typical outcome. Your own self-assessment suffers the same bias internally. You remember the strategy that worked and the seat where you thrived; you under-weight the ideas you abandoned, the year you nearly got fired, the regime in which your approach quietly bled. The honest version of your track asks not "how did my best book do?" but "how would my *full process*, including everything I tried and dropped, have done against the *full population* of managers, including the ones who washed out?" That is the survivorship-adjusted number, and it is almost always the smallest and most honest of all — which is precisely why it is the one to grade yourself against.

A worked illustration of the benchmark adjustment makes the haircut concrete. Suppose Maya's fund-equivalent net return is **10.4%** in a year (from the gross-to-net example above), her book ran at a net-long beta of about **0.6** to the market, and the market returned **14%** that year. The beta-explained part of her return is 0.6 × 14% = **8.4%**. Her alpha — the return *not* explained by simply holding market exposure she could have bought for a few basis points — is therefore roughly 10.4% − 8.4% = **2.0%**. That 2% is the number she can honestly charge a performance fee for; the other 8.4% an investor could have captured with a cheap, levered index. A "great" 24% gross year, run through fees, beta, and an honest benchmark, can leave a real alpha in the low single digits. That is not a reason to despair — a persistent 2% to 4% of true alpha is a genuine, valuable edge if it is *repeatable and capacious*. It is a reason to stop quoting yourself the 24%.

Figure 6 lays out the four versions of "the same track" — gross, net, audited, survivorship-adjusted — and what each one strips away. The number gets smaller and more honest at every step, and the version an allocator believes is the one at the bottom.

![A four-row grid showing gross, net, audited, and survivorship-adjusted versions of a track record and what each one still hides](/imgs/blogs/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test-6.png)

#### Worked example: Maya's 15% gross becomes 10.4% net

Suppose Maya's strategy, run as a fund, earns a **15% gross** return in a year — a strong number. Now charge it the classic **2-and-20**. The 2% management fee comes off first: 15% minus 2% leaves **13%** of net-of-management-fee profit. The 20% performance fee applies to that profit: 20% of 13% is **2.6%**. Subtract it, and the investor keeps:

> 15% gross − 2% management − 20% × (15% − 2%) = 15% − 2% − 2.6% = **10.4% net**.

So a 15% gross year — the kind of number that makes Maya's floor buzz — is a **10.4% net** year to the investor. Fees took **30.7%** of the gross return. Run the same arithmetic on a 10% gross year and the investor keeps just **6.4% net**. The lesson is not that fees are unfair; it is that *the great number in your head is the gross number, and the number you have to sell, defend in due diligence, and beat a benchmark with is the net number that is roughly a third smaller.* Figure 4 plots the whole gross-to-net relationship.

![A grouped bar chart comparing gross and net returns across a range of gross levels, showing a 2-and-20 fee turning 15% gross into about 10.4% net](/imgs/blogs/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test-4.png)

#### Worked example: the founders-class fee does not save the headline

A natural objection is: "I will charge less, so the haircut is smaller." Many launches do exactly this — a **founders share class** at **1.5% and 15%** for early or large investors, sometimes 1% and 10%, to make the early ticket attractive. Run Maya's 15% gross through the 1.5-and-15 terms: the 1.5% management fee leaves **13.5%** of profit, and the 15% performance fee on that is 15% × 13.5% = **2.025%**. The investor keeps:

> 15% gross − 1.5% management − 15% × (15% − 1.5%) = 15% − 1.5% − 2.025% = **11.475% net**.

So cutting to a founders class lifts the net from 10.4% to about **11.5%** — roughly a single point of net return back to the investor. That helps the *raise*, because it sweetens the early ticket, but it barely changes the central truth of Test 1: even on generous terms, the number an investor receives is materially below the gross number in your head, and it must *still* survive the benchmark adjustment and the audit. The founders class is a fundraising tool, not a way to make a thin edge thick. *If your edge only looks like an edge at zero fees, it was never an edge; it was the fee you were not charging yourself.*

If, after the haircut, the benchmark adjustment, and the audit, your real net alpha is small — and for many honest founders it is — Test 1 has already done its job. You do not need to take the next three tests. You need to keep building a track inside a platform that pays the bills while you do.

## Test 2 — is it repeatable?

Passing Test 1 means your number is *real*. Test 2 asks whether it is *yours* — whether it is skill you can reproduce, or luck you happened to catch. This is the test that humbles the most people, because the human mind is built to see patterns and stories in noise, and two good years is a very compelling story.

Start with the uncomfortable arithmetic of luck. Take two thousand managers who all have **exactly zero** true edge — pure coin-flippers, each running a book with a realistic volatility (say 14% a year) for two years. With no skill whatsoever, what do their realized two-year returns look like? Not a tight cluster around zero. They spread into a wide bell, and the right tail is *fat*: in the simulation behind Figure 2, roughly **14% of these zero-edge managers** post an annualized return above **+10%**, and about **6%** clear **+15%** a year, with the luckiest touching the low thirties. None of them has any skill at all. Luck alone manufactures a crowd of "stars."

![A histogram of two-year annualized returns for two thousand managers who all have zero true edge, with a fat right tail of managers who look like stars by luck alone](/imgs/blogs/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test-2.png)

This is the trap Maya is standing in. Her two great years are a sample of size two. Even if she had no edge at all, there was a meaningful chance she would post numbers exactly this good. Her returns are *consistent* with skill — but they are also *consistent* with being one of the lucky coin-flippers. Two data points cannot tell the two apart. The only thing that can is more data, the right statistics, and an honest accounting of how many things she tried.

**Sample size and the t-statistic.** The standard tool for asking "is this return distinguishable from zero?" is the **t-statistic**: roughly, your Sharpe ratio times the square root of the number of years. A useful rule of thumb is that you want a t-stat around **2** before you treat a result as statistically real rather than noise. Rearranged, the number of years you need is approximately **(2 / Sharpe)²**. The implication is brutal for short tracks: a genuinely good Sharpe of 1.0 still needs about **four years** of data to clear a t-stat of 2, and a more typical discretionary Sharpe of 0.5 needs **sixteen**. Two years at a Sharpe of 1.0 gives a t-stat around 1.4 — interesting, not proven.

**Deflated Sharpe and the multiple-testing problem.** It is worse than a single small sample, because you did not try only one strategy. Over a career you tried many ideas, kept the ones that worked, and quietly dropped the ones that did not. Each thing you tried was another lottery ticket, and the more tickets you buy, the more likely one of them looks great by chance. The honest correction for this is the **deflated Sharpe ratio**, which discounts your observed Sharpe for the number of independent trials behind it and for the shortness of the sample. The full machinery — purged cross-validation, the deflated Sharpe, why a backtest's Sharpe is almost always overstated — lives in [overfitting, purged CV, and the deflated Sharpe](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/overfitting-purged-cv-deflated-sharpe-quant-research), and you should read it before you trust your own number. The one-line version: *a Sharpe you computed after picking the best of many tries is not the Sharpe you will live with going forward.*

**Live, out-of-sample, costed.** The gold standard for repeatability is not a backtest at all; it is **live trading**, **out of sample** (after the idea was formed), with **real costs** taken out. A backtest is a hypothesis; a live track is evidence. This is why "my backtest is my track record" is a misconception we correct below, and why allocators discount paper records heavily. If your repeatability case rests on a simulation rather than realized, costed PnL, you have a hypothesis you like, not an edge you have proven. The discipline of building and validating a signal so it survives this transition is covered in [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/backtesting-done-right-quant-research).

#### Worked example: how many years Maya needs to prove a 1.0 Sharpe

Maya believes her true Sharpe ratio is **1.0**. To distinguish that from a zero-edge fluke at a t-stat of **2**, she needs roughly:

> years ≈ (2 / Sharpe)² = (2 / 1.0)² = **4 years**.

She has **2 years**, which gives a t-stat of about Sharpe × √2 = 1.0 × 1.41 = **1.41** — below the bar. If her true Sharpe is actually **0.7** (very respectable, and plausible once beta is stripped out), she needs (2 / 0.7)² ≈ **8 years**. And if, after accounting for the dozen ideas she tried over her career, her deflated Sharpe is **0.5**, she needs about **16 years** of live data to be confident the edge is real. *The honest conclusion is not that Maya has no skill; it is that two good years are statistically incapable of proving she does, and launching on them is a bet that her luck was skill — a bet she cannot yet support with the data.*

This is where many honest, talented people stop, and they are right to. The repeatability test does not say "you are not good." It says "you cannot yet *prove* you are good, even to yourself, and the cost of being wrong is your savings and several years." The disciplined move is to keep accumulating a clean, live, costed track — inside a seat that pays you — until the sample is long enough to speak.

## Test 3 — is it capacious?

Suppose Maya passes the first two tests: her track is real and, with a few more years, statistically credible. There is a third trap, and it is the one that ambushes the *technically strongest* founders — the quants and systematic traders whose edge is most measurable. The trap is **capacity**: an edge that is real and repeatable at your *personal* size can vanish at the *AUM you need to run a business*.

The mechanism is your own market impact. When you trade, you move prices against yourself: you lift the offer when you buy, hit the bid when you sell, and the more you trade, the more you move. A signal that predicts a 50-basis-point move is pure profit if you can get in and out for 5 basis points of cost. Scale the position up tenfold and your cost might be 30 basis points; scale it up again and the cost swallows the signal entirely. The same applies to **turnover**: a high-turnover strategy pays the impact cost over and over, so it hits its capacity ceiling far sooner than a low-turnover one. Your edge, in dollars, is not a fixed percentage — it is a percentage that *decays as size grows*, and at some AUM it crosses zero.

This is why the most dangerous sentence an aspiring founder can say is "I'll figure out capacity later." Capacity is not a detail to optimize after launch; it is a property of the strategy that determines *whether the business exists*. A strategy with a \$50M capacity ceiling cannot support an institutional-grade fund, because — as we will see in the fundability test and in [the founder's readiness](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-founders-readiness-when-to-launch) — you need on the order of **\$100M** of AUM just to cover the fixed costs of running a credible operation. If your edge dies at \$50M, you have a wonderful personal trading account and no fund.

**Capacity decay is non-linear, which is what makes it dangerous.** If impact costs rose smoothly and predictably with size, you could just price them in and stop at the AUM where the edge thinned to your liking. The trouble is that impact is *convex*: doubling your size more than doubles your cost, because you exhaust the visible liquidity at the top of the book and have to reach into thinner and thinner quotes, and because other participants detect a large persistent flow and trade ahead of it. So a strategy can look perfectly healthy as it scales from \$20M to \$50M to \$100M — the edge thins gently — and then fall off a cliff between \$200M and \$400M as impact costs go vertical. A founder watching only the smooth early part of that curve concludes capacity is "fine" right up until it is catastrophically not, which is exactly how a fund raises into a ceiling it cannot see and then spends a year wondering why the edge "stopped working." It did not stop working; the fund simply grew past the size at which the work was possible.

**How to test capacity honestly.** The clean way is to estimate, for your actual strategy: the **dollar volume** you would need to trade per day at a given AUM and turnover, as a fraction of the **average daily volume (ADV)** of the names you trade, and the **market impact** that fraction implies. A common practitioner guardrail is to keep your daily trading to a small fraction of ADV (a few percent) in any name; once you exceed that, impact rises sharply and non-linearly. Then ask: at the AUM I need, does the after-impact edge still clear my fees and costs? If it does not, the edge is not capacious, full stop.

#### Worked example: where Maya's edge breaks on size

Maya's long/short book trades mid-cap names. Her strategy turns over the portfolio roughly **four times a year** (a 25% average holding period). Her edge before costs is **6% a year** gross. The names she trades have a median average daily volume of about **\$15M**, and she wants to keep her daily participation under **5% of ADV** to avoid moving prices, which caps her tradeable volume at about **\$750,000 per name per day**.

At **\$50M** AUM with, say, 40 names and 4x turnover, her annual trading is \$50M × 4 = **\$200M** of volume — comfortably within capacity, and impact costs are small, so most of the 6% survives. Now scale to **\$200M** AUM: annual trading is **\$800M**, her per-name daily needs quadruple, and to stay under 5% of ADV she must either trade the same names more slowly (giving up timely entries, eroding the signal) or push into less liquid names (raising impact). Realistically her impact cost climbs from a fraction of a percent to **2% to 3% a year**, cutting the gross edge roughly in half. By **\$500M**, staying disciplined on impact would force holding periods so long that the signal she is trading has largely decayed before she is fully in — the edge approaches zero. *Maya's strategy is real, repeatable, and has a hard capacity ceiling somewhere around \$150M to \$250M; above it the business of running money destroys the very edge it was built on, and pretending otherwise just means blowing up more slowly with more of other people's money.*

The capacious-strategy founder and the capacity-constrained founder face completely different businesses. The first can scale toward the **\$1B** "club" that controls roughly 90% of industry assets. The second has a real but small edge that can support, at best, a boutique — and must price the whole launch decision around that ceiling. Knowing which one you are, *before* you launch, is the entire point of Test 3.

## Test 4 — are you fundable?

The first three tests are about the *strategy*. The fourth is about *you*, and about whether the world will give you money to run. A real, repeatable, capacious edge is necessary but not sufficient. Plenty of people with genuine edges never raise a dollar, because they lack capital, temperament, or a story allocators can buy. Fundability has three parts.

**Capital and runway.** Launching is not free, and it does not pay you for a long time. You will pay for legal formation — a Delaware LP plus a Cayman master-feeder, with a PPM, LPA, subscription documents, and an investment management agreement runs roughly **\$50,000 to \$150,000** one-time. You will pay an annual run-rate for an administrator, an auditor, outside legal and compliance, technology, market data, and an order management system that, for an institutional-grade small fund, commonly totals **\$0.5M to \$2M a year**. And the incentive fee — your real upside — pays *nothing* until you are above your high-water mark, which on day one you are not. So you need **runway**: enough personal savings to live on, and enough business capital (or day-one AUM) to fund the operation, through a launch period that is structurally loss-making. The honest question is whether you have roughly **18 months** of both personal and business runway, because that is how long it can take to reach break-even AUM and post the first fee-earning year. A founder who launches with 6 months of runway is one bad quarter from being forced to shut down a fund that was actually working.

**Temperament.** Running money inside a firm and running your own fund are different jobs psychologically. As a founder you are not just the trader; you are the boss, the fundraiser, the compliance officer's manager, the person who signs the lease and the person investors call at the bottom of a drawdown demanding to know what you are doing. You will have months where you are flat or down *and* burning fixed costs *and* fielding redemption questions, with no firm above you to absorb the stress. The discipline of [not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up) and of [decision-making under uncertainty](/blog/trading/quant-careers/decision-making-under-uncertainty-the-seniors-edge) is harder when it is your name on the door and your savings on the line. Temperament is not measurable, but it is testable: have you held your discipline through a real drawdown before, with real money, when it would have been easy to abandon it? If you have never been tested under that exact pressure, assume you do not know how you will respond.

**The story allocators buy, and the ODD veto.** Even with capital and nerve, you have to raise, and raising is a *sales and trust* problem, not a returns problem. Allocators — funds-of-funds, pensions, endowments, family offices — buy a *narrative*: who you are, why your edge exists and persists, why you specifically can run it, and why your operation will not lose their money to an avoidable mistake. And they apply **operational due diligence (ODD)**, a separate, *veto-wielding* process from investment due diligence. ODD does not care how good your returns are; it checks segregation of duties, independent administration and pricing, cash-movement controls, valuation policy, counterparty setup, compliance, key-person risk, and business continuity. A fund with a stellar track record gets *rejected* on ODD over a single cash-control gap. We devote a whole post to surviving it — [operational due diligence, the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto) — but at this stage the point is simpler: your fundability includes whether your *operation* will pass a test that has nothing to do with your trading. If you cannot articulate, today, who would custody the assets, who strikes the NAV, who signs the audit, and who can move cash, your fundability story has a hole an ODD analyst will find.

#### Worked example: Maya's runway test

Maya has **\$400,000** in personal savings and a partner whose income covers about half the household. Her family's burn is **\$12,000 a month**, so her share is **\$6,000 a month**, or **\$72,000 a year**. On the business side, she models legal formation at **\$100,000** one-time and a lean first-year run-rate of **\$600,000** (a small administrator, audit, outside compliance, modest tech and data, no office). She expects to launch with **\$20M** of friends-and-family plus her own capital, which at a 1.5% management fee throws off **\$300,000** a year — only half her fixed costs — and her incentive fee is zero until she is in profit above the high-water mark.

So in year one she runs a business that *loses* roughly \$600,000 − \$300,000 = **\$300,000** before any incentive fee, which she must fund from capital, *while* drawing \$72,000 a year to live. Across **18 months** to plausible break-even, her personal draw is about **\$108,000** and the cumulative business shortfall is on the order of **\$450,000**. Her \$400,000 of savings cannot cover both the personal draw *and* the business shortfall; she is short by roughly the business gap unless she either raises more day-one AUM, cuts the operation below institutional-credible levels (which fails ODD), or finds a seeder. *Maya's edge might be real, but on these numbers she is not yet fundable on her own balance sheet — the honest move is to raise more committed day-one capital, line up a seed deal, or wait until her savings can absorb the J-curve before she signs a single legal document.*

#### Worked example: Maya's day-zero ODD-readiness check

Runway is the part of fundability you can count; the operational story is the part that quietly fails great-returns funds. So before she pitches anyone, Maya scores herself the way an ODD analyst would, on the controls that decide the veto. The test is binary per line, and a single hard "no" can sink the allocation no matter how good the track is.

- **Independent administrator striking the NAV?** Maya plans to use a recognized third-party administrator from day one — *pass*. (Self-administration is the Madoff red flag and an automatic fail.)
- **Independent custody of assets?** Cash and securities held at a prime broker and custodian, not in an account she alone controls — *pass*.
- **Independent auditor signing annual financials?** She has budgeted **\$30,000 to \$60,000** a year for a recognized audit firm — *pass*. (A tiny unknown auditor is a flag.)
- **Segregation of duties / who can move cash?** This is her gap. As a one-person shop, Maya can both initiate and approve a wire — a textbook ODD failure. The fix is a documented dual-control process and, ideally, an outside party in the cash-movement chain. *Provisional fail until fixed.*
- **Documented valuation policy and compliance manual?** Drafted with outside counsel before launch — *in progress*.
- **Key-person and business-continuity plan?** A one-person fund *is* key-person risk; she needs a written succession and continuity plan to even start the conversation — *in progress*.

Maya passes the parts that cost money (admin, custody, audit) and fails the parts that cost *process* (segregation of duties, written policies). *The honest reading is that her operation is fundable only after she closes the cash-control gap and writes the policies; a stellar 18% track does not buy her a single point on the ODD scorecard, and an allocator's analyst will find the gap she found in five minutes — better that she find it first.*

There are three honest fixes when fundability fails, and recognizing which one applies is part of the test. The first is to **raise more committed day-one capital** — friends, family, former colleagues, a lead investor — so the management fee covers more of the run-rate from the start and the J-curve is shallower. The second is to **take a seed deal**: a seeder provides day-one AUM, historically on the order of \$25M to \$150M, in exchange for a slice of the management-company economics, typically 15% to 25% of revenue, often with a multi-year lock-up. A seed deal trades away permanent upside for survival, and whether that trade is worth it is its own analysis — but it can turn an unfundable launch into a fundable one overnight. The third is the hardest and most honest: **wait**. Keep building the track inside a paying seat, accumulate personal savings until your balance sheet can absorb the launch loss, and re-take the test in two years with a longer track and a deeper runway. The founders who choose "wait" are invisible in the statistics; the ones who refused to are disproportionately represented in the liquidation data.

This is why fundability is a test, not an afterthought. The market is full of people with genuine edges who launched undercapitalized, ran out of runway in month fourteen, and shut down a fund that would have worked at month thirty. Their edge was real. Their fundability was not. The two failures look identical from the outside — a closed fund — and only one of them was avoidable.

## The honest scorecard

Put the four tests together and you have a scorecard you can actually fill in, in writing, before you spend a dollar. The discipline is to score each gate **pass or fail**, with the *evidence*, and to treat any single failure as a reason not to launch — not a reason to push ahead harder. Here is how to grade each row honestly.

**Real?** Pass only if your track is **net of fees**, **independently administered and audited** (or you have a credible, funded plan to make it so from day one), shown **complete** with no cherry-picking, and **benchmarked** against a volatility-matched, beta-adjusted alternative. If your "great" number is gross, self-reported, or mostly market beta, this is a fail, and it is the most common fail.

**Repeatable?** Pass only if your **live, costed, out-of-sample** track is long enough that the t-statistic clears roughly 2 *after* deflating for the number of strategies you tried. Two good years at a Sharpe of 1.0 is a fail on sample size alone; you have a promising start, not a proven edge.

**Capacious?** Pass only if your after-impact edge still clears your fees and costs at the **AUM you need to cover fixed costs** — on the order of \$100M for an institutional-grade operation. If your edge dies below your break-even AUM, you have a personal account, not a fund, and this is a fail no amount of fundraising fixes.

**Fundable?** Pass only if you have roughly **18 months** of personal and business runway, a temperament tested under real drawdown, and a story — plus an operational setup — that survives both an allocator's investment pitch and a separate ODD review. Missing runway, untested nerve, or an operation with a controls gap is a fail you can sometimes fix (raise more, hire ops, find a seeder) but must not ignore.

The honest funnel in Figure 5 shows where this leads at the population level. Of 100 aspiring founders, perhaps 45 have a genuinely real track, perhaps 20 of those clear the repeatability bar, perhaps 12 of those have a capacious strategy, and perhaps 6 of those are fundable — leaving a single-digit handful who should honestly launch. The numbers are illustrative, but the *shape* is not: each gate is a real filter, and the cumulative effect is that the great majority of people who feel ready to launch should not. The honest ones discover this on a scorecard. The rest discover it eighteen months and several hundred thousand dollars later.

![A funnel showing one hundred aspiring founders thinning through the real, repeatable, capacious, and fundable tests down to a single-digit handful who should launch](/imgs/blogs/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test-5.png)

The point of the scorecard is not to talk you out of your dream. It is to make the *go* decision survivable. A founder who launches having honestly passed all four gates has a real chance. A founder who launches having quietly failed two of them is funding lawyers, administrators, and a lease out of savings to discover what the scorecard would have told them for free.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs do more damage at this stage than any market move, because they let an aspiring founder skip the honest test and feel justified doing it. Each is wrong in a specific, costly way.

**"Two good years means I have alpha."** This is the headline error, and it is purely a sample-size mistake. As Figure 2 shows, with *zero* true edge, roughly 14% of managers post a 10%-plus annualized return over two years by luck alone. Two years cannot distinguish skill from a lucky run; the t-statistic from two years at even a strong Sharpe does not clear the bar for statistical significance. Two good years means you have a *promising start worth continuing to track* — inside a seat that pays you — not a proven edge worth betting your savings on.

**"High returns prove skill."** No — high returns prove *high returns*, which can come from skill, from leverage, from beta, or from luck, and the four look identical on a PnL line. A 30% year from a 2x-levered net-long book in a bull market is mostly beta and leverage; strip those out and the alpha may be small or negative. The honest test is always *risk-adjusted, beta-adjusted, and net of leverage's true cost*. The biggest numbers are the most likely to be flattered by something other than skill, which is exactly why allocators discount them until the source is identified.

**"My backtest is my track record."** A backtest is a hypothesis you fit to history; a track record is realized, costed, out-of-sample PnL. The gap between them is enormous, and it runs almost entirely in one direction: backtests are optimistic, because of overfitting, look-ahead bias, ignored transaction costs, and survivorship in the data. A backtest that earns a Sharpe of 2.0 routinely becomes a live Sharpe of 0.5 or worse once it meets real costs and real markets. Allocators know this and discount paper records to near zero. If your repeatability case rests on a simulation, you do not yet have a track record; you have an idea you like.

**"I'll figure out capacity later."** Capacity is not a post-launch optimization; it is the property that decides whether your business can exist. An edge that dies below your break-even AUM means the act of running enough money to pay the bills destroys the edge. You cannot "figure it out later" because by then you have raised the capital, signed the leases, and discovered the ceiling with other people's money — the most expensive possible time to learn it. Estimate capacity *before* you launch, from your turnover, the liquidity of your names, and your impact, and price the entire decision around the ceiling you find.

**"Raising is easy if you can trade."** Two of the most independent skills in this business are *generating returns* and *raising capital*. Many superb traders are mediocre fundraisers, and raising is a trust-and-narrative sale that takes months of meetings, references, and ODD reviews regardless of your numbers. A real edge gets you in the room; it does not close the allocation. Budget for the raise as a separate, hard, slow project — covered in the capital-raising track of this series — not as a formality that follows automatically from good performance.

**"Offshore and complex structures mean I'm serious."** A Cayman master-feeder is a tool for a specific tax and investor situation, not a badge of seriousness, and over-engineering your structure before you have proven an edge just burns formation capital and creates an operation you cannot yet afford to run credibly. The honest test comes *before* the structure. Pass it first; design the entity to fit the investors and edge you have actually proven, not the fund you wish you had.

**"My friends and family will fund the gap."** A common launch plan leans on a soft circle of supportive capital to bridge the J-curve. The problem is twofold. First, friends-and-family money is the *least* sticky capital you will ever take: it redeems first, fastest, and most emotionally at the bottom of a drawdown, exactly when you can least afford an outflow — and a redemption from someone at your own dinner table is a personal as well as a financial blow. Second, it is rarely enough. A handful of one-hundred-thousand-dollar tickets does not move you toward the institutional break-even AUM; it postpones the real raise rather than replacing it. Treat friends-and-family capital as a genuine vote of confidence and a bridge, never as the answer to fundability — and size your runway as though it could all walk in a bad quarter, because some of it will.

## How it plays out in the real world

The history of hedge funds is, in large part, a history of people who skipped or failed the honest test, raised money anyway, and discovered the truth at scale. The named blow-ups are extreme, but each one is a magnified version of a gate this post asks you to clear.

**Capacity and concentration: Amaranth.** Amaranth Advisors lost roughly **\$6.6B** — about 65% of its more than \$9B in AUM — in roughly a week in 2006, on concentrated natural-gas spread bets run by a single trader. The strategy had worked at smaller size; scaled up and concentrated, the positions became so large relative to the market that the fund *was* the market, and when the spread moved against it there was no one to sell to without crushing the price further. That is the capacity test failing in real time: an edge that survived at one size destroyed itself at another. The fund collapsed.

**Leverage masquerading as skill: LTCM and Archegos.** Long-Term Capital Management lost about **\$4.6B** in months in 1998, running balance-sheet leverage around 25 to 30 times plus roughly \$1.25T of derivatives notional; it took a **\$3.6B** recapitalization organized by the New York Fed with 14 banks to unwind it safely. Its founders included Nobel laureates. The returns that drew investors were, in large part, *leverage applied to small, correlated convergence trades* — high returns that looked like skill until the correlated tail arrived and the leverage cut the other way. Archegos, a family office, repeated the pattern in 2021: roughly **\$10B-plus** of losses to prime brokers (Credit Suisse alone around \$5.5B) from concentrated total-return swaps and hidden leverage spread across multiple banks; the principal was convicted of fraud in 2024. Both are the "high returns prove skill" misconception at industrial scale — the returns were real until you asked *where they came from*, and the answer was leverage and concentration, not a durable edge.

**The operational veto: Madoff.** Madoff is the canonical reminder that the fundability and ODD test is not bureaucratic theater. Roughly **\$65B** of fabricated statement value and about **\$17.5B** of real principal lost, sustained for years, with no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, self-custody, and returns too smooth to be real. Every red flag was *operational*, not investment-related, and the allocators who ran genuine ODD walked away long before the collapse. The lesson for an honest founder is the mirror image: the controls that an ODD analyst checks — independent admin, independent custody, independent audit, segregation of duties — are exactly the controls that make your fund fundable, and their absence is exactly what gets a great-returns fund rejected.

**The luck-launch pattern: two good years, then reversion.** Most failures that matter to *you* are not Amaranth; they are the unnamed funds that launched on exactly Maya's evidence. The pattern is so common it is almost a genre: a PM posts two or three strong years inside a firm during a favorable regime, raises a modest fund on that record, and then reverts to the mean — because the "edge" was a combination of beta, a friendly regime, and luck that two or three years could never separate from skill. The fund spends a year or two flat-to-down, never gets above its high-water mark, earns no incentive fee, burns through its runway on fixed costs, and quietly winds down. No fraud, no blow-up, no headline — just a track record that was real but not *repeatable*, launched before the sample could speak. The launch-survivorship data in Figure 7 is, in large part, this pattern repeated thousands of times. The founders who avoided it are the ones who treated their two good years as a hypothesis and stayed in a paying seat to test it, rather than as a proof and a reason to quit.

**The capacity-launch pattern: the strategy that scaled out of its edge.** A quieter cousin afflicts the strongest quantitative founders. A systematic strategy genuinely works at the founder's personal or proprietary size; the founder raises aggressively on a clean, even audited track; the fund grows past the strategy's capacity ceiling; and the edge — measured honestly, after impact — fades to zero even as the AUM and the management-fee revenue look healthy. For a while the management fee disguises the problem: the business is profitable on fees even as the *strategy* has stopped adding alpha. Then the underperformance becomes undeniable, redemptions start, AUM falls back through the ceiling, and the fund is left smaller, scarred, and no longer able to tell investors a credible alpha story. The edge was real and repeatable; it was never *capacious* at the size the founder raised to. That is Test 3 failing after launch instead of before it — the expensive way.

**The quiet majority: the funds that just died.** The famous blow-ups are the exception. The rule is quieter and more relevant to you: most launches simply *fail*, undramatically. On industry estimates, roughly **7% to 11% of hedge funds liquidate every year** (with spikes in 2008 and 2020), launches and liquidations run roughly in balance quarter to quarter, and a large fraction of new funds do not survive **three to five years**. Figure 7 shows the shape: by around year three, roughly half of a launch cohort is gone. These are not frauds or famous flame-outs. They are mostly people with thin edges, short tracks, undercapitalized launches, or capacity ceilings they discovered too late — people who failed the honest test, launched anyway, and ran out of runway before the edge (if it was even there) could compound. The honest founders in that population are the ones who *did not* launch, kept building inside a paying seat, and are not in the liquidation statistics at all.

![A survivorship curve showing the percentage of fund launches still operating from year one to year five, with roughly half gone by year three](/imgs/blogs/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test-7.png)

The through-line of every one of these stories is the spine of this whole series: a hedge fund is a business that sells an *aligned promise* — to compound capital with skill, inside a structure that protects the investor. The honest test is simply asking, before you make that promise to anyone, whether you can keep it: is the *skill* real and repeatable (the promise is credible), can the *structure* protect the capital (it is sound), and can you *survive* long enough to compound (you are fundable). Fail the test quietly now, on a scorecard, or fail it loudly later, with other people's money. Those are the only two options, and the first one is free.

## When this matters / Further reading

This test matters at exactly one moment, and it is the moment Maya is in: *before* the first dollar leaves your account for a lawyer, an administrator, or a lease. Everything else in this series — formation, capital raising, building the operation, compliance, survival math — assumes you have passed the honest test. If you have not, those posts will teach you, in expensive detail, how to build a credible structure around an edge that is not real, repeatable, capacious, or fundable, and the structure will not save you. The kindest thing this post can do is send some readers back to their seats with a clear conscience and a plan to keep building, and send the rest forward knowing the foundation is sound.

The founders who last are the ones who keep grading themselves as harshly as an allocator would — long after anyone is making them.
