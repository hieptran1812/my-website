> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A hedge fund is *pooled private capital* run by a manager (the general partner) for investors (the limited partners), who pay a management fee plus a share of profits — a structure built to align the manager with the investor.
>
> - The three nouns that define it: **pooled** (one vehicle holds everyone's money), **private** (sold by private placement to accredited or qualified investors, not the retail public), and **aligned** (the manager's biggest payday only arrives when investors make money).
> - It is defined as much by what it is **not**: not a mutual fund (retail, daily-liquid, heavily regulated), not private equity or venture capital (illiquid, drawn-down capital, a 10-year lock), not a prop firm (the firm's *own* money, no outside investors), not a plain separately managed account.
> - The "**hedge**" in the name is largely a historical misnomer — it comes from Alfred Winslow Jones's 1949 long/short fund. Today "hedge fund" is a *regulatory and structural* category, not a promise to hedge anything.
> - The number to remember: the classic fee is **"2 and 20"** — 2% of assets per year plus 20% of the profits — though industry averages have compressed to roughly **1.4% and 16%** (HFR, 2024–2025).

A friend who is a very good software engineer once asked me, over dinner, what exactly a hedge fund *is*. She had heard the phrase her whole life. She knew it meant "rich people" and "Wall Street" and probably "something a little shady." When I started to answer, she cut in with the real question underneath the question: "Isn't it just a fancy mutual fund? Or is it more like — rich people pooling money to gamble?"

That confusion is the most common one in finance, and it is worth taking seriously, because almost every wrong intuition about hedge funds traces back to it. The mutual fund in her retirement account and a hedge fund look superficially identical: both pool money from many investors, both hire a professional to invest it, both report a value per share. But they live in completely different legal universes, serve completely different investors, charge completely different fees, and are allowed to do completely different things. The mutual fund is a *public* product, sold to anyone, watched closely, and built to be boring and safe. The hedge fund is a *private* arrangement, sold only to a narrow class of wealthy and institutional investors, lightly regulated, and built to let a skilled manager do almost anything — go short, use leverage, trade derivatives, concentrate — in pursuit of returns.

This post is the foundation stone of the series. Before we can talk about *how to build* a hedge fund — the legal structure, the service providers, the capital raise, the survival math — we have to be exact about what the thing *is*. So we are going to define it from zero: the entities, the money, the fee, the regulation, and the four cousins it is constantly confused with. By the end you will be able to answer my friend's question in one clean sentence, and you will understand why the word "hedge" is the least accurate part of the name.

![A diagram showing investors and a manager both connected to one pooled fund, which holds the positions and pays fees back to the manager](/imgs/blogs/what-a-hedge-fund-actually-is-1.png)

Figure 1 is the whole thing in five boxes. Investors (the limited partners) put capital into a pooled vehicle (the fund). A manager (the general partner) runs that vehicle under a contract. The fund holds the positions, whose marks make the value go up and down, and out of the fund flow the fees that pay the manager. Hold this picture in your head; everything else in this post is a detail hanging off one of these five boxes.

## Foundations: the building blocks of a fund

Let me introduce a recurring cast so the moving parts stay concrete. **Maya** is a portfolio manager who has spent eight years running a long/short equity book inside a large multi-strategy firm. She is good — good enough that allocators have started asking when she will go out on her own. She decides to launch her own fund, which she names the **Meridian Fund**. We will follow Maya and Meridian through this whole series. Right now, we just need to define the parts.

**Pooled capital.** The defining first feature of a fund is that it *pools* money. Instead of managing fifty separate brokerage accounts for fifty investors, Maya raises money into a single legal vehicle — one entity, one brokerage account, one set of books. Every investor owns a slice of that single pool. The advantage is enormous: Maya makes one trade and every investor participates in it proportionally; she does one set of accounting, one audit, one tax return at the fund level. The pool is the fund. When people say "the Meridian Fund has 100 million USD," they mean the pool holds \$100,000,000 of investor capital.

**The two roles: GP and LP.** A hedge fund is almost always organized as a *limited partnership* (or an LLC structured to behave like one). A partnership has two kinds of partner:

- The **general partner (GP)** runs the business and bears unlimited liability for it. In a fund, the GP is Maya's management entity — the company that makes the investment decisions, signs the trades, and gets paid the fees. The GP is the *manager*.
- The **limited partners (LPs)** are the investors. They contribute capital and share in the profits and losses, but their liability is *limited* to the money they put in — they cannot lose more than their investment, and they have no say in the day-to-day management. The LPs are the *clients*.

This GP/LP split is the legal heart of the structure, and we will return to it constantly. For now, the one-line version: **the GP runs the money, the LPs own the money, and a contract binds them.**

One more nuance, because it surprises people: in a real fund, "the manager" is usually *two* legal entities, not one. There is the **general partner entity** — a small company that sits inside the fund's limited partnership as the GP and is the legal recipient of the incentive fee — and there is the **management company** (sometimes called the investment adviser or "ManCo"), a separate operating company that employs Maya and her team, signs the office lease, and receives the management fee. Why split them? Liability and tax. The GP entity bears the partnership's unlimited liability, so you keep it thin and isolated; the management company is where the actual business — the people, the contracts, the payroll — lives, and you want *that* shielded from the fund's liabilities. So when we say "the GP" loosely, the precise picture is: an adviser/management company that does the work, plus a GP entity that holds the legal partner role, both controlled by Maya, with the fund's LPs on the other side of the table. The full anatomy of these entities is its own post; for now, just know that "the manager" is a small stack of related companies, not a single box, and that this is normal rather than evasive.

**AUM — assets under management.** This is the total size of the pool: the dollar value of everything the fund manages. When Meridian raises \$100M and that money grows to \$110M, its AUM is \$110M. AUM is the single most important number in the fund business, because — as we will see — the management fee is a percentage of it, the cost of running the fund is roughly fixed, and therefore *survival is a question of whether AUM is large enough*. A fund's whole life is a fight to grow and keep AUM.

**NAV — net asset value.** The fund computes, on a schedule (usually monthly), what one "unit" of the pool is worth. That is the **net asset value per share** (or per unit). It works exactly like a mutual fund's price: take the total value of everything the fund owns, subtract what it owes (including accrued fees), and divide by the number of units outstanding. If the pool is worth \$110M net of liabilities and there are 1,000,000 units, the NAV per unit is \$110.00. Investors buy in and redeem out at NAV. NAV is how the fund keeps score and how every investor's stake is valued fairly relative to every other's.

There is a subtlety here that becomes a whole post later in the series but is worth flagging now: *who computes the NAV matters more than almost anything else.* In a properly run fund, the NAV is struck not by the manager but by an **independent fund administrator** — a third-party firm that receives the trade and position data, prices the holdings against independent market sources, accrues the fees, and produces the official NAV. The manager does not get to mark their own book. This independence is precisely what Madoff did *not* have — he produced his own statements, and the "NAV" he reported was fiction. So when we say "the fund computes the NAV," the honest version is "an independent administrator computes the NAV, and the manager is one of the parties who *checks* it." Self-struck NAV is one of the brightest red flags in the business, and learning why is half of what operational due diligence is about.

**The management fee.** The GP charges an annual fee equal to a small percentage of AUM — classically **2%**, now often less. This fee is charged *whether the fund makes money or loses money*. It is the steady, predictable revenue that pays the firm's bills: salaries, rent, data, the administrator, the auditors, the lawyers. On Meridian's \$100M, a 2% management fee is \$2,000,000 per year. The management fee keeps the lights on.

**The incentive fee (a.k.a. performance fee).** The GP also takes a share — classically **20%** — of the fund's *profits*. This is the part that makes hedge funds famous and rich. If Meridian earns \$10M of profit in a year, a 20% incentive fee is \$2M to Maya. Critically, the incentive fee is only charged on *gains*: in a flat or losing year, it is zero. (And because of the *high-water mark*, which we will define below and explore in depth in [a dedicated post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark), it is zero until the fund has recovered all prior losses, too.)

**The alignment.** Put the two fees together and you get the structural promise at the center of the whole industry: the manager makes serious money *only when the investors make money*. The management fee keeps the firm alive; the incentive fee makes the manager wealthy, and it pays only on profit. A manager who loses money collects the small management fee and watches the big incentive fee evaporate. This is the "aligned" in our TL;DR — the incentive fee is, quite literally, a call option the manager holds on the fund's profits, and an option pays nothing when it is out of the money. But the headline design intent is alignment: the manager eats what they cook.

It is worth being honest about the *limits* of that alignment, because a thoughtful founder needs to see both sides. An option's value goes *up* with volatility, and that creates a subtle, perverse incentive: a manager who is far behind, with the incentive fee hopelessly out of reach for the year, may be tempted to take *more* risk — to swing for the fences — because the downside of the gamble falls mostly on the LPs while the upside, if it works, switches the manager's option back into the money. The high-water mark partially fixes this (it forces the manager to climb all the way back before earning a cent), and most allocators specifically diligence for "gambling for resurrection" behavior. But the alignment is not perfect; it is a *good* incentive structure with a known failure mode, not a magic one. Real alignment comes from the high-water mark plus the manager's *own capital* invested in the fund — when Maya has several million dollars of her own money sitting alongside the LPs', her downside is real and the incentive to gamble shrinks. That manager co-investment is one of the first things an allocator checks, and we will return to it when we talk about raising capital.

The deeper point is that the fee model is doing two jobs at once, and they pull in opposite directions. The management fee is *insurance for the manager* — a predictable income that survives a bad year so the firm does not have to fire its team the moment returns dip. The incentive fee is *insurance for the investor* — it makes the bulk of the manager's pay contingent on actually delivering. A fund with *only* a management fee (like a mutual fund) gives the manager no reason to outperform; a fund with *only* an incentive fee would force the manager to bet the firm's survival on every year's returns. Two-and-twenty is the industry's worn-in compromise between those two failure modes, and most of the variation you see — lower management fees for big institutional tickets, higher hurdles, longer crystallization periods — is just allocators and managers renegotiating exactly where on that spectrum the deal should sit.

**Accredited investor (briefly).** You cannot sell a hedge fund to just anyone. Because it is a private, lightly regulated product, US securities law restricts who is allowed to invest. The basic gate is the **accredited investor**: an individual with income over 200,000 USD (300,000 USD jointly) for the last two years, *or* a net worth over 1,000,000 USD excluding their home. Larger or unlimited-investor funds use a higher bar — the **qualified purchaser** (at least 5,000,000 USD in investments). The logic, which we will unpack in the legal section, is that these investors are presumed sophisticated enough — and wealthy enough to absorb a loss — that they do not need the heavy consumer protections wrapped around public products. The practical effect: a hedge fund is a club, and most people are not eligible to join.

That is the entire vocabulary you need. A hedge fund is **pooled capital** (the fund), run by a **GP** for **LPs**, sized by **AUM**, scored by **NAV per share**, paid a **management fee** plus an **incentive fee** that together **align** manager and investor, and offered only to **accredited or qualified** investors. Everything that follows builds on those nine terms.

## The "hedge" misnomer

Here is the surprise: the most defining word in the name describes the least defining feature of the modern industry. To see why, we have to go back to 1949 and a sociologist-turned-journalist named **Alfred Winslow Jones**.

Jones was writing an article for *Fortune* about investment fashions when he became convinced that ordinary investors were leaving money on the table by being merely *long* — by only owning stocks and hoping they went up. He had an idea. What if you held a portfolio of stocks you thought were *good* (long positions), and simultaneously *sold short* a basket of stocks you thought were *bad* — borrowing them, selling them, and profiting if they fell? The long book would make money in up markets; the short book would make money in down markets and, crucially, would *offset* — would *hedge* — the general market risk in the long book. What was left, in theory, was the return from his stock-picking *skill*, with much of the market's swing netted out.

To run this idea, Jones did three things that turned out to be the template for an entire industry. First, he used the long/short structure — a "**hedged**" fund, a fund that hedged out market risk. Second, he used **leverage** to amplify the bet. Third, and most consequentially for our purposes, he organized it as a **private partnership** and took a **20% cut of the profits** as his fee, explicitly modeling the carry on the practice of Phoenician sea captains who kept a fifth of the profits from a successful voyage. The private structure let him avoid the heavy regulation of public funds; the performance fee aligned him with his investors; and the "hedged fund" — quickly slurred into "hedge fund" — gave the thing its name.

Now here is the misnomer. Jones's *strategy* was hedging. But the industry that grew up around his *structure* kept the name and threw out the requirement. Today, the overwhelming majority of funds that call themselves hedge funds do not hedge in Jones's sense at all. A **global macro** fund makes huge directional bets on currencies and interest rates. A **distressed credit** fund buys the bonds of bankrupt companies. A **trend-following** managed-futures fund rides momentum, long or short, across dozens of markets. An **activist** fund takes concentrated long stakes and agitates for change. None of these is "hedged." Some are about as far from market-neutral as you can get — they are *more* exposed to a single risk than a normal portfolio, not less.

So what actually makes them all "hedge funds"? Not the hedging. It is the **structure** Jones invented and the **regulatory category** it lives in:

1. A **private** pooled vehicle (a partnership), not a public one.
2. Sold to **accredited or qualified** investors only, via private placement.
3. Charging a **performance fee** (the 20%), which public funds in the US are generally forbidden from doing for ordinary investors.
4. **Lightly regulated** relative to public funds, which is precisely what lets them short, lever, and trade derivatives freely.

In other words, "hedge fund" today is a label for a *legal wrapper and a fee model*, not for an investment philosophy. The cleanest modern definition is almost circular but correct: a hedge fund is a privately offered, performance-fee-charging pooled investment vehicle that relies on exemptions from the regulations governing public funds. The hedging is optional. The structure is the whole point.

You can watch the name drift from strategy to structure across the industry's history. For its first two decades after Jones, the long/short equity model dominated, and "hedge fund" genuinely meant something close to "a fund that hedges." Then, through the 1970s, 80s, and 90s, managers discovered they could pour almost *any* strategy into Jones's private, performance-fee wrapper — Soros and Druckenmiller's currency bets, the merger-arbitrage desks, the bond shops, the commodity trend-followers — and investors did not care that none of it "hedged," because what they were buying was *access to a skilled manager inside a flexible, lightly-regulated structure*. The strategy became incidental; the wrapper became the brand. By the time the industry crossed a trillion dollars in the mid-2000s, "hedge fund" was unambiguously a structural-and-regulatory term of art, and the people who run them mostly find the etymology a mild embarrassment — a 75-year-old marketing accident that happens to have stuck. For a founder, the practical lesson is to stop hearing "hedge fund" as a description of *what you will do* and start hearing it as a description of *how you will be organized*. Maya is not promising to hedge; she is choosing to operate inside a particular box.

This matters for a founder like Maya in a very practical way. When she sets up Meridian, she is not signing up to hedge — she is signing up to operate inside a specific legal and regulatory box that lets her run her long/short strategy freely, charge a performance fee, and take outside money from sophisticated investors. The series ahead is about that box: how to build it, fill it, and keep it from leaking.

## What it is NOT: hedge fund vs mutual fund vs PE/VC vs prop vs SMA

The fastest way to understand any category is to draw the borders around it. A hedge fund sits in a neighborhood of four close cousins, and people confuse it with all of them. Figure 2 lays the five side by side across the dimensions that actually distinguish them.

![A comparison matrix showing hedge fund, mutual fund, private equity, prop firm, and separately managed account across investors, liquidity, fees, regulation, and source of capital](/imgs/blogs/what-a-hedge-fund-actually-is-2.png)

Let us walk the borders one at a time.

**Hedge fund vs mutual fund.** This is the confusion my friend started with, and it is the most important to clear up. A **mutual fund** (in the US, a registered investment company under the Investment Company Act of 1940 — "the '40 Act") is a *public* product. Anyone can buy it: you can put 500 USD into one through a phone app this afternoon. In exchange for being open to the unsophisticated public, it is wrapped in heavy consumer protection. It must offer **daily liquidity** — you can redeem at the day's NAV any business day. It must publish its holdings, its prospectus, and its fees. It faces strict limits on leverage and on how much it can short. And — the killer constraint for our purposes — under the Advisers Act it generally **cannot charge a performance fee** to ordinary retail investors; it lives on a flat expense ratio, often well under 1%, and frequently far less for an index fund.

A hedge fund is the photographic negative of all of that. It is **private**, open only to accredited or qualified investors. It does **not** offer daily liquidity — your money is typically locked up for a period and then redeemable only quarterly, with a notice period. It does **not** publish its holdings. It is allowed to short, lever, and trade derivatives with few of the '40 Act's restrictions. And it charges a **performance fee** — the thing the mutual fund cannot. The trade is explicit: the hedge fund gives up the giant retail market and accepts a club of wealthy investors, and in return it gets freedom — to use the full toolkit and to charge for performance.

**Hedge fund vs private equity / venture capital.** PE and VC are also private partnerships sold to accredited and institutional investors, and they also charge "2 and 20." So far they look like hedge funds. The difference is *what they buy and how the capital flows.* PE and VC invest in **illiquid** assets — whole private companies (PE) or startups (VC) — that cannot be sold on any given Tuesday. To match that, they use a completely different capital mechanism: a **closed-end, drawn-down** structure. Investors *commit* capital up front (say, 50 million USD), but the fund **calls** that money in pieces over the first few years only as it finds deals, and the fund has a fixed life — typically about **10 years** — over which it buys companies, improves them, and eventually sells them to **return** capital plus gains. There are no quarterly redemptions; your money is locked for the life of the fund. A hedge fund, by contrast, is typically **open-ended**: it holds mostly liquid, tradeable assets (stocks, bonds, futures), takes all your capital at once, marks it to market continuously, and lets you redeem periodically. The slogan: **hedge funds trade liquid markets and let you leave; PE/VC own illiquid companies and lock you in for a decade.**

**Hedge fund vs prop firm.** A **proprietary trading firm** (a "prop firm") trades the firm's *own* money. There are no outside investors, no LPs, no fund, and therefore no management fee and no performance fee in the hedge-fund sense — the traders are employees who earn a salary and a bonus tied to the firm's profits. This is the single cleanest distinction in the whole list: **a hedge fund manages other people's money; a prop firm manages its own.** That one difference changes everything downstream. A prop firm has no investors to raise from, no redemptions to fear, no quarterly investor letters, no operational due diligence to pass. It also has no permanent fee stream — it lives entirely on its own trading P&L. The market-making and high-frequency giants you have heard of are mostly prop firms; many of the great discretionary investors run hedge funds. The firm-archetype landscape — prop versus HFT versus pod shop versus systematic fund — is its own deep subject, covered in [the firm archetypes guide](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund); for our purposes, the line is simply *whose money is at risk.*

**Hedge fund vs SMA.** A **separately managed account (SMA)** is the un-pooled version of the same skill. Instead of putting money into Maya's pooled fund, a single large investor — say a sovereign wealth fund — opens *its own* brokerage account, keeps full ownership and custody of the assets, and hires Maya to trade *that account* under an investment management agreement. The investor can watch every position in real time, can pull the money on its own terms, and often negotiates a lower fee. It is the same manager running the same strategy, but in a one-client, transparent, client-owned wrapper rather than a pooled, opaque, manager-controlled one. Large institutions increasingly demand SMAs precisely because they want that transparency and control. From Maya's side, running SMAs alongside the fund adds operational complexity (more accounts, more reconciliations) but can land big sticky tickets. The distinction: **a fund pools many investors into one vehicle the manager controls; an SMA is one investor's own account that the manager merely trades.**

**A word on the liquidity terms, because they are load-bearing.** The matrix says a hedge fund offers "locked up then quarterly with notice," and that deserves unpacking, because the liquidity terms are not arbitrary fine print — they are how the fund protects itself from its own investors. A typical set of terms looks like this: a **lock-up** of about a year (you cannot redeem at all during it, "soft" if there is an early-exit penalty, "hard" if there is simply no exit); thereafter a **redemption frequency** of quarterly (some funds monthly, some annual); a **notice period** of 30 to 90 days (you must tell the fund in advance that you want out); and **gates** — an investor-level gate of around 25% (you can only pull a quarter of your stake in any one period) and fund-level gates that cap total redemptions. In a crisis, a fund may invoke **side pockets** to wall off illiquid positions, or in the extreme **suspend** redemptions entirely.

Why all this machinery? Because the deepest risk in the whole structure is a **liquidity mismatch** — promising investors more liquidity than the *assets* can actually provide. If Maya offers monthly redemptions but holds positions that take three months to sell without crushing the price, then a wave of redemption requests forces her to dump assets into a falling market, which hurts the *remaining* investors, which prompts *them* to redeem, and so on — a redemption cascade that has killed many funds with perfectly good strategies. The lock-up, the notice period, and the gates exist to slow that cascade down and give the manager time to liquidate in an orderly way. They are the structural answer to the fact that a fund's liabilities (investor capital that can leave) and its assets (positions that take time to sell) do not always move on the same clock. This is a recurring theme of the series, and the asymmetry between the mutual fund (which legally *must* match daily-liquid assets to daily redemptions) and the hedge fund (which is *allowed* to lock capital up precisely so it can hold less-liquid, higher-returning assets) is one of the real economic reasons the private structure exists at all.

If you internalize Figure 2, you will never again confuse the five. The hedge fund is the privately-offered, accredited-only, performance-fee, lightly-regulated, *pooled* vehicle that trades *liquid* markets and lets investors *redeem periodically*. Change any one of those — make it public, make it retail, drop the performance fee, lock it for a decade, use only the firm's money, or un-pool it into a single account — and you have walked into one of the four neighboring categories.

## The legal wrapper

We have said a hedge fund is "private." That word is doing a lot of work, and it is worth understanding *why* the fund is private and *how* the law makes that possible — at least at the level a founder needs before the dedicated legal posts. (The full machinery of [registration and regulation under the SEC, CFTC, and Reg D](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d) is its own post; here is the conceptual frame.)

**Why private at all?** In the US, if you want to sell an investment to the general public, you must *register* the offering with the Securities and Exchange Commission — a slow, expensive, disclosure-heavy process designed to protect ordinary people who cannot evaluate the risk themselves. Mutual funds do exactly this and accept the heavy regulation that comes with it. A hedge fund does *not* want to do that — registration would forbid the performance fee, cap the leverage and shorting, force daily liquidity, and bury the manager in compliance. So instead, a hedge fund relies on **exemptions**: provisions in the securities laws that say "you do not have to register *if* you only sell privately, to a limited and sophisticated audience."

**The private placement.** The relevant exemption is **Regulation D** under the Securities Act of 1933. The most common path, **Rule 506(b)**, lets a fund raise an unlimited amount from an unlimited number of *accredited* investors (plus up to 35 non-accredited ones, in practice usually zero) *as long as it does not engage in general solicitation* — meaning it cannot advertise to the public, cold-call strangers, or put up a billboard. Maya can market Meridian only through pre-existing relationships and private conversations. A newer alternative, **Rule 506(c)**, *does* allow public advertising, but in exchange every single investor must be *verified* as accredited (not just self-certified). Most hedge funds choose 506(b) and stay quiet. This is what "private placement" means: the fund is offered privately, by exemption, to a qualified audience, never registered for public sale.

**The investor-count exemptions.** There is a second law to dodge: the Investment Company Act of 1940, the one that makes mutual funds into mutual funds. A pooled investment vehicle would normally be caught by it. Hedge funds escape through one of two exemptions, and the choice shapes the fund's investor base:

- **Section 3(c)(1)** allows a fund with **no more than 100 beneficial owners**. It is the simpler path, used by smaller and newer funds. The limit on investors is the cost.
- **Section 3(c)(7)** allows an **unlimited** number of investors, but *every* one of them must be a **qualified purchaser** (the higher 5 million USD bar). It is the path larger, more institutional funds use, because it removes the 100-investor ceiling — at the price of only being able to take the wealthiest investors.

**Why the accredited / qualified gate exists.** Stack these together and the logic of the wealth thresholds becomes clear. The whole edifice — the Reg D exemption, the 3(c)(1)/3(c)(7) exemptions — rests on a single premise: *these investors are sophisticated and wealthy enough that they do not need the public-market protections.* The accredited-investor and qualified-purchaser tests are the law's blunt proxy for "can evaluate the risk and survive a loss." It is imperfect (wealth is not the same as sophistication), but it is the gate, and it is *why* a hedge fund is allowed to be opaque, illiquid, and aggressive in ways a mutual fund never could be. The privacy is not for secrecy's sake; it is the price of admission to the lighter regulatory regime, and the wealth gate is the bouncer at the door.

Figure 6 captures the resulting split-screen: the public fund on one side — open, liquid, transparent, regulated — and the private fund on the other — gated, locked, opaque, free. They are two genuinely different animals, and the difference is the legal wrapper.

## "2 and 20" in plain numbers

The phrase "2 and 20" is the most famous shorthand in the industry, and it is worth grinding through the actual arithmetic, because the structure is more interesting than the slogan. "2 and 20" means a **2% annual management fee** on assets plus a **20% incentive fee** on profits. Figure 4 traces how one dollar moves through the fund before we put numbers on it; Figure 5 shows the fee waterfall on a good year, which is what we will compute now.

![A pipeline showing limited-partner capital flowing into the fund, into positions, generating profit and loss, splitting into fees, and leaving a net return to the investor](/imgs/blogs/what-a-hedge-fund-actually-is-4.png)

#### Worked example: 2-and-20 on a 100 million dollar fund returning 10% gross

Maya's Meridian Fund starts the year with \$100,000,000 of AUM. She has a good year: the portfolio returns **10% gross** — that is, before fees. Let us split that into the two fees and find what the LP actually keeps.

- **Gross profit.** 10% on \$100,000,000 is \$10,000,000 of trading gains before any fee.
- **Management fee.** 2% of the \$100,000,000 of assets is \$2,000,000. This is charged on *assets*, not profits, so it comes off regardless of performance. (In practice it is usually charged monthly on average AUM; we will use the simple annual version here.)
- **Profit after the management fee.** \$10,000,000 of gains minus the \$2,000,000 management fee leaves \$8,000,000 of net-of-management-fee profit.
- **Incentive fee.** The 20% incentive fee is charged on the profit. Charging it on the \$8,000,000 profit *after* the management fee gives 20% × \$8,000,000 = \$1,600,000. (Funds differ on whether the incentive fee is computed before or after the management fee; charging it on the after-management-fee profit, as here, is common and investor-friendly.)
- **Total fees.** \$2,000,000 + \$1,600,000 = \$3,600,000.
- **Net to the LP.** \$10,000,000 of gross profit minus \$3,600,000 of total fees leaves **\$6,400,000** for the investors — a **6.4% net return** on their \$100M.

So a 10% gross year becomes a 6.4% net year, and the manager earns \$3.6M of fees on the way. Notice the split inside that \$3.6M: \$2M of it is the steady management fee that would have shown up even in a flat year, and only \$1.6M is the performance-linked incentive fee. Figure 5 is exactly this waterfall.

![A fee waterfall stack starting from a 10 million dollar gross return, subtracting a 2 million management fee and a 1.6 million incentive fee, leaving 6.4 million net to the investor](/imgs/blogs/what-a-hedge-fund-actually-is-5.png)

*The headline 10% gross return is really a 6.4% net return once both fees are taken — the gap between gross and net is the price of the manager's skill.*

The fees themselves have come down a lot from the classic 2-and-20. Figure 3 puts the headline against the reported industry averages.

![A bar chart comparing the headline 2-and-20 fee against compressed industry averages of about 1.4 percent management and 16 percent incentive](/imgs/blogs/what-a-hedge-fund-actually-is-3.png)

#### Worked example: the same year under compressed 1.5-and-15 fees

The "2 and 20" headline is now closer to legend than to median. Reported industry averages have compressed to roughly **1.3–1.5% management** and **15–17% incentive** (HFR fee data, 2024–2025), and newer or smaller managers often go lower still to win early capital — a "**founders share class**" might charge **1.5% and 15%** or even **1% and 10%** to day-one investors. Let us re-run the identical 10% gross year under **1.5 and 15**.

- **Management fee.** 1.5% of \$100,000,000 = \$1,500,000.
- **Profit after the management fee.** \$10,000,000 − \$1,500,000 = \$8,500,000.
- **Incentive fee.** 15% × \$8,500,000 = \$1,275,000.
- **Total fees.** \$1,500,000 + \$1,275,000 = \$2,775,000.
- **Net to the LP.** \$10,000,000 − \$2,775,000 = **\$7,225,000**, a **7.225% net return**.

The same 10% gross year now returns 7.2% net to the LP instead of 6.4% — an extra **0.825 percentage points** of return that the fee compression handed straight to the investor. For Maya, the fee cut costs her management company \$825,000 of revenue in this single year (\$3.6M minus \$2.775M). That tension — the manager wants high fees, the investor wants low ones, and the market clears somewhere in between — runs through the whole business, and it gets a full treatment in [the post on fees and terms and the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark).

*Every tenth of a percent shaved off the fee is a tenth of a percent added to the investor's net return — fees are not a footnote, they are a direct transfer between the two sides of the table.*

#### Worked example: NAV-per-share mechanics when investors subscribe and redeem

The fees are only fair if every investor is valued correctly relative to every other, and that is the job of **NAV per share**. Suppose Meridian launches on January 1 with two investors who together put in \$100,000,000. The fund sets the opening NAV at a clean \$1,000.00 per unit, so it issues 100,000 units (\$100,000,000 ÷ \$1,000). Investor A's \$60M buys 60,000 units; investor B's \$40M buys 40,000 units.

Now run the year. The portfolio grows the pool to \$110,000,000 *before fees* — but we have to net out the accrued fees to get the true NAV. Using the 2-and-20 from above, total fees on this \$10M gain are \$3,600,000, so the *net* asset value of the pool is \$110,000,000 − \$3,600,000 = \$106,400,000. With 100,000 units still outstanding, the **NAV per unit is now \$1,064.00** (\$106,400,000 ÷ 100,000). That is the 6.4% net return showing up in the unit price: \$1,000 grew to \$1,064.

Now a *third* investor, C, wants to subscribe on December 31 with \$10,640,000. She does not get units at the old \$1,000 price — that would let her steal a year of gains she did not earn. She **subscribes at the current NAV of \$1,064**, receiving \$10,640,000 ÷ \$1,064 = **10,000 units**. The pool is now \$117,040,000 across 110,000 units — and the NAV per unit is unchanged at \$1,064. C's entry diluted no one; she paid full freight for her slice. Redemptions work in reverse: if A later wants out, she sells her units back to the fund *at the prevailing NAV*, and the fund pays her units × NAV in cash.

*NAV per share is the fund's fairness machine — it lets investors come and go at different times without anyone capturing or surrendering returns they did not earn.*

#### Worked example: splitting the management fee from the incentive fee over two years

The two fees behave very differently across time, and a two-year example makes the difference vivid. Take Meridian at \$100M and watch a good year followed by a bad year.

**Year 1 (up 10%).** As computed above: \$2M management fee + \$1.6M incentive fee = \$3.6M total. The fund ends at a NAV of \$1,064 per unit (its new peak, its **high-water mark**).

**Year 2 (down 10%).** The portfolio loses 10%, so the \$106.4M pool falls to roughly \$95.76M before fees. Watch what happens to each fee:

- **Management fee.** Still charged — it is on *assets*, not gains. 2% of the (average) ~\$100M of assets is again roughly \$2,000,000. The investor pays the management fee even in a losing year. This is the fee's defining feature and its defining criticism.
- **Incentive fee.** **Zero.** There is no profit, so there is nothing to take a share of. Worse for Maya, the **high-water mark** now bites: the NAV fell below its prior peak of \$1,064, and Maya cannot charge *any* incentive fee in the future until the NAV climbs back above \$1,064. She has to first make the investors whole on the loss, for free, before her incentive fee switches back on.

So across the two years Maya's management company collected about \$4,000,000 of management fees (steady, both years) and only \$1,600,000 of incentive fees (year 1 only, then nothing). The management fee is the *salary* that keeps the firm alive through droughts; the incentive fee is the *bonus* that only arrives in good years and that the high-water mark can switch off for a long stretch after a drawdown. This asymmetry is why a fund's survival depends so heavily on AUM and the management fee — and it is the entire subject of the dedicated [high-water-mark post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark).

*The management fee is a salary the manager collects rain or shine; the incentive fee is a bonus that pays only above the previous peak — and after a bad year, that peak can sit out of reach for a long time.*

## Common misconceptions

The structure invites a predictable set of wrong beliefs. Here are six worth correcting, because each one trips up smart people — including, sometimes, the founders themselves.

**Myth 1: "A hedge fund hedges."** We have already dismantled this, but it bears repeating because it is the most common error of all. The name is a 1949 fossil. Most modern hedge funds take large directional or concentrated bets and are *less* hedged than a diversified retirement portfolio, not more. The word describes a *structure and fee model*, not a risk posture. If you remember one thing from this post, make it this.

**Myth 2: "It's just rich people gambling."** The opposite of hedging is the opposite myth — that hedge funds are reckless casinos. Some blow up spectacularly (we will name them shortly), but the median fund is a small, grinding business run by people obsessed with *not* losing money, because a large drawdown can be a death sentence: it switches off the incentive fee via the high-water mark, it triggers redemptions, and it can end the firm. Far from gambling, the disciplined ones treat capital preservation as the first job. The relationship between edge, sizing, and ruin is a real and quantitative subject — see [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up) — and the funds that survive are the ones that take it seriously.

**Myth 3: "2 and 20 makes the manager rich on day one."** Run the numbers on a *small* fund and the myth collapses. A new fund with, say, \$30M of AUM collects 2% = \$600,000 of management fee — and that has to cover the administrator, the auditor, the lawyers, the data, the office, and *every salary* before the founder takes home a cent. Institutional-grade fixed costs commonly run **0.5–2 million USD per year**, which means a small fund can be *losing money at the management-company level* even while charging "2 and 20." The incentive fee only gets rich in good years on *large* AUM. We compute the brutal break-even AUM in a later post; the short version is that you need roughly **80–100 million USD** just to cover institutional-grade fixed costs from the management fee. The fee model gets you rich at scale, not at launch.

**Myth 4: "Offshore = shady."** Many hedge funds run an *offshore* vehicle — classically a Cayman Islands fund alongside a Delaware one, in a "master-feeder" structure. This is not tax evasion or secrecy; it is *plumbing*. US taxable investors invest through the Delaware partnership (which passes income through to them), while non-US investors and US tax-exempt institutions (like pensions and endowments) invest through the Cayman feeder to avoid an entity-level tax problem peculiar to their situation. The offshore vehicle exists to solve a tax-and-eligibility puzzle for legitimate investors, not to hide anything — the fund still has US auditors, US administrators, and US regulators looking at it. The structure gets its own post; for now, retire the reflex that offshore means dirty.

**Myth 5: "The SEC doesn't watch small funds."** Light regulation is not *no* regulation. Even a small private-fund adviser that is too small to fully register with the SEC is usually an **Exempt Reporting Adviser**, files a public Form ADV, is subject to the antifraud provisions of the securities laws, must follow the **Marketing Rule** when it advertises performance, and can be examined. Cross the **100 million USD** regulatory AUM line and full SEC registration kicks in; cross **150 million USD** of private-fund assets and the confidential **Form PF** systemic-risk filing kicks in too. A founder who treats "we're small" as "we're invisible" is setting up the compliance blow-up that kills more funds than bad trades do — the subject of the [registration and regulation post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d).

**Myth 6: "If you can trade, you can run a fund."** This is the founder's myth, and it is the thesis of this entire series. Maya's edge — her ability to pick stocks — is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. Running a fund is running a *business*: forming the legal entity, hiring an administrator and auditor, building the operations and reconciliation, passing operational due diligence, raising capital from skeptical allocators, surviving the cash-flow drought before AUM scales, and not tripping a compliance wire. A brilliant trader with no business can lose the whole thing to an ops failure or a redemption cascade. The skill that makes the returns and the skill that keeps the fund alive are different skills. Learning the second one is what the rest of this series is about, and it is the natural continuation of [the senior's path from PM to founder](/blog/trading/quant-careers/from-senior-to-pm-or-starting-your-own).

## How it plays out in the real world

Step back from the definitions and look at the actual industry, because the abstractions only mean something against real scale, real money, and real failures.

**The size.** The global hedge fund industry manages somewhere around **4.5 to 5.0 trillion USD** as of 2026, by the estimates of data providers like HFR, Preqin, and BarclayHedge (the exact figure varies by source and by what you count). That is up from under half a trillion in the late 1990s — the industry crossed roughly 1 trillion USD around 2004 and 4 trillion USD around 2021–2022. Figure 7 traces that growth.

![A line chart showing global hedge fund assets under management growing from under half a trillion dollars in the late 1990s to roughly four and a half to five trillion by 2026](/imgs/blogs/what-a-hedge-fund-actually-is-7.png)

**The concentration.** That headline number hides the most important fact about the industry: it is brutally *concentrated*. There are perhaps **8,000 to 10,000** hedge funds globally, but the **billion-dollar club** — funds managing over 1 billion USD — controls roughly **90%** of all industry assets. The median fund is far smaller; most managers run **under 100 million USD**. So the picture of "the hedge fund industry" as a handful of famous multi-billion-dollar names is half right and half deeply misleading: those names control most of the *money*, but they are a tiny minority of the *funds*. When Maya launches Meridian at \$100M, she is a typical-sized fund and a long way from the giants — and that is the normal starting point, not a disappointment.

This concentration is not an accident; it is a gravity well created by the cost structure we sketched in Myth 3. Because the fixed cost of running an institutional-grade fund is roughly *constant* regardless of size — the administrator, the auditor, the lawyers, the data feeds, and the compliance program cost about the same whether you run \$50M or \$5B — the management fee on a small fund barely covers the bills, while the management fee on a large fund is almost pure profit at the management-company level. A fund at \$80–100M is fighting to break even; a fund at \$1B collecting even a compressed 1.4% management fee is pulling in \$14M a year of fee revenue against the same \$1–2M of fixed cost. That asymmetry pulls capital toward the funds that already have it, because allocators also prefer size (it signals durability and lets them write a large enough ticket to matter), and so the rich get richer. The whole survival arc of a small fund — which is the central drama of this series — is the fight to climb out of the break-even zone and into the part of the curve where the economics finally work. Most launches never make it, which is exactly why the math of break-even AUM and the discipline of keeping costs down get their own dedicated treatment later on.

**The launches and the graveyard, side by side.** In a typical recent year the industry has seen on the order of *hundreds* of launches and *hundreds* of liquidations per quarter — roughly balanced, sometimes net-negative (HFR's quarterly reports track this). Read that correctly: the industry is not a one-way escalator of new money. It is a churning population where new funds open at roughly the rate old ones close. For a founder, this is the sobering frame for the whole endeavor — launching is common, and so is dying, and the difference between the two is usually not the quality of the trades but the soundness of the business underneath them.

**The named types.** The single label "hedge fund" hides a zoo of strategies, and naming a few makes the diversity concrete:

- **Long/short equity** — Maya's strategy; the direct descendant of A. W. Jones. Buy good stocks, short bad ones.
- **Global macro** — directional bets on currencies, rates, and commodities driven by a view of the world economy (the style associated with names like Soros's Quantum Fund and Bridgewater).
- **Quantitative / systematic** — funds that trade on models and code rather than discretionary judgment (Renaissance, Two Sigma, D. E. Shaw), the technical machinery of which lives in the [quantitative-finance series on building and evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research).
- **Event-driven** — merger arbitrage and distressed credit, profiting from corporate events.
- **Multi-strategy "pod shops"** — large platforms (Millennium, Citadel, Point72) that allocate capital across dozens of independent teams under tight risk control.

All of them share the same wrapper — private, accredited-only, performance-fee, lightly regulated, pooled — and almost none of them "hedge" in Jones's original sense.

**The failures.** Finally, the industry is honest only when you include its graveyard, because the famous blowups teach the structure's real risks better than any definition. Roughly **7 to 11% of hedge funds liquidate every year**, and a large fraction of new launches do not survive three to five years. The legendary failures each carry a lesson this series will return to:

- **Long-Term Capital Management (1998)** lost about **4.6 billion USD** in months on convergence trades run at perhaps 25–30× balance-sheet leverage, requiring a **3.6 billion USD** rescue organized by the New York Fed. Lesson: leverage plus correlated tail risk.
- **Amaranth Advisors (2006)** lost about **6.6 billion USD** — roughly two-thirds of its assets — in about a week on concentrated natural-gas bets. Lesson: concentration and the illusion of capacity.
- **Archegos (2021)** was technically a *family office*, not a hedge fund, but it caused **over 10 billion USD** of losses *to its prime brokers* through hidden leverage spread across multiple banks via total-return swaps. Lesson: hidden leverage and counterparty blind spots.
- **Madoff (revealed 2008)** was not a hedge fund at all but a **Ponzi scheme** wearing the costume of one — with roughly **65 billion USD** of fabricated statement value. It is the canonical *operational* failure: no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, self-custody of assets, and "too smooth" returns that no real strategy produces. Lesson: independent administration, custody, and audit are not optional niceties — they are the things that make a fund *not a fraud*.

Notice the pattern across these four. Two of them (LTCM and Amaranth) were *investment* failures — brilliant people who took too much leverage or too much concentration and got run over by a market that moved against them. The other two (Archegos and Madoff) were *structural* failures — hidden leverage that the counterparties could not see, and a fund that was a fraud from the start because it had none of the independent machinery a real fund has. For a founder, the lesson is that you can die two completely different ways: from a bad trade, or from a bad business. This series spends comparatively little time on the first (the strategy and risk-management craft lives in the sibling [quantitative-finance series](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research)) and almost all of its time on the second, because the second is the one that founders consistently underestimate. Maya knows how to avoid Amaranth's mistake — she is a careful risk-taker. What she does not yet know is how to avoid Madoff's *structure* — and the gap between a great trader and a great fund is precisely that knowledge.

That last lesson is the bridge to the rest of the series. The thing that separates a real fund from Madoff is not the strategy — it is the *structure*: independent service providers, real custody, real audits, real controls, and investors who check all of it through operational due diligence. Maya's job, once she has the edge, is to build that structure soundly enough that an allocator's diligence team says yes — and to keep it sound enough that the fund survives long enough to compound.

## When this matters / Further reading

If you take one thing from this post, take the one-sentence definition: **a hedge fund is pooled private capital, run by a manager (the GP) for accredited or qualified investors (the LPs), who pay a management fee plus a share of profits — a privately offered, lightly regulated, performance-fee vehicle that trades liquid markets and lets investors redeem periodically.** It is defined as much by the four cousins it is *not* — the public mutual fund, the decade-locked PE/VC fund, the own-money prop firm, the single-client SMA — as by what it is. And the "hedge" in the name is a 1949 fossil; the structure, not the hedging, is the whole point.

This matters the moment you stop thinking of a hedge fund as a *strategy* and start thinking of it as a *business* — which is exactly the shift this series asks of you. Maya's stock-picking is the easy part. The hard part, the part that decides whether Meridian is alive in five years, is the structure, the operations, the compliance, the capital, and the survival math.
