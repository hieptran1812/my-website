> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The net asset value (NAV) is the single number that *is* the fund: it sets the price at which money comes in, the price at which money goes out, and the base on which every fee is charged. Strike it wrong and you have mispriced every investor at once.
>
> - **NAV = assets − liabilities − accrued fees**, then divided by shares to get the **NAV per share**. Every subscription, redemption, and fee is computed against it, so a one-cent error per share scales straight into real dollars across the whole investor base.
> - The **independent administrator** strikes the official NAV from third-party data (positions from the prime broker, prices from vendors) so that no investor ever has to take the manager's word for what the book is worth. The manager keeps a **shadow NAV** only to catch the administrator's errors, never to override the official number.
> - The **valuation policy** governs the hard part: **Level 1** assets price off a quoted market, **Level 2** off observable inputs, and **Level 3** off the manager's own model — and Level 3 is where almost every NAV scandal lives, because the person who marks the position is the person whose fee depends on the mark.
> - The one fact to remember: Bernard Madoff struck his own NAV with no independent administrator and a tiny unknown auditor. The fabricated returns were the symptom; the missing administrator was the disease, and it is the first thing an operational reviewer checks.

Maya almost did not open the email. It was the first week of the month, the Meridian Fund's administrator had just sent over the draft NAV pack for sign-off, and she had a dozen more urgent things on her desk. But a number in the valuation summary caught her eye on the way past: the fund's position in a small, thinly-traded convertible bond was still carried at par, \$100, the same mark it had held for three months. She knew — because she had been on the phone with two dealers the previous week trying to trim it — that the bond was not worth par. The real bid was closer to \$92. Nobody had been able to print a trade in it for weeks, the administrator's pricing vendor had simply rolled the last available quote forward, and the position had quietly stayed at par on the books the whole time.

It was an eight-cent error on one bond. It felt small. Then Maya did the arithmetic that turned her stomach. The position was \$8 million of face. Carrying it at \$100 instead of \$92 overstated the fund's assets by roughly \$640,000. The fund had struck last month's NAV on that inflated number — and last month, two investors had redeemed a combined \$4 million at that NAV. They had been paid out at a price that was too high. The cash to overpay them had come from somewhere: it had come, dollar for dollar, out of the pockets of the investors who *stayed*. A single stale mark, left unchallenged for one extra month, had quietly transferred money from her loyal long-term LPs to two people walking out the door. And it was, technically, her administrator's mark — which meant it was now her problem to explain.

This is the thing about NAV that no one tells a portfolio manager until they are running their own fund: the net asset value is not a reporting detail you compute after the real work of trading is done. It *is* the fund. It is the price at which every new dollar buys in, the price at which every departing dollar cashes out, and the base on which you charge every fee you will ever earn. A fund is, at bottom, a promise to compound capital fairly across a pool of investors — and the NAV is the one number that makes "fairly" mean something. Get it right and the promise holds. Get it wrong, even by accident, even by eight cents on one bond, and you have silently mispriced everyone in the pool at the same time.

This post is about that number: what it is, who is allowed to compute it, how the monthly machine that produces it works, and where it goes wrong. Figure 1 is the whole production line on one page — positions and prices coming in on the left, the official NAV being struck in the middle, and the investor statements going out on the right. We will spend the rest of the post earning the right to read every box on it.

## Foundations: what NAV is and who strikes it

Before any of the mechanics, you need about a dozen terms firmly in place. If you trade for a living, the *markets* side of all this is familiar — you know what a mark is, you know a bond can be hard to price. What is usually a blank page is the *business* layer: who is allowed to strike the official number, what policy governs the judgment calls, and how the whole thing turns into the statement your investor reads. Let us build it from zero.

**Net asset value (NAV).** The total value of the fund: everything it owns, minus everything it owes, minus the fees that have built up but not yet been paid. If the Meridian Fund holds \$122 million of long positions and cash, owes \$1.5 million on its shorts and unpaid expenses, and has accrued \$0.5 million of fees it has not yet crystallized, its NAV is \$122M − \$1.5M − \$0.5M = \$120 million. That \$120 million is the fund.

**NAV per share (or NAV per unit).** The NAV divided by the number of ownership shares outstanding. If the \$120 million fund has 100,000 shares, the NAV per share is \$1,200. This is the *price* of the fund: an investor putting in \$1.2 million buys 1,000 shares; an investor redeeming 500 shares is paid \$600,000. Every transaction with an investor happens at the NAV per share. We will return again and again to this single point, because almost everything that matters about NAV follows from it.

**The administrator (fund administrator, the "admin").** The independent outside firm that strikes the NAV, keeps the official register of who owns what, processes the money coming in and going out, accrues the fees, and produces the investor statements. The word that carries all the weight is *independent*: the administrator works off third-party data and does not take the manager's word for the value of the book. We cover the administrator as one of the providers in [the service-provider stack: prime broker, administrator, auditor](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor); here it is the central character.

**Mark-to-market.** Valuing a position at the price the market is currently quoting. An S&P 500 stock that closed at \$182.40 is marked at \$182.40 — there is nothing to argue about, because a deep, active market printed that price for everyone to see. Mark-to-market is the easy, honest case.

**Mark-to-model.** Valuing a position by running a model, because no observable market price exists. A bespoke over-the-counter option, a private loan, a distressed claim with no active market — these have no screen price, so someone has to *estimate* their value from inputs and assumptions. Mark-to-model is the hard, dangerous case, because the estimate depends on the assumptions, and the assumptions depend on who is choosing them.

**The fair-value hierarchy (Level 1, 2, 3).** The accounting framework (under both US GAAP and IFRS) that sorts every position by how observable its pricing inputs are. **Level 1** = a quoted price in an active market for the identical asset (mark-to-market, no judgment). **Level 2** = no direct quote, but the inputs are observable — comparable bonds, a yield curve, broker quotes — so the price is derived from real market data. **Level 3** = the inputs are unobservable, so the value rests on the manager's own model and assumptions (mark-to-model, maximum judgment). The further down this ladder a position sits, the more the NAV depends on someone's opinion, and the harder it is for an outsider to check.

**Accrual.** Recognizing a cost or a revenue as it is *earned or incurred*, smoothly over time, rather than only when cash actually moves. The 2% management fee is not paid in one lump; the fund accrues roughly one-twelfth of it each month, so the NAV already reflects the fee the manager has earned even though the cash transfers later. Accrued fees are a liability of the fund — money it owes but has not yet paid — which is why they are subtracted in the NAV build-up.

**Capital account.** The running ledger of a single investor's stake in the fund: what they put in, what they have taken out, their share of the fund's profits and losses, and the fees charged against them. Add up every investor's capital account and you get the fund's total NAV. The capital account is how the one fund-level number gets divided fairly among the many investors who own it.

**The close.** The end-of-period process — usually monthly for a hedge fund — of capturing every trade, reconciling every position and every dollar of cash against outside records, applying every price, accruing every fee, and only then striking the official NAV and producing statements. "The monthly close" is the assembly line, and the NAV is what comes off the end of it.

Here is the through-line that connects all of these. A hedge fund sells one promise: *trust me with your capital and I will compound it fairly inside a structure that protects you.* The NAV is the word *fairly* made concrete. It is the mechanism that ensures the investor who buys in today, the investor who redeems tomorrow, and the investor who stays for ten years are all priced off the same honest measure of what the fund is worth. The administrator exists so that this measure is struck by someone other than the person whose pay depends on it. The valuation policy exists so that the judgment calls — the Level 3 marks — follow a written rule rather than a convenient guess. And the monthly close exists so that nothing slips through unreconciled. Strip out any one of them and the promise gets less credible. Strip out the administrator, as we will see, and the promise stops being institutionally investable at all.

## How NAV is calculated

Start with the simplest possible version and then add the real-world complications one at a time. At its core, NAV is a subtraction:

> **NAV = total assets − total liabilities − accrued fees**

and then a division:

> **NAV per share = NAV ÷ shares outstanding**

That is genuinely the whole formula. Everything difficult about NAV lives inside the three quantities on the right-hand side, not in the arithmetic. Figure 2 shows the build-up as a stack: you start with gross assets, peel off the liabilities, peel off the accrued fees, and what remains is the net asset value, which divided by shares gives the price.

![A vertical stack showing the NAV build-up: gross assets at the top, then liabilities subtracted, then accrued fees subtracted, leaving net assets, which divided by shares gives NAV per share](/imgs/blogs/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator-2.png)

**Total assets** is the market value of everything the fund owns: long positions, cash sitting at the prime broker and the bank, securities pledged as margin, dividends and interest that have been earned but not yet received (these are *receivables*), and the value of any derivatives that are in the money. Each long position is valued at its mark — which is where the fair-value hierarchy does its work, because a Level 1 stock is trivial to value and a Level 3 private position is a judgment call.

**Total liabilities** is everything the fund owes: the value of the short positions (a short is an obligation to buy a security back, so it is a liability that grows when the stock rises), unsettled trades the fund must pay for, accrued expenses like the audit fee and administrator fee, and any borrowing. Note the asymmetry that trips people up: a long stock going up *increases* assets, but a short stock going up *increases liabilities* — both of which move NAV the way your P&L tells you they should.

**Accrued fees** are the management fee and any incentive fee the manager has earned over the period but not yet been paid. We accrue them so the NAV is *net of fees* — the number that reflects what the investor actually has a claim on, after the manager's cut. This is not optional bookkeeping. An investor redeeming mid-year must be charged their fair share of fees up to that date, and the only way to do that is to have been accruing the fee into the NAV all along. We cover the fee mechanics — the high-water mark, the hurdle, crystallization — in [fees and terms: the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark); here, the point is simply that the accrued fee is a liability that gets subtracted before the NAV is struck.

#### Worked example: striking the NAV per share on a \$120 million book

It is the end of June. The Meridian Fund's administrator pulls the positions and prices and builds the NAV. Gross assets come to \$122.0 million: \$90 million of long equity positions, \$28 million of cash and margin sitting at the two prime brokers, and \$4 million of receivables (dividends declared and interest accrued but not yet paid in). Liabilities are \$1.5 million: \$1.0 million of unsettled purchases awaiting settlement and \$0.5 million of accrued expenses (audit, admin, legal, market data). The management fee has accrued \$0.18 million for the month and the incentive fee has accrued \$0.32 million against the year's gains so far, for \$0.5 million of accrued fees in total.

The net asset value is \$122.0M − \$1.5M − \$0.5M = **\$120.0 million**. The fund has 100,000 shares outstanding, so the NAV per share is \$120.0M ÷ 100,000 = **\$1,200.00**. Every investor transaction this month — a new subscription, a redemption, the calculation of each LP's profit share — happens at \$1,200.00 per share. If the administrator had instead struck the NAV at \$1,201.00, a redeeming investor pulling 1,000 shares would be overpaid by \$1,000 — money that comes out of the remaining investors. *The arithmetic is trivial; the discipline of getting every input exactly right before the division is the entire job.*

Now watch how leverage and the share count complicate the picture. Suppose the Meridian Fund had run gross exposure of 200% — \$240 million of positions on \$120 million of equity, financed by the prime broker. The assets line would balloon to over \$240 million and the liabilities line would carry the matching financing, but the *net* asset value — the equity the investors own — would still be \$120 million. NAV measures the investors' stake, not the size of the book the manager is running on top of it. A reader who only sees "NAV \$120M" learns nothing about whether the fund is running flat or levered three-to-one; that lives in the exposure report, not the NAV. This is why allocators ask for gross and net exposure *alongside* the NAV — the single number deliberately nets all of that out.

There is also a timing distinction inside the word "NAV" that trips up new founders. Most funds publish two NAVs each period: an **estimated NAV** and a **final NAV**. The estimated NAV comes out fast — often within two or three business days of month-end — and is a preliminary figure, useful to investors who need an early read but explicitly marked as subject to change. The final NAV comes out later, usually within one to two weeks, after every reconciliation is clean, every Level 3 mark is committee-approved, every corporate action is captured, and every break is cleared. The final NAV is the one that is *binding* — the price at which subscriptions and redemptions actually settle and fees actually crystallize. A founder must be disciplined about which number is which: telling an investor an estimated NAV of \$1,205 per share and then settling their redemption at a final \$1,200 is a small embarrassment if you flagged it as an estimate, and a serious trust problem if you did not. The gap between estimated and final is itself a diagnostic — a fund whose estimates routinely drift far from its finals is a fund whose marks are soft or whose reconciliation is weak, and an attentive allocator watches that gap.

## The independent administrator

Now the central question of the entire post: *who is allowed to compute this number?* The honest answer that costs funds their launches is "not the manager." The whole reason the administrator exists is that the person who runs the money cannot be the same person who values the money, because their pay depends on the value being high.

The administrator is an outside firm — names you will hear include Citco, SS&C/GlobeOp, State Street, Northern Trust, NAV Consulting, Apex, and a long tail of smaller shops — whose entire business is striking NAVs for funds that are not their own. They sit between the manager and the investor and do four jobs that the manager is deliberately *not* allowed to do alone.

**They strike the official NAV.** The administrator pulls the fund's positions directly from the prime broker (not from the manager's spreadsheet), pulls prices from independent market-data vendors (Bloomberg, ICE, Refinitiv, Markit), values the book, accrues the fees per the written policy, and computes the NAV per share. Because every input came from a third party, an outsider can later re-trace the work. That re-traceability is the product. An institution is not paying the administrator for arithmetic — they could do arithmetic themselves — they are paying for a number that was struck by someone with no financial interest in it being high.

**They keep the investor register (the "transfer agency" function).** The administrator maintains the official record of who owns how many shares, in which class, with what high-water mark and what lock-up status. When you read that a fund has an "independent register," this is it: the manager cannot quietly add a phantom investor or fudge the share count, because the official ownership record sits at the administrator.

**They process subscriptions and redemptions.** Money coming in and going out flows through the administrator, who confirms the investor is eligible (accredited, qualified, KYC/AML cleared), strikes them in or out at the correct NAV per share, and updates the register. This is a cash-control point: the administrator confirms that subscription money actually arrived before issuing shares, and computes the exact redemption payment.

**They produce the investor statements.** Each month the administrator sends every investor a statement of their capital account — opening balance, flows, P&L, fees, ending balance — from the *independent* books. When an investor gets a statement from Citco rather than from the manager's own letterhead, they are getting a number the manager did not type.

### Choosing and paying for an administrator

For a founder, the administrator is a hire, not a vendor you toggle on. The choice carries real weight in operational due diligence, because an allocator's analyst recognizes the established names and is wary of the unknown ones — the Madoff auditor was fatal precisely because no one had heard of it. A well-known administrator is itself a piece of credibility: it signals that a competent, recognized third party has agreed to put its name on your NAV and has run its own onboarding diligence on you before doing so.

The cost is modest relative to what it buys. A small fund typically pays an administrator somewhere in the range of \$30,000 to \$100,000 or more per year, scaling with the number of investors, the number of positions, the valuation complexity (a Level 1 equity book is cheap to administer; a portfolio thick with Level 3 marks and derivatives is not), and the reporting cadence. That is a meaningful line in a small fund's budget — part of the roughly \$0.5M to \$2M of annual fixed costs an institutional-grade fund carries before it earns a dollar — but it is the single most defensible expense in the stack. Set against the alternative, which is being un-investable to every institution that runs ODD, the administrator fee is not a cost to be minimized; it is the entry ticket. We price out the full fixed-cost stack and its break-even implications in [the service-provider stack: prime broker, administrator, auditor](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor).

The relationship matters as much as the name. The administrator and the manager must agree, in writing, on the service-level terms: how many business days after month-end the draft NAV arrives, how breaks are escalated and resolved, who has authority over a disputed Level 3 mark, and how the shadow-NAV reconciliation is documented. A cheap administrator that takes three weeks to strike a NAV and waves through breaks is worse than no administrator at all, because it gives the *appearance* of independence without the substance. What an ODD analyst is really testing is whether the independence is real — whether the administrator actually pulls its own data and actually resists a manager who wants a favorable mark — or merely nominal.

### Why independence matters

The reason independence is not a nicety is written in the largest fraud in the history of the industry. Bernard Madoff's investment business had **no independent administrator**. Madoff "struck" his own NAVs, sent his own statements on his own letterhead, used a **tiny, unknown three-person auditor** (Friehling & Horowitz) that no real institution had ever heard of, and held the assets in self-custody rather than at an independent prime broker. The returns were a fabrication — a smooth, almost straight-line ascent that no real strategy produces — but the fabrication was only *possible* because no independent party ever struck the number or confirmed the assets existed. When the scheme collapsed in December 2008, the fabricated statement value was around \$65 billion and the actual principal lost was roughly \$17.5 billion.

The lesson the industry took from Madoff is precise and it is the one an operational reviewer checks first: it is not enough for the returns to look good. *Someone independent must strike the NAV, someone independent must hold the assets, and someone independent must audit the year.* A manager who self-administers is not necessarily a fraud — but they have removed the one control that would catch a fraud, and from the outside you cannot tell the difference. This is exactly the gap the operational-due-diligence analyst is paid to find, and it is why we give it its own treatment in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto). An allocator's ODD memo that reads *"manager self-administers; no independent NAV"* ends the conversation regardless of the Sharpe ratio.

### The shadow NAV

If the administrator strikes the official number, what does the manager's own accounting do? It produces a **shadow NAV** — the fund's own internal estimate of the NAV, computed in parallel — and its job is the opposite of what you might guess. The shadow NAV does not *override* the administrator's number. It exists to *catch the administrator's errors*.

Administrators are good but not infallible. They miss a corporate action, apply a stale vendor price, fat-finger a position quantity, or mis-key a subscription. The manager — who knows the book intimately — runs a shadow NAV precisely so that when the administrator's draft pack arrives, there is something independent to check it against. If the manager's shadow says NAV per share is \$1,200.40 and the administrator's draft says \$1,205.10, that 40-basis-point gap is a **break**, and somebody investigates it before the NAV is signed off. Maybe the administrator missed a dividend; maybe the manager's shadow has the stale mark. Either way, the discrepancy gets resolved by going back to the source data, not by one side simply deferring to the other.

Figure 4 shows the two-book structure. The same source data — positions from the prime broker, prices from the vendors — flows to both the administrator and the manager. The administrator produces the *official* NAV that investors transact on; the manager produces the *shadow* NAV; and the two are reconciled, with every break investigated rather than waved through. The official number is the one the world uses; the shadow exists only to keep the official one honest.

![A branching diagram showing source data of prime broker positions and vendor prices flowing to both the administrator and the manager, the administrator producing the official NAV that investors transact on and the manager producing a shadow NAV, with both feeding into a reconciliation and break report](/imgs/blogs/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator-4.png)

The deep point here is one of *control design*, not arithmetic. You do not get a trustworthy number by having one very careful person compute it. You get a trustworthy number by having two parties compute it from the same raw data and then forcing every difference between them to be explained. That is the same logic that runs through the whole [operating model: front, middle, and back office](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office) — the fund's middle and back office reconcile the manager's books against the prime broker and the administrator every single day, so that the month-end NAV is the culmination of a daily discipline rather than a heroic monthly scramble.

It is worth slowing down on what a NAV **break** actually is, because the reconciliation between the manager's shadow and the administrator's official strike is where the whole control either works or quietly fails. A break is any line where the two sets of books disagree: a position the manager carries at a different mark than the administrator, a quantity that does not tie, a dividend the manager accrued that the administrator missed, a fee computed two different ways, a subscription booked in one and not the other. When the administrator sends the draft NAV pack — usually a few business days into the new month — the manager's operations team lays the shadow NAV next to it and runs a line-by-line difference. Most months the two agree to the penny on the overwhelming majority of lines, and the handful of breaks are small and explainable.

The discipline is in how the breaks are handled. Each break gets one of three dispositions. It is a *manager error* (the shadow had a stale price or a mis-booked trade), in which case the manager corrects the shadow and the administrator's number stands. It is an *administrator error* (the admin missed a corporate action or applied a wrong quantity), in which case the manager flags it, the administrator fixes the official books, and the NAV is restruck before sign-off. Or it is a *genuine valuation disagreement* — most often on a Level 2 or Level 3 mark — which escalates to the valuation policy and the committee rather than being settled by whoever shouts louder. What must never happen is the fourth path: a break that is simply *plugged* or netted away to make the two numbers tie, because a plugged break is an un-investigated error hiding in the NAV. An operational reviewer will ask to see the break log and will look specifically for breaks that were closed without an explanation — that is the signature of a reconciliation that is theater rather than control.

Consider a concrete break. The administrator's draft says the Meridian Fund's NAV per share is \$1,205.10; the manager's shadow says \$1,200.40 — a gap of \$4.70 per share, or about \$470,000 on 100,000 shares, roughly 0.4% of NAV. That is far too large to ignore. The operations team drills in and finds the cause: the administrator booked a \$500,000 dividend on a long position as *received* this month, but the shadow correctly shows the stock went ex-dividend with the payment not due until next month, so it should be a *receivable*, not cash, and a different position was double-counted by \$30,000. Both are administrator errors. The manager documents them, the administrator restrikes, and the official NAV comes down to match the shadow's \$1,200.40. Had the manager not kept a shadow, the fund would have struck — and transacted investors at — a NAV that was \$4.70 per share too high, overpaying anyone who redeemed and over-accruing the incentive fee. The shadow NAV is not redundant bookkeeping; it is the only thing standing between an administrator's honest mistake and a wrong price for every investor.

## Valuation policy

Now to the part of NAV that actually keeps founders and administrators up at night. For the vast majority of a typical long/short equity book, valuation is trivial: the positions are Level 1, they have a closing price, and the administrator marks them to that price with zero judgment. The trouble starts the moment a position has no clean market price — and every interesting strategy eventually holds some of those.

The **valuation policy** is the written document — agreed between the manager, the administrator, and signed off as part of governance — that says exactly how every kind of position will be valued and who resolves a dispute. It is not optional and it is not a formality. The valuation policy is what turns "we value Level 3 positions at fair value" from a phrase into a process: which pricing source is primary, which is the fallback, how a stale price is detected, when a third-party valuation agent is brought in, and who has the final say. A fund without a clear, followed valuation policy is a fund where the marks are decided ad hoc — which is exactly the conflict an allocator is terrified of. We treat the governance side of this in depth in [valuation and conflicts: governance](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/valuation-and-conflicts-governance); here we care about the mechanics of the three levels.

Figure 3 lays out the three levels of the fair-value hierarchy against how each is priced, who verifies the mark, and how much NAV risk it carries.

![A matrix comparing Level 1, Level 2, and Level 3 assets across how each is priced, who verifies the mark, the NAV risk each carries, and example instruments, showing risk rising as inputs move from quoted prices to manager models](/imgs/blogs/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator-3.png)

**Level 1 — quoted prices in an active market.** A large-cap stock, a Treasury, an exchange-traded future. There is a closing price that the whole world can see, the administrator pulls it straight from the exchange feed, and there is nothing to dispute. The NAV risk is essentially zero because the manager has no input into the number. If your fund is entirely Level 1, valuation is a non-issue and your ODD on this point is a formality.

**Level 2 — observable inputs, no direct quote.** A corporate bond that has not traded today but whose price can be derived from comparable bonds, the issuer's curve, and dealer quotes; an interest-rate swap valued off the observable yield curve; a less-liquid stock priced off the last available quote plus a comparables check. The administrator uses pricing vendors and broker quotes and cross-checks them. There is *some* judgment — which quote to trust, how stale is too stale — but the inputs are anchored to real market data, so the mark is defensible. The NAV risk is moderate: the main danger is a quote that has gone stale (the convertible-bond problem from Maya's opening), where the vendor simply rolls the last print forward and nobody notices the market has moved underneath it.

**Level 3 — unobservable inputs, mark-to-model.** A private loan, a distressed claim with no active market, an early-stage private position, a bespoke OTC structure, a side-pocketed illiquid bet. There is no market price and no observable comparable, so the value rests on a model and the manager's assumptions. This is where almost every NAV scandal lives, for a reason that is structural rather than moral: *the person best placed to mark the position is the manager, and the manager's incentive fee depends on the mark being high.* A 10% bump in a Level 3 mark flows straight into NAV, straight into the high-water mark, and straight into the incentive fee. The control against this is exactly to *take the mark out of the manager's hands*: a valuation committee, an independent third-party valuation agent, and the year-end auditor all scrutinize Level 3 marks, and the valuation policy spells out who wins a dispute.

To make this concrete, a serious valuation policy for Level 3 assets is built as a *pricing-source hierarchy* — a ranked, ordered list of where the mark comes from, so that the choice is made by the policy rather than by whoever is feeling optimistic that month. A typical hierarchy reads something like this. First, a recent transaction in the actual instrument: if the same loan or the same private position changed hands at a real price in the last quarter, that price is the mark, full stop. Second, independent broker quotes: two or three dealers who make a market in the instrument are asked for indicative levels, and the mark is set off those (often the average, sometimes the more conservative side). Third, a third-party valuation agent: a specialist firm — Houlihan Lokey, Duff & Phelps/Kroll, Lincoln International, and the like — is retained to value the position independently, which is the gold standard for genuinely illiquid privates. Only when none of those is available does the policy fall to the fourth tier: a manager model — a discounted-cash-flow or comparable-company calculation built by the manager — and even then the model's inputs, the discount rate, the comparables, the growth assumptions, are documented and reviewed rather than chosen freely each period.

The crucial distinction inside that hierarchy is **broker quotes versus models**. A broker quote is a number a market participant with skin in the game is willing to stand behind, at least indicatively; a model is a number the manager constructs from assumptions. Broker quotes are higher in the hierarchy precisely because they are harder to manipulate — you cannot simply *decide* a dealer's quote, whereas you can absolutely choose a discount rate that flatters a DCF. The danger with broker quotes is that they can be "shopped": a manager who does not like the level from Dealer A keeps calling until Dealer B gives a friendlier number, then uses B. A good valuation policy closes that door by naming the dealers in advance, requiring all quotes received to be retained (not just the convenient one), and routing the final choice through the valuation committee.

The **valuation committee** is the body — typically the chief financial officer or chief operating officer, the chief compliance officer, sometimes an independent member, and explicitly *not* the portfolio manager acting alone — that approves every Level 3 mark before it enters the NAV. Its job is to be the institutional check on the manager's optimism: to ask why a mark moved (or, more tellingly, why it *did not* move when the world changed), to insist the pricing hierarchy was followed, and to document the rationale so the year-end auditor can re-trace it. The committee is the difference between "the manager marked it" and "the firm marked it, under a policy, with a record." When an operational reviewer asks how a Level 3 position is valued, the answer they want is the pricing hierarchy plus the committee minutes — not "the PM's best estimate."

#### Worked example: a Level 3 mark dispute and its effect on NAV

The Meridian Fund holds a \$6 million (cost) position in a private credit instrument — a direct loan to a mid-size company — that has no market price. It is a Level 3 asset, valued by a model. At the last quarter-end the valuation committee carried it at \$6.0 million, par. This quarter, the borrower has missed a covenant and a comparable deal repriced lower in the secondary market. Maya, who wants to be conservative, proposes marking it down to \$5.4 million — a 10% haircut. A more aggressive manager in her seat might argue the covenant breach is technical and the loan should stay at par.

Watch what the 10% haircut does to the fund. The Meridian Fund's NAV before this mark is \$120 million. Cutting the loan by \$0.6 million takes NAV to \$119.4 million — a 0.5% hit to NAV per share, from \$1,200.00 to \$1,194.00. But the second-order effect is the one that reveals the conflict. The fund's incentive fee accrues 20% of gains above the high-water mark. The \$0.6 million write-down erases \$0.6 million of gains, which erases \$120,000 of accrued incentive fee (20% × \$0.6M). So marking the loan *down* costs Maya \$120,000 of her own fee this quarter. The aggressive manager who keeps it at par protects that \$120,000 — and books an incentive fee on a gain that may not be real. This is precisely why the mark cannot be the manager's call alone: every Level 3 write-down is the manager voting against their own paycheck, and the valuation policy, the committee, and the independent valuation agent exist so the vote is not left to the conflicted party. *When the person who marks the asset is the person whose fee depends on the mark, the only honest answer is to give the mark to someone else.*

#### Worked example: the stale Level 2 mark from the hook

Return to the convertible bond from Maya's opening. The fund holds \$8 million of face value in a thinly-traded convertible carried at \$100 (par). It is a Level 2 asset — there *are* observable inputs in principle, but the bond has not printed a trade in weeks, so the pricing vendor has been rolling the last quoted price forward. The true bid, per the two dealers Maya called, is around \$92.

Carrying \$8 million of face at \$100 instead of \$92 overstates the position by \$8M × 8% = \$640,000, and therefore overstates NAV by \$640,000 — about 0.53% on the \$120 million fund, lifting NAV per share from a true \$1,193.60 to a reported \$1,200.00. Last month, two investors redeemed a combined \$4 million at the inflated \$1,200.00. Had the bond been marked at \$92, the correct NAV per share would have been \$1,193.60, and those redemptions would have paid out 4,000,000 ÷ 1,200.00 = 3,333.3 shares × the true \$1,193.60 = roughly \$3,978,667 — about \$21,300 less than they were actually paid. That \$21,300 of overpayment came directly out of the investors who stayed. *A stale mark is not a harmless rounding error: it silently moves money from the investors who hold to the investors who leave, and the longer it sits, the more it moves.* The fix is a stale-price policy in the valuation document — a rule that any quote older than N days triggers a re-mark against fresh dealer levels — so the administrator catches it before the NAV is struck, not three months later.

## The monthly close

Everything so far has been about *what* the NAV is and *who* strikes it. The monthly close is *how* it actually gets done — the assembly line that runs in the first business days of every month and ends in a signed-off NAV and a stack of investor statements. For a founder, understanding the close is what turns NAV from a black box the administrator owns into a process you can supervise. Figure 7 lays out the checklist.

![A grid showing the monthly-close checklist in eight numbered steps: trade capture, position reconciliation, cash reconciliation, pricing, corporate actions, fee accrual, clearing breaks, and final sign-off and publication of the NAV](/imgs/blogs/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator-7.png)

**1. Trade capture.** Every trade executed up to the cutoff must be booked — nothing left in a trader's blotter, nothing settling next month that should have been accrued this month. A missed trade is a missed position, and a missed position is a wrong NAV.

**2. Position reconciliation.** The fund's books, the prime broker's records, and the administrator's records must all agree, position by position, share for share, across every prime broker. A *break* — a position that does not tie — must be chased down and resolved, not netted away. With multiple prime brokers (standard for any serious fund after Lehman), this reconciliation runs across every counterparty.

**3. Cash reconciliation.** Every dollar of cash — at each prime broker, at the bank, in transit — must tie to the ledger. Cash recon is the single most important operational control in the entire fund, because cash is what a fraud actually steals. An administrator that confirms the cash independently is the control that Madoff's structure lacked.

**4. Pricing.** Every position gets its mark. Level 1 off the exchange feed, Level 2 off vendors and broker quotes (with the stale-price check), Level 3 off the model and the valuation committee. The valuation policy governs every judgment call here.

**5. Corporate actions.** Dividends, stock splits, coupon payments, rights issues, mergers — anything the issuer did during the month must be reflected. A missed dividend is a missed receivable and a wrong NAV.

**6. Fee accrual.** The management fee accrues for the month; the incentive fee accrues against gains above the high-water mark; expenses (audit, admin, legal, data) accrue. The NAV is struck *net* of all of it.

**7. Breaks cleared.** The administrator's draft NAV is reconciled against the manager's shadow NAV, and every difference is explained before sign-off. This is the gate: no break is waved through.

**8. Sign-off and publish.** Only when every step is clean does the NAV get struck officially, the capital accounts get allocated, and the statements go out to investors. Most hedge funds publish a final monthly NAV within the first week or two of the following month, sometimes preceded by an estimated NAV a few business days earlier.

#### Worked example: the equalization adjustment for a mid-period subscriber

Here is a subtle close problem that catches almost every new founder, and it is pure NAV arithmetic. The Meridian Fund charges a 20% incentive fee with a high-water mark, crystallizing annually. The fund began the year at \$1,000.00 per share. By the end of June it has risen to \$1,200.00 per share — a 20% gain, all of it above the high-water mark, so 20% of that gain is owed as incentive fee at year-end.

Now a new investor, Daniel, subscribes \$1.2 million at the end of June, at the \$1,200.00 NAV per share, getting 1,000 shares. Here is the problem: that \$1,200.00 NAV is already *net* of the incentive fee accrued on the gain from \$1,000 to \$1,200 — a gain that happened *before Daniel was in the fund*. If nothing is done, Daniel has effectively pre-paid an incentive fee on a gain he never earned. Worse, if the fund then *falls* back toward \$1,000, the existing investors' accrued fee reverses out and they get it back, but Daniel — who came in at the top — has subsidized a fee on gains that were not his.

The fix is an **equalization adjustment** (or an equalization-credit / depreciation-deposit method, or series accounting). The mechanics vary, but the goal is identical: each investor is charged incentive fee only on the gains *they* actually experienced, measured from *their* entry NAV, not the fund's. Under one common approach, Daniel pays an "equalization credit" at subscription that effectively buys him out of the pre-existing accrued fee, so his high-water mark is set at his \$1,200.00 entry and he is charged 20% only on gains above that. Under series accounting, Daniel is simply issued a new series of shares (a "June series") with its own high-water mark, and the series are consolidated later. *The single fund-level NAV is not enough to charge fees fairly across investors who entered at different times — equalization is the machinery that makes the incentive fee fair to a mid-period subscriber, and getting it wrong means systematically over- or under-charging your newest investors.*

This is the kind of thing that lives entirely inside the administrator's systems and the fund documents, and it is one more reason a credible fund does not run its capital accounts in a spreadsheet. The arithmetic of fair fee allocation across a register of investors entering and exiting at different NAVs is genuinely hard, and it is exactly the work the administrator is paid to do correctly every month.

## Capital accounts and investor statements

The NAV is one number for the whole fund, but the fund has many owners, and each owns a different slice that has grown, shrunk, and been charged fees differently depending on when they came in and how much. The **capital account** is how the single fund-level NAV gets divided fairly among them. Every investor has one, and it is a running roll-forward:

> opening balance + subscriptions − redemptions + allocated P&L − allocated fees = ending balance

Add up every investor's ending capital-account balance and you get the fund's total NAV. The capital account is the bridge between "the fund is worth \$120 million" and "you, specifically, own \$14 million of it." Figure 5 traces one investor's capital account across a year.

![A waterfall chart of one investor's capital account over a year, starting at USD 10 million, rising with a subscription, falling with a redemption, rising with profit and loss, and falling with management and incentive fees to an ending balance of USD 14 million](/imgs/blogs/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator-5.png)

#### Worked example: a capital-account roll-forward for an LP

Follow one of Maya's investors — call her the LP — through a full year in the Meridian Fund. She begins the year with a capital account of \$10.0 million. Early in the year she adds \$5.0 million (a subscription), striking in at the then-current NAV per share. Mid-year, needing liquidity elsewhere, she redeems \$3.0 million, striking out at that period's NAV per share. Over the year her share of the fund's gross profit and loss comes to +\$2.6 million. Against that, she is charged her share of the management fee — \$0.18 million — and, because the fund finished above her high-water mark, her share of the incentive fee — \$0.42 million.

Roll it forward: \$10.0M + \$5.0M − \$3.0M + \$2.6M − \$0.18M − \$0.42M = **\$14.0 million** ending balance. That \$14.0 million is what her year-end statement shows, and the administrator computes it from the *independent* books so she never has to take Maya's word for it. Notice three things the roll-forward makes visible. First, her subscriptions and redemptions are not P&L — moving money in and out does not make her richer or poorer, it just changes her stake. Second, the fees are charged on *her* allocated gains, computed against *her* high-water mark, which is why the equalization machinery from the previous example matters. Third, the statement is auditable: every line ties to a NAV per share on a specific date and a share count in the register. *A capital account is just the fund's one NAV, divided honestly across time and across owners — and the honesty is enforced by the fact that an outsider computes it.*

The monthly investor statement is the capital account made legible. It shows the opening balance, the flows, the P&L, the fees, and the closing balance, along with the period and year-to-date returns, both gross and net of fees — the **net-of-fee** presentation is not just good manners, it is what the SEC's Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1, in force since November 2022) requires when performance is shown. When that statement comes from the administrator's systems rather than the manager's letterhead, it carries the one thing that makes it trustworthy: it was produced by someone with no stake in the number being flattering.

## Illiquid marks and side pockets

The hardest valuation problem in a fund is not pricing a single illiquid position — it is the **liquidity mismatch** that arises when a fund offering monthly or quarterly redemptions holds positions that take months to sell. The convertible bond from Maya's hook is the small version; the large version is a fund that has, say, 15% of its NAV in genuinely illiquid Level 3 positions while promising investors quarterly liquidity. If everyone redeems at once, the fund must sell the *liquid* positions to fund the redemptions — leaving the remaining investors holding a fund that is now *more* concentrated in the illiquid stuff. The redeemers get out clean at the struck NAV; the stayers are left over-weighted in exactly the positions whose marks are most uncertain.

The structural tool that solves this is the **side pocket**. A side pocket is a separate sub-account inside the fund into which a specific illiquid position is segregated. Once a position is side-pocketed, only the investors who were in the fund *at the time it was created* participate in it; new subscribers do not buy into it, and redeeming investors cannot pull their share of it until it is realized. The side pocket is marked separately (often more conservatively, sometimes at cost until a realization event), and its gains and losses accrue only to the investors who own it.

The side pocket does two jobs at once. First, it fixes the fairness problem: an investor who redeems out of the liquid portfolio does not get to dump their share of the illiquid position onto the stayers at a possibly-stale mark, and a new subscriber does not accidentally buy a slice of a legacy illiquid bet. Second, it protects the NAV of the main fund from the worst valuation uncertainty: the hardest-to-mark position is walled off, so a dispute over its value does not contaminate the price at which everyone else transacts. We cover the investor-facing terms — gates, lock-ups, and side pockets — from the liquidity-design angle in [liquidity terms: lock-ups, gates, side pockets](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets); here the point is purely about valuation, that side-pocketing is how a fund quarantines the Level 3 marks that would otherwise poison the whole NAV.

#### Worked example: how a \$2M overstatement over-pays a redeemer and dilutes the stayers

This is the mechanism behind Maya's opening fear, made fully explicit. The Meridian Fund is at \$120 million NAV across 100,000 shares — \$1,200.00 per share — and it holds a Level 3 position that *should* be marked down by \$2 million but, because nobody re-ran the model after the position deteriorated, is still carried \$2 million too high. The fund's true NAV is therefore \$118 million, a true NAV per share of \$1,180.00, but the reported NAV per share is the overstated \$1,200.00.

Now an investor — call him the redeemer — pulls his entire \$12 million stake at the reported price. At \$1,200.00 per share he redeems 10,000 shares and is paid \$12,000,000. But his fair share of the *true* \$118 million fund was 10,000 × \$1,180.00 = \$11,800,000. He has been over-paid by exactly \$200,000. That \$200,000 did not come from nowhere; it came out of the fund's cash, which belonged to everyone. After he leaves, the fund has paid out \$12.0 million in cash but its remaining true assets are \$118M − \$11.8M = \$106.2 million held against the redeemer's fair claim — except the fund actually parted with \$12.0M, so the true remaining assets are \$118M − \$12.0M = \$106.0 million, split across the 90,000 shares the stayers still hold. Those 90,000 shares are now worth \$106.0M ÷ 90,000 = \$1,177.78 each, versus the \$1,180.00 they were truly worth before the redemption. The overstatement transferred \$200,000 from the 90,000 staying shares to the one departing investor — about \$2.22 per remaining share, scraped quietly off everyone who stayed loyal. *An overstated mark is a wealth transfer from the patient to the departing, executed silently through the redemption price, and the only defense is to strike the right NAV before anyone transacts on it.* When the position is genuinely illiquid and the mark is genuinely uncertain, the cleaner fix is to side-pocket it — so the redeemer cannot cash out his slice of the disputed mark at all until it is realized, and the transfer simply cannot happen.

Side pockets have a dark history too, which is why allocators watch them closely. In 2008, a number of funds used side pockets — and outright suspensions of redemptions — to trap investors in positions they could not exit, sometimes for years, and a few managers abused the tool to hide losses in marks no one could check. The legitimate use is narrow: a genuinely illiquid position, side-pocketed transparently, marked conservatively, with the policy disclosed up front in the fund documents. The abusive use — side-pocketing a position simply to avoid marking it down, or to lock investors in — is exactly what an operational reviewer probes. ## Common misconceptions

**"NAV is just an accounting formality I can compute after the real work."** The opposite is true: NAV *is* the fund. It is the price of every subscription, every redemption, and the base of every fee. There is no "real work" that NAV reports on after the fact — the NAV is the thing the work produces, and a wrong NAV mis-prices every investor simultaneously. A founder who treats it as back-office hygiene rather than the core product is one stale mark away from Maya's opening problem.

**"If my returns are good, the NAV process is a detail allocators won't care about."** Allocators care about the NAV process *more* than the returns, because great returns with a weak NAV process is the exact signature of a fraud. Madoff had the best returns in the room. The operational-due-diligence analyst is not impressed by your Sharpe; she is checking whether an independent party struck the number and whether the cash is where you say it is. A fund can be rejected on the NAV process alone, regardless of performance.

**"I can save money by striking the NAV myself in the early days."** Self-administration is not a cost saving, it is a credibility liability that ends institutional fundraising before it starts. The line in an ODD memo — *manager self-administers; no independent NAV* — is fatal. The few thousand dollars a month an administrator costs a small fund is the price of being investable at all, and it is the single best-spent dollar in the budget.

**"Level 3 marks are an accounting technicality that only affects illiquid funds."** Every fund eventually holds something hard to price — a halted stock, a thinly-traded bond, a position in a name that stopped printing. The fair-value hierarchy is not a feature of exotic credit funds; it is a description of reality that applies to any book the moment a position loses its clean market price. The discipline of a written valuation policy with a stale-price rule is what catches the problem before it reaches the NAV.

**"The administrator's number is automatically right, so I don't need my own books."** The administrator is independent and competent but not infallible — they miss corporate actions, apply stale prices, and fat-finger quantities like anyone. The shadow NAV exists precisely because the administrator can be wrong, and a manager who blindly signs off the administrator's draft without a reconciling shadow has surrendered the second control that protects the number. Independence works *because* two parties check each other, not because one is assumed perfect.

**"Side pockets are a trick to trap investors."** Used legitimately — a genuinely illiquid position, segregated transparently, marked conservatively, disclosed in advance — a side pocket *protects* the remaining investors by walling off the most uncertain marks from the price everyone transacts at. The abuse (side-pocketing to avoid a markdown, or to lock investors in) is real and is exactly what ODD probes, but the tool itself is a fairness mechanism, not a trap.

**"The fund's NAV is just my trading P&L, so if I know my P&L I know the NAV."** Trading P&L is one input into NAV, not the whole of it. The NAV also subtracts accrued fees, reflects subscriptions and redemptions that change the share count without changing per-share value, captures receivables and payables the trading screen never shows, and depends on the marks chosen for every Level 2 and Level 3 position. A founder can have a perfect mental tally of the day's trading gains and still be surprised by the struck NAV, because a dividend was reclassified, an expense accrued, a stale mark caught, or a fee crystallized. The NAV per share is the investor's price; the P&L is one ingredient in it. Confusing the two is how managers end up arguing with their own administrator about a number the manager only half-computed.

## How it plays out in the real world

The names that anchor this material are the failures, because the failures are where the missing controls become visible.

**Madoff (revealed December 2008)** is the canonical NAV-and-administration failure, and it is worth being precise about *why* the structure, not the returns, is what an honest review would have caught. There was no independent administrator — Madoff produced his own statements. The auditor was a three-person shop (Friehling & Horowitz) that a fund of that purported size would never use, and which turned out not to be doing real audits at all. The assets were self-custodied rather than held at an independent prime broker, so no outside party ever confirmed the securities existed. Any one of these gaps should have stopped an allocation; together they describe a fund that had removed every single independent check on its NAV. Roughly \$65 billion of fabricated statement value evaporated; about \$17.5 billion of real principal was lost. The post-Madoff industry standard — independent administrator, independent custody, recognized auditor — is a direct response, and it is exactly the stack an ODD analyst now verifies as a baseline.

**The 2008 side-pocket and gating wave** is the other real-world lesson. As liquidity vanished, dozens of reputable funds discovered their liquidity mismatch the hard way: they had offered quarterly redemptions on books that included positions they could not sell at any reasonable price. Some used side pockets and suspensions legitimately to protect remaining investors from a fire-sale of illiquid marks; others used the same tools to trap investors and avoid marking losses. The episode is why every serious valuation policy now spells out, in advance, the stale-price rule, the side-pocket trigger, and who has authority to suspend — so that in the next crisis the tool is governed rather than improvised. It is also why the [valuation and conflicts governance](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/valuation-and-conflicts-governance) of a fund is reviewed as carefully as its returns.

On the everyday side, the real-world texture is mundane and relentless: a fund's middle and back office reconcile positions and cash against every prime broker *daily*, so that the month-end close is a confirmation rather than a discovery. The administrator pulls positions and prices independently, strikes a draft NAV, and the manager's shadow NAV catches the breaks — a missed dividend here, a stale convertible mark there — before sign-off. The fund publishes an estimated NAV within a few business days and a final NAV within a week or two. Investors get statements from the administrator, not the manager. None of this is glamorous, and all of it is what an allocator is buying when they pay a small fund's six-figure annual service-provider stack to be institutional-grade. The number that *is* the fund is only as trustworthy as the independent machine that strikes it.

For Maya, the convertible-bond episode became the thing that forced the Meridian Fund's NAV process to grow up. She and the administrator wrote a stale-price rule into the valuation policy — any vendor quote older than five business days triggers a re-mark against fresh dealer levels. She formalized the shadow NAV so the monthly reconciliation against the administrator's draft was a documented control, not a favor she did in her spare time. And the next time an allocator's ODD analyst asked her the question that had killed her earlier raise — *who actually strikes the NAV?* — the answer was clean: an independent administrator, off third-party data, reconciled against a documented shadow, governed by a written valuation policy. The 14% returns had never been the problem. The number that *is* the fund finally being struck by someone other than her was the thing that made the 14% investable.

## When this matters / Further reading

NAV, accounting, and the administrator matter the moment you stop trading other people's money and start *being* the fund someone else trusts. The NAV is the price of every dollar in and every dollar out and the base of every fee — so the discipline of striking it correctly, independently, and on a governed schedule is not back-office hygiene, it is the core of the promise you are selling. The administrator's independence is what makes the number trustworthy; the valuation policy is what makes the judgment calls defensible; the monthly close is what makes nothing slip through; the capital accounts are what make the one number fair across many owners. Get the NAV wrong and you have not made a bookkeeping error — you have silently mispriced everyone you are responsible to.
