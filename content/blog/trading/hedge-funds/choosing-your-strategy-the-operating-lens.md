> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Your strategy choice is also an operating choice: each family sets your capacity, your data and tech bill, your headcount, your prime-brokerage and leverage needs, and which allocators will fund it. Pick the strategy you can both *run* and *operate* with the capital and team you have.
>
> - **Capacity is destiny.** A merger-arb book might cap out around a few hundred million to low billions; a multi-strat platform can absorb tens of billions. Choosing a low-capacity strategy at low launch AUM means your edge gets diluted long before you reach break-even.
> - **Tech is not optional and not uniform.** A discretionary long/short desk can run on terminals and spreadsheets for well under one million USD a year; a systematic shop's data, infrastructure, and engineers can run into several million before it manages a dollar.
> - **Headcount follows the strategy, not the AUM.** A systematic fund needs researchers and engineers on day one; a focused discretionary PM can launch with two analysts. Payroll, not trading losses, sinks most early funds.
> - **The one number to remember:** the AUM at which a strategy's alpha decays below its own fee is the real ceiling. Past that point you are charging two-and-twenty for beta — and sophisticated allocators can see it.

Maya is one of the best systematic equity researchers her old fund ever employed. Over six years she built a cross-sectional signal stack — fundamentals, estimate revisions, a half-dozen alternative datasets, a careful execution model — that delivered a real, repeatable edge inside her employer's risk budget. When she decided to launch the Meridian Fund, she assumed the hard part was behind her. She had the alpha. She had two researchers willing to follow her. She had a verbal soft-circle of roughly 40 million USD from former colleagues and one family office.

Then she priced the operation. The point-in-time fundamental data that made her signal work was quoted at over 250,000 USD a year. The tick-level history she needed to validate her execution model added six figures more. A backtest grid that could re-run her universe overnight, a production OMS, a risk system, two engineers to keep it all alive — by the time she finished a serious budget, her *fixed* annual run-rate was north of two million USD before she paid herself a cent. On 40 million USD of AUM at a 1.5% management fee, she would collect 600,000 USD a year. She was three and a half million dollars short of even covering her own costs, and the strategy she was so good at was the single most expensive kind of fund to operate.

That is the gap this post is about. Maya did not have a strategy problem. She had an *operating* problem disguised as a strategy choice. The market mechanics of her signal were fine — those live in sibling posts like [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research) and [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research). What no one teaches the brilliant researcher is that *every strategy family is also a business model with a fixed cost base, a capacity ceiling, a headcount, and a natural set of funders* — and that you must choose the strategy you can run **and** operate, not just the one you can run. Figure 1 is the lens this whole post argues for: the same seven families, scored not on returns but on what they demand of you as an operator.

## Foundations: the major strategy families

Before we can apply the operating lens, we need a shared vocabulary. A **strategy family** is a broad way of making money in markets that funds organize themselves around. Within each family there are dozens of specific approaches, but the family sets the shape of the business. Let me define the seven that matter, building each from zero. The *mechanics* of how each one actually generates returns live in the quantitative-finance and quant-careers series — I will define each family just enough to reason about its operations, and link out for the trading detail.

A note on the units we will use repeatedly. **AUM** is assets under management — the total capital the fund manages. The **management fee** is an annual percentage of AUM (historically 2%, now often 1.3–1.5%) that pays the bills regardless of performance. The **incentive fee** (or performance fee) is a share of profits (historically 20%, now often 15–17%). **Capacity** is the maximum AUM a strategy can manage before its returns degrade. **Turnover** is how much of the book you trade per year — a 100% annual turnover means you replace the entire portfolio once a year. **ADV** is average daily volume — how much of a security trades on a typical day, which caps how fast you can build or exit a position without moving the price. Keep these five in your head; the operating lens is mostly arithmetic on them.

**Long/short equity (L/S equity).** The classic hedge fund: buy stocks you expect to outperform (longs), sell short stocks you expect to underperform (shorts), and earn the spread between them while hedging out some of the market's direction. A discretionary L/S manager researches companies one at a time — reading filings, building models, meeting management — and runs a relatively concentrated book of high-conviction names. This is the family Maya's old colleague down the hall ran with three people. The edge is fundamental insight; the operation can be lean. For the research craft itself, the careers series covers [the four paths](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer) a markets professional can take inside such a shop. *Operating reality:* a discretionary L/S desk runs on a few terminals (roughly \$150,000–\$250,000 a year in data), a headcount of three to eight, gross leverage typically 1.5–2.5x, one or two prime brokers (liquid equities are easy to finance), and a book that can usually be liquidated in days — the cheapest serious strategy to operate, which is exactly why it is the most common launch.

**Global macro.** Bets on the direction of whole economies, expressed through liquid instruments: government bonds, currencies, equity indices, commodities, and their derivatives. A macro manager forms a view — say, that a central bank will cut rates faster than the market expects — and expresses it across rates, FX, and equities. The edge is a top-down read on policy, growth, and flows. The book is highly liquid (you can move tens of billions in Treasuries or major currencies), so capacity is large, but the data and research surface is broad and the discipline is brutal; for why directional risk-taking is so unforgiving, see [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up). *Operating reality:* macro's data bill is moderate (economic data, pricing, news — call it \$200,000–\$500,000 a year), but it carries a heavy *risk-governance* cost — an experienced risk function and usually a COO from day one, because the directional book has fat tails — plus multiple prime and clearing relationships spanning rates, FX, and commodities. Leverage is achieved cheaply through futures and swaps rather than borrowing, the book is highly liquid, and headcount is moderate (roughly five to fifteen). The expense is in governance, not in datasets.

**Quant / systematic.** Strategies where the decisions are made by code, not by a human looking at a screen. A quant equity fund ranks thousands of stocks by a model — value, momentum, quality, plus proprietary signals — and holds a diversified long/short book rebalanced on a schedule. A statistical-arbitrage fund trades mean-reversion between related securities. The edge is a small statistical advantage repeated across thousands of positions. The defining operating fact: the strategy *is* an infrastructure. The data pipeline, the backtest engine, the execution system, and the engineers who maintain them are not overhead on top of the strategy — they are the strategy. The mechanics of building and validating these signals are in [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research) and [backtesting done right](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/backtesting-done-right-quant-research). *Operating reality:* this is the most expensive strategy to launch — point-in-time fundamental data, pricing, alternative datasets, tick history, and a backtest cluster routinely sum to \$1,000,000 or more a year before salaries; the headcount is researcher-and-engineer heavy (ten to forty in a serious shop); leverage and prime needs are moderate-to-high depending on whether the book is gross-neutral stat-arb or a directional factor book; and the equities book itself is liquid. The infrastructure, not the trades, is the cost center.

**Event-driven.** Bets on corporate events: mergers, spinoffs, bankruptcies, restructurings, index inclusions. The flagship sub-strategy is **merger arbitrage** — when Company A announces it will buy Company B at 50 USD a share and B trades at 47 USD, you buy B and capture the 3 USD spread if the deal closes (and lose more if it breaks). The edge is legal, regulatory, and probabilistic judgment about whether a specific deal will complete. The crucial operating fact is that your opportunity set is *exogenous*: you can only deploy capital when there are deals to trade. Deal flow, not your conviction, caps the book. *Operating reality:* the data and tech bill is low-to-moderate (filings, deal databases, pricing — call it \$150,000–\$300,000 a year), headcount is modest (four to twelve, with legal and analytical depth), and the equity book is liquid for the announced names. But two operating facts bite: capacity is structurally low because deal flow is finite, and returns are *lumpy* — you earn small spreads steadily and lose larger amounts when a deal breaks, so the cash-flow profile and the risk system must handle event risk that does not show up in normal volatility.

**Credit.** Strategies in the debt markets: corporate bonds, leveraged loans, distressed debt, structured credit. A credit manager might buy the bonds of a company they think is mispriced, short the bonds of one heading for trouble, or buy the debt of a bankrupt company expecting a favorable restructuring. The edge is credit analysis and, often, legal expertise in bankruptcy. The defining operating fact is **illiquidity**: many credit instruments trade by appointment, not continuously, so positions take weeks to build and unwind, valuation is genuinely hard, and your fund's liquidity terms must match the assets — a point that connects to [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis). *Operating reality:* credit's data bill is moderate (pricing services, ratings, issuance and covenant data), headcount is moderate (five to fifteen, often including restructuring and legal specialists), and leverage is meaningful but the binding constraint is the *liquidity mismatch* — an illiquid book forces longer lock-ups, longer notice periods, and frequently **side pockets** for the truly illiquid positions, all of which shrink the pool of investors willing to fund you and raise the bar on your valuation policy, the part of operations an ODD team scrutinizes hardest in a credit fund.

**Multi-strategy.** Not a single strategy but a *platform* that runs many strategy "pods" or "books" under one roof — an L/S equity team here, a macro team there, a quant team, a credit team — with a central risk and capital-allocation function moving money between them. The edge is diversification across uncorrelated bets plus tight risk management plus the ability to fire underperforming books fast. The defining operating fact: it is the most expensive and largest-headcount model in the industry, and it is almost never how you *launch* — it is what some funds grow into. The firm-archetype distinctions (pod shop vs single-strategy fund) are drawn carefully in [the firm archetypes](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund). *Operating reality:* a platform carries the data and tech needs of *every* strategy it runs, a central risk and technology function on top, fifty to several hundred people, deep multi-prime financing relationships, and a treasury operation managing leverage across pods. Annual fixed costs run into the tens of millions. No first-time founder launches this; you reach it only after a single-strategy fund has compounded enough capital and reputation to hire teams and build the central infrastructure.

**CTA / managed futures.** Commodity trading advisors run systematic, usually trend-following, strategies in liquid futures markets across asset classes. When a market trends up, they go long; when it trends down, they go short; they ride the trend until it reverses. The edge is a persistent (if cyclical) trend premium harvested with discipline across dozens of liquid markets. The defining operating fact: extremely high capacity (futures markets are deep and liquid) and a relatively lean headcount (the strategy is code), but a real signal-research and execution infrastructure — the data and systems intensity is high even when the team is small. *Operating reality:* a CTA needs deep futures price history across dozens of markets, an execution and order-management system, and a research engine — a real seven-figure-ish tech and data commitment — but only a lean team of three to ten, because the strategy is automated. Leverage is embedded in the futures (margin-efficient), the book is among the most liquid in the industry, and clearing relationships matter more than equity-style prime brokerage. The unusual profile — high capacity, high tech intensity, low headcount, very high liquidity — is why CTAs scale so well and why allocators buy them specifically for crisis diversification.

A second axis cuts across all seven: **discretionary** versus **systematic** execution. A discretionary manager makes the final call by judgment; a systematic manager codifies the decision into rules a machine executes. L/S equity, macro, event-driven, and credit are usually discretionary (though quant versions of each exist); quant equity and CTA are systematic by definition. This axis is the single biggest driver of the operating footprint, because it trades headcount for infrastructure. A discretionary process puts the edge in a person's head and needs analysts to feed that head; a systematic process puts the edge in code and data, and needs engineers and datasets to feed the code. The same strategy *family* — equity long/short — looks completely different as a business depending on which side of this axis it sits, and we will return to that contrast repeatedly because it is where most founders misjudge their own costs.

Those seven are not exhaustive — there are convertible-arb funds, volatility funds (see [hedging a portfolio with options](/blog/trading/options-volatility/hedging-a-portfolio-with-options-protective-puts-collars-and-tail-risk)), activist funds, and more — but they cover the overwhelming majority of industry assets and they span the full range of the operating lens. Figure 4 sorts them by *how* they make money, which is the first cut a founder should make.

![A taxonomy tree sorting hedge fund strategies into four branches — directional, relative value, event-driven, and systematic — with specific strategies as leaves under each branch](/imgs/blogs/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens-4.png)

The four branches matter because they predict the operating footprint. **Directional** strategies (L/S equity, macro) bet on which way something moves; they tend to need fewer positions and can be run discretionarily and leanly. **Relative-value** strategies (credit, stat-arb) bet on the spread between two things; they need more positions, more financing, and more pricing infrastructure. **Event-driven** strategies depend on an exogenous calendar of corporate actions, which caps capacity. **Systematic** strategies run on code, which trades headcount for infrastructure. Keep that map in mind as we now turn the lens.

## The operating lens

Here is the central argument. When a founder evaluates a strategy, they almost always ask "can I generate returns with it?" That is necessary but radically insufficient. The right question is: **what does this strategy demand of me as an operator, and can I meet those demands with the capital and team I actually have at launch?** Six dimensions make up the lens.

**1. Capacity — how much AUM the edge absorbs.** Every edge has a ceiling. As you put more money behind a strategy, you trade larger sizes, which moves prices against you (market impact), forces you into less-liquid names, and pushes you into the more crowded part of the opportunity set. Beyond some AUM, the *marginal* dollar earns less than the *average* dollar, and eventually adding capital lowers the fund's return. That ceiling is the strategy's capacity. It ranges over roughly two orders of magnitude across our seven families. Capacity is not a nice-to-have number; it is the single most important operating constraint, because it interacts with everything else. We give it its own section below.

**2. Data and technology intensity.** What does it cost, per year, to get the inputs and run the machinery the strategy needs? For a discretionary L/S desk, this might be a few Bloomberg or FactSet terminals and broker research — tens to low hundreds of thousands of dollars. For a systematic equity fund, it is a stack: clean point-in-time fundamental data, pricing data, possibly several alternative datasets, a backtesting cluster, an execution system, and the engineers to run them — comfortably into the millions. Figure 2 is the ladder.

![A five-rung ladder of data and technology intensity from low for a discretionary long short desk through moderate, high, and very high to extreme for high frequency trading, with example fund types beside each rung](/imgs/blogs/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens-2.png)

The ladder climbs from a desk that can run on a few terminals (low) through funds that need clean institutional data feeds (moderate), to quant shops needing alternative data and a backtest grid (high), to systematic-futures and machine-learning operations needing tick history and a research cluster (very high), to high-frequency operations needing colocation, FPGAs, and dedicated engineers (extreme). Where your strategy sits on this ladder sets a *fixed* annual cost that you owe whether you make money or not. That is the part Maya missed.

**3. Headcount.** Who do you need in seats on day one, and what do they cost? This is the largest cost for most funds and the one founders most consistently underestimate. A focused discretionary PM can launch with two analysts and outsource everything else. A systematic fund needs at minimum a few researchers and a couple of engineers before it can trade — the strategy literally does not run without them. A multi-strat platform needs whole teams plus a central risk function. Crucially, headcount is driven by the *strategy*, not by your AUM: a systematic fund at 50 million USD needs nearly the same engineering team as one at 500 million USD, because the code that runs 50 million is the same code that runs 500 million. That fixed-headcount property is why systematic strategies have a high *minimum* viable AUM.

**4. Book liquidity.** How fast can you turn your positions into cash? A macro book of Treasuries and major-currency futures can be liquidated in a day; a distressed-credit book might take months. Book liquidity sets two things: how quickly you can react to a redemption or a risk event, and what liquidity terms you can responsibly offer investors. The cardinal operational sin is a **liquidity mismatch** — offering monthly redemptions on assets that take months to sell. A founder running an illiquid strategy must structure longer lock-ups and longer notice periods, which in turn shrinks the pool of investors willing to fund them.

**5. Leverage.** How much borrowed money does the strategy need to make its edge meaningful? A concentrated L/S equity book might run at 1.5–2x gross exposure. A relative-value or stat-arb strategy harvests a small spread and needs more leverage — often several times the capital — to make that spread worth charging a fee on. Higher leverage means tighter financing relationships, more margin, more sensitivity to a prime broker pulling lines in a stress, and a fatter tail. Leverage is where strategy choice meets [risk of ruin](/blog/trading/options-volatility/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading): the same edge run at 4x is a different business, and a different blow-up risk, than at 1.5x.

**6. Number of prime brokers.** A **prime broker** (PB) is the bank that finances your leverage, lends you securities to short, holds your assets, and clears your trades. A discretionary L/S fund trading liquid US equities might launch with a single mini-prime. A larger or more leveraged fund needs **multiple primes** — both to diversify counterparty risk (the lesson of Lehman in 2008, when assets pledged to a failing prime were frozen) and to get enough financing and securities-lending capacity. More primes means more relationships to maintain, more reconciliation, more operations staff, and a higher AUM threshold before a top-tier PB will even take you. Strategy drives prime count: the more leverage and the more shorting, the more primes.

Notice that these six dimensions are not independent. Capacity interacts with headcount (a low-capacity strategy can never spread fixed headcount over enough AUM). Data intensity interacts with capacity (you pay for the expensive data whether you run 40 million or 400 million). Leverage interacts with prime count and with liquidity. The operating lens is really one question asked six ways: *does the fixed cost and risk this strategy imposes fit the capital and team I have?*

There is a clean way to collapse the six dimensions into a single founder-level decision. Four of them — data/tech, headcount, prime relationships, and the compliance and risk infrastructure they imply — sum into one number: the **annual fixed cost** the strategy owes regardless of performance. The other two — capacity and book liquidity — set the *shape* of the business: capacity caps how large the AUM (and therefore the fee revenue) can ever get, and liquidity sets how fast that AUM can leave and what terms you can offer to slow it down. So the lens reduces to a comparison: divide the fixed cost by your management-fee rate to get a break-even AUM; compare that floor to the capacity ceiling to get your viable operating window; then ask whether the capital you can actually raise lands inside that window and whether the liquidity you can offer matches the assets you will hold. If the window is wide and reachable, the strategy is operable; if the floor is near the ceiling, or the reachable capital is below the floor, it is not — no matter how good the alpha. Every later worked example in this post is just an instance of that one comparison.

A subtle but important consequence: the fixed-cost number is **largely independent of AUM**, while the revenue scales *with* AUM. That asymmetry is why small funds are so fragile and why strategy choice is so consequential. The expensive-infrastructure strategies have a high fixed cost that does not shrink when you launch small — so they punish a slow or small raise harshly — while the lean strategies carry a low fixed cost that a modest raise can cover, buying the founder time to compound a track record. Choosing a strategy is, in large part, choosing how much fixed cost you are committing to carry before you know whether the raise will come.

## Capacity vs alpha

This is the dimension that quietly kills the most founders, so it gets its own treatment. The core idea is simple and unforgiving: **as AUM rises, alpha falls.** The relationship is not linear and not gentle near the ceiling. There are two mechanisms.

The first is **market impact**. To deploy more capital you must trade in larger size, and large orders move the price against you. If you want to buy 1% of a stock's average daily volume, you barely move it; if you want to buy 30% of ADV, you push the price up as you buy and give some of your edge back to the market. Doubling AUM at fixed turnover roughly doubles the size you must trade in each name, dragging more of your trades into the impact-heavy regime.

The second is **opportunity-set exhaustion**. Every strategy has a finite set of attractive trades. A merger-arb fund can only trade the deals that exist. A small-cap value fund can only buy the cheap small-caps that are liquid enough to hold. As you scale, you exhaust the best opportunities and are forced into marginal ones — lower expected return, higher risk — or into larger, more crowded names where your edge is thinner because everyone else is there too.

Put the two together and you get a capacity curve: the *total* dollars of alpha the fund produces rises with AUM, peaks, and then falls; the *percentage* return per dollar declines the whole way and eventually goes negative at the margin. The practical threshold a founder must respect is the AUM at which the strategy's net alpha decays below its own fee. Past that point you are charging two-and-twenty for what is, after impact, market beta — and sophisticated allocators, who run [their own diligence](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch) on your factor exposures, will see it.

Capacity ranges enormously across families. Figure 3 puts illustrative bands on it.

![A horizontal bar chart on a log scale showing illustrative capacity in billions of dollars for seven strategies, from merger arbitrage at the low end to multi-strategy platforms at the high end, spanning roughly one hundred times](/imgs/blogs/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens-3.png)

These bands are illustrative and vary widely by sub-strategy, market regime, and how aggressively a manager is willing to let returns degrade — treat them as order-of-magnitude, not precise. But the shape is real and structural: event-driven and credit strategies cap out earliest (deal flow and instrument illiquidity bind), L/S equity and quant equity are in the middle (and a *concentrated* L/S book caps far below a diversified quant one), macro and CTA are high (liquid instruments), and multi-strat platforms are highest because they are really a basket of many strategies, each with its own capacity, summed.

The founder's takeaway is counterintuitive: **a low-capacity strategy is not safe just because it is small.** A merger-arb fund with a 1-billion-USD ceiling sounds like plenty of room when you launch at 40 million. But if your edge starts decaying at, say, 600 million, and your fixed costs require 250 million just to break even comfortably, your *viable operating window* — above break-even AUM, below capacity decay — might be only a few hundred million dollars wide. Raise too slowly and you starve; raise too fast and you dilute the edge. We preview the full version of this argument, and the way platforms try to escape the capacity ceiling by stacking strategies, in [scaling capacity vs alpha and the platform](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/scaling-capacity-vs-alpha-and-the-platform).

#### Worked example: the capacity math

Maya's old systematic equity signal had an annual turnover of about 300% — she replaced her book three times a year. Suppose she runs it at 200 million USD of AUM. Her gross trading volume per year is 200 million × 3 = 600 million USD of buys plus 600 million of sells, call it 1.2 billion USD of total trading.

Now ask how that lands in the market. Spread across roughly 250 trading days, that is about 4.8 million USD traded per day. If her universe is mid-cap US equities with a typical name's ADV around 20 million USD, and she holds, say, 150 names, her average position is 200 million ÷ 150 ≈ 1.33 million USD, and her average *daily* trading in any one name is a small fraction of that name's ADV — comfortable, low impact.

Now scale her to 2 billion USD. Turnover is the same 300%, so she now trades 12 billion USD a year, about 48 million USD a day. Her average position is 13.3 million USD; rebuilding a third of the book each cycle, she is now routinely trying to move multiple millions of dollars in names whose ADV is 20 million — she is trading 15–25% of ADV in her active names on active days. Impact costs that were a rounding error at 200 million are now eating tens of basis points per turnover, and at 300% turnover that compounds into a meaningful chunk of her gross alpha. *The same signal that is pristine at 200 million is partly arbitraged away by her own trading at 2 billion — the strategy did not change, the operating constraint did.*

#### Worked example: a different strategy, a much lower ceiling

Now contrast a merger-arbitrage fund, where capacity is set not by trading impact but by the *supply of deals*. Suppose in a given year there are 50 actionable announced deals the fund's analysts can underwrite, and that prudent risk limits cap any single deal at 5% of the fund and target a diversified book of perhaps 20–25 positions at a time. The average target's tradeable float — the shares actually available to arbitrageurs without moving the deal spread — might be \$200,000,000 across the live deals you can hold.

If the fund is at \$300,000,000 of AUM, a 5% position is \$15,000,000, and 20 such positions is \$300,000,000 fully invested across the available deals — comfortable. Push the fund to \$1,500,000,000 and a 5% position is \$75,000,000; now you are trying to hold \$75,000,000 in deals whose tradeable float is a fraction of that, so you either breach your concentration limit, chase smaller and riskier deals to stay diversified, or sit in cash earning nothing while you wait for deal flow. The spread you can capture per dollar collapses. *Merger arb does not decay because you trade too fast; it decays because the world only announces so many deals — the ceiling is exogenous, often only a few billion dollars, and no amount of skill raises it. A low-capacity strategy at high AUM is just expensive cash.*

#### Worked example: the AUM at which alpha decays below the fee

Let me put a number on the ceiling. Suppose Meridian's gross alpha (before fees, before impact) is 8% a year at small size, and that empirically her impact cost grows roughly with the square of AUM in her relevant range — a standard rule of thumb — so that net alpha is approximately 8% − k·(AUM)². Calibrate k so that at 2 billion USD she has given back about 2% to impact (k·(2bn)² = 2%, the level the previous example implied). Then at 3 billion she gives back 2% × (3/2)² = 4.5%; at 4 billion, 2% × (4/2)² = 8% — the entire gross alpha is gone to impact.

Her management-plus-incentive fee load on a 1.5%/15% schedule, against an 8% gross return, is roughly 1.5% + 15% × (8% − 1.5%) ≈ 1.5% + 1.0% = 2.5% a year. So her investors' *net* alpha is 8% − impact − 2.5%. That goes to zero when impact reaches 5.5%, which on our calibration happens at AUM ≈ 2bn × √(5.5/2) ≈ 3.3 billion USD. Above roughly 3.3 billion, Maya's investors earn less than they pay her in fees — she is charging an active fee for a closet index. *The real capacity ceiling is not where the fund stops making money; it is where the fund stops making money for the client after the fee — and a good allocator computes exactly this before they invest.*

#### Worked example: does a seed deal make the expensive strategy reachable?

The capacity window also tells us whether a **seed deal** can rescue an expensive strategy. A seeder provides day-one capital — historically 25 to 150 million USD — in exchange for a share of the *management-company* economics, typically 15 to 25% of revenue, often with a multi-year lock-up. The question is whether the day-one AUM moves the founder inside her viable window by enough to justify giving up that revenue share.

Maya's systematic fund needs roughly 2 million USD of fixed cost a year (1.0 million data/tech plus the engineers and overhead from the worked examples). At a 1.5% management fee, her break-even AUM is 2.0M ÷ 0.015 ≈ 133 million USD. Her reachable capital is 40 million — almost 100 million short of break-even. A seeder offers 75 million USD of anchor capital for 20% of management-company revenue and a four-year lock. With the seed, her day-one AUM is 40 + 75 = 115 million USD — still a touch under break-even, but close enough that a normal first-year raise of 20 to 40 million pushes her over it, and well inside her 3.3-billion-USD capacity ceiling.

What does the 20% give-up cost her? On, say, an eventual 300 million USD of AUM at 1.5%, the management fee is 4.5 million USD a year; 20% of the management-company revenue is roughly 900,000 USD a year handed to the seeder for the lock-up term. Over four years, perhaps 3 to 4 million USD of foregone economics — the price of a business that exists at all. Without the seed, the systematic fund is a 40-million-USD operation burning 1.4 million USD a year of the founder's and investors' capital with no path to break-even; with it, the strategy is back inside its operating window. *A seed deal does not change a strategy's capacity or its fixed cost — it changes which strategies are reachable, by importing the day-one AUM that an expensive strategy needs to clear its own break-even floor. That is why so many high-fixed-cost launches are seeded and so many lean ones are not.*

## Who funds each strategy

A strategy you can run and operate is still worthless if no one will fund it. The allocator landscape is segmented, and different investor types are structurally drawn to different strategies. Understanding this *before* you choose your strategy is how you avoid building a fund nobody in your reachable network will buy.

The major allocator types, and what they want: **Funds-of-funds (FoFs)** allocate other people's money across many hedge funds; they are the most willing to back early-stage and emerging managers, but they negotiate hard on fees and capacity rights and they diversify across strategies. **Public and corporate pensions** write large tickets but move slowly, are gated by **investment consultants**, and demand size, a multi-year audited track record, and institutional operations before they will look at you. **Endowments and foundations** have long horizons and sophisticated staffs; they like differentiated, less-correlated strategies and can tolerate illiquidity. **Family offices** are the most flexible and relationship-driven — they often seed people they know personally, which is how many founders get their day-one capital. **Sovereign wealth funds** write enormous tickets but need large capacity to deploy them, so they gravitate to macro, multi-strat, and large quant funds. **Insurers** want stable, lower-volatility return streams, often in credit. **RIAs and private banks** aggregate high-net-worth money and like liquid, understandable strategies.

Now map money to strategy. **L/S equity** is fundable by almost everyone but faces the most competition, so a new L/S fund needs a genuinely differentiated angle. **Global macro** appeals to large institutions and sovereigns who value its diversification and capacity, but they want a proven discretionary process and a strong drawdown history. **Quant / systematic** funds attract data-savvy endowments, FoFs, and sovereigns — but allocators apply heavy operational and model-risk diligence and often want to understand (at least at a high level) what the model does. **Event-driven and merger-arb** funds appeal to investors wanting an equity-light, lower-beta return; their capacity constraint, though, caps the ticket sizes they can accept. **Credit** funds attract insurers, pensions, and endowments comfortable with illiquidity and longer lock-ups. **Multi-strat platforms** attract the largest institutions precisely because they offer diversified, risk-managed, capacity-scalable exposure — but you do not launch one of these; you grow into it. **CTA / managed futures** funds are bought specifically for crisis diversification (trend-following often does well when equities crash), so they appeal to allocators building [all-weather, risk-parity-style](/blog/trading/cross-asset/all-weather-and-risk-parity-owning-every-regime) portfolios.

The matching is not arbitrary; it falls out of each allocator's own balance sheet and governance. An **insurer** has long-dated, predictable liabilities and a regulator watching its volatility, so it is drawn to credit and lower-volatility strategies whose return streams it can model and whose illiquidity it can tolerate — and it is allergic to a high-volatility macro book. An **endowment** with a multi-decade horizon and no near-term redemption pressure can lock capital up for years, which is exactly what a distressed-credit or illiquid event-driven strategy needs, so endowments are natural funders of strategies that liquid-minded investors avoid. A **pension** answers to a board and a consultant and must defend every allocation in a committee meeting, which biases it toward strategies with long track records, large capacity, and a clean operational story — pushing it toward established L/S, large macro, and platforms, and away from a clever but young systematic fund the committee cannot easily explain. A **family office** has none of these governance constraints and can back a person on conviction, which is why first-time founders' day-one checks so often come from a family office that knows them — and why the strategy that office will fund is whatever they trust *the founder* to run, almost regardless of the family's stated preferences. The practical lesson: the allocator type you can realistically reach first dictates which strategies are even on your menu, because their constraints, not your pitch, decide what they are allowed to buy.

There is also a hard **minimum-ticket and minimum-AUM** structure layered on top. Most institutions will not allocate below a certain fund size — many pensions and their consultants will not look at a fund under 250 to 500 million USD, and a single allocation rarely exceeds 10 to 20% of the fund's AUM (no allocator wants to be more than a fifth of your book, because then your fund's survival depends on them). That math sets a floor on who can fund you: a 60-million-USD fund simply cannot accept a 100-million-USD pension ticket, because it would be 60% of the fund and would breach the allocator's concentration limit. Early-stage capital therefore comes disproportionately from family offices, emerging-manager programs, and small funds-of-funds whose mandate is precisely to write smaller, earlier tickets — often in exchange for fee discounts (a [founders share class](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money) at 1.5%/15% or better) or capacity rights that let them keep their economics as you grow.

The operational subtext is the one founders miss: **the allocator who likes your strategy also gates you on operations.** Institutional allocations run two separate diligence tracks. **Investment due diligence** asks "is the edge real?" — it studies the track record, the process, the risk management. **Operational due diligence (ODD)** asks "will the structure protect my money?" — it checks that the administrator is independent, that pricing is not done by the manager alone, that cash movements require multiple authorizations, that there is a valuation policy, a business-continuity plan, and clean prime-broker arrangements. ODD is a *separate, veto-wielding* process: an endowment that loves your quant signal will still kill the allocation over a cash-control gap or a non-independent administrator, no matter how good the returns. The canonical lesson is **Madoff**, revealed in December 2008 — fabricated statement value around 65 billion USD, with no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, and self-custody. Every red flag was an ODD failure, and modern ODD exists to catch exactly that profile. A pension that wants macro will additionally require institutional infrastructure — a real risk system, a COO, multiple primes — that you cannot afford at the AUM you are launching with.

So the funder you can reach early (often a family office or a small FoF) must match a strategy whose operating bar you can actually clear at launch AUM. Strategy, funder, and operating capability are a single triangle: the strategy sets the operating bar, the operating bar gates which funders will clear you, and the funders you can reach set how much capital you can raise — which, back at the start, determines whether the strategy is operable at all. Founders who pick a strategy in isolation and then go looking for funders discover the triangle the hard way, during a raise that stalls because the only allocators who like the strategy are the ones whose ODD bar the launch operation cannot clear. We develop the allocator and ODD side fully in the capital-raising track of this series.

#### Worked example: headcount cost at launch by strategy

Let me show why the funder-strategy match is really an operating-cost match. Take three founders launching at the same 75 million USD of AUM, and price the *minimum* team each strategy requires.

A **discretionary L/S equity** founder: herself (PM) plus two analysts. Loaded cost (salary, benefits, payroll tax, a modest bonus pool) might run 250,000 USD for each analyst and a deferred draw for the PM — call it 600,000 USD a year in people. Add outsourced operations and you are lean.

A **discretionary global macro** founder: herself plus a research analyst and a trader/execution person, plus a strong need for an experienced risk/COO hybrid because macro books carry large directional risk. Call it 4 people at a blended 300,000 USD, roughly 1.2 million USD in people.

A **systematic quant** founder (Maya): herself plus two researchers plus two engineers — the strategy does not run without all five. At a blended 350,000 USD (quant researchers and engineers are expensive), that is 1.75 million USD in people *before* the data and infrastructure bill we priced in the hook.

On 75 million USD at a 1.5% management fee, all three collect 1.125 million USD a year. The L/S founder's people cost fits comfortably and leaves room for the rest of the budget. The macro founder is tight. The systematic founder is *underwater on payroll alone* and needs either much more AUM or a seeder. *The same launch AUM is comfortable, tight, or impossible depending only on the strategy's mandatory headcount — and that, not the elegance of the alpha, is what determines whether the fund can open its doors.*

## Matching strategy to your edge and capital

We can now assemble the decision. The founder has three things: an **edge** (what you are genuinely better at than the market), a pool of **reachable capital** (what you can actually raise at launch, honestly assessed, not hoped for), and a **team** (who will follow you and what they cost). The job is to pick the strategy that fits all three. Figure 7 is the cascade.

![A seven-stage pipeline flowing from choosing a strategy through setting capacity, sizing the data and tech bill, planning headcount, picking primes and leverage, summing fixed costs, and arriving at the minimum viable AUM and team](/imgs/blogs/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens-7.png)

Read the pipeline as a forcing function. A strategy choice sets a capacity (which caps how big the business can ever get), a data/tech bill and a headcount plan (which set your fixed cost), and a prime/leverage profile (which sets your counterparty and risk needs). Sum the fixed costs, divide by your management-fee rate, and you have a **break-even AUM**. Compare that to the capital you can actually raise and to the strategy's capacity, and you have your answer: *is there a viable operating window between break-even and capacity that I can reach with my reachable capital?*

Three rules fall out of this.

**Rule 1: match fixed cost to launch AUM, not to target AUM.** Founders budget as if they will be at 300 million USD in year two. They launch at 60 million. The strategy whose fixed costs require 100 million to break even will burn the fund's (and the founder's) capital during the years it takes to raise — if it survives them at all. Choose a strategy whose break-even AUM is *below* what you can plausibly raise in year one, and let the capacity headroom be your growth runway.

**Rule 2: respect the capacity-vs-break-even window.** A strategy whose break-even AUM is close to its capacity ceiling has almost no viable operating window. A strategy with a low break-even and a high ceiling — a lean discretionary L/S desk, say — has a wide window and forgives slow raising. Maya's systematic equity strategy had a *high* break-even (expensive to operate) and a *moderate* ceiling (mid-cap impact). That narrow window is exactly why it was the wrong launch business for her capital, even though it was the right *strategy* for her skill.

To make Rule 2 concrete, compare two windows. Maya's lean discretionary L/S desk breaks even near 25 to 30 million USD (low fixed cost) and its concentrated book caps out, say, around 1 to 1.5 billion USD before her best ideas run out of liquidity. Her window is roughly 30 million to 1.2 billion — a band more than a billion dollars wide, reachable on day one with a family-office check, and forgiving of a slow raise. Her systematic equity fund breaks even near 130 million USD (high fixed cost, from the seed-deal example) and its mid-cap impact caps it near 3.3 billion. The window — 130 million to 3.3 billion — is wider in absolute dollars but starts far above her reachable 40 million, so the *reachable* portion of it is empty until she finds more capital. The lean strategy's window is smaller but *reachable*; the expensive strategy's window is larger but *unreachable from where she stands*. Reachability, not width, is what decides operability.

**Rule 3: match the funder to the operating bar you can clear.** If your reachable capital is a couple of family offices, you need a strategy whose ODD bar and operating cost you can satisfy on day one. If you have a seeder lined up willing to underwrite a heavier infrastructure in exchange for [a share of the management company](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money), you can choose a more expensive strategy. The seed deal does not change the strategy's economics — it changes *which* strategy is reachable.

Leverage and prime relationships deserve a place in this decision too, because they quietly raise the operating bar. A strategy that needs 4x leverage to make its thin spread worth a fee is a strategy that needs multiple prime brokers (for financing capacity and counterparty diversification), tighter margin management, and a risk function that can survive a prime pulling lines in a stress. A top-tier prime broker will not onboard a sub-100-million-USD fund as a full client, so a highly leveraged strategy at small launch AUM is forced onto mini-primes with less capacity and worse terms — or is simply not operable until it is bigger. Picking a leverage-hungry relative-value strategy at launch therefore imports a prime-brokerage and risk-governance bar that compounds the capital problem: you need more AUM not just to break even on fixed cost, but to be a client a real prime will finance at the leverage the strategy requires.

Figure 6 makes the headcount-and-systems contrast concrete: two operations running the same AUM look nothing alike.

![A before and after comparison of a lean discretionary long short desk with three to five people and modest costs versus a tech heavy systematic shop with fifteen to forty people, alt data, automated execution, and several million dollars of fixed cost](/imgs/blogs/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens-6.png)

The left operation and the right operation can manage the identical dollar amount and produce comparable Sharpe ratios. But the left founder breaks even at a fraction of the AUM the right founder needs, can launch with a family-office check, and survives a slow raise. The right founder needs a seeder or a large anchor and a fast raise or she dies. *Same returns, different businesses* — and the choice between them is the operating lens.

#### Worked example: tech and data cost, systematic vs discretionary

Let me price the data-and-tech line for two of Maya's would-be peers at launch, holding AUM at 75 million USD.

**Discretionary L/S desk.** Three Bloomberg terminals at roughly 25,000 USD each ≈ 75,000 USD. FactSet or a research aggregator ≈ 50,000 USD. A simple cloud OMS/portfolio system for a small fund ≈ 50,000 USD. Total data/tech ≈ 175,000 USD a year. That is about 0.23% of AUM — meaningful but easily absorbed by a 1.5% management fee.

**Systematic quant shop (Maya).** Point-in-time fundamental data ≈ 250,000 USD. Pricing and reference data ≈ 100,000 USD. Two alternative datasets ≈ 200,000 USD. Tick history for execution modeling ≈ 150,000 USD. Cloud compute for an overnight backtest grid ≈ 150,000 USD. Production OMS, risk, and execution infrastructure ≈ 150,000 USD. Total data/tech ≈ 1.0 million USD a year — and that is *before* the two engineers who keep it running. That is about 1.3% of AUM, nearly the entire management fee, spent before a single salary or office cost.

On 75 million USD at 1.5%, the management fee is 1.125 million USD. The discretionary founder spends 175,000 USD of it on tech and has roughly 950,000 USD left for people and overhead. The systematic founder spends 1.0 million on tech and has 125,000 USD left — for five people and an office. *The data-and-tech bill alone reclassifies the same launch AUM from comfortable to impossible, which is why systematic strategies have such a high minimum viable AUM and why so many brilliant quant researchers either join a platform, raise a much larger fund, or take a seed deal rather than launch small.*

#### Worked example: total fixed cost and break-even AUM at USD 100M

Now combine tech and people into the single number that matters — total fixed cost — and convert it to a break-even AUM, for both archetypes sitting at \$100,000,000 of AUM. Every small fund also owes a baseline of institutional-grade overhead regardless of strategy: an administrator (\$50,000–\$100,000), an annual audit (\$40,000), outside legal and compliance (\$100,000), and office, insurance, and miscellaneous (\$110,000) — call it roughly \$300,000 a year of shared baseline.

**Discretionary L/S desk.** Tech and data \$175,000 + people (a PM draw plus two analysts) ≈ \$700,000 + the \$300,000 baseline ≈ \$1,175,000 of total fixed cost. At a 1.5% management fee, break-even AUM = \$1,175,000 ÷ 0.015 ≈ \$78,000,000. At \$100,000,000 the fund collects \$1,500,000 in management fees, clears its \$1,175,000 of costs, and the founder is modestly above water on the management company before earning a cent of performance fee.

**Systematic quant shop.** Tech and data \$1,000,000 + people (the founder plus two researchers and two engineers) ≈ \$1,750,000 + the \$300,000 baseline ≈ \$3,050,000 of total fixed cost. Break-even AUM = \$3,050,000 ÷ 0.015 ≈ \$203,000,000. At \$100,000,000 the systematic fund collects the same \$1,500,000 in management fees but owes \$3,050,000 — it is burning roughly \$1,550,000 a year, covered only by performance fees in a good year or by the founder's own capital in a bad one.

*At the identical \$100,000,000 of AUM, the discretionary desk is a viable management company and the systematic shop is losing about a million and a half dollars a year — break-even sits near \$78M for one and near \$203M for the other. That two-and-a-half-times gap in the minimum viable AUM is the operating lens in one number: the strategy you choose sets the AUM you must reach before the lights stay on.*

## Common misconceptions

**"Any strategy scales — just raise more money."** No. Every strategy has a capacity ceiling set by market impact and a finite opportunity set, and that ceiling ranges over roughly 100x across families (Figure 3). A merger-arb fund cannot become a 30-billion-USD fund by trying harder; the deals do not exist. The way platforms reach tens of billions is not by scaling one strategy but by *summing many* uncorrelated strategies, each managed near its own capacity. Believing your strategy will scale to any size is the single most expensive planning error a founder makes, because it leads to a fixed-cost base that only a never-reached AUM could ever support.

**"Systematic is cheaper than discretionary because the computer does the work."** Backwards. The computer *replaces analysts*, but it *requires* data, infrastructure, and engineers that cost far more than the analysts it replaced. Maya's systematic operation costs several times what a discretionary L/S desk of the same AUM costs (Figure 6 and the two worked examples). Automation lowers marginal cost per trade, which is wonderful at scale, but it dramatically raises the *fixed* cost and therefore the minimum viable AUM. Systematic is cheaper to *operate at a billion dollars* and ruinously expensive to *launch at fifty million*.

**"You can run macro out of a bedroom."** This one is half-true and dangerous. You can *form macro views* anywhere — the research is intellectual, the instruments are liquid, and you do not need alternative data. But running an *institutional* macro fund that allocators will fund requires a serious risk function (macro books carry large directional risk and fat tails), multiple prime and clearing relationships across asset classes, and the operational infrastructure to satisfy ODD. The strategy is cheap in *data* and expensive in *risk governance and operations*. Founders who confuse "I can express a macro view cheaply" with "I can operate a macro fund cheaply" discover the difference during their first 8% drawdown, with no risk system and no COO.

**"Capacity doesn't matter early — I'm tiny, I have miles of room."** Capacity matters from day one because it interacts with your *fixed cost*. A low-capacity strategy gives you a narrow window between break-even AUM and the AUM where alpha decays. If your strategy breaks even at 100 million and decays at 600 million, your entire viable life is a 500-million-USD-wide band, and you must navigate it without either starving or diluting. A founder who ignores capacity early picks a strategy whose ceiling they will hit before they have built a sustainable management company — and then has to either accept decaying returns or close the fund to new money and forgo the fee growth that makes the business worth running.

**"The best strategy is the one with the highest backtested Sharpe."** A high Sharpe on a strategy you cannot operate is a fantasy. The relevant Sharpe is the *net, after-fee, after-impact, at-your-actual-AUM* Sharpe, run on infrastructure you can actually afford, by a team you can actually pay. A 2.0-Sharpe systematic signal that needs 3 million USD of annual infrastructure is, at 50 million USD of launch AUM, a negative-net-of-fixed-cost business. A 1.0-Sharpe discretionary L/S process that breaks even at 30 million is a real, fundable company. Choose on the operable Sharpe, not the backtested one — and beware that backtested Sharpes are themselves usually inflated (see [overfitting and the deflated Sharpe](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/overfitting-purged-cv-deflated-sharpe-quant-research)).

**"I'll just pick the strategy I'm best at and figure out the operations later."** Operations are not a downstream detail; they are a *gating constraint on which strategies you can choose*. Maya was best at systematic equity, and choosing it anyway nearly bankrupted her before launch. The disciplined move is to let the operating lens *filter* your strategy menu down to the families you can run and operate with your real capital and team, and then pick the one within that filtered set where your edge is strongest. Skill chooses among the operable strategies; it does not override operability.

## How it plays out in the real world

The industry's structure is the operating lens made visible. Of a global hedge fund industry of roughly 4.5–5.0 trillion USD as of 2026 (HFR, Preqin, BarclayHedge estimates), the assets are heavily concentrated: funds managing over 1 billion USD — the "billion-dollar club" — control the large majority of the money, while the median fund is far smaller and most managers run under 100 million USD. Figure 5 shows the rough split by strategy family.

![A bar chart showing the illustrative share of hedge fund industry assets by strategy family, with equity long short and multi strategy platforms together holding about half of the industry](/imgs/blogs/choosing-your-strategy-the-operating-lens-5.png)

Equity long/short and multi-strat platforms together hold roughly half the industry's assets — the former because it is the most established family with the widest funder base, the latter because the platform model is the one that successfully *escapes* the single-strategy capacity ceiling and can therefore absorb the largest institutional tickets. The shares are illustrative and vary by source and bucketing, but the structural story is robust: the families that can grow large (L/S, multi-strat, macro, CTA) hold more assets than the families that hit capacity ceilings early (event-driven, niche credit).

The named blow-ups in this series' case studies are, at root, operating-lens failures. **Amaranth Advisors** lost roughly 6.6 billion USD — about 65% of its assets — in roughly a week in 2006 on concentrated natural-gas spread bets run by a single trader. That was a capacity-and-concentration failure: a strategy whose real capacity was far below the size the fund put behind it, in a book too concentrated and too illiquid to exit when the trade moved. **Long-Term Capital Management** in 1998 ran relative-value convergence trades — a low-capacity, leverage-hungry strategy — at balance-sheet leverage around 25–30x with over a trillion dollars of derivatives notional; when the spreads it was harvesting widened instead of converging, the leverage that made the thin edge worth charging a fee on became the mechanism of a 4.6-billion-USD loss and a Fed-organized recapitalization. **Archegos** in 2021 was a family office that hid roughly 10-billion-USD-plus of concentrated, highly leveraged total-return-swap exposure across multiple prime brokers, each blind to the others — a prime-count-and-leverage failure that cost its banks (Credit Suisse alone roughly 5.5 billion USD) dearly. In every case the *operating* dimensions of the strategy — capacity, leverage, liquidity, prime relationships — were the cause, not a stock-picking error.

The survivors built the operating lens into the strategy choice from the start. The discretionary L/S funds that lasted launched lean, broke even early, and grew into capacity. The systematic shops that lasted either raised enough to support the infrastructure or took seed capital to underwrite it. The multi-strat platforms that dominate the asset-weighted league tables did so by industrializing the operating model — central risk, shared infrastructure, many capacity-bounded pods summed into a large, scalable whole. None of them chose a strategy without choosing the business that came with it.

Maya's resolution, for the record: she did not launch the expensive systematic fund at 40 million USD. She did one of two things real founders in her position do. The disciplined version: she launched a *concentrated discretionary L/S* fund — a strategy she could also run well, with a break-even under 30 million USD, fundable by her family-office contacts on day one — and built the systematic capability inside it over years as AUM grew. The ambitious version: she took a seed deal, giving up a fifth of her management-company economics for 75 million USD of anchor capital, which moved the systematic strategy back inside her reachable operating window. Both are sound. Launching the systematic fund at 40 million with no seed was the one option that was not.

## When this matters / Further reading

Apply the operating lens **before** you commit to a strategy, **before** you write the first line of a pitch deck, and **before** you sign a single service-provider engagement. The moment to run the Figure 7 cascade — strategy → capacity → data/tech → headcount → primes/leverage → fixed cost → minimum viable AUM and team — is when the fund is still an idea, because the answer should *change which strategy you launch*. It matters most for the founder whose skill points at an expensive strategy (systematic, large macro, multi-pod) but whose reachable capital is small: that founder's job is to find the version of their edge that fits a strategy they can both run and operate, or to find the seed capital that widens the window.

Concretely, you have done this right when you can state, in one sentence each: the capacity ceiling of your strategy, its annual fixed cost, the AUM at which it breaks even, the AUM at which its alpha decays below its fee, and the specific allocator type you will reach for day-one capital — and when the band between break-even and decay is wide enough to be a business, and reachable with the money you can actually raise.
