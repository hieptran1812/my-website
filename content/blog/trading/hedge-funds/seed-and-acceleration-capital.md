> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A seed deal solves the chicken-and-egg of launching a fund — you need capital to be credible, and you need to be credible to raise capital — by trading day-one money for a permanent slice of the business you are building.
>
> - A seeder typically writes a cheque of **\$25M to \$150M** of day-one capital in exchange for **15% to 25% of your management-company revenue**, sometimes a **GP equity stake** of the management company itself, plus a multi-year lock-up, capacity rights, and most-favored-nation fee terms.
> - The seeder is not buying a fund return; the seeder is buying a *call option on you as a manager* — cheap to write, enormous if you compound to a billion dollars, and worth nothing if you fail.
> - The price is steep and, on a revenue share, effectively permanent: a 20% share of fee revenue on a fund that grows to \$1.5B can hand the seeder more than the original \$75M cheque within roughly 13 years — and keep paying after that.
> - The one number to remember: a seed cheque can put you over the **~\$80M break-even AUM on day one**, turning years of cash-burn into immediate viability — which is exactly why it is worth so much, and priced accordingly.

Maya had spent eighteen months and a small fortune in legal bills building the Meridian Fund, a Delaware limited partnership wrapped around a long/short equity strategy she had run inside a much larger shop for nine years. She had the track record, the tear sheet, the prime-brokerage agreement, and the service-provider stack ready to go. What she did not have was money. Her warm list of friends, family, and former colleagues had soft-circled about \$15 million. At a 1.5% management fee, \$15 million throws off \$225,000 a year — against a fixed run-rate of roughly \$1.2 million. She would burn cash from the first day and keep burning it for years, hoping performance and word-of-mouth dragged her up to the roughly \$80 million of assets where the fees finally cover the bills.

Then a seeder called. A firm whose entire business is putting day-one capital into new managers offered her \$75 million — five times her warm money, enough to clear break-even on the morning of launch — in exchange for 20% of her management company's revenue and a 15% equity stake in the management company itself, locked up for three years. Maya sat at her kitchen table with a legal pad and did the arithmetic that every founder eventually does. The \$75 million would make her credible, viable, and fundable overnight. The 20% revenue share would follow her for the life of the firm. She was being offered the start line in exchange for a permanent fifth of everything she was about to build. Was that a gift or a mortgage?

This post is the answer to Maya's question, generalized. A seed deal is the single most consequential financing decision most founders ever make, and it is sold to them by people who profit when they sign. We are going to take it apart: what seed capital actually is, why seeders exist and what they are really buying, what a deal looks like clause by clause, the dollars you give up over a decade, how acceleration capital differs, who the named players are, how the deal is negotiated, when it is worth it, and the myths that trip up smart people who can run money but have never sold a piece of a company. Figure 1 is the whole trade on one page; the rest of the post earns the right to read it.

This is the financing layer of the [capital-raising reality every founder hits](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-capital-raising-reality-chicken-and-egg): when your warm money is not enough to reach viability, a seeder will sell you the difference, and the currency you pay in is ownership of your own firm.

## Foundations: what seed capital is

Before the economics, the vocabulary. Everything in this post is a recombination of nine terms. If you trade for a living, the *markets* part of a fund is second nature; it is this *capital-formation* layer that is usually blank. Define each one cold and the rest of the post falls into place.

**Seed capital.** A large, committed allocation of money provided to a fund *at or before launch* — the day-one assets that get a new manager from zero to a viable size. Unlike a normal investor's subscription, seed capital is not just money in the fund; it comes bundled with rights and economics attached to the *management company*. A seed allocation is simultaneously an investment in the fund and a purchase of a slice of the founder's business. It is the difference between launching with \$15 million you raised yourself and launching with \$90 million because someone wrote you a \$75 million cheque.

**The seeder.** The firm or family office that provides the seed capital. Seeders are specialists — their entire business model is identifying promising new managers, financing their launch, and capturing part of the upside if those managers grow. A seeder is not a passive limited partner hoping for a good return on \$75 million; the seeder is an active partner in the *enterprise value* of your firm. A seeder operates like a venture capitalist for asset managers: they back many launches, expect most to be mediocre, and rely on one or two compounding into billion-dollar businesses to make the whole portfolio work.

**Acceleration capital.** Capital provided *after* launch, to a manager who already has a track record and some assets but needs a step-change in size to reach the next threshold. An accelerator backs a manager who has proven they can run the business and now needs to get from, say, \$60 million to \$200 million. Because the manager is past the riskiest stage, the give-up is smaller — a lower revenue share, often time-boxed rather than permanent, and frequently no GP stake. Acceleration is the cheaper, later-stage cousin of seed.

**Revenue share.** The most common form a seeder's economics take: a fixed percentage — typically **15% to 25%** — of the management company's *gross fee revenue* (or sometimes its net profit). If the management company collects \$5 million in fees this year and the seeder has a 20% revenue share, the seeder receives \$1 million. A revenue share is a claim on the *top line* of your business, and unless the contract caps it in time, it persists for as long as the firm exists. This is the term that quietly costs the most.

**GP stake.** An equity ownership interest in the management company (or the general-partner entity) itself — not the fund. A 15% GP stake means the seeder owns 15% of the *business*, with whatever governance, information, and exit rights the contract specifies. Where a revenue share is a claim on cash flow, a GP stake is a claim on *ownership* — it survives a sale of the firm, may carry a vote, and dilutes the founders permanently. We cover how these stakes evolve as a firm matures in [capital structure, evolution, and GP stakes](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/capital-structure-evolution-and-gp-stakes).

**Lock-up.** A period during which the seeder agrees not to redeem its capital from the fund — typically **two to four years**. The lock-up is the seeder's commitment that this is sticky, anchor money, not hot money that flees at the first drawdown. Sticky capital is the whole point of a seed: it lets the founder tell every other prospective investor "I have \$75 million locked up for three years," which is far more credible than "I have \$15 million that can leave next quarter." For the mechanics of lock-ups, gates, and redemption terms generally, see [liquidity terms: lock-ups, gates, and side pockets](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets).

**Capacity right.** A contractual guarantee that the seeder can put *more* money into the strategy as it scales, up to some agreed amount, on agreed terms. Strategies have finite capacity — a niche statistical-arbitrage book might only absorb \$300 million before its edge decays — so a capacity right is valuable: it reserves the seeder a seat at the table for the strategy's most scalable years, ahead of the institutions queuing up later. The seeder is buying not just today's allocation but the *option to grow with you*.

**Most-favored-nation (MFN) terms.** A clause guaranteeing the seeder fee terms at least as good as any other investor receives. If you later grant a sovereign wealth fund a 1%/10% fee deal, MFN automatically resets the seeder to 1%/10% too. MFN protects the seeder from being undercut by a bigger, later investor and is standard in seed contracts. It also constrains *you*: you cannot offer aggressive terms to win a big later allocation without those terms cascading back to the seeder.

**The management fee and the incentive fee.** The two revenue lines a fund manager earns. The **management fee** is a fixed annual percentage of assets under management (AUM) — historically 2%, now closer to 1.3% to 1.5% — that pays the bills regardless of performance. The **incentive fee** (or performance fee) is roughly 15% to 20% of profits above a prior peak, paid only when the fund makes money. The seeder's revenue share usually applies to *both* lines combined. We unpack these in full in [how a hedge fund makes money](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-a-hedge-fund-makes-money); here, hold the fact that the seeder's share is a slice of *that* combined revenue.

With those nine terms in hand — seed capital, seeder, acceleration, revenue share, GP stake, lock-up, capacity right, MFN, and the two fee lines — we can follow the money.

## Why seeders exist: buying a call option on a manager

A seeder is not in the business of earning a return on \$75 million of fund capital. If that were the goal, the seeder would simply be a large limited partner, taking the fund's return on its money and nothing more. The reason a seeder demands a slice of the *management company* on top of its fund return is that the seeder is buying something far more valuable than a year's performance: the seeder is buying a *call option on the founder's entire future business*.

Here is the logic. The value of a hedge-fund management company is roughly proportional to its fee revenue, which is roughly proportional to its AUM. A founder who launches at \$75 million and grinds out a respectable career managing \$200 million has built a modest business. A founder who compounds to \$1.5 billion has built a small fortune — fee revenue of \$20 million-plus a year, a management company worth a large multiple of that. The seeder cannot know in advance which founder is which. So the seeder backs a *portfolio* of launches, knowing most will plateau or fail, and structures each deal so that the rare manager who compounds into a billion-dollar business pays for the entire book many times over.

That payoff shape — limited downside, unlimited upside, contingent on the manager succeeding — is exactly the shape of a call option. The seeder's "premium" is the \$75 million cheque plus the operational support; the "strike" is implicit in the revenue-share percentage; the "expiry" is never, because the revenue share persists. When a manager fails, the seeder loses some of its fund capital (cushioned by the fund's own risk controls) and the revenue share goes to zero — a bounded loss. When a manager compounds, the seeder's revenue share grows without limit. A seeder running a portfolio of twenty seeds needs only two or three to become large for the whole strategy to clear its hurdle. This is why the named seeders behave like venture capitalists, not like pension funds.

This framing also explains *why the price feels so high to the founder*. You, the founder, are not buying an option; you are *writing* one to the seeder, and you are writing it on the single asset you cannot diversify — your own firm. The seeder spreads its risk across twenty managers; you have one. The seeder's expected outcome is dominated by its winners; your outcome is binary. That asymmetry is the heart of every seed negotiation: the seeder is pricing a diversified option book, and you are selling a slice of your only egg.

There is a second reason seeders can extract so much, and it is one founders routinely overlook: the seeder usually brings more than money. A seasoned seeding platform arrives with an operational toolkit a first-time manager would otherwise have to assemble from scratch — relationships with prime brokers and administrators that can compress your fees and shorten your onboarding, a compliance template that has already survived dozens of regulatory exams, introductions to the allocators in the seeder's own network, and the implicit credibility of the seeder's name on your investor list. When an institutional allocator's operational due diligence team sees that a respected seeder has already vetted your operation and committed locked-up capital, a chunk of their own work is done for them. That endorsement is worth real money in faster, larger subsequent allocations, and the seeder prices it into the deal alongside the cheque. A founder evaluating a seed should therefore value not just the \$75 million but the platform — though they should also be honest that some seeders offer little beyond the cash, in which case the price should reflect that.

The flip side is that the seeder's interests and the founder's interests are not perfectly aligned. The seeder wants AUM to grow as fast as possible, because the revenue share scales with assets; the founder may want to *cap* assets to protect the strategy's edge, since many strategies decay as they scale. A seeder with a 20% revenue share and a capacity right will push you toward taking more money than your alpha can absorb — and the resulting performance drag is borne mostly by you and your other investors, while the seeder still collects on the larger management fee. This misalignment is subtle and chronic, and it is one more reason to cap the capacity right and to keep governance rights out of any GP stake: you do not want a partner whose economics reward the very asset bloat that kills your returns.

#### Worked example: the seeder's option math across a portfolio

A seeder commits \$75 million each to twenty new managers — \$1.5 billion deployed. Suppose the base case for each manager is grim: twelve fail outright within five years (the revenue share dies, and the fund capital takes a haircut), six plateau around \$150 million (a 20% revenue share on roughly \$3 million of annual fee revenue is \$600,000 a year each), and two compound to \$1.5 billion (a 20% share on roughly \$20 million of fee revenue is \$4 million a year each). Add it up: the six plateaus throw off \$3.6 million a year combined; the two winners throw off \$8 million a year combined. That is \$11.6 million a year of revenue-share income from the survivors, plus the seeder still earns the fund return on its surviving capital, plus those revenue shares *keep paying* and the winners may keep growing. Against a portfolio whose downside on any single name is capped at the fund-level loss, the two winners alone justify the whole \$1.5 billion programme.

*The seeder does not need you to succeed; the seeder needs the few who do to compound enough to carry the rest — which is why the seeder can afford to be patient on twenty deals and ruthless on the terms of each.*

## What a seed deal looks like

A seed term sheet is a short document with enormous consequences. Strip away the legal language and a typical deal has six moving parts, and the founder negotiates each one against the others. Figure 2 traces the central tension — the value of the revenue share the founder gives up against the one-time cheque the seeder writes — over the life of a successful firm.

![A line chart comparing the cumulative dollars a founder gives up through a twenty percent revenue share against the flat seventy-five million dollar cheque, with the give-up line crossing the cheque around year thirteen](/imgs/blogs/seed-and-acceleration-capital-2.png)

**The cheque.** The headline number: how much day-one capital the seeder commits. Historically this has ranged from about **\$25 million** for a smaller manager to **\$150 million or more** for a sought-after launch. The cheque is what gets you to viability, and bigger is better for credibility — but a bigger cheque buys the seeder a bigger claim, so there is a tension between launching large and giving up little.

**The revenue share.** The percentage of management-company revenue the seeder receives, typically **15% to 25%**. This is usually the most negotiated term because it is the one that compounds. A founder will fight for the low end and a short duration; the seeder will push for the high end and permanence.

**The GP stake (sometimes).** Whether the seeder takes equity ownership in the management company, and how much — often around **15%** when present. Not every seed includes a GP stake; some are pure revenue shares. A GP stake gives the seeder governance and a share of any eventual sale, which is why founders resist it harder than the revenue share.

**The lock-up.** How long the seeder's capital is committed and cannot redeem — typically **two to four years**. A longer lock-up is more valuable to the founder (stickier anchor capital) and is often *traded against* a lower revenue share: "I will lock up for four years if you take 18% instead of 22%."

**The capacity right.** How much additional capital the seeder may add as the strategy scales, and on what terms. This protects the seeder's ability to grow with a winner and is more contentious for capacity-constrained strategies.

**MFN and the fee terms.** The seeder's own fee terms (often discounted via a founders share class) and the guarantee that no later investor gets better terms. Seeders almost always negotiate a founders share class — say 1.5%/15% or 1%/10% against a standard 1.5%/20% — *in addition to* the revenue share, because they are both an early investor and a business partner.

The deal is a bundle, and the founder's leverage lies in trading these terms against one another. A manager with a hot track record and a scarce strategy can demand a smaller cheque-to-give-up ratio; a first-time manager with soft demand takes what is offered. The single most important thing to understand is that **the revenue share and GP stake are not fees — they are sales of the business**, and they do not stop when the lock-up ends. The lock-up governs only the seeder's *capital*; the economics persist according to their own (often perpetual) terms.

One distinction inside the revenue share deserves its own attention because it changes the dollars materially: is the seeder taking a share of *gross revenue* or of *net profit*? A gross-revenue share — 20% of every dollar of fee income, before the management company pays a cent of its costs — is the harshest version for the founder, because it is collected even when the firm is losing money on a net basis. A net-profit share — 20% of what is left after the firm pays its run-rate — is gentler, because it only bites once the business is profitable, and it shrinks in lean years. Seeders prefer gross because it is simpler to audit and pays them sooner; founders should fight for net, or at least for a gross share that steps down once the firm clears a profitability threshold. The difference between a 20% gross share and a 20% net share, on a firm that spends most of its early revenue on fixed costs, can be the difference between the founder drawing a salary and the founder funding the gap out of savings.

The founders share class is a second piece founders tend to miss. Because the seeder is *also* an early, large investor in the fund, it almost always negotiates a discounted fee class on its own capital — a founders class at, say, **1.5%/15%** or even **1%/10%** against the standard **1.5%/20%** the fund charges everyone else. This is logically separate from the revenue share: the seeder pays you *less* in fees on its \$75 million *and* takes a cut of the fees you collect from everyone. On the seeder's \$75 million at a 1% management fee, you collect only \$750,000 instead of the \$1.125 million you would collect at 1.5%. Combined with the 20% revenue share on the rest, the seeder's all-in economic footprint on your firm is larger than the headline 20% suggests. Founders evaluating a term sheet should compute the *total* economic give-up — discounted fees on the seed capital, plus the revenue share, plus the value of the GP stake — not just the single most visible number.

#### Worked example: the Meridian seed term sheet

Maya's seeder offers: a **\$75 million** cheque; a **20% share** of management-company revenue; a **15% GP stake** in the management company; a **three-year lock-up**; a capacity right to add up to **\$50 million** more as the fund scales; and a founders share class of **1.5%/15%** with MFN. Compare that to her bootstrapped alternative: **\$15 million** of warm money, no revenue share, no GP stake, no lock-up, and 100% ownership. The seed quintuples her launch AUM and clears break-even immediately, at the cost of one-fifth of her revenue forever and 15% of her company. On day one, the seed looks like a triumph — \$90 million total versus \$15 million. The question, as Figure 2 shows, is what that 20% share is worth across the next decade.

*A seed term sheet is not a loan and not a fee; it is the partial sale of a company that does not yet exist, priced by someone who has done this many times and knows exactly what a compounding firm is worth.*

## The economics: what you give up

Now the arithmetic that ruined and saved Maya's launch week in equal measure. The seeder's economics come out of the *management company* — the operating business you own, not the fund itself. (For why the fund and the management company are separate entities, with separate jobs, see [how a hedge fund makes money](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-a-hedge-fund-makes-money).) A revenue share is a claim on the management company's top line; a GP stake is a claim on its ownership. Both reduce what reaches the founder's pocket.

Start with the revenue share, because it is the one founders systematically underestimate. A 20% share *sounds* survivable — you keep 80%, after all. But a revenue share is a claim on your *gross* revenue, taken *before* your costs. In the early years, when your revenue barely covers your fixed costs, giving up 20% of the top line can mean giving up a much larger fraction of your *profit* — sometimes all of it, sometimes more than all of it. The revenue share is senior to your own paycheck.

#### Worked example: a 20% revenue share on a fund that grows to \$500M AUM

Suppose Meridian compounds to **\$500 million** of AUM and, in a good year, earns a 12% gross return. The management fee at 1.5% is **\$7.5 million**. The incentive fee at 15% of the 12% gain (roughly \$60 million of profit) is about **\$9 million**. Total management-company revenue: about **\$16.5 million**. The seeder's 20% revenue share takes **\$3.3 million** off the top. Now subtract the firm's costs — say \$4 million of fixed run-rate at this scale plus \$3 million of staff and partner draws beyond Maya's own — and the management company nets, before Maya's distribution, about \$9.5 million. The seeder's \$3.3 million is roughly a third of what Maya herself takes home. On \$500 million of AUM, in a *good* year, the revenue share alone is \$3.3 million — every year, regardless of whether Maya has a good year or a flat one, because the management fee piece of it is collected even when performance is zero.

*A revenue share taken off the gross is far heavier than it looks against the net: 20% of the top line can easily be a third or more of the founder's take-home, and it is collected in flat years when the founder earns almost nothing.*

Now the duration problem, which Figure 2 makes concrete. The seeder's \$75 million cheque is a *one-time* event. The revenue share is *forever* (or for a very long stated term). In the early years the share is small in dollar terms because revenue is small — cheap for the founder, which is why it feels harmless at signing. But as the firm compounds, the annual give-up grows, and the *cumulative* dollars handed back eventually exceed the original cheque. On a fund that compounds toward \$1.5 billion, the cumulative 20% revenue share crosses the \$75 million the seeder originally wrote at around **year thirteen** — and then keeps climbing. After that point, every dollar of revenue share is pure surplus to the seeder, who has already been repaid for the cheque and continues to collect.

This is the central honesty of the seed trade: it is *cheap when you are small and expensive when you are large*. The founders who most regret their seed deals are precisely the ones who succeeded — because success is what makes the permanent revenue share so costly. The founders who fail never reach the part of the curve where the deal hurts; they are out of business long before. A seed deal is therefore a bet that pays the seeder most when you win, which is exactly the call-option shape from earlier, seen now from the founder's side.

#### Worked example: a GP-stake dilution scenario

Layer the GP stake on top. Maya's seeder owns **15%** of the management company. Five years in, Meridian is managing \$600 million, the management company is throwing off roughly \$5 million a year of distributable profit after costs and the revenue share, and a GP-stakes buyer offers to value the firm at **10 times** earnings — \$50 million. Maya wants to sell a slice to diversify her own wealth and fund growth. But the seeder already owns 15% of that \$50 million — **\$7.5 million** of enterprise value belongs to the seeder, on top of the \$3 million-plus of annual revenue share the seeder has been collecting. If Maya sells another 20% to the new buyer, she is now down to 65% of her own firm: 15% to the seeder, 20% to the new buyer. The GP stake did not just take cash flow; it took a permanent, dilutive ownership claim on the *sale* of the business — the single largest liquidity event a founder ever has.

*A revenue share takes your cash flow; a GP stake takes your equity — and equity is what you sell at the end, so a GP stake reaches into the one transaction that was supposed to make you whole.*

#### Worked example: the NPV trade-off of the seed

Put it all together as a present-value comparison, which is the calculation Maya did at her kitchen table. The seed gives her, today, a benefit: she launches at \$90 million instead of \$15 million, clears the \$80 million break-even immediately, draws a salary from year one, and — crucially — becomes *fundable*, because locked-up anchor capital is what allocators want to see. Call the present value of all that, conservatively, the avoided cash-burn plus the accelerated AUM growth: on the order of **\$8 million to \$12 million** of value created in the first few years versus bootstrapping. Against that, she gives up a revenue share whose present value, discounted at say 12% over the firm's expected life and probability-weighted across success and failure, might be **\$10 million to \$25 million**, plus 15% of an uncertain future enterprise value. If Maya is confident she will compound to a billion dollars, the NPV of the give-up dwarfs the benefit and she should bootstrap if she possibly can. If she thinks her odds of reaching viability *without* the seed are low — that without \$75 million she is one bad quarter from shutting down — then the seed's benefit (existing at all) is close to infinite and the price is worth paying.

*The seed is worth it precisely when you cannot reach the start line without it, and a bad deal precisely when you could have — the NPV turns entirely on your honest probability of surviving unseeded.*

## Acceleration capital: the cheaper, later cousin

A seed deal prices the riskiest moment — backing a manager with no track record and no assets, when the failure rate is highest. Once a manager has launched, survived a couple of years, and built a real track record, the risk profile changes completely, and so does the price of capital. That later-stage money is **acceleration capital**.

An accelerator backs a manager who has already proven the business works — there is a fund, a NAV history, an operational stack that has passed real [operational due diligence](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates), and some assets. What the manager lacks is *scale*: enough AUM to reach the institutional thresholds (often \$250 million to \$500 million) where the big, slow allocators will look. The accelerator provides a step-change cheque to get the manager over that hump.

Because the manager is past the binary survival risk, the give-up is materially smaller. Acceleration typically takes a **lower revenue share** — single digits up to the low teens rather than 15% to 25% — and frequently makes it **time-boxed** (a share that runs for, say, five to seven years and then expires, rather than forever) or **AUM-capped** (the share applies only up to a certain AUM and falls away above it). Acceleration deals also more often **skip the GP stake** entirely, leaving the founder's ownership intact. In exchange, the accelerator usually gets a lock-up and capacity rights, just like a seed.

The trade-off is symmetric to the seed: acceleration is cheaper because it is less risky for the provider, but it is only available *after* you have survived the period when you most needed the money. You cannot get acceleration terms at launch, because at launch you have not yet proven the thing acceleration prices. Many founders therefore face a sequencing question: take an expensive seed now to guarantee survival, or bootstrap through the danger zone hoping to reach the point where cheaper acceleration becomes available. The right answer depends entirely on how confident you are in clearing the danger zone alone.

#### Worked example: seed versus acceleration on the same firm

Compare two paths for Meridian. Path one: take the **\$75 million seed at 20% forever plus a 15% GP stake** at launch. Path two: bootstrap to \$15 million, survive three years to a \$60 million track record, then take **\$100 million of acceleration capital at an 8% revenue share that expires after six years, no GP stake**. Path two gives up dramatically less — 8% versus 20%, time-boxed versus permanent, no equity dilution — and ends with the founder owning 100% of a larger firm. But path two requires surviving three years of cash-burn below break-even on \$15 million, with a real chance of shutting down before acceleration is ever on the table. Path one guarantees survival at a permanent price; path two offers a far better outcome *conditional on surviving* the gauntlet that path one was designed to skip.

*Acceleration is the prize for surviving the danger zone unseeded — cheaper, often temporary, and equity-preserving — but it is only available to founders who lived long enough to qualify for it.*

## The named seeders

Seeding is a specialist business, and a handful of firms have done it at scale for years. Knowing the players helps because they have different styles — some take pure revenue shares, some specialize in GP stakes, some bundle in operational infrastructure. These are the names a founder will encounter, drawn from the public record as of 2026; treat the specifics as illustrative of *types*, since deal terms are private.

**Blackstone** runs one of the largest seeding and strategic-capital operations through its hedge-fund solutions arm (historically Blackstone Alternative Asset Management, BAAM) and its Strategic Capital vehicles, which take stakes in established managers. Blackstone's scale means it can write very large cheques and offer a manager instant institutional credibility, at the price of dealing with a giant counterparty.

**Goldman Sachs Petershill** specializes in **GP stakes** — buying equity ownership in established alternative-asset managers rather than seeding launches. Petershill is the archetype of the firm that wants a slice of the *management company* itself, with the governance and exit economics that ownership implies.

**Investcorp**, **Reservoir Capital**, **Capital Z Partners**, and **PAAMCO** (Pacific Alternative Asset Management Company, now part of a larger group) have all run dedicated seeding programmes over the years, providing day-one or early-stage capital in exchange for revenue shares and sometimes equity. Each has backed multiple launches and built portfolios on the call-option logic described earlier.

The pattern across all of them is the same: they are *not* charities and they are *not* ordinary investors. They are professional buyers of manager upside, and they price accordingly. A founder negotiating with any of them is negotiating with a counterparty who has seen hundreds of these deals, knows the distribution of outcomes cold, and structures each term to capture the upside while bounding the downside. The founder, doing this once, is at an information disadvantage — which is the single best argument for hiring a lawyer who has papered seed deals before and for talking to other seeded managers about what they actually gave up.

The styles do differ in ways that matter to a founder choosing among term sheets. A large platform like Blackstone can write a \$100 million-plus cheque and lend its name as instant institutional credibility, but you are one small line in an enormous book and may get little hands-on attention. A pure GP-stakes buyer like Petershill cares most about the equity and the long-run enterprise value, so its terms lean toward ownership and exit economics rather than a heavy near-term revenue share. A dedicated seeding boutique may write a smaller \$30 million cheque but stay close, opening doors and helping with operations in a way a megaplatform will not. There is no universally best seeder; there is only the best fit for a given founder's gap. A manager who needs credibility above all should weight the platform's name; a manager who needs operational help should weight the boutique's attention; a manager confident of compounding should weight whoever offers the shortest revenue-share duration and the cleanest buy-back. The questions to ask every seeder are the same: what exactly do you bring beyond the cheque, which of your past managers can I call as references, how do your economics behave if I grow versus if I plateau, and what does it take for me to buy you out.

It is also worth naming what a seed is *not*, so the founder knows the alternatives on the menu. A seed is not a bank loan — there is no interest and no repayment schedule, just a permanent or long-dated claim on revenue. It is not a normal anchor LP, who takes only the fund return. And it is not the only way to launch large: some managers reach scale through a single big managed account from one institution, or through a strategic relationship where an allocator funds a dedicated vehicle in exchange for fee breaks but no equity. Those routes give up less of the business but are harder to land and offer less operational support. The seed sits in the middle of this menu — more give-up than a managed account, more support and certainty than going it alone.

## Negotiating the deal

Because a seed deal is a bundle of six terms, the founder's leverage lies in trading them against one another rather than fighting any single number to the death. A few principles govern the negotiation.

**Cap the duration of the revenue share.** The single most valuable thing a founder can win is a *sunset* on the revenue share — a clause that the share expires after, say, eight or ten years, or steps down over time. A seeder will resist this hard, because the perpetual tail is where the option value lives. But even a long-dated sunset transforms the NPV of the deal: a 20% share for ten years is worth a fraction of a 20% share forever. If you can trade a longer lock-up or a higher early share for a hard expiry, take it.

**Trade the lock-up for the share.** Seeders value sticky capital. A founder who offers a four-year lock-up instead of two has given the seeder something it genuinely wants — and can ask for a lower revenue share or a smaller GP stake in return. The lock-up costs the founder little (the founder *wants* the capital to stay) and buys real concessions.

**Resist the GP stake harder than the revenue share.** A revenue share is a claim on cash flow; a GP stake is a claim on the eventual sale of the business and often on governance. If the seeder insists on a GP stake, negotiate it down in size, strip out any governance or veto rights, and pin down the buy-back and exit terms precisely — because that stake will be sitting on your cap table the day you try to sell your firm, as the dilution example showed.

**Pin down MFN scope.** MFN is standard, but its *scope* is negotiable. Does it apply only to fee terms, or to all terms? Does a small friends-and-family allocation at a discount trigger it? An overbroad MFN can handcuff you in every future negotiation. Narrow it to genuine fee terms with arm's-length investors.

**Get a buy-back right.** The best seed deals include a founder's right to *buy back* the seeder's economics at a pre-agreed price or formula after some period. This converts a permanent obligation into a callable one: if you compound and the revenue share becomes painful, you can pay to be free of it. A buy-back right is the founder's equivalent of the seeder's option — and most founders forget to ask for it.

**Negotiate from competing term sheets.** Nothing improves terms like a second seeder at the table. The single biggest determinant of how good a deal you get is how much the seeders want *you* — which is a function of your track record, the scarcity of your strategy, and whether more than one of them is bidding. A founder with two term sheets gets a materially better deal than a founder with one, because the call-option buyers are now competing for the same option.

#### Worked example: trading lock-up for revenue share

Maya's opening term sheet is **20% revenue share, three-year lock-up, 15% GP stake, no sunset, no buy-back**. She counters: she will accept a **four-year lock-up** (which she is happy to give — she wants the capital to stay) and a slightly larger **\$85 million** cheque, in exchange for an **18% revenue share that sunsets after ten years**, a **GP stake reduced to 10%** with no governance rights, and a **buy-back right** at 8 times the then-current revenue share after year five. The seeder gets stickier capital and deploys more money; Maya caps the tail, halves the dilution, and gives herself an exit from the obligation. The headline cheque went *up* and the long-run give-up went *down* — because she traded the terms the seeder values (lock-up, deployed capital) for the terms she values (sunset, buy-back, less equity).

*The founder who treats a seed as a single number loses; the founder who treats it as a bundle of tradable terms can often win a materially better deal without the seeder feeling worse off, because the two sides value the terms differently.*

## When a seed deal is worth it

Strip away the detail and the decision reduces to one honest question: **can you reach the start line without it?** Figure 3 lays out the four funding routes and what each costs.

If your warm money plus your own capital gets you over the **~\$80 million break-even** with enough cushion to survive a bad first year, you almost certainly should *not* take a seed, because you would be selling a permanent slice of your firm to solve a problem you do not have. ![A stacked diagram showing the four rights a seeder receives — a revenue share, a GP equity stake, a capacity right, and most-favored-nation fee terms](/imgs/blogs/seed-and-acceleration-capital-4.png)

If, on the other hand, your honest assessment is that without day-one anchor capital you are one drawdown from shutting down — that you cannot reach viability and you cannot become fundable, because allocators will not look at a sub-scale fund with no locked-up capital — then the seed's benefit is not "a bit of extra AUM." It is *existing at all*. And the present value of existing is, for practical purposes, enormous, which means almost any reasonable seed price is worth paying.

The break-even math makes this concrete. Figure 5 shows the two trajectories: a seeded \$75 million launch sits *above* the break-even line from day one, drawing a salary and compounding from a position of strength, while a bootstrapped \$15 million launch spends years *below* the line, burning cash and praying performance drags it up before the runway ends.

#### Worked example: seeded \$75M versus bootstrapped \$15M to break-even

The bootstrapped Meridian launches at **\$15 million**. To reach the **\$80 million** break-even purely through performance at, say, 10% net per year plus modest inflows, it takes roughly **five years** — five years during which the \$225,000 management fee (at 1.5% on \$15 million) covers a fraction of the \$1.2 million fixed cost, and Maya funds the gap out of her savings or a tiny salary. The seeded Meridian launches at **\$90 million** (\$75 million seed plus \$15 million warm), clears break-even on day one, pays Maya a real salary from month one, and — because allocators see locked-up anchor capital — raises faster from there. The seed does not just add \$75 million; it eliminates the five-year cash-burn gauntlet and the existential risk of dying inside it. *For a founder who would not survive the gauntlet, the seed is not expensive — it is the only door that leads anywhere.*

The clean before-and-after is in Figure 6: the same founder, the same strategy, two launches — one small and fully owned, one large and partly sold.

![A before-and-after comparison of a bootstrapped fifteen million dollar launch that is fully owned but burns cash against a seeded seventy-five million dollar launch that is partly sold but viable from day one](/imgs/blogs/seed-and-acceleration-capital-6.png)

The decision rule, then, is not "is a seed good or bad" — it is "is my unseeded survival probability high or low." A founder with a scarce strategy, a strong track record, and \$40 million of soft-circled warm money should fight to bootstrap and keep the whole firm. A founder with a good-but-not-scarce strategy and \$10 million of warm money, facing a market where allocators will not look below \$100 million, should take the seed and be grateful it was offered. The terms matter, but the threshold question is binary: do you need the start line bought for you?

Timing sharpens the rule further. The cost of a seed, per dollar of give-up, is highest at launch and falls every year you survive — because each surviving year retires some of the risk the seeder was pricing. A founder who can scrape together enough warm money to launch at \$20 million and survive even eighteen months has bought themselves into a different market: one where acceleration capital, managed accounts, and ordinary institutional tickets become available on far better terms than any day-zero seed. So the question is not only "can I reach viability alone" but "can I reach the *next financing milestone* alone" — survive long enough, with a clean track record and clean operations, and the expensive day-one seed is replaced by cheaper, later money that takes no equity. The founders who win this game are usually the ones who found a way to delay the financing decision by even a year, because a year of survival is the cheapest credibility there is. The trap is delaying so long that you run out of runway and die six months before the cheaper money would have arrived — which is exactly the failure mode a seed is designed to eliminate, and exactly why the decision is so hard.

## Common misconceptions

Smart markets people get these wrong precisely because they have never sold a piece of a company. Naming them is half the cure.

**"A seed is just a big investor."** No. A normal limited partner gives you money and takes the fund's return on it. A seeder gives you money *and takes a slice of your management company* — your revenue, sometimes your equity — on top of the fund return. Treating a seeder like a large LP is the single most expensive misunderstanding a founder can have, because it leads you to undervalue what you are giving away.

**"The revenue share ends when the lock-up ends."** No. The lock-up governs only the seeder's *capital* — when they can redeem their money from the fund. The revenue share and GP stake run on their own terms, which are usually permanent unless you negotiated a sunset. A founder who thinks "I just have to get through three years" is confusing the lock-up with the economics. The economics outlive the lock-up by decades.

**"20% sounds survivable — I keep 80%."** Only if you confuse revenue with profit. The 20% is taken off your *gross* revenue, before costs. In a year where your revenue barely exceeds your fixed costs, giving up 20% of the top line can be giving up most or all of your profit. The revenue share is senior to your paycheck, and in lean years it is the seeder who gets paid first.

**"A seed proves I have arrived."** A seed proves a *seeder* thinks you might compound into a business worth a slice of. That is genuine validation, but it is also the seeder buying a cheap option on your upside. The seeder's willingness to back you is a signal, not a coronation — and the price they extract reflects exactly how risky they think you are.

**"I can always buy the seeder out later."** Only if you negotiated a buy-back right, and most founders do not. Without a contractual buy-back at a pre-agreed price, you cannot force the seeder to sell its revenue share or GP stake back to you; you are stuck with it, and the seeder will name a price that captures the very upside you created. Ask for the buy-back *before* you sign, when you have leverage, not after, when you do not.

**"Seeding and GP-stakes are the same thing."** Related but distinct. Seeding is *day-one or early-stage* capital for a *launch*, priced for binary survival risk. A GP-stakes purchase (the Petershill model) is buying equity in an *established* manager that already has scale and a track record. The earlier you sell, the more you give up per dollar — which is why a seed costs more than a GP-stake sale on the same firm five years later.

## How it plays out in the real world

In practice, the seeding market is a small, repeat-player game where reputation and information asymmetry dominate. The named seeders — Blackstone, Petershill, Investcorp, Reservoir, Capital Z, PAAMCO and a handful of others — see most of the credible launches in any given year, compare them against a long internal history of outcomes, and price each deal against a known distribution. The founder, doing this once or twice in a career, is negotiating against a counterparty with hundreds of comparable deals in its memory. That asymmetry is the defining feature of the market, and it is why founders who talk to several previously-seeded managers before signing get materially better terms than those who do not.

The most common real-world regret is not "I shouldn't have taken a seed" — for many funds, the seed was the only path to existing. The most common regret is **"I gave up a permanent revenue share when I could have negotiated a sunset or a buy-back."** Founders who compound into real success discover that the perpetual 20% they signed away at launch, when revenue was tiny and the share felt harmless, becomes the most expensive line item in their business once they are managing a billion dollars. The successful founder pays the most for the seed, in absolute dollars, precisely because success is what makes the permanent share so valuable to the seeder. Figure 7 shows the typical negotiating band on the headline terms.

![A range chart showing typical seed-deal terms as bands — cheque size from twenty-five to one hundred fifty million dollars, revenue share from fifteen to twenty-five percent, and lock-up from two to four years](/imgs/blogs/seed-and-acceleration-capital-7.png)

The opposite regret exists too, though it is quieter because the founders who feel it are out of business. These are the managers who *refused* a seed out of pride or an overestimate of their own fundraising ability, bootstrapped at \$15 million, burned through their savings for two years below break-even, and shut down six months before the inflows that would have saved them. For them, the lesson is the mirror image: the seed they declined was the door they needed, and the permanent revenue share they were so reluctant to grant would have been a cheap price for staying alive. There is no public roll-call of these failures — funds that die small die quietly — but every seeder has a stack of term sheets from managers who said no and did not make it.

The honest synthesis is that a seed deal is neither a triumph nor a trap; it is a *financing instrument with a specific shape*, cheap when you are small and expensive when you are large, that is worth precisely as much as your inability to reach the start line any other way. The founder's job is to size that inability honestly — to resist both the pride that refuses a needed seed and the fear that grabs an unneeded one — and then, if a seed is the right call, to negotiate the bundle so the permanent tail is capped, the equity dilution is minimized, and the buy-back door is left open. Get that right, and the seed becomes what it should be: the price of the start line, paid once, by a founder who knew exactly what it cost.

## When this matters / Further reading

Seed and acceleration capital matters the moment your warm money runs short of the size you need to be viable and fundable — which, for most first-time managers in a market where allocators will not look below roughly \$100 million, is most of the time. The decision turns on one honest question: can you reach the start line without selling a slice of your firm? If yes, keep the whole company. If no, a seed is the door, and your job is to walk through it on the best terms you can negotiate — a capped revenue share, minimal equity dilution, a long lock-up traded for concessions, and a buy-back right held in reserve.
