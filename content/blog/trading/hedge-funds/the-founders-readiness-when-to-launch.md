> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Leaving a paying seat to launch a fund is a personal-finance and timing bet as much as a markets one: you trade a sure income for a 12–24 month J-curve before the business pays you anything.
>
> - The opportunity cost is the comp you give up *plus* the deferred comp and clawbacks you leave on the table — for a senior PM that can be 2–3 million USD before the fund returns a dollar.
> - Your personal runway is savings divided by monthly burn. Aim for **24 months unpaid**, never plan to pay yourself a salary in year one, and cut your burn before you cut the cord.
> - A co-founder who owns the business and operations halves your key-person risk and frees you to run the book — the single highest-leverage de-risking decision a solo trader can make.
> - The non-compete and garden leave are not dead time; they are the clock that sets your entire launch timeline, and they decide which part of your track record actually comes with you.
> - **The one number to remember: on a 50 million USD launch, the management company does not pay you back until about month 18.**

Maya has the offer math memorized. She runs a long/short-equity book inside a multi-strategy fund, and last year her total compensation — base, bonus, and the deferred slug that vests over three years — came to a little over **\$1,000,000**. Her seat is good. Her risk limits are generous, the platform pays for her data and her two analysts, and when she has a bad quarter the firm absorbs the fixed costs while she licks her wounds. On paper she has already won.

And yet, the week before this conversation, she sat in her kitchen at 11 p.m. with a legal-pad budget and could not sleep. She wants to launch her own fund — the Meridian Fund, named for nothing in particular, just a word she liked. The arithmetic she keeps redoing is brutal: to do it, she walks away from roughly **\$2,000,000** of near-certain comp over two years, forfeits an unvested deferred slug worth several hundred thousand more, signs a lease on her own infrastructure, and pays *herself* nothing for at least a year while she pays everyone else. The markets question — *does she have an edge?* — she thinks she has answered. The question keeping her awake is different and harder: *can she afford to be right slowly?*

That is the real founder's-readiness question, and almost nobody frames it correctly. The pitch decks, the prime-brokerage capital-introduction teams, and the "fund-in-a-box" vendors all talk about the upside — the management-company equity you build, the 2-and-20 economics, the freedom. They are selling you something, and they get paid when you launch. What they skip is the part Maya was doing on the legal pad: launching is a **personal-finance and timing decision** layered on top of the markets decision, and you can have a real edge and still not be ready, because readiness is about whether you can survive the gap between leaving and getting paid. This post is about that gap — and Figure 1 is the mental model for the whole thing.

## Foundations: what readiness means

Most people collapse "am I ready to launch a fund?" into one question — *am I good enough at trading?* — and that is the one question that, while necessary, is the least likely to be your binding constraint. Plenty of genuinely skilled investors launch and die; plenty of merely good ones survive and compound. The difference is almost never the alpha. It is whether the founder was *ready* across three separate dimensions, and readiness in each is a distinct test.

**Financial readiness** is whether your *personal* balance sheet can survive the launch. Not the fund's balance sheet — yours, the household one. Can you go 18 to 24 months without drawing a salary? Do you have a cash buffer that covers your mortgage, your kids' school, your health insurance, and your own anxiety, with margin to spare? This is the readiness dimension that markets people most consistently underrate, because they are used to being paid steadily and have never run their household as a startup that loses money for two years.

**Professional readiness** is whether your edge and your reputation actually travel. Do you have a track record you can *show* — clean, attributable, and not legally chained to your current employer? Will an allocator look at your numbers and believe they were *you* rather than the platform's risk system, the firm's research stack, or a teammate's signal? Does your edge survive contact with the honest test — is it real, repeatable, and yours? (That last question is its own essay; see [do you have an edge — the honest test](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/do-you-have-an-edge-the-honest-test).)

**Structural readiness** is whether the *business* can be stood up around you. Do you have, or can you quickly assemble, the service-provider stack — administrator, auditor, prime broker, counsel, compliance — and the human partners to run the parts of the business that are not trading? Crucially, does the legal architecture of your *exit* — the non-compete, the garden leave, the intellectual-property terms in your employment contract — permit you to launch at all, and on what timeline?

You are ready when all three columns clear. A founder who is financially set and structurally prepared but whose "edge" was really the platform's is not ready. A brilliant investor with a clean, portable track record who needs a paycheck next month is not ready either. The honesty mandate of this whole series applies here first: **most launches fail, and the most common cause is not a bad trade — it is a founder who ran out of personal runway, money, or patience before the business turned the corner.** Before we get to structure and teams, we have to talk about the two pieces of math that decide whether you can even afford to begin: the opportunity cost of leaving, and the runway you need to survive.

A note on a concept that runs through this entire post: the **J-curve**. In a fund-launch context, the J-curve is the shape your personal and business cash flow traces over the first two years. It starts negative — you have left your comp behind, the fund is small, the fixed costs are real, and you are paying yourself nothing — dips to a trough somewhere in the first year, and only climbs back above water once the assets under management (**AUM**, the total capital the fund manages) and therefore the fee revenue have grown enough to cover the costs and finally pay you. The whole readiness question is: *can you survive the bottom of the J?* Everything that follows is a way of measuring how deep your particular J goes and how long you sit at the bottom.

## The opportunity cost

Start with what you give up, because it is the largest and most underweighted number in the entire decision. Opportunity cost is not the money you spend launching — it is the money you *don't earn* by leaving. For a senior markets professional that figure is enormous, certain, and recurring, and it stops the moment you resign.

Maya's seat pays a little over **\$1,000,000** a year, all-in. If she stays, that is roughly **\$2,000,000** of compensation she would collect over the two years it takes a new fund to find its feet — and it is *near-certain* money. She does not have to raise it, she does not have to perform spectacularly to earn it, and the firm bears the fixed costs of her operation. Against that, the founder's cash flow in the early years is not just smaller; for a while it is *negative*. She is not earning \$1,000,000; she is *spending* her own savings to keep the lights on while she earns nothing.

This is the trade that Figure 2 draws. The blue line is the cumulative comp Maya gives up by leaving — a steady climb to \$2M over two years and \$3M over three. The green line is her cumulative take-home from the management company she founds. It starts negative, sinks into a trough in year one when she is paying herself zero against real fixed costs, claws back toward break-even in year two as AUM grows, and only in year three — if she has a good performance year that crystallizes an incentive fee — does the green line cross above where staying would have left her. That crossing point, somewhere around year three on a clean launch, is the founder's J-curve in its starkest personal form.

![Line chart comparing cumulative pay from staying in the seat against the founder's cumulative cash flow over three years, with the founder line crossing above only in year three](/imgs/blogs/the-founders-readiness-when-to-launch-2.png)

There is a second, sneakier piece of opportunity cost: the **deferred compensation** you forfeit by leaving. Most senior seats at established funds pay a meaningful slice of the bonus as deferred comp — equity, fund interests, or a cash bonus that vests over three to five years. Leave before it vests and, depending on the contract, you walk away from it, or worse, trigger a **clawback** that lets the firm reclaim comp already paid if you join a competitor. Maya has roughly **\$600,000** of unvested deferred comp that disappears the day she resigns. That is not a small rounding error on the decision; it is more than a year of many people's gross salary, gone, and it should be added to the opportunity-cost side of the ledger.

#### Worked example: the opportunity cost of leaving the seat

Maya is weighing two years away from her seat to launch the Meridian Fund. The comp she foregoes:

- Year-one comp not earned: **\$1,000,000**
- Year-two comp not earned: **\$1,000,000**
- Unvested deferred comp forfeited on resignation: **\$600,000**
- **Total sure money given up over two years: \$2,600,000**

Against that, what does she *get* by leaving? Not cash — for two years she gets negative cash. What she gets is **equity in her own management company**: a business that, if the Meridian Fund reaches \$120M of AUM in three years at a founders-class 1.5% management fee, throws off **\$1,800,000 a year of management-fee revenue** before any performance fee, of which she owns 100% (minus costs). The question is not "is \$2,600,000 a lot?" — it obviously is. The question is whether the *capitalized value* of owning the management company exceeds \$2,600,000 plus the risk that the fund never reaches scale. If she believes she can build a durable \$300M fund, the management-company equity alone is worth many multiples of the comp she gave up; if she suspects she will plateau at \$40M, she has paid \$2,600,000 to buy herself a worse-paid, more stressful job.

*The opportunity cost is not the cash you spend launching — it is the certain millions you stop earning, and you are betting the equity value of your own firm will eventually exceed it.*

It is worth sitting with the *shape* of that bet, because it is unusual. When Maya stays in her seat, her payoff distribution is narrow: she earns roughly \$1M a year with low variance, the firm absorbs her fixed costs, and her downside in a bad year is a smaller bonus, not a negative number. When she launches, she swaps that narrow distribution for a violently skewed one. The most likely outcome — statistically, given that most launches stay small and a large fraction don't survive five years — is that she earns *less* than she would have in the seat, possibly much less, and possibly negative for the first two years. But the *right tail* is enormous: if the Meridian Fund compounds to \$300M or \$500M, she owns a management company throwing off \$4.5M–\$7.5M a year of fee revenue plus performance fees, and that equity is worth a multiple of its annual cash flow if she ever sells a stake or takes a GP-stakes deal. She is trading a steady salary for a lottery ticket with a real, skill-driven probability of paying off — which is a perfectly rational trade *if and only if* she can survive the long left side of the distribution without being wiped out. Readiness, in this framing, is simply the question of whether she has the runway to hold the option long enough for the right tail to have a chance to show up.

There is one more subtlety that markets people, of all people, should appreciate: the value of the option *decays* if you wait too long, but it also *appreciates* with every year you stay and build a cleaner, longer, more attributable track record. There is no universally right age or seniority to launch — only a personal crossover point where your track record is strong enough to raise capital, your edge is proven enough to believe in, and your savings are deep enough to survive the J-curve. Launch before that point and the runway isn't there; wait long past it and you may find a comfortable seat, golden handcuffs of ever-larger deferred comp, and a family situation make the leap progressively harder to justify. The honest readiness question includes this timing element: *is now my crossover, or am I either too early on the track record or too deep in the handcuffs?*

#### Worked example: the year-by-year cash-flow comparison

It helps to lay the two paths side by side, year by year, so the J-curve is not a vague worry but a set of numbers Maya can stare at on the legal pad. Assume she launches the Meridian Fund at \$50M, grows to ~\$85M by end of year two and ~\$120M by end of year three, runs at the founders-class 1.5% management fee, charges a 15% performance fee on gains above the high-water mark, and carries ~\$1.2M of annual fixed costs. She pays herself \$0 in year one, a token \$120,000 in year two, and a real salary in year three once the fund can carry it.

**Path A — stay in the seat (cumulative comp):**

- End of year 1: **\$1,000,000**
- End of year 2: **\$2,000,000**
- End of year 3: **\$3,000,000** — steady, certain, low-variance.

**Path B — found the Meridian Fund (cumulative take-home from the management company):**

- Year 1: management fee 1.5% × \$50M = \$750,000; fixed costs ~\$1.2M *including* the formation drag amortized and a first hire; performance fee \$0 (roughly flat first year). Management company runs at a small loss, Maya draws \$0. Cumulative personal take-home: **−\$450,000** (she is funding part of the shortfall from savings).
- Year 2: average AUM ~\$70M; management fee ~\$1,050,000; costs ~\$1.2M; assume a modest up year crystallizing a small performance fee of ~\$400,000. Management company nets roughly \$250,000, Maya draws \$120,000. Cumulative personal take-home: **about +\$100,000** — she has barely climbed back above where she started two years ago, while Path A is at \$2,000,000.
- Year 3: average AUM ~\$100M; management fee ~\$1,500,000; costs ~\$1.3M; a good performance year on \$120M crystallizes a ~\$3,000,000 performance fee. The management company nets several million, Maya takes a real salary plus profit distribution. Cumulative personal take-home: **about +\$3,400,000** — and only now does Path B pull ahead of the \$3,000,000 Path A had banked safely.

The table makes the brutal truth concrete: through the end of year two, Maya is roughly **\$1,900,000 behind** where she would have been by staying (\$100,000 versus \$2,000,000), and she has been living on savings the whole time. The founder path only wins in year three, and only *if* she has a genuinely good performance year — strip out the year-three performance fee and the green line never crosses the blue line at all. That is the bet in full: two years of being millions behind, funded by your own savings, wagered on the third year going right.

*The founder J-curve is not a metaphor; it is a year-by-year table where you spend two years far behind your old comp and only the third good year — if it comes — pulls you ahead.*

There is one more subtlety that markets people, of all people, should appreciate: the value of the option *decays* if you wait too long, but it also *appreciates* with every year you stay and build a cleaner, longer, more attributable track record. There is no universally right age or seniority to launch — only a personal crossover point where your track record is strong enough to raise capital, your edge is proven enough to believe in, and your savings are deep enough to survive the J-curve. Launch before that point and the runway isn't there; wait long past it and you may find a comfortable seat, golden handcuffs of ever-larger deferred comp, and a family situation make the leap progressively harder to justify. The honest readiness question includes this timing element: *is now my crossover, or am I either too early on the track record or too deep in the handcuffs?*

The honest framing is this: a paying seat is a bird in the hand worth roughly a million dollars a year. You are giving it up to chase an option whose payoff is real but uncertain and *delayed*. The delay is the whole problem, which is why the next number — your personal runway — is the one that actually decides whether you can afford to wait for the payoff. For a fuller treatment of how to value the comp, the deferred slug, and the clawbacks you are weighing, [quant compensation, demystified](/blog/trading/quant-careers/quant-compensation-demystified) walks through the structures piece by piece.

## The personal runway

Runway is the simplest and most important calculation in this post, and it is the one founders most often refuse to do honestly. Your personal runway is the number of months you can pay your household bills with no income from the fund. The formula is not complicated:

> **Personal runway (months) = liquid savings ÷ monthly personal burn.**

Liquid savings means cash and assets you can actually spend without selling your house or breaking a retirement account at a penalty. Monthly personal burn means everything your household spends to live — mortgage or rent, food, childcare and school fees, health insurance (which you now pay yourself, and which is shockingly expensive once you leave a corporate plan), transport, debt service, and the ordinary cost of a life you are not willing to dismantle entirely. If you carry a lifestyle calibrated to a \$1M income, your burn is high, and your runway is short even with substantial savings.

The reason runway is binding is that **you will not pay yourself a salary for a long time, and you should plan never to do so in year one.** This is the single most counterintuitive fact for a markets person who has always been an employee. As founder, you are the residual claimant: everyone else — the administrator, the auditor, the prime broker, your COO, your analyst — gets paid before you do, because their bills are contractual and yours is discretionary. The management fee on a small fund barely covers the fixed costs (we will do that math in a moment), and a performance fee pays nothing until the fund is both up *and* above its high-water mark. So your household income for the first year is, realistically, **zero from the fund** — it is whatever your savings can provide. Founders who plan to draw \$300,000 in year one are quietly planning to bleed the fund's working capital or to skip an institutional-grade expense (an independent administrator, a real auditor) that allocators will later veto them for skipping.

![Bar chart showing months of personal runway from a fixed savings buffer under three monthly-burn scenarios, with a 24-month target line and a 12-month danger line](/imgs/blogs/the-founders-readiness-when-to-launch-4.png)

Figure 4 shows why burn matters as much as savings. Maya has built a **\$600,000** liquid savings buffer earmarked for the launch. At her current household burn of **\$40,000 a month** — the life a \$1M income buys — that buffer lasts just **15 months**, below the danger line, and she would be forced to start drawing a salary right when the fund is most fragile. If she cuts to a deliberate launch budget of **\$25,000 a month** — keep the house, drop the second car and the help, freeze the lifestyle — the same \$600,000 stretches to **24 months**, comfortably past the J-curve trough. And if she went truly lean at **\$15,000 a month**, she would have **40 months**. The lesson is that *cutting your burn buys more runway than almost anything else you can do*, and it is entirely within your control, unlike fundraising or returns.

#### Worked example: Maya's personal runway

Maya runs the numbers before she resigns:

- Liquid savings earmarked for the launch: **\$600,000**
- Planned launch-period household burn: **\$25,000 / month** (cut from \$40,000)
- **Runway = \$600,000 ÷ \$25,000 = 24 months**

Now she stress-tests it. What if she has to fund a one-time \$50,000 health-insurance and emergency cushion up front? Spendable savings drop to \$550,000, and runway falls to **22 months** — still fine. What if the fund's working capital comes up short in month 14 and she has to inject \$100,000 of her own savings to cover an audit bill the management fee can't yet carry? Now her personal pool is \$450,000 against \$25,000/month, or **18 months** of runway — and that 18 months has to reach the point where the fund pays her. It just barely does, which is exactly why 24 months of *planned* runway is the right target: it absorbs the shocks that always come.

*Runway is savings divided by burn, and the cheapest way to extend it is not to earn more — it is to spend less, starting the day you decide to launch.*

Two pieces of the burn deserve special attention because they ambush founders who have only ever been employees. The first is the **benefits cliff**: when you leave a large firm, you lose the employer-subsidized health insurance, the retirement match, the life and disability cover, and often a slate of perks you never priced because they came out of payroll invisibly. Replacing comprehensive health coverage for a family on the open market can run \$2,000–\$3,000 a month, and it is now a line in *your* household budget. The second is **taxes and timing**: as an employee your taxes were withheld; as a founder drawing nothing, you have no withholding, but the moment the management company does pay you — often as a lump at year-end when a performance fee crystallizes — you owe estimated taxes, and that lump is smaller after tax than it looks. Build both into the burn so the runway number is honest rather than optimistic.

The third piece of runway is the one no spreadsheet captures: the **psychological cost** of unpaid time. There is a specific kind of stress in watching your savings drain month after month while you pour your days into a business that pays you nothing and might not work. It is corrosive to the very judgment you need to run risk well. A founder who is privately terrified about the mortgage makes worse trading decisions — sizes up to "catch up," holds losers hoping to avoid crystallizing a bad month, takes the redemption call personally. The 24-month runway is not only a financial buffer; it is a *psychological* one, the margin that lets you trade your edge rather than your fear. Founders who launch on 6 months of runway are not just financially fragile; they are about to make their worst decisions at the moment their decisions matter most.

The readiness rule that falls out of this: **do not launch until you can survive 24 months unpaid at a burn you have already cut.** If your runway is 8 months, you are not financially ready, no matter how good your edge is — you will be forced into a desperate raise or a salary draw at the worst possible moment, and desperation is visible to allocators and corrosive to decision-making. Building runway is boring, slow, and entirely unglamorous, and it is the most important pre-launch work most founders skip. The discipline mirrors the one that keeps a trader from blowing up — never bet more than you can survive losing — applied to your own household balance sheet rather than the book; the same risk-of-ruin logic that governs position sizing governs the size of the personal bet you are making by launching at all.

## Solo vs co-founder

Once the personal math says you *can* launch, the next decision shapes everything: do you do it alone, or with a partner? This is not a question of ego or generosity. It is a question of risk, and specifically of **key-person risk** — the danger that the entire enterprise depends on one person who can get sick, get distracted, burn out, or simply be unable to be in two places at once. A solo founder *is* the key-person risk, in its purest form.

Think about what running a fund actually requires, and notice how little of it is trading. You have to run the book — the part you know. You also have to raise capital and manage investor relationships (**IR**), which is a full-time relationship business with a sales cadence, a CRM, and quarterly letters. You have to stand up and run operations: trade settlement, NAV oversight, cash controls, vendor management, the daily reconciliation that keeps the administrator honest. You have to own compliance and the regulatory calendar. And you have to do all of this while *also* watching the market in real time and not losing money. A solo founder wears every one of these hats, which means that on the days the market is hardest — the days that decide your year — you are also the person fielding a redemption call, signing a vendor contract, and answering an allocator's operational due-diligence questionnaire. That is not a recipe for good decisions.

Figure 3 contrasts the two structures. On the left, the solo founder owns the book, the raise, and the operations, and the firm's key-person risk is total: if Maya is out for a month, the Meridian Fund effectively stops. On the right, a co-founder split assigns trading to the PM, hands the business-building and IR to a partner, and brings in a hired COO for operations — and now the risk is spread across people, no single illness or distraction halts the firm, and each person is doing the job they are actually good at.

![Before and after comparison showing a solo founder wearing every hat versus a co-founder split that distributes trading, business, and operations across people](/imgs/blogs/the-founders-readiness-when-to-launch-3.png)

The case for a co-founder is overwhelmingly about **de-risking**, and allocators see it that way too. A two-person founding team where one runs the money and the other runs the business reads, to an operational-due-diligence analyst, as a more durable firm: there is segregation of duties from day one, the business will not collapse if the PM has a bad month, and someone is unambiguously accountable for the parts of the firm that are not alpha. A common and effective split is **PM / business**: one founder owns investing and risk, the other owns capital-raising, investor relations, and the commercial side, with a hired COO sitting under both to own day-to-day operations and compliance. This is the structure many successful launches use, and it maps onto the deeper choice covered in [from senior to PM or starting your own](/blog/trading/quant-careers/from-senior-to-pm-or-starting-your-own).

The cost of a co-founder is **equity** — you are giving away a slice of the management company, the most valuable thing you will ever own. How much is the negotiation. A non-investing business co-founder who joins at formation, brings relationships, and works for nothing for two years has taken real founder risk and deserves real founder equity — often 20% to 40% of the management company, sometimes split differently between management-fee and performance-fee economics. The math is the same as any partnership: you are trading a smaller slice of a more-likely-to-succeed pie for a larger slice of a riskier one. If the co-founder genuinely doubles your odds of survival and lets you focus entirely on the book, even a 30% give-up can be the highest-expected-value decision you make.

The *mechanics* of the split are worth getting right at formation, because they are nearly impossible to renegotiate later. Three things have to be decided and documented before a dollar is raised. First, the **equity percentage** in the management company — the entity that collects the fees and that you would one day sell a stake in. Second, **vesting**: founder equity that vests over three or four years protects everyone, because a co-founder who walks in year one should not keep the full stake they would have earned over a decade; without vesting, an early breakup leaves a departed partner owning a permanent slice of your firm's economics, which is both unfair and a fatal red flag to future allocators. Third, the **economic split between management-fee and performance-fee revenue**, which can differ from the headline equity number: a business co-founder might take 30% of management-fee economics (the stable revenue that funds the operation) but only 15% of performance-fee economics (the reward for the alpha that is, after all, mostly the PM's). And alongside the economics, the **governance**: who has the final call on a risk decision, who can hire and fire, what happens if the partners disagree, and a buy-sell mechanism for the worst case where one founder wants out. Founders who skip the prenuptial agreement because the relationship feels strong on day one are the ones who litigate it on day five hundred.

A concrete frame: suppose Maya gives a business co-founder **25% of the management company**, vesting over four years. In the \$120M, year-three scenario where the management company nets \$1M after costs and her salary, the co-founder's slice is \$250,000 of annual cash flow — real money. But the relevant comparison is not "Maya kept 75% instead of 100%"; it is "Maya kept 75% of a fund that *survived and reached \$120M* versus 100% of a fund that, run solo, had a meaningfully higher chance of dying in the J-curve or never getting past the institutional-viability threshold because she was too busy doing operations to raise capital." A 75% stake in a living firm beats 100% of a dead one every time, which is why the equity give-up is best understood as buying down the probability of failure rather than as pure dilution.

The honest caveat: a *bad* co-founder is worse than no co-founder. A misaligned partner — different risk appetite, different time horizon, different definition of success — is a slow-motion blow-up that is very hard to unwind once equity has been granted and capital has been raised. The de-risking only works if the partnership is genuinely complementary and the values genuinely aligned. Choose a co-founder the way you would choose a marriage, because the divorce is roughly as expensive.

## The non-compete, garden leave, and your IP

Here is where the personal-finance decision collides with the legal one, and where many otherwise-ready founders discover their timeline is not theirs to set. Buried in your employment contract are three clauses that will shape your launch more than any pitch deck: the **non-compete**, the **garden-leave** provision, and the **intellectual-property** assignment. Read them before you do anything else, ideally with an employment lawyer, because they answer the question of *when* you can launch and *what you can take with you* — and the answers are often unwelcome.

A **non-compete** is a clause that bars you, for a defined period after you leave, from competing with your employer — typically by launching or joining a rival fund, sometimes by soliciting the firm's investors or employees. Their enforceability varies enormously by jurisdiction and is in flux; some places sharply limit them, others enforce reasonable ones routinely, and the financial industry has historically used them aggressively. The practical reality for a senior PM is that you should assume yours is real and plan around it.

What does a typical one look like in practice? For a senior portfolio manager at an established fund, the restrictive-covenant package usually bundles several clauses that operate together. There is the **non-compete** proper — often six months to a year, occasionally longer for the most senior people — barring you from running a competing strategy. There is a **non-solicitation of investors**, frequently *longer* than the non-compete (one to two years), barring you from approaching the firm's LPs; this one matters enormously because it can prevent you from raising from the exact allocators who know your work best. There is a **non-solicitation of employees**, barring you from poaching your old team for a year or two — which constrains who you can bring with you. And there is a **confidentiality** clause covering the firm's positions, models, and investor list essentially forever. A first-time founder reads "non-compete" and thinks about the trading restriction; the experienced lawyer reads the whole package and sees that the investor non-solicit and the team non-solicit often bind harder than the trading ban, because they shape *who funds you* and *who you build with*.

The practical planning move is to get the contract in front of an employment lawyer *before* you give any notice, map every clause's duration and start date, and then negotiate what you can. Surprisingly often there is room: firms will sometimes shorten a garden leave, waive a non-solicit for a specific allocator who was *your* relationship before you joined, or clarify an ambiguous IP definition — but only if you ask before you have burned the bridge, and only if you have leverage (a clean reputation, a non-acrimonious departure, a firm that would rather not litigate). Negotiating the exit is part of the launch, not a formality to rush through on your last day.

**Garden leave** is a related and, for funds, often more binding mechanism: a period — commonly three to six months, sometimes a year — during which you have resigned but are still technically employed and *paid*, kept away from the desk, the data, and the firm's positions, and contractually prohibited from starting your new venture in earnest. You are, literally, sitting in the garden. It is the firm's way of letting your information go stale before you can use it against them. Garden leave feels like punishment, and founders treat it as dead time. It is not. It is *paid time during which you cannot trade but absolutely can do the unglamorous formation work* — and reframing it that way is one of the most useful mindset shifts in this whole post.

The **IP and track-record** question is the one that determines whether your edge actually travels. Your employment contract almost certainly assigns to the firm any models, code, signals, and research you developed while employed — and possibly a broad definition of "developed." That means the systematic signal you built, the backtest infrastructure, the data pipelines: those very likely stay behind. What comes with you is *you* — your skill, your judgment, your process, and, critically, your **track record**, but only in the attributable, defensible form your contract and the regulators permit. Showing performance you cannot substantiate, or that legally belongs to the platform, is both a compliance problem under the marketing rules and a credibility problem the first time an allocator's lawyer asks for the source data. The clean version of "my track record comes with me" is narrower than founders hope, and getting it right is non-negotiable.

It is worth being precise about what you *can* and *cannot* take, because founders routinely get this wrong and the cost of getting it wrong is a lawsuit or a failed diligence. You **cannot** take: the firm's source code, even code you wrote (it is work-for-hire and belongs to the employer); the firm's proprietary data sets, vendor contracts, or data pipelines; the firm's investor list or any document marked confidential; and frequently not even your own research notes if they live on the firm's systems. You **can** take: your skill and your process — the way you think about markets, which no contract can assign away; your professional reputation and relationships, subject to the non-solicit limits; and, critically, your **performance track record**, but only the slice that is genuinely attributable to *your* decisions and that you can *substantiate* with records you are permitted to keep. The right move during your remaining time at the firm is to work with your future compliance counsel on a clean, defensible record of your attributable performance — the kind that survives the SEC marketing rule's requirement to show net-of-fee numbers and that an allocator's operational-due-diligence team can verify. If your only "track record" is a number you remember and cannot prove, you do not have a track record; you have a claim, and claims fail diligence.

This is also why the IP problem *forces the launch timing*. If your edge was a systematic signal that belongs to the firm, you cannot simply rebuild it the day you leave — you have to build a *new*, clean, independently-derived strategy, and that takes time and must be demonstrably untainted by the firm's IP. A discretionary stock-picker carries more of their edge in their head and travels more cleanly; a systematic quant whose alpha lived in firm-owned code faces a much harder, slower rebuild and a much higher bar to prove independence. Knowing which kind of founder you are tells you how much of your garden-leave window must go to *rebuilding the edge* versus *building the business* — and for a systematic founder, that rebuild can be the longest pole in the whole launch.

Figure 5 ties this together as a timeline, because the legal clauses *are* the schedule. You resign; you enter garden leave for three to six months; during that paid, trading-free window you form the legal entities, line up service providers, and quietly do the parts of the raise that don't require you to be actively managing money; the non-compete clock expires; you complete your first close and launch. The non-compete and garden-leave terms don't just delay you — they *define* when each milestone can happen, which is why you cannot plan a launch date until you have read your contract.

#### Worked example: the cost of a six-month garden leave

Maya's contract specifies a **six-month garden leave** at full base salary (\$400,000/year, so \$200,000 over the six months), during which she cannot launch or solicit. Founders instinctively price this as a six-month delay and a half-million-dollar drag on the J-curve. The honest accounting is more nuanced:

- Six months of *paid* base salary during garden leave: **+\$200,000** (income, not cost)
- Six months of delayed launch, pushing the fund's break-even out by half a year: a real opportunity cost in deferred fee revenue, but the fund wasn't earning anything in those months anyway
- Six months of *unable to manage money*, so her track record goes stale — the gap allocators will ask about: the genuine cost, partly mitigated because she can keep a personal-account paper portfolio (within compliance) to bridge it
- Six months of *time she can use* for formation, vendor selection, and soft-circling LPs: **a hidden asset** that compresses the post-leave timeline

Net, the six-month garden leave costs Maya far less than the \$500,000+ founders assume, *if* she treats the paid window as launch-preparation time rather than a sentence. The real cost is the staleness of the track record and the delayed first close — not the calendar itself.

*Garden leave is paid downtime from the desk, not downtime from the business — the founders who use it to do formation work arrive at their launch date already half-built.*

#### Worked example: the all-in cost from resignation to first revenue

Now add up everything Maya spends — out of pocket and in opportunity cost — in the gap between resigning and the management company collecting its first management fee. This is the number that has to fit inside her runway, and it is larger than the launch-cost line items alone suggest:

- **One-time formation legal** (Delaware LP + Cayman master-feeder + the offering documents and the investment-management agreement): **\$50,000–\$150,000**. Call it **\$100,000** for the Meridian Fund.
- **Pre-launch service-provider setup and retainers** (administrator onboarding, audit engagement, compliance build, outside counsel beyond formation): **~\$50,000** before the fund is even live.
- **Pre-revenue operating burn** (a data subscription, an OMS, a first operations hire who has to start before launch, office and insurance) for the ~3–6 months of garden leave plus the ramp to first close: **~\$150,000–\$250,000**. Call it **\$200,000**.
- **Personal burn during the unpaid stretch** beyond the garden-leave salary — say six months of garden-leave pay covers part of it, but the months after garden leave and before the fund pays her are on her own dime: at \$25,000/month for the ~6 unpaid months between garden leave ending and the first fee, **~\$150,000** of personal savings consumed.
- **Opportunity cost of the whole window** — the comp she is not earning while all of this happens — folds back into the \$2.6M from the earlier example.

Add the cash items: roughly **\$100,000 + \$50,000 + \$200,000 = \$350,000** of *business* cash that has to exist before a single management fee arrives, plus **~\$150,000** of *personal* savings to live on. So before the Meridian Fund earns its first dollar, Maya needs on the order of **\$350,000 of working capital in the management company** and a personal runway that can absorb the unpaid months — and the management fee, when it finally arrives at \$62,500/month on \$50M, takes most of a year to pay back even the \$350,000 of setup.

*The gap between resignation and first revenue is not free: it is several hundred thousand dollars of business working capital plus personal savings, all spent before the fee meter starts running.*

The readiness rule here: **map your legal exit before you build your launch plan, and assume the contract sets the timeline.** A founder who tells allocators "I'm launching in Q3" without having read their own non-compete is setting a date they don't control. The disciplined version is to treat the garden-leave end date as the gating milestone and work backward from it — formation during the window, first close as the clock expires.

## The team you must have on day one

Even a solo founder cannot run a fund alone, because some jobs cannot be done by the person running the book, both for operational reasons and because allocators will not allow it. The principle that decides who you need on day one is **segregation of duties**: the person who decides the trades cannot also be the only person who moves the cash, calculates the NAV, and signs off on compliance. A founder who is the sole signatory on the fund's bank account *and* the only person who values the portfolio is, to an operational-due-diligence analyst, a Madoff-shaped risk regardless of how honest they are — because the controls that would catch fraud or error simply don't exist. (The infamous case is the canonical lesson: no independent administrator, no real auditor, self-custody. Madoff's returns were great; the operations were a fiction, and ODD existed to catch exactly that.)

So the day-one team — owned, hired, or outsourced — has to include the functions you cannot credibly do yourself:

- A **COO / CFO** (or a strong outsourced equivalent) who owns operations, vendor management, cash controls, and the firm's books. This is the role a co-founder often fills, or the first hire a solo founder makes. They are the reason the administrator's NAV gets independently checked and the reason a wire requires two sets of eyes. *Cost:* a full-time COO/CFO at a small fund runs roughly **\$150,000–\$300,000+** all-in, or a fraction of that for an outsourced arrangement at launch. *Consequence of skipping:* you become the sole signatory and the sole valuer — the segregation-of-duties failure that fails ODD outright, and the single most common reason a great-returns fund gets a hard "no" from institutional capital.
- **Compliance** — a Chief Compliance Officer, in-house or outsourced to a specialist firm, plus outside regulatory counsel. The CCO owns the regulatory calendar, the code of ethics, the marketing-rule reviews, and the relationship with the regulator. *Cost:* outsourced compliance plus counsel at launch is commonly **\$50,000–\$150,000/year**; a full-time CCO comes later. *Consequence of skipping:* you do not get to skip this because you are small — the rules apply at launch (Form ADV, the marketing rule, the code of ethics), and a missed filing or a non-compliant performance ad is the kind of unforced error that ends a young firm or triggers an examination it cannot survive.
- An **independent administrator** (third-party, not you) who calculates the official NAV, and an **independent auditor** who audits the fund annually. *Cost:* administrator **~\$30,000–\$100,000+/year**, audit **~\$30,000–\$60,000/year**. *Consequence of skipping:* a fund without an independent administrator and a real auditor is uninvestable for any serious allocator, full stop — this is the precise gap that defined Madoff, and ODD teams treat self-administration or a tiny unknown auditor as a fatal red flag no matter how clean you are.
- The **trade you cannot do yourself**: if your strategy needs an execution trader, a quant developer, or a risk officer that you cannot also be while running the book, that is a day-one hire, not a someday hire. *Cost:* a senior execution trader or quant developer is **\$150,000–\$400,000+** depending on the role and market. *Consequence of skipping:* you try to trade, code, and watch risk simultaneously, and the day the market is hardest — the day that decides your year — is the day you are doing all three badly at once.

Figure 6 makes the distinction explicit: which skills the founder must *own* personally, and which can be *hired* or outsourced. The things you must own are the things that *are* the promise to investors — the investing edge itself, the risk discipline that keeps you from blowing up, the founder story that raises the capital, and the culture decisions about who you bring in. Everything else — operations, compliance, technology, finance, even most of the IR execution — can be bought or rented, and should be, because trying to do it all yourself is how you end up doing none of it well.

![Grid contrasting founder skills that must be owned personally with functions that can be hired or outsourced across investing, risk, operations, compliance, sales, and technology](/imgs/blogs/the-founders-readiness-when-to-launch-6.png)

The honest cost note: this team is expensive, and it is the bulk of the fixed costs that make small funds bleed. But the costs are not optional, because the alternative — skipping the administrator, doubling as your own compliance officer, self-custodying assets — fails operational due diligence and therefore fails to raise institutional capital. You can sequence the build, but you cannot skip the *functions*.

Sequencing is where a smart founder saves money without cutting the corners allocators check. The pattern is **outsource the function on day one, internalize it only when scale justifies the salary.** Compliance is the clearest example: at launch you retain an outsourced-CCO firm and outside regulatory counsel for a fraction of a full-time chief compliance officer's cost, and that is entirely credible to allocators because the *function* is independent and real; you bring compliance in-house only when the fund is large enough that a dedicated CCO is both affordable and warranted by the regulatory load. Operations follow the same arc: an outsourced COO or a fund-administration relationship that handles the back office, before the AUM supports a full-time operations chief. Prime brokerage scales the same way — a **mini-prime or introducing broker** serves a sub-\$100M fund perfectly well, and you graduate to a tier-one prime (or, after the lessons of 2008, *multiple* primes to avoid single-counterparty concentration) as the book grows. What you must *not* do is sequence away the independence: the administrator and auditor are third-party and day-one, not "once we can afford it," because their independence is the entire point.

There is one more day-one role that founders forget because it has no title: the **outside generalist**, usually your fund-formation lawyer, who quarterbacks the launch. They are not a hire and not a permanent fixture, but in the formation window they coordinate the entity setup, the regulatory registrations, the service-provider contracts, and the document drafting, and a good one has done it dozens of times and will keep you from the expensive mistakes a first-time founder makes by default. Budget for them as a one-time launch cost (the kit's \$50k–\$150k+ of formation legal lives largely here) and treat the relationship as part of the team even though they never appear on the org chart. For who to hire and in what order as you scale past launch, see [building the team — who to hire and when](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when).

#### Worked example: the founders-class cash flow in year one

Maya launches the Meridian Fund with **\$50,000,000** of day-one AUM, mostly from a founders share class at **1.5% management / 15% performance** (a discount to standard 2-and-20, given to early and large investors — see the kit's fee data). Her year-one management company P&L, paying herself zero:

- Management-fee revenue: 1.5% × \$50M = **\$750,000**
- Annual fixed costs (institutional-grade small fund, illustrative): administrator ~\$60,000, audit ~\$40,000, outside legal and compliance ~\$120,000, technology, market data and OMS ~\$150,000, office and insurance ~\$80,000, one operations or research hire ~\$200,000 = **~\$650,000** before her own pay
- Performance fee in year one: assume a roughly flat first year (no gains above the high-water mark) = **\$0**
- **Net to the management company before Maya's salary: \$750,000 − \$650,000 = \$100,000**
- **Maya's salary: \$0** — she lives on her personal runway

So in year one, on a respectable \$50M launch, the *entire* business throws off about \$100,000 of cushion, and Maya takes home nothing from the fund. If AUM had been \$40M, the management fee would have been \$600,000 — *below* the fixed costs, and the management company would have lost money, with Maya injecting personal savings to keep it alive. This is precisely why ~\$80M–\$100M of AUM is the common "institutional viability" threshold: below it, a 1.5% fee can't reliably carry an institutional-grade cost base.

*A 50 million dollar launch is a business that just barely funds its own existence in year one and pays the founder nothing — which is exactly why personal runway, not fee revenue, keeps the founder fed.*

## The readiness checklist

Pulling the three dimensions together, here is the test to run on yourself before you resign. You are ready when you can honestly check every box — not most of them.

**Financial readiness**

- You have **24 months** of personal runway at a burn you have *already cut*, not the burn you have today.
- Your plan assumes **zero salary in year one** and a modest one only once the fund can carry it.
- You have a separate reserve so that a personal shock does not force you to raid the fund's working capital, and you can absorb at least one unexpected expense without breaking the runway.
- You have priced the full opportunity cost — comp foregone *plus* forfeited deferred comp and any clawbacks — and you still want to do it.

**Professional readiness**

- You have a track record that is **clean, attributable, and portable** — defensible to an allocator's lawyer and compliant with the marketing rules.
- Your edge has survived the honest test: it is real, repeatable, and *yours*, not the platform's risk system or a teammate's signal.
- You have at least a soft-circled anchor LP or a credible path to one — you are not launching purely on hope.

**Structural readiness**

- You have read your **non-compete, garden leave, and IP** clauses with a lawyer and built your timeline around them.
- You have lined up — or have a concrete plan and budget for — the **service-provider stack**: independent administrator, auditor, prime broker, counsel, compliance.
- You have decided **solo vs co-founder**, and if solo, you have a day-one plan for the COO/compliance functions you cannot do yourself.
- Segregation of duties exists from day one: the person trading is not the only person moving cash and valuing the book.

If you are missing boxes in only one column, you have specific, fixable work to do — build the runway, clean up the track record, line up the COO. If you are missing boxes across all three, you are not close, and the kindest thing the math can tell you is *not yet*.

## Common misconceptions

Founders walk in with a set of beliefs that the J-curve quietly demolishes. Naming them is the cheapest way to avoid learning them the expensive way.

**"I'll pay myself from day one."** No, you won't — not if you want the fund to survive operational due diligence. The management fee on a small fund barely covers the institutional-grade fixed costs; a performance fee pays nothing until you are up *and* above the high-water mark. As founder you are the residual claimant, paid last, after the administrator, the auditor, the prime broker, and your team. Plan to pay yourself **zero in year one** and live on your personal runway. Founders who budget a year-one salary are usually planning, without admitting it, to underfund the operation — and that is exactly what allocators screen for.

**"My track record comes with me."** Only the part that is legally and provably yours. Your models, code, and signals very likely belong to your current employer under the IP-assignment clause. Your *attributable, substantiated* performance can travel, but only in the form your contract and the marketing rules permit, and the first thing a serious allocator's lawyer does is ask for the source data. The portable asset is your *skill and process*; the firm's infrastructure stays behind. Plan for a track-record gap during garden leave, and have an honest, compliant story for it.

**"I can do the COO job too."** You can do it badly, part-time, on the days the market most needs your full attention — which is to say you can't. More to the point, allocators *won't let you*: a founder who is the sole signatory on the bank account and the only person valuing the portfolio fails operational due diligence on segregation-of-duties grounds, regardless of competence. The operations, compliance, and finance functions are not a hat you wear; they are roles that must visibly belong to someone who is not you.

**"Garden leave is dead time."** It is the opposite. Garden leave is *paid* time during which you cannot trade but absolutely can form your entities, select your service providers, soft-circle your anchor LPs, and arrive at your launch date half-built. The founders who treat it as a sentence waste it; the ones who treat it as runway compress their post-leave timeline by months. The genuine cost of garden leave is the staleness of your track record, not the calendar.

**"A co-founder just dilutes me."** A *good* co-founder is the highest-leverage de-risking decision a solo trader can make — they let you run the book full-time, they cover the business and operations you'd otherwise do badly, and they read to allocators as a more durable firm. The equity you give up buys a meaningfully higher probability of survival. (A *bad* co-founder, conversely, is worse than none — so the screen is rigorous complementarity and aligned values, not just a warm body.)

**"If my returns are good, the rest takes care of itself."** Returns are necessary and nowhere near sufficient. Funds die from operational failures, redemption cascades, and compliance blow-ups far more often than from bad trades. The readiness that matters is the readiness to *run the business* — the runway, the structure, the team — wrapped around the returns. The promise you sell investors is not just performance; it is performance delivered inside a structure that protects them, and that structure is the part you're least prepared for if you've only ever sat in a seat.

## How it plays out in the real world

The data behind the math is sobering, and it is the honest backdrop every prospective founder should hold in mind. The hedge-fund industry manages roughly **\$4.5–5.0 trillion** globally (HFR/Preqin/BarclayHedge estimates, as of 2025–2026), but the assets are extraordinarily concentrated: funds managing over **\$1 billion** — the "billion-dollar club" — control on the order of **90%** of the industry's assets, while the *median* fund is far smaller and most managers run **under \$100M**. The billion-dollar names you read about are the survivors and the exceptions; the modal launch is small, and a large fraction of launches do not survive **three to five years**. Roughly **7–11% of hedge funds liquidate every year** (with spikes in crisis years like 2008 and 2020), and in recent years launches and liquidations have been roughly balanced — for every fund that opens, one closes.

What kills the launches that die is rarely a spectacular trading blow-up; it is far more often the slow grind of the J-curve outlasting the founder's runway. A founder who launched at \$30M, never crossed the institutional-viability threshold, paid themselves nothing for two years, and then either ran out of personal savings or simply lost the will to keep losing money — that is the typical death, and it is a *readiness* failure, not a markets failure. The founder either misjudged the depth of the J-curve, underbuilt the runway, or tried to do every job alone and burned out.

The break-even math is the heart of it, and Figure 7 draws it for the Meridian Fund. With \$50M of launch AUM and a 1.5% founders-class fee, the management company collects about **\$62,500 a month** in fees against roughly **\$100,000 a month** of fixed costs — a net burn of about **\$37,500 a month** at the start. As Maya builds a track record and the AUM grows toward \$120M over two years, the fee revenue rises until the *cumulative* cash flow finally turns positive around **month 18**. Until then, the business is in the red on a cumulative basis, the founder is paid nothing, and survival depends entirely on whether the runway reaches the crossing point.

![Line chart of cumulative management-company cash flow from launch showing a J-curve that dips negative and crosses break-even around month eighteen](/imgs/blogs/the-founders-readiness-when-to-launch-7.png)

#### Worked example: the break-even timeline

The Meridian Fund's management company, month by month, illustrative:

- Launch AUM: **\$50,000,000**; founders-class fee **1.5%** → **\$62,500/month** in management-fee revenue at the start
- Fixed run-rate: **~\$1,200,000/year** → **~\$100,000/month**
- Net monthly cash flow at launch: \$62,500 − \$100,000 = **−\$37,500/month**
- As the track record builds, AUM grows along an S-curve toward **~\$120M** by month 24, lifting fee revenue past the fixed costs
- **Cumulative cash flow crosses zero around month 18** — the deepest hole is about **−\$0.2M** of cumulative business losses, all of which the founder (or seed capital) must fund out of working capital while drawing no salary

So the question "how much do I need?" has two answers that founders conflate. The *fund* needs to survive its cumulative business losses of a couple hundred thousand to month 18 — funded by working capital or a seeder. The *founder* needs to survive **18-plus months of zero personal income** — funded by personal runway. They are separate pools, and confusing them is how founders end up raiding the fund to pay the mortgage, which is both an operational red flag and a fast way to undercapitalize the business.

*Break-even is not a date the markets give you; it is a race between your AUM growth and your runway, and the founder who wins is usually the one who simply had more months in the tank.*

This is also where seed capital changes the calculus, and why it is worth understanding before you launch (it is its own subject). A seeder providing, say, \$75M of day-one AUM in exchange for a share of the management-company economics doesn't just deepen the fund's pockets — it pulls the entire J-curve crossing forward, sometimes from month 18 to month 6, by getting you past the institutional-viability threshold immediately. The price is real (often 15–25% of management-company revenue, with a multi-year lock-up), but the thing you are buying is *time*, and time is the scarce resource the whole readiness question turns on. Whether to take that trade — to give up a fifth of your firm's economics to compress the J-curve — is one of the most consequential decisions a founder makes, and it connects directly to the AUM-and-break-even math in [surviving the J-curve to break-even AUM](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/surviving-the-j-curve-break-even-aum).

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters at exactly one moment: when a genuinely skilled markets professional is staring at a legal pad, deciding whether to leave a paying seat. The temptation is to treat the decision as a referendum on talent — *am I good enough?* — when the binding question is almost always personal-financial and structural: *can I afford to be right slowly, and have I built the runway, the structure, and the team to survive the gap before the business pays me?* The founders who launch and last are not the most talented ones; they are the ones who priced the full opportunity cost, cut their burn and stacked 24 months of runway, used garden leave as build-time rather than dead time, brought in the partner and the COO who let them run the book, and went in clear-eyed that the management company would not pay them back until around month 18.

If you can check every box in all three columns of the readiness checklist, you are ready to start the much longer journey of actually building the thing. If you can't, the kindest and most expensive-to-ignore advice is: *not yet* — fix the missing column first.
