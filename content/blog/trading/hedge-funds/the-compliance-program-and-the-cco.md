Maya had her best year and her worst phone call in the same week. The Meridian Fund — her long/short equity book, fourteen months old, USD 180 million in assets — was up 21% net, comfortably ahead of the market and ahead of every peer she tracked. She was, by any measure she had been taught to use, winning. Then one of her analysts, a sharp twenty-eight-year-old she had poached from a bank, came into her office looking pale and said he needed to tell her about a call.

He had spoken to a consultant from an expert network — one of those firms that connects investors with industry specialists for paid "primary research" calls. The consultant was a current employee of a semiconductor supplier. Somewhere in the conversation, the analyst now realized, the man had described his employer's *unannounced* next-quarter bookings in specific numerical detail, two weeks before the earnings release. The analyst had not traded on it. But he had taken notes, the notes were in the firm's research folder, and the fund held a position in that supplier's largest customer. He wanted to know if this was a problem.

It was the kind of problem that does not stay small. Material non-public information — MNPI — had entered the building. Whether the fund had *traded* on it was, in a sense, beside the point; the firm now had to prove that it had not, and that it had handled the information correctly the moment it arrived. If Maya had a compliance program that worked — a CCO to call, a wall-crossing log to fill in, a restricted list to add the name to, an order system that would block the trade — then this was a contained incident, documented and survivable. If she did not, then a great year had just become the opening chapter of a criminal case, and the prosecutors would not be impressed by her Sharpe ratio.

This post is about the system that decides which of those two stories Maya gets to live: the **compliance program**, and the officer who owns it. *Brief caveat before we start: this is an explanation of how compliance programs work, not legal advice — every fund's obligations turn on its specific facts, and you retain real securities counsel for the actual rules.* Figure 1 is the map. It shows the program not as a stack of documents but as a connected system, with the CCO at the center, each control feeding the next, and the whole apparatus pointed at a single outcome: staying in business. We will earn the right to read it over the rest of the post.

## Foundations: what a compliance program is

If you trade for a living, the markets side of your future firm is the part you already understand. The compliance side is almost certainly a blank page, and it is the page on which funds and careers are most often lost. So let us build it from zero. The mental model is one sentence: **a compliance program is the set of written rules, designated people, and recorded controls that an investment adviser maintains so that it obeys the securities laws — and, just as importantly, can prove that it obeyed them.** Every term below is one component of that machine.

**Compliance program.** The whole system. Under the Investment Advisers Act, a registered adviser is required by **Rule 206(4)-7** — the "compliance rule" — to adopt written policies and procedures reasonably designed to prevent violations of the securities laws, to designate a chief compliance officer to administer them, and to review them at least annually. That single rule is the legal spine of everything in this post. It does not tell you *what* your policies must say; it tells you that you must have them, follow them, and check them.

**Chief compliance officer (CCO).** The person the firm designates to own and run the compliance program. The CCO writes and maintains the manual, administers the controls, trains the staff, conducts the annual review, and is the firm's point of contact when a regulator calls. The role carries real personal exposure: the SEC has, in cases of serious program failure, charged CCOs individually. The CCO is not a clerk who files forms; the CCO is the person whose job is to be able to say *no* to the founder.

**Compliance manual.** The written rulebook — the document Rule 206(4)-7 requires. It states, in plain procedures, how the firm handles personal trading, MNPI, trade allocation, best execution, marketing, gifts and entertainment, conflicts of interest, books and records, and more. It is meant to be read and followed by real people, not drafted once and entombed. A manual that describes a firm that does not exist — controls nobody runs — is worse than no manual, because it documents your own failure to follow your own rules.

**Code of ethics.** A specific document required by **Rule 204A-1**. It sets the standard of conduct the firm expects, requires "access persons" (employees who see the fund's trades or holdings) to report their personal securities holdings and transactions, requires pre-clearance of certain personal trades, addresses conflicts and gifts, and obligates employees to comply with the securities laws and to report violations. The code of ethics is where the firm's duty to put investors first becomes a set of enforceable personal rules for each employee.

**Material non-public information (MNPI).** Information that is both *material* — a reasonable investor would consider it important in deciding to buy or sell — and *non-public* — not yet disseminated to the market. Unannounced earnings, a pending merger, an FDA decision not yet released, a major contract win or loss: all MNPI. Trading securities while in possession of MNPI, or passing it to someone who trades (tipping), is **insider trading**, which is both a civil violation and a federal crime. MNPI is the single most dangerous substance a hedge fund can come into contact with, and the controls around it are the part of the program that keeps people out of prison.

**Insider trading.** Buying or selling securities in breach of a duty, on the basis of MNPI. There is no single statute that says "insider trading"; it is built from the antifraud provisions — principally **Section 10(b)** of the Securities Exchange Act and **Rule 10b-5** — and decades of case law. What matters for a founder is the practical reality: the penalties are disgorgement of profits, civil fines, an industry bar, and, for the serious cases, criminal prosecution and prison.

**Personal-trading pre-clearance.** The control under which an employee must request and receive written approval *before* placing a trade in his own account. Compliance checks the requested security against the restricted list and against the fund's own activity, then approves or denies, and logs the answer. The purpose is to ensure no employee front-runs the fund, trades against it, or trades a name the firm is restricted from — and, crucially, to leave a record proving they did not.

**Restricted list.** A maintained, dated list of securities that the firm and its employees may not trade, because the firm holds MNPI about them, is engaged in a transaction involving them, or is otherwise conflicted. When MNPI about a company enters the firm, the name goes on the restricted list and the order management system blocks any trade in it. The restricted list is the operational expression of "we know something; therefore we do not trade."

**Expert network.** A firm that, for a fee, connects investors with industry consultants — current and former executives, doctors, suppliers — for "primary research" calls. Used correctly, expert networks are a legitimate research tool. Used carelessly, they are a pipeline for MNPI straight from an insider into a fund, which is exactly how the most famous insider-trading prosecutions of the last two decades were built. The controls around expert-network use are a load-bearing part of any modern program.

**SEC examination.** The process by which the SEC's examination staff inspects a registered adviser — reviewing its program, its records, and its conduct against the rules. An exam opens with a document-request letter, proceeds through interviews and on-site or remote review, and ends with a letter of deficiencies or, in serious cases, a referral to the enforcement division. Surviving an exam is not about charming the examiners; it is about being able to hand them the records that prove your program ran.

Here is the through-line that connects all of these. A hedge fund sells a promise: *trust me with your capital, and I will compound it inside a structure that protects you and obeys the law.* The compliance program is the half of that promise that has nothing to do with returns and everything to do with whether the firm is *allowed to keep operating.* Performance keeps the promise credible. Compliance keeps the firm *legal*. And because a compliance failure does not cost you a bad quarter — it costs you the firm, your registration, and sometimes your liberty — compliance is not overhead you minimize. It is the system that decides whether there is a fund at all next year.

## Why compliance is existential

Before the existential argument, one organizing idea that the rest of the post hangs on. Every control in the program does two jobs at once: it prevents a specific harm, and it produces a specific record that proves the prevention happened. Figure 2 lays the program out that way — each control area in a row, the harm it is designed to stop, and the record an examiner can demand to verify it ran. Pre-clearance prevents front-running and leaves a pre-clearance log reconciled against broker statements. The restricted list prevents trading on MNPI and leaves a dated list plus the order-system rejects. Expert-network rules prevent receiving inside information from a consultant and leave call logs and attestations. The code of ethics prevents undisclosed conflicts and bribery and leaves a gift log and signed attestations. Marketing review prevents misleading performance claims and leaves the net-of-fee numbers and the source data behind them. The pattern is the whole point: an examiner does not ask whether you *have* a control; she asks to see the record that proves it ran, and no record means no control.

![A matrix diagram mapping each compliance control area to the harm it prevents and the record an examiner demands, covering personal-trade pre-clearance, the restricted list and MNPI, expert-network calls, the code of ethics and gifts, and marketing and performance review](/imgs/blogs/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco-2.png)

Hold that grid in mind, because it explains why the cost of skipping a control is not the missing policy but the missing *proof*. Now to why the whole thing is existential rather than merely prudent.

Founders chronically misprice compliance because they price it like an expense. An expense trades money for a service; you minimize it. But compliance is not an expense — it is *insurance against ruin*, and the thing it insures is the entire enterprise. The right mental frame is not "what does this cost me," but "what is the distribution of outcomes if I get it wrong."

Consider that distribution honestly. A bad trade costs you a few percent of NAV; you apologize to investors, you climb back over the high-water mark, life goes on. (We covered the mechanics of that recovery in [fees and terms: the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark).) A bad *strategy year* costs you maybe redemptions and a lean year of management fees. These are survivable. They live in the body of the distribution.

A compliance failure lives in the tail, and the tail is total loss. An insider-trading conviction does not dent your returns; it ends your career, possibly with a prison sentence, and it typically ends your fund, because investors do not stay in a fund whose founder is under indictment. A serious examination deficiency can produce an enforcement action, a fine, an industry bar, and the kind of headline that triggers a redemption cascade no gate can hold. The asymmetry is the entire point: you are trading a known, bounded, annual cost — the price of a real program — against an unbounded, ruinous, low-probability event. That is the textbook shape of something you insure, not something you skimp on.

#### Worked example: the true cost of a compliance failure

Suppose the Meridian Fund cuts corners. Maya decides a full compliance program is "premature" at USD 180 million and saves the money: she skips a real CCO, uses a thin off-the-shelf manual, and does not build MNPI controls. Tally what she "saves": maybe an outsourced CCO at \$60,000 a year, plus another \$40,000 of legal and systems work — call it \$100,000 a year avoided.

Now run the tail. The expert-network call happens. With no controls, the analyst's notes sit in the research folder, the fund trades the customer's stock as usual, and there is no wall-crossing log, no restricted-list entry, no blocked order — nothing to prove the trade was clean. Two years later the consultant is charged, his call records are subpoenaed, and the Meridian Fund's name surfaces. Now Maya is spending \$2,000,000 and counting on criminal-defense counsel, the SEC has opened a parallel civil case, her prime broker is reviewing the relationship, and her investors have filed redemptions for \$140,000,000 of the \$180,000,000. Even in the *good* outcome where she is never convicted, the fund does not survive the eighteen months of legal siege and the redemptions. She "saved" \$100,000 a year and lost the entire franchise — the management-company value, her track record, her reputation, her ability to ever raise again.

*The arithmetic of compliance is not the annual cost versus zero; it is the annual cost versus the small chance of losing everything, and that is a trade you take every single time.*

This existential framing is why the rest of the post treats each control not as a checkbox but as one more wall between the founder and ruin. Figure 1 drew the walls as a connected system for a reason: insider-trading prosecutions, examination deficiencies, and marketing-rule violations do not respect your org chart, and a gap in any one control is the gap the tail event walks through.

## The CCO

Every compliance program has a single human owner, and the law requires it: the chief compliance officer. Understanding this role — what it is, who fills it, and how it is structured — is the founder's first compliance decision.

**What the CCO actually does.** The CCO administers the program end to end. She writes and updates the compliance manual and the code of ethics. She runs personal-trade pre-clearance and reviews employee brokerage statements. She maintains the restricted list and the MNPI/wall-crossing log. She reviews marketing materials against the marketing rule before they go out. She trains employees, collects their annual code-of-ethics attestations, and keeps the gift-and-entertainment log. She conducts the **annual compliance review** that Rule 206(4)-7 demands and documents it. And when an examiner calls or a problem like Maya's analyst surfaces, she is the person who responds. The CCO's deliverable is not opinions; it is *records* — the evidence that each of those controls ran.

**The independence problem.** The CCO's hardest and most important job is to be able to say *no* to the people who pay her, including the founder. That is structurally awkward at a small fund, where the CCO may report directly to the founder and depend on him for her bonus. The conflict is real, and serious allocators probe it during operational due diligence — we covered that veto-wielding process in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto). The mitigations are: give the CCO genuine authority and access, document that authority in the manual, never let the founder override a compliance "no" without a written, defensible rationale, and — at larger firms — build a reporting line that does not run entirely through the person the CCO must sometimes police.

**The annual review is the CCO's signature deliverable.** Rule 206(4)-7 requires the firm to review the adequacy and effectiveness of its compliance program at least once a year, and to document that it did. This is not a calendar formality. A real annual review tests whether each control actually worked over the prior year: did pre-clearance catch the trades it should have, did the restricted list get used, were the marketing materials reviewed, did anything slip? It identifies gaps, recommends fixes, and records both. When an examiner arrives, the dated annual-review memo is one of the very first things requested, and its quality is a direct read on whether the CCO has been doing the job or merely holding the title. A review that says "everything is fine" every year, with no findings and no changes, is itself a red flag — no real program runs a year without something to tighten.

**The CCO's supervisory dimension.** Beyond running controls, the CCO is part of the firm's supervisory structure — the system by which the firm oversees its employees to prevent and detect violations. Failure to supervise is its own basis for an enforcement action, separate from the underlying misconduct: a firm can be sanctioned not because it traded on MNPI but because it failed to maintain a system reasonably designed to catch an employee who did. This is why training matters as much as policies. The CCO who trains analysts to recognize MNPI on a call, who runs forensic reviews of trading around news events, and who documents that training and those reviews, is building the supervisory record that distinguishes a firm that got unlucky from a firm that was negligent. The distinction is often the difference between a contained problem and a firm-ending one.

**In-house versus outsourced.** A founder has a real choice in how to staff the role. A small launching fund frequently uses an **outsourced CCO** — a specialist compliance-consulting firm that supplies a named individual to serve as CCO, builds the manual, and runs the program as a service. As the fund grows, it typically brings the role **in-house**, hiring a dedicated CCO (often combined with general-counsel or COO duties early on), and may keep an outside compliance consultant for the heavy lifting and the annual review. There is no single right answer; the answer is set by AUM, strategy complexity, and how much MNPI risk the strategy generates. A systematic equity fund touching no inside information has a lighter compliance load than an event-driven fund whose entire edge involves talking to people about specific companies.

#### Worked example: CCO cost, in-house versus outsourced

Run the numbers for the Meridian Fund at two sizes. At launch, USD 180 million, Maya outsources: a reputable compliance-consulting firm serves as CCO and runs the program for roughly \$60,000 to \$120,000 a year, depending on the work. That is the right call — it is cheaper than a salaried hire, and she gets a team with examination experience rather than one nervous junior person.

Now fast-forward: the fund is at USD 900 million, the strategy involves regular expert-network calls, and an institutional allocator's ODD team has flagged that an outsourced CCO is "too thin" for the firm's MNPI risk. Maya hires a dedicated in-house CCO at a fully-loaded cost of perhaps \$300,000 to \$450,000 a year, and *keeps* an outside consultant for the annual review and surge work at another \$80,000. Her compliance line has gone from roughly \$90,000 to well over \$450,000. As a share of a 1.5% management fee on \$900M — about \$13,500,000 of management revenue — that is a few percent of revenue. At \$180M it was a meaningfully larger share of a much smaller pie, which is exactly why the outsourced model exists for small funds.

*The CCO question is not "in-house or outsourced" in the abstract; it is "what does my AUM and my MNPI risk require," and the answer scales up as both grow.*

## The compliance manual and the code of ethics

These two documents are the written foundation of the program. They are not the program — the program is the controls actually running — but they are where the rules are stated, and an examiner reads them first to learn what you *claim* to do, then checks whether you did it.

**The compliance manual** is the operating rulebook. A real one is specific to the firm: it describes *your* strategy, *your* service providers, *your* actual workflows. It covers, at minimum, the personal-trading policy and pre-clearance procedure, the policy on MNPI and insider trading, trade allocation and aggregation (so no account is systematically favored), best execution, the marketing and advertising policy, gifts and entertainment, political contributions (the "pay-to-play" rule), conflicts of interest, books-and-records retention, business continuity, and the procedure for the annual review itself. The cardinal sin is the **boilerplate manual**: a generic template, lightly edited, describing controls the firm does not actually run. That is the document examiners most want to find, because the gap between the written policy and the real practice is itself a violation of Rule 206(4)-7.

**The code of ethics**, required by Rule 204A-1, is narrower and more personal. It establishes the standard of conduct, identifies the firm's **access persons**, and imposes on them concrete duties: report your personal securities holdings on joining and annually; report your personal transactions (usually quarterly, often via duplicate brokerage statements sent straight to compliance); pre-clear the trades the code says require it; disclose conflicts and outside business activities; observe gift limits; and report any violation you become aware of. Every access person signs an **annual attestation** that they have read the code and complied with it. That attestation is not a formality — it is a dated record an examiner will ask for, and a signed false attestation is its own serious problem.

**Books and records.** Threaded through both documents is an obligation founders systematically underestimate: the **books-and-records rule** (Advisers Act Rule 204-2) requires a registered adviser to make and keep specified records — order memoranda, the trade blotter, communications relating to recommendations and advice, advertising, financial records — for defined periods, generally five years with the first two readily accessible. In practice this captures email and electronic communications, which is why a modern firm archives messaging in a system the CCO can search. The cases where a firm's records do not match its manual, or where required communications were not retained, are some of the most common deficiencies examiners cite. The practical instruction is blunt: if a control runs, it must leave a retained, retrievable record, because the examiner's entire method is to ask for the record and see whether it exists.

**Gifts, entertainment, and pay-to-play.** Two specific conflict areas live in the code and get their own logs. **Gifts and entertainment** are capped and recorded because lavish gifts to or from counterparties, brokers, or clients can corrupt the firm's judgment or create the appearance of a quid pro quo; a typical policy sets a dollar limit per recipient per year and logs every gift given or received. **Pay-to-play** — the practice of making political contributions to officials who can influence public-pension allocations — is restricted by Advisers Act Rule 206(4)-5, which can impose a two-year ban on receiving compensation from a government client after a covered contribution. For a fund that hopes to manage public-pension money, an uncleared political contribution by the founder or a marketer can quietly forfeit that relationship for two years, which is why contributions are pre-cleared and logged like trades.

The link between the two documents is the link between *firm* obligations and *individual* obligations. The manual binds the firm; the code binds each employee. Together they answer the examiner's first question — *what are you supposed to be doing?* — and everything else in the exam is checking whether the records show you did it.

## Personal trading and pre-clearance

Personal trading is where the abstract duty "put the fund's interest first" becomes a daily, recorded control. The risk it addresses is concrete: an employee who knows what the fund is about to do could trade ahead of it in his own account (front-running), trade against it, or trade a name the firm is restricted from. Pre-clearance is the control that makes that provably impossible — or, if it happens, provably caught.

Figure 3 shows the workflow. The employee files a written pre-clearance request — security, side, size — *before* trading. Compliance screens the name against two things: the **restricted list**, and the fund's own current and anticipated activity. If the name is clean, compliance approves in writing, the approval is valid for a defined window (typically that trading day only), and the employee may trade. If the name is on the restricted list or in conflict with the fund's activity, compliance **denies**, and the denial is logged. After the fact, the employee's actual trades — captured via duplicate brokerage statements sent directly to compliance — are reconciled against the pre-clearance log, monthly, to confirm that every trade was approved and no unapproved trades slipped through.

![A pipeline diagram showing the personal-trade pre-clearance workflow: an employee files a written request, compliance checks the restricted list and for conflicts, then either approves for that day or denies and logs the refusal, with monthly reconciliation against broker statements](/imgs/blogs/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco-3.png)

The reason the request and the answer must both be *in writing and before the trade* is evidentiary. The control's real product is not the approval; it is the *record* that the employee asked first and that compliance checked. That record is what lets the firm prove, two years later when an examiner or a prosecutor asks, that a given personal trade was not informed by what the fund knew. Without the record, even an entirely innocent trade looks suspicious, and "I did nothing wrong" is a much weaker position than "here is the dated pre-clearance showing I checked and was cleared."

#### Worked example: a pre-clearance that gets denied

Maya's analyst — the same sharp one — wants to buy 500 shares of a mid-cap industrial in his personal account. He files the pre-clearance request at 9:15 a.m. Compliance runs the two checks. The restricted list is clean for that name. But the conflict check is not: the Meridian Fund's investment committee decided yesterday to build a 3% position in that exact stock, and the trader is working the order this week. If the analyst buys now, in front of the fund's accumulation, he benefits personally from the price impact the fund's own buying will create — a textbook conflict, whether or not he consciously intended it.

Compliance denies the request and logs it: *denied, fund actively accumulating, 09:22.* The analyst does not trade. Three weeks later, the fund has finished building its position and the name is no longer in conflict; the analyst re-requests, is cleared, and buys his 500 shares. The cost to him was a three-week delay. The benefit to the firm was avoiding a situation where an employee profited alongside — and ahead of — the fund, which is the kind of fact pattern that turns an examination into an enforcement referral.

*Pre-clearance is not about catching crooks; it is about making sure that even your honest people leave a clean, dated record that no one front-ran the fund.*

## MNPI and insider-trading controls

This is the part of the program that keeps people out of prison, so it deserves the most care. The governing principle is counterintuitive and worth stating precisely: **the goal of MNPI controls is not to ensure the firm never *hears* inside information — that is impossible for an active research shop — but to ensure that the moment it does, the information is rendered un-tradeable and the firm's clean conduct is documented.** You cannot un-hear a tip. You can make it impossible to act on, and prove you did so.

Figure 4 contrasts a weak and a robust response to the *same* event — a specific, non-public deal detail arriving on a research call. The weak firm has no wall-crossing log and no restricted list, so nobody records that MNPI entered. The fund trades the name normally; no block fires. The outcome is that the firm cannot prove its innocence, and one trade becomes a criminal case. The robust firm does four things, fast and in writing: the analyst flags the possible MNPI to the CCO immediately; the event is logged as a **wall-crossing** (who knows, when, and what); the name goes on the **restricted list** the same day; and the order management system **blocks** any trade in the name until the list clears. The outcome is documented clean conduct the firm can hand to an examiner. The two firms heard the identical information. Only the second one can prove what it did about it.

![A before-and-after diagram comparing weak MNPI controls, where a tip goes unrecorded and the fund trades the name and cannot prove innocence, against robust controls, where the recipient flags it to the CCO, the event is logged and the name restricted, trading is blocked, and the firm can demonstrate clean conduct](/imgs/blogs/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco-4.png)

Figure 7 zooms into the robust response as a sequence, because the order and the timing matter. **Trigger:** an employee receives a possibly material, non-public detail. **Escalate:** flag it to the CCO immediately — do not act on it, do not share it further. **Wall-cross:** log who now knows, when, and what, and restrict the information to that named list of people. **Restrict:** add the security to the restricted list, dated and timestamped. **Block:** the order system rejects any trade in the name — a hard stop, not a warning. **Clear:** remove the name only when the information has become public or gone stale, and log the removal too. The whole sequence happens in hours, in writing, and its product is a complete, dated paper trail showing the firm caught the MNPI and neutralized it.

![A pipeline diagram showing the end-to-end handling of a single MNPI event in six steps: trigger, escalate to the CCO, wall-cross and log who knows, add the security to the restricted list, block trading in the order system, and clear the name only when the information is public or stale](/imgs/blogs/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco-7.png)

There is a crucial subtlety about *materiality* and *use* that founders get wrong. People assume MNPI "only matters if you trade on it." It is far more dangerous than that. Possession of MNPI restricts you whether or not you act: if you hold the information and trade the security, the law's presumption runs against you, and proving you traded for unrelated reasons is a brutal, expensive fight. Tipping — passing the information to someone who trades — is itself a violation even if you never trade yourself. And the line of what is "material" is not bright; specific unannounced numbers, pending corporate events, and "the kind of thing that moves the stock when announced" all qualify. The only safe posture is the one the controls enforce: when in doubt, treat it as MNPI, escalate, and restrict. The cost of over-restricting is a missed trade. The cost of under-restricting is a federal case.

#### Worked example: an MNPI wall-crossing scenario

Return to Maya's opening call. The robust version plays out like this. At 2:40 p.m. the analyst realizes the expert-network consultant — a current employee of the semiconductor supplier — has just given him specific unannounced next-quarter bookings, two weeks before earnings. He does not finish the call casually; he ends it, and at 2:48 he emails the CCO: *possible MNPI received on [supplier] re: Q-bookings, expert-network call, need guidance.* The CCO logs the wall-crossing at 2:55, restricting knowledge to the analyst, herself, and the founder. By 3:10 the supplier *and* its largest customer — the name the fund actually holds — are both on the restricted list, because the bookings detail is material to the customer too. The order management system now blocks any trade in either name. The fund holds a USD 9,000,000 position in the customer; it does not add, trim, or touch it until the information is public.

Two weeks later the supplier reports earnings publicly; the bookings number is now disseminated. The CCO clears both names off the restricted list, logs the removal, and the fund can trade normally again. If, a year from now, the consultant is charged and the Meridian Fund's call records are subpoenaed, Maya hands over a complete dated trail: the 2:48 escalation, the 2:55 wall-cross, the 3:10 restriction, the blocked orders, the untouched position, the clean clearance. The firm did not trade on MNPI, and — this is the part that saves her — it can *prove* it.

*The defense against an insider-trading case is not built in the courtroom; it is built in the ten minutes after the tip arrives, in the logs.*

## Expert networks

Expert networks deserve their own section because they are the single feature of the modern research landscape most likely to inject MNPI into a fund, and because the cautionary history here is not hypothetical — it is the defining enforcement story of the last two decades.

An expert network is a legitimate business: for a fee, it arranges calls between investors and industry consultants — current and former employees, physicians, suppliers, channel-checkers — to help investors understand an industry. Used within bright lines, it is ordinary primary research. The danger is the consultant who is a *current* employee of a public company and who, in the course of a paid call, conveys his employer's MNPI — unannounced results, a pending deal, a product problem. The moment that happens, the investor is holding inside information, and if the firm then trades, it is insider trading. The expert network is the conduit; the fund is the one prosecuted.

This is exactly how the **Galleon** and **SAC Capital** cases were built. In the Galleon matter, founder Raj Rajaratnam was convicted in 2011 of orchestrating a wide insider-trading ring fed in part by industry contacts and consultants; he received an eleven-year prison sentence — at the time the longest ever for insider trading — and was ordered to pay penalties and forfeiture totaling well over USD 150 million. The broader government investigation into expert-network-driven trading swept up dozens of people across multiple funds and consulting firms. And in the **SAC Capital** matter, the firm pleaded guilty in 2013 to securities and wire fraud arising from insider trading by its employees, agreed to pay roughly **USD 1.8 billion** in total penalties, and was forced to stop managing outside money — converting into a family office. Founder Steven Cohen later settled with the SEC and faced a temporary supervisory bar. Figure 6 puts those penalties in scale.

![A bar chart on a log scale comparing illustrative headline penalties for insider-trading and compliance cases: Galleon at roughly 156 million dollars, SAC Capital at roughly 1.8 billion dollars, and smaller illustrative amounts for a monitoring failure and a typical compliance-program fine, annotated that SAC had to return outside capital so the firm itself ended](/imgs/blogs/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco-6.png)

The lesson the survivors learned is the one that shapes every modern program: **controls around expert-network use are mandatory and specific.** A robust firm requires compliance pre-approval before engaging an expert network; bars calls with current employees of companies the fund trades, or routes them through strict scripting and chaperoning; obtains written **attestations** from each consultant that they will not disclose MNPI and are permitted by their employer to consult; logs every call — who, when, which consultant, which topic; and trains analysts to recognize and immediately escalate the moment a call drifts toward MNPI. None of this makes expert networks safe to use carelessly; it makes them usable carefully, with a record that proves the care.

#### Worked example: the cost when expert-network controls fail

Take a peer of Maya's — call the firm a USD 600 million event-driven fund — that uses expert networks heavily and controls them loosely: no pre-approval, no attestations, no call log, analysts free to call whomever. One analyst regularly speaks to a consultant who is a current employee at a medical-device maker, and trades the stock around those calls. Three years later the consultant is charged; the call records lead straight to the fund. Now the firm faces a parallel SEC civil action and a DOJ criminal investigation. The analyst is charged personally. The fund's prime broker freezes new financing pending review. Investors redeem; within a year the fund is wound down.

Set aside whether anyone is ultimately convicted — the *process* alone is fatal. Legal costs run into the millions. The redemptions take the AUM well below break-even, which we walked through in [the fund documents and structure](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund). The track record, however good, is now radioactive; no allocator touches a fund named in an insider-trading case. The firm that "saved" the cost of a real expert-network control loses everything the control was protecting. Contrast SAC: even a USD 1.8 billion penalty was, in dollars, survivable for a firm that large — but the forced return of all outside capital was not. The franchise died regardless of whether the fine could be paid.

*Expert networks are not the danger; uncontrolled expert networks are. The control is cheap, and the absence of it is the most expensive thing in this entire post.*

## Surviving the SEC exam

For a registered adviser, an SEC examination is not an *if* but a *when*. The examination program inspects advisers on a risk-prioritized cycle; a newly registered adviser can expect attention relatively early, and any adviser can be selected for cause or at random. The founder's job is not to survive the exam by performance on the day — it is to have run a real program every day, so that the records exist when asked.

An exam opens with a **document-request letter**, and the request is a comprehensive test of whether your program actually operated. Figure 5 lays out the standard categories. The examiner asks for the compliance manual and the dated **annual-review memo** that Rule 206(4)-7 requires (who reviewed it, when, what they found). The code of ethics, the signed employee attestations, and the gift log. The personal-trade pre-clearance log and employees' broker statements. The dated history of the restricted list and the MNPI/wall-crossing log. The trade blotter, allocation records, and best-execution reviews. Expert-network contracts, call logs, and consultant attestations. The valuation policy and the NAV and pricing records from the administrator. The marketing decks and DDQs, *plus the source data behind every performance number*. And the regulatory filings — Form ADV, Form PF — together with email archives, the org chart, and service-provider contracts.

![A grid diagram showing the categories of documents the SEC requests at the start of an examination, including the compliance manual and annual review, the code of ethics and attestations, the personal-trade pre-clearance log, the restricted list and MNPI log, the trade blotter, expert-network records, the valuation policy and NAV records, marketing materials with source data, and regulatory filings](/imgs/blogs/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco-5.png)

Stare at that list and the central truth becomes obvious: **almost everything requested is a *record produced by a control running over time.*** You cannot manufacture a year of pre-clearance logs, a dated restricted-list history, or signed annual attestations in the two weeks you are typically given to respond. The exam is not a test you cram for; it is a test of whether the program was real all along. The firms that fail are not the ones with a bad day — they are the ones whose controls never actually ran, so the records do not exist or, worse, exist but contradict the manual.

**How a prepared firm behaves during an exam.** The day the exam letter arrives, the prepared firm does a small number of things well. It assigns a single point of contact — usually the CCO, supported by outside compliance counsel — so the firm speaks with one voice and nothing is volunteered carelessly. It produces exactly what is requested, completely and on time, and it does not improvise documents to fill gaps; a fabricated or backdated record turns a deficiency into a fraud. It prepares the people who will be interviewed so they answer truthfully, narrowly, and without speculation. And it treats the examiners professionally rather than defensively, because the examiner's read on whether the firm has a *culture of compliance* colors how the marginal questions are resolved. None of this is theater; it is the natural behavior of a firm whose records are real, and the absence of it — disorganized production, contradictory answers, missing logs — is precisely what signals to an examiner that the program existed only on paper.

The mechanics of the exam itself: after the document review, examiners conduct interviews — with the CCO, the founder, key staff — and probe specific transactions and conflicts. The exam ends one of three ways. Best case: a **no-deficiency** letter, or a short list of minor items you remediate. Common case: a **deficiency letter** identifying weaknesses you must fix and confirm in writing. Worst case: a **referral to enforcement**, when the examiners find conduct serious enough — fraud, undisclosed conflicts, a program that exists only on paper — to warrant an action. The single best predictor of which outcome you get is not how well you talk on exam day; it is whether your CCO has been producing real records, conducting the annual review, and saying *no* when she had to, for every quarter leading up to it.

#### Note on registration and thresholds

Whether you are registered with the SEC, with a state, or operate as an exempt reporting adviser turns on your regulatory AUM and your investor base — generally SEC registration at USD 100 million regulatory AUM, with a private-fund-adviser exemption below USD 150 million of US private-fund assets. We covered those thresholds and Form ADV in [registration and regulation: SEC, CFTC, and Reg D](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d). The compliance-program obligations in this post attach most fully to *registered* advisers, but even an exempt adviser is subject to the antifraud provisions — which means the MNPI and insider-trading controls in particular are not optional regardless of registration status. *Do not read "I am not yet registered" as "the insider-trading rules do not apply to me"; they apply to everyone.*

## Common misconceptions

The compliance program attracts a specific set of comfortable, expensive errors. Name them so you do not make them.

**"Small funds aren't examined, so I can wait."** False, and dangerous. The SEC examines advisers across the size spectrum, and newly registered advisers often draw attention precisely *because* they are new and unproven. More to the point, the antifraud provisions — the insider-trading rules — apply to a fund of any size, registered or not. A small fund that trades on MNPI is prosecuted exactly like a large one. Size buys you no exemption from the part of compliance that sends people to prison.

**"Compliance is just paperwork."** This is the costliest myth, and it inverts the truth. The paperwork is the *output*, not the substance. The substance is a system of controls that actually run: requests checked before trades, MNPI escalated and restricted in real time, marketing reviewed before it goes out. A binder of policies nobody follows is worse than nothing, because the gap between your written manual and your real practice is itself a violation an examiner is trained to find. Real compliance is a verb.

**"MNPI only matters if you actually trade on it."** False, and it is the misconception most likely to end a career. Possession of MNPI restricts you whether you trade or not; if you hold it and trade, the burden of explaining yourself is crushing. Tipping someone who trades is itself a violation even if you never trade. And you do not get to decide after the fact that the information "wasn't really material." The only safe rule is the one the controls enforce: escalate and restrict the moment there is doubt.

**"My returns will protect me."** They will do the opposite. Exceptional returns are what *attract* scrutiny — from regulators wondering how you do it, from short-sellers, from journalists. SAC and Galleon were not obscure underperformers; they were storied, high-returning franchises, and that is precisely why their fall was so total. Performance is not a compliance defense; in an insider-trading case it is part of the prosecution's narrative.

**"An outsourced CCO ticks the box."** It can be the right *choice*, but it does not, by itself, make you compliant, and sophisticated allocators know it. An outsourced CCO who shows up quarterly and rubber-stamps a generic manual provides the form of a program without the substance. What matters is whether the controls run daily and produce real records — and whether the CCO, in-house or outsourced, has the authority and the willingness to say no. ODD teams probe exactly this; a thin outsourced arrangement at a firm with high MNPI risk is a frequent reason an allocation is declined.

**"If I'm honest, I don't need controls."** Honesty is necessary and insufficient. The controls do not exist because you intend to cheat; they exist because (a) you cannot prove your honesty without records, (b) your employees are not all you, and (c) the law presumes against you once MNPI is in the building. Maya in the opening scene was entirely honest — and entirely exposed, until the controls gave her a way to prove it. Good intentions do not produce the dated paper trail that wins the case.

## How it plays out in the real world

Strip away the theory and the pattern across two decades of enforcement is remarkably consistent. The funds that died of compliance failures did not die of clumsy paperwork; they died of *controls that were missing precisely where the firm's strategy created risk.*

**SAC Capital** is the canonical case, and its lesson is the one in this post's TL;DR. The firm pleaded guilty in 2013 to fraud arising from insider trading by multiple employees, agreed to roughly USD 1.8 billion in penalties, and — the decisive blow — was barred from managing outside capital, converting into the Point72 family office. The fine, enormous as it was, was survivable for a firm of that scale. The *loss of the franchise* was not. The deeper lesson is structural: SAC's culture and information flows created enormous MNPI risk, and the controls were not strong enough, or independent enough, to neutralize it. The firm that returns can rebuild a track record; the firm that loses the right to manage outside money is over.

**Galleon** is the case that defined the expert-network and consultant risk. Raj Rajaratnam's 2011 conviction and eleven-year sentence, built on wiretaps and a web of tippers including industry consultants, sent a generation of funds running to build real expert-network controls — pre-approval, attestations, call logs, chaperoning. The funds that already had those controls watched the prosecutions as a vindication; the ones that did not scrambled, and some did not survive the scrutiny. The entire modern apparatus around expert networks exists because of what Galleon proved a careless one could become.

**Madoff** sits slightly apart — a Ponzi scheme rather than an insider-trading case — but it belongs here because it is the canonical demonstration that the *absence* of independent controls is itself the warning. No independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, self-custody: the missing operational and compliance stack was the tell, and an honest review would have caught it on the structure alone, before any returns analysis. We treated Madoff at length in the operational-due-diligence post, but it reinforces this one's thesis: the controls are not bureaucracy layered on top of a real business; in a fraud, their *absence* is the business.

The unifying thread is the asymmetry this post opened with. None of these firms died of a bad trade. They died of a control that was missing where it mattered — and in every case the cost was not a fine to be absorbed but a franchise, a career, and sometimes a person's freedom. That is why the founder who treats compliance as a cost to minimize has mispriced the single decision most likely to determine whether the fund exists in five years.

#### Worked example: the program as a balance sheet of risk

One last way to hold it. Treat the program as buying down a set of low-probability, high-severity risks for a known annual premium. Maya's mature program might cost, all in, roughly \$500,000 a year at USD 900 million AUM — CCO, consultant, systems, legal, training. Against that, set the risks it buys down: an insider-trading case (tail cost: the firm, a career, prison), a marketing-rule violation (fine plus a forced restatement of performance — see [marketing and the SEC marketing rule](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule)), a personal-trading scandal (front-running headline, redemptions), an exam referral to enforcement (action, bar, headline). Each is individually unlikely in a given year; collectively, over a fund's life, the probability that *something* in that set fires is not small, and the severity of any one of them is total.

A \$500,000 premium against a portfolio of ruin-class risks is not expensive — it is one of the cheapest pieces of insurance the firm buys, far cheaper per dollar of protection than the prime-brokerage and counterparty risk we discussed in [cash, treasury, and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk). The founder who balks at the premium has simply not priced the tail.

*Compliance is the only line item on the budget where spending less can cost you the entire company — and that is exactly why it is the line item to get right first.*

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the day you decide to manage other people's money under your own name, and it matters most in the moments you least expect — the analyst who took one call, the marketing deck with one unsupported number, the employee who traded before he asked. A compliance program is the system that turns those moments from catastrophes into contained, documented, survivable incidents. It is owned by a CCO with the authority to say no, written in a manual and a code that describe what you actually do, enforced through pre-clearance and restricted lists and order-system blocks, and proven by the records an examiner can demand. None of it makes you money. All of it keeps you in business — which, since a dead fund earns nothing, is the precondition for ever making money at all.

Build the program before you launch, size it to your AUM and your strategy's MNPI risk, give your CCO real teeth, and treat every log as evidence you may one day need to prove your innocence. The founders who do this disappear into the boring, uneventful majority who never make a compliance headline. The ones who skip it show up in the next version of Figure 6. *And, once more: this is an explanation, not legal advice — retain real securities counsel for the rules that bind your specific fund.*
