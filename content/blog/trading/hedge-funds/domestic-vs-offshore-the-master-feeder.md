> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Different investors carry different tax problems, so a hedge fund builds a different door for each one: US taxable money enters through a Delaware limited partnership, while US tax-exempt money and non-US money enter through an offshore corporate feeder (usually a Cayman exempted company). The master-feeder structure pools both feeders into one trading "master" fund so the manager runs a single book.
>
> - A Delaware LP is a pass-through: gains flow to US taxable investors and they pay their own tax once. That is exactly what those investors want.
> - A pension or endowment fears **UBTI** on leverage, and a non-US investor fears **ECI** plus a 30% **withholding** tax. A Cayman corporate feeder "blocks" both — it is a tax wall, not a hiding place.
> - The master-feeder pools both feeders into one master fund so there is one portfolio, one set of trades, one risk book — and P&L is split back to each feeder by ownership percentage.
> - Offshore is about **tax-neutrality, not secrecy.** The Cayman feeder pays no entity-level tax so the structure does not add a second layer of tax; it does not let a US person dodge US tax.
> - The one number to remember: the offshore layer can add **on the order of \$150,000–\$200,000 a year** in extra run-rate cost, so you only build it when there is real tax-exempt or non-US money to serve.

Maya had been running the Meridian Fund for fourteen months. Long/short equity, a clean Delaware limited partnership, a respectable Sharpe, and a story that allocators seemed to like. So when a mid-sized public pension's investment staff invited her in for a second meeting, she let herself believe the hard part was over. They liked the returns. They liked her process. They wanted to allocate around \$40,000,000.

Then their operational due diligence analyst asked one question that ended the conversation: "You're a Delaware LP, correct? We can't invest in that directly — we'd take on UBTI on your leveraged book, and our tax counsel won't sign off. Do you have a Cayman feeder?" Maya did not. She had never heard the term UBTI. She had built a perfectly good fund with exactly one door, and that door was the wrong shape for \$40,000,000 of the money she most wanted. The pension allocated elsewhere, to a competitor whose returns were worse but whose structure was right.

This is the most common avoidable mistake a first-time founder makes: building a single onshore vehicle, then discovering that half the capital they want to raise legally cannot walk through it. The fix is not exotic and it is not shady. It is the **master-feeder structure** — the standard architecture of the global hedge fund industry — and the reason it exists is almost entirely about tax. Figure 1 is the whole idea on one page: two feeders for two kinds of investor, pooled into one master fund that does all the trading.

![Graph showing US taxable investors entering an onshore Delaware LP feeder, US tax-exempt and non-US investors entering an offshore Cayman feeder, both feeders flowing into a master fund that trades in the market](/imgs/blogs/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder-1.png)

By the end of this post you will understand exactly which investor needs which vehicle and why, how money and P&L move through the structure, what the offshore layer actually costs, and — just as important — what offshore funds are *not*. The single biggest misconception in this whole topic is that "offshore" means "secret" or "tax-dodge." It means neither. It means **tax-neutral**, and that distinction is the difference between a structure your allocators' lawyers will bless and one they will run from.

## Foundations: onshore, offshore, and the tax words you need

Before any of the structure makes sense, you need a small vocabulary. None of these terms are hard once you see what problem each one names. I will define every one from zero, because the whole topic is just these words arranged into a decision.

**Onshore and offshore.** "Onshore" means a vehicle organized inside the United States — for a US hedge fund, almost always a **Delaware limited partnership**. "Offshore" means a vehicle organized outside the US, in a jurisdiction that levies no income tax on the fund itself — for hedge funds, overwhelmingly the **Cayman Islands**, with the British Virgin Islands, Bermuda, and Ireland/Luxembourg as alternatives for particular investor bases. "Offshore" is a geographic word that has acquired a sinister connotation it does not deserve. All it means here is "organized in a no-tax jurisdiction so the fund layer does not add a tax."

**Delaware limited partnership (LP).** A partnership formed under Delaware law. It has a **general partner** (the GP — controlled by the manager, with management authority and unlimited liability) and **limited partners** (the LPs — the investors, who are passive and whose liability is capped at what they invested). The defining feature for our purposes is that an LP is a **pass-through** (or "flow-through") entity for US tax: the partnership itself pays no income tax. Instead, each item of income, gain, loss, and deduction "flows through" to the partners in proportion to their interest, and each partner reports it on their own return. The fund issues each LP a **Schedule K-1** each year listing their share. One layer of tax, paid by the investor. We will return to why this is exactly right for one kind of investor and exactly wrong for another.

**Cayman exempted company.** A company incorporated in the Cayman Islands under the law that lets a company be "exempted" — meaning it does no business with Cayman residents and is exempt from any (currently nonexistent) Cayman income tax for a guaranteed period. To a US investor's eye it behaves like a corporation: investors are **shareholders**, not partners, and they own **shares** in the fund rather than a partnership interest. A corporation is *not* a pass-through. Income is earned at the company level and does not flow through to shareholders until the company distributes it. That single property — the corporate wall between fund-level income and the investor — is the entire reason the offshore feeder exists. It is the thing that "blocks."

**Tax-exempt investor.** An investor that does not pay US income tax on its investment returns: a **public or corporate pension plan**, a **university endowment**, a **charitable foundation**, an **IRA or other retirement vehicle**. These are the largest pools of allocatable capital in the world. They are tax-exempt by statute — but, crucially, that exemption has a hole in it, and the hole is named UBTI.

**UBTI — unrelated business taxable income.** A tax-exempt organization is exempt from tax on income related to its exempt purpose and on ordinary investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains). But it is *not* exempt on income from an "unrelated trade or business" — and, under the tax rules, income that a tax-exempt investor earns on a **leveraged** ("debt-financed") investment is treated as **UBTI** and is taxable to the otherwise tax-exempt investor. A hedge fund that borrows to lever its book, or that shorts (which is treated as a form of leverage), generates exactly this kind of debt-financed income. If a pension invests directly in a leveraged Delaware LP, its share of that leveraged income becomes UBTI, and the pension — which exists precisely so its returns are *not* taxed — suddenly owes tax and has to file. Its tax counsel will say no. This is the wall Maya hit.

**ECI — effectively connected income.** ECI is income that a non-US person earns that is "effectively connected" with a US trade or business. A non-US investor who invests directly into a US partnership that is engaged in a US trade or business can be treated as engaged in that business themselves, which drags them into the US tax net: they would owe US tax on the ECI and have to file a US return. Most pure trading of securities for a fund's own account benefits from a "trading safe harbor" that keeps it from being a US trade or business — but the safe harbor is narrow, certain strategies (lending, origination, some derivatives, real estate) fall outside it, and no sensible non-US investor wants to bet their entire US-filing exposure on staying inside a safe harbor. They want a structure that removes the question entirely.

**Withholding.** Separate from ECI, the US imposes a **30% withholding tax** (reducible by treaty) on certain US-source payments to non-US persons — most relevantly **US-source dividends**. If a non-US investor holds a US partnership interest, US dividends flowing through can be subject to that 30% bite. A corporation organized offshore changes the analysis: the investor holds shares in a non-US company, not a flow-through interest in a US partnership.

**Blocker.** A "blocker" is any entity — almost always a corporation — interposed between an investor and an investment specifically to **block** an unwanted tax characteristic from flowing through. A C-corporation blocks the flow-through of UBTI or ECI because a corporation is not a pass-through: the bad income stops at the corporate wall. The offshore Cayman feeder is, functionally, a blocker that tax-exempt and non-US investors share. It "blocks" UBTI for the pension and "blocks" ECI/withholding exposure for the foreign investor in one entity.

**Feeder.** A **feeder fund** is an investor-facing vehicle that does no trading itself; it exists only to collect capital from a particular class of investor and "feed" that capital into a central trading vehicle. A fund has one feeder per investor type — typically one onshore feeder (the Delaware LP) and one offshore feeder (the Cayman company).

**Master fund.** The **master fund** is the central pooled vehicle that actually holds the portfolio and does all the trading. Both feeders invest essentially all of their cash into the master. The master is the single book. It is usually itself a Cayman entity, structured to be tax-transparent so it does not add a layer of tax — but its job is operational, not investor-facing: the master is where the manager trades, where the prime-brokerage accounts sit, and where the P&L is generated.

Two more practical points before the routing. First, **why Cayman specifically** and not some other no-tax jurisdiction. There is no single law that says hedge funds must use the Cayman Islands; Cayman won the market on its merits. It is tax-neutral (no income, capital gains, or withholding tax at the entity level). It has a deep, decades-old ecosystem of fund-formation lawyers, administrators, professional directors, and auditors who do nothing but service funds, so the paperwork is fast and standardized. Its courts apply English common law, which US and European institutional investors trust. And it has a credible regulator — the **Cayman Islands Monetary Authority (CIMA)** — under which most funds register and file audited financials, so the jurisdiction reads as "regulated and tested," not "wild west," to an allocator's lawyers. Bermuda, the British Virgin Islands, Ireland, and Luxembourg serve particular niches (Ireland and Luxembourg especially for European-distributed funds under the EU's UCITS and AIFMD regimes), but for a US-managed hedge fund raising globally, Cayman is the path of least resistance.

Second, the **legal nature of what an investor receives** differs by feeder, and it matters. In the onshore Delaware LP, an investor receives a **limited partnership interest** — a capital-account balance that goes up and down with the fund and a K-1 each year. In the offshore Cayman company, an investor receives **shares** — usually a non-voting participating share class whose value is the fund's NAV per share. A fund will commonly issue **multiple share classes or series** in the offshore feeder (and multiple sub-classes of LP interest onshore) to handle different fee terms, different lock-ups, founders-class discounts, and the mechanics of charging the incentive fee fairly across investors who came in at different times — a problem we will return to, because it is one of the few places the corporate feeder is genuinely more fiddly than the partnership.

Hold those eleven words and these two practical notes. The rest of this post is just routing each kind of money to the vehicle that solves its specific problem, and then pooling the vehicles so the manager only has to trade once. Figure 2 is the routing table in full.

## Why US taxable investors use a Delaware LP

Start with the simplest case, because it explains why you cannot just make everyone use the offshore vehicle.

A US taxable investor — a wealthy individual, a family office, a taxable corporation — pays tax on their investment income. What they want from a fund vehicle is to be taxed **once**, **as if they held the underlying positions themselves**, and to keep the *character* of their income. Long-term capital gains, for instance, are taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income; qualified dividends get a preferential rate. A pass-through partnership preserves all of that: the K-1 the fund sends each year tells the investor their share of long-term gains, short-term gains, dividends, interest, and so on, each keeping its character, and the investor pays the right rate on each at their own marginal level. The partnership pays nothing.

Now picture what would happen if you routed a US taxable investor through a Cayman corporation instead. The corporation earns the income. A US person who owns shares in a foreign corporation that is mostly holding passive investments runs straight into the **passive foreign investment company (PFIC)** rules, or the **controlled foreign corporation (CFC)** rules — punitive regimes designed to stop US persons from parking income in offshore corporations to defer or avoid US tax. Under PFIC, the US investor can face an interest charge on deferred income and lose the favorable character of capital gains, turning everything into ordinary income with a penalty. Far from helping, the offshore corporate vehicle actively *hurts* a US taxable investor.

So the rule is symmetric and tidy: **a US taxable investor belongs in the pass-through Delaware LP**, where they are taxed once, at the right rates, on a K-1 — and they belong nowhere near the offshore corporation, which exists to solve a problem they do not have and would create new ones if they used it.

Why Delaware specifically, among US states? The same reason Delaware dominates US corporate law generally: a mature, predictable body of partnership and LLC law, a specialized business court (the Court of Chancery) whose judges decide commercial disputes without juries, strong contractual freedom (Delaware lets the partnership agreement override most default rules, so the GP can write the economics it wants), and a formation process that is fast and cheap. A fund can be a Delaware LP or a Delaware LLC; the LP is traditional, with the manager's GP entity holding management authority and the investors as limited partners, but the substance is the same pass-through either way. The manager structures the **general partner** as a separate limited-liability entity so that the unlimited liability that attaches to a general partner is absorbed by a shell rather than by the founder personally — a detail that matters enormously the day something goes wrong. Note too that pass-through is a *federal* concept; investors may still owe **state** tax on their share, and a fund with investors or activities in multiple states can create state-filing footprints. But none of that changes the core: one layer of federal tax, paid by the investor, character preserved. That is the deal a taxable investor signed up for, and the Delaware LP delivers it with a body of law everyone in the industry already understands.

#### Worked example: a US taxable investor's tax on a leveraged year

Suppose the Meridian master fund returns 15% gross in a year, partly using leverage, and an individual investor — call him David — has \$2,000,000 in the onshore Delaware LP. His gross share of the gain is \$300,000. Say the K-1 breaks it into \$200,000 of long-term capital gain and \$100,000 of short-term gain and ordinary income from the leveraged and short legs. The fund pays no tax. David pays his own tax: roughly 20% on the \$200,000 long-term portion (\$40,000) and roughly 37% on the \$100,000 ordinary portion (\$37,000), for about \$77,000 total. He keeps roughly \$223,000 of the \$300,000.

Critically, the fact that the fund used leverage does **not** create a separate tax problem for David. He is taxable anyway; UBTI is a concept that only matters to an investor who is *supposed* to be tax-exempt. For a taxable individual, leverage just means more of the income is short-term/ordinary — a rate question, not an eligibility question. The Delaware LP is exactly the right home for him.

*A taxable investor wants one layer of tax with the character preserved, and a pass-through LP delivers precisely that — leverage changes his rate, not his right to be there.*

## Why tax-exempt and non-US investors need offshore

Now the two investor types that broke Maya's pension deal — and that, between them, control the large majority of the institutional capital a serious fund wants to raise.

### The pension's problem: UBTI on leverage

A tax-exempt investor's entire reason for existing — a pension paying retirees, an endowment funding scholarships — is that its investment returns are not taxed. UBTI is the exception that bites. The moment a tax-exempt investor's money is in a **leveraged** vehicle, its share of the debt-financed income becomes unrelated business taxable income, and the tax-exempt entity owes tax and must file. For a pension, that is not merely an expense; it is a governance and reputational landmine. Their investment policy and their tax counsel will simply forbid investing in any vehicle that generates UBTI.

Almost every hedge fund uses leverage or shorts. A long/short equity book that is 150% long and 80% short is leveraged. So a tax-exempt investor cannot, as a practical matter, invest into the onshore Delaware LP. They need the **corporate blocker**: a Cayman exempted company. They buy shares in the Cayman feeder. The Cayman feeder, a corporation, earns its share of the master's leveraged income at the corporate level. Because a corporation is not a flow-through, the leveraged income does not pass through to the pension as UBTI — it stops at the corporate wall. The pension simply owns shares in a foreign corporation and receives its return as the change in its share value, with no UBTI to report. The corporate feeder has *blocked* the bad income. And because the Cayman company pays no Cayman income tax, no second layer of entity tax is created either: the structure is **tax-neutral** for the pension, not tax-adding.

This is the single most important sentence in the whole topic: **the offshore corporate feeder turns a flow-through of toxic UBTI into a clean equity investment in a no-tax company.** That is what "blocking" means.

#### Worked example: UBTI on a leveraged position, onshore vs offshore

Take the same Meridian fund and a public pension, the (illustrative) Statewide Retirement System, considering a \$30,000,000 allocation. The master is running roughly 2× gross exposure. Suppose in a given year the pension's \$30,000,000 share earns \$3,600,000 of net gain, and the fund's accountants determine that, because much of the book is debt-financed, about \$2,400,000 of that would be characterized as debt-financed UBTI if the pension held a flow-through interest.

If the pension invests directly into the Delaware LP: that \$2,400,000 is UBTI. At a roughly 21% corporate-equivalent UBTI rate, the pension owes about \$504,000 in tax it would otherwise never pay — and it now has a federal filing obligation. Its return is reduced and its tax-exempt status has been partially undone for that investment.

If instead the pension buys \$30,000,000 of shares in the Cayman feeder: the feeder, a corporation, earns the income at the entity level. No UBTI flows to the pension. The pension's tax is **\$0**, exactly as its exempt status intends. The Cayman company pays no Cayman tax. The pension's full \$3,600,000 of economic gain is preserved, and its tax counsel signs off.

*For a tax-exempt investor, the offshore feeder is not a luxury — it is the difference between a \$0 tax bill and a six-figure one on income they are statutorily meant to keep.*

### The foreign investor's problem: ECI and withholding

A non-US investor — a foreign family office, an overseas pension, a sovereign fund, an offshore bank's clients — has a different but parallel fear. They do not want to be dragged into the US tax system. If they invest directly into a US partnership that is found to be engaged in a US trade or business, they can be treated as engaged in that business and owe US tax on the **effectively connected income**, with a US filing obligation. And even where the trading safe harbor protects them from ECI, US-source dividends flowing through the partnership can be hit with the **30% withholding** tax. Either way, direct investment into the onshore Delaware LP exposes them to US tax friction they will not accept.

The same Cayman feeder solves it. By buying shares in a non-US corporation rather than a flow-through interest in a US partnership, the foreign investor holds an offshore equity investment. The corporate feeder absorbs the US tax characteristics at its level. The foreign investor is insulated from ECI and avoids the partnership-level withholding exposure on the flow-through. The same blocker that protects the pension from UBTI protects the foreign investor from ECI and withholding — which is exactly why both share one offshore feeder.

#### Worked example: withholding for a non-US investor

Suppose a Swiss family office invests \$10,000,000, and over a year the portion of the fund's return attributable to US-source dividends on that stake is \$200,000. If they held a flow-through US partnership interest, a 30% withholding tax — \$60,000 — could be applied to those US dividends (treaty relief might reduce it, but only with paperwork and never to zero on dividends). Routed instead through the Cayman corporate feeder, the dividends are earned at the corporate level; the foreign shareholder's return arrives as the change in share value of a non-US company, and the direct partnership-level dividend-withholding exposure is removed. The investor keeps the economics and stays out of the US tax net.

*A non-US investor will trade a slightly more expensive structure for the certainty of never having to file a US return — the corporate feeder buys that certainty.*

It is worth being precise about why the corporate blocker works where the partnership does not, because it is the crux of the whole design. A US partnership is **fiscally transparent**: for tax purposes it does not exist as a separate taxpayer, so whatever the partnership does, its partners are treated as doing. If the partnership is engaged in a US trade or business, the foreign partner is too; if the partnership earns UBTI-generating debt-financed income, the tax-exempt partner earns it too. A corporation is the opposite — it is a **separate taxpayer**, an opaque wall. Whatever the corporation does stays at the corporation; the shareholder owns only the share. So interposing a corporation between the master and the investor converts a transparent flow-through (which carries the bad characteristic straight to the investor) into an opaque equity holding (which does not). That is the entire mechanism. The corporation is offshore only so that the corporate wall does not itself add a layer of corporate income tax — a US C-corporation blocker would block the bad income but pay US corporate tax on it, defeating the purpose for these investors. Cayman gives you the wall without the tax. This is also why the structure is genuinely *neutral* rather than *advantageous*: it removes a penalty the investor would otherwise suffer; it does not manufacture a benefit they could not get by holding the assets directly in a world without UBTI and ECI rules.

There are edge cases worth knowing exist, even if they sit outside a first launch. Some US tax-exempt investors who are especially UBTI-sensitive will only invest through a feeder that is itself a "blocker corporation" *and* will scrutinize whether the fund uses any leverage at all. Some strategies that fall outside the trading safe harbor — direct lending, origination, certain real-estate or physical-commodity exposure — need extra blocker corporations even for the offshore feeder, because the safe harbor that normally protects securities trading does not reach them. And US insurance companies sometimes access hedge funds through **insurance-dedicated funds (IDFs)** for their own tax reasons. The common thread is always the same: the investor's tax position dictates the wrapper, and the manager's job is to offer the wrapper rather than to argue the investor out of their tax counsel's view. You will never win that argument, and you should not try.

## The master-feeder structure

Here is the question that makes the structure click. You now have two feeders — a Delaware LP for the taxable money and a Cayman company for the tax-exempt and non-US money. If each feeder traded its own book, you would be running **two funds**: two portfolios, two sets of trades, two prime-brokerage relationships, two risk systems, and — worst of all — the two would inevitably drift apart. The same trade idea would get slightly different fills, slightly different sizing, slightly different timing across the two books. Investors in one feeder would earn a different return than identical investors in the other, purely because of operational friction. That is intolerable: the whole promise of the fund is that everyone gets the manager's skill, applied the same way.

The master-feeder structure solves this with one move. Both feeders invest essentially all of their cash into a single **master fund**, and the master does all the trading. There is one portfolio, one set of positions, one set of prime brokers, one risk book. The feeders are just holding companies that own a slice of the master in proportion to the capital they contributed. The manager runs the master and only the master; the feeders are administrative shells that issue interests or shares to investors and pass the cash up.

Mechanically, money climbs and P&L descends. Figure 3 traces the full path:

![Pipeline showing investors subscribing cash, feeders receiving USD and issuing shares, feeders investing all cash into the master, the master trading one book, daily P&L marked at the master, P&L split by ownership percentage, each feeder NAV moving, and investor NAV updating](/imgs/blogs/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder-3.png)

1. An investor subscribes — wires cash to the appropriate feeder (taxable to the LP, tax-exempt/non-US to the Cayman company) and receives a partnership interest or shares.
2. The feeder takes that cash and immediately invests almost all of it into the master fund, receiving an interest in the master.
3. The master pools every feeder's cash and trades the single book.
4. The master's portfolio generates daily P&L, marked at the master level.
5. That P&L is allocated to each feeder **by its ownership percentage of the master** — if the onshore feeder owns 60% of the master and the offshore feeder owns 40%, they get 60% and 40% of the day's gain or loss.
6. Each feeder's net asset value (NAV) rises or falls by its allocated share.
7. Each investor's NAV inside their feeder moves with the feeder.

Because every feeder owns the same master, every investor earns the *same gross return* on the *same portfolio*, regardless of which door they came in through. Differences between investors come only from fee class (a founders-class investor pays less) and from the small structural costs particular to a feeder — never from the manager trading two different books.

#### Worked example: allocating master-fund P&L between the two feeders

The Meridian master fund holds \$100,000,000 and returns a clean \$12,000,000 (12%) in a year. The onshore Delaware LP feeder contributed \$60,000,000 and the offshore Cayman feeder contributed \$40,000,000 — so the onshore feeder owns 60% of the master and the offshore feeder owns 40%.

The \$12,000,000 gain is allocated by ownership share: the onshore feeder is allocated \$7,200,000 (60% of \$12,000,000) and the offshore feeder is allocated \$4,800,000 (40%). Each feeder's NAV rises by its slice. Inside the onshore feeder, David's \$2,000,000 interest (one-thirtieth of that feeder's \$60,000,000) is credited with his proportionate share, about \$240,000 before fees; inside the offshore feeder, the pension's \$30,000,000 of shares (three-quarters of that feeder's \$40,000,000) is credited with about \$3,600,000 before fees. Same 12% gross return for everyone — only the door differs.

If the year had been a 12% *loss* instead, the identical arithmetic runs with a minus sign: the onshore feeder absorbs \$7,200,000 of loss, the offshore feeder \$4,800,000, in exact proportion to ownership. The master never plays favorites; it just splits by percentage.

*One book, one return, split by ownership percentage — that is the entire economic engine of the master-feeder, and it is why investors can trust they are all getting the same fund.*

It helps to see the whole thing as a stack of legal layers between the investor and the actual market position, because every layer is a distinct entity with its own books, its own audit, and its own role. Figure 7 lays out the five layers.

![Stack diagram of five layers from the investor through the feeder fund, the master fund, the prime broker account, down to the position in the market](/imgs/blogs/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder-7.png)

Read it top to bottom. The **investor** holds either an LP interest (onshore) or shares (offshore). One layer down, the **feeder fund** is the entity the investor actually owns a piece of — the Delaware LP or the Cayman company. Below that, the **master fund** is what the feeders own a piece of; it is the pooled trading vehicle and the place the portfolio lives. Below the master, the **prime broker account** is where the master's cash, financing, custody, and short-sale borrows sit — the master does not trade directly with the world, it trades through one or more prime brokers. And at the bottom, the **position** itself — a specific long or short in the market. Money is contributed at the top and works its way down to become a position; value flows back up the same five layers when the position is marked. Each layer is a real entity a regulator, an auditor, and an allocator's operational due diligence team can inspect — which is precisely why the structure is the opposite of opaque. A fund that could not show you a clean chain from investor to position would fail diligence; the master-feeder is designed to make that chain legible.

A subtlety the diagram hides: subscriptions and redemptions move through the same plumbing in reverse. When a new investor subscribes \$5,000,000 to the onshore feeder mid-quarter, that feeder takes the cash and subscribes it up into the master, buying more of the master at the current NAV. The onshore feeder's ownership percentage of the master ticks up; the offshore feeder's ticks down correspondingly, even though the offshore feeder did nothing — its slice of a now-larger pie is a smaller fraction. When an investor redeems, the chain runs the other way: the investor redeems from their feeder, the feeder redeems a matching amount from the master (forcing the master to raise cash, sometimes by selling positions), and the cash flows back down. This is why **liquidity terms have to line up across the layers**: the feeder cannot offer monthly redemptions if the master can only be redeemed quarterly, or the feeder would owe cash it cannot get. Aligning lock-ups, notice periods, and gates across feeder and master is a real part of designing the structure, not an afterthought.

The trickiest mechanical wrinkle lives in the **offshore corporate feeder's incentive fee**. In the onshore partnership, the incentive fee is easy: it is an **allocation** of profit to the GP's capital account, computed per-partner against each partner's own high-water mark, because a partnership tracks a separate capital account for every partner. A corporation does not have per-shareholder capital accounts — everyone in a share class owns identical shares with one NAV per share. So if the fund simply charged the incentive fee at the share-class level, an investor who bought in *after* a drawdown would unfairly subsidize the high-water marks of investors who bought in at the peak (or vice versa). The industry solves this two ways. **Series accounting** issues a new *series* of shares for every subscription date, each with its own high-water mark, and consolidates them into the main class once they are above water — clean but operationally heavy, since the administrator tracks dozens of series. **Equalization** keeps one share class but uses an "equalization credit/debit" mechanism (an equalization factor, sometimes a depreciation deposit) to true-up each subscriber so everyone effectively pays incentive fees only on their own gains above their own entry point — elegant in concept, notoriously confusing in the documents. Either way, the *point* is the same fairness the partnership gets for free: each investor pays the incentive fee only on their own profit above their own high-water mark. This is the single biggest reason a fund administrator earns its fee on the offshore side.

There is a deliberate design choice hidden in "the master is a Cayman entity, made tax-transparent." The master is typically organized offshore so it sits in a no-tax jurisdiction, but it elects to be treated as a **partnership (tax-transparent)** for US purposes, so that income flowing up to the *onshore* Delaware LP feeder retains its pass-through character for David and the other taxable investors. The offshore *feeder*, by contrast, is a corporation precisely so it blocks. The same master can feed a transparent flow-through up to taxable investors and a corporate block down to tax-exempt and foreign investors, because transparency and blocking happen at the feeder layer, not the master. That is the quiet elegance of the design: one trading entity, two different tax treatments layered on top, each tuned to the investor it serves.

## Standalone vs master-feeder vs mini-master

You do not always need the full structure. There are three common architectures, and the right one depends entirely on who your investors are.

**Standalone fund.** A single fund vehicle with no master. If you will only ever raise from US taxable investors, a single Delaware LP is perfectly correct, cheaper, and simpler. Many small, friends-and-family launches start exactly here. The cost is that you have built one door, and it is the wrong shape for tax-exempt and non-US money. The day a pension or a foreign family office wants in, you have to restructure — which is disruptive and expensive mid-life. Maya's mistake was choosing a standalone when she had institutional ambitions.

A purely offshore standalone — a single Cayman fund with no US feeder — is the mirror image: correct if you will only ever raise from non-US and US tax-exempt investors and have no US taxable money, common for funds marketed primarily outside the US.

**Master-feeder.** The full structure of Figure 1: an onshore Delaware LP feeder *and* an offshore Cayman feeder, both feeding one master. This is the standard for any US fund that wants to raise from all three investor types. It is the default you build when you are serious about institutional capital. Figure 4 contrasts the standalone with the master-feeder directly — the standalone reaches one kind of money; the master-feeder reaches all three through one book.

![Before-after diagram contrasting a standalone Delaware LP that serves only US taxable investors and blocks tax-exempt and non-US money with a master-feeder that serves all three investor types through one trading book](/imgs/blogs/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder-4.png)

**Mini-master.** A lighter variant. Instead of three separate entities, the **onshore Delaware LP doubles as the master**: the offshore Cayman feeder invests *into the Delaware LP*, and US taxable investors also invest directly into that same Delaware LP. So the Delaware LP plays two roles at once — it is both the onshore feeder for taxable investors and the master that the offshore feeder feeds into. You save one entity (no separate standalone master), which trims setup and ongoing cost, while still giving tax-exempt and non-US investors their corporate blocker via the Cayman feeder. The mini-master is popular with smaller funds that want full investor reach without paying for a third entity. The trade-off is slightly less clean separation and some complexity if the fund later grows into the full three-entity master-feeder; some managers also prefer the full structure because keeping the master separate from any feeder simplifies certain reporting and side-pocket mechanics.

The decision rule is simple. Only US taxable money for the foreseeable future, want minimum cost: **standalone Delaware LP**. Only non-US and US tax-exempt money: **standalone Cayman fund**. All three investor types, cost-sensitive, smaller: **mini-master**. All three investor types, institutional ambitions, want the cleanest separation: **full master-feeder**. The mistake is never "I built a master-feeder I didn't need" — it is "I built a standalone and then a \$40,000,000 allocation needed a door I hadn't built."

To make the choice concrete: a manager launching with \$15,000,000 of their own and friends-and-family money, all US taxable, should launch a standalone Delaware LP and spend the saved \$175,000 a year on a better data feed and an extra analyst. A manager launching with \$15,000,000 of taxable money but with a credible pipeline of pension and foreign interest — say a seeder dangling \$50,000,000 that happens to be a foreign institution — should launch the master-feeder (or at least a mini-master) from day one, because the offshore door is the *precondition* for the very capital that justifies its cost. The wrong move in either direction is expensive: an unneeded offshore layer bleeds a tiny fund, and a missing one forecloses the institutional money that would have made the fund viable. The judgment is about your *capital pipeline*, not your *strategy* — a market-neutral fund and a global-macro fund with the same investor mix make the same structural choice.

## The cost and complexity of going offshore

The offshore layer is not free, and the honest reason most small funds launch standalone is cost. Adding a Cayman feeder (and, in the full structure, a separate master) roughly doubles several of your recurring expense lines and adds a few you did not have at all.

What the offshore layer adds, in round, illustrative US terms as of 2026:

- **A second formation bill.** One-time legal formation for a US-only standalone runs roughly \$50,000 at the low end; building the full master-feeder — a Delaware LP, a Cayman exempted company, a Cayman master, plus the offering documents and constitutional documents for each — can push one-time legal toward the \$100,000–\$150,000+ range. The incremental offshore legal work alone is commonly \$30,000–\$75,000.
- **A second audit.** Each fund entity is audited annually. A standalone pays one audit (~\$30,000–\$60,000); a master-feeder audits the consolidated structure, which adds materially — call it \$30,000–\$50,000+ of incremental annual audit.
- **Cayman directors.** A Cayman fund needs independent directors (often two, frequently provided by a professional directorship firm). Director fees commonly run \$15,000–\$40,000+ a year combined.
- **A Cayman registered office, CIMA registration, and regulatory fees.** A Cayman fund typically registers with the Cayman Islands Monetary Authority (CIMA), maintains a registered office, and pays annual government and CIMA fees — on the order of \$25,000–\$40,000+ a year all-in.
- **More administration.** The fund administrator now strikes NAV for multiple entities and does the inter-entity bookkeeping, raising the admin bill.

Add it up and the offshore layer commonly adds **on the order of \$150,000–\$200,000 a year** of incremental run-rate cost versus a single Delaware LP, plus the extra one-time legal. Figure 6 puts the two side by side.

![Bar chart comparing the annual recurring costs of a standalone Delaware LP and a master-feeder across fund admin, audit, legal and compliance, Cayman directors, and Cayman registered office and CIMA fees, with the master-feeder totaling roughly 175 thousand USD more per year](/imgs/blogs/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder-6.png)

#### Worked example: the extra cost delta of the offshore layer

Suppose the Meridian standalone Delaware LP runs an annual fixed cost of about \$190,000: roughly \$60,000 of admin, \$40,000 of audit, and \$90,000 of outside legal and compliance. Building the full master-feeder pushes those lines up and adds two new ones: admin rises to about \$105,000 (multiple entities), audit to about \$75,000 (consolidated), legal and compliance to about \$120,000, plus about \$35,000 of Cayman directors and about \$30,000 of Cayman registered office and CIMA fees. New total: about \$365,000. The incremental cost of the offshore layer is roughly **\$175,000 a year**.

Now weigh it against the prize. Recall that to cover \$1,200,000 of fixed costs from a 1.5% management fee you need about \$80,000,000 of AUM (\$1,200,000 ÷ 0.015). The \$175,000 of incremental offshore cost is, by itself, covered by the management fee on roughly \$11,700,000 of additional AUM (\$175,000 ÷ 0.015). Maya's lost pension ticket was \$40,000,000. The offshore layer that would have won it pays for itself many times over the moment it lets in a single institutional allocation of that size — but it is pure dead weight if no tax-exempt or non-US money ever shows up.

*The offshore layer is an investment that only pays off if it lets in capital that could not otherwise come — build it when the institutional money is real, not before.*

One caution on the arithmetic above: these are illustrative ranges, and the real numbers swing widely with strategy and complexity. A simple long/short equity master-feeder sits near the low end; a fund trading swaps, holding illiquid positions in side pockets, or running multiple share classes and series can push the offshore overhead well past \$200,000 a year once you add the extra audit work, the legal hours, and a more expensive administrator. Get a written fee estimate from each service provider before you commit — the difference between a quote and a guess is often \$50,000 a year.

That is the genuine founder's tension. Build the offshore layer too early and you are burning \$175,000 a year of a tiny fund's budget to serve investors you do not yet have. Build it too late and you lose the \$40,000,000 allocation that needed it, the way Maya did. The usual resolution: launch lean if your day-one capital is friends, family, and your own taxable money; have your counsel pre-design the offshore feeder so it can be bolted on quickly; and stand it up the moment a tax-exempt or non-US allocator gets serious. Several "fund-in-a-box" providers and umbrella platforms also let a small manager rent a Cayman feeder cheaply at first and graduate to a standalone structure later.

## Common misconceptions

This topic attracts more myths than almost any other in fund formation, because the word "offshore" carries decades of bad television. Here are the ones that matter, named and corrected.

**"Offshore means shady, secret, or illegal."** This is the big one, and it is simply false. A Cayman hedge fund feeder is a fully disclosed, regulated, audited entity. Its US investors report it on their US returns; its manager reports it to the SEC; the fund registers with CIMA, files audited financials, and complies with US anti-money-laundering, FATCA, and CRS information-reporting regimes that share account data with tax authorities worldwide. There is no secrecy: the IRS and other tax authorities know exactly who owns what. "Offshore" here means "organized in a no-tax jurisdiction so the fund layer does not stack a second tax on top," nothing more. Cayman is chosen because it has a deep bench of fund-services lawyers, administrators, and directors, a tested legal system, and tax-neutrality — not because anything is hidden.

**"Offshore lets a US person avoid US tax."** No. A US taxable person who tries to route through the offshore corporate feeder runs straight into the PFIC and CFC anti-deferral rules, which are designed precisely to stop that and which make the offshore vehicle *worse* for them, not better. US persons belong in the onshore Delaware LP and pay their full US tax there. The offshore feeder helps *tax-exempt* US investors (whose problem is UBTI, not avoidance — they are supposed to be untaxed) and *non-US* investors (who are not US taxpayers in the first place). It does nothing for a US taxable person except create problems.

**"Every fund needs a Cayman feeder from day one."** No. If your capital will only ever be US taxable money, a standalone Delaware LP is correct and far cheaper. The offshore layer costs roughly \$150,000–\$200,000 a year; spending that to serve investors you do not have is a waste. You add the offshore feeder when real tax-exempt or non-US money is in sight — not as a reflex.

**"Offshore investors get a different (better or worse) deal."** No. The master-feeder exists precisely so every investor earns the *same* gross return on the *same* book. The feeders own the master in proportion to capital and split P&L by ownership percentage. Differences come only from fee class and the small structural costs of a particular feeder — never from the manager favoring one book.

**"The offshore feeder is where the manager keeps their own money / hides fees."** No. The feeders are passive shells; all trading and all economics happen at the master, and fees are charged transparently per the fund documents and disclosed in audited financials. The structure is about *investor tax position*, not about routing economics to the manager.

**"You can just convert a standalone to a master-feeder for free later."** It is possible but not free or seamless: restructuring mid-life means new entities, new documents, investor consents, potential tax and accounting consequences, and legal bills. It is far cheaper to have counsel design the structure so the offshore feeder can be added cleanly than to bolt it on under time pressure when an allocator is waiting. Plan the door before you need it.

## How it plays out in the real world

The master-feeder is not a clever trick a few funds use; it is the **default architecture of the global hedge fund industry**. The vast majority of US-managed hedge funds that take institutional capital run some version of the Delaware-LP-plus-Cayman-feeder structure, and the Cayman Islands is the dominant domicile for the offshore side — home to many thousands of registered investment funds — precisely because of its tax-neutrality, its mature fund-services ecosystem, and its tested law. When an industry that manages on the order of **\$4.5–5.0 trillion** (HFR/Preqin/BarclayHedge estimates, 2025–2026) overwhelmingly converges on one structure, it is because the structure solves a real, recurring problem: serving taxable, tax-exempt, and non-US investors out of one book without taxing any of them twice.

Watch how it determines who can invest. A US public pension's investment staff might love your returns, but their **operational due diligence** team — a separate, veto-wielding function — checks the structure, and "do you have a vehicle that doesn't generate UBTI for us?" is one of the first questions. A fund with no offshore feeder is, for a leveraged strategy, simply ineligible for most tax-exempt institutional money, no matter how good the numbers. This is exactly the wall Maya hit, and it is why a founder with institutional ambitions builds the offshore layer before the institutions arrive, not after. The service-provider stack that supports the structure — administrator, auditor, prime broker, Cayman directors — is itself a thing allocators scrutinize, which is why understanding [the service-provider stack](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor) matters as much as the legal skeleton.

Watch, too, how the structure interacts with the management-company-versus-fund split. Recall from [the fund structure](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund) that the *manager* (the management company and the GP) is a separate set of entities from the *funds* themselves. The master-feeder sits entirely on the *fund* side: the manager signs one investment-management agreement with the master and runs that one book; the feeders are the funds, and the manager's economics — the management fee and the incentive allocation — are charged at the feeder or master level per the documents. The founder owns the management company; the master-feeder is the product the management company runs. Keeping that line clear is what stops a founder from confusing "the fund did well" with "I got paid," and it is the same line that determines what registration and reporting obligations attach where — which is its own deep topic in [registration and regulation](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d).

The named failures of the industry are, notably, *not* failures of the master-feeder structure itself. LTCM, Amaranth, Archegos, and Madoff all blew up for reasons orthogonal to onshore-versus-offshore — leverage and concentration (LTCM, Amaranth), hidden counterparty exposure (Archegos), or outright fraud and the absence of an independent administrator and custodian (Madoff). The lesson runs the other way: the master-feeder is the *boring, correct* plumbing that lets a fund serve a broad investor base cleanly. The exciting, fatal mistakes happen in the portfolio and in the controls — not in the choice of a Cayman feeder, which is about as controversial as choosing a bank.

The "not secrecy" point is worth nailing down with the actual reporting regimes, because they are what makes the myth absurd. Under **FATCA** (the US Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act), the Cayman feeder must identify its US account holders and report them, so the IRS sees exactly which US persons own shares. Under the **Common Reporting Standard (CRS)**, an OECD framework that Cayman participates in, the feeder reports non-US account holders to their home tax authorities, so a German investor's stake is reported to Germany. The fund files audited financials, the manager files Form ADV with the SEC if registered, and the structure is documented in offering memoranda that disclose every entity and every fee. There is nowhere to hide a dollar. A founder who pitched a Cayman feeder as a way to keep an investor's holdings secret would be describing a crime that the structure is specifically built *not* to enable — the information-sharing is automatic and mandatory. Offshore tax-neutrality and offshore secrecy are simply different things, and the modern regulatory machinery has made the second one practically impossible inside a regulated fund.

One more real-world nuance worth flagging: the rise of the **separately managed account (SMA)**. The very largest allocators — sovereign funds, the biggest pensions — sometimes do not want to be in your commingled feeder at all; they want their money in a dedicated account they own, that you trade alongside the master under the same model, often with bespoke fees and full transparency. An SMA sidesteps the feeder question entirely for that investor (they own the account, so their tax position is their own problem to structure), but it adds operational complexity for the manager, who now trades the master *and* one or more parallel accounts. For most founders, the master-feeder is the workhorse and SMAs are a later, large-investor accommodation — but it is worth knowing the master-feeder is not the only door, just the standard one.

## When this matters / Further reading

Get the structure question settled before you take a dollar of outside capital, because it determines who can legally invest in you. If your capital will only ever be US taxable money, launch a standalone Delaware LP and keep your costs low — but have your counsel design it so a Cayman feeder can be added cleanly the day institutional money appears. If you have institutional ambitions, build the master-feeder up front: the roughly \$150,000–\$200,000 a year it costs is covered by the management fee on a single institutional allocation, and the alternative is losing those allocations the way Maya lost her \$40,000,000 pension ticket to a structural gap she did not know she had.

Remember the one sentence that defeats the biggest myth: **offshore is about tax-neutrality, not secrecy.** The Cayman feeder is a fully disclosed, audited, regulated corporate blocker that turns toxic UBTI and ECI into a clean equity investment in a no-tax company — it does not hide anything, it does not help a US taxable person avoid US tax, and it is the standard plumbing of a multi-trillion-dollar industry. You build it because different investors carry different tax problems, and the founder's job is to build the right door for each one while running a single, undivided book.
