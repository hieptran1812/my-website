> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A "lightly regulated" hedge fund still lives inside a dense rulebook, and getting any one rule wrong means the fund is operating illegally. You must navigate four separate regimes at once: who advises (SEC), what you trade (CFTC), how you sell the interests (the Securities Act), and what the fund itself counts as (the Investment Company Act).
>
> - The four regimes do not overlap and do not substitute for each other. You can be a perfectly exempt adviser and still blow up your offering with one advertising email; you can have a flawless private placement and still be an illegal commodity pool because you traded one futures contract too many.
> - The money thresholds are bright lines. SEC adviser registration generally kicks in at **\$100M** of regulatory assets; the private-fund exemption (the exempt reporting adviser) runs up to **\$150M**; Form PF reporting starts at **\$150M** of private-fund assets and gets heavier at **\$1.5B**. These are current as of 2026 and are indexed or revised over time.
> - "Anyone with money can invest" is false. There are three separate investor gates — **accredited investor** (income over \$200,000 or net worth over \$1M), **qualified client** (net worth over \$2.2M, needed before you can charge a performance fee), and **qualified purchaser** (over \$5M in investments, needed for a 3(c)(7) fund) — and they answer three different legal questions.
> - The one sentence to remember: a hedge fund is not unregulated; it is *exempt*, and an exemption is a privilege you keep only by obeying its conditions. *This post is educational, not legal advice — every real launch needs fund counsel.*

Maya is three weeks from launching the Meridian Fund. The Delaware limited partnership is formed, the Cayman master sits behind it, the prime-brokerage account is funded with her own seed capital, and a private-placement memorandum sits finished in her inbox. She is, by her own honest reckoning, ready to manage money. What she is about to do, with the best intentions and complete confidence, is break federal securities law before she has taken a single outside dollar.

Her plan for the morning is simple. She has built, over a decade on a multi-strategy desk, a network of perhaps four hundred people who respect her trading. She is going to email two hundred of them — former colleagues, the wealthy founder she sat next to at a conference, three family offices she met on a panel, a LinkedIn connection who once told her he "loves finding emerging managers" — with a warm, confident note. The subject line writes itself: *"Launching the Meridian Fund — targeting 15%+ net, would love to have you in."* It is the most natural thing in the world. It is also, depending on exactly which exemption her lawyer structured the offering around, capable of vaporizing her entire private-placement exemption — turning a legal offering into an unregistered public securities offering, the kind the SEC sues people for. One sentence, sent to one too-distant contact, and Meridian is no longer a private fund. It is a violation.

That is the trap at the center of hedge fund regulation, and it is the opposite of how the industry is usually described. Hedge funds are sold to founders as *lightly regulated* — and relative to a mutual fund, they are. But "lightly regulated" does not mean "unregulated." It means the fund operates inside a set of carefully drawn *exemptions* from the heavy public-markets rules, and every one of those exemptions comes with conditions. Break a condition and you do not get a warning; you lose the exemption, and without the exemption the fund's whole structure is illegal. The freedom to short, lever, concentrate, and charge a performance fee is not a gift. It is rented, and the rent is paid in compliance.

![A matrix showing four regulatory regimes — SEC adviser law, CFTC and NFA, the Securities Act, and the Company Act — with the actors each covers, the trigger, and the consequence of getting it wrong](/imgs/blogs/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d-1.png)

Figure 1 is the whole landscape in one grid. Four separate bodies of law touch a hedge fund, each governing a different actor: the SEC's adviser rules govern *the manager*, the CFTC and NFA govern *funds that trade futures*, the Securities Act governs *the sale of the fund's interests*, and the Investment Company Act governs *the fund entity itself*. They are independent. Satisfying one says nothing about the others. A founder who thinks "I'm a small fund, the SEC doesn't care about me" has confused the adviser regime (where small really can mean exempt) with the securities-sale regime (where size is irrelevant and one advertising mistake is fatal). This post walks each of the four regimes from zero, shows you the dollar thresholds and the bright lines, and works through the exact decisions Maya has to make before she can legally accept the first wire.

## Foundations: the regulatory alphabet

Before any of the regimes make sense, you need the vocabulary, because hedge fund regulation is delivered almost entirely in acronyms and section numbers. Let me define every term the rest of the post uses, from zero. None of this is hard once the words stop being noise.

**Adviser, RIA, and ERA.** Anyone who, for compensation, gives advice about securities to a fund is an **investment adviser**. The federal law that governs advisers is the **Investment Advisers Act of 1940**. An adviser who has crossed the registration threshold and signed up with the SEC is a **registered investment adviser (RIA)** — registered, examined, and subject to the full rulebook. An adviser who is *below* the threshold but who advises only private funds can use a lighter status: the **exempt reporting adviser (ERA)**, which still has to file a stripped-down public form but skips full registration and examination. So the three states an adviser can be in are: state-registered (tiny), an ERA (a private-fund adviser below the federal line), or a full SEC-registered RIA (above the line). Which one Maya is depends almost entirely on her assets.

**Regulatory assets under management.** The threshold that decides Maya's adviser status is not the marketing number she quotes to investors. It is **regulatory assets under management (regulatory AUM)** — a specific, defined figure the SEC tells you how to compute. It is gross, not net: you count the full market value of the assets in the fund without subtracting borrowings or leverage. A fund running \$80M of investor equity but \$160M of gross positions because it borrowed to lever up may have a regulatory AUM near \$160M, not \$80M. This catches founders off guard constantly, because a levered fund crosses the registration line at far less investor capital than the headline suggests.

**Form ADV and Form PF.** The two main filings an adviser makes. **Form ADV** is the registration-and-disclosure form: Part 1 is structured data about the firm (who owns it, how much it manages, its disciplinary history); Part 2 is a plain-English brochure describing the strategy, fees, and conflicts. Both ERAs and full RIAs file a Form ADV, just different amounts of it. **Form PF** ("PF" for *private funds*) is a separate, confidential filing that does not go to investors at all — it goes to the SEC (and on to the systemic-risk regulators) so the government can monitor whether private funds are building up risk that could threaten the financial system. Form PF is the post-2008 invention: regulators were blind to hedge fund leverage going into the crisis and built Form PF so they would not be blind again.

**CPO and CTA.** The commodities world has its own adviser vocabulary. A **commodity pool operator (CPO)** is the person who operates a *pooled* vehicle that trades commodity interests — futures, options on futures, and most swaps. A hedge fund that trades futures is, by definition, a commodity pool, and Maya as its operator is a CPO unless she qualifies for an exemption. A **commodity trading advisor (CTA)** is someone who advises others about trading commodity interests — a managed-futures manager running separate accounts is the standard example. The regulator here is the **Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC)**, and the self-regulatory body that actually runs registration and exams is the **National Futures Association (NFA)**.

**Reg D and the accredited investor.** When Maya sells interests in Meridian, she is selling securities. The default rule is that you must register a securities offering with the SEC (an enormous, public, IPO-style process) *unless* you fit an exemption. The exemption hedge funds use lives in **Regulation D** (Reg D) under the Securities Act of 1933, specifically **Rule 506**, which lets you raise unlimited money in a *private placement* — a sale to a limited, sophisticated audience rather than the general public. The basic person you are allowed to sell to is an **accredited investor**: an individual rich enough or high-earning enough that the law presumes they can fend for themselves without the protections of a registered public offering.

**The Investment Company Act and the qualified purchaser.** A fund that pools money to invest in securities is, on its face, an **investment company** under the **Investment Company Act of 1940** — the same statute that governs mutual funds, with all the heavy restrictions that make mutual funds boring and safe. A hedge fund must *not* be a registered investment company, so it relies on one of two exemptions: **Section 3(c)(1)** (a fund with no more than 100 beneficial owners) or **Section 3(c)(7)** (a fund whose investors are all **qualified purchasers** — a much higher wealth bar than accredited, but with no headcount cap). These are the two doors out of the Company Act, and choosing between them is one of the first structural decisions a founder makes.

**The qualified client.** Finally, a subtle one that founders often miss until it costs them. Under the Advisers Act, you generally cannot charge a *performance fee* to an ordinary client — that is the whole reason public mutual funds rarely have one. The exemption that lets a hedge fund charge its 20% is **Rule 205-3**, and it requires every investor paying the performance fee to be a **qualified client**: someone with enough net worth or enough money managed by the adviser that the law lets them agree to a performance fee. Accredited is the gate to *invest*; qualified client is the gate to be *charged the incentive fee*; qualified purchaser is the gate for one particular fund structure. Three gates, three thresholds, three purposes — and we will untangle them carefully later.

That is the alphabet. RIA and ERA describe the manager; CPO and CTA describe the commodities role; Reg D and accredited describe the sale; 3(c)(1) and 3(c)(7) describe the fund; qualified client describes the fee. Everything below hangs off one of these terms.

## SEC investment-adviser registration

Start with the regime that governs Maya herself: is she required to register as an investment adviser, and with whom? The answer is decided by a tiered system keyed to her regulatory AUM, and the tiers have surprisingly sharp edges.

![A decision tree showing how three questions about assets, investors, and instruments pick a fund's registrations and exemptions](/imgs/blogs/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d-2.png)

Figure 2 lays out the three questions every founder answers — how much AUM, which investors, and whether the fund touches futures — and shows that the adviser question is just the first branch. The adviser tiers run like this. At the very bottom, an adviser managing only small amounts registers with their *home state's* securities regulator, not the SEC; the federal government does not want to spend its examiners on tiny shops. As the firm grows, it crosses into federal territory and must register with the SEC. And in between sits a special carve-out for advisers who manage *only private funds* — the exempt reporting adviser.

Why does the federal/state split exist at all? The 1996 reforms that created it reflect a simple division of labor: the SEC has finite examiners and wants to spend them on advisers large enough to matter systemically, while state regulators are closer to the small, local advisers who manage retail-adjacent money. So Congress drew a size line and split jurisdiction across it. The line has moved over time — it sat at \$25M before the Dodd-Frank reforms raised the main threshold to \$100M and created the "mid-sized adviser" band — which is exactly why a founder confirms the current figure rather than trusting a number remembered from an old textbook. The thresholds in this post are the post-Dodd-Frank lines current as of 2026.

Here are the lines, current as of 2026 and worth confirming against the live rule text because the SEC adjusts them. An adviser whose regulatory AUM is at or above **\$100M** generally must register with the SEC as a full RIA. (There is a buffer band between \$90M and \$100M where you may register early; once above \$100M, registration is mandatory, and once below \$90M you generally drop back to state registration.) Below the federal line, you register with your state. But the private-fund carve-out overrides the simple AUM story: an adviser who advises **solely private funds** and has less than **\$150M** in private-fund assets in the United States qualifies as an *exempt reporting adviser*. The ERA does not fully register. It does not get examined the way a full RIA does. It files only a short subset of Form ADV — enough for the SEC to know the firm exists and roughly what it does — and renews that filing annually.

![A horizontal number line on a log scale marking the state-registration zone, the RIA registration threshold at 100 million dollars, the ERA ceiling and Form PF threshold at 150 million dollars, and the enhanced Form PF threshold at 1.5 billion dollars](/imgs/blogs/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d-6.png)

Figure 6 puts the dollar thresholds on a single number line, because the geometry is what trips people. The state-registered zone sits below roughly \$100M. The full-RIA obligation begins at \$100M of regulatory AUM. The ERA ceiling — and, not coincidentally, the Form PF threshold — sits at \$150M of private-fund assets. And far to the right, at \$1.5B, a hedge fund adviser crosses into *enhanced* Form PF reporting: more frequent filings with far more detail, because at that size your positions matter to the stability of the whole system. The lines are not evenly spaced and they do not all measure the same thing — \$100M is regulatory AUM, \$150M is private-fund AUM, and the gross-versus-net distinction means a levered fund hits them at less investor capital than you would guess.

Notice the gap that the carve-out creates. A private-fund adviser between \$100M and \$150M is *above* the \$100M full-RIA line but still *below* the \$150M private-fund line — and so can remain an ERA rather than fully registering. The "advises solely private funds" condition is what buys that headroom. The moment Maya takes on a single separately managed account for an individual outside the fund, she is no longer advising solely private funds, the ERA exemption can fall away, and at her asset level she may snap straight to full RIA registration. This is a genuinely common own-goal: a founder does a favor for a wealthy friend by managing a side account, and accidentally converts the whole firm from an exempt reporting adviser into one that must fully register.

What does full RIA registration actually cost Maya in obligations? She files the complete Form ADV (Parts 1 and 2). She adopts a written compliance program and designates a **chief compliance officer (CCO)** — a real, named person responsible for the rules, which we cover in depth in [the compliance program and the CCO](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco). She becomes subject to SEC examination — periodic, sometimes unannounced reviews of her books, her trading, her marketing, and her valuation. She must keep extensive records, follow the custody rule (which is why the independent administrator and the annual audit exist), and comply with the Marketing Rule covering how she advertises performance, the subject of [marketing and the SEC Marketing Rule](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/marketing-and-the-sec-marketing-rule). And once she crosses \$150M of private-fund assets, she layers Form PF on top of all of it. Registration is not a checkbox; it is a permanent operating burden, and it is one of the costs that makes sub-scale funds so hard to run profitably.

#### Worked example: crossing the \$100M RIA-registration line

Meridian launches with \$60M of investor equity and, because Maya's long/short book uses modest leverage, roughly \$90M of gross long-plus-short exposure. Her regulatory AUM is computed gross, so it sits around \$90M — below the \$100M full-RIA line and comfortably inside ERA territory, since she advises only the fund. She files as an ERA: a short Form ADV, no full registration, no examinations. Good year follows. By month fourteen the fund has grown to \$110M of equity and, at the same leverage, about \$165M of gross exposure. Her regulatory AUM has blown through both lines. She now exceeds \$100M (must fully register as an RIA) *and* \$150M of private-fund assets (must file Form PF). The trigger was not a deliberate decision; it was a good run. A founder who is not watching the gross number can be over the line for months before noticing, and "we didn't realize we'd grown into registration" is not a defense the SEC accepts. The practical move is to engage compliance counsel *before* you approach the line — typically while still in the \$70–90M band — so the registration is filed in the buffer rather than scrambled after the breach.

*The lesson: registration is triggered by your gross regulatory AUM, not your marketing number, and a successful fund crosses the line by growing — so you plan for it before you hit it, not after.*

## CFTC and the NFA

The second regime has nothing to do with how big Maya is or who her investors are. It is triggered purely by *what she trades*. If Meridian touches futures, options on futures, or most swaps — collectively, "commodity interests" — then the fund is a **commodity pool** and Maya is its **commodity pool operator**, regulated by the CFTC and the NFA, unless she fits an exemption. This catches founders by surprise because the word "commodity" is misleading. Commodity interests are not just oil and corn. Stock-index futures, Treasury futures, currency futures, VIX futures, and a wide universe of swaps all count. A long/short equity fund that hedges its market exposure with a few S&P 500 futures has, technically, made itself a commodity pool.

The good news for most launching funds is the **de minimis exemption** under **CFTC Rule 4.13(a)(3)**. The rule says, roughly: if your fund's use of commodity interests is small enough, you do not have to register as a CPO at all. "Small enough" is defined by two alternative tests, and you only need to pass one of them. The first test caps the *initial margin and premium* committed to commodity-interest positions at no more than 5% of the fund's liquidation value. The second caps the aggregate *net notional* of those positions at no more than 100% of the fund's liquidation value. A fund using futures purely to hedge a sleeve of its book, or to express a modest tactical view, usually clears the 5% margin test easily and claims the exemption. A fund whose *whole strategy* is futures — a managed-futures or global-macro program running large notional positions — will blow through both caps and must register.

Claiming 4.13(a)(3) is not automatic. You file a **notice of exemption** through the NFA, electronically, before you rely on it, and you renew it annually. You also represent that your investors are appropriately sophisticated. Miss the filing or the renewal and you can lose the exemption even though your trading would have qualified — a paperwork failure, not a trading failure, but a failure that makes you an unregistered CPO. And there is a separate consequence founders forget: the moment a fund relies on 4.13(a)(3), its marketing materials and offering documents must carry specific CFTC-mandated disclosure language. Drop the exemption legend out of the PPM and you have a documentation problem layered on top of everything else.

If Maya's strategy *does* require real futures exposure, registration as a CPO means she (and likely her firm) join the NFA, pass the regulatory exams or qualify their associated persons, adopt the CFTC's disclosure-document and reporting regime, and submit to NFA audits. The CFTC's rulebook is genuinely separate from the SEC's — different forms, different examiner, different recordkeeping — so a futures-heavy fund effectively runs two compliance programs in parallel. This is one reason a founder thinks hard about whether a strategy truly *needs* listed futures or whether the same exposure can be obtained through instruments that keep the fund under the de minimis line.

There is a second exemption worth knowing because it interacts with the first. **CFTC Rule 4.7** does not exempt you from CPO registration, but it grants substantial relief from the disclosure, reporting, and recordkeeping burden *if* the fund sells only to highly sophisticated investors — "qualified eligible persons," a CFTC bar broadly comparable to the qualified-purchaser idea. So a fund that genuinely needs to trade large futures positions, and therefore cannot use 4.13(a)(3), can still lighten the load by registering as a CPO and then claiming 4.7 relief — provided its investor base clears that higher sophistication bar. The pattern that emerges across both the CFTC and the SEC is consistent: the more sophisticated and wealthy your investors, the lighter the regulatory hand, because the regulators presume sophisticated investors can protect themselves. Investor quality is not just a marketing question; it is a lever on your entire compliance burden. A founder choosing between a 3(c)(1) fund full of merely-accredited investors and a 3(c)(7) fund of qualified purchasers is also, implicitly, choosing how heavy the rulebook will sit on the firm.

#### Worked example: which Company Act exemption for a fund with 80 investors, some not qualified purchasers

Set the futures question aside and turn to the fund's status under the Investment Company Act, because this is where the investor *count* and the investor *quality* collide. Maya has soft-circled commitments from 80 investors. Most are wealthy individuals and family offices who clearly clear the accredited bar; about 30 of them, though, are accredited but *not* qualified purchasers — they have, say, \$1.5M to \$3M of net worth but nowhere near \$5M in investments. Which exemption can Meridian use?

If Maya wants a **3(c)(7)** fund, *every single investor* must be a qualified purchaser. Her 30 merely-accredited investors disqualify the whole fund from 3(c)(7) — it is all-or-nothing, with no allowance for a few who fall short. So 3(c)(7) is out unless she turns away those 30 investors or waits for them to grow wealthier. That leaves **3(c)(1)**, which caps the fund at 100 beneficial owners but lets in accredited investors who are not qualified purchasers. With 80 investors she is under the 100-owner cap, so 3(c)(1) works today — but she has only 20 slots of headroom. If Meridian succeeds and 50 more accredited individuals want in, she cannot take them without converting to 3(c)(7) (and thereby losing anyone who is not a qualified purchaser) or launching a parallel 3(c)(7) vehicle for the larger investors. The structural choice she makes at 80 investors quietly constrains who she can raise from for the life of the fund.

*The lesson: 3(c)(1) buys you lower-tier investors at the price of a hard 100-owner ceiling; 3(c)(7) buys you unlimited headroom at the price of turning away everyone who is not a qualified purchaser — and you cannot have both at once.*

## The Securities Act: Reg D 506(b) vs 506(c)

Now the regime that nearly cost Maya everything in the opening: the sale of the fund's interests. Selling a limited-partnership interest in Meridian is selling a security. The default under the Securities Act of 1933 is that every securities offering must be *registered* with the SEC — the full, public, prospectus-and-roadshow process that companies use to go public. No hedge fund does that. Instead, hedge funds raise capital through a **private placement**, relying on the exemption in Regulation D, Rule 506. Rule 506 comes in two flavors, and the difference between them is the single most consequential — and most violated — distinction in fund marketing.

![A before-and-after comparison of Reg D 506(b), which forbids general solicitation but allows self-certified accredited status, against 506(c), which allows advertising but requires verification of every investor](/imgs/blogs/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d-7.png)

Figure 7 frames the trade. Under **Rule 506(b)** — the path the overwhelming majority of hedge funds use — you may raise unlimited capital from an unlimited number of accredited investors (plus up to 35 non-accredited but sophisticated investors, though most funds take zero of those to keep things clean). In exchange for that freedom, you accept one hard prohibition: **no general solicitation**. You may not advertise the offering to the public. No website touting the fund's returns to all comers, no mass email to a list of strangers, no press interview pitching the fund, no conference pitch to a room you do not know. The legal theory is that a private placement is private only if you sell to people with whom you have a *substantive, pre-existing relationship* — people you actually know well enough to assess. The flip side of accepting that prohibition is a lighter diligence burden: under 506(b), you may generally *rely on the investor's own representation* that they are accredited (the checkbox in the subscription documents), subject to a reasonableness standard.

Under **Rule 506(c)** — created by the JOBS Act in 2012 — the deal flips. You *may* advertise. You can put performance on a public website, email strangers, speak to the press, pitch from a stage. General solicitation is permitted. But the price of that freedom is steep: **every single investor must be an accredited investor, and you must take reasonable steps to *verify* it** — not just accept their checkbox, but actually confirm it, by reviewing tax returns, bank and brokerage statements, or obtaining a written confirmation from the investor's accountant, lawyer, or broker. The self-certified box is not enough under 506(c). And there is no room for the 35 non-accredited investors that 506(b) tolerates — under 506(c), one non-accredited investor blows the exemption.

Now return to Maya's morning email. If Meridian's offering is structured as a **506(b)** placement — which it almost certainly is, because that is the default and she has not built the verification machinery for 506(c) — then her warm note to two hundred contacts is a catastrophe waiting to happen. The contacts she knows well, the former colleagues and the family offices she has a real relationship with, are fine. But the LinkedIn connection she barely knows, the founder she met once at a conference, the people forwarded the email by a friend — those are *general solicitation*. Emailing the offering to people with whom she has no substantive pre-existing relationship is exactly the public marketing that 506(b) forbids. One such email and the SEC can take the position that the entire offering was conducted as a general solicitation, voiding the 506(b) exemption for *every* investor, not just the strangers. The fund would then be an unregistered public offering of securities — a serious violation, with rescission rights for investors and potential enforcement for Maya.

The cruelty of the rule is that you cannot fix a blown 506(b) by saying "well, everyone who invested was accredited anyway." The exemption is about the *manner* of the offering, not just the *result*. Once you have generally solicited, you have generally solicited, and 506(b) is gone. Your only retreat is to have *intended* 506(c) from the start and to have verified everyone — which Maya did not. This is why the standard advice to a launching manager is rigid: under 506(b), say nothing public about the offering. Talk about your *background* and your *views on markets* freely; never advertise the *fund* or its *terms* or its *returns* to anyone you do not genuinely, pre-existingly know. The line between "building my reputation" and "soliciting for my fund" is the line a compliance lawyer earns their fee drawing.

So why does 506(c) exist if it is so demanding? For a manager who wants to build a public profile — a fund with a content strategy, a founder who speaks publicly about the fund, a firm raising from a broad base of high-net-worth investors it cannot all pre-know — 506(c) is the only legal way to do it. The verification cost is real but manageable with a third-party verification service. The choice between 506(b) and 506(c) is therefore strategic: do you raise quietly from people you know (506(b), the norm), or do you market publicly and pay the verification tax (506(c), the exception)? You generally pick one at the outset and build the offering around it; you do not casually drift between them.

#### Worked example: the accredited-investor test for an investor with \$250,000 income or \$1.5M net worth

A prospective investor, call him David, wants into Meridian. Maya needs to know whether he is accredited, because under her 506(b) offering she can only take accredited (and a tightly limited number of non-accredited) investors. The individual accredited tests, current as of 2026, are alternatives — David needs to satisfy *one*. The income test: individual income over \$200,000 in each of the last two years (or \$300,000 jointly with a spouse), with a reasonable expectation of the same this year. The net-worth test: net worth over \$1M, *excluding the value of his primary residence*. There are also qualification routes by professional credential — holders of an active Series 7, Series 65, or Series 82 license qualify regardless of wealth.

David earns \$250,000 a year and has held that income for three years. He clears the income test on its own — \$250,000 exceeds the \$200,000 individual line in each of the last two years — so he is accredited even before you look at his balance sheet. Separately, his net worth is \$1.5M, but \$700,000 of that is the equity in his home. Stripping the primary residence out leaves \$800,000, which is *below* the \$1M net-worth line. So David fails the net-worth test but passes the income test, and since the tests are alternatives, he is accredited. Under 506(b), Maya may rely on David's representation of these figures in the subscription documents, subject to reasonableness; under 506(c) she would have to verify them with his tax returns or a letter from his accountant.

*The lesson: accredited status is an either-or test — meet the income line or the net-worth line — and the primary-residence carve-out from net worth is the detail that quietly disqualifies house-rich, investment-poor applicants.*

## The Investment Company Act: 3(c)(1) vs 3(c)(7)

We touched the Company Act in the 80-investor example; now treat it properly, because the choice between its two exemptions shapes who Maya can ever raise from. Recall the problem: a fund that pools money to invest in securities looks exactly like a mutual fund to the Investment Company Act, and a mutual fund is hemmed in by leverage limits, liquidity requirements, board-governance rules, and a flat ban on performance fees to ordinary investors. A hedge fund must escape that statute entirely. The two escape hatches are Section 3(c)(1) and Section 3(c)(7), and a fund picks exactly one.

![A matrix comparing 3(c)(1) and 3(c)(7) on investor cap, who can invest, and the trade-off](/imgs/blogs/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d-3.png)

Figure 3 sets them side by side. **Section 3(c)(1)** exempts a fund that has no more than **100 beneficial owners** and is not making a public offering. Its great advantage is that those 100 owners need only be *accredited* investors — the lower wealth bar — so a 3(c)(1) fund can accept the merely-wealthy, not just the seriously-wealthy. Its constraint is the hard 100-owner ceiling. (A narrow expansion exists for a "qualifying venture capital fund" of no more than \$12M, which may have up to 250 owners, but that does not help a typical hedge fund.) Counting beneficial owners has subtleties — certain look-through rules can cause a single investing entity, like a fund-of-funds, to count as multiple owners if it was formed specifically to invest in your fund — but the headline is simple: 100 slots, accredited investors allowed.

**Section 3(c)(7)** exempts a fund whose investors are *all* **qualified purchasers** and which is not making a public offering. There is no statutory cap on the number of investors. The constraint is the wealth bar: every investor must clear the qualified-purchaser threshold — \$5M in investments for an individual (or \$25M for most entities), far above the accredited line. So 3(c)(7) buys you unlimited headroom for growth but slams the door on anyone below the qualified-purchaser bar. One practical ceiling does sneak in from a different statute: once a fund has 2,000 or more holders (or crosses certain asset thresholds), the Securities Exchange Act's Section 12(g) can force public reporting, so 3(c)(7) funds practically keep their holder count below roughly 2,000 even though the Company Act itself imposes no limit.

The strategic shape of the choice is now clear. A first-time manager raising from a mix of wealthy individuals and family offices, many of whom are accredited but not qualified purchasers, almost always launches as **3(c)(1)** — it is the only way to take that capital, and 100 owners is plenty of room for a launch. A manager who knows they will raise large tickets from institutions and ultra-wealthy investors, and who wants unlimited headroom to scale, launches as **3(c)(7)** and simply will not accept anyone below the qualified-purchaser line. And a manager who wants both audiences runs *two funds in parallel* — a 3(c)(1) vehicle for accredited investors and a 3(c)(7) vehicle for qualified purchasers — both feeding the same trading book through a master-feeder structure, which is one of the reasons the [domestic vs offshore master-feeder](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund) architecture exists. The exemption you choose is not a formality; it is a decision about the entire shape of your investor base for years.

## Investor qualification

The three investor gates have appeared piecemeal — accredited for the sale, qualified purchaser for 3(c)(7), qualified client for the fee. Now line them up, because founders confuse them constantly and the confusion is expensive. They are three *different* tests, with three *different* thresholds, answering three *different* legal questions.

![A grid laying out accredited investor, qualified client, and qualified purchaser as three columns, each with its income test, net-worth test, and what it unlocks](/imgs/blogs/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d-4.png)

Figure 4 stacks them so the escalating thresholds are visible at a glance. **Accredited investor** is the entry gate — the test under Reg D that decides whether someone is allowed to invest in a private fund *at all*. Income over \$200,000 (or \$300,000 joint) for two years, or net worth over \$1M excluding the home, or certain securities licenses. This is the lowest bar and the most common one; it is what makes the 3(c)(1) world possible.

**Qualified client** is the gate that decides whether Maya can charge that investor a *performance fee*. Under Advisers Act Rule 205-3, a registered adviser may charge a performance fee only to a qualified client: someone with at least \$1.1M in assets managed by the adviser, *or* a net worth over \$2.2M. (These figures are indexed for inflation and the SEC revises them periodically, so they are current-as-of figures, not permanent ones.) This gate sits *above* accredited — a \$1M-net-worth investor can be accredited but fall short of the \$2.2M qualified-client bar. The consequence is concrete: a registered adviser cannot charge the 20% incentive fee to an investor who is accredited but not a qualified client. This is one reason the qualified-client test, not the accredited test, is often the binding constraint on a registered fund's minimum economics.

**Qualified purchaser** is the highest gate and the one that unlocks the 3(c)(7) structure. At least \$5M in investments for an individual, or \$25M for most institutions. An investor who clears \$5M in investments comfortably exceeds the accredited and qualified-client bars below it, so qualified purchasers are automatically eligible for everything — but the reverse is emphatically not true. The gates nest: every qualified purchaser is a qualified client and accredited, every qualified client is accredited, but a merely accredited investor may be neither of the higher two.

#### Worked example: the qualified-client test (\$2.2M net worth) needed to charge the incentive fee

Maya has registered as a full RIA (she grew past \$100M). A prospective investor, Elena, is clearly accredited — her income is \$400,000 and her net worth is \$1.8M excluding her home. She wants to invest \$2M in Meridian. Here is the snag Maya did not anticipate. Because Maya is now a *registered* adviser, Rule 205-3 applies: she may charge the 20% performance fee *only* to qualified clients. Elena's net worth of \$1.8M is below the \$2.2M qualified-client threshold, and her \$2M investment is below the \$1.1M-managed test only in the wrong direction — wait, \$2M *exceeds* \$1.1M managed, so Elena actually qualifies under the assets-managed prong: a client who will have at least \$1.1M under the adviser's management at the time of investment is a qualified client even if her total net worth falls short of \$2.2M.

So Elena, investing \$2M, clears the qualified-client bar via the \$1.1M-managed test and can be charged the incentive fee — even though her \$1.8M net worth misses the \$2.2M net-worth prong. Now change the facts: suppose Elena wants to invest only \$500,000. That is below the \$1.1M-managed line, and her \$1.8M net worth is below the \$2.2M line, so she fails *both* prongs and is not a qualified client. Maya, as a registered adviser, cannot charge her the performance fee. Maya's choices are to admit Elena into a no-incentive-fee share class (charging only the management fee), to decline the small ticket, or — if Maya were an *unregistered* ERA rather than a registered RIA — to note that the federal qualified-client requirement under Rule 205-3 may not bite the same way, though state rules and the fund documents can still impose it. The interaction between *registration status* and the *qualified-client* requirement is exactly the kind of thing that makes "this is not legal advice" a load-bearing sentence.

*The lesson: the performance fee is gated by the qualified-client test, which has two alternative prongs — \$2.2M net worth or \$1.1M managed by the adviser — and a small ticket from a moderately wealthy investor can fail both, forcing a management-fee-only share class.*

## Ongoing obligations

Before the recurring obligations, it helps to see the *order* in which the launch pieces lock into place, because the regimes are not satisfied simultaneously — each step gates the next, and doing them out of sequence creates expensive rework.

![A pipeline showing the compliance sequence from forming the entities through choosing exemptions, filing with the SEC, setting investor gates, and accepting capital, ending with the threshold crossing into full registration](/imgs/blogs/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d-5.png)

Figure 5 walks the sequence. First Maya forms the entities — the management company, the general partner, and the fund itself — because every later decision attaches to a legal person that has to exist first. Then she picks her Investment Company Act exemption (3(c)(1) for a launch with accredited investors), because that choice dictates who she may admit. Then she picks her Reg D path (506(b), no advertising), because that dictates how she may approach those investors. Then she claims the CFTC de minimis exemption if her futures usage is light. Only then does she file her exempt-reporting-adviser Form ADV with the SEC — the regulators want to see a structured firm, not an idea. With the filing in place she sets the investor gates in her subscription documents, confirms accredited status and qualified-client status where the fee requires it, and finally accepts capital and files her Form D within 15 days of the first sale. Far down the road sits the threshold crossing: when she passes \$100M she layers on full RIA registration and Form PF. The arrows run one way because the dependencies run one way; you cannot meaningfully file with the SEC before the entities exist, and you cannot accept a wire before the gates are set.

With the launch sequence in place, the relationship becomes a permanent one. Passing the launch gauntlet is not the end of the regulatory relationship; it is the beginning of it. Every regime that let Maya in imposes a continuing duty, and a fund dies from neglecting these as surely as from a bad trade. Run through the recurring calendar.

**The adviser filings.** Whether ERA or full RIA, Maya updates her Form ADV at least annually (and promptly for material changes — a new strategy, a new conflict, a change in ownership). A full RIA also maintains the written compliance program, conducts an annual compliance review, and keeps the books and records the SEC can demand on examination. Once over \$150M of private-fund assets, she files Form PF — quarterly or annually depending on her size and type, and on an accelerated, event-driven basis for the largest advisers, who must now report certain stress events within days. The 2023–2024 Form PF amendments specifically added rapid event reporting for large hedge fund advisers, so the \$1.5B-plus tier is more burdensome than it was even a few years ago.

**The CFTC filings.** If Maya relies on the 4.13(a)(3) de minimis exemption, she *re-affirms* that notice with the NFA every year — miss the annual affirmation and the exemption lapses. If she is a registered CPO, she files the CFTC's periodic account statements and annual reports, maintains the disclosure document, and submits to NFA examination.

**The securities-offering filings.** After her first sale under Reg D, Maya files a **Form D** with the SEC (a brief notice of the exempt offering) within 15 days of the first sale, and files amendments and state "blue sky" notices as investors come in from different states. The offering exemption is not a one-time event; it is a status she maintains by continuing to honor its conditions — no general solicitation under 506(b), continued verification under 506(c), accredited-only intake.

**The investor-facing duties.** Independent of the regulators, the fund's own documents and the law impose duties to investors: delivering audited financial statements annually (the custody rule effectively requires this for registered advisers), striking an independent NAV, honoring the liquidity and gate terms in the [fund documents](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-documents-ppm-lpa-subscription), and treating investors fairly across share classes. These are where operational due diligence — the allocator's veto — focuses, and we cover the [LP landscape and what allocators check](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-lp-landscape-who-allocates) elsewhere in the series.

The cost of all of this is real money and real attention. A registered fund's compliance and legal run-rate easily runs into the low hundreds of thousands of dollars a year — outside compliance consultants, the CCO's time, counsel for the annual ADV update and Form PF, the audit. It is one of the fixed costs that makes the break-even AUM math so unforgiving for a small fund, and it is why a founder treats compliance not as a nuisance but as a permanent line item in the survival budget.

#### Worked example: the annual compliance calendar and its cost

Walk Meridian's first full year as a \$120M registered RIA. In the first quarter, Maya's outside counsel updates Form ADV Parts 1 and 2 and files the annual amendment — call it \$25,000 in legal time. Throughout the year, a compliance consultant runs the firm's program: testing trade allocations, reviewing marketing materials against the Marketing Rule, conducting the annual compliance review, and prepping for a possible SEC exam — perhaps \$90,000 across the year. The fund's auditor delivers the annual audited financials that the custody rule requires — roughly \$45,000. Because Meridian is above \$150M of private-fund assets after a strong year, Maya now files Form PF, adding counsel and consultant time — another \$30,000. The de minimis CFTC affirmation is a small NFA filing but must not be missed. Add the CCO's salaried time and the total regulatory run-rate lands somewhere around \$200,000 for the year — on \$120M of AUM, that is roughly 0.17% of assets consumed by compliance alone, before a single dollar of trading cost or salary. On a \$30M fund, the same fixed compliance burden would be over 0.6% of assets, which is precisely why sub-scale funds struggle: the rulebook costs nearly the same whether you run \$30M or \$300M, and only scale spreads it thin.

*The lesson: compliance is a largely fixed cost, so it is brutal as a percentage of a small fund and trivial as a percentage of a large one — regulation is quietly one of the strongest forces pushing the industry toward size.*

## Common misconceptions

The regulation of hedge funds is surrounded by confident, wrong folklore. Here are the six that do the most damage to first-time founders.

**"Small funds fly under the SEC's radar."** The opposite is closer to true. Small funds get *adviser-registration* relief — below the thresholds you may be a state-registered adviser or an ERA rather than a full RIA — but that relief is about *who supervises the manager*, not about whether the rules apply. The Securities Act offering rules, the Investment Company Act fund-status rules, and the antifraud provisions apply to a \$5M fund exactly as they apply to a \$5B fund. Reg D does not care how small you are; one general-solicitation email blows the exemption at any size. And the SEC's antifraud authority — the catch-all that lets it pursue misrepresentation, misappropriation, and misvaluation — reaches every adviser, registered or not. "Too small to matter" is a fantasy that has ended careers.

**"Hedge funds are unregulated."** They are *exempt*, not unregulated, and exempt is a precise legal status with conditions attached. A hedge fund operates by claiming exemptions from registration regimes (the Investment Company Act, the Securities Act's registration requirement, sometimes the Advisers Act and the CFTC's CPO rules). Each exemption has eligibility requirements you must continuously satisfy. The freedom hedge funds enjoy — to short, lever, concentrate, and charge performance fees — flows directly from those exemptions, and the exemptions can be lost. Calling that "unregulated" is like calling a driver with a license "unregulated" because they are allowed to drive.

**"Anyone with money can invest."** No. There are explicit wealth and sophistication gates — accredited, qualified client, qualified purchaser — and they are not suggestions. A 506(b) fund may take only accredited investors (with a tiny non-accredited allowance most funds decline); a 3(c)(7) fund may take only qualified purchasers; a registered adviser may charge the performance fee only to qualified clients. "Money" is not the test; *the specific statutory threshold for the specific structure* is the test, and admitting an ineligible investor can void an exemption for everyone.

**"I can advertise once I have a track record."** A track record changes nothing about the solicitation rules. If the fund is a 506(b) offering — the default — you may *never* generally solicit, no matter how good your numbers are. Advertising the fund's returns publicly is exactly the prohibited act. To advertise legally you must be a 506(c) offering and verify every investor as accredited, *and* you must comply with the SEC Marketing Rule on how performance is presented (net-of-fee alongside gross, no cherry-picking, strict rules on hypothetical and back-tested performance). The track record is what you *show* once you are legally allowed to show it; it does not by itself grant permission.

**"Registering with the SEC means the SEC has approved my fund."** Registration is disclosure, not endorsement. The SEC does not bless your strategy, vet your returns, or vouch for the fund. Telling an investor "we're SEC-registered, so you're protected" is itself a compliance problem — the Form ADV explicitly forbids implying that registration is an endorsement. Registration means you have filed the forms and submitted to oversight; it does not mean the government thinks your fund is a good investment.

**"The exemptions are forever once I qualify."** Every exemption is a *continuing* status, not a one-time achievement. The ERA exemption evaporates if you take a single non-fund client or cross \$150M. The 506(b) exemption evaporates the moment you generally solicit. The 3(c)(1) exemption evaporates if you exceed 100 owners. The 4.13(a)(3) exemption evaporates if your futures usage grows past the de minimis caps *or* if you forget the annual affirmation. You do not qualify once and relax; you re-qualify every day by continuing to satisfy the conditions and re-file the notices on schedule.

## How it plays out in the real world

The rules become vivid the moment you look at what happens when they are ignored, and the industry's most famous disasters are, underneath, regulatory failures as much as trading failures.

**Madoff is the canonical exemption-and-oversight failure.** Bernard Madoff's operation, revealed in December 2008, was the largest Ponzi scheme in history — roughly \$65B in fabricated statement value, with around \$17.5B of principal actually lost. The fraud was not a clever trade; it was a complete absence of the independent controls that the regulatory structure is supposed to force. Madoff served as his own broker-dealer, struck his own "NAV," used a tiny unknown auditor, and had no independent administrator pricing the book. Every red flag that operational due diligence is designed to catch was present and ignored. The lesson the industry took — and that allocators now enforce through ODD — is that the *independent* pieces of the structure (a real administrator striking NAV, a credible auditor, genuine custody) are not regulatory box-checking. They are the load-bearing walls. A founder who treats the administrator and the audit as expensive nuisances has not understood why investors will not allocate without them.

**Archegos shows what "exempt" does not exempt you from.** Bill Hwang's Archegos Capital Management was a *family office* — a structure that, because it manages only the family's own money and takes no outside investors, is generally exempt from adviser registration. That exemption did nothing to protect anyone when, in 2021, Archegos's enormous, hidden, swap-financed leverage unwound and inflicted more than \$10B of losses on its prime brokers, with Credit Suisse alone losing around \$5.5B. Hwang was convicted of fraud in 2024. The point for a founder is sobering: being exempt from *registration* does not exempt you from the *antifraud* laws or from market discipline. The family-office exemption removed the SEC examiner; it did not remove the consequences of misrepresentation and concentration. Exemption is narrow; the antifraud net is total.

**LTCM and Amaranth show that regulation is not the only thing that kills a fund.** Long-Term Capital Management's 1998 collapse — roughly \$4.6B lost in months on highly levered convergence trades, requiring a \$3.6B recapitalization organized by the New York Fed — and Amaranth Advisors' 2006 implosion — about \$6.6B lost in a week on concentrated natural-gas spreads — were not primarily compliance failures. They were risk failures. But both reshaped the regulatory landscape afterward: the post-2008 push for Form PF and systemic-risk monitoring traces directly to the recognition, crystallized by LTCM, that large private funds can threaten the system while remaining almost invisible to regulators. The regulatory regime a founder navigates today was built, in large part, out of the wreckage of funds that thought they were too sophisticated to need it.

The everyday reality, for a founder who never makes headlines, is quieter but just as real. The fund that loses its 506(b) exemption because a junior employee posted returns on a public LinkedIn page. The ERA that accidentally becomes a full RIA because it took one separately managed account and never refiled. The fund that forgot its annual 4.13(a)(3) affirmation and spent a year as an unregistered commodity pool. The manager who charged a performance fee to an investor who turned out not to be a qualified client, and had to refund it. None of these make the news. All of them are expensive, and all of them are avoidable with competent counsel and a compliance calendar. The dramatic blowups teach the principle; the boring own-goals are what actually happen to ordinary funds.

## When this matters / Further reading

The regulatory architecture matters the moment you move from *managing money* to *managing other people's money inside a pooled vehicle* — which is the exact moment a hedge fund comes into existence. Everything in this post is a precondition Maya must satisfy before she can legally accept the first outside wire: she must know her adviser status (state, ERA, or RIA) and her regulatory AUM; she must know whether her trading makes her a commodity pool and whether she clears the de minimis exemption; she must run her offering as a disciplined 506(b) private placement (or a verified 506(c) one) and never blur the line into general solicitation; she must pick 3(c)(1) or 3(c)(7) and gate her investors accordingly; and she must charge her performance fee only to qualified clients. Get the structure right once and you maintain it forever; get one piece wrong and the fund is operating illegally regardless of how well it trades.

The single most important thing to internalize is the frame from the very top: a hedge fund is not unregulated, it is *exempt*, and an exemption is a privilege you keep only by continuously obeying its conditions. The freedom to run a concentrated, levered, short-selling, performance-fee-charging fund is rented from the regulators, and the rent is a permanent compliance program. A founder who resents that program has misjudged the deal; a founder who builds it well turns it into a selling point, because the allocators who write the largest checks will not invest in a fund whose regulatory house is not in perfect order.

A genuine and load-bearing caveat: **this post is educational, not legal advice.** The thresholds and rule numbers are current as of 2026 and are revised, indexed, and reinterpreted over time; the application of any rule depends on facts this post cannot know; and no founder should structure a fund from a blog post. Every real launch runs the entire structure past securities counsel and, where futures are involved, commodities counsel. What this post gives you is the map — enough understanding to ask your lawyer the right questions and to recognize the bright lines before you walk across one.
