> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A fund is far more likely to be ended by a phished wire, a dead vendor, an unbacked-up laptop, or the sudden loss of one person than by a bad trade; these operational risks deserve the same first-class attention founders lavish on alpha.
>
> - **Wire fraud is the single most common way money walks out the door.** A spoofed email from the founder, an urgent tone, a new account number, and one person acting alone is all it takes. Dual authorization plus a callback to a number on file defeats it for almost no cost.
> - **The SEC treats cybersecurity as a standing focus** because a fund holds investor data and the ability to move cash. Examiners ask about access controls, incident response, and vendor oversight, and "we are too small to be a target" is the belief that gets small funds breached.
> - **Key-person risk is structural, not sentimental.** If the founder is the firm, the fund has a single point of failure that the limited partnership agreement turns into a redemption right the moment that person is gone. A deputy and a written succession plan are survival infrastructure.
> - **The one fact to keep:** the defense is cheap and the incident is not. An annual cyber and controls program that runs around \$120k protects against a single spoofed wire that can cost \$2M and lost investor allocations that can cost far more.

A portfolio manager I will call **Maya** had built her **Meridian Fund** into a tidy \$200M long/short equity book, and on a Tuesday afternoon her operations lead nearly wired \$2M to a criminal. The email had come from Maya's address — or what looked exactly like Maya's address — while she was, everyone knew, traveling to meet an allocator. It was short, it was in her voice, and it was urgent: a confidential deal, close today, wire \$2M to the account in the attached instructions, keep it quiet until it is signed. The operations lead had the wire half-staged in the bank portal. The only thing that stopped it was a rule Maya had grumbled about installing six months earlier — that any wire over a threshold needed a second person to approve it, and that the second person had to *call Maya back* on the number in the firm's contact file, not reply to the email. The second approver called. Maya, mid-flight layover, had no idea what he was talking about. The wire was held. The "deal" did not exist. The email was a spoof, the account belonged to a fraudster, and \$2M stayed in the Meridian Fund because of a control that cost almost nothing and that Maya had nearly skipped.

This is the part of running a fund that has nothing to do with markets and everything to do with whether the firm is still standing next quarter. When you only *run money*, someone else owns the boring infrastructure: the email security, the vendor contracts, the backup that runs every night, the rule about who can move cash, the plan for what happens if a key person is hit by a bus. The moment you launch your own fund, all of that is yours, and the uncomfortable truth is that **funds are ended by operational failures far more often than by market losses.** A bad trade costs you some performance. A phished wire, a failed administrator, a breached laptop, or the sudden loss of your one indispensable person can cost you the fund itself — and, in the cyber and fraud cases, your investors' trust and the regulator's patience along with it.

This post is about those unglamorous risks: vendor and third-party failure, the business-continuity plan most founders treat as a box to tick, cybersecurity as the SEC actually examines it, key-person dependency, and the fraud controls — dual authorization and callbacks above all — that stop money from leaving. Figure 1 is the map: six non-market risks, the worst case if each is ignored, and the first-line control that stops it. It is the mental model for the whole post — name the harm, then name the defense.

## Foundations: the non-market risks

Before any of the war stories make sense, the vocabulary has to be solid. None of these terms is hard, but together they describe the second half of running a fund — the half that has nothing to do with whether your stock picks are right. Assume you can value a portfolio in your sleep but have never had to think about who owns the wire-release button.

**Operational risk.** The risk of loss from failed internal processes, people, systems, or external events — everything that is not market risk, credit risk, or liquidity risk. A fat-fingered trade, a missed reconciliation, a vendor that goes dark, a stolen laptop, a fraudulent wire, an employee who quits with the only knowledge of how a process runs: all operational risk. Regulators and allocators take it seriously precisely because it is diffuse and unglamorous, and because the canonical fund failures — the ones we cover under [how funds die](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk) — are very often operational at root.

**Vendor and third-party risk.** A modern fund is assembled from outside providers: the administrator that strikes the net asset value, the prime broker that holds and finances assets, the auditor, outside counsel, the order-management and risk systems, cloud applications, the IT managed-service provider, market-data feeds. Each of those is a *third party*, and each is a way the fund can fail through no fault of its own trading. A vendor can go bankrupt, get breached, lose your data, make a pricing error, or simply be unreachable on the day you need it. **Third-party risk** is the risk that flows into the fund from everyone it depends on but does not control.

**Business continuity and disaster recovery.** A **business-continuity plan (BCP)** is the written, tested answer to the question: if the normal setup is unavailable — the office is inaccessible, the internet is down, a key person is out, a vendor has failed — how does the fund still trade, settle, value itself, communicate, and recover? **Disaster recovery (DR)** is the narrower technical piece: how data and systems are backed up and restored. A serious BCP names who does what, where people work from, how cash controls still function remotely, and a *recovery time objective* — how many hours, not days, until the fund is operating again.

**Cybersecurity.** The protection of the fund's systems and data against unauthorized access, theft, and disruption. For a fund the prize is specific and valuable: it holds **investor data** (Social Security numbers, bank details, subscription documents) and it holds **the ability to move cash**. That combination makes even a small fund worth attacking. The threats are mundane and effective — phishing, stolen credentials, ransomware, and the one that hits funds hardest, business-email compromise.

**Business-email compromise (BEC) and wire fraud.** **Wire fraud** is any scheme to divert the fund's money to an account the attacker controls. The dominant form is **business-email compromise**: the attacker either breaks into a real inbox or spoofs one, impersonates an authority figure — usually the founder — and instructs a finance or operations person to send a wire or change a payment beneficiary. It works because it attacks people and process, not technology, and because urgency and authority short-circuit judgment. It is, by reported losses, the single most costly category of cyber-enabled financial crime.

**Key-person risk.** The risk that the fund depends so heavily on one individual — usually the founder, sometimes the sole portfolio manager — that the person's sudden death, disability, or departure threatens the firm. At a small fund this is acute: one person may hold the strategy, the investor relationships, the trading authority, and half the operational knowledge in their head. Investors fear it so much that the fund's legal documents typically encode a **key-person clause**: if the named person stops devoting substantial time to the fund, investors gain rights — often the right to redeem, sometimes a suspension of the incentive fee or even an orderly wind-down.

**Fraud controls and segregation of duties.** **Fraud controls** are the internal checks that make theft and error hard to commit and easy to detect. The bedrock principle is **segregation of duties**: no single person should both initiate and complete a sensitive action. The person who can place a trade should not also be the person who can move the cash; the person who requests a wire should not be the only person who can release it. The two specific controls this post leans on are **dual authorization** — two people must approve a payment above a threshold — and the **callback**: before acting on a payment instruction or a change to bank details, someone independently verifies it by calling a known, pre-recorded number, never the contact details in the request itself.

Hold those terms together and the thesis becomes mechanical rather than philosophical: a fund survives only if the money cannot be stolen with a single email, the operation can keep running when its normal setup fails, the investor data is genuinely protected, and the firm does not evaporate the day one person leaves. None of that is alpha. All of it is what keeps the alpha alive long enough to compound. The discipline of treating these as first-class risks, not afterthoughts, is the same business-function mindset we develop in [risk management as a business function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function).

There is a structural reason these risks are systematically under-attended, and it is worth naming because the founder's own psychology is the first vulnerability. Market risk is *visible and continuous*: it shows up in the daily marks, it is the thing the founder spent a career learning to price, and it generates the dopamine of being right. Operational risk is *invisible until it is catastrophic*: nothing in the daily P&L tells you the wire control is missing, the BCP is untested, or the deputy who could run the book in an emergency does not exist. The cost of the missing control is zero on every ordinary day and the entire firm on the one day it matters. That payoff shape — a long string of zeros punctuated by a single catastrophe — is exactly the shape humans price worst, and it is exactly why founders under-invest in defenses that feel like bureaucracy right up until they are the only thing standing between the firm and the end. A useful reframe is to treat each of these controls as a cheap option on survival: you pay a small, certain premium (the friction of dual authorization, the day a year spent testing the BCP, the salary of a deputy) to hedge a low-probability, fund-ending loss. A founder who can price a tail hedge in the portfolio can price one in the operation; the instinct is identical, only the underlying is the firm itself.

The other reason these risks compound is that they are *correlated with the worst possible moment*. The phished wire arrives when the founder is traveling and unreachable, because the attacker chooses that moment. The vendor failure bites during a market dislocation, when everyone needs their administrator at once. The key-person event and the redemption wave can arrive together, because the news of the founder's absence is itself what triggers the redemptions. Operational failures do not arrive politely spaced out on calm days; they cluster on the days the fund is already stressed, which is precisely when the controls have to work without the founder personally holding everything together.

## Vendor and third-party risk

A fund founder's instinct is to treat the service-provider stack as solved once the contracts are signed. The administrator strikes the NAV, the prime broker holds the assets, the IT provider keeps the laptops running, the contracts are filed, and attention returns to the book. That instinct is exactly wrong, because the providers are now load-bearing walls of the firm, and a wall you have stopped inspecting is the one that falls.

Start with what can go wrong. A vendor can simply **fail as a business** — a small administrator or an under-capitalized tech vendor can go insolvent, and suddenly the function it performed has no owner. A vendor can be **breached**, and because it holds the fund's data or has access to the fund's systems, the attacker reaches the fund through the vendor's open door. A vendor can make an **error** — a mispriced position from the administrator, a botched reconciliation, a corrupted data feed — that flows straight into the NAV investors rely on. Or a vendor can simply be **unreachable** on the day it matters: the support line that goes to voicemail during a market dislocation, the contact who left the firm and was never replaced in your records.

The defense is **vendor due diligence and oversight**, and it is ongoing, not a one-time check at onboarding. Before you hire a vendor, you assess its financial stability, its track record, its security posture, and crucially its own controls. A serious provider can produce a **SOC 2 report** — an independent auditor's attestation about its security, availability, and confidentiality controls. A provider that cannot produce one, or bristles at the question, is telling you something. After you hire the vendor, oversight continues: you confirm the administrator is genuinely independent and pricing assets from its own sources, you keep the SOC 2 reports current, you maintain backups of your own data so you are not wholly dependent on the vendor's, and for the most critical functions you keep a **second provider identified and contractable** so that a failure is a switch, not a cliff.

This connects directly to the operational architecture we lay out in [the operating model: front, middle, and back office](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office). The middle and back office are largely *built out of vendors*, and the founder's job is not to perform those functions personally but to *own the risk* that they fail. The independence of the administrator is the single most scrutinized point: the canonical fraud cases turned on a fund that self-administered or used a captive, tame provider, and the lesson allocators absorbed is that an independent administrator with its own pricing is not a nicety but a control.

It is worth being precise about *why* administrator independence is a control rather than a convenience, because it is the hinge of the most important fraud defense at the fund level. When the administrator strikes the NAV from its own records — receiving position and cash data directly from the prime broker and custodian, pricing securities from independent market sources, and reconciling against the manager's books rather than simply accepting them — there is a party outside the manager who can confirm that the assets the fund claims to hold actually exist and are worth what the fund says. Remove that independence and the manager is marking its own homework, which is the exact condition under which Madoff fabricated returns for decades. The practical test an operational-due-diligence team applies is simple: does the administrator price the book independently, does it send investor statements directly rather than through the manager, and does it reconcile to the prime and custodian rather than to the manager's spreadsheet? A "yes" to all three converts the administrator from a bookkeeping vendor into the fund's most important external control.

Vendor oversight has a *cadence*, not just an onboarding checkbox, and a founder who wants to pass an ODD review should be able to describe it. Annually, the fund should refresh the SOC 2 reports for its critical vendors, confirm each vendor's financial health and that key personnel have not turned over, and review the contracts for the right to terminate, the data-return obligations on exit, and the liability terms. A vendor register — a simple list of every third party, what it does, what data or system access it has, and when it was last reviewed — is the artifact that demonstrates the oversight is real. The register also forces a useful question for every vendor: if this one failed tomorrow, what is the plan? For the administrator, the auditor, and the prime broker, the answer should be a named alternative and a rough migration timeline. For lower-criticality vendors, the answer might simply be "we could do without it for a week," which is a perfectly good answer as long as someone has actually thought it through.

#### Worked example: the cost of an over-concentrated vendor

Maya's Meridian Fund used a single small administrator — cheap, responsive, and run by two people Maya liked. The administrator's two principals were the firm. When one of them had a medical emergency during a quarter-end, NAV production stalled for nine days. Maya could not produce the investor statements her largest LP required by contract, the LP's operational-due-diligence team flagged the delay, and Maya spent the next two quarters answering questions about her administrator's resilience rather than discussing returns. The eventual fix cost real money: she migrated to an established administrator at roughly \$70k a year versus the \$35k she had been paying, and she absorbed a one-time transition cost. The lesson was not that the small administrator was dishonest — it was excellent — but that a two-person vendor is a single point of failure, and a fund that builds on one has simply moved its key-person risk one entity to the left.

*The cheapest vendor is rarely the cheapest vendor once you price in the morning it cannot answer the phone.*

## Business continuity

Most founders write a business-continuity plan because an allocator's due-diligence questionnaire demands one, file it, and never look at it again. That is the failure mode. A BCP is not a document; it is a *capability*, and a capability you have never tested is a capability you do not have.

The plan has to answer concrete questions about a bad day, and Figure 5 lays them out as a checklist. Can the fund **trade** if the office is inaccessible — is there remote access to the order-management system, a backup way to reach the broker, and are the logins held somewhere other than one person's laptop? Can the fund **settle and value itself** — can the administrator strike a NAV from its own records even if the manager's office is dark? Can the fund **reach the people it needs** — are the contact details for staff, the prime broker, the administrator, and the LPs stored off the primary email system, so a compromise of that system does not also take down the contact list? Are **data and backups** offsite, encrypted, and genuinely restorable, with a recovery time objective measured in hours? Do the **cash controls survive** the disruption — does dual authorization still function remotely, so that a crisis does not become the moment a single person can move money unchecked? And is there **vendor failover** — a named second provider and the contractual ability to switch quickly?

![A nine-cell grid figure showing the business-continuity checklist with cells for trading anywhere, settling and valuing, reaching people, data and backups, surviving cash controls, vendor failover, and a wide bottom cell on testing the plan annually](/imgs/blogs/operational-cyber-and-key-person-risk-5.png)

The single most important sentence about a BCP is the one in the bottom row of that checklist: **test it.** Run a tabletop exercise once a year in which you pretend the office is gone and the founder is unreachable, and watch where the plan breaks — invariably it breaks somewhere surprising, like the one login nobody else has or the broker line that only the founder is authorized to call. Review the plan after every real incident, however small. An untested BCP is a comfort blanket; a tested one is a control. Allocators know the difference, and an operational-due-diligence team will ask not whether you have a BCP but when you last *tested* it and what you found.

The recent history of forced continuity tests is instructive, because the industry has run two enormous live drills it did not choose. The 2008 failure of Lehman Brothers was a continuity event for every fund that had Lehman as a prime broker: overnight, the entity that held and financed their assets was in bankruptcy, and the funds that survived best were the ones that had already spread across multiple primes and could keep operating while their Lehman assets were frozen. The 2020 shift to fully remote work was a continuity test of a different kind — the office became unavailable for every firm at once — and it separated the funds whose BCP was real (cloud systems, remote trading access, dual authorization that worked from home) from the funds whose continuity plan assumed everyone was physically in one room. A founder writing a BCP today should treat both as case studies: the plan must survive losing a critical vendor *and* losing the physical office, because both have happened to the whole industry within living memory. The test you run voluntarily once a year is cheap insurance against the test the world runs on you without warning.

#### Worked example: the cost of a real business-continuity setup

Maya priced out making Meridian's continuity real rather than nominal. Cloud-based document and email systems with proper backup ran a few thousand dollars a year. A second, independent internet path and the ability for the team to work fully from home cost almost nothing once the systems were cloud-based. The order-management system already supported secure remote access; she paid for an extra authenticated seat for her deputy. She formalized a contract with a backup IT managed-service provider — a small retainer of a few thousand dollars a year — so that if her primary IT vendor failed she had a named replacement. She ran a half-day tabletop exercise annually, costing a day of everyone's time. All in, turning the BCP from a filed document into a tested capability cost Meridian on the order of \$15k a year. Against a fund running \$200M, that is under one basis point — a rounding error against the management fee, and a fraction of what a single lost trading day during a dislocation would cost.

*A continuity plan is cheap; discovering you do not have one on the worst day of the year is not.*

## Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is where the founder's instinct is most dangerously wrong, because the instinct is "we are small, nobody is targeting us, and the IT vendor handles security." Every clause of that sentence is false in a way that gets small funds hurt.

Start with the attack surface, because you cannot defend what you have not mapped. Figure 2 shows it: an attacker does not need to beat the trading strategy, only to find one unlocked door. The doors are **email** (the founder's and the operations team's inboxes, which can be spoofed or compromised), **vendors** (the administrator, prime broker, IT provider, and cloud apps, any of which can be the way in), **wires** (a single instruction to change a payee or a bank detail), and **investor data** (the Social Security numbers, subscription documents, and bank information the fund is legally obligated to protect). Every path the attacker wants leads to one of two prizes: the **cash** — move \$2M to a new account — or the **data** — which can be sold, leaked, or held for ransom.

![A graph figure showing the attack surface of a small fund with an attacker node connecting through email, vendors, wires, and investor data nodes that funnel toward the cash and a data breach as the two outcomes](/imgs/blogs/operational-cyber-and-key-person-risk-2.png)

The dominant threat by financial loss is **business-email compromise**, and it is worth being precise about why it works. The attacker is not breaking encryption or exploiting a clever software bug. The attacker is exploiting *people and process*: they spoof or compromise the founder's email, they pick a moment when the founder is plausibly unreachable (traveling, in meetings), they inject urgency and confidentiality so the target does not pause to verify, and they ask a single trusted employee to move money or change a beneficiary. Technology controls — multi-factor authentication, email authentication standards that make spoofing harder — reduce the odds, but the control that actually defeats BEC is a *process* control, and we will get to it in the fraud-controls section: dual authorization and a callback. No amount of email security removes the need for two humans and a phone call before money moves.

Trace a real BEC attack end to end, because the sophistication is in the social engineering, not the technology, and seeing the steps is what inoculates a team. First, *reconnaissance*: the attacker learns who the founder is, who handles operations and payments, and the firm's tone, mostly from public sources — the website, regulatory filings, social media, a conference agenda showing the founder will be traveling. Second, *the foothold or the spoof*: either the attacker compromises a real inbox (a phished password, reused credentials from another breach) and lurks silently reading the firm's email for weeks, or they register a look-alike domain that differs by a single character and spoof from there. The compromised-inbox version is the more dangerous, because the attacker can study how the firm actually phrases payment requests and time the strike to a real moment of activity. Third, *the strike*: an email in the founder's voice, at a plausible moment, with a specific dollar amount and account, and the two psychological levers — authority ("this is the founder") and urgency ("must close today, keep it confidential"). The confidentiality instruction is not incidental; its job is to stop the target from doing the one thing that defeats the attack, which is asking someone else. Fourth, *the laundering*: if the wire goes out, the money is rapidly moved through mule accounts and often across borders within hours, which is why recovery is so rare once a payment leaves. Every step of that chain is defeated not by better software but by a culture in which an urgent, confidential payment request from anyone, however senior, triggers a callback rather than compliance.

The baseline cyber hygiene for a fund is not exotic and not expensive. **Multi-factor authentication (MFA)** on every account that touches email, banking, or systems — so a stolen password alone is not enough. **Security awareness training** for everyone, because phishing targets people; a team that has been trained to be suspicious of urgent wire requests is the cheapest and most effective control you can buy. **Endpoint security** — managed, encrypted, patched laptops, so a stolen or lost device is not a stolen data set. **Email authentication** standards configured properly to make outright spoofing harder. **Encrypted backups** kept offline so that ransomware, which encrypts your data and demands payment, has no leverage if you can simply restore. And an **incident-response plan** written before an incident: who you call, how you contain it, what you are legally obligated to disclose and to whom, and how you communicate to investors. Vendor oversight, from the previous section, is part of cybersecurity too — your security is only as strong as the security of the vendors who can reach your systems and data.

Notice the ordering of effectiveness, because it is the opposite of where founders instinctively spend. The controls that prevent the most expensive losses are the *cheapest and least technical*: training people to distrust urgent payment requests, requiring MFA, and enforcing dual authorization with a callback. The expensive, sophisticated tooling — advanced threat detection, managed security operations, penetration testing — matters more as the fund grows and holds more data and more systems, but it does not substitute for the basics, and a small fund that buys a fancy detection product while skipping MFA and a wire callback has spent money in exactly the wrong order. The right sequence for a launching fund is: lock down identity (MFA everywhere), lock down money (dual authorization plus callbacks), train the humans, back up the data offline, write the incident plan, and oversee the vendors — and only then, as scale justifies it, layer on the heavier tooling. Most of that baseline is process and discipline rather than purchased software, which is precisely why it is affordable for a fund of any size and why there is no excuse for skipping it.

### The SEC's focus

This is not optional diligence; it is a regulatory expectation. **Cybersecurity has been a standing examination priority for the SEC** for years, and the logic is exactly the logic above: an investment adviser holds sensitive client data and controls the movement of client assets, which makes it both a target and a custodian of trust. SEC examinations routinely probe an adviser's cybersecurity governance — access controls, data protection, vendor management, incident response and recovery, and how the firm tests and updates its program. The agency has brought enforcement actions against advisers for inadequate cybersecurity policies and for failures to protect client information, and the broader rulemaking direction has pushed toward formal cyber risk-management programs and incident disclosure for advisers and funds. The compliance dimension of this — written policies, the chief compliance officer's ownership, the evidence an examiner demands — is developed in [the compliance program and the CCO](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco); the point here is that a founder cannot treat cyber as purely an IT matter. It is a documented, examined, board-and-CCO-level obligation, and "we outsourced it to the IT vendor" is not an answer an examiner accepts.

The asymmetry is the whole argument, and Figure 6 makes it visible. The defense is cheap; the incident is not.

![A bar chart figure comparing illustrative per-incident losses for wire fraud, ransomware, data-breach remediation, and lost investor allocations in red against the much smaller annual cost of a cyber program in green](/imgs/blogs/operational-cyber-and-key-person-risk-6.png)

#### Worked example: the cost of a data breach

Suppose Meridian skips the basics — no MFA, occasional training, a backup that has not been tested in a year — and an employee's credentials are phished. The attacker is in the email and file systems for a week before anyone notices. The direct remediation is expensive: forensic investigators to determine what was accessed, legal counsel to advise on notification obligations, mandatory notification to affected investors whose personal data was exposed, credit monitoring offered to those investors, and the staff time consumed for months. Call the direct remediation roughly \$500k, an illustrative but unremarkable figure for a breach involving investor data. That is painful but survivable. The part that is not necessarily survivable is the *second-order* cost. Meridian's largest prospective allocator — a fund-of-funds that had been weeks from committing \$50M — completes its operational due diligence, learns of the breach and the missing controls, and walks. Two existing LPs, spooked, redeem at the next window. The lost and withdrawn capital dwarfs the remediation bill; in the chart it is the \$4M bar, and even that understates the franchise damage. Against all of this, the annual cyber program that would have prevented the breach — MFA, training, managed endpoints, tested backups, an incident plan, vendor oversight — costs Meridian on the order of \$120k a year.

*The breach is not primarily an IT bill; it is an allocation you never receive because an ODD analyst found the gap before your returns ever got a hearing.*

The incident-response plan deserves more than a single line, because the difference between a contained incident and a firm-ending one is whether the plan existed before the alarm went off. A real plan names, in advance, the *people*: who is the incident lead, which outside forensic firm you call, which law firm advises on breach-notification law, which insurer is notified under the cyber policy, and who speaks to investors. It names the *steps*: how you isolate affected systems without destroying the forensic evidence you will need, how you reset credentials, how you decide whether to restore from backup. It names the *legal obligations*: which investors and regulators must be told, on what timeline, and in what form, because data-breach notification is a legal duty in most jurisdictions, not a courtesy. And it names the *communication*: a pre-drafted template for telling LPs what happened, what data was affected, and what you are doing about it, written calmly in advance rather than under panic. A fund that has rehearsed this — even a tabletop walkthrough once a year — responds in hours; a fund improvising it responds in days, and in a breach the days are when the damage compounds.

#### Worked example: a ransomware hit and the decision not to pay

Suppose a different Meridian failure: an employee opens a malicious attachment, and within hours ransomware has encrypted the firm's file server and demanded \$300k in cryptocurrency for the decryption key. Two paths diverge entirely on a decision Maya made months earlier about backups. In the version where Meridian kept encrypted, offline backups tested quarterly, the answer is straightforward: do not pay. The incident-response plan kicks in, the firm isolates the infected machines, wipes them, restores the file server from last night's clean backup, and is operating again within a day. The cost is the forensic investigation to confirm nothing was exfiltrated, the staff time, and a hard lesson — call it \$50k all in, plus a tense day. In the version where backups were an afterthought — never tested, or stored on the same network the ransomware reached and encrypted too — Maya faces a genuinely awful choice: pay \$300k to a criminal with no guarantee the key works and a real chance of being targeted again, or lose the data entirely and possibly the fund. The two outcomes were decided not in the moment of the attack but on the ordinary day Maya chose to keep tested, offline backups. Ransomware has no leverage over a fund that can simply restore.

*Whether ransomware is a bad day or a fatal one is decided entirely by a backup discipline you set up long before the attack.*

## Key-person risk

Every small fund has a single point of failure, and it is almost always a person. The founder is the strategy, the track record, the investor relationships, the trading authority, and frequently the operational glue. Investors know this, allocators probe it, and the fund's own legal documents encode it — which means key-person risk is not a vague worry but a contractual mechanism with teeth.

The mechanism is the **key-person clause** in the limited partnership agreement. It names the individual or individuals the fund depends on and specifies what happens if one of them ceases to devote substantial time to managing the fund — through death, disability, departure, or distraction. The consequences are designed to protect investors: commonly the clause triggers a **redemption right** (investors may withdraw, often with the normal lock-up and gate waived), sometimes a **suspension of the incentive fee accrual**, and in the most protective documents a pathway to an orderly **wind-down** if the firm cannot continue without the named person. The reason the clause exists is that an investor allocated to *that person's judgment*; if the judgment is gone, the basis of the investment is gone, and the investor wants out before a leaderless firm makes things worse.

Figure 7 shows what a real key-person plan looks like the day it is needed — not a clause to fear but a sequence to execute. The trigger fires: the founder dies, is incapacitated, or suddenly departs. A **named deputy** steps in — someone who already has trading authority and knows the positions, not a person who has to be located and briefed from scratch. The book is **risked down** along a pre-agreed path: reduce gross exposure, hedge, or hold, but do not let a shaken team improvise new risk. **Everyone is notified** — LPs, the board if there is one, the administrator, the prime broker, and the regulator where required. The **key-person clause fires**, giving investors their redemption right and suspending the incentive fee. And then the **fork**: either the firm stabilizes and continues under the deputy, or it winds down the fund in good order. The point of the plan is that the worst day is a process the fund has already thought through, not an improvised scramble while the book bleeds.

![A pipeline figure showing a key-person succession plan in six stages from the trigger event through a deputy stepping in, risking down the book, notifying stakeholders, the key-person clause firing, and the fork between stabilizing or winding down](/imgs/blogs/operational-cyber-and-key-person-risk-7.png)

Reducing key-person risk is mostly about *building a firm rather than a personality cult*. That means hiring and empowering a genuine deputy who can run the book in an emergency — which ties directly into [building the team: who to hire and when](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when), where the sequencing of the first operational and investment hires determines how exposed the founder remains. It means documenting processes so knowledge does not live only in one head. It means buying **key-person insurance** — a life-and-disability policy on the founder, owned by the management company, that provides cash to keep the firm running or to fund an orderly wind-down. And it means being honest with investors about the plan, because an LP who has seen a credible succession story is far less likely to bolt at the first sign of trouble.

Key-person risk also has a quieter, non-catastrophic form that founders miss because they fixate on the dramatic version. The founder does not have to die for key-person risk to bite; the founder merely has to become *unavailable* in ordinary ways — extended illness, a family emergency, burnout, or the simple fact of being in a multi-day meeting with an allocator while the desk needs a decision. A firm in which only the founder can approve a wire, only the founder knows the password to the trading system, and only the founder has the relationship with the largest LP is a firm that degrades every time the founder steps away, not just when the founder is gone for good. The remedy is the same boring duplication that defeats every other risk in this post: a deputy with real authority, credentials in a shared vault rather than one person's memory, and documented procedures that let the firm run for a week without the founder physically present. A founder who cannot take a two-week vacation without the firm seizing up has a key-person problem long before any accident.

There is a subtle tension a founder must navigate honestly. The very thing that raised the capital — *this person's* judgment, *this person's* track record — is the thing the key-person clause exists to protect against. Investors allocated to Maya, not to a process, and they are right to want out if Maya is gone. The founder cannot resolve that tension by pretending the firm is not about her; she can only manage it by building enough depth that her absence is survivable and by being candid with investors that the depth exists. The most credible succession stories are concrete: a named deputy who has actually traded the book during the founder's vacations, a written plan investors have seen, and key-person insurance sized to fund the firm through a transition. Vagueness here reads as denial, and an ODD team that hears "I have not really thought about what happens if you are gone" has heard everything it needs to.

#### Worked example: a key-person event and the redemption right

Suppose Maya is seriously injured in an accident and cannot manage Meridian for an extended period. The LPA names her as the key person, and her absence beyond the defined window triggers the key-person clause. Meridian holds \$200M. The clause gives every investor the right to redeem at the next quarter-end with the normal one-year lock-up waived, and it suspends the incentive fee from the trigger date. Two outcomes diverge sharply. In the firm Maya built as a personality cult, there is no deputy with trading authority, the team freezes, investors representing \$150M of the \$200M file to redeem, and the firm is forced into a fire-sale wind-down that destroys value for everyone. In the firm Maya built with a documented succession plan and a deputy who has run the book before, the deputy risks the portfolio down on the pre-agreed path, communicates a clear plan to LPs within days, and although some investors still redeem — say \$60M — enough capital stays for the firm to survive Maya's recovery or to wind down in good order rather than in panic. Same event, same clause; the difference is entirely the plan that existed before the trigger.

*The key-person clause is not the risk; the risk is having no answer the day the clause fires.*

## Fraud controls

Return to the hook, because it is the most common and most preventable way money leaves a fund, and it is defeated by controls so simple they feel insulting until the day they save you. Figure 3 walks through the \$2M wire-fraud attempt against Meridian and the two controls that stopped it.

![A pipeline figure showing a wire-fraud attempt in six stages from the spoofed email lure through operations receiving it, the dual-authorization rule requiring a second approver, the callback control verifying with the founder, the founder denying the request, and the wire being held](/imgs/blogs/operational-cyber-and-key-person-risk-3.png)

The fraud relies on two things: **urgency** and **a single person acting alone**. The spoofed email manufactures urgency — close today, keep it quiet — to stop the target from pausing to verify. And it assumes one trusted employee can move money on their own authority. The two controls remove exactly those two assumptions.

**Dual authorization** removes the single-actor assumption. Any payment above a defined threshold requires two designated people to approve it; one person, however senior, cannot release the wire alone. This is segregation of duties applied to cash: the person who can *request* a payment is not the only person who can *release* it. The threshold should be low enough to catch a meaningful fraud and high enough not to gum up routine operating expenses — many funds set it at a few tens of thousands of dollars and require dual sign-off above that.

**The callback** removes the urgency assumption and is the control that actually defeats business-email compromise. Before acting on any payment instruction — and especially before acting on a *change to existing bank details*, which is the highest-risk request of all — the approver independently verifies the request by calling a known, pre-recorded number. Not the number in the email. Not a reply to the email thread. The number in the firm's contact file, recorded before any of this started. Because the entire BEC playbook depends on the victim staying inside the channel the attacker controls, a single phone call to an out-of-band number collapses it. The fraudster cannot answer Maya's real phone.

A few more controls round out the defense. **Beneficiary verification** treats every new payee and every change to existing bank details as a high-risk event requiring callback verification, because changing where an existing, legitimate payment goes is the fraud that slips past people who would have questioned a wholly new wire. This *beneficiary-change attack* deserves special fear: the attacker does not invent a new payment that might raise eyebrows; they wait for a real, expected payment — a fee to a vendor the firm pays every quarter, a redemption to an investor — and simply change the bank details at the last moment, often with a plausible cover story ("our bank changed, please update our details"). The payment itself looks entirely routine; only the destination is wrong. The only reliable defense is to verify every change of existing bank details by callback to a number you already had on file, treating the request to change details as more suspicious than a request to make a new payment, not less.

**Standing payment instructions** for routine, recurring payments — to the administrator, to the auditor — reduce the number of ad-hoc wires that have to be judged in the moment, because a payment that follows a pre-verified template is harder to redirect than one typed fresh each time. **Logging and reconciliation** ensure every payment, approved or denied, leaves a record that the administrator and the annual audit can check, so that a fraud that somehow gets through is caught fast rather than discovered months later; the denied-request log matters as much as the approved one, because a pattern of denied attempts is itself the early signal of an active attack. And the **independent administrator** is the backstop here too: when the party that reconciles the cash is outside the manager, a fraudulent payment that escaped the manager's own controls still has to survive an independent reconciliation, which is one more place it can be caught. All of this is the cash-control layer that sits on top of the treasury and counterparty machinery covered in [cash, treasury, and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk): treasury decides *whether* the cash should move; fraud controls decide *whether the instruction to move it is real*.

#### Worked example: the \$2M wire stopped by dual authorization and a callback

Walk the hook through the numbers. The spoofed email instructs Meridian's operations lead to wire \$2M to a "deal" account. Meridian's dual-authorization threshold is \$50k, so a \$2M wire cannot be released by the operations lead alone; a second approver must sign off. The second approver's mandatory step is a callback to Maya on the number in the contact file. He calls; Maya, traveling but reachable, says she sent no such email and authorized no such deal. The wire is held, the email is reported as phishing, and the firm's incident plan kicks in to check whether any system was actually compromised. The cost of the control that saved \$2M was effectively zero — a policy, a threshold in the bank portal, and a thirty-second phone call. Now run the counterfactual: had Meridian set its dual-authorization threshold above \$2M, or had it allowed the callback to be "verified" by replying to the email, the \$2M would have left the account and, as the FBI consistently reports for business-email-compromise losses, very likely been moved beyond recovery within hours. The difference between a fund that loses \$2M and one that does not is a thirty-second call to the right number.

*The control that defeats a sophisticated fraud is almost always boring, cheap, and the first thing a rushed founder is tempted to skip.*

## Common misconceptions

These risks survive because comfortable beliefs keep founders from spending the modest effort that would defend against them. Six worth naming and killing.

**"We are too small to be a target."** This is the belief that gets small funds breached. Attackers are not hand-picking famous funds; they run automated phishing at scale and follow the money. A small fund holds investor Social Security numbers and bank details and has the ability to move large sums — that is precisely the profile a business-email-compromise attacker wants, and a small fund usually has *weaker* controls than a large one, which makes it an easier target, not a less interesting one. Size protects you from a nation-state's attention, not from the volume crime that actually empties accounts.

**"Cybersecurity is the IT vendor's problem."** The IT vendor manages devices and infrastructure; it does not own the firm's risk, and it is itself a third party that can be breached and become the path in. The controls that actually stop the most expensive losses — dual authorization, callbacks, training your people to distrust urgent wire requests — are *business-process* controls the founder owns, not technology the vendor configures. And the SEC examines the *adviser*, not the IT vendor, on cybersecurity governance. Outsourcing the work does not outsource the responsibility.

**"The BCP is a formality for the due-diligence questionnaire."** A plan you have never tested is a plan you do not have. The whole value of a business-continuity plan is that the worst day becomes a rehearsed process rather than an improvised scramble, and you only discover where it breaks by testing it — invariably it breaks at the one login nobody else has or the broker line only the founder can call. The allocator who asks "when did you last test your BCP and what did you find" is checking for exactly this, and "we wrote it" is not an answer.

**"Strong returns will carry us past any operational gap."** They will not, because operational due diligence is a *separate, veto-wielding* process from investment due diligence, as we cover in detail in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto). A fund with a brilliant track record and a missing wire control, a captive administrator, or no succession plan gets rejected by the ODD team before the investment team ever debates the returns. The market risk is what you are paid to take; the operational risk is what disqualifies you from being paid at all.

**"Key-person planning is morbid and premature."** It is neither. It is contractual infrastructure that already exists in your LPA whether you plan for it or not — the key-person clause will fire on the trigger regardless of whether you have a deputy ready. Planning does not make the event more likely; it makes the event survivable. A founder who has built a real deputy and a written succession plan has converted a fund-ending event into a manageable one, and signaled to investors that the firm is more durable than one person.

**"Fraud controls slow us down for no real benefit."** Dual authorization and callbacks add seconds to routine payments and a phone call to large ones. That friction is the entire point: the fraud depends on speed and a single actor, and the control reintroduces a pause and a second human. A founder who finds the wire control annoying should remember that the annoyance is the same mechanism that holds the \$2M in the account when the spoofed email arrives — which it eventually will.

## How it plays out in the real world

The named disasters that founders study for *market* lessons are, on closer inspection, often operational at the core. **Madoff** is the canonical case, and its lesson is squarely in this post's territory. The scheme survived for decades not because the fake returns were clever but because the *controls everyone now treats as mandatory were absent*: there was no independent administrator, the auditor was a tiny, unknown shop incapable of auditing a firm that size, and Madoff self-custodied the assets, so there was no independent party to confirm the securities even existed. Every one of those is an operational and fraud-control failure. The roughly \$65B of fabricated statement value and the roughly \$17.5B of lost principal were enabled by the *absence of segregation of duties* between the person running the money and the people supposed to be independently verifying it. The entire modern operational-due-diligence checklist — independent administrator, independent custody, a real auditor, segregation of duties — is, in a sense, a direct response to Madoff.

On the cyber and wire-fraud side, the pattern is less famous per incident but far more common in aggregate. Business-email-compromise losses across all sectors run into the billions of dollars per year by the FBI's reporting, and financial firms are squarely in the crosshairs because they move money for a living. The losses rarely make headlines individually — a fund that wires \$2M to a fraudster usually does not advertise it — but the steady drumbeat of these incidents is exactly why dual authorization and callbacks have moved from "nice to have" to table stakes in any operational-due-diligence review. Allocators ask about wire controls specifically because they have seen the failures.

The third-party and counterparty dimension has its own cautionary case in **Archegos**, the family office whose 2021 collapse cost its prime brokers more than \$10B — Credit Suisse alone reported losses around \$5.5B. The headline lesson is usually framed as hidden leverage, but the operational lesson runs the other way, from the *banks'* perspective: Archegos had spread concentrated, swap-based positions across multiple prime brokers, and no single counterparty could see the whole picture. Each prime thought it understood its own exposure; none understood the total. For a fund founder the lesson is double-edged. Your prime brokers are third parties whose health is your counterparty risk, and a prime that takes a catastrophic loss elsewhere — as several did on Archegos — can tighten terms, pull financing, or fail in ways that flow straight into your operation regardless of how your own book is positioned. The third-party risk is not only that your vendor is breached or goes bankrupt; it is that your vendor is exposed to *someone else's* failure, and that exposure becomes yours through the contract you both signed. Spreading critical relationships, reading the terms that govern what happens when a counterparty is stressed, and watching the financial health of the firms you depend on are all part of the same vendor-oversight discipline.

Key-person events play out quietly and constantly. Funds close when a founder retires without a successor, when a sole portfolio manager has a health event, when a key person leaves to start their own firm and takes the relationships. These rarely become public scandals; they become quiet wind-downs and returned capital. The key-person clause does its job — investors get out — but the firm that built no successor simply ends, while the firm that did sometimes survives. The difference is never visible in the track record and always visible in the operating-model documents an ODD team reads.

The thread through all of it is the one Figure 4 draws: a fund built as a single point of failure — one person who holds everything, one device with no backup, one approver who can move cash alone — fails the first time any single thing is unavailable, while a resilient fund has duplicated the boring essentials so that any one failure is an inconvenience, not an ending. Resilience is not heroics or cleverness. It is the unglamorous duplication of every single thing the fund cannot run without.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters most in the months *before* you launch and in the first review by a serious allocator — because operational, cyber, and key-person controls are exactly what an operational-due-diligence team checks, and they have a veto. Build the wire controls, the tested BCP, the cyber baseline, and the succession plan *before* the ODD analyst asks, not after, because retrofitting them under examination signals immaturity at the worst possible moment. And maintain them continuously: a control that was real at launch and quietly decayed — the BCP never re-tested, the dual-authorization threshold raised "just this once," the deputy who left and was never replaced — is the gap that the spoofed email or the key-person event finds.

The deeper point for a founder is one of altitude. You launched a fund because you can run money, and running money will always feel like the real work. But the firm dies from the boring risks far more often than from the exciting ones, which means the founder's job is to give operations, cybersecurity, and continuity the same first-class attention as the portfolio. The promise you sell investors is not only that you will compound their capital with skill, but that you will do it inside a structure that protects them — and these controls *are* that structure.
