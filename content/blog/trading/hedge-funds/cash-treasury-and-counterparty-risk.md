> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Funds die at the treasury desk far more often than at the trading desk; the cause of death is almost never a bad idea, it is the cash and collateral plumbing failing under stress.
>
> - **Margin and collateral move every day.** A falling book raises the margin your prime broker demands at the same moment it drains the cash you have to meet it. The cascade, not the loss, is what kills you.
> - **Your prime broker can fail, and your cash can fail with it.** Rehypothecation means assets pledged to a prime can be reused, frozen, and tied up for years if that prime collapses. Lehman 2008 is the lesson, and it is why multi-prime is now standard.
> - **A deliberate liquidity buffer is a position, not idle cash.** Size it to a stress week, not a calm one. The fund that holds \$45M aside survives the \$37M draw that wipes out the fund holding nothing back.
> - **The one fact to keep:** a fund is solvent only as long as its treasury desk can find the cash to meet the next call. Operations, not alpha, is what runs out first.

A portfolio manager I will call **Maya** ran a long/short equity book she had every right to be proud of. Her **Meridian Fund** held \$200M, her shorts were well-chosen, her longs were cheap, and over three years her gross calls had been right far more often than wrong. In the spring of a bad year, the market dropped hard for six straight sessions. Her positions were *fine* — her thesis on every name was intact, and within two months most of those names had recovered. None of that mattered. By the fourth day of the drawdown, her single prime broker was demanding more margin than she had cash to post, the prime started selling her positions out from under her to protect its own loan, and her investors — watching the daily marks — filed redemption requests she could not fund. Meridian closed. Not because Maya was wrong about a single stock, but because the treasury desk ran out of cash before the trading desk was proven right.

This is the part of running a fund that nobody who has only ever *run money* has had to think about. When you are a PM inside a larger firm, someone else worries about where your cash sits, who your prime broker is, what happens if that prime broker fails, and whether you can meet a margin call at 6 a.m. on a Monday. The moment you launch your own fund, that someone is you. And the uncomfortable truth, repeated by every operationally-scarred founder you will ever meet, is that **funds die at the treasury desk far more often than at the trading desk.** A wrong trade costs you some performance. A broken cash-and-collateral process costs you the fund.

This post is about that plumbing: how cash and collateral actually move, what counterparty risk really is, why your prime broker is a counterparty you can lose money to, the lesson the industry learned from Lehman in 2008, why serious funds spread across multiple primes, how custody and asset protection work, and how to size a liquidity buffer that lets you survive a bad week instead of dying in it. Figure 1 is the map: a normal day at the treasury desk, where the cash and collateral have to clear margin, financing, and reconciliation before the book is ever "flat and funded."

## Foundations: the plumbing of cash and risk

Before any of the war stories make sense, the vocabulary has to be solid. None of these terms is hard, but together they form a system, and the system is where funds break. Assume you can value a portfolio in your sleep but have never had to *fund* one.

**Treasury.** Inside a fund, treasury is the function that makes sure the right amount of cash is in the right account at the right time. It manages the fund's cash balances, meets margin calls, moves collateral, decides where idle cash sits, monitors how much the fund owes each counterparty, and watches the liquidity runway — how many days of demands the fund could meet before it ran dry. At a large fund this is a desk of its own; at Maya's fund it is Maya, her operations lead, and a spreadsheet that must never be wrong. Treasury is not glamorous. It is also the single most common place a fund quietly becomes insolvent while the trading desk thinks everything is fine.

**Margin.** When a fund borrows to take a position, or holds a derivative, the counterparty demands collateral against the risk that the fund cannot pay what it owes. That collateral is *margin*. There are two kinds, and confusing them is dangerous.

- **Initial margin** is the cushion posted up front when a position is opened — a percentage of the position's value (the *haircut*) that protects the counterparty against the position moving against the fund before it can be closed out. If a prime lends against a stock with a 25% initial margin requirement, the fund must put up \$25 of its own cash for every \$100 of stock financed.
- **Variation margin** is the daily settlement of gains and losses. Each day the position is marked to market; if it lost value, the fund posts cash to the counterparty to cover the loss; if it gained, cash flows back. Variation margin is the relentless daily drip that drains a fund in a drawdown — every day the book falls, more cash leaves to meet the call.

**Collateral.** The assets a fund pledges to back its obligations. Cash is the cleanest collateral. Securities can be pledged too, but a counterparty applies a haircut to them — it will lend less than they are worth, because in a forced sale it might not recover full value. In a stressed market, haircuts *widen* (the counterparty trusts the collateral less), which means the fund must post *more* collateral for the same position at the exact moment cash is scarcest.

**Counterparty risk.** The risk that the other side of a deal — your prime broker, your derivatives dealer, your clearing bank — fails to perform: it goes bankrupt, freezes accounts, defaults on a return of your collateral, or simply cannot return your assets when you ask. A fund has counterparty risk to *everyone it transacts with*, and the largest single concentration of it is usually the prime broker.

**Prime broker (PB).** The bank that provides the core services a fund needs to operate: financing and leverage (lending against the book), margin, securities lending (so the fund can borrow shares to short), custody of assets, trade execution and clearing, and capital introduction (helping the fund meet investors). The prime broker sits at the center of the fund's operations — and therefore at the center of its counterparty risk. We unpack the execution and financing side in detail in [the trade lifecycle and prime brokerage](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/trade-lifecycle-execution-and-prime-brokerage); here the lens is what happens when that prime is *also* the place all your assets live.

**Rehypothecation.** The single most important word in this post. When a fund pledges assets to a prime broker as collateral, the prime is often permitted — under the prime brokerage agreement — to *reuse* those assets: to lend them out, pledge them to its own creditors, or finance its own activities. The collateral has been pledged, then re-pledged ("rehypothecated") down a chain. This is efficient and cheap in normal times. It is catastrophic when the prime fails, because the fund's assets are now entangled in the failed prime's bankruptcy, and getting them back can take *years*. Rehypothecation is why "my assets are safe at my prime broker" is one of the most dangerous beliefs a founder can hold.

**Custody.** Where assets are *held*. Assets in true custody are held in the fund's name, segregated from the custodian's own balance sheet, and not available to the custodian's creditors if the custodian fails. Assets sitting in a prime broker's margin account are a different animal — they are collateral, potentially rehypothecated, and exposed to the prime's failure. The distinction between *custodied* and *margined* assets is the distinction between assets you will get back and assets you might not.

**Liquidity buffer.** Cash (or near-cash like government money-market funds) the fund deliberately holds aside, not invested in the strategy, available to meet margin calls, redemptions, and expenses in a bad week without forcing the fund to sell positions at the worst possible time. The buffer is not lazy cash. It is a position — a put option on survival — and sizing it correctly is one of the most important risk decisions a founder makes. Treating risk as a business function, not just a portfolio number, is the broader theme we develop in [risk management as a business function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function).

Hold those ten terms together and the thesis of this post becomes mechanical rather than philosophical: a fund stays solvent only as long as its treasury function can find the cash to meet the next call from its counterparties, while keeping enough of its assets genuinely protected that a single counterparty's failure cannot freeze the whole operation. Lose either of those and the quality of the trading book is irrelevant.

## Cash and collateral management

Start with the daily reality, because cash management sounds boring until you watch it fail in real time.

Every trading day, a fund's positions are marked to market. That movement flows two ways at once. It changes the fund's net asset value (NAV) — the per-share value investors care about — and it changes what the fund owes its counterparties. A losing day means variation margin flows *out*: the fund posts cash to its prime and its derivative counterparties to cover the day's losses. A winning day means cash flows back in. On top of that, the prime broker is financing the book — lending against the long positions and against the short proceeds — and charges interest on that financing daily. Idle cash, meanwhile, should not sit dead in a non-interest-bearing account; treasury sweeps it into government money-market funds or short Treasury bills to earn the cash rate. And at the end of every day, the fund's own books must be reconciled against the prime broker's records: every position, every cash balance, every financing charge. A *break* — a discrepancy between the fund's books and the prime's — is a treasury fire that must be put out before the next day opens, because a break can mean a missed margin call, a misbooked position, or, in the worst case, the early signal of fraud.

That whole loop is Figure 1. The point of drawing it is that none of it is about alpha. It is the cost of *being allowed to hold positions at all.*

The reason cash management is hard is that the demands on cash are correlated with each other and with the worst market conditions. In a calm market, margin requirements are stable, haircuts are tight, financing is cheap, and investors are happy. In a stressed market, four things happen at once: variation margin spikes because the book is losing money daily; initial margin and haircuts widen because the prime is suddenly less willing to lend against stressed collateral; financing gets more expensive or is pulled entirely; and investors start asking for their money back. The cash demands all arrive together, and they arrive precisely when selling assets to raise cash is most expensive. This is the cascade.

Collateral management is the discipline of meeting those demands without doing more damage than necessary. The first rule is to know, every single day, the difference between the fund's *total* assets and its *free* cash — the cash actually available to meet a call. A fund can be "rich" on paper and dead on cash. The second rule is to understand which of your assets can be posted as collateral and at what haircut, because in a pinch you would rather post a Treasury bill at a 1% haircut than be forced to sell an illiquid equity at a fire-sale price. The third rule is to never let a single counterparty's margin terms be a black box: you must know your prime's right to raise margin, change haircuts, and demand collateral intraday, because in a crisis it will use every one of those rights.

There is a fourth rule that founders learn the hard way: the *order* in which you raise cash matters as much as having it. A treasury desk under pressure has a ladder of sources, from cheapest to most damaging. At the top sits the deliberate liquidity buffer — cash held aside for exactly this moment, costing nothing to deploy. Below that comes posting high-quality collateral the fund already holds, such as Treasury bills, which the prime accepts at a tiny haircut without forcing a sale. Below that comes drawing on a committed financing line if one was negotiated in calmer times. Only at the bottom of the ladder, when the cheaper rungs are exhausted, does the fund sell positions into a falling market — and that bottom rung is where the permanent damage happens, because forced sales realize losses, shrink the fee-earning asset base, and signal distress to every counterparty watching the tape. A founder who has mapped this ladder *before* the bad week works calmly down it; a founder who has not, panics straight to the bottom rung. The entire architecture of this post — buffer, multi-prime, custody, conservative terms — is really about adding rungs to the top of that ladder so the fund never has to touch the bottom.

A subtler point concerns the *currency and form* of collateral. A prime broker does not value all collateral equally. Cash in the base currency is worth its face. Government bills carry a small haircut. Liquid blue-chip equities carry a larger one. Illiquid or concentrated positions may not be accepted as collateral at all, or accepted at a punitive haircut that makes them nearly worthless as a funding source. This creates a trap that catches funds running less-liquid strategies: the very positions that are hardest to sell in a stress are also the ones the prime will not lend against, so a fund that *looks* well-collateralized in calm markets can find its collateral suddenly worthless as funding precisely when it needs cash. The lesson is that a treasury desk must track not just the *value* of the book but its *fundability* — how much genuine, prime-acceptable collateral capacity the fund actually has — and that number is always smaller, and falls faster in a stress, than the headline NAV.

#### Worked example: a \$24M margin call in a 10% drawdown

Maya's Meridian Fund holds \$200M of capital and runs the book at roughly 2× gross leverage — about \$400M of gross exposure (longs plus shorts), financed by her prime. Her prime's blended initial margin requirement on the book is 20%, so on day zero the prime holds about \$80M of margin against the \$400M gross, and Maya keeps a free-cash buffer of \$60M against required margin of \$40M on her net financed long exposure. (We will use round numbers; the point is the *shape*, not the decimals.)

Now the market falls 6% over a bad week, and Maya's net long tilt means the book loses roughly 10% of its \$200M capital — about \$20M — over those days. Three things compound. First, variation margin: she must post cash daily to cover the marked losses, draining her free cash. Second, the prime *widens haircuts* on a now-stressed portfolio, so required margin rises from \$40M toward \$90M even though her gross exposure has, if anything, fallen. Third, financing costs tick up. Figure 4 traces the two lines that matter — required margin climbing and free cash draining — and the day they cross.

By day four, required margin (~\$76M) exceeds free cash available (~\$24M). Maya now has a \$24M-plus call she cannot meet from cash. The cash has to come from somewhere: she either sells positions into a falling market (locking in losses and shrinking the book), draws on a pre-arranged liquidity buffer if she sized one, or — if she has neither — the prime broker sells her positions *for* her, at prices it chooses, to protect its loan. The trade thesis was never tested. The treasury desk lost first.

*A drawdown does not kill a fund by being large; it kills by raising the cash you owe at the same moment it drains the cash you have.*

![A line chart with day on the horizontal axis and USD millions on the vertical axis showing required margin rising from 40 to 90 while free cash available falls from 60 to 10, with the lines crossing at day four where forced sales begin](/imgs/blogs/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk-4.png)

The chart is the whole argument in one picture. The amber line (margin required) and the blue line (free cash available) start comfortably apart — a \$60M buffer against \$40M of margin looks safe in a calm market. But the two lines move *toward* each other under stress, because the same drawdown that drains cash also raises the margin demand. The day they cross is the day the fund stops being in control of its own selling. Everything before that day is a treasury problem you can manage; everything after it is a liquidation someone else runs for you.

## Counterparty risk: your prime broker can fail

Here is the belief that has killed funds: *the prime broker is a giant, regulated bank, so my cash and assets sitting there are safe.* It is wrong in two distinct ways, and a founder must internalize both.

First, **the prime broker can fail.** This is not a hypothetical. Major prime brokers have collapsed (Lehman Brothers, Bear Stearns) or come within a hair of it. When a prime fails, the fund is not a privileged creditor with a guaranteed claim on its own assets. It is a *counterparty* with a claim that depends entirely on how its assets were held — in segregated custody (you get them back, eventually) or as rehypothecated collateral (you join the queue of the prime's creditors in a bankruptcy that can drag on for years).

Second, even when the prime survives, **it can act against you in a crisis.** The prime's first duty is to protect itself, not to keep your fund alive. In stress it will raise your margin, widen your haircuts, reduce or pull your financing, and — if you cannot meet a call — sell your positions to cover its loan. These are contractual rights it negotiated into the prime brokerage agreement, and in a crisis it will exercise all of them. A prime that loves you in calm markets can be the proximate cause of your death in a bad week, entirely within its rights.

Figure 2 maps where a fund's exposure actually lives. The fund (blue, center) is connected to several primes and a custodian and a clearing house and a money fund. Each of those connections is a counterparty relationship — a separate place the fund's cash or assets sit, and a separate point of failure. The naive mental model is "my assets are *at my fund*." The real picture is "my assets are scattered across half a dozen counterparties' balance sheets, and my survival depends on the weakest of them."

![A layered graph with the fund at the left connecting to three prime brokers and a money fund, with two of the primes connecting onward to a custodian and a clearing house, showing the fund's exposure spread across multiple counterparties](/imgs/blogs/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk-2.png)

The reason this matters more for a fund than for an ordinary investor is leverage and rehypothecation. An ordinary brokerage account holds your stock in your name; the broker cannot use it. A prime brokerage relationship is built on the prime financing your book and, in exchange, having the right to use your pledged assets. That arrangement is what makes leverage cheap. It is also what converts "my assets" into "assets entangled with my prime's solvency." The cheaper your financing, the more of your assets you have likely allowed the prime to rehypothecate, and the more exposed you are if it fails.

A second-order point that founders miss: counterparty risk is not only about your *own* exposure. It is about whether your counterparty has hidden exposures that could blow it up. A prime that has extended huge, hidden leverage to *another* client — through total-return swaps, say — can be destabilized by that other client's collapse, and then your assets are caught in the wreckage. This is the lesson of Archegos in 2021: a single family office, Bill Hwang's Archegos Capital Management, used concentrated total-return swaps spread across multiple primes to build hidden leverage, and when it collapsed it inflicted over \$10B in losses on those prime brokers — Credit Suisse alone lost roughly \$5.5B. The primes' other clients were exposed to the instability of primes that had taken a blind, concentrated bet. Your counterparty's counterparty risk is your problem too. When everything correlates in a crisis — your losses, your margin calls, your prime's other clients' losses — diversification you thought you had evaporates, a dynamic we explore from the portfolio side in [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis).

This is why sophisticated treasury desks monitor the *health of their counterparties* as a live risk, not a one-time onboarding check. The signals are the same ones the market watches for any financial institution: the prime's credit spreads (the cost of insuring its debt against default), its share price relative to peers, the tone of its earnings calls, downgrades from rating agencies, and news of large losses or regulatory action. A prime whose credit-default-swap spread is blowing out is a prime that funds should be quietly reducing exposure to, *before* the headline confirms what the market already suspects. Bear Stearns and Lehman both telegraphed their distress in the credit markets for months before they failed; the funds that moved assets early survived intact, and the funds that waited for certainty were frozen. The uncomfortable reality is that there is no neutral, cost-free way to monitor a counterparty's health — reducing exposure to a wobbling prime is expensive and can itself accelerate the prime's distress if many clients do it at once. But a founder who has never even *looked* at their prime's credit spread is flying blind on the single largest counterparty risk the fund carries. Treasury is partly a credit-analysis function aimed at your own brokers, and a founder who came up purely on the equity desk has to learn to read those signals.

A related blind spot is the *concentration of services* at a single counterparty. A prime broker is not just where your assets sit — it is often your executing broker, your securities lender (the source of the shares you borrow to short), your financing provider, and your cash custodian, all in one relationship. That bundling is convenient and cheap, but it means a single counterparty's failure does not just freeze your assets; it simultaneously cuts off your ability to execute, to borrow shorts, to finance the book, and to access cash. The failure is *correlated across every service you depend on*, because they all run through the same entity. Multi-prime is partly a defense against asset loss, but it is just as much a defense against losing every operational capability at once. A fund with two primes can still execute, finance, and short through the survivor while the failed prime is unwound; a fund with one prime loses all of it in a single morning.

## The Lehman 2008 lesson: rehypothecation and frozen assets

September 2008 is the case every fund founder must know cold, because it is the moment the industry learned that "my prime is a big safe bank" was a fatal assumption.

When Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy, it was a prime broker to a great many hedge funds. Those funds had pledged assets to Lehman as collateral against financing. Crucially, much of that collateral had been *rehypothecated* — reused by Lehman to fund its own activities, particularly through Lehman's London entity, where the rules on reuse were more permissive than in the United States. When Lehman collapsed, the funds discovered an ugly distinction. Assets that had been kept in genuine segregated custody were recoverable. Assets that had been rehypothecated were not the fund's property anymore in any practical sense — they were tangled in the estate of a bankrupt broker. Funds that thought they held liquid, accessible positions found those positions *frozen*, claimable only as unsecured creditors in a bankruptcy that took years to resolve and paid back cents on the dollar for a long time.

The damage was not limited to the funds that lost assets outright. Even funds whose assets were eventually recoverable were operationally crippled in the moment: they could not access cash to meet calls at other counterparties, could not trade the frozen positions, and could not meet redemptions. A fund with a perfectly good portfolio could be functionally dead because that portfolio was stuck inside a failed prime. The trading book was fine. The treasury arrangement killed the fund.

Three lessons came out of Lehman and they are now industry orthodoxy.

1. **Rehypothecation is a real, sizable exposure, not a technicality.** The amount of your assets a prime is permitted to reuse is a number you must know, negotiate, and cap. US rules limit customer-asset reuse more tightly than the historical UK regime did, which is one reason where your prime *books* your assets matters as much as who the prime is.
2. **Segregated custody is qualitatively safer than margined assets at a prime.** Assets held in true custody, in the fund's name, segregated from the prime's balance sheet, survive the prime's failure. Assets sitting as rehypothecated collateral may not.
3. **Single-prime concentration is a survival risk, not just an inconvenience.** If everything you own sits at one prime and that prime fails, you are frozen entirely. If your assets are split across multiple primes plus an independent custodian, one prime's failure freezes a fraction, not the whole.

#### Worked example: rehypothecation exposure if a single prime holding \$140M fails

Suppose Maya had run Meridian the easy way — one prime, all assets there. Of her \$200M, say \$140M of long positions are financed and pledged to the prime, and under the prime brokerage agreement the prime is permitted to rehypothecate up to, say, 140% of her debit balance. With \$60M of financing extended to her, the prime could lawfully reuse up to roughly \$84M of her assets — pledging her stock onward to its own creditors. The remaining \$56M of the pledged \$140M, plus whatever she held in segregated custody, would be in a different, safer bucket.

Now the prime fails. The roughly \$84M of rehypothecated assets is no longer practically Maya's — it is entangled in the prime's bankruptcy estate, and Maya is one creditor among many waiting for a multi-year resolution. She might recover most of it eventually, but "eventually" does not pay this month's margin call at her *other* counterparties, fund this month's redemptions, or keep her fund trading. The instant the prime fails, a \$200M fund is operating as though it has tens of millions frozen and inaccessible. That is enough to trigger a death spiral even if the assets are ultimately recoverable.

*Rehypothecation does not feel like risk when financing is cheap and the prime is healthy; it feels like risk the morning the prime is gone and your liquid book turns out to be locked in someone else's bankruptcy.*

The deeper point is timing. Rehypothecation risk is invisible in normal times — it is the thing that makes your financing cheap, so you are *rewarded* for accepting it right up until the day it costs you the fund. Risk that pays you to ignore it is the most dangerous kind, because the market trains you to take more of it. The founder's job is to price that risk *before* the crisis, when it is still abstract, and to refuse the cheapest financing if it requires giving a single prime unlimited reuse of everything you own.

## Multiple primes: spreading the counterparty risk

The direct answer to single-prime concentration is to use more than one prime broker. After Lehman, multi-prime went from an exotic choice to the institutional default, and any operational due diligence team — the people who can veto an allocation regardless of returns, as we cover in [operational due diligence, the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto) — will ask a fund of any size how its prime relationships are structured.

The logic is the same as portfolio diversification, applied to *counterparties* instead of positions. If all your assets sit at one prime, that prime's failure is a 100% loss of access. If your assets are split across three primes, one prime's failure freezes roughly a third while two thirds keep operating — and "keep operating" is the difference between a hard quarter and a dead fund. Figure 3 is the before-and-after: the single-prime "Lehman trap" on the left, where one prime's failure freezes everything and the fund cannot trade or redeem, versus the multi-prime setup on the right, where one prime's failure leaves two primes live and the fund keeps operating.

![A before-and-after comparison with a single-prime column on the left where the prime's failure freezes all cash and rehypothecated longs and the fund cannot trade, and a multi-prime column on the right where cash is split across three primes, custody assets are segregated, and one prime's failure leaves the fund operating](/imgs/blogs/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk-3.png)

Multi-prime is not free, and the honesty mandate requires being clear about the costs. Each prime relationship carries fixed operational overhead: separate accounts to reconcile daily, separate margin terms to track, separate financing rates to optimize, separate legal agreements to negotiate and maintain, separate technology connections. Spreading a book across primes can also mean *less* favorable financing on each one, because primes offer their best terms to clients who give them the whole relationship and the most assets to rehypothecate. A small fund that splits a modest book across three primes may find none of the three treats it as a priority client, gets worse financing rates from all of them, and burns operational time it does not have. This is why very small funds often start with a single prime — or a *mini-prime* (an introducing broker that gives small funds access to a large prime's infrastructure under one umbrella) — and add primes as they grow. The right number of primes is a function of size and complexity, not a moral absolute.

The trade-off, then, is concentration risk against operational cost and financing efficiency. A \$200M fund at two-to-three primes is a defensible institutional setup. A \$30M fund at one mini-prime is a reasonable, honest starting point — provided the founder understands the exposure and has a plan to diversify as assets grow. What is *not* defensible is a \$500M fund parking everything at one prime because it is simpler, and telling investors their cash is "safe at the bank." That fund is one prime-broker headline away from a redemption cascade.

#### Worked example: spreading \$200M across three primes and the cost of doing so

Maya decides to split Meridian's \$200M across three primes — call them Prime A, Prime B, and Prime C. She allocates roughly \$90M to Prime A (her lead, best financing), \$70M to Prime B, and \$40M to Prime C (a mini-prime for a slice of the book and as a backup). Now model the failure she feared.

If Prime A — her largest — fails, roughly \$90M is frozen and \$110M across B and C keeps working. That is still a serious blow, but Meridian can meet calls at B and C, keep trading there, and fund near-term redemptions from its buffer while the Prime A estate is sorted out over the following years. Compare that to the single-prime case, where Prime A's failure would have frozen the entire \$200M and ended the fund. Diversifying counterparties converted a fatal event into a survivable one.

The cost: Maya now reconciles three sets of books every day instead of one, negotiates and tracks three margin agreements, and accepts that none of the three primes gives her the rock-bottom financing rate she could have squeezed from a single all-in relationship — say her blended financing cost rises by 10 to 20 basis points on the financed book. On \$140M of financing, 15 basis points is roughly \$210,000 a year. That is the *premium* she pays for counterparty diversification — and like any insurance premium, it looks like pure cost in every calm year and like the smartest money she ever spent in the one bad one.

*Spreading across primes is buying insurance against a counterparty failure; the premium is paid in basis points and operational hours every year, and it is cheap relative to the event it prevents.*

## Custody and asset protection

Multi-prime spreads the risk; *custody* changes its character. The two work together, and a founder needs both.

The core distinction, again, is between assets that are *yours* in a legally protected sense and assets that are *collateral* exposed to a counterparty's failure. Assets in genuine custody are held in the fund's name, segregated from the custodian's own assets, and ring-fenced from the custodian's creditors. If the custodian fails, those assets are not part of its bankruptcy estate — they are the fund's property, returnable. Assets sitting in a prime broker's margin account, by contrast, are collateral the prime may have rehypothecated, and they are exposed to the prime's failure. The same security — the same 10,000 shares of the same company — can be safe or at risk depending purely on *where it is booked*.

This is why serious funds think carefully about how much of the book *needs* to be at a prime (because it is financed, shorted against, or actively traded) and how much can sit in segregated custody (because it is not being financed and does not need to be reusable by the prime). Unencumbered long positions — assets the fund owns outright, with no financing against them — do not need to be in a prime's rehypothecation pool at all. Moving them into custody removes them from the prime-failure exposure entirely. The fund gives up nothing but the prime's willingness to offer slightly better all-in terms in exchange for having more assets to reuse.

Custody also intersects with one of the most important fraud-prevention controls in the entire industry: the **independent administrator**. We cover the administrator's role in NAV and books in detail in [NAV, accounting, and the administrator](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator), but the asset-protection angle belongs here. A fund should never self-custody its assets and never strike its own NAV in a black box. The reason is Madoff. Bernard Madoff's fraud, revealed in December 2008, was a Ponzi scheme with roughly \$65B of fabricated statement value and about \$17.5B of real principal lost. It was the canonical *operational* failure: there was no independent administrator, no real custody, a tiny unknown auditor, and self-reported, suspiciously smooth returns. Investors who did serious operational due diligence — who asked "who actually holds the assets and who independently verifies they exist" — declined to invest, and they were right. Independent custody and an independent administrator are not bureaucratic boxes to tick. They are the controls that make it *impossible* for a manager to fabricate the existence of assets, and their absence is the brightest red flag in the business.

Figure 7 puts numbers on where Maya's \$200M actually sits, layer by layer from safest to most exposed: \$70M in segregated custody (held away from any prime, safest), \$45M in free cash and government money funds (the liquidity buffer), \$40M margined at the prime (pledged as collateral), and \$45M of rehypothecated longs (reused by the prime, most at risk if it fails). The visual claim is that the fund's assets are not one safe pool — they are a stack of decreasing protection, and the founder's job is to push as much as possible up toward the safe end without starving the book of the financing it needs.

![A four-layer nested stack showing where 200 million dollars of fund assets sit, from segregated custody of 70 million at the top through 45 million in the liquidity buffer and 40 million margined at the prime to 45 million of rehypothecated longs at the bottom labeled most at risk](/imgs/blogs/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk-7.png)

The point of the stack is that *protection is a spectrum the founder controls*, not a binary the prime hands you. Every dollar can be pushed up toward custody or left down in the rehypothecation pool, and the position of each dollar is a deliberate choice with a cost (financing efficiency) and a benefit (survival of a prime failure). A founder who has never looked at this stack does not know how exposed the fund is. A founder who has looked at it, and decided, has done the single most important piece of treasury work there is.

## The liquidity buffer: cash for a bad week

The final piece — and the one most directly under the founder's control — is the deliberate liquidity buffer. Multi-prime and custody protect you from a *counterparty* failing. The buffer protects you from the ordinary, much more common event: a bad week in which margin calls, a partial redemption, and ongoing expenses all demand cash at once, and you must meet them without dumping positions into a falling market.

The buffer is the most misunderstood line on a fund's balance sheet, because it *looks* like a drag on returns. Cash held aside earns the cash rate, not the strategy's return, so an investor glancing at the fund thinks "why is 20% of my money in money-market funds instead of working?" The answer is that the buffer is a position — specifically, a put option on the fund's survival. It pays nothing in the 49 calm weeks and everything in the one bad week, exactly when the fund without a buffer is being forced to liquidate at the bottom. A fund manager who cannot explain to investors why a buffer is a position rather than lazy cash has not understood their own risk. The same logic — paying a premium to own protection against the tail — is the heart of [hedging a portfolio with options](/blog/trading/options-volatility/hedging-a-portfolio-with-options-protective-puts-collars-and-tail-risk); the buffer is the cash-management version of the same trade.

The discipline is to size the buffer against a *stress* scenario, not a calm one. The wrong question is "how much cash do I usually need?" The right question is "what is the worst week I should survive without forced selling, and how much cash does that week demand?" That worst week stacks several demands at once: a spike in variation margin as the book draws down, an increase in required margin as haircuts widen, a partial redemption from a nervous investor, and the ongoing fee and expense run-rate. Sum those, add a margin of safety, and that is the buffer.

#### Worked example: sizing a liquidity buffer for a stress week

Maya models Meridian's worst plausible week. She estimates the stacked cash demands: a \$18M spike in variation margin as the book draws down sharply; another \$10M as the prime widens haircuts on a stressed portfolio; a \$8M partial redemption from one nervous investor whose notice period happens to expire that quarter; and \$1M of fees and expenses that run regardless. That sums to a \$37M stress draw — the cash Meridian could plausibly need in a single bad week, none of it driven by Maya being wrong about a position.

She decides to hold a \$45M liquidity buffer — the \$37M stress draw plus roughly \$8M of headroom for the demand she did not model. That \$45M sits in government money-market funds and short Treasury bills, earning the cash rate, spread away from her primes so a prime failure does not freeze the buffer itself. Figure 6 stacks the demands against the buffer: the \$45M green bar covers the \$37M of stacked red-and-amber demands with room to spare. In the calm quarters, that \$45M "costs" Maya the spread between the cash rate and her strategy return — a real drag she must explain to investors. In the bad week, that same \$45M is the reason Meridian meets every call from cash, sells nothing into the panic, and is still standing when the names recover two months later.

*The buffer is sized to the week you fear, not the week you expect; a fund that holds enough cash to survive its worst plausible week never has to find out whether its best positions would have recovered.*

![A two-bar chart comparing stacked cash demands in a stress week of 37 million dollars made up of variation margin, wider haircuts, a partial redemption, and fees against a liquidity buffer of 45 million dollars that covers the draw with headroom](/imgs/blogs/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk-6.png)

There is a direct connection between the buffer and the fund's *terms* — the lock-ups, gates, and notice periods that govern how fast investors can pull money. The whole architecture of liquidity terms, which we cover in [liquidity terms: lock-ups, gates, and side pockets](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/liquidity-terms-lockups-gates-side-pockets), exists to make the fund's *liabilities* (redemptions) match its *assets* (how fast it can raise cash without damage). A fund that offers monthly redemptions on a book that takes months to sell has a liquidity mismatch that no buffer can fully cover; a fund whose terms give it time, plus a buffer sized to the stress week, has a treasury that can survive the bad week the strategy will eventually deliver. Terms and buffer are two halves of one liquidity defense.

Figure 5 ties the whole post together: it is the mitigant matrix, showing that no single control protects against everything. Multiple primes protect cash and positions if a prime fails, but do not by themselves fund a bad week. Independent custody protects positions from a prime failure but is slower to access. No-rehypothecation terms protect specific assets from being reused but leave cash exposed. The liquidity buffer funds the bad week but does not protect against a prime failing. Daily reconciliation catches breaks early but does not move cash. Only the full stack — multiple primes, independent custody, no-rehypothecation terms on the assets that can bear them, a stress-sized buffer, and disciplined daily reconciliation — protects cash, positions, and the ability to keep trading all at once.

![A matrix with five counterparty-risk mitigants as rows and three protections as columns showing that multiple primes, independent custody, no-rehypothecation terms, a liquidity buffer, and daily reconciliation each protect a different combination of cash, positions, and the ability to keep trading](/imgs/blogs/cash-treasury-and-counterparty-risk-5.png)

## Common misconceptions

The treasury function attracts a particular set of comfortable beliefs, every one of which has helped kill a fund.

**"My cash is always safe at the prime broker."** No. Cash and assets at a prime are exposed to the prime's failure, and pledged assets may be rehypothecated and frozen in a bankruptcy. "Safe" applies to assets in segregated custody, in the fund's name, ring-fenced from the custodian's creditors — not to collateral in a margin account. The whole Lehman lesson is that this belief is false, and believing it is how a fund with a good book ends up frozen.

**"One prime is simpler, so it is fine."** Simpler, yes; fine, only if the fund is small and the founder has consciously priced the concentration risk and has a plan to diversify. For a fund of any size, single-prime concentration is the exact exposure that ended funds in 2008. Simplicity that concentrates a survival risk is not simplicity worth having. A mini-prime is a reasonable single starting point for a tiny fund; an all-eggs-in-one-prime setup for a large fund is negligence dressed as efficiency.

**"A liquidity buffer is lazy cash dragging my returns."** The buffer is a position — a put option on survival that pays in the one week the strategy is underwater and the calls are arriving. Judging it by its return in calm weeks is like judging insurance by the years your house did not burn down. The right benchmark is "did it let me avoid forced selling in the worst week," not "did it match the strategy's return."

**"Rehypothecation is a legal technicality I do not need to understand."** It is the single largest determinant of how much of your book survives a prime failure, and it is the reason your financing is cheap. The amount of your assets a prime can reuse is a negotiated number you must know and cap. A founder who has never asked their prime "how much of my collateral can you rehypothecate, and where do you book it" does not know how exposed the fund is.

**"If my positions are right, the treasury stuff sorts itself out."** This is exactly backwards and it is the thesis of the post. Maya's positions were right; her treasury arrangement killed the fund before the positions were proven. Being right about the market does not generate cash on the day a margin call arrives. Treasury is upstream of being right.

**"The regulators and the size of the bank protect me."** Banks have failed; large primes have collapsed or nearly so. Regulation shapes how assets *should* be held (US reuse limits are tighter than the historical UK regime), which is exactly why *where* your prime books your assets matters — but no regulator hands your frozen assets back to you the morning a prime fails. Protection comes from how *you* structured your custody and primes, not from the counterparty's size.

**"My administrator and auditor mean my treasury is covered."** They are necessary, not sufficient. An independent administrator verifies that the assets exist and the NAV is struck honestly; an auditor checks the financial statements once a year. Neither one meets a margin call, neither one moves your assets out of a failing prime's rehypothecation pool, and neither one sizes your buffer for you. Those are live, daily treasury decisions the founder owns. Confusing the controls that prevent *fraud* with the controls that prevent *insolvency* is how a clean, well-audited fund still dies in a bad week.

## How it plays out in the real world

The named failures in this part of the business are not about bad trades. They are about treasury, counterparty, and concentration risk — and they are the reason every modern operational due diligence checklist exists.

**Lehman Brothers, 2008** is the foundational case, already covered: a prime broker's collapse froze the rehypothecated assets of the funds that used it, crippling funds whose portfolios were otherwise fine. The direct consequence was the industry's pivot to multi-prime, tighter scrutiny of rehypothecation terms, and the rise of operational due diligence as a veto-wielding process separate from investment analysis. Allocators learned that a fund's *survival* depends on its plumbing, and they started checking the plumbing before the returns.

**Archegos Capital Management, 2021** is the modern counterparty-risk case from the *other* side. Archegos, Bill Hwang's family office, built enormous hidden leverage through total-return swaps spread across multiple prime brokers, each of which could see only its own slice and none of which saw the full, concentrated position. When the underlying stocks fell, Archegos could not meet its margin calls, and the primes — racing to liquidate before each other — absorbed over \$10B in losses, with Credit Suisse alone losing roughly \$5.5B. For a fund founder, Archegos cuts two ways. It is a lesson about *your own* hidden leverage and concentration, which can blow up your counterparties; and it is a lesson that *your prime's other clients* are a risk to you, because a prime destabilized by another client's collapse is a prime that may pull your financing or, in the extreme, fail.

**Long-Term Capital Management, 1998** is the leverage-and-margin case. LTCM ran balance-sheet leverage around 25 to 30 times, plus roughly \$1.25T of derivatives notional, on convergence trades that all correlated when Russia defaulted. As the trades moved against it, the margin calls cascaded faster than LTCM could meet them, and the fund lost about \$4.6B in months, requiring a \$3.6B recapitalization organized by the New York Fed with 14 banks. The treasury lesson is brutal and direct: leverage that looks safe because each position is "low risk" becomes lethal when the positions correlate, the margin calls stack, and there is no buffer to meet them. The trades were not even *wrong* in the long run — many converged eventually — but LTCM, like Maya, could not survive long enough to be proven right.

**Madoff, revealed December 2008** is the custody-and-administration case. Roughly \$65B of fabricated statement value, about \$17.5B of real losses, enabled entirely by the *absence* of the controls this post argues for: no independent custody, no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, self-reported returns. The funds and investors who insisted on independent custody and an administrator who could verify the assets actually existed did not invest. Madoff is the proof that custody and administration are not bureaucracy — they are the controls that make a particular category of catastrophe impossible.

Across all four, the pattern is identical and it is the pattern of this post: the cause of death was the plumbing, not the picks. LTCM and Maya died of margin cascades with no buffer. Lehman's clients died of rehypothecation and single-prime concentration. Archegos died of hidden leverage and gave its primes a counterparty-risk lesson. Madoff's investors died of absent custody and administration. A founder who builds the treasury function as if it were as important as the trading function — multiple primes, independent custody, no-rehypothecation terms where they fit, a stress-sized buffer, daily reconciliation, and an independent administrator — has removed the four most common ways a fund actually dies.

There is a broader cultural point worth stating plainly. The mistake every one of these failures shares is treating operations as a back-office cost center subordinate to the front-office alpha engine. The founders who survive treat the treasury and operations function as a *first-class* part of the business — staffed, resourced, and respected on par with the trading desk — because they have internalized that the trading desk's job is to make money and the treasury desk's job is to make sure the fund is still alive to keep making it. The discipline of not blowing up, which the careers literature frames as [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up), is at the fund level overwhelmingly a *treasury* discipline, not a trading one.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you stop being a PM whose cash someone else manages and become a founder who *is* the treasury desk. It matters most acutely in the weeks you would least like to think about it: a market drawdown, a redemption wave, a prime-broker headline. The brutal asymmetry of treasury risk is that it is invisible and free in every calm period — cheap financing, simple single-prime setups, minimal cash drag all *reward* you for taking it — and then presents the entire bill in one bad week. The founder's job is to pay for the protection while it is still cheap and abstract: negotiate rehypothecation caps before you need them, add the second and third prime before the first one is in the headlines, move unencumbered assets into custody before a prime fails, and size the buffer to the stress week before the stress week arrives.

Internalize the core fact and let it govern every treasury decision: **a fund is solvent only as long as its treasury desk can find the cash to meet the next call.** Build the plumbing — multiple primes, independent custody, conservative rehypothecation terms, a deliberate liquidity buffer, an independent administrator, disciplined daily reconciliation — as carefully as you build the portfolio, and you remove the ways funds actually die. Build a brilliant book on top of fragile plumbing, and you become the next Maya: right about the market, and out of business anyway.
