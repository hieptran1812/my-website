> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Operational due diligence is a separate process from investment due diligence, run by a different team, and it holds a veto: a fund with spectacular returns can be, and routinely is, rejected on operations alone.
>
> - ODD checks the things that have nothing to do with whether the strategy makes money: who can move cash, who strikes the NAV, whether the administrator and auditor are real and independent, how the book is valued, how the firm survives losing its founder or its building.
> - The single biggest checkbox is an **independent administrator** — an outside firm that prices the book — because the alternative is a manager marking his own homework. The second is **segregation of duties**: the person who instructs a trade, the person who moves the cash, and the person who strikes the NAV must be three different parties.
> - ODD is not a formality and small funds are not exempt. Allocators have walked away from funds returning 20% a year over a single cash-control gap, because the downside of an ops failure is total loss, not a bad quarter.
> - The one fact to remember: Bernard Madoff had no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, and self-custody. The missing operational stack — not the fake returns themselves — is what an honest ODD review would have caught, and is exactly what ODD now exists to catch.

Maya took the call standing up, which is how she knew it had not gone well before the analyst said a word. The Meridian Fund was up 19% net in its first fourteen months. A USD 50 million ticket from a respected fund-of-funds had been, in the polite language of allocators, "soft-circled" — verbally promised, pending the last box. The investment team had spent three days with her. They had loved the book, stress-tested her risk, called two of her old PMs, and come away convinced she had a real edge. The deal was, everyone agreed, done.

Then a second team she had barely met — the operational-due-diligence group, a separate unit reporting to a different head — asked to walk through how the fund actually ran. Maya, proud of how lean she had kept it, explained that she pulled positions from the prime broker, marked them herself, and that she personally held the authority to move the fund's cash. One signature. Hers. "It is faster that way," she said. "I am the founder; I am not going to defraud my own fund." The ODD analyst, a calm man named Daniel, thanked her, asked two follow-up questions, and ended the call. The next morning the allocation was gone. Not paused. Gone. The memo, which a friendly contact later read to her, was three sentences long and ended: *founder can move fund cash unilaterally; no independent NAV; recommend decline.* The 19% net was not mentioned. It was, to the ODD team, simply not relevant.

Maya had run into the single most underestimated gatekeeper in the business of funds. She had spent a decade learning to make money and zero days learning the thing Daniel was actually checking: that a credible fund is built so that no one — including the founder — can quietly cheat, lose, or vanish without an outsider catching it. That check has a name, a separate team, and a power that no amount of performance can override. It is **operational due diligence**, and it holds a veto.

This post is about that veto: what ODD is, why it sits apart from the investment decision, exactly what it checks, what makes an analyst pick up the phone and kill a deal, how funds fail it, how to prepare for it, and the one case — Madoff — that explains why the whole apparatus exists. Figure 1 is the map: the two diligence tracks running in parallel, and the operational gate that the great-returns track cannot open by itself. We will spend the rest of the post earning the right to read it.

## Foundations: what ODD is

Before any nuance, you need a clean mental model and about nine definitions. The model is one sentence: **an allocator decides whether to invest by running two separate investigations at once — one into whether the fund can make money, and one into whether the fund is safe to hold money in — and the second one can stop the whole thing.** Everything in this post is that idea applied to specific operational areas. Let us define the players and terms from zero, because if you trade for a living the *markets* side of this is obvious to you and the *business* side is usually a blank page.

**Operational due diligence (ODD).** The allocator's process of verifying that a fund's operations are sound: that the books are kept and the NAV is struck independently, that cash cannot be moved by one person, that the auditor and administrator are real and reputable, that the book is valued by a defensible policy, that the regulatory machinery works, and that the fund survives the loss of its founder or its building. ODD is run by a dedicated team and produces a memo that the investment committee reads alongside the performance analysis. Critically, that memo can say *decline* on its own.

**Investment due diligence (IDD).** The other track — the one most people picture when they hear "due diligence." IDD asks: is the edge real, is the track record honest, is the risk what the manager claims, is the strategy capacity-constrained, can the returns persist? It studies the alpha and the portfolio. We cover the substance of that analysis — building and evaluating a signal, backtesting, overfitting — in the quant-research series; here the only thing you need to hold is that IDD and ODD are *different investigations done by different people asking different questions.*

**Segregation of duties.** The principle that the person who can *instruct* a transaction, the person who can *move the cash* to settle it, and the person who can *strike the NAV* that values it must be three different parties. The point is that no single individual can both transact and verify his own work. When one person holds two or three of those powers, that person can — whether through fraud or just an undetected error — make the fund's reported value diverge from reality, and no one inside the firm is positioned to catch it.

**Independent administrator (the "admin").** The outside firm that does the fund's books and, above all, **strikes the net asset value (NAV)** — the official per-share value of the fund — from third-party data, not from the manager's say-so. The admin pulls positions from the prime broker, prices them against market-data vendors, accrues the fees, keeps the investor register, and sends investors their statements. *Independent* is the load-bearing word: the value investors are asked to trust is computed by someone who does not work for the manager and has no incentive to inflate it. We go deep on the mechanics in [NAV, accounting, and the administrator](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator); here it is the single biggest ODD checkbox.

**Net asset value (NAV).** The value of the fund per unit of ownership. If total assets minus liabilities is USD 52 million across 50,000 shares, NAV per share is USD 1,040. Every fee, subscription, and redemption is computed against the NAV. The single most important governance question about any fund is: *who computes this number, and can an outsider check their work?* ODD exists largely to answer it.

**Independent pricing.** A subset of the admin's job worth naming separately because it is where the worst fraud and the worst honest errors live. Liquid positions — listed stocks, futures — price themselves at the close. Illiquid or hard-to-value positions — distressed debt, private deals, exotic derivatives, thinly-traded names — do not, and someone has to decide what they are worth. Independent pricing means that "someone" is not the manager alone; it is a vendor, a pricing service, or a valuation committee with outside input. A manager who marks his own illiquid book can paint the NAV he wants.

**Cash controls.** The rules and machinery that govern how the fund's money moves. Sound cash controls mean a payment requires more than one person to release, the destination is on a pre-approved whitelist, the bank confirms by callback, and the administrator reconciles every movement. The failure mode ODD fears most is simple: one person — usually the founder — can wire fund cash out alone. That single fact ended Maya's USD 50 million.

**Valuation policy.** A written document, agreed in advance, that says exactly how every kind of position in the fund will be valued, by whom, how often, and what happens when a price is disputed. A fund with a real valuation policy has decided the rules before it has any incentive to bend them. A fund without one is deciding each month, with money on the line, how to mark a book — which is how "temporary" mismarks become permanent holes.

**Key-person and business continuity.** Two related survival questions. *Key-person risk* asks what happens to the fund if the founder is hit by a bus, quits, or is arrested — does the whole operation live in one person's head, or is there documented succession and a team that can run things? *Business continuity / disaster recovery (BCP/DR)*, plus **cyber**, asks whether the fund survives losing its office, its systems, or its data to an outage, a fire, or a hack — is there a tested backup site, are there cyber controls, can trades still settle on a bad day? ODD checks both, because a fund that cannot survive a Tuesday with no power is not a fund an institution can responsibly hold.

Here is the through-line that connects all nine. A hedge fund sells a promise: *trust me with your capital and I will compound it inside a structure that protects you.* IDD tests the first half — the compounding. ODD tests the second half — the protection. And because the second half is where a fund goes to *zero* rather than merely to a bad year, ODD's "no" weighs more than IDD's "yes." A strategy that disappoints loses you a few percent. An operational failure — a fraud, a mismark, a stolen wire, a counterparty blowup, a founder who vanishes — can lose you everything. That asymmetry is why ODD holds a veto, and it is the whole subject of this post.

## Why ODD is separate and can veto

The natural question for a founder is: why is this a *separate* team at all? Why not let the people who studied the returns also check the operations? The answer reveals everything about how allocators actually think, and it is worth getting exactly right, because it tells you why charm and a great track record will not save you here.

**The two questions are answered by different evidence, by different skills, and against different downside.** IDD is a markets exercise: it takes a portfolio manager's eye, a quant's skepticism about backtests, an understanding of edge and capacity. ODD is an audit-and-controls exercise: it takes someone who reads service-provider contracts, who knows what a clean audit opinion looks like, who can spot a related-party administrator, who asks who has signing authority on the bank account. These are genuinely different professions. The person who can tell you whether a momentum signal is overfit is usually not the person who can tell you whether the wire-transfer process has a control gap, and vice versa.

**Separation removes the conflict.** This is the deeper reason. An investment analyst who has spent three days falling in love with a manager's edge has a powerful psychological incentive to wave the operational stuff through — they *want* the deal, they have invested ego in it, the returns are seductive. If the same person who fell for the returns also signs off on the operations, the operational check is compromised by the very enthusiasm that makes it necessary. So serious allocators deliberately wall the ODD team off: a different reporting line, a different head, often a different floor. The ODD analyst is rewarded for catching problems, not for closing deals. Daniel, in Maya's story, had no stake in her returns. His job was to find the reason to say no, and he found it.

**The veto follows from the asymmetry.** Picture the two answers in a grid. If IDD says yes and ODD says yes, you allocate. If IDD says no, you decline regardless of operations — bad returns are bad returns. The interesting cell is IDD-yes, ODD-no: a fund with real, attractive returns and an operational defect. Here the allocator declines, every time, because the operational defect is not a discount to apply to the expected return — it is a tail risk of total loss that no return premium compensates for. There is no Sharpe ratio high enough to make "the founder can steal the money" acceptable. So ODD's no is absolute. It is not weighed against the returns; it ends the conversation.

Figure 1 drew this as two tracks and a gate. Internalize the shape: the investment track can be a triumph, and the deal still dies at the operational gate, because the gate is not asking "are the returns good enough to risk this?" — it is asking "is this safe to own at all?"

There is a second-order reason institutions are so strict, and it is fiduciary. A pension or endowment is investing other people's money — pensioners, students, beneficiaries. When a fund blows up on a strategy bet, the allocator can defend it: *we backed a real edge and it didn't work, that is the nature of the asset class.* When a fund blows up on an operational failure the allocator *could have caught in diligence* — a fraud, a missing administrator, a control gap — that is indefensible. The allocator's own board, its own regulators, its own beneficiaries will ask: *you had an ODD process; why didn't it catch this?* The career risk and the fiduciary risk of an operational miss are far worse than of an investment miss. That is why the ODD analyst is, quietly, one of the most powerful people in the allocation process, and why "but the returns are exceptional" is precisely the sentence that does not move them.

This separation also changes the *order* in which the work happens at the most disciplined institutions. Rather than run a full investment diligence and only then check operations, the largest allocators run a preliminary operational screen *first* — a short questionnaire and a few confirming calls — before they commit their investment team's time. The reason is purely economic: investment diligence is expensive in senior-analyst hours, and there is no point spending forty hours validating a momentum signal if a five-minute call to the administrator is going to reveal that there *is* no administrator. So a founder with an operational gap often never even reaches the investment conversation; the screen kills the relationship before the returns are ever discussed. The lesson for Maya, and for any founder, is that operations are not the *last* hurdle you clear after the returns have won the day. At a well-run allocator they are frequently the *first*, and a clean operational story is what buys you the right to have your returns examined at all.

#### Worked example: the cash-control gap that costs a USD 50 million allocation

Walk through the exact mechanism that ended Maya's deal, in numbers. The Meridian Fund has USD 80 million in AUM. The soft-circled ticket is USD 50 million, which would take the fund to USD 130 million — past the institutional-viability threshold and into the size where larger allocators will look. The management company earns roughly 1.5% on AUM, so that USD 50 million is worth about \$750,000 a year of management-fee revenue alone, before any performance fee.

Now the control gap. Maya is the sole authorized signer on the fund's bank account. To move money out, she logs in, enters a destination, and releases the wire. No second person approves it. No callback confirms it. The administrator learns about it after the fact, if at all. The ODD analyst does a simple thought experiment: *if Maya wanted to wire \$10 million of fund cash to a personal account tomorrow, what would stop her?* The answer is nothing — no control, only her good character. And ODD does not allocate on character; it allocates on controls, because the entire reason controls exist is that you cannot diligence a person's future honesty.

So the analyst prices the gap. The probability that Maya is a fraud is low — but the *cost* if she is, or if her credentials are phished and a criminal moves the cash, is the entire allocation: \$50 million to zero, plus the headline risk of having put pensioners' money into a fund anyone could drain. Expected loss is small probability times catastrophic magnitude, and against a strategy whose entire annual *expected return* over a risk-free benchmark might be a few percent, the math is hopeless. The analyst declines. The fix would cost Maya almost nothing — a second authorized signer and a bank callback, a few hours of setup — but she did not have it on the day of the call, and ODD does not allocate on promises to fix things later.

*A single missing control turned a \$750,000-a-year relationship into a three-sentence rejection memo, because ODD prices the worst case, not the likely one.*

## What ODD checks

ODD is not a vibe; it is a checklist, and the checklist is remarkably stable across allocators. Figure 2 lays it out as a grid of areas against what each one verifies. The unifying question behind every row is the same: *who controls this, and can an outsider independently check the controller?* Let us walk the checklist, because knowing it is half of passing it.

![A matrix showing operational due diligence areas as rows against what each area verifies and who must be independent](/imgs/blogs/operational-due-diligence-the-veto-2.png)

**Segregation of duties.** Can any single person both transact and verify? ODD maps who can instruct a trade, who can move cash, who can strike or influence the NAV, and who reconciles. If those powers concentrate in one or two people — common at small funds where the founder does everything — that is a finding. A three-person shop can still segregate if it is deliberate about it; we work through exactly how below.

**Independent administration.** Who strikes the NAV? The answer must be a named third-party administrator, not the manager. ODD will call the administrator directly, confirm the engagement, and ask what data the admin uses and whether the manager can override the admin's prices. A self-administered fund — manager strikes his own NAV — is for most institutional allocators an automatic decline. This is the biggest single checkbox.

**Independent pricing.** For everything that does not price itself at the close, how is the mark set, and by whom? ODD looks for a vendor pricing source, a pricing hierarchy (which source is used first, which is the fallback), and a valuation committee for the hard cases. A manager who self-marks an illiquid book without an independent check is a finding even if the marks happen to be honest, because the *control* is missing.

**Cash-movement controls.** How does money leave the fund? ODD wants dual authorization (two people to release a payment), pre-set payee whitelists (you cannot wire to a brand-new account without a separate setup step), bank callbacks, and administrator reconciliation. The nightmare they are screening for is the sole signer — the Maya gap.

**Valuation policy.** Is there a written valuation policy, agreed in advance, covering every instrument type? ODD reads it. A fund that can hand over a real document — "level-1 assets at vendor close, level-2 at a model with these inputs, level-3 by the valuation committee quarterly with this dispute process" — is miles ahead of a fund that improvises each month.

**Counterparty and prime-brokerage setup.** Who holds the assets, and what happens if they fail? After Lehman in September 2008 showed that assets pledged to a failing prime broker can be frozen — the rehypothecation risk — serious funds run **multiple** primes so no single counterparty failure freezes the whole book. ODD checks the prime relationships, the custody arrangements, the cash sweeps, and the concentration: is everything sitting at one counterparty, and is that counterparty creditworthy? Hidden or undisclosed leverage spread across multiple prime brokers is a specific thing they hunt for — it is exactly what Archegos used to hide a position that ultimately cost its prime brokers over USD 10 billion. The subtlety ODD probes here is that multi-prime, which solves the concentration problem, can *create* a transparency problem: a fund that splits a position across several primes may be the only party that sees the whole position, while each individual prime sees only its slice. ODD therefore asks not just "do you use more than one prime?" but "who, internally, aggregates the total exposure, and could any single counterparty be surprised by the size of what you are running against the others?" A fund that cannot answer that crisply is carrying exactly the blind spot Archegos exploited, and it is a finding regardless of whether the leverage is currently large.

**Compliance.** Is there a real compliance program? ODD checks the regulatory registrations and filings (Form ADV, Form PF where applicable, CFTC/NFA registration if the fund trades derivatives), a written compliance manual, a code of ethics with personal-trading surveillance, and either a genuine chief compliance officer or an outsourced one from a recognized firm. We go deep on the regulatory machinery in [registration and regulation: SEC, CFTC, Reg D](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/registration-and-regulation-sec-cftc-reg-d); for ODD, the question is whether the compliance function actually exists and runs.

**Key-person and succession.** What happens if the founder is gone tomorrow? ODD wants documented succession, key-person provisions in the fund documents (which may let investors redeem if the named key person departs), and evidence that more than one person understands how the firm runs. A fund that lives entirely in the founder's head is a key-person risk no matter how good the founder is.

**Business continuity, disaster recovery, and cyber.** Can the fund survive an outage, a disaster, or a hack? ODD wants a written, *tested* BCP — not a binder no one has opened, but a plan with evidence of a real test — a backup site or remote-work capability, data backups, and cyber controls (multi-factor authentication, email security, an incident-response plan). Cyber has risen sharply on the checklist because a phished founder is exactly the path by which a sole-signer cash gap becomes a stolen wire.

That is the checklist. Notice what is *not* on it: the Sharpe ratio, the alpha, the strategy thesis. Those live entirely on the IDD side. ODD does not care how the money is made; it cares whether the money is safe.

#### Worked example: segregation of duties in a three-person shop

Founders push back here: "I have three people. How am I supposed to segregate duties like a billion-dollar fund?" The answer is that segregation is about *deliberate role design plus outside firms*, not headcount. Walk through the Meridian Fund as a three-person shop and see how it can pass.

The three people are Maya (founder/PM), a chief operating officer named Reuben, and an analyst named Sofia. Naively, Maya does everything. Properly segregated, the powers split like this. Maya **instructs trades** — she places orders with the executing broker — but she has *no* authority to move the fund's cash and does *not* strike the NAV. Reuben **operates**: he confirms trades, reconciles to the prime broker, and *initiates* a wire when one is needed — but he cannot *release* it alone. The release requires a *second* approver: either Maya or an outside director co-signs, and the bank calls back a known contact to confirm. The **NAV is struck by the administrator**, an outside firm, from prime-broker positions and vendor prices; nobody in the three-person shop strikes it, and nobody can override it.

Now run the test ODD runs. Can any one person both transact and verify? Maya can trade but cannot pay or mark. Reuben can initiate a payment but cannot release it alone or mark the book. Sofia researches and has no cash or pricing authority at all. The NAV comes from outside. The answer is no — no single person can move money out or fabricate the value — and the fund passes the segregation check with three people, because the segregation came from design and from hiring an independent administrator, not from having a big team.

Compare the cost. The independent administrator runs perhaps USD 30,000 to USD 100,000-plus a year. Setting up dual authorization and a payee whitelist at the bank costs essentially nothing but an afternoon. Appointing an independent director costs a modest annual fee. For a fund managing USD 80 million, the entire segregation apparatus is a small fraction of the management-fee revenue — and it is the difference between passing and failing ODD.

*Segregation is bought with role design and a few outside firms, not with headcount, which is why even a three-person fund has no excuse to fail it.*

## The biggest red flags

Some findings are weightings in a memo. A few are deal-enders on their own — the things that make an ODD analyst stop the diligence and pick up the phone. Figure 3 isolates the most fundamental one, segregation of the three core powers, and Figure 6 catalogs the red flags as a group. Let us name the ones that kill, because if your fund has any of them, no amount of preparation elsewhere will save you.

![A branching graph showing a fund transaction splitting into three separate powers — instruct the trade, move the cash, strike the NAV — held by different parties and converging on one verified number](/imgs/blogs/operational-due-diligence-the-veto-3.png)

**Self-administration.** The manager strikes his own NAV, with no outside firm. This is the marking-your-own-homework finding, and for most institutional allocators it is an automatic decline. The investors are being asked to trust a number computed by the person who profits from it being high. Even an honest manager looks identical, from the outside, to a dishonest one here — and ODD cannot tell them apart, so it declines both.

**A sole cash signer.** One person can wire fund money out with no co-approval and no callback. This is Maya's gap. It is fatal because the worst case — fraud or a phished credential — is total loss, and there is no control standing between the fund and that worst case.

**Self-custody.** The fund's assets sit at the manager's own broker-dealer or an entity the manager controls, rather than at an independent, regulated custodian. If the manager both holds the assets and reports their value, there is nothing an outsider can verify. This is the structure Madoff used, and it is on every ODD analyst's mind for that reason.

**A tiny, unknown, or related-party auditor.** A three-person accounting firm signing the audit of a multi-billion-dollar fund is a screaming flag — the firm is either not capable of the work or not independent of the manager. Worse is a *related-party* provider: an administrator or auditor owned by, or financially entangled with, the manager. Independence is the entire value of these firms; a related-party "independent" administrator is a contradiction, and ODD treats it as one.

**Too-smooth returns.** Impossibly steady gains with almost no down months. This is the one red flag that lives on the border between ODD and IDD, and Madoff is exactly why ODD pays attention to it: returns that never fluctuate are not a sign of skill, they are a sign that the returns are not real — that someone is smoothing them, marking conservatively in good months and generously in bad ones, or simply making them up. A strategy that genuinely earns 1% a month with no volatility either does not exist or is being mismarked.

**An opaque strategy.** The manager will not, or cannot, explain how the returns are made. Some opacity is legitimate — managers protect their edge — but a manager who cannot describe the *shape* of the strategy, the instruments, the risks, well enough for an analyst to understand how money is made and lost is hiding something, and ODD does not allocate into a black box.

**Stale or self-marked illiquid positions.** Hard-to-value positions that the manager prices himself, with marks that do not move when the market does. This is where mismarks hide. A position carried at par for three years while comparable assets fell is either a genuinely special asset or a mark the manager is unwilling to take down, and ODD wants an independent price to tell the difference.

Figure 6 lays the catalog out together, so you can see the family resemblance at a glance.

![A grid cataloging the operational red flags that end an allocation, including self-administration, a sole cash signer, self-custody, an unknown auditor, too-smooth returns, and key-person risk](/imgs/blogs/operational-due-diligence-the-veto-6.png)

The common thread, visible across Figures 3 and 6, is *who controls the money and who verifies it.* Every red flag is a place where the manager holds a power that should be split, or where an outsider's independent check is missing. None of them is about whether the strategy is good. That is the point: a great strategy with any of these flags is more dangerous than a mediocre strategy with none, because the great returns are what attract the capital that the operational gap then puts at risk.

## How funds fail ODD

Funds fail ODD in patterns, and the patterns are illustrative — not a precise survey, but a stable ranking that every ODD consultant will recognize. Figure 5 ranks them. Understanding the distribution tells a founder where to spend the preparation budget.

![A horizontal bar chart ranking the operational gaps that most often cause a fund to fail operational due diligence, with no independent administrator and cash-control gaps at the top](/imgs/blogs/operational-due-diligence-the-veto-5.png)

**The largest cluster is independence failures** — no independent administrator, self-custody, related-party providers. These dominate because they are structural: a fund that decided to self-administer to save money has built the failure into its foundations, and it cannot be papered over in a diligence meeting. The fund either has an independent admin or it does not, and ODD finds out with one phone call to the named administrator.

**The second cluster is cash-control gaps** — the sole signer, no callbacks, no whitelist. These are the most *fixable* of the serious failures, which is the tragedy of them: a fund fails on a gap that an afternoon at the bank would have closed. Maya's loss is the canonical example.

**The third cluster is valuation failures** — self-marked illiquid books, no written valuation policy, stale marks. These are most common at funds holding genuinely illiquid assets — credit, private deals, distressed — where there is real discretion in the marks and no vendor price to fall back on. ODD scrutinizes these funds hardest, because this is where an honest-looking NAV can hide a hole.

**Then come weaker clusters** — an unknown auditor, undisclosed leverage, key-person concentration, untested BCP, weak cyber. These are less often the *sole* deal-ender, but they accumulate: a fund with three or four of them looks like a firm that has not taken operations seriously, and the cumulative impression sinks the allocation even when no single finding is fatal.

The deeper lesson is that funds fail ODD on operations, not on returns — which is exactly what Figure 5's title says. A founder who spends every spare hour polishing the track record and none building the operational stack has optimized the wrong thing. The returns get you into the room. The operations decide whether you leave with a check.

#### Worked example: the true cost of failing ODD

Founders underprice the cost of failing ODD because they see only the lost ticket. Account for the whole thing, in Maya's numbers, and it is far worse.

The direct cost is the USD 50 million allocation — about \$750,000 a year of management-fee revenue, plus the performance fee on a now-larger base. Call the all-in first-year revenue impact, with a 19% gross year, on the order of \$2 million of foregone fees. That alone hurts a fund running on tight margins.

The indirect costs are larger. First, the **re-pitch delay**: Maya cannot simply call back next week. She must build the missing controls — an independent administrator engagement takes weeks to onboard; appointing an independent director and re-papering the bank authorities takes more. Then she must let the controls *season* — an allocator wants to see the new process running for a quarter or two, not promised. Realistically she is six to nine months from a credible re-pitch. That is six to nine months of growth she does not get, compounding against her.

Second, the **reputation cost**. The ODD world is small and analysts talk. A *manager self-administers; founder can move cash alone; recommend decline* memo does not stay in one allocator's files; the same finding will surface at the next allocator's diligence, because ODD teams share the questions even when they do not share the memos. Maya is not just rejected by one fund-of-funds; she is now a known operational risk that every subsequent ODD will probe for, and she has to walk in already on the back foot.

Third, the **opportunity cost of the founder's time**. The months Maya spends retrofitting controls under pressure are months she is not researching, not trading, not raising. Building the operational stack *before* the pitch is cheap; retrofitting it *after* a failed pitch, against a deadline, with a black mark already in circulation, is expensive in every currency a founder has.

*Failing ODD costs far more than the lost ticket: it costs the re-pitch delay, a reputation finding that follows you, and the founder-hours burned retrofitting what should have been built on day one.*

## Preparing for ODD

The good news for founders is that ODD is not an exam you cram for; it is a state you build into the fund from the start, and a fund built right passes almost mechanically. The cash-control workflow in Figure 7 is the spine of it — how a wire leaves a fund that passes — and the rest of preparation is making every checklist item answerable with a document and a phone number rather than a promise.

![A pipeline diagram showing the cash-control workflow with dual authorization, a pre-set payee whitelist, a bank callback, release, and administrator reconciliation](/imgs/blogs/operational-due-diligence-the-veto-7.png)

**Hire the independent stack before you pitch.** An independent administrator, a recognized auditor, an independent custodian via your prime broker, fund counsel, and a compliance consultant — the [service-provider stack](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-service-provider-stack-pb-admin-auditor) — is not overhead you bolt on once you are big. It is the thing that makes you institutionally investable at all, and ODD will call every one of these firms to confirm the engagement. Have them in place and contactable before the first ODD meeting.

**Build the cash-control workflow and never deviate from it.** Figure 7 is the standard: a request drafted by operations against an approved invoice, a destination on a pre-set whitelist that a *different* person maintains, a second approver who co-signs, a bank callback to a known contact, release only after both approvals and the callback, and reconciliation by the administrator the next day. The workflow is deliberately slow and multi-handed; that slowness is the feature. Document it as a written policy and follow it for *every* payment, including the small ones — ODD will ask for examples and a single sloppy exception undermines the whole control.

**Write the policies down — before you need them.** A written valuation policy, a compliance manual, a code of ethics, a business-continuity plan, an incident-response plan. ODD does not just ask whether you do these things; it asks to *read the document*, and a real document written in advance reads completely differently from one assembled the night before a meeting. The valuation policy in particular should be agreed *before* you hold any hard-to-value position, so it was written without an incentive to favor a particular mark.

**Test the BCP and keep evidence.** A continuity plan no one has tested is a binder, not a control. Run a real test — a day working from the backup arrangement, a simulated loss of the primary system — and keep the evidence: a memo, a date, what worked and what you fixed. ODD distinguishes sharply between "we have a plan" and "we tested our plan in March and here is what happened."

**Document succession and key-person coverage.** Write down who runs the firm if the founder is gone, make sure more than one person understands the operations, and align the key-person provisions in your fund documents with reality. A founder who can hand ODD a one-page succession memo and name a deputy who actually knows the systems answers the bus question that sinks lone-genius funds.

**Run your own mock ODD.** The single highest-return preparation is to hire an ODD consultant — or a friendly allocator's analyst — to run a *mock* operational review before any real one. They will produce the same memo a real ODD team would, while there is still time and no deal on the line to fix the findings. The cost of a mock review is trivial against the cost of failing a real one, as the previous worked example showed. Maya, had she done this, would have had Daniel's three-sentence memo in hand months before the USD 50 million was ever soft-circled, and the cash-control gap would have been a closed item rather than a deal-ender.

The unifying idea is that preparing for ODD means making the fund's operations *verifiable by an outsider* — every number traceable to a third party, every power split, every policy written, every plan tested. A fund built that way does not prepare for ODD so much as continuously *be* ready for it. The preparation is the operating model itself; we cover building that model in [the operating model: front, middle, and back office](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office).

## Madoff: the ODD failure ODD exists to prevent

Every control in this post traces back, directly or indirectly, to one case. Bernard Madoff ran the largest Ponzi scheme in history, revealed in December 2008: roughly USD 65 billion in fabricated statement value, with around USD 17.5 billion in actual principal lost. For decades he reported smooth, consistent returns to a roster of sophisticated investors — funds-of-funds, wealthy individuals, charities — who never received what they thought they were buying, because there were no real trades behind the statements. He was paying old investors with new investors' money, which works until redemptions exceed inflows, which is what the 2008 crisis finally forced.

Here is the part that matters for this post: **Madoff was not undone by clever financial engineering that no one could have spotted. He was undone by the absence of exactly the operational controls ODD checks.** Score his operation against the modern ODD checklist and it fails on nearly every line. There was no independent administrator — Madoff's own firm produced the statements; he marked his own homework, and the "trades" on those statements never happened. There was no independent custody — the assets, such as they were, sat inside his own broker-dealer, so no outsider could confirm they existed. The auditor was a tiny, unknown three-person firm signing off on what was reported as a multi-billion-dollar operation, which no real audit of that size could plausibly have been. The returns were impossibly smooth — steady positive months with almost no volatility, the single statistical signature that should make any analyst ask how the returns are made and whether they are real. And the strategy was opaque — a "split-strike conversion" he would not let anyone examine closely, which is to say a black box.

#### Worked example: the Madoff red flags scored against a modern ODD checklist

Take the checklist from this post and run Madoff's operation through it, finding by finding, the way an ODD analyst would today.

| ODD check | What a sound fund shows | What Madoff showed | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Independent administrator | Outside firm strikes NAV from third-party data | Self-produced statements; no independent NAV | **Fail** |
| Independent custody | Assets at a regulated, separate custodian | Self-custody inside his own broker-dealer | **Fail** |
| Auditor | Recognized firm capable of the engagement | Tiny three-person firm for a multi-billion book | **Fail** |
| Independent pricing | Vendor prices, verifiable trades | "Trades" that never occurred; no verifiable marks | **Fail** |
| Return profile | Volatility consistent with the strategy | Impossibly smooth, almost no down months | **Fail** |
| Strategy transparency | Manager can explain how money is made | Opaque "split-strike" black box | **Fail** |
| Segregation of duties | Trade, cash, and NAV split across parties | All powers concentrated in Madoff's firm | **Fail** |

Seven core checks; seven fails. Notice the brutal simplicity of it: an ODD analyst running this list did not need to be a forensic accountant who could trace the fraud. They needed only to ask the standard questions — *who strikes the NAV? where are the assets held? who is the auditor? can I verify a trade?* — and the answers would have failed the fund on independence alone, long before anyone had to prove the trades were fake. Some did exactly that. A number of serious ODD-driven investors and consultants *declined* to invest with Madoff specifically because the operational structure could not be verified — the most-cited being the analyst Harry Markopolos, who flagged it for years, but also several funds-of-funds whose ODD teams could not get comfortable with the missing administrator and the self-custody and walked away. They did not have to prove the fraud. They only had to notice that the operations were unverifiable, and decline.

*Madoff failed every core ODD check, which means the fraud was catchable without proving the fraud — the missing controls were the finding, and that is precisely why ODD now insists on them.*

There is one more dimension of the Madoff case that is directly instructive for an allocator, and it is the role of the **feeder funds**. Much of Madoff's capital did not come to him directly; it came through funds-of-funds and feeder vehicles that pooled their clients' money and handed it to him, often charging their own fees on top. Those intermediaries existed precisely to provide diligence and oversight — that was the service their clients paid for — and many of them failed to perform the one check that mattered: confirming, independently, that the assets existed and the trades were real. The collapse therefore indicted not only Madoff but the entire chain of supposed gatekeepers who had outsourced their trust rather than verified it. The lesson the surviving allocators internalized is that you cannot subcontract operational diligence to someone else's reputation. If you are the one writing the check, you — or an ODD team you control — must independently confirm the administrator, the custody, the auditor, and the trades, no matter how many respected names have already invested. A long list of sophisticated co-investors is *not* an operational control; it is a list of people who may, like you, have skipped the check.

That is the lesson the whole industry took from Madoff, and it is why ODD has the veto it has. The investors who lost money had, in many cases, done thorough *investment* diligence — they understood the purported strategy, they liked the returns, they had relationships going back years. What they had not done, or not done independently enough, was *operational* diligence: insist on an independent administrator, confirm custody, vet the auditor, question the impossibly smooth returns. The post-2008 world treats that as the unforgivable miss, and the ODD function exists to make sure it never recurs. When Daniel killed Maya's allocation over a cash-control gap, he was not being bureaucratic. He was being exactly the control that, applied to Madoff, would have saved his investors everything.

## Common misconceptions

**"Great returns pass any due diligence."** This is the costliest myth, and it is exactly backwards. ODD does not look at returns at all. A fund returning 25% a year is rejected on a cash-control gap the same way a fund returning 8% is, because the gap's downside — total loss — is identical regardless of the returns, and there is no return high enough to compensate for "the money can be stolen." Great returns get you into the ODD room. They do nothing for you once you are in it.

**"ODD is a formality once the investment team is sold."** ODD is a separate team with a separate reporting line and a *veto*. The investment team being sold is precisely why the ODD team is walled off from them — the enthusiasm that closes the investment case is the bias the operational case must be protected from. Daniel ended Maya's deal after the investment team had already fallen in love with the fund. The "yes" from one team does not soften the "no" from the other; the no wins.

**"Small funds are exempt — ODD is for the big institutional managers."** Small funds are scrutinized *harder* on operations, not softer, because small funds are exactly where the founder does everything and the controls are thin. A billion-dollar platform has a COO, a CCO, a full back office, and multiple primes; a three-person shop has a founder who, by default, instructs trades, moves cash, and marks the book. The smaller you are, the more an allocator worries about segregation — and the more important it is that you have deliberately built it anyway, as the three-person worked example showed.

**"An independent administrator is a nice-to-have you add when you scale."** An independent administrator is the single biggest ODD checkbox, and for most institutional allocators self-administration is an automatic decline. It is not a scaling milestone; it is a precondition for taking institutional money at all. Hire it before the first ODD meeting, not after you cross USD 250 million.

**"ODD is about catching fraud."** ODD catches fraud, but its everyday job is catching *control gaps and honest errors* — the sole signer who is not a thief but could be phished, the self-marked book that is wrong by accident, the untested BCP that fails on the one day it is needed. Most ODD findings are not fraud; they are places where, *if* something went wrong, no one would catch it. The control is the point, independent of anyone's honesty.

**"If my operations are clean, ODD is just a box-tick."** Clean operations make ODD *passable*, not free. You still have to make every item *verifiable by an outsider* — produce the written policies, confirm the engagements, evidence the BCP test, name the second signer. A fund with genuinely sound operations that cannot *demonstrate* them to an analyst still struggles, because ODD allocates on evidence, not on assurances. Preparing the evidence is itself work, and it is work worth doing before the pitch.

## How it plays out in the real world

In practice, ODD has hardened from a courtesy into a gate over the last two decades, and the trajectory maps almost exactly onto the named failures. Before LTCM in 1998, before the post-Lehman custody scare of 2008, and above all before Madoff was revealed in December 2008, operational diligence at many allocators was light — a conversation, a few questions, a trust-based sign-off riding on the investment team's enthusiasm. Madoff ended that era. The investors who lost roughly USD 17.5 billion in principal had, in many cases, done real investment work and skipped or under-weighted the operational work, and the industry's response was to build ODD into a separate, empowered, veto-wielding function. Figure 4 captures the world that produced — two funds with the same returns, opposite verdicts, decided entirely on whether operations can be independently verified.

![A before-and-after comparison of two funds with identical returns, one failing operational due diligence because the manager controls operations and one passing because outsiders verify every number](/imgs/blogs/operational-due-diligence-the-veto-4.png)

Today, the shape of a real ODD process is consistent. A dedicated team — in-house at large allocators, outsourced to specialist ODD consultants at smaller ones — sends a detailed operational questionnaire, reviews the service-provider contracts and the audited financials, calls the administrator and auditor directly to confirm engagements and ask their own questions, walks the cash-control and valuation processes step by step, often visits the office, and produces a memo with a rating. That memo is read by the investment committee *alongside* the investment analysis, and a "decline" or even a "conditional — fix these items first" rating stops or stalls the allocation regardless of the returns. The largest institutions will not even start IDD until a preliminary operational screen is passed, precisely so they do not waste investment-team hours on a fund that ODD will kill anyway.

The other failures reinforce the same lesson from different angles. Archegos in 2021 — a family office, Bill Hwang — hid leverage across multiple prime brokers using total-return swaps and cost those primes over USD 10 billion when it collapsed; the lesson ODD took was to probe counterparty setup and hunt for undisclosed leverage spread across primes, which is now a standard checklist item. Amaranth in 2006 lost roughly USD 6.6 billion in about a week on concentrated natural-gas bets in a single book; the lesson was concentration and the absence of independent risk oversight. LTCM in 1998 lost about USD 4.6 billion on leverage and correlated convergence trades; the lesson was that even Nobel laureates running real strategies can blow up, so the *structure* must be sound independent of the people. Each of these reads in the ODD memo as a checklist item, and together they explain why the modern ODD analyst is empowered to say no to anyone.

For a founder, the real-world implication is liberating once you accept it: ODD is *predictable*. It is not a subjective taste test you might fail for reasons you cannot anticipate. It is a known checklist, stable across allocators, that rewards a fund built to be verifiable by an outsider. Build the independent stack, segregate the three powers, write the policies, test the BCP, document succession, and run a mock review — and ODD becomes a process you pass rather than a verdict you fear. Maya, after her rejection, did exactly that: independent administrator, dual cash authorization with a bank callback, an independent director, a written valuation policy, a tested BCP, a one-page succession memo. Nine months later she re-pitched, the ODD memo came back clean, and the USD 50 million wired. The returns had not changed at all. The fund had simply, finally, become one an institution could safely hold.

## When this matters / Further reading

Operational due diligence matters the moment you decide to take institutional money — which, for most founders, is the moment the fund becomes a real business rather than a personal account with outside friends. It is the gate between "I can run money" and "an institution will let me run theirs," and it is decided not by your returns but by whether an outsider can verify how you run the money. Build for ODD from day one: it is far cheaper to construct a fund that is verifiable than to retrofit verifiability after a rejection that follows you. The single highest-leverage move is the one Maya made too late — a mock ODD review before you ever pitch, so the three-sentence rejection memo arrives while you can still act on it for free.

The deeper frame is the series spine: a hedge fund sells an aligned promise to compound capital inside a structure that protects the investor. ODD is the test of the *structure* half of that promise. Investment diligence tests whether you can compound; operational diligence tests whether the investor is protected while you do. A founder who treats operations and controls as first-class — as seriously as the alpha — passes the gate that quietly decides most allocations. A founder who treats them as overhead to add later meets Daniel, and learns that 19% net is not, by itself, an answer to "who can move the cash?"
