> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The order in which you hire is as consequential as whom you hire: the first real hire at most funds is the person who runs the *business* so the founder can run the *book*, and investment headcount comes last, not first.
>
> - Hire the COO (and later the CFO) before a second analyst. A brilliant trader buried in reconciliations and SEC letters has thrown away the only thing the fund sells: her edge.
> - The hiring sequence follows AUM: a COO and a part-time compliance officer at launch, full-time operations and compliance around \$250M, investment seats once the operation is solid, a CFO and dedicated investor relations near \$1B.
> - Compensation is base + bonus + equity or profit share, and the structure must align people without bankrupting the J-curve. Salary plus bonus burns cash; eat-what-you-kill pays only on real production; partnership trades cash today for loyalty over a decade.
> - Every key hire creates key-person risk. The same person who lets you sleep at night is the one whose departure can trigger a key-person clause and a wave of redemptions, so vest the equity and document the bench.

A few months after launch, Maya — a long/short equity portfolio manager who left a respected multi-manager platform to run the Meridian Fund — found herself at 11 p.m. on a Thursday matching a prime-broker position file against her own spreadsheet, line by line, because the numbers were off by a few thousand dollars and she could not strike a clean net asset value until they agreed. She had spent her morning on the phone with a junior associate at her law firm about a Form ADV amendment, her afternoon walking a prospective allocator's operational-diligence analyst through her cash-control "process" (there wasn't really one; it was Maya), and now her evening reconciling. She had not looked at a single chart, read a single earnings transcript, or formed a single new investment idea in three days.

Here is the part that made it tragic rather than merely busy: the month before, with \$45M of day-one capital and exactly one open seat she could afford, Maya had hired a *second analyst*. A sharp one. Someone to help her cover more names, build more models, find more alpha. It felt like the obvious move — she was an investor; she hired an investor. But the second analyst could not strike the NAV, could not answer the SEC, could not run the audit, could not keep the lights on. So the analyst built models while Maya drowned in operations, and the one resource the fund could not replace — Maya's attention on the market — leaked away an hour at a time.

The mistake was not the analyst. The analyst was a fine hire eventually. The mistake was the *sequence*. Maya hired a second person who picks stocks before she hired the first person who runs the business. Figure 1 shows the sequence she should have followed: the first real hire at a launching fund is almost always a chief operating officer — the person whose entire job is to let the founder trade while the business actually runs — and investment headcount comes last, not first.

![A timeline showing the hiring sequence across rising assets under management, from a founder plus chief operating officer at launch, to outsourced operations and a part-time compliance officer around fifty million dollars, to full-time compliance and an operations analyst around two hundred fifty million dollars, then the first research analyst, then a chief financial officer and investor relations near one billion dollars.](/imgs/blogs/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when-1.png)

This post is about getting that sequence right, and about paying the people you hire in a way that aligns them with the fund's survival without burning through the thin cash of the early years. We will build every concept from zero — what a COO actually does, what a CCO is for, how the three big compensation models differ, what equity in a management company really means, why every key hire is also a risk — and we will ground all of it in numbers, because the entire decision is a numbers decision: payroll is the single largest line item in a small fund's budget, and in the J-curve years it can eat the management fee whole.

## Foundations: the roles a fund needs

Before we can talk about *when* to hire, we have to be precise about *what* each role does. A hedge fund is, mechanically, a small business that happens to manage a large pool of capital, and like any business it has functions that must be performed by *someone* — the only question is whether that someone is a dedicated hire, an outsourced vendor, or the exhausted founder at 11 p.m. Let us define each role from the ground up.

The **management company** is the operating business the founder owns — the entity that employs people, signs the office lease, and earns the fees. The **fund** is the separate legal vehicle that holds investor capital. The management company is the *advisor* to the fund; the people we are hiring are employees of the management company, not the fund. (If that distinction is new, the post on [the fund structure: management company versus fund](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund) lays it out.) Everyone we discuss below — the COO, the CFO, the analysts — works for and is paid by the management company, out of the fees the management company earns.

**AUM** is assets under management — the size of the capital pool. It drives almost every hiring decision because it drives revenue: the **management fee** (historically the "2" in "2 and 20," now compressed to roughly 1.3% to 1.5% per year industry-wide as of 2026) is charged as a percentage of AUM and pays the bills regardless of performance, while the **incentive fee** (the "20," now closer to 15% to 17%) is a share of profits and pays nothing in a down year. A small fund lives or dies on the management fee, because the incentive fee is an option that expires worthless whenever the fund is below its prior peak. (The post on [how a hedge fund makes money](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-a-hedge-fund-makes-money) walks the full fee math.)

**NAV** is net asset value — the official, struck value of the fund per share, the number every investor's stake is measured against and the number from which fees are calculated. Striking the NAV correctly and independently is a core operational job, and it is the first thing an allocator's diligence team will probe.

Now the roles themselves:

The **COO** — chief operating officer — is the person who runs the business of the fund so the founder can run the money. In a small fund the COO owns operations (trade settlement, reconciliation, working with the administrator), vendor management (the prime broker, the fund administrator, the auditor, the law firm, the technology stack), the budget and cash management of the management company, human resources, the office, insurance, and very often investor relations and a large slice of compliance until those grow into their own seats. The COO is, in the early years, "everything that is not picking trades." This is why the COO is usually the first real hire: every hour the COO takes off the founder's plate is an hour the founder can spend on the only thing that makes the fund worth investing in.

The **CFO** — chief financial officer — owns the financial machinery: the management company's own books and budget, the fund's financial statements and the annual audit, tax (the fund's, the management company's, and the partners' K-1s), expense allocation between the management company and the fund, and the deep mechanics of fee calculation, crystallization, and the high-water mark. At a small fund the COO and CFO are frequently the *same person* (a "COO/CFO"); the roles separate as the fund scales and the financial complexity — multiple share classes, offshore feeders, side letters, GP-stake investors — outgrows one person. We will treat them together as "the business hire" and split them where it matters.

The **CCO** — chief compliance officer — is the person legally responsible for the fund's compliance program. Under the Investment Advisers Act, a registered investment adviser *must* designate a CCO; it is not optional. The CCO owns the compliance manual, the code of ethics, personal-trading pre-clearance, the Form ADV and (above the threshold) Form PF filings, marketing-rule review of every pitch deck and tear sheet, books-and-records retention, and the annual compliance review. At launch this is very often a *part-time* role — an outsourced compliance consultant or a fractional CCO who serves several small funds — because a tiny fund cannot justify a full-time senior compliance salary, but the *function* is mandatory from day one. (The dedicated post on [the compliance program and the CCO](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco) goes deep on what that program contains.)

**Operations** — the back and middle office staff — are the people who actually do the daily grind once the fund is too big for the COO to do it alone: confirm and settle trades, reconcile the internal books against the prime broker and the administrator every single day, feed positions to the administrator who strikes the official NAV, manage corporate actions, and clear "breaks" (mismatches) before they become problems. (The full anatomy is in [the operating model: front, middle, and back office](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-operating-model-front-middle-back-office).) For a launching fund most of this is *outsourced* to the fund administrator and the prime broker; the first internal operations hire — an "ops analyst" — typically arrives somewhere around \$250M, when the volume and the allocator expectations both demand a dedicated internal owner.

**Investment talent** — analysts, additional portfolio managers, traders, quantitative researchers — is the headcount that directly produces returns: more eyes on more names, more models, more execution capacity. This is the headcount founders instinctively want to hire first and should usually hire *last*, because it is the only headcount that does not keep the fund *alive* — it only makes the fund *better*, and a fund that is better but operationally fragile gets vetoed by every serious allocator.

Then there are the **compensation structures**, which we will define properly below but introduce here so the vocabulary is in place. **Salary plus bonus** is the conventional package: a fixed base paid every month plus a discretionary or formulaic annual bonus. **Eat-what-you-kill** ties a person's pay directly to the profit-and-loss they personally generate — common for traders and portfolio managers who run their own books. **Profit share** (sometimes called a "carry" or a "bonus pool" funded from the incentive fee) gives a person a slice of the fund's performance fee, so they earn only when investors earn. **Equity or partnership** gives a person an *ownership* stake in the management company itself — a share of the *business*, not just of one year's profit.

**Vesting** is the schedule on which granted equity or deferred compensation actually becomes the recipient's to keep: a four-year vest with a one-year "cliff," for example, means nothing vests until the first anniversary, then the rest vests gradually, so a person who leaves early forfeits the unvested portion. Vesting is the mechanism that turns a grant into a retention tool — the "golden handcuff."

Finally, **key-person risk** is the risk that the fund depends so heavily on one or two individuals that their departure, disability, or death would impair it. It is so central that LPs negotiate explicit **key-person clauses** into the fund documents — provisions that, if a named key person stops being actively involved, give investors special redemption rights or even trigger an orderly wind-down. Every key hire reduces one risk (the founder doing everything badly) while creating another (a new person without whom the machine stops). Managing that tension is half of what good team-building is.

With the vocabulary in place, we can answer the three questions in order: who comes first, when each role enters, and how to pay them.

## Why the COO/CFO comes first

The single most common, most expensive, most *intuitive* hiring mistake a founder makes is the one Maya made: hire a second investor before hiring the first operator. The logic feels airtight — "I'm an investor, my edge is investing, so I should hire more investing capacity." It is exactly backwards, and the reason it is backwards is a statement about what is *scarce* and what is *replaceable* at a launching fund.

What is scarce is the founder's attention on the market. That is the entire product. An allocator is not buying the Meridian Fund's reconciliation process; they are buying Maya's ability to find and size long/short equity positions better than the alternatives. Every hour Maya spends matching position files, drafting compliance attestations, or chasing the administrator is an hour subtracted from the one input the fund cannot manufacture anywhere else. A second analyst *adds* a second person's investing capacity, which is genuinely useful — but it does nothing to protect the *founder's* capacity, which is the asset the LPs actually paid for.

What is replaceable — by a hire, by a vendor, or by a process — is everything else. The reconciliation can be done by a COO and an administrator. The SEC correspondence can be handled by a CCO and outside counsel. The audit can be run by a CFO and an audit firm. None of these jobs require Maya's specific genius; they require competence, diligence, and ownership. So the first hire should be the person who *absorbs the replaceable work*, freeing the irreplaceable person to do the irreplaceable thing.

There is a second reason the operator comes first, and it is about *credibility with allocators*, which is the lifeblood of raising capital. Serious institutional money does not just evaluate returns; it runs **operational due diligence** — a separate, veto-wielding review of segregation of duties, independent valuation, cash controls, and the strength of the non-investment team. (The post on [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto) details what that review tests.) An ODD analyst who walks in and discovers that the portfolio manager is also the person who moves cash, marks the book, *and* reconciles it will fail the fund on segregation-of-duties grounds no matter how good the returns are. A credible, named, experienced COO is frequently a *precondition* for the kind of capital that gets a fund past break-even. The operator hire is therefore not just a productivity decision; it is a fundraising decision.

Figure 5 makes the contrast concrete: the solo founder owning five jobs, doing each badly, versus the built-out team where each function has an owner and the founder gets her attention back.

![A before-and-after comparison. On the left, a solo founder owns four jobs at once: trading the book, reconciling cash and positions, answering SEC and limited-partner letters, and running the audit and books, all shaded as overload. On the right, a built-out team gives each function a dedicated owner: the founder trades with full attention, a chief operating officer runs operations and vendors, a chief compliance officer owns compliance, and a chief financial officer owns finance and tax.](/imgs/blogs/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when-5.png)

#### Worked example: a COO's comp at launch versus the cost of not having one

Maya can hire an experienced COO for a total package of roughly \$250k — say a \$220k base plus a modest first-year bonus and a sliver of profit share. On a fund with \$45M of AUM earning a 1.5% management fee, that is \$675,000 of annual management-fee revenue, and the COO's \$250k consumes about 37% of it. That is a frightening fraction, and it is exactly why founders flinch and hire an analyst instead.

Now price the alternative. Without a COO, Maya personally absorbs roughly 25 to 30 hours a week of operational, compliance, and vendor work — call it 28 hours. Her edge, conservatively, is what justifies the fund's existence: assume her investment work, properly resourced, supports the kind of returns that earn the incentive fee on \$45M. If those 28 hours a week degrade her returns by even 1.5 percentage points a year — a modest assumption when you are exhausted and three days behind on the market — that is a 1.5% drag on \$45M, or about \$675,000 of foregone gross return for investors. On the 20% incentive fee, that is roughly \$135,000 of foregone performance fee to the management company *in a single year*, before you count the slower compounding, the missed allocator meetings, and the ODD failures that cost the fund its next \$50M of capital entirely.

So the COO does not "cost" \$250k. The COO costs \$250k *minus* the six-figure performance fee Maya recovers by getting her attention back, *minus* the new capital the COO's credibility unlocks, *minus* the catastrophic-error insurance of having someone whose full-time job is to notice the cash that did not arrive. Priced correctly, the COO is the cheapest hire on the org chart.

*The first hire is not the person who makes the fund better at investing; it is the person who lets the founder keep being the reason anyone invested.*

## The hiring sequence by AUM

The right sequence is not a list of titles; it is a function of AUM, because AUM determines both how much revenue you have to pay people and how much operational and regulatory weight the fund is carrying. Figure 2 lays the roles against the milestones — when each enters, roughly what it costs, and the survival question it answers.

![A matrix with five role rows — chief operating officer, chief compliance officer, operations, analyst, and chief financial officer — against three columns: when to hire, rough cost, and why this role. The chief operating officer enters at launch at two hundred to three hundred fifty thousand dollars to free the founder to trade. The compliance officer is part-time at launch at fifty to two hundred thousand dollars to keep the SEC and limited partners calm. Operations enters around two hundred fifty million dollars. The analyst enters after operations is solid. The chief financial officer enters around one billion dollars.](/imgs/blogs/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when-2.png)

**At launch (often \$25M to \$75M of AUM).** The team is the founder plus a COO (frequently a combined COO/CFO), plus a *part-time* or outsourced CCO, plus a stack of vendors doing the heavy lifting: the fund administrator strikes the NAV and keeps the official books, the prime broker provides custody and financing, the auditor and the law firm are on retainer. The whole point of this configuration is to buy institutional-grade *function* without institutional-grade *headcount cost*. The founder trades; the COO runs everything else and manages the vendors; compliance is real but fractional.

**Around \$50M to \$100M.** This is the survival zone — roughly the break-even band where the management fee starts to cover an institutional-grade fixed-cost base. (The post on [surviving the J-curve: break-even AUM](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/surviving-the-j-curve-break-even-aum) does the full math; the short version is that covering roughly \$1.2M of fixed costs from a 1.5% fee requires about \$80M of AUM.) The team does not balloon here; it hardens. The part-time CCO may move toward full-time, the COO/CFO may bring on a single junior to handle the growing operational load, and the founder finally has enough cash flow to *consider* the first investment hire without bankrupting the budget.

**Around \$250M.** Now allocators expect a real internal operation. The first dedicated **operations** analyst comes in to own the daily reconciliation and trade support that the COO has been doing personally. The CCO is typically full-time by now, because the regulatory weight — Form PF if the fund's private-fund AUM crosses \$150M, marketing-rule review of an expanding set of materials, a heavier examination posture — justifies it. And *this* is finally where investment headcount belongs: the **first analyst** to widen the idea funnel, and not long after, perhaps a **second portfolio manager** if the strategy has the capacity. The order within this milestone matters too — operations and compliance are hardened *before* the second and third investment seats, because a bigger book on a fragile operation is a bigger accident waiting to happen.

**Around \$1B.** The fund is now an institution. A dedicated **CFO** splits off from the COO to own the financial complexity (multiple feeders, side letters, GP-stake investors, a real tax function). Dedicated **investor relations** takes the allocator-facing load off the founder and the COO. The research team grows into multiple analysts and traders, and the operational team grows in proportion. The hires are no longer about survival; they are about scaling the machine without breaking the controls.

Figure 3 plots the headcount path against AUM, and it makes a point that surprises founders: for years, the *control* headcount (operations plus compliance) runs ahead of the *investment* headcount. The investment seats only outnumber the control seats well past \$500M. The fund is, for most of its early life, more of a regulated business than a research shop, and the team should reflect that.

![A step chart of team headcount against assets under management on a log scale. Total headcount rises from two people at twenty-five million dollars to eighteen at one billion dollars. A dashed line for operations and compliance seats runs ahead of a dashed line for investment seats until past five hundred million dollars, where the investment seats finally pull ahead. Annotations note that the first hire is the chief operating officer, not a second analyst, and that investment seats only outgrow control past about five hundred million dollars.](/imgs/blogs/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when-3.png)

A caution about the chart: the exact headcounts are illustrative and vary enormously by strategy. A systematic fund running on code may stay tiny at \$1B; a fundamental long/short fund covering hundreds of names needs more analysts sooner; a credit fund needs heavy operations early because the instruments are operationally complex. The *shape* — control before investment, the founder's business hire first — generalizes; the exact numbers do not.

## Compensation structures

Once you know whom to hire and when, the harder question is *how to pay them*, because compensation at a launching fund is a tightrope: pay too little and you cannot attract or keep the people whose competence keeps the fund alive; pay too much in fixed salary and you bankrupt the J-curve, the multi-year stretch where the fund is burning cash before the incentive fee starts paying. There are three dominant models, and the founder's job is to match the model to the role.

Figure 6 compares them across the four questions that matter: who bears the risk, what the cash cost is at launch, which role each model suits, and how long it retains a person.

![A grid comparing three compensation models — salary plus bonus, eat-what-you-kill, and partnership or equity — across four questions. For who bears the risk: the fund bears it in salary plus bonus, the trader bears it in eat-what-you-kill, and it is shared between co-owners in partnership. For cash cost at launch: high and paid monthly for salary plus bonus, low until profit and loss lands for eat-what-you-kill, and low cash but giving up equity for partnership. For the best-fit role: operations and chief operating officer and analysts for salary plus bonus, a profit-and-loss-owning trader or portfolio manager for eat-what-you-kill, and the founding inner circle for partnership. For how long it retains: a year at a time, only while they produce, and years via vesting respectively.](/imgs/blogs/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when-6.png)

**Salary plus bonus** is the conventional package and the right one for most non-P&L roles: a fixed base paid monthly plus an annual bonus that is either discretionary (the founder decides) or formulaic (tied to fund performance, individual goals, or both). The base provides stability — a COO or a compliance officer needs to know the rent is covered regardless of whether the fund is up or down — and the bonus, funded out of the year's fees, aligns the person with the fund's annual results. The downside is the cash cost: the base is paid *every month, in good years and bad*, which is precisely the cash a J-curve fund is short of. This is the right model for the COO, the CFO, the CCO, the operations team, and the analysts — people whose contribution is real but not directly measurable as a P&L number.

**Eat-what-you-kill** ties a person's pay to the profit-and-loss they personally generate. A portfolio manager running their own sub-book might take a modest base plus a percentage — say 10% to 20% — of the net P&L their book produces. This is the natural model for a P&L-owning trader or PM, because it aligns them perfectly with their own production: they make a lot when their book makes a lot, little when it does not, and the fund's cash cost scales with the revenue the person actually generates rather than being fixed. The risks are well known: it can push a trader toward outsized risk-taking (their downside is capped at their base; their upside is uncapped), it can fracture a fund into competing silos that do not share ideas, and it does almost nothing to retain a person in a *bad* year — a trader who kills nothing eats nothing, and walks.

**Profit share** is the model that aligns a person with the *investors'* outcome rather than their own desk: a slice of the fund's incentive-fee pool, paid only when the fund earns its performance fee, which it earns only when investors make money above the high-water mark. A profit-share grant to a senior non-P&L person — a COO, say — ties their reward to the fund clearing its high-water mark, which is exactly the alignment an allocator wants to see. Because the incentive fee pays zero in a year below the prior peak, profit share costs the fund nothing when the fund has nothing to share, which is why it is so attractive in the J-curve: it is variable, performance-contingent, and conserves cash precisely when cash is scarce.

In practice, a single hire's package usually *blends* these. A COO might get a salary-plus-bonus base for stability *plus* a profit-share slice for alignment *plus*, if they are senior and the founder wants to lock them in, a small equity grant for the long term. Figure 4 shows that stack and what each layer is for.

![A vertical stack showing the four layers of a total compensation package, widest and most foundational at the bottom. The top layer is the base salary, the floor, paid whether the fund is up or down, around two hundred to three hundred thousand dollars for a chief operating officer. Below it is the annual bonus, which aligns the year and is funded out of the year's fees, often half to two times the base in a good year. Below that is profit share or carry, a slice of the incentive-fee pool that pays only when limited partners make money and is zero below the high-water mark. The bottom and most foundational layer is equity or partnership, a stake in the management company that vests over three to five years and is the retention mechanism.](/imgs/blogs/building-the-team-who-to-hire-and-when-4.png)

#### Worked example: eat-what-you-kill versus salary plus bonus for a trader

Suppose Maya brings on a portfolio manager, Daniel, to run a \$50M sub-book inside the Meridian Fund. Two ways to pay him.

Under **salary plus bonus**, Maya offers a \$200k base plus a target bonus of \$200k, so \$400k total in a normal year. The fund pays the \$200k base monthly no matter what Daniel's book does. If Daniel's book makes \$5M (a 10% gross return on his \$50M), Maya is delighted, and she pays the \$400k. If Daniel's book *loses* \$3M, Maya still owes the \$200k base, and probably a chunk of the bonus to retain him — so a losing book costs the fund perhaps \$300k in pay. The fund bears the risk.

Under **eat-what-you-kill**, Maya offers Daniel a \$120k base plus 15% of the *net* P&L his book generates. If Daniel makes \$5M, he earns \$120k + (15% × \$5M) = \$120k + \$750,000 = \$870,000 — far more than the salary model, but every dollar of it is funded by the \$5M he produced, of which the fund and its investors keep the other \$4.25M (before the fund-level incentive fee). If Daniel's book *loses* \$3M, he earns just his \$120k base; there is no kill, so there is little to eat, and the fund's cash cost is low exactly when its revenue is low.

The two models price risk to entirely different parties. Salary-plus-bonus makes Daniel's pay smooth and the *fund's* cash flow lumpy; eat-what-you-kill makes Daniel's pay lumpy and the *fund's* cash cost track revenue. For a launching fund desperate to conserve cash in the J-curve, eat-what-you-kill for a P&L-owning hire is often the safer structure — but Maya must cap Daniel's leverage and risk explicitly, because his asymmetric payoff (capped downside, uncapped upside) is an incentive to swing for the fences with the fund's capital.

*Pay the people who produce a measurable P&L on what they produce; pay the people who keep the lights on a stable salary; and never let an uncapped-upside, capped-downside trader size his own risk.*

## Equity and partnership

Profit share aligns someone with a *year* of performance. Equity aligns them with the *business*. Equity — a partnership stake in the management company — is the deepest, most expensive, and most carefully rationed retention tool a founder has, because giving it away means giving away a permanent slice of every future dollar the management company earns, including the dollars earned long after the recipient has stopped contributing.

To be precise about what is being granted: recall that the founder owns the *management company*, the operating business that advises the fund and collects the fees. Equity in the management company is a share of *that business's* economics — typically a share of the net profits (management fee plus incentive fee, minus the costs of running the firm) and, in a true partnership, a vote in how the firm is run. This is categorically different from profit share, which is a contractual slice of one pool with no ownership and no permanence; equity is *ownership*, and ownership is forever unless it is structured to be otherwise.

Three design choices make equity workable rather than ruinous:

**Vesting** turns a grant into a retention tool. A grant that vests immediately is a gift; a grant that vests over four years with a one-year cliff is a *reason to stay four years*. The recipient earns the equity gradually by continuing to show up, and forfeits the unvested portion if they leave early. Without vesting, a founder can hand someone 10% of the firm, watch them leave after eight months, and still owe them 10% of the firm's economics forever. Vesting is non-negotiable on every equity grant.

**Profits interests versus capital interests.** In most US management companies structured as LLCs or partnerships, key employees are granted *profits interests* — a right to a share of the *future* growth in the firm's value and profits — rather than *capital interests*, which would entitle the holder to a slice of the firm's value as it exists today (and trigger an immediate tax bill). Profits interests align the recipient with the firm's *future* without the founder having to write a check or the recipient having to pay tax on a phantom gain. The mechanics are tax-technical and belong with the CFO and the tax counsel, but the founder should know that "giving someone equity" almost always means a vesting profits interest, not a literal slice of today's enterprise value.

**Good-leaver / bad-leaver and clawback terms.** Partnership agreements specify what happens to vested equity when a partner leaves: a "good leaver" (retirement, disability, departure on good terms) may keep or be bought out of their vested stake at a fair value; a "bad leaver" (departure for cause, breach of non-compete, leaving to compete) may forfeit even vested equity. These terms protect the founder from the worst case — a partner who walks out the door to launch a competitor, taking both the equity and the relationships.

#### Worked example: an equity grant and its dilution

Maya owns 100% of Meridian's management company. To lock in her COO, she grants him a 10% profits interest, vesting over four years with a one-year cliff. What does that cost her?

In year one, suppose the management company nets \$500,000 after all costs (on a small fund, the management fee minus the fixed-cost base, plus whatever incentive fee the fund earned). The COO's *vested* share at the end of year one — after the cliff — is one-quarter of his 10%, so 2.5%, worth \$12,500 that year. Modest. But fast-forward to a year when the Meridian Fund has grown to \$500M, earns a strong return, and the management company nets \$8M. The COO is now fully vested at 10%, so his stake is worth \$800,000 *that year* — and every year thereafter — purely from the equity, on top of any salary and bonus. Over a decade in which the firm nets, say, \$40M cumulatively, his 10% is worth \$4M.

Maya gave up \$4M of cumulative economics to retain one person. Was it worth it? If the COO's presence is what kept the fund operationally credible enough to *reach* \$500M — if his absence would have meant an ODD failure that capped the fund at \$80M — then 90% of a large firm is worth vastly more than 100% of a small one, and the dilution paid for itself many times over. If, instead, Maya granted equity reflexively to a replaceable hire, she has permanently diluted herself for nothing. The lesson is not "equity is expensive" or "equity is cheap"; it is that equity is the most *leveraged* compensation decision on the board, and it should be reserved for the small inner circle whose long-term presence genuinely changes the firm's trajectory.

*Equity is not a bonus; it is a marriage. Grant it only to the people you want to still be co-owners with in ten years, vest it so they have to earn it, and structure the exit so a divorce does not cost you the firm.*

## Key-person risk

Here is the cruel symmetry of team-building: the same hire that solves a risk creates one. A COO who absorbs all the operational and vendor knowledge becomes someone without whom the operation stumbles. A star trader who drives half the returns becomes someone whose departure could halve the fund's appeal. The founder herself is the ultimate key person — at most launching funds, the fund *is* the founder, and an allocator knows it.

This is not a soft concern; it is written into the legal documents. LPs negotiate **key-person clauses** into the limited partnership agreement: provisions that name specific individuals (almost always the founder, sometimes a second key PM) and specify that if a named person ceases to be "actively and substantially involved" in managing the fund — through departure, prolonged absence, disability, or death — then a defined consequence triggers. The consequence is typically one of: a *suspension of the investment period* (no new capital deployed), an *enhanced redemption right* (investors can pull capital without the usual lock-up or notice), or, in the extreme, a *mandatory orderly wind-down*. The clause exists because LPs are, quite reasonably, buying a *person's* skill, and they want a contractual exit if that person disappears.

The practical implication is that a wave of departures, or the loss of the founder, is not just an HR event — it is a potential *redemption cascade* that can force the fund to liquidate positions into a falling market to meet exits, which is one of the most common ways funds die. So key-person risk must be *managed*, not just acknowledged, on several fronts at once:

- **Vest the equity and defer the comp.** A key person whose wealth is tied up in unvested equity and deferred profit share has a powerful financial reason to stay. The golden handcuff is the single most effective retention mechanism a founder has, which is the deeper reason equity and deferral matter beyond their cash-conservation appeal.
- **Document and cross-train.** A COO who carries the entire operational map in his head is a key-person risk all by himself. The map — the vendor relationships, the reconciliation process, the cash-control procedures — must be *documented* so that a replacement can pick it up, and critical functions should have at least a second person who understands them. ODD analysts probe exactly this: "what happens if your COO is hit by a bus?"
- **Key-person insurance.** The management company can buy life and disability insurance on the founder and other key people, with the firm as beneficiary, so a death or disability does not leave the business unable to fund an orderly transition or a wind-down. It is cheap relative to the risk and signals seriousness to allocators.
- **Build a bench and a succession story.** The most durable answer to key-person risk is a team deep enough that no single departure is fatal — a second PM who could carry the book, a COO with a capable deputy, a documented succession plan. A fund that can credibly answer "the strategy survives the founder" is worth more, and raises more, than one that cannot.

Notice the tension with the rest of this post. Concentrating decision-making and equity in a tight inner circle is good for alignment and cheap on cash; spreading knowledge, vesting broadly, and building a bench is good for resilience. The founder is constantly trading these off, and the right balance shifts as the fund scales: a \$45M launch is *allowed* to be founder-dependent because there is no money for a bench, but a \$1B institution that is still a single point of failure is a fragile institution, and sophisticated LPs will price that fragility into how much they allocate and on what terms.

## Hiring investment talent

We have spent most of this post arguing that investment headcount comes *last*. That is a statement about *sequence*, not about *importance* — eventually the research team is where the fund's future edge is grown, and hiring it well is its own discipline. A few principles, kept brief because the deep craft of evaluating investment talent lives in the careers series.

**Hire to widen the funnel, not to clone the founder.** The first analyst's value is leverage on the founder's process: covering more names, building and maintaining more models, running the screens and the monitoring so the founder's scarce attention lands on the highest-value decisions. The worst first analyst hire is a second version of the founder who wants to run their own book on day one; the best is someone who multiplies the founder's existing process before they earn the autonomy to extend it.

**Pay P&L owners on P&L, support roles on salary.** This is the compensation lesson from earlier, applied. An analyst who supports the founder's book is a salary-plus-bonus hire, because their contribution is real but not cleanly separable into a P&L number. A portfolio manager who runs an independent sub-book is an eat-what-you-kill (or hybrid) hire, because their P&L *is* separable, and tying their pay to it both aligns them and conserves the fund's cash. Misapplying the models — paying a support analyst on eat-what-you-kill, or a P&L-owning PM on flat salary — either creates perverse incentives or overpays for a bad year.

**Hiring a second PM creates a fork in the road for the firm's identity.** A fund with one PM is a *boutique* selling that PM's edge. The moment Maya hires a second PM running a second book, the Meridian Fund starts to become a *platform* — a firm that allocates capital and risk across multiple managers and takes a cut of each. That is a legitimate and lucrative model, but it is a different business, with different compensation (eat-what-you-kill across competing books), different risk management (netting and correlation across books), and different key-person dynamics (now you depend on several people, each of whom could leave and take their book's track record). Founders should make that transition deliberately, not stumble into it because a talented person wanted a seat. (The careers-side view of how firms organize around PMs and books is in [the firm archetypes: prop versus HFT versus pod-shop versus systematic fund](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund).)

**Sequence the investment hires within the research function, too.** The same logic that puts the operator before the investor applies inside the research team: hire the leverage role before the autonomy role. A founder running a fundamental long/short book should hire, in order, a generalist analyst who supports the existing process across the whole book, then a sector specialist who deepens coverage where the founder is thin, then — only once the process is humming and the AUM supports it — a second PM with the autonomy to run independent capital. Hiring the autonomous PM first, before the supporting analysts exist, recreates Maya's original error one level down: it adds capacity the founder cannot yet integrate while leaving the founder's own process under-resourced. The right first investment hire multiplies the founder; the wrong one competes with her for attention and capital.

#### Worked example: the first analyst's leverage on the founder's process

Maya finally hires her first analyst, Priya, at \$200k base plus a \$50k target bonus, once the fund has crossed \$250M and the operation is solid. Is Priya worth \$250k? Frame it as leverage, not as a standalone P&L. Before Priya, Maya could actively cover perhaps 25 names; the rest of her universe sat un-modeled and un-monitored. With Priya building and maintaining models and running the daily monitoring, Maya's active universe widens to perhaps 45 names, and — more importantly — Maya's *own* hours shift from maintenance to the highest-value decisions: sizing, catalysts, and the handful of variant-perception calls that actually drive returns. If that re-allocation of Maya's attention adds even half a percentage point of annual return on \$250M, that is \$1,250,000 of additional gross return, of which the 20% incentive fee is \$250,000 — Priya's entire package recovered from the founder's freed attention alone, before counting Priya's own direct contribution.

*The first analyst is not bought for her own alpha; she is bought for the founder's alpha she unlocks by taking the maintenance work off the founder's desk.*

#### Worked example: payroll versus the management fee at \$100M AUM

Maya's fund reaches \$100M of AUM. At a 1.5% management fee, that is \$1,500,000 of annual management-fee revenue — the steady, performance-independent cash she runs the business on. Now stack the payroll she would carry at that size: a COO/CFO at \$280k, a full-time CCO at \$180k, an operations analyst at \$130k, and her first investment analyst at \$220k. That is \$810,000 of base-plus-bonus payroll, plus Maya's own draw of, say, \$300k — \$1,110,000 of total payroll, or about 74% of the \$1.5M management fee.

That leaves roughly \$390,000 of management fee to cover *everything else*: the administrator (\$60k), the audit (\$45k), outside legal and compliance (\$80k), technology and market data (\$120k), office, insurance, and the rest — which, added up, comfortably exceeds the \$390k that remains. At \$100M, on the management fee alone, the Meridian Fund is running at a *loss*, and the only thing that closes the gap is the incentive fee — the option that pays nothing in a year below the high-water mark. This is the J-curve in one number, and it is exactly why over-hiring fixed salary is the fastest way to die: every salaried head you add raises the AUM you must reach before the management fee covers the lights, and lengthens the stretch where you are betting the firm's survival on the incentive fee showing up.

Figure 7 plots payroll as a percentage of management-fee revenue across AUM, and it carries the central warning of this post: below roughly \$100M, payroll devours the management fee, and only scale — a \$500M or \$1B AUM that grows fee revenue faster than headcount — pulls the ratio back to something sustainable.

## Common misconceptions

**"Hire analysts first — you're an investor, so build investing capacity."** This is the founder's instinct and it is backwards. The scarce, irreplaceable asset is the founder's attention on the market; the replaceable work is operations, compliance, and finance. The first hire should *absorb the replaceable work* so the irreplaceable person can do the irreplaceable thing. An analyst adds investing capacity but does nothing to protect the founder's, and a fund whose founder is drowning in reconciliations is squandering the only thing it sells. Operators before investors.

**"Equity is free — it's not cash, so it costs me nothing."** Equity is the most expensive compensation you can grant, precisely because it is *not* cash. Cash is a one-time cost; a permanent profits interest is a slice of *every future dollar* the management company earns, including the dollars earned long after the recipient stops contributing. Without vesting, good-leaver/bad-leaver terms, and careful rationing to a true inner circle, "free" equity can dilute a founder out of millions for a hire who leaves in a year. Equity feels free because the bill arrives in the future; it is one of the largest bills the founder will ever sign.

**"Compensation is just salary."** Salary is the *floor* of a comp package, not the package. Total compensation is base plus bonus plus profit share plus equity, and the layers above the base are the ones that *align* and *retain* people. A founder who thinks in salary alone will either overpay in fixed cash (bankrupting the J-curve) or fail to retain key people (because the golden handcuff of vesting deferred comp was never built). The whole art is in structuring the variable, performance-contingent, deferred layers so that pay conserves cash when cash is scarce and locks in the people whose departure would hurt.

**"A great track record means I can skip the operational hires."** No allocator who runs operational due diligence will let you. Operational diligence is a *separate, veto-wielding* process from investment diligence, and a fund with stellar returns but a founder who moves cash, marks the book, and reconciles it all herself will be rejected on segregation-of-duties grounds. The operational hire is not a luxury you earn after the track record; for institutional capital, it is frequently a *precondition* of the track record being investable at all.

**"Eat-what-you-kill is always the cheapest way to pay."** It conserves cash in a bad year, which is genuinely valuable in the J-curve, but it is not free of cost — it carries a behavioral price. A trader with capped downside (his base) and uncapped upside (his percentage) has an incentive to take more risk than the fund wants, and a firm full of eat-what-you-kill books can fracture into competing silos that hoard ideas and ignore portfolio-level risk. Use it for P&L-owning roles, but cap the leverage and risk explicitly, and never apply it to support roles whose contribution is not a clean P&L number.

**"More people means a stronger, safer firm."** Every hire that absorbs critical knowledge also *creates* key-person risk, and an under-managed key person — one whose process lives only in their head, whose comp is fully vested, whose departure would trip a key-person clause — is a single point of failure that can trigger a redemption cascade. Headcount only makes a firm safer if the knowledge is documented, the equity is vested, the functions are cross-trained, and there is a credible bench. Adding people without managing the dependency they create trades one fragility for another.

## How it plays out in the real world

The case for hiring the operator first is written most starkly in the funds that *didn't*, or that let their controls lag their assets — and the canonical lessons in this series come from the failures.

The most direct is **Madoff**, the largest Ponzi scheme ever uncovered (revealed December 2008, roughly \$17.5B of principal lost against tens of billions in fabricated statement value). Madoff is the textbook *operational* failure: no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, self-custody of assets, and "too smooth" returns that no one with control of the books was positioned or incentivized to question. The fraud was possible because the functions this post argues you must *separate and staff independently* — valuation, custody, administration, audit — were all controlled by, or captive to, one person. The lesson for a legitimate founder is the same as the lesson for the allocator: independent, separately staffed control functions are not bureaucratic overhead; they are the structural difference between a credible fund and a fraud, and an ODD team's entire job is to verify they exist.

The leverage-and-concentration blowups carry the *key-person* lesson. **Amaranth Advisors** lost roughly \$6.6B — about 65% of its assets — in roughly a week in 2006 on concentrated natural-gas spread bets run largely by a single trader, Brian Hunter. **Archegos**, a family office, lost north of \$10B (and cost its prime brokers, Credit Suisse most of all, billions) in 2021 through concentrated, hidden, highly leveraged total-return swaps directed by one person, Bill Hwang. And **LTCM** lost about \$4.6B in 1998 on convergence trades levered roughly 25 to 30 times, run by a small group of brilliant founders whose shared conviction was itself a concentration risk. In each, the firm's fate rode on a tiny number of people and a tiny number of correlated decisions — the extreme of key-person risk, where the "person" is also the whole investment process. The defensive lesson is the one in this post's key-person section: spread the knowledge, cap the individual risk, and never let the firm's survival depend on a single un-checked decision-maker.

On the positive side, the funds that scale durably are the ones that built the *business* early. The platforms that grew from a single PM into multi-billion-dollar institutions did it by hiring real COOs and CFOs, building independent risk functions, separating their offices, and turning equity and deferred comp into a retention engine for an inner circle — exactly the sequence Figure 1 describes. The boring hires came first; the brilliant returns were what those boring hires *protected*. As of 2026, the funds in the "billion-dollar club" — which control roughly 90% of industry assets — are almost uniformly funds with deep, separated, professionally staffed teams, while the median fund remains small, often founder-dependent, and statistically far more likely to be among the 7% to 11% that liquidate in any given year.

A grounded note on costs and ranges. The salary figures throughout this post — a COO at \$200k to \$350k, a CCO at \$50k to \$200k depending on whether the role is fractional or full-time, an analyst at \$150k to \$400k — are illustrative US ranges as of 2026 and vary widely by location, strategy, the candidate's seniority, and how much of the package is variable versus fixed. The break-even and payroll math is likewise illustrative: it depends on the management-fee rate, the fixed-cost base of the chosen vendors, and the strategy's operational complexity. What does *not* vary is the structure of the decision — the sequence (operator before investor, control before research), the cash arithmetic of the J-curve, and the trade-off between alignment and resilience that every comp and equity decision sits on. Get the structure right and the numbers are survivable; get the sequence wrong, like Maya almost did, and no amount of investment talent will save a fund whose founder cannot afford the attention to use it.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment a founder has any capital and any cash to spend on people — which is to say, from the week of launch. The decision of *who* to hire first, *when* to add each role as AUM grows, and *how* to pay them is not a back-office afterthought to be sorted out once the returns roll in; it is one of the highest-leverage strategic decisions a founder makes, because it determines whether the founder's scarce attention stays on the market, whether the fund passes operational diligence, and whether payroll bankrupts the J-curve before the incentive fee ever arrives.

The single thing to carry away: hire the person who runs the business before you hire the second person who picks the stocks, pay people in a structure that conserves cash when cash is scarce and locks in the inner circle for the long term, and treat every key hire as both the solution to one risk and the source of another.
