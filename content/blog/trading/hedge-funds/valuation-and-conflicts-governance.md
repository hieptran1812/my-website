> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The valuation committee and the conflicts policy are where the manager's interests and the fund's interests sit closest together — close enough to collide. Every one of the recurring flashpoints is a place where doing what is good for the manager quietly takes money from the fund, and the only defense is a control that does not run through the manager.
>
> - The four collision points keep recurring: **valuation** (who marks the hard positions, since the mark sets the fee), **allocation and cross-trades** (which fund gets the good fill), **soft dollars** (using the fund's commissions to buy the manager's research), and **expense allocation** (which bills are the fund's and which are the management company's).
> - **Expense allocation is the SEC's favorite enforcement area** for private-fund advisers — pushing the management company's own overhead onto the fund's expense line is the single most common conflicts case, and it is almost always charged as a breach of the adviser's fiduciary duty.
> - The fix in every case is the same shape: a **written policy**, an **independent check** (the administrator, the valuation committee, the CCO), **disclosure** to investors, and a **documented, repeatable method** an auditor or an examiner can re-trace. A conflict you disclosed and controlled is a fact of the business; a conflict you hid is a fraud.
> - The one fact to remember: you are a **fiduciary**. The legal default is that every doubtful call resolves *against* the manager and *for* the fund. Mishandle these and you face lawsuits, fines, disgorgement, or an industry bar — not because the trade was bad, but because you took money that was not yours.

Maya thought she had built a clean fund. The Meridian Fund had an independent administrator striking the NAV, a real auditor, a compliance manual on the shelf, and a CCO who took the job seriously. So when the SEC examiner asked, on day two of a routine examination, to see the firm's expense-allocation policy and a year of the supporting invoices, Maya handed them over without a second thought. It was the kind of request she expected. She was not worried about it.

The examiner spent an afternoon with the invoices and came back with a single, quiet question. There was a line item — about \$50,000 over the year — for a market-data and analytics subscription that the management company used. It had been billed to the fund. So had a slice of the firm's compliance-consultant retainer, and most of the cost of a third-party marketing deck the firm had commissioned to raise new capital. None of it was large on its own. None of it felt dishonest to Maya; the data fed the research, the compliance work protected the investors, the marketing brought in new partners. But the examiner's question was not about whether the costs were reasonable. It was narrower and sharper: *under your own fund documents, whose bill is this — the fund's, or the management company's?* And when Maya went back and read the expense clause in her own limited partnership agreement carefully, for what was honestly the first time since her lawyer drafted it, she realized the document did not say what she had assumed it said. Several of those costs were the management company's to bear out of the management fee. She had been quietly running the firm's overhead through the fund. Not much. But for two years. To every investor at once.

This is the part of running a fund that has nothing to do with whether you can trade. The collisions in this post are not market risks; they are *conflict* risks — the structural fact that the person running the money and the person valuing the money and the person deciding whose bill is whose is, in a small fund, often the same person, and that person's economic interest does not always point the same way as the fund's. The valuation committee, the allocation policy, the soft-dollar rules, and the expense-allocation method exist for exactly one reason: to put a control between the manager's interest and the fund's money at every point where they touch. Figure 1 is the whole map on one page — each flashpoint, which way the manager benefits if it goes unchecked, and the specific control that keeps the fund whole. We will spend the rest of the post earning the right to read every cell of it.

## Foundations: the conflicts built into the structure

Before any of the mechanics, you need the vocabulary, because the words here are precise and the law leans on them. If you trade for a living, the *markets* side is familiar — you know what a mark is, you know one trade can fill several accounts. What is usually a blank page is the *governance* layer: the legal duty you owe, the specific places your interest and the fund's interest diverge, and the controls that are supposed to sit on each divergence. Let us build it from zero.

**Fiduciary duty.** The legal obligation, as an investment adviser, to act in the best interest of your client — the fund and, through it, its investors — and to put their interests ahead of your own. Under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, an adviser is a fiduciary. This is the foundation under everything else in the post: it means that wherever your interest and the fund's interest collide, the default rule is that the collision resolves *in the fund's favor*, and that you must either eliminate the conflict or fully and fairly disclose it and manage it. A fiduciary who quietly resolves a conflict in their own favor has not made a business decision; they have breached a legal duty.

**Conflict of interest.** Any situation where the manager has an incentive to act in a way that benefits the manager at the fund's expense. A conflict is not, by itself, wrongdoing — conflicts are structurally unavoidable in a business where you both run the money and get paid out of it. The wrongdoing is failing to disclose a conflict and failing to control it. The entire regulatory architecture around advisers is built on a simple premise: *disclose your conflicts, and manage them so the client is not harmed.* Hiding one converts a manageable fact into a fraud.

**Valuation and the mark.** The *mark* is the price at which a position is carried on the books. *Valuation* is the process of deciding that price. For a liquid stock there is nothing to decide — the market quotes a price and you use it. For an illiquid or bespoke position there is no quoted price, so someone has to estimate it, and the estimate depends on the assumptions, and the assumptions depend on who is choosing them. The conflict is direct and brutal: the manager's incentive fee is charged on the NAV, the NAV depends on the marks, and the manager is the person closest to the marks. The person who values the position is the person whose pay depends on the value being high. We cover the NAV machine itself — the administrator, the fair-value hierarchy, the monthly close — in [NAV, accounting, and the administrator](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/nav-accounting-and-the-administrator); here the focus is the *governance* that sits on top of the marks.

**The valuation committee.** The internal body — typically the CFO, the COO, the CCO, and sometimes an outside member — that reviews and approves the valuation of hard-to-price positions before the NAV is struck. The committee's job is to take the marking decision out of the sole hands of the person whose fee depends on it. It does not replace the independent administrator; it sits between the portfolio manager who proposes a mark and the administrator who strikes the official number, and it documents *why* a Level 3 position is carried where it is.

**Fair value.** The price at which a position would change hands between a willing buyer and a willing seller in an orderly transaction — an exit price, not a hopeful one. The fair-value hierarchy sorts positions by how observable their pricing is: **Level 1** is a quoted price in an active market (no judgment), **Level 2** is derived from observable inputs like comparable bonds or broker quotes (some judgment), and **Level 3** is the manager's own model with unobservable inputs (maximum judgment). Almost every valuation conflict lives at Level 3, because that is where the number rests on an opinion and the opinion belongs to the person being paid on it.

**Allocation.** When the manager runs more than one account — two funds, a fund plus separately managed accounts, an onshore feeder and an offshore feeder — and a single trade is intended for several of them, *allocation* is the decision of how to split the fill among them. The conflict appears when the fill is partial, or when one trade turns out well and another badly: the manager has an incentive to steer the good fills to the account that pays the manager more (a higher-fee fund, a seeded fund where the manager owns more economics, or the manager's own personal capital).

**Cross-trade.** A transaction in which one account managed by the same manager buys a security directly from another account managed by the same manager — Fund A sells the bond that Fund B buys, with the manager on both sides. Done at a fair, independent price with proper approval, a cross-trade can save both funds the cost of crossing the market spread. Done at a price the manager picks, it is a tool for quietly moving value from one fund to another — typically dumping a loser on the fund least able to object.

**Soft dollars.** An arrangement in which the manager directs the fund's trades to a broker who charges a higher commission, and the broker uses part of that commission to pay for research and brokerage services the manager uses. The fund's commissions, in other words, buy the manager's research. The conflict is plain: the manager gets the research, but the *fund* pays for it through inflated commissions, and the manager has an incentive to over-trade or to over-pay for execution to generate more soft-dollar credits. Federal law carves out a narrow *safe harbor* — Section 28(e) of the Securities Exchange Act — that makes this legal, but only for genuine research and brokerage, and only when the commission is reasonable for the value received.

**Expense allocation.** The decision of which costs are borne by the *fund* (and therefore reduce the investors' returns) and which are borne by the *management company* (and therefore come out of the manager's management fee). The fund pays its own operating costs — audit, administrator, brokerage, fund-level legal. The management company pays the cost of running the manager's business — salaries, rent, technology, marketing, firm-level compliance. The conflict is the most basic one in the entire structure: the manager writes both checks, and has an incentive to push as many bills as possible onto the fund's side of the line. This is the SEC's single most active conflicts enforcement area for private-fund advisers, and we give it its own section below.

**The management fee and the management company.** The 2% (or, more typically now, 1.3–1.5%) annual management fee is paid by the fund to the management company. It is *supposed* to be the budget out of which the manager runs the business — pays its people, its rent, its technology. The expense-allocation conflict is, at bottom, a fight over what that fee is meant to cover: if the manager can also bill the fund for the firm's overhead, the manager has been paid twice for the same costs, and the management fee becomes pure profit. We work the fee mechanics — the rate, the high-water mark, the crystallization — in [fees and terms: the high-water mark](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/fees-and-terms-the-high-water-mark); here the point is which side of the line a given bill belongs on.

Here is the through-line that connects all of these. A hedge fund sells one promise: *trust me with your capital and I will compound it fairly inside a structure that protects you.* Every conflict in this post is a place where the manager can quietly break the "fairly" part — by marking a position to inflate a fee, by steering a good fill to a favored account, by buying research with the fund's commissions, by billing the firm's rent to the fund. None of these is a trading decision. Each is a transfer of value from the fund to the manager, dressed up as ordinary business. The governance — the committee, the policy, the independent check, the disclosure, the audit trail — exists to make that transfer impossible to do quietly. Strip out the governance and the promise stops being credible; an allocator who runs operational due diligence on you, which we cover in [operational due diligence: the veto](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/operational-due-diligence-the-veto), will find the gap and walk. The rest of this post is the four collisions, one at a time, and the control that sits on each.

## Valuation governance

Start with the conflict that has produced the most famous frauds in the industry's history, because it is the cleanest example of the whole pattern. The manager runs the money. The manager's incentive fee is charged as a percentage of the fund's gains, computed off the NAV. The NAV is the sum of the position marks. And for the positions that have no quoted market price — the Level 3 marks — the person best placed to decide the mark is the manager. So the manager decides the number that decides the manager's pay. Left ungoverned, this is not a temptation; it is a loaded gun on the table.

The control is a chain, and every link must be independent of the manager's pay. Figure 2 shows it. The portfolio manager *proposes* the marks under a written valuation policy. The valuation committee *reviews and approves* them. The independent administrator, fed by third-party pricing, *strikes* the official NAV. And only that official NAV bases the incentive fee. The manager touches the front of the chain — proposing the mark — but cannot reach the back of it alone.

![A graph showing the valuation chain from the portfolio manager proposing marks under a written policy, through the valuation committee and the independent administrator fed by third-party pricing, to the official NAV that bases the incentive fee](/imgs/blogs/valuation-and-conflicts-governance-2.png)

**Who marks.** For Level 1 positions, nobody marks — the market does. A liquid equity closes at a price the whole world can see, and the administrator carries it there. The conflict is zero, which is exactly why a Level 1 equity book is the easiest fund to administer and the one ODD analysts worry about least. The conflict scales with the share of the book that is Level 2 and especially Level 3. A fund holding private credit, distressed claims, bespoke derivatives, or thinly-traded small caps is a fund where the manager's judgment is doing real work in the NAV — and where the governance has to do real work too.

**Who checks.** The written valuation policy is the first check: it says, position type by position type, *how* a mark is to be determined — which vendor, which model, which inputs, what to do when a price goes stale, how often a Level 3 mark is re-validated. The policy matters because it removes the discretion to mark conveniently. If the policy says a private loan is valued by a discounted-cash-flow model with a discount rate benchmarked to comparable issuance, then the manager cannot simply assert a higher value; they have to change an input, and a changed input is visible and challengeable.

**The committee.** The valuation committee is the second check. It meets — monthly for most funds, more often around quarter-ends and audits — and walks the Level 3 positions one by one. For each, the manager presents the proposed mark and the supporting evidence; the committee, which includes people whose pay does *not* move with the mark (the CFO, the CCO), interrogates it and either approves it or sends it back. The committee's minutes are the audit trail. When the year-end auditor arrives, the first thing they test on the hard positions is whether the marks followed the policy and whether the committee actually challenged them — or whether the "committee" was a rubber stamp that approved whatever the PM proposed. A valuation committee that has never once pushed back on a mark is not a control; it is theater, and an auditor or examiner can tell the difference.

**The independent administrator.** The administrator is the final check. It strikes the *official* NAV from third-party data — positions from the prime broker, prices from independent vendors — and the manager's marks feed in only where no third-party price exists, and even then only after the committee has approved them. The administrator is the reason an investor never has to take the manager's word for what the book is worth. The cautionary tale, as always, is Madoff: no independent administrator, self-struck NAVs on his own letterhead, a tiny unknown auditor — the marks were a pure fabrication precisely because no independent party ever struck or checked them. The lesson the industry took is that independence in valuation is not a nicety; it is the difference between a fund an institution can invest in and one it cannot.

A founder building this chain has to make two design choices that allocators scrutinize. The first is *who sits on the committee*. The weakest version is a committee that is the portfolio manager plus two of the PM's direct reports — a body that, on paper, exists, but whose every member's pay and standing depend on the marks the PM proposes. The stronger version puts genuine economic distance between the committee and the marks: a CFO and a CCO who are paid out of the management fee regardless of the incentive fee, and, in the strongest version, an independent outside valuation member or an independent director on an offshore board who has no stake in the marks at all. The second choice is *evidence*. Each Level 3 mark should carry a documented basis — the model, the inputs, the comparable transactions, the broker quotes obtained — that the committee actually examined and that the year-end auditor will later re-perform. When the auditor tests valuation, they are not asking whether the marks were profitable; they are asking whether the marks followed the written policy and whether the committee's challenge was real. A fund whose audit comes back with no valuation issues, year after year, is a fund whose conflict on the marks has been governed into a non-event — which is exactly the outcome the whole chain exists to produce.

#### Worked example: how an inflated Level 3 mark steals the incentive fee

The Meridian Fund holds a \$10 million face position in an illiquid private credit note. There is no quoted market. The fund's valuation policy says it is carried at a model value, and the model spits out a fair value of \$9.2 million given current comparable spreads. The fund's other positions are Level 1 equities, and over the year they have produced a gain that, with the note marked at \$9.2 million, leaves the fund up 9.0% net of the management fee — comfortably above its prior high-water mark.

Now suppose the manager, instead of carrying the note at the model's \$9.2 million, marks it at par, \$10 million, on the argument that "it will recover and the model is too conservative." That single \$800,000 of extra carried value flows straight into the NAV. On a \$120 million fund, it lifts the year's gain by roughly 0.67 of a percentage point, from 9.0% to about 9.67%. At a 20% incentive fee, that extra gain hands the manager an additional incentive fee of about 20% of \$800,000, or \$160,000 — on a position that has not actually recovered and may never. The manager has, in effect, written a \$160,000 check to themselves drawn on the fund, and the cost lands on the investors who will hold the note when the real exit price arrives below par. *The mark did not change the cash the note will eventually return; it changed only who got paid on the difference, and when.* This is why the marking decision cannot sit with the person being paid on it — and why the committee's job is to make the manager defend \$10 million against a model that says \$9.2 million, in writing, before anyone is paid a cent.

There is a second-order trap here that catches even honest managers. Because the incentive fee crystallizes (usually annually) and the high-water mark resets, an inflated mark at year-end is not a loan the manager pays back later — it is a *permanent* transfer once the fee is locked in. If the note later marks down to its true value, the fund's NAV falls and the manager simply earns no incentive fee on the way back down to the high-water mark; the \$160,000 already paid is gone. The asymmetry — the manager keeps the upside of an aggressive mark but the fund eats the downside — is exactly why valuation governance is not optional bookkeeping. It is the control that stops a one-time mark from becoming a one-way payment.

## Allocation and cross-trade conflicts

The second collision appears the moment a manager runs more than one pool of capital. Almost every real firm does: an onshore feeder for US taxable investors and an offshore feeder for everyone else (the [master-feeder structure](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/domestic-vs-offshore-the-master-feeder)), a flagship fund plus a few separately managed accounts, a main fund plus the manager's own personal capital traded alongside. As soon as one trade idea is meant for several accounts, two conflicts open up: *allocation* (how to split the fill) and *cross-trades* (transacting between the accounts).

**Allocation.** Suppose the manager wants to buy 100,000 shares of a stock for two funds — Fund A and Fund B — and the market only fills 40,000 before the price runs away. Who gets the 40,000? If Fund A pays the manager a 20% incentive fee and Fund B pays 10%, the manager has an economic incentive to put the good fill in Fund A. If the manager's own personal capital trades alongside, the temptation is to fill the personal account first and the clients second — the classic "front-running your own clients" abuse. The control is a *written pre-trade allocation policy* that fixes the rule *before* the outcome is known. The standard default is **pro-rata**: each account gets a share of the fill in proportion to its intended order size, so the partial fill of 40,000 against a combined 100,000 order is split 40% to each account's intended size regardless of which fund pays more. Pro-rata works precisely because it removes discretion: the manager cannot decide after the fact who got the good fill, because the rule decided it before the fill existed. Where the firm departs from pro-rata — for genuine reasons like minimum lot sizes, tax considerations, or differing mandates — the policy must say so in advance and the departures must be documented and reviewed, because every departure is a place the manager could be favoring one account.

**Cross-trades.** Now suppose Fund A wants to *sell* a position that Fund B wants to *buy*. Instead of A selling into the market and B buying from the market — paying the bid-ask spread twice and the commission twice — the manager can *cross* the position directly from A to B at a single price. Done right, this is a legitimate efficiency: both funds save the spread, and the position moves at a fair price. Done wrong, it is one of the cleanest ways to move value between funds, because the manager sets the price and is on both sides. A manager who wants to flatter Fund A's returns can sell A's losing position to Fund B at an above-market price; Fund A books a smaller loss, Fund B overpays, and the only loser is the set of investors in B, who never agreed to be the manager's dumping ground.

The control on cross-trades is the strictest in this whole post, because the conflict is the most direct — the manager is literally on both sides. Figure 4 shows the workflow that makes a cross-trade defensible. It is not a single approval; it is a gate sequence, and skipping any gate is what turns a legitimate cross into an enforcement case.

![A pipeline showing the cross-trade approval workflow, moving from the need arising through an eligibility check, an independent price, committee approval, documented consent, and finally settlement with an audit trail](/imgs/blogs/valuation-and-conflicts-governance-4.png)

The gates are: confirm both funds' documents *permit* cross-trades and that neither account is one where they are flatly prohibited (an ERISA plan asset account, for instance, where the prohibited-transaction rules bite hard); strike an *independent price* — the third-party midpoint, never a price the manager picks; obtain *committee and CCO approval* with no commission flowing to the manager from the cross; *disclose and obtain consent* as the conflict rules require, since a principal or cross transaction under Section 206(3) of the Advisers Act carries specific disclosure-and-consent obligations; and finally *settle and record* with a full audit trail. Every gate exists to answer one examiner question: *how do I know the manager did not pick the price to favor one fund?* If the answer is "an independent vendor set the midpoint, the committee approved it, and both funds consented," the cross is fine. If the answer is "the manager decided," it is a fiduciary breach.

#### Worked example: a cross-trade at a fair midpoint between two funds

The Meridian Fund (Fund A) needs to reduce a corporate bond position because of a redemption; a separate account the same firm manages (Fund B) has fresh cash and wants to add exactly that bond. The bond's independent vendor price is a midpoint of \$98.50, with a market bid of \$98.20 and an offer of \$98.80 — a 60-cent round-trip spread on a \$2 million face position.

If Fund A sold into the market at the \$98.20 bid and Fund B bought from the market at the \$98.80 offer, the two funds together would lose the full spread: A receives \$1,964,000 and B pays \$1,976,000, a combined \$12,000 of spread-and-friction handed to the market makers. Crossed at the independent \$98.50 midpoint instead, A receives \$1,970,000 and B pays \$1,970,000 — each saves about \$6,000 versus crossing the spread, and no money leaves the two funds for the street. The cross is legitimate *because* the price is the independent midpoint that neither fund can complain about, the committee approved it, and both sets of investors were disclosed to. *The efficiency is real and shared; the danger is only ever that the manager picks a price off the midpoint to slide value from one fund to the other — which is why the midpoint must come from a third party, not from the manager.* Move that cross to \$98.80 instead of \$98.50, with no independent price and no consent, and the same transaction quietly transfers \$6,000 from Fund B's investors to Fund A's — and becomes a Section 206(3) violation.

## Soft dollars

The third collision is the most misunderstood, because it is dressed up in a way that makes it sound free. *Soft dollars* are the practice of paying for research and brokerage services with the fund's trading commissions rather than with the manager's own hard cash. A broker charges the fund, say, a few cents per share more than bare execution would cost, and bundles into that commission the cost of research the manager consumes — analyst reports, data, models, access to corporate management. The manager gets the research; the fund pays for it through the inflated commission.

The reason this is legal at all is a narrow statutory safe harbor: **Section 28(e)** of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. It protects a manager from a claim that paying more than the lowest available commission is a breach of fiduciary duty — *but only* if the extra commission buys genuine **research or brokerage** that provides lawful assistance to the manager's investment decision-making, and *only* if the commission is reasonable in relation to the value of the services received. Everything turns on what counts as "research and brokerage." Figure 5 draws the line: things that aid an investment decision (analysis, research seminars, quote feeds used for analysis) are inside the safe harbor; things that run the manager's *business* (office hardware, rent, salaries, order-management software, travel, compliance, marketing) are outside it, and must be paid with the manager's own hard dollars.

![A grid separating soft-dollar uses inside the Section 28(e) safe harbor, like research and decision-aiding data, from uses outside it, like office hardware, travel, and the manager's own operating costs](/imgs/blogs/valuation-and-conflicts-governance-5.png)

The conflicts inside soft dollars are layered, and a founder has to see all of them. First, the obvious one: the *fund* pays for research the *manager* uses, so there is a temptation to acquire more research than the fund needs, because someone else is paying. Second, a subtler one: the manager has an incentive to *over-trade* — to churn the portfolio — because every trade generates commissions and therefore soft-dollar credits. A manager whose research budget is funded by commission flow has a quiet reason to keep the turnover high, and that turnover is itself a drag on the fund's returns and a cost the investors bear. Third, the bundling hides the price: because the research arrives bundled into the commission, neither the manager nor the investor sees a clean invoice for what the research actually cost, which is exactly the opacity 28(e) was meant to constrain.

The control is, again, the familiar shape: a written soft-dollar policy that limits use to 28(e)-eligible research and brokerage; a commission budget that caps the total soft-dollar spend and is reviewed against the value received; disclosure to investors in the fund documents and Form ADV that the manager uses soft dollars and how; and a mixed-use allocation rule for any service that has both a research and a non-research use — a data terminal used 70% for research and 30% for back-office operations must have the 30% paid in hard dollars, with the split documented and defensible. The trend across the industry, accelerated by the European unbundling rules under MiFID II, has been toward *paying for research in hard dollars* and shrinking soft-dollar arrangements, precisely because the cleanest way to kill the conflict is to take the fund's commissions out of the manager's research budget entirely.

#### Worked example: a soft-dollar research budget and the mixed-use line

The Meridian Fund expects to pay roughly \$400,000 in trading commissions over the year. Of that, the manager proposes to direct \$120,000 toward soft-dollar research credits — independent analyst research and a sector-data subscription that genuinely feed the investment process. So far, this is inside the safe harbor: the research aids investment decisions, and \$120,000 of soft credits on \$400,000 of commissions is a defensible fraction provided the commission rates are reasonable.

Now the mixed-use problem. The manager also wants to fund a \$30,000 market-data terminal through the same soft-dollar arrangement. But the terminal is used about 60% for genuine research and analysis and about 40% for order entry and trade booking — a back-office function that is plainly the *manager's* operating cost, not 28(e)-eligible research. The correct handling is to split it: 60%, or \$18,000, may be paid in soft dollars; the remaining 40%, or \$12,000, must be paid by the management company in hard dollars. If the manager instead runs the full \$30,000 through soft dollars, the firm has charged \$12,000 of its own operating cost to the fund's commissions — a 28(e) violation and, functionally, an expense-allocation breach wearing a soft-dollar costume. *The safe harbor protects research that helps you pick stocks; it never protects the cost of running your office, and a mixed-use item has to be cut along that exact line.* The discipline is to document the use split, keep the supporting basis, and be ready to show an examiner the \$18,000/\$12,000 logic.

## Expense allocation

Now the collision the SEC cares about most, and the one that caught Maya. *Expense allocation* is the question of which bills are the fund's and which are the management company's — and it is the SEC's single most active conflicts enforcement area for private-fund advisers. The reason it dominates enforcement is structural: it is the most common conflict (every fund has expenses), the most tempting (pushing a bill to the fund directly increases the manager's net economics), the easiest to do quietly (a misallocated invoice does not look like fraud; it looks like accounting), and the easiest for an examiner to *prove* (the invoices and the LPA either match or they do not). Figure 6 shows an illustrative ranking of where private-fund conflict enforcement actually lands — and expense allocation sits at the top.

![A bar chart showing an illustrative ranking of SEC private-fund conflict enforcement by category, with expense allocation the largest, ahead of undisclosed fees, valuation, conflicts, allocation, and soft dollars](/imgs/blogs/valuation-and-conflicts-governance-6.png)

The governing line is simple to state and hard to live by: **the management fee is meant to cover the cost of running the manager's business; the fund pays only its own operating costs.** Figure 3 shows the same five bills landing two different ways — the improper "exam finding" column quietly billing the firm's overhead to the fund, and the proper column splitting each bill by who the cost actually serves.

![A before-and-after figure contrasting improper expense allocation, which bills manager salaries rent and marketing to the fund, with proper allocation, which leaves only fund-specific costs on the fund](/imgs/blogs/valuation-and-conflicts-governance-3.png)

The bills that are clearly the **fund's** are the costs of the fund existing and operating as a vehicle: the audit of the fund's financial statements, the administrator who strikes the fund's NAV, the fund's brokerage and interest, the fund-level legal work (the LPA, the offering memorandum, regulatory filings the fund makes), the organizational costs of forming the fund, and the directors' fees of an offshore fund's board. The bills that are clearly the **manager's** are the costs of running the asset-management business: the salaries and bonuses of the manager's employees, the firm's rent, its technology and order-management systems, the cost of *raising* capital (marketing decks, placement-agent fees, travel to pitch investors), and the firm's own compliance and overhead. Figure 7 stacks the two columns with the LPA's expense clause as the dividing line.

![A vertical stack showing fund-borne costs like audit administrator and brokerage above the LPA expense line, and management-company costs like salaries rent and marketing below it](/imgs/blogs/valuation-and-conflicts-governance-7.png)

The trouble lives in three grey zones, and these are exactly where the enforcement cases cluster:

**The fund-document mismatch.** What a fund *may* charge depends entirely on what the LPA's expense clause says. A broadly drafted clause that lets the fund bear "expenses related to its operations" is not a license to charge anything; the SEC's position is that an expense is properly the fund's only if it is *both* permitted by the document *and* actually a fund expense rather than the manager's overhead. The most common enforcement finding is precisely Maya's: the manager charged the fund for a cost that the LPA, read carefully, assigned to the management company — or that was a manager overhead masquerading as a fund operating cost. The defense is to read your own expense clause as if you were the examiner and to allocate strictly within it.

**Shared services and mixed-use costs.** Some costs genuinely serve both the manager and the fund — a compliance consultant who works on both the firm's program and the fund's filings, a data subscription used for both research and operations, an in-house employee who spends part of their time on fund accounting and part on the firm's business. The correct treatment is a documented, reasonable allocation method, applied consistently, that splits the cost by who it actually serves. The wrong treatment — and a frequent enforcement finding — is to default the whole shared cost to the fund because the fund is the easier place to bury it.

**Undisclosed or novel expenses.** Charging the fund for a category of expense that investors were never told about — accelerated monitoring fees, a portion of the manager's own back-office, "broken-deal" costs in a hybrid vehicle — is a fiduciary problem even if the LPA is silent, because the silence is not consent. If you intend to charge the fund for something investors would not expect, the safe path is to disclose it specifically before you do it.

The control on expense allocation is the most prosaic in the post and the most load-bearing: a *written expense-allocation policy* that names, category by category, which side bears each cost and how shared costs are split; *CCO review* of the actual invoices against that policy and the LPA on a regular cadence; and an *audited allocation method* — the year-end auditor and, eventually, the SEC examiner both test whether the bills that hit the fund match what the documents permit. None of this is glamorous. All of it is what stands between a founder and the most common enforcement case in the business.

#### Worked example: is the \$50,000 cost the fund's or the manager's?

Maya is looking at four bills and has to decide, for each, whether it is the fund's or the management company's. This is the exact exercise the examiner ran on her.

The first is the fund's annual **audit fee of \$45,000**. This is unambiguously the *fund's* cost: it is the cost of auditing the fund's own financial statements, it is a benefit to the investors (an independent audit is what makes the fund investable), and the LPA names it. Charge it to the fund.

The second is a **\$50,000 market-data and analytics subscription** the management company uses across the firm. This is the one that bit her. The subscription supports the manager's research and operations generally — it is part of the cost of running the asset-management business — so it is the *manager's* cost, payable out of the management fee, unless the LPA specifically permits the fund to bear research data *and* the subscription genuinely aids the fund's investment decisions (in which case a 28(e) soft-dollar or a documented mixed-use split might apply). Absent that, charging the full \$50,000 to the fund is the classic misallocation: the firm's overhead pushed onto the fund. The correct answer for Maya was that this was the management company's bill.

The third is **\$30,000 for a marketing deck and placement work** to raise new capital. This is plainly the *manager's* cost — raising AUM grows the management company's fee base; it is not a benefit the existing fund investors should pay for. Charge it to the management company. (Placement-agent fees are a notorious enforcement subcategory precisely because some managers tried to route them to the fund.)

The fourth is a **\$40,000 compliance-consultant retainer**, of which the consultant estimates about 25% was spent on the *fund's* regulatory filings and 75% on the *firm's* compliance program. The correct treatment is a documented split: \$10,000 (25%) to the fund, \$30,000 (75%) to the management company, with the consultant's time records supporting the allocation. Defaulting the whole \$40,000 to the fund is the shared-services error. *Add it up: of these four bills the fund should bear the \$45,000 audit and \$10,000 of the consultant — \$55,000 — and the management company should bear the \$50,000 data subscription, the \$30,000 marketing, and \$30,000 of the consultant — \$110,000. Maya had been charging most of the \$110,000 to the fund.* The lesson is that "is this expense reasonable?" is the wrong question; the right question is always "whose bill is this, under the document?"

## Personal and structural conflicts

Beyond the four headline collisions sit a set of conflicts that come from the *people* and the *structure* rather than from a single decision, and a serious conflicts policy has to address them too.

**Personal trading and front-running.** The manager and the firm's employees have their own brokerage accounts, and a conflict arises whenever an employee could trade ahead of the fund — buying a stock personally just before the fund buys it (pushing the price up against the fund), or selling personally just before the fund sells. The control is a written personal-trading policy under the Advisers Act's code-of-ethics rule (Rule 204A-1): employees pre-clear personal trades, report their holdings and transactions, are barred from trading in securities the fund is actively trading, and are subject to blackout windows. The CCO reviews the reports against the fund's trading. We treat the compliance machinery — the code of ethics, the CCO's role, the testing — in [the compliance program and the CCO](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-compliance-program-and-the-cco).

**Material non-public information.** A manager who comes into possession of MNPI — through an expert network, a board seat, a relationship — faces the conflict of using it to the fund's advantage, which is illegal insider trading. The control is an information barrier and a restricted list: securities about which the firm may have MNPI are walled off from trading. This is a conflict between the manager's hunger for an edge and the law, and the policy exists to keep the firm on the right side of it.

**Side letters and preferential terms.** When one large investor negotiates a side letter giving them better fees, more liquidity, or more transparency than other investors in the same fund, a conflict arises between that investor and the rest. The most dangerous version is a *preferential liquidity* term: an investor who can redeem faster than others can get out ahead of them in a crisis, leaving the slower investors holding the illiquid tail. The SEC's private-fund adviser rules have pushed hard on disclosing material preferential terms to all investors, precisely because an undisclosed side letter is a conflict the disadvantaged investors never agreed to. We cover the investor-relationship and disclosure side of this in [governance and the investor relationship](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/governance-and-the-investor-relationship).

**Capacity and the flagship-vs-new-fund conflict.** When a manager launches a second fund or strategy, a conflict opens over *capacity* — whose orders get the limited capacity in a capacity-constrained strategy, and whether the manager is favoring the newer (and possibly higher-fee or more personally-owned) vehicle over the original investors who backed the founder first. The control is, again, a written capacity-allocation policy and disclosure to both sets of investors.

The thread through all of these is the same as the headline four: a conflict is not wrongdoing, but an *unmanaged, undisclosed* conflict is. The structural conflicts are harder than the transactional ones precisely because they do not show up as a single misallocated invoice or a single bad mark; they show up as a pattern, and the control is a policy plus a disclosure plus a CCO who actually reviews.

## Common misconceptions

Several beliefs about valuation and conflicts are not just wrong; they are the exact beliefs that produce enforcement cases. Each of these has been the operative assumption in a real action.

**"I mark my own book, so as long as I'm honest it's fine."** No. Even a scrupulously honest manager who marks their own illiquid book has *removed the control* that would catch a problem, and from the outside an examiner or an allocator cannot tell an honest self-marked book from a dishonest one. The fiduciary standard is not "did you mark it honestly?"; it is "did you build a process that does not depend on your honesty?" An independent administrator and a real valuation committee are not insults to your integrity; they are the structure that lets investors trust the number without having to trust you personally. A founder who insists on marking their own book is, in ODD terms, a founder who has failed before the conversation starts.

**"Soft dollars are basically free research — the broker pays for it."** No. The fund pays for it, through a commission that is higher than bare execution would cost. "Free" research bought with the fund's commissions is research the investors paid for. The 28(e) safe harbor makes it *legal*, but it never makes it free, and it never covers your office rent, your software, or your salaries. Treating soft dollars as free money is exactly the mindset that leads to over-trading and to running operating costs through commissions.

**"Any expense related to the fund can be charged to the fund."** No, and this is the belief that caught Maya. The test is not "is this expense related to the fund?" — almost everything a manager does is in some sense related to the fund. The test is "is this a *fund* operating cost, or is it the cost of running *my business*, and does the LPA actually permit it?" The cost of raising new investors is related to the fund, but it grows the manager's fee base and belongs to the management company. The data subscription is related to the fund, but it is part of running the firm. Relatedness is not the standard; the document and the fund-vs-manager line are.

**"A cross-trade between my own funds is just internal bookkeeping — no harm done."** No. A cross-trade is a real transaction at a real price between two sets of investors, and the manager is on both sides. Without an independent price and proper consent, it is a tool for moving value from one set of investors to another, and it is specifically regulated under Section 206(3). "It's all my funds anyway" is the precise rationalization that turns an efficiency into a fiduciary breach.

**"The SEC doesn't look at expense allocation at small funds — it's too small to matter."** No. Expense allocation is the most common private-fund conflicts enforcement area precisely *because* it is everywhere and easy to prove. The amounts in many cases are modest — tens of thousands of dollars, like Maya's — but the SEC pursues them anyway, because the conflict is a fiduciary breach regardless of size, and because the agency wants the deterrent. A small fund is not below the radar on expense allocation; it is squarely on it.

**"As long as my conflicts are disclosed somewhere in the PPM, I've done my job."** Almost, but not quite. Disclosure is necessary and it is the foundation of the whole regime — but the SEC has been increasingly clear that disclosure must be *full and fair* (specific enough that an investor actually understands the conflict and how it is managed), and that some conflicts must be *managed*, not merely disclosed. A boilerplate "the manager may have conflicts of interest" buried on page 40 does not cover an undisclosed preferential redemption term or a misallocated expense. Disclose specifically, and control what you disclose.

## How it plays out in the real world

The pattern in real enforcement is remarkably consistent, and it is worth seeing the categories concretely, because each one maps to a control you can build.

The largest category, year after year, is **expense allocation and undisclosed fees**. The SEC's private-fund enforcement record is thick with cases where a manager charged the fund for costs that were the management company's — back-office salaries, the manager's own compliance and overhead, travel, technology — or charged "shared" expenses entirely to the fund without a reasonable allocation, or collected fees (monitoring fees, accelerated fees in hybrid vehicles, undisclosed mark-ups) that were not adequately disclosed. The remedy in these cases is almost always the same: disgorgement of the improperly charged amounts (the fund gets paid back), a civil penalty on top, and a censure. The dollar amounts are frequently modest — which is the point. The agency is not chasing the size; it is enforcing the line, and a founder who treats the fund as a slush fund for firm overhead will eventually meet an examiner who reads the invoices against the LPA.

**Valuation** is the category with the spectacular failures. The canonical lesson is Madoff: no independent administrator, self-struck NAVs, a tiny unknown auditor, self-custody — every valuation control simply absent, which is what let a pure fabrication run for years. Beyond outright fraud, the more common valuation enforcement is subtler: a manager who marked illiquid Level 3 positions aggressively to inflate reported returns and the incentive fee, overrode or pressured the administrator, or ran a valuation "committee" that rubber-stamped the PM's marks. The control that defeats all of these is the chain in Figure 2 — written policy, real committee with members whose pay does not move with the mark, and an independent administrator who actually resists.

**Cross-trades and allocation** show up as cases where a manager crossed positions between funds at prices that favored one fund, or allocated good fills to higher-fee or proprietary accounts and bad fills to clients. The remedy, again, is disgorgement plus penalty, and the control is the pre-trade pro-rata allocation policy and the independent-price, consent-gated cross-trade workflow.

And the structural backdrop to all of it is the law itself: the **Investment Advisers Act** makes the adviser a fiduciary, and the SEC's **examination priorities** for private-fund advisers have for years featured conflicts of interest, fees and expenses, valuation, and undisclosed preferential terms at the very top of the list. The **private-fund adviser rules** the SEC has pursued in recent years push specifically on expense disclosure, preferential-treatment disclosure, and the prohibition on certain charges without disclosure or consent. A founder does not have to read every rule to internalize the message, which is the message of this entire post: *wherever your interest and the fund's interest collide, you are a fiduciary, the doubt resolves against you, and the only safe path is a written policy, an independent check, full disclosure, and a documented method an examiner can re-trace.*

The reason this matters more than almost any single trade is the asymmetry of the downside. A bad trade costs the fund some money and the manager some reputation, and the fund moves on. A conflicts failure — a misallocated expense, an inflated mark, a self-dealt cross — costs the manager disgorgement, a penalty, a public censure, and in the worst cases an industry bar that ends the career. Worse, it is the kind of failure that an allocator's operational due diligence is specifically built to find *before* they invest, and the kind that, once found after they invest, triggers the redemption that can kill a fund. The governance in this post is not compliance overhead grafted onto the real business; in the calculus of what actually kills funds — an ops failure, a redemption cascade, a compliance blow-up — it *is* the real business of keeping the promise credible enough to survive.

## When this matters / Further reading

Valuation and conflicts governance matters the day you run a single dollar of someone else's money, but it becomes existential the day you run *more than one pool* of it, hold *anything* you have to mark yourself, or write a check that could plausibly be the fund's or the management company's. If your book is all liquid equities, one fund, and a clean expense clause, the conflicts are light — but you still need the policy, because the allocator's ODD will ask for it and the examiner will test it. The moment you add an illiquid sleeve, a second vehicle, a soft-dollar arrangement, or a side letter, the governance stops being a formality and becomes the thing that keeps you out of an enforcement order.

The founder's checklist that falls out of this post is short and unforgiving. Have an **independent administrator** strike the NAV and never override it alone. Run a **real valuation committee** with members whose pay does not move with the marks, and keep its minutes. Write a **pre-trade allocation policy** (pro-rata by default) and a **cross-trade workflow** gated on an independent price and consent. Confine **soft dollars** to genuine 28(e) research, split mixed-use costs, and disclose the arrangement. Above all, write an **expense-allocation policy**, read your own LPA expense clause as if you were the examiner, and have the CCO check the invoices against it on a cadence — because expense allocation is the single most common place founders get caught, and it is entirely avoidable.
