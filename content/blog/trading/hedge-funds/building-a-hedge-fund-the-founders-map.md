> **TL;DR** — A hedge fund is not a trading strategy; it is a *business* that sells an aligned promise — to compound capital with skill, inside a structure that protects the investor. Building one is a seven-stage journey from "I have an edge" to "USD 1 billion and still alive."
>
> - The founder's whole job reduces to three things: keep the promise **credible** (performance), the structure **sound** (operations + compliance), and the capital **sticky** (investors) — long enough to compound.

## Foundations: what a hedge fund actually is, and the three-pillar promise

Strip away the mystique and a hedge fund is a small, deliberately exclusive arrangement. A manager pools money from a limited set of wealthy and institutional investors and invests it under a contract that says, in effect: *I will try to grow this, I will take a slice of what I make, and here are the rules that protect you while I do it.* Every term below is a piece of that arrangement. Read this section slowly; the rest of the series assumes you own these words.

**General partner (GP) versus limited partner (LP).** A hedge fund is usually a *limited partnership*. The **general partner** is the entity that runs it and bears unlimited liability — in practice, this is a tiny shell controlled by the founder, not the founder personally. The **limited partners** are the investors: they put in capital, they share in the gains and losses, but their liability is *limited* to what they invested. The GP makes the decisions; the LPs supply the money. When you "start a hedge fund," what you really own is the management company sitting behind the GP — and that distinction, which we will return to repeatedly, is the single most misunderstood thing about the whole business.

**Pooled, private capital.** The money is *pooled* — every investor's dollars sit in one commingled vehicle and share one net asset value per share. And it is *private*: a hedge fund does not sell to the general public the way a mutual fund does. It relies on exemptions under US securities law that let it skip the heavy public-fund registration *as long as* it only takes money from sophisticated investors and does not advertise broadly. We will unpack those exemptions — Reg D, the 3(c)(1) and 3(c)(7) carve-outs, accredited investors, qualified purchasers — in the formation track. For now: "private" is why a hedge fund can charge what it charges and trade how it trades.

**AUM — assets under management.** The total dollar value of capital the manager runs. It is the number that determines almost everything else: your fee revenue, your credibility with allocators, whether you clear regulatory thresholds, and whether you can afford an institutional-grade operation. When people say a fund is "USD 500 million," they mean its AUM.

**NAV — net asset value.** The value of the fund's holdings minus its liabilities, usually expressed *per share* (or per unit). NAV is the fund's heartbeat: it is struck (calculated) on a regular cycle — typically monthly — by an *independent administrator*, and it is the number investors subscribe and redeem at. Because the incentive fee is charged on the *growth* of NAV, the integrity of how NAV is calculated is not an accounting detail; it is the foundation of trust. The single biggest fraud in the industry's history, Madoff, was at root a NAV fabricated by a manager who self-administered and self-priced.

**The two fees.** This is how the manager gets paid, and it is the famous "**2 and 20**":

- The **management fee** — historically 2% of AUM per year (now compressed to roughly 1.3–1.5% on average, per HFR fee data) — is charged regardless of performance. It is meant to *keep the lights on*: salaries, rent, data, the service-provider stack. It is not where founders get rich; it is the runway that lets the business exist.
- The **incentive fee** (also called the performance fee) — historically 20% of profits (now nearer 15–17% on average) — is the real upside. It is the manager's share of the gains. Crucially, it is an *option*: it pays handsomely when you make money and pays exactly *zero* when you do not. We will see how brutal that asymmetry becomes in a drawdown.

**High-water mark.** The protective rule attached to the incentive fee. The manager earns the performance fee only on gains *above the previous peak NAV*. If the fund drops 20% and then climbs, the manager collects no incentive fee until the NAV has clawed all the way back above its old high. This is what stops a manager from being paid twice for the same dollar — and it is what can starve a firm of cash for years after a bad stretch. Some funds layer a **hurdle rate** on top: the incentive fee applies only to returns *above* a benchmark (the cash rate, or a fixed percentage like 5%), so the manager is paid for skill, not for the risk-free return an investor could have earned in Treasury bills. And the incentive fee **crystallizes** — actually locks in and gets paid — on a schedule, usually annually. Until it crystallizes, the "fee" the manager has earned on paper can still evaporate if the fund gives the gains back before year-end.

**Accredited investor and qualified purchaser.** Because a hedge fund is private, it can only take money from investors the law deems sophisticated enough to fend for themselves. An **accredited investor** (the lower bar) is, broadly, an individual with income over USD 200,000 (USD 300,000 joint) for two years, or a net worth over USD 1M excluding their primary residence. A **qualified purchaser** (the higher bar) holds at least USD 5M in investments. Which exemption your fund uses — and therefore which investors you can take — is a formation decision with permanent consequences: it caps how many investors you can have and shapes who you can ever raise from. This is the first place where a structural choice quietly fences in your future.

**The service-provider stack.** A credible fund cannot be run by the manager alone, and not only because of workload — because *independence* is the point. An **administrator** strikes the NAV and keeps the books, independently of the manager who would otherwise be grading his own homework. An **auditor** signs off on the annual financials. A **prime broker** finances the book, lends the shares you need to short, holds the assets, and introduces you to capital. A **compliance consultant** and outside **legal counsel** keep you on the right side of the rules. Their *names* are part of your credibility: an allocator's first question is often not "what is your Sharpe?" but "who is your administrator and who is your auditor?" — because a real third party in those seats is what separates a fund from a Madoff.

**"The promise."** Put those pieces together and a hedge fund is a *promise with a structure around it*. The manager promises to compound the LPs' capital with skill. The fee aligns the manager with that goal — the manager only makes the big money if the LPs do. And the structure — the documents, the administrator, the auditor, the prime broker, the compliance program — exists to make sure the manager *can't* break the promise even if tempted, and to *prove* to a wary investor that he won't.

One more piece makes the promise *aligned* rather than merely transactional: the founder's own money. Allocators expect the manager to have a meaningful share of personal net worth invested in the fund alongside the LPs — "skin in the game," or what is sometimes called the GP commitment. A manager who eats their own cooking feels every drawdown the way the investors do, which is the strongest possible alignment a fee schedule cannot buy. Combined with the incentive fee (the manager only makes the big money if the LPs do) and the high-water mark (the manager is not paid twice for recovering losses), the structure is engineered so that the manager's incentives and the investor's interests point in the same direction. That alignment is the *reason* sophisticated investors tolerate the high fees: they are paying for a manager whose wealth rises and falls with theirs, inside a structure that makes it hard to profit while the investor loses. Break the alignment — charge fees on assets you are not really managing, take risk the LPs did not sign up for, pay yourself in a down year — and you have broken the promise, even if no law was technically violated.

That promise rests on three pillars, and Figure 2 is the mental model for the entire series.

![A graph showing the aligned promise of a hedge fund branching into three pillars: credible performance built on a repeatable edge net of fees, sound structure built on operations and compliance, and sticky capital built on investors who stay through a drawdown, all converging on the goal of having time to compound](/imgs/blogs/building-a-hedge-fund-the-founders-map-2.png)

- **Credible performance.** Not just returns — *credible* returns: an edge that is repeatable, measured net of fees, free of survivorship bias, and verifiable by a third party. An allocator does not trust your word; she trusts your audited track record and your administrator's NAV.
- **Sound structure.** The operational and legal machine that processes every trade, strikes the NAV independently, segregates duties, controls cash movement, and keeps you compliant. This is the pillar founders most underrate and the one that kills the most funds.
- **Sticky capital.** Investors who stay — through a flat year, through a drawdown, through a scary headline. Capital that runs at the first wobble is worse than no capital, because it forces you to sell your best positions at the worst time. The terms in your documents (lock-ups, gates, notice periods) are tools to make capital sticky; investor trust is what makes those tools unnecessary.

Hold all three at once and you buy the only thing that matters: *time to compound*. A 15% net return is a miracle of wealth creation if you can run it for twenty years and a footnote if you blow up in year three. The founder's entire job is to keep all three pillars standing long enough for compounding to do its work. Every chapter of this series is, in the end, about one pillar.

It helps to see what a hedge fund is *not*, because the contrasts sharpen the definition. A **mutual fund** is the opposite in almost every dimension: it is public, anyone can buy it, it is heavily regulated under the Investment Company Act, it cannot use much leverage or short-selling, and it charges a flat fee with no performance cut. A **private-equity** or **venture** fund pools private capital like a hedge fund, but it locks that capital up for years and buys whole companies or stakes in startups rather than trading liquid markets; its fee crystallizes on realized exits, not annual NAV. A **proprietary trading firm** trades the partners' own money, so it has no outside investors, no LPs, no incentive fee, and none of the investor-protection machinery — which is exactly why a prop trader who wants to run *other people's* money has to learn everything in this series. And a **separately managed account (SMA)** gives one investor their own segregated account that the manager trades under a mandate; it is a common *bridge* to a fund, but it is not a pooled vehicle and it does not by itself make you a fund manager. The hedge fund sits in a specific box: pooled, private, lightly regulated, performance-fee-driven, liquid-market, and sold only to the sophisticated. Every term we just defined is a side of that box.

The three pillars are not independent, either — they reinforce or undermine each other, which is why a founder cannot simply be "great at one." Credible performance *makes capital sticky*: investors who believe in the edge ride out a drawdown instead of redeeming into it. Sound structure *makes performance credible*: an independent administrator and a clean audit are what turn "trust me, the returns are real" into "here is the third-party proof." And sticky capital *protects performance*: a stable capital base means you never have to liquidate good positions to meet a redemption, so the structure does not force the strategy into a fire sale. A weakness in any one pillar leaks into the other two. That coupling is why funds rarely die of a single cause; they die of a *cascade* — a drawdown that shakes capital that forces selling that deepens the drawdown — and why the founder has to defend all three at once rather than optimizing the one they find most interesting.

#### Worked example: where the 2-and-20 money actually comes from

Maya's Meridian Fund launches with USD 100M of AUM and charges a fairly standard 1.5% management fee and a 20% incentive fee. Suppose she returns a gross 12% in year one, so the fund makes USD 12M of gains.

- **Management-fee revenue:** 1.5% × USD 100M = **\$1,500,000**. This arrives whether she is up or down. It pays the team and the bills.
- **Incentive-fee revenue:** 20% × USD 12M of gains = **\$2,400,000**. This is the upside — and it only exists because the fund made money.

In a *good* year, the incentive fee (\$2.4M) dwarfs the management fee (\$1.5M): total fee revenue is **\$3.9M**. But notice the dependency. If Maya returns 0% instead of 12%, the incentive fee is **\$0** and her entire firm runs on the \$1.5M management fee alone. The management fee is the floor that keeps her alive; the incentive fee is the elevator that makes her rich — and the elevator only runs when the market cooperates.

*The management fee buys survival; the incentive fee buys wealth — and a founder who confuses the two builds a cost base that only a great year can support.*

## The founder's journey: seven stages

The series is organized as seven tracks, mirroring the seven stages in Figure 1. Here is the whole arc in one page, so you always know where any given post sits in the journey.

**Stage one — what a hedge fund actually is.** Before you can build one, you have to see it clearly as a legal-economic object: pooled private capital, the GP/LP split, the two fees, the alignment, and how it differs from a mutual fund, a private-equity fund, a venture fund, a prop shop, or a separately managed account. This stage is also where you take the honest pre-launch test — *is my edge real, repeatable, and large enough to matter net of fees and costs?* — and decide whether you are personally and financially ready to leave the seat. (Start with [what a hedge fund actually is](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/what-a-hedge-fund-actually-is).)

**Stage two — legal and structural formation.** This is where the abstract promise becomes a set of contracts and entities. You build the three-entity skeleton (management company, fund, investment manager), decide between a domestic vehicle and an offshore master-feeder, draft the document stack (PPM, LPA, subscription, IMA), set your fees and liquidity terms, assemble your service-provider stack, and figure out which regulators you answer to. The structure you build here quietly determines your fate years later. (See [the fund structure: management company versus fund](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-fund-structure-management-company-vs-fund).)

**Stage three — raising capital.** The hardest part, and the one traders most underestimate. There is a chicken-and-egg trap: no track record means no capital, and no capital means no track record. This stage covers the friends-and-family round, the economics of taking seed capital, the landscape of who actually allocates (funds-of-funds, pensions, endowments, family offices, sovereign wealth), the pitch toolkit, and the separate, veto-wielding operational due-diligence process that can kill a great-returns fund stone dead.

**Stage four — building the operation.** The machine. What happens to a trade from idea to settled-and-reconciled across the front, middle, and back office; the technology stack (OMS, risk, market data); the prime-brokerage relationship; the NAV-and-administrator process; cash, treasury, and counterparty risk; and the order in which you hire — COO and CFO before more PMs, almost always.

**Stage five — risk, compliance, and governance.** Staying alive and legal. Risk as an *independent* function rather than a spreadsheet the PM owns; the compliance program and the chief compliance officer; valuation and conflicts governance; the SEC Marketing Rule; operational, cyber, and key-person risk; and the governance structures (the offshore board, the LP advisory committee) that act as controls, not decorations.

**Stage six — maintaining and growing the fund.** The long game. Surviving the J-curve until the management fee covers the lights; the high-water-mark trap in a drawdown; investor relations and retention; the capacity-versus-alpha trade-off as you scale; and evolving the business itself — new funds, permanent capital, even selling a stake in your management company. (The survival math lives in [surviving the J-curve and break-even AUM](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/surviving-the-j-curve-break-even-aum).)

**Stage seven — failure, crisis, and the endgame.** How it ends. The failure taxonomy (blow-ups, fraud, style drift, the redemption death-spiral, slow death by fee compression); the crisis playbook from 2008 and 2020; winding down gracefully and returning capital; and a capstone that walks a single fictional fund from thesis to USD 1B, linking every prior post. (The capstone is [the founder's playbook from launch to one billion](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-founders-playbook-launch-to-one-billion).)

If you are reading this as someone who can already run money — a PM, a senior researcher, a prop or quant trader — you have stage one's *performance* pillar mostly solved. This series is the other six stages: everything between "I have an edge" and "I have a business that survives." It is the natural continuation of the careers question, [from senior to PM or starting your own](/blog/trading/quant-careers/from-senior-to-pm-or-starting-your-own) — the moment you stop asking "how do I get hired and promoted?" and start asking "how do I build the thing that hires people?"

The stages are roughly sequential but not strictly so, and the dependencies are worth naming. Formation (stage two) and raising (stage three) interlock: you cannot finalize your fee terms and your liquidity terms without knowing who your anchor investors are and what they will demand, and you cannot pitch credibly without a structure to pitch. Operations (stage four) and compliance (stage five) are two faces of the same machine — the segregation of duties that operations needs is exactly what the compliance program and the auditor demand — so they get built together, not one after the other. And the survival math of stage six is not a "later" concern; it is the constraint that shapes every earlier decision, because the break-even AUM you computed determines how much runway you need before you launch at all. A founder who treats the seven stages as a checklist to complete in order will be ambushed by the couplings; a founder who treats them as a *system* — where a choice in formation echoes through raising, operations, and survival — builds something that holds together. That systems view is what the capstone, [the founder's playbook from launch to one billion](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-founders-playbook-launch-to-one-billion), walks end to end on a single fictional fund.

Notice, too, where the *weight* of the series sits. A first-time founder expects the hard part to be the investing and is surprised to find it is the raising and the operating. The strategy and the alpha — the part you already know — get a single track here, and even that track views strategy through the *operating* lens (capacity, data needs, headcount, how liquid the book must be), because the market mechanics live in the sibling series. Four of the seven tracks are about structure, capital, operations, and compliance: the parts of the business that have nothing to do with picking positions and everything to do with whether the fund is still standing in three years. That allocation of attention *is* the thesis. If this series spent six tracks on alpha and one on operations, it would be lying to you about where funds die.

## The brutal odds

Here is the part the vendors do not put on the brochure. The hedge fund business is concentrated, attritional, and far less forgiving than the headline returns of the famous funds suggest. The names you know — the multi-billion-dollar funds whose letters get leaked and whose founders give interviews — are a survivorship-biased sample. For every one of them there are dozens of funds that launched with a real edge, ran for two or three years, never crossed break-even, and quietly returned what capital they had left and closed. You do not read about those, which is exactly why the industry looks more lucrative and more durable than it is. Three facts set the stage and correct the picture.

**Most funds are small.** The global industry runs something like USD 4.5–5.0 trillion across roughly 8,000–10,000 funds (HFR, Preqin, and BarclayHedge estimates vary, but they cluster there as of 2026). The mental picture most people have — a hedge fund as a billion-dollar colossus — describes the *exception*, not the rule. The median fund is small; the typical *launch* is smaller still, often in the tens of millions. Figure 3 makes the concentration visible: the great majority of funds by *count* run under USD 100M, while the small minority of funds over USD 1B control the overwhelming share of *assets*.

![A grouped bar chart contrasting the share of funds by count against the share of total industry assets across four size buckets, showing that about 68 percent of funds run under 100 million dollars while funds over 1 billion dollars control roughly 70 percent of all assets](/imgs/blogs/building-a-hedge-fund-the-founders-map-3.png)

In illustrative round numbers (HFR/Preqin-style estimates, as of 2026): roughly **68% of funds run under USD 100M** yet control only about **5% of industry assets**, while the funds *over* USD 100M control roughly **95%** of assets — and the billion-dollar club alone owns something on the order of **70%**. The implication for a founder is stark. The economics that make hedge funds sound lucrative — the fee on huge AUM — accrue to a tiny minority. If you launch, you are almost certainly launching into the small-fund tier, where the management fee barely covers costs and the whole business rides on performance and growth.

**Most funds die — and they die early.** Industry mortality runs at roughly **7–11% of funds liquidating per year**, with spikes in crisis years like 2008 and 2020. Apply attrition like that to a cohort of launches, weight it toward the early years (when a fund has the least cushion and the least track record), and you get the survivorship curve in Figure 5: by year five, roughly *half* of a launch cohort is gone.

**Break-even is around USD 100M.** Because most of your costs are fixed — the administrator, the auditor, the compliance consultant, the legal counsel, the data and technology — your management fee has to cover them before you earn a cent of upside. As we will compute below, an institutional-grade small fund commonly carries fixed costs in the **USD 0.5M–2M per year** range, and covering even USD 1.2M of that from a 1.5% management fee requires about **USD 80M of AUM**. Many allocators and consultants won't even look at a manager below **USD 250M–500M**. So there is a punishing valley: you need scale to be viable, but you need a track record to get the capital that gives you scale, and you have to fund the wait out of your own pocket.

These three facts compound into a single structural reality: the hedge fund business has **negative operating leverage at small size and positive operating leverage at large size**. Below break-even, every dollar of fixed cost is a larger percentage drag the smaller you are, so a sub-scale fund bleeds; above break-even, the same fixed cost spreads over a bigger base and the management fee turns into pure margin. The curve bends sharply right around that USD 80–100M mark. That bend is why the median founder's first two years are an act of *endurance* rather than profit — the goal is not to make money, it is to cross the bend before the runway runs out. It is also why the launch-AUM number on day one matters so much: a fund that launches at USD 75M is one good year from viability, while a fund that launches at USD 15M needs to *quintuple* its assets before the business even works, and assets are exactly what is hardest to gather without a track record. The starting point on the curve quietly sets the odds.

There is one more honest caveat about the costs. The figures above describe an *institutional-grade* fund — the kind that can pass operational due diligence and take pension or endowment money. You can launch far cheaper than that: a friends-and-family fund with a light administrator and a small auditor might run on a few hundred thousand dollars a year. But cheap and institutional are different products. The cheap version cannot raise from the allocators who write the big checks, so it is stuck in the small-fund tier by construction. Part of the founder's strategy is deciding *which* fund to build — and that decision sets the cost base, the break-even, and therefore the runway you must fund before launch.

#### Worked example: the survivorship math of a launch cohort

Suppose 100 funds launch together and the industry's roughly 9%-per-year liquidation rate applied to them, compounding. The probability a given fund is *still alive* after `n` years is approximately `(1 − 0.09)^n`:

- After 1 year: `0.91^1 ≈ 91%` survive.
- After 3 years: `0.91^3 ≈ 75%`.
- After 5 years: `0.91^5 ≈ 62%`.

That is the *gentle* version, applying the average rate evenly. In reality attrition is front-loaded — a launch with USD 30M and no track record is far more fragile than a USD 2B veteran fund — so the realistic launch-cohort curve is steeper early, landing closer to **half gone by year five** (the shape in Figure 5). Either way, the message is the same: surviving is not the base case; it is the achievement.

*Survival is the product, not a given — and because attrition compounds, every year you stay alive raises the odds that you reach the scale where the business finally works.*

![A line chart tracing how many of 100 hedge fund launches are still trading each year, falling from 100 at launch toward about 48 by year five, with the band of failed funds shaded above the survivor curve](/imgs/blogs/building-a-hedge-fund-the-founders-map-5.png)

#### Worked example: the break-even AUM that decides whether you survive

Maya tallies the Meridian Fund's fixed annual run-rate — the costs she pays whether or not she makes money:

- Administrator: \$60,000
- Audit: \$45,000
- Outside legal and compliance: \$90,000
- Technology, market data, and OMS: \$120,000
- A bare-bones salary for one operations hire, plus rent and insurance: \$885,000

That totals roughly **\$1,200,000 per year** of fixed cost. At her 1.5% management fee, the AUM she needs just to break even on those costs is:

`break-even AUM = fixed costs ÷ management-fee rate = \$1,200,000 ÷ 0.015 = \$80,000,000`

So the Meridian Fund must reach **USD 80M of AUM** before the management fee alone covers the lights — before a single dollar of incentive fee, before Maya pays *herself* a real salary, before any of it becomes a business rather than a burn. She launched at USD 32M. She is running at a loss, funding the gap out of her own savings and a thin slice of incentive fee in good months, racing to grow into viability before the runway ends.

*The management fee is a percentage of a number you do not yet have — which is why the first job of a new fund is not to make money trading, but to survive long enough to gather enough assets that the fee covers the fixed cost of existing.*

## The three things that actually kill funds

Ask a new founder what they are afraid of and they will usually say "a bad trade." That fear is misplaced. A single losing position produces a *drawdown*, and a drawdown — painful as it is — is survivable; it is the normal weather of running risk. What actually *kills* funds is structural. Figure 6 lays out the four candidate killers against how *often* they strike and how *fatal* they are, with a named example of each.

![A matrix comparing four ways a hedge fund can fail, scored by how often each happens, how fatal it is, and a named example, showing that a bad trade is usually survivable while operations failures, redemption cascades, and fraud are the terminal events](/imgs/blogs/building-a-hedge-fund-the-founders-map-6.png)

The three that do the real damage are not on most founders' radar.

**An operations or reconciliation failure.** The unglamorous machine breaks. A trade is booked wrong and nobody catches it because the same person who placed it also reconciles it. Cash moves on a single signature with no second pair of eyes. The valuation policy is vague and a stale price sits on the books for a month. None of this is a market event — and that is exactly why it is so dangerous: it festers quietly until an audit, a redemption, or a fraud exposes it, and by then the damage to investor trust is done. This is why allocators run *operational* due diligence as a separate, veto-wielding process: a fund with a beautiful track record gets rejected because its cash controls have a gap.

**A redemption cascade.** This is the death spiral, and it is mechanical. A fund has a bad stretch; some investors ask for their money back; to pay them, the manager has to sell positions; selling pushes prices down (especially in illiquid or concentrated books), which deepens the drawdown, which scares *more* investors into redeeming, which forces *more* selling. The classic cautionary tale is **LTCM in 1998**, which lost roughly **USD 4.6B in months** as its convergence trades moved against it at 25–30× leverage and the liquidity it counted on simply vanished; the New York Fed had to organize a **USD 3.6B** recapitalization with fourteen banks to stop the contagion. The trigger looked like a trade; the killer was the liquidity-and-leverage structure underneath it.

**A compliance or fraud blow-up.** The regulators arrive, or the auditor finds something, or the manager crosses a line. The two poles here are **Archegos** and **Madoff**. Archegos Capital Management — technically a family office (Bill Hwang) — built **USD 10B+** of hidden leverage through total-return swaps spread across multiple prime brokers who each saw only their slice; when the positions turned, the unwind cost the banks billions (Credit Suisse alone reported around **USD 5.5B**) and Hwang was later convicted of fraud. Madoff was the opposite — not a trading blow-up but a pure **Ponzi**, roughly **USD 65B of fabricated statement value** built on the absence of every control that the structure track of this series will insist on: no independent administrator, a tiny unknown auditor, self-custody, and returns that were suspiciously smooth.

Notice the pattern. **Amaranth** (a USD 6.6B natural-gas loss in roughly a week in 2006 — about 65% of its USD 9B-plus AUM) is the one entry that looks like "a bad trade" — and even there, the real failure was *concentration and capacity*, an entire book riding on one trader's spread bet that the market simply could not absorb when it had to be unwound. The other three are not trades at all. They are failures of the structure and the controls. That is the thesis of this whole series in one figure: **treat operations, compliance, and investor trust as first-class as alpha, because that is where funds actually die.**

Why does the instinct point the wrong way? Because the bad trade is the *visible* risk — it is what a trader spends every day managing, and it is the part of the business they already control well. The structural killers are *invisible* until they fire. An ops gap does not show up in the P&L; it shows up the day an auditor or an allocator finds it. A liquidity mismatch does not hurt in a calm market; it is the silent assumption that the assets can be sold at today's price in tomorrow's panic, and it only bills you when correlations go to one and the bids disappear — the dynamic explored from the markets side in [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis). And a compliance line does not seem crossed until the regulator says it was. The founder's discipline, then, is to spend disproportionate attention on the risks that *feel* abstract precisely because they are the ones that are not yet showing up anywhere on the screen.

There is a useful way to read the matrix: move down the rows and the failures get *rarer but more terminal*. A bad trade is common and survivable. An ops failure is frequent and slow-then-sudden — survivable if caught, fatal if it festers. A redemption cascade is the classic spiral, mechanically self-reinforcing once it starts. And fraud is rare but absolute: there is no recovery from a Madoff, only a prison sentence and total loss. The founder's job is not to eliminate the top row — drawdowns are the unavoidable weather of running risk — but to make absolutely sure the bottom three rows never fire, because those are the ones with no path back. You can lose money and recover. You cannot lose your administrator's independence, your investors' trust, or your freedom and recover.

#### Worked example: what a flat year does to the manager's take

It is worth feeling the asymmetry of the incentive fee directly, because it is the mechanism behind the redemption-and-retention squeeze. Take the Meridian Fund at USD 100M again, with a 1.5% management fee and 20% incentive fee, and walk two years.

**Year one — up 12%.** Management fee = 1.5% × USD 100M = \$1.5M. Incentive fee = 20% × USD 12M = \$2.4M. Total firm revenue ≈ **\$3.9M**. Maya pays her team well and banks a strong year. The high-water mark resets to the new, higher NAV.

**Year two — down 10%.** The fund falls from roughly USD 112M back to about USD 101M. Management fee = 1.5% × ~USD 101M ≈ **\$1.5M**. Incentive fee = **\$0** — not only is there no profit this year, but the fund is now *below* its high-water mark, so even next year's gains earn no incentive fee until the NAV climbs back above the year-one peak. Maya's firm now runs on the management fee alone, which barely clears her USD 1.2M fixed cost. And this is precisely the moment her best analyst gets a guaranteed offer from a platform — at exactly the moment she has no incentive-fee pool to match it.

*The incentive fee is an option that pays zero when you are underwater, so a drawdown does not just dent returns — it starves the firm of cash exactly when it most needs to retain the people who generate the returns.*

## Common misconceptions

The mythology around starting a fund is thick, and most of it is sold by people who profit from your launch. Here are the myths that do the most damage, each named and corrected.

**Myth 1: "You just need good returns."** Good returns are the price of *entry*, not the prize. They make the *performance* pillar credible — but a fund with a great track record and a cash-control gap fails operational due diligence and never gets the allocation. A fund with great returns and capital that bolts at the first drawdown gets forced into a fire sale. Returns get you in the room; structure and stickiness keep you alive. The other two pillars are not optional add-ons; they are co-equal.

**Myth 2: "The fund is the business you own."** No — *the management company is the business you own*; the fund is the pool of other people's money. This is not a pedantic distinction. The fees flow up to the management company, the management company is what you can one day sell a stake in, and the legal separation between the entity that holds investor capital and the entity that is *your business* is what protects you and what allocators inspect. Get this wrong and you misunderstand your own balance sheet. (Figure 4 previews the skeleton.)

![A graph of the three-entity skeleton showing the founder owning the management company, which operates the investment manager that manages the fund, with investors putting capital into the fund and fees flowing back up to the founder](/imgs/blogs/building-a-hedge-fund-the-founders-map-4.png)

#### Worked example: what the management company actually nets

The fees are revenue, not profit — the management company is a business with a cost base, and the founder's take is what is left after the bills. Walk the Meridian Fund's strong first year (up 12% on USD 100M) all the way to Maya's pocket.

- **Total fee revenue:** management fee \$1.5M + incentive fee \$2.4M = **\$3.9M**.
- **Less fixed run-rate:** the \$1.2M of administrator, audit, legal/compliance, technology, rent, and the one operations salary.
- **Less the rest of the team:** suppose Maya pays an analyst and a junior trader a combined \$700,000 in salary and bonus.

That leaves the management company with about **\$3.9M − \$1.2M − \$0.7M = \$2.0M** of pre-tax profit in a *good* year — a genuinely good outcome, and the reason founders take the risk. But run the same arithmetic on a *flat* year: revenue collapses to the \$1.5M management fee alone (no incentive fee), the same \$1.9M of costs still has to be paid, and the management company *loses* roughly **\$400,000** — which Maya funds from last year's profit or her own capital. The same fee schedule produces a USD 2M profit or a USD 0.4M loss depending entirely on whether the market cooperated.

*The fund's return is volatile, but the management company's profit is even *more* volatile, because a fixed cost base sits underneath an incentive fee that swings between its full value and zero — which is why founders obsess over the management fee that does not move.*

**Myth 3: "Raising is easy if you can trade."** It is the single most underestimated stage. Allocators are professionally skeptical, slow, and consultant-gated; they want size, an audited track record, institutional operations, and a clean ODD before they write a check — and many won't look at you below several hundred million in AUM. The chicken-and-egg problem (no track record → no capital → no track record) is real, and it is why the *raising* track of this series is the longest and the hardest. Being a brilliant trader does not make you a fundraiser; they are different jobs.

**Myth 4: "2 and 20 makes you rich on day one."** Run the math we just ran. At a USD 32M launch, a 1.5% management fee is **\$480,000 a year** — gross, before you pay the administrator, the auditor, the lawyers, the data bill, and a single salary. The incentive fee only exists if you make money, and it pays *zero* in a flat or down year. Most founders fund a loss for the first couple of years out of their own savings. The "2 and 20 is a license to print money" image describes a USD 5B fund, not a USD 32M one.

**Myth 5: "Small funds fly under the SEC's radar."** They do not. The regulatory framework reaches small managers through the exempt-reporting-adviser regime, state registration, the anti-fraud provisions that apply to *everyone*, and the Marketing Rule that governs what you can say in a pitch. Crossing roughly USD 100M of regulatory AUM generally triggers full SEC registration; below that you are not invisible, just differently regulated. Treating compliance as something that only matters once you are big is exactly the mistake that turns a survivable mistake into a terminal one.

**Myth 6: "Offshore equals shady."** The Cayman feeder in a master-feeder structure is about *tax neutrality* for non-US and tax-exempt investors, not secrecy. A US pension that pays no tax does not want to be dragged into the US tax net by investing through a US partnership; a Cayman vehicle lets it invest cleanly. Offshore is a standard, well-lit part of the institutional structure — the formation track explains exactly why it exists and who it serves.

## How it plays out in the real world

Step back from the myths and look at the actual shape of the industry, because the numbers explain the strategy. The hedge fund world is a **barbell**, and where you sit on it determines almost everything about how you must build.

At one end is the **platform** — the multi-manager "pod shops" and the mega-funds with tens of billions under management. They run dozens or hundreds of portfolio managers under one risk umbrella, with a centralized operation, a deep compliance function, multiple prime brokers, and the scale to absorb the fixed costs of institutional infrastructure as a rounding error. For them, the management fee on huge AUM is a real business by itself, and the operational machine is a moat. (The careers series covers the platform model from the *employee's* side in [the firm archetypes: prop versus HFT versus pod shop versus systematic fund](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund) — this series is about being the *founder* on the other side of that.)

At the other end — where you will almost certainly start — is the **boutique**: a lean shop under USD 100M, the founder wearing three hats, a mini-prime or introducing broker, one administrator, a part-time compliance consultant, and a runway measured in months. Figure 7 puts the two side by side, because the journey of this series is precisely the transformation from the left column to the right.

![A before-and-after comparison contrasting a boutique fund at launch with under 100 million dollars and a lean one-person operation against a billion-dollar franchise with a full executive team, multiple prime brokers, a big-four audit, and fees that fund the firm](/imgs/blogs/building-a-hedge-fund-the-founders-map-7.png)

The boutique's whole problem is the **fixed-cost valley** we computed: institutional-grade infrastructure costs roughly the same whether you run USD 50M or USD 500M, so the small fund pays a punishing fixed cost as a percentage of AUM. A USD 50M fund paying USD 1.2M of fixed costs is burning **2.4% of assets a year** on infrastructure alone — more than its entire management fee. A USD 500M fund paying the same USD 1.2M is burning **0.24%**. That is the entire reason allocators favor scale and the entire reason the barbell exists: the middle is the hardest place to survive.

This is also why **seed capital** exists as an entire sub-industry. A seeder — historically firms like Blackstone's strategic-capital arm, Goldman's Petershill for GP stakes, or specialists like Investcorp and Reservoir — gives a new fund day-one capital, on the order of USD 25M–150M, in exchange for a share of the *management-company economics*, typically 15–25% of revenue or profits, often with a multi-year lock-up. The trade is stark: you sell a fifth of your business *forever* to buy enough day-one AUM to get over the break-even bend and earn a track record. For a founder launching at USD 32M, a USD 75M seed allocation is the difference between a fund that is one year from viability and a fund that is years away — but the price is permanent dilution of the very asset (the management company) that this series keeps insisting is the thing you actually own. The economics of that trade-off are their own post in the raising track; the point here is that the seed industry exists precisely *because* the fixed-cost valley is so deadly that founders will sell a chunk of their company to cross it faster.

It is worth painting what *good* looks like, because the survivorship numbers can read as pure discouragement. The funds that make it tend to share a shape: they launch with enough capital (often via an anchor investor or a seed deal) to be within striking distance of break-even; they put institutional operations and a real service-provider stack in place from day one, so they pass ODD when the bigger checks come knocking; they keep their capital sticky through their first drawdown by over-communicating rather than going quiet; and they grow into the right side of the operating-leverage curve, where the management fee alone funds the firm and every incentive-fee dollar is upside. None of that requires a once-in-a-generation Sharpe. It requires treating the *business* as seriously as the book. That is the difference between the fund in the left column of Figure 7 and the franchise in the right column — and it is entirely within a disciplined founder's control.

The named failures anchor the lesson. **LTCM** was not a stupid fund — it was run by Nobel laureates (Merton and Scholes) and the most respected bond trader of his generation (Meriwether). It died from **leverage and liquidity**, a structural choice, not a dumb trade. **Amaranth** died from **concentration and capacity** — too much of the book in one trader's natural-gas spread. **Archegos** died from **hidden leverage and counterparty blind spots** — every prime broker saw only its own slice of a position that was enormous in aggregate. **Madoff** died from the **absence of independent controls** — no real administrator, no real auditor, no real custody. Four of the most famous failures in the industry, and not one of them is a story about a manager who simply picked the wrong stock. Every one is a story about structure.

That is what it means to say a hedge fund is a *business*. The investing is necessary but not sufficient. The thing that survives to compound is the one whose founder built the boring machine — the operations, the controls, the relationships — as carefully as the trading book. The portfolio/risk discipline that keeps a *book* from blowing up is covered from the strategy side in [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up); this series adds the layer that keeps the *firm* from blowing up.

## When this matters / Further reading

This map matters the moment you stop thinking of your edge as the product and start thinking of the *fund* as the product. If you can run money, you have a head start on exactly one of the three pillars. The other two — sound structure and sticky capital — are learnable, and they are what the rest of this series teaches. The discipline that separates the founders who reach USD 1B from the ones who liquidate in year three is not better trading. It is the willingness to treat operations, compliance, and investor trust as first-class work: to keep the promise credible, the structure sound, and the capital sticky, long enough for compounding to win.
