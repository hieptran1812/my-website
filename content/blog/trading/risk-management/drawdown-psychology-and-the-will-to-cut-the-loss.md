> [!important]
> **The one idea:** the math of cutting a loss is trivial, but the act of cutting is the single hardest thing in trading, because you have to do it while you are deep in a drawdown, at the exact moment your judgment is at its worst and every instinct screams hold.
> - **Loss aversion** makes a realized loss hurt about twice as much as an equal gain feels good, so you hold a losing position purely to avoid the pain of crystallizing the loss, even when the recovery math says cut now.
> - The **high-water-mark trap** turns a drawdown into a perverse option: just below your prior peak, the incentive flips toward *taking more risk* to claw back faster, the so-called gamble for resurrection.
> - **Sunk cost** glues you to a losing trade ("I'm already down, I can't sell now"), and your **decision quality collapses** the deeper you go underwater, so the worst choices get made at the worst times.
> - The only reliable defense is **pre-commitment**: decide the exit while you are calm, write the rule down, automate it, add a cooling-off period, and size small enough that you can sit the drawdown without panic-selling the bottom.
> - It all ties back to the survival spine: a capped −20% drawdown needs only +25% to recover, an uncapped −50% needs +100% and a −90% needs +900%, so **the trader who can cut is the trader who survives to compound.**

There is a moment that every trader, fund manager, and long-term investor eventually meets, and almost nobody is ready for it the first time. You are in a position. It has gone against you. Not catastrophically, not yet, but enough that the number on the screen is red and getting redder. You had a plan. You even, maybe, had a level in mind where you said you would get out. And now you are sitting at that level, or past it, and you are not getting out. You are telling yourself a story instead. The story is some version of *it'll come back*. You have heard, somewhere, that the worst thing you can do is sell at the bottom, and what if this is the bottom, and what if you sell and it rips back the very next day? So you hold. And the position keeps going against you, and the story gets more elaborate, and somewhere along the way you stop being a person executing a plan and become a person hostage to a hope.

This post is about that moment, and about why it is so much harder than it has any right to be. The arithmetic of cutting a loss is the easiest math in all of finance: if a position is below the line where your plan said to exit, you exit. A child could do the calculation. And yet cutting the loss is, by a wide margin, the single hardest act in the entire discipline of trading. The hard part is never the math. The hard part is that you have to perform the act *while you are deep in a drawdown* — which is to say, at precisely the moment when loss aversion, hope, the sunk-cost grip, and the high-water-mark trap have all aligned to push you toward holding, or worse, toward adding. The drawdown is not just a state of your account. It is a state of your *mind*, and it is the state in which your judgment is least trustworthy. That is the cruel design of the whole problem: the decision that matters most has to be made at the moment you are least equipped to make it.

![Two traders starting the same fifteen percent drawdown, one holding and hoping and adding until the account blows through, the other cutting at a written rule and surviving with capital and nerve intact](/imgs/blogs/drawdown-psychology-and-the-will-to-cut-the-loss-1.png)

Figure 1 is the whole post in one image, and it is the picture to carry with you. Two traders. Same starting drawdown — both down 15% at the same moment, on the same kind of position. The trader on the left has no pre-set exit; he feels the loss, refuses to realize it, hopes for the round-trip, and as he nears his old high-water mark he actually *adds size* to claw it back faster, so his risk goes up exactly as his equity falls. The loss compounds, his decisions get worse, and he blows through the account; now he is down 50% and needs a +100% gain just to see even again. The trader on the right made one different choice, and she made it long before this day: she wrote down a maximum-drawdown rule in the calm, and when the position hit her −20% line, the exit fired automatically. The rule acted; the panic didn't. Her loss is capped at −20%, she needs only +25% to recover, and she is still in the game. Same pain, same moment, two endings — and the only difference is whether the exit was decided in advance. Everything below builds that figure from first principles: why the drawdown wrecks your judgment, the specific psychological traps that push you to hold, and the one defense that reliably works.

This is the in-the-moment companion to two earlier posts in the series. [The drawdown and the underwater curve](/blog/trading/risk-management/drawdown-and-the-underwater-curve-the-risk-you-actually-feel) built the *measurement* — what a drawdown is, the underwater curve, max drawdown, time to recover. ## Foundations: the building blocks of cutting a loss

Before we get to the psychology, let's define every term from zero so nothing later rests on a word you'd have to guess at.

**Drawdown.** A **drawdown** is the percentage drop from the highest value your account (or a position) has ever reached, down to where it is now. If your peak was \$120,000 and you are sitting at \$96,000, your drawdown is (96,000 − 120,000) / 120,000 = −20%. By construction it is always zero or negative — it measures only your distance below your best. The full mechanics, the underwater curve, and the statistics live in [the drawdown post](/blog/trading/risk-management/drawdown-and-the-underwater-curve-the-risk-you-actually-feel); here we just need the word.

**High-water mark.** Your **high-water mark** (HWM) is the highest value your account has reached so far — a running maximum that only ratchets up, never down. It's the reference point a drawdown is measured *from*. The term comes from hedge-fund fee contracts, where a manager only earns a performance fee on gains *above* the previous high-water mark, and that fee structure creates a specific perverse incentive in a deep hole that we'll dissect below and that is covered from the manager's seat in [the high-water-mark trap in a drawdown](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-high-water-mark-trap-in-a-drawdown).

**Realizing (or crystallizing) a loss.** A loss is **unrealized** (or "on paper") while you still hold the position — the price has moved against you, but you haven't sold, so the loss is not yet locked in. The moment you sell, the loss is **realized**: it is now a fact, booked, permanent, no longer a story that might still have a happy ending. This distinction is purely psychological — your wealth is exactly the same whether you've sold or not, because a position is worth its current market price regardless. But the *feeling* of an unrealized loss and a realized loss are worlds apart, and that gap is the engine of most bad holding decisions.

**Cutting the loss.** To **cut a loss** is to exit a losing position — to realize the loss deliberately, to stop the bleeding, to free the capital. A **stop-loss** is a pre-set price or level at which you will cut, ideally placed as a resting order so it executes without you. **De-grossing** (or de-risking) is the portfolio-level version: reducing your *overall* exposure — selling down across the book — rather than exiting one trade.

**Loss aversion.** **Loss aversion**, the central finding of Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory, is the empirical fact that losses loom larger than equivalent gains. Across many experiments, the pain of losing \$100 is roughly **twice** as intense as the pleasure of gaining \$100 — the standard estimate of the loss-aversion coefficient is about 2.25. We are not neutral machines weighing expected value; we are creatures who feel losses about twice as hard, and that asymmetry distorts every decision made in the red.

**Sunk cost.** A **sunk cost** is money already spent (or lost) that cannot be recovered no matter what you do next. The **sunk-cost fallacy** is letting that unrecoverable money drive your forward-looking decisions — "I've already lost \$8,000 on this, I can't sell now" — when the rational question is only ever *given where I am right now, is this the best use of this capital going forward?*

**Disposition effect.** The **disposition effect** is the well-documented tendency to *sell winners too early and hold losers too long* — to realize gains quickly (to lock in the good feeling) but defer realizing losses (to avoid the bad one). It is loss aversion expressed as a trading pattern, and it is exactly backwards from what survival requires. It gets its own full treatment in [the behavioral risk tilt post](/blog/trading/risk-management/behavioral-risk-tilt-doubling-down-and-the-disposition-effect); here it's a named cause.

**Gamble for resurrection.** To **gamble for resurrection** is to take *more* risk when you are deep in a hole, in a bid to claw back to even in one swing rather than grind back slowly. It is the behavior the high-water-mark trap incentivizes, and it is how a recoverable drawdown becomes a terminal one.

**Pre-commitment.** **Pre-commitment** is binding your future self to a decision made by your present, calmer self — deciding the exit *now*, in writing, and removing your future, panicking self's ability to override it. It is the single most powerful tool in this entire post, and the playbook at the end is built on it.

With drawdown, high-water mark, realizing a loss, cutting, loss aversion, sunk cost, the disposition effect, gambling for resurrection, and pre-commitment defined, you have the whole vocabulary. Now let's see why these forces combine into a trap.

## Why loss aversion makes you hold the loser

Start with the most basic force, the one underneath everything else. When a position goes against you and you are sitting on an unrealized loss, two completely different questions are competing in your head, and they have different answers.

The first question is the *rational* one: given where this position is right now, is holding it the best use of my capital going forward? This question doesn't care at all what you paid. It looks only at the present — the current price, the current outlook, the current risk — and asks whether you would *buy this position today* at this price with this capital. If the answer is no, you should be out, whether you're up or down, because your entry price is irrelevant to what happens next.

The second question is the *felt* one, and it's the one that actually drives your hand: if I sell now, I have to *realize this loss*, and realizing it will *hurt*. As long as I hold, the loss is just a number on a screen, a story that could still end well. The moment I sell, it becomes a permanent fact about my judgment and my account. So holding is not really a forecast that the position will recover. Holding is **the avoidance of pain.** You are not predicting; you are flinching.

Loss aversion is what makes that flinch so powerful. Because a realized loss hurts about twice as much as an equal gain feels good, the prospect of clicking "sell" and booking the loss is genuinely aversive in a way that holding — which defers the pain indefinitely — is not. And here is the cruelest part of the shape of that pain: it is *steepest near zero*. The value function of prospect theory bends most sharply right at the origin, which means the very first increment of realized loss feels worse, per dollar, than later increments. The first cut feels worst. This is exactly why people who can't cut a small loss find it even harder once the loss is small-but-real, and then somehow *easier* to ignore once it's catastrophic — by the time you're down 60% you've gone numb, and the position has become a thing you no longer look at.

![Loss aversion curve showing the felt urge to hold rising and most steep near zero plotted against the exact recovery gain needed curve rising toward one hundred percent at minus fifty percent drawdown](/imgs/blogs/drawdown-psychology-and-the-will-to-cut-the-loss-2.png)

Figure 2 puts the two forces on one chart, and the tension is the whole point. The red curve is the felt pain of realizing the loss — the loss-aversion urge to hold — modeled with the standard prospect-theory value function (loss-aversion coefficient 2.25). Notice it is steepest right near zero: the first cut feels worst. The amber curve is the *exact* recovery math from the series spine — the gain you'll need to get back to even from a drawdown of depth `d`, which is `g = d / (1 − d)`. At −50% it crosses +100%: hold a half-loss and you owe a double to recover. The two curves are pulling in opposite directions, and the gap only widens as you hold. Loss aversion says hold; the recovery math says cut, and cut *early*, because every extra increment you ride down makes the climb back disproportionately steeper.

#### Worked example: holding a loser hoping to get back to even

You have a \$100,000 account and a single position that is now down 20% — it's worth \$80,000 and you have an \$20,000 unrealized loss. Your strategy's honest forward-looking expectation for this position from here is roughly flat to slightly negative; the thesis that made you buy it has broken. Two paths.

**Path A — you cut.** You sell, realize the \$20,000 loss, and now hold \$80,000 in cash. To get back to your old \$100,000 high-water mark, you need to make \$20,000 on \$80,000, which is a gain of 20,000 / 80,000 = **+25%**. That is a real climb, but it's an achievable one on a fresh, full \$80,000 of capital redeployed into something with a live edge.

**Path B — you hold, hoping for the round-trip.** You keep the position. Because the thesis is broken, it drifts further: down another 20% of the *remaining* value, so \$80,000 × (1 − 0.20) = \$64,000. Your drawdown is now (64,000 − 100,000) / 100,000 = **−36%**. To recover from here you need to make 36,000 / 64,000 = **+56%** — and you need to make it *on the very position that just demonstrated it has no edge*. You didn't avoid the loss by holding; you deepened it, and you more than doubled the gain you now need.

The decisive number is the comparison: cutting at −20% left you needing +25% on fresh capital; holding for the hoped-for round-trip left you needing +56% on dead capital. *Holding to avoid the pain of a small realized loss is how you manufacture a large one — the recovery math punishes every extra inch you ride down, and it punishes it on exactly the position least able to earn it back.*

The second-order effect here is the one that really compounds the damage: while your capital is trapped in a broken position you're holding "until it comes back," it is not available for the next opportunity that *does* have an edge. The cost of not cutting is not only the deeper loss — it's the entire return you forgo on every trade you couldn't take because your money was tied up nursing a loser. That opportunity cost is invisible (it never shows up as a line item), which is exactly why it's so easy to ignore and so expensive to incur.

There's a third-order effect that's subtler still, and it's about *attention* rather than capital. A losing position you're refusing to cut doesn't just trap your money — it traps your *focus*. You check it constantly. You read the news for it differently, hunting for the bit of good news that confirms the round-trip is coming. It colors your mood, which colors every *other* decision you make that day. A single un-cut loser can quietly degrade the quality of an entire book, because the trader running it is no longer thinking clearly about anything — they're managing a wound. Cutting the loss isn't only about freeing capital; it's about freeing the part of your mind that the open loss has hijacked, so you can go back to making good decisions on everything else.

And there's a specific cognitive distortion worth naming because it does so much of the holding for you: the **round-trip fallacy** — the belief that a position "owes" you a return to your entry price, that getting back to even is a natural resting state the market is somehow obliged to deliver. It isn't. The price has no memory of what you paid; the move back to your entry is exactly as likely (or unlikely) as any other move of the same size, conditioned only on the current outlook. A position down 30% is not "due" a +43% recovery any more than a fresh position is "due" a +43% gain — and yet the round-trip fallacy makes the first feel inevitable and the second feel implausible, when forward, they are the *same trade*. Believing the loser will round-trip is just sunk cost and the disposition effect wearing the costume of a forecast.

## The high-water-mark trap: the incentive to gamble for resurrection

Loss aversion explains why you *hold*. The high-water-mark trap explains something more dangerous — why, near your prior peak, you might *add risk*, deliberately, in a bid to claw back fast. This is the gamble for resurrection, and it has a precise structure that makes it almost irresistible at exactly the wrong time.

Here's the mechanism. Take a fund manager who earns a 20% performance fee, but — as is standard — only on gains *above* the previous high-water mark. Now put that manager 20% below the high-water mark, in a real drawdown. What does their fee payoff look like as a function of how much risk they take with the remaining book? It's an *option*. The downside of a big swing-for-the-fences bet is, in fee terms, already mostly gone — the manager earns no performance fee whether they end the year down 20% or down 40%, because both are below the high-water mark, so the *additional* downside costs them nothing in fees. But the upside is all theirs: if a wild bet happens to pay off and vaults the NAV back over the high-water mark, the fee meter starts running again on every dollar above it. Limited downside, unlimited upside — that is the payoff of a call option, and a call option is *worth more when volatility is higher*. So the rational move, for the manager's own fee, is to take *more* risk, not less, precisely when they're underwater. The investor pays for that gamble; the manager pockets the convexity.

![The high-water-mark trap payoff showing the manager expected performance fee rising as more risk is taken near the prior peak while the investor expected wealth falls, a call-option-like incentive to gamble for resurrection](/imgs/blogs/drawdown-psychology-and-the-will-to-cut-the-loss-3.png)

Figure 3 computes that payoff explicitly. It puts a manager 20% below their high-water mark, then sweeps the amount of *extra* risk (volatility) they take on the remaining book, and for each risk level it evaluates the expected performance fee over a seeded ensemble of year-end outcomes. The amber curve — the manager's expected fee — *rises* as they take more risk: the call-option payoff at the high-water mark rewards volatility. The red curve — the investor's expected wealth, net of fee — *falls*. The two move in opposite directions, and that divergence is the trap in one picture: the person making the decision is paid to gamble, and the person whose money it is foots the bill. You do not need to be a fee-earning manager to feel a version of this. Any trader near their old peak feels the pull to "just get back to even" in one good trade — to size up, to press, to recover the lost ground in a single swing rather than grind. That pull is the same convexity acting on your psychology rather than your fee schedule.

#### Worked example: the high-water-mark gamble on a \$10,000,000 book

You manage a \$10,000,000 book and you're down to \$8,000,000 — a 20% drawdown, sitting \$2,000,000 below your high-water mark. You earn 20% of gains above the high-water mark. Year-end is approaching. Compare two choices.

**The disciplined book.** You keep risk normal — say a modest positive edge that gives you a roughly 60% chance of clawing back to \$9,000,000 (still below the mark, no fee) and a 40% chance of slipping to \$7,500,000. Your expected performance fee is essentially **\$0**, because you're below the high-water mark in both outcomes. Painful, but your investors' expected capital is preserved around \$8,400,000, and you live to earn fees next year once you grind back.

**The gamble for resurrection.** You double your risk to try to vault back over \$10,000,000 in one shot. Say this gives a 35% chance of finishing at \$11,000,000 and a 65% chance of finishing at \$5,500,000. Your expected *fee*: there's a 35% chance you clear the mark by \$1,000,000, on which you earn 20% = \$200,000, so your expected fee is 0.35 × \$200,000 = **\$70,000**. Your investors' expected capital, though, is now 0.35 × \$11,000,000 + 0.65 × \$5,500,000 = \$3,850,000 + \$3,575,000 = **\$7,425,000** before fees — about \$1,000,000 *worse* than the disciplined path.

The arithmetic is stark: the gamble adds \$70,000 of expected fee *for you* while destroying roughly \$1,000,000 of expected wealth *for your investors*. *The high-water mark turns a drawdown into a call option on someone else's money, and the deeper you are, the more the option pays you to swing — which is exactly why a written, pre-committed risk limit has to bind the manager's hands before the drawdown ever arrives.* The firm-level governance that exists to stop this — performance-fee clawbacks, hard risk limits, an independent risk seat — is the subject of [the high-water-mark trap from the GP seat](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-high-water-mark-trap-in-a-drawdown); here the lesson is personal: the instinct to size up to get back to even is not insight, it's the trap talking.

## The sunk-cost grip and the breaking thesis

There's a third force, quieter than the other two but just as adhesive: sunk cost. Once you're down meaningfully on a position, a new and entirely irrational consideration creeps into your decision — *the money you've already lost*. "I can't sell now, I'm already down \$15,000." "If I sell here I've made the loss real and wasted everything I've put into this." The lost \$15,000 starts steering the wheel, even though it is, by definition, gone regardless of what you do next.

The correct frame is brutally simple and worth tattooing somewhere: **your entry price is irrelevant to every decision from here forward.** The only question that should ever drive a hold-or-sell choice is the one from earlier — *would I buy this position, at this price, with this capital, today?* If yes, hold (or even add, but for the *right* reason — a live edge, not a hope). If no, sell, and the fact that you're down doesn't change the answer by a single dollar. The market does not know or care what you paid. Your future returns depend only on the current price and the forward outlook, never on your cost basis. The cost basis matters for your taxes and for your ego, and for nothing else.

Sunk cost is especially vicious when combined with a *breaking thesis*. The honest reason to be in any trade is a thesis — a reason you expect it to work. When a position goes deep against you, very often that move *is information*: it's the market telling you the thesis is wrong, or early, or already priced. The disciplined response is to treat the drawdown as evidence and re-underwrite the trade from scratch. But sunk cost does the opposite: it makes you cling *harder* to the original thesis precisely because abandoning it would mean admitting the loss was a mistake. So you don't update. You defend. You go looking for confirming evidence and explaining away the disconfirming kind. The drawdown that should have triggered a re-think instead triggers a doubling-down — the [disposition effect](/blog/trading/risk-management/behavioral-risk-tilt-doubling-down-and-the-disposition-effect) and motivated reasoning working together to keep you in exactly the trade you should be exiting.

#### Worked example: cut at the rule versus hold and hope on the same path

Take a single losing position and run it two ways on a \$100,000 account. The position is in a regime that mostly grinds down — the thesis has quietly broken — and we'll follow the *same* price path both times. The only difference is whether a written exit exists.

**Trader who cuts at the rule.** Before entering, she wrote: *cut at a 20% drawdown, no exceptions.* As the position grinds down, it eventually breaches \$80,000 (−20% from the \$100,000 start). The rule fires — ideally as a resting stop, so it executes without a decision in the moment — and she's flat at \$80,000, with \$80,000 of capital preserved and free to redeploy. Her recovery requirement is +25%, on fresh, full capital, into something with a live edge.

**Trader who holds and hopes.** He had no written rule, or had one and overrode it ("just give it a little more room"). He rides the same path all the way down. By the end of the stretch the position is worth about **\$61,941** — a −38% drawdown. To recover he needs to make 38,059 / 61,941 ≈ **+61%**, on the same broken position, with most of his account's risk budget still consumed by it.

![Same losing equity path drawn two ways, one capped flat at eighty thousand dollars by a written twenty percent rule, the other riding the full path down to about sixty-two thousand dollars](/imgs/blogs/drawdown-psychology-and-the-will-to-cut-the-loss-4.png)

Figure 4 draws both on the identical seeded path. The green line is the cut-at-the-rule trader: it follows the position down to the −20% line, where the rule fires, and then goes flat — the loss is capped at \$80,000 and the capital is preserved. The red line is the hold-and-hope trader: same start, same path, but it rides all the way to about \$61,941. The shaded gap between them is the difference one written rule made on one identical sequence of prices. *The path was the same for both traders — the only variable was a decision made before the pain began, and that single pre-commitment is the entire distance between a −20% setback and a −38% one.* The deeper truth is that this gap *compounds* across a career: it isn't one trade, it's every trade, and a trader who reliably caps the loss and one who reliably rides it end up in radically different places, which we'll see in Figure 7.

## Why the drawdown itself destroys your judgment

Now we arrive at the meta-problem, the thing that makes all of the above so much worse than it looks on paper. Everything we've discussed — resisting loss aversion, ignoring sunk cost, refusing the gamble for resurrection — requires *good judgment*. And the cruelest fact of drawdown psychology is that the drawdown is itself the condition in which your judgment is worst. The decision that matters most has to be made in the mental state least capable of making it well.

What actually happens to a person deep in a drawdown is well documented across stress and decision research, and every piece of it points the same way. **Attention narrows** — under threat, your focus collapses onto the source of the threat (the red number) and you lose the wide view that good risk decisions require. Your **time horizon shortens** — stressed people heavily discount the future and fixate on relieving the present pain, which is precisely the wiring that makes "get back to even *today*" feel urgent. Your **risk appetite flips** — prospect theory's most important second finding is that people are risk-*averse* in the domain of gains but risk-*seeking* in the domain of losses: offered a sure loss versus a gamble that might avoid it, most people take the gamble, even when it's worse on average. That is the gamble for resurrection, baked into human wiring, switched on automatically by being underwater. And **impulse control degrades** — willpower is depletable, stress depletes it, and a long grinding drawdown is a sustained drain on exactly the faculty you need to *not* do the destructive thing at 3 a.m.

There's a physiological layer underneath all of this that's worth taking seriously rather than dismissing as soft. A real, money-losing drawdown is not an intellectual event; it's a *threat*, and the body responds to it the way it responds to threats — elevated cortisol, a narrowed, vigilant attention, a bias toward fast action over slow deliberation. Steve Cooper and John Coates's work on the physiology of risk-takers found measurable hormonal shifts in traders during losing streaks, shifts that push toward exactly the wrong behavior: more impulsivity, distorted risk perception, a kind of learned helplessness in prolonged drawdowns. You are not imagining the fog. The decision feels harder in the hole because, biologically, it *is* harder — your prefrontal, deliberative machinery is partly offline and your fast, threat-driven machinery is in charge. This is not a character flaw to be willed away; it's a predictable physiological state, which is precisely why the answer is architectural rather than motivational.

The time dimension makes it worse in a way the depth dimension doesn't capture. A sharp, fast drawdown is at least *over* quickly — you hit a level, the rule fires, you're out. A slow, grinding drawdown is a different kind of damage: weeks or months of sitting underwater, each day a small fresh drain on willpower, each day another chance for the round-trip fallacy to whisper. It is the *duration* underwater, not just the depth, that breaks people — the trader who could have taken a clean −20% hit in an afternoon instead bleeds out over a quarter, and somewhere in week six, exhausted, makes the catastrophic decision they'd never have made on day one. This is why the time clocks of a drawdown, covered in [the underwater-curve post](/blog/trading/risk-management/drawdown-and-the-underwater-curve-the-risk-you-actually-feel), are a psychological risk and not merely a statistical one.

![Decision quality curve falling steeply as drawdown depth increases, with a green clear-headed zone, an amber danger band where most bad decisions get made, and a red cornered zone deep underwater](/imgs/blogs/drawdown-psychology-and-the-will-to-cut-the-loss-6.png)

Figure 6 makes the shape of the problem explicit. The blue curve is decision quality — 1.0 is your calm, clear-headed self at a new high, and it decays toward 0, the cornered self deep in the hole. The shaded zones tell the story: a green clear-headed band near the top, an amber *danger band* in the middle where most catastrophic decisions actually get made (it's not at the very bottom — by then you've often gone numb; it's in the −10% to −30% stretch where you're hurting badly but still have enough capital left to do real damage), and a red cornered zone where there's no line left to defend. The curve crosses its midpoint near −18%: *by the time you're down less than a fifth, half your judgment is already gone.* The exact shape is illustrative, but the direction is not — loss-domain risk-seeking and stress-driven attentional narrowing are among the most replicated findings in the field. And the implication is the single most important sentence in this post: **because the quality of your decision-making collapses as you go underwater, you cannot rely on making good decisions in the hole. You have to make the decision before you get there.**

#### Worked example: the deciding-in-the-hole tax

Quantify the cost of waiting until you're underwater to decide. Two versions of the same trader, same \$100,000 account, same losing position, same true exit point at −15% where the edge is gone.

**Decides in the calm.** Before the trade, judgment at full strength, she sets a hard −15% stop as a resting order. When the position hits \$85,000, the order fires automatically. Loss realized: \$15,000. Done. Recovery needed: 15,000 / 85,000 ≈ +17.6%. No decision was made *in* the drawdown at all — the calm self made it in advance and the market executed it.

**Decides in the hole.** Same trader, but she'll "watch it and decide." At −15% her decision quality is, per the curve above, already degraded; loss aversion and the sunk-cost grip are active. She decides to give it "a bit more room." The position slides to −30% (\$70,000) before she finally capitulates — at the worst point, in the worst mental state. Loss realized: \$30,000. Recovery needed: 30,000 / 70,000 ≈ +42.9%.

The difference — a \$15,000 realized loss versus a \$30,000 one, +17.6% to recover versus +42.9% — is the **deciding-in-the-hole tax.** It's the price of trying to make a clear-headed decision in a state engineered to prevent one. *You don't pay this tax because you're undisciplined or weak; you pay it because the drawdown literally degrades the machinery you'd use to be disciplined — which is why the entire game is to move the decision out of the hole and into the calm.*

## The only reliable defense: pre-commit the exit before the pain

If you've followed the argument to here, the solution is almost forced. The problem is that the in-drawdown self makes bad decisions. You cannot fix that self in the moment — you've tried, everyone has, willpower is not enough against wiring this old. So you don't try to out-discipline the drawdown in real time. Instead, you take the decision *away* from the in-drawdown self entirely. You decide the exit in advance, while you are calm, and you build enough structure around that decision that your future, panicking self cannot override it. That is pre-commitment, and it is the only thing that reliably works.

![Pre-commitment flow showing decide the rule in the calm, write it down, then automate the exit, add a cooling-off period, and size to survive emotionally, all feeding into the exit firing on rule with capital and nerve intact](/imgs/blogs/drawdown-psychology-and-the-will-to-cut-the-loss-5.png)

Figure 5 lays out the structure. It starts at a new high — the calm, clear-headed moment — where you *decide the rule* (a max-drawdown limit, a de-gross trigger). You *write it down*: a specific number on a one-page risk plan, not a vague intention to "be careful." From the written rule, three reinforcements branch out. You *automate the exit* — a resting stop or a scheduled de-gross — so the rule acts without you, removing the moment-of-pain decision altogether. You add a *cooling-off period* — any override of the rule has to wait 24 to 48 hours, which kills the 3 a.m. impulse to tear the rule up. And you *size to survive emotionally* — small enough that you can actually sit the drawdown without panic-selling the bottom. All three feed the outcome: the exit fires on the rule, the loss is capped, and you're still in the game. Let's take each piece in turn, because the details are where this lives or dies.

**Write the rule in the calm.** The single most important move is temporal: make the decision when you are *not* in pain. At a new high, or before a trade is even on, your judgment is at full strength (the top of the Figure 6 curve). That is the moment to decide, in concrete numbers, what you'll do when things go wrong — *not* a vague "I'll manage the risk," but specific, checkable rules. "I cut any single position at a 20% loss." "If the book draws down 12% from its high, I halve gross exposure." "I never add to a losing position." Write them on one page. The act of writing makes the rule a *commitment*, an external object you can be held to, rather than a private intention that conveniently dissolves under stress. A rule you can't state in one specific sentence with a number in it is not a rule — it's a wish.

**Automate the exit.** A written rule still requires you to *execute* it in the moment, and the moment is exactly when you're compromised. So remove yourself from the loop wherever you can. Place the stop as a resting order with your broker the instant you enter, so it fills mechanically when the level trades — no decision, no hesitation, no story. For portfolio-level rules, schedule the de-gross: when the book hits the drawdown trigger, the reduction happens on a rule, not on a feeling. Automation converts your calm-self decision into an action that your panic-self can't *not* take. This is the deepest reason resting stops beat mental stops: a mental stop is a decision you've promised to make in the worst possible state, and the whole point is that you can't trust yourself to make it.

**Add a cooling-off period.** You can't automate everything, and there are legitimate reasons to occasionally revisit a rule. So protect the rule with friction: make any *override* expensive. The rule is that you can change the plan, but not *right now* — any modification waits a fixed cooling-off period, say 24 to 48 hours. This single mechanism defuses almost all the damage, because the destructive decisions in a drawdown are overwhelmingly *impulsive* — the 3 a.m. "I'll just give it more room," the panicked size-up to get back to even. Force those impulses to survive a day of daylight and most of them evaporate. The cooling-off period is a pre-commitment device aimed specifically at the impulsivity that the degraded-judgment state produces.

There's an old name for this entire family of moves: a **Ulysses contract**, after the sailor who had himself lashed to the mast so he could hear the Sirens without steering the ship onto the rocks. He didn't trust his future self to resist the song — so he removed his future self's ability to act on the temptation, while he was still safely able to decide. Every piece of this playbook is a strand of that rope. The written rule is the decision made on dry land; the resting stop is the knot your future hands can't untie; the cooling-off period is the delay that lets the song fade before you can change course. The framing matters because it reframes the whole problem away from *willpower* — "be more disciplined next time" — and toward *architecture*: build the structure now, while you're the version of yourself who can, so the version who can't never gets the chance to wreck you.

A cheap and powerful addition to the rope is an **accountability partner** — another trader, a risk manager, a colleague — to whom you've shown your written rules and who has standing permission to ask, when you're underwater, "is this the plan, or is this the drawdown talking?" The value isn't the advice; it's that saying your rationalization out loud to another human, who has the written rule in front of them, strips it of its 3 a.m. plausibility. Most drawdown rationalizations cannot survive being spoken to someone who already knows what you said you'd do. This is exactly the function an independent risk seat serves at a fund — covered from the firm's side in [risk management as a business function](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function) — and the solo trader can approximate it with one honest friend.

**Size so you can survive the drawdown emotionally.** Here is the piece almost everyone misses, and it ties straight back to the survival spine. Even a perfect, automated, pre-committed rule fails if your position is so large that the drawdown is *emotionally* unsurvivable — if being down that many dollars makes you tear up the rulebook regardless of what you wrote. The defense is to size *down*: take positions small enough that a normal drawdown is *tolerable*, not just financially but psychologically. The right position size isn't only the one that keeps your risk of ruin acceptable (that's the [position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin](/blog/trading/options-volatility/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading) calculation); it's also the one that lets you *sleep* through the drawdown without doing something stupid. A position you can hold calmly through a −20% move is worth far more than a position twice the size that you'll panic out of at −10%. *Sizing is not just a math problem about ruin; it's a psychology problem about whether you'll be able to follow your own rules when it counts.*

#### Worked example: the compounding payoff of cutting

Now stretch the lens from one trade to a career, because that's where the real money is. Two traders, same \$100,000 start, same strategy with a small positive edge most of the time but occasional deep bad regimes. They run the *identical* sequence of returns. The only difference: one lets losers run (full exposure even in deep drawdowns), the other caps each drawdown at a −20% rule by de-grossing (halving exposure until a new high is made).

Over a long run, the let-it-run trader takes the full force of every bad regime. Each deep drawdown not only loses money, it triggers the recovery asymmetry — a −50% leg needs +100% back, a −70% leg needs +233% back — so the bad stretches do disproportionate, hard-to-undo damage to terminal wealth. The cap-at-the-rule trader dampens exactly those worst legs: by de-grossing into a deep drawdown, she limits how far underwater each episode takes her, so each recovery requires a *smaller* gain and her capital base survives intact to compound on the good stretches.

![Two long-run equity curves on a log scale, the cap-at-the-rule path ending far above the let-losers-run path, showing capping each drawdown compounds into higher terminal wealth](/imgs/blogs/drawdown-psychology-and-the-will-to-cut-the-loss-7.png)

Figure 7 runs exactly that comparison on a seeded sequence over 1,200 periods. The red line lets losers run; it ends at about **\$66,616** — actually *below* the \$100,000 start, because a couple of deep regimes did damage the recovery math couldn't undo. The green line caps each drawdown at the −20% rule; it ends at about **\$100,670**, roughly **1.5×** the let-it-run path. Same returns, same start — the *only* difference is that one trader's drawdowns were capped and the other's weren't. That ratio understates the real-world gap, because it doesn't count the trades the let-it-run trader couldn't take with capital trapped in deep holes. *Cutting the loss is not a defensive tax on your returns; over a career it is one of the largest sources of terminal wealth you have, because it is the mechanism that keeps the recovery asymmetry from compounding against you.* This is the survival spine made concrete: you can only compound if you stay in the game, and capping the drawdown is how you stay in the game.

## Common misconceptions

**"Selling at a loss means locking in the loss — it's better to hold and wait for it to come back."** This is the single most expensive piece of folk wisdom in trading, and it's exactly backwards. Your loss is already real the moment the price moves against you — your wealth is your position's *current* value whether you've clicked sell or not. Holding doesn't undo the loss; it only defers the *feeling* of it while exposing you to a deeper one. The \$100,000 account down to \$80,000 has lost \$20,000 of real wealth right now, sold or not. Holding a broken position to "get back to even" needed +56% versus the +25% you'd need on cut, redeployed capital — holding didn't avoid the loss, it grew it.

**"A good trader has the discipline to cut losses in the moment."** Discipline matters, but framing this as a willpower problem is why so many disciplined people still blow up. The whole point of Figure 6 is that the drawdown *degrades the very faculty you'd use to be disciplined* — attention narrows, impulse control depletes, risk-seeking switches on. Relying on in-the-moment discipline is relying on your weakest self at its weakest moment. The traders who survive aren't the ones with the most willpower in the hole; they're the ones who took the decision out of the hole entirely, with written, automated, pre-committed rules.

**"Adding to a loser is smart — you're averaging down to a better price."** Averaging down can be rational, but *only* if the thesis is intact and you'd genuinely buy more at this price as a fresh decision with a live edge. In a real drawdown that condition is almost never what's actually driving the add. What's driving it is the gamble for resurrection — the high-water-mark convexity and the urge to get back to even fast — dressed up as "averaging down." The tell is the reason: if you're adding because the position is *cheaper* and the edge is *stronger*, fine; if you're adding because you're *down* and want to recover faster, that's the trap, and your risk is rising exactly as your equity falls.

**"My entry price tells me whether to hold or sell."** Your entry price is irrelevant to every forward-looking decision. The market doesn't know what you paid, and your future returns depend only on the current price and the outlook. "I can't sell, I'm down \$15,000 on it" is the sunk-cost fallacy — that \$15,000 is gone regardless. The only valid question is whether you'd buy this position, at this price, with this capital, today. Cost basis matters for taxes and ego, nothing else.

**"A mental stop is the same as a real stop — I'll just sell when it hits the level."** A mental stop is a promise to make a hard decision in the worst possible mental state, and the entire thesis of this post is that you can't trust yourself to keep that promise. A resting stop executes mechanically; a mental stop executes only if your degraded, loss-averse, hoping-for-the-round-trip self chooses to honor it at the exact moment all its instincts say don't. The two are not remotely equivalent. The whole value of a stop is that it removes the in-drawdown decision — and a mental stop puts the decision right back.

**"If I just size up after a drawdown, I can get back to even faster."** This is the gamble for resurrection stated as a plan, and the high-water-mark math (Figure 3, and the \$10,000,000-book example) shows precisely why it destroys wealth: sizing up into a drawdown raises your risk exactly when your capital base is smallest and least able to absorb another leg down. The expected payoff to *you* (especially with a performance fee) can look positive while the expected wealth of the *account* falls hard. The correct response to a deep drawdown is to take *less* risk, not more — de-gross, don't double down.

## How it shows up in real markets

The case for cutting isn't theoretical. The most expensive blow-ups in financial history are, at their core, stories of people who *couldn't cut* — who held, hoped, and added as the drawdown deepened, until the position cut *them*.

**Long-Term Capital Management, August–September 1998.** LTCM is the archetype of the failure to cut. Through 1998, as their convergence trades moved against them in the flight to quality after Russia's default, the fund's positions went deep underwater — and the response, broadly, was not to cut but to *hold and in places add*, in the conviction that the spreads "had to" converge eventually. They were, in effect, gambling for resurrection at industrial scale, with the conviction of Nobel laureates behind the thesis and roughly **25:1** balance-sheet leverage and around **\$1.25 trillion** of gross derivative notional amplifying every inch the market moved against them. The drawdown didn't trigger a re-think; it triggered a doubling-down, exactly as the sunk-cost-plus-broken-thesis dynamic predicts. By September the fund had lost about **\$4.6 billion** of capital in roughly four months and required a roughly **\$3.6 billion** Fed-organized consortium rescue. The lesson is not "their model was wrong" — it's that *even a correct long-run thesis kills you if you can't survive the drawdown to see it pay off*, and they couldn't cut to survive it. The crowded, levered nature of the trade is dissected in [the LTCM case study](/blog/trading/game-theory/case-study-ltcm-1998-the-crowded-genius-trade).

**Amaranth Advisors, September 2006.** Amaranth lost about **\$6.6 billion** — most of it in a single week — on concentrated, levered natural-gas calendar spreads (the March/April spread). As the position moved against the fund, the response was to hold and press a concentrated, illiquid bet rather than cut it down, until the loss became one of the largest single-fund blow-ups on record. It's the same pattern in a different market: a deep drawdown on a concentrated position, met with conviction and size instead of a cut, in a book too illiquid to exit gracefully once the move was underway. The will to cut has to operate *before* the position is so large and so illiquid that cutting is itself catastrophic — which is why position and concentration limits ([the one trade that can end you](/blog/trading/risk-management/concentration-and-position-limits-the-one-trade-that-can-end-you)) and the discipline of this post are two halves of the same defense.

**Archegos Capital Management, March 2021.** Archegos held enormous, swap-financed, concentrated single-stock positions — leverage on the order of **5x or more** via total-return swaps, hidden from each prime broker. When the underlying names began to fall, the fund was deep in a drawdown on positions it could not quietly exit, and the unwind cascaded into roughly **\$10 billion** of aggregate losses for its prime brokers (Credit Suisse alone took about **\$5.5 billion**). Archegos is what happens when there is no functioning cut-and-de-gross discipline at all and the leverage is concentrated and opaque: the drawdown doesn't get capped, it gets *amplified* by forced selling, and the gamble-for-resurrection structure plays out as a fire sale.

**Volmageddon, February 5, 2018.** The short-volatility trade — selling vol for the carry — is a slow drip of profit that, in a drawdown, turns into a cliff. When the VIX roughly doubled in a single day (about **17.3 to 37.3**), the inverse-VIX product XIV lost about **96%** of its value after the close and was terminated. The holders who'd been collecting the carry for years faced the cut-or-hold decision in the worst possible conditions — a violent, reflexive move with no time to deliberate — and the product's own mechanical rebalance gave them no chance to cut. It's the extreme case of why pre-commitment and automation matter: when the move is fast enough, there is *no* in-the-moment decision to be made, and only the rule you set in advance protects you. The full mechanics are in [the Volmageddon case study](/blog/trading/options-volatility/case-study-volmageddon-2018-and-the-short-vol-blowup).

The thread through all four is the same. None of these failures were primarily failures of analysis. They were failures of the *will to cut* — of people, often brilliant people, who got deep into a drawdown and held, hoped, and pressed instead of cutting and de-grossing, because every force this post described was acting on them at once. The defense, in every case, would have been a pre-committed, automated, hard limit set in the calm — a line that fired before the drawdown got deep enough to take judgment and the option to cut away.

## The risk playbook

The will to cut cannot be summoned in the moment; it has to be *installed* in advance. Here is the concrete discipline.

- **Write the rule in the calm, with a number in it.** At a new high or before the trade, set specific, checkable limits: a single-position cut level (e.g. −20%), a book-level de-gross trigger (e.g. halve gross at −12% from the high), and a hard "I never add to a loser to get back to even" line. One page. A rule without a number is a wish.
- **Automate the exit.** Place stops as *resting orders* the moment you enter, not as mental notes. Schedule portfolio de-grossing on the drawdown trigger. The rule must be able to act without your in-the-moment participation, because your in-the-moment self is exactly the part you can't trust.
- **Protect the rule with a cooling-off period.** Allow yourself to change the plan, but never right now — any override waits 24 to 48 hours. This single piece of friction defuses the impulsive, 3 a.m. decisions that cause most drawdown damage.
- **Size so the drawdown is emotionally survivable.** Pick a position size you can hold *calmly* through a normal drawdown without tearing up the rulebook. A smaller position you can follow your rules on beats a larger one you'll panic out of. Sizing is a psychology decision as much as a ruin-math one ([position sizing and risk of ruin](/blog/trading/options-volatility/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading)).
- **Re-underwrite, don't defend.** Treat a deep drawdown as *information* about your thesis, not as a personal affront to be argued with. Ask only "would I buy this, here, today?" Ignore your entry price entirely — it is irrelevant to every forward decision.
- **Take less risk when underwater, never more.** The correct response to a deep drawdown is to de-gross, full stop. The urge to size up to get back to even is the high-water-mark trap talking; obey the rule, not the urge.
- **Remember why you're doing all this.** Each capped drawdown needs a far smaller gain to recover (−20% needs +25%; an uncapped −50% needs +100%), and over a career that asymmetry, respected, compounds into dramatically higher terminal wealth (Figure 7). Cutting is not a tax on returns — it is how you stay in the game long enough to compound at all.

The single sentence to keep: **decide the exit in the calm, automate it, and size so you can survive the drawdown without overriding your own rule** — because the trader who can cut is the trader who survives to trade tomorrow.
