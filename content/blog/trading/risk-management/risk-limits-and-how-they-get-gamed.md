> [!important]
> **One sentence:** every risk limit creates an incentive to route around it, so a single limit is always gameable — durable risk control comes from layering several complementary, hard-to-game constraints.
> - A **limit** is a hard number the book is not allowed to cross — a VaR ceiling, a loss budget, a position or concentration cap, a stop-out level.
> - Each limit has a **signature gaming move**: lower reported VaR by *selling* tail risk, dodge a single-name cap by *splitting* the position, beat the reporting date by *window-dressing*, beat the methodology by *method-shopping* for the smallest number.
> - The dangerous trick is the one that makes the *reported* number look **safer** while the *real* exposure gets **larger** — selling the tail is the classic.
> - Blow-ups that lived *inside* the limits are the rule, not the exception: Volmageddon's short-vol desks, Archegos hidden across prime brokers, all reported "fine" right up to the end.
> - The fix is not a tighter single limit; it is **multiple complementary limits** (gross, net, concentration, stress) plus continuous monitoring, so no single move clears all of them at once.
> - Survival logic: a limit you can game is a limit that lets you blow up while looking compliant — and you can only compound if you are still in the game.

A risk limit is supposed to be the thing that stops you before a bad day becomes a fatal one. It is the number on the risk report, the line the trader is not allowed to cross, the mechanism that lets a firm hand a person millions of dollars of capital and still sleep at night. And on paper it works: set a ceiling, measure against it, halt anyone who breaches.

The trouble is that a limit is not a law of physics. It is a *measured number*, and the moment you start measuring something and rewarding people for keeping it low, you create an incentive to make the *measurement* low — which is not the same thing as making the *risk* low. The two can move in opposite directions. A desk can lower its reported Value-at-Risk while quietly tripling the size of the loss it will take on the worst day. It can sit under a single-name cap while holding three times the cap in one disguised theme. It can be perfectly compliant on the one day the limit is checked and dangerously levered on the other 248. None of this requires a rogue trader or a fraud; it is the ordinary, rational response of a smart person who is graded on a number.

This post is the governance counterpart to a sibling post on how the VaR statistic itself misleads — there, the angle was that the *number is a bad estimate*; here, the angle is that *even a perfect number, used as a binding limit, invites people to route around it*. We will build the whole limit toolkit from zero, then walk through every standard way each limit gets gamed, ground each one in dollar math on a recurring account, look at real blow-ups that happened entirely *within* the stated limits, and end on the only durable answer: a layered set of complementary constraints, each closing the gap the others leave open. Figure 1 is the map — the four most common limits down the side, and for each, the gaming move it invites and the real risk it leaves behind.

## Foundations: what a risk limit actually is, and the toolkit

Before we can talk about gaming a limit, we need to be precise about what a limit *is* and what each kind is trying to stop. A **risk limit** is a pre-agreed maximum on some measured quantity of a book, enforced by a control function that is separate from the person taking the risk. "Pre-agreed" matters: the number is set *before* the trade, in calm conditions, by people who are not in the heat of the position. "Measured" matters: there is a specific formula and a specific frequency. "Enforced by a separate function" matters: the trader proposes risk, the risk desk measures and can veto. We will lean on this separation throughout; it is also the subject of a dedicated sibling post on the productive conflict between the two seats.

A limit only does its job if it is *binding* — if there are realistic, profitable things the trader would like to do that the limit forbids. A limit set so loose that no plausible position ever touches it is decorative; it shows up on the report, satisfies the auditor, and stops nothing. The whole art of limit-setting is calibrating limits tight enough to bite on real risk-taking but not so tight that the desk cannot function. And the whole *problem* this post is about is that once a limit bites, the trader has a strong incentive to make the *bite* go away — ideally by changing the measured number rather than the underlying risk, because changing the risk usually means giving up the trade.

Here is the toolkit, each defined from zero.

**Value-at-Risk (VaR) limit.** VaR answers: "On a normal bad day, how much could I lose?" Formally, a one-day 99% VaR of \$250,000 means that on 99 of 100 days, the loss should not exceed \$250,000 — the loss only crosses that line on the worst 1% of days. A *VaR limit* says the book's measured VaR may not exceed some ceiling. It is the headline risk number at most trading firms because it collapses an entire portfolio into a single dollar figure that desks can be compared on and capital can be sized to. Its great weakness — central to everything below — is that it says nothing about *how bad the bad day is when it comes*. The 1% of days beyond the line could be losses of \$300,000 or \$9,000,000; VaR is silent. That silence is the seam traders pry open.

**Loss limit (drawdown limit / stop-out).** A loss limit is even simpler: "If you lose \$X, you stop." It can be a daily loss limit (down \$50,000 on the day, flatten the book), a cumulative drawdown limit (down 8% from the high-water mark, cut size in half), or a hard stop-out (down 15%, the account is liquidated and you are done). Loss limits are realized-loss-based — they trigger on money that has actually been lost, not a model's estimate — which makes them harder to argue with but also blunter: they fire after the damage, not before. They have their own gaming move, subtler than the others: because a loss limit triggers on *marked* losses, a trader holding hard-to-value or illiquid positions can keep the *mark* generous — value the position at the model price rather than the price a buyer would actually pay — so the loss stays unrealized on paper and the limit never trips. The position is underwater; the mark says it isn't; the loss limit sleeps. This is "marking to model" or "marking to myth," and it is how a losing book can run straight past a stop-out that was supposed to catch it. The defense is independent valuation — the people who mark the book cannot be the people who trade it — which is, again, the separation-of-functions principle that runs through every part of a real limit system.

**The incentive, stated plainly.** Notice what all of these have in common before we get to the gaming. Every limit takes some property of the book — a loss estimate, a realized loss, a size, a concentration — reduces it to a single measured number, and rewards the trader for keeping that number under a line. The reward is real: bonus, capital allocation, autonomy, the simple freedom to keep trading. So the trader is now optimizing two things at once — the actual P&L of the strategy, *and* the measured number the limit watches. When those two goals point the same way, the limit works beautifully. When they can be separated — when there is a move that improves the measured number without giving up the real bet — a rational, non-fraudulent, well-meaning trader will find it, because the incentive points straight at it. None of what follows requires bad people. It requires only ordinary people responding to the gradient the limit creates.

**Position limit.** A cap on the *size* of an exposure: no more than \$1,000,000 notional in any single name, no more than 500 contracts in one future, no more than 5% of the book in any one position. Position limits are pre-trade and mechanical — easy to check, hard to dispute — which is exactly why the gaming move against them is mechanical too: if the cap is per-name, hold the same bet under several names.

**Concentration limit.** A cousin of the position limit that caps exposure to a *group* rather than a single name: no more than 20% of the book in one sector, one country, one factor, one counterparty, one theme. Concentration limits exist precisely because position limits are too narrow — they catch "too much of one stock" but miss "too much of one *idea* spread across ten stocks." A well-built concentration limit is one of the harder limits to game, which is why it does so much of the work in the layered solution at the end.

**Gross and net exposure limits.** *Net* exposure is longs minus shorts — your directional bet. *Gross* exposure is longs plus shorts — the total amount of capital at work, in either direction. A market-neutral book can have near-zero *net* exposure and enormous *gross* exposure: \$10,000,000 long and \$10,000,000 short nets to zero but grosses to \$20,000,000, and that \$20,000,000 is what gets hurt if the longs and shorts move against you together (a basis blow-up, a factor reversal). The distinction is load-bearing: a limit that only caps *net* exposure can be sailed straight past by a book that is enormous on a gross basis but balanced. "Limit the gross, not just the net" is one of the four playbook rules at the end.

Each of these limits is sensible on its own. Each is also gameable on its own. The rest of the post is the gaming, limit by limit, and then the layering that fixes it.

#### Worked example: what "binding" means in dollars

Take the recurring **\$100,000 retail account**. Suppose the broker sets a single-name position limit of 10% — no more than \$10,000 in any one stock. You have a high-conviction view on one chip company and would love to put \$30,000 to work. The limit *binds*: it forbids a trade you actively want. Now the incentive is set. You can (a) obey and put on \$10,000, giving up two-thirds of the position you wanted; (b) drop the trade; or (c) find a way to hold \$30,000 of the same bet without any single \$10,000 line tripping the cap — buy \$10,000 of the chip company, \$10,000 of its main supplier (which moves almost identically), and \$10,000 of a semiconductor ETF that is heavy in both. Three lines, each at the \$10,000 cap, zero breaches on the report, and roughly \$30,000 of exposure to the same theme. *A limit that binds on the number you report, but not on the risk you actually hold, has not reduced your risk — it has only taught you to disguise it.*

## The first gaming move: sell the tail to lower reported VaR

This is the most important trick in the post because it is the one that makes the reported number *better* and the real risk *worse* at the same time — the cleanest possible inversion of what a limit is for.

Start with the mechanism. VaR measures the loss at, say, the 1st percentile — the threshold the loss only crosses on the worst 1% of days. Now suppose you sell **deep out-of-the-money options** — say, puts struck far below the current price, options that only pay out (cost you) if the market crashes hard. Most of the time, those crashes do not happen, so you simply collect the premium: a small, steady stream of income every day. That income does two things to your distribution of daily outcomes. First, it shifts your everyday P&L slightly positive — you are getting paid to wait. Second, and more subtly, if you are also using the premium to run a tighter core book, your *ordinary-day* losses shrink. A tighter, slightly-right-shifted body of outcomes means the 1st percentile — the VaR point — moves *in*. Your reported VaR goes *down*. You look safer.

But the options you sold did not vanish. They are a contingent liability that fires only in the deep tail — beyond the 1% point VaR even looks at. On the rare crash day, those deep puts you sold blow up, and the loss is enormous. So while your 99% VaR fell, your *99.9%* loss — the real worst-case — exploded. You have sold insurance against disasters, banked the premiums, and recorded a *lower* risk number because the disaster you are now exposed to is too rare to register in the statistic the limit watches. Figure 2 shows both distributions side by side: the plain book and the same book after selling the tail.

![Two daily profit and loss distributions showing reported 99 percent VaR falling while the 99.9 percent tail loss explodes after selling deep out of the money options](/imgs/blogs/risk-limits-and-how-they-get-gamed-2.png)

There is a deeper reason VaR is structurally helpless against this, worth making explicit because it generalizes. VaR is a *quantile* — it reads off the loss at one specific point in the distribution (the 1st percentile) and reports nothing about the shape of the distribution beyond that point. Two books can have the *identical* 99% VaR and wildly different catastrophes: one loses \$250,000 at the 99% point and \$280,000 at the 99.9% point (a thin tail), the other loses \$250,000 at the 99% point and \$2,000,000 at the 99.9% point (a fat, sold tail). VaR cannot tell them apart, because it stops looking exactly where they start to differ. Selling the tail is the trade that exploits this gap on purpose: it deliberately moves risk *past* the quantile the limit reads, into the region the limit is blind to. The mathematical fix — measuring the *average* loss beyond the quantile rather than the loss *at* it — is conditional VaR (expected shortfall), which has its own post in this series; a limit built on expected shortfall is far harder to game by tail-selling, because the average-of-the-tail rises sharply the moment you start selling deep options. But most firms still set the limit on plain VaR, and so the incentive persists.

The second-order effect is convexity. A plain, un-optioned book tends to lose *linearly* as the market moves against it — twice the move, roughly twice the loss. A book that has sold deep options loses *convexly* into the tail — the loss accelerates as the move gets larger, because the sold options go from worthless to deeply in-the-money fast. So the very signature of the tail-selling trick is a book whose loss curve bends downward into the tail. A risk system that tracked the *second derivative* of the loss — how the loss accelerates with the move, not just the loss at one point — would flag the tail-seller immediately. Almost none do, because VaR is a first-percentile number, not a curvature measurement. Watching convexity is one of the playbook rules at the end precisely because it catches the trade that VaR cannot.

This is not a hypothetical maneuver; it is the structural reason short-volatility strategies are so seductive to anyone graded on VaR. They print steady income, they report low risk, and they look like skill — right up until the tail day, when they don't. The variance-risk-premium dynamic behind this ("selling vol pays until it doesn't") has its own dedicated treatment in the options series, linked at the end; here the point is narrower and about governance: *a VaR limit actively rewards selling the tail, because selling the tail is the one trade that lowers the measured number while raising the real one.*

#### Worked example: halving reported VaR by selling the tail

Take the **\$10,000,000 book**. Run plainly, it has a 1-day 99% VaR of about **\$207,000** and a 99.9% worst-case of about **\$277,000** — the bad day and the disaster day are not far apart, because the book has no hidden tail.

Now the desk sells a strip of deep-OTM puts that bring in steady premium and lets it run a tighter core. The everyday distribution narrows. The new 99% VaR drops to about **\$174,000** — a fall of roughly **\$33,000**, a sixth lower. On the risk report, the desk just *reduced* its risk by a sixth while *making money* on the premium. The risk manager is pleased; the VaR limit has more headroom than before.

But the sold puts are still there. On the 0.1% crash day, they detonate, and the 99.9% loss is now about **\$794,000** — up from \$277,000, an increase of roughly **\$517,000**, or nearly *triple*. The book reports one-sixth less risk and carries nearly three times the catastrophe. *Any limit that watches only the 99% point is not just blind to this trade — it pays a bonus for it, because the trade's whole signature is "lower the number you measure, raise the number you don't."* This is the same blind spot the sibling VaR post dissects from the statistical side; the governance lesson is that the limit inherits the blind spot and turns it into an incentive.

## The second gaming move: window-dress the reporting date

A limit is only as continuous as its measurement. If risk is checked at the close on the last day of the month or quarter — a common arrangement, because that is when reports are produced and capital is allocated — then the limit binds *on that date* and nowhere else. The trader's incentive is immediate and obvious: be small on the snapshot date, be whatever size you like the rest of the time.

This is **window dressing**: cutting risk right before the reporting date so the measured number is low, then putting the risk straight back on once the snapshot has been taken. It is the portfolio equivalent of cleaning the house only on the days the inspector visits. The book that the report describes — small, tame, well within limits — is a book that existed for two days a quarter. The book that actually held risk, earned the P&L, and could blow up is the one that lived on every other day, and the report never saw it.

Figure 3 makes the gap visible: true daily exposure runs well above the limit most of the time, dips sharply for the few days around each quarter-end snapshot, and pops right back up. The green dots — the only points the limit actually measures — all sit comfortably under the line, while the amber area they were sampled from sits well above it.

![Window dressing chart showing gross exposure run far above the limit on ordinary days but cut to compliant levels only on the quarterly reporting snapshot dates](/imgs/blogs/risk-limits-and-how-they-get-gamed-3.png)

Window dressing is the most purely *informational* of the gaming moves — it changes nothing about the strategy or its real risk, only what the observer sees. That makes it both the easiest to do and, once you know to look, the easiest to catch: the defense is simply to measure risk *continuously* (or on random, unannounced dates) rather than on a predictable schedule. A limit on the *average* daily exposure over the period, or on the *maximum* intra-period exposure, cannot be window-dressed, because there is no single date to clean up for. We will see this is exactly why a continuously-monitored limit appears as the one row in the final figure that catches the window-dressing trick outright.

It is worth being clear that window dressing is not free, which is why it tells you something when you see it. Unwinding a \$34,000,000 book down to \$18,000,000 and rebuilding it costs real money — spreads, market impact, financing — every quarter. A desk that pays that cost four times a year is revealing that the limit binds hard enough on the snapshot date to be worth the friction of dodging it, which means the *true* book is running well above what the firm thinks it approved. The cost is a signal. A risk function that notices a recurring spike in trading activity in the few days before each reporting date — costs incurred for no apparent strategy reason — has caught the window-dresser by the trail of transaction costs, even without a continuous risk feed. The same logic applies at the market level: there is a well-documented tendency for funds to sell their losers and pile into winners right before quarter-end reporting so the holdings statement shows only the names that did well, a cosmetic reshuffle that costs the investors real spread for no economic gain. The pattern is identical — clean up for the snapshot, pay the friction, revert after — and the defense is identical: judge the book on its average over the period, not its costume on report day.

A subtler variant games the *high-water mark* and drawdown-limit timing rather than the exposure snapshot. A drawdown limit that resets on a calendar boundary — "you may lose at most 8% in any calendar quarter" — invites a trader who is up 7% near quarter-end to take *more* risk, not less, because a loss in the last week eats this quarter's budget while a loss in the first week of next quarter starts a fresh budget. The limit's *time-boxing* creates a perverse incentive to swing hardest exactly at the boundary. This is the drawdown-limit cousin of window dressing: the gaming move is timing the risk to the measurement window rather than hiding the size, and the fix is the same family — measure on a rolling window, not a calendar-reset one, so there is no boundary to exploit.

#### Worked example: the gross exposure the report never sees

The **\$10,000,000 book** has a gross-exposure limit of **2.0x capital** — measured at each quarter-end. On the ordinary trading day the desk runs about **3.4x**, i.e. **\$34,000,000** of gross long-plus-short exposure. That is well over the limit, but the limit is not watching on ordinary days.

In the four sessions before each quarter-end, the desk unwinds down to about **1.8x** (\$18,000,000) for the snapshot, then rebuilds. Across the four snapshot dates, the *reported* gross averages about **1.8x** — comfortably under the 2.0x limit. The report says "compliant, with room to spare." The *true* daily average is about **3.4x** — roughly **1.9x the reported figure**, and **70% over the limit** on a typical day. If the bad day lands in week six of the quarter, when the desk is running \$34,000,000 rather than \$18,000,000, the loss is nearly *double* what the limit was sized to allow. *A limit measured on a known date does not constrain your risk; it constrains your risk on that date — and the market does not schedule crashes around your reporting calendar.*

## The third gaming move: split the position to dodge a single-name cap

Position and concentration caps are mechanical, which makes them feel robust — you either hold more than the cap of one name or you don't, and the system can check it automatically. But "one name" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. A single-name cap stops you from holding too much of *one ticker*; it does not, by itself, stop you from holding the same *bet* under several tickers that move together. If you want \$3,000,000 of exposure to one theme and the single-name cap is \$1,000,000, you split the theme into legs, each under the cap.

The legs do not even have to be obviously related. The primary name takes one slice. A close supplier or competitor whose stock moves with a 0.9 correlation takes another. A theme-heavy ETF that holds both takes a third. The same name held in a *second account* — a different fund, a different legal entity, a sub-account — takes a fourth. Each line is individually under the \$1,000,000 cap; not one of them trips the limit; and the combined exposure to the theme is the full \$3,000,000 the cap was meant to prevent. Figure 6 lays this out: four blue legs, each under the red single-name line, summing to an amber combined bar three times the cap.

![Bar chart showing a single theme bet split into four legs each under the single name cap while the combined exposure is three times the cap](/imgs/blogs/risk-limits-and-how-they-get-gamed-6.png)

The reason this works is that the position cap measures the wrong unit. It measures *names* when the risk lives in *themes* (or factors, or counterparties). The defense is the concentration limit — a cap on the theme, the sector, the factor, the counterparty, rather than the individual line. A concentration limit defined on the right grouping catches the split position immediately, because the split changes the *names* but not the *theme*, and the concentration limit is watching the theme. This is exactly why splitting a name "slips by" the position and net limits in the final figure but is "caught" by the concentration cap and the stress test.

The hard part is the *grouping definition*, and it is a genuine arms race. Group by sector classification, and the trader finds a name that is in a different official sector but moves with the theme anyway — a logistics company that lives or dies on chip demand sits in "industrials," not "technology," and slips the sector cap while carrying the bet. Group by official sector and the proxy escapes; group too aggressively by statistical correlation and you start lumping together names that happen to have co-moved by accident, capping positions that are not really the same bet. The robust answer is to define concentration on *factor exposure* — measure how much of the book's risk loads on a common driver (a semiconductor factor, a rates factor, a single counterparty's credit) regardless of which tickers carry it — which is the subject of the marginal-and-component-VaR post in this series, because decomposing risk to the factor level is exactly how you build a concentration limit the split cannot dodge. Even factor-based concentration is not perfectly game-proof: a trader who knows the factor model can find exposure the model doesn't span. But it raises the cost of the split enormously, and that is the point of a layer — not to be unbeatable alone, but to be one more bar the position has to fit between. A single concentration limit, however well-defined, is still one limit; the durable system stacks it with the others.

#### Worked example: \$3,000,000 of one bet under a \$1,000,000 cap

The **\$10,000,000 book** has a single-name cap of **10%** = **\$1,000,000** per name. The desk wants \$3,000,000 in one semiconductor theme. It builds:

- **\$950,000** in the primary chip name (account A) — under the cap.
- **\$900,000** in its main supplier, ~0.9 correlated — under the cap.
- **\$650,000** in a theme-heavy semiconductor ETF — under the cap.
- **\$500,000** in the same primary name held in account B — under the cap.

Total: **\$3,000,000** of exposure to one theme, every single line under the \$1,000,000 cap, zero breaches on any single-name report. On a day the theme drops 20%, the book loses about **\$600,000** — *six percent of the entire fund on one idea*, from a control system that was certain no single position exceeded \$1,000,000. *A single-name cap measures tickers; risk is carried by themes — so the cap counts the wrong thing, and counting the wrong thing carefully does not make it the right thing.* This is the failure that the dedicated concentration-and-position-limits post in this series treats at length; here it is one gaming move among several, and the takeaway is that the cure for it (a concentration limit) is one *layer*, not the whole answer.

## The fourth gaming move: shop the method for the smallest number

VaR is not a single number you read off a meter; it is an *estimate*, and there are several accepted ways to compute it that disagree — sometimes by a lot — on the very same book and the very same day. The three standard methods:

- **Historical VaR** replays the book through actual past returns and reads off the empirical 1st-percentile loss. It captures whatever fat tails were in the lookback window, but only those — if the window happened to be calm, the tail it reports is thin.
- **Parametric (variance-covariance) VaR** assumes returns are normally distributed and computes the 1st-percentile loss from the mean and standard deviation (the 99% point is mean minus 2.326 standard deviations). It is fast and smooth, but the normal assumption systematically *understates* the tail, because real markets have fatter tails than the bell curve — exactly the trap that the fat-tails post in this series is about.
- **Monte Carlo VaR** simulates many possible days from a chosen statistical model (often a fat-tailed one) and reads off the simulated 1st percentile. It is the most flexible and usually the most honest about tails — and therefore often the *largest* number.

Now add the governance layer. The firm sets a VaR limit. The desk gets to choose, or at least influence, which methodology produces its reported VaR. If the methods disagree and the desk reports the smallest one, the limit binds on whichever method happens to score lowest — which is to say, on nothing. Figure 5 shows the same \$10,000,000 book and the same day under all three methods, against a single VaR limit: two methods breach it, one fits under it, and the desk reports the one that fits.

![Bar chart of one book's 99 percent VaR under historical parametric and Monte Carlo methods against a single limit line, with one method fitting under the limit and two breaching](/imgs/blogs/risk-limits-and-how-they-get-gamed-5.png)

This is method-shopping, and it is subtler than the other tricks because nobody is hiding anything — every method is firm-approved, every number is "correct," and the choice can be defended in a meeting. The sibling VaR post covers *why* the methods disagree (it is the statistics that diverge); the governance point here is that *the freedom to choose the method turns an honest disagreement into a gaming move*. The defense is to fix the methodology in advance and apply it uniformly, so the desk does not get to pick after seeing the numbers — and, better, to never let a single VaR limit be the only constraint, because then the choice of method only changes one of several numbers that all have to clear.

Method-shopping has quieter cousins that exploit the same freedom-of-measurement seam. There is *lookback-window shopping*: historical VaR depends on which past period you replay the book through, and a calm window reports a thinner tail than a turbulent one. A desk that gets to choose a two-year lookback that happens to exclude the last crash will report a smaller number than one forced to include it — same book, same day, different history, lower limit usage. There is *holding-period shopping*: a one-day VaR scaled to ten days the naive way (multiply by the square root of ten) understates the real ten-day risk if losses cluster, so the choice of horizon and scaling rule moves the number. And there is *correlation shopping*: the diversification benefit a portfolio claims depends entirely on the correlation matrix you feed the model, and correlations estimated in calm periods are lower — more diversification, lower VaR — than the correlations that actually prevail in a crisis, when everything moves together. Every one of these is a legitimate modeling choice in isolation; every one, handed to a desk that is graded on the output, becomes a dial the desk turns toward "smaller." The unifying defense is the same: take the modeling choices *away* from the person whose number they determine, fix them centrally, and stress-test the book against the assumptions the chosen model leaves out.

### The meta-lesson: a measure that becomes a target stops measuring

Stand back from the four tricks and you are looking at a single, general phenomenon, one economists named decades ago: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The instant you take a statistic that was an honest *description* of risk and turn it into a *limit* people are graded against, you change their behavior toward the statistic itself, and the statistic starts describing the gaming rather than the risk. VaR was a fine *description* of a book's everyday risk; turned into a binding limit, it became a target, and the rational response — sell the tail, shop the method — degraded it as a measure. This is not a flaw in VaR specifically; *any* single number used as a binding limit suffers it. Replace VaR with expected shortfall and traders will optimize against expected shortfall; replace that with stress loss and they will optimize against the specific scenarios you chose. There is no un-gameable single number, because the gaming is a response to *being a single target*, not to the particular formula.

This is why the answer is never "find the perfect metric." The perfect metric does not exist, and if it did, making it a binding limit would corrupt it. The answer is structural, and it has two parts. First, *multiplicity*: several limits that bind on different properties, so that optimizing against one is constrained by the others — the cage, not the hurdle. Second, *separation*: the person who measures and the person who is measured must be different people with different incentives, because a limit the trader can re-measure to taste is no limit at all. The separation point deserves its own emphasis. Each gaming move we have walked through depends on the trader controlling some piece of the measurement — the marks (loss limits), the method (VaR), the snapshot timing (window dressing), the grouping (concentration). Take each of those controls out of the trader's hands and into an independent risk function's, and the move gets much harder. The mark is set by an independent valuation team. The VaR method is fixed by the risk committee. The measurement dates are random and unannounced. The concentration grouping is defined centrally on a factor model the desk does not control. This is the institutional reason firms separate the risk-taking seat from the risk-measuring seat — the subject of the productive-conflict post — and it is not bureaucracy for its own sake; it is the only thing that keeps a limit from being something the trader can quietly redefine until it no longer binds.

#### Worked example: picking the method that fits under the limit

The **\$10,000,000 book** has a 1-day 99% VaR limit of **\$250,000**. On one day, the three methods report:

- **Historical:** about **\$266,000** — **breaches** the \$250,000 limit.
- **Parametric-normal:** about **\$233,000** — **fits** under the limit.
- **Monte Carlo (fat-tailed):** about **\$266,000** — **breaches** the limit.

The two methods most honest about tails both say the book is over the line; the normal-distribution method, which is known to understate tails, is the only one that fits. If the desk is allowed to report parametric, it clears the limit by about **\$17,000** of headroom — while two of its own three accepted models say it is in breach by about **\$16,000**. The spread between the smallest and largest estimate is about **\$33,000**, more than the headroom itself, so the *choice of method entirely determines* whether the book is compliant. *When the limit binds on whichever number the desk gets to pick, the limit binds on nothing; the only honest version is a method fixed before the number is known.*

## Why one limit is never enough — and what a binding set looks like

Step back and notice the common shape of all four gaming moves. In every case, the trader keeps the *real* bet they want and changes only the *one number* the *one limit* measures: sell the tail (VaR ignores it), split the name (the single-name cap doesn't see the theme), window-dress (the snapshot misses the other days), method-shop (the limit binds on the kindest estimate). Each trick is tailored to one limit's specific blind spot. Which suggests the cure: if every limit has a blind spot, *cover each blind spot with a different limit*. The trick that beats limit A has to also beat limits B, C, and D — and the moves that beat them are different moves, often contradictory ones.

This is the heart of the post. A single limit is a hurdle: one move clears it. A *set* of complementary limits is a cage: there is no single move that clears all the bars at once, because they bind on different things. Figure 4 contrasts the two — one gameable limit on the left with its one escape route, versus four overlapping limits on the right that each catch what the others miss.

![Before and after comparison of one gameable risk limit with a single escape route versus four complementary limits that each catch a different gaming move](/imgs/blogs/risk-limits-and-how-they-get-gamed-4.png)

Make this concrete with the four gaming tricks against four limits. Lay them out as a grid — each trick a row, each limit a column, a cell marked "caught" if that limit stops that trick and "slips by" if the trick gets past it. The pattern is the whole argument:

- **Selling the tail** slips past the net VaR limit (that's the point), past gross exposure (the sold options barely register as notional), and past the concentration cap (it's diversified across strikes) — but a **stress-loss limit** that prices the book under a crash scenario catches it instantly, because the crash is exactly when the sold tail detonates.
- **Long-and-short the same theme** (net near zero) slips past a *net* VaR limit — net exposure is tiny — but is caught by the **gross** limit (the longs and shorts both count), by the **concentration** cap (it's all one theme), and by **stress** testing (a factor reversal hits both legs together).
- **Splitting one name** across accounts and proxies slips past the **single-name** position cap and the net limit, but is caught by the **concentration** cap (the theme is unchanged) and by a **stress** scenario on that theme.
- **Window dressing** is caught by *any* limit that is measured **continuously** — on the average or maximum over the period rather than on a known snapshot date — which is why it shows as caught across the board once the limits are not date-bound.

Figure 7 is that grid. The crucial reading is *columnar and rowwise at once*: every **column** has at least one red cell — no single limit catches everything, so no single limit is sufficient. But every **row** has at least one green cell — every trick is caught by *some* limit. So the *full set* of four limits has no all-green escape row: there is no gaming move that slips past all of gross, net, concentration, and stress simultaneously, because the moves that beat one are caught by another.

![Grid showing four gaming tricks against four risk limits, with each trick caught by at least one limit and no single limit catching all four tricks](/imgs/blogs/risk-limits-and-how-they-get-gamed-7.png)

That is what "binding limits come from multiple complementary constraints" means precisely: not that you pile on more of the same kind of limit, but that you choose limits whose blind spots *don't overlap*, so that the gaps in one are covered by the others. A second VaR limit at a different confidence level adds little — it has nearly the same blind spot. A stress-loss limit added to a VaR limit adds a lot, because it sees exactly the tail VaR is blind to. The skill is picking limits that are *orthogonal in what they miss*.

#### Worked example: closing every gap on the \$10,000,000 book

Put a real layered set on the **\$10,000,000 book** and watch each trick die:

- **Net VaR limit \$250,000.** Catches ordinary directional risk. The desk sells the tail; reported VaR drops to \$174,000; this limit is now *more* satisfied. Gap open.
- **Add a stress-loss limit: max \$1,500,000 loss in a −20% / vol-spike crash scenario.** Now re-price the tail-selling book under that crash: the sold puts lose roughly **\$794,000** in the tail plus the rest of the book's crash loss — the scenario loss blows through \$1,500,000. The stress limit catches what VaR missed. The desk cannot both collect tail premium *and* pass the stress test.
- **Add a gross limit: 2.0x = \$20,000,000.** The desk tries the market-neutral version — \$15,000,000 long, \$15,000,000 short, net near zero, net-VaR tiny. But gross is now \$30,000,000, over the \$20,000,000 cap. Caught.
- **Add a concentration limit: 15% = \$1,500,000 per theme.** The desk splits one name into \$950,000 + \$900,000 + \$650,000 + \$500,000 = \$3,000,000 of one theme. The single-name cap saw four compliant lines; the concentration limit sees one \$3,000,000 theme against a \$1,500,000 cap — breach. Caught.
- **Measure all four continuously, on the period average and maximum, not on quarter-end.** Window dressing has no snapshot to clean up for. Caught.

There is now no single move that satisfies all five constraints while keeping the real bet. The desk can still take risk — that's the job — but it has to take risk that is *genuinely* within the firm's appetite on every axis at once, which is exactly what a limit system is supposed to enforce. *One limit is a number to beat; the right four limits are a shape your real position has to fit inside, and there is no disguise that fits all four holes at once.*

## Common misconceptions

**"If the book is under every limit, it is safe."** No — it is *compliant*, which is a statement about the measured numbers, not the real risk. The tail-selling book in our example was under its VaR limit by design while carrying nearly triple the catastrophe loss. Compliance and safety coincide only when the limits have no blind spots the position is exploiting. A book reporting \$174,000 of VaR and \$794,000 of hidden tail is "under the limit" and one crash from ruin.

**"A tighter limit fixes a gameable limit."** No — tightening a single limit just lowers the bar without removing the escape route. Cut the VaR limit from \$250,000 to \$150,000 and the desk simply sells *more* tail to get under it, carrying an *even larger* hidden catastrophe. The fix is a *different* limit (a stress-loss limit) that sees the tail, not a smaller version of the limit that is already blind to it.

**"Window dressing is fraud."** Usually not, legally — the trader genuinely held a compliant book on the reporting date and genuinely reports it. That's what makes it durable: nobody lied. The number is true; it is just *unrepresentative*. The defense is structural (continuous or random-date measurement), not a rule against lying, because no lie was told.

**"Splitting a position to dodge a cap is obvious and easy to catch."** It is obvious only if your monitoring groups exposures by *theme*, and most position systems group by *name*. Four lines under a \$1,000,000 single-name cap, summing to \$3,000,000 of one theme, look perfectly clean on a name-by-name report. You catch it only with a concentration limit defined on the right grouping — and defining that grouping well enough that no proxy leg escapes it is genuinely hard, which is why splitting works as often as it does.

**"VaR is just wrong, so a VaR limit is useless."** Too strong. A VaR limit catches ordinary directional risk well — it is the right tool for the everyday bad day. Its failure is specific: it is blind to the deep tail and sensitive to method choice. The lesson is not to throw it out but to *never let it stand alone*; paired with a stress-loss limit that covers its blind spot, a VaR limit pulls real weight.

**"More limits always means safer."** No — *more of the same kind* of limit adds bureaucracy without coverage. Two VaR limits at 95% and 99% share nearly the same blind spot; the second buys you almost nothing against tail-selling. Safety comes from limits that are *complementary* — orthogonal in what they miss — not from sheer count. A short list of well-chosen, non-overlapping limits beats a long list of near-duplicates.

## How it shows up in real markets

Limit-gaming is not a textbook curiosity; the largest blow-ups of recent decades happened *inside* the stated limits, often reporting "fine" right up to the day they didn't.

**Volmageddon (February 5, 2018).** The short-volatility complex — products and desks that *sold* volatility for steady premium — is the tail-selling trick at industrial scale. For years these positions printed smooth income and reported tame risk, because selling the tail lowers the measured number while the catastrophe waits off the end of the distribution. On 2018-02-05, the VIX jumped from roughly 17 to 37 in a single session — about a **+116%** move, the largest one-day VIX percentage rise on record — and the tail detonated. The flagship inverse-VIX product, XIV, lost about **96%** of its value after the close and was terminated. The desks were "within VaR" because VaR never priced the day they were actually exposed to. This is the canonical case that a VaR limit alone *invites* the very trade that kills you; it has a dedicated case-study post in the options series, linked below.

**Archegos (March 2021).** Archegos held enormous, concentrated single-stock positions — but it held them through **total-return swaps** at multiple prime brokers, so each bank saw only *its* slice and none saw the whole. This is the splitting trick across counterparties rather than accounts: the same concentrated bet, sliced so that no single counterparty's exposure report tripped a concentration alarm. Leverage was roughly **5x or more**, hidden by the fact that no one institution could see the total size. When the underlying stocks fell, the whole structure unwound at once, inflicting over **\$10 billion** of losses on the prime brokers — about **\$5.5 billion** at Credit Suisse alone. Each bank's limits were satisfied; the *aggregate* position, which no limit measured, was the one that blew up. The fix the industry reached for afterward was exactly the layered, *cross-counterparty* concentration view that single-bank limits had missed.

**Amaranth (September 2006).** A single concentrated, levered natural-gas calendar-spread bet — within the fund's own limits as it understood them — lost about **\$6.6 billion** in roughly a week. The concentration was the risk, and a name-by-name or instrument-by-instrument limit did not capture that the entire book was one trade in an illiquid market. It is the concentration blind spot in its purest form: the limits measured positions; the risk was the *single theme* those positions all expressed.

**LTCM (August–September 1998).** Long-Term Capital Management ran balance-sheet leverage near **25:1** and roughly **\$1.25 trillion** of gross derivative notional on under \$5 billion of equity, across convergence trades that *looked* diversified by any name-level or net-exposure measure. When the flight to quality hit, the supposedly-independent trades all moved together — correlations went to one — and the diversification the risk reports relied on evaporated. The book lost about **\$4.6 billion** in four months and required a roughly **\$3.6 billion** Fed-organized rescue. The limits that mattered (gross leverage, true concentration once correlations converged) were not the ones being watched. This is the crowded-trade failure mode treated in the game-theory series; for limits, the lesson is that *net* and *diversified-by-name* are exactly the measures a correlated, levered book sails past.

**COVID dash-for-cash (February–March 2020) and the yen-carry unwind (August 2024).** These two are the *correlation-shopping* failure made real. Risk limits that grant a diversification benefit — lower VaR because the positions supposedly offset — compute that benefit from a correlation matrix, and almost everyone estimates that matrix from recent, calm data, where correlations are modest. In a crisis those correlations snap toward one: in the COVID crash the VIX hit a record **82.69** close on 2020-03-16 and the S&P 500 fell about **34%** peak to trough in weeks, with stocks, bonds, gold, and credit all selling off together as everyone scrambled for cash. The diversification the limits had credited *vanished exactly when it was needed*, so books that were "within VaR" on the calm-period correlation matrix were running far more real risk than the number said. The yen-carry unwind on 2024-08-05 — the Nikkei's worst day since 1987, down **12.4%**, with the VIX spiking to an intraday **65.7** — was the same mechanism on a crowded funding trade: a position that looked diversified and well-financed deleveraged in days. The governance lesson is direct and feeds the playbook: never let a limit's diversification benefit rest on a calm-period correlation matrix; stress the book under correlations going to one, because that is the regime that actually bankrupts you, and it is precisely the regime a calm-estimated VaR limit is blind to.

The thread through all four: the blow-up was *compliant*. Nobody breached the limit that was being measured. They breached the limit that *should* have been measured — the tail, the aggregate, the theme, the gross — and that gap is precisely where a single-limit regime leaves the door open.

## The risk playbook

Concrete rules for building limits that bind, drawn from everything above:

1. **Layer complementary limits, not duplicates.** Pair every limit with one that covers its blind spot: VaR with a **stress-loss** limit (covers the tail VaR misses), single-name caps with a **concentration** limit (covers the theme the name-cap misses), net exposure with a **gross** limit (covers the balanced-but-huge book). Choose limits *orthogonal in what they miss*; reject limits that merely restate one you already have.

2. **Limit the gross, not just the net.** A market-neutral book with near-zero net can carry enormous gross exposure that all hits at once in a factor or basis blow-up. Cap longs-plus-shorts, not just longs-minus-shorts, or you have left the single largest hole open.

3. **Stress the limit — price the book under the scenario VaR ignores.** Run the portfolio through an explicit crash (a −20% equity move, a vol spike, a correlation-to-one event) and cap the *loss in that scenario*. This is the one limit that catches tail-selling, because the sold tail's whole nature is to be invisible to everyday statistics and catastrophic in the scenario. Make the stress limit binding, not advisory.

4. **Monitor convexity and continuity.** Track *how the book's loss accelerates* as the move gets larger — a book that loses linearly is very different from one that loses convexly into the tail (the signature of sold options). And measure every limit **continuously**, on the period average and maximum, or on random unannounced dates — never on a known snapshot — so window dressing has nothing to dress for.

5. **Fix the method before you see the number.** Set the VaR methodology, the grouping for concentration, and the stress scenarios *in advance*, in calm conditions, and apply them uniformly. Any limit where the desk chooses the measurement after seeing the trade is a limit that binds on whatever number the desk picks — which is no limit at all.

6. **Treat a quiet, low-VaR, high-income book as a question, not an answer.** The position that reports the *least* risk while earning *steady* money is, far more often than chance, a position that has *sold the tail* — it looks best on the report precisely because its danger is off the end of the distribution the report measures. Investigate the calmest desk hardest.

The survival logic underneath all of it: you can only compound if you are still in the game, and a limit you can game is a limit that lets you blow up while looking compliant. The point of layering is not paperwork — it is to make sure that when the report says "within limits," the *real* risk is within limits too, so that the one bad day finds a book that was actually sized to survive it rather than one that was merely measured to look like it.
