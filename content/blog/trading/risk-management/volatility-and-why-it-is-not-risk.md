> [!important]
> **The one idea:** volatility — the standard deviation of returns — is the default number the whole industry calls "risk," and it misleads, because uncertainty and danger are not the same thing.
> - Volatility is **symmetric**: it punishes a +20% upside surprise exactly as hard as a −20% loss, even though only one of those can end you.
> - It quietly assumes a **stable, roughly normal world** — but real returns are skewed and fat-tailed, so one number hides the crash sitting in the left tail.
> - It measures how much your account **wiggles**, not whether you suffer a **permanent loss of capital** — a quiet slide to ruin can have low volatility, and a violent recovery can have high volatility.
> - It is **path-blind**: two assets with the identical volatility can have a −7% maximum drawdown and a −50% one, and vol cannot tell them apart.
> - It is **not constant**: volatility clusters and switches regimes, so quoting a single number is quoting a fiction. The practical upshot: use vol where it is honest (comparing similar things, scaling positions day to day), and switch to **drawdown and tail measures** the moment survival is the question.

A risk report is the most reassuring document in finance, and that is exactly the problem. Somewhere in every fund, on the morning of every crisis that ever happened, there was a clean spreadsheet with a single number on it labeled "volatility" or "risk," and that number was small, and it was calm, and it was right up until the moment it was catastrophically wrong. The traders running the short-volatility funds that detonated on February 5, 2018 were not looking at scary numbers. Their realized volatility had been *low* for months — that was the whole point of the trade, and the whole reason it was so crowded. The number that was supposed to measure their risk was, if anything, telling them they had less of it than usual. Then in a single afternoon the VIX more than doubled and one of those products, XIV, lost roughly 96% of its value after the close and was terminated. The risk number had described the calm perfectly. It had said nothing about the danger.

This is not a story about one exotic product. It is a story about the single most important confusion in all of risk management: the belief that **volatility is risk**. Volatility — the up-and-down jiggle of your returns, measured as their standard deviation — became the default risk number for good reasons. It is easy to compute, it shows up in every textbook, it is the σ in every formula, and for a lot of everyday purposes it is genuinely useful. But it answers a question — *how much does this thing move around?* — that is not the question a survivor actually cares about, which is *how likely am I to lose money I can never get back?* Those are different questions. Treating the answer to the first as if it were the answer to the second is the category error that this entire post exists to dismantle.

Look at Figure 1, because it is the whole argument in one picture. On the left is a return distribution that is symmetric and honest: a clean bell curve where big up days and big down days are equally likely and equally sized. On the right is a distribution that is negatively skewed: most days are small gains, and then, rarely, there is a violent loss that erases years of those small gains. Here is the unsettling part. **These two distributions can have the identical standard deviation.** They can produce the exact same "volatility = 15%" on the risk report. One of them is a perfectly reasonable thing to own; the other one is a trade that will eventually take your account to zero. And the number everyone calls "risk" cannot tell them apart.

![Same volatility very different danger before and after comparison of a symmetric return distribution and a negatively skewed one sharing the identical standard deviation](/imgs/blogs/volatility-and-why-it-is-not-risk-1.png)

This post opens Track B of the series — *measuring risk* — and it has to come first, because before we learn to measure risk well, we have to understand why the standard measure is broken. We are going to build volatility from absolutely nothing: what a standard deviation is, why it became the universal risk number, and what it genuinely does well. Then we are going to take it apart, one crack at a time — its symmetry, its hidden normality assumption, its blindness to permanent loss, its blindness to the path, and the fact that it is not even a constant. By the end you will hold the distinction that separates people who survive from people who blow up: the difference between being **uncertain** and being **at risk**. Because the trader's first job is not to make money — it is to not blow up, and you can only compound if you are still in the game. Mistaking volatility for risk is one of the most reliable ways to leave the game early.

## Foundations: what volatility actually is, from zero

Before we can criticize volatility, we have to be able to compute it, and to do that we have to define every term from the ground up. If you already know what a standard deviation is, skim this; if you don't, this section is the floor everything else stands on.

**Return.** A return is the percentage change in your money over a period. Start a day with \$100,000 and end with \$101,000 and your return was +1%; end with \$99,000 and it was −1%. We write returns as fractions when it is cleaner: +1% is +0.01. The single most important fact about returns is that they **multiply** your wealth: a return *r* turns wealth *W* into *W × (1 + r)*.

**The mean (average) return.** The mean is the plain average you learned in school: add up all your daily returns and divide by how many there were. If a string of days returned +1%, −1%, +0.5%, −0.5%, the mean is 0.25%. The mean tells you the *center* of your returns — where they cluster — but it says nothing about how *spread out* they are around that center. Two assets can have the exact same mean return and feel completely different to own, because one of them barely moves and the other one lurches all over the place. Capturing that spread is the entire job of volatility.

**Deviation.** A deviation is how far a single return sits from the mean. If the mean daily return is 0.25% and today's return was +2%, today's deviation is +1.75%. If today's return was −1.5%, the deviation is −1.75%. Deviations are the raw material of volatility: a series whose returns sit close to the mean has small deviations, and a series whose returns swing wildly has large ones.

**Variance.** Here is the one slightly clever step. To summarize the spread in a single number, you cannot just average the deviations — they cancel out, because the positive ones and negative ones sum to zero by construction (that is what the mean *is*). So instead you **square** every deviation first, which makes them all positive, then take the average of those squared deviations. That average is the **variance**. Squaring is doing something specific and consequential that we will return to with a vengeance: it treats a deviation of +5% and a deviation of −5% as *exactly the same size*, because (+0.05)² and (−0.05)² are both 0.0025. Variance is direction-blind by design.

**Standard deviation — this is volatility.** Variance is in awkward squared units (percent-squared, which means nothing to a human). To get back to normal units, you take the square root of the variance. *That square root is the standard deviation*, and the standard deviation of returns is what we call **volatility**. It is, in one sentence, the typical size of the wiggle around the average return. A volatility of 1% per day means returns typically land within about a percent of the mean on either side; a volatility of 4% per day means they typically swing four times as far.

**Annualizing.** Volatility is usually quoted per year so different assets can be compared on one scale. Because variances of independent days add up, the standard deviation scales with the square root of time: to turn a daily volatility into an annual one, you multiply by the square root of the number of trading days in a year, about √252 ≈ 15.9. So a daily volatility of 1% becomes an annual volatility of roughly 16%. Whenever this post says "16% annualized vol," it means daily wiggles of about 1% accumulated over a year. That √-time scaling will itself become a problem later, because it assumes each day is an independent draw — and real markets are nothing of the sort.

**Why it became *the* number.** Volatility won the job of "risk" for three reasons, all of them about convenience rather than correctness. First, it is a single number, and humans and committees love a single number. Second, it slots cleanly into the math: it is the σ in the Sharpe ratio (return per unit of volatility), in mean-variance portfolio optimization, in the Black–Scholes option formula, in Value-at-Risk. Once an entire apparatus is built on σ, σ becomes the definition of risk by sheer inertia. Third, for the narrow purpose it was invented for — comparing the spread of similar, well-behaved assets — it works. The trouble starts the instant you forget it is a measure of *spread* and start treating it as a measure of *danger*.

#### Worked example: computing the volatility of a \$100,000 account

Let's make a standard deviation concrete with our recurring \$100,000 retail account. Suppose over five days the account posts daily returns of +2%, −1%, +1%, −3%, and +1%.

Step 1 — the mean. Add them: 2 − 1 + 1 − 3 + 1 = 0, divided by 5 = **0% mean daily return**. The account ends roughly where it started.

Step 2 — the deviations from that mean (the mean is 0, so the deviations equal the returns): +2%, −1%, +1%, −3%, +1%.

Step 3 — square each deviation: (2)² = 4, (1)² = 1, (1)² = 1, (3)² = 9, (1)² = 1, in percent-squared units.

Step 4 — the variance is the average of those squares: (4 + 1 + 1 + 9 + 1) / 5 = 16 / 5 = **3.2 percent-squared** per day.

Step 5 — the standard deviation is the square root: √3.2 ≈ **1.79% per day**. Annualize it: 1.79% × √252 ≈ **28.4% per year**.

Now look at what this 1.79% number quietly did. The worst single day was −3% — on \$100,000 that is a \$3,000 loss, and that is the day that actually hurt. But the +2% day (a \$2,000 gain) contributed *more* to the volatility than the −1% days did, because 2² = 4 is larger than 1² = 1. The volatility number was made *bigger* by a good day. Hold that thought; it is the first crack.

*Volatility is the typical size of the move, computed by squaring deviations so that a gain and a loss of the same size count identically — which is the seed of everything wrong with it.*

One more thing worth pausing on, because it explains *why* the number behaves the way it does. There is a free choice hiding in the variance: to make the deviations all positive before averaging, you could have taken their **absolute value** instead of squaring them. The absolute deviation — average |return − mean| — is a perfectly sensible measure of spread, and it has a nicer property: it treats a 3% move as exactly three times as significant as a 1% move, linearly, the way a human would. Squaring does not. Squaring makes a 3% move *nine* times as significant as a 1% move (3² = 9 versus 1² = 1), because it weights by the *square* of the size. So the standard deviation is dominated, disproportionately, by the largest moves in the sample. Statisticians chose squaring anyway, for reasons of mathematical convenience — squared errors give you clean calculus, the variances of independent things add, and it falls out of the normal distribution naturally. But that convenience has a hidden cost for risk: because the biggest moves dominate the squared sum, and because the biggest moves are exactly the ones that are *least* well-described by a few months of data, the volatility number is most sensitive to precisely the part of the distribution it is worst at estimating. The choice that made the math tidy also made the number fragile in the tail.

## Crack one: volatility is symmetric, but danger is not

Return to that squaring step, because it is the original sin. When you square a deviation, you throw away its sign. A +5% surprise and a −5% surprise contribute *the same amount* to the variance, and therefore the same amount to the volatility. The math literally cannot distinguish "I made a surprising amount of money today" from "I lost a surprising amount of money today." To volatility, they are identical events.

This is bizarre when you say it out loud. No human investor on earth feels the same way about a surprise gain and a surprise loss. A surprise gain is a happy problem — you have more money than you expected. A surprise loss is the thing you bought risk management to avoid. And yet the standard risk number weights them equally. A fund manager who had a sudden +30% month would never, ever describe that month as "risky," but volatility records it as a giant spike of risk, exactly as large as a −30% month. The number is measuring something — surprise, uncertainty, dispersion — but it is not measuring *danger*, because danger has a direction and volatility refuses to look at it.

You can repair part of this by splitting the standard deviation into its two halves: the part coming from days *above* the mean (the upside, the good surprises) and the part coming from days *below* the mean (the downside, the losses). The downside half is called the **semideviation** or downside deviation, and it is a genuinely better risk number because it only counts the moves you fear. Figure 3 does exactly this split on a return series that is negatively skewed — many small gains, occasional violent losses — the kind of return stream that vol most badly misrepresents.

![Bar chart splitting one standard deviation into upside semideviation from good days and downside semideviation from bad days showing the total blends both](/imgs/blogs/volatility-and-why-it-is-not-risk-3.png)

The decomposition is exact and a little beautiful. The total volatility of that series is 14.5% annualized. Split it, and the upside semideviation is 8.0% and the downside semideviation is 12.1%. And because variances add, those two halves combine by the Pythagorean rule: √(8.0² + 12.1²) = √(64 + 146.4) = √210.4 ≈ 14.5%. The headline volatility number is the diagonal of a right triangle whose two legs are the good surprises and the bad ones — and it blends them into a single figure that is larger than either, dominated partly by the upside you do not fear. When someone tells you a strategy has "15% volatility," roughly half of that number, in the symmetric case, is being generated by the *good* outcomes. You are being charged risk for your gains.

#### Worked example: how a great month inflates "risk" on the \$10,000,000 book

Take the \$10,000,000 book. Over twelve months it posts eleven quiet months of about +0.5% each, and then one explosive month of +25% — a genuinely fantastic stretch, the kind that makes a career. In dollars: eleven months of about +\$50,000 and one month of +\$2,500,000, ending the year up roughly +\$3,050,000, a +30.5% year. By any sane definition this was a low-*danger* year: the book never had a meaningful losing month. It never came close to a permanent loss.

Now compute its volatility. The monthly mean is about (11 × 0.5% + 25%) / 12 ≈ 2.54%. The eleven quiet months each deviate about −2% from that mean (small squared contributions), but the +25% month deviates a massive +22.5% from the mean — and squaring that gives 22.5² ≈ 506 percent-squared, which dominates the entire variance. The annualized volatility of this book lands around **23%** — a high, alarming-looking number. A risk committee reading "23% volatility" would flag this book as one of the riskier things in the firm. It was, in fact, one of the *safest* — its entire volatility was manufactured by a single spectacular *gain*.

*Volatility charged this book a huge risk premium for the best month it ever had; a measure that flags your winners as danger is measuring the wrong thing.*

This is the first and most fundamental reason volatility is not risk: **risk has a sign and volatility does not.** Survival depends entirely on the left tail — the losses — because of the [asymmetry of losses](/blog/trading/risk-management/the-asymmetry-of-losses-why-a-50-percent-loss-needs-a-100-percent-gain): a −50% drawdown needs a +100% gain to recover, and a −90% needs +900%. The downside is where ruin lives. A risk number that gives equal billing to the upside is, in a precise sense, looking in the wrong direction half the time.

## Crack two: volatility assumes a stable, normal world

There is a second assumption buried inside the volatility number, and it is even more dangerous than the symmetry, because it is invisible. When you collapse an entire distribution of returns down to a single number — its standard deviation — you are implicitly assuming that the standard deviation is *enough to describe the distribution*. And that is only true for one special, well-behaved shape: the **normal distribution**, the bell curve. For a normal distribution, the mean and the standard deviation tell you everything; once you know those two numbers, the whole curve is pinned down, including exactly how often extreme events happen.

Real market returns are not normal. They are **skewed** — usually negatively, meaning the left tail (losses) is longer and fatter than the right tail (gains), which is exactly the dangerous-distribution side of Figure 1. And they are **fat-tailed** (the technical word is leptokurtic), meaning extreme moves happen far, far more often than a bell curve predicts. The bell curve says a five-standard-deviation daily move is essentially impossible — under a normal distribution it is about a 1-in-3.5-million event, which works out to roughly once every 13,900 years of trading days. And yet equity indices realize a five-sigma day every few years. The October 19, 1987 crash, when the S&P 500 fell about 20.5% in a single session, was something like a twenty-standard-deviation event on the prevailing volatility — a move the normal distribution says should not occur even once in the entire age of the universe many times over. It happened on a Monday.

Here is why this guts the volatility number specifically. Volatility is a *complete* description of risk only if the distribution is normal. The moment the real distribution has a fat left tail, the standard deviation systematically *under-counts* the danger, because the danger is concentrated in exactly the rare extreme losses that the standard deviation, computed from mostly-ordinary days, barely registers. The number tells you what a typical week looks like, and a typical week is fine. It tells you almost nothing about the week that matters, the one that hands you a loss the bell curve swore was impossible. This is the [normal distribution trap](/blog/trading/risk-management/fat-tails-and-the-normal-distribution-trap) — and it is worth its own post, because under-counting disasters by orders of magnitude is how risk models stay comforting right up until the collapse. The deeper mathematics of why markets are fat-tailed, and how to model the tail properly, lives in the [probability distributions for markets](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/probability-distributions-for-markets-math-for-quants) treatment; here the working takeaway is enough: **a single volatility number silently assumes a thin-tailed, symmetric world that does not exist.**

#### Worked example: what the normal assumption tells you to expect, and what you get

Take the \$100,000 account in a strategy with a daily volatility of 1% — a calm 16%-annualized profile. Under the normal assumption, a −3% day is a 3-standard-deviation event, expected about once every 740 trading days, call it once every three years. A −5% day is a 5-sigma event, expected once in roughly 13,900 years. So the normal model, looking at the volatility number, tells the risk manager: a single-day loss of \$5,000 is a once-in-three-years event, and a \$5,000 loss... wait, that is the same \$5,000. Let me restate properly: a −3% day costs \$3,000 and "should" happen once every few years; a −5% day costs \$5,000 and "should" essentially never happen.

In reality, strategies with this exact volatility profile print −5% and worse days every few *years*, not every few millennia — off by a factor of several thousand. The \$5,000 loss the model labeled impossible is, empirically, just a bad Tuesday that arrives on a schedule. So the volatility number did not merely mis-estimate the tail; it mis-estimated it by three or four orders of magnitude, and it did so while looking completely accurate about the boring middle of the distribution where 99% of the days live.

*The volatility number is exquisitely accurate about the days that don't matter and catastrophically wrong about the days that do, because it assumes a bell curve and the real tail is nothing like a bell.*

## Crack three: volatility measures uncertainty, not permanent loss

Now we reach the heart of the post, the distinction that everything else has been circling: **volatility measures uncertainty, and risk is the chance of a permanent loss of capital.** These are different questions, and Figure 4 lays them side by side because conflating them is the single most expensive mistake in the discipline.

![Before and after comparison contrasting volatility as a measure of uncertainty against risk as the chance of a permanent loss of capital](/imgs/blogs/volatility-and-why-it-is-not-risk-4.png)

Start with what volatility is asking. Volatility asks: *how much does this value wiggle around its trend?* That is a statement about the **width of the distribution of outcomes** — about your uncertainty. A \$100,000 account that swings up to \$130,000, back down to \$110,000, up to \$125,000 over a year, and ends at \$110,000, was extremely *volatile* and also *up 10%*. It wiggled enormously. It also made money and never came close to ruin. Its high volatility described real uncertainty about where it would be on any given day — but it described no real risk in the sense that matters, because the capital was never permanently impaired.

Now look at what risk is asking. Risk — the thing you actually built a risk-management discipline to control — asks: *how likely am I to lose capital I can never get back?* That is a statement about **ruin**, not about wiggle. And here is the killer: a position can have *low* volatility and *high* risk at the same time. Picture (this is Figure 4's right column) a \$100,000 account that does not lurch around at all but instead creeps quietly downward, losing a fraction of a percent most days, drifting from \$100,000 to \$90,000 to \$70,000 to \$40,000 over a long stretch, and then just... stays there, impaired. Its daily volatility was *low* the entire time — small, calm, undramatic moves. Its risk was as high as it gets: it lost 60% of the capital permanently, which the [recovery math](/blog/trading/risk-management/the-asymmetry-of-losses-why-a-50-percent-loss-needs-a-100-percent-gain) says now needs a +150% gain just to break even. Low volatility, high risk. The number you trusted called it safe the whole way down.

This is not a hypothetical curiosity. It is the exact profile of the most dangerous trades in markets: ones that earn a small, steady, low-volatility return most of the time and then, rarely, lose everything. Selling far out-of-the-money options. Picking up nickels in front of a steamroller. Carry trades. Their volatility is low *by construction* — that smoothness is the product they sell — and their risk is enormous, hiding entirely in the rare event that volatility, computed from all the calm days, never sees. The economics of why these trades pay until they don't is the [variance risk premium](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt) story; what matters here is the measurement failure: **a low volatility number is not evidence of low risk, and can be evidence of the opposite.**

There is also a horizon hiding in here that volatility ignores. The same daily wiggle means something completely different depending on how long you hold and whether you can choose your exit. For a long-term holder of a diversified, unleveraged position, daily volatility is mostly noise that washes out over years — the uncertainty about *today's* price is irrelevant if you are not selling today. For a leveraged trader who can be margin-called tomorrow, that identical daily volatility is an existential threat, because a single bad day can force the exit and convert a temporary drop into a realized, permanent loss. Volatility, measured the same way for both, describes the *asset*; the risk it implies depends on the *holder*. Two people can own the identical position with the identical volatility and one of them is barely exposed to risk while the other is one bad print from ruin — which is, once again, the gap between a property of the distribution and a fact about survival.

The reverse confusion is just as common and just as costly. People flee assets because they are "too volatile" — they panic-sell at the bottom of a drawdown because the wiggle has become unbearable — when the actual risk of permanent loss may be low. Volatility is uncomfortable; it is not the same as dangerous. An investor who can stomach the wiggle and is not forced to sell (no leverage, no margin call, no redemption deadline) experiences a high-volatility, low-risk asset very differently from one who is forced to realize the loss at the worst moment. Whether volatility *becomes* risk depends on whether you are forced to sell — which is a fact about your *financing and your discipline*, not about the asset's standard deviation.

#### Worked example: the quiet ruin versus the loud survivor

Two \$100,000 accounts over one year. Account Q ("quiet") loses 0.3% on 200 of the 250 trading days and is flat the rest — tiny, calm, low-volatility moves, nothing that would ever trip an alarm. Compounding 0.997 two hundred times: 100,000 × 0.997²⁰⁰ ≈ \$54,900. Account Q is down about 45%, which now requires a +82% gain to recover — and it got there so smoothly that its annualized volatility was a sleepy ~5%. By the volatility number, Account Q was one of the *safest* things you could own. It was, in fact, quietly ruined.

Account L ("loud") swings violently: it has thirty days of ±4% moves scattered through the year, a hair-raising ride, but the gains and losses roughly offset and it ends the year at \$101,000, up 1%. Its annualized volatility is a frightening ~28%. By the volatility number, Account L was five-and-a-half times "riskier" than Account Q. In reality Account L preserved capital and Account Q destroyed it. The risk number got the ranking exactly backwards: it called the survivor dangerous and the casualty safe.

*Volatility ranks the loud-but-intact account as far riskier than the quiet-but-ruined one — which is precisely backwards if survival is what you mean by risk.*

## Crack four: volatility is path-blind

There is a fourth, sneakier failure, and it survives even if you fix the first three. Suppose you correctly account for skew and fat tails and downside. You still have a problem, because **volatility is a property of the *distribution* of returns, and it knows nothing about the *order* in which they arrive.** Two assets can draw from the identical pool of daily returns — same mean, same standard deviation, same skew, same everything about the distribution — and yet deliver wildly different experiences, because the *path* differs. And the path is where survival is decided.

Figure 2 makes this undeniable. It shows two assets, each scaled to the *exact same* 16.0% annualized volatility, simulated over three years, both ending at essentially the same value. By the volatility number, and even by the end-of-period return, these two assets are interchangeable. They are not. Asset A is mean-reverting — when it drops it tends to pull back up — so it never digs itself a deep hole; its maximum drawdown is a mild −7%. Asset B trends and then flips regime — it climbs in a self-reinforcing way and then rolls over into a sustained decline — so it carves out one brutal peak-to-trough fall of −50%. Identical volatility. Identical destination. One of them barely scratched you; the other one put you in a hole that needs a +100% gain to climb out of.

![Two account value paths with identical sixteen percent annualized volatility where one suffers a seven percent maximum drawdown and the other a fifty percent drawdown](/imgs/blogs/volatility-and-why-it-is-not-risk-2.png)

The number that *does* capture this difference is the **maximum drawdown** — the largest peak-to-trough fall along the path — and it is the risk you actually feel, the one that gets people fired, gets funds redeemed, and gets traders to do something desperate at the worst possible moment. Volatility, being a single average of squared deviations, throws away all the sequencing information that drawdown depends on. You could literally shuffle the daily returns of Asset B into a random order and get the same volatility every time, while the maximum drawdown would change with every shuffle. Volatility is invariant to the one thing — the order of events — that determines whether you survive the journey. The full anatomy of drawdown, time-underwater, and why the equity curve's worst stretch is the risk that breaks people is its [own post](/blog/trading/risk-management/drawdown-and-the-underwater-curve-the-risk-you-actually-feel); the point here is the indictment of vol: **a measure that is blind to the path is blind to the drawdown, and the drawdown is the risk that ends careers.**

#### Worked example: same volatility, very different survival on the \$10,000,000 book

Run the \$10,000,000 book through the two paths in Figure 2, both at 16% annualized volatility. On Asset A, the worst the book ever sees is a −7% drawdown — at the trough, the book is at about \$9,300,000, down \$700,000 from its peak. Uncomfortable, survivable, recovered quickly; the −7% needs only about a +7.5% gain to make whole. The book is never in danger, no investor redeems, no risk limit is breached, nobody panics.

On Asset B, the *same volatility* produces a −50% drawdown. At the trough the book is at about \$5,000,000 — down \$5,000,000 from its peak. That is a catastrophe in slow motion: the [recovery math](/blog/trading/risk-management/the-asymmetry-of-losses-why-a-50-percent-loss-needs-a-100-percent-gain) now demands a +100% gain — a *doubling* of what is left — just to get back to even. Long before that, the investors have redeemed, the prime broker has cut the leverage, the high-water mark has trapped the manager's fees, and the book is very likely dead. Two books, the same risk number on the report, and one of them is fine while the other is a smoking crater — *because of the path the volatility number could not see.*

*Volatility certified these two books as equally risky on the morning of the worst day; only the drawdown, which tracks the path, told you which one was about to fail.*

## Crack five: volatility is not even constant

The final crack is that we have been talking about *the* volatility as if there were one stable number to measure. There is not. Volatility itself **changes over time**, in two specific and well-documented ways, and a single number papers over both, turning a dynamic, regime-switching quantity into a static fiction.

The first phenomenon is **volatility clustering**: big moves tend to be followed by big moves, and calm tends to be followed by calm. Markets do not deliver their volatility evenly, like a metronome; they deliver it in bursts. A stretch of sleepy, low-volatility days, then suddenly a cluster of violent ones bunched together, then calm again. This is one of the most robust empirical facts in all of finance — it is why volatility is *forecastable* at all, and why the whole GARCH family of models exists. Figure 5 shows a return series with exactly this texture: long quiet passages punctuated by clusters of turbulence, with the stormy stretches shaded.

![Daily return series exhibiting volatility clustering where calm periods and stormy periods bunch together over three years](/imgs/blogs/volatility-and-why-it-is-not-risk-5.png)

The series in Figure 5 has a full-sample volatility of about 16% annualized — a perfectly ordinary number. But that number is true in *no single moment* of the series. During the calm clusters the realized volatility is far below 16%; during the stormy clusters it is far above. Quoting "16%" describes the average of two different worlds the asset lurches between, and describes neither one. If you sized your position for the 16% number, you were over-exposed in the calm (when 16% overstated the risk) and dangerously under-prepared in the storm (when it understated it).

The second phenomenon is **regime shifts**, and Figure 6 makes the lie explicit. It plots a 21-day rolling realized volatility — vol measured over a moving one-month window — for a series that steps between a low-vol regime and a high-vol regime. In the calm regimes the rolling vol sits around 10–12%. In the storm regimes it leaps to 36–42%. And the single full-sample number — the one you would put on the risk report — is 19%, marked by the dashed line. Look at where that dashed line falls: it is *above every calm regime and below every storm regime.* The 19% number is wrong in every single regime the asset actually occupies. It is not even a useful average; it is a value the asset is almost never near.

![Rolling realized volatility stepping between a low volatility regime near ten percent and a high volatility regime above thirty five percent with a single full sample number that is wrong in every regime](/imgs/blogs/volatility-and-why-it-is-not-risk-6.png)

Clustering also quietly breaks the annualization formula we set up in the Foundations. Recall that you turn a daily volatility into an annual one by multiplying by √252 — but that √-time rule rests on an assumption we can now see is false: that each day is an *independent* draw, uncorrelated with the days around it. Volatility clustering is precisely a *violation* of that independence — a turbulent day makes the next day more likely to be turbulent too. When moves are positively correlated through time (as they are during a storm cluster, when a trend feeds on itself), risk accumulates *faster* than √-time, so the √252 number *understates* the true annual risk during exactly the regimes that matter. The clean √-time scaling, like the normal assumption and the symmetry, is one more place where volatility quietly assumes a well-behaved world in order to compress itself into a single number — and one more place where the real world declines to cooperate.

This is why "volatility is not constant" is not a footnote — it is the structural reason a single number cannot do the job. Risk is not a fixed property of an asset that you measure once and write down. It is a *state* that the market moves between, and the state can change faster than any trailing measurement can update. By the time your one-month rolling volatility has caught up to the new storm regime, you have already lived through the part of the storm that does the damage. Whether the past distribution even *predicts* the future one — the stationarity assumption every volatility estimate quietly leans on — is a deep problem in its own right, and it fails exactly when you need it most. The practitioner's move is not to find a better single number; it is to stop expecting a single number to carry the load.

#### Worked example: sizing on the wrong regime's volatility

Suppose you run the \$100,000 account on the asset in Figure 6 and you size your position using a volatility target — a sensible discipline where you scale exposure so the position contributes a fixed amount of risk. You target, say, \$1,000 of daily risk, and you size using the *full-sample* volatility of 19% (a daily vol of about 19% / 15.9 ≈ 1.2%). To put \$1,000 at risk per day at a 1.2% daily move, you hold a position of about \$1,000 / 0.012 ≈ \$83,000.

Now the asset enters a calm regime where realized vol is actually 10% (daily ≈ 0.63%). Your \$83,000 position now risks only about \$83,000 × 0.0063 ≈ \$520 a day — you are *under*-risked, leaving return on the table. Fine, merely suboptimal. But then the asset flips into a storm regime where realized vol is 42% (daily ≈ 2.6%). Your *same* \$83,000 position now risks \$83,000 × 0.026 ≈ \$2,160 a day — more than double your intended budget — at the exact moment the tail is fattest and the drawdowns are deepest. You sized for the average and got crushed in the storm. The correct discipline ([volatility targeting done right](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt) uses the *current* regime's vol, not the full-sample number) is to let your size *fall* as realized vol rises — but that only works if you treat volatility as the moving, regime-switching quantity it is, never as a constant.

*Sizing on a single full-sample volatility over-risks you in the calm and over-exposes you in the storm — the one combination guaranteed to maximize the damage.*

## The most dangerous state: when low vol is the warning sign

Pull the cracks together and you arrive at a genuinely counterintuitive conclusion that every survivor eventually learns: **a low realized volatility reading is often the most dangerous state, not the safest.** This is the calm-before-the-storm mechanism, and Figure 7 traces it.

![Causal flow showing how low realized volatility leads to rising leverage and crowded vol selling that builds fragility until a shock triggers a forced unwind and a volatility storm](/imgs/blogs/volatility-and-why-it-is-not-risk-7.png)

The mechanism is reflexive — the calm *creates* the conditions for the storm. It runs like this. A long stretch of low realized volatility is read, by both humans and risk models, as safety. So two things quietly happen. First, **leverage creeps up**: vol-targeting strategies and risk models mechanically permit larger positions when measured volatility is low (if the risk per dollar is small, you are allowed more dollars), so exposure builds across the system precisely *because* vol is low. Second, **vol-selling pays**: in a calm market, shorting volatility — selling options, selling variance, the [short-vol carry trade](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt) — earns a steady, attractive return, so more and more capital piles into the same trade. Both forces converge on the same outcome: the position becomes **crowded and fragile** — the same trade, at maximum size, sitting on top of thin liquidity, with everyone holding it for the same reason. The calm did not remove the risk; it *concentrated* it, and hid it.

Then any shock arrives — and it does not need to be a big one, because the calm built the fuel and the shock is only the spark. The crowded, levered, short-vol position must be unwound all at once: margin calls force selling, stop-outs trigger more selling, and the [exit becomes a stampede](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game) through a door that everyone is trying to use simultaneously. Realized volatility explodes — not in spite of the preceding calm but *because of it.* The deepest, fastest losses in market history come disproportionately out of the calmest tape. Volatility, the number, was at its lowest right before the catastrophe, telling everyone they had never been safer. It was measuring the calm correctly and the danger not at all.

This is the deepest reason volatility is not risk. Not only does it fail to *measure* danger — in this reflexive setting, low volatility is *causally upstream* of danger. A market that has been calm for a long time is a market in which fragility has had a long time to build. The risk number going down is, past a point, a reason for concern, not comfort. The survivors are the ones who, when the risk report is at its most reassuring, ask the uncomfortable question: *what has all this calm let everyone build up, and what happens when it has to come undone at once?*

## Common misconceptions

**"Low volatility means low risk."** This is the central error, and we have now seen it fail four different ways. A quietly-bleeding account can lose 45% at a sleepy 5% volatility (the \$100,000 quiet-ruin example). A short-vol carry trade earns its low volatility by construction and hides its risk entirely in the rare tail. And in the reflexive case of Figure 7, sustained low volatility is causally *upstream* of the biggest blowups. Low vol is sometimes low risk and sometimes the lowest-risk-looking moment before the worst loss — the number alone cannot tell you which.

**"Volatility and risk are basically the same thing."** They answer different questions. Volatility asks how much an outcome wiggles (uncertainty); risk asks how likely you are to suffer a permanent loss of capital (ruin). A \$100,000 account that swings to \$130,000 and back to \$110,000 is highly volatile and was never at meaningful risk; a \$100,000 account that drifts calmly to \$40,000 and stays there had low volatility and was ruined. The two numbers can rank the same pair of assets in *opposite* order, as the loud-survivor-versus-quiet-casualty example showed.

**"If I annualize and report the standard deviation, I have measured the risk."** You have measured the *spread*, under the assumption that the spread is enough — which is only true for a symmetric, thin-tailed, stationary normal distribution, and real returns are none of those. The standard deviation under-counts a fat left tail by orders of magnitude: it called a \$5,000 single-day loss a once-in-13,900-years event when the real frequency is once every few years. A volatility number is exquisitely precise about the boring middle and silent about the tail that matters.

**"Two assets with the same volatility carry the same risk."** Only if you also ignore the path, and the path is where drawdown lives. Figure 2's two assets had the *identical* 16% volatility and the *identical* end value, yet one had a −7% maximum drawdown and the other a −50% one — the difference between a non-event and a career-ending crater. Volatility is invariant to the order of returns; drawdown is not; survival depends on the order.

**"A high-volatility asset is too risky to own."** Volatility is discomfort, not danger. Whether the wiggle *becomes* a permanent loss depends on whether you are *forced to sell* at the bottom — which is a fact about your leverage, your margin, and your redemption terms, not about the asset's standard deviation. An unleveraged long-term holder who can ride out the wiggle faces far less risk from a volatile asset than a levered holder who gets a margin call at the trough. Risk is co-determined by the position *and* the financing, and volatility sees only the position.

**"Volatility is useless, then."** No — it is useful *within its lane*. For comparing the dispersion of similar, well-behaved assets; for scaling positions day to day so each contributes comparable risk; for pricing options; as a fast, cheap first read on how much something moves. The error is not *using* volatility; it is mistaking it for a complete measure of danger and forgetting to switch to drawdown and tail measures the moment survival is the question.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Volmageddon, February 5, 2018.** The cleanest case study in the entire post. For most of 2017, equity-index realized volatility was historically *low* — and that calm was the whole investment thesis of a crowded short-volatility trade. Inverse-VIX products like XIV paid a steady, smooth, low-volatility return by being short vol, and capital poured in precisely *because* the risk number was so quiet. On February 5, 2018 the VIX roughly doubled in a day, jumping about 20 points to close near 37.3 — the largest one-day percentage rise in its history. The forced rebalancing of the short-vol products turned the move into a reflexive cascade, and XIV lost roughly 96% of its net asset value after the close and was terminated. The volatility number had been at its most reassuring right before the detonation. This is Figure 7's mechanism playing out in real dollars and real dates. The full anatomy of that blow-up is its [own case study](/blog/trading/options-volatility/case-study-volmageddon-2018-and-the-short-vol-blowup); the lesson here is that *low realized vol was the setup, not the safety.*

**COVID, February–March 2020.** A demonstration that volatility is a regime, not a constant. The market spent early 2020 in a calm, low-vol regime, and then in a matter of weeks the VIX went from the high teens to a record close of **82.69** on March 16, 2020, while the S&P 500 fell about 34% peak-to-trough in the fastest bear market on record. No trailing volatility estimate could have updated fast enough to size you correctly through that regime shift — by the time the one-month rolling vol had caught up to the storm, the −34% drawdown had already happened. The full-sample volatility of 2020 is a number that describes neither the calm of January nor the chaos of March; it is the 19%-that-is-wrong-everywhere of Figure 6, writ large.

**The yen-carry unwind, August 5, 2024.** Another low-vol-into-storm reflexive cascade, and a recent one. A crowded, levered funding-carry trade — borrow cheap yen, buy higher-yielding assets — had built up over a long calm stretch in which its realized volatility looked benign. When the funding conditions shifted, the crowded position had to unwind all at once: the Nikkei fell about 12.4% in a single session, its worst day since 1987, and the VIX spiked intraday to about 65.7. The calm had let the leverage and crowding build (Figure 7's left side); the unwind delivered the storm (its right side). The trade's low historical volatility was, once again, a measure of how much fragility had been allowed to accumulate, not a measure of safety.

The thread through all three is identical and is the whole thesis: **the risk numbers were comforting right up until the collapse, because they were measuring the calm and not the danger.** In each case the volatility was low or falling — symmetric, normal-assuming, path-blind, regime-ignoring, reflexively dangerous — and the people who survived were the ones who did not mistake that low number for low risk.

## The volatility playbook

Volatility is a tool. Used in its lane it is genuinely useful; used as a stand-in for danger it will get you killed. Here is how to hold both truths at once.

**When volatility is a fine proxy for risk — use it freely:**
- **Comparing similar, well-behaved assets.** Ranking the dispersion of two diversified equity funds, or two liquid currency pairs, over a stable period — vol is a fast, cheap, fair comparison.
- **Day-to-day position scaling.** Sizing so each position contributes comparable risk works well *as long as you use the current regime's realized vol*, let your size fall as vol rises, and re-estimate constantly. Vol-targeting is a legitimate discipline; static vol-targeting is not.
- **Pricing and hedging derivatives.** Implied volatility is the right language for options, where the symmetric, distributional view is exactly what you want. Leave the Greeks to the [options series](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt).
- **A first, cheap read.** As the opening glance at "how much does this thing move," before you do the real work.

**When to stop trusting volatility and switch to survival measures — the moment any of these is true:**
- **The question is survival, not comparison.** If you are asking "can this end me?", volatility is the wrong tool. Switch to **maximum drawdown** and **time underwater** — the path-aware measures of how deep the hole gets and how long you stay in it.
- **The return stream is skewed or has a hidden tail.** Anything that earns a smooth, steady return most of the time and could lose a lot rarely — short options, carry, "picking up nickels" — has low volatility *by design* and large risk in the tail. Use **downside semideviation**, and **tail measures** (Value-at-Risk's honest cousin, [expected shortfall](/blog/trading/risk-management/fat-tails-and-the-normal-distribution-trap), which averages the losses beyond the cliff), never the symmetric standard deviation.
- **You are or could be forced to sell.** If you carry leverage, margin, or redemption deadlines, the asset's wiggle can be *converted* into a permanent loss at the worst moment. Then the relevant risk is your **drawdown-to-margin-call distance**, not your volatility.
- **The market has been calm for a long time.** Past a point, treat persistently low realized vol as a warning that fragility is building — ask what leverage and crowding the calm has permitted (Figure 7), and reduce, do not add.

**The three rules to carry out of this post:**
1. **Separate uncertain from risky.** Before reacting to any wiggle, ask the survival question explicitly: *is this just movement, or is this a path toward a loss I cannot get back?* Sell discomfort and you sell at the bottom; survive permanent-loss risk and you stay in the game.
2. **Never quote a single volatility number for a survival decision.** Pair every vol figure with a maximum drawdown and a tail estimate. If those three disagree — low vol, big drawdown, fat tail — believe the drawdown and the tail.
3. **Distrust the calm.** When the risk report is at its most reassuring, that is exactly when to ask what everyone has been allowed to build up while the number was low.

The deepest takeaway is the one this series keeps returning to. Risk management is the discipline of *staying in the game*, and you cannot manage what you are mis-measuring. Volatility is the number the whole industry hands you and calls "risk," and it is symmetric when danger has a sign, normal-assuming when the tail is fat, uncertainty-measuring when ruin is the question, path-blind when drawdown decides survival, and constant in your spreadsheet when it is a regime in the market. Knowing precisely *how* it misleads is what lets you use it where it is honest and reach for drawdown and tail measures where it is not — which is to say, exactly where survival is on the line.
