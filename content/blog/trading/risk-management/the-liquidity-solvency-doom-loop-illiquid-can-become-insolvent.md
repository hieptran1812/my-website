> [!important]
> **One sentence:** A firm that is solvent but illiquid can be *forced* into actual insolvency — the doom loop is that being unable to sell at fair value forces a fire sale, the fire-sale price becomes the new mark, the mark eats your capital, you look insolvent, your funders flee, and now you have to sell even more.
> - **Liquidity ≠ solvency.** Solvent means assets are worth more than what you owe. Liquid means you can turn assets into cash *right now*. You can be one without the other.
> - A liquidity crisis is a **timing** problem; a solvency crisis is a **value** problem. The doom loop is the bridge that turns the first into the second.
> - The damage is done at one specific moment: **the forced sale**. A paper loss only becomes a permanent loss when you are made to realize it.
> - **SVB 2023** is the cleanest modern case: good government bonds, a huge unrealized loss, a deposit run that forced the sale, and a solvent-looking bank gone in days.
> - The whole defensive game is **never being a forced seller**: hold liquidity buffers, term out your funding, size so a margin call never controls your timing.

A trader can be *completely right* about every position they own and still be wiped out by the end of the week. That sentence sounds like a contradiction — if you are right, you make money, surely? — but it is the single most expensive lesson in finance, and people relearn it in every crisis. You can own assets that are genuinely worth more than you paid, that will pay you back in full if you simply hold them, and still lose everything. Not because you were wrong about *value*, but because you ran out of *cash* at the wrong moment, and someone else got to decide when you sold.

This is the liquidity-solvency doom loop, and it is the mechanism behind a startling fraction of the failures in market history — from a tiny levered options trader getting a 9 a.m. margin call, to Lehman Brothers, to Silicon Valley Bank vanishing over a long weekend in March 2023. The shape is always the same. A firm has good assets but cannot sell them at fair value today. To raise cash, it dumps them at fire-sale prices. Those fire-sale prices become the official mark on the rest of the book. The mark eats the capital cushion. Now the firm *looks* insolvent — assets below liabilities — even if its assets would have paid par at maturity. Its lenders and depositors see the thin capital, panic, and pull their funding. The firm has to sell still more. And so the loop closes, each turn tighter than the last, until there is nothing left to sell.

The figure below is the whole post in one picture. Read it top to bottom, then follow the dashed red arrow back up: every step makes the next one worse, and the last step feeds straight back into the first. That feedback — *forced selling causes the very insolvency that forces more selling* — is what makes this a doom loop rather than a one-time setback. Our job in this post is to take that loop apart, joint by joint, with real dollar arithmetic, so you can see exactly where it can be broken. Because it can be broken — and the place to break it is *before* the first forced sale, not during it.

![The liquidity-solvency doom loop drawn as a closed vertical cycle: an illiquid firm is forced into a fire sale, which marks down its capital, makes it look insolvent, makes funders flee, and forces it to sell even more, looping back to the top](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-solvency-doom-loop-illiquid-can-become-insolvent-1.png)

This is, at heart, a survival story, which is the spine of this entire series: *you only get to compound if you are still in the game tomorrow.* A liquidity failure does not just dent your returns; it ends the game with assets you still believe in sitting in someone else's hands. The asymmetry of losses we keep coming back to — that a −50% drawdown needs a +100% gain to recover, that ruin is absorbing — applies with extra force here, because the doom loop manufactures drawdowns out of positions that were never supposed to lose money. Survive the liquidity loop, and a temporary mark recovers on its own. Get caught in it, and a temporary mark becomes a permanent epitaph.

## Foundations: liquidity, solvency, and why they are not the same thing

Before we can build the loop, we need four words defined from zero, because almost everyone uses them loosely and the loop lives precisely in the gap between them.

**Solvency** is about *value*. You are solvent if the total value of everything you own (your assets) is greater than the total of everything you owe (your liabilities). The difference — assets minus liabilities — is your **equity**, also called your **capital** or your **cushion**. If you own \$10,000,000 of assets and owe \$9,200,000, your equity is \$800,000, and you are solvent. If a fall in asset values pushes assets below \$9,200,000, your equity goes negative and you are **insolvent**: you literally do not own enough to cover what you owe. Solvency is a statement about the *level* of value.

**Liquidity** is about *timing and conversion*. You are liquid if you can turn your assets into cash quickly, at or near their fair value, *right now*, in the size you need. A pile of cash is perfectly liquid. A 10-year government bond is highly liquid — there is a deep, continuous market, and you can sell millions in minutes at a price very close to the screen. A boutique commercial building, a stake in a private company, or a thinly traded corporate bond is *illiquid*: there are few buyers, the spread between what a buyer will pay and what you think it is worth is wide, and selling in a hurry means selling cheap. Liquidity is a statement about *speed of conversion to cash at fair value*.

Here is the crucial point the whole post hangs on: **these two are independent.** Lay them out as a two-by-two and all four corners exist in the real world:

- **Solvent and liquid** — the healthy state. Good assets, plenty of ready cash, more than enough to cover what you owe. Most firms most of the time.
- **Insolvent and liquid** — you have cash on hand, but your assets are genuinely worth less than your debts. A failing business that is burning a war chest. Time does not save you; the hole is real.
- **Solvent but illiquid** — *this is the dangerous corner, and the entire subject of this post.* Your assets are worth more than your liabilities (you are solvent), but you cannot convert them to cash fast enough to meet a near-term demand. You are right about value and short on cash at the same time. A bank holding good long bonds against deposits that can leave tomorrow lives here; so does a fund with a great private-equity stake and a redemption due Friday.
- **Insolvent and illiquid** — the graveyard. You owe more than you own *and* you cannot raise cash. This is where the doom loop deposits you, and once you are here there is no clever trade out — only a rescue, a restructuring, or a failure.

The names matter because regulators, lenders, and counterparties make life-or-death decisions based on which corner they think you're in, and they often guess wrong under pressure. A solvent-but-illiquid firm denied a loan dies needlessly; an insolvent firm handed a loan just burns the loan too. The doom loop's signature trick is to *move you between corners faster than anyone can re-diagnose you* — you enter a crisis demonstrably solvent and exit it demonstrably insolvent, with the move happening in the few hours it takes to force a sale.

The reason "solvent but illiquid" is so dangerous is that it does not stay put. Left alone, it is survivable — the assets pay off if you can just wait. But under pressure, it does not get to wait. The doom loop is the machine that drives a firm from the third corner ("solvent but illiquid") into the fourth ("insolvent and illiquid"), and it does so through one specific, irreversible act: **the forced sale.**

#### Worked example: solvent but illiquid, in dollars

![A solvent-but-illiquid balance sheet with ten million dollars of assets, nine point two million dollars of fixed liabilities, and an eight hundred thousand dollar equity cushion, then re-marked at fair and fire-sale values until equity goes negative](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-solvency-doom-loop-illiquid-can-become-insolvent-5.png)

> Here is a small fund with a clean, simple book, in concrete numbers.
>
> - **Assets:** \$10,000,000 of high-quality bonds. They will pay back par in full at maturity. Held to maturity, they are worth \$10,000,000.
> - **Liabilities:** \$9,200,000 borrowed (short-term financing and client cash that can be withdrawn).
> - **Equity (cushion):** \$10,000,000 − \$9,200,000 = **\$800,000**. That is an 8% cushion: assets are 8% above liabilities.
>
> This firm is **solvent**: it owns \$800,000 more than it owes. But its assets are **illiquid in size right now** — it cannot sell \$5,000,000 of them this afternoon without moving the price hard against itself. A lender suddenly demands \$2,000,000 back by tomorrow. The firm has the *value* — it owns \$10,000,000 of good bonds — but not the *cash*, and not the time to sell calmly. It is solvent but illiquid. Nothing is wrong with its assets. Everything is wrong with its timing.
>
> *Solvency tells you whether you would survive given infinite time; liquidity tells you whether you survive the next 24 hours — and the market only ever lets you keep the second one.*

With those four words pinned down, we can build the loop one joint at a time. We'll start where the trouble starts: the moment a paper loss is forced to become a real one.

## The hinge of the whole thing: a paper loss is not a real loss until you sell

Everything downstream in the doom loop depends on one deceptively simple fact, so let's nail it first. **A decline in the marked value of an asset you still hold is a paper loss. It becomes a real, permanent loss only when you sell.** Hold the asset, and a temporary dip can fully recover — most obviously for a bond, which is *contractually* worth par at maturity no matter how its price wobbles in between. Sell into the dip, and you have crystallized the loss: you have swapped a temporarily-cheap asset for permanently-fewer dollars, and there is no path back.

This is the difference between a *temporary* illiquidity discount and a *permanent* loss of capital. When you cannot sell at fair value, the market offers you a discount — a fire-sale price below what the asset is truly worth. That discount is not a loss yet. It is an *option* the market is forcing on you: realize the loss now, or hold and wait. If you can hold, the discount evaporates as the asset pulls back to its fundamental value. If you are forced to sell, the discount becomes your loss, forever.

![A bond bought at par dips below par after rates rise, then pulls back to par at maturity; holding to maturity realizes zero loss while a forced sale at the trough crystallizes a permanent loss per one hundred dollars of face](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-solvency-doom-loop-illiquid-can-become-insolvent-2.png)

The chart shows it cleanly. A bond is bought at par (\$100 per \$100 of face). Rates rise, so its mark-to-market price — what you'd get if you sold today — sags below par for a couple of years. But it is a *bond*: at maturity it repays exactly \$100. So the price *must* pull back to par as maturity approaches, and the dip in the middle is, by construction, temporary. Two paths split from that dip. **Hold** (the green dot at maturity): you collect par, your capital loss is \$0, the whole dip was noise. **Forced sale** at the trough (the red dot): you crystallize the full discount as a permanent loss per \$100 of face. Same bond. Same fundamentals. The only difference is *who controlled the timing of the sale* — and that is the difference between zero loss and a wound that never heals.

#### Worked example: the same bond, held versus force-sold

> Take a 10-year government bond bought at par with a 1.5% annual coupon — exactly the kind of long-duration, low-coupon paper that flooded balance sheets when rates were near zero in 2020–2021. You buy \$1,000,000 of face at par, so you pay \$1,000,000.
>
> Now rates rise and the prevailing yield on a comparable bond climbs to 4.5%. The price of *your* bond — the present value of its remaining cash flows discounted at the new 4.5% yield — drops to about **\$76.26 per \$100 of face**. (You can verify it by discounting ten 1.5% coupons and the par repayment at 4.5%; we plot the full curve in the next figure.) So on a \$1,000,000 position:
>
> - **If you hold to maturity:** you collect every 1.5% coupon and \$1,000,000 of par at the end. Your capital loss is **\$0**. You earned a low yield, sure, but you lost no principal.
> - **If you are forced to sell today** at \$76.26: you receive \$762,600. You have crystallized a loss of \$1,000,000 − \$762,600 = **\$237,400**, or about −23.7% of the position. Permanently.
>
> Nothing about the bond changed. The issuer is just as creditworthy; it will still repay par. The entire \$237,400 difference is the *forced-seller penalty* — the price of having someone else control your timing.
>
> *The bond does not care whether you panic; the loss exists only if you let the market make you sell while it is cheap.*

This is the joint that the doom loop attacks. The loop's whole purpose is to *force* the sale — to remove your ability to hold — so that a temporary, recoverable, paper discount becomes a real, permanent, capital-destroying loss. Understand this one hinge and the rest of the loop is just the machinery that turns it.

## Mark-to-market: how a paper loss leaks onto the balance sheet anyway

If a paper loss is not a real loss until you sell, you might think a solvent-but-illiquid firm is safe as long as it holds. It isn't — and the reason is **mark-to-market accounting**, the practice of valuing assets at their current market price rather than what you paid or what they'll be worth at maturity. Mark-to-market is honest and useful: it tells you what your book is worth *if you had to liquidate today*. But in a doom loop it becomes the transmission belt that carries a fire-sale price from one corner of the market onto everyone's balance sheet.

Here is the mechanism, concretely. When *anyone* sells an asset, that trade prints a price. If a distressed holder dumps a bond at \$76, that \$76 is now the observed market price — and every other holder of that bond (and of similar bonds) must mark *their* holdings to \$76 too, even though they have not sold and have no intention of selling. They have not realized a loss. But their balance sheet now *shows* one. Their reported equity drops. And the people who fund them — lenders, counterparties, depositors, regulators — react to the reported number, not to the philosophical distinction between realized and unrealized.

![The same 10-year 1.5% coupon bond shown two ways as yields rise from 1.5% to 5.5%: hold-to-maturity value stays flat at par while mark-to-market value falls steeply, with the widening wedge between them as the unrealized loss](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-solvency-doom-loop-illiquid-can-become-insolvent-3.png)

The figure draws the wedge. The green line is the **hold-to-maturity** value of our bond: flat at par, \$100, because if you wait you get par regardless of where yields go. The blue line is the **mark-to-market** value: the price you'd get selling today, which falls steeply as the prevailing yield rises. At a 4.5% yield the mark is about \$76 — a roughly \$24 unrealized loss per \$100 of face that exists *only on the mark-to-market line*. The red wedge between the two lines is the whole drama of the doom loop: it is a loss that is completely unreal if you can hold, and completely real if you are forced to sell. The wider that wedge, the more a forced sale will hurt, and the more your reported equity diverges from your fundamental equity. This exact shape — a long, low-coupon book sitting underwater on a mark-to-market basis while still money-good to maturity — is what brought down Silicon Valley Bank, which we'll dissect in the case study section. The wider the gap between an asset's hold value and its sell-today value, the more dangerous that asset is to hold with runnable funding, regardless of how safe its credit is, because the whole gap converts to a realized loss the instant you're forced to sell.

The cruel twist is that mark-to-market makes the loop *self-feeding*. The first distressed seller prints a low price. That low mark damages the reported equity of every similar holder. Some of *those* holders are now too thin on capital to keep their positions, or get a margin call against the lower mark, so *they* become forced sellers too. Their selling prints an even lower price. And around it goes. The accounting that was supposed to give an honest snapshot instead synchronizes everyone's distress, which is exactly how an idiosyncratic problem at one firm becomes a market-wide fire sale. (This is also why correlation jumps toward one in a crisis — every levered holder is forced to sell the same things at the same time. We treat that failure mode in depth in [when correlation goes to one](/blog/trading/risk-management/when-correlation-goes-to-one-the-diversification-that-vanishes-in-a-crisis).)

## Liquidity crisis versus solvency crisis — and the bridge between them

We now have enough to draw the distinction that experts argue about in every crisis: *is this a liquidity crisis or a solvency crisis?* It matters enormously, because the cures are opposite. The cure for a liquidity crisis is **time and cash** — a loan, a backstop, a buyer of last resort — and once the panic passes the firm is fine because its assets were always good. The cure for a solvency crisis is **new capital or a wind-down** — there is a real hole in the balance sheet, and no amount of lending fills it, you can only refill the equity or shut the thing down. Pour liquidity into a truly insolvent firm and you just lose the liquidity too. Refuse liquidity to a merely illiquid firm and you murder a solvent business.

![A two-panel comparison: a liquidity crisis is a cash-flow timing problem where good assets cannot be sold fast enough, while a solvency crisis is a balance-sheet problem where assets are worth less than liabilities, with forced selling as the bridge between them](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-solvency-doom-loop-illiquid-can-become-insolvent-4.png)

The figure lays the two side by side. On the left, the **liquidity crisis**: the *cause* is that good assets can't be sold today at fair value, the *symptom* is that cash owed now exceeds cash on hand now — a pure timing mismatch — and crucially, *given time*, the assets pay par and the firm is fine. It was solvent all along. On the right, the **solvency crisis**: the *cause* is that assets are genuinely worth less than liabilities, the *symptom* is that total assets fall below total liabilities at any honest price, and *time does not help* — you must recapitalize, restructure, or fail.

The doom loop is the **bridge** that carries a firm from the left panel to the right panel. A firm starts in a liquidity crisis — solvent, but short on cash and unable to sell calmly. The forced fire sale crystallizes losses and re-marks the book at fire-sale prices. Those marks eat the capital cushion. And the firm crosses the bridge: it is now, on paper and then in reality, in a solvency crisis. *The forced realization of a temporary loss is the mechanism that converts illiquidity into insolvency.* This is the single most important idea in the post: a liquidity problem is not "a smaller version of" a solvency problem — it is a *different* problem that the doom loop *transforms into* a solvency problem if you let it run.

This is also why the honest answer to "liquidity or solvency?" in the middle of a crisis is often *"it started as liquidity and is becoming solvency as we speak."* That ambiguity is not a failure of analysis; it is the doom loop doing its work in real time. The faster a backstop arrives, the more likely the firm is still on the liquidity side of the bridge and can be saved. The longer everyone debates, the more forced sales occur, and the more the firm drifts onto the solvency side — at which point the cheap rescue is no longer possible and only an expensive recapitalization or a failure remains.

#### Worked example: walking the \$10,000,000 book across the bridge

> Return to our fund: \$10,000,000 of good bonds, \$9,200,000 of liabilities, \$800,000 of equity (an 8% cushion). It is solvent. A lender demands \$2,000,000 back tomorrow. The firm cannot raise \$2,000,000 by calmly selling at fair value — the book is illiquid in that size on that timeline. So it is in a **liquidity crisis**: solvent, but cash-short. Watch it cross the bridge.
>
> **Stage 1 — book value (hold).** Assets marked at hold-to-maturity value: \$10,000,000. Equity = \$10,000,000 − \$9,200,000 = **+\$800,000.** Solvent.
>
> **Stage 2 — fair mark-to-market.** Rates have risen, so a fair market price marks the assets at \$9,400,000 (a 6% unrealized discount). Equity = \$9,400,000 − \$9,200,000 = **+\$200,000.** Still solvent — thin, but the firm owns more than it owes. A 6% drawdown needs only a +6.4% recovery to undo (using the recovery math `g = d / (1 − d)`), and the bonds pull back to par on their own if held.
>
> **Stage 3 — fire-sale mark.** To raise the \$2,000,000 *now*, the firm dumps bonds into a thin market at fire-sale prices, and the whole book re-marks at \$8,600,000 (a 14% discount). Equity = \$8,600,000 − \$9,200,000 = **−\$600,000.** **Insolvent.** Assets are now below liabilities. The firm crossed the bridge — and it did so not because its assets got worse, but because it was *forced to mark them at the price of a forced sale.*
>
> *The exact same bonds that left the firm solvent with \$800,000 to spare left it insolvent by \$600,000 — the only thing that changed was that the market got to set the sale price instead of the calendar.*

Notice the arithmetic asymmetry the series keeps hammering. Going from +\$800,000 to +\$200,000 to −\$600,000 is a slide of \$1,400,000 in equity on a book whose *fundamental* value never fell below \$10,000,000. That \$1,400,000 was manufactured entirely by the forced sale and the marks it printed. None of it had to happen. This is the whole reason "never be a forced seller" is the punchline of the post — being a forced seller is the act that converts a manageable timing problem into a fatal value problem.

## The funding run: why the timing mismatch is the real killer

So far we've treated the demand for cash as a single event ("a lender wants \$2,000,000"). In a real doom loop it is not one event — it is a *run*, and runs have a terrible arithmetic of their own: the demand for cash **accelerates** while your ability to raise cash at fair value **stays slow and capped**. The gap between those two is the liquidity hole, and once it opens past your capacity, you are a forced seller whether you like it or not.

Why does the demand accelerate? Because funding is a coordination game with a self-fulfilling structure. Every short-term lender and every depositor knows that if *enough* of the others pull their money, the firm will be forced to sell at fire-sale prices and may not be able to repay everyone — so the rational move, if you suspect others are leaving, is to leave *first*, before the firm runs dry. That logic compounds: each withdrawal makes the firm look weaker, which scares the next funder, who withdraws, which scares the next. A run does not need anyone to be malicious or even wrong; it just needs each participant to correctly anticipate the others. (This is the strategic, game-theoretic core of bank runs and crowded exits; for the formal version see [crowded trades and the exit game](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game).)

Why does your ability to raise cash stay slow? Because **market depth is finite.** You cannot sell your whole book at fair value in an afternoon — selling pressure moves the price against you, so each additional dollar you raise comes at a worse and worse price. Selling slowly preserves the price but takes time you don't have; selling fast raises cash now but at fire-sale discounts. Either way, your fair-value fundraising capacity is *capped per unit time*, while the run's demand for cash is *compounding*. Those two curves are on a collision course.

![A funding run shown as two cumulative curves over ten days: cash demanded by fleeing funders accelerates and saturates toward the full book size while cash raisable at fair value rises slowly and caps out, with the widening gap marked as the forced-seller trap](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-solvency-doom-loop-illiquid-can-become-insolvent-6.png)

The chart makes the collision visible. The red line is cumulative cash *demanded* by fleeing funders — it accelerates early and saturates toward the full \$10,000,000 book as the run gathers everyone in. The green line is cumulative cash the firm can *raise by selling at fair value* — slow, roughly linear, and capped by market depth at a few million dollars. The amber region between them is the **liquidity hole**: the cash the firm owes but cannot raise honestly. Once that hole opens past a critical width (here, around day 4, where it crosses \$2,000,000), the firm faces a binary choice — dump assets at fire-sale prices, or default. Both feed the doom loop. The fire-sale option marks down the book and triggers the solvency cascade; the default option *is* the failure. There is no third door once the curves have crossed, which is why the entire defensive discipline is about making sure they never cross.

#### Worked example: a \$100,000 retail account gets its own private run

> The doom loop is not only a big-institution phenomenon — a leveraged retail trader runs the identical machine in miniature, except the "funder" is the broker and the "run" is a margin call. Start with a \$100,000 account. You buy \$300,000 of an asset on margin — 3:1 leverage — borrowing \$200,000 from your broker. Your equity is \$300,000 − \$200,000 = \$100,000.
>
> The position is solid long-term, but it's in a thin, illiquid name. It drops 20% to \$240,000. Your equity is now \$240,000 − \$200,000 = **\$40,000**. Your broker's maintenance margin requires equity of at least 25% of the position value: 25% × \$240,000 = \$60,000. You're short by \$60,000 − \$40,000 = **\$20,000**. That is your margin call — your broker's version of a funder demanding cash *now*.
>
> You believe the asset will recover; held, it might well be worth more than \$300,000 again. But you cannot *hold* — you don't have \$20,000 of spare cash to post, and the broker won't wait. So the broker becomes the forced seller: it liquidates part of your position into the thin market at a fire-sale price, say another 8% below the screen. You've crystallized losses you never chose to take, the lower print drops the mark further, and if it trips the maintenance level again, the broker sells *more*. Your private doom loop. (The mechanics of margin calls and the funding spiral get the full treatment in [funding and margin spirals](/blog/trading/risk-management/funding-and-margin-spirals-when-your-lender-becomes-your-risk).)
>
> *Leverage doesn't just amplify your gains and losses — it hands a stranger the keys to your timing, and the doom loop is what they do with those keys.*

The lesson buried in the run is that **liquidity risk is a timing risk, and timing is everything.** A firm with the exact same assets and liabilities survives or dies based purely on whether it can match the *timing* of its cash needs to the *timing* of its cash sources. Match them — short obligations against liquid assets, long obligations against illiquid ones — and no run can force you. Mismatch them — short obligations funding illiquid assets — and you have built the doom loop into your own balance sheet, just waiting for the spark.

## Why leverage makes the loop inescapable

We keep mentioning leverage, so let's make its role explicit, because leverage is the accelerant that turns a survivable liquidity squeeze into an inescapable doom loop. Leverage does two things to the loop simultaneously, and both are fatal.

First, leverage **shrinks the cushion** that absorbs the mark-to-market hit. An unlevered investor who owns assets outright can ride any paper loss down to zero and back — they have no lender to answer to, so a temporary mark is genuinely temporary, and they are *never* a forced seller. Add leverage and you've introduced a liability that does not shrink when your assets do. Our \$10,000,000 book with \$9,200,000 of liabilities had only an 8% cushion; an 8% fire-sale discount wipes it out. The same book funded with \$5,000,000 of liabilities has a 50% cushion and shrugs off the same discount. **The thinner the cushion, the smaller the fire-sale discount needed to push you insolvent**, and the more easily the loop ignites.

Second, leverage **manufactures the forced sale** through margin. When you borrow against assets, the lender marks those assets to market continuously and demands more collateral the moment the mark falls — a margin call. That margin call *is* the forced-sale trigger. It removes your ability to hold at precisely the moment holding would save you. An unlevered holder facing a 20% paper loss does nothing and waits; a levered holder facing the same 20% loss gets a call, can't meet it, and is liquidated into the dip. Leverage converts "a paper loss I can wait out" into "a margin call I cannot meet," and that conversion is the doom loop's on-switch.

#### Worked example: how much fire-sale discount it takes to kill you, by leverage

> Take a \$10,000,000 asset book and fund it three ways. The question: what fire-sale discount on the assets wipes out the equity (pushes you insolvent)?
>
> - **Low leverage — \$3,000,000 liabilities, \$7,000,000 equity (70% cushion).** You go insolvent only when assets fall below \$3,000,000, a **70% fire-sale discount.** Essentially unkillable by a liquidity event; you can ride almost anything to maturity.
> - **Moderate leverage — \$7,000,000 liabilities, \$3,000,000 equity (30% cushion).** Insolvent when assets fall below \$7,000,000, a **30% discount.** Survivable in a normal squeeze, dangerous in a real fire sale.
> - **High leverage — \$9,200,000 liabilities, \$800,000 equity (8% cushion).** Insolvent when assets fall below \$9,200,000, just an **8% discount.** A single bad day of forced selling does it. This is our doom-loop fund.
>
> The fundamental assets are identical in all three. The only variable is how much of the cushion leverage has eaten. At 8% cushion, you are one fire sale from insolvency; at 70%, the doom loop can't get a grip. *Leverage is the dial that sets how close to the cliff edge you build your house — and the doom loop is the gust of wind that's always coming.*

This is why the survival mathematics of the series points so insistently away from high leverage. The deeper treatment of how leverage warps the arithmetic of compounding lives in [leverage and the arithmetic of ruin](/blog/trading/risk-management/leverage-and-the-arithmetic-of-ruin); the point here is narrower and sharper — leverage is what makes the doom loop *inescapable* by both thinning your cushion and removing your right to hold.

## Reflexivity: why the loop accelerates instead of settling down

A normal economic process is *self-correcting*: when something gets cheap, buyers step in, and the price stabilizes. The doom loop is the opposite — it is **self-reinforcing**, or reflexive, which is the property that makes it so dangerous. Each turn of the loop does not damp the next turn; it amplifies it. Understanding why is what separates people who respect liquidity risk from people who get caught by it, because reflexivity is what makes the loop *fast* and what makes it *impossible to ride out once it starts*.

The reflexivity has two engines that reinforce each other. The first is the **margin/haircut spiral.** When you borrow against assets, the lender doesn't just demand more collateral as the mark falls — it also raises the *haircut*, the discount it applies to your collateral's value when deciding how much to lend against it. In calm times a lender might lend \$95 against \$100 of bonds (a 5% haircut). In stress, seeing the same bonds wobble, the lender raises the haircut to 20%, lending only \$80 against the same \$100. That haircut increase is itself a demand for cash: you must find the extra \$15 *immediately*, even if the bond's mark hasn't moved at all. So now there are two independent forces draining your cash — the falling mark *and* the rising haircut — and both get worse precisely when you can least afford them. The haircut spiral means the loop can tighten even when fundamental prices are flat, purely because lenders got nervous. (The full anatomy of haircuts and margin spirals is in [funding and margin spirals](/blog/trading/risk-management/funding-and-margin-spirals-when-your-lender-becomes-your-risk).)

The second engine is **price impact that grows with stress.** Market depth — the size you can trade without moving the price — does not stay constant; it *evaporates* exactly when everyone needs it. In calm markets, lots of buyers stand ready, so a forced seller can unload size at a small discount. In a stressed market, the natural buyers have stepped back (they're nursing their own losses or smell blood), so the same forced sale moves the price far more. The fire-sale discount you'll suffer is not fixed at, say, 8% — it *widens* as more sellers arrive and buyers retreat. So the third joint of the loop (the mark-to-market loss) gets deeper on each turn, which means the fourth joint (looking insolvent) arrives faster, which means the fifth joint (funding pulled) is more violent. Each lap around the loop is worse than the last. That is reflexivity, and it is why a liquidity crisis that isn't stopped early tends not to stabilize on its own but to *run to completion* — to the firm's failure — in a startlingly short time.

#### Worked example: one firm's fire sale becomes the whole market's mark

> Now lift the lens from one firm to a market of similar firms, because reflexivity is also how an idiosyncratic problem becomes a systemic one. Suppose ten funds each hold \$10,000,000 of the same illiquid bond, each funded with \$9,200,000 of short-term money — each with our familiar 8% cushion. Nine are perfectly healthy. One — call it Fund A — has a client redemption and must raise \$2,000,000 today.
>
> Fund A can't sell calmly, so it dumps bonds at a 14% fire-sale discount, printing a price of \$86 per \$100. That print is now *the* observed market price. By mark-to-market, all ten funds — including the nine that did nothing wrong — must re-mark their books from \$10,000,000 to \$8,600,000. Watch what that does to the nine bystanders:
>
> - Each bystander's equity drops from +\$800,000 to \$8,600,000 − \$9,200,000 = **−\$600,000.** On paper, all nine are now insolvent — purely because of *Fund A's* forced sale.
> - Their lenders see the negative equity and the rising volatility, raise haircuts, and demand cash. Several bystanders now *also* can't meet the calls and become forced sellers themselves.
> - Their selling prints an even lower price — say \$80 — which re-marks everyone *again*, deeper. Equity at \$80 is \$8,000,000 − \$9,200,000 = **−\$1,200,000.** The hole doubles.
>
> One redemption at one fund, transmitted through mark-to-market and amplified by haircuts, has driven the whole cohort insolvent and triggered a market-wide fire sale — even though the underlying bonds will still pay par to anyone who can hold them. *Reflexivity is how a single forced seller becomes everyone's problem: the mark is shared, so one firm's panic is mechanically debited from every balance sheet that owns the same thing.*

This system-level reflexivity is the bridge from a single-firm doom loop to a full financial crisis, and it is exactly why central banks intervene as lenders of last resort. The backstop's job is to break the reflexive chain — to stop the forced selling so the shared mark can recover toward fair value — before the cascade marks a whole system insolvent. We saw both the single-firm version (SVB) and the system version (2008) in the cases above; reflexivity is the common engine that scales one into the other. Critically, it also explains the timing: because the loop amplifies rather than damps, there is no "stable middle" where you can calmly assess and exit. By the time you've recognized the loop, you are already several laps in, which is why every rule in the playbook below is about acting *before* the first turn.

## Common misconceptions

**"If my assets are good, I can't go bust."** False, and this is the central misconception the entire post exists to correct. Good assets protect you only if you can *hold* them. Our \$10,000,000 fund owned bonds that would pay par in full — impeccable assets — and still went insolvent by \$600,000 when a forced sale marked them at \$8,600,000. Solvency given time and solvency given a 24-hour cash demand are different questions, and the market only ever asks the second one.

**"A liquidity crisis is just a small solvency crisis."** False. They are *different* problems with *opposite* cures. A liquidity crisis is cured by time and cash and leaves the firm whole; a solvency crisis needs new capital or a wind-down. The doom loop is precisely the process that *converts* the first into the second through forced selling. Treating them as the same thing leads to the two classic policy errors: refusing liquidity to a solvent firm (killing it needlessly) or pouring liquidity into an insolvent one (throwing good money after bad).

**"Unrealized losses don't matter as long as I don't sell."** Half-true, and the missing half is fatal. The *loss* is unreal until you sell — correct. But mark-to-market makes the *reported* loss real immediately, and your funders react to the reported number. SVB's unrealized bond loss was about \$15 billion sitting quietly off the income statement; it didn't become a "real" loss until the run forced the sale — but the *visibility* of it is exactly what triggered the run. Unrealized losses don't matter until everyone can see them and decides to act, at which point they matter completely.

**"Big, well-capitalized firms are safe from this."** False. Size and capital change the *threshold*, not the *mechanism*. Bear Stearns and Lehman were enormous and nominally well-capitalized days before they failed; SVB was the 16th-largest bank in the United States. The doom loop scales — a thinner cushion just means a smaller discount kills you, and big firms often run thin cushions because leverage is profitable. Capital adequacy measured at fair-value marks can evaporate the instant a fire sale re-marks the book.

**"I'd see it coming and sell first."** Usually false, because the doom loop is *fast* and *reflexive*. By the time the run is visible, you are one of the funders racing for the exit, and so is everyone else — which is the run. SVB went from "fine" to seized in roughly 48 hours over a weekend. The time to act is not when the loop starts; it's months earlier, when you decide how much liquidity to hold and how much leverage to run. The doom loop gives you no time to react once it's turning.

**"Mark-to-market is the villain — without it, no fire sale spreads."** Tempting but wrong. Hiding the marks (carrying assets at cost while they're underwater) doesn't remove the loss; it just delays the recognition and lets a thinly-capitalized firm keep operating until the gap is even bigger — see the savings-and-loan crisis, where regulatory forbearance turned a manageable problem into a much larger one. Mark-to-market is the messenger. The disease is the maturity mismatch — funding illiquid assets with runnable short-term money — and the cure is structural, not accounting.

## How it shows up in real markets

The doom loop is not a theoretical curiosity. It is the proximate cause of the most consequential failures in modern finance. Two cases — one a single firm, one a whole system — show the identical machine running at different scales.

### Silicon Valley Bank, March 2023: the textbook case

SVB is the cleanest illustration of the loop ever recorded, because every joint is visible and uncontested. During 2020–2021, flooded with deposits from tech startups, SVB bought an enormous portfolio of long-dated, low-coupon government and agency bonds — high-quality, money-good-to-maturity assets, the safest credits on earth. Then rates rose sharply through 2022. The mark-to-market value of that long-duration book fell hard (exactly the blue-line collapse in our mark-to-market figure), leaving a large **unrealized** loss. By the end of 2022, the unrealized loss on the held-to-maturity book was roughly **\$15 billion** — and the bank's tangible common equity was only about **\$16 billion.** The hidden paper loss was nearly the size of the *entire* capital cushion. On a hold-to-maturity basis the bonds were fine; on a mark-to-market basis the bank's economic equity was almost gone.

![Three horizontal bars comparing Silicon Valley Bank's roughly sixteen billion dollar tangible common equity, its roughly fifteen billion dollar unrealized held-to-maturity loss, and the roughly forty-two billion dollar single-day deposit outflow that forced the sale and crystallized the loss](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-solvency-doom-loop-illiquid-can-become-insolvent-7.png)

The figure puts the three numbers side by side, and the shape tells the whole story: the unrealized loss (amber) is about the size of the entire equity cushion (green) — but it was *hidden*, sitting in held-to-maturity accounting, invisible until forced realization. Then came the spark. To shore up liquidity, SVB sold part of its *available-for-sale* bonds at a loss and announced a capital raise. That announcement made the hidden loss visible. Depositors — a concentrated, well-connected, almost entirely uninsured base of startups and venture funds who all talk to each other — did the rational thing in a coordination game and ran. The outflow reached roughly **\$42 billion in a single day** on March 9, 2023, with tens of billions more queued. No bank can sell assets fast enough at fair value to meet a \$42 billion daily run; the green fundraising line cannot rise that fast. The liquidity hole blew open, the forced sale crystallized the loss, the bank was insolvent on realized marks, and the FDIC seized it on March 10. The whole loop — solvent-but-illiquid, forced sale, crystallized loss, insolvency, run — ran to completion in about 48 hours. SVB did not fail because its assets were bad. It failed because a maturity mismatch plus a concentrated runnable funding base let a liquidity crisis cross the bridge into a solvency crisis before anyone could stop it.

### 2008: the doom loop as a system

The global financial crisis was the same machine wired across the entire financial system at once. The canonical crisis facts from this series' record (`data_risk.CRISES`) and the broader history line up joint-for-joint. Investment banks and shadow-banking vehicles held mortgage-related assets funded with very short-term money — overnight repo and commercial paper, the most runnable funding there is. When house prices turned and the marks on mortgage securities fell, the loop ignited. Forced sales by the first distressed holders printed lower prices; mark-to-market carried those prices onto everyone else's books; thinning capital triggered margin calls and repo lenders demanding bigger haircuts; the haircuts forced more sales; the sales printed still lower prices. Correlations across every risk asset rushed toward one as every levered holder was forced to sell whatever they could at the same time — the diversification-failure mode we cover separately. Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers were not killed by a slow erosion of fundamentals; they were killed in days by funding runs that no liquid asset sale could outpace. The mark-to-market transmission belt turned a housing problem into a synchronized fire sale across mortgages, credit, equities, and eventually everything.

What finally stopped it is the most important policy lesson of the loop: it took a lender of last resort — the Federal Reserve and the Treasury — providing essentially unlimited liquidity and capital to break the feedback. The point of a backstop is to *give firms back the ability to hold*, to stop the forced selling, so that assets that were always money-good could pull back to fair value instead of being crystallized at fire-sale prices. The 2008 and 2020 backstops worked because the underlying assets, for the most part, really were on the liquidity side of the bridge — given time and a halt to the forced selling, they recovered. (The full crisis-response playbook, from the GP seat, is in [the crisis playbook 2008 and 2020](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/the-crisis-playbook-2008-and-2020).) For comparison, COVID in March 2020 ran the same loop at warp speed — the VIX hit a record 82.69 close on March 16, 2020, the S&P fell about 34% peak-to-trough, and a "dash for cash" forced selling even of safe Treasuries — until the Fed's backstop halted the spiral within weeks.

Across both cases the diagnosis is identical and the moral is the same: **the firms that died were forced sellers; the firms that survived had the liquidity, the term funding, or the backstop to keep holding.** That is the entire game. As the failure taxonomy in the hedge-fund series documents, liquidity-driven death is one of the most common ways funds die — not because they were wrong, but because they couldn't hold long enough to be right ([how hedge funds die: the failure taxonomy](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/how-hedge-funds-die-the-failure-taxonomy)).

## The risk playbook: how to never be a forced seller

The doom loop has exactly one true defense, and every rule below is a way of stating it: **arrange your affairs so that no one else ever controls the timing of your sales.** A forced seller is the only kind of seller the loop can hurt. Remove your forced-seller status and the loop has nothing to grab.

**1. Hold a real liquidity buffer, sized to your worst plausible run — not your average day.** Keep enough cash and genuinely liquid assets (T-bills, money-market funds) to meet your largest believable near-term cash demand without selling anything illiquid. The buffer's job is to let you *wait*. If a \$2,000,000 call can force our fund to fire-sale, then our fund needed \$2,000,000+ of liquid reserves before the call ever came. Size the buffer to the worst run you can foresee, then add a wide margin — runs are always bigger and faster than the base case.

**2. Term out your funding; match liability duration to asset duration.** The maturity mismatch is the structural cause of the loop. Fund illiquid, long-dated assets with long-dated, locked-up liabilities — term loans, lock-up capital, long-duration deposits — not overnight repo or hot money that can leave tomorrow. The more of your funding that *cannot run*, the smaller the run that can ever hit you. SVB's and 2008's failures were maturity mismatches first and everything else second.

**3. Run a cushion thick enough to survive a fire-sale mark, not just a fair mark.** Stress your balance sheet at *forced-sale* prices, not orderly ones. If a 30% fire-sale discount would push you insolvent, you are running too much leverage for an illiquid book. Our worked example showed a 70% cushion makes the loop unable to get a grip and an 8% cushion makes a single bad day fatal. Pick the cushion by asking: *what discount could a forced sale of my book actually print, and would I survive it?*

**4. Never size a position so a margin call can control you.** Leverage is the loop's on-switch because it manufactures the forced sale through margin. Size every levered position so that even a severe adverse move leaves you able to meet the call *from your buffer* without selling the position itself. If meeting a foreseeable margin call requires selling the thing the call is against, you are already inside the doom loop; you've just not been triggered yet.

**5. Diversify your funding sources and avoid a concentrated, runnable funder base.** SVB's funders were a tight, homogeneous, uninsured group who all ran together — a coordination-game tinderbox. Spread your funding across many independent sources whose incentives to run are uncorrelated, so that no single coordinated stampede can demand everything at once. Concentrated funding is as dangerous as a concentrated position.

**6. Pre-commit your crisis plan, because the loop gives you no time to think.** Decide *now*, in calm conditions, exactly what you'll do at each drawdown level: what you'll sell first (always the most liquid, never the distressed asset), where your committed backup credit lines are, what your absolute leverage ceiling is. The doom loop runs in hours; SVB went in 48. A plan written during the run is written by a forced seller. Write it before.

The thread through all six is the survival spine of this series: *you only compound if you survive, and you only survive a liquidity crisis if you keep the right to hold.* A temporary mark recovers on its own — that is the gift the market gives the patient. The doom loop is the machine that steals that gift by forcing the sale. Defend your right to hold, and a liquidity crisis is a bad month you wait out. Lose it, and a liquidity crisis is the end of the game, played out over a weekend, with assets you still believe in being sold by someone else at prices you'd never accept.
