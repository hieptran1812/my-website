> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A market maker continuously quotes a price to buy (the bid) and a price to sell (the ask) so that you can always trade; the gap between them — the spread — is how they get paid for taking the other side and bearing the risk.
>
> - The spread decomposes into three classic costs: **order-processing** (the per-trade plumbing), **inventory risk** (being stuck holding a position you didn't want), and **adverse selection** (the danger that whoever just traded with you knows something you don't).
> - Makers manage inventory by **skewing** their quotes — leaning prices against a building position — and try to end the day **flat**, because an unhedged overnight position is an uncompensated bet.
> - The business moved from privileged NYSE floor **specialists** and NASDAQ phone **dealers** to electronic, high-speed firms like **Citadel Securities, Virtu, and Jane Street** — speed replaced floor privilege.
> - Liquidity is **not free or guaranteed**: in stress, makers widen their quotes or pull them entirely. In the May 6, 2010 flash crash the whole tape gapped in minutes. The one number to remember: a mega-cap like Apple trades at roughly a **1 basis-point** spread, while a micro-cap can cost **80 bp** — an 80× tax on the same round trip.

## When you press "sell," someone is already standing there

At 9:47 on an ordinary Tuesday you decide to sell 100 shares of a large company. You tap "sell market," and a fraction of a second later you own \$10,000 less stock and \$10,000 more cash. You never met the buyer. You never waited for one to show up. The trade just *happened*.

Who was on the other side? Almost certainly not another retail investor who happened to want exactly your 100 shares at exactly that instant. Far more likely it was a **market maker** — a firm whose entire job is to stand in the middle of the market all day, every day, posting a price at which it will buy from you and a slightly higher price at which it will sell to someone else, and to do this for thousands of stocks at once. You sold *to its bid*. Seconds later it will probably sell those same shares *at its ask* to a buyer who shows up, pocketing the few cents in between.

That few-cents gap is the **bid-ask spread**, and it is the quiet, continuous fee that makes the modern market feel frictionless. It is also one of the most misunderstood prices in finance. People regard the spread as a rip-off, a hidden tax, a relic. It is none of those things. It is the *price of immediacy* — the cost of being able to trade right now instead of waiting and hoping. This post is about who charges that price, how they calculate it, where it comes from, and the uncomfortable truth at the end: the people who make your trades instant can, and do, step away exactly when the market needs them most.

![Three stacked cost components add up to the bid-ask spread a market maker charges](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-1.png)

This sits at the heart of the series' spine. A capital market turns savings into long-term investment through two engines: a **primary market** that *creates* securities to raise money, and a **secondary market** that *trades* them afterward. The secret that makes the whole machine run is that **secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible** — nobody buys a newly issued 30-year bond or a freshly listed share unless they believe they can sell it tomorrow morning without crashing the price. Market makers are the people who make "tomorrow morning" a credible promise. They are the standing army of the secondary market. Understand them and you understand why a security is *sellable* at all.

## Foundations: what a market maker actually does

Before any jargon, an everyday-money analogy. Walk into a currency-exchange kiosk at an airport. The board shows two numbers for euros: it will *buy* your euros at 1.05 dollars and *sell* you euros at 1.10 dollars. The kiosk is not predicting where the euro is going. It does not care. It makes money on the *gap* — buy low from departing tourists, sell high to arriving ones, over and over. As long as roughly equal numbers of people come and go, it pockets five cents on every euro that passes through, and it never has to be right about exchange rates.

A market maker in a stock or a bond is that kiosk, scaled up and sped up by a factor of a million. Strip away the technology and the job is exactly this:

- **Quote a two-sided market, continuously.** At every instant it publishes a *bid* (the price at which it will buy) and an *ask*, also called the *offer* (the price at which it will sell). Both quotes carry a *size* — how many shares it is willing to trade at that price. "I'll buy 5,000 at \$9.99 and sell 5,000 at \$10.01."
- **Take the other side of whatever comes.** If you want to sell, it buys. If you want to buy, it sells. It does not get to choose; that is the deal. By standing ready on both sides, it guarantees that a trader who wants *immediacy* can always have it.
- **Earn the spread, manage the inventory, repeat.** Each round trip — buy at the bid, sell at the ask — earns the spread. But the buys and sells never arrive in perfectly matched pairs, so the maker is constantly holding a temporary position (its *inventory*) that it must manage and unwind.

Let's define the core terms once, cleanly, because everything else builds on them:

- **Bid** — the highest price a buyer (here, the maker) is currently willing to pay.
- **Ask / offer** — the lowest price a seller (the maker) is currently willing to accept.
- **Spread** — ask minus bid. If the bid is \$9.99 and the ask is \$10.01, the spread is \$0.02, or 2 cents.
- **Mid** — the midpoint, (bid + ask) / 2, here \$10.00. This is the market's best single guess at "fair value."
- **Half-spread** — half the spread, \$0.01 here. A trader who buys at the ask pays the mid plus a half-spread; a seller receives the mid minus a half-spread. So one round trip — buy then sell — costs the *full* spread.
- **Basis point (bp)** — one hundredth of a percent. A 2-cent spread on a \$10 stock is 2 / 1000 = 0.2%, or **20 bp**. Quoting spreads in basis points lets us compare a \$10 stock to a \$400 one fairly.

The reason a maker can profit without forecasting is the same reason the kiosk can: it is selling a *service* — immediacy — not making a *bet* on direction. Its edge is structural, not predictive. But "without forecasting" hides a deep problem we will spend much of this post on: sometimes the person trading with you *is* forecasting, and is right, and that is where the spread's hardest cost comes from.

It helps to see *why* this role is necessary at all, because the alternative is instructive. Consider a market with no makers — just buyers and sellers posting orders and waiting. Such a market is called a *pure order-driven* or *agency* market, and it can work, but it suffers from a timing problem: a buyer who wants to trade *now* may have to wait minutes or hours for a natural seller to arrive at the same price, and vice versa. The two sides of every trade rarely show up at the same instant. The maker's economic function is to **bridge that timing gap** — it stands in as the instant counterparty for whichever side arrives first, warehouses the resulting position, and waits (with its own capital at risk) for the natural other side to show up later. It converts a market that would otherwise be *episodic* — trades happen only when two opposite wishes coincide — into a *continuous* one where trade is always available. That continuity is the whole product. The spread is what the market pays the maker to provide it.

#### Worked example: one clean round trip

A maker quotes a stock bid \$9.99 / ask \$10.01. A seller hits the bid: the maker **buys 100,000 shares at \$9.99**, paying \$999,000. Moments later a buyer lifts the ask: the maker **sells those 100,000 shares at \$10.01**, receiving \$1,001,000. Profit on the round trip: \$1,001,000 − \$999,000 = **\$2,000**, which is exactly the \$0.02 spread × 100,000 shares. The maker never had a view on whether the stock was going up or down; it earned the spread by being there for both sides. **Intuition:** the spread is rent on standing in the middle, not a reward for being right about price.

That \$2,000 looks like free money. It is not — it is gross revenue against which three real costs are charged. Decomposing those costs is the whole intellectual core of market making, so we take them one at a time.

### A few more foundations the rest of the post leans on

Before the cost decomposition, three more terms earn their keep throughout the discussion.

**Quoted size and depth.** A quote is not just a price; it is a price *for a quantity*. "Bid \$9.99 for 5,000 / ask \$10.01 for 5,000" means the maker will buy up to 5,000 shares at \$9.99 and sell up to 5,000 at \$10.01. Beyond that size the maker either re-quotes worse prices or steps back. The total quantity available near the top of the book is the market's **depth**, and it matters as much as the headline spread: a one-cent spread good for only 100 shares is far less useful to a large investor than a two-cent spread good for 50,000. When people say liquidity "dried up," they often mean depth collapsed even though the headline spread looked normal for a moment.

**Immediacy vs. patience.** Every trader faces a choice. Hit the maker's quote now and pay the spread (you *demand* liquidity — you are a *liquidity taker*), or post your own resting limit order and wait for someone to come to you (you *supply* liquidity — you become, briefly, a maker yourself, and you might *earn* the spread instead of paying it). The spread is precisely the price difference between being impatient and being patient. A maker is a firm that has industrialized patience: it posts resting quotes at scale and collects the impatience premium from everyone in a hurry.

**Mark-to-market.** A maker values its inventory continuously at the current mid. If it is long 30,000 shares acquired at \$10.00 and the mid slips to \$9.98, it has an unrealized loss of 30,000 × \$0.02 = \$600 *right now*, before it sells anything. This continuous revaluation is why inventory risk is felt instantly, not only at the moment of unwinding — and why risk managers at a market-making firm watch the net position in every name in real time.

## Decomposing the spread: the three costs a maker must recover

Academics and practitioners agree on a clean three-way split of where the spread comes from. A maker who sets the spread too low to cover all three will, slowly or quickly, go broke. A maker who sets it higher than needed will be undercut by a competitor quoting tighter. The competitive spread is, roughly, the *sum* of these three costs.

### Cost 1: order-processing — the plumbing

Every trade costs something mechanical to execute: exchange fees, clearing and settlement fees, the cost of the technology and connectivity, the staff, the regulatory capital tied up. In the human-floor era this was large — a specialist's overhead was substantial. In the electronic era it has been crushed almost to nothing per share, which is precisely why spreads have collapsed over two decades. But it is never zero, and at enormous volume even a fraction of a cent per share adds up. Order-processing is the floor under the spread: the irreducible cost of doing the trade at all.

There is a wrinkle here worth knowing, because it complicates the simple picture. Many exchanges run a **maker-taker** fee model: they *pay a small rebate* to the order that posts (adds) liquidity and *charge a fee* to the order that takes it. A typical US equity rebate is on the order of \$0.0020–\$0.0030 per share to the maker, with a slightly larger fee to the taker. So part of a maker's economics is not the headline spread at all — it is the rebate it harvests by posting resting quotes. This is why some firms will quote at a price that looks like it earns *zero* spread: they are playing for the rebate, not the spread. It also means the "true" cost a taker pays is the spread *plus* the access fee, and the "true" revenue a maker earns is the spread *plus* the rebate. The order-processing component, in other words, can be slightly *negative* for a disciplined maker — the venue is paying it to provide the quote. None of this changes the three-cost logic; it just shifts where the order-processing line sits.

A second wrinkle: the same trade generates *clearing and settlement* obligations downstream (T+1 in US equities since 2024), and the capital a maker must post to back those obligations is a real, financed cost. A maker running a huge book ties up balance sheet, and balance sheet has a funding rate. For a high-volume firm this is a genuine line item, even if it is invisible on any single trade.

### Cost 2: inventory risk — being stuck with a position

Here is where it stops being free money. Recall that buys and sells do not arrive in matched pairs. Suppose three sellers in a row hit the maker's bid before any buyer shows up. The maker is now **long 30,000 shares** it did not want — it is holding inventory. While it holds that inventory, the *price can move against it.* If bad news hits and the stock drops 2% before the maker can sell, it loses 2% on 30,000 shares — a loss that can dwarf the pennies of spread it earned acquiring them.

So part of the spread is compensation for **bearing price risk on involuntary inventory.** The maker is, briefly and repeatedly, a forced speculator. The wider the price might swing while it holds the position — that is, the more *volatile* the stock — the more inventory risk costs, and the wider the maker must quote to be paid for it. This is why spreads blow out the instant volatility spikes: the inventory-risk component of the spread is, in effect, a volatility premium.

Inventory risk has a second dimension beyond volatility: *how long* the maker is likely to be stuck holding the position before it can offload it — its **expected holding period**. In a deep, fast-turning mega-cap, an unwanted long is gone in seconds; the price barely has time to move against it. In a thin micro-cap that trades a few thousand shares a day, the same unwanted long might sit for hours or days, exposed to every wiggle the whole time. Inventory risk scales roughly with volatility *times the square root of the holding period* — the same logic that makes a longer-dated option cost more. So a maker in a slow name faces a double penalty: each share it is stuck with is exposed to price moves for far longer, *and* there are fewer competing makers to share the load. Both effects push its quotes wider, which is one of the mechanical reasons the spread-by-tier chart fans out so dramatically.

The classic inventory model of Stoll, Ho, and Amihud-Mendelson formalizes this: a maker has a *preferred* (usually flat) inventory level, and it tilts its quotes to pull its actual inventory back toward that target, demanding more spread the further it is pushed from it and the riskier the holding. We keep the narrative; the formal optimization — how a risk-averse maker sets quotes as a function of its current inventory and the stock's volatility — is exactly what the [market-making simulator](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-simulator-quant-research) builds out.

### Cost 3: adverse selection — the trader who knows something

This is the deepest and most counterintuitive cost, and it is the reason a perfectly run, infinitely fast, zero-overhead market maker would *still* charge a spread.

Think about who chooses to trade with you. The maker posts a quote and waits. Most of the people who hit it are **uninformed** — they are rebalancing a 401(k), raising cash for a house, following a mechanical strategy. They have no special information; trading with them is the kiosk's dream. But a fraction are **informed** — they are trading *because* they know something the maker doesn't: an earnings leak, a pending upgrade, a large order about to hit. And here is the cruel asymmetry: the informed trader only trades with you when they are *right*. They buy from your ask just before the stock pops; they sell into your bid just before it drops. You systematically end up holding the wrong side against exactly the people who know best.

![A maker fills both uninformed and informed traders, earning from one and losing to the other](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-4.png)

This is **adverse selection**, and the formal model — Glosten and Milgrom (1985) — captures the intuition precisely: because the maker can't tell an informed trader from an uninformed one *before* the trade, every fill carries a probability that it is the informed side, and the maker must widen the spread enough that the profits from the many uninformed trades cover the losses to the few informed ones. The spread is the price at which the maker breaks even *on average* across the population of traders hitting it. (We keep the narrative here; for the formal pricing model — the probability of informed trading, the Bayesian quote updates, the order-book dynamics — see the [order-book simulator](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/order-book-simulator-quant-research) and [market-making simulator](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-simulator-quant-research) write-ups, which build the math explicitly.)

The deep consequence: **the spread can never go to zero in a market with any private information.** Even with free trading and infinite speed, the adverse-selection floor remains, because someone, somewhere, sometimes knows more than the maker. This is why even the most liquid stock on earth has a positive spread.

There is a subtler corollary that explains a great deal of market microstructure: makers will *segment* flow by how informed it is likely to be. Retail flow — your 100-share rebalance — is overwhelmingly uninformed and therefore cheap to fill, so makers compete hard for it (this is the entire economics of wholesalers paying brokers for retail order flow; the flow is *good* precisely because it carries little adverse selection). Flow arriving through channels associated with informed traders — large institutional orders, hedge-fund algorithms — is treated with more suspicion, and a maker will quote it wider, fill it in smaller pieces, or decline it. The single national best bid and offer you see on a screen is an average; the *effective* spread a given trader gets depends on what the maker infers about how informed they are. The spread, in other words, is not one number — it is a maker's running estimate, trade by trade, of the danger that *you* know something.

This also reframes what "news" does to a quote. When a maker's quote is *stale* — the price has moved but the maker hasn't updated yet — an informed (or merely fast) trader will pick it off, lifting the still-cheap ask or hitting the still-rich bid. Getting picked off on a stale quote is just adverse selection wearing a stopwatch. That is why speed is so valuable to a modern maker: the faster you can cancel and re-quote when information arrives, the less often you are the one holding the stale price when the informed trader strikes. Speed is, at bottom, an *adverse-selection-reduction technology*.

#### Worked example: the adverse-selection loss that eats the spread

Our maker is happily earning \$0.02 per round trip. Then an informed buyer lifts its ask: the maker **sells 100,000 shares at \$10.01**, collecting \$1,001,000. But the buyer knew an upgrade was coming. Within seconds the stock gaps to a \$10.20 mid. To get back to flat the maker must now **buy 100,000 shares at ~\$10.20**, paying \$2,040,000 — wait, that's the wrong size; to cover the 100,000 it shorted, it buys 100,000 at \$10.20 for \$1,020,000. Net on this round trip: \$1,001,000 − \$1,020,000 = **−\$19,000**. One informed trade just wiped out *nine and a half* of its \$2,000 clean round trips. **Intuition:** the maker doesn't fear being wrong about price; it fears the counterparty being right — and it prices that fear into every quote.

#### Worked example: how wide must the spread be to survive the informed?

Suppose that out of every 100 traders who hit the maker, 95 are uninformed (it earns the \$0.01 half-spread from each) and 5 are informed (each costs it, on average, \$0.15 because the price moves against it). Per 100 trades: uninformed revenue = 95 × \$0.01 = **\$0.95**; informed losses = 5 × \$0.15 = **\$0.75**. Net = +\$0.20 across 100 trades, or \$0.002 per trade — thin but positive. Now suppose informed flow rises to 10 of 100 (a rumor is circulating): informed losses = 10 × \$0.15 = **\$1.50** against uninformed revenue 90 × \$0.01 = **\$0.90**, a **−\$0.60** loss. To get back to break-even the maker must widen its half-spread until uninformed revenue again covers informed losses — here roughly to \$0.0167, a 67% wider spread. **Intuition:** spreads widen not because makers got greedy but because the *mix* of who is trading shifted toward the informed.

## Inventory management: how a maker leans against its own book

A maker is not passive once it has inventory. The whole art of the business is **inventory management** — actively steering the book back toward flat without giving up the spread. There are three levers.

### Lever 1: skewing the quotes

The simplest and most powerful tool is to **skew** — to shift both quotes in the direction that attracts the trades it wants. Suppose the maker has become **long 50,000 shares** and wants to sell some down. It lowers *both* its bid and its ask. The lower ask makes it more attractive for buyers to lift (so the maker sells, reducing the long); the lower bid makes it less attractive for sellers to hit (so the maker buys less, not adding to the long). It has tilted the whole market against its position, nudging the order flow to do the unwinding for it.

![A maker lowers both quotes when long to attract sellers and lean its inventory back toward flat](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-2.png)

Crucially, skewing is not the same as widening. The *spread* — the distance between bid and ask — can stay the same width; the maker just moves the whole pair down. It is still quoting a competitive two-sided market; it has merely revealed, through its prices, that it would rather sell than buy right now. Symmetrically, a maker that is short skews *up* to attract sellers and discourage buyers.

The amount of skew is proportional to how far the maker is from its target inventory and how risky the name is. A maker long a tiny, comfortable position barely skews at all. A maker that has been run over — long ten times its comfortable size in a volatile name — skews hard, perhaps moving its quotes several cents and shrinking the size it shows on the bid, signaling loudly "I am full, please take this off my hands." If you have ever watched a fast-moving stock and seen the bid and offer both drift in one direction without obvious news, you may be watching makers skew in unison as they all end up leaning the same way after absorbing a wave of one-sided flow. When *every* maker is long and skewing down at once, their collective lean *becomes* the price move — a feedback loop that is benign in normal times and dangerous in a stampede.

#### Worked example: skewing to shed a long position

The maker is long 50,000 shares, acquired at an average \$10.00. Its flat quote would be \$9.99 / \$10.01. To lean against the long, it skews the pair down 2 cents to **\$9.97 / \$9.99**, keeping the 2-cent spread. A buyer now lifts the \$9.99 ask for 30,000 shares: the maker sells 30,000 at \$9.99, cutting its long from 50,000 to **20,000**. It gave up \$0.02 per share versus its original \$10.01 ask (30,000 × \$0.02 = \$600 of "skew cost") to offload risk fast — cheap insurance against the stock falling while it sat on 50,000 shares. **Intuition:** skewing trades a sliver of spread for a faster return to flat; the maker pays to *not be a speculator*.

### Lever 2: hedging

When a maker can't unwind the underlying fast enough, it **hedges** — it offsets the price risk with a related instrument. A maker long a basket of tech stocks might sell index futures; an options market maker long calls will short the underlying stock to neutralize its directional exposure (its *delta*). Hedging doesn't make the inventory disappear, but it strips out the part of the risk the maker isn't paid to hold — the broad market direction — leaving only the residual it can manage. (The mechanics of hedging derivatives exposure — the Greeks — belong to options trading; we link out rather than re-derive: see the broader [options and volatility](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-simulator-quant-research) material.)

### Lever 3: getting flat by the close

Why does a maker want to be **flat** — zero net position — by the end of the day? Because the spread it earns is compensation for *intraday* immediacy risk, not for an *overnight directional bet.* If it carries a big long position home, anything can happen while the market is closed: an earnings miss, a geopolitical shock, a downgrade. That overnight gap risk is uncompensated — nobody paid the maker a spread to take it. A position held overnight has quietly converted the maker from a liquidity provider into a speculator, and speculation is a different (and for a maker, unwanted) business. So as the close approaches, makers skew aggressively, hedge what they can't unwind, and accept a worse price to flatten. End-of-day flattening is itself a recurring liquidity event — part of why volume and spreads behave differently in the last half hour.

There is a capital reason on top of the risk reason. A flat book at the close requires far less overnight financing and regulatory capital than a large directional position; carrying inventory home is expensive in balance-sheet terms even if the price doesn't move. And there is a discipline reason: a firm whose traders are *allowed* to carry positions home will, sooner or later, have a trader who quietly turns a market-making desk into a punting desk and blows up. Keeping the mandate "be flat by the close" bright-line keeps the business *being* a market-making business. The closing auction — the single large cross that sets the official closing price on most exchanges — exists partly because so many participants, makers included, want to transact a big quantity at one clean reference price right at the bell. The maker's daily rhythm, then, is: quote all day, accumulate and shed inventory continuously, and converge hard to flat into the close.

The exception that proves the rule is the **designated market maker** with affirmative obligations (more on these below): a DMM may be *required* to hold inventory it would rather not, precisely because its job includes providing liquidity when no one else will. The obligation is the price of the franchise. A pure electronic maker with no such obligation simply stops quoting when the math turns against it — which is the entire problem we turn to at the end.

## How wide is the spread, really? It depends entirely on liquidity

The single most important empirical fact about spreads is that they vary enormously across securities, and the variable that drives them is **liquidity** — how easily you can trade size without moving the price. The more liquid the security, the thinner the spread, because all three cost components shrink: more competing makers drive down the order-processing margin, more volume means inventory is unwound faster (less inventory risk), and deeper, more-watched names have proportionally less private information per trade (less adverse selection).

![Bid-ask spread by liquidity tier, from mega-cap to micro-cap](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-3.png)

The pattern is stark. A mega-cap like Apple trades at roughly a **1 basis-point** spread — a penny on a \$200 stock is half a basis point per side. By the time you reach micro-caps, spreads of **80 bp** or more are normal: the same round trip costs 80× as much. This is not a quirk; it is the three costs scaling with thinness. Few makers compete in an obscure micro-cap, each trade is a larger fraction of daily volume (so inventory sits longer), and a single informed trader looms much larger in a name nobody watches.

This is why the spread is best understood as a **tax on a round trip whose rate is set by liquidity.** Put real money through it and the tiering becomes visceral.

![Round-trip spread cost on a fifty thousand dollar trade across liquidity tiers](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-8.png)

A useful mental check: the spread you *see* (the quoted spread) is not always the spread you *pay* (the effective spread). For small orders, a wholesaler often fills you a hair *inside* the quote, so your effective spread is narrower than quoted. For large orders, you exhaust the quoted size and walk up (or down) the book, so your effective spread is *wider* than quoted — this extra cost of size is called **market impact**, and for an institution moving millions of shares it dwarfs the headline spread entirely. The two-cent quote is the price of immediacy *for a small trade*; the true cost of immediacy rises with the size you demand relative to the depth on offer. Makers price this directly: ask for more size than they show and they quote you worse, because filling you is a bigger inventory and adverse-selection risk.

#### Worked example: the spread as a tax on a \$50,000 round trip

You buy and later sell \$50,000 of stock. The spread cost of the round trip is roughly notional × (spread in bp / 10,000). In a **mega-cap** at 1 bp: \$50,000 × 0.0001 = **\$5**. In a **mid-cap** at 8 bp: \$50,000 × 0.0008 = **\$40**. In a **small-cap** at 25 bp: \$50,000 × 0.0025 = **\$125**. In a **micro-cap** at 80 bp: \$50,000 × 0.008 = **\$400**. Same dollar trade, same intent — but the liquidity tax ranges from a cup of coffee to a meaningful bite. **Intuition:** you don't pay for the stock, you pay for the *ease of getting out of it* — and that ease is what makers sell.

#### Worked example: widening the quote when volatility doubles

A maker quotes a stock 2 cents wide (\$9.99 / \$10.01) when daily volatility is 1%. The inventory-risk slice of that spread scales with how far the price can move while the maker holds the position — roughly with volatility. Suppose volatility doubles to 2% on the morning of a Fed decision. The inventory-risk component must roughly double to keep the maker compensated for the larger potential swing on its book. If inventory risk was about half the original spread (1 cent of the 2), doubling it adds a cent, taking the quote to about **3 cents wide** (\$9.985 / \$10.015) before accounting for any rise in adverse selection — which also climbs on event days. **Intuition:** wider spreads on volatile days aren't gouging; they're the maker repricing a riskier service.

#### Worked example: a maker's day, in aggregate

Put the pieces together over a full session. A maker quotes a liquid stock at a 2-cent spread and trades **2,000,000 shares** of round-trip volume across the day (1,000,000 bought, 1,000,000 sold). Gross spread capture if every trade were a clean round trip: 1,000,000 × \$0.02 = **\$20,000**. Add maker rebates at \$0.0020 per share on the ~2,000,000 shares it posted into: +\$4,000. Now subtract the costs: order-processing/clearing, say \$0.0005/share × 2,000,000 = \$1,000; adverse-selection losses to informed flow, say \$6,000 on the day; and a small inventory mark-to-market loss from skewing out of positions at worse prices, say \$2,000. Net P&L ≈ \$20,000 + \$4,000 − \$1,000 − \$6,000 − \$2,000 = **\$15,000** — earned on \$10M+ of shares turned over, with the book ending flat. **Intuition:** the business is high-volume and thin-margin; the maker wins by being everywhere a little, not anywhere a lot, and adverse selection is the cost line that can flip a good day red.

## The evolution: from floor privilege to speed

The *function* — quote two sides, earn the spread, manage inventory — has not changed in a century. *Who* performs it, and *how*, has been transformed twice over.

![Market making evolved from NYSE specialists to NASDAQ dealers to electronic high-speed firms](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-7.png)

**The NYSE specialist (the old monopoly).** For most of the twentieth century, each NYSE-listed stock was assigned to a single **specialist** who stood at a physical post on the floor and was obligated to make a fair, orderly market in that name. The specialist had a privileged view — they could see the order book and the flow before anyone else — and in exchange bore an affirmative obligation to provide liquidity, especially when others wouldn't. The edge was *positional and informational*: you made money because you stood at the post and saw the flow. Spreads were correspondingly wide — eighths and sixteenths of a dollar (12.5 and 6.25 cents) were standard tick sizes, which set a hard floor on how tight a spread could be.

**The NASDAQ dealer (competing quotes by phone and screen).** NASDAQ grew up differently: instead of one specialist per stock, *multiple* dealers competed to post quotes, originally by phone and then on screens. Competition should have tightened spreads — and eventually did — but a notorious 1994 academic study found dealers avoiding odd-eighth quotes, effectively colluding to keep spreads artificially wide, which triggered an SEC investigation and reforms. The lesson stuck: market making is only cheap for the public when makers genuinely *compete*.

**Decimalization and Reg NMS (the structural reset).** Two changes in the 2000s broke the old world open. **Decimalization** (2001) replaced fractional ticks with penny increments, instantly allowing spreads as tight as a cent and slashing the per-share margin. **Regulation NMS** (2005, effective 2007) knit the exchanges into a single electronic, price-time-priority national market system, where orders route to the best price anywhere. Together they turned market making from a privileged human craft into an *electronic, competitive* business where the edge is no longer where you stand but how fast and how smart your code is.

**The electronic / HFT market maker (today).** The result is the modern landscape: firms like **Citadel Securities, Virtu Financial, Jane Street, and Hudson River Trading** make markets in thousands of names across asset classes, holding positions for milliseconds, quoting tighter than any human specialist ever could, and earning a sliver of a cent on staggering volume. Speed replaced floor privilege: the firm that can update its quote a microsecond faster when news hits is the firm that *avoids* getting picked off by the informed trader (cutting its adverse-selection cost) — so speed is, at bottom, a tool for managing the third cost component. **Designated market makers (DMMs)** still exist on the NYSE as a vestige of the specialist — they carry obligations to quote at the open and close and in stress — but they are electronic firms now, not a human with an informational monopoly. The trade is explicit: a DMM gives up the freedom to simply walk away, and in return gets a recognized franchise in its assigned names. Similar designated-maker arrangements exist in other markets — on many exchanges a newly listed company or a thinly traded ETF will *pay* a maker (or grant it rebates) to commit to quoting tight, two-sided markets in its security, because the issuer understands the spine point viscerally: if no one will make a market in your shares, your shares are hard to sell, and hard-to-sell shares are worth less and harder to issue. Liquidity provision is a service the *issuer* sometimes has to buy.

The throughline across all three eras is worth stating plainly. The maker's edge migrated from **information** (the specialist saw the book first) to **competition and speed** (whoever quotes tightest and updates fastest wins the flow). What did *not* change is the three-cost structure: a 1920s specialist and a 2020s HFT both charged a spread to cover order-processing, inventory risk, and adverse selection. Technology changed the *size* of each cost — crushing order-processing, speeding inventory turnover, and arming makers against adverse selection — which is why spreads fell by an order of magnitude. But the *shape* of the business is a century old.

A huge slice of this activity happens *off* the public exchanges. **Wholesalers** (Citadel Securities and Virtu again, prominently) pay retail brokers for their order flow and internalize it — they fill your "sell 100 shares" trade directly off their own book, usually at a tiny price improvement over the public quote, rather than sending it to an exchange. This is why so much volume never touches a lit exchange.

![Off-exchange share of US equity volume rising over time](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-5.png)

The off-exchange share of US equity volume has climbed steadily — from around 30% in 2010 toward nearly half today — driven by wholesaler internalization and dark venues.

![US equity trading volume split between lit exchanges and off-exchange venues](/imgs/blogs/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity-6.png)

By the mid-2020s roughly 45% of US equity volume traded off-exchange. That fragmentation — where the trade actually happens and what it means for price discovery — is its own large topic; see [lit markets, dark pools, and the fragmented tape](/blog/trading/capital-markets/lit-markets-dark-pools-and-the-fragmented-tape) for the venue map, and [order types and how an order travels to the market](/blog/trading/capital-markets/order-types-and-how-an-order-travels-to-the-market) for how your specific order gets routed to one of these makers.

## When liquidity vanishes: the spread is not a guarantee

Here is the part everyone forgets when markets are calm. A market maker has *no obligation* (with narrow exceptions for DMMs) to keep quoting. Quoting is voluntary, and it is only profitable when the three costs are manageable. When they explode, the rational maker does exactly what you'd do: it widens its spread dramatically, shrinks its quoted size, or pulls its quotes entirely and steps aside. Liquidity is *provided*, not *guaranteed* — and it is most likely to be withdrawn precisely when it is most needed.

The canonical illustration is the **flash crash of May 6, 2010.** Amid an already-jittery market, a large automated sell program in E-mini S&P 500 futures hit a thin order book. As prices dropped, the informed-flow signal screamed and inventory risk spiked; electronic makers, unable to tell a temporary air-pocket from a genuine crash, did the rational thing and *pulled their quotes.* With the makers gone, there was nothing to absorb the selling. The Dow fell about **1,000 points (roughly 9%) in minutes**, and some stocks printed absurd prices — shares changing hands for a penny, others for \$100,000 — before the market snapped back almost as fast. No fundamental news caused it. It was a liquidity event: the standing army of makers stepped back, and immediacy evaporated.

The deep point is structural, not a one-off glitch. The adverse-selection and inventory-risk components of the spread are *highest exactly when the market is most stressed* — when volatility is extreme and the fear of informed flow is at its peak. So the maker's incentive to widen or withdraw is strongest at the worst possible moment for everyone else. This is the fundamental fragility of voluntary liquidity: it is abundant and cheap when you don't need it, and scarce and expensive (or absent) when you do.

Walk the cost decomposition through a crash and the withdrawal becomes inevitable rather than cowardly. As prices fall fast, realized volatility spikes, so the **inventory-risk** premium the maker needs explodes — anything it buys could be down several percent before it can sell. At the same time, a violent one-directional move is *exactly* the signature of informed selling, so the maker's estimate of **adverse selection** jumps — every seller hitting its bid might know something terrible. With both of the risky cost components surging, the spread that would compensate the maker becomes enormous; quoting a competitive tight market would be financial suicide. The rational maker widens until its quote is effectively a no-fill price, or simply cancels. It is not panicking; it is doing the math, and the math says *step back*. The tragedy is that when every maker does this math at once, the bids disappear together, and the absence of bids *is* the crash.

There is a reflexive twist that makes it worse. In calm times, makers' standing quotes dampen volatility — they absorb a sell wave and lean against it, cushioning the move. That dampening makes the market *look* deep and stable, which encourages everyone to assume liquidity will be there. So participants size positions and strategies as if the calm-time depth were permanent. When stress hits and the makers step back, the cushion that everyone implicitly relied on vanishes precisely when the most people are leaning on it. Liquidity that is *expected* but not *contracted* is the most dangerous kind, because its disappearance is correlated across the whole market and arrives without warning.

Regulators responded with **circuit breakers** and **limit up–limit down** rules that pause trading when prices move too far too fast, buying time for makers to reassess and re-quote rather than flee. But these are speed bumps, not guarantees that someone will be on the other side. The promise "you can always sell tomorrow morning" is *almost* always true — and the rare moments it isn't are the ones that define a crisis.

## Common misconceptions

**"The spread is a rip-off — makers are just skimming."** The spread is a *fee for a service* (immediacy) that covers three real costs. In the most liquid stocks, that fee is about **1 bp** — competition has driven the maker's margin to slivers of a cent. The skim, where it exists, is in the *thin* names where few makers compete; the fix is more competition, which is exactly what decimalization and Reg NMS delivered. A world with no makers wouldn't have a zero spread; it would have *no instant trade at all*.

**"Market makers bet on the direction of stocks."** A pure market maker is the opposite of a directional bettor — it tries to be **flat** and earn the spread regardless of direction. Its nightmare isn't being wrong about price; it's *adverse selection* (the counterparty being right) and *inventory* it can't unwind. Direction is a risk it actively sheds via skewing and hedging, not a bet it seeks.

**"Tighter spreads always mean a better, safer market."** Tight spreads mean *cheap immediacy in normal times*, but they can coexist with *fragile* liquidity — thin quoted size and makers ready to vanish. The 2010 flash crash happened in a market with historically tight headline spreads. Spread width measures the *price* of liquidity; it doesn't measure its *resilience.*

**"Payment for order flow means you get a worse price."** It's more subtle. Wholesalers buying retail flow typically give a small *price improvement* over the public quote — because retail flow is overwhelmingly uninformed, it has low adverse-selection cost, so the maker can profitably fill it inside the spread. The genuine debate is about price discovery and conflicts of interest, not whether your specific 100-share fill is worse than the exchange quote (it usually isn't).

**"Speed (HFT) is just a tax that adds nothing."** Speed is primarily how a modern maker *avoids adverse selection* — updating its quote before an informed trader can pick off a stale price. Faster makers can afford to quote *tighter*, which is part of why spreads fell so far. Speed also creates fragility (faster withdrawal in stress), so it's a genuine tradeoff, not pure waste — but "adds nothing" is wrong.

**"The maker's profit equals the spread."** No — the spread is *gross* revenue, before order-processing, before the losses to informed flow, and before the inventory marks from skewing out of positions at worse prices. As the daily-P&L worked example showed, a maker quoting a 2-cent spread on 2,000,000 shares grosses \$20,000 but nets closer to \$15,000 after costs, and a single bad adverse-selection day can turn the net negative even though the gross spread looks healthy. The spread is what the maker *charges*; its profit is what survives the three costs.

**"More venues and dark pools mean less liquidity for ordinary investors."** Fragmentation sounds bad, but for a small order it is mostly invisible: routing logic and the national best bid/offer rule mean your order still gets the best price across venues, and the off-exchange wholesalers competing for your flow generally improve on it. The real cost of fragmentation falls on *large* orders and on *price discovery* — when a lot of volume trades in the dark, the public quote reflects less of the true supply and demand. That is a market-quality concern worth taking seriously, but it is not the same as "you personally get a worse fill."

## How it shows up in real markets

**The flash crash, May 6, 2010.** As above: the textbook case of liquidity withdrawal. ~1,000 Dow points in minutes, trades printing from a penny to \$100,000, then a near-full recovery the same afternoon. The mechanism was makers rationally pulling quotes when inventory and adverse-selection risk spiked — and the regulatory aftermath (limit up–limit down, refined circuit breakers) was an attempt to make withdrawal less catastrophic.

**The penny-pilot and the tick-size debate.** After decimalization tightened spreads to a cent, regulators worried that *too-tight* ticks left makers too little margin to bother quoting illiquid small-caps — so the SEC ran a **tick-size pilot (2016–2018)** widening ticks to 5 cents for some small-caps to see if fatter spreads would lure more makers and more liquidity. The results were mixed, which itself proves the point: the spread is a delicate balance between *cheap for traders* and *worth it for makers*, and you can't tighten it past the makers' three costs without driving them away.

**Citadel Securities and the 2021 meme-stock episode.** When GameStop and other meme stocks went vertical in January 2021, the firms internalizing the retail tsunami — Citadel Securities prominent among them — faced enormous, volatile inventory and adverse-selection risk on names whose "fair value" was anyone's guess. Quoted spreads on the affected names widened sharply and some brokers restricted trading, partly because the clearinghouse margin tied to that volatility surged. It was a live demonstration that even the largest, fastest makers reprice (and partners pull back) when inventory and information risk explode.

**Treasury market "flash events."** Even the deepest market on earth — US Treasuries — has had liquidity air-pockets (notably October 15, 2014, when the 10-year yield swung wildly with no news). The makers there are electronic too, and they widen and withdraw under stress just like equity makers. Depth and tight headline spreads did not prevent a momentary evaporation. The yield mechanics of Treasuries themselves belong elsewhere — see [the yield curve explained](/blog/trading/fixed-income/the-yield-curve-explained-the-most-important-chart-in-finance) — but the *liquidity* behavior is the same machine described here.

**The frictionless retail trade.** The everyday case: you sell 100 shares and a wholesaler fills you instantly, often a hair inside the public quote. The whole apparatus of this post — two-sided quoting, the three-cost spread, inventory skewing, the race for speed — runs invisibly so that your tap feels like nothing happened. That invisibility is the product working as designed.

**Corporate bonds and the dealer-quote market.** Not every secondary market is a fast electronic order book. Most corporate bonds still trade in a *dealer* market: you call (or message) several dealers, each quotes you a bid and an ask, and you trade with the best. Spreads here are far wider than in equities — often dozens of basis points or more — because the same three costs are all larger: a given bond may not trade for days (huge inventory risk and holding period), few dealers make markets in any one issue (little competition), and the dealer worries you may know something about the issuer's credit (adverse selection). The mechanics differ — phone and chat rather than microseconds — but the cost decomposition is identical. It is the same machine, slowed down and made human, which is the clearest proof that the three costs are fundamental rather than artifacts of electronic trading.

**ETF market making and the arbitrage leash.** Exchange-traded funds add a fascinating wrinkle: an ETF can be *created* and *redeemed* in kind against its underlying basket. A maker who gets long ETF shares it can't sell can hand them to the issuer and receive the underlying stocks (redemption), or assemble the basket and create new ETF shares to deliver (creation). This gives the ETF maker an extra inventory-management lever that a single-stock maker lacks — its ultimate hedge is the basket itself — and it is why liquid ETFs can quote spreads even tighter than some of their underlying holdings. The maker is arbitraging the ETF against its components, and that arbitrage is the leash that keeps the ETF price glued to its net asset value.

## The takeaway: liquidity is a service, priced and provided by someone

Step back to the spine. A security is only worth issuing in the primary market if it is *sellable* in the secondary market — if a buyer believes she can get out tomorrow morning without crashing the price. Market makers are the institution that makes "tomorrow morning" credible. They are not a sideshow to the secondary market; they *are* the mechanism by which it provides liquidity at all. The bid-ask spread is the visible price of that liquidity, and now you can read it: a tight spread says "many makers compete here, inventory turns fast, little private information"; a wide one says the opposite.

Three things to carry away. First, the spread is **not arbitrary** — it is the sum of order-processing, inventory risk, and adverse selection, and you can reason about why it widens (volatility up → inventory risk up; rumor circulating → adverse selection up; few competitors → all three up). Second, the maker's whole craft is **staying flat** — skewing quotes, hedging, flattening by the close — because the moment it carries an unhedged position it has stopped selling immediacy and started speculating. Third, and most important: this liquidity is **provided, not guaranteed.** It is cheapest when you need it least and can vanish when you need it most, because the maker's costs spike exactly in a crisis. The frictionless market you tap into on a calm Tuesday is a *standing offer*, not a law of nature — and understanding who stands behind it, and why they might step away, is the difference between trusting the machine and understanding it.

This also reframes how to read the rest of the secondary market. When you hear that a stock is "liquid," you now know that means makers compete to quote it tight and deep; when an asset is called "illiquid," you know its makers demand a fat spread for the inventory and information risk of holding it. Liquidity is not a property of the asset alone — it is a statement about whether anyone is willing to stand in the middle and at what price. And because issuance in the primary market depends on that willingness, the health of the market-making business is, quietly, one of the load-bearing conditions for the whole capital-allocation machine to function. A company can only raise money by selling claims that someone, somewhere, will make a market in afterward.

The next time your "sell" fills instantly, you'll know there was someone on the other side — quoting two prices, managing a book, racing to update before the informed trader gets there, and charging you a few basis points for the privilege of not having to wait. That few basis points is the price of a market that works.
