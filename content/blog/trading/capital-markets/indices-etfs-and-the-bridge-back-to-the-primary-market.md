> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An ETF is the one place in all of finance where the secondary market and the primary market are wired together in real time: every time the ETF's traded price drifts from the value of the stocks inside it, a professional firm called an Authorised Participant either creates new ETF shares or destroys old ones in the *primary* market until the gap closes.
>
> - An **index** is just a rules-based basket; how you *weight* it (by price, like the Dow, or by market value, like the S&P 500) decides which companies actually move it.
> - Being **added to an index** is a forced-buying event — passive funds *must* buy, so the flow is predictable and gets front-run. This is "the index effect".
> - The ETF's killer feature is **creation/redemption**: APs swap a basket of the underlying shares for brand-new ETF shares, or hand ETF shares back for the basket — and that primary-market swap is what keeps the ETF's price glued to the value of what it holds.
> - The number to remember: ETFs hold roughly **\$10 trillion+** in the US and are among the most-traded securities on earth, yet most of that trading never touches the primary market — until the price strays, and then it does.

On the morning of a normal trading day, a fund manager in Boston sells \$300 million of an S&P 500 ETF. Nothing happens to Apple, Microsoft, or any of the other 498 companies in the index. No new shares are issued, no boardroom is consulted, no company even knows. The seller simply hands the ETF shares to a buyer on an exchange, the way you'd sell a single share of stock. This is the secondary market doing what it does: transferring an existing claim from one owner to another, with the companies themselves entirely uninvolved.

But suppose the selling is so heavy that the ETF's price on the exchange falls *below* the combined value of the 500 stocks it represents. Now something remarkable happens — something with no equivalent for an ordinary share of stock. A specialist trading firm steps in, buys up the cheap ETF shares, walks them over to the fund company that issued the ETF, and says: "redeem these for me." The fund company *destroys* those ETF shares and hands back the 500 underlying stocks, which the firm sells. The ETF's share count just shrank. A trade in the secondary market reached across and changed the supply of securities in the primary market.

That bridge — the moment secondary trading loops back into primary creation and destruction — is the subject of this post, and it is the clearest, most literal demonstration of this whole series' central idea: a capital market is one machine with two engines, a **primary** engine that creates securities and a **secondary** engine that trades them, and the two are not separate worlds but two sides of the same mechanism. The ETF is where you can watch them touch.

## Foundations: indices, funds, and the primary/secondary split

Before the clever part, the plain parts. Three things need defining from zero: an index, a fund, and the primary-versus-secondary distinction that the rest of this post hangs on.

**An index is a number that summarises a basket.** Before the formal version, a word on why anyone bothered to invent one. In the late nineteenth century, a journalist named Charles Dow wanted a single daily figure that captured "how the market did today" — a headline number a newspaper reader could follow without tracking dozens of individual stocks. That impulse, to compress a sprawling market into one comparable, trackable number, is the entire reason indices exist. An index is a *yardstick*: it lets you say "the market is up 1.2% today," lets a fund manager be measured against a benchmark, and — crucially for this post — gives a rules-based recipe that a fund can mechanically follow. The moment an index became a recipe rather than just a thermometer, it became investable, and that's when the machinery in this post switched on.

Start with your household budget. If you want a single figure for "how expensive is my weekly shop", you pick a fixed basket of groceries — bread, milk, eggs, coffee — and track what that exact basket costs week to week. You're not tracking *every* product in the supermarket; you've chosen a representative set and a rule for combining their prices into one number. A stock index is the same idea pointed at companies. The S&P 500 is a basket of (about) 500 large US companies; its "level" — that famous number, say 5,400 — is a rule-based summary of what those 500 stocks are worth, rescaled. The Dow Jones Industrial Average is a basket of 30 big American firms. The Nasdaq-100 is 100 large non-financial companies listed on the Nasdaq. None of these is a tradeable thing in itself. An index is a *measurement*, like a thermometer reading. You cannot buy "the S&P 500" any more than you can buy "72 degrees Fahrenheit".

**A fund is a pot of money that *is* tradeable.** To actually own the basket, you need a fund — a legal vehicle that holds the real stocks and divides ownership of them into shares you can buy. A traditional **mutual fund** does this once a day: you send money, and after the market closes the fund calculates the value of everything it holds, divides by the number of shares outstanding, and gives you shares at that price. An **exchange-traded fund (ETF)** does it differently — its shares trade all day long on an exchange, like a stock, at whatever price buyers and sellers agree on. That "trades like a stock but holds a basket like a fund" combination is what makes the ETF special, and it's exactly what forces the creation/redemption machinery we'll get to.

**Net asset value (NAV)** is the anchor concept. The NAV of a fund is the per-share value of what it actually holds: total value of all the stocks in the basket, minus any costs, divided by the number of fund shares outstanding. If a fund holds \$1 billion of stock and has 10 million shares, its NAV is \$100 per share. NAV is the *fair* value — what each share is really worth based on its contents. The whole drama of an ETF is the relationship between its NAV and its *market price* (what it actually trades for on the exchange). They should be equal. The mechanism that keeps them equal is the bridge.

**The primary/secondary split** is the spine of this series, so let's pin it precisely. The **primary market** is where securities are *created* and capital is *raised*: a company sells brand-new shares in an IPO, a government auctions brand-new bonds, an ETF issuer mints brand-new ETF shares. Money flows from investors to the issuer; the supply of the security goes up. The **secondary market** is where those already-existing securities are *traded* between investors: you buy a share from another investor on an exchange. No new security is created; the company gets none of the money; ownership simply changes hands. (For the machinery of that trading — the order book and the matching engine — see [Inside an Exchange: The Matching Engine and the Order Book](/blog/trading/capital-markets/inside-an-exchange-the-matching-engine-and-the-order-book).)

One more building block before the spine: the difference between an **open-end** and a **closed-end** fund, because the ETF is a clever hybrid and you can't appreciate it without the contrast. A **closed-end fund** issues a fixed number of shares once, in an IPO, and then never creates or destroys more. Its shares trade among investors on an exchange, and because the share count is frozen, the price is set purely by supply and demand for *the shares* — which can drift far from the value of what the fund holds. Closed-end funds routinely trade at 10–20% discounts to their net asset value, and there's nothing to close the gap. An **open-end fund** (the classic mutual fund) does the opposite: it creates and destroys its own shares on demand, at NAV, once a day, so its share count breathes with investor flows and its price is *always exactly* NAV — but you can only transact once a day, at a price you don't know in advance.

The ETF takes the best of both: it trades on an exchange all day like a closed-end fund (intraday liquidity, a live price), *and* its share count breathes like an open-end fund (so it can't drift permanently from NAV). The trick that lets it have both — exchange trading and an elastic share count — is exactly the creation/redemption mechanism, run by APs rather than by the fund itself. Hold that contrast in mind: a closed-end fund is what an ETF would degrade into if the APs ever walked away.

The reason these two markets exist together, and the reason this series keeps repeating it: **secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible.** Nobody buys a newly-issued 30-year bond or a fresh IPO share unless they're confident they can sell it tomorrow morning to someone else. The promise of an easy exit is what makes people willing to enter. For almost every security, those two markets are connected only loosely — an IPO happens once, then the stock trades in the secondary market for decades with no further primary activity. The ETF is the exception that proves the rule: its primary and secondary markets are connected *continuously, automatically, every single trading day*, by arbitrage. That's why it's the perfect capstone for understanding the secondary market — it's where you can finally see the two engines mesh.

## What an index actually is: a rules-based basket

An index has three moving parts: the **constituents** (which securities are in it), the **weights** (how much each one counts), and the **rules** (how and when the first two change). Get those three right and you have a working index; the rest is bookkeeping.

The deepest decision is weighting, because it silently determines which companies actually drive the number. There are three common schemes.

**Price-weighting** counts each stock in proportion to its *share price*. The Dow Jones Industrial Average works this way: you add up the share prices of all 30 stocks and divide by a special number called the divisor. The bizarre consequence is that a company is influential in the Dow *because its stock has a high dollar price per share*, which has nothing to do with how big the company is. A firm trading at \$500 a share moves the Dow ten times as much as a firm trading at \$50 a share, even if the \$50 company is worth five times more in total. Price-weighting is a historical accident — it was easy to compute by hand in 1896 — and almost no serious index uses it today.

**Market-cap-weighting** counts each stock in proportion to its total market value (share price × number of shares outstanding). This is what the S&P 500, the Nasdaq-100, and nearly every important index use. A \$3 trillion company counts for a hundred times more than a \$30 billion company. This matches economic intuition: the index reflects where the value actually is. It also has a self-correcting property — as a stock rises, its weight rises automatically, so a cap-weighted fund doesn't need to trade just because prices moved.

**Float-adjusted market-cap-weighting** is the refinement everyone actually uses. "Float" means the shares genuinely available to public investors — it excludes shares locked up by founders, governments, or strategic holders who never trade them. If a company is 80% owned by its founder and only 20% trades freely, weighting it by its *full* market cap would overstate how much of it the index can really buy. So the S&P 500 weights by *float-adjusted* cap, counting only the tradeable portion. This matters enormously in markets like Vietnam, where state ownership and foreign-ownership limits shrink the effective float — see [Foreign Flows, ETFs, and the Index Effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam) for how that plays out.

![Matrix comparing price-weighted and market-cap-weighted indices on weighting, big movers, and divisor role](/imgs/blogs/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market-3.png)

#### Worked example: a price-weighted vs cap-weighted index from three stocks

Take three companies. **Alpha**: price \$300/share, 10 million shares → market cap \$3 billion. **Beta**: price \$30/share, 500 million shares → market cap \$15 billion. **Gamma**: price \$60/share, 200 million shares → market cap \$12 billion.

Build a **price-weighted** index (Dow-style): add the prices, \$300 + \$30 + \$60 = \$390, and divide by 3 (a simple divisor) → index level **130**. Notice who dominates: Alpha is 300/390 ≈ **77%** of the index, even though Alpha is the *smallest* company by a wide margin. If Alpha rises 10% (to \$330) the index rises to (330 + 30 + 60)/3 = \$140, a 7.7% move — driven almost entirely by the tiny company, simply because its share price is high.

Now build a **cap-weighted** index. Total market cap = \$3bn + \$15bn + \$12bn = \$30 billion. Alpha's weight = 3/30 = **10%**; Beta = **50%**; Gamma = **40%**. If Alpha rises 10%, total cap rises by \$300 million to \$30.3 billion — a **1%** index move. The same 10% jump in Alpha that swung the price-weighted index 7.7% barely registers here.

The intuition: weighting isn't a technicality — it decides whose news the index reacts to. Price-weighting lets a small, high-priced company tail-wag the dog; cap-weighting tracks where the economic value really sits.

### The divisor: keeping the number continuous

Here's a problem. Suppose Alpha does a 2-for-1 stock split: every \$300 share becomes two \$150 shares. The company is worth exactly the same; nothing real happened. But in our price-weighted index, the price sum just dropped from \$390 to \$240, which would crater the index level from 130 to 80 for no reason. That would be absurd — the index is supposed to measure value, not get fooled by cosmetic share-count changes.

The fix is the **divisor**, the number you divide by. Instead of dividing by a fixed 3, you adjust the divisor so the index level doesn't jump at the moment of a non-economic change. Before the split the index was \$390 / divisor = 130, so the divisor was 3.0. After the split the price sum is \$240, and we want the level to *stay* at 130, so we solve \$240 / divisor = 130 → divisor = **1.846**. From then on you divide by 1.846. The divisor absorbs the split, the index glides through smoothly, and over years of splits, additions, and removals the divisor drifts far from its starting value. (The Dow's divisor today is well below 1, which is why a \$1 move in one Dow stock moves the index by more than a point.) Cap-weighted indices have the same machinery, applied when constituents are added or removed rather than when they split.

The divisor is a small idea with a big job: it makes the index a *continuous* series across structural changes, so that when the number moves, it's because value moved — not because the basket's plumbing was rearranged.

The same logic applies to cap-weighted indices, just triggered by different events. When a company is added to the S&P 500, the index would suddenly contain more total market value, which would jump the level upward for a non-economic reason. So the divisor is adjusted at the instant of inclusion to absorb the addition and keep the level continuous. Same for deletions, for large secondary offerings that change a company's share count, for spin-offs, and for big buybacks. The index provider runs a small back office that does nothing but recompute the divisor every time the basket's composition changes, so that the published level only ever reflects price movement, never reshuffling. This is unglamorous and essential — an index that jumped every time a constituent changed would be useless as a benchmark, because you could never tell a real market move from a bookkeeping artefact.

There is one more subtlety worth knowing: **rebalancing schedules**. Indices don't change constituents continuously; they change them on a calendar. The S&P 500 reviews quarterly (and adds or drops names as needed in between for events like mergers). FTSE Russell famously reconstitutes its entire family once a year in late June — "Russell rebalance day" — which has become one of the highest-volume trading days of the year in the US precisely because so much passive money has to reshuffle at once. The fact that the rebalance is scheduled and rules-based is what makes it predictable, and predictability is what creates the index effect we turn to shortly. Everything traces back to the same root: an index is a *transparent rule*, and transparent rules can be anticipated.

### The index provider as a paid gatekeeper

Who decides what's in the S&P 500? A company — S&P Dow Jones Indices — with a committee that applies published rules (minimum market cap, profitability, US domicile, sufficient float, sector balance). MSCI, FTSE Russell, and Nasdaq run their own indices the same way. These firms are not charities. They license the index to fund companies, who pay a fee to call their fund "an S&P 500 fund," and they sell the data. Index providers have quietly become some of the most profitable businesses in finance, because trillions of dollars now track their decisions. When a committee decides to add or drop a company, it is, in effect, issuing an instruction that hundreds of billions of passive dollars must obey. That power — being the gatekeeper to automatic inflows — is the hinge for the next section.

![US equity market capitalisation by year from 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market-2.png)

The chart above shows why this gatekeeping matters more every year: the total US equity market the indices carve up has roughly doubled over a decade, from about \$26 trillion to nearly \$58 trillion. A larger pie tracked by more passive money means each inclusion decision moves more dollars.

## Index inclusion as an event: the index effect

If passive funds are *required* by their own mandate to hold the index basket, then the day a stock joins the index, every fund tracking that index must buy it — and the day a stock leaves, every fund must sell it. This is not a choice or a forecast; it's a mechanical obligation written into the fund's rules. And because the change is *announced in advance* (S&P typically announces a few business days before the effective date), everyone in the market knows the forced buying is coming. Predictable forced flow is a trader's dream. This is the **index effect**.

![Pipeline of the index effect from announcement to fast money to forced fund buying to fading pop](/imgs/blogs/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market-5.png)

The sequence: the provider announces that XYZ joins the index, effective at the close on a future date. Fast-moving traders immediately buy XYZ, anticipating that index funds will *have* to buy it at the effective date. Their buying pushes the price up before the date even arrives. Then, at the rebalance, the index funds pile in — they typically trade at the closing auction on the effective day, because their benchmark is struck at the close and they want to match it exactly. That concentrated demand spikes the price (and volume) on the day. Afterwards, with the forced buyers satisfied, the artificial premium often fades over the following weeks. Historically the announcement-to-inclusion pop for S&P 500 additions ran several percent in the 1990s, though it has compressed as more traders learned to anticipate it — when everyone front-runs the same event, the easy money thins out.

#### Worked example: a \$50 billion index buying a newly-added stock

Suppose a stock, NewCo, is added to an index with a 0.10% target weight. The total assets tracking that index — index funds plus ETFs benchmarked to it — come to \$50 billion. To hold NewCo at its index weight, those funds collectively must buy 0.10% × \$50 billion = **\$50 million** of NewCo.

Now ask: how big is that relative to NewCo's normal trading? Say NewCo trades **\$10 million** of stock on an average day. The funds need to absorb the equivalent of **five full days** of normal volume, and most of it must clear in a single closing auction. When demand of that size hits a stock whose sellers haven't grown to match, the price has to rise to coax out enough sellers. A pop of several percent is unsurprising. If NewCo's float is small — say only \$2 billion of stock trades freely — then \$50 million is 2.5% of the entire free float being bought essentially at once.

The intuition: an index inclusion converts a committee's announcement into a fixed-size buy order that the whole world can see in advance, and a known, forced buyer is the easiest thing in markets to trade ahead of.

This is also where index investing stops being purely "passive." A fund that *must* buy at the close, in size, on a publicly-known day, is a price-taker handing a known edge to faster traders. Some index providers have responded by spreading inclusions over multiple days or using volume-weighted methods to reduce the giveaway. But the deeper point stands: **inclusion is a primary-market-like event happening in the secondary market** — a committee decision creates a wave of demand for a security, the same way a deal announcement does. It's a preview of the tighter coupling the ETF makes total.

### Who pays for the index effect, and who collects

It's worth being precise about who wins and who loses, because the index effect is not a victimless quirk. The **loser** is the index fund's existing shareholders. When the fund is forced to buy NewCo at a price that's already been pushed up by front-runners, it overpays relative to where NewCo would have traded absent the inclusion. That overpayment is a small, permanent drag on the fund's return — a hidden cost of being mechanically predictable. Estimates of this cost vary, but for the S&P 500 it has historically run on the order of a few basis points a year, which sounds trivial until you multiply it by trillions of dollars and decades.

The **winner** is whoever supplied the stock to the forced buyers at the inflated price: the front-running traders who bought early and sold into the rebalance, and the existing holders of NewCo who happened to be selling that day. There's a genuine transfer of wealth here, from disciplined passive savers to nimble active traders, paid out as the price of predictability. It's the same trade-off that runs through the whole secondary market: liquidity and transparency have a cost, and someone always collects it.

This tension has pushed index providers and fund managers to get cleverer. Some funds now trade *around* the official rebalance date rather than slamming everything through the close, accepting a little tracking error in exchange for a better average price. Some index providers have moved to phasing large additions in over several days. The arms race between forced buyers and front-runners is itself a small illustration of market efficiency: once an edge is widely known, competition erodes it. The early-1990s S&P inclusion pop of 3–5% has compressed precisely because everyone learned the game.

## The ETF and its killer feature: creation and redemption

Now the heart of it. An ordinary closed-end fund issues a fixed number of shares once and then lets them trade. If demand for its shares outruns demand for its holdings, the fund's price can float far above or below its NAV — closed-end funds routinely trade at 10–20% discounts to what they hold, and there's no mechanism to close the gap. The ETF solves this with an elastic share count and a special class of trading partner.

That partner is the **Authorised Participant (AP)** — typically a large bank or market-making firm (think a Goldman Sachs, a Jane Street, a Citadel Securities) that has signed an agreement with the ETF issuer. The AP has a unique right that ordinary investors don't: it can transact directly with the fund in the *primary* market, creating or destroying ETF shares in large blocks called **creation units** (commonly 25,000 or 50,000 ETF shares at a time). For the broader role these firms play as the liquidity providers of the whole market, see [How a Price Is Made: Discovery, Arbitrage, and Efficiency](/blog/trading/capital-markets/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency).

**Creation** works like this. The ETF issuer publishes, every morning, the exact basket of stocks that makes up one creation unit — say, the 500 S&P stocks in their index proportions, plus a little cash for odds and ends. An AP that wants new ETF shares goes into the secondary market, *buys that exact basket of 500 stocks*, delivers the basket to the issuer, and in return receives a creation unit of brand-new ETF shares. The issuer has just *minted* ETF shares out of thin air — well, out of the stocks the AP delivered. This is unambiguously a primary-market transaction: new securities created, basket of value handed over, share count rises. The AP can now sell those fresh ETF shares to investors on the exchange.

**Redemption** is the mirror image. An AP that holds ETF shares (or buys them cheaply on the exchange) hands a creation unit's worth back to the issuer and receives the underlying basket of 500 stocks in return. The issuer *destroys* those ETF shares; the share count falls. The AP then sells the basket of stocks in the secondary market. Again: a primary-market transaction, securities destroyed, in reverse.

Two design details make this elegant. First, it's an **in-kind** swap — stocks for ETF shares, not cash for ETF shares. The fund never has to sell its holdings to meet redemptions; it just hands them out. This is hugely tax-efficient in the US, because the fund can hand out its most-appreciated shares and never realise a capital gain, which is a big reason ETFs distribute far fewer taxable gains than mutual funds. Second, the AP bears all the trading friction — it's the one buying or selling the basket in the market — so existing fund shareholders aren't diluted by other investors' comings and goings, the way mutual-fund holders can be.

#### Worked example: creating one creation unit

An AP wants to create one creation unit of an S&P 500 ETF, defined as 50,000 ETF shares. The published basket for one creation unit is the proportional slice of all 500 stocks, with a total market value of, say, **\$5,000,000** (so each ETF share represents \$100 of underlying stock — the NAV is \$100). The AP goes into the secondary market and buys exactly that \$5,000,000 basket of 500 stocks. It delivers the basket to the issuer. The issuer mints **50,000** new ETF shares and hands them to the AP. The AP now owns 50,000 ETF shares, each backed by \$100 of stock it just bought. No cash changed hands with the fund — it was stocks in, ETF shares out. The ETF's total shares outstanding rose by 50,000, and its assets rose by \$5,000,000, so NAV per share is unchanged at \$100.

The intuition: the AP didn't get anything for free — it paid \$5,000,000 of stock for \$5,000,000 of ETF. Creation only becomes *profitable* when the ETF can be sold for more than the basket cost, which is exactly the arbitrage we turn to next.

### The AP ecosystem: who they are and why they show up

It's worth dwelling on *why* an AP signs up for this job, because the answer reveals how the whole mechanism is held together by self-interest rather than rules. APs are not charities appointed to keep ETFs fair; they are profit-seeking trading firms — large banks like Goldman Sachs and Bank of America, and electronic market makers like Jane Street, Citadel Securities, and Virtu. They become APs because the create/redeem right is a privileged seat: only APs can transact directly with the fund in the primary market, which makes them the gateway through which all net new demand or supply must pass. That position lets them capture the arbitrage profit whenever the ETF strays from NAV, and it lets them quote tight two-sided markets in the ETF on the exchange, earning the bid-ask spread on the enormous volume that flows through popular ETFs.

A given ETF typically has several APs under contract — often a dozen or more for a big fund — and only a few of them are active on any day. This redundancy matters for resilience: if one AP pulls back, others can step in. The concern in a crisis is *correlated* withdrawal — every AP simultaneously deciding the basket is too risky to touch. That's rare, but it's the scenario that keeps regulators up at night, because the arbitrage link depends on at least one AP being willing to act. In the big liquid ETFs, that willingness has never seriously failed; in tiny niche ETFs, it's a real fragility.

There's also a distinction worth drawing between an AP and an ordinary **market maker** in the ETF. Anyone can make markets in an ETF on the exchange — quoting a bid and offer, profiting from the spread, hedging their inventory by trading the underlying basket. But only an AP can actually create or redeem. In practice the big firms wear both hats: they make markets in the ETF *and* hold the AP licence, so when their inventory builds up too far in one direction, they clear it by creating or redeeming rather than dumping it on the exchange. The market-making keeps the ETF's day-to-day spread tight; the AP right keeps the ETF tethered to NAV. Together they make the ETF behave like the basket it represents, only easier to trade.

## How that keeps price = NAV: the secondary-to-primary loop, made literal

Why would an AP bother? Because the create/redeem right is a licence to arbitrage, and the arbitrage is what forces the ETF's market price to track its NAV. Here's the engine.

The ETF trades on the exchange at a market price set by ordinary supply and demand. Its NAV is the value of the basket it holds. Most of the time these are within a hair of each other. But suppose a wave of buyers pushes the ETF's market price *above* NAV — the ETF is trading "rich" or at a **premium**. Now the AP sees free money: it can buy the cheap *basket* of stocks in the market, deliver it to create new ETF shares at NAV, and immediately sell those new ETF shares at the higher market price, pocketing the difference. Crucially, this very activity closes the gap: the AP's buying of the basket nudges the stocks up (raising NAV), and its selling of ETF shares nudges the ETF price down (lowering the premium). The AP keeps doing it until premium ≈ 0.

The mirror case: a wave of sellers pushes the ETF's market price *below* NAV — trading "cheap" or at a **discount**. The AP buys the cheap ETF shares in the market, redeems them for the more-valuable basket of stocks, and sells the stocks, pocketing the difference. Its buying of ETF shares lifts the ETF price; its selling of the basket lowers NAV; the discount closes.

This is the moment to connect it to the series spine, because it is the most literal example you will ever find. **A trade in the secondary market (the ETF straying from NAV) triggers a transaction in the primary market (creation or redemption of ETF shares).** Secondary and primary aren't separate worlds here — they're two ends of one arbitrage trade, executed by the same firm in the same minute. The secondary-market liquidity (the ability to trade ETF shares cheaply) and the primary-market elasticity (the ability to create/redeem at NAV) are the two halves of a single machine, and the machine's output is: *ETF price = NAV, continuously.* Everything this series has said about the two engines being one — this is where you can put your finger on the gear that connects them.

#### Worked example: an ETF creation arbitrage at a premium

The ETF's NAV is \$100.00 (the basket is worth \$100 per ETF share). But heavy buying has pushed its market price to **\$100.30** — a 30-cent premium, 0.30%. An AP acts on one creation unit of 50,000 shares.

- It buys the underlying basket in the market for 50,000 × \$100.00 = **\$5,000,000**.
- It delivers the basket and the issuer mints 50,000 new ETF shares (the create).
- It sells those 50,000 ETF shares at the market price of \$100.30 → 50,000 × \$100.30 = **\$5,015,000**.
- Gross profit = \$5,015,000 − \$5,000,000 = **\$15,000**, i.e. \$0.30 × 50,000.

Out of that \$15,000 the AP pays trading commissions, the basket's bid-ask spread, and a small creation fee to the issuer — say \$3,000–\$5,000 all in — leaving a real profit of roughly **\$10,000–\$12,000** for a few minutes' work, and the act of doing it pushed the premium back toward zero. The intuition: the AP's profit *is* the premium, so APs compete the premium away — which is exactly why you, the retail buyer, almost never overpay for an ETF.

#### Worked example: a redemption when the ETF trades at a discount

Now the opposite. Panic selling drops the ETF's market price to **\$99.70** while NAV is still \$100.00 — a 30-cent discount. The AP acts on 50,000 shares.

- It buys 50,000 ETF shares in the market for 50,000 × \$99.70 = **\$4,985,000**.
- It delivers them to the issuer and redeems for the underlying basket worth 50,000 × \$100.00 = **\$5,000,000**.
- It sells the basket in the market for **\$5,000,000**.
- Gross profit = \$5,000,000 − \$4,985,000 = **\$15,000**, again \$0.30 × 50,000.

The AP's buying of ETF shares lifts the depressed ETF price; its selling of the basket trims NAV; the discount narrows. The intuition: discounts are just as arbitrageable as premiums, so an ETF gets pulled back to fair value from *both* sides — the create leg fixes premiums, the redeem leg fixes discounts.

The size of the gap that's left over isn't zero — it's roughly the AP's total cost to do the trade (spreads + fees + the risk of price moving while it works). For a giant, liquid ETF holding mega-cap US stocks, that cost is a fraction of a basis point, so the ETF tracks NAV to within pennies all day. For an ETF holding illiquid stuff — emerging-market small caps, high-yield bonds that trade by appointment — the cost of assembling or unwinding the basket is large, so the band within which the price can wander is wider. That observation is the door into the systemic questions.

### Two different gaps: premium/discount versus tracking error

People conflate two distinct measures of "how well does this ETF do its job," and separating them sharpens the whole picture.

The first is the **premium/discount to NAV** — the gap we've been arbitraging, between the ETF's *market price* and the *current value of its basket*, measured at the same instant. This is the AP's responsibility, and for liquid ETFs it stays within a basis point or two. It's a statement about the ETF's plumbing: is the create/redeem arbitrage working right now?

The second is **tracking error** — how closely the fund's *return over time* matches the *index's* return. Even a perfectly-arbitraged ETF will drift from its index because of frictions the arbitrage can't touch: the fund's expense ratio (a 0.20% fee is a guaranteed 0.20%/year of underperformance), the cost of trading when the index rebalances, dividend timing (the fund holds cash for a few days between receiving a dividend and reinvesting it), and sometimes deliberate sampling (a fund tracking a 3,000-stock index may hold only the 1,200 most important names to save on trading costs, accepting a little drift). Tracking error is a statement about the fund's *design and costs*, not its moment-to-moment pricing.

#### Worked example: separating premium from tracking error

Take a bond ETF over one year. Its underlying index returned **+6.00%**. The fund charges a **0.15%** expense ratio and lost another **0.10%** to trading and cash drag, so the fund's NAV returned **+5.75%** — a tracking error of −0.25% versus the index, entirely explained by costs. Separately, on the last day of the year, the ETF happened to close trading at a **0.08% premium** to its NAV because of late buying. An investor who bought at that closing price paid 0.08% over fair value. The two numbers measure different things: the −0.25% is the cost of owning the fund for a year; the +0.08% is the cost of buying it at that particular moment. A careful investor minimises the first by choosing a low-fee, well-run fund, and minimises the second by trading when the ETF's spread is tight (mid-session, not the chaotic first and last minutes). The intuition: NAV-tracking is the AP's job and it's nearly free; index-tracking is the fund manager's job and it costs you the expense ratio, every year, forever.

## Why it matters: passive's rise, ETFs as the most-traded securities, and the systemic questions

Step back and look at scale. Index funds and ETFs together now own roughly **28%** of the entire US stock market — more than any other category except households' direct holdings.

![Bar chart of who owns US equities by holder type, share in percent](/imgs/blogs/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market-4.png)

That share has climbed for forty years for a simple, brutal reason: most active managers fail to beat their index after fees, so investors have voted with their feet for cheap funds that just *buy the basket*. The arithmetic is unforgiving and was spelled out by the economist William Sharpe in 1991: before costs, the average actively-managed dollar must earn exactly the market return, because together active managers *are* the market; after costs, the average active dollar must therefore *underperform* the market by its (higher) costs. This isn't a claim that no manager can win — some do — it's a claim that they can't *all* win, and the average active investor is mathematically destined to trail a cheap index fund by roughly the fee difference. Decades of data have borne this out, and the money has followed the math. An S&P 500 index fund charging 0.03% a year will, over decades, beat the majority of stock-pickers charging 1%, almost entirely on the cost difference. ETFs, with their intraday tradability and tax efficiency, became the preferred wrapper for this passive wave — and they are now among the most heavily-traded securities on any exchange. On a busy day, a single S&P 500 ETF (SPY) can trade more *dollars* than any individual stock in America.

But notice the elegant fact buried in all that volume: **the overwhelming majority of ETF trading never touches the primary market.** When you buy SPY from another investor, no creation happens — it's a pure secondary-market swap. Creation and redemption only fire when the *net* demand for the ETF can't be met by existing shares changing hands, i.e. when the price would otherwise stray from NAV. The secondary market handles the routine flow; the primary market is the pressure-release valve that engages only when needed. That's the same division of labour the whole capital market runs on, compressed into a single product. ETF trading sits squarely in the off-exchange and lit-venue mix that defines modern US equity microstructure:

![Off-exchange share of US equity volume by year](/imgs/blogs/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market-6.png)

Before the hard questions, notice how this loops all the way back to primary issuance — the other engine of the machine. A deep, liquid ETF market changes the calculus of *raising* capital. When a company knows that being added to a major index will bring a wave of automatic passive buying, index inclusion becomes a milestone worth engineering toward — companies time secondary offerings, manage their float, and even structure share classes with an eye on index eligibility rules. More broadly, the existence of cheap, liquid index products draws household savings off the sidelines and into equities, which deepens the pool of capital available to fund new issuance: every dollar that flows into an S&P 500 fund is a dollar more demand for the shares of the 500 companies in it, supporting their valuations and lowering their cost of raising fresh equity. The secondary-market product (the ETF) feeds the primary-market function (companies raising money) by enlarging and stabilising the savings pool that primary issuance taps. That's the spine again, viewed from altitude: a better secondary market makes primary issuance cheaper and more reliable, which is the entire reason the two engines are bolted together.

Now the hard questions, because a product this big has system-level consequences.

**Does indexing distort price discovery?** Price discovery — the market's job of figuring out what things are worth — depends on someone actually analysing companies and trading on their views. A pure index fund does no analysis; it buys every constituent in proportion, sending money to good and bad companies alike. If *everyone* indexed, prices would stop reflecting fundamentals, because no one would be doing the work that sets them. We're nowhere near 100% passive, and active traders still set prices at the margin (and arbitrageurs keep the ETF honest). But the worry is real: as the passive share rises, the price-setting is done by an ever-smaller slice of active capital, which could make prices noisier and more prone to herding. The honest answer is that we don't know the tipping point, and reasonable economists disagree.

**What happens in stress when the basket is illiquid but the ETF still trades?** This is the sharpest fear, and it gets the spine wrong if you're not careful. Consider a high-yield bond ETF. The underlying bonds might trade only a few times a day; in a panic, they might not trade at all, so dealers stop quoting. But the ETF keeps trading on the exchange, every second, because that's where the liquidity has migrated. In March 2020, several bond ETFs traded at steep *discounts* to their stated NAV — at one point a major investment-grade bond ETF was about 5% below NAV. Critics screamed that the ETF was "broken." It wasn't. The ETF's traded price was the *real, live* price of that risk; the NAV was *stale*, built from yesterday's or last-hour's marks on bonds that hadn't actually traded. The discount wasn't a failure of the ETF — it was the ETF doing **price discovery** that the frozen underlying market couldn't. The arbitrage band simply widened to reflect how expensive it had become to transact the basket.

![Before and after comparison of an ETF in stress versus calm conditions](/imgs/blogs/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market-7.png)

That doesn't make ETFs risk-free. If APs ever stepped back entirely — if no firm were willing to create or redeem because the basket was untradeable at any price — the arbitrage link would snap and the ETF would trade purely on its own supply and demand, like a closed-end fund. In a deep enough crisis that's conceivable for the most illiquid corners (leveraged or thinly-traded niche ETFs). But for the big, liquid funds, the 2020 episode is reassuring: the machinery bent and held. The lesson is to match the ETF's promised liquidity to its holdings' real liquidity — an ETF is never more liquid than the basket behind it *in the moment that matters*.

**Does the create/redeem machine itself amplify shocks?** A related worry is that redemptions could force fire-sales. When investors flee an ETF, APs redeem shares and receive the underlying basket — which, for an in-kind ETF, they then sell *themselves*, on their own schedule, hedged. The fund never sells under duress; it hands out securities. This is structurally gentler than a mutual fund, which must sell holdings to raise cash for redeeming investors, potentially dumping assets into a falling market and sticking the *remaining* shareholders with the bill and the tax. The ETF's in-kind design pushes the selling onto the redeeming party and out of the shared pool. That said, ETFs that hold genuinely illiquid assets sometimes redeem *in cash* rather than in kind (because handing over a slice of an untradeable bond portfolio is impractical), and a cash-redeeming ETF *does* have to sell holdings — reintroducing exactly the fire-sale channel the in-kind design was meant to avoid. So the reassurance is conditional: in-kind ETFs of liquid assets are robust; cash-redeeming ETFs of illiquid assets carry the old mutual-fund risk in a new wrapper.

**What about the concentration of APs and index providers?** A handful of firms make markets in and arbitrage the biggest ETFs, and a handful of providers (S&P, MSCI, FTSE Russell) define the indices that trillions track. That concentration is efficient in calm times and a single point of fragility in a crisis. If the dominant market maker in a class of ETFs had to step back to manage its own risk, spreads in those ETFs would widen and the arbitrage band would loosen across the whole class at once. Regulators have studied this and broadly concluded the big-ETF ecosystem is resilient, but the honest position is that we've never stress-tested it in a true systemic seizure with the passive share as high as it is today.

## Common misconceptions

**"When I buy an ETF, the fund buys the underlying stocks."** Almost never. The overwhelming majority of ETF trades are secondary-market swaps between investors — the fund does nothing and buys nothing. Underlying stocks are bought only when an AP *creates* a unit to meet net new demand, which is a small fraction of total ETF trading. Your \$1,000 ETF purchase usually just moves an existing ETF share from a seller to you.

**"The ETF price is set by the fund company at NAV."** No. The market price is set by supply and demand on the exchange, like any stock, and can sit above or below NAV. NAV is the *anchor*, not the *price*. What keeps the two close is AP arbitrage, not a rule. Remove the APs and the ETF would float away from NAV like a closed-end fund.

**"A premium or discount to NAV means the ETF is broken."** Usually it means the *NAV is stale*. For bond and international ETFs, NAV is built from prices that may be hours or a day old, while the ETF trades live. In March 2020 bond-ETF discounts of several percent were the ETF telling the truth about prices the frozen underlying market couldn't. The discount was information, not breakage.

**"Index funds are truly passive — they don't move markets."** They're passive in *strategy* but forced in *timing*. On rebalance days an index fund *must* buy the additions and sell the deletions, in size, at the close. That predictable forced flow is the index effect — and being a forced, telegraphed buyer is the opposite of being invisible. See [The Life of a Security: From Idea to Delisting](/blog/trading/capital-markets/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting) for how inclusion and deletion bracket a security's tradeable life.

**"The Dow is the best gauge of the US stock market."** The Dow is 30 stocks, price-weighted — a methodology so quirky that a high-priced stock can dominate it regardless of company size. The S&P 500 (500 stocks, float-cap-weighted) is a far better measure of the actual market. The Dow survives on familiarity, not rigour.

## How it shows up in real markets

**SPY, the original ETF (1993).** State Street's SPDR S&P 500 ETF was the first US ETF and remains one of the most-traded securities on the planet, regularly turning over tens of billions of dollars a day. Its create/redeem machinery has run for three decades through crashes and booms, and SPY tracks the S&P 500 NAV to a fraction of a basis point on a normal day — a living demonstration that the arbitrage works at enormous scale.

**The Tesla S&P 500 inclusion (December 2020).** When S&P announced Tesla would join the index, it was the largest company ever added. Index funds tracking the S&P 500 had to buy roughly **\$80 billion** of Tesla at the December 18, 2020 rebalance to hold it at weight — one of the biggest single-name index trades in history. Tesla's stock ran up about 70% between the announcement and the inclusion date as the market front-ran the forced buying, then the demand was absorbed in a record closing auction. It was the index effect at maximum volume: a committee decision converted into an \$80 billion forced buy order the whole world watched coming.

**The March 2020 bond-ETF discounts.** As COVID froze credit markets, investment-grade and high-yield bond ETFs traded at discounts of 3–5% to their stated NAVs. Headlines declared ETFs broken. The Federal Reserve even announced it would buy bond ETFs as part of its emergency facilities. In hindsight the discounts were the ETFs price-discovering through a market where the underlying bonds had simply stopped trading; as liquidity returned, the discounts closed and the funds tracked NAV again. The episode is now the canonical case study for "the ETF is never more liquid than its basket, but it can be more *informative* than its basket."

**Vietnam and the index-effect trade.** In smaller markets the index effect is amplified by limited float and concentrated foreign flows. When a Vietnamese stock is added to a major regional index or a popular Vietnam ETF rebalances, the forced buying can be a large fraction of the stock's free float, producing sharp, predictable moves that local traders position around. The mechanics — float adjustment, foreign-ownership limits, ETF-driven flow — are exactly the ones in this post, in a market where they bite harder. See [Foreign Flows, ETFs, and the Index Effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).

## The takeaway: where the two engines mesh

This series has argued from the start that a capital market is one machine with two engines — a primary engine that creates securities and raises capital, a secondary engine that trades them and provides liquidity — and that the secret holding it together is that secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible. For most securities that link is loose and intermittent: a company issues stock once and the secondary market takes over for decades.

The ETF is where the link becomes tight, visible, and continuous. Every time the secondary price of an ETF strays from the value of what it holds, an arbitrageur reaches across into the primary market and creates or destroys shares until the gap closes. The secondary market sets the price; the primary market supplies the elasticity; the arbitrage is the gear connecting them, turning thousands of times a day. You can finally *see* the two engines as one mechanism, because in an ETF they're bolted together by economic self-interest and they run in real time.

So here is the way to use all of this. When you look at an ETF, don't see a fund — see a live demonstration of the entire capital market in miniature. The market price is the secondary market's verdict. The NAV is the primary market's anchor. The tiny, ever-present gap between them is the cost of the plumbing — the spreads, fees, and risk the APs charge to keep the two aligned. And when that gap blows out in a crisis, it isn't the machine breaking; it's the machine *reporting* that the underlying market has stopped functioning, and telling you the real price before the slow-moving basket can. The genius of the ETF isn't that it eliminated the distance between trading and issuing. It's that it priced that distance, and then hired competitive arbitrageurs to keep it as small as the world allows. That, in the end, is what a capital market is: a system for finding the price of distance — between buyers and sellers, between savers and projects, between today's liquidity and tomorrow's investment — and for keeping that price as honest as competition can make it.
