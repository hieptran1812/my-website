> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A security is created once to raise money and then trades forever without ever sending the issuer another cent; understanding that single handoff explains the entire capital market.
>
> - A security is *born* in the **primary market** (issuance), where cash flows from buyers to the issuer exactly once, then *lives* in the **secondary market**, where it changes hands between investors who never pay the issuer again.
> - Its life is punctuated by **corporate actions** — dividends, splits, buybacks, and rights issues for stock; coupons, calls, and amortisation for bonds — and behind every trade runs a hidden **clear → settle → custody** chain.
> - It *ends* differently by type: a bond simply matures and is redeemed (or defaults); a stock ends only through buyback, merger, bankruptcy, or delisting.
> - The one number to remember: if a company raises **\$500M** in its IPO and the stock later trades **\$50bn** of volume, the company's share of that \$50bn is **\$0**.

## A Tuesday morning that explains everything

On the morning of an initial public offering, a company's chief financial officer stands on an exchange balcony, presses a button, and watches a stock symbol flicker to life on a wall of screens. Reporters call it the moment the company "goes public." Within seconds the price is bouncing — up 30%, back down, up again — and a number on a ticker is suddenly worth tens of billions of dollars more, or less, than it was the night before.

Here is the part that surprises almost everyone. By the time that price starts bouncing, the company has *already been paid*. Every dollar it will ever raise from selling those shares was wired to its bank account the night before, at a fixed price set in a quiet room full of bankers. The frenzied trading on the screen — the part that makes the news — sends the company nothing. Not a cent. From that second onward, the company is a spectator to its own stock. Two strangers buy and sell its shares back and forth, and the company watches the price like everyone else.

That single, counterintuitive fact — **the issuer gets paid once, and everything after that is investors trading among themselves** — is the master key to the entire capital market. Once you internalise it, the whole machine snaps into focus: why companies obsess over their IPO price but can do nothing about Tuesday's close, why a liquid secondary market is the thing that makes the primary market possible, why there is an enormous invisible plumbing system that nobody outside finance has heard of, and why the life of a bond looks so different from the life of a stock. This post walks one share and one bond through their entire lives, stage by stage, so you can see every handoff for yourself.

![The eight stages of a security's life from financing need to redemption or delisting](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-1.png)

A **capital market** is, at its heart, a machine that turns *savings* into *long-term investment*. Someone has spare money they don't need today (a saver, a pension fund, a retiree); someone else needs money for a multi-decade project (a railway, a chip factory, a government deficit). The capital market is the set of institutions, rules, and contracts that connects them — and the connecting object is the **security**: a tradable financial contract that represents a claim on future cash. This series treats that machine as a system with two engines — a primary market that *creates* securities and a secondary market that *trades* them — joined by plumbing and run by intermediaries. This post is the map. Every later post in the series zooms into one stage you are about to walk through.

## Foundations: what a security is, and the two markets it lives in

Before we trace a life, we need three definitions, built from zero.

**A security is a tradable promise.** When you lend a company money, you could write the loan on a napkin: "Acme owes Hiep \$1,000, payable in ten years, with \$50 of interest each year." That napkin is a contract, but it is awful to trade — nobody else can easily verify it, value it, or take it off your hands. A **security** is that same promise, but standardised, documented, legally registered, and made *fungible* — meaning one unit is interchangeable with any other identical unit, the way one \$10 bill is interchangeable with another. Standardisation is what turns a one-off promise into something a million strangers can buy and sell without ever meeting the issuer. The two great families of securities are **equity** (ownership — a share of stock, a residual claim on whatever is left after everyone else is paid) and **debt** (a loan — a bond, a senior claim to fixed payments). Almost everything else is a variation or combination of those two.

**The primary market is where securities are born.** This is the *issuance* market. A company or government creates brand-new securities and sells them to investors, and the cash from that sale goes to the issuer. This happens once per batch of securities. An initial public offering (IPO) is a primary-market event; so is a company selling new bonds; so is the U.S. Treasury auctioning new notes. The primary market is the only place the issuer ever raises money from these securities.

**The secondary market is where securities live.** After issuance, the original buyers are free to sell to other investors, who sell to others, for as long as the security exists. This is the *trading* market — the stock exchange, the bond dealer network, the screens on the news. Crucially, the cash in a secondary trade flows *between investors*. The issuer is not a party to the transaction and receives nothing.

Here is the deep, non-obvious point that this whole series turns on: **the secondary market is what makes the primary market possible.** No saver would ever fund a 30-year railway if the only way to get their money back was to wait 30 years. They fund it because they know that tomorrow morning, if they change their mind, they can sell their claim to someone else in seconds. **Liquidity** — the ability to convert a security back into cash quickly, at a fair price — is the promise the secondary market makes, and that promise is precisely what unlocks the long-term capital the primary market raises. A patient saver and an impatient one can hold the *same* 30-year claim, because the impatient one can always sell to the next patient one. The secondary market is a liquidity transformer: it lets society fund permanent, illiquid things (factories, infrastructure, governments) with the impermanent, liquid savings of individuals.

One more term you'll need throughout: an **issuer** is the entity that creates the security and is obligated by it — the company whose stock it is, or the borrower who must repay the bond. Keep your eye on the issuer as we go, because the single most clarifying question at every stage is: *does the issuer receive money here, or not?*

It's worth pausing on *why* standardisation is the magic ingredient, because it's easy to wave past. Your napkin loan to Acme is a perfectly valid contract — but to sell it, you'd have to find a buyer, let them read the napkin, let them investigate whether Acme is good for the money, negotiate a price, and draft a fresh contract assigning the loan to them. That is hours of work and a lawyer's fee for a single transfer. A *security* removes every one of those frictions. Its terms are public and identical for every unit, so a buyer doesn't need to read your specific copy. Its issuer is subject to disclosure rules, so the public can assess the credit. It is registered in a central system, so transfer is a database update, not a re-drafted contract. And it is **fungible** — your Acme share is identical to every other Acme share — so buyers don't care *which* unit they get, the way you don't care which specific \$10 bill the cashier hands you. Strip away those frictions and a private promise that took hours to transfer becomes a public security that transfers in milliseconds, millions of times a day. That frictionlessness *is* liquidity, and liquidity is the whole game.

A capital market also can't run on issuers and investors alone; it needs **intermediaries** to bridge the gap between people who have money and people who need it. Investment banks structure and underwrite issuance. Exchanges and dealers provide the venue to trade. Brokers route retail orders. Custodians and clearinghouses move and safeguard the securities. Ratings agencies and auditors certify quality. Each intermediary takes a small toll — a spread, a fee, a commission — in exchange for removing a friction that would otherwise stop the saver and the borrower from ever meeting. We won't dwell on each one here (this series has a whole track on intermediaries), but notice them as we pass: every stage of a security's life has a specialist standing beside it, getting paid to make that stage work.

#### Worked example: a security is a claim, priced by its cash flows

Suppose Acme issues a bond: it promises to pay you \$50 every year for ten years and then return \$1,000 at the end. The bond is just that stream of promised cash: ten payments of \$50 (total \$500 of interest) plus one payment of \$1,000. If you buy it at issuance for \$1,000, your total cash back is \$1,500 over ten years. Whether the bond is "worth" exactly \$1,000 later depends on interest rates and Acme's health — but the *contract* never changes. The security is the promise; the price is the market's running opinion of that promise. (How rates move a bond's price is the job of the [fixed-income series](/blog/trading/fixed-income/the-yield-curve-explained-the-most-important-chart-in-finance) — we own the bond's *life*, not its pricing math.) The intuition: a security is a frozen set of future cash flows, and a market is a crowd continuously re-pricing that frozen set.

## Stage 1 and 2: a financing need, then a structuring decision

Every security starts not in a market but in a need. A firm wants to build a \$500M chip factory but has only \$200M in the bank. A government must fund a \$1.5 trillion deficit. A homeowner wants \$400,000 to buy a house. In each case, someone has a use for capital today and a plan to generate cash to pay it back tomorrow. That gap — *capital needed now, cash available later* — is the seed of every security ever issued.

The firm has a menu of ways to fill the gap. It could borrow from a bank (a private loan), but banks have limits and want collateral. It could find a single wealthy backer (private equity or venture capital). Or it could turn to the *public* capital market and issue securities to thousands of investors at once. The capital market's advantage is scale and price: by reaching a crowd of competing buyers, the issuer often raises more, at a better cost, than any single lender would offer. The trade-off is disclosure — public securities come with a legal obligation to tell the world about your business, which we'll return to.

Once a firm decides to use the capital market, **structuring** is the decision of *what kind* of security to issue and on what terms. The first fork is the deepest: **debt or equity?**

**Equity (selling ownership).** The firm sells shares. Each share is a slice of ownership — a residual claim on profits and, if the firm is ever wound up, on whatever assets remain after all debts are paid. Equity holders get paid *last* (they are at the bottom of the stack), which is why they bear the most risk and demand the highest long-run return. The firm gets permanent capital it never has to repay, but it gives away a piece of itself and dilutes existing owners.

**Debt (borrowing).** The firm sells bonds. Each bond is a loan with fixed terms: a face value (say \$1,000), a coupon (the interest rate), and a maturity (when the principal comes back). Bondholders get paid *before* equity holders — they have a senior, contractual claim. The firm keeps full ownership but takes on a fixed obligation it must meet or default. Debt is "cheaper" capital (interest is usually tax-deductible and the return demanded is lower because the risk is lower) but it is a leash: miss a payment and creditors can force you into bankruptcy.

Structuring also decides the *size* (how much to raise), the *terms* (coupon rate, maturity, whether the bond is callable, whether the shares carry special voting rights), and the *price guidance*. These choices are the craft of the **investment bank** that advises the issuer — a topic we build on rather than re-derive ([how an investment bank actually makes money](/blog/trading/finance/inside-an-investment-bank-how-they-make-money) covers the firm; we own the *deal mechanics*). The structuring decision is where a vague "we need money" becomes a concrete, sellable contract.

#### Worked example: debt versus equity for the same \$500M factory

Acme needs \$500M for a factory. **Option A — equity:** it sells 25 million new shares at \$20 each, raising \$500M. It owes nothing back, but if Acme already had 75 million shares, the new shares mean existing owners now hold 75 / 100 = 75% of the company instead of 100%. **Option B — debt:** it issues \$500M of 10-year bonds at a 5% coupon. It keeps 100% ownership but must pay 0.05 × \$500M = \$25M in interest every year for ten years (\$250M total) and return the \$500M at the end. If the factory earns \$80M a year, debt is comfortable (interest is well covered) and shareholders keep all the upside. If the factory earns only \$15M a year, the \$25M interest bill is a crisis — debt becomes a noose, while equity would have quietly absorbed the bad year. The intuition: equity shares the risk and the upside with new owners; debt keeps both but adds a fixed bill that doesn't care whether business is good.

## Stage 3: issuance — the one time the cash flows to the issuer

This is the moment the security is born and the *only* moment the issuer is paid. It deserves the most care, because everything in the rest of this series flows from understanding it.

In a typical IPO, the issuer hires an investment bank (or a syndicate of several) as **underwriter**. The underwriter's job is to (1) help finalise the structure and disclosure, (2) gauge demand from large institutional investors through a process called **bookbuilding** — literally building a "book" of who wants how many shares at what price — and (3) set the final **offer price**. In a *firm-commitment* underwriting, the bank actually buys the entire issue from the company at the offer price and then resells it to investors, taking on the risk that it can't resell at that price. For that risk and effort, the underwriter keeps a **spread** (the gap between what investors pay and what the issuer receives) — historically around 7% for a U.S. IPO, less for large deals.

Walk the cash carefully. Say Acme sells 25 million shares at a \$20 offer price. Investors pay 25M × \$20 = \$500M. The underwriter's 7% gross spread is \$35M, so the issuer nets \$500M − \$35M = \$465M. That \$465M lands in Acme's account. *That is the entire purpose of the security from Acme's point of view, and it happens exactly once.* The full life of an issuance — from the bank winning the mandate to the first trade — is its own deep topic ([the IPO process, end to end](/blog/trading/capital-markets/the-ipo-process-end-to-end-from-mandate-to-first-trade)); here we just need the single fact that the cash arrives now and never again.

![Primary market versus secondary market showing cash reaches the issuer only once](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-2.png)

Why is there a bank in the middle at all, taking 7%? Because pricing a brand-new security is genuinely hard and genuinely risky. The issuer has never had a public price before, so nobody knows what investors will pay. Set the offer price too high and the deal flops — unsold shares, a falling price, a humiliated issuer. Set it too low and the issuer leaves money on the table. The underwriter earns its spread by *discovering* the price: during **bookbuilding**, it markets the deal to dozens of large institutional investors, collects indications of how many shares each would buy at various prices, and assembles a demand curve. From that curve it sets a single offer price that it believes will clear the entire issue and trade up modestly afterward. In a firm-commitment deal, the bank then *guarantees* that price to the issuer by buying the whole issue itself — so if demand evaporates overnight, the loss is the bank's, not the issuer's. The spread is the price of that price-discovery service and that risk transfer.

The same logic governs a bond issuance, just with the roles reversed: the issuer is the borrower, the buyers are the lenders, and the cash flows from lenders to the issuer once, at the offering. After that, the issuer's only job is to make the promised coupon and principal payments on schedule — it does not get more money no matter how the bond trades. A bond is, if anything, an even cleaner example of the "paid once" rule: the issuer receives the proceeds at the auction or offering, then pays out coupons and principal on a fixed schedule for years, and the lively secondary trading of that bond — the price rising and falling as interest rates move — never sends the issuer another dollar nor costs it one. The issuer's obligations were frozen the day the bond was sold.

Issuance volume is wildly cyclical, because it depends on whether investors are in the mood to fund new things. When markets are euphoric, issuers rush to raise capital while buyers are eager; when markets are fearful, the primary market can slam shut for months. The U.S. IPO market makes this vivid.

![US IPO proceeds by year from 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-3.png)

In 2021, a euphoric market, U.S. companies raised roughly \$142bn in traditional IPOs. In 2022, after rates spiked and stocks fell, that collapsed to about \$8bn — a ~94% drop in a single year. The factories and deficits that needed funding did not change that fast; what changed was the secondary market's appetite. This is the spine of the whole series in one chart: **primary issuance is only possible when the secondary market is willing to provide liquidity.** When investors fear they won't be able to sell tomorrow, they won't buy today, and the issuance window closes.

#### Worked example: the underwriting spread and what the issuer actually nets

Acme's IPO: 25,000,000 shares at a \$20.00 offer price. **Gross proceeds** = 25,000,000 × \$20.00 = \$500,000,000. The underwriting syndicate's **gross spread** is 7%, so the syndicate keeps 0.07 × \$500,000,000 = \$35,000,000. **Net proceeds to Acme** = \$500,000,000 − \$35,000,000 = \$465,000,000. If instead Acme were a giant, well-known issuer, the spread might be 2%, keeping \$10,000,000 and netting \$490,000,000 — bigger, safer deals pay smaller spreads. The intuition: the headline "\$500M IPO" is gross; the issuer pockets the proceeds minus the bankers' cut, and that single net number is the entire financial point of going public.

## Stage 4: listing — getting the keys to the secondary market

Issuance creates the security and puts it in investors' hands. **Listing** is the separate step of admitting that security to trade on an organised **exchange** — the New York Stock Exchange, Nasdaq, the London Stock Exchange, the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE). For most large IPOs, listing happens the same day as issuance, which is why people blur the two; but they are distinct steps, and a security can be issued without being listed (privately placed bonds trade "over the counter" with no exchange at all).

An exchange is, mechanically, a referee and a meeting place. It maintains the **order book** — the live list of who wants to buy and sell, at what prices and quantities — and runs a **matching engine** that pairs buy and sell orders by price-time priority. To be listed, an issuer must meet the exchange's standards (minimum market value, minimum number of public shareholders, financial-reporting requirements) and pay listing fees. In return it gets the thing that makes its security valuable to investors: a visible, continuous, regulated venue where its shares can be bought and sold with confidence. (The exchange's internals — the matching engine, the order book ladder — are their own deep dive: [inside an exchange](/blog/trading/capital-markets/inside-an-exchange-the-matching-engine-and-the-order-book). We build on the classic overview in [stock exchanges and clearinghouses](/blog/trading/finance/stock-exchanges-and-clearinghouses).)

Why does listing matter so much? Because **a listed security is a liquid security**, and liquidity is what the secondary market exists to provide. The act of listing is, in effect, the issuer handing its security the keys to the liquidity machine. Once listed, the security can be valued every second by the crowd, used as collateral, included in index funds, and — most importantly for the issuer's *next* financing — sold by today's investors to tomorrow's, which is exactly the reassurance that made today's investors willing to fund the IPO. Listing is the bridge from "born" to "alive."

## Stage 5: secondary trading — the long life, and the issuer becomes a spectator

Now the security settles into the longest phase of its life, which can last decades: **secondary trading**. Investors buy and sell among themselves. Prices move on news — earnings, rate decisions, scandals, takeover rumours, the mood of the market. And through all of it, the issuer is a bystander. When Acme's stock doubles, Acme receives nothing; when it halves, Acme pays nothing. The shares are out in the world now, and the world trades them.

This is worth dwelling on because it is so widely misunderstood. People speak of a company "losing \$10bn in market value today" as if \$10bn had been withdrawn from the company's accounts. Nothing left the company. What happened is that the *price at which investors are willing to trade the existing shares* fell, so the sum of (shares × price) — the **market capitalisation** — dropped. That number is a market opinion about the company's future, not the company's bank balance. The company's cash, factories, and contracts are exactly as they were yesterday.

So what *does* the secondary market do for the issuer, if it sends no cash? Three things, all indirect but enormous. First, it **prices the firm continuously**, which sets the terms on which the firm can raise *more* capital later (a high stock price makes a future share sale cheaper). Second, it **disciplines management** — a falling price attracts activist investors and acquirers, a rising one rewards good decisions. Third, it **provides the liquidity** that made the original issuance possible and keeps the door open for the next one. The secondary market is the issuer's permanent, public report card and its line of credit to the future, even though it is not its cashier.

It's also worth knowing *who* makes the secondary market liquid, because a market that's "open" isn't automatically a market you can actually trade in. Continuous, tradeable prices are provided largely by **market makers** (also called liquidity providers or, in some markets, dealers): firms that stand ready to both buy and sell a security at all times, quoting a price to buy (the **bid**) and a slightly higher price to sell (the **ask**). The gap between them, the **bid-ask spread**, is their compensation for the service and the risk of holding inventory. When you sell 100 shares "to the market," you're usually selling to a market maker who'll resell them moments later; you don't have to wait for another investor to want exactly your 100 shares right now. That instant availability is what liquidity *feels* like, and it's why a deeply traded mega-cap costs almost nothing to trade (a spread of a basis point or two) while a thinly traded micro-cap can cost you a percent or more just to get in and out. The formal models of how market makers set spreads and manage adverse selection live in the [quantitative-finance order-book and market-making material](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-simulator-quant-research); here the point is simply that the secondary market's liquidity is *manufactured* by participants who profit from providing it, not a free property of the exchange.

This is also why the issuer, despite getting no cash from trading, cares intensely about its secondary-market price and liquidity. A company that wants to raise more equity in three years — a **follow-on offering**, which *is* a primary-market event that pays the issuer again — will sell those new shares at a price anchored to wherever the secondary market is trading. A firm whose stock trades at \$80 raises far more from selling 10 million new shares than one whose stock has sagged to \$8. Likewise, a company with a high, liquid stock can use its shares as currency to acquire other firms, and can grant employees stock options that are actually worth something. The secondary market doesn't pay the issuer, but it sets the *exchange rate* at which the issuer can convert its equity into everything it might want next.

![US equity market capitalisation by year from 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-6.png)

The aggregate size of the secondary market dwarfs the primary market by orders of magnitude. U.S. equity market capitalisation grew from roughly \$26 trillion in 2014 to about \$58 trillion in 2024. Against that stock of value, annual IPO proceeds of tens of billions are a rounding error — the primary market is a tiny faucet feeding an ocean of secondary trading. That ratio is the quantitative face of "the issuer gets paid once": the value created and exchanged in the secondary market is hundreds of times larger than the cash the primary market ever delivered to issuers, and none of that secondary value flows back to them.

#### Worked example: \$500M raised once, \$50bn traded, \$0 to the issuer

This is the single arithmetic that the whole series turns on, so it is worth doing slowly. Acme raises **\$500M** in its IPO — gross proceeds, before the bankers' cut. That is the one and only time the *issuer* is on the receiving end of cash from these shares. Now run the clock forward five years. Suppose Acme's stock is liquid and heavily traded: average daily volume of, say, \$40M, over roughly 250 trading days a year, for five years. Total secondary turnover = \$40M × 250 × 5 = **\$50,000,000,000** — fifty billion dollars of Acme shares changing hands. How much of that \$50bn reached Acme? **\$0.** Every one of those dollars flowed from one investor to another; Acme was not a party to a single trade. Put the two numbers side by side: the issuer's lifetime cash from these shares is \$500M (a one-time event), while the cumulative value that traded *over* those same shares is \$50bn (100× larger) — and the issuer's share of the larger number is exactly zero. The intuition: the primary market is a single faucet that fills the issuer's bucket once; the secondary market is an ocean those shares then swim in forever, and no matter how big the ocean gets, not one drop ever flows back up the faucet.

#### Worked example: the IPO pop, and who actually captures it

Acme prices its IPO at \$20.00 the night before. The next morning, trading opens and the first secondary trade prints at \$26.00 — a 30% "pop." Who got that \$6 per share? Not Acme — Acme sold at \$20 and was already paid. The \$6 gain went to whoever was *allocated* shares at the \$20 offer price (mostly the underwriter's favoured institutional clients) and then sold into the opening. On 25 million shares, that is 25,000,000 × \$6 = \$150,000,000 of value that the *primary buyers* captured and the *issuer* did not. Across the U.S. market, this first-day pop has averaged anywhere from about 12% in calm years to over 40% in frothy ones.

![Median IPO first-day pop by year that the issuer never captures](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-8.png)

A persistent first-day pop is money the issuer "left on the table" — it could in principle have priced higher and netted more. The intuition: the IPO pop is a real transfer of wealth, but it flows from the issuer to the first secondary sellers, never to the issuer, and the bigger the pop the more the company under-priced its one and only payday.

## Stage 6: clearing, settlement, and custody — the invisible plumbing behind every trade

Here is where almost everyone's mental model of a "trade" is wrong. When two investors agree on a price on the exchange, *nothing has actually moved yet.* The match is a **promise**: I will deliver shares, you will deliver cash. Turning that promise into a finalised, irreversible exchange of value is the job of the post-trade plumbing — and it is a multi-step chain that the trading public never sees.

![Post-trade flow from matched trade through clearing settlement and custody](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-5.png)

**Clearing.** After the match, a **central counterparty (CCP)** steps into the middle of the trade through a legal process called **novation**: the single buyer-to-seller contract is replaced by two contracts — the CCP becomes the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer. This sounds like bureaucratic theatre, but it is the load-bearing wall of modern markets. It means you no longer care whether the anonymous stranger on the other side of your trade is good for the money; you only face the CCP, which is heavily capitalised and backed by a default fund. The CCP converts a web of bilateral counterparty risk into a hub-and-spoke system where everyone faces one trusted center.

**Netting.** Because the CCP sees all of your trades, it can **net** them: if you bought 1,000 shares of Acme and sold 900 the same day, you only need to receive 100, not gross-settle 1,900. Netting collapses an enormous gross volume of obligations into a tiny net residue. Real clearinghouses net away the vast majority of gross obligations — the DTCC, the U.S. equity clearer, regularly nets daily gross obligations measured in the trillions down to a small fraction for actual settlement. Netting is the reason the plumbing can handle billions of trades a day without moving a corresponding mountain of cash.

**Settlement.** This is the moment the trade becomes real: cash moves from buyer to seller and the security moves the other way, simultaneously and irrevocably (**delivery versus payment**, so neither side can be left holding nothing). Settlement happens a fixed number of business days after the trade — the **settlement cycle**. For decades this was T+5, then T+3, then T+2; in May 2024 U.S. equities moved to **T+1**, meaning settlement one business day after the trade.

![US equity settlement cycle shortening from T+5 to T+1 over fifty years](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-7.png)

The half-century march from T+5 to T+1 is a quiet revolution in reducing risk: the shorter the gap between trade and settlement, the less time either side has to default before the deal is final, and the less collateral must be posted to cover that window.

What if a member of the CCP *does* fail before settling — goes bankrupt mid-day with open trades the CCP has guaranteed? This is the scenario the entire clearing edifice is built to survive, and it's worth a moment because it's where the "trust the center" promise is tested. The CCP absorbs the loss through a pre-agreed sequence called the **default waterfall**. First it seizes the defaulter's own **initial margin** — collateral the member posted up front precisely to cover this. If that's not enough, it taps the defaulter's contribution to the **guarantee fund** (a mutual pool every member pays into). Only then does the CCP put in a slice of its *own* capital — its "skin in the game," deliberately placed ahead of the survivors so the CCP has a real incentive to manage risk well. If losses still aren't covered, the CCP draws on the *surviving* members' guarantee-fund contributions, and as a last resort can levy **assessments** on them. Each layer is bigger and more mutualised than the last, so a single member's failure is contained by that member's own collateral in almost every realistic case, and only a truly extreme event ever reaches the shared layers. This waterfall is why a CCP can credibly stand between millions of anonymous trades: it has a deep, ordered stack of money standing behind its guarantee. (The full post-trade lifecycle — fails, buy-ins, and the default waterfall in detail — is its own deep topic: [what happens after the trade](/blog/trading/capital-markets/what-happens-after-the-trade-the-post-trade-lifecycle).)

**Custody.** Finally, who holds the security and records that you own it? You almost never hold a paper certificate. The security sits in **custody**, recorded electronically. In most modern markets, shares are held in the name of a central securities depository (in the U.S., DTC) and a chain of custodians and brokers records the **beneficial owner** — you. The issuer keeps a **registrar** that tracks who is entitled to dividends and votes. This is the issuer→registrar handoff: once the security is in the secondary market, the issuer no longer knows or cares who trades it minute to minute; it relies on the registrar and the depository to tell it who to pay and who can vote when a corporate action comes around.

#### Worked example: how netting shrinks what actually settles

A broker's clients trade Acme heavily during the day: total buys of 5,000,000 shares and total sells of 4,950,000 shares. Without netting, the broker would have to settle both legs — deliver \$99M of shares' worth one way and receive \$100M the other — moving roughly \$199M (at \$20/share) across the system. With CCP netting, the broker only settles the *difference*: it must receive a net 50,000 shares and pay a net 50,000 × \$20 = \$1,000,000. The plumbing moved \$1M instead of \$199M — a 99.5% reduction. The intuition: netting is why the world's settlement systems can clear staggering trading volumes with a tiny fraction of the cash actually changing hands, which is also why the whole system is far less fragile than the raw trade counts suggest.

## Stage 7: corporate actions — events that punctuate the life

Over a security's long secondary life, the issuer periodically *does something* to it — pays it cash, changes its count, or alters its terms. These are **corporate actions**, and they are where the issuer briefly steps back onto the stage. They fall into two families, equity and debt, and the registrar and custody chain from Stage 6 are what make them work — the issuer tells the registrar "pay everyone on the books as of date X," and the plumbing distributes the money or the new shares to whoever the records say is the owner.

![Corporate actions tree for equity and debt securities](/imgs/blogs/the-life-of-a-security-from-idea-to-delisting-4.png)

### Equity corporate actions

**Dividends.** The company shares profit with owners by paying cash per share. A \$0.50 dividend on a share means the holder of record receives \$0.50 per share. Critically, the share price typically drops by roughly the dividend amount on the **ex-dividend date** — the day from which a new buyer no longer gets the upcoming dividend — because the company is now worth that much less cash. A dividend is not free money; it is the company handing you a piece of its own value.

**Stock splits.** The company divides each share into more shares — a 2-for-1 split turns one \$80 share into two \$40 shares. Nothing of value changes: you own twice as many shares at half the price, and your total stake is identical. Splits are cosmetic, done to make the share price feel "affordable" or to fit index rules. A **reverse split** does the opposite (often to escape a delisting threshold by lifting a penny-stock price back over an exchange minimum).

**Buybacks.** The company uses cash to buy its *own* shares in the secondary market and retire them. This is the mirror image of issuance: instead of selling new shares to raise cash, it spends cash to reduce the share count. Each remaining share now represents a larger slice of the company, so earnings per share rise mechanically even if profits don't. A buyback is a way to return cash to shareholders that, unlike a dividend, lets them choose whether to sell.

**Rights issues.** The company raises *new* primary capital from *existing* shareholders by offering them the right to buy new shares, usually at a discount, in proportion to their current holding. This is a primary-market event happening mid-life: cash flows to the issuer again. A shareholder who takes up the rights maintains their ownership percentage; one who ignores them gets **diluted** (their slice shrinks) and may even lose value, because the new shares are sold below market price.

### Debt corporate actions

**Coupons.** The bond's scheduled interest payments — the \$50-a-year in our earlier example. These are contractual, not discretionary like dividends: miss one and you are in default.

**Calls.** Many bonds are **callable**, meaning the issuer has the right to repay early, usually when interest rates have fallen and it wants to refinance cheaply. A call ends the bond's life ahead of schedule and hands the principal back to holders, who must now reinvest at the new, lower rates — which is exactly why callable bonds pay a bit more to compensate.

**Amortisation.** Some debt repays principal gradually over its life rather than all at once at maturity (a home mortgage is the everyday example). Each payment is part interest, part principal, so the outstanding balance shrinks steadily to zero.

#### Worked example: a share's whole secondary life — IPO to dividend to split to buyback

You buy 1,000 shares of Acme at the \$20 IPO price: cost = 1,000 × \$20 = \$20,000. Over the next two years:
1. The stock trades up to \$40 on good results. Your stake is now worth 1,000 × \$40 = \$40,000 (on paper; Acme received none of this gain).
2. Acme pays a \$0.50 dividend. You receive 1,000 × \$0.50 = \$500 in cash, and the share price drops by about \$0.50 to ~\$39.50 on the ex-date. Your *total* (shares + cash) is essentially unchanged at the moment of payment: \$39,500 + \$500 = \$40,000.
3. Acme does a 2-for-1 split. You now own 2,000 shares at ~\$19.75 each. Total = 2,000 × \$19.75 = \$39,500. Nothing changed but the units.
4. Acme buys back 10% of all shares. With fewer shares outstanding, each remaining share represents more of the company; if the price rises ~5% to \$20.75 as a result, your 2,000 shares are worth 2,000 × \$20.75 = \$41,500.

The intuition: across dividends, splits, and buybacks, the company is constantly rearranging *how* value is packaged into shares, but only the dividend and the buyback actually move cash — and through all of it, the original \$20,000 you paid went to Acme exactly once, at the very start.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000 bond's entire life, coupon by coupon

You buy a new 10-year Acme bond at issuance for its \$1,000 face value with a 5% annual coupon. Its life is a fixed schedule:
- **Year 0:** you pay \$1,000 to the issuer (primary market — the issuer is paid, once).
- **Years 1 through 10:** you receive 0.05 × \$1,000 = \$50 each year. Over ten years that is 10 × \$50 = \$500 of coupons.
- **Year 10:** along with the final \$50 coupon, the issuer **redeems** the bond — repays the \$1,000 principal.

Total cash back over the life: \$500 (coupons) + \$1,000 (principal) = \$1,500, against the \$1,000 you paid. If you *sell* the bond to another investor in year 4, you've simply transferred the remaining schedule (six more coupons plus principal) to them at whatever price the secondary market agrees — the issuer keeps paying coupons to whoever the registrar shows as the holder, indifferent to who that is. The intuition: a bond's whole life is a pre-written schedule of cash, and the secondary market just decides who is sitting in the chair to collect each payment.

#### Worked example: what a rights issue does to a holder

Acme is trading at \$40 and announces a **1-for-4 rights issue at \$30** — for every 4 shares you own, you may buy 1 new share at \$30. You own 400 shares (worth 400 × \$40 = \$16,000) and are offered 100 new shares at \$30 (cost \$3,000). If you take up the rights: you now own 500 shares and have paid an extra \$3,000. The theoretical price after the issue settles to a blend: (400 × \$40 + 100 × \$30) / 500 = (\$16,000 + \$3,000) / 500 = \$38.00. Your 500 shares are worth 500 × \$38 = \$19,000, exactly your \$16,000 + \$3,000 — you're whole. But if you *ignore* the rights, you still own 400 shares now worth 400 × \$38 = \$15,200 — you've lost \$800 to dilution, because new shares were sold below the old price and you didn't claim your share of the discount. The intuition: a rights issue is the issuer raising fresh capital from current owners, and a holder who doesn't participate quietly transfers value to those who do.

## Stage 8: the end — how a security dies

Securities do not live forever, and equity and debt die in completely different ways. This asymmetry is one of the most important and least appreciated facts about the two instrument families.

**A bond has a built-in death date.** Every bond carries a maturity. When it arrives, the issuer pays back the face value, the final coupon clears, and the bond simply ceases to exist — this is **redemption**, the normal, clean death of a debt security. A callable bond may die earlier (the issuer calls it); an amortising bond dies gradually as its principal is repaid down to zero. The unhappy death of a bond is **default**: the issuer fails to pay a coupon or the principal. Then the bond enters restructuring or bankruptcy, where holders fight over the remains according to seniority — senior creditors first, then juniors, with equity holders last and usually wiped out.

**A stock has no natural death date.** Equity is *permanent* capital; a share can in principle live as long as the company does. So a stock ends only through a deliberate event:
- **Buyback to zero / going private:** the company (or a buyer) purchases all the public shares and takes the company private, and the stock stops trading publicly.
- **Merger or acquisition:** another company buys Acme; Acme's shares are converted into cash or into the acquirer's shares, and the old ticker disappears.
- **Bankruptcy:** if the company fails, equity is the *last* claim in line. After creditors are paid from whatever assets remain, equity holders usually get little or nothing — the shares become worthless and are delisted.
- **Delisting:** the security is removed from the exchange. This can be voluntary (going private) or forced — the company fell below the exchange's minimum price, market value, or reporting standards. A forced delisting doesn't necessarily make the shares worthless (they may still trade over the counter), but it strips away the liquidity, visibility, and credibility that listing provided. The security is, in effect, exiled from the secondary market that gave it life.

Notice the deep asymmetry one more time: **a bond's default is a breach of a promise to pay a fixed amount; a stock's worthlessness is simply the residual claim turning out to be worth nothing.** Bondholders are owed a specific number and fight to recover it; shareholders were never owed anything specific — they owned a claim on "whatever is left," and sometimes what's left is zero. That is the price of being at the top of the upside and the bottom of the stack.

#### Worked example: the same firm's bond and stock at the end

Acme fails. At bankruptcy it has \$300M of assets to distribute against \$500M of bonds outstanding and millions of shares. The bondholders are senior: they split the \$300M, recovering \$300M / \$500M = \$0.60 on the dollar — a holder of a \$1,000 bond gets back \$600. The shareholders are last in line: after the \$300M is exhausted on the senior \$500M of debt, there is \$0 left for equity. A holder of 1,000 shares once worth \$40,000 receives \$0, and the stock is delisted. The intuition: at the end, debt and equity reveal their true natures — the bondholder had a fixed claim and recovered part of it; the shareholder had a residual claim and recovered nothing, which is the exact mirror of the unlimited upside they'd have enjoyed had Acme thrived.

## Common misconceptions

**"When a company's stock goes up, the company makes money."** No. The company was paid once, at issuance. After that, price moves are between investors. A rising stock helps the company *indirectly* — cheaper future fundraising, happier employees with stock options, more acquisition currency — but no cash flows to the company from secondary trading. The clearest proof is the ratio in this post: against \$58 trillion of U.S. equity market cap, annual IPO proceeds are tens of billions. The secondary ocean sends the issuers nothing.

**"A stock split makes me richer."** No. A 2-for-1 split doubles your share count and halves the price; your total stake is unchanged to the cent. Splits change the packaging, not the value. The only thing that changes your wealth is the price the market is willing to pay for the underlying company, not how many slices it's cut into.

**"A trade is done the moment the price matches."** No. The match is a promise. Behind it runs clearing (a CCP novates and nets), settlement (cash and shares actually move, now T+1 in the U.S.), and custody (the depository and registrar record the new owner). A trade isn't *final* until settlement completes a day later, and the entire apparatus of CCPs, netting, and default funds exists precisely because the gap between trade and finality is where risk lives.

**"A dividend is free money the company gives me."** No. The company hands you a piece of its own value, and the share price drops by roughly the dividend on the ex-date. You've converted a sliver of your stake from "value inside the company" to "cash in your hand." It's a portfolio rearrangement, not a windfall — useful, but not a gift.

**"Bonds and stocks are just two flavours of investment."** They are structurally opposite at the two moments that matter most. At the *top*, a bond is a senior, fixed, contractual claim with a death date; a stock is a junior, residual, perpetual claim with no death date. At the *end*, a defaulting bond recovers part of a specific owed amount, while a failed stock — the last claim in line — usually recovers nothing. The whole risk-and-return difference between them flows from that single ordering: who gets paid first, and who is owed a fixed number versus "whatever's left."

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2021-to-2022 IPO window slamming shut.** In 2021, U.S. companies raised roughly \$142bn in traditional IPOs amid euphoric markets; in 2022, after the Fed began hiking and equities sold off, that fell to about \$8bn. Companies that had filed to go public in 2022 quietly withdrew or waited years. Nothing about their factories or business plans changed in twelve months — what changed was the secondary market's willingness to absorb new supply. This is the series spine in the wild: when secondary-market liquidity dries up, the primary market closes, because issuers can only sell new securities into a market that's willing to buy and able to resell.

**The U.S. switch to T+1 in May 2024.** For the first time since 2017, U.S. equity settlement moved from T+2 to T+1 — settlement one business day after the trade. Brokers, custodians, and funds spent two years rebuilding operations to compress every post-trade step into a single overnight window. Why bother? Because the shorter the settlement cycle, the less time exists for a counterparty to fail before the trade is final, and the less collateral the CCP must demand to cover that window. The whole point of Stage 6 — that a trade is only a promise until it settles — is exactly why shaving a day off settlement was worth a multi-year, market-wide effort.

**A first-day IPO pop that priced money away.** When a closely watched company prices its IPO at, say, \$38 and opens for secondary trading at \$54, that ~40% pop is a transfer: the value flowed to the favoured clients who were allocated shares at \$38 and could sell into the opening, not to the issuer, who had already locked in \$38. On a large deal, that gap is hundreds of millions of dollars the company "left on the table" — the cost of mis-pricing its one and only payday. It's a recurring debate precisely because the issuer can never get a second chance at that price.

**Vietnam's secondary market growing up.** Vietnam's stock market capitalisation rose from around a quarter of GDP a decade ago toward roughly 60% in recent years, while the number of securities trading accounts climbed past nine million. As the secondary market deepened — more participants, more daily turnover, more reliable settlement — the primary market followed, with larger and more frequent equitisations and listings, because issuers could finally trust that investors would be able to resell. Foreign flows on HOSE swing the index hard in both directions, a reminder that secondary-market liquidity is also a channel through which global sentiment reaches a local issuer's cost of capital. (Foreign flows and the mechanics of how they move a frontier market are covered in [foreign flows and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)

## The takeaway: one handoff explains the whole machine

If you remember one thing from this entire series, make it the handoff at Stage 3. **A security is created once to raise money, and then it trades forever without ever paying the issuer again.** Everything else in the capital market is a consequence of that single fact:

- The **primary market** exists to perform that one-time cash transfer (Stages 1–3) — needs, structuring, issuance.
- The **secondary market** exists so that the buyers in that transfer can change their minds later (Stages 4–5) — listing and trading — and it is *this very escape hatch* that made them willing to fund the issuer in the first place. Liquidity tomorrow is the price of capital today.
- The **plumbing** exists to make the secondary market's promises real and safe (Stage 6) — clearing, settlement, custody — because a trade is only a promise until cash and shares actually, irrevocably move.
- **Corporate actions** are the issuer briefly stepping back onto the stage to pay, repackage, or refinance (Stage 7), routed to owners through the registrar and custody chain.
- And the **end** (Stage 8) reveals the deep asymmetry that was baked in at structuring: debt has a fixed claim and a death date; equity has a residual claim and no natural death, which is why one defaults and the other simply expires worthless.

When you next read that a company "lost \$20bn in value" or "raised \$2bn in its IPO" or that the market "moved to T+1," you now have a stage to file each headline under, and a question to ask of each: *is the issuer being paid here, or are investors just trading among themselves?* Nine times out of ten the answer is the latter — and knowing which time is the tenth is the beginning of understanding how money finds its best use. Every later track in this series zooms into one of these eight stages. This was the map; now we go deep.
