> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The most profitable firms in finance often provide no capital at all; they sell the *measurement and access* that make a market usable, and they collect that toll forever.
>
> - Exchanges stopped being member-owned clubs and became listed for-profit companies; today they earn far more from **listings, market data, and co-location** than from matching trades.
> - **Index providers** (S&P Dow Jones, MSCI, FTSE Russell) earn a tiny basis-point fee on every dollar that tracks their indices — and their inclusion decisions can force billions of dollars to move with no business news at all.
> - **Credit rating agencies** (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) run on an **issuer-pays** model baked into regulation; that conflict helped stamp AAA on the securities that detonated in 2008.
> - The one fact to remember: these are **toll-collectors on the market's plumbing** — recurring, near-monopoly revenue is why a data or index business is worth more than the exchange's trading floor it grew out of.

## A morning that moved \$50 billion without a single earnings report

On the evening of November 4, 2020, S&P Dow Jones Indices put out a one-page press release. Tesla, it announced, would be added to the S&P 500 — the most-tracked stock index on Earth — effective December 21. No new product had shipped. No quarter had closed. Elon Musk had not done anything that day. A committee had simply decided that one company now met the criteria to join a list.

The stock rose roughly 70% between that announcement and the inclusion date. The reason was almost mechanical: an estimated \$11 trillion was benchmarked to the S&P 500, and a large chunk of that money is run by passive index funds that are *required* to hold every constituent in proportion to its weight. The instant Tesla joined the list, those funds had to buy — collectively tens of billions of dollars of stock — regardless of what they thought Tesla was worth. A private firm, selling a list, had reached into the market and forced capital to flow.

That is the strange, under-appreciated power at the center of this post. We have spent this series on the people who actually move capital — issuers who sell securities, investors who buy them, banks who underwrite, clearinghouses who guarantee. But humming around that core is a **supporting cast** of firms that never put a dollar of their own capital at risk in the market and yet are among the most profitable companies in all of finance. They sell the *infrastructure of measurement and access*: the venue, the benchmark, the rating, the data feed, the vote recommendation. They are toll-collectors, and the toll never stops.

![Map of the supporting cast firms around the core market](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-1.png)

Recall the spine of this series: a capital market turns savings into long-term investment through a primary market that *creates* securities and a secondary market that *trades* them, joined by plumbing and run by intermediaries. The supporting cast sits one layer out from the intermediaries. The bank earns a fee for *doing* a deal; the exchange, the index, and the agency earn a fee for *measuring and hosting* the market the bank operates in. Their genius is that their revenue is recurring and near-monopoly, while the bank has to win the next deal all over again.

## Foundations: what a "toll-collector" business actually is

Before any jargon, here is the everyday-money analogy. Picture the market as a giant farmers' market in a town square. The farmers (issuers) bring goods; the shoppers (investors) bring money; brokers help them haggle. Now notice who *else* gets paid. Someone owns the square and rents out stalls — that is the **exchange**. Someone publishes the weekly "Top 100 Best-Selling Goods" list that shoppers use to decide what's worth buying — that is the **index provider**. Someone inspects each farmer's produce and stamps it "Grade A" or "Grade C" so shoppers trust it — that is the **rating agency**. Someone sells a daily price sheet of every stall's prices — that is the **data vendor**. And someone advises the big shoppers on how to vote at the market's annual rules meeting — that is the **proxy advisor**.

None of these five grows food or buys it. They sell *access to the square* and *trustworthy measurement of what's in it*. And because every farmer and every shopper needs those services, the toll-collectors capture a slice of all the activity without ever taking the risk of owning a single tomato.

Let me define the five precisely, because the rest of the post is just these five seen up close.

- **Exchange.** A regulated venue that lists securities and matches buy and sell orders — NYSE, Nasdaq, the London Stock Exchange, the CME for futures. Historically member-owned mutual clubs; now nearly all are publicly listed for-profit companies.
- **Index provider.** A firm that constructs and maintains rules-based lists of securities — the S&P 500, the FTSE 100, MSCI Emerging Markets — and licenses the right to track or name a product after them.
- **Credit rating agency (CRA).** A firm that publishes an opinion on the likelihood a borrower repays its debt, expressed as a letter grade (AAA down to D). The big three are Moody's, S&P Global Ratings, and Fitch.
- **Market-data vendor.** A firm that aggregates and redistributes prices, news, and analytics — Bloomberg and Refinitiv being the giants, alongside the exchanges' own data arms.
- **Proxy advisor.** A firm that researches corporate ballots and tells institutional investors how to vote their shares — Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) and Glass Lewis dominate.

What unites them is the **toll-collector economics**: a position astride the market's plumbing that lets them charge a recurring fee on activity they don't perform. The bigger the markets get, the bigger the toll base.

It helps to separate two things these firms sell. The first is **access** — the right to use a venue, a network, or a name. An exchange sells access to its order book and its tape; a data vendor sells access to its terminal network; an index provider sells the right to attach its name to your fund. The second is **measurement** — an authoritative number or judgment the market agrees to treat as official. The S&P 500 level, the NYSE closing price, a Moody's rating, an ISS recommendation: each is a measurement that becomes load-bearing because everyone coordinates around it. A toll-collector's moat is strongest when it sells *both* at once: a measurement that you can only access through them. That is why these businesses are so hard to dislodge — a competitor can build a better index, but it cannot easily make the world agree that its number is *the* number.

![The size of the markets being measured](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-2.png)

That chart is the toll base. Roughly \$115 trillion of global equity and \$140 trillion of global bonds sit out there, every dollar of it listed somewhere, benchmarked to something, and — if it's debt — rated by someone. The supporting cast doesn't own any of it. They tax the act of measuring it.

## Exchanges: from member-owned clubs to listed monopolies on data

For most of their history, exchanges were **mutuals** — owned by their members, the brokers who traded on them. The New York Stock Exchange was a not-for-profit association whose "seats" were owned by member firms; you bought a seat to get the right to trade on the floor. The exchange's job was to run the room, not to earn a profit for outside shareholders. That changed in a wave of **demutualization** around the turn of the millennium. Exchanges converted to for-profit corporations and listed their own shares. The NYSE merged into the publicly traded Intercontinental Exchange (ICE) in 2013; Nasdaq, the LSE (now LSEG), Deutsche Börse, and the CME are all listed companies with shareholders to answer to.

The moment an exchange has shareholders, the question becomes: *how do you actually make money?* The intuitive answer — "they take a cut of every trade" — turns out to be the *smallest* part of the story.

![Where a modern exchange earns its revenue](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-3.png)

Trading fees are commoditized and have been competed down toward zero, because order flow can route to dozens of venues and any venue that charges too much loses the flow. So the modern exchange makes its real money in three places that are *sticky*:

1. **Listings.** A company that wants its shares on the NYSE or Nasdaq pays an initial listing fee plus a recurring annual fee. Listing is prestige and liquidity, and once you're listed you rarely leave — so it's recurring revenue with low churn.
2. **Market data.** The exchange sees every quote and trade that happens on it, and it *sells that data back* to the market — to brokers, funds, terminals, and algorithms. This is the crown jewel.
3. **Connectivity and co-location.** High-frequency firms will pay enormous sums to place their servers in the *same building* as the exchange's matching engine, shaving microseconds off the round trip. Renting rack space and ultra-low-latency cross-connects is pure margin.

It's worth pausing on *why* trading fees got competed to near-zero while data fees didn't. Order flow is **contestable**: a trade can execute on the NYSE, on Nasdaq, on Cboe's venues, on a dozen alternative trading systems, or be internalized by a wholesaler — so if one venue charges too much, the flow simply routes elsewhere, and competition grinds the fee down. The *data*, by contrast, is **not contestable**: there is only one record of what traded on the NYSE, and only the NYSE can sell it. The CME is the only place that knows the official settlement prices for its own futures. So the same firm faces brutal competition on the commodity it provides (matching) and zero competition on the byproduct it generates (the data record). A rational for-profit exchange therefore stops fighting over the commodity and monetizes the byproduct — which is exactly what every major exchange has done.

The strategic insight is that an exchange is really a **data and technology company that happens to run an auction**. ICE makes more from data and analytics than from the NYSE's trading. LSEG bought Refinitiv in 2021 for around \$27 billion precisely to become a data business; trading is now a minority of its revenue. The matching engine is the loss-leader that generates the proprietary data, and the data is what you sell forever.

#### Worked example: an exchange's data revenue versus its trading fees

Suppose a large exchange does \$2.5 billion of annual revenue, split as in the figure: \~20% transaction fees, \~45% market data and analytics, \~20% listings, \~15% connectivity. The trading desk's flashy headline — billions of shares matched per day — produces only \$2.5B × 0.20 = **\$500 million**. Market data produces \$2.5B × 0.45 = **\$1.125 billion**, more than double. Now compare *margins*: matching trades requires running and policing a fair auction (real cost), while reselling data you already collected costs almost nothing to deliver. So data isn't just bigger on the top line — it's far bigger on the bottom line.

The intuition: the exchange's most valuable asset isn't its order book, it's its *monopoly on the record of what happened in that order book*.

There is a deeper reason demutualization changed exchange behavior so completely. A member-owned exchange answers to the brokers who trade on it, and those brokers want *low* trading costs — so a mutual has no incentive to gouge its own owners on fees. A *shareholder*-owned exchange answers to investors who want growing profit, and the brokers are now customers to be charged, not owners to be served. The same room, the same matching engine, but the incentive flipped from "keep costs low for members" to "extract maximum recurring revenue from users." That flip is the quiet engine behind every market-data-fee dispute of the past two decades — it isn't villainy, it's simply what a for-profit corporation with a monopoly input is built to do.

Who bears the cost of that flip? Ultimately the end investor. Brokers pass data and connectivity fees into the cost of trading; funds pass them into expense ratios; the saver who owns the fund pays a sliver of the exchange's data monopoly without ever seeing a line item for it. The toll is invisible precisely because it's buried so deep in the plumbing.

That monopoly is also the source of an ongoing fight. Because there is exactly one authoritative record of trades on the NYSE, the NYSE can price its data feed however it likes, and you cannot buy NYSE data anywhere else. Firms that need the data to comply with **best-execution** rules — the legal duty to get clients the best available price — argue that exchanges abuse this captive position, raising data and connectivity fees year after year. The SEC has repeatedly intervened in these **market-data-fee** disputes, rejecting fee filings and pushing for cheaper consolidated feeds. The exchanges respond that data is genuinely valuable and that they invest heavily in the infrastructure. Both are partly right; the structural fact is that a monopoly seller of a mandatory input will always be tempted to price it like a monopolist.

## Index providers: selling a list, moving the market

An index is, at its core, just a *rule for making a list*. "Take the 500 largest US companies that meet these liquidity and profitability criteria, weight them by market value, and rebalance quarterly." That rule, applied consistently, produces the S&P 500. The provider doesn't manage any money. It publishes a number — the index level — and a methodology. So how does publishing a list become one of the best businesses in finance?

The answer is **licensing**. Every ETF or index fund that wants to track the S&P 500, or call itself an "S&P 500 fund," or use the index's data, pays S&P Dow Jones a licensing fee. That fee is typically a few basis points (hundredths of a percent) on the **assets under management** (AUM) tracking the index. A few basis points sounds trivial until you remember the AUM is measured in trillions.

![US equity market cap, the AUM that pays index and data fees](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-6.png)

That growing pile of equity — roughly \$58 trillion in US market cap by 2024 — is the base the index fee feeds on. As the world shifted from active stock-picking to passive index funds over the past two decades, the assets benchmarked to a handful of indices exploded, and so did the licensing revenue of the three firms that own the dominant benchmarks: **S&P Dow Jones Indices**, **MSCI**, and **FTSE Russell**.

#### Worked example: an index provider's licensing fee on tracking AUM

Say an ETF sponsor runs a \$300 billion fund tracking the S&P 500 and pays S&P Dow Jones a licensing fee of 3 basis points on assets. That's \$300,000,000,000 × 0.0003 = **\$90 million per year**, from a single fund, for the right to use a list. The index provider's marginal cost of serving that fund is essentially zero — the index is computed once and licensed to everyone. Now multiply across hundreds of funds and many indices: MSCI alone has tens of trillions of dollars benchmarked to its indices.

The intuition: the index fee is a tiny tax on a gigantic, growing base — the purest toll-collector model in the entire market.

Now the part that makes index providers genuinely powerful, not merely profitable: **inclusion decisions**. Because so much money *passively* tracks the major indices, the decision to add or remove a security is no longer a neutral act of measurement. It is a command to billions of dollars.

![The index inclusion effect on passive demand](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-5.png)

When a stock is added to an index, every passive fund tracking it must buy enough to match the new weight — creating sudden, price-insensitive demand. When it's deleted, they must sell. This is the **index effect**, and it operates at the level of whole countries, not just single stocks.

The biggest inclusion battles are about **country reclassification**. MSCI and FTSE Russell each sort the world's markets into tiers — Developed, Emerging, Frontier. Moving a country up a tier (say, from Frontier to Emerging) triggers a wave of passive buying as global Emerging-Market funds add the country's stocks to match the new index. China's domestic A-shares were gradually added to MSCI's Emerging Markets index starting in 2018, channeling tens of billions of dollars of foreign inflows into Shanghai and Shenzhen. **Vietnam** has spent years lobbying FTSE Russell and MSCI to be upgraded from Frontier to Emerging status, reforming settlement and foreign-ownership rules specifically to qualify — because an upgrade would mechanically draw billions of dollars of index-fund money into Vietnamese stocks. A private firm in London or New York holds, in effect, a switch that can redirect global capital flows toward an entire nation.

#### Worked example: the cost to a company of an index deletion

Consider a mid-cap company with a \$4 billion market value and 30% of its float held by funds tracking the index it belongs to. The provider announces the company will be **dropped** at the next rebalance. The trackers holding 30% × \$4B = \$1.2 billion of stock must now sell, regardless of the company's fundamentals, into a market that knows the forced selling is coming. If front-runners and the supply-demand imbalance push the price down even 8% around the deletion, that's roughly \$320 million of market value erased — not because the business changed, but because a list did.

The intuition: index inclusion is "free real estate" in investors' portfolios, and an index provider giveth and taketh away with the stroke of a methodology committee.

There is a second-order effect worth dwelling on, because it explains a lot of modern market behavior. The more money that indexes passively, the *more powerful inclusion decisions become*, which makes the index provider more powerful, which attracts still more passive money — a self-reinforcing loop. Two decades ago an index addition barely moved a large stock; today, with so much capital benchmarked, the index effect is strong enough that traders front-run announced changes, and companies engineer their share counts and free float to qualify. The benchmark has become an actor in the very market it was built to describe — the financial equivalent of a thermometer that, once enough people watch it, starts changing the temperature.

This also reframes who bears the risk. A passive index fund deliberately holds *no view* on whether Tesla or a newly-promoted country is fairly priced; it buys because the rules say to. The judgment about what belongs in the market's core has been delegated, almost invisibly, from millions of investors to a few methodology committees. That delegation is efficient and cheap — it's the whole point of index investing — but it concentrates an extraordinary amount of price-setting influence in private hands that face none of the consequences if the call is wrong.

This is why companies obsess over staying in indices, why exchanges and index firms negotiate fiercely, and why the methodology committees of S&P and MSCI are quietly among the most consequential, least-known bodies in global finance. We trace how indices feed back into fund flows and primary issuance in the sibling post on [indices, ETFs, and the bridge back to the primary market](/blog/trading/capital-markets/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market).

## Credit rating agencies: the issuer-pays conflict at the heart of debt

If an index provider grades stocks by *size*, a **credit rating agency** grades bonds by *safety* — the likelihood the borrower repays. The letter scale (AAA, AA, A, BBB at the top of "investment grade"; BB and below as "high yield" or "junk") compresses a complex credit analysis into a symbol every investor recognizes. The three dominant agencies — Moody's, S&P Global Ratings, and Fitch — rate the overwhelming majority of the world's rated debt.

To grasp the scale of the agencies' franchise, look at how much debt gets issued — and therefore rated — every year.

![US debt issuance by type in 2023](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-4.png)

The US alone issued well over \$20 trillion of debt in 2023, dominated by Treasuries but including \$1.4 trillion of corporate bonds, hundreds of billions of municipals, and the structured products (MBS and ABS) that sit at the center of this story. Almost every dollar of non-Treasury debt is rated, and most large issues are rated *twice* (by two of the big three) because many investor mandates require two ratings. That is the revenue river the agencies tap. Now the structural oddity that defines the whole business: **the issuer pays for the rating.** When a company or government wants to sell a bond, it hires and pays the agency to rate that bond. The investors who *rely* on the rating pay nothing. So the agency's paying customer is the very entity whose debt it is supposed to judge impartially.

![The issuer-pays conflict of interest in ratings](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-7.png)

That diagram is the conflict in one picture. The issuer wants the *highest* rating (it lowers their borrowing cost), the issuer is the one writing the check, and the issuer can "shop" — quietly ask several agencies for a preliminary view and hire the one that signals the friendliest grade. The agency that wants the business has a standing incentive to be generous. It's as if restaurants paid the health inspectors and could fire any inspector who gave them a C.

So why does anyone trust the system? Partly **reputation** — an agency that hands out AAAs that default loses the credibility that is its entire franchise. But mostly because ratings are **hardwired into regulation**. This is the second oddity. The agencies hold a quasi-governmental license: the SEC designates them as **Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organizations** (NRSROs), and countless rules and mandates *reference* their ratings. A money-market fund may be legally allowed to hold only "investment grade" paper; a pension fund's mandate may forbid anything below A; bank capital rules historically gave lower capital charges to higher-rated assets. When the rulebook says "investment grade," it is outsourcing the definition to three private companies. That regulatory embedding is what makes a rating *compulsory* rather than merely useful — and it's why issuers must pay even an agency they distrust.

#### Worked example: a rating agency's fee on a \$1 billion bond issue

A corporation issues a \$1,000,000,000 bond and needs it rated to sell to mainstream investors. Agencies typically charge a few basis points of the issue size, plus annual surveillance fees. At, say, 4 bps, the upfront fee is \$1,000,000,000 × 0.0004 = **\$400,000** to one agency — and large issuers often buy two ratings, so call it \$700,000–\$800,000 all in, plus recurring surveillance. The issuer pays this gladly, because a one-notch better rating can shave, say, 20 bps off the coupon: on a \$1B 10-year bond, 0.20% × \$1B × 10 years ≈ **\$20 million** of interest saved. Paying \$400k to potentially save \$20M is an easy trade — which is exactly why the pressure to inflate ratings is so strong.

The intuition: the issuer's incentive to pay for a *good* rating dwarfs the agency's fee, so the conflict isn't a rounding error — it's the dominant force in the room.

### When the conflict detonated: structured products in 2008

The issuer-pays conflict is survivable for plain bonds, where an agency can be embarrassed by a default. It became catastrophic for **structured products** — securitizations that pool thousands of loans (mortgages especially) and slice them into tranches sold to investors. We cover the mechanics of [how banks turn loans into securities](/blog/trading/banking/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities) elsewhere; here the point is what the agencies did with them.

In the mid-2000s, Wall Street banks pooled subprime mortgages and engineered the cash flows so that a large slice of each deal could be stamped **AAA** — the same grade as US Treasuries — even though the underlying loans were made to weak borrowers.

![US securitization issuance and the 2008 collapse](/imgs/blogs/the-supporting-cast-exchanges-data-indices-and-ratings-8.png)

Securitization issuance ran near \$700–750 billion a year in 2006–2007, much of it mortgage-related, and then collapsed by more than 75% as the loans defaulted and the AAA ratings proved fantasy. The agencies had three compounding problems. First, **the conflict was sharpest here**: a handful of banks were repeat customers generating enormous, lucrative deal flow, so losing one bank's structured business was a real threat. Second, **the analysts didn't fully model the risk** — their models assumed national home prices wouldn't fall together, which is exactly what happened. Third, **the issuers could shop and re-engineer**: if an agency balked, the bank could tweak the structure or take the deal to a friendlier rater. The result was AAA stamps on securities that lost most of their value, and the ratings that money-market funds and pensions had been *legally required* to trust turned to dust.

It is worth being precise about *who bore the loss*, because it's the opposite of who earned the fee. The agencies collected their rating fees upfront, on hundreds of billions of dollars of issuance, in the boom years — and those fees were not clawed back when the securities defaulted. The losses fell on the investors who had trusted the AAA stamps: money-market funds that "broke the buck," pension funds, foreign banks, and ultimately taxpayers who funded the bailouts. This is the toll-collector model at its most dangerous. The agency's revenue is tied to the *volume* of issuance it rates, not to whether those ratings turn out to be correct, so in a boom its incentive is to keep the deals flowing, not to slam on the brakes. The party that profits from the measurement and the party that suffers when the measurement is wrong are completely different people — and that separation is exactly what a healthy incentive structure is supposed to prevent.

The 2010 Dodd-Frank Act tried to defang the problem — stripping mandatory ratings references out of some rules, increasing CRA liability and disclosure — but the issuer-pays model and the NRSRO oligopoly both survived largely intact. The agencies are still paid by issuers, still designated by the SEC, and still three firms control the market. The conflict was documented, criticized, and then mostly left in place.

## Data and indices: why measurement became the new profit center

Step back and notice a pattern across exchanges, index providers, and rating agencies: the most valuable thing each one owns is not a service performed once, but a **stream of data and measurement that the market is forced to keep paying for**. This is why the data-and-index layer has quietly become the most attractive profit center in finance.

The exemplar is **Bloomberg**. A Bloomberg Terminal costs roughly \$30,000 per user per year, and there are around 350,000+ terminals in the world. That is on the order of \$10 billion a year in recurring subscription revenue — from a product whose marginal cost to add one more user is tiny. Bloomberg is privately held and provides no capital to any market; it sells the *measurement* of markets. The reason traders pay \$30k a year and almost never cancel is **network effects and switching costs**: everyone you deal with is on Bloomberg, the chat (IB) is where deals get done, and rebuilding your workflow elsewhere is painful. That stickiness is the moat.

The same logic explains why ICE and LSEG paid tens of billions to *become* data companies, and why MSCI — a pure index-and-analytics firm with no trading or capital at all — trades at a richer valuation multiple than many banks. Data and index revenue is **recurring, high-margin, and near-monopoly**: there's one S&P 500, one authoritative NYSE tape, one dominant terminal. Compare that to an investment bank, which we covered in [how an investment bank makes money across ECM, DCM, M&A, and trading](/blog/trading/capital-markets/inside-an-investment-bank-ecm-dcm-ma-and-trading): the bank earns large fees but has to win each deal afresh and rides the cycle up and down. The toll-collector earns less per transaction but collects it from everyone, every year, forever.

#### Worked example: the recurring-revenue premium of a data business

Compare two firms each earning \$1 billion of annual profit. Firm A is an investment bank: its profit is cyclical (it can halve in a bad year) and deal-driven, so the market values it at, say, 10× earnings — a \$10 billion valuation. Firm B is a data-and-index firm: its \$1 billion is recurring subscription and licensing revenue with 95% renewal and a fat margin, so the market values it at 30× earnings — a \$30 billion valuation. Same profit, **3× the value**, purely because of *how* the dollar is earned. That gap is why every exchange in the world is racing to reclassify itself as a data company.

The intuition: in finance, the market pays far more for a dollar that recurs automatically than for a dollar you have to go hunt for again next quarter.

This is also a quiet competition concern. Because there are so few suppliers of essential market data and benchmarks, and because regulation hardwires demand for them, these firms have pricing power that looks a lot like a regulated utility's — except they aren't regulated like one. The market-data-fee fight, the index-licensing-cost grumbles from ETF sponsors, and antitrust scrutiny of exchange mergers all stem from the same root: a near-monopoly on a mandatory input.

## Proxy advisors: the firms that quietly run shareholder votes

The last member of the supporting cast is the least visible and, per dollar of its own revenue, arguably the most influential: the **proxy advisor**. When you own shares, you get to vote — on directors, on executive pay, on mergers, on shareholder proposals. A large index fund might own pieces of 3,000 companies and face tens of thousands of ballot items across a proxy season, far more than it can research in depth. So it outsources the analysis to a proxy advisor, chiefly **ISS** or **Glass Lewis**, which publishes a recommendation — *for* or *against* each item — and often votes the shares automatically according to those recommendations.

The leverage here is enormous and concentrated. Two firms effectively guide a large share of institutional votes. A negative ISS recommendation on a pay package or a director can swing a meaningful percentage of the vote, because so many funds follow the guidance by default. Companies now lobby proxy advisors the way they lobby index committees and rating analysts — courting a private firm whose opinion moves outcomes they can't otherwise control. Critics argue this hands outsized power over corporate governance to two unaccountable, conflict-prone firms (ISS, for instance, also sells consulting services to the very companies it rates on governance). Defenders argue proxy advisors are a rational, cost-effective answer to the impossible scale of voting across thousands of holdings — the same scale-and-outsourcing logic that created index funds in the first place.

There is a feedback loop here identical to the index one. As more money flows into passive index funds, those funds collectively own a larger and larger share of every public company — but they own it *passively*, with no analyst following each name. They still must vote, though, on thousands of ballots. So the rise of indexing mechanically increases the demand for outsourced voting, which concentrates governance influence in ISS and Glass Lewis. The same structural force — capital getting cheaper and more passive — strengthens the index provider, the data vendor's network, *and* the proxy advisor simultaneously. The supporting cast doesn't just coexist with the rise of passive investing; it is the layer that profits most from it.

#### Worked example: the leverage of a single proxy recommendation

Suppose a company's executive pay package faces a "say-on-pay" vote, and 60% of the shares are held by institutions that follow ISS guidance by default. If ISS recommends *against*, roughly 0.60 × the institutional block swings to "no" almost automatically — call it 50%+ of the total vote — enough to defeat the package or, at minimum, to humiliate the board into renegotiating. The cost to the company of courting ISS is a modest consulting and engagement effort; the cost of ignoring ISS can be a failed vote that triggers governance downgrades and activist pressure worth far more. So a firm whose own revenue is a few hundred million dollars exerts leverage over pay decisions worth billions.

The intuition: when enough voters default to one advisor's recommendation, that advisor's opinion stops being advice and becomes, in effect, the outcome.

Notice the pattern repeating one more time: a firm that owns no shares and provides no capital nonetheless shapes how the market's capital *behaves*, by selling measurement and guidance that everyone defaults to. The proxy advisor is the governance-layer toll-collector.

## Common misconceptions

**"Exchanges make their money from trading fees."** Mostly false. For the big listed exchanges, trading is roughly a fifth of revenue and the lowest-margin fifth at that. The real money is in **listings, market data, and co-location** — recurring, sticky, high-margin streams. An exchange is best understood as a data company with an auction attached.

**"An index just passively reflects the market, so it's neutral."** No. Because trillions of dollars *passively follow* the major indices, the act of including or excluding a security forces price-insensitive buying or selling. The index doesn't merely measure the market; at the margin it *moves* it. A reclassification decision can redirect tens of billions of dollars toward or away from an entire country.

**"A AAA rating means the bond is safe."** It means *one private firm, paid by the issuer,* judged default unlikely under its model. In 2008, AAA-rated mortgage securities lost most of their value because the models were wrong and the issuer-pays conflict pushed ratings up. A rating is an opinion with a commercial conflict baked in, not a guarantee — read it as "the issuer paid for this grade," not "this is risk-free."

**"Rating agencies are like regulators, so they must be impartial."** They have a *quasi*-regulatory role — the NRSRO designation and the rules that reference ratings — but they are for-profit firms paid by the issuers they rate. The regulatory embedding gives them power *without* giving them a public mandate, which is the worst of both worlds for impartiality.

**"Bloomberg and these data firms are just expensive software."** The \$30k-a-year terminal isn't priced on its software cost; it's priced on **network effects and switching costs**. You pay because everyone you trade with is on it and leaving means cutting yourself off from the deal flow. That's a near-monopoly on a communication-and-data network, which is why the revenue recurs almost automatically.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The Tesla S&P 500 inclusion (2020).** As the opening showed, S&P's decision to add Tesla forced an estimated tens of billions of dollars of index-fund buying into a single name on a single rebalance date. The episode is the cleanest real-world proof that an index provider's list is not a passive mirror but an active lever on capital flows.

**The MSCI China A-shares inclusion (2018 onward).** MSCI's phased addition of mainland Chinese stocks to its Emerging Markets index channeled large, sustained foreign inflows into Shanghai and Shenzhen, and reshaped how global EM portfolios allocate. It also showed the *negotiating* power of an index firm: MSCI extracted market-access and trading-reform concessions from Chinese authorities as a condition of inclusion.

**Vietnam's reclassification campaign.** Vietnam has spent years reforming foreign-ownership limits, settlement mechanics, and pre-funding rules with the explicit goal of an FTSE Russell (and eventually MSCI) upgrade from Frontier to Emerging status — because the upgrade would mechanically pull billions of passive dollars into HOSE-listed stocks. We trace this dynamic in the Vietnam-specific post on [foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam). It's a vivid illustration of a sovereign state restructuring its market to satisfy a private index committee.

**The 2008 ratings failure.** AAA-stamped subprime securitizations — backed by the issuer-pays model and hardwired into the mandates of money funds and pensions — collapsed when the loans defaulted, helping turn a housing downturn into a global crisis. It remains the canonical case of the supporting cast's measurement being trusted, mandated, and catastrophically wrong.

**The LSEG–Refinitiv deal (2021).** The London Stock Exchange Group paid roughly \$27 billion to acquire the Refinitiv data business, an explicit bet that the future of an "exchange" is data and analytics, not matching trades. Post-deal, trading is a minority of LSEG's revenue — the clearest corporate confession that the toll-collector's data stream is worth more than the trading floor it grew from.

## The takeaway: follow the toll, not the trade

The lesson of the supporting cast is that in a mature capital market, the most durable profits accrue not to whoever takes the most risk but to whoever sits astride the most traffic. Exchanges, index providers, rating agencies, data vendors, and proxy advisors put no capital at risk in the market itself. They sell the *measurement and access* that make the market usable, and because that measurement is mandatory — embedded in regulation, in fund mandates, in workflow, in the very definition of "the market return" — the toll recurs automatically and the base only grows.

Tie it back to the spine of this series. Secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible, and intermediaries are what make both work. The supporting cast is the layer that makes the *intermediaries* legible: you can't list without an exchange, can't benchmark a fund without an index, can't sell a bond to a regulated buyer without a rating, can't run a trading desk without the data feed. They are the connective tissue of trust and access. That's an indispensable public function — and it's owned by a handful of unregulated, near-monopoly, for-profit firms whose conflicts (issuer-pays ratings, monopoly data pricing, conflicted proxy advice) keep producing the same fights decade after decade.

So when you next read that an exchange "lost money on lower trading volumes" or that an index "added a stock," look one layer deeper. Ask who collects the toll, how recurring it is, and whether the firm doing the *measuring* has an incentive to measure honestly. In capital markets, the firm that owns the ruler is often worth more than the firms being measured — and understanding why is most of what separates a sophisticated reader of the market from a naive one.
