> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnam's stock market trades fine, but its post-trade plumbing — clearing, settlement, and the rule that you must put cash in *before* you buy — is the single biggest thing keeping the world's largest funds out, and fixing it is the technical heart of the upgrade story.
>
> - **VSDC** (the Vietnam Securities Depository and Clearing Corporation, formerly VSD) is the one central institution that clears, settles, and custodies every trade on HOSE and HNX, sitting under the State Securities Commission (SSC).
> - Vietnam settles **T+2** (two business days), one day slower than the global **T+1** standard — and until a 2022 reform the shares and cash were only usable late on T+2, effectively T+2.5.
> - The real blocker is **100% pre-funding**: a foreign fund must have all the cash sitting in the account *before* it can place a buy order, while the rest of the world pays at settlement (Delivery-versus-Payment). Global funds find this unworkable.
> - The fix is a **central counterparty (CCP)** plus the September-2024 **Circular 68** allowing **non-pre-funding (NPF)** for foreign institutional buys, riding on the long-delayed **KRX trading system** that finally went live in 2025.
> - The one fact to remember: **until the plumbing is DvP- and CCP-grade and pre-funding is gone, global capital will not fully trust the market — fixing the plumbing is fixing the trust.**

## The morning a billion-dollar fund could not press "buy"

Picture the trading desk of a large emerging-markets fund in London. It is 2023, the VN-Index is climbing, and a portfolio manager wants to put roughly \$10 million into a Vietnamese blue-chip before the open in Ho Chi Minh City. In almost every market she trades — Seoul, Mumbai, Taipei, Warsaw — she does this the same way: she sends the order, the trade executes, and two days later (or, since 2024, one day later) her custodian pays cash and receives shares in a single, simultaneous swap. Her cash stays invested, earning a return, right up until that settlement instant.

In Vietnam, she cannot do that. Before her broker will accept the buy order, the *full* purchase amount has to already be sitting, in cash, in the trading account. Not at settlement — before the order. So to buy \$10 million of stock on Wednesday, she has to wire \$10 million on Monday, watch it sit idle and earn nothing for two or three days, and only then place the trade. For a fund that runs dozens of orders a week across a portfolio, that is a permanent, dead pool of cash she can never put to work, plus an operational headache her global custody bank is not even set up to handle. The compliance officer's verdict is short: we cannot operate like this at scale.

What makes this so striking is that, on every surface measure, Vietnam looks ready. The economy has grown for years; the stock market has real depth, with hundreds of listed companies and, at its 2021 peak, a market capitalization north of 90% of GDP; tens of trillions of dong change hands on a normal day. Retail participation has exploded, with the number of trading accounts more than quadrupling in six years. None of that is the problem. The problem lives entirely in the post-trade plumbing — in the rules that govern *how* a trade turns into ownership and money — and it is invisible to anyone looking only at the index chart.

That single friction — not the size of the market, not the quality of the companies, not the level of the index — is the technical reason Vietnam has spent years knocking on the door of "emerging market" status at index providers like FTSE and MSCI and being told to wait. This post is about the machinery behind that friction: the depository that holds everyone's shares, the settlement cycle that decides when money and stock actually change hands, the pre-funding rule that scares off the big money, and the two reforms — a central counterparty and a brand-new exchange system built by the Korea Exchange — that are finally rebuilding the plumbing.

![Pre-funding before a buy versus paying at settlement under DvP](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-1.png)

That figure is the whole story in one picture: on the left, the Vietnamese model locks your cash for days before you even trade; on the right, the global model lets your cash work until the very instant you receive the shares. Everything that follows is an explanation of why that gap exists and how it gets closed.

## Foundations: what clearing and settlement actually are

Before any of the Vietnam-specific detail makes sense, we need the vocabulary, built from zero. None of this assumes you have ever read a finance textbook.

When you "buy a stock", the moment your order matches a seller's order on the exchange is *not* the moment you own the share. Matching is just a promise: I will pay you this much money, you will give me this many shares. Turning that promise into reality is a two-step back-office process that the industry calls the **post-trade lifecycle**.

**Clearing** is the bookkeeping step. After the exchange matches thousands of trades, someone has to work out who owes what to whom — net. If over a day you bought 1,000 shares of a company and sold 600 of the same, you do not move 1,600 shares; you net to a delivery of 400. This netting is enormously efficient: instead of settling every single trade gross, the system collapses millions of obligations into a handful of net positions per participant. (For the full mechanics of netting and how a central counterparty supercharges it, see our sibling post on [what happens after the trade](/blog/trading/capital-markets/what-happens-after-the-trade-the-post-trade-lifecycle).)

**Settlement** is the step where the promise becomes real: cash moves from buyer to seller, and shares move from seller to buyer, and ownership legally changes. The gold-standard way to do this is **Delivery-versus-Payment (DvP)** — the two legs happen simultaneously, so neither side can be left having paid without receiving, or having delivered without being paid. DvP is the bedrock of trust in modern markets: it removes **principal risk**, the risk that you hand over your money or your shares and the other side simply does not come through.

**Custody** is the boring-but-essential job of holding the shares safely once you own them. In a modern market you do not get a paper certificate; your ownership is an electronic entry in a central ledger.

The institution that runs the central ledger is the **Central Securities Depository (CSD)**. Almost every market has exactly one. It is the master record of who owns which securities, and it is usually the entity that performs (or oversees) clearing and settlement too. In the United States it is the DTCC; in South Korea it is KSD; in Vietnam, as we will see, it is VSDC.

A **central counterparty (CCP)** is a more advanced layer that some markets bolt on. Through a legal process called **novation**, the CCP steps into the middle of every trade: the buyer's counterparty becomes the CCP, and the seller's counterparty becomes the CCP. Now nobody worries about whether the *other trader* is good for the money — they only face the CCP, which is heavily capitalized and backed by a default fund. The clearinghouse post explains exactly [how a CCP removes counterparty risk](/blog/trading/capital-markets/the-clearinghouse-how-a-ccp-removes-counterparty-risk); for our purposes the key point is that a CCP is what makes it safe to *not* pre-fund, because the CCP guarantees the trade will settle even if a participant stumbles.

One more piece of context makes the Vietnam debate legible: there is a widely accepted international yardstick for what "good plumbing" looks like. Global standard-setters publish principles for financial-market infrastructures, and index providers translate those into concrete access criteria — is settlement DvP, is there a central counterparty or an equivalent guarantee, can foreign investors operate without pre-funding, are the settlement and custody arrangements robust, can money move in and out freely? A market is not judged against a vague ideal but against this shared checklist that every promoted market has had to satisfy. When commentators say Vietnam is "close" to an upgrade, they mean it is ticking these boxes one by one, and that the remaining unticked boxes — historically, pre-funding above all — are post-trade plumbing items, not economic or corporate-quality ones.

Here is the spine of this whole series, and it is exactly why the plumbing matters so much: a capital market is a machine that turns savings into long-term investment, and that machine only runs if the **secondary market is liquid** — if you can sell your claim tomorrow morning. Liquidity attracts issuance and foreign capital. But liquidity is only as trustworthy as the plumbing underneath it. If settlement can fail, or if the rules make it impossibly expensive for big institutions to participate, the market stays shallow and the upgrade never comes. **Trustworthy plumbing is the precondition for liquidity, and liquidity is the precondition for everything else.**

It helps to slow down on each of these because they are easy to blur together, and the entire Vietnam debate turns on distinctions between them. Clearing is *figuring out what is owed*; settlement is *actually moving it*; custody is *holding it afterwards*. A market can be excellent at one and weak at another. Vietnam, for instance, has perfectly competent custody — VSDC's electronic ledger is reliable, ownership is unambiguous — and reasonable settlement timing at T+2. What it has historically lacked is the *clearing-side* guarantee that lets a buyer trade without pre-funding. The reform is almost entirely about the clearing layer, which is why understanding the layer-by-layer split matters before anything else.

A second piece of vocabulary you will meet constantly is **principal risk** versus **replacement-cost risk**. Principal risk is the catastrophic kind: you pay and receive nothing, or you deliver and get nothing — you lose the whole principal. DvP exists precisely to kill principal risk, by making the two legs atomic. Replacement-cost risk is milder: a trade fails, nobody loses their principal, but the non-failing side has to go back into the market and re-do the trade at a possibly worse price. The difference matters because pre-funding is a blunt, expensive way to eliminate principal risk (make the buyer pay up front so they can never fail to pay), whereas a CCP eliminates the *same* principal risk far more elegantly, by guaranteeing the trade and managing the residual replacement-cost risk with margin. Two tools, same goal; one is crude and one is modern, and Vietnam is mid-way through swapping the crude one for the modern one.

A third concept: the difference between a **clearing member** and an ordinary participant. In a CCP system, not every broker faces the clearinghouse directly. A smaller broker may clear *through* a larger clearing member, who is the one that posts margin to the CCP and is legally on the hook. This tiering is how risk concentrates in a small number of well-capitalized firms that the CCP can actually monitor. When Vietnam's reform talks about a securities company "standing behind" a foreign client's unfunded buy, it is describing exactly this clearing-member role — the firm absorbs the short-window credit risk because it, in turn, faces a CCP that guarantees the final settlement.

#### Worked example: why netting matters

Suppose three brokers trade a stock among themselves in one day. Broker A buys 10,000 shares from B and sells 8,000 to C. Broker B buys 8,000 from C. Without netting, that is three separate gross deliveries totalling 26,000 shares and three cash movements. With netting, the clearer computes each broker's *net* position: A is net long 2,000, B is net flat (bought 8,000, sold 8,000), C is net short 2,000. The entire day collapses to a single delivery of 2,000 shares from C to A and one matching cash payment. **Netting turns a tangle of gross obligations into a tiny number of net ones — which is why clearing exists before settlement.**

## The depository: VSDC and how it sits under the SSC

Vietnam's central securities depository is the **Vietnam Securities Depository and Clearing Corporation (VSDC)**. It was created out of the former Vietnam Securities Depository (VSD) — the rename to "VSDC" deliberately puts the word "Clearing" in the title, signalling the institution's expanding role as the market modernizes. VSDC is the one place where every share traded on the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE), the Hanoi Stock Exchange (HNX), and the UPCoM board is recorded, cleared, and settled.

![Where VSDC sits in Vietnam's post-trade stack under the SSC](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-2.png)

Read that pipeline left to right and you have the life of a Vietnamese trade. An investor places an order through a licensed securities company (the local term for a brokerage). The exchange — HOSE or HNX — matches it. From there everything funnels into VSDC, which performs three jobs in sequence: it **clears** (nets the day's obligations across all members), it **settles** (moves the net cash and net shares on T+2), and it **custodies** (keeps the master ownership ledger). Sitting above the whole chain is the **State Securities Commission (SSC)**, the regulator that licenses participants, writes the rules, and ultimately decides when reforms like non-pre-funding go live.

For the broader architecture of HOSE, HNX, UPCoM and the SSC — who lists where, how the boards differ — see the sibling post on [Vietnam's capital market: HOSE, HNX, UPCoM and the SSC](/blog/trading/capital-markets/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc). Here we stay on the post-trade layer, because that is where the upgrade fight is being fought.

Why does it matter that there is a single central depository? Because centralization is what makes netting and DvP possible at all. If ownership records were scattered across dozens of brokers' private ledgers, no one could net the market down or guarantee a simultaneous swap. The CSD is the trusted middle — and in Vietnam, VSDC is also the institution that will host the future central counterparty. The reform of the plumbing runs *through* VSDC.

It is worth dwelling on how much a CSD does that investors never see. Beyond holding the master ledger, VSDC handles **corporate actions** — when a company pays a dividend, splits its stock, or runs a rights issue, the depository is the entity that credits the right cash or new shares to the right owners on the right date. It manages **securities registration**, the process by which a newly listed company's shares are dematerialized and entered into the central system in the first place. It runs the **settlement accounts** that interface with the banking system so that the cash leg of every trade can actually move through commercial banks and ultimately the central bank's payment rails. And it maintains the **investor identification** framework — the trading-account numbering that the trading-accounts chart above is counting. Every one of these functions has to scale and modernize in lock-step with the trading engine, which is part of why the KRX migration was such a large, multi-year undertaking rather than a simple software swap.

There is also a governance dimension worth noting. Because VSDC is a single point through which the entire market settles, it is itself **systemically important** — if VSDC failed operationally, the market would stop. That is exactly why the SSC's oversight, the capital backing the future CCP, and the default-fund design are not bureaucratic box-ticking but the load-bearing safeguards of the whole system. A central counterparty concentrates risk on purpose, so that it can be managed in one well-defended place rather than diffused unmonitored across hundreds of brokers; the price of that concentration is that the center must be fortress-grade. Building that fortress — the capital, the margin models, the default waterfall — is the substantive work behind the headline reforms.

## The settlement cycle: Vietnam's T+2, the world's T+1, and the 2.5-day quirk

"T+2" means settlement happens two business days after the trade date T. Buy on Monday (T), and cash and shares change hands on Wednesday (T+2). For most of the developed world the standard moved to **T+1** in 2024 — the United States, Canada and Mexico switched in May 2024, and India had already gone further, with much of its market on T+1 and pilots of same-day T+0. Vietnam remains on T+2.

![T plus two versus T plus one settlement exposure window timeline](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-5.png)

One extra day may not sound like much, but every day a trade is unsettled is a day of **counterparty exposure** — the window in which the other side might fail, prices might move, and the clearer is on the hook to make things right. Shortening the cycle is a direct reduction of systemic risk, which is exactly why the global trend runs only one direction: T+3 → T+2 → T+1 → eventually T+0. The general principle here is covered in the [post-trade lifecycle](/blog/trading/capital-markets/what-happens-after-the-trade-the-post-trade-lifecycle) sibling; the Vietnam-specific wrinkle is what makes the local story unusual.

That wrinkle: for years, even though Vietnam was nominally T+2, the shares and cash were only credited and *usable* late in the afternoon of T+2. A buyer who settled on Wednesday could not actually sell those shares until Thursday — effectively a **T+2.5** experience. In 2022 a reform freed the assets on the *morning* of T+2, so an investor could sell shares (or use proceeds) the same day they settled rather than waiting another session. It sounds like a small operational tweak, but for active traders it meaningfully increased how fast capital could recycle — a half-day of liquidity handed back to the whole market.

This quirk is a useful reminder that the *headline* settlement cycle and the *experienced* settlement cycle can differ. A market can advertise "T+2" while delivering something slower in practice if the assets are not actually usable until late on the settlement day. Conversely, a market can advertise T+2 but feel faster if intraday processing frees assets early. The honest way to compare two markets' plumbing is therefore not to read the label but to ask: at what precise moment can the investor next *use* the cash or the shares? By that test, pre-2022 Vietnam was meaningfully slower than its T+2 label implied, and the 2022 reform closed most of that gap without changing the label at all. It is the kind of unglamorous, high-impact fix that characterizes good plumbing reform — invisible in the marketing, very visible to the people who trade every day.

There is a deeper point here about why settlement timing is so jealously guarded by regulators. Every open settlement obligation is a small promise that has not yet been kept, and the sum of all unkept promises at any instant is the market's standing **settlement risk**. Shortening the cycle shrinks that sum; lengthening it grows it. When a major market moves from T+2 to T+1, it is not chasing speed for its own sake — it is deliberately reducing the pile of unkept promises the system carries, so that a shock (a failed member, a sudden price gap) finds fewer half-finished trades to disrupt. Vietnam's eventual move toward T+1 should be read in exactly that light: less a convenience upgrade than a reduction in the system's standing risk, made safe to attempt only once a CCP can guarantee the compressed timetable.

#### Worked example: T+2 vs T+1 exposure-days on a 1,000,000,000 VND trade

Take a trade of **1,000,000,000 VND** (one billion dong, roughly **\$40,000** at about 25,000 VND to the dollar). Under T+2 the trade is unsettled for two business days; under T+1, one. If we crudely price counterparty exposure as the chance the position has to be unwound at a loss during the open window, and assume daily price moves of about 1.5%, then the *expected* adverse move scales with the square root of time. Two days of exposure is √2 ≈ 1.41× the risk of one day — about 41% more exposure on the same 1,000,000,000 VND trade. On a single small ticket that is a few hundred thousand dong of expected risk; across a market settling tens of trillions of dong a day it is a large, permanent overhang. **Cutting the cycle from T+2 to T+1 would shave roughly 30% off the settlement-risk window the whole market carries.**

![HOSE average daily trading value by year in trillion VND](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-6.png)

The scale is the point. Average daily trading value on HOSE jumped from around 5 trillion VND in 2018 to over 21 trillion VND at the 2021 retail-driven peak, settling back to the mid-teens since. Multiply that daily flow by an extra day of unsettled exposure and you see why regulators treat the settlement cycle as a systemic-risk dial, not an operational footnote.

Why is Vietnam not simply on T+1 already, given the global trend? Because shortening the cycle is far harder than it sounds. T+1 compresses the entire post-trade workflow into a single overnight: trade affirmation, allocation, funding, foreign-exchange (for foreign investors who must convert into dong), and instruction to custodians all have to happen in hours rather than days. For a domestic-only market this is achievable; for a market that wants to court *foreign* capital across time zones it is genuinely hard, because a London or New York fund's working day barely overlaps Hanoi's. The United States' May 2024 move to T+1 caused a measurable, if temporary, spike in failed trades and FX-funding stress for exactly these cross-border reasons. The lesson Vietnam's regulators take from that is sequencing: build the CCP and the modern engine first, get foreign access working under non-pre-funding, and only *then* consider compressing the cycle — because a CCP makes a shorter cycle safe, while a shorter cycle without a CCP just multiplies fail risk.

There is a subtle interaction with pre-funding here, too. Pre-funding and a long settlement cycle compound each other for the foreign investor: not only must the cash be in the account *before* the trade, it then sits idle through the full T+2 window before settlement extinguishes the obligation. The float cost we will compute shortly is driven by *both* levers — the requirement to pre-position cash and the number of days it stays trapped. Fixing pre-funding via non-pre-funding attacks the first lever directly; shortening the cycle would attack the second. The reform program is, in effect, working both ends of the same lockup.

## The cash leg: how money actually moves, and why it constrains the design

It is tempting to focus entirely on the securities leg — who holds the shares, when they move — and forget that every trade has a *cash* leg that has to move through the banking system at the same time. Understanding the cash leg explains a lot about why Vietnam's reforms are sequenced the way they are.

When VSDC settles a net position, the share movement happens on its own ledger, but the matching cash movement happens through commercial banks and ultimately the central bank's payment system. For a domestic investor trading in dong, this is straightforward: the cash sits in a dong account at a local bank, and the settlement bank debits and credits accordingly. For a *foreign* investor, two extra steps intrude. First, the investor's money usually arrives as foreign currency and must be converted to dong through the foreign-exchange market before it can settle a trade — and that conversion has its own timing, cost, and operational chain. Second, foreign capital flows are watched by the authorities for balance-of-payments and currency-stability reasons, so there are reporting and account-structure requirements that a purely domestic flow never touches.

These cash-leg realities are *why* pre-funding existed in the first place. If you cannot be sure the cash will arrive and convert in time for settlement, the conservative fix is to demand it up front. Pre-funding is, at bottom, a way of solving a cash-leg uncertainty by brute force. The more elegant solution — let the broker and the CCP carry the obligation while the cash converts and arrives over the settlement window — requires confidence that the FX and payment plumbing will reliably deliver dong on T+2. So the cash leg is not a side issue; it is part of the same trust problem. A CCP guarantee on the securities side only works if the cash side can be relied upon to show up, which is one more reason the reform is being phased in carefully rather than switched on overnight.

#### Worked example: the FX-plus-settlement chain for a foreign buy

Trace a single foreign buy of **1,000,000,000 VND** (≈ \$40,000) end to end. The fund instructs its global custodian; the custodian's local sub-custodian in Vietnam must (1) have or obtain dong, converting roughly \$40,000 of foreign currency at the prevailing rate; (2) ensure the dong is in the right settlement account; (3) match the trade details with VSDC; and (4) deliver cash against the shares on T+2. Under pre-funding, steps 1–2 had to be finished *before* the order — so the \$40,000 was converted to dong and parked, exposed to two days of currency drift and zero yield, before the trade even existed. Under non-pre-funding, the order is placed first and the broker carries the obligation while the FX and funding chain completes over the settlement window. **The reform does not remove the FX-and-funding chain; it lets that chain run in parallel with the trade rather than as a precondition for it, which is the whole operational difference between "park your cash first" and "pay at settlement."**

## The pre-funding problem: the single biggest friction for foreign institutions

Now we reach the heart of the matter. Vietnam has required, by rule, that the **cash be fully in the account before a buy order is placed** — 100% pre-funding. You cannot buy on the promise to pay at settlement; you must have paid first.

To understand why this is such a deal-breaker, you have to understand how a global institutional fund is built to operate. A large fund settles **DvP**: it instructs its global custodian to pay cash *at* settlement, simultaneously against receipt of shares. The cash never leaves the fund's productive control until the exact instant the shares arrive. The fund's entire operating model — its cash management, its custody contracts, its internal controls — assumes this. Asking it to pre-fund is asking it to rebuild its plumbing for one small market, and to accept a permanent drag of idle cash plus the operational risk of money sitting in a foreign broker's account before any trade exists.

Look closer at *why* this clashes so badly with a global fund's controls, because it is not only about lost yield. A large institutional fund typically operates under a strict rule that it will not deliver value (cash or securities) except against simultaneous receipt of the other leg — that is the literal meaning of DvP, and it is often hard-coded into the fund's custody agreement and its regulator-mandated risk controls. Pre-funding asks the fund to do precisely the thing its rulebook forbids: to part with cash *in advance*, with no security received and no simultaneous swap, trusting that a foreign broker in a frontier market will hold it safely and execute the trade. For many funds this is not a matter of preference or cost — it is a hard compliance prohibition. The cash, once wired, is also sitting outside the fund's normal custody chain, which raises questions about segregation, what happens in a broker insolvency, and how the position is even valued before the trade exists. A regulated pension fund or a UCITS vehicle in Europe may simply be unable to tick the box that says "yes, we are comfortable pre-positioning client money with an unaffiliated broker." So pre-funding does not merely tax the willing; it disqualifies a whole tier of the most conservative, most patient, largest investors — exactly the capital an index upgrade is supposed to attract.

There is a further wrinkle on the *sell* side that compounds the problem. Because shares must be in the account before a sale and cash before a buy, a foreign investor cannot easily run the natural cash-management trick of selling one position to fund the purchase of another on the same day — the sale proceeds are not available in time under the old timing. So the pre-funding regime does not just lock up cash for buys; it makes the whole portfolio harder to turn over efficiently. A global manager who rebalances across a basket of Vietnamese names finds that what is a single netted, same-day operation in Seoul becomes a multi-day, cash-heavy, operationally fiddly sequence in Ho Chi Minh City.

#### Worked example: the float cost of pre-funding a \$10M buy

Suppose a fund wants to buy **\$10,000,000** of Vietnamese stock and, because of pre-funding, must park that cash in the account for **3 days** before the order executes. The fund's cash would otherwise earn, say, a 5% annual money-market yield. The float cost is:

\$10,000,000 × 5% × (3 / 365) = **\$4,109** of forgone yield on a single trade.

That looks small until you annualize the behavior. A fund running, say, \$500,000,000 dedicated to Vietnam and turning the portfolio over a few times a year, with cash idle on average several days per cycle, can easily forgo six figures of yield annually — plus the harder-to-quantify operational cost of a custody setup its systems were never designed for. **Pre-funding is not one fee; it is a permanent tax on every dollar a foreign fund tries to deploy in Vietnam, which is why the largest allocators simply stay underweight or out.**

This is the top blocker for the index upgrade. When FTSE Russell and MSCI assess a market for promotion from "frontier" to "emerging", they survey exactly these large institutional investors and ask: can you operate here at scale? The recurring complaint is pre-funding (and the related issue of foreign ownership limits — covered separately in [foreign flows and ownership limits](/blog/trading/capital-markets/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds)). The full upgrade checklist — what Vietnam must fix to win promotion and unlock passive inflows — is the subject of [the emerging-market upgrade](/blog/trading/capital-markets/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix). Pre-funding sits at the top of that list.

![Foreign net buy and sell on HOSE by year in trillion VND](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-3.png)

You can see the hesitation in the flows. Foreign investors net-sold heavily in 2021, came back as net buyers in 2022, then sold again in 2023 and far more in 2024. Foreign flows are driven by many things — the dollar, global risk appetite, valuations — but the structural friction of pre-funding caps how *committed* the marginal global allocator can be. A market that is operationally painful to enter does not get the patient, large, passive capital that an index upgrade would unleash. (The mechanics of how index inclusion drives passive buying are detailed in our Vietnam-stocks piece on [foreign flows, ETFs and the index effect](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)

## The NPS / CCP reform: removing the lockup

So how do you let a foreign fund buy without pre-funding *and* keep the seller safe from a buyer who might not pay? The answer the whole world uses is to put a guarantor in the middle: a **central counterparty**, or at minimum a clearing member who stands behind the buy.

![How a CCP and non-pre-funding remove the pre-funding lockup](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-7.png)

The mechanism, step by step: the foreign fund places its buy with **no pre-funding**. Its broker — a licensed securities company acting as a **clearing member** — accepts the order and effectively guarantees it. Through novation, the CCP (hosted by VSDC) becomes the counterparty to both sides and guarantees settlement. The seller delivers shares; the fund pays cash at T+2 settlement, the normal DvP way. The credit risk that used to be solved by making the buyer pre-fund is now *absorbed by the broker* during the short window between trade and settlement — managed with margin, capital, and the CCP's default fund. This is exactly the model that lets a London-based fund trade Seoul or Mumbai without parking cash in advance.

The elegance of this design is that it dissolves a false trade-off. The old regime treated the choice as binary: either protect the seller by forcing the buyer to pre-fund (safe, but it drives away foreign capital), or let buyers trade unfunded (attractive, but it exposes everyone to a buyer who might not pay). The CCP refuses the dilemma. It protects the seller — in fact it protects *everyone* — by interposing a guaranteed counterparty, while leaving the buyer free to pay at settlement like the rest of the world. The residual risk, that the buyer fails during the short settlement window, does not vanish; it is sized, collateralized with margin, and backstopped by the default waterfall, so it becomes a small, well-managed, well-capitalized exposure rather than a market-wide source of fear. That is the entire intellectual move behind a clearinghouse, and it is why every serious market eventually adopts one. Vietnam is not inventing anything; it is importing the standard solution, adapted to its sequencing constraints.

Notice, too, what the model demands of the *broker* in the interim Circular 68 phase, before the full CCP is live. The securities company has to evaluate each foreign client's ability to pay, decide how much unfunded exposure it is willing to carry, and hold capital against the possibility that a client fails. In effect, the broker is doing a miniature version of the CCP's job on its own balance sheet until the central guarantee is fully built. This is why the early rollout is concentrated, deliberate, and watched closely: the system is testing whether brokers can prudently carry foreign settlement risk before that risk is mutualized at the center.

Vietnam's first concrete step came in **September 2024**, when the SSC issued **Circular 68/2024/TT-BTC**, which (among other reforms) permits **non-pre-funding (NPF)** for purchases by **foreign institutional investors**. Under NPF the securities company assesses the client's creditworthiness and lets it buy without the cash being fully in the account first, taking on the settlement obligation itself. It is not yet a full CCP guaranteeing every trade in the market — it is a targeted, broker-led first step aimed squarely at the foreign-institution complaint that index providers kept flagging. The roadmap beyond Circular 68 is a proper CCP model run through VSDC, which would generalize the guarantee across all participants and eventually enable shortening the settlement cycle and adding products.

The broker-led design of Circular 68 is a clever interim answer, but it is worth being honest about what it asks of the securities companies. When a broker lets a foreign institution buy without pre-funding, the broker is extending what amounts to very-short-term, unsecured credit for the settlement window. If the client fails to deliver the cash on T+2 — because of an FX glitch, an operational error, or in the worst case a default — the broker is on the hook to settle anyway. That means brokers must hold more capital, run credit assessments on their foreign clients, and manage a new category of risk they did not carry before. Smaller securities companies may not have the balance sheet to offer NPF at scale, which is why the early phase concentrates the facility among the larger, better-capitalized brokers. This is the clearing-member tiering from the foundations section showing up in practice: the risk migrates to the firms strong enough to bear it.

The eventual full CCP changes this picture qualitatively rather than just quantitatively. Under a true CCP, the guarantee does not rest on any single broker's balance sheet; it rests on the CCP's own capital, a margin posted by every clearing member sized to cover plausible price moves over the settlement window, and a mutualized **default fund** that the surviving members top up if one of them fails. This layered defense — margin first, then the defaulter's contribution, then the CCP's own capital, then the mutual fund — is the standard "default waterfall" every developed-market clearinghouse runs, and it is what lets the system honor every trade even when a member goes down. Building it is the substantive, multi-year project that sits behind the word "CCP" in Vietnam's roadmap.

#### Worked example: how an NPF/CCP model removes the lockup

Return to our \$10,000,000 buy. Under the old rule the fund pre-funds \$10,000,000 for 3 days at a float cost of \$4,109 per trade. Under NPF, the fund places the order with no cash in advance; its broker carries the obligation and posts, say, a 10% margin — \$1,000,000 — to the CCP for the two-day window, against which the broker charges a small financing fee, perhaps \$10,000,000 × 1% annualized × (2/365) ≈ **\$548**. The fund's \$10,000,000 stays invested until settlement; its float cost on this trade drops from \$4,109 to roughly the modest financing fee, and the operational nightmare of pre-positioning cash disappears entirely. **The CCP/NPF model does not make the credit risk vanish — it relocates it from the buyer's idle cash to the broker's margin and the CCP's guarantee, which is exactly the trade-off every developed market has already made.**

## The KRX system: the platform the reform rides on

You cannot run a modern central counterparty, intraday risk management, and a shorter settlement cycle on an aging trading engine. Vietnam's exchanges had outgrown their legacy systems, and the planned upgrade was a new trading-and-clearing platform built by the **Korea Exchange (KRX)**. It was years late — contracted back in 2012, repeatedly delayed — and became something of a running symbol of the market's modernization struggles. It finally **went live in 2025**.

![Vietnam securities trading accounts in millions by year](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-4.png)

The urgency is visible in the account-growth chart: Vietnam's number of securities trading accounts roughly quadrupled from about 2.2 million in 2018 to over 9 million by 2024, a retail boom that repeatedly pushed the old matching engine to its capacity limits (HOSE famously suffered overload-driven trading halts during the 2021 surge). A market growing that fast simply needs more capable infrastructure.

The KRX saga is also a case study in how hard it is to replace the beating heart of a live market. You cannot simply switch off the exchange for a month and bolt on a new system; the migration has to preserve every open position, every account, every corporate-action schedule, and it has to be tested exhaustively because a bug in the matching or settlement logic could mis-price or mis-deliver real money at national scale. The repeated delays from the original 2012 contract were partly the usual large-IT-project troubles and partly the genuine caution that comes from knowing a botched go-live would be catastrophic. When a system this central finally goes live, the conservative path is to turn features on gradually — first stable matching at higher capacity, then the risk-management and clearing features, then the more advanced products — rather than flip everything at once. So the KRX go-live is best read not as a single finish line but as the opening of a runway: the platform that *can* host a CCP, intraday settlement, and a shorter cycle, with those capabilities switched on in sequence as the market and the regulator gain confidence.

It is also worth separating the two things the engine buys you. The first is raw **throughput and resilience** — the ability to match a surging order flow without freezing, which directly fixes the 2021 overload embarrassment and improves the day-to-day experience for the millions of retail accounts in the chart. The second, and more strategically important, is **risk-management headroom**: a modern engine can compute margin and monitor exposures intraday, continuously, rather than in an overnight batch. That continuous risk view is the technical precondition for a CCP, because a clearinghouse has to know, at any moment, how much it would lose if a member defaulted right now. Without it, the guarantee is blind. The legacy system could clear trades; it could not run a living risk engine. That, more than speed, is why the upgrade was non-negotiable for the reform.

What the KRX system enables is the technical foundation for everything else in this post:

- **Faster, higher-capacity matching**, so the exchange can handle surging retail volume without freezing.
- **Intraday functionality** — features like intraday trading of just-bought positions and tighter risk monitoring through the day.
- **A path to a central counterparty**, the clearing architecture that makes non-pre-funding safe market-wide.
- **A shorter settlement cycle and new products** (such as broader derivatives and intraday settlement) once the CCP layer is in place.

#### Worked example: how the KRX system enables intraday and a shorter cycle

Recall the half-day liberated by the 2022 reform, when assets were freed on the morning of T+2 instead of the afternoon. A modern engine with intraday risk management and a CCP can compress that further. Suppose intraday netting and continuous margining let the system move to **T+1**: on a market settling roughly **17 trillion VND** in average daily value, cutting the unsettled window from two days to one removes about **17 trillion VND** of average daily exposure from the clearer's books at any given moment, and frees buyers' capital a full day sooner to recycle into new trades. **A faster engine is not a luxury feature — it is the prerequisite that turns the reform roadmap, from CCP to T+1 to new products, from a wish list into something the system can physically execute.**

## Common misconceptions

**"Vietnam's market is too small or too risky for big funds — that's why they stay out."** Wrong on the diagnosis. The market is large enough and the companies are real; HOSE's market cap reached roughly 90%+ of GDP at the 2021 peak. The binding constraint is operational, not fundamental: pre-funding and the absence of a CCP make it impractical for global custody models, regardless of how attractive the stocks are.

**"T+2 versus T+1 is a rounding error — who cares about one day?"** One day is a 41% increase in the settlement-risk window (√2 of single-day exposure) and, across a market settling tens of trillions of dong daily, a large standing risk overhang. It is precisely why every major market is racing toward T+1 and T+0. The cycle is a systemic-risk dial.

**"Non-pre-funding means foreign funds can buy with no money down and the risk just disappears."** No. The credit risk is not eliminated; it is *relocated* to the broker (the clearing member) and the CCP, who manage it with margin, capital, and a default fund. NPF works only because someone well-capitalized stands behind the trade — which is the entire point of a central counterparty.

**"Circular 68 already fixed the upgrade problem."** It is a major first step, allowing NPF for foreign institutional buys, but it is broker-led, not a full market-wide CCP. The complete fix — a proper CCP at VSDC, generalized to all participants, plus the operational track record index providers want to see — is still being built. Reforms are judged by index providers on implementation and durability, not announcement.

**"Foreign investors are blocked from Vietnam mainly by ownership limits, not pre-funding."** Both are real blockers, but they bite differently. Ownership limits (the foreign-ownership cap, or "room") restrict *how much* of a given company foreigners can hold; pre-funding restricts *the act of trading itself* for everyone, on every name, regardless of room. A fund can route around a tight room with structures like depositary receipts or non-voting shares, but pre-funding is a wall at the front door that no clever structure removes. That is why index providers singled it out as the priority fix, and why Circular 68 targeted it first. The ownership-limit story is covered in its own sibling post; here the point is simply that pre-funding is the more fundamental, harder-to-route-around constraint.

**"Once the KRX system is live, the upgrade is automatic."** The engine is necessary but not sufficient. Going live unlocks the *ability* to run a CCP, intraday risk management, and a shorter cycle — but each of those still has to be designed, capitalized, tested, and operated cleanly for long enough that global investors and index providers trust it. The platform is the runway; the plane still has to taxi, take off, and fly a few clean circuits before anyone calls the journey complete.

**"The KRX system is just a faster computer for trading."** It is the infrastructure layer that makes the *risk-management* reforms possible. Without a modern engine you cannot run intraday margining, a CCP, or a shorter cycle. The trading-speed improvement is the visible part; the clearing and risk architecture underneath is what unlocks the upgrade.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2021 retail boom and the HOSE overload.** As trading accounts surged past 4 million in 2021 and average daily value blew past 21 trillion VND, HOSE's legacy matching system hit capacity and suffered afternoon trading freezes — a vivid demonstration that infrastructure, not investor appetite, was the binding constraint. The episode hardened the case for finishing the long-delayed KRX migration.

**The 2022 settlement reform.** Moving asset availability to the morning of T+2 from the afternoon looks tiny on paper, but it gave active traders back roughly half a session of usable liquidity per trade and recycled capital faster across the whole market — proof that post-trade timing changes have real, market-wide liquidity effects.

**Circular 68 and the September-2024 NPF go-live.** The SSC's decision to allow non-pre-funding for foreign institutions was timed and framed explicitly around the FTSE Russell review cycle. It was the most-watched single regulatory move in Vietnamese capital markets in years precisely because the index providers had told the market, repeatedly and publicly, that pre-funding was the blocker.

**The KRX go-live in 2025.** After more than a decade of delays, the platform finally entered service, removing the "the system isn't ready" excuse and opening the technical path to a CCP, intraday features, and a shorter cycle.

**The contrast with peers who got there first.** It is instructive to look at how comparable markets cleared the same hurdle. South Korea — whose exchange built Vietnam's new system — runs a full CCP and DvP settlement and is a developed-market index member. India moved aggressively, putting much of its market on T+1 and piloting T+0, precisely to deepen foreign participation. Taiwan and the Gulf markets each had their own version of the access-and-plumbing debate before promotion. The pattern is consistent across every one of them: the upgrade did not come when the index rose or the economy grew, but when the *post-trade access constraints* — pre-funding, settlement risk, the absence of a guarantee — were removed and the change had been running cleanly long enough for global investors to trust it. Vietnam is following the same script, just later and with the KRX delays as an extra chapter.

**Why announcements are not the finish line.** A recurring feature of these episodes is the gap between a reform being *issued* and being *believed*. Index providers explicitly want to see a facility like non-pre-funding working in practice, across a review cycle, with real foreign institutions using it and no settlement mishaps, before they upgrade. So even a well-designed Circular 68 buys promotion only after a track record accumulates. This is why the reform story is measured in years, not headlines: the market is being asked to prove the plumbing holds under real load, not merely to legislate that it should.

![VN-Index year-end level showing the 2021 peak and recovery](/imgs/blogs/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system-8.png)

Lay the index over this history and the plumbing story snaps into focus: the VN-Index roughly tripled from 546 at end-2014 to a 1,498 peak in 2021, fell to 1,007 in 2022, and clawed back toward 1,267 by 2024. The level is driven by earnings, rates, and sentiment — but the *ceiling* on how much committed global capital can participate is set by the plumbing. An upgrade-grade post-trade system is what would let the patient, passive, index-tracking money in, and that is the prize the reforms are chasing.

## The takeaway: fixing the plumbing is fixing the trust

Step back and the Vietnam story is a clean illustration of this series' core idea. A capital market turns savings into long-term investment only if its secondary market is liquid enough that investors trust they can exit. Liquidity attracts issuance and foreign capital. But liquidity is downstream of trust, and trust is downstream of plumbing. You can have great companies, a rising index, and millions of new retail accounts — and still be stuck at "frontier" status, because the largest, most patient pools of global capital cannot operate in a market that makes them pre-fund every trade and offers no central counterparty to guarantee settlement.

That is why the unglamorous back-office layer — VSDC, the settlement cycle, the pre-funding rule, the CCP roadmap, the KRX engine — is not a footnote to Vietnam's upgrade. It *is* the upgrade. The market's investability is decided not in the boardrooms of its listed companies but in the wiring diagram of its clearing and settlement system, and that wiring has been the thing holding it back.

It is also a reminder that financial-market reform is rarely a single dramatic act. Vietnam's progress has come as a chain of individually modest steps — renaming and re-scoping the depository as VSDC, freeing assets on the morning of T+2 in 2022, issuing Circular 68's non-pre-funding allowance in 2024, finally bringing the KRX system live in 2025 — each one a brick, none of them a finish line. The reason the sequence matters more than any one step is that trust compounds: each clean reform that holds under real load makes the next one more credible, until the cumulative track record is long enough that the largest global allocators are willing to commit. That is the patient, unglamorous arithmetic of building a market the world will trust. Circular 68's non-pre-funding allowance and the live KRX system are the first real bricks in a DvP- and CCP-grade foundation. When that foundation is finished — pre-funding gone, a CCP guaranteeing trades, a shorter settlement cycle running on a modern engine — the index providers' surveys will start coming back clean, the passive inflows will follow, and the market's liquidity will deepen enough to support far larger issuance.

It is also worth naming what the payoff looks like if the program succeeds, because it is large and concrete. An upgrade from frontier to emerging at a major index provider mechanically triggers buying from every passive fund that tracks the emerging-market index — money that *must* hold Vietnamese stocks in proportion to their index weight, regardless of any manager's view. Estimates of the one-time and ongoing inflows from such an upgrade run into the billions of dollars, dwarfing the float costs and operational frictions the reform removes. That passive money is also the *patient* kind: it does not chase momentum or flee at the first wobble, so it deepens the market structurally rather than adding to its volatility. Deeper, more stable demand for shares is exactly what lowers the cost of capital for Vietnamese companies and makes large new issuance viable — which closes the loop back to this series' spine, where secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible. The plumbing reform, in other words, is not an end in itself; it is the unlock for cheaper capital across the whole economy.

And there is a feedback loop worth naming explicitly. As the plumbing improves and foreign participation deepens, the market becomes more liquid; greater liquidity narrows spreads and lowers the cost of trading, which attracts still more participants and more issuance; the larger, deeper market then justifies further infrastructure investment, which improves the plumbing again. This virtuous circle is exactly what an upgrade is meant to ignite. The hard part is reaching the threshold where it starts to spin on its own — and for Vietnam, that threshold is defined almost entirely by the post-trade items this post has walked through.

The lesson generalizes well beyond Vietnam: whenever you evaluate any market, do not stop at the index level or the company quality. Ask how the plumbing works — how trades clear, when they settle, whether settlement is DvP, whether there is a central counterparty, and whether a foreign institution can operate the way it operates everywhere else. The answer to those plumbing questions tells you, better than any price chart, whether the world's capital can truly trust the place. **Fix the plumbing and you fix the trust; fix the trust and the capital comes.**
