> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The IPO is the *first* time a company sells shares to the public; it is almost never the last. A listed firm has a whole menu of ways to raise more capital, and the right one depends on how fast it needs the money, how much dilution it can stomach, and how big a discount it must concede.
>
> - A **primary** follow-on mints brand-new shares and the cash goes to the *company* (dilutive); a **secondary** follow-on is just an existing holder selling old shares, so the cash goes to *them* (non-dilutive).
> - A **rights issue** hands existing shareholders the pro-rata right to buy new shares at a discount, which is why — done right — it protects them from dilution; the price resets to the **theoretical ex-rights price (TERP)**.
> - **Accelerated bookbuilds** and **block trades** raise institutional money overnight at a small discount to the last close; **at-the-market (ATM)** programs dribble shares into the open market; **PIPEs** sell to a handful of investors fast, often at a 10–20% discount.
> - The one fact to remember: issuing equity is read as a *signal* — "management thinks the shares are dear" — so the stock usually drops a few percent on the announcement, before a single new share has changed hands.

## A second trip to the well

In June 2020, in the depth of the pandemic, Carnival Corporation — the cruise operator whose ships were sitting empty in harbors around the world — needed cash, and fast. Its IPO was a distant memory; the company had been public for decades. But being already listed turned out to be its lifeline. Over a frantic few weeks Carnival sold new common shares, sold convertible bonds, and sold senior secured notes, raising more than \$10 billion in total without ever doing anything as slow and ceremonial as an IPO. The marketed share sale priced at a discount to where the stock was trading; the converts let buyers turn debt into equity later if the shares recovered. None of it would have been possible if there were not a deep, liquid secondary market quoting a price for Carnival stock every second of every trading day.

That is the quiet truth this whole series keeps circling back to. A capital market is a **machine that turns savings into long-term investment**, and it runs on two engines: a **primary market** that *creates* securities to raise money, and a **secondary market** that *trades* them to provide liquidity. We tend to romanticize the IPO as *the* primary-market event — the bell, the confetti, the founder on the exchange floor. But the IPO is one door into the primary market, and once a company is through it, the secondary market's constant pricing of its shares unlocks a far richer set of doors. A firm that can be sold tomorrow morning can be financed tonight.

This post is the practitioner's map of those doors. We will walk the full menu — follow-on offerings, rights issues, accelerated bookbuilds and block trades, at-the-market programs, private placements and PIPEs, and convertible bonds — and for each one answer the three questions that actually decide which a CFO picks: *How fast? How dilutive? How big a discount?* Along the way we will do the arithmetic that makes dilution and the theoretical ex-rights price concrete, and we will keep returning to the single most counter-intuitive fact about raising equity: the announcement itself moves the price, usually down, because of what it *signals*.

## Foundations: what changes once a company is already public

Before the menu, the groundwork. If you have not read the companion posts, the [IPO process end to end](/blog/trading/capital-markets/the-ipo-process-end-to-end-from-mandate-to-first-trade) explains how a company goes public in the first place, and [debt vs equity](/blog/trading/capital-markets/debt-vs-equity-the-two-ways-to-raise-capital) explains the fundamental fork between borrowing and selling ownership. Here we assume the IPO already happened, and we focus on what is now *different*.

**A security is a standardized, transferable claim.** A share of common stock is a claim on a slice of a company's residual value and its future profits. The reason any of this works — the reason a long-term claim is worth holding — is that you can sell it. The secondary market provides a continuous price and a buyer on demand. That liquidity is not a nice-to-have; it is the precondition for the primary market. Nobody buys a freshly issued share at \$40 unless they are confident they can sell it at roughly \$40 a minute later. Every tool in this post leans on that fact.

**Three things changed at the IPO.** First, there is now a *public price* — a live, observable mark for the company's equity that updates every second the market is open. Second, there is now a *public float* — shares already in the hands of outside investors and trading freely. Third, the company now lives under *continuous disclosure*: it files quarterly and annual reports, it discloses material events promptly, and the market already knows a great deal about it. Those three changes are exactly why raising more money post-IPO is so much faster and cheaper than the IPO itself. The slow, expensive parts of an IPO — building the disclosure record from scratch, educating investors who have never heard of the company, discovering a price with no prior reference — are mostly already done.

**Two coordinates define every follow-on tool.** The first is **dilution**: does the deal create new shares (which spreads the same company over more claims, shrinking each existing holder's percentage and per-share earnings), or merely move existing shares between investors? The second is **the discount**: at what price do the new buyers get in, relative to the last traded price? Speed is the third axis, but speed mostly *causes* the discount — the faster you need to place stock, the bigger the price concession buyers demand for taking it off your hands without time to think. Hold those three coordinates — dilution, discount, speed — in your head and the whole menu organizes itself.

**Who are the players?** The **issuer** is the listed company (or sometimes a large existing shareholder). The **investment bank** runs the deal as bookrunner — the same role it plays in an IPO, and the same one detailed in [inside an investment bank](/blog/trading/finance/inside-an-investment-bank-how-they-make-money). The **buyers** are mostly institutions: mutual funds, pensions, hedge funds, sovereign wealth funds — the deep pools of savings the capital market exists to channel. And the **incumbent shareholders** are the silent fourth party at the table, because every dilutive deal is, in part, a transfer of value from them to the new buyers unless they are given a way to participate.

**The shelf registration is the quiet enabler.** Most of the speed in post-IPO raising comes from a piece of paperwork called a **shelf registration**. Instead of registering each new batch of shares from scratch, a seasoned issuer files a single document — in the US, a Form S-3 "shelf" — that pre-registers a large dollar amount of securities it *may* sell over the next few years. The shares then sit "on the shelf," ready to be taken down whenever the company wants. When the moment comes — a strong stock, an acquisition to fund, a balance sheet to repair — the company can issue off the shelf in hours rather than the weeks a fresh registration would take. The shelf is precisely what makes an overnight ABB or an ATM drip possible: the regulatory work is already done, so all that remains is to find the buyers and set the price. A company that qualifies as a "well-known seasoned issuer" can take down shelf securities almost instantly. This is the machinery behind the cliché that a listed company can "raise money overnight" — the overnight part is real, but only because the months of paperwork happened in advance.

**The cost of the capital is not just the discount.** Every deal on this menu carries a **gross spread** — the fee the underwriting bank keeps, expressed as a percentage of the money raised. For a marketed follow-on it might be 2–4%; for a routine overnight block it can be under 1.5%; for an IPO it is famously around 7% in the US. On top of the spread sit the discount to market and the legal, accounting, and listing costs. So when a company raises, say, \$200 million, it does not net \$200 million: it nets the proceeds minus the spread, and it sold the shares below the prevailing price to begin with. A reader trying to judge whether a raise was "good" for existing holders has to net all three — discount, spread, and use of proceeds — against what the company does with the money. Cheap capital deployed badly still destroys value; expensive capital deployed brilliantly still creates it.

![US equity market cap by year showing growth from twenty-six to fifty-eight trillion dollars](/imgs/blogs/beyond-the-ipo-follow-ons-rights-issues-and-private-placements-2.png)

That backdrop matters because every follow-on issues *into* an existing pool of equity. The US equity market grew from roughly \$26 trillion in 2014 to about \$58 trillion at the end of 2024. A \$500 million follow-on by a mid-cap firm is a rounding error against that pool — which is exactly why the secondary market can absorb new issuance so smoothly. The depth of the trading market is the shock absorber for the issuance market.

It also helps to see how lumpy the *first* trip to the well is, because follow-on activity tends to move with it. IPO volume is famously cyclical — a boom in 2021 followed by a near-total freeze in 2022 — and follow-on windows open and shut on the same market mood.

![US IPO proceeds by year from twenty-fourteen to twenty-twenty-four](/imgs/blogs/beyond-the-ipo-follow-ons-rights-issues-and-private-placements-4.png)

When markets are buoyant and stocks are rich, the issuance window is wide open: IPOs price, follow-ons clear at thin discounts, and ATMs sell into strength. When markets seize up, the window slams — the 2022 collapse from \$142 billion of US IPO proceeds in 2021 to about \$8 billion was mirrored by a sharp drop in follow-on activity, because the same fear that kills IPOs makes institutions demand fatter discounts for any new paper. The menu is always available in theory; in practice the market decides when the doors are open.

## The follow-on offering: primary vs secondary

Start with the workhorse. A **follow-on offering** (also called a "follow-on public offer", FPO, or simply a "secondary offering" — a confusing piece of jargon we will untangle in a moment) is the sale of additional shares by an already-listed company, using a prospectus much like the IPO's but far shorter because the disclosure record already exists.

The single most important distinction — and the one most people get wrong — is between a **primary** follow-on and a **secondary** follow-on. These two words describe *who is selling the shares and where the cash ends up*, and they have nothing to do with the primary-vs-secondary *market* split that this series is built on. (Yes, the terminology overloads "primary" and "secondary." Blame a century of accreted Wall Street slang. We will be explicit every time.)

- A **primary follow-on** sells *newly created* shares. The company prints fresh stock and sells it. The share count goes up — every existing holder now owns a smaller slice of the same company — and the cash flows *into the company's bank account*. This is dilutive, and it is the one that funds growth, pays down debt, or shores up the balance sheet.
- A **secondary follow-on** sells *shares that already exist*. A large holder — a founder, a venture-capital fund, a private-equity owner, an early employee with a big stake — sells their shares to new investors. The share count does *not* change. There is *no* dilution. And the cash flows to the *selling shareholder*, not to the company. The company raises nothing; it is just a venue for a big holder to cash out.

![Before and after comparison of a primary follow-on versus a secondary follow-on and where the cash goes](/imgs/blogs/beyond-the-ipo-follow-ons-rights-issues-and-private-placements-3.png)

Many real deals are *mixed*: a company issues some new shares (primary) while early backers sell some of theirs (secondary) in the same offering. The prospectus splits the proceeds explicitly — "of the X shares offered, Y are being sold by the company and Z by the selling shareholders." When you read that a firm "raised \$300 million in a follow-on," check whether the company actually got the \$300 million or whether some of it went to insiders walking out the door. The market reads those two very differently: a primary raise to fund growth is one story; insiders dumping stock is another.

#### Worked example: dilution and EPS from a \$200M primary follow-on

A company has 100 million shares outstanding and earns \$150 million a year, so its earnings per share (EPS) is \$150M ÷ 100M = \$1.50. The stock trades at \$40, a price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio of about 26.7×. The company does a primary follow-on, selling new shares at \$38 (a small 5% discount to the market price) to raise \$200 million.

New shares issued = \$200,000,000 ÷ \$38 ≈ 5,263,158 shares. The new share count is 100,000,000 + 5,263,158 ≈ 105,263,158.

If the \$200 million just sits in the bank earning nothing, next year's EPS falls to \$150,000,000 ÷ 105,263,158 ≈ \$1.425 — a dilution of about 5%. Each existing holder's slice of the *earnings* shrank by 5% because the same profit is now spread over 5% more shares.

But the cash is not meant to sit idle. Suppose the company uses the \$200 million to build a plant that will earn a 15% return — \$30 million of new profit a year. Then next year's earnings are \$180 million over 105.26 million shares = \$1.71 EPS, *higher* than the \$1.50 it started with. **The intuition:** dilution is only destructive if the company invests the new money at a return below what shareholders already earned on it — issuing equity is value-accretive precisely when the project beats the cost of the capital raised.

### Marketed vs overnight execution

A follow-on can be executed two ways, and the choice is a trade-off between price and certainty.

A **marketed** follow-on looks like a mini-IPO. The bank publishes the deal, the management team does an abbreviated roadshow (sometimes just a day or two of calls), the book is built over a few days to two weeks, and the deal prices at a modest discount — often 2–5% below the last close. The advantage is that you get the best price from a broad set of buyers who had time to do work. The disadvantage is **market risk**: the stock is trading the whole time, and if it falls during the marketing window, your deal gets cheaper or breaks. Worse, the announcement itself usually knocks the price down (the signalling effect, which we will get to), so by the time you price, you may be selling well below where you started.

An **overnight** follow-on — usually structured as an accelerated bookbuild, covered below — compresses all of that into a single evening. You give up the broad marketing and accept a slightly bigger discount in exchange for *speed and certainty*: the deal is done before the stock can move against you. Carnival's 2020 raises were fast precisely because, in a crisis, certainty was worth more than the last few cents of price.

There is also a **greenshoe** (over-allotment option) on many marketed follow-ons, just as on IPOs. The bank is granted the right to sell up to ~15% more shares than the headline deal size, and it covers that extra by short-selling into the offering and then either buying back in the open market (if the price weakens, which stabilizes the stock) or exercising the greenshoe to buy the extra shares from the company (if the price holds). The mechanism lets the bank support the price in the first days after the deal and lets the company quietly raise a bit more if demand is strong. It is one of the small ways the secondary market and the primary market are stitched together: the stabilizing buys happen *in* the trading market to make the *issuance* land smoothly.

A subtler point: a follow-on does not exist in a vacuum of supply. The shares already trading set a reference price every second, and the *float* — the freely tradable shares already out there — determines how much new supply the market can swallow before the price sags. A company with a thin float and thin daily volume cannot place a large block without a steep discount, because there simply are not enough natural buyers to absorb it quickly. A mega-cap with billions of shares trading daily can place a huge follow-on with barely a ripple. This is the same liquidity logic that governs [bid-ask spreads and trading costs](/blog/trading/finance/stock-exchanges-and-clearinghouses) in the secondary market, now applied to issuance: the deeper the trading market, the cheaper the raise.

## Rights issues: dilution with the antidote built in

Here is the elegant idea at the heart of the rights issue, and it solves the central unfairness of a plain primary follow-on. When a company issues new shares to outside buyers at a discount, existing shareholders are diluted *and* they watch new investors get a bargain price they were not offered. A **rights issue** fixes both problems by offering the new shares **first, pro-rata, to the people who already own the company**.

In a rights issue, every existing shareholder receives — for free — the *right* to buy a set number of new shares at a fixed, discounted price, in proportion to their current holding. A "1-for-4 rights issue" means: for every 4 shares you own, you get the right to buy 1 new share at the offer price. If you own 4% of the company and you exercise all your rights, you still own 4% of the (now larger) company afterward. **Your percentage ownership is protected.** The dilution that would otherwise transfer value to outsiders is neutralized, because *you* are the buyer of the new shares.

This is why rights issues are the default equity-raising method across much of Europe, Asia, and emerging markets, including Vietnam, where company law and listing rules often *require* that new shares be offered to existing holders first (a "pre-emption right"). In the US, by contrast, pre-emption rights are usually waived, which is why American companies lean on plain follow-ons and the faster tools below. The legal default shapes which tool a market reaches for.

### The theoretical ex-rights price (TERP)

Because the new shares are sold at a discount, the *average* price of all shares falls after the issue. The market reflects this by re-pricing the stock to its **theoretical ex-rights price (TERP)** on the ex-rights date — the day the stock starts trading without the attached right. TERP is just a weighted average of the old price and the new discounted price.

![Rights issue calendar from announcement through ex-rights, rights trading, subscription, and new-share listing](/imgs/blogs/beyond-the-ipo-follow-ons-rights-issues-and-private-placements-5.png)

#### Worked example: TERP of a 1-for-4 rights issue at \$8 when the stock is \$10

A stock trades at \$10. The company announces a 1-for-4 rights issue at a subscription price of \$8 (a 20% discount to the market price). Take a shareholder who owns exactly 4 shares.

Before the rights issue, those 4 shares are worth 4 × \$10 = \$40.

They get the right to buy 1 new share at \$8. If they exercise, they pay \$8 and now hold 5 shares. Their total outlay and value is \$40 + \$8 = \$48, spread over 5 shares.

TERP = \$48 ÷ 5 = \$9.60 per share.

So on the ex-rights date, the stock "drops" from \$10 to \$9.60 — but that is not a real loss. The shareholder who exercised holds 5 shares at \$9.60 = \$48, exactly the \$40 they had plus the \$8 they put in. **The intuition:** the \$0.40 fall is purely mechanical, the arithmetic of blending a \$10 price with \$8 new shares; a holder who takes up their rights loses nothing, while one who ignores them really is diluted.

The value of a single right is the gap between TERP and the subscription price: \$9.60 − \$8.00 = \$1.60 per new share. That number is why renounceability matters so much.

### Renounceable vs non-renounceable rights

What if a shareholder does not want to put in more money? This is where rights issues split into two flavors.

A **renounceable** rights issue makes the rights themselves *tradable*. For the subscription period, the rights trade on the exchange as their own line item (a "nil-paid" right). A shareholder who does not want to subscribe can *sell* their rights to someone who does, and pocket roughly their theoretical value — about \$1.60 each in the example above. That sale compensates them for the dilution they are accepting by not participating. This is the fair version: even a non-participant is made roughly whole.

A **non-renounceable** rights issue gives shareholders no such out. If you do not exercise your rights, they lapse worthless and you are simply diluted — your slice shrinks and you got nothing for it. These are harsher on passive holders and are often used when a company needs the money urgently and wants to pressure shareholders to participate. (Some jurisdictions soften this with a "rump placement" or "compensatory payment," where unexercised rights are sold to institutions after the deadline and the proceeds returned to non-participants — a partial substitute for tradability.)

#### Worked example: the cost of ignoring your rights

Return to the holder of 4 shares worth \$40 at \$10 each, facing the 1-for-4 rights at \$8 with TERP \$9.60.

If the rights are *renounceable* and they sell their 1 right at \$1.60, after the ex-rights date their 4 shares are worth 4 × \$9.60 = \$38.40, plus the \$1.60 from selling the right = \$40.00. They are made whole.

If the rights are *non-renounceable* and they do nothing, their 4 shares are still worth 4 × \$9.60 = \$38.40, and the right lapses for \$0. They are out \$1.60 — a 4% loss on a \$40 position — purely for not acting.

**The intuition:** a renounceable right is a small, tradable asset handed to you for free; throwing it away in a non-renounceable deal is the only way a rights issue actually costs a shareholder money, which is why "do nothing" is the wrong default.

### Why deep-discount rights are common in a crisis

When a company is in trouble and *must* raise equity, it often prices the rights at a steep discount — 30%, 40%, sometimes more below the market price. Why give away so much? Because a deep discount makes the issue almost certain to be taken up: the bigger the gap between TERP and the subscription price, the more obviously worthwhile it is to exercise, and the less likely the deal fails. The discount looks dramatic but is largely cosmetic for participating holders — recall that a participating shareholder is made whole regardless of the discount, because they are buying the cheap shares themselves. The deep discount is mostly a device to guarantee the money arrives.

### The underwriter and the rump

A company doing a rights issue usually needs the money for certain, but it cannot force every shareholder to subscribe. To guarantee the proceeds, it hires a bank to **underwrite** the issue on a firm-commitment basis: the bank promises to buy any new shares that shareholders fail to take up, so the company gets its full target no matter what. For taking that risk the bank charges an underwriting fee (often 1.5–3% of the amount underwritten). The unexercised portion — the shares left over after the subscription deadline — is called the **rump**, and the bank places it with institutions in a quick bookbuild, much like an ABB, returning any premium over the subscription price to the non-subscribing holders (in renounceable structures) or keeping it (in others, per the deal terms). The rump placement is the safety valve that lets a company commit to "we will raise \$1 billion" before knowing how many holders will actually pay up.

This is also where a deep discount earns its keep on the *underwriter's* side. The lower the subscription price relative to TERP, the smaller the chance the stock falls below the subscription price during the offer period — and if the stock ever trades below the subscription price, no rational holder would exercise (they could just buy cheaper in the market), the issue fails, and the underwriter is stuck with the whole rump. The deep discount is thus partly a fee-saving device: a steeper discount means a less risky underwrite, which means a cheaper underwriting fee. The company trades a scarier-looking headline price for a more certain, cheaper guarantee.

## Accelerated bookbuilds and block trades: capital overnight

Sometimes a company or a big shareholder needs to move a large slug of stock *now* — before earnings, before a market move, before a window closes. The tool is the **accelerated bookbuild (ABB)**, and its defining feature is speed: the entire process happens overnight, between the close of one trading session and the open of the next.

![Pipeline of an accelerated bookbuild from market close through wall-crossing, book building, pricing, and trading before the open](/imgs/blogs/beyond-the-ipo-follow-ons-rights-issues-and-private-placements-7.png)

Here is the sequence. After the market closes, the bank ("the bookrunner") launches the deal to a pre-selected list of institutional investors — sometimes after quietly "wall-crossing" a few large funds beforehand, meaning bringing them over the confidentiality wall to gauge demand under a non-disclosure agreement. The bank takes orders for a few hours, building a *book* of demand at various prices. Based on that book it sets a clearing price — typically a few percent below the last close — allocates the shares to the funds that bid, and confirms the trades. By the time the market reopens, the placement is done and the stock starts trading with the new shares already in institutional hands.

The discount is the price of speed and certainty. Because buyers are committing capital overnight with no time for deep diligence, and because the seller wants a guaranteed clean print, the shares clear *below* the last close — usually 3–7%. That discount is the mirror image of the IPO "pop": in an IPO, new shares are priced *below* where they will open so they jump up; in an ABB, shares are priced *below* the last close so institutions are paid to absorb them fast.

![IPO first-day pop by year compared with the follow-on and accelerated bookbuild discount zone](/imgs/blogs/beyond-the-ipo-follow-ons-rights-issues-and-private-placements-9.png)

#### Worked example: an ABB clearing 5% below last close

A private-equity fund owns 40 million shares of a company that closed at \$25.00. It wants out of a 10-million-share chunk fast and hires a bank to run an overnight block via ABB. The book builds and the deal clears at \$23.75 — a 5% discount to the \$25.00 close.

Proceeds to the seller = 10,000,000 × \$23.75 = \$237,500,000. Had they been able to sell at the full \$25.00 they would have received \$250,000,000, so the discount "cost" them \$12,500,000 — the price of getting \$237.5 million in a single overnight print instead of dribbling 10 million shares into the market over weeks and risking the price sliding as everyone saw them selling.

The bank takes a fee — say 1.5% of proceeds, about \$3,562,500 — and may do the deal on a **bought-deal** (risk) basis, buying the whole block from the seller at a fixed price and reselling it, in which case the bank eats any shortfall if the book is soft. **The intuition:** an ABB converts a hard-to-sell large position into immediate certain cash, and the 5% discount plus the fee is what the seller pays for transferring the risk of a sloppy, price-moving exit onto institutions and the bank.

A **block trade** is the close cousin: a single large parcel of existing shares sold off-market, often directly negotiated between a seller and a bank (or between two institutions), at a discount to the prevailing price. The mechanics of how a block crosses the market connect to [how an exchange and clearinghouse handle large trades](/blog/trading/finance/stock-exchanges-and-clearinghouses) — the block is printed and then cleared and settled through the same plumbing as any trade. Note that an ABB or block trade can be either primary (new shares, company gets the cash) or secondary (a holder selling, they get the cash); the *structure* is about speed, the *primary/secondary* label is about who sells.

## At-the-market (ATM) programs: the slow drip

Everything so far has been a discrete event — a deal you announce, price, and close. An **at-the-market (ATM) program** is the opposite: a *standing facility* that lets a company sell new shares directly into the open market, gradually, at prevailing prices, whenever it chooses, over months.

The mechanics are simple and clever. The company files a shelf registration and hires a bank as sales agent. Then, on days of its choosing, the company tells the agent to sell some shares — say, up to 10% of that day's trading volume — into the regular order flow on the exchange. The buyers are just whoever happens to be buying the stock that day; they have no idea (and need not be told in advance) that some of their purchase is freshly issued stock. Because the shares sell *at the market price* rather than at a negotiated discount, there is essentially no discount to concede — the great advantage of the ATM.

The trade-offs are real, though. An ATM is slow: you cannot raise a large sum quickly without becoming a visible, persistent seller that weighs on the price. It works best for raising modest amounts opportunistically — topping up the balance sheet when the stock is strong, funding ongoing capital needs, or letting a company "average in" its issuance rather than betting everything on a single deal's pricing. Biotech and energy companies, which have lumpy, ongoing cash needs and volatile stock prices, are heavy ATM users. The dilution is the same per share as any primary issue, but it arrives in a thin, continuous trickle rather than a single jolt, which is gentler on the share price and on existing holders' nerves.

There is a behavioral edge to the ATM that CFOs prize: it lets a company sell *into strength*. When the stock spikes on good news, the company can quietly lift its ATM selling that day and capture the high price; when the stock is weak, it pauses. Over a year this can produce a meaningfully better average issuance price than a single deal struck on whatever day the company happened to need the money. The risk is the opposite temptation — a company that keeps an ATM running indefinitely can become a structural overhang, a known seller whose presence caps the stock, because every uptick is met by company supply. Disclosure rules require companies to report ATM sales, so the market eventually sees the issuance in the share-count line of the next filing; the trickle is quiet day to day but not secret over a quarter. The honest framing is that an ATM is a faucet, not a fire hose: superb for steady, opportunistic top-ups, useless for a sudden large need.

#### Worked example: raising \$50M through an ATM without a discount

A company's stock trades around \$20 and turns over about 1 million shares a day. It wants to raise \$50 million but does not want to spook the market with a big announced deal. It runs an ATM, selling into roughly 8% of daily volume — about 80,000 shares a day — at the prevailing price near \$20.

Daily proceeds ≈ 80,000 × \$20 = \$1,600,000. To raise \$50 million it needs about \$50,000,000 ÷ \$1,600,000 ≈ 31 trading days of selling — call it six to seven weeks of light, opportunistic issuance.

Total new shares ≈ \$50,000,000 ÷ \$20 = 2,500,000. Against, say, 50 million shares outstanding, that is 5% dilution — but spread over weeks and sold at the full market price, so there is no negotiated discount to give away. **The intuition:** an ATM trades speed for price, dribbling stock into existing liquidity so the company captures the full market price and avoids the announcement shock, at the cost of not being able to raise a large sum in a hurry.

## Private placements and PIPEs: capital from a chosen few

Public offerings are slow because they require a registered prospectus and a broad solicitation. A **private placement** skips much of that by selling securities to a *small number of sophisticated investors* in a transaction that is exempt from full public registration. In the US the workhorse exemption is **Regulation D** (often combined with sales to "accredited investors" and "qualified institutional buyers"); other jurisdictions have analogous private-offer carve-outs. Because the offering is private, it can be arranged in days rather than weeks, with far less disclosure overhead — the trade-off being that the buyers are restricted and the shares they receive are typically *restricted stock* that cannot be freely resold for a holding period.

The most common flavor for a *listed* company is the **PIPE** — a **P**rivate **I**nvestment in **P**ublic **E**quity. A PIPE is a private placement of a public company's shares (or convertible securities) to one or a handful of investors — a hedge fund, a private-equity firm, a strategic partner, a sovereign fund. The company gets a large check fast and with certainty; the investor gets a meaningful stake, usually at a discount to the market price, and often with extra rights (board seats, anti-dilution protection, registration rights so they can eventually sell). The classic use case is a company that needs capital quickly and either cannot wait for a public deal or wants a single anchor investor's commitment and validation.

The discount on a PIPE is typically larger than on a marketed follow-on — often 10–20% — because the investor is buying illiquid restricted stock, providing certainty, and frequently negotiating from a position of strength (the company needs the money). That discount is the cost of speed, certainty, and a single committed buyer, just as it is everywhere else on this menu — the size of the discount tracks how badly the issuer needs to transfer the risk and how illiquid the buyer's position will be.

PIPEs come in flavors that shade from benign to predatory. A **traditional (fixed-price) PIPE** sells shares at a set price; everyone knows the dilution up front. A **structured PIPE** uses convertible preferred shares or convertible notes whose conversion price *floats* — it resets lower if the stock falls. These "death-spiral" structures earned their grim nickname because a falling stock triggers conversion at ever-lower prices, issuing ever more shares, which pushes the stock lower still, in a self-reinforcing collapse. A retail investor seeing a small, struggling company announce a structured PIPE should read it as a serious warning sign, not a capital-raising success. The structure itself encodes how much leverage the company had: a healthy firm raises through a clean fixed-price deal, while a desperate one accepts toxic terms because no one else will fund it.

The flip side is the **anchor PIPE**, where a respected long-term investor — a sovereign fund, a blue-chip private-equity firm, a strategic industry partner — takes a large stake at a modest discount with a multi-year lockup. Here the discount buys not just capital but *validation*: the market often reads "Berkshire is putting in \$5 billion" or "the national pension fund took 10%" as a credibility signal that outweighs the dilution, and the stock can actually rise on the news. Same instrument, opposite message — which is the recurring lesson of this whole post.

#### Worked example: a death-spiral reset

A small company raises \$10 million through a structured PIPE: convertible notes that convert at 80% of the lowest closing price over the prior 20 days. When the deal is struck the stock is \$2.00, so the conversion price is 0.80 × \$2.00 = \$1.60, implying \$10,000,000 ÷ \$1.60 = 6.25 million shares if converted now.

Suppose bad news drops the stock to \$1.00. The conversion price resets to 0.80 × \$1.00 = \$0.80, so the same \$10 million now converts into \$10,000,000 ÷ \$0.80 = 12.5 million shares — double the dilution. As those shares hit the market the price sags further, resetting the conversion price lower again, issuing still more shares. **The intuition:** a floating-conversion PIPE transfers the downside risk of the stock onto existing holders through escalating dilution, which is exactly why a clean fixed-price raise signals strength and a death-spiral structure signals a company out of better options.

#### Worked example: a PIPE priced at a 12% discount

A listed company's stock trades at \$15.00. It needs \$120 million quickly and arranges a PIPE with a single private-equity fund. The shares are placed at a 12% discount: \$15.00 × (1 − 0.12) = \$13.20 per share.

Shares issued = \$120,000,000 ÷ \$13.20 ≈ 9,090,909 shares. Against 60 million shares previously outstanding, the new investor ends up owning 9.09M ÷ (60M + 9.09M) ≈ 13.2% of the company — instantly a major shareholder, often with a board seat.

The \$1.80-per-share discount (\$15.00 − \$13.20) is, in effect, a \$16.4 million transfer of value (9.09M × \$1.80) from existing holders to the new investor, in exchange for \$120 million of certain capital delivered in days. **The intuition:** a PIPE buys speed and certainty with a chunky discount and a concentrated new owner, which is why the market often reads a PIPE as a sign the company could not or would not raise money the cheaper, slower public way.

## Convertible bonds: borrowing now, equity maybe later

The last item on the menu is a hybrid that lives on the border between debt and equity: the **convertible bond**. (We will not re-derive bond pricing here — for the mechanics of how bonds are priced and how yields work, see [the yield curve explained](/blog/trading/fixed-income/the-yield-curve-explained-the-most-important-chart-in-finance). Our concern is the *issuance* angle: why a listed company would raise capital this way.)

A convertible bond is a regular corporate bond — it pays a coupon and promises to repay principal at maturity — *plus* an embedded option for the holder to convert it into a fixed number of the issuer's shares at a preset "conversion price." If the stock rises above the conversion price, the bondholder converts and captures the upside as equity; if the stock languishes, they hold the bond, collect coupons, and get their principal back. For the holder it is a bond with an equity kicker — downside protection plus upside participation.

For the *issuer*, a convertible is attractive for two reasons. First, the embedded option is valuable to buyers, so they accept a *lower coupon* than they would on a plain bond — the company borrows more cheaply. Second, the conversion price is set *above* today's stock price (a "conversion premium," often 25–40% over the current price), so if conversion ever happens, the company effectively sold equity at a price well above today's — a kind of deferred, premium-priced follow-on. The catch is dilution: if the stock soars and bonds convert, new shares appear and existing holders are diluted, just as in any equity raise — only later, and only if the company did well. That is why a convertible is often called "quasi-equity."

This sits beside the much larger debt-issuance machine. A convertible borrows the *form* of a bond but is reaching for the *function* of equity. The relative scale is worth keeping in mind: corporate equity follow-ons are tiny next to the bond market a company can also tap.

#### Worked example: the conversion math on a convertible bond

A company with stock at \$40 issues \$300 million of convertible bonds with a 2% coupon and a 30% conversion premium. The conversion price is \$40 × 1.30 = \$52. Each \$1,000 bond converts into \$1,000 ÷ \$52 ≈ 19.23 shares.

Compare that to a plain bond at, say, a 6% coupon. On \$300 million, the convertible saves (6% − 2%) × \$300M = \$12 million in interest a year — the value of the option the company handed buyers.

If the stock never reaches \$52, no one converts, the company repays \$300 million at maturity, and no shares are created — it was just cheap debt. If the stock rises to \$70, holders convert: the \$300M ÷ \$1,000 = 300,000 bonds become 300,000 × 19.23 ≈ 5.77 million shares. The company "sold" equity at \$52 — well above the \$40 it traded at when the deal was struck. **The intuition:** a convertible lets a company borrow cheaply today and, only if things go well, turn that debt into equity at a premium price — paying for the cheap coupon with dilution that arrives exclusively in the happy scenario.

## The signal: why the stock usually drops on the news

Now the most counter-intuitive thing about raising equity, and the thread that runs through the whole menu. When a company announces it is issuing new shares, the stock price usually *falls* a few percent — not because of the future dilution (the market can do that arithmetic instantly), but because of what the decision *signals* about management's private view.

The logic, formalized decades ago as "pecking-order" and "adverse-selection" theory, runs like this. Management knows more about the company's true value than outside investors do. A rational management team prefers to issue new shares when it believes they are *overvalued* — selling something dear is good business — and avoids issuing when shares are *cheap* (it would rather borrow, or buy back, than sell a bargain). Investors know that management thinks this way. So the very act of choosing to issue equity is read as evidence that management considers the stock at least fairly valued, probably dear. The market promptly marks the price down to reflect that inferred view. Empirically, US seasoned equity offerings tend to be associated with an announcement-day drop on the order of 2–3%, with bigger drops for deals that look like distress financing.

This is why the *structure* of a raise matters as much as the amount. A rights issue offered pro-rata to existing holders sends a weaker negative signal than a discounted placement to outsiders, because management is asking its *current* owners to put in more — a vote of confidence, not an exit. A raise explicitly earmarked for a named, value-creating acquisition reads better than a vague "general corporate purposes." A PIPE to a respected strategic investor can even read *positively* — a smart-money endorsement — despite the discount. The same dollars raised can move the stock up or down depending on what the chosen tool signals about why the company needs the money and what management thinks it is worth.

The mirror image makes the signal vivid. A **buyback** — a company using cash to repurchase its own shares — is the exact opposite transaction, and the market reads it the opposite way. Management buys back stock when it believes the shares are *cheap*; doing so concentrates ownership and lifts per-share metrics, and it signals confidence. So a company that issues equity is telling you "we think these shares are at least fairly priced, and we would rather have cash than hold them at this level," while a company buying back is telling you "we think these shares are cheap and we would rather own more of them than hold cash." A management team that issues equity high and buys it back low is doing right by long-term holders; one that does the reverse — issuing when desperate and cheap, buying back at the top with borrowed money — is quietly destroying value. Watching the *direction* of a company's share-count over years is one of the cleanest reads on whether management treats the stock as a currency to mint when dear or a bargain to hoard when cheap.

There is one important caveat to the gloomy default. The signalling penalty is sharpest for *unexpected, discretionary* raises by mature companies. For firms whose whole model is to raise and deploy capital — REITs, growth-stage biotechs, certain financials — regular issuance is *expected*, priced in, and barely moves the stock, because the market already models continuous equity funding as part of the business. The negative signal comes from *surprise* and from *implied distress*, not from issuance per se. A growth company that has told investors for years "we will fund expansion with periodic equity raises" pays little penalty for doing exactly that; a stable, cash-generative company that suddenly announces a discounted placement pays a steep one, because the surprise itself is the information.

## Common misconceptions

**"A follow-on always dilutes existing shareholders."** Only a *primary* follow-on (new shares) dilutes. A *secondary* follow-on moves existing shares from one investor to another — the share count is unchanged and no one is diluted; the cash simply goes to the selling holder, not the company. And a *rights issue*, even though it creates new shares, does not dilute holders who take up their rights, because they buy the new shares pro-rata themselves.

**"The ex-rights price drop is a loss."** No. When a stock falls from \$10 to \$9.60 on the ex-rights date, that \$0.40 is pure arithmetic — the blended price of old shares at \$10 and new shares at \$8. A holder who exercises their rights ends up with exactly the value they had plus the cash they put in. The only way to actually lose value in a rights issue is to ignore a *non-renounceable* right, letting it lapse for nothing.

**"Issuing at a discount means the company is being ripped off."** The discount is the *price of certainty and speed*, not a giveaway. An overnight ABB clears 5% below the close because institutions are absorbing a large block in hours with no diligence window; a PIPE discount of 12% pays a single investor for taking restricted, illiquid stock fast. The alternative — dribbling the same stock into the market over weeks — risks a worse average price as everyone front-runs the visible seller. The discount buys away that risk.

**"A convertible bond is just cheap debt."** It is cheap debt *until the stock rises past the conversion price*, at which point it becomes equity sold at a premium — and dilutes existing holders. The low coupon is not a free lunch; the company paid for it with an option that costs it shares precisely in the scenario where it succeeded. The "saving" on interest is the price the holder paid for the upside.

**"Big follow-ons are rare; the IPO is the main event."** In aggregate, listed companies raise far more through follow-ons, rights issues, ATMs, and converts over their lives than they ever raised at IPO. The IPO is the *first* and most visible primary-market event, but the secondary market's continuous pricing makes every subsequent raise faster and cheaper — which is exactly why companies keep coming back to the well.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2020 capital-raising wave.** When the pandemic hit, the speed of the follow-on machine was on full display. Cruise lines, airlines, and hotel chains — businesses with revenue near zero and bills still due — raised tens of billions through marketed follow-ons, overnight ABBs, PIPEs, and convertibles within weeks. Carnival's roughly \$10 billion across multiple instruments is the headline case, but the broader point is structural: these firms survived because they were *already listed*, with a public price and a liquid market that let banks place huge slugs of new equity and quasi-equity almost overnight. A private company of the same size, with no public market, would have faced a far slower, costlier scramble — or bankruptcy. The secondary market was the difference between survival and collapse.

**The European rights-issue tradition.** When large European banks needed to rebuild capital after the 2008 crisis and again during the eurozone stresses of 2011–2012, the standard tool was the deeply discounted rights issue. UK and continental company law gives shareholders pre-emption rights, so banks raised tens of billions by offering new shares pro-rata to existing holders, often at 30–40% discounts to guarantee take-up. The deep discounts looked alarming in headlines, but participating shareholders were largely protected by the pro-rata structure and the TERP arithmetic — the discount was a device to make the issue succeed, not a transfer away from holders who acted.

**The ABB as the institutional workhorse.** Day to day, the most common large equity transaction in developed markets is not the IPO but the overnight block — a private-equity sponsor selling down a post-IPO stake, a government privatizing a tranche of a state holding, a corporate selling a cross-holding. These print after the close at a few percent discount, clear before the open, and rarely make the front page. They are the routine machinery of moving large equity stakes, and they exist only because the secondary market is deep enough to quote a price and absorb the supply overnight. A familiar pattern: when a venture-backed company IPOs, its early investors are locked up for ~180 days; the day the lockup expires, those holders frequently exit not by dribbling into the market but through a single negotiated block or ABB, taking a few percent discount in exchange for a clean, certain exit of a stake too large to sell any other way.

**The convertible boom of low-rate eras.** Convertible issuance surges when interest rates are low and stock volatility is high — exactly the conditions of 2020–2021. With borrowing costs near zero and shares swinging wildly, the embedded equity option in a convertible was worth a great deal to buyers, so issuers could sell converts at coupons near 0% while setting conversion premiums of 40% or more. Companies as different as electric-vehicle makers, cloud-software firms, and the same battered travel operators raised tens of billions this way: near-free money today, equity sold at a premium tomorrow *only if* the stock ran. When rates rose sharply in 2022–2023, the math flipped — the option was worth less and straight debt got expensive — and many of those convertibles became refinancing headaches. The episode is a clean illustration that the *choice* among menu items is driven by the macro backdrop as much as by the company: the same firm reaches for converts when rates are low and volatility high, and for plain equity or debt when they are not. (How rates set the backdrop is the domain of [the yield curve](/blog/trading/fixed-income/the-yield-curve-explained-the-most-important-chart-in-finance) and macro policy, which we link out to rather than re-derive.)

**Vietnam and pre-emption rights.** In Vietnam's market, rights issues (*phát hành cho cổ đông hiện hữu* — issuance to existing shareholders) are a dominant way listed firms raise equity, because the legal framework leans on pre-emption. Banks and real-estate developers in particular have used large rights issues to fund growth and meet capital requirements. The wrinkle is foreign ownership: when foreign holders hit a company's ownership cap, their ability to take up rights can be constrained, which interacts with the dilution math in ways the local rules try to manage — a topic the companion post on [foreign flows and ownership limits](/blog/trading/capital-markets/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds) takes up directly. For a beginner, the lesson is that the *same* tool — a rights issue — behaves differently depending on a market's legal defaults and its rules on who is allowed to buy.

## The takeaway: the IPO opens a door, not a checkout

Step back and the menu collapses into one idea. The IPO is romanticized as the moment a company "raises capital," but it is better understood as the moment a company *acquires a public price and a liquid market for its shares*. That acquisition is what makes every later raise — the follow-on, the rights issue, the overnight block, the ATM drip, the PIPE, the convertible — fast, cheap, and routine. The secondary market is not a sideshow to the primary market; it is the engine that makes repeat issuance possible, exactly as this series' spine insists: **secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible.**

So when you read that a company "raised \$300 million," train yourself to ask the three questions that decode the deal. *Primary or secondary* — did the company get the cash, or did an insider cash out? *How dilutive* — new shares (and were existing holders offered them first, via rights), or just a transfer of old ones? *And how big a discount* — which tells you how badly the company needed certainty and speed, and therefore how the market is likely to read the raise. The discount and the structure are not footnotes; they are the message. A pro-rata rights issue to fund a clear project and a deep-discount PIPE to plug a balance-sheet hole both "raise equity," but they tell you opposite stories about what management believes and how much trouble the company is in.

That is the real skill here. The arithmetic of dilution and TERP is mechanical — you can do it on a napkin. The judgment is in reading what the *choice of tool* reveals. A company that can pick from this whole menu is a company the capital market has decided to keep funding; the particular door it walks through tells you what it thinks of its own shares, and what the market thinks of its story. Follow the cash, count the shares, and read the discount as the signal it is.
