> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An emerging-market upgrade is not a prize a country wins for being attractive; it is the global market formally certifying that a country has built enough of the machine — the settlement plumbing, the foreign access, the trustworthy rules — to be safely handed serious institutional capital.
>
> - Index providers (FTSE Russell and MSCI) sort the world's stock markets into **Frontier → Secondary Emerging → Advanced Emerging → Developed**. A reclassification *up* forces every passive fund tracking the higher index to buy that market — by rule, not by choice.
> - The prize is two things at once: a one-time wave of mechanical inflows (commonly cited at **roughly \$5–8 billion** of combined passive and active money for Vietnam) and a permanent **lower cost of capital** for the country's firms.
> - The criteria are all about *plumbing and access*, not about the economy being good: settlement without pre-funding (delivery-versus-payment), a real clearing counterparty, foreign-ownership room, easy foreign registration, English disclosure, and a convertible currency.
> - Vietnam has been on FTSE's watchlist for **Secondary Emerging since 2018**; the **September 2024 non-pre-funding reform (Circular 68)** and the **KRX trading-system go-live** were the key unlocks. The number to remember: a market that *passes the checklist* is one the world has agreed to fund cheaply.

On the morning a market gets upgraded, nothing about its companies changes. The factories still make the same shoes, the banks hold the same loans, the property developers owe the same debts. And yet, within months, billions of dollars of foreign money pour in, share prices jump, and Vietnamese companies suddenly find it cheaper to raise capital. What changed was not the economy. What changed was a *label* — and the label, it turns out, is one of the most powerful pieces of plumbing in global finance.

That label is a **market classification**. A handful of index companies — most importantly FTSE Russell and MSCI — sit in London and New York and decide which "tier" each country's stock market belongs to. Their verdict moves more money than almost any single decision a finance minister can make, because trillions of dollars are managed by funds that are *contractually required* to mirror those indices. When a country moves up a tier, those funds have no discretion: they must buy. The classification is the bridge between a country's domestic plumbing and the world's largest pools of savings.

This post is about that bridge — and specifically about the long campaign Vietnam has run to cross it. We will see what the tiers mean, why an upgrade triggers mechanical buying, exactly what the checklist demands, where Vietnam stands after its 2024 reforms, what is still broken, and why the whole thing can disappoint even when it "works." Throughout, hold onto the spine of this series: a capital market is a machine that turns savings into long-term investment, and an upgrade is the global market certifying that the machine is finally trustworthy enough to fund.

## Foundations: what a market classification actually is

Start with the everyday version. Suppose you run a careful, slow-moving pension fund in the Netherlands managing the retirement savings of half a million teachers. You want some of that money to grow in fast-developing Asian economies — but you cannot personally vet every stock exchange in the world. So you outsource the judgment. You write into your fund's rulebook: *"We invest in line with the FTSE Emerging Index."* From that day, you don't decide which countries are "emerging." FTSE decides. If FTSE says a country is emerging, your money is allowed there. If FTSE says it is merely "frontier," your money stays out, because your rulebook forbids it.

Multiply that one pension fund by thousands of institutions — sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies, university endowments, exchange-traded funds (ETFs) sold to ordinary savers — and you get the real picture. An **index** is just a defined list of stocks with defined weights. An **index provider** is the company that maintains that list. The two that matter for country classification are **FTSE Russell** (owned by the London Stock Exchange Group) and **MSCI** (Morgan Stanley Capital International). A **classification** is the tier they assign a whole country's market to. The reason this matters so enormously is the rise of **passive investing**: funds that don't try to pick winners but simply hold whatever is in the index, in the index's proportions, automatically.

### The four rungs of the ladder

Both providers use broadly the same ladder, with slightly different names. From the bottom:

- **Frontier** — the smallest, least liquid, hardest-to-access markets. Real economies, real companies, but the plumbing is immature: it may be hard for a foreigner to open an account, hard to get money out, slow or risky to settle a trade. Vietnam currently sits here in FTSE's classification.
- **Secondary Emerging** (FTSE) — the rung Vietnam is trying to reach. Markets that are investable for big institutions but still carry more access friction than the top tier. This is where the *large* emerging-market money lives.
- **Advanced Emerging** (FTSE) — larger, deeper emerging markets like Brazil, Malaysia, or Taiwan that clear a higher bar on access and infrastructure.
- **Developed** — the United States, Japan, the United Kingdom, the eurozone, Australia. Deep, liquid, fully accessible, with mature settlement and rule of law.

MSCI uses **Frontier → Emerging → Developed** (it folds FTSE's two emerging rungs into one). The exact taxonomy differs, but the principle is identical: you climb the ladder by *building the machine*, not by growing the economy. A country can be richer and faster-growing than its neighbour and still rank lower, purely because its settlement system is clunkier or its foreign-ownership rules are tighter. **The ladder grades plumbing, not GDP.** That is the single most important and most counterintuitive fact in this entire subject.

Why do the providers split markets at all, rather than running one global index of every listed company on earth? Because the buyers they serve have wildly different risk appetites. A conservative European insurer is allowed by its own regulator to hold only "investable" markets where money can be moved freely and trades settle reliably; a specialist frontier-markets fund is *paid* to hunt in the riskier, less accessible corners. The tiers exist to let each kind of capital find its matching risk level. A market's classification is therefore a statement about *what kind of money is allowed to come*. Frontier money is small, adventurous, and patient. Emerging money is vast, rule-bound, and demanding about access. Developed money is the largest and most patient of all, but it insists on near-perfect plumbing. Climbing the ladder, then, is not about prestige — it is about unlocking a progressively larger and more patient class of investor, each of which has its own non-negotiable requirements for trusting your market with its savings.

The flip side of "the ladder grades plumbing" is that fixing plumbing is *slow and structural*, not a matter of policy announcements. You can cut interest rates overnight; you cannot conjure a central counterparty, a freely convertible currency, or a deep domestic investor base by decree. This is why upgrade campaigns take years — Vietnam's FTSE watchlist stretch ran from 2018 well into the mid-2020s — and why the providers survey the actual users rather than reading the rulebook. A reform that looks complete on paper but breaks at the settlement desk simply does not count, no matter how it is described in a press release.

#### Worked example: why the label, not the economy, moves the money

Consider two countries. Country A grows its economy at 7% a year, its companies are wildly profitable, but a foreigner who wants to buy a share must wire the full purchase amount into the country *days before* placing the order, and getting the money back out requires regulatory sign-off. Country B grows at 3%, dull and mature, but a foreigner can open an account in a day, trade instantly, and repatriate funds the same week.

A global passive EM fund managing \$50 billion does not get to prefer Country A's growth. Its rulebook says "hold the index." If the index provider classifies Country A as *frontier* because of the pre-funding friction, then **\$0** of that \$50 billion flows to Country A through the EM fund — regardless of the 7% growth. If Country B is *emerging* at a 1.5% index weight, the fund mechanically holds \$50bn × 1.5% = **\$750 million** of Country B. The intuition: in a passive world, the classification *is* the access valve, and growth without the right label buys you nothing from the largest investors.

## Why an upgrade triggers mechanical buying

Here is the mechanism that turns a label into a flood of money. A passive fund's entire job is to *track* its index with minimal deviation — what the industry calls minimising **tracking error**, the gap between the fund's returns and the index's returns. The fund manager is not paid to have opinions. They are paid to mirror the index precisely and cheaply.

So when an index provider announces that a new country will be *added* to the Emerging Markets index — say, at a 0.5% weight — every fund that tracks that index now has a problem. On the day the change takes effect (the **effective date**, often six months to a year after the announcement), the index will contain Vietnam at 0.5%. If the fund holds 0% Vietnam on that day, its tracking error explodes. To avoid that, the fund must buy enough Vietnamese stock to reach 0.5% of its assets — and it must do so *around* the effective date, because that is when the benchmark itself changes.

This is the **index effect**, and it is purely mechanical. The fund manager is not betting on Vietnam. They are forced buyers, executing a rule. Multiply across every EM-tracking fund in the world and the forced buying runs to billions of dollars concentrated into a short window.

![How an index upgrade converts a country weight into forced passive buying](/imgs/blogs/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix-7.png)

#### Worked example: sizing the mechanical inflow

Let us put rough numbers on it. Suppose the total pool of money benchmarked to the major emerging-market indices (FTSE Emerging and MSCI EM, across passive and benchmark-aware active funds) is on the order of **\$1.5 trillion** dedicated to passive tracking, plus a larger active pool that watches the same benchmark. Assume Vietnam, on inclusion, receives an index weight of about **0.5%**.

The pure passive math is straightforward: \$1.5 trillion × 0.5% = **\$7.5 billion** of stock that passive funds *must* buy, simply to stop deviating from the benchmark. In practice the number people cite for Vietnam is **roughly \$5–8 billion** of combined passive and active flows, because not all active managers move at once and weights get phased in. Even taking the conservative \$5 billion end: against a Vietnamese market where foreign investors in a *whole year* have net-sold on the order of 90 trillion VND (about \$3.6 billion), an incoming \$5 billion is enormous. The intuition: the upgrade does not invite foreigners to consider Vietnam — it *orders* a specific, rule-bound subset of them to buy it.

There is a subtlety worth dwelling on: the size of the forced inflow depends on *two* numbers multiplied together — the size of the money pool tracking the index, and the country's weight within it. Vietnam's weight, in turn, depends on its total investable market value once foreign-ownership room is accounted for. This is why the unfixed criteria are not separable from the prize: a market with tight ownership caps gets a *smaller* index weight, because the index can only count the shares foreigners are actually able to buy. Two countries of identical total market size can receive very different inflows if one has freely available foreign room and the other does not. The checklist is not just a gate you pass or fail — passing it more fully literally increases the cheque the world writes you.

That forced demand is the front half of the prize. The back half is quieter and more lasting.

### The deeper prize: a lower cost of capital

When a permanent new class of buyers is structurally obligated to hold your market, the price of your companies' shares is supported by a thicker, more diversified base of demand. Higher and steadier share prices mean a lower **cost of equity** — the return investors demand to hold a company's stock. A lower cost of equity directly lowers the company's **cost of capital**, the blended price it pays to fund itself through both equity and debt. (We don't re-derive cost of capital here — the [equity-research series](/blog/trading/equity-research) owns valuation mechanics; we own the market-structure cause.)

This is the spine of the whole series made concrete. The capital market's entire purpose is to channel savings into long-term investment as cheaply as possible. An upgrade widens the pipe: it connects Vietnamese firms to the deepest pools of patient global savings, and in doing so lowers the toll those firms pay to build factories, fund expansions, and finance growth. The label is the certification; the cheaper capital is the reward the certification unlocks.

#### Worked example: the cost-of-capital drop for an issuer

Take a Vietnamese company planning to raise capital to build a new plant. Before the upgrade, its limited, frontier-tier investor base demands a cost of equity of, say, **15%** — a high hurdle reflecting illiquidity and access risk. The company can only fund projects expected to return more than 15%.

After the upgrade, with global EM funds now structural holders and the access-risk premium compressed, suppose the company's cost of equity falls to **12.5%**. On a planned \$200 million equity raise, that 2.5-percentage-point drop is worth \$200m × 2.5% = **\$5 million per year** in reduced return that investors require — money that now stays with the project rather than being demanded by capital providers. More importantly, projects returning between 12.5% and 15% that were previously *un-fundable* suddenly clear the hurdle. The intuition: the upgrade doesn't just hand companies a one-time price pop, it permanently lowers the bar a project must clear to be worth building — which is the real economic point of a capital market.

## The criteria: what the checklist actually demands

If the prize is so large, why doesn't every government just declare itself emerging? Because the index providers don't take a country's word for it. They run a **checklist** of accessibility criteria, and crucially they *survey the people who would have to invest* — the global asset managers — asking whether each criterion is genuinely met in practice, not just on paper. A reform that exists in a circular but doesn't work at the trading desk does not count.

The criteria cluster into a few buckets, and almost every one maps to a piece of plumbing we have built up across this Vietnam track.

![The upgrade scorecard listing each criterion and Vietnam's status in 2025](/imgs/blogs/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix-3.png)

**Settlement cycle and the pre-funding problem.** When you buy a stock, there is a gap between agreeing the trade and actually exchanging cash for shares — the **settlement cycle**, written as T+2 (two business days after the trade). The gold standard is **delivery-versus-payment (DvP)**: the shares and the cash move *simultaneously* at settlement, so neither side can be cheated. Vietnam's historical problem was **pre-funding**: a foreign investor had to have the full cash sitting in the account *before* the buy order could even be placed. That meant wiring millions of dollars into Vietnam, leaving it idle (earning nothing, exposed to currency moves), just to be allowed to trade. For a global fund running tight cash management, this is a deal-breaker. Removing pre-funding — letting a broker accept the order and arrange settlement on a proper DvP basis — was Vietnam's single biggest blocker. (The clearing and settlement mechanics live in [Clearing and settlement in Vietnam: VSDC and the KRX system](/blog/trading/capital-markets/clearing-and-settlement-in-vietnam-vsdc-and-the-krx-system).)

**A real clearing counterparty (CCP).** A **central counterparty** is an institution that stands in the middle of every trade, becoming the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer, so that if your trade partner defaults, the CCP still makes you whole. It is the trust backstop of a modern market. A genuine CCP lets brokers and custodians extend credit and settle on DvP without each party bearing the other's default risk. Vietnam's move toward a CCP under VSDC, riding on the new KRX system, is the structural fix that makes non-pre-funded settlement durable rather than a temporary workaround.

**Foreign-ownership limits and available room.** Many Vietnamese companies cap the share of stock foreigners may collectively own — the **foreign-ownership limit (FOL)**, often 49% or lower, and in sensitive sectors like banking far tighter. Once foreigners hit the cap on a desirable stock, no more can buy at the listed price; the shares trade at a premium in off-market deals. For an index provider this is a problem: if a passive fund is *required* to buy a stock but there is *no room*, the index is uninvestable in practice. The room — and the workarounds for it — are the subject of [Foreign flows and ownership limits: the room and the workarounds](/blog/trading/capital-markets/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds).

**Foreign-access registration.** Before a foreigner can trade at all, they must register for a securities trading code and open the right accounts. If that process is slow, opaque, or requires physical presence, it is friction the index providers count against the market. Vietnam has streamlined this materially.

**Market information in English.** Global investors must be able to read company disclosures, exchange rules, and regulatory filings in English. A market where the critical information is only in the local language is, for a foreign allocator, effectively dark. Vietnam's English disclosure is improving but still partial — major firms publish in English, smaller ones often do not.

**Currency convertibility.** Finally, the money has to be able to get *out*. If a foreign investor cannot reliably convert their Vietnamese dong proceeds back into dollars and repatriate them, the whole proposition fails. The dong is not freely convertible the way the dollar or yen is; this is a slower, structural constraint tied to monetary policy and the central bank, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV). For a *Secondary Emerging* classification the providers are somewhat tolerant of a managed currency — many emerging markets run them — but it remains a sharper hurdle for the *Developed* tier and a permanent point of friction that global funds price in.

The deeper logic tying all six criteria together is the round trip. A foreign investor's money has to make a complete journey: register and open an account, convert dollars to dong, place an order, have it settle safely, hold the position with assured ownership rights, then sell, convert back, and repatriate. The classification asks one question at every step of that journey — *can the money move freely and safely here?* A single broken step poisons the whole trip, which is why a market can pass five criteria and still fail to attract the capital: a fund will not begin a journey it cannot finish. This is also why the providers weight the criteria by how badly each one blocks the round trip. Pre-funding and convertibility sit near the top precisely because they block the *entry* and *exit* — the two points where money is most exposed.

#### Worked example: a criterion-by-criterion scorecard with the pre-funding fix

Score Vietnam against FTSE's Secondary Emerging checklist as of 2025, marking each pass or fail:

- Settlement / DvP without pre-funding — **PASS** (fixed by Circular 68, September 2024)
- Foreign-access registration — **PASS** (streamlined trading-code process)
- Clearing / movement toward CCP — **IN PROGRESS** (KRX go-live; CCP build underway)
- Foreign-ownership room on key names — **PARTIAL / FAIL** (FOL still binds on banks and other favourites)
- English disclosure — **PARTIAL** (large caps yes, broad market no)
- Currency convertibility — **FAIL by developed-market standards** (restricted dong)

The single highest-value fix was the pre-funding one. Before September 2024, a foreign fund wanting to buy \$10 million of a Vietnamese stock had to wire that \$10 million in advance and let it sit idle; at a 5% annual opportunity cost, just *two weeks* of idle cash on a steady program of trades is real money — on \$10m that is \$10,000,000 × 5% × (14 / 365) ≈ **\$19,200** of dead carry per trade-cycle, every cycle. Removing pre-funding eliminates that entirely. The intuition: most of the checklist is about removing exactly this kind of friction tax that makes a market technically open but practically painful.

## Where Vietnam stands

Vietnam's campaign is not new. FTSE Russell added Vietnam to its **watchlist for possible reclassification to Secondary Emerging in September 2018**. A watchlist is a public statement that says, in effect: *you are close, here is what's missing, fix it and we'll promote you.* For years the answer to "what's missing?" was dominated by two items — the pre-funding requirement and the absence of a clean DvP settlement model.

### The 2024–2025 unlocks

Two developments changed the picture decisively. First, in **September 2024, the State Securities Commission issued Circular 68**, which removed the pre-funding requirement for foreign institutional investors. From late 2024, a foreign institution could place a buy order without the full cash sitting in the account first, with the securities firm bridging settlement — a genuine non-pre-funded, DvP-style model. This directly answered FTSE's headline complaint.

Second, the long-delayed **KRX trading and post-trade system went live**, modernising the exchange and clearing infrastructure operated alongside VSDC and laying the technical foundation for a true central counterparty. Where Circular 68 was the *rule* change, KRX was the *machine* change underneath it — the system that makes modern settlement and eventual CCP clearing operationally real rather than a manual patch.

By **September 2025**, with the pre-funding reform in live use and the infrastructure upgraded, the market context pointed strongly toward FTSE confirming the upgrade to Secondary Emerging — an announcement expected to be made with an effective date some months later, following the provider's standard practice of a long lead time so funds can prepare. The pattern across all index upgrades is the same: *announce now, take effect later*, precisely so the mechanical buying can be orderly rather than chaotic.

MSCI remains further off. MSCI's Emerging Markets criteria are stricter on several fronts — particularly foreign-ownership room and the equal treatment of foreign investors — and MSCI had not, by 2025, placed Vietnam on its review list for upgrade in the way FTSE had. The realistic sequence is FTSE first, MSCI later, possibly by years. This matters because the MSCI EM index is tracked by an even larger pool of money than FTSE's; the *full* prize only arrives with both.

![VN-Index level at year end from 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix-2.png)

The VN-Index's path tells the backstory. From around 546 at the end of 2014, the market roughly tripled to a peak near 1,498 at the end of 2021 on a retail-and-liquidity boom, then fell hard to about 1,007 in 2022 as global rates rose and a domestic bond-market scare hit, before recovering toward 1,267 by the end of 2024. An upgrade arriving into a market that has *already* recovered is a different animal from one arriving into a depressed one — a point we'll return to when we discuss the risk.

### The market has been getting deeper underneath the label

The classification debate can make it sound as if nothing real is changing — but underneath, the Vietnamese market has been maturing on exactly the dimensions an upgrade certifies. Consider market depth relative to the economy.

![Vietnam stock market capitalisation as a percent of GDP](/imgs/blogs/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix-4.png)

Market capitalisation as a share of GDP climbed from about **26% in 2014 to a peak near 93% in 2021**, settling around 62% by 2024. A ratio in the 60–90% range puts Vietnam squarely in the size class of an emerging, not a frontier, market — the economy genuinely outgrew the frontier label. The 2021 spike and subsequent pullback also flag the volatility that comes with a still-maturing market, which is part of why the access criteria matter so much: thin, volatile markets are exactly where institutional investors most need the protection of solid settlement and clear exit routes.

The investor base has deepened too. The institutional foundation that an upgrade plugs into rests partly on a growing domestic participation, and partly on the structural reforms that make foreign participation safe. (For how the index-inclusion flow has played out in the Vietnamese ETF context specifically, see the companion piece [Foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)

![Vietnam securities trading accounts, cumulative, in millions](/imgs/blogs/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix-8.png)

Domestic trading accounts exploded from about **2.2 million in 2018 to over 9 million in 2024** — a retail-driven deepening of the local market that gives any incoming foreign flow a thicker pool of counterparties to trade against. A deeper domestic base also makes the *index effect* less destabilising: forced foreign buying has more local supply to meet it.

### What is still unfixed

An FTSE upgrade to Secondary Emerging would be a milestone, but it is not the end of the campaign — and it certainly does not satisfy MSCI. Four items remain genuinely hard.

The first is **foreign-ownership room**. Many of the most desirable Vietnamese stocks — banks above all — sit at or near their foreign-ownership caps. When a passive fund is forced to buy a stock that has no room, it cannot get the shares at the listed price; it must either pay an off-market premium, use a substitute, or under-weight the name and accept tracking error. A common workaround is the **non-voting depositary receipt (NVDR)**, an instrument that gives a foreigner the economic exposure of a share — dividends and price moves — without the voting rights that trigger the ownership cap. Vietnam has discussed NVDR-style structures precisely to relieve this pressure, because without more usable foreign room, the *weight* Vietnam earns in the index stays smaller than its raw market size would suggest.

The second is **English disclosure**. Large-cap firms publish financials and announcements in English, but the broad market does not, and exchange and regulatory material is unevenly translated. For a global allocator running hundreds of names, a market where half the information is effectively dark is harder to invest in at scale.

The third is a **true central counterparty**. The KRX system and Circular 68 created a workable non-pre-funded settlement model, but a fully-fledged CCP — with a default fund, a clear waterfall of who absorbs losses when a member fails, and the legal certainty that comes with novation — is still being built out. Until that exists in mature form, the settlement model leans on brokers and custodians extending credit, which works but is not the developed-market gold standard.

The fourth is **currency convertibility**, the slowest of all to change because it is bound up with monetary sovereignty. A freely convertible dong would remove the last big friction on the round trip, but it is a decade-scale macroeconomic decision, not a securities-market reform. This is the criterion that most clearly separates an FTSE Secondary Emerging upgrade (achievable now) from the deeper MSCI and eventual developed-market recognition (years away).

#### Worked example: how a binding ownership cap shrinks the prize

Suppose Vietnam's total stock market is worth roughly \$250 billion, but on the names global funds most want — banks and a few blue chips making up, say, 30% of that value — foreigners are already at the cap with no room. The index can only count the *freely investable* float. If \$75 billion of the \$250 billion is effectively closed to new foreign buying, the investable market the index recognises is closer to **\$175 billion**, not \$250 billion. A weight calculated on \$175bn rather than \$250bn is about **30% smaller** — and so is the forced inflow it generates. On a hypothetical \$7.5 billion full-room inflow, the ownership cap alone could shave it toward **\$5 billion or less**. The intuition: every unfixed criterion does not just risk the upgrade — it quietly taxes the size of the reward even when the upgrade happens.

## Common misconceptions

**"An upgrade means the economy got better."** No. The economy and the classification are nearly independent. The upgrade certifies *access and plumbing* — settlement, ownership room, convertibility — not growth or profitability. A country can grow fast for a decade and stay frontier because foreigners can't easily get money in and out; another can stagnate and stay developed because its infrastructure is flawless. The label grades the machine, not the engine the machine serves.

**"The flows arrive on the announcement day."** They don't. Index providers deliberately announce months ahead of the **effective date** to give funds time to prepare. The mechanical passive buying concentrates *around the effective date*, not the announcement. Much active money, anticipating this, front-runs by buying earlier — which sets up the "buy the rumor" dynamic discussed below.

**"FTSE and MSCI are the same thing."** They use similar ladders but different rulebooks and different effective dates, and they track different (overlapping) pools of money. FTSE folds emerging into two rungs; MSCI uses one. A country can be FTSE-emerging while still MSCI-frontier — which is precisely Vietnam's likely position for some time. The full prize requires *both* providers, and they move on independent schedules.

**"Once a market hits a foreign-ownership cap, foreigners just stop and the price is fair."** Not quite. When desirable stocks hit their FOL, foreign demand spills into off-market premium deals and into instruments designed to synthesise exposure. The listed price understates true foreign demand. This is why ownership *room* — not just the existence of a cap — is what index providers actually score.

**"An upgrade is permanent."** It is not. Index providers also *downgrade* markets that let their access regress — as has happened to markets that imposed capital controls or trading restrictions during crises. The classification is a continuous certification, not a one-time graduation; a country that breaks its plumbing can be demoted, and the same mechanical funds that were forced to buy become forced sellers.

## How it shows up in real markets

### The pre-funding fix in practice

Before Circular 68, the daily reality for a foreign fund was the pre-funding tax we quantified above: cash wired in days early, sitting idle, exposed to the dong. The reform's importance is best seen as a *workflow* change at the trading desk.

![Before and after the pre-funding reform for a foreign buy order](/imgs/blogs/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix-5.png)

In the old world, a portfolio manager who wanted to act on a view had to first move money, wait for it to clear, and only then trade — by which point the opportunity might have moved. In the new world, they place the order on the strength of the broker's settlement arrangement and fund it on the standard T+2 timeline, with delivery-versus-payment protecting both sides. That is not a cosmetic change; it is the difference between a market a global fund can run a normal process in and one it cannot.

### The "buy the rumor, sell the fact" risk

Here is the trap. Because active investors *anticipate* the upgrade, much of the price gain happens during the run-up — long before the passive money is forced to arrive. By the time the effective date comes and the mechanical buyers show up, the early movers are sitting on profits and looking to sell *into* that forced demand. The result can be a market that rallies hard into the decision and then sells off right when the "guaranteed" buying materialises. Traders call this **buy the rumor, sell the fact**.

#### Worked example: a "buy the rumor" run-up in the VN-Index

Suppose the VN-Index sits at **1,200** when upgrade chatter intensifies. Anticipating the eventual forced inflows, active foreign and domestic momentum money pushes it to **1,380** over the following months — a 15% run-up "pricing in" the upgrade. The day the upgrade is confirmed, the passive flows are still six months away (effective date lag), but the early buyers now want to lock in their gain. They sell into the news. The index drops back toward **1,290** even though the *fundamental* event — the upgrade — was entirely positive.

Run the arithmetic on a fund that bought the rumor: in at 1,200, out at 1,380, a clean +15%. A fund that waited for the "certain" event and bought at 1,380 on confirmation day is down to 1,290, a **−6.5%** loss, despite being right about the upgrade. The intuition: in markets, a known good outcome is paid for *in advance*, so the certain event is often the worst possible entry point — the certification is real, but the easy money was made by those who funded the machine *before* the world agreed it was trustworthy.

### Foreign flows have been a headwind, not a tailwind

It is worth grounding the optimism. The mechanical-inflow story is about what *will* happen on inclusion. What has actually been happening is the opposite.

![Foreign net buying on HOSE by year, in trillion VND](/imgs/blogs/the-emerging-market-upgrade-what-vietnam-must-fix-6.png)

Foreigners were net *sellers* of Vietnamese stocks in most recent years — a modest net buy in 2022 (about +27 trillion VND) surrounded by heavy net selling, culminating in roughly **−90 trillion VND (about \$3.6 billion) of net selling in 2024**. This is the global backdrop: as US rates rose, money flowed *out* of emerging markets generally. The upgrade thesis is precisely that index inclusion would *reverse* this — converting a discretionary outflow into a rule-bound inflow. The contrast between the red bars of recent reality and the green forced-buying of an upgrade is the whole investment case in one chart, and also its honest caveat: the structural buyer has to actually arrive to overwhelm the cyclical seller.

There is a second, subtler risk beyond the timing game: the *quality* of the money that arrives. Forced passive buying is, by construction, price-insensitive — the fund buys because the index says to, not because the stock is cheap. Price-insensitive demand pushes valuations up regardless of fundamentals, which can leave a freshly-upgraded market trading richer than its earnings justify. And the same flows reverse mechanically on a downgrade or a re-weighting. A market that grows dependent on index flows for support is, in a sense, renting demand rather than earning it. The durable prize is the *cost-of-capital* benefit, which rests on the plumbing being genuinely sound; the flow itself is the more fickle, double-edged part of the gift.

### The global context: every market plays this game

Vietnam is not unique. The index-inclusion dance is a standard feature of global market structure. China's domestic A-shares were phased into the MSCI EM index over several years starting in 2018, with weights stepped up gradually precisely to avoid a disorderly flood. Markets have been downgraded too when access deteriorated. The rules of the road — how cross-border access, settlement standards, and investor protections are judged — are themselves shaped by the big regulatory regimes covered in [Global market structure: Reg NMS, MiFID, and cross-border trading](/blog/trading/capital-markets/global-market-structure-reg-nms-mifid-and-cross-border). The classification system is, in a sense, the market's own informal regulator of *who gets to play with whom*.

## The takeaway: an upgrade is the world certifying the machine

Step back and the emerging-market upgrade stops looking like an arbitrary verdict from a London office and starts looking like exactly what this series says a capital market is *for*. The whole machine exists to turn savings into long-term investment as cheaply as possible. The bottleneck for a developing country is *trust*: the world's largest pools of patient savings will not fund your companies unless they are confident they can get their money in, trade it fairly, and get it back out. An upgrade is the formal, money-moving statement that this confidence has been earned.

That reframes everything Vietnam has done. Circular 68 was not bureaucratic housekeeping — it removed the single biggest reason a global fund could not run a normal process in Vietnam. The KRX system was not just an IT upgrade — it is the settlement-and-clearing backbone that makes modern, trustworthy post-trade plumbing real. The push toward a true CCP, broader English disclosure, more foreign-ownership room, and eventual currency convertibility are not boxes to tick for their own sake; each one removes a specific reason the world does not yet fully trust the machine. The reward for finishing the list is permanent: a deeper, cheaper, more stable source of funding for every Vietnamese company that needs to build something.

So when you read that an upgrade could bring "\$5–8 billion in inflows," translate it. That number is the world betting that Vietnam's machine is finally sound. The deeper number — the one that compounds for decades — is the lower cost of capital that follows, which means more factories built, more companies funded, more savings turned into investment. The label is just the certificate. The machine underneath is the point. And the unfinished items on the checklist — the binding ownership caps, the partial disclosure, the restricted dong — are not nitpicks. They are the precise list of reasons the world's certification is still conditional. Finish them, and the bridge is fully open.
