> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A market price is not announced by anyone; it emerges from the order flow as buyers and sellers reveal what they will pay and accept, and arbitrage stitches all those local prices into one consistent system.
>
> - Price discovery is aggregation: the order book pools thousands of private opinions about value and collapses them into a single tradeable number.
> - Informed traders push the price toward fundamental value; the market maker, unable to tell who is informed, learns from the *direction* of flow and widens its spread to survive — that is adverse selection.
> - Arbitrage is the enforcer of the law of one price: the same risk must trade at the same price everywhere, or someone trades the gap until it closes.
> - The efficient-market hypothesis claims prices already reflect available information; the limits-of-arbitrage literature explains why prices can still stay wrong — arbitrage costs money, carries risk, and runs out of capital.

On the morning of a hot IPO, you can watch a price get born. The night before, the deal is priced — say a banker and the company agree to sell shares at \$30. That number is a guess, dressed up with comparable companies and a demand book, but still a guess. Then the stock opens for secondary trading and something different happens. For a few chaotic seconds the exchange holds back the first trade while orders pile in on both sides. Buy orders at \$38, \$40, \$45. Sell orders from early investors happy to take \$42. The opening cross matches them and prints a number — \$41.50 — that nobody decreed. It is the price at which the most shares can change hands, the level where the crowd's eagerness to buy exactly meets its willingness to sell.

That \$41.50 was not handed down by the company, the bank, or the exchange. It was *discovered*. It is the sum of a few thousand private opinions — each trader's own view of what the company is worth, weighted by how much money stands behind that view — netted into one figure that everyone can now see on a screen and trade against. The rest of this post is about that process: where a price comes from, what moves it, what keeps prices in different places consistent with each other, and the deep question of whether the resulting number is "right".

This is a market-structure story. We will not value the company — that is the job of [equity research](/blog/trading/equity-research/the-three-financial-statements-how-they-connect), and we link out to it rather than re-derive a discounted cash flow. We will not build the formal market-making model — that lives in [quantitative finance](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/order-book-simulator-quant-research). Our job is the *machine*: how the secondary market turns dispersed information into a price, and why that matters for the whole capital-markets system.

## Foundations: what a price is, and what the secondary market is for

Start from zero. A **security** is a tradeable claim — a share of a company's future profits, a bond that promises fixed payments. A **capital market** is the machine that turns savings into long-term investment. It runs on two engines. The **primary market** *creates* securities: a company sells new shares or bonds to raise money it can build with. The **secondary market** *trades* those already-created securities between investors — when you buy a share of Apple, Apple gets none of your money; you buy it from another investor.

Here is the secret that makes the whole thing work, and it runs through every post in this series: **secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible.** Nobody hands a company money for a 30-year project unless they believe they can sell their claim on it tomorrow morning at a fair price. The secondary market's job is to be that escape hatch — and to do that job, it must continuously produce one thing: a *trustworthy price*. This post is about how it produces that price.

A **price**, in market terms, is simply the level at which a buyer and a seller agree to transact right now. But "the price" of a stock that trades all day is really a stream of agreements, each one a fresh negotiation. The mechanism that hosts those negotiations is the **order book** — a continuously updated list of every standing offer to buy (a **bid**) and to sell (an **offer** or **ask**), sorted by price. The highest bid and lowest offer are the **best bid and offer**; the gap between them is the **bid-ask spread**. We covered the machinery of the book in [inside an exchange: the matching engine and the order book](/blog/trading/capital-markets/inside-an-exchange-the-matching-engine-and-the-order-book) — here we care about what it *does to information*.

**Price discovery** is the name for the process by which trading reveals a price. The word "discovery" is doing real work: the price is not set, computed, or imposed; it is *uncovered* through the act of trading, the way the depth of a lake is discovered by lowering a line until it touches bottom. Every order a trader submits is a small confession: "I think this is worth at least this much" (a bid) or "at most this much" (an offer). The book collects those confessions and the matching engine finds where they cross.

Why does this matter beyond the trading floor? Because a firm's **cost of capital** — the rate of return investors demand to fund it — is read straight off these prices. A high stock price means cheap equity; a low bond yield means cheap debt. Good price discovery in the secondary market feeds directly back into the primary market, setting the terms on which real companies can raise real money to build real things. A market that discovers prices badly misallocates the economy's savings. That is the stake.

It is worth slowing down on the difference between a *price* and a *quote*, because beginners blur them. A **quote** is an offer to trade that has not yet happened: a bid says "I am willing to buy at this level", an offer says "I am willing to sell at this level". A **trade** (a "print") happens only when someone *crosses* the spread — a buyer accepts a standing offer, or a seller accepts a standing bid. "The price" you see ticking on a screen is usually the *last traded price*, the most recent print. But the *informative* state of the market is the whole book of quotes around it: the depth (how many shares are available at each level), the imbalance (more buyers or more sellers), and the spread. A last price of \$40 means very different things if there are a million shares bid right behind it versus a hundred. So when we say "the price", we really mean a rich, layered object — last trade, best bid and offer, and the depth behind both — that the market is continuously updating. The single number is just its most visible summary.

There is also a difference between a price being *quoted* and a price being *firm*. A market maker's quote is a promise to trade a stated size; beyond that size, the next available price may be worse. This is why a large order "walks the book": a fund that wants to buy 500,000 shares cannot get them all at the best offer — it lifts the offer at \$40.00 for the first 2,000 shares, then \$40.02 for the next, then \$40.05, and so on, paying progressively more as it consumes liquidity. The *effective* price it pays is an average across all those levels, well above the headline quote. That gap — between the quoted price and the price a real order actually achieves — is **market impact**, and it is one of the largest hidden costs in investing. Price discovery is not just about *where* the price is; it is about *how much* trades at each level, because that determines who can actually transact at the quoted number.

### Aggregation: the book as a vote-counting machine

The single most important idea in this post is that a price is an **aggregation of dispersed information**. No one person knows what a company is worth. A supplier knows orders are slowing. A regional manager knows a store is busy. An analyst knows a competitor just stumbled. Each holds a fragment. The market's genius — Hayek's old insight about prices as information-transmission — is that you do not need to collect all those fragments in one head. You just need each fragment-holder to *trade* on what they know. The buying and selling pushes the price, and the price ends up reflecting the sum of all the fragments without anyone ever assembling them.

Figure 1 shows the shape of it: many traders, each with a private view and a private amount of money, send orders into the book; the matching engine collapses them into one clearing price; the price is then broadcast to every screen in the world. The book is a vote-counting machine where votes are weighted by dollars, and the tally is the price.

This dollar-weighting is essential and easy to miss. A market price is not a poll of opinions — it is a poll of *opinions backed by capital*. If a thousand small retail investors believe a stock is worth \$50 but two large funds with conviction and balance sheets believe it is worth \$60 and are willing to buy aggressively, the price moves toward \$60, because the funds bring more buying pressure to the book. This is a feature, not a bug: it means the people who are most confident — confident enough to risk real money — have the most influence on the price, and over time the people who are *right* accumulate more capital to deploy while those who are wrong lose it. The market has a built-in mechanism for weighting opinions by track record, mediated through who has money to trade with. It is imperfect (a loud bubble can hand temporary capital to the wrong crowd), but it is far better than one-person-one-vote for the purpose of estimating value.

The flip side of dollar-weighting is that price discovery degrades when capital concentrates or withdraws. If the marginal buyer disappears — everyone who wanted to buy already has — the price stops being pushed up even if the "true value" is higher; there is simply no flow to express the view. Liquidity and discovery are joined at the hip: you can only discover a price through trades, and trades require willing counterparties with capital. A market with no flow has no price, only a stale last-trade number that means less with every passing minute it sits unrefreshed.

#### Worked example: how the opening cross finds a price

Suppose a newly listed stock has these pre-open orders. Buyers: 5,000 shares bid at \$45, another 8,000 at \$42, another 20,000 at \$40. Sellers: 6,000 shares offered at \$38, 10,000 at \$41, 15,000 at \$44. The exchange's opening **auction** looks for the single price that maximizes shares traded.

At \$41, who trades? Every buyer willing to pay \$41 or more: that is the \$45 and \$42 bids = 5,000 + 8,000 = 13,000 shares of demand. Every seller willing to accept \$41 or less: the \$38 and \$41 offers = 6,000 + 10,000 = 16,000 shares of supply. The matchable volume is the smaller side: **13,000 shares** clear at \$41.

Try \$40 instead: demand becomes 5,000 + 8,000 + 20,000 = 33,000; supply is still 6,000 + 10,000 = 16,000 (the \$44 sellers still will not sell at \$40). Matchable = 16,000 shares. That is *more* volume, so \$40 beats \$41. The auction picks the price that trades the most, and here it lands near **\$40**. The intuition: the opening price is the level where the eager money on both sides exactly balances — push it higher and sellers flood in faster than buyers; push it lower and the reverse. The crowd's collective view is \$40, and no committee chose it.

One more thing the worked example reveals: the opening auction is a *batch* mechanism — it collects orders over a window and crosses them all at one price — whereas the rest of the trading day runs *continuously*, matching each incoming order against the standing book the instant it arrives. Both are price-discovery mechanisms, but they discover differently. The batch auction is good at aggregating a lot of pent-up interest into a single robust price (which is why exchanges use auctions at the open, the close, and after a trading halt — exactly the moments when discovery is hardest and most needs many orders pooled together). Continuous trading is good at incorporating a steady stream of new information tick by tick. A well-designed market uses both: auctions to set anchor prices at fragile moments, continuous matching to refine the price between them. The 2010 flash crash, which we return to later, is in part a story of what happens when continuous trading runs into a book too thin to discover a sensible price and there is no auction to pause and re-pool the orders.

The market cap that price discovery is operating on is enormous and growing — US-listed equity is worth roughly \$58 trillion, which is the aggregate verdict of this same mechanism running across thousands of stocks every second.

![US equity market cap by year 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-2.png)

The chart climbs from about \$26 trillion in 2014 to roughly \$58 trillion in 2024, with a sharp dip in 2022. Every wiggle is price discovery at work: the 2022 drop is not value destroyed by decree but a collective downward revision, millions of sell orders nudging prices down as traders re-priced future cash flows against higher interest rates. The number is a summary of an argument the whole market is constantly having.

## Informed versus uninformed flow: how news gets into the price

So far the book sounds like a neutral tally. But not all orders carry the same information. This is where price discovery gets subtle, and where the secondary market earns its keep.

Split every order into two buckets. **Uninformed flow** (also called liquidity or noise trading) is trading for reasons unrelated to the security's value: a pension fund rebalancing, an index fund taking in a new contribution, a retiree selling to pay a bill, a trader closing a position to go home flat. These traders are not betting that the price is wrong — they just need to transact. **Informed flow** is the opposite: traders who buy or sell *because they believe the current price is wrong* and they know something the price does not yet reflect.

The deep point: informed traders are the engine of price discovery. When someone who has correctly worked out that a company's earnings will beat expectations starts buying, their buying pushes the price up — *before* the earnings are announced. By the time the news is public, the price has often already moved most of the way, because the informed flow dragged it there. The market price ends up reflecting information that has not even been published yet. That is "semi-strong efficiency" in action, and we will name it formally later.

There is a striking corollary here that confuses newcomers: **a stock can fall on good news.** A company beats earnings, the stock drops 5%, and the headline writers scratch their heads. What happened is that informed flow had already pushed the price up *in anticipation* of an even bigger beat; when the merely-good number arrived, it was worse than what the price had baked in, so the price corrected down to the new, lower expectation. The news that moves a price is never the absolute number — it is the *surprise*, the gap between what was announced and what the price already reflected. Markets trade on the derivative, not the level. This is the single most important consequence of price discovery for anyone trying to react to headlines: by the time you read the headline, the price has usually already discounted the part of it that was predictable, and only the surprise component is left to trade. We dig into this reaction mechanism in the [event-trading material](/blog/trading/event-trading/cpi-day-how-every-asset-reacts-to-one-inflation-print) on how assets respond to scheduled news.

How fast does information get impounded? In the most liquid, most-watched markets — large-cap US equities, major government bonds, the most-traded currency pairs — the answer is "within seconds to milliseconds for public news, and progressively over hours or days for information that has to be pieced together by analysts." Scheduled macro releases like the US jobs report or an inflation print move bond and currency prices in the *first few milliseconds* after release, as automated systems parse the number and trade on the surprise faster than a human can read it. Company-specific fundamental insight that requires diligence — channel checks, supplier conversations, reading footnotes — gets impounded more slowly, over days and weeks, as the informed minority accumulates positions without revealing their hand too fast. The speed of discovery is itself a measure of how much informed capital is competing in a given market.

### The market maker's problem: adverse selection

Now stand in the shoes of a **market maker** — a firm that continuously quotes both a bid and an offer, earning the spread by buying low and selling high to whoever comes along. (We dug into the economics of this role in [market makers and the spread: who provides liquidity](/blog/trading/capital-markets/market-makers-and-the-spread-who-provides-liquidity).) The market maker faces a brutal asymmetry. When someone hits its offer (buys from it), the maker does not know if it just sold to a pension fund rebalancing (harmless) or to an informed trader who knows the stock is about to jump (a loss in the making). It only learns *after* the price moves.

This is **adverse selection**: the maker systematically trades against better-informed counterparties at exactly the wrong moments. It sells right before prices rise and buys right before they fall, because that is precisely when informed traders want to deal with it. The maker cannot identify informed flow in advance — but it can do two things. First, it can **learn from the direction of flow**: a steady stream of buys is evidence that informed money thinks the stock is cheap, so the maker raises its quotes. Second, it can **widen the spread** to charge everyone a toll that compensates for the losses to the informed minority.

Figure 3 traces this. An informed trader who knows the value is \$102 lifts the maker's \$100.10 offer. The maker cannot see "informed" stamped on the order, but it sees a buy, infers some chance the buyer knows something, and ratchets its quote upward toward \$102 while widening the spread to defend itself. The news gets impounded into the price not because the news was announced, but because the maker *learned from being traded against*.

![Market maker learning from order direction and widening the spread](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-3.png)

#### Worked example: a news shock impounded through the book

A biotech trades at a \$50.00 bid / \$50.10 offer, with 2,000 shares on each side at the top of the book. The market expects a drug-trial result; the consensus is roughly a coin flip. A fund's analyst has done deep diligence and privately concludes the trial will succeed, which they estimate is worth \$58 per share.

The analyst's fund starts buying. It lifts the 2,000 shares offered at \$50.10 (cost: \$100,200). The offer is now gone; the next sellers sit at \$50.40. The market maker, seeing aggressive buying, pulls its cheap offers and re-quotes \$50.35 / \$50.55 — wider and higher. The fund keeps buying, taking 2,000 at \$50.55, then the maker moves to \$51.20 / \$51.50. Other traders see the price climbing on volume, infer someone knows something, and join. Within minutes, with no announcement, the stock is at \$54 — most of the way to the analyst's \$58.

When the trial result is officially released hours later and confirms success, the stock jumps the last bit to \$58, but **the bulk of the move already happened during trading.** The fund captured roughly \$58 − \$51 = \$7 per share on the shares it accumulated; the market maker, having sold early at \$50.10–\$50.55, took a loss on those — its adverse-selection cost. The intuition: information enters a price through the act of informed trading, and the market maker pays the tuition for that information by being on the wrong side first.

There is an elegant decomposition of the spread that makes the adverse-selection idea concrete. The bid-ask spread compensates the market maker for three distinct things: **order-processing costs** (the fixed cost of running the operation, clearing fees, technology); **inventory risk** (the danger that after buying, the maker holds the stock while the price falls before it can offload); and **adverse selection** (the loss to better-informed counterparties). Of these, adverse selection is the most interesting because it is the part that *varies with information asymmetry*. In a sleepy micro-cap where any order might be an insider, the adverse-selection component dominates and the spread balloons. In Apple, where the overwhelming majority of flow is harmless index and retail trading, adverse selection is tiny and the spread is razor-thin. So the spread is, in a real sense, a *thermometer for how much the market maker fears the person on the other side knows something it does not.*

This is why the spread is wider on stocks with more uncertainty and thinner trading: the maker faces more adverse selection and demands more compensation. The pattern is visible across the whole market.

![Bid-ask spread by liquidity tier from mega-cap to micro-cap](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-4.png)

A mega-cap like Apple trades on a spread of about 1 basis point — a hundredth of a percent — because there is so much harmless liquidity flow and so little chance any single trade is decisively informed. A micro-cap can trade on an 80-basis-point spread, eighty times wider, because almost any order in a sleepy stock might be someone who knows something, and the maker prices that risk in. Tighter spreads are the signature of better price discovery: more eyes, more flow, more information already in the price. The formal version of this trade-off — quoting against informed flow, inventory risk, optimal spread-setting — is modeled in [the market-making simulator](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-simulator-quant-research); here the take is structural: **the spread is the price of information asymmetry, and it shrinks as a market gets deeper.**

## Arbitrage: the enforcer of one price

Price discovery explains how *a* price forms in *one* place. But the same risk often trades in many places at once — a company can be listed in two countries, an index exists both as a basket of stocks and as a futures contract, an ETF trades while the stocks inside it also trade. What stops these prices from drifting apart? **Arbitrage.**

An **arbitrage** is a trade that locks in a profit with no risk by exploiting a price difference for the same (or equivalent) thing. The simplest form: if gold is \$2,000 in London and \$2,005 in New York, you buy in London, sell in New York, and pocket \$5 risk-free. Arbitrageurs hunt these gaps. Their hunting is self-defeating in the best way: buying in London pushes London's price up, selling in New York pushes New York's price down, and the gap closes. The act of capturing the difference *destroys* the difference.

This enforces the **law of one price**: identical risks must trade at identical prices, because any deviation is a free lunch that gets eaten instantly. Arbitrage is, in a phrase, *the invisible hand's hand* — the mechanism by which Adam Smith's metaphor actually operates. The invisible hand allocates capital efficiently only if prices are consistent, and arbitrage is what makes them consistent.

The "same risk" qualifier is load-bearing and worth dwelling on. Two things are arbitrage-equivalent if they pay off identically in every future state of the world. A share and an ADR on that same share are equivalent (one is a wrapper for the other). An index future at expiry and the basket of stocks at expiry are equivalent (both settle to the same index level). A bundle of cash flows can be assembled two different ways — buy a bond directly, or replicate it with a portfolio of zero-coupon strips — and those must cost the same. The deeper the equivalence, the harder the no-arbitrage constraint binds. Where the equivalence is only *approximate* — two different companies in the same industry, a bond versus a "similar" bond — the trade is not true arbitrage but **relative value**, and the gap can persist or widen because nothing forces exact convergence. The distinction between true arbitrage (forced convergence) and relative value (hoped-for convergence) is exactly where the limits-of-arbitrage problems we will meet later bite hardest.

Arbitrage also explains a feature of markets that otherwise looks like magic: how a price in one venue *propagates* to every other venue almost instantly. When a large buyer moves the price of a stock on its primary exchange, the ADR in another time zone, the index future, the options implied price, and every ETF holding the stock all adjust within milliseconds — not because a central authority broadcasts the new price, but because thousands of arbitrage relationships each tug their connected prices into line. The market is a web of no-arbitrage constraints, and a shock at any node ripples out along the threads until the whole web is consistent again. That web is why you can talk about "the price" of a thing at all, rather than a different price in every venue.

### The four classic arbitrages that knit markets together

**Dual-listed shares and ADRs.** Many large foreign companies trade both on their home exchange and in the US via an **American Depositary Receipt** — a US-traded certificate representing a fixed number of the home shares, held by a custodian bank. If the home-market price (converted to dollars) and the ADR price diverge beyond the cost of converting one into the other, arbitrageurs trade until they reconverge.

**ETF versus basket.** An [exchange-traded fund](/blog/trading/capital-markets/indices-etfs-and-the-bridge-back-to-the-primary-market) holds a basket of stocks but trades as a single security. If the ETF trades above the value of its holdings (its net asset value), an **authorized participant** buys the underlying stocks, delivers them to the fund in exchange for new ETF shares, and sells those shares — pushing the ETF price back down to NAV. This create/redeem arbitrage is the bridge that keeps an ETF honest.

**Index versus futures.** A stock-index future is a contract to buy the index at a set price on a future date. Its fair value is the index level plus the cost of carry. If the future trades richer than that, you sell the future and buy the basket; if cheaper, the reverse. This is **index arbitrage**, and it is one of the largest arbitrage activities in markets.

Figure 5 walks the index-arbitrage trade. The future is rich — 5,050 versus a fair basket value of 5,040, a 10-point gap that looks free. The arb sells the future at 5,050 and buys the basket at 5,040. At expiry both must converge to the same spot index level, so the 10-point gap is captured almost regardless of which way the market moves.

![Index arbitrage selling the rich future and buying the cheaper basket](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-5.png)

#### Worked example: an index-arbitrage trade

The S&P 500 index is at 5,000. A futures contract expiring in three months should, by no-arbitrage, trade at the index plus net carry — say financing cost of 1.0% over the quarter minus 0.5% in expected dividends, a net +0.5%, giving a fair future of 5,000 × 1.005 = 5,025. Instead the future trades at 5,040, **15 points rich.**

The arbitrageur sells one future at 5,040 (each point is worth \$50, so the contract notional is 5,040 × \$50 = \$252,000) and simultaneously buys the basket of 500 stocks in index proportion, costing 5,000 × \$50 = \$250,000 plus financing. Over the quarter, the stocks earn \$250,000 × 0.5% = \$1,250 in dividends, and financing the position costs \$250,000 × 1.0% = \$2,500, a net carry of −\$1,250. At expiry the future settles to the spot index, say it is anywhere — say 5,100. The short future loses (5,100 − 5,040) × \$50 = \$3,000; the long basket gains (5,100 − 5,000) × \$50 = \$5,000; plus the −\$1,250 net carry. Total: −\$3,000 + \$5,000 − \$1,250 = **+\$750**, which is exactly the 15-point mispricing (15 × \$50 = \$750) captured. The intuition: by holding both legs to convergence, the arb harvests the gap between the actual future price and its fair value, and the market's direction washes out.

#### Worked example: an ADR-versus-ordinary arbitrage

A European company's ordinary shares trade at €90.00 in Frankfurt. Its US ADR represents one ordinary share and trades at \$100.00 in New York. The EUR/USD rate is 1.08, so the ordinary share is worth 90 × 1.08 = **\$97.20** in dollar terms — yet the ADR trades at \$100.00, a **\$2.80 (2.9%) premium.**

An arbitrageur sells 10,000 ADRs short at \$100.00 (proceeds \$1,000,000) and buys 10,000 ordinary shares in Frankfurt at €90.00 (cost €900,000 = \$972,000 at 1.08). It then instructs the custodian to convert the ordinaries into ADRs to cover the short. Ignoring fees, it has locked in \$1,000,000 − \$972,000 = **\$28,000** for delivering equivalent shares. This very trading — selling ADRs, buying ordinaries — pushes the ADR down and the ordinary up until the premium shrinks to the cost of doing the conversion (custody fees, FX, borrow). The intuition: an ADR is just a wrapper around the home share, so the two prices cannot diverge by more than the cost of unwrapping one into the other.

## The efficient-market hypothesis and its limits

If informed traders push prices toward value and arbitrageurs enforce consistency, you arrive at a famous claim: **the efficient-market hypothesis (EMH)** — that asset prices already reflect all available information, so you cannot reliably beat the market without taking more risk. It comes in three strengths.

- **Weak form:** prices reflect all *past price* information. You cannot beat the market using only charts of historical prices, because any pattern would already be traded away. (This is the honest core behind the skepticism toward pure technical analysis.)
- **Semi-strong form:** prices reflect all *public* information — past prices plus earnings, news, filings. As soon as information is public, it is in the price; reacting to a press release is too late. Our biotech example above is semi-strong efficiency: the price moved before and as the news landed.
- **Strong form:** prices reflect *all* information, including private/insider information. Almost nobody believes the strong form literally — if it were true, insider trading could never be profitable, which is plainly false (and illegal precisely because it is profitable).

What EMH really claims is humbler than its reputation. It does not say prices are *correct* — it says they are *unbiased given the information available* and that *systematically* beating them is hard because doing so requires either better information or bearing more risk. The strongest evidence is practical: the majority of active fund managers underperform a cheap index fund over long horizons, after fees. If markets were full of easy mispricings, that would not happen.

There is a beautiful self-reference at the heart of EMH that the economist Sanford Grossman and Joseph Stiglitz pointed out: markets *cannot* be perfectly efficient, because if they were, no one would have any incentive to gather information. Gathering information — research, diligence, analysis — costs money. Traders only pay that cost because they expect to profit by trading on what they learn before the price reflects it. But that profit *is* the inefficiency. So a perfectly efficient market would offer no reward for the very activity that makes it efficient, and the information-gathering would stop, and the market would become inefficient. The resolution is an equilibrium with *just enough* inefficiency to pay informed traders for their effort. Markets are efficient *to the degree that the marginal informed trader is fairly compensated* — no more, no less. This is why a beginner should neither believe "the market knows everything" nor "the market is dumb and I can beat it easily". The truth is calibrated: prices are hard to beat precisely because so many well-funded people are trying, and the residual edge available is roughly equal to the cost of the effort to find it.

Another subtlety: efficiency is *relative to an information set and a horizon*. A market can be efficient with respect to public news (semi-strong) while leaving room for those with genuine private analysis. It can price the next quarter's earnings well while badly mispricing a slow-moving structural shift a decade out, because the arbitrage horizon for the long-dated bet is too long for most capital to hold. "Is the market efficient?" is never a yes/no question — it is "efficient with respect to what information, over what horizon, given how much arbitrage capital is willing to bet on the answer?"

### The limits of arbitrage

But EMH has real limits, and the deepest critique is not "markets are irrational" — it is that **arbitrage itself is costly, risky, and capital-constrained.** This is the Shleifer–Vishny "limits of arbitrage" argument, and it is the most important caveat in this entire post. For arbitrage to keep prices right, arbitrageurs must be able to put on the trade and *hold it until the gap closes*. Three frictions break that:

1. **Cost.** Trading is not free: spreads, fees, the cost of borrowing a stock to short it, financing the long leg. If the mispricing is smaller than the cost of trading it, no one corrects it, and it persists.
2. **Risk.** Many "arbitrages" are not truly risk-free — they are *relative-value* bets that two prices will converge. They can diverge further before they converge. A merger can break; a "cheap" bond can get cheaper.
3. **Capital constraints.** This is the killer. An arbitrageur trades with borrowed money and faces **mark-to-market margin**: if the position moves against them on paper, the lender demands more collateral. If they cannot post it, they are forced to liquidate — *at the worst possible moment*, right when the mispricing is largest and the eventual profit is most certain. As Keynes put it, the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent.

Figure 7 is the anatomy of this failure. The arb opens correctly with a 5% gap and posts margin. The gap widens to 9% — a paper loss. The lender issues a margin call. Unable to add cash, the arb is forced to exit at a realized loss. *Then* the gap closes — but the arb is already out and captures none of the convergence it correctly predicted.

![Limits of arbitrage timeline ending in a forced exit before convergence](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-7.png)

#### Worked example: right trade, margin-called before convergence

A fund spots two near-identical bonds: Bond A yields 5.00%, Bond B yields 5.30%, though they carry the same risk — a 30-basis-point mispricing that "must" converge. The fund buys \$100M of cheap Bond B and shorts \$100M of rich Bond A, financed at 20× leverage, so it posts only \$5M of its own equity.

Instead of converging, the spread *widens* to 60 basis points — the kind of thing that happened to [LTCM in 1998](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed) as a global flight to quality blew relative-value spreads apart. The position's mark-to-market loss is roughly the spread move times duration times notional: a 30-bp adverse move on a 7-year-duration \$100M position is about 0.30% × 7 × \$100M = **\$2.1M** of loss. Against \$5M of equity, that is a 42% drawdown. The prime broker raises margin requirements and demands \$3M more collateral. The fund does not have it. It is forced to unwind at the 60-bp spread, crystallizing the \$2.1M loss. Six months later the spread converges to zero — the trade was *right* — but the fund captured none of it. The intuition: being correct is not enough; you must be solvent and funded long enough for the market to agree with you, and leverage shortens that runway brutally.

This is why prices can stay wrong. Bubbles are the spectacular case: during the dot-com mania, short-sellers who correctly judged that internet stocks were overvalued were repeatedly margined out as prices rose further before collapsing. The mispricing was real, the eventual correction came, but the arbitrage that "should" have prevented the bubble was disarmed by its own funding constraints. Efficiency is a *tendency*, enforced by arbitrage capital, and that capital is finite.

There is a second, subtler limit worth naming: **synchronization risk**, identified by Abreu and Brunnermeier. Even arbitrageurs who all privately know a price is wrong cannot profit unless they trade *together* — one fund shorting a bubble alone just loses money as the bubble inflates further. The correction only comes when enough capital attacks the mispricing at once, but no individual arbitrageur knows when the others will move. So rational arbitrageurs may *ride* a bubble for a while, planning to get out just before it bursts, which makes the bubble bigger and longer-lived rather than smaller. The very smart money, far from always correcting mispricings, sometimes amplifies them because the coordination needed to correct them does not exist. This is why "the smart money knows it's a bubble" and "the bubble keeps growing" are not contradictory — they are the same equilibrium.

A third limit is **noise-trader risk**, from De Long, Shleifer, Summers, and Waldmann: the unpredictability of irrational traders is itself a risk that deters arbitrage. If sentiment can push a price further from value before reverting, then betting against the crowd carries the risk that the crowd gets *more* wrong before it gets right — and that risk, being undiversifiable and hard to hedge, demands compensation, which means arbitrageurs require a wider gap before they will step in. The mispricing that arbitrage tolerates is therefore not zero but a band whose width grows with how erratic the noise traders are. Put all three limits together — cost, capital constraints, synchronization, noise-trader risk — and you have a complete answer to the puzzle of why an "efficient" market can host persistent, sometimes enormous, mispricings. Arbitrage is powerful but not omnipotent; it is a finite resource that can be overwhelmed.

## What makes price discovery good or bad

If price discovery is the secondary market's core product, it is worth asking what separates a market that discovers prices well from one that does it badly. Four ingredients matter, and they reinforce each other.

**Liquidity.** A liquid market — many willing buyers and sellers, tight spreads, deep books — can absorb large orders without big price swings, which means the price reflects considered demand rather than the accident of who happened to be trading at one moment. An illiquid market gaps around on small orders, and its "price" is noisy and unreliable. This is why the same news moves a micro-cap 15% but a mega-cap 0.5%: not because the news matters more to the small company, but because the small company's thin book cannot absorb the flow without lurching.

**Participation by informed traders.** A price is only as smart as the smartest money trading it. Markets that attract sophisticated, well-resourced analysts and arbitrageurs discover prices faster and more accurately, because more fragments of dispersed information get expressed through trades. A market dominated entirely by uninformed flow — pure index-tracking with no one doing fundamental work — would slowly lose its information content, because no one is doing the work of figuring out what things are actually worth. This is the genuine worry behind the rise of passive investing: if everyone indexes, who sets the prices the index is built from? In practice, a small minority of active capital still does the discovery and the rest free-rides, but the balance matters.

**Transparency.** The more of the order flow that happens in the lit, public order book — where every quote and trade is visible — the more information the price reflects and the more everyone can trust it. As we saw, the migration of flow off-exchange is a real tension: internalized and dark trading can offer better execution to individual orders while subtly starving the public price of the information those orders carry.

**Low friction for arbitrage.** Cheap, fast, well-capitalized arbitrage keeps prices internally consistent and corrects mispricings quickly. When arbitrage is expensive or constrained — high transaction costs, hard-to-borrow stocks, scarce risk capital — prices can drift apart and stay wrong longer. The health of arbitrage capital is, in a real sense, the health of market efficiency itself.

#### Worked example: measuring discovery quality through the spread cost

Suppose you want to buy \$1,000,000 of a stock. In a deeply liquid mega-cap with a 1-basis-point spread, your round-trip cost from the spread alone is about \$1,000,000 × 0.01% × 2 ≈ \$200, plus negligible market impact — call it \$300 all-in. In an illiquid small-cap with a 25-basis-point spread, the spread cost is \$1,000,000 × 0.25% × 2 = \$5,000, and walking a million-dollar order through a thin book might add another \$5,000 of impact, for \$10,000 all-in. The same \$1M position costs roughly **thirty times more** to establish in the illiquid name. The intuition: the spread and market impact are the *tax you pay to participate in price discovery*, and that tax is a direct, measurable readout of how good the discovery is — tight in liquid markets, punishing in thin ones.

## Common misconceptions

**"Someone sets the price."** No one does. There is no committee, no exchange official, no company executive who dictates the trading price. The exchange runs the matching engine; the company files disclosures; but the *number* is an emergent property of order flow. Even the IPO price set by bankers is just a starting guess that the secondary market immediately overrides — which is exactly why the first-day "pop" exists.

**"The current price is the company's true value."** EMH does not claim this. The price is the market's best *unbiased estimate* given public information, weighted by the dollars behind each opinion. It can be wrong — sometimes badly, for a long time — when arbitrage capital is too scarce or too scared to correct it. "Unbiased" means it is not *systematically* too high or too low, not that it is exactly right on any given day.

**"Arbitrage is risk-free free money."** Pure arbitrage (same asset, two prices, instant) is nearly extinct because it is so easy that it is captured in microseconds by [matching-engine](/blog/trading/capital-markets/inside-an-exchange-the-matching-engine-and-the-order-book)-speed players. What remains and pays is *relative-value* arbitrage, which carries convergence risk and funding risk. The LTCM example shows how "free money" can margin you to death.

**"More trading always means better prices."** Not quite. What improves price discovery is *informed* trading and deep, competitive liquidity. A huge volume of pure noise — wash trades, churning — adds no information. And as off-exchange trading grows, some informed flow is internalized away from the public quote, a real microstructure debate.

![Off-exchange share of US equity volume 2010 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-6.png)

That chart shows off-exchange execution — dark pools and wholesale internalization — rising from about 30% of US equity volume in 2010 to roughly 47% by 2024. The worry is that if too much flow trades in the dark, the lit order book that does the price discovery sees less of the information and the public price becomes a thinner signal. So far the lit market remains the reference; but the trend is a live question about how much trading a market can hide before discovery degrades.

**"Efficient markets mean you cannot make money."** They mean you cannot *easily and reliably* beat the market without an edge — better information, faster execution, or bearing risk others will not. The people who *do* beat it (and get paid for it) are precisely the informed traders and arbitrageurs whose activity *makes* the market efficient. Efficiency is the by-product of their competition, not a law that forbids profit.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The flash events.** On May 6, 2010, the US "flash crash" saw major indices fall about 9% and recover within minutes. Price discovery momentarily broke: liquidity providers pulled their quotes as a large automated sell program hit a thin book, and with no bids the price gapped down to absurd levels (some stocks printed at a penny). It is the clearest demonstration that a price is only as good as the orders standing behind it — withdraw the flow and the "price" becomes meaningless. The system has since added circuit breakers and auction-based reopenings precisely to give discovery time to re-aggregate.

**LTCM, 1998.** Long-Term Capital Management ran exactly the relative-value arbitrages described above, at enormous leverage. When Russia defaulted and spreads blew out worldwide, its correct convergence bets moved against it faster than its capital could absorb, and it was forced to unwind into a falling market — the textbook limits-of-arbitrage failure, large enough that the Fed coordinated a rescue. Read the full story in [LTCM 1998: when genius failed](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed).

**The IPO pop, every year.** The persistent first-day jump on IPOs is price discovery correcting the banker's deliberately conservative offer price. The chart below shows the median first-day return running double-digits most years and spiking above 40% in 2020. That gap is the difference between a negotiated primary price and the secondary market's immediate verdict — and it is a direct cost to issuing companies, which sold too cheap.

![Median first-day IPO return by year 2018 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-8.png)

This is the spine made concrete: the secondary market's price discovery sets the benchmark against which the primary market is priced. A consistent pop tells bankers and issuers that the [bookbuilding price](/blog/trading/capital-markets/bookbuilding-and-price-discovery-how-the-ipo-price-is-set) was below what the open market would pay — feedback that flows straight back into how the next deal is priced.

**ETF arbitrage in a stress test.** In March 2020, some bond ETFs traded at steep discounts to their stated NAV. Critics cried "ETFs are broken!" — but the more accurate read was that the ETF's continuously-traded price was *discovering* the real, lower value of illiquid bonds faster than the stale NAV (built from dealer marks that had not updated). Arbitrage was doing its job; it was the underlying bond prices that were lagging. The episode showed price discovery migrating to the most liquid venue.

**Vietnam and information asymmetry.** In an emerging market like Vietnam, where retail investors dominate and disclosure is thinner, adverse-selection costs are higher and spreads wider — the [foreign-flow and index-effect dynamics](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam) are amplified precisely because informed flow (often foreign institutions) moves prices sharply against a less-informed local crowd. It is the same mechanism as everywhere, turned up by a thinner information environment.

**The GameStop episode, 2021.** In January 2021, a coordinated retail crowd pushed GameStop's stock from under \$20 to nearly \$500, while well-funded hedge funds short the stock — who by most fundamental measures were "right" that the company was overvalued — were forced to cover at devastating losses. It is a near-perfect illustration of limits of arbitrage colliding with synchronization and noise-trader risk: the short-sellers' valuation case may have been correct, but their capital and their margin could not survive a crowd that, for a time, had both coordination and conviction. The eventual decline came, but several funds were destroyed before it arrived. Being right about value is necessary but not sufficient; you must also survive the path, and the path is set by flow, not fundamentals, in the short run.

**Closed-end fund discounts.** A long-standing embarrassment for strict EMH is that closed-end funds — which hold a fixed portfolio of securities and trade as a single stock — routinely trade at persistent discounts (sometimes 10–20%) to the market value of what they hold. The assets are *observable and liquid*, so this looks like a free \$0.85-on-the-dollar lunch. Yet the discount persists for years because the arbitrage (buy the fund, short the holdings) carries the risk that the discount widens before it narrows, and there is no forced-conversion mechanism to guarantee convergence. It is a clean, decades-long demonstration that "the price equals fundamental value" is an approximation enforced by arbitrage, and arbitrage has limits.

## The takeaway: a price is a verdict, not a fact

Here is the way to hold all of this in your head. A market price is not a measurement of value the way a thermometer measures temperature. It is a *verdict* — the running tally of an argument the whole market is having, where everyone votes with their capital and the loudest votes are the ones backed by the most conviction and the most money. Price discovery is the courtroom; informed traders are the witnesses who move the verdict toward the truth; the market maker is the clerk who learns from who is testifying and prices its own risk accordingly; and arbitrage is the appeals system that forces every courtroom in the world to reach a consistent verdict on the same evidence.

The efficient-market hypothesis is the claim that this system usually reaches a fair verdict fast. The limits-of-arbitrage literature is the reminder that the appeals system can run out of money before justice is served — that a price can stay wrong as long as the capital needed to correct it stays scarce or scared. Both are true at once, which is why markets are *mostly* efficient and *occasionally* spectacularly not.

It helps to picture the cast of characters whose interaction produces the verdict, because each plays a structural role and the price is the resultant of all of them. The **uninformed liquidity traders** — index funds, rebalancers, retirees, corporates — provide the volume that makes a market tradeable at all; they are the water the informed fish swim in, and without them spreads would be impossibly wide because market makers would face nothing but adverse selection. The **informed traders** — fundamental analysts, activist investors, anyone doing genuine research — are the ones who actually move the price toward value; they are paid for their work out of the pockets of the uninformed, which is the Grossman–Stiglitz toll. The **market makers** stand in the middle, quoting continuously, learning from flow, and earning the spread as compensation for inventory and adverse-selection risk; they are the grease that lets the other groups transact without waiting for an exact counterparty. And the **arbitrageurs** patrol the boundaries between venues and instruments, enforcing consistency so that the verdict reached in one courtroom binds all the others. Remove any one group and price discovery degrades: no liquidity traders, no volume; no informed traders, no information; no market makers, no continuous price; no arbitrageurs, no consistency. The price is what emerges when all four are present and competing.

![Off-exchange share of US equity volume 2010 to 2024 reprise](/imgs/blogs/how-a-price-is-made-discovery-arbitrage-and-efficiency-6.png)

That structure also tells you where to look when a price seems "wrong". Ask which group is missing or overwhelmed. A flash crash is market makers withdrawing. A bubble is uninformed flow overwhelming informed capital while synchronization keeps arbitrage on the sidelines. A persistent closed-end-fund discount is arbitrage that cannot guarantee convergence. A thinly-traded micro-cap with a wild price is simply too few participants of any kind. The framework turns "the market is irrational" — a non-explanation — into a diagnosis: *which part of the discovery machine is broken right now, and why?*

And this matters far beyond traders. The verdict the secondary market reaches every second is the number a company's cost of capital is read from, the benchmark an IPO is priced against, the signal that tells the economy where its savings should flow. When price discovery works — deep liquidity, tight spreads, informed flow, well-funded arbitrage — the capital market does its real job well: it points the world's savings at their most productive use. When it breaks, capital is misallocated. So the next time you see a number tick on a screen, remember that nobody put it there. Thousands of people did, all at once, with their money — and that is the quietly astonishing thing the secondary market does, all day, for free.
