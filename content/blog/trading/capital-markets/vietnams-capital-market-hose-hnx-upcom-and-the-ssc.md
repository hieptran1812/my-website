On 28 July 2000, a small room in Ho Chi Minh City opened for its first-ever trading session. Two companies were listed. The matching happened by hand-fed orders, prices were called out, and the whole "exchange" traded a few hundred million dong — a few tens of thousands of US dollars. A country of 80 million people had a stock market that, on day one, you could have fit inside a single American retail brokerage branch.

Twenty-four years later, that same market clears something like **17 trillion dong a day** — on the order of \$700 million — across nine million accounts, with an index futures contract, a corporate-bond market that blew up and got rebuilt, and a serious bid to be reclassified from "frontier" to "emerging" by the global index providers. Vietnam went from two listed stocks to a recognizable capital market in one working lifetime.

This post is the opening of the Vietnam track. If you have read Tracks A through G, you already know the *machine*: a primary market that creates securities, a secondary market that trades them, a plumbing layer that clears and settles, intermediaries who connect savers to issuers, and a regulator who polices disclosure. Vietnam has built every one of those layers. The interesting part is *where the build is still thin* — because that is precisely where the market is not yet trusted, and trust is the thing that lets savings turn into long-term investment.

## Foundations: what a capital market has to have, and how Vietnam got each piece

Before the Vietnam-specific names, anchor the general shape. A capital market is a machine that moves savings into investment. For it to work at all, four things must exist together.

First, **securities** — standardized claims on a business or a borrower. A *share* is a slice of ownership in a company; a *bond* is a loan you can resell. The whole point of a security is that it is *transferable*: you can buy your slice today and sell it to a stranger next month without anyone re-negotiating the underlying deal.

Second, a **primary market** that creates those securities. When a company sells shares to the public for the first time — an *initial public offering*, or IPO — it raises cash it can spend on factories and people. That is real capital formation: savings becoming productive assets.

Third, a **secondary market** where existing securities trade among investors. No new money reaches the company here; you are just buying my old shares. It feels like a side-show, but it is the engine that makes everything else possible. Here is the secret that runs through this entire series: **secondary-market liquidity is what makes primary issuance possible.** Nobody buys a slice of a 30-year business unless they believe they can sell that slice tomorrow morning at a fair price. Kill the resale market and the IPO market dies with it.

Fourth, **plumbing and a referee** — a clearing-and-settlement system so that when you buy, you actually receive the shares and the seller actually receives the cash, plus a regulator who forces companies to tell the truth so investors can price them. Without the plumbing, trades fail; without disclosure, every price is a guess.

Vietnam, starting essentially from zero in 2000, had to assemble all four. There were no listed companies because almost everything was state-owned. There was no investing public because there had been nothing to invest in. There was no regulator with securities expertise, no depository, no broker-dealer industry. The story of Vietnam's market is the story of building those four pillars from scratch — and the order in which they were built still shapes how the market behaves today.

It helps to set the macro backdrop. Vietnam began its *Doi Moi* ("renovation") reforms in 1986, gradually shifting from a centrally planned economy toward a "socialist-oriented market economy." For the next decade, capital was allocated the old way: the state owned the productive enterprises, the state banks lent to them on direction, and households saved in gold, dollars, and cash because there was no domestic asset to hold a long-term claim in. A stock exchange is, in effect, a decision to let *prices set by strangers* — rather than a planning ministry — allocate a slice of the nation's capital. That is a profound institutional choice, and 2000 was the year Vietnam made it. Everything since has been about widening that slice and making the prices trustworthy enough that more savings flow through it.

The sequencing matters because it created path dependencies the market still carries. Vietnam built the *trading* layer (an exchange, a price-discovery venue) before it built a deep *disclosure* and *enforcement* layer, and it built a retail investor base before it built a deep institutional one. Both orderings are the reverse of how developed markets matured, and both explain recurring features of Vietnam's market: episodes where issuance outran disclosure (the 2022 bond crisis), and a tape dominated by individuals rather than funds. When you see those features later, trace them back to the order the pillars were poured.

#### Worked example: how a secondary market "creates" primary capital

Suppose a Vietnamese consumer-goods firm wants to raise **1,000 billion dong** (≈ \$40M) to build a new plant. It can only sell those shares at a good price if buyers believe they can exit later. Say investors demand a *liquidity discount* of 5 percentage points of expected return for an illiquid stock versus a liquid one. On a stock investors would otherwise pay 20× earnings for, that discount might push the multiple they will pay down to roughly 16×. On 80 billion dong of annual earnings, that is the difference between raising 1,600 billion dong and 1,280 billion dong — a **320 billion dong (≈ \$13M) gap** created by nothing but the promise of a working secondary market. The lesson: liquidity is not a nicety bolted onto the market; it is priced straight into how much capital a real business can raise.

## A short history: from two stocks to nine million accounts

Vietnam's market did not arrive fully formed. It was legislated into existence and then grew in distinct waves, each adding a layer of the machine.

![Vietnam capital market timeline from 2000 first session to 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc-3.png)

The **first session in July 2000** in Ho Chi Minh City — then the Securities Trading Center, later the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange, HOSE — had just two listed companies and traded only a few sessions a week. Trading was a managed, almost ceremonial affair. For the first several years the market was a curiosity: thin, dominated by a handful of names, and prone to wild swings on tiny volume.

In **2005**, a second center opened in Hanoi, which would become the **Hanoi Stock Exchange (HNX)**. The two-venue structure was deliberate: a main board in the south for larger companies, a junior board in the north for smaller ones. In **2009**, a third tier was added — **UPCoM**, the Unlisted Public Company Market — to capture the enormous population of companies that were *public* (they had many shareholders, often from privatization) but had not formally *listed* on an exchange.

The supply of listings came overwhelmingly from one source: **equitization** (in Vietnamese, *co phan hoa*) — the conversion of state-owned enterprises into joint-stock companies. We will return to the mechanics, but the key fact is that Vietnam's listings were not, in the early years, hungry entrepreneurs raising growth capital. They were former government departments and state firms being partly privatized. That origin still colors the market: many large listed names are former or current state champions in banking, energy, and telecoms.

Equitization deserves a moment because it is the single most important fact about where Vietnam's listings *came from*. In a Western market, the IPO pipeline is fed by founders and venture capitalists who build companies and eventually sell shares to the public to raise growth capital or cash out. Vietnam had almost none of that in 2000. What it had was a vast portfolio of state-owned enterprises — banks, breweries, dairy producers, ports, power companies — that the government wanted to partly privatize, both to raise cash and to impose market discipline on sleepy state firms. Equitizing those firms and floating a minority stake became the conveyor belt that fed the exchange its listings. The upside: the market got large, real, cash-generating companies from day one. The downside: the state often retained a controlling stake, free floats were small, and minority shareholders had limited say — a governance pattern that lingers across the blue-chip board today.

The 2007 boom-and-bust was formative. The VN-Index roughly tripled into early 2007 on a wave of first-time retail enthusiasm, then collapsed in the global financial crisis. A generation of Vietnamese investors learned, expensively, that the market goes down too. The years that followed were a long grind of institution-building: a real depository, a 2010 Securities Law, broker consolidation, and the slow accumulation of listings.

It is worth dwelling on what those "long grind" years actually built, because the unglamorous institution-building is the real story. A market needs more than an exchange. It needs a central depository so that shares exist as book entries that can be transferred safely rather than paper certificates that can be forged or lost — Vietnam built that (today the VSDC). It needs a broker-dealer industry to intermediate between savers and the market — Vietnam grew dozens of securities firms, then watched many consolidate or fail as the 2007 bubble deflated. It needs accounting and audit standards so that disclosed numbers mean something. It needs a legal framework defining what a security is, who can issue one, and what an issuer must tell investors. None of this moves an index on the day it is enacted, and none of it makes headlines. But it is the difference between a trading venue and a capital market, and the decade after 2007 is when Vietnam quietly poured most of that foundation.

The modern era has three landmarks. In **2017**, the derivatives market launched with **VN30 index futures**, giving the market its first exchange-traded hedging and leverage tool. In **2019**, a new **Securities Law** (effective 2021) modernized the entire legal framework — disclosure, investor categories, the regulator's powers. And in **2021**, the government created the **Vietnam Exchange (VNX)**, a holding company that sits over HOSE and HNX, consolidating two competing venues into one corporate group. By the end of 2024 the market had roughly **nine million trading accounts** and a VN-Index around 1,267.

![VN-Index year-end level from 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc-2.png)

Look at the index path and you see the personality of the market: a powerful surge into the 2021 peak of 1,498 — fueled by lockdown-era retail account openings and cheap money — then a sharp 2022 drawdown to near 1,007 as global rates rose and the domestic corporate-bond scandal broke, then a partial recovery. This is a high-beta, retail-driven, sentiment-sensitive index. It does not glide; it lurches.

## The exchanges: HOSE, HNX, and UPCoM under the VNX

The three venues are not redundant. They are tiers, and a company moves up or down them as it grows, shrinks, or runs afoul of listing rules.

**HOSE** is the big board. It hosts the largest, most liquid companies — the banks (Vietcombank, BIDV, Techcombank), the conglomerates (Vingroup), consumer names (Vinamilk, Masan), and industrials. The **VN-Index** tracks HOSE; the **VN30** tracks its 30 largest, most liquid names and is the underlying for the futures market. If a foreign fund wants Vietnam exposure, it is almost always buying HOSE names. HOSE is where the liquidity and the institutional attention concentrate.

**HNX** is the junior equity board plus two specialized functions. It lists smaller-cap companies, but more importantly it is the home of the **bond market** — both government bonds (the primary auctions and secondary trading of Vietnamese government debt run through HNX) and the listed corporate-bond market — and it hosts the **derivatives** venue where VN30 futures trade. So HNX is doing triple duty: small-cap equities, the bond machinery, and the futures market.

**UPCoM** is the catch-all for *public but unlisted* companies. Vietnam has thousands of firms that became joint-stock companies through equitization and acquired hundreds or thousands of shareholders, but never met (or never bothered with) full exchange listing standards. UPCoM gives those shares a regulated place to trade, with looser requirements and a wider price band. It is messier and riskier than HOSE, but it brought a huge swath of the economy onto a transparent venue rather than leaving it in an opaque over-the-counter grey market.

Why three venues and not one? Partly history — the Hanoi center grew up separately from the Ho Chi Minh one, and a young market did not want to force every small company onto the same demanding board as the blue chips. Partly function — a junior board with looser listing standards lets smaller firms access public capital without meeting big-board governance bars they could not yet clear, while a separate unlisted-company market (UPCoM) drags the grey-market shares of thousands of equitized firms into the transparent regulated daylight. The tiering is a deliberate on-ramp: a firm can be public on UPCoM before it is mature enough to list, list on HNX before it is large enough for HOSE, and graduate to HOSE when it is a genuine blue chip. The cost of that flexibility is fragmentation — three rulebooks, three price bands, three pools of liquidity — which is exactly what the VNX consolidation set out to tame.

Above all three sits the **Vietnam Exchange (VNX)**, the 2021 holding company. Before VNX, HOSE and HNX were separate state-owned entities that, somewhat awkwardly, competed. Consolidation under VNX was meant to rationalize the structure: one group, a planned division of labor (HOSE for equities, HNX for bonds and derivatives), shared technology, and a single counterpart for international index providers and exchanges to deal with. It is the institutional tidying-up that mature markets do — fewer, bigger, better-governed venues.

#### Worked example: where a company lists as it grows

Take a mid-size manufacturer that equitized with 400 shareholders and 200 billion dong (≈ \$8M) of equity. Too small and too lightly traded for HOSE, it lands on **UPCoM**, where the price band is a wide +/- 15% and disclosure is lighter. Five years later it has grown to 2,000 billion dong (≈ \$80M) of equity, profits, and a clean audit. It meets HNX's listing bar, moves up, and its price band tightens to +/- 10%. Another five years and it is a 20,000 billion dong (≈ \$800M) blue chip; it lists on **HOSE**, where the band is the tightest +/- 7% and the institutional buyers live. The lesson: the three venues form a ladder, and the price band tightens as you climb because larger, more-watched companies are less prone to manipulation and need less room.

## The regulator: the SSC, and why it is not an SEC

Every capital market needs a referee whose real product is *trust*. In the United States that referee is the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), an *independent* federal agency whose commissioners are appointed for fixed terms and who answers, ultimately, to Congress and the courts rather than to the executive branch's policy goals.

Vietnam's referee is the **State Securities Commission (SSC)** — and the crucial structural fact is that the SSC sits *inside* the **Ministry of Finance**. It is not an independent agency; it is a body of the government's finance ministry. That difference matters more than it sounds.

An independent regulator can, in principle, take actions that are unpopular with the government of the day — halt a politically connected IPO, fine a state champion, force disclosure the state would rather suppress — because its mandate and budget are insulated. A regulator embedded in a ministry operates within the government's broader objectives: hitting a GDP target, financing the budget, supporting state enterprises. None of that makes the SSC ineffective — it writes and enforces real rules — but it does mean the regulator's independence is structurally limited, and global investors price that in. When they ask "can I trust the disclosure, the enforcement, the protection of minority shareholders?" the honest answer is "improving, but with a state thumb nearby on the scale."

![Vietnam stock market cap as percent of GDP from 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc-4.png)

You can read that trust gap directly off the size of the market. Vietnam's total stock market capitalization runs around **60% of GDP** — it touched 93% in the 2021 bubble, then fell back. A developed market typically carries equity worth **150% of GDP or more** (the US is often well above 150%). Some of that gap is simply that Vietnam is a younger, faster-growing, less-financialized economy where more of the corporate sector is still private or state-held. But part of it is the trust discount: a market where disclosure is shallower, enforcement is newer, minority-shareholder protection is weaker, and the regulator is not independent will support a smaller stock of equity relative to the real economy, because every claim trades at a slightly lower multiple. Closing that gap is, in large part, what "completing the machine" means.

The **2019 Securities Law** (effective January 2021) was a serious upgrade. It strengthened disclosure requirements, clarified the categories of professional versus ordinary investors, tightened rules around private bond placements (a gap the 2022 turmoil would brutally expose), raised penalties for violations, and modernized the SSC's supervisory powers. It is the legal backbone the modern market runs on.

#### Worked example: the cost of a trust gap

Suppose two otherwise identical companies — same earnings, same growth — list in two markets. In a market with a fully trusted, independent regulator, investors pay 18× earnings. In a market where investors demand an extra 3 percentage points of return for governance and enforcement risk, the multiple they will pay might fall to roughly 14×. On 100 billion dong of earnings that is **1,800 billion dong of market value versus 1,400 billion dong** — a 400 billion dong (≈ \$16M) "trust discount" applied to the very same business. The lesson: regulatory credibility is not vague; it is a number subtracted from every valuation in the market until the credibility is earned.

## What trades: stocks, bonds, futures, and warrants

A market is defined by its instruments. Vietnam's set is now broad, but each piece arrived at a different stage of maturity and each carries a different risk lesson.

![Equitization pipeline turning a state-owned enterprise into a listing](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc-5.png)

**Stocks** are the heart of it. The listing supply, as noted, came largely from **equitization** — the privatization pipeline shown above. A wholly state-owned enterprise is valued, restructured, and converted into a joint-stock company; a slice is sold to investors (sometimes a strategic investor, sometimes the public, sometimes employees); and the new shares eventually list on HOSE or UPCoM. This is how the secondary market got its supply: not from a thousand startups, but from the controlled, sequenced sale of the state's own businesses. Equitization is genuine primary-market capital formation, but with the government as the selling shareholder rather than a founder.

Stocks also matter because they are how the broad public first touches the capital market. For most Vietnamese households, a brokerage account is the first time their savings become a *priced, transferable, exchange-traded claim* on a business rather than a deposit, a gold bar, or property. That is the savings-into-investment machine reaching ordinary people — and it is why the explosion in account numbers is not just a volume statistic but a genuine financial-inclusion milestone, for better (households can compound wealth in productive assets) and worse (households can also lose money fast in a leveraged, volatile market they may not fully understand).

**Government bonds** are the quiet, large part of the market. The Vietnamese state finances itself partly by issuing bonds through auctions run on HNX, and those bonds trade in a secondary market dominated by banks. This is the price of money for the whole economy — exactly the role the US Treasury market plays — and it is institutionally the most developed fixed-income segment. (For how bonds are *priced* and what duration means, this series links out; we own issuance and venue, not the math.)

**Corporate bonds** are the cautionary tale. Through the late 2010s and into 2021, Vietnamese companies — especially property developers — raised enormous sums through *privately placed* corporate bonds, often sold to retail investors who did not understand they were buying unsecured developer debt with thin disclosure. In **2022**, the music stopped: high-profile arrests over bond-issuance fraud (the Tan Hoang Minh and Van Thinh Phat cases), a wave of defaults and restructurings, and a near-freeze of new issuance. It was Vietnam's own miniature structured-credit crisis: a primary market that ran far ahead of the disclosure and investor-protection plumbing, exactly the imbalance the spine of this series warns about. The regulatory response (tighter private-placement rules under Decree 65 and amendments) was the market re-learning that issuance without trust eventually seizes.

**Index futures** launched in 2017 — specifically **VN30 futures**, a cash-settled contract on the VN30 index. What is striking is *who* trades them: the contract is overwhelmingly **retail**-driven, used as a leveraged directional bet far more than as an institutional hedge. In many sessions, futures notional turnover rivals or exceeds the cash equity market. That is the inverse of how futures markets usually mature, and it tells you the derivative arrived before a deep institutional base existed to use it for hedging.

**Covered warrants** rounded out the toolkit from 2019 — these are securities issued by brokers that give the holder the right to buy an underlying stock at a set price, listed and traded like a stock on HOSE. They give retail investors leveraged exposure to single names in a regulated, exchange-listed wrapper. (For options mechanics and Greeks, this series links out; we own the listing and trading venue, not the pricing model.)

Notice the pattern across all five instruments: each was a new layer of the machine, and each arrived in roughly the order a market matures — equities first, then government bonds, then a wider corporate-bond market, then derivatives and warrants for hedging and leverage. But Vietnam added the leverage tools (futures, warrants, margin) before it had a deep institutional base to use them prudently, so they were adopted overwhelmingly by retail as directional bets. That mismatch — sophisticated tools in mostly retail hands — is a recurring theme, and it is why each new instrument has tended to amplify the market's swings before it stabilized them.

#### Worked example: the 2022 corporate-bond air pocket

Take a property developer that issued **5,000 billion dong** (≈ \$200M) of three-year private-placement bonds at 11%, sold largely to retail buyers chasing yield over the 6% on bank deposits. The developer was rolling these — repaying maturing bonds by issuing new ones. When new issuance froze in 2022 and the rollover failed, the developer faced a 5,000 billion dong wall it could not refinance. Bondholders who thought they held a safe 11% deposit-substitute discovered they held unsecured, illiquid, defaulting developer paper. The lesson: a primary market that outruns disclosure and a secondary market that cannot reliably reprice risk will, sooner or later, hand the bill to whoever was least equipped to read the prospectus.

## Who trades: retail dominance, nine million accounts, and the foreign segment

If you remember one structural fact about Vietnam's market, make it this: it is a **retail** market.

![Vietnam securities trading accounts from 2018 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc-6.png)

Individual domestic investors are routinely **85% or more of daily trading volume** on HOSE. The number of trading accounts exploded from around 2.2 million in 2018 to roughly **9.2 million by 2024** — a near-quadrupling, with the steepest jump in the 2021 lockdown era when low deposit rates, time at home, and a rising index pulled in millions of first-time investors. (Note that accounts overstate unique people — an investor can hold several — but the direction is unmistakable: a mass-market base formed fast.)

A retail-dominated market behaves differently from an institution-dominated one. It is more momentum-driven, more sentiment-sensitive, more prone to herding into the same handful of hot names, and more reactive to rumor than to disclosed fundamentals. It is also more vulnerable to the leverage cycle: **margin lending** by brokers amplifies the up-moves and forces selling on the down-moves, which is part of why the VN-Index lurches rather than glides.

Margin deserves emphasis because it is the hidden accelerator in a retail market. Vietnamese brokers extend margin loans against stock collateral; when prices rise, collateral values rise, borrowing capacity rises, and investors buy more — a self-reinforcing climb. When prices fall, the same mechanism runs in reverse: collateral shrinks, brokers issue *margin calls*, and forced selling tips into more forced selling. In a market where the marginal buyer is a leveraged individual rather than a long-horizon pension fund, these cascades are sharper and faster. Several of the VN-Index's most violent down-days have been margin-call avalanches, not fundamental repricings. It is the same dynamic that makes any heavily retail, heavily leveraged market — from 1929 New York to 2015 Shanghai — prone to air pockets.

Why is the market so retail in the first place? Three reasons. First, the institutional base is genuinely young: Vietnam's domestic mutual-fund and pension industry is small relative to the size of household savings, so there is no large pool of professional money to set prices. Second, foreign institutions face the ownership limits and pre-funding frictions described below, which cap how much of the float they can hold. Third, Vietnamese households have a high savings rate and, after generations of holding gold and dollars, an appetite for a domestic asset that can compound — and with bank deposit rates low for stretches of the 2020s, stocks became the obvious alternative. The result is a market where the dominant investor is an individual, and the dominant behavior is therefore individual psychology at scale.

The **foreign-investor segment** is small in volume share but outsized in importance. Foreign institutions — funds, ETFs, strategic investors — are the marginal price-setters for the blue chips and the gatekeepers of an emerging-market upgrade. Their flows are watched obsessively. The chart of foreign net buying and selling tells a story of episodic confidence: net selling in 2020–2021 and again heavily in 2024, with a notable net-buy year in 2022 around the corporate-bond shakeout. Foreign access is constrained by *foreign-ownership limits* (the "room") on many sectors — a topic this track covers in depth in its own post — which is one of the structural frictions standing between Vietnam and a "trusted by global capital" stamp.

![HOSE average daily trading value from 2018 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc-8.png)

The turnover chart shows the same pulse: average daily value on HOSE ran around 5 trillion dong before 2021, spiked to 21.6 trillion dong in the 2021 retail frenzy, and settled in the mid-teens of trillions since. A typical day's ~17 trillion dong of turnover, with ~85% retail, means individual investors alone churn roughly **14–15 trillion dong (≈ \$600M) of stock a day**. That is a deep, liquid retail market by frontier standards — and an unusually thin institutional one.

#### Worked example: the retail share of a single day

Take a day with **17 trillion dong** of HOSE turnover (≈ \$700M at roughly 24,500 dong per dollar). If domestic retail is 86% of that, retail traded **14.6 trillion dong (≈ \$600M)**, domestic institutions and proprietary desks maybe 7% or 1.2 trillion dong, and foreigners the remaining 7% or about 1.2 trillion dong (≈ \$49M). The lesson: prices on any given day are set far more by millions of individuals reacting to news and each other than by a handful of institutions running models — the opposite of a developed market, and the single biggest driver of how Vietnam's market *feels*.

## Market structure quirks: price bands, lot sizes, and the T+ cycle

Three micro-rules shape every Vietnamese trade, and they trip up investors who arrive from developed markets expecting US conventions.

![Comparison of HOSE HNX and UPCoM price bands and lot sizes](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-capital-market-hose-hnx-upcom-and-the-ssc-7.png)

**Price-limit bands.** Each venue caps how far a stock can move from its prior-session reference price in a single day: **+/- 7% on HOSE**, +/- 10% on HNX, and +/- 15% on UPCoM. Hit the upper limit and the stock is at "ceiling" (in Vietnamese, *tran*, shown purple on the board); hit the lower limit and it is at "floor" (*san*). The band is a circuit breaker meant to dampen the volatility of a thin, retail-driven market — but it has side effects. Bad news that "should" knock a stock down 25% gets metered out over several locked-limit-down days, with sellers stacked up unable to exit. The band slows panic, but it also delays price discovery and can trap liquidity.

**Lot sizes.** HOSE trades in board lots of **100 shares**; orders that are not multiples of 100 (odd lots) trade separately and less conveniently. For a retail investor this means the minimum position in a 100,000-dong stock is 100 shares = 10 million dong (≈ \$400) — a real barrier to small accounts diversifying, and a reason cheaper stocks attract disproportionate retail attention.

**The settlement cycle.** Vietnam runs a **T+2** cash-settlement cycle on stocks (a trade on Monday settles Wednesday), historically with a quirk that bought shares were not sellable until they actually arrived in the account on the settlement day — so a buyer was effectively locked in for the cycle, unable to flip same-day. This is *slower* than the US, which moved to T+1 in 2024, and it is one of the plumbing items on the emerging-market-upgrade checklist. The clearing and settlement machinery itself — run by the depository, the VSDC, and modernized with the new KRX trading system — gets its own post in this track.

There is a fourth quirk worth flagging because it bites foreign investors specifically: **pre-funding**. On many transactions a foreign investor has historically had to have the cash sitting in the account *before* placing a buy order, rather than settling two days later like a domestic institution. For a global fund running cash efficiently across dozens of markets, parking idle money in Vietnam ahead of every trade is a real friction and a real cost — and it is one of the precise items the index providers cite as a blocker to an emerging-market upgrade. Reforms in 2024 and 2025 to relax pre-funding for institutional foreign buyers were aimed squarely at clearing that hurdle. It is a small-sounding plumbing detail with an outsized effect on whether the world's largest pools of capital will treat Vietnam as investable.

Taken together, these micro-rules are not arbitrary. Each is a deliberate design choice a young, thin, retail-heavy market made to protect itself: bands to dampen mob volatility, lot sizes to keep the order book clean, a slower cycle and pre-funding to make sure trades actually settle in a market still building its credit infrastructure. The trouble is that the same rules that protect a frontier market are exactly the ones a mature market sheds — and shedding them is what the upgrade journey is about.

#### Worked example: a +/- 7% band capping a HOSE move

Take a HOSE stock with a prior-session reference price of **50,000 dong**. The +/- 7% band sets a ceiling of 50,000 x 1.07 = **53,500 dong** and a floor of 50,000 x 0.93 = **46,500 dong**. Suppose overnight news justifies a 20% drop to ~40,000 dong (≈ \$1.63). On day one the stock can only fall to the 46,500 floor and locks there, with a queue of unfilled sell orders. On day two the reference resets to 46,500, the new floor is ~43,200, and it locks again. It takes roughly three locked-limit-down sessions for the price to grind to its "true" ~40,000 level — and for those three days, holders who want out cannot get out. The lesson: the band does not change where the price ends up, only how long it takes and who gets trapped on the way down.

## Common misconceptions

**"Vietnam's stock market is basically a casino."** It is retail-heavy and sentiment-driven, but it is a *real* capital market with a depository, a settlement cycle, a disclosure regime, and genuine capital formation through equitization and follow-on issuance. The casino framing misses that millions of Vietnamese households are, for the first time in history, holding transferable claims on the country's largest businesses. That is savings turning into investment — the machine working, if imperfectly.

**"The three exchanges compete for listings like NYSE and Nasdaq."** No. Since the 2021 VNX consolidation they are tiers of one group with a planned division of labor — HOSE for big-board equities, HNX for bonds and derivatives, UPCoM for unlisted public companies. A company sits on the venue that matches its size and disclosure level, not the one offering the best deal.

**"The SSC is Vietnam's SEC."** Functionally similar, structurally different. The SSC sits *inside* the Ministry of Finance and is not independent of the government the way the US SEC is. That structural fact is a real input into how much global capital trusts the market, and it is priced into valuations as a discount.

**"Foreigners drive the market."** Foreigners are the marginal, watched, price-setting buyers of the blue chips and the key to an index upgrade — but they are only around 7% of daily volume. On any given day, prices are made by domestic retail. Foreign *flows* matter at the margin and for sentiment; domestic retail makes the tape.

**"Price-limit bands protect investors."** They dampen single-day volatility, but they do not stop a stock from reaching its fair value — they only delay it across multiple locked-limit sessions, during which holders who want to sell often *cannot*. The band trades one risk (a violent single-day crash) for another (trapped liquidity and slow price discovery).

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2021 retail surge.** In a single year, trading accounts jumped from roughly 4.3 million to 6.8 million as lockdowns, near-zero deposit rates, and a rising index pulled millions of first-time investors in. Average daily HOSE turnover more than tripled to 21.6 trillion dong, and the VN-Index hit its all-time peak of 1,498. It was Vietnam's version of the global 2021 retail boom — and like everywhere else, the conditions that created it (cheap money, time at home) reversed in 2022.

**The 2022 corporate-bond turmoil.** As global rates rose, Vietnam's privately placed corporate-bond market — built largely on property-developer paper sold to under-informed retail buyers — seized up. High-profile fraud arrests, a rollover freeze, and a cascade of defaults followed. The VN-Index fell from 1,498 to near 1,007 over the year. It was a textbook case of a primary market running ahead of its disclosure and investor-protection plumbing, and the regulatory tightening that followed (Decree 65 and its amendments) was the market rebuilding the trust layer after the fact.

**The VNX consolidation, 2021.** Folding HOSE and HNX under a single holding company was the unglamorous institutional maturation that markets do as they grow up: fewer, better-governed venues; a clear division of labor; a single counterpart for international index providers. It is the kind of plumbing reform that does not move the index on the day but raises the ceiling on how trusted the market can become.

**The emerging-market upgrade bid.** Vietnam has spent years working to be reclassified by index providers (FTSE Russell, and eventually MSCI) from "frontier" to "secondary emerging." The blockers are exactly the thin layers of the machine: foreign-ownership limits (the "room"), the pre-funding requirement that foreigners deposit cash before trading, settlement-cycle frictions, and disclosure depth. Each fix is a layer of the machine being completed — and the upgrade, when it comes, is the global market formally certifying that the trust has been earned.

## The takeaway: a machine being assembled in public

Step back and the whole point of this series snaps into focus. A capital market turns savings into investment through a primary market, a secondary market, a plumbing layer, intermediaries, and a regulator. Developed markets built those layers over a century or more, and we mostly experience them as finished infrastructure. Vietnam built them in twenty-four years, and you can still see the scaffolding.

That visibility is the gift of studying Vietnam. The corporate-bond blow-up showed what happens when primary issuance outruns the disclosure layer. The retail dominance shows what a market looks like before a deep institutional base forms. The price bands and the slower settlement cycle show the plumbing still being upgraded. The SSC's place inside the Ministry of Finance shows a regulator whose independence — and therefore whose trust premium — is still being negotiated. And the emerging-market-upgrade bid is, in effect, a checklist of which layers of the machine the global market does not yet consider complete.

Every place the build is thin is a place the market is not yet fully trusted, and every gain in trust shows up as a higher multiple, a deeper book, a larger foreign allocation, and ultimately more capital flowing to Vietnamese businesses. That is the lens for the rest of this track: not "is Vietnam's market good or bad," but "which layer of the machine is being completed next, and what will that unlock?" The next three posts pick up exactly there — the clearing-and-settlement plumbing, the foreign-ownership "room," and the upgrade checklist itself.
