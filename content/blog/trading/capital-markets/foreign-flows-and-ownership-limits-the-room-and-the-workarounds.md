> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnam lets the world buy its companies only up to a capped share — "the room" — and that cap is both a shield for national control and a brake on the very capital an emerging-market upgrade would unlock.
>
> - The Foreign Ownership Limit (FOL) caps the foreign-held share of a Vietnamese firm: generally 49%, banks 30%, and some sectors 0% or conditional. When a stock is "full", foreigners can only buy from other foreigners — often at a 5-15% premium.
> - To buy at all, a foreigner needs a securities trading code, an Indirect Investment Capital Account (IICA) in VND, and a local custodian bank — the plumbing before any order reaches HOSE.
> - The workarounds — a foreign-to-foreign premium board, proposed Non-Voting Depositary Receipts (NVDRs, copied from Thailand), ETFs, and swaps — all try to hand foreigners economics without breaching the cap.
> - The one number to remember: foreigners net-sold roughly **90 trillion VND (about \$3.6 billion)** on HOSE in 2024, the heaviest selling on record, and the index felt every billion even though foreigners are a minority of daily volume.

On a normal trading morning in Ho Chi Minh City, a London-based emerging-markets fund decides it wants more of a Vietnamese bank — say a well-run lender every foreign analyst already likes. The portfolio manager sizes the order, the trader routes it to a local broker, and then nothing happens. Not because the price is wrong, but because there are no shares to buy. The bank's foreign room is full. Every share a foreigner is allowed to own is already owned by a foreigner. The order sits there until another foreigner decides to sell — and that seller knows exactly how scarce his shares are, so he wants a premium.

That small, frustrating moment is the whole story of foreign access to Vietnam in miniature. The country runs a real, growing capital market — a machine for turning domestic and global savings into long-term investment in Vietnamese firms — but it has bolted a deliberate limit onto how much of that machine the rest of the world is allowed to own. Understanding that limit, the plumbing you must pass through to reach it, and the clever structures people invent to get around it, tells you more about an emerging market than any valuation model.

This post is about "the room": what the foreign ownership cap is, why it exists, what breaks when it fills, and why foreign flows — even from a minority of investors — visibly move the VN-Index.

## Foundations: what a foreign ownership limit actually is

Start from zero. A **share** is a claim on a slice of a company — its profits (dividends) and, usually, a vote on how it is run. A **capital market** is the machine that lets companies sell those claims to raise money (the primary market) and lets investors trade them afterward (the secondary market). The secret that makes the whole machine run is liquidity: nobody funds a long-lived business by buying a share unless they believe they can sell that share to someone else tomorrow morning. For Vietnam, a huge pool of potential "someone elses" lives abroad — global pension funds, emerging-market funds, sovereign wealth — and they bring exactly the kind of patient, long-term capital a developing economy wants.

A **Foreign Ownership Limit (FOL)** is a legal cap on how much of a single company foreigners may own *in total*. If a company's FOL is 49%, then no matter how many foreign investors want in, the combined foreign stake can never exceed 49% of the shares. The space left between the current foreign holding and that cap is what everyone calls **"the room"** (in Vietnamese, *room ngoai*). If foreigners hold 30% and the cap is 49%, there is 19% of room left. If they hold 49%, the room is zero — the stock is "full" or "hết room" (room exhausted).

Here is the everyday-money version. Consider a members-only club that has decided no more than 49 of its 100 lockers can be rented to out-of-towners. Locals can rent any of the other 51 freely. Once 49 out-of-towners hold lockers, a 50th can only get one by buying it off an existing out-of-towner — and since the supply is fixed by rule, that locker changes hands above its "fair" rent. The FOL does the same thing to a stock: it fixes the foreign supply, and a fixed supply against rising foreign demand produces a premium.

The caps in Vietnam are tiered by how strategic the sector is:

- **General companies: 49%** is the historical baseline, raised toward 100% for many non-restricted sectors after the 2015 reform (Decree 60), but only if the company itself opts in and its business lines allow it.
- **Banks: 30%** total foreign ownership, with a single strategic foreign investor capped at 20% and other limits below that. Banks are treated as systemically sensitive, so the cap is tighter and rarely loosened.
- **Conditional or restricted sectors: 0% to a low cap** — telecoms, defense, certain media and "conditional business lines" can be effectively closed to foreigners or capped well below 49%.

So the FOL is not one number; it is a sector-by-sector dial. And the dial is set by policy, not by the market.

### Why caps exist at all

Three reasons, all about **control rather than capital**:

1. **Strategic and state control.** Many of Vietnam's largest listed firms are former or partial state-owned enterprises (SOEs). The state wants to keep control of banks, utilities, telecoms, and resource firms — both for sovereignty and to manage them as policy tools. A cap guarantees that no foreign coalition can ever take voting control.
2. **Financial-stability fear.** Hot foreign money can flood in and rush out. A cap on the foreign *stock* of ownership is a crude but real brake on how violently a sudden foreign exit can drain a company's register.
3. **Sequencing the opening.** Emerging markets typically liberalize in stages. Caps let policymakers open the door part-way, watch what happens, and widen it deliberately rather than all at once.

Notice what is *not* on that list: raising more capital for Vietnamese firms. The cap does the opposite. It is a control instrument that, as a side effect, throttles the inflow of exactly the patient capital the market most wants to attract. Hold that tension — we return to it at the end, because it is the lever an emerging-market upgrade pulls.

### A short history of the cap

Vietnam did not start with a 49% baseline by accident; the number is the residue of a slow, deliberate opening. When the Ho Chi Minh exchange opened in 2000 with a handful of listed companies, the foreign cap was a tiny 30% — the state was barely willing to let outsiders touch the new market at all. As the market proved it would not destabilize the economy, the cap was widened: to 49% for ordinary companies in 2003, then, after years of lobbying by foreign funds frustrated by full-room favorites, the landmark **Decree 60 in 2015** allowed many non-restricted companies to raise their foreign limit all the way to 100% — *if* the company opted in and none of its business lines fell into a restricted category.

The catch is in that conditional. Decree 60 did not abolish the cap; it gave companies *permission* to raise it, and many of the most attractive firms either could not (because they had a restricted business line buried in their charter) or chose not to (because management and the state wanted to keep control). So a decade after Decree 60, the market is a patchwork: some firms run at 100% foreign room and never fill, while the marquee banks and consumer names sit pinned at 49% or 30% and stay full. The reform widened the *option* without widening the *reality* for the stocks foreigners most want. That gap between the legal ceiling and the practical room is where the premium lives.

## What happens when the room is full

When a stock hits its cap, the ordinary open market stops working for foreigners. A foreign buyer can no longer place a normal order and match against whoever is selling. Instead, the foreign buyer can only match against a foreign *seller*, because a local seller's shares would just leave the foreign bucket and re-open the room rather than transfer foreign-to-foreign. Brokers run this as a separate **foreign-to-foreign** market — sometimes called the **premium board** — where a foreigner buys a "full-room" name directly from another foreigner, off the central order book, at a negotiated price.

That price is almost always above the on-screen local price. Why? Because the foreign supply is fixed by law and the foreign demand is not. A scarce, legally rationed asset trades at a scarcity premium. Historically these premiums have run anywhere from a few percent to 30%+ for the most coveted full-room names. The premium is the market's price tag on the FOL itself: it measures how much foreigners would pay to relax the cap if they could.

#### Worked example: paying a premium for a full-room stock

A foreign fund wants to buy a Vietnamese consumer-staples leader whose foreign room has been full for months. The on-screen (local) price is **100,000 VND** per share. The only way in is the foreign-to-foreign board, where current foreign holders will part with shares at **112,000 VND** — a **12% premium**. The fund wants 5 million shares.

- Local-price cost (theoretical): 5,000,000 × 100,000 = **500 billion VND**.
- Actual cost on the premium board: 5,000,000 × 112,000 = **560 billion VND**.
- Premium paid: 560 − 500 = **60 billion VND**, roughly **\$2.4 million** at about 25,000 VND per USD.

The fund pays an extra \$2.4M not for the business but for the *right to own a capped asset*. That 12% is a pure access cost — and it is invisible to a domestic investor, who can buy the same share at 100,000. The lesson: in a capped market, the price you pay depends on *who you are*, which is the opposite of how a clean secondary market is supposed to work.

The deeper problem is that the premium board is thin and opaque. Trades are negotiated, not continuously quoted, so a foreigner who needs to exit a full-room name in a hurry may find no foreign buyer at all — the very liquidity the secondary market is supposed to provide evaporates exactly when the cap binds.

### What the premium actually measures

It is worth being precise about *why* the premium exists and what it tells you, because it is one of the few places a market puts a clean price tag on a regulation. The local price of a share is set by the full population of buyers — millions of domestic retail investors plus the foreigners who still have room. The premium-board price is set by a much smaller population: only foreigners, trading a legally fixed quantity of shares among themselves. Whenever you ration supply and let demand float, you get a price above the unrationed market. The size of the premium therefore moves with two things: how badly foreigners want the name (demand) and how little of it ever turns over foreign-to-foreign (supply). A beloved bank with sticky long-term foreign holders who never sell can carry a fat, persistent premium for years; a less-loved full-room name might trade at only a sliver above local.

That makes the premium a useful sentiment gauge. A *widening* premium on the flagship names says foreigners are scrambling for exposure they cannot get on the open market — bullish for the underlying, and a hint that an upgrade would unleash buying. A *collapsing* premium (or full-room names suddenly having room again) says foreigners are leaving, which is exactly what the record net selling of 2021-2024 looked like up close: room opening on names that had been jammed full for years, because the marginal foreigner was a seller, not a buyer.

#### Worked example: when foreign selling re-opens the room

A bank has a 30% foreign cap and has sat at the full 30% for three years, trading at a 10% premium on the foreign-to-foreign board. In a year of heavy foreign outflows, foreign holders sell **2% of the company** back into the market — but a foreign seller can only sell to a local buyer once the foreign-to-foreign demand is exhausted, so foreign ownership drops from 30% to **28%**.

- Room re-opens: there is now **2%** of room for any foreigner to buy at the *local* price.
- The premium board goes quiet: why pay a 10% premium to a foreign seller when you can buy the same shares at the local price straight off the order book?
- The visible premium compresses toward **zero** while the room stays open.

A foreigner watching this sees the cap stop binding — temporarily. The intuition: the premium is not a fixed feature of a stock; it is a live readout of whether foreign demand is currently pressing against the cap, and it deflates the moment foreigners turn net sellers.

## The plumbing: how a foreigner actually gets in

Before any of this — before you can hit the room or pay a premium — a foreign investor has to be allowed to trade at all. Vietnam, like most emerging markets, gates access through registration, a dedicated account, and local custody. This is the "intermediaries and plumbing" layer of the capital-market machine, and for foreigners it is a real cost of entry.

![Pipeline of the steps a foreign investor takes to trade a Vietnamese stock, from trading code to repatriation](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds-3.png)

The steps:

1. **Securities Trading Code (STC).** A foreign investor must register with the Vietnam Securities Depository and Clearing Corporation (VSDC) to receive a trading code. No code, no trades. (For more on VSDC's role in the post-trade machine, see the clearing-and-settlement post linked below.)
2. **Indirect Investment Capital Account (IICA).** The foreigner opens a single, dedicated VND-denominated bank account through which *all* investment money must flow — in and out. Foreign currency is converted to VND on the way in; proceeds are converted back and repatriated through the same account. The IICA is the chokepoint regulators use to see and, in principle, control foreign flows.
3. **Local custodian.** A custodian bank (often a global custodian's local arm or a Vietnamese bank) holds the shares and settles the trades on the foreigner's behalf.
4. **Local broker and order.** Only then can the investor place orders on HOSE (the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange) through a licensed broker.

#### Worked example: the cost of the on-ramp for a \$40M mandate

A mid-size global fund wins a **\$40 million** Vietnam mandate (about **1,000 billion VND**). Before it earns a single dong of return it pays:

- Custody fees: say 0.15% per year on assets → 0.0015 × \$40,000,000 = **\$60,000 / year**.
- FX conversion on the way in: a ~0.1% spread on \$40M = **\$40,000**, paid once at entry (and again on exit).
- Brokerage and a layer of legal/registration setup: a few tens of thousands of dollars up front.

Call it roughly **\$100,000+** in first-year frictions before alpha. None of this is the FOL — it is just the access plumbing — but it stacks on top of the premium a foreigner may also pay for full-room names. The intuition: emerging-market access is not free, and these costs are exactly what an MSCI/FTSE upgrade is meant to lower by standardizing the rails.

This plumbing matters for the spine of the whole series. A capital market grows by attracting capital, and capital arrives through rails. The cheaper and clearer the rails, the more of the world's savings can fund Vietnamese firms. Vietnam's rails work — but the manual on-ramp (settlement quirks, pre-funding requirements, the IICA chokepoint) is one of the things index providers flag as still too clunky for a "developed-emerging" stamp.

### The pre-funding problem

One rail in particular has been a sore point: **pre-funding**. Historically, a foreign investor buying a Vietnamese stock had to have the cash sitting in the account *before* the order could be placed — you could not buy on the expectation of settling a day or two later, the way you can in developed markets. For a global fund running money across dozens of countries, pre-funding is expensive: cash that has to sit idle in VND ahead of every trade is cash not earning a return anywhere else, and converting it in and out across the FX spread adds cost on each round trip.

This is not a trivial gripe. MSCI and FTSE both list the absence of a delivery-versus-payment standard and the pre-funding requirement among the access barriers keeping Vietnam in the frontier category. In late 2024 Vietnam moved to relax pre-funding for institutional foreign investors — letting brokers extend short-term credit so foreigners no longer needed the full cash up front — precisely to clear this barrier. It is a concrete example of the access plumbing, not the FOL, being the binding constraint on the upgrade.

#### Worked example: the carrying cost of pre-funding

A fund trades **\$5 million** of Vietnamese stocks a week and, under the old rule, must keep that cash pre-funded in VND for an average of two days before each trade settles. If idle cash could otherwise earn **5%** a year in US Treasury bills:

- Idle cash: \$5,000,000 held two days early, every week.
- Foregone yield: \$5,000,000 × 5% × (2/365) ≈ **\$1,370 per trade cycle**.
- Over 50 trading weeks: ≈ **\$68,000 a year** of pure carrying cost, on top of FX spreads and custody.

That is dead-weight friction — money lost to the *process* of investing, not to any investment decision. The intuition: removing pre-funding does not change a single company's fundamentals, but it lowers the toll on the on-ramp, and a lower toll means more of the world's capital is willing to drive in.

## The workarounds: getting economics without breaching the cap

Because the FOL binds on the most-wanted stocks, the market has invented a ladder of structures to give foreigners exposure without adding to the legally-capped foreign register. Each one separates *economics* (dividends and price moves) from *control* (the vote and the registered foreign stake).

### 1. The foreign-to-foreign premium board

The first and bluntest workaround we have already met: buy a full-room stock from another foreigner on the premium board. It does not relax the cap; it just rations the fixed foreign supply by price. It is a workaround for *access*, not for the cap itself, and it is the reason "full room" names carry a visible premium.

### 2. Non-Voting Depositary Receipts (NVDRs) — Thailand's fix

The cleanest proposed solution is the **Non-Voting Depositary Receipt (NVDR)** — a wrapper around the share. A local entity (in Thailand, a subsidiary of the stock exchange) holds the underlying ordinary share and issues a receipt to the foreigner. The receipt passes through all the *economics* — dividends, rights, and the price change — but the *vote* stays with the local issuer of the receipt (who typically abstains or votes as instructed only within limits). Because the registered owner of the share is the local entity, the share does **not** count against the foreign ownership limit.

![Before and after an NVDR splits an ordinary share into economic rights for the foreigner and a vote held locally](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds-5.png)

Thailand introduced NVDRs in 2000 precisely to escape its own foreign-limit problem, and they became a large, liquid share of foreign activity on the Thai exchange. Vietnam has discussed adopting an NVDR-style instrument for years — it appears repeatedly in capital-market development plans and securities-law debates — but as of the mid-2020s it has not been fully implemented at scale. The hold-up is partly legal (Vietnamese company law ties economic and voting rights together more tightly) and partly political (an NVDR effectively widens foreign economic ownership, which is the thing caps were designed to limit).

#### Worked example: how an NVDR splits the share

A foreigner wants exposure to a full-room Vietnamese firm. Under an NVDR scheme:

- The foreigner pays **100,000 VND** and receives one NVDR (no premium, because the cap is not being breached — the share is owned locally).
- The firm pays a **3,000 VND** dividend. The NVDR holder receives the full **3,000 VND** — the economics pass straight through.
- The firm holds a shareholder vote. The NVDR carries **zero votes**; the local issuer abstains. The foreigner gives up control entirely.

Compare that to the premium board, where the same foreigner would have paid **112,000 VND** for a real share *with* a vote. The NVDR trade says, in effect: *I do not want the vote — I want the return — so charge me the local price and keep the control onshore.* That is exactly the bargain a control-protective government can accept: foreign capital funds the firm, but foreign hands never reach the steering wheel. It is the most elegant resolution of the tension at the heart of this post, which is why Vietnam keeps proposing it.

### 3. ETFs and index funds

The simplest practical workaround for many foreigners is to skip individual full-room stocks entirely and buy a **fund**. An exchange-traded fund (ETF) that tracks Vietnamese equities — whether a domestic ETF listed on HOSE or a foreign-listed Vietnam ETF — holds a basket on the investor's behalf. The foreigner buys *fund units*, not the underlying capped shares, so the FOL on any single name is the fund's problem to manage, not the end-investor's.

This is why ETFs have become such an important door into Vietnam. A foreign investor who cannot get room in a flagship bank can still get diversified Vietnamese exposure through a Vietnam ETF, and many of the largest foreign positions in the market are held via these funds. For the mechanics of how index funds connect back to the underlying market, see the indices-and-ETFs sibling post and the dedicated Vietnam foreign-flows piece linked below.

#### Worked example: an ETF as a route around a full FOL

A foreign investor wants 20% of a \$10M Vietnam allocation in a flagship bank whose room is full and whose premium board is asking a 15% markup.

- Direct route: \$2,000,000 of the bank at a 15% premium = an extra **\$300,000** in access cost, *if* a foreign seller can even be found.
- ETF route: buy **\$2,000,000** of a Vietnam ETF that already holds the bank near its index weight. The investor pays the fund's expense ratio (say 0.6% / year = **\$12,000**) and a small bid-ask spread on the ETF units, but **no per-name premium** and no waiting for room.

The ETF delivers most of the desired exposure at a fraction of the access cost. The trade-off: the investor gets the bank only at its index weight inside a basket, not a concentrated bet. The intuition: when the cap rations direct ownership, pooled vehicles become the path of least resistance — which is why ETF flows are a big part of *foreign* flows in capped markets.

### 4. P-notes, swaps, and dividend structures

At the more synthetic end, an offshore bank can write a **participatory note (P-note)** or a **total-return swap** to a foreign client: the bank holds the Vietnamese shares (using its own access and room), and the client receives the economic return through a derivative contract booked offshore. The client never appears on the Vietnamese register at all. These give fast, flexible exposure but add counterparty risk (you are exposed to the bank, not just the stock) and opacity (regulators dislike not seeing the ultimate owner). They are a workaround of last resort for investors who cannot or will not set up the full local on-ramp.

The common thread across all four: **separate the economics from the control.** That is the only way to feed foreign capital to a Vietnamese firm without breaching a cap that exists to protect control. Every workaround is a different packaging of the same trade.

It is worth ranking them by how much friction and risk each carries, because a foreign investor genuinely chooses among them. The premium board gives you a *real* share with a *real* vote, but only if a foreign seller exists and only at a premium — highest access cost, lowest counterparty risk. An NVDR (where available) gives you the economics at the local price with zero vote and almost no counterparty risk, since it is exchange-sponsored — the cleanest option, which is exactly why it does not yet exist at scale in Vietnam. An ETF gives you diversified, instant, cheap access but only at index weights inside a basket — you cannot express a concentrated single-name view. A P-note or swap gives you a fast, flexible, concentrated bet but layers on counterparty risk and offshore opacity — the most friction-light to execute and the most fragile if the writing bank stumbles. The ladder runs, roughly, from "expensive but solid" (premium board) to "cheap but synthetic" (swaps), and a foreigner picks the rung that matches how much control, concentration, and counterparty exposure they are willing to trade for access.

The reason all four coexist is that none of them is free, and none fully substitutes for simply raising the cap. They are the market routing around a constraint, and every routing has a toll. That is the signature of a binding regulation: when a rule blocks the most direct path, capital does not give up — it builds detours, and the detours themselves become a cottage industry of brokers, custodians, and structurers who earn fees on the friction.

## Why foreign flows move the VN-Index

Here is the puzzle. Foreign investors are a *minority* of daily turnover on HOSE — domestic retail investors do the large majority of trading, especially since the account boom of 2021. So why does the financial press obsess over the daily "khoi ngoai" (foreign bloc) net buy/sell number, and why does the VN-Index so often turn on it?

![VN-Index year-end level from 2014 to 2024 showing the 2021 peak and 2022 drop](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds-4.png)

Three reasons:

1. **Concentration in large caps.** Foreign money does not spread evenly. It piles into the biggest, most liquid, index-heavy names — the banks, the consumer leaders, the property and industrial flagships. Those same names dominate the VN-Index by capitalization weight. So a foreign flow that is small as a share of *total* turnover can be large as a share of trading in the *handful of stocks that drive the index*.
2. **Sentiment signal.** Foreigners are read — rightly or not — as the "smart, patient" money. When the foreign bloc is a persistent net seller, domestic investors take it as a verdict on Vietnam and often sell alongside, amplifying the move. The foreign number is a headline that *moves other people*, which makes it self-fulfilling.
3. **Marginal price-setter.** In a market where domestic flows are often momentum-driven and retail-dominated, a steady foreign bid or offer can be the marginal buyer/seller that sets the direction at the top names — the place where price discovery for the whole index happens.

So the foreign bloc punches above its volume weight: minority of turnover, but concentrated in the index's spine and amplified by sentiment.

There is a feedback loop hiding in reason two that is worth spelling out. Vietnamese financial media report the daily foreign net number on the front page, every day, as a headline statistic. Domestic retail investors — who dominate volume and who often trade on momentum and sentiment rather than fundamentals — read that number as a signal of what the "informed" money is doing. A run of foreign selling becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy: foreigners sell a few large caps, the headline says "foreigners net sold for the tenth straight session", domestic investors interpret it as bad news and sell too, and the index falls by more than the raw foreign flow alone would justify. The foreign bloc thus moves the market twice — once through its own trades, and again through the much larger domestic flow it influences. This is why a flow that is 2% of turnover can set the tone for 100% of the index.

![Foreign net buy and sell on HOSE by year from 2019 to 2024 in trillion VND](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds-2.png)

### The 2021-2024 record net selling

The chart above tells a stark story. After modest net buying in 2019, foreigners turned net sellers in 2020 (about −16 trillion VND), sold hard in 2021 (about −58 trillion VND), bought back during the 2022 crash (about +27 trillion VND, buying the dip), sold again in 2023 (about −25 trillion VND), and then in **2024 net-sold roughly 90 trillion VND** — the heaviest annual foreign selling on record.

What drove the multi-year exit?

- **Rate differentials.** As the US Federal Reserve hiked aggressively in 2022-2023, US dollar assets paid more and the VND weakened, pulling capital out of emerging markets like Vietnam toward higher-yielding, safer dollar paper. (For the mechanism, see the macro-trading material on rate differentials and flows.)
- **No upgrade yet.** Vietnam remained a "frontier" market in the MSCI classification rather than "emerging", so the large passive emerging-market funds that *would* be forced to buy Vietnam on an upgrade simply were not allocated there. Active foreigners trimmed while waiting.
- **Better stories elsewhere.** Capital is global and impatient; India and other emerging markets offered competing narratives.

#### Worked example: a 90 trillion VND net-sell and its index drag

Suppose foreigners net-sell **90 trillion VND (about \$3.6 billion** at roughly 25,000 VND/USD) over a year, and HOSE's average daily trading value is about **17.5 trillion VND** (the 2024 figure). Spread evenly across ~250 trading days, that is **360 billion VND of net foreign selling per day** — roughly **2% of total daily turnover**.

Two percent sounds small. But concentrate it: if 80% of that selling lands in the ten largest index names (where foreigners actually trade), then in *those* stocks foreigners are dumping ~290 billion VND/day. If those ten names do, say, 4-5 trillion VND of the daily turnover, foreign selling is **6-7% of turnover in the index's spine** — and persistent one-directional selling at that scale is enough to cap rallies and set a heavy tone for the whole index. The intuition: it is not the share of *total* volume that matters, it is the share of volume *in the stocks that move the index*, and there foreigners loom large.

The flip side is the upside case. The same concentration that makes foreign selling a drag makes foreign *buying* a powerful tailwind — which is exactly what an emerging-market upgrade is expected to trigger, as passive funds are forced to allocate billions into the same large caps.

## How the cap throttles the capital an upgrade would unlock

Now we can see the full tension, and it is the punchline of this post. Vietnam's market is small relative to its economy and wants to grow.

![Vietnam stock market capitalization as a percent of GDP from 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds-6.png)

Market cap as a share of GDP climbed from about 26% in 2014 to a peak near 93% in 2021, then settled around 60% — respectable but far from a deep developed market. To deepen, the market needs more capital, and the largest available pool is foreign. The single biggest catalyst on the horizon is reclassification from **frontier** to **emerging** by MSCI and FTSE, which would mechanically funnel billions of passive dollars into Vietnamese large caps.

![Matrix showing what foreign ownership caps protect versus what they cost across control, pricing, and upgrade](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds-7.png)

But here is the bind. The same FOL that protects strategic control is one of the things index providers cite as a barrier: when the most attractive stocks are perpetually full of room, a passive fund forced to track the index *cannot actually buy them at the index weight* without paying premiums or finding no shares at all. A cap that makes the best stocks uninvestable for foreigners is, by definition, a cap on the capital an upgrade would deliver. Loosening the room — through higher FOLs, NVDRs, or cleaner access plumbing — is precisely the lever that unlocks the upgrade. (The dedicated upgrade post linked below walks through the full checklist Vietnam must clear.)

So the room is not a technicality. It is a deliberate national decision about how much of the world's savings is allowed to fund Vietnamese firms — and adjusting it is the central trade-off of Vietnam's market development. Caps buy control; they cost capital. The upgrade is the moment a country decides the capital is worth more than the extra increment of control.

### The mechanics of an upgrade's flow

Why do markets care so intensely about a label change from "frontier" to "emerging"? Because of how **passive money** works. A passive fund — an index tracker or ETF benchmarked to, say, MSCI Emerging Markets — does not choose stocks; it is *obligated* to hold whatever the index holds, at the index's weights. The MSCI Emerging Markets index is tracked by trillions of dollars. The moment Vietnam enters that index at some weight, every passive fund tracking it must buy Vietnamese stocks to match — not because a manager likes Vietnam, but because the rules force it.

That is forced, price-insensitive, large-scale buying concentrated in exactly the large-cap names that carry the most index weight — the same banks and consumer leaders that today sit full of room. Estimates for the one-time passive inflow on a Vietnam upgrade have ranged from a few billion to upwards of \$5-8 billion, with more following from active emerging-market funds that benchmark to the same index. Against a market doing 17.5 trillion VND (~\$700M) of trading a day, even a few billion dollars of forced buying is enormous.

#### Worked example: the upgrade's forced buying versus the cap

Suppose an upgrade triggers **\$5 billion** of passive inflow, of which 60% is mandated into the ten largest VN-Index names. That is **\$3 billion** that *must* buy the top ten — but several of those names are full-room banks capped at 30% with no shares available to foreigners at the local price.

- The passive funds cannot simply not buy — the index says hold them.
- So they pay whatever it costs on the foreign-to-foreign board, bidding premiums *up*, or they buy NVDRs and ETFs if those exist.
- If neither route can absorb \$3 billion, the index provider's own rules strain: a market you cannot actually buy at index weight is a market that fails the "investability" test.

This is the bind in numbers: the upgrade *delivers* the foreign capital, but the FOL *blocks* it from reaching the very stocks it is mandated to buy. The intuition: a cap and an upgrade pull in opposite directions, and the only way to make both work is to relax the room — by raising FOLs, by NVDRs, or both — which is exactly why every Vietnamese capital-market roadmap puts them at the center.

## Common misconceptions

**"Foreigners can't buy Vietnamese stocks."** They can — fully, on the open market, for any name that still has room, which is most of the market. The limit only binds on a subset of full-room favorites. The friction is real but the door is open.

**"The premium board price is the 'real' price."** No. The premium reflects access scarcity, not company value. A domestic investor buys the same share at the lower local price. The premium measures the FOL, not the business — two investors pay different prices for the identical claim.

**"Foreigners dominate the market, so their flows obviously move it."** The opposite is true on *volume*: domestic retail does most of the trading. Foreigners move the index not by volume but by *concentration* in index-heavy large caps and by the *sentiment signal* their net flow sends to domestic investors.

**"An NVDR is the same as owning the stock."** Economically yes, politically no. An NVDR holder gets the dividends and the price, but **no vote**. That missing vote is the entire point: it is what lets foreign capital in without handing over control, and it is why a control-protective government can accept NVDRs where it rejects raising the raw cap.

**"The FOL is just protectionism with no cost to Vietnam."** It has a clear cost: it rations exactly the foreign capital an emerging-market upgrade would unlock, and it forces premiums and workarounds that make the market more expensive and opaque for the patient money it most wants. The cap is a genuine trade-off, not a free lunch.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The full-room banks.** For years, Vietnam's best-run private banks ran with foreign room at or near the cap, trading at visible foreign-to-foreign premiums while their domestic-priced shares sat lower on screen. Foreign funds that wanted them had to queue for room or pay up — a daily, concrete demonstration of the FOL at work.

**The 2024 record exit.** The roughly 90 trillion VND of net foreign selling in 2024 coincided with a still-frontier classification, a strong dollar, and high US rates. The VN-Index spent much of the year grinding sideways near 1,200-1,270 despite a recovering domestic economy — a textbook case of concentrated foreign selling capping an index even as domestic accounts (over 9 million by 2024) kept growing.

![HOSE average daily trading value from 2018 to 2024 in trillion VND](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-and-ownership-limits-the-room-and-the-workarounds-8.png)

**The liquidity boom.** Average daily trading value on HOSE exploded from about 5 trillion VND in 2018 to over 21 trillion in 2021 as domestic retail accounts flooded in, then settled near 17.5 trillion by 2024. That domestic depth is what *reduced* foreigners' share of total volume even as their flows stayed pivotal at the top names — and it is part of why Vietnam now meets the *size and liquidity* tests for an upgrade, leaving access and the FOL as the binding constraints.

**The NVDR debate.** Vietnam's repeated inclusion of an NVDR-style instrument in capital-market development roadmaps — modeled explicitly on Thailand's two-decade-old system — is the clearest signal that policymakers know the cap is throttling capital and are looking for a way to let economics flow without surrendering control.

## The takeaway: the room is a dial on how much the world can fund Vietnam

If you remember one thing, make it this: **the foreign ownership limit is a control instrument with a capital cost.** It guarantees that strategic Vietnamese firms stay in domestic hands, and in exchange it rations the foreign savings that would otherwise pour into the market's best names — forcing premiums, spawning NVDR and ETF and swap workarounds, and standing as one of the last barriers to an emerging-market upgrade.

That makes "the room" a perfect lens on the spine of this whole series. A capital market grows by attracting capital. Vietnam has built the engines — a primary market that creates securities, a secondary market deep enough now to trade them, and plumbing (VSDC, custody, the IICA) to settle them. What it has *deliberately* capped is how much of that machine the world is allowed to own. Every debate about raising the FOL, adopting NVDRs, or smoothing the access rails is really one debate: *how much foreign capital do we let in, and how much control do we keep?*

Watch the daily foreign net number, watch the premiums on full-room names, and watch the upgrade timeline. They are three readouts on the same dial. When Vietnam finally turns it — and the structure of every reform plan says it intends to — the capital that has been waiting outside the room is what reprices the market.
