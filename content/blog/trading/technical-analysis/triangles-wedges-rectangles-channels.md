> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When price stops trending and starts compressing into a triangle, wedge, rectangle, or channel, it is telling you that supply and demand have reached a *temporary* equilibrium. The boundaries you draw around that compression are not magic — they are sloped support and resistance, and the pattern matters because it hands you three things at once: a defined breakout point, a measured target, and a tight invalidation.
>
> - A **trendline** is dynamic support or resistance: a sloped line through two or more turning points where supply or demand keeps showing up. The patterns in this post are just pairs of these lines.
> - A **triangle** is two converging lines (ascending = flat top, rising lows; descending = the mirror; symmetrical = both sides squeezing). A **wedge** is two lines tilting the *same* way. A **rectangle** is a flat range; a **channel** is a trend running between two parallel rails.
> - The **measured target** is the pattern's widest height projected from the breakout point. An ascending triangle with a flat \$110 top and a \$10 base targets \$120; you risk a few dollars to a stop just back inside.
> - The honest part: the textbook tendencies are real but **weak**. A symmetrical triangle is close to a coin flip, an ascending triangle leans up only mildly, and the apex is a magnet for **stop-hunts** — the cleanest-looking break is often the fakeout.
> - A channel is just a trend with rails: you buy near the lower rail and sell near the upper rail until it breaks. The break ends the trend; it does not predict the next one.

Here is a pattern you will see on every chart you ever pull up, in every market that has ever traded: price trends for a while, then it stops trending and starts *coiling*. The swings get smaller. The highs and lows pinch toward each other, or run between two parallel rails, or chop sideways in a box. Draw a couple of straight lines around that compression and you have one of four shapes — a triangle, a wedge, a rectangle, or a channel. These are the most-drawn shapes in all of technical analysis, and they are also among the most misunderstood, because most people are taught the shape without ever being taught the *mechanism*.

This post is about the mechanism. By the end you should never again look at a triangle and think "it breaks up because triangles break up." You will see it for what it is: a picture of supply and demand reaching a fragile balance, bounded by lines that are nothing more than support and resistance on a slope. We will build every term from zero, ground every idea in a worked example with real dollar figures, and then push to the depth a serious trader respects — including the uncomfortable truth that the breakout-direction edge these shapes are famous for is far weaker than the textbooks pretend, and that the apex of a triangle is one of the favorite hunting grounds for the people who move size.

The diagram below is the mental model for the whole piece. There are exactly four families, and they differ only in how their two boundary lines are arranged: converging to a point (triangle), both tilting the same way (wedge), flat and parallel (rectangle), or sloped and parallel (channel). Everything else — the order-flow story, the target, the stop — falls out of that one distinction.

## Foundations: a trendline is dynamic support and resistance

Before we can talk about triangles or channels, we have to talk about the single ingredient they are all made of: the **trendline**. Get this one idea right and the rest of the post is just arrangements of it. Get it wrong and you will spend years drawing lines that look meaningful and aren't.

### What a trendline actually is

A **trendline** is a straight line drawn through two or more points on a chart where price *turned* — where a fall stopped and reversed up, or a rise stopped and reversed down. Once drawn, the line projects forward into the future, and the claim it makes is simple: *price will probably keep finding support (or resistance) along this sloped line, the same way it finds support or resistance at a flat horizontal level.*

That is why we call a trendline **dynamic support and resistance**. A normal support level — the kind covered in [support and resistance, and why levels exist](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/support-and-resistance-why-levels-exist) — is a *horizontal* price where buyers keep stepping in. A trendline is the same thing, except the price where buyers step in *moves over time*. In an uptrend, each successive low is higher than the last; connect those rising lows with a straight line and you have an **up trendline** that acts as a rising floor. Buyers are willing to support price, but only at a higher and higher price each time — so the support level itself slopes up.

The mechanism underneath is identical to a flat level. Resting buy orders, trader memory, and prior structure all cluster — but here they cluster along a slope rather than at a single price, because the market's idea of "cheap" is rising along with the trend. When price falls back to the up trendline, it is falling back to where demand has repeatedly overwhelmed supply *adjusted for the trend*. If that demand shows up again, price bounces off the line. If it doesn't, the line breaks, and the trend that the line was tracking is in question.

### How to draw a trendline without fooling yourself

You need a minimum of **two touches** to draw a trendline — two points define a line. But two points define *any* line; with only two touches you have drawn a line that is, so far, just a coincidence. The line earns credibility with each *additional* touch that respects it: a third touch, a fourth, where price comes down to the line and bounces, tells you that real participants are treating that sloped level as meaningful.

Here is the honest tension, and it is the same tension that runs through all of technical analysis. More touches make a line look more reliable — and they also make it more *obvious*, which means more traders see it, more orders cluster around it, and (as we will see in the section on the apex trap) it becomes a more attractive target for a deliberate fakeout. More touches is genuinely better evidence, right up until the line is so well-known that it becomes a liability.

There is a second trap, and it is the more common one for beginners: **over-fitting**. Over-fitting means torturing the data until it agrees with the line you wanted to draw. If you slide the line around, change the slope, ignore the wicks that poke through, and cherry-pick which lows to connect, you can make *any* trendline "work" in hindsight. A line that requires you to ignore three violations to keep two touches is not a trendline; it is a drawing. A useful discipline: draw the line that respects the *most obvious* turning points without contorting, accept that some wicks will poke through (a trendline is a zone, not a laser), and if you find yourself negotiating with the chart, you have already lost. The chart does not care what you want it to do.

The boundaries of every pattern in this post are just trendlines — sloped (or flat) support and resistance. A triangle is two trendlines converging. A channel is two parallel trendlines. Once you internalize that a pattern boundary is *the same thing* as a support or resistance level, the patterns stop being a memorized zoo of shapes and become a single idea wearing different costumes: *supply and demand are compressing, and these lines are where they keep meeting*.

### Wicks, bodies, and where to anchor the line

One practical question trips up almost every beginner: when you draw a trendline through a turning point, do you connect the *extreme* of the move (the tip of the wick — the lowest price touched) or the *body* (where the candle opened and closed)? There is no universal right answer, and that ambiguity is itself important: it is the clearest proof that a trendline is a **zone**, not a single mathematical line. The honest practice is to draw the line where it captures the *most* turning points cleanly, accept that wicks will sometimes poke through on one side and bodies on the other, and treat the line as a band a few ticks wide rather than a one-pixel boundary.

This connects directly to why false breakouts are so common. If a trendline is really a zone two or three ticks wide, then a "break" by exactly one tick is not a break at all — it is noise inside the zone. A price that pokes a hair past the line and closes back inside has not broken anything; it has tested the edge of the band and held. Reading the line as a band rather than a laser is most of what separates traders who get repeatedly faked out from traders who wait for a *decisive* close beyond the zone. Throughout this post, when I say "price breaks the line," I mean a decisive move clear of the zone, not a single tick.

### Slope and timeframe: the same shape means different things

A trendline's **slope** carries information. A gently rising up trendline (a few percent per week) describes a slow, sustainable advance — the kind that can run for a long time. A near-vertical up trendline (price rising steeply, almost parabolically) describes an unsustainable blow-off that tends to break sooner and more violently. The same is true of the patterns built from these lines: a wide, slowly-forming channel is a more durable trend than a steep, narrow one, and a small triangle that forms over a few days carries less weight than one that took months to build.

This is why **timeframe** matters so much when reading these patterns. A triangle on a 5-minute chart and a triangle on a weekly chart are the same *shape*, but they are not the same *event*. The weekly triangle represents months of accumulated supply-demand balance among large, slow participants; its breakout, when it comes, tends to be larger and more durable. The 5-minute triangle represents a few hours of intraday noise; its breakout is smaller, faster, and far more often a fakeout, because the order clusters are thinner and easier to spike through. A useful rule of thumb: the *higher* the timeframe a pattern forms on, the more weight its breakout deserves and the larger its measured move tends to be. When you read about a pattern "working," always ask: on what timeframe? The same shape is a high-conviction setup on the daily and pure noise on the one-minute.

#### Worked example: drawing an up trendline and reading the break

Suppose a stock makes a low at \$100, rallies to \$108, pulls back to a higher low at \$103, rallies to \$110, pulls back to \$106. You connect the two rising lows — \$100 and \$103 — and extend the line forward. Its slope is about \$1 of rising support per session in this toy example. A few sessions later, price falls back toward the line, which has now risen to about \$108, and bounces there to \$112. That bounce is your *third touch* — the line just earned real credibility, because the market respected a level you projected before price got there.

Now the line is at \$110 and price closes at \$108, a dollar *below* the line. That is a **trendline break**: demand failed to show up where it had three times before. It does not guarantee a crash — price can fall a little and re-establish a shallower trend — but it tells you the specific demand that defined the old uptrend is gone. You would not keep buying that line as if nothing happened. The line did its job: it gave you a place where the trend was either intact (a bounce) or broken (a close through), with a small distance between the two, which is exactly what makes it tradable. Hold onto that idea — *a defined point where you are either right or wrong, with a small gap between them* — because it is the entire reason the patterns in this post are worth studying.

## Triangles: ascending, descending, symmetrical

A **triangle** is the most common coil pattern: two trendlines that *converge* toward a point called the **apex**. As the lines pinch together, the swings get smaller and smaller — price is being squeezed into an ever-tighter range. That squeeze is the visual signature of supply and demand coming into balance. The three flavors of triangle differ only in *which* line is sloped, and that single difference encodes three completely different order-flow stories.

![Three side by side triangles showing an ascending triangle with a flat ceiling and rising lows, a descending triangle as its mirror, and a symmetrical triangle with both sides converging](/imgs/blogs/triangles-wedges-rectangles-channels-2.png)

### The ascending triangle: buyers stepping up into a fixed ceiling

An **ascending triangle** has a *flat top* and a *rising bottom*. The flat top is a horizontal resistance level — say \$110 — where sellers keep showing up with the same offer every time price reaches it. The rising bottom is an up trendline: each pullback bottoms at a higher low than the last.

Read that as an order-flow story and it is vivid. At \$110 there is a **fixed wall of supply** — perhaps a large seller working a big order, or a cluster of resting sell limit orders, that doesn't move. Every time price rallies to \$110, that supply absorbs the buying and price stalls. But on each pullback, buyers step in *sooner* — at \$103, then \$105, then \$107. The buyers are getting more aggressive; they are willing to pay up. Demand is rising into a fixed supply. The picture is of a coiled spring: as the rising lows compress against the flat ceiling, the available space shrinks, and eventually either the wall of supply at \$110 gets eaten through (price breaks up) or the buyers give up and the rising-low line breaks (price breaks down).

The textbook says an ascending triangle "breaks up," and the bias is real — but it is *mild*. Across large studies of historical patterns, ascending triangles resolve upward somewhere in the rough neighborhood of 55–70% of the time depending on how you define the pattern and the market, which is a genuine edge but nowhere near the certainty the shape's reputation implies. Roughly a third of the time it breaks down. We will make this honesty concrete in the worked examples and the scorecard figure; for now, hold the order-flow intuition (rising demand into fixed supply leans up) loosely, not religiously.

### The descending triangle: the exact mirror

A **descending triangle** is the ascending triangle flipped upside down: a *flat bottom* and a *falling top*. The flat bottom is a horizontal support level — say \$90 — where buyers keep showing up with the same bid. The falling top is a down trendline: each rally peaks at a lower high than the last.

The order-flow story is the mirror image. At \$90 there is a **fixed shelf of demand** — a large buyer, or resting buy limit orders, that doesn't move. Every dip to \$90 gets absorbed. But on each bounce, sellers step in *sooner* — at \$98, then \$96, then \$94. Sellers are getting more aggressive; they are willing to accept less. Supply is rising into a fixed demand. The coil compresses downward, and eventually either the demand shelf at \$90 gets eaten through (price breaks down) or the sellers exhaust themselves and the falling-high line breaks (price breaks up). The textbook bias is *down*, and again it is real but mild — comparable in strength to the ascending triangle's upward lean, just pointed the other way.

### The symmetrical triangle: balance, and a coin flip

A **symmetrical triangle** has *both* lines sloping: a falling top *and* a rising bottom, converging toward each other roughly symmetrically. Lower highs and higher lows at the same time. Neither side has the upper hand. Sellers are accepting less on each bounce *and* buyers are paying more on each dip. It is a genuine standoff — supply and demand compressing equally into the apex.

Because neither side is more urgent, the honest breakout direction of a symmetrical triangle is **close to a coin flip**. This is the most important thing to internalize about symmetrical triangles, and it contradicts a lot of folklore: the symmetrical triangle does *not* reliably continue the prior trend, and it does *not* have a meaningful directional edge on its own. It tells you that a move is coming (the compression has to resolve eventually) and roughly *how big* the move will be (the measured target, covered below) — but it does not tell you *which way*. That is not a flaw to be fixed with a clever indicator; it is the literal meaning of the shape. The right way to trade a near-50/50 pattern is to not pre-commit to a direction: you wait for the break, then trade in the direction it actually breaks, with a stop that pays you if you're wrong quickly. The expectancy of *that* approach — taking the break either way at a good reward-to-risk — is what makes the pattern worth trading even with no directional edge, and we will compute it explicitly later.

### What "the prior trend" does and doesn't tell you

A common refinement you will hear is that a symmetrical triangle "usually continues the prior trend" — that a triangle forming in an uptrend tends to break up, and one in a downtrend tends to break down. There is a *grain* of truth to this in some markets and some studies, because a triangle inside a strong trend is often just a *pause* in that trend (the larger force reasserts itself). But the effect is weak and unreliable enough that you should not lean on it. The prior trend is, at most, a feather on the scale — a reason to slightly favor one direction in your planning, never a reason to enter before the break confirms it. If the prior trend gave a strong directional edge, the symmetrical triangle wouldn't be near a coin flip, and it is. Use the prior-trend context to *prepare* (have a plan for the more-likely direction ready), not to *predict*.

### Where the apex falls, and the time limit

One subtle and useful feature of a triangle is that its two converging lines define an **apex** — the point where they would meet if extended — and that apex sits at a specific point in *time*, not just price. As price approaches the apex, the pattern is running out of room: the range has compressed to nearly nothing, and the breakout *must* happen soon, because price cannot keep coiling once the lines meet. A widely-used guideline is that triangles tend to resolve somewhere between roughly halfway and three-quarters of the way to the apex; a pattern that drifts all the way into the apex without breaking often produces a weak, indecisive, fakeout-prone move rather than a clean one. The practical implication: the *best* breakouts tend to come before the apex, with room still left in the pattern, while breaks that happen right at the apex — where compression is maximal and order clusters are densest — are the most likely to be hunted. This is the structural reason the apex is a trap, which we will return to in detail.

#### Worked example: a descending triangle breaking down

To balance the ascending-triangle example, walk the mirror. A stock forms a descending triangle: a flat support shelf at \$90 (a fixed buyer holding the bid) and a falling-highs trendline above it. The widest part of the triangle, near its start, was about \$8 tall (price ranged from roughly \$90 to \$98 when the pattern began). The rallies get weaker — \$98, then \$96, then \$94 — as sellers accept less and less, while the \$90 floor holds each dip.

Eventually a dip *closes below* \$90 at \$89, the floor cracks, and the demand shelf is gone. You short the breakdown at \$88.50 (below the broken floor, confirming the close) with a stop at \$92 — back inside the triangle, above the broken support, so you are out cheaply if it was a fakeout. Risk = \$92 − \$88.50 = \$3.50. The measured target projects the \$8 base *down* from the break: \$90 − \$8 = \$82. Reward = \$88.50 − \$82 = \$6.50. Reward-to-risk ≈ 1.9-to-1. **The intuition: the descending triangle is the ascending triangle's reflection in every respect — fixed demand, rising supply, a downward lean that is real but mild — and the trade is built the same way: enter on the confirmed break, target the projected base height, and stop just back inside.** As with the ascending case, the down-lean is a tendency worth a small tilt, not a certainty; descending triangles break *up* a meaningful fraction of the time, especially in strong bull markets where the overall tide is rising.

## Wedges: rising and falling

A **wedge** looks like a triangle at a glance — two converging lines, a narrowing range — but there is one crucial difference: in a wedge, *both* lines tilt the **same** direction. A rising wedge has both lines sloping up; a falling wedge has both lines sloping down. That shared tilt changes the meaning entirely, and it produces the wedge's defining quirk: **a wedge tends to break against its own slope.**

![A before and after comparison contrasting a rising wedge that tends to break down with a falling wedge that tends to break up, both narrowing as momentum fades](/imgs/blogs/triangles-wedges-rectangles-channels-3.png)

### The rising wedge: an uptrend running out of fuel

A **rising wedge** has both boundary lines sloping up, but the *lower* line (the rising-lows trendline) is steeper than the *upper* line (the rising-highs trendline), so the two converge as they climb. Price is still making higher highs and higher lows — it still looks like an uptrend — but look closer and the picture is one of *fading momentum*. Each new high is only marginally higher than the last; the upper boundary is flattening out relative to the lower one. The buyers are still in control, but barely, and they are getting less and less for their effort. The lows are catching up to the highs.

That is why a rising wedge tends to break **down**, against its upward slope. The shape is the visual signature of an advance that is exhausting itself: the rallies are getting weaker even as price grinds higher, and when the rising-lows line finally gives way, there is little demand left underneath to catch the fall. A rising wedge often appears at the *end* of an uptrend (a topping pattern) or as a counter-trend bounce inside a larger downtrend that is about to resume.

### The falling wedge: a downtrend running out of sellers

A **falling wedge** is the mirror: both lines slope down, but the *upper* line (falling highs) is steeper than the *lower* line (falling lows), so they converge as they descend. Price is still making lower highs and lower lows — it still looks like a downtrend — but the selling is *fading*. Each new low is only marginally lower than the last; the decline is losing steam. When the falling-highs line breaks, there is little supply left overhead, and price tends to pop **up**, against the wedge's downward slope. A falling wedge often marks the *end* of a downtrend or a pullback inside a larger uptrend.

### Why the distinction from a channel matters

Here is the trap that catches almost everyone: a wedge and a channel can look nearly identical at first glance, because both have two lines tilting the same way. The difference is whether the lines are **converging** or **parallel**.

A **channel** has two *parallel* rails — the distance between them stays roughly constant as they slope along. Price oscillating in a channel is a *healthy, ongoing trend*: the swings are steady, the momentum is intact, and you can keep buying the lower rail and selling the upper rail. A **wedge** has two *converging* lines — the distance between them *shrinks*. Price coiling in a wedge is a trend *losing momentum*: the range is compressing because one side is fading. The channel is a trend that's working; the wedge is a trend that's tiring. Mistake a wedge for a channel and you will keep buying the lower rail of a "channel" that is actually a rising wedge about to collapse — buying weakness right before it breaks down. The fix is mechanical: before you trade a sloped two-line pattern, check whether the rails are parallel (channel) or pinching together (wedge). It takes two seconds and it is the difference between trading with the trend and trading into its exhaustion.

#### Worked example: a rising wedge that breaks down

Let's put numbers on it. A stock has rallied for weeks and now forms a rising wedge. The rising-lows line currently sits at \$104 and the rising-highs line at \$108, so the wedge is \$4 tall at its current width and narrowing. Price has been grinding higher in smaller and smaller swings — the last "rally" only gained \$1 before stalling. The widest part of the wedge, back where it began, was about \$8 tall (it ran from roughly \$100 to \$108 when the pattern started).

Price drifts down to the \$104 lower line and, this time, slices through it and closes at \$103. That is the break — against the upward slope, as rising wedges tend to do. You short at \$103.50 on the break, with a stop at \$106 (back inside the wedge, above the broken line). Your risk is \$103.50 − \$106 = −\$2.50 — you lose \$2.50 if the break fails and price climbs back in. The **measured target** projects the wedge's widest height (\$8) down from the break: \$104 − \$8 = \$96. Reward if it works: \$103.50 − \$96 = \$7.50. So you are risking \$2.50 to make \$7.50, a reward-to-risk of about 3-to-1. **The intuition: a wedge is a trend running out of fuel, and it usually breaks the way the fuel ran out — against its own slope — so you fade the slope on the break, not with it.**

#### Worked example: a falling wedge breaking up

The mirror, for symmetry. A stock has been declining and forms a falling wedge: both lines slope down, but the falling-highs line is steeper, so the two converge. The upper (falling-highs) line currently sits at \$106 and the lower (falling-lows) line at \$102, and the selling is visibly fading — the last "decline" only dropped \$1 before buyers stepped in. The widest part of the wedge, where it began, was about \$10 tall (price ran from roughly \$112 down to \$102 as the pattern formed).

Price pushes up to the \$106 upper line and, this time, closes *above* it at \$107 — the break, against the wedge's downward slope, as falling wedges tend to resolve. You buy at \$106.50 with a stop at \$103 (back inside the wedge, below the broken line), so risk = \$106.50 − \$103 = \$3.50. The measured target projects the \$10 height *up* from the break: \$106 + \$10 = \$116. Reward = \$116 − \$106.50 = \$9.50, a reward-to-risk of about 2.7-to-1. **The intuition: the falling wedge is the rising wedge upside down — a downtrend running out of sellers — and it breaks up against its own slope, so you buy the break and target the wedge's full height projected upward.** Notice how identical the *arithmetic* is to the ascending-triangle example: enter on the confirmed break, project the widest height, stop just back inside. Once you see that every coil pattern reduces to the same three numbers — entry, target, stop — the zoo of shapes collapses into one repeatable procedure.

## Rectangles and channels

Triangles and wedges are *converging* patterns — the range shrinks toward an apex. The last two families are *non-converging*: the boundaries stay parallel. A rectangle is flat and parallel; a channel is sloped and parallel. Both are traded the same way at heart — buy the floor, sell the ceiling — but one is a range and the other is a trend.

### The rectangle: a range, which is just repeated support and resistance

A **rectangle** (also called a *trading range* or just a *range*) is price bouncing between a flat support level and a flat resistance level — a horizontal box. Say support at \$98 and resistance at \$108. Price falls to \$98, buyers absorb the selling, it bounces to \$108, sellers absorb the buying, it falls back to \$98, and so on. A rectangle is *literally* the support-and-resistance picture from the [earlier post](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/support-and-resistance-why-levels-exist), repeated several times — there is nothing new in it except the repetition.

Mechanically, a range means supply and demand are in balance *at two fixed prices*: there's a buyer big enough to hold \$98 and a seller big enough to hold \$108, and price ping-pongs between them while neither gives way. Ranges form when a market has no new information to push it anywhere — it is, in a sense, the market's resting state between trends. Most of the time, most markets are ranging, not trending, which is why learning to read a rectangle is more useful than the trend-obsessed presentation of most charting books would suggest.

You trade a rectangle two ways, and they are opposites. *Inside* the range, you fade the edges: buy near \$98 (support), sell near \$108 (resistance), betting the box holds. *At* the edge, you can trade the **break**: if price closes decisively above \$108 or below \$98, the box has resolved and a new move may begin. The measured target for a rectangle break is the box's height (\$108 − \$98 = \$10) projected from the break: a break above \$108 targets \$118; a break below \$98 targets \$88.

The two approaches are in genuine tension, and which one is right depends on a judgment you cannot fully automate: *is the range likely to hold, or is it ending?* Early in a range, when the box is freshly established and the market is clearly directionless, fading the edges is the higher-probability play — most touches of a range boundary bounce, because that is what a range *is*. Late in a range, after many tests, or when a catalyst looms, the breakout becomes more likely and fading the edge gets dangerous, because the test that breaks the box is the one that takes out everyone who faded it. A subtle tell: each test of a range boundary tends to *weaken* it slightly (the resting orders that hold the level get consumed a little each time), so the *fifth* test of resistance is more likely to break than the *first*. This is the same "levels weaken with repeated testing" mechanism from the support-and-resistance post, and it means a range cannot last forever — eventually one wall gets eaten through.

#### Worked example: fading a range edge, then flipping to the break

You are trading a stock ranging between \$98 support and \$108 resistance. On the third touch of \$98, you buy at \$98 with a stop at \$95 (risk \$3) and a target of \$107 (just below the \$108 ceiling, to sell before the crowd). It works: price bounces to \$107, you take \$9 of profit, a 3-to-1 result. You could run this fade several times.

But on the next rally, price reaches \$108 and, instead of stalling, *closes above it* at \$109.50 on heavy participation. The range has broken. Now you flip your thinking entirely: the old resistance at \$108 should become new support (polarity), and the measured move projects \$108 + \$10 = \$118. You can enter the breakout at \$109.50 with a stop at \$106.50 (back inside the old box, risk \$3) targeting \$118 — a reward of \$8.50 for \$3 of risk, about 2.8-to-1. **The intuition: a range is a setup you can milk repeatedly while it holds, and the *same* boundary you were fading becomes your breakout trigger the moment it gives way — the skill is recognizing the regime change from "range holding" to "range broken" and switching from fade to follow.**

### The channel: a trend with rails

A **channel** is a rectangle that has been tilted — two *parallel* trendlines, both sloping the same way, with price oscillating between them. An **up-channel** (also called an *ascending channel*) has both rails sloping up; price is trending higher but in an orderly, oscillating way, repeatedly bouncing off the lower rail (rising support) and stalling at the upper rail (rising resistance). A **down-channel** is the mirror, sloping down.

The key insight is that *a channel is a trend with rails*. The lower rail is dynamic support — the up trendline through the rising lows. The upper rail is dynamic resistance — a parallel line through the rising highs. As long as price keeps respecting both rails, the trend is healthy and you have a repeatable setup: buy near the lower rail, sell near the upper rail, over and over, harvesting the oscillation while riding the underlying trend.

![A rising channel with two parallel rails, the price zig-zagging between them, marked with a buy at the lower rail, a sell at the upper rail, and a stop just below](/imgs/blogs/triangles-wedges-rectangles-channels-4.png)

### Trading the rails versus trading the break

There are two completely different ways to trade a channel, and confusing them is a classic error. **Trading the rails** means playing the oscillation *while the channel holds*: you buy near the lower rail with a stop just below it, and you sell (or take profit) near the upper rail. You are betting the trend continues in its orderly way. **Trading the break** means waiting for price to *violate* a rail — to close outside the channel — and trading the new move. A break of the *lower* rail of an up-channel is a warning that the uptrend is faltering; a strong break of the *upper* rail can signal an acceleration (the trend speeding up) or, often, exhaustion.

A crucial honesty point: **the break of a channel ends the old trend; it does not predict the next one.** When an up-channel's lower rail breaks, all you know for certain is that the orderly uptrend is over. Price might reverse into a downtrend, or it might just go sideways into a range, or it might form a new, shallower up-channel. The break is information about what *stopped*, not a forecast of what *starts*. This is why "the channel broke, so I'll short" is a weaker trade than it feels — you are inferring a new trend from the death of an old one, which is a guess dressed as a signal.

### How to draw the second rail

A channel needs two parallel lines, but the price action usually only hands you one clearly. The standard construction is to draw the line you can see best — typically the one with the most clean touches — and then draw a *parallel* copy of it through the most prominent point on the other side. In an up-channel, you might first draw the lower rail through two or three rising lows (the up trendline you already know how to draw), then drag a parallel line up to touch the single highest high in between. That parallel line becomes your upper rail, your projection of where resistance should appear on future rallies.

The honest caveat is that the second rail is a *projection*, not a confirmed level, until price actually touches and respects it. A channel where price has tested *both* rails several times is a real, tradable channel. A "channel" you drew by slapping a parallel line on a single trendline, where the upper rail has never actually been touched, is closer to wishful thinking — you have assumed the trend is orderly without evidence that it is. As with everything in trendline work, the more genuine touches each rail has, the more the channel has earned your trust; a rail price has respected three times is a level, while a rail price has never reached is a guess.

#### Worked example: trading a channel by the rails

You identify a clean up-channel. The lower rail (rising support) currently sits at \$98 and the upper rail (rising resistance) at \$108 — the channel is about \$10 wide and sloping up over time. Price pulls back to the lower rail and touches \$98. You buy at \$98 with a stop at \$96 — just below the rail, so that if the rail fails (the channel breaks), you are out quickly with a \$2 loss. Your target is the upper rail at \$108.

If it works, you make \$108 − \$98 = \$10 per share, risking \$2 to make \$10 — a reward-to-risk of 5-to-1 on this single oscillation. Because a channel gives you *repeated* touches, you can take this trade again on the next pullback to the (now higher) lower rail, and again, as long as the channel holds. Each trade risks the small distance to your stop and targets the full width of the channel. **The intuition: a channel turns a trend into a repeatable, defined-risk setup — buy the lower rail, sell the upper, with a tight stop just outside — and the edge comes from the lopsided geometry, risking the narrow gap to the rail to make the full channel width.** The day the lower rail breaks and your \$96 stop fills, the setup is simply over; you do not keep buying a rail that no longer holds.

#### Worked example: not every touch reaches the far rail

Real channels are messy, and a worked example that only shows the clean case is dishonest. Suppose you buy the lower rail again at the next pullback — now at \$100, because the channel has risen — with a stop at \$98 (risk \$2) and a target at the upper rail, now \$110. But this time the rally stalls at \$107, two-thirds of the way across, and rolls back over. What do you do?

This is where managing the trade matters more than the entry. If you took the full ride hoping for \$110, you watched a \$7 open profit shrink back toward your entry. A more robust approach inside a channel is to *scale out* — sell part of the position into strength as price approaches the upper rail, rather than betting on a precise touch of \$110. If you'd sold half at \$106 (a \$6 gain on that half) and trailed a stop up under the rest, you would have banked most of the move even though price never reached the upper rail. **The intuition: in a channel, the *next* touch of the far rail is never guaranteed, so treat the measured target (the opposite rail) as a zone to harvest into, not a price the market owes you — the geometry gives you a great reward-to-risk, but only if you actually take the reward.** This same discipline applies to every measured target in this post.

## The breakout, the target, and the apex trap

Every coil pattern eventually resolves with a **breakout** — price closing decisively outside one of the boundary lines. The breakout is the moment the temporary equilibrium ends and a directional move begins. This section is about the three things that matter at the breakout: how to compute the target, how to recognize the false breakout, and why the obvious line gets hunted.

![A tree diagram showing that every coil pattern provides three things, a defined entry at the boundary break, a measured target from the widest height, and a tight invalidation stop just inside the pattern](/imgs/blogs/triangles-wedges-rectangles-channels-8.png)

### The measured move: the pattern's widest height, projected

The **measured move** (or *measured target*) is the standard way to set a profit target on a coil-pattern breakout, and it is wonderfully simple: take the **widest height** of the pattern — the vertical distance between the two boundaries at the pattern's widest point, usually near where it began — and project that same distance from the breakout point in the direction of the break.

For an ascending triangle with a flat top at \$110 whose base (the widest part) was \$10 tall, an upside breakout targets \$110 + \$10 = \$120. For a rectangle \$10 tall breaking up from \$108, the target is \$108 + \$10 = \$118. For a wedge \$8 tall breaking down from \$104, the target is \$104 − \$8 = \$96. The logic is a rough one: the pattern represents a certain amount of "stored energy" (the range it compressed from), and the breakout tends to travel about that far before running into new supply or demand. It is a heuristic, not a law — sometimes price overshoots the target wildly, sometimes it falls short — but it gives you a *defined, objective* target set in advance, which is far better than improvising one in the heat of the move.

### The false breakout near the apex, and the volume question

A **false breakout** (or **fakeout**) is when price closes outside the boundary — triggering everyone who was waiting for the break — and then *reverses back inside* the pattern, leaving the breakout traders trapped on the wrong side. False breakouts are common, and they are most common near the **apex** of a triangle, where the pattern has compressed to its tightest and the obvious lines are at their most obvious.

The classic confirmation tool is **volume** — the number of shares or contracts traded. The textbook says a *real* breakout comes on *expanding* volume (lots of participation, conviction behind the move), while a *false* breakout comes on weak volume (few participants, no conviction). There is truth in this: a breakout that nobody trades is suspect. But the volume signal is **shaky** in practice. Volume can spike on a fakeout just as easily as on a real break — in fact a deliberate stop-hunt *produces* a volume spike, because triggering a cluster of stop orders is itself a burst of trading. And in many modern markets, especially 24-hour crypto, volume is noisy, fragmented across venues, and easy to misread. Treat expanding volume as *mild* supporting evidence for a breakout, never as proof. A breakout on heavy volume can still be a fakeout, and a quiet breakout can still be real.

### Why the obvious trendline gets hunted

Here is the part the textbooks tend to leave out. The reason the apex is so treacherous is the same reason any obvious level is treacherous: **the obvious line is where everyone's orders cluster, which makes it the most profitable place to run a fakeout.** When a triangle compresses toward its apex, every chart-reader on the planet has drawn the same two lines. Breakout traders place buy stop orders just above the upper line (to enter automatically if it breaks up) and sell stop orders just below the lower line. Traders already positioned inside the pattern place their protective stops just outside the lines too. The result is a dense cluster of resting stop and breakout orders piled just beyond the obvious boundaries.

That cluster is a target. A large participant who wants to *buy* a lot can push price *down* through the lower line, triggering the stop-sell and breakout-sell orders parked there, which floods the market with forced selling — and the large buyer quietly absorbs all of it at a good price, then lets price snap back up. To a chart-reader it looks like a downside breakout that instantly failed. It wasn't a breakout at all; it was a **stop-hunt**, a deliberate spike engineered to harvest the orders the obvious line attracted. The cleaner and more obvious the pattern, the more orders pile up at its edges, and the more inviting the hunt.

![A price chart showing a triangle compressing into its apex, then a fake spike up through the obvious line into a stop-hunt zone, followed by a sharp reversal back inside the triangle](/imgs/blogs/triangles-wedges-rectangles-channels-6.png)

This is not a reason to ignore patterns — it is a reason to *trade them with humility*. It is why so many experienced traders wait for a *close* beyond the line rather than reacting to the first wick through it, why they prefer to enter on a *retest* (price breaks out, comes back to the line, and the old boundary now holds as new support or resistance), and why they keep their stops *back inside* the pattern rather than right at the obvious spot where everyone else's stops sit. The deeper treatment of how to tell a real break from a hunted one — and how to position for the fakeout itself — is the subject of [breakouts versus fakeouts](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/breakouts-vs-fakeouts), the sibling post to this one.

### Three ways to enter a breakout, and what each costs

There is no single "correct" breakout entry — there is a tradeoff between getting a better price and getting more confirmation, and you pick your spot on that spectrum based on how much fakeout risk you can stomach.

The most aggressive entry is the **anticipatory** one: you buy *inside* the pattern, near the lower line of an ascending triangle for instance, betting the break will come the way you expect. This gets you the best possible price and the tightest stop, but you are exposed to the pattern breaking the *other* way, and you have no confirmation at all. It is the highest-reward, highest-risk entry, suited to traders who are confident in the directional read and willing to be wrong often.

The middle entry is the **break** itself: you enter when price closes decisively beyond the line — \$110.50 in our ascending-triangle example. You have *some* confirmation (a close beyond the zone, not just a wick) but you are still vulnerable to the fakeout that closes beyond the line and then reverses. This is the textbook entry and the one most of our worked examples use.

The most conservative entry is the **retest**: you wait for the breakout, then wait *again* for price to pull back to the broken line and hold it as new support (this is **polarity** — old resistance becoming new support — covered in the [support and resistance post](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/support-and-resistance-why-levels-exist)). The retest is the highest-confirmation entry because the fakeouts have mostly already washed out: a level that breaks and then *holds on the retest* is behaving like a real break, not a hunt. The cost is that many of the strongest breakouts never look back — they run straight to the target — so by waiting for a retest you miss the best moves entirely. You trade a higher hit rate for fewer trades and a worse average price.

There is no free lunch among these three. The aggressive entry catches every move but eats every fakeout; the retest entry dodges most fakeouts but misses the runaway breaks. Most traders settle on the break-with-confirmation as a compromise, or split the difference — a partial position on the break, adding on the retest. The right choice depends on your fakeout tolerance and your patience, not on which one is "correct."

![An ascending triangle with a flat ceiling at one hundred ten dollars, rising lows, a breakout entry at one hundred ten fifty, a measured target zone at one hundred twenty, and a stop at one hundred seven, with the reward to risk annotated](/imgs/blogs/triangles-wedges-rectangles-channels-5.png)

#### Worked example: an ascending triangle breakout and its reward-to-risk

Now the headline example, with every number explicit. A stock forms an ascending triangle. The flat top — the resistance ceiling — is at \$110. The rising-lows trendline started near \$100, so the **base** of the triangle (its widest height) is \$110 − \$100 = \$10. Over several weeks, the lows climb — \$100, \$103, \$106 — squeezing toward the \$110 ceiling.

Price finally closes above \$110. You enter the breakout at **\$110.50** (a little above the line, to confirm the close rather than chase the first wick). You compute the **measured target** by projecting the \$10 base from the breakout: \$110 + \$10 = **\$120**. You place your **stop at \$107** — back inside the triangle, below the most recent higher low, so the trade is invalidated if price falls back in and the breakout proves false.

Now the arithmetic that decides whether this is a good trade:

- **Risk per share** = entry − stop = \$110.50 − \$107 = **\$3.50**.
- **Reward per share** = target − entry = \$120 − \$110.50 = **\$9.50**.
- **Reward-to-risk ratio** = \$9.50 ÷ \$3.50 ≈ **2.7-to-1**.

So you are risking \$3.50 to make \$9.50. If you trade 100 shares, you risk \$350 to make \$950. **The intuition: the ascending triangle's value is not its mild upward bias — it's that it hands you a defined entry, a measured target, and a tight stop in one package, so even a coin-flip-ish edge can be profitable when the winners are nearly three times the size of the losers.** That last clause is the whole game, and it connects directly to expectancy, which we tackle next.

#### Worked example: breakout-direction honesty and expectancy

Let's confront the honesty point head-on with numbers, because this is where most pattern-trading advice quietly falls apart. Suppose you trade *symmetrical* triangle breakouts — the near-coin-flip pattern — by taking the break in whichever direction it actually breaks, at a **2-to-1** reward-to-risk (you risk \$1 to make \$2 on each trade, in R-multiples, where 1R is the dollar amount you risk). Suppose your honest hit rate is **55%** — slightly better than a coin flip, because catching the *real* break direction once it's revealed is marginally easier than guessing in advance, and you're filtering for clean setups.

Your expectancy per trade, in R-multiples, is:

$$E = (p_{\text{win}} \times R_{\text{win}}) - (p_{\text{loss}} \times R_{\text{loss}}) = (0.55 \times 2) - (0.45 \times 1) = 1.10 - 0.45 = +0.65R$$

where $p_{\text{win}}$ is the win probability (0.55), $R_{\text{win}}$ is the average win in R (2), $p_{\text{loss}}$ is the loss probability (0.45), and $R_{\text{loss}}$ is the average loss in R (1). A positive expectancy of **+0.65R per trade** means that over many trades, you expect to make about two-thirds of your risk amount, on average, every time you trade. If you risk \$100 per trade, that's about \$65 of expected profit per trade, before costs. Over 100 such trades, roughly +65R, or about \$6,500 on \$100 risk units.

Now watch how fragile that is. Drop the reward-to-risk to **1-to-1** (you risk \$1 to make \$1) and keep the 55% hit rate: $E = (0.55 \times 1) - (0.45 \times 1) = +0.10R$ — still positive, but barely, and easily erased by trading costs and the occasional fakeout. Drop the hit rate to a true **50%** at 1-to-1 and $E = 0$ — you're a coin flip paying the house a spread, which is a slow way to go broke. **The intuition: the symmetrical triangle has essentially no directional edge, so the *only* thing that makes it tradable is the reward-to-risk geometry — you must risk much less than you aim to make, because your win rate alone will not save you.** The full machinery of why a high win rate can still lose money, and a low one can still win, lives in [expectancy, and why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies). The short version: in pattern trading, the *size* of your wins versus losses matters far more than how often you're right.

## Common misconceptions

These are the beliefs that get beginners into trouble. Each one feels intuitive, and each one is wrong in a way that costs money.

**"Ascending triangles always break up (and descending always break down)."** No. The upward lean of an ascending triangle is real but *mild* — it resolves up perhaps 55–70% of the time depending on the study and the market, which means it breaks *down* roughly a quarter to a third of the time. Treating the upward break as a near-certainty is how you end up holding a long position into a downside break with no plan. The correct stance is to *prepare for both directions* and let the actual break tell you which way to trade — with a stop that gets you out cheaply when the "obvious" direction doesn't happen. A mild bias is worth a small tilt in position sizing, not a religious conviction.

**"The more trendline touches, the more reliable the line."** Half true, and the false half is dangerous. More touches *are* better evidence that real participants respect the line — up to a point. But the more touches a line has, the more *obvious* it is, the more traders are watching it, and the more orders cluster at its edges, which makes it a *better target for a fakeout*, not a worse one. A line with eight perfect touches is not eight times safer than a line with three; it may be more dangerous, because everyone's stops are sitting in the same place. Worse, chasing more touches tempts you into **over-fitting** — sliding the line and ignoring violations to manufacture touches that aren't really there. Two or three clean, honest touches beat five tortured ones every time.

**"A wedge is the same as a channel."** This is the single most expensive confusion in this post. They look similar — two lines tilting the same way — but a channel's rails are *parallel* (steady momentum, a healthy trend you can keep trading by the rails), while a wedge's lines *converge* (fading momentum, a trend that's tiring and likely to break against its slope). If you trade a rising wedge as if it were an up-channel, you will keep buying its lower line — buying weakness — right up until it collapses. Always check: parallel or pinching? That one glance changes the trade completely.

**"The apex is the best entry, because the pattern is about to resolve."** The opposite is closer to the truth. The apex is the *most dangerous* place to enter, because it is where the pattern is tightest, where the lines are most obvious, where the order clusters are densest, and therefore where stop-hunts and false breakouts are most likely. Patterns frequently fake out *at* the apex and then break *for real* only after a messy shakeout. Entering on the first poke through an apex line is precisely the trade the hunters are hoping you'll take. Waiting for a confirmed close, or a retest, costs you a little of the move but saves you from the bulk of the fakeouts.

**"A measured target is a price the market is obligated to reach."** It is a *heuristic*, not a promise. The measured move is a reasonable, objective, set-in-advance estimate of how far a breakout might travel, derived from the pattern's height. Sometimes price blows past it; often price falls short and reverses before reaching it. Treat it as a planning tool for setting a target and computing reward-to-risk *before* you enter, not as a guarantee you can lean your whole position on. Many traders scale out *into* the target zone rather than betting on a precise touch.

**"A pattern that fails was a bad pattern."** No — failure is part of the distribution, not evidence the analysis was wrong. If a setup wins 60% of the time, it *loses* 40% of the time *by design*, and any given loss tells you nothing about whether you read the chart correctly. The mistake is treating each trade as a verdict on your skill rather than one draw from a probabilistic process. The way you judge a pattern strategy is over *many* trades, by its expectancy, not over one trade by whether it happened to work. This is the single most important mental shift in all of pattern trading, and it is exactly why the [expectancy post](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies) is the companion to this one: a perfectly-read pattern can lose, a sloppily-read one can win, and only the aggregate matters.

**"Bigger, longer patterns are always more reliable than small ones."** Mostly true and worth a caveat. A pattern that forms over months on a daily chart does generally deserve more weight than one that forms over an hour on a 5-minute chart — more participants, more accumulated supply-demand information, a larger and more durable measured move. But "bigger" is not a license to skip the same discipline: a months-long ascending triangle can still break down, still get faked out at the apex, and still demand a stop. The timeframe raises the *base rate* of a clean resolution; it does not turn a probabilistic setup into a certain one. Respect the bigger pattern more, but trade it with the same humility.

## How it shows up in real markets

Patterns are easiest to believe when you've seen them play out at scale. Here are several named, concrete examples across different markets and eras. As always, these are descriptions of historical price behavior as-of the dates given, not predictions and not advice; the point is to see the mechanism, not to imply any of it repeats on command.

### A multi-month ascending triangle that broke out cleanly

Through the back half of 2020 and into early 2021, large-cap technology stocks repeatedly built ascending-triangle-like structures: a flat ceiling where supply kept capping rallies, and a rising series of higher lows as buyers stepped in earlier on each dip. Apple's shares, for instance, spent stretches of that period grinding into prior highs with rising lows beneath, and when the ceiling finally gave way, price extended in roughly the measured-move fashion before finding new resistance. The mechanism on display is exactly the order-flow story from earlier: fixed supply at a known price, rising demand compressing into it, and an upside resolution once the wall was eaten through. The lesson is not "this stock goes up" — it's that the *shape* encoded a real supply-demand standoff, and the break encoded its resolution. As-of any specific date, the pattern was a probabilistic setup, not a certainty; plenty of similar-looking ascending triangles in other names broke the other way.

### A rising wedge top in a crypto rally

Bitcoin's parabolic advance into its November 2021 all-time high near \$69,000 traced, on several timeframes, the classic rising-wedge signature: price kept making higher highs and higher lows, but the highs were coming with less and less force — each new peak only marginally above the last — while the lows compressed upward beneath them. The momentum was visibly fading even as the price headline kept rising. When the lower line gave way in the following weeks, there was little demand underneath, and the decline was severe and sustained through 2022. This is the rising wedge's whole thesis made vivid: an uptrend running out of fuel, breaking *against* its own upward slope. Crypto is a useful teacher here precisely because its moves are large and its sentiment swings are extreme, which makes the "fading momentum into a wedge" mechanism unusually legible. (As-of late 2021; the figures are historical.)

### A long rectangle range in an index

Stock indices spend large fractions of their time *ranging*, not trending, and those ranges are textbook rectangles. The S&P 500, for example, has repeatedly carved out multi-month horizontal boxes — bounded by a clear support shelf and a clear resistance ceiling — during periods when the macro picture offered no decisive push in either direction (various stretches across 2015, 2018, and 2023, depending on which box you measure). Inside such a range, the tradable behavior is the rectangle's bread-and-butter: price fades the ceiling and bounces off the floor, again and again, until a catalyst arrives and the box resolves with a measured-move-sized thrust in the breakout direction. The everyday usefulness of recognizing a rectangle is that it stops you from forcing trend trades on a market that is, for the moment, simply oscillating — and it tells you where the edges are so you can trade the oscillation or wait for the break. (Ranges cited as-of their respective periods.)

### A channel that defined a multi-year trend, then broke

The clearest channels show up in long, orderly trends. The U.S. equity bull market from 2009 through early 2020 spent long stretches inside well-defined ascending channels: price would touch a rising lower rail, bounce to a rising upper rail, pull back to the lower rail, and so on, with the rails staying roughly parallel for years. Traders who recognized the channel could buy the lower rail and sell the upper, harvesting the oscillation while the trend ran. The instructive part is the *end*: when the COVID crash of February–March 2020 sliced through the lower rail, the multi-year channel was over — but, true to the honesty point, the break of that channel did not by itself predict what came next. What followed was not an orderly downtrend but a violent crash and an equally violent recovery into a new, steeper channel. The break told you the old orderly trend had ended; it did not forecast the next regime. (As-of the 2009–2020 window and the early-2020 break.)

### A descending triangle that broke the "wrong" way

It is worth ending the case studies with a *failure* of the textbook bias, because honesty requires it. Descending triangles are supposed to break down — and many do — but in strong uptrending markets, plenty of them break *up* instead, as the overall bull tide overwhelms the local supply-demand picture. Individual growth stocks during the 2020–2021 advance repeatedly formed descending triangles (falling highs into a flat support shelf) that "should" have broken down by the textbook, only to break *up* and continue higher, trapping the traders who shorted the "obvious" breakdown. The mechanism is exactly the honesty point of this whole post: the directional bias of a pattern is a mild local tendency that a stronger force — here, a powerful bull market — can simply override. A trader who shorted every descending triangle as a sure thing, ignoring the dominant uptrend, would have been repeatedly stopped out. The lesson is to read the pattern *in context*: a descending triangle in a downtrend is a higher-conviction short than the same shape fighting a roaring bull market, where the tide is against you. (As-of the 2020–2021 window; described generically.)

### A symmetrical triangle resolving on news

Symmetrical triangles often form ahead of a known catalyst — an earnings report, a central-bank decision, a major economic release — because the market is genuinely undecided and compresses while it waits. Currency pairs and individual stocks routinely coil into symmetrical triangles in the days before such an event, then break hard in whichever direction the news dictates. The pattern correctly told you a big move was coming and roughly how big (the measured target), but it had *no* idea which way — the direction was decided by information the chart couldn't contain. This is the symmetrical triangle's honest signature in the wild: a reliable *magnitude* signal and a near-worthless *direction* signal, which is exactly why you trade the break rather than the anticipation. (Behavior described generically; any specific instance is as-of its event date.)

## When this matters to you / further reading

If you take one thing from this post, make it this: the coil patterns are not a bestiary of magical shapes that "predict" the market. They are pictures of supply and demand reaching a temporary balance, bounded by trendlines that are nothing more than sloped support and resistance. Their genuine value is structural, not predictive — each one hands you a *defined breakout point*, a *measured target* from its widest height, and a *tight invalidation* just back inside the pattern. That package is what lets you compute a reward-to-risk *before* you enter and decide, on numbers rather than hope, whether a trade is worth taking.

Where this touches you directly: the moment you can tell a converging pattern (triangle, wedge — a coil that *must* resolve) from a non-converging one (rectangle, channel — a state you can trade repeatedly), and the moment you can tell a parallel channel from a pinching wedge, you have most of what you need to read a sideways or coiling market instead of standing around waiting for a "trend." And the moment you internalize that the textbook break direction is a mild tendency rather than a law — and that the obvious line is the one most likely to be hunted — you stop being the predictable order flow that the fakeout is designed to harvest.

For the natural next steps: the sibling post on [breakouts versus fakeouts](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/breakouts-vs-fakeouts) goes deep on telling a real break from a hunted one and on how to position for the fakeout itself; [continuation patterns: flags and pennants](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants) covers the *short, sharp* coil patterns that punctuate a strong trend rather than ending it; the foundational [support and resistance, and why levels exist](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/support-and-resistance-why-levels-exist) explains the order-flow machinery beneath every trendline here; and [expectancy, and why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies) is the piece that turns "this pattern works X% of the time" into an actual decision about whether to trade it. Patterns give you the structure; expectancy tells you whether the structure pays.
