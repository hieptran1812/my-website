> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Trends do not move in a straight line; they pause, and those pauses form recognizable shapes — flags, pennants, and rectangle-as-pause patterns — that resolve in the trend's original direction more often than not.
>
> - A *flag* is a sharp *pole* (an impulsive move) followed by a shallow *counter-trend channel* (a gentle pullback that drifts against the trend). A *pennant* is the same pole followed by a *converging triangle* that coils to a point. Both are pauses, not reversals.
> - The honest mechanism is mundane: after a fast move, early buyers take profits and new buyers wait for a better price, so supply and demand reach a *temporary* balance. The flag is what that balance looks like on a chart. No magic, no hidden hand.
> - These patterns matter for two practical reasons: they give a *defined-risk entry* to rejoin a trend (buy the breakout, stop just beyond the flag), and a *measured move* target — project the pole's height above the breakout — with a real, if rough, statistical basis.
> - The single number to remember from our worked example: a \$10 pole (price ran from \$100 to \$110), a flag that drifts back to \$107, a breakout entry at \$108.50 with a stop at \$106, and a measured target of \$117 — that is a **\$8.50 reward against a \$2.50 risk, a reward-to-risk of about 3.4 to 1.**
> - But continuation is a *tendency, not a law*. Flags fail. A flag that breaks the wrong way is not noise — it is information, and a tight stop just beyond the flag is the entire reason the failure costs you a small, planned amount instead of a large, surprising one.

"The trend is your friend — until the bend at the end." You have probably seen the rhyme. What nobody tells you is what the friend does *in between*: it stops to catch its breath. A strong move up does not climb forever in a clean diagonal line. It surges, it rests, it surges again. Those rests are not random pauses — they fall into a small family of shapes that show up again and again across every market that has ever been charted, from a 1920s railroad stock to a 2021 cryptocurrency. This article is about reading those shapes honestly: what they are, why they form, what they actually predict, and — just as important — how often they lie.

![A bull flag mental model showing a sharp pole from one hundred to one hundred ten dollars, a shallow pullback flag drifting to one hundred seven, a breakout entry at one hundred eight fifty, a measured target at one hundred seventeen, and a stop at one hundred six](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post, and you should keep it in your head as you read. Price runs hard from \$100 to \$110 — that vertical-ish run is the *pole*. Then it stops climbing and drifts gently back down to \$107 in a tidy little channel that slopes *against* the move — that drift is the *flag*. When price pushes back up through the top of the flag at \$108.50, that is the *breakout*, and it is the moment a trader rejoins the trend with a known risk: a stop just below the flag at \$106. The expectation, if the pattern behaves, is that price travels roughly the height of the pole again — another \$10 — to a *measured-move target* near \$117. Every section below is detail hung on that one picture.

This is educational material about how price patterns are described and traded, not advice to buy or sell anything. We are going to be relentlessly honest about the parts that work, the parts that are folklore, and the parts that are simply a coin flip dressed up in a name. This post sits alongside several companions in the same series. It builds directly on [trend and market structure](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trend-and-market-structure) — you cannot have a *continuation* pattern without first having a trend to continue — and it is a close sibling of [breakouts versus fakeouts](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/breakouts-vs-fakeouts), since a flag resolves with a breakout that may or may not hold. It also leans on [support and resistance, and why levels exist](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/support-and-resistance-why-levels-exist) for why the edges of a flag matter, and on [expectancy, and why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies) for the honest accounting of whether trading these patterns actually makes money.

## Foundations: why a trend pauses

Before we can name the shapes, we need to be clear about what a trend is and why a trending market ever stops to rest. Get the mechanism right and the patterns stop looking like mystical chart-magic and start looking like the obvious consequence of a few people taking profits.

### A quick recap of trend

A *trend* is not a vibe; it is a structure you can read mechanically off a chart. An *uptrend* is a sequence of *higher highs* (each peak above the last peak) and *higher lows* (each trough above the last trough) — a staircase climbing to the right. A *downtrend* is the mirror image: lower highs and lower lows. Everything else is a *range*, a sideways market with no clean direction. That is the whole definition, developed at length in [trend and market structure](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trend-and-market-structure); we only need the skeleton here.

The crucial property for our purposes is the one in that definition that beginners skip over: **a trend climbs in pushes and pulls back between them.** It is a staircase, not a ramp. The pushes are the part everyone notices; the pullbacks are the part everyone misreads as "the trend is breaking." In fact the pullbacks are *where the continuation patterns live*. A flag is just a particular, well-behaved pullback — one orderly enough to have a shape, an entry, and a target.

### The flagpole: an impulse move

The first ingredient of every continuation pattern is the *pole*, also called the *flagpole* or the *impulse leg*. An *impulse move* is a sharp, fast, one-directional run in price — the kind of move where buyers (in an uptrend) overwhelm sellers and price gaps upward in a near-vertical line on the chart. In our running example, price runs from \$100 to \$110 in a handful of bars. That \$10 vertical run is the pole.

Why does an impulse happen at all? Usually because something tipped the balance decisively in one direction: an earnings surprise, a piece of news, a break above a long-watched resistance level that triggered a wave of buying, or simply a cascade of buy orders that found no sellers willing to hold them back. The defining feature of a pole is that it is *fast and steep* — price covers a lot of ground in little time, and volume (the number of shares or contracts traded) is typically heavy, because a lot of people are transacting in a hurry.

The pole matters for two reasons. First, its *steepness* is the evidence that one side has the upper hand — a near-vertical move up is a loud statement that demand is winning. Second, its *height* becomes the yardstick for the measured move later: the market that just ran \$10 in one breath is a market with enough momentum that, after a rest, it can plausibly run roughly \$10 again. We will make that precise in the measured-move section, but hold the intuition now: **the pole is both the proof of momentum and the ruler for the target.**

### Profit-taking and a temporary balance

Now the part that demystifies everything. After a sharp \$10 run, why does price stop and drift sideways or gently down instead of just continuing? The honest answer has nothing to do with chart geometry and everything to do with ordinary human behavior and order flow.

*Order flow* is just the stream of buy and sell orders hitting the market. After a fast run-up, three things happen at once:

- **Early buyers take profits.** The people who bought near \$100 are now sitting on a quick \$10 gain. A fraction of them sell to lock it in. That selling is supply hitting the market — and supply pushes price down or, at least, stops it from rising.
- **New buyers hesitate.** The people who *missed* the move from \$100 to \$110 do not want to chase price at the top of a vertical run. They wait for a pullback, a better entry. So the eager demand that drove the pole thins out near the highs.
- **Nobody panics.** Crucially, in a *healthy* pause, the early buyers who hold are not scared into dumping their whole position, and no flood of new sellers appears. The selling is *measured* — just profit-taking — not a stampede.

The result is a *temporary balance* between supply (profit-takers selling) and demand (buyers waiting for a dip). Price drifts modestly against the trend — back from \$110 toward \$107 — in a tidy, low-energy way, with volume *contracting* because the urgency is gone. That drift, with its contracting volume, is the flag. It is not a reversal. It is the market pausing to let profit-takers and dip-buyers swap inventory before the next leg.

This is the whole secret, and it is deliberately unglamorous. **A flag is what profit-taking and a buyers' pause look like on a chart when neither side is in a hurry.** When the dip-buyers have had their fill at the lower prices and the profit-takers have finished selling, demand reasserts itself, price pushes back through the top of the flag, and the trend resumes. The breakout is the moment the temporary balance tips back in the trend's favor.

### Continuation versus reversal, up front

It is worth stating the central caveat before we go any further, because the entire premise of this article rests on a *tendency*, not a certainty.

A *continuation pattern* is a consolidation (a pause) that resolves in the *same* direction as the trend that preceded it. A *reversal pattern* is a consolidation that resolves in the *opposite* direction — the trend ends and turns around. Here is the uncomfortable truth: **the very same shape can be either.** A flag in an uptrend usually breaks up (continuation), but sometimes it breaks down (reversal). At the moment you are looking at it — mid-pause, before the break — you cannot know with certainty which it will be. You can only assign a probability, and the probability favors continuation, which is why these are called continuation patterns.

So the honest framing, which we will return to repeatedly, is this: a flag is a *bet* that the trend continues, with the odds tilted in your favor, and a built-in way to be *wrong cheaply* if the bet loses. The pattern does not tell you the future. It gives you a setup where, on average and over many trades, the wins are bigger than the losses — provided you respect the stop. That "provided you respect the stop" is the entire game, and we will spend a whole section on it.

## The bull flag and bear flag

With the mechanism in hand, let us build the most common continuation pattern precisely: the flag. It comes in two flavors, one for uptrends and one for downtrends, and they are perfect mirror images.

![A side by side comparison of a bear flag and a bull flag, showing the bear flag as a sharp down pole with a weak rally channel that breaks down, and the bull flag as a sharp up pole with a gentle pullback channel that breaks up](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-2.png)

### Construction of a bull flag

A *bull flag* forms in an uptrend and has exactly two parts:

1. **The pole (the impulse):** a sharp, steep up-move. In our example, price runs from \$100 to \$110 — a \$10, roughly vertical advance on heavy volume.
2. **The flag (the pullback channel):** a gentle drift *down and slightly against* the trend, bounded by two roughly parallel lines (a *channel*). The drift is shallow — price falls only from \$110 to about \$107, a modest \$3 give-back — and it slopes *down* while the trend is *up*. That counter-slope is the signature: the flag leans the opposite way to the pole, like a flag flapping back against the pole that holds it. (That visual is exactly where the name comes from: the steep pole, then the little rectangular flag drooping off the top of it.)

Two practical qualifiers separate a real flag from a random wiggle:

- **The flag is shallow.** A healthy bull-flag pullback typically retraces only a modest fraction of the pole — often a third or less. If price gives back most of the \$10 pole (say it falls from \$110 all the way to \$102), the "flag" has retraced so deeply that the bullish momentum is in real doubt; it is no longer a confident pause but a near-complete reversal of the impulse. Shallow is healthy; deep is suspect.
- **The flag is orderly and tight.** The drift happens in a narrow, low-volatility channel, not a wild swinging mess. Tight and quiet signals that no panic is occurring — exactly the controlled profit-taking we described. A loose, violent pullback is a fight, not a rest.

### The bear flag is the mirror

A *bear flag* forms in a downtrend and is the exact inverse. The pole is a sharp *down*-move (price drops from, say, \$110 to \$100). The flag is a gentle *rally* — a weak bounce that drifts *up and slightly against* the downtrend, from \$100 back up to about \$103, in a tight channel that slopes *up*. The break that resumes the trend is a *break down* through the bottom of the flag, near \$101.

Everything we say about bull flags applies to bear flags with the signs flipped. For the rest of this article we will mostly work with bull-flag examples for concreteness, but every entry, stop, target, and caveat has a mirror-image bear-flag version. The market does not care which direction it is trending; the geometry is symmetric.

### The order-flow story, retold on the chart

Map the mechanism from the foundations section onto the bull-flag shape and it clicks. The pole is the *impulse* — demand overwhelming supply. The down-sloping flag is the *profit-taking and pause* — early buyers selling a little, new buyers waiting, volume drying up, price easing back to a level (around \$107) where the waiting buyers find the price attractive again. The breakout up through the top of the flag is the *demand reasserting itself* — the dip-buyers step in, the brief supply of profit-taking is exhausted, and price pushes back above \$108.50 to continue the run.

This is why the flag's *counter-trend slope* is meaningful rather than cosmetic. A pullback that drifts gently *against* the trend, on shrinking volume, is the picture of orderly profit-taking with no new conviction on the sell side. If instead the pullback were steep, fast, and on heavy volume, that would be a different story — that would be aggressive sellers, not lazy profit-takers, and the bullish read would be in jeopardy. The slope and the volume together are the tell.

Every flag walks the same four stages in order — impulse, pause, breakout, measured move — and it is worth seeing them laid out as a life cycle, because only one stage (the breakout) is the moment you actually commit capital with a defined risk.

![The life cycle of a flag laid out as four stages: an impulse pole on heavy volume, a counter-trend pause on contracting volume, a breakout on expanding volume that gives a defined-risk entry, and a measured-move extension that targets one pole-height higher](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-8.png)

The diagram makes the sequence explicit: the impulse pole runs \$100 to \$110 on surging volume; the pause drifts back to \$107 on contracting volume; the breakout closes above \$108.50 on expanding volume, which is your defined-risk entry; and the measured move projects the \$10 pole to a target near \$117. Notice that the only stage where you take on risk is the breakout — everything before it is observation, and everything after it is management against the target zone.

### The breakout entry

Here is where the pattern becomes tradeable. The *breakout* is the moment price closes back above the upper boundary of the flag, signaling the pause is over and the trend is resuming. In our example, the top of the flag channel sits around \$108, and a trader takes the entry on a break and close above it — say \$108.50.

Why enter on the *breakout* rather than buying inside the flag at the cheaper \$107? Because buying inside the flag is buying a pause that has not yet resolved — you are guessing it will break up. Buying the breakout means waiting for price to *prove* it is resuming the trend before committing. You pay a slightly worse price (\$108.50 instead of \$107) in exchange for evidence. This is the same early-versus-confirmed tradeoff that runs through all of technical analysis: enter early and cheap but more often wrong, or enter later and confirmed but at a worse price. Most flag traders wait for the breakout — and, ideally, for the breakout to come on *expanding volume*, which we will see is the honest filter that separates real breaks from fakeouts.

The defined-risk part is what makes the entry attractive: the stop sits just below the flag, at \$106 in our example. If price breaks out and then falls back *into and through* the flag to \$106, the bullish thesis is simply wrong — the pause was not a pause, it was a top — and you exit with a small, known loss. We will compute the exact reward-to-risk shortly.

### Honest reliability

Now the part that most flag tutorials conveniently omit. How often does a bull flag actually break up and reach its target?

The honest answer is: *more often than a coin flip, but nowhere near a sure thing, and the edge depends heavily on volume and context.* Published studies of chart patterns — the most cited being the decades of pattern statistics compiled by Thomas Bulkowski — put the rate at which flags and pennants break in the expected (continuation) direction and reach a measured target somewhere in the rough neighborhood of 50% to 65%, with wide variation depending on the exact definition, the market, the timeframe, and crucially whether the breakout was confirmed by a surge in volume. These are not precise, universal constants; different studies and different markets give different numbers, and you should distrust anyone who quotes a single decimal place as gospel.

The practical takeaway is not a magic percentage. It is this: **a flag tilts the odds modestly in your favor, and the entire profitability of trading it comes from pairing that modest edge with a favorable reward-to-risk ratio.** A 60% continuation rate is worthless if your losses are as big as your wins. It becomes a genuine edge only when the measured-move target is several times the size of the stop — which, as we will see, is exactly what the geometry of a flag tends to give you. The win rate is half the story; the payoff ratio is the other half, and the two together are what [expectancy, and why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies) is all about.

## Pennants and the symmetrical pause

A *pennant* is the flag's close cousin. It has the same pole and the same job — a pause that resolves with the trend — but the consolidation has a different shape.

![A side by side comparison of a pennant and a flag, showing the pennant as a converging triangle that coils to an apex and the flag as a parallel channel that drifts gently, both following the same sharp pole](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-3.png)

### Converging consolidation after an impulse

Where a flag consolidates inside two *parallel* lines (a channel of constant width), a pennant consolidates inside two *converging* lines (a small symmetrical triangle that narrows to a point, called the *apex*). After the same sharp pole from \$100 to \$110, price swings up and down but in *progressively smaller* swings — \$108, then \$106, then \$108, then \$107, then a tighter \$107.50 — so that the highs are getting lower and the lows are getting higher, squeezing toward a tight apex. The pattern looks like a little triangular pennant flying off the top of the flagpole, which is exactly where the name comes from.

The *symmetrical* in "symmetrical pause" means the two converging lines slope toward each other at roughly equal angles — the highs descending at about the same rate the lows are ascending. (If the pattern were lopsided — a flat top with rising lows, or a flat bottom with falling highs — it would be an *ascending* or *descending triangle*, which carry their own slight directional biases. The clean pennant is the symmetric case.)

### How it differs from a flag

The shapes are cousins, and many traders use the terms loosely or interchangeably, but the distinction is real:

| Feature | Flag | Pennant |
|---|---|---|
| Consolidation shape | Parallel channel, constant width | Converging triangle, narrowing to an apex |
| Slope | Drifts gently against the trend | Coils sideways, symmetric squeeze |
| Range over time | Roughly constant | Shrinking — swings get smaller |
| What it signals | Orderly profit-taking, steady drift | Tightening balance, coiling energy |
| Volume pattern | Contracts through the flag | Contracts, often more sharply, toward the apex |

The underlying mechanism is the same — a temporary supply/demand balance after an impulse — but the pennant's *converging* shape tells a slightly more specific story: the disagreement between buyers and sellers is *tightening*. Each swing is smaller because neither side will let price get far from the consolidation midpoint. This is a coil, a compression of energy, and the longer it coils the more violent the eventual release tends to be — though, again, the *direction* of the release is not guaranteed by the coil itself.

### Volume drying up, then expanding on the break

The volume signature of a pennant is its most reliable tell, and it is even more pronounced than in a flag. As the triangle coils toward its apex, *volume dries up* — often dramatically, falling to a fraction of the pole's volume — because activity is shrinking along with the price range. The market is going quiet, holding its breath. Then, on the breakout out of the apex, *volume expands* sharply as the coil releases and the trend resumes.

This contract-then-expand volume pattern is so characteristic that its *absence* is a warning. A pennant that breaks out on *flat or falling* volume is suspect — the release lacks the conviction that a real continuation needs, and such breakouts fail more often. We will return to volume as the honest read in its own section, because it is the single most useful filter for distinguishing a continuation that will hold from one that will fail.

One practical note on timing: pennants should resolve *before* price reaches the apex of the triangle. A pattern that coils all the way into the point and beyond, going dead-flat, has lost its energy — the coil has uncoiled into a drift, and the continuation bias weakens. The useful pennant breaks out while there is still some range left in the triangle, typically somewhere between halfway and two-thirds of the way to the apex.

## The measured move

We now have the entry. The other half of a tradeable pattern is the *target* — where you expect price to go if the continuation works. For flags and pennants, the classic target is the *measured move*, and it is worth understanding both how it is computed and why it is a rough expectation rather than a promise.

![A measured move projection showing the ten dollar pole height measured from one hundred to one hundred ten, then transplanted above the breakout to project a target near one hundred seventeen dollars](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-4.png)

### Projecting the pole's height from the breakout

The measured move rests on a simple, almost naive idea: **the move after the pause will be roughly the same size as the move before it.** The pole ran \$10; expect the move after the breakout to run roughly \$10 too. To get a price target, you take the height of the pole and add it to the breakout point (or, in one variant, to the base of the flag).

Let us be precise with the two common conventions, because they give slightly different targets and people argue about which is "correct":

- **Project from the flag's base (the aggressive target).** The pole ran from \$100 to \$110, a height of \$10. The flag drifted down to a base around \$107. Add the \$10 pole to the \$107 base: target = \$107 + \$10 = **\$117**. This is the figure on our mental-model diagram, and it is the most commonly cited convention: measure the pole, transplant it onto the bottom of the flag.
- **Project from the breakout point (the conservative target).** Take the breakout at \$108.50 and add the \$10 pole: target = \$108.50 + \$10 = **\$118.50**. Slightly higher, and arguably more honest because the breakout is the point at which you actually commit.

In practice these two land close together (\$117 versus \$118.50), and the difference is smaller than the noise in any real market. We will use **\$117** as our headline target for the running example, since it matches the most standard "project the pole onto the flag base" convention, and note that the conservative version sits a dollar or two higher.

The intuition for *why* this rough equality holds is the momentum story from the foundations. The pole's height is a measure of how much force the trending side could muster in one push. After a rest that does not reverse the move, that same force is, on average, capable of roughly another push of similar size before it exhausts. It is not a law of physics — it is a statistical regularity that holds *on average and over many cases*, with enormous variation in any single instance.

### Why it is a rough expectation, not a promise

This is the part to internalize, because misusing the measured move is one of the most common ways traders fool themselves. **The measured move is a rough, average expectation — a center of a wide distribution — not a precise price that the market is obligated to hit.**

Three honest caveats:

- **Many moves fall short.** Plenty of valid breakouts run only half or two-thirds of the measured target before stalling or reversing. If you treat \$117 as a guarantee and refuse to take profits until price hits it exactly, you will repeatedly watch a \$117 target stall at \$114 and then give it all back.
- **Some moves overshoot.** Equally, strong trends blow well past the measured target. The measured move is a *minimum reasonable expectation* in a strong trend, not a ceiling. Treating it as a hard exit means leaving large gains on the table in the best trades.
- **The "precision" is an illusion.** Quoting a target as "\$117" implies a precision the method does not have. The honest target is a *zone* — "somewhere around \$115 to \$119, centered near \$117" — and the real decision is how you manage the trade as price approaches that zone (scale out, trail a stop, or exit), not whether price prints the exact number.

The right way to use the measured move is as a *planning tool for reward-to-risk*, not as a crystal ball. Before you take the trade, you compute the measured target to estimate the potential reward, compare it to the stop-defined risk, and decide whether the reward-to-risk justifies the trade. After you are in, you manage the position against the *zone*, not the decimal. The measured move earns its keep at the *entry decision*, where it tells you whether the trade is worth taking — not at the exit, where the market does what it wants.

#### Worked example: a bull flag from pole to target, and the reward-to-risk

Let us walk the full trade with explicit numbers, the way you would actually plan it.

You are watching a stock that has just run hard. Here are the facts of the setup:

- **The pole.** Price ran from \$100 to \$110 in a few bars on heavy volume. Pole height = \$110 − \$100 = **\$10**.
- **The flag.** Price then drifted down gently to about \$107 in a tight, down-sloping channel on *shrinking* volume over the next several bars. Shallow (a \$3 give-back of the \$10 pole, about 30%) and orderly — a healthy flag.
- **The entry.** Price pushes back up and closes above the top of the flag at **\$108.50**. You enter the long here, on the breakout.
- **The stop.** You place a stop just below the flag at **\$106**. If price falls back through the entire flag to \$106, the pause was not a pause — the thesis is dead, and you are out.
- **The target.** The measured move: add the \$10 pole to the flag base of \$107, giving a target of **\$117**.

Now the accounting that decides whether this trade is worth taking:

- **Risk per share** = entry − stop = \$108.50 − \$106 = **\$2.50.** This is your *R* — one unit of risk, the most you intend to lose on this trade per share.
- **Reward per share** = target − entry = \$117 − \$108.50 = **\$8.50.**
- **Reward-to-risk ratio** = \$8.50 / \$2.50 = **3.4 to 1.**

So the trade risks \$2.50 to make \$8.50 — a reward-to-risk of about 3.4 to 1. Expressed in *R* units (the language used throughout [expectancy, and why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies)), you are risking 1R to make 3.4R. That favorable geometry is the whole reason flags are worth trading: even if the breakout only continues to target a *minority* of the time, a 3.4-to-1 payoff can produce a positive expectancy. (We will compute exactly how often it needs to work in the expectancy example below.)

**The single sentence of intuition: the value of a flag is not that it continues often — it is that, when it continues, the measured move pays several times the small risk you took at the flag's edge.**

## Volume and the honest read

If you remember only one *filter* from this entire article, make it volume. Volume is the closest thing flags and pennants have to a lie detector, and understanding how it behaves — and how often that behavior actually shows up — separates the trader who reads patterns honestly from the one who sees a flag in every random wiggle.

![A two panel chart showing price forming a bull flag on top and volume bars below, with heavy volume on the pole, contracting volume through the flag pause, and expanding volume on the breakout](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-5.png)

### The classic pattern: contract in the flag, expand on the break

*Volume* is simply the number of shares (or contracts, or coins) that change hands in a given bar. It measures *participation* — how many people are transacting, how much conviction is behind the move. The classic, textbook volume signature of a healthy continuation pattern has three stages:

1. **Heavy volume on the pole.** The impulse move happens on a surge of volume. Lots of people are buying in a hurry; that is what drives the near-vertical run. Heavy pole volume is the evidence that the move is real and not a thin, illiquid spike.
2. **Contracting volume in the flag.** As price drifts into the pause, volume *dries up*. Fewer people are transacting because the urgency is gone — the profit-takers are trickling out and the dip-buyers are waiting patiently. Low, shrinking volume in the flag is the signature of an *orderly* pause rather than a fight. It says: nobody is panicking, nobody is aggressively selling, this is just a rest.
3. **Expanding volume on the breakout.** When price breaks out of the flag, volume *expands* again — ideally to something approaching the pole's volume. This surge is the evidence that demand has genuinely reasserted itself, that real buyers (not just a few stragglers) are pushing price back up. The breakout volume is the confirmation.

The story these three stages tell is coherent: a burst of conviction (pole), a quiet pause where conviction rests but does not reverse (flag), and a fresh burst of conviction in the same direction (breakout). When you see that volume sequence, the continuation read is at its strongest.

### How often that actually holds

Now the honest part. The textbook volume signature is *real* — it does show up, and confirmed breakouts genuinely do continue and reach target more often than unconfirmed ones. But two caveats keep it from being a magic key:

- **It is a tendency, not a rule.** Plenty of perfectly good flags do *not* show a clean volume contraction, and some excellent continuations break out on unremarkable volume, especially in markets or timeframes where volume data is noisy or thin. Volume is a strong *probabilistic* filter, not a binary gate. A flag without textbook volume is weaker evidence, not invalid evidence.
- **The edge from volume confirmation is meaningful but not enormous.** As the reliability matrix below summarizes, volume-confirmed breakouts reach the measured target meaningfully more often than low-volume breakouts of the same shape — the difference is real and worth filtering for. But "more often" might be the difference between roughly 60% and roughly 45%, not the difference between certainty and chance. Even confirmed breakouts fail a substantial fraction of the time.

So use volume as your *primary filter*: prefer flags that contract and break out on expanding volume, be skeptical of those that do not, but never mistake the filter for a guarantee. The matrix below summarizes the honest reliability picture across the main continuation shapes, split by whether volume confirmed the breakout — and the caveats column is as important as the percentages.

![A reliability matrix comparing bull and bear flags, pennants, rectangle pauses, and failed breaks across whether volume expands on the break or stays flat, showing volume-confirmed breakouts reach the measured target far more often than low-volume breakouts, with an honest caveats column noting small samples and study variation](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-7.png)

Read the matrix as *rough, illustrative bands*, not precise constants — the exact numbers vary by study, market, timeframe, and the precise definition of the pattern, and the honest caveat is that the samples behind any such table are small and the variation is large. What survives all that variation is the *direction* of the effect: across every shape, a breakout that expands on volume reaches its measured target meaningfully more often than the identical shape that breaks on flat or falling volume. The bottom row is the reminder that ties the whole article together — when the break goes the *wrong* way, that is itself a signal, and the stop just beyond the flag is the entire risk control.

### Low-volume breakouts as a warning

The most actionable use of volume is as a *warning*. A breakout on *low or falling* volume — price pokes above the flag but barely anyone is buying — is the classic setup for a *fakeout* (a false breakout that immediately reverses; see [breakouts versus fakeouts](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/breakouts-vs-fakeouts) for the full mechanism). The logic is direct: if a breakout is genuine, demand has reasserted itself, and reasserted demand *shows up as volume*. A breakout with no volume is a breakout with no buyers behind it — price drifted above the line on inertia, not conviction, and inertia does not sustain a trend.

This is why a low-volume breakout is a yellow flag even when the price action looks perfect. The shape says "continue"; the volume says "but nobody is actually buying." When the shape and the volume disagree, the honest read is to trust the volume — or at least to demand a tighter stop and a smaller position, because the probability of a fakeout just went up.

#### Worked example: reading the volume profile through a clean flag

Let us put numbers on the volume sequence so it is concrete. Suppose the average daily volume of this stock in quiet times is about 1 million shares. We watch the volume through the pattern:

- **The pole (days 1–3):** volume runs **3.0, 3.2, 2.8 million shares** — roughly three times normal. Heavy. This is real buying conviction driving the \$100-to-\$110 run.
- **The flag (days 4–7):** volume falls to **1.1, 0.9, 0.7, 0.8 million shares** — dropping toward and below normal as price drifts from \$110 to \$107. The contraction is textbook: participation is fading, the pause is orderly, nobody is dumping.
- **The breakout (day 8):** volume surges back to **2.6 million shares** as price closes above \$108.50. The expansion confirms that demand has returned in force.

Compare that to a *failed-looking* version: the same price shape, but the breakout day prints only **0.8 million shares** — no expansion, just a quiet drift above the line. The price says "breakout"; the volume says "on what conviction?" That low-volume break is exactly the one to be skeptical of, and statistically it is the one more likely to reverse back into the flag and stop you out.

**The single sentence of intuition: in a healthy continuation, volume tells the same story as price — heavy, then quiet, then heavy again — and when the breakout's volume does not show up, the breakout is borrowing against conviction it does not have.**

## When continuation fails

Everything so far has described the pattern *working*. This section is about the pattern *failing*, and it is the most important section in the article, because how you handle the failures is what determines whether trading these patterns makes or loses money over time.

![A failed bull flag showing the same pole and flag setup, but price breaks down through the bottom of the flag, hits the stop at one hundred six dollars, and the loss is capped at one R or two dollars fifty per share](/imgs/blogs/continuation-patterns-flags-pennants-6.png)

### The failed flag

A *failed flag* is a flag that breaks the *wrong* way. In an uptrend, instead of breaking up and resuming the advance, price breaks *down* out of the bottom of the flag and keeps falling. The pause was not a pause — it was the beginning of a reversal, or simply a place where the buyers ran out and the sellers took over.

This happens, and it happens often enough that you must plan for it before every trade. There is no version of flag trading where you avoid failed flags; they are a permanent feature of a probabilistic edge. A pattern that continues 60% of the time *fails 40% of the time*, and 40% is not rare — it is two trades in five. If you are not prepared, emotionally and mechanically, for the failure, you will not last long enough to collect the wins.

### The flag that becomes a reversal

A particularly instructive failure is the flag that *becomes* a reversal pattern. Recall the central caveat from the foundations: the same shape can be continuation or reversal, and you cannot know which mid-pause. Sometimes what looked like a healthy, shallow flag deepens — price keeps drifting down, retraces more than half the pole, breaks below the flag, and then below the *base of the pole itself*, turning the whole structure into a top.

The early warning signs that a flag is curdling into a reversal are worth knowing:

- **The pullback gets too deep.** A flag that retraces well past half the pole (price falling from \$110 back toward \$103 or below, more than 70% of the \$10 pole) is no longer a confident pause. The deeper the retrace, the more the bullish momentum is in doubt.
- **The pullback gets disorderly.** A flag that drifts on *rising* volume, or in wide violent swings rather than a tight quiet channel, is showing aggressive selling, not lazy profit-taking. That is sellers taking control, not a rest.
- **The break comes down, not up.** The decisive signal is simply the direction of the break: a close below the bottom of the flag, rather than above the top, says the supply/demand balance tipped *against* the trend, not with it.

None of these guarantees a reversal — markets do whatever they do — but together they shift the probability away from continuation, and they are reasons to size smaller, demand more confirmation, or stand aside.

### Why a tight stop beyond the flag is the whole risk control

Here is the single most important idea in the article, and it is not a chart pattern at all — it is a risk-control principle. **The reason flag trading can be profitable despite failing 40% of the time is that the failures are cheap and the wins are large, and the thing that makes the failures cheap is a tight stop just beyond the flag.**

Look again at the geometry. The entry is \$108.50; the stop is \$106, just below the flag. The risk is \$2.50. When the flag fails, price breaks down, hits \$106, and you are out for a loss of \$2.50 per share — one *R*. That is it. A planned, small, survivable loss. Meanwhile, when the flag works, the measured move pays \$8.50 — more than three times the risk.

This is the entire engine. The flag's shape gives you a *natural, tight place to put the stop*: right at the level that, if breached, proves the pattern wrong. You are not guessing where to exit; the pattern tells you. Below the flag, the continuation thesis is dead by definition, so that is where the stop goes. Because the flag is shallow and tight, that stop is *close* to the entry — which makes the risk small relative to the measured-move reward. The pattern is profitable *because its geometry makes the stop tight and the target far.*

If you move the stop too far away "to give the trade room," you destroy the very thing that makes the pattern work — you turn a 3.4-to-1 trade into a 1.5-to-1 trade, and now you need a much higher win rate to break even. And if you trade flags *without* a stop, you are no longer trading a defined-risk pattern; you are gambling that every pause continues, and the 40% that do not will eventually deliver a loss large enough to erase a long string of wins. **The stop is not an optional add-on to the pattern. The stop is the pattern.** Without it, the favorable reward-to-risk that justifies the trade simply does not exist.

#### Worked example: the failed flag and the minus one R loss

Let us run the *same* setup as before, but this time it fails, so you can see exactly what the failure costs and why it is survivable.

The setup is identical: pole from \$100 to \$110, flag drifting to \$107, breakout entry at \$108.50, stop at \$106, measured target \$117. You take the long at \$108.50.

But this time, after the breakout, price does not run. It stalls, rolls over, and falls back *into* the flag. The dip-buyers who were supposed to push it up do not show up in force; a wave of selling appears instead. Price slides through \$108, through \$107, and closes below \$106. Your stop triggers.

The accounting:

- **Exit (stop) price** = \$106.
- **Loss per share** = entry − exit = \$108.50 − \$106 = **\$2.50.**
- **In R units** = \$2.50 / \$2.50 = **−1R.** Exactly one unit of planned risk. No more.

That is the whole damage. You lost \$2.50 a share — a small, known, planned amount — and you are out, free to take the next setup. The failure did not blow up your account; it cost you one R, exactly as designed. Compare that to the \$8.50 (3.4R) you stood to make if it had worked. **The single sentence of intuition: a failed flag is not a disaster, it is a \$2.50 line item — and the only reason it is a small line item rather than a catastrophe is that the stop sat just beyond the flag, where the pattern itself told you to put it.**

There is a second, subtler lesson here. The failed flag is also *information*. A flag in an uptrend that breaks *down* instead of up is telling you something about the balance of supply and demand: the buyers who carried the pole could not carry the breakout, and sellers stepped in at the highs. That is evidence — weak evidence from one event, but evidence — that the trend may be tiring. A trader who notices that the *last few* flags in a run are failing is reading a change in character, which is exactly the kind of structural warning that [trend and market structure](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trend-and-market-structure) is about. The failure costs you 1R, but it also hands you a data point about whether the trend is still healthy.

#### Worked example: measured-move expectancy — does the edge actually exist?

We have claimed repeatedly that the favorable reward-to-risk is what makes flags worth trading even though they fail often. Let us prove it with the expectancy arithmetic, because a claim about profitability is empty without the numbers.

*Expectancy* is the average profit or loss you can expect per trade, over many trades, given your win rate and your average win and loss sizes. The formula, developed in full in [expectancy, and why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies), is:

$$E = p_{\text{win}} \times W - p_{\text{loss}} \times L$$

where $p_{\text{win}}$ is the probability of a winning trade, $W$ is the average win size (in R), $p_{\text{loss}}$ is the probability of a loss, and $L$ is the average loss size (in R).

Now plug in our flag. Suppose — generously but not absurdly — that confirmed bull-flag breakouts reach the measured-move target **60% of the time**, and the other 40% hit the stop. Our reward-to-risk was 3.4 to 1, but let us be conservative and round the target down: assume the winners average **+2.5R** (price reaches a good portion of the target, not always the full \$8.50) and the losers cost the full **−1R** (the stop). Then:

$$E = (0.60 \times 2.5\text{R}) - (0.40 \times 1.0\text{R})$$
$$E = 1.50\text{R} - 0.40\text{R} = +1.10\text{R per trade}$$

A positive expectancy of **+1.1R per trade**. Over 100 such trades, risking 1R each, the expected net gain is about **+110R**. If 1R is \$250 (our \$2.50 risk on a 100-share position), that is an expected **+\$27,500** over 100 trades — before costs, and with all the usual caveats about whether the 60% and the 2.5R hold up in *your* market and *your* execution.

Now watch how fragile that is. Drop the win rate to **45%** (a low-volume, unconfirmed breakout regime) and keep the same payoffs:

$$E = (0.45 \times 2.5\text{R}) - (0.55 \times 1.0\text{R}) = 1.125\text{R} - 0.55\text{R} = +0.575\text{R per trade}$$

Still positive, but barely half the edge — and after trading costs and slippage (say 0.1R per trade), it shrinks further. And if you let the win rate fall to **30%** *and* let your average winner shrink to **1.5R** because you keep taking profits too early:

$$E = (0.30 \times 1.5\text{R}) - (0.70 \times 1.0\text{R}) = 0.45\text{R} - 0.70\text{R} = -0.25\text{R per trade}$$

*Negative.* The same pattern, traded with a worse win rate and clipped winners, *loses money*. **The single sentence of intuition: a flag is not profitable because it is a flag — it is profitable only when the win rate and the reward-to-risk together clear the expectancy bar, which is exactly why volume confirmation (which lifts the win rate) and letting winners run to the measured move (which protects the payoff) are not optional polish but the load-bearing parts of the edge.**

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs reliably trip up people learning continuation patterns. Each is wrong, and the *why* is more useful than the correction.

### "Flags always continue the trend"

They do not. This is the single most dangerous misconception, because it leads people to trade flags without a stop, certain that the pause "has to" resolve up. In reality, a flag *usually* continues the trend — the odds are tilted in your favor — but a substantial minority of flags fail, breaking the wrong way and turning into reversals. The honest mental model is a weighted coin, not a certainty. You are taking a bet with favorable odds *and* a cheap way to be wrong, not collecting a guaranteed payout. Treating "usually" as "always" is how a trader with a real edge blows up: they stop respecting the stop, and the 40% of trades that fail eventually deliver a loss big enough to wipe out a long run of wins.

### "The measured move is a precise target"

It is not. The measured move — project the pole's height above the breakout — is a rough, *average* expectation drawn from a wide distribution of outcomes. Many valid breakouts fall short of it; strong trends blow past it. Quoting "\$117" as if the market is obligated to print that exact number is a false precision the method never claimed. The measured move is best used at the *entry decision* to estimate whether the reward-to-risk justifies the trade, and managed as a *zone* (roughly \$115 to \$119) rather than a decimal once you are in. If you wait for the exact number to take profits, you will repeatedly watch winners stall a dollar short and reverse.

### "Any consolidation is a flag"

It is not. A flag is a *specific* shape with *specific* prerequisites: it must follow a genuine impulse *pole* (a sharp, steep, high-volume move), it must be *shallow* (retracing only a modest fraction of the pole), and it must be *orderly and tight* (a quiet, low-volume drift, not a wild swinging range). A sideways chop with no preceding pole is not a flag — it is a range, and ranges do not carry a directional bias. Stretching the definition to call every pause a flag means trading "patterns" that have none of the properties that give a real flag its edge. The pole is not optional decoration; without a real impulse before it, the consolidation has no momentum to continue and no measuring stick for the target.

### "You don't need volume confirmation"

You do — or rather, ignoring volume throws away the single most useful filter you have. The shape of a flag tells you *where* the levels are; the volume tells you whether the breakout has real conviction behind it. A breakout on heavy, expanding volume is materially more likely to continue than a breakout on thin, flat volume of the identical price shape. Skipping volume means treating those two breakouts as equal when they are not, which lowers your win rate and, as the expectancy example showed, can flip a profitable edge into a losing one. Volume is not a sure thing, but it is the closest thing to a lie detector these patterns have, and discarding it is discarding free information.

### "A bigger pole means a bigger guaranteed move"

The measured move scales with the pole, so a \$20 pole projects a \$20 target rather than a \$10 one — but "scales with" is not "guarantees." A larger pole gives a larger *target*, not a larger *certainty*. In fact, very large, very steep poles can be *exhaustion* moves — the final, climactic burst of a trend that is about to reverse — in which case the flag that follows is more likely to fail than continue. Pole size sets the scale of the potential reward; it does not raise the probability of reaching it, and at the extreme it can lower it.

### "Flags only matter on intraday charts"

They appear on *every* timeframe, from one-minute charts to weekly charts, because the underlying mechanism — an impulse, profit-taking, a pause, then resumption — is the same at every scale. A flag on the weekly chart represents weeks of consolidation after a months-long pole; a flag on the five-minute chart represents minutes of consolidation after an hour-long pole. The geometry, the entry logic, the measured move, and the volume read are identical; only the time axis changes. The mistake is assuming a pattern that works on one timeframe is a special property of that timeframe, when it is really a fractal feature of how trends move.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstractions earn their keep when you can point at real charts. Here are several named episodes and recurring scenarios, with as-of caveats, where continuation patterns (and their failures) played out in public. These are illustrative descriptions of past price behavior, not forecasts, and the figures are approximate and as-of the dates given.

### A momentum stock's staircase of bull flags

The most vivid place to see bull flags is a high-momentum stock during a strong run. Through 2020 and into early 2021, a number of widely-followed growth and technology stocks advanced in a repeating *staircase*: a sharp pole up on an earnings beat or a sector surge, a tight multi-day flag of orderly profit-taking on shrinking volume, a breakout to a new high on expanding volume, and then another pole. Traders who recognized the pattern got a *series* of defined-risk entries to rejoin the same trend — each flag a fresh chance to enter with a tight stop just below the consolidation. The texture is the lesson: a strong trend is not one smooth move but a sequence of pole-flag-pole-flag, and each flag is a discrete, tradeable pause. The catch, always, is that you do not know which flag in the staircase is the *last* one before the trend tops — which is why every entry, even in the strongest run, needs its own stop. (Behavior described is illustrative of that era's momentum names; as-of the 2026 historical record, and individual stocks are volatile.)

### A commodity bear flag

Continuation patterns work identically in downtrends, and commodities — which trend strongly on supply-and-demand shifts — show clean bear flags. Crude oil's collapse in the first half of 2020 is a stark example: after a sharp drop (the pole down), price would stage a weak, low-volume rally (the flag up, drifting against the downtrend), and then break down again to new lows as the relentless oversupply reasserted itself. A trader reading the bear flag had a defined-risk *short* entry on each break down through the bottom of the flag, with a stop just above the flag's high. The mechanism is the mirror of the bull flag: a brief, half-hearted bounce as some short-sellers take profits and a few bargain-hunters buy, followed by the resumption of the dominant downtrend when the selling pressure overwhelms them. (The 2020 oil collapse was an extreme episode including a brief negative-price event in the futures; figures as-of the 2026 historical record, and commodities can move violently on geopolitics and storage dynamics.)

### A crypto pennant before a big move

Cryptocurrencies, being young, liquid, and exceptionally volatile, show pennants in fast-forward, often coiling for hours or days before an explosive release. Bitcoin's run through 2020 into 2021 included repeated episodes where price made a sharp pole up, then coiled into a tightening pennant on visibly *drying* volume — the daily ranges shrinking day after day as the market went quiet — before bursting out on a surge of volume to a new leg higher. The pennant's contract-then-expand volume signature is especially clean in crypto because the markets trade 24/7 and the volume data is granular. The same caveat applies even more forcefully here than elsewhere: crypto pennants also fail dramatically, breaking the wrong way into sharp drops, and the volatility means a failed pattern can move against you faster and further than in slower markets — so the tight stop matters more, not less. (Prices and behavior approximate, as-of the 2026 historical record; crypto is among the most volatile asset classes and these moves were unusually large.)

### A textbook flag that failed on weak volume

For the essential counter-example — because the failures are the whole reason we keep a stop — consider the recurring pattern of a *perfect-looking* flag that breaks out on *no volume* and immediately reverses. This is not a single famous episode but a constant feature of markets: a stock runs up (a clean pole), consolidates in a tidy down-sloping flag, and then pokes above the top of the flag — but the breakout day prints *below-average* volume. No conviction shows up. Within a day or two, price falls back into the flag, breaks the bottom, and stops out everyone who bought the breakout. The shape was textbook; the volume was the tell. Traders who demanded volume confirmation either skipped the trade or took it with a tight stop and lost only 1R when it failed. Traders who ignored volume because "the pattern looked perfect" learned the expensive way that a flag without buyers behind the breakout is a trap. This scenario plays out thousands of times across markets every year and is the single best argument for the volume filter. (Illustrative recurring scenario, not a specific dated event.)

### A rectangle pause that resolved with the trend

Flags and pennants are not the only continuation shapes. A *rectangle* — price bouncing sideways between a flat support floor and a flat resistance ceiling after an impulse — can also act as a continuation pattern when it forms mid-trend. In strongly trending markets, you frequently see a rectangle pause: price runs up, then trades sideways in a horizontal band for an extended stretch (longer and flatter than a flag), with buyers defending the floor and sellers capping the ceiling, until demand finally wins and price breaks out of the top to resume the advance. The measured-move logic still applies — project the height of the pole above the breakout — and the stop still goes just beyond the opposite edge of the rectangle. The rectangle is a more *neutral* pause than a flag (a flat range carries less directional bias than a counter-trend channel), so it is correspondingly more likely to break either way, and volume confirmation on the breakout matters even more. (Illustrative of a common mid-trend consolidation; behavior varies by market and as-of the 2026 historical record.)

### A pole so steep it was exhaustion, not a pause

The honest final scenario is the one that breaks the rule. Sometimes a pole is so violent — a near-vertical, climactic, panic-buying spike — that the consolidation which follows is not a healthy flag but the rounding top of an *exhausted* trend. The buying that produced the spike was the *last* of the demand, not a sustainable wave, and the "flag" that follows quickly curdles into a reversal: a deep, disorderly pullback that breaks the bottom of the consolidation and keeps falling. This is the dark side of "a bigger pole means a bigger move" — the steepest poles are sometimes blow-off tops, and the continuation pattern that should follow instead becomes a reversal. The defense is the same as always: the stop just beyond the flag turns even this nasty failure into a planned 1R loss instead of a ruinous one. The lesson is that no pattern overrides the need for the stop, and the most exciting-looking setups (the steepest poles) are sometimes the most dangerous. (Illustrative of blow-off / exhaustion dynamics seen across many markets and eras; as-of the 2026 historical record.)

## When this matters to you / further reading

If you take one thing from this article, make it the honest framing, because it is what turns a pattern from a superstition into a tool: **a flag or pennant is a bet that a paused trend continues, with the odds tilted modestly in your favor and a tight stop just beyond the pause that makes being wrong cheap.** The shape does not predict the future. It gives you a defined-risk entry to rejoin a trend, a measured-move target with a real-if-rough statistical basis, and — most importantly — a natural place to put the stop that makes the whole reward-to-risk arithmetic work.

It also tells you where the difficulty really lives. Spotting a flag on a finished chart, where the breakout and the run to target are already visible, is trivial. The hard part is that, in real time, you are always at the right edge of the chart looking at a pause that has not yet resolved — and you cannot know whether *this* flag is one of the 60% that continue or one of the 40% that fail. Every serious approach to trading these patterns is a way of managing that uncertainty: filtering with volume to improve the odds, sizing the position so a 1R loss is survivable, letting winners run to the measured move so the payoff is large, and accepting a steady drip of small losses on the failures as the cost of capturing the occasional large continuation.

Where this touches your own learning next:

- **Start with the trend.** A continuation pattern is meaningless without a trend to continue, and reading trend structure — higher highs and higher lows, the change of character that warns a trend is tiring — is the prerequisite. Begin with [trend and market structure](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/trend-and-market-structure), and notice that a *series* of failing flags is itself a structural warning.
- **Understand the breakout honestly.** A flag resolves with a breakout, and breakouts fail constantly. The mechanics of why a breakout holds or reverses — and how to tell a real break from a fakeout — are the natural companion to this post; see [breakouts versus fakeouts](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/breakouts-vs-fakeouts).
- **Respect the levels at the flag's edge.** The top and bottom of a flag are short-term support and resistance, and they behave the way all levels do — they hold until they do not, and the break is where the action is. The deeper logic of why price stalls and breaks at specific levels is in [support and resistance, and why levels exist](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/support-and-resistance-why-levels-exist).
- **Do the expectancy math before you trust any pattern.** The whole question of whether trading flags makes money comes down to win rate times payoff, net of costs — and a pattern with a 70% win rate can lose money while one with a 40% win rate prints, depending on the payoffs. Work through [expectancy, and why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies) and re-run the flag's numbers for your own market and execution.

None of this is advice to trade anything. It is a vocabulary and a set of rules for *describing and risk-managing* a paused trend, stated as honestly as the problem allows: a flag is a pause that usually — not always — resolves with the trend, worth trading only when the measured move pays several times a tight, pattern-defined stop, and worth abandoning the instant price breaks the wrong way and tells you the bet was wrong.
