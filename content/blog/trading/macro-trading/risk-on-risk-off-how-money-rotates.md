> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Global markets breathe in two phases: **risk-on**, when money floods into stocks, credit, emerging markets and crypto, and **risk-off**, when it sprints for the safety of the dollar, US Treasuries, gold and the Japanese yen — and reading which phase you're in turns dozens of separate trade decisions into one.
>
> - A "risk asset" pays you for taking risk (stocks, high-yield credit, emerging markets, crypto); a "safe haven" is what everyone runs to when they want to *stop* taking risk (the dollar, Treasuries, gold, the yen). In a panic, money rotates out of the first basket and into the second, almost mechanically.
> - The **VIX** — the market's 30-day implied-volatility "fear gauge" — is the single best regime thermometer. Its long-run average is about **19.5**; calm years close near 12-17, and genuine risk-off events have spiked it to **37.3 (Feb 2018)**, **82.7 (Mar 2020)** and **65.7 (Aug 2024)**.
> - Havens are havens for concrete reasons — liquidity, *negative beta* to risk, and reserve status — not magic. The dollar rallied to a **103.5** year-end close (a **114.8** intraday peak) through the 2022 everything-down year precisely *because* everything else was falling.
> - The dangerous part: in a real crisis, correlations rush toward 1 and your diversification quietly stops working. The one number to remember: when the VIX is near **15** you can run a normal book; when it's near **35** the same position is more than twice as risky, so you cut it in half before you think about anything else.

On the morning of August 5, 2024, a trader in New York woke up to a market that had, overnight, decided to be afraid of everything at once. The Japanese stock market had just suffered its worst single day since the 1987 crash, the Nikkei down more than 12%. The yen, which had been quietly weakening for years, had snapped violently *stronger*. US equity futures were deep in the red before the cash open, the S&P 500 set to fall hard. Bitcoin had been cut by roughly a fifth in a few days. And the one print that summarized the whole thing — the number that told you this was not a random bad day but a *regime change* — was the VIX, the market's fear gauge, which spiked to **65.7** intraday, a level seen only a handful of times in history and otherwise reserved for the 2008 crisis and the 2020 pandemic crash.

Nothing fundamental about the world had changed overnight. No war had started, no bank had failed, no company had reported disastrous earnings. What had happened was a *positioning* event: a single enormous trade — borrowing cheap yen to buy higher-yielding assets everywhere else, the "yen carry trade" — began to unwind all at once, and as it unwound, it forced selling across every risk asset on the planet simultaneously. Stocks, crypto, emerging-market currencies and commodities all fell together, while the yen, the dollar and US Treasuries all caught a bid together. That is risk-off in its purest form: not a story about any single asset, but a coordinated *rotation* of money out of one basket and into another.

This is the single most useful lens in all of macro trading, and it is the subject of this entire post. Markets do not move asset by asset on their own private logic as often as beginners think. Much of the time, they move in two great breathing phases — **risk-on**, when capital reaches *out* toward reward, and **risk-off**, when capital pulls *back* toward safety — and almost everything you trade is, underneath, a bet on which phase you're in. The figure above is the mental model: a seesaw with risk assets on one side and safe havens on the other, and money sloshing from one end to the other as fear rises and falls. Learn to read which way the seesaw is tilting, and you can position your *whole book* — every position you hold — to a single macro decision instead of forty separate ones.

![Seesaw with risk assets on one side and safe-haven assets on the other and money sloshing between them](/imgs/blogs/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates-1.png)

The payoff of getting this right is enormous, and the cost of getting it wrong is brutal. A trader who is long a "diversified" book of stocks, corporate bonds, emerging-market currencies and a little crypto *feels* diversified — until a risk-off wave hits and discovers that all of those were the *same trade* wearing different costumes. That is the trap this post is built to keep you out of. We will start from absolute zero — what a risk asset even is, what makes a haven a haven, what correlation and beta mean — then build up to the canonical risk-on and risk-off baskets, the precise reasons the dollar and yen and Treasuries rally in a panic, why correlations rise exactly when you need them to fall, the myths that get beginners hurt, and finally the playbook: how to read the regime off three or four dials and size your entire book to one call.

## Foundations: risk assets, safe havens, correlation, and the VIX

Let's build the vocabulary from money you already understand, because every fancy term here is just a label on a plain idea.

**A risk asset is anything that pays you a premium for the chance that you might lose money.** Think of it as the difference between two ways to store \$10,000. You could put it in a government-insured savings account and earn, say, 4% with essentially no chance of loss. Or you could buy shares of a company, where you *might* make 15% but might also lose 30%. The stock has to *offer* you more, on average, to compensate you for the risk that it could fall apart — otherwise no rational person would hold it instead of the safe account. That extra expected return is called the **risk premium**, and the assets that carry one are **risk assets**: stocks (equities), corporate bonds (especially low-quality "high-yield" or "junk" bonds), emerging-market stocks and currencies, commodities like copper and oil, and crypto. They all share one DNA strand: they go *up* when the world feels confident and growth looks good, and *down* when fear rises. They are pro-cyclical — they ride the economic cycle.

**A safe haven is the opposite: an asset people run *toward* precisely when they want to stop taking risk.** It is where money hides. The classic havens are the **US dollar** (specifically cash and short-term US government bills), **US Treasury bonds** (IOUs of the US government), **gold**, and the **Japanese yen** (and historically the Swiss franc). A haven's job is not to make you rich; it's to *not fall — or even rise — exactly when everything else is falling*. When a trader sells stocks in a panic, the cash from that sale has to go somewhere, and "somewhere safe" is the haven basket. So in a risk-off wave, money doesn't vanish — it *rotates*. It leaves the risk side of the seesaw and lands on the haven side. Understanding that conservation — every dollar that leaves stocks has to land somewhere — is the key to seeing why these moves are so coordinated.

What actually *makes* something a haven? It is not tradition or vibes; it comes down to three concrete properties, and an asset needs all three to be a true haven.

**Property one: deep liquidity.** Liquidity is the ability to sell a large amount *fast* without crashing the price. In a panic, what you need above all is to *get out* — to convert your asset to spendable safety right now, in size, without a fire-sale discount. The US Treasury market is the single deepest, most liquid market on Earth; you can sell billions of dollars of Treasuries in minutes near a fair price. That depth is *why* it's the ultimate haven. An illiquid asset — a building, a small-cap stock, an obscure altcoin — is the opposite of a haven, because the moment you most need to sell it is the moment no one will buy it at a fair price. We cover this in depth in [what liquidity means for market, funding, and global traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-liquidity-means-market-funding-global-traders); for now, hold onto the idea that *havens are liquid and risk assets, when stressed, become illiquid*.

**Property two: negative (or low) beta to risk.** **Beta** is a measure of how much an asset moves *with* the broad risk market (proxied by the stock market). A beta of 1.0 means the asset moves one-for-one with stocks; a beta of 2.0 means it moves twice as hard in the same direction (high-beta tech, leveraged crypto); a beta of 0 means it's indifferent to the stock market; and a *negative* beta means it tends to move *against* stocks — up when they're down. Havens have low or negative beta to risk. US Treasuries have *historically* had negative beta to stocks: when stocks crash, investors buy Treasuries, pushing their price up. That negative beta is the whole reason a haven cushions a portfolio — it leans the other way when you need it to. (We'll see later, in 2022, the terrifying case where that beta flipped *positive* and the cushion vanished.)

**Property three: reserve / store-of-value status.** A haven has to be something the *whole world* already trusts and holds, so that in a crisis the demand for it is structural and global, not just a few traders' preference. The dollar is the world's reserve currency — central banks hold about 58% of their reserves in it, the world borrows roughly \$13 trillion in it, and most commodities are priced in it (we unpack this fully in [the dollar system: why USD rules markets and the DXY](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy)). Gold has been money for five thousand years and is no one's liability — it can't default. The yen and franc are havens for a more mechanical reason we'll get to (the carry trade). Reserve status means that when fear spikes, there's a pre-existing, planet-wide pool of people who *want* this asset, so the bid shows up reliably.

Now the regime gauge. **The VIX is the market's fear thermometer**, and it is the most important single number in this whole post. Formally, the VIX is the **30-day implied volatility of the S&P 500** — a number, quoted in annualized percentage points, that the options market *implies* about how much stocks are expected to move over the next month. You don't need the math to use it; just understand what it captures. When traders are calm and complacent, they don't pay up for insurance (options), so implied volatility is low and the VIX sits in the low teens. When traders are scared and scrambling to hedge, they bid up the price of options frantically, and the VIX spikes. So the VIX is, in plain terms, *the price of fear* — what the market is paying to protect itself.

The numbers give you a usable map. The VIX's **long-run average is about 19.5**. Calm, complacent years end well below it: the VIX closed 2017 near **11.0**, 2019 near **13.8**, 2023 near **12.5**. A reading in the **low-to-mid teens means risk-on / complacency** — the market is relaxed. A reading in the **20s means caution**, and a sustained move **above 30 means genuine risk-off / panic**. And the true crises blow far past that: the VIX spiked to **37.3 in February 2018** (the "Volmageddon" vol-spike), to an all-time high of **82.7 in March 2020** (the pandemic crash), and to **65.7 in August 2024** (the yen-carry unwind we opened with). The chart below shows the calm year-end closes against those violent spikes — and it's worth staring at, because the *gap* between the placid closes and the panic spikes is the visual definition of a regime change.

![VIX year-end closes as a line with red markers for the 2018, 2020 and 2024 panic spikes](/imgs/blogs/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates-2.png)

The last foundational concept is **correlation**, and it's the one that does the most damage when misunderstood. Correlation measures how two assets move *together*, on a scale from +1 (they move in perfect lockstep) through 0 (no relationship) to −1 (they move exactly opposite). Diversification — the only "free lunch" in finance — depends entirely on holding things with *low or negative* correlation, so that when one falls, another holds up. A portfolio of ten stocks that all have correlation +0.9 to each other is barely diversified at all; it's basically one big bet. The cruel feature of risk-off events, which we'll devote a whole section to, is that correlations *rise toward 1 exactly when fear strikes*. In normal times your holdings look nicely spread out; in a crisis they all turn out to be the same trade. That single fact — **correlations are not constant; they spike in a panic** — is the difference between a trader who survives a risk-off wave and one who gets carried out.

It's worth being precise about how correlation and beta relate, because traders conflate them and it matters. Beta tells you *how much* an asset moves for a given move in the market — its sensitivity, its slope. Correlation tells you *how reliably* it moves with the market — the tightness of the relationship, how much scatter there is around that slope. An asset can have a high beta but a low correlation (it moves a lot, but not dependably in sync — say an individual biotech stock that's volatile for its own reasons), or a low beta but a high correlation (it moves a little, but very reliably in the same direction — say a low-volatility utility ETF). For risk-on/risk-off purposes you care about *both*: in a panic, the assets that hurt you most are the ones that have *both* high beta (they fall a lot) *and* high correlation to the risk regime (they fall *together with everything else*, so they don't offset each other). High-beta tech and crypto are the textbook example — big moves, tightly synced to the regime — which is exactly why they're the first things a disciplined trader cuts when the dials turn risk-off.

One more foundational distinction prevents a lot of confusion: **volatility is not the same as risk-off, and a rising VIX is not always bearish in the way beginners assume.** Volatility measures the *size* of moves in either direction; risk-off is specifically about the *direction* of money (out of risk, into havens). They usually rise together — fear makes moves bigger *and* sends money to safety — but not always. A market can be volatile *up* (a violent short-covering rally can spike realized volatility while being the opposite of risk-off). What makes the VIX a risk-off gauge in practice is that the options market structurally pays up for *downside* protection far more than upside, so a VIX spike is almost always a fear-of-falling spike. Keep the distinction in mind, because it's why you read the VIX *alongside* the dollar and credit spreads rather than alone — the other dials confirm that a vol spike is genuine risk-off and not just noise.

#### Worked example: a risk-off day, tallied across a balanced book versus a barbell

Let's make this concrete with a single bad day and two different books, so you can *feel* the mechanism in P&L. Suppose a classic risk-off day hits. A typical cross-asset tally for such a day looks like this: the **S&P 500 falls 3%**, the **dollar (DXY) rises 1%**, **gold rises 1.5%**, and the **10-year Treasury yield falls 15 basis points** (a basis point is one hundredth of a percentage point, so 15 bp = 0.15%). Remember that bond *prices* move opposite to yields: a 15 bp drop in the 10-year yield is roughly a **+1.3%** price gain on the bond (the 10-year has a duration of about 8.5, so price change ≈ −duration × yield change = −8.5 × −0.15% ≈ +1.3%).

Now take **Book A, "balanced but naive"**: \$1,000,000 split as 70% S&P 500 (\$700,000), 20% high-yield credit (\$200,000, which behaves like equity with a beta around 0.4, so call it −1.2% on the day), and 10% emerging-market FX (\$100,000, down 2% as EM sells off in risk-off). The P&L:

```
S&P 500:      700,000 x -3.0%  = -21,000
High-yield:   200,000 x -1.2%  =  -2,400
EM FX:        100,000 x -2.0%  =  -2,000
-----------------------------------------
Total Book A                   = -25,400   (-2.54% of the book)
```

Every sleeve is a different *name* but the same *trade* — long risk. The "diversification" did nothing because all three are on the risk side of the seesaw.

Now take **Book B, "barbell"**: the same \$1,000,000, but split 60% S&P 500 (\$600,000), 25% long Treasuries (\$250,000, +1.3% as yields fall), and 15% gold (\$150,000, +1.5%). The P&L:

```
S&P 500:      600,000 x -3.0%  = -18,000
Treasuries:   250,000 x +1.3%  =  +3,250
Gold:         150,000 x +1.5%  =  +2,250
-----------------------------------------
Total Book B                   = -12,500   (-1.25% of the book)
```

Book B lost *half* as much, not because it was less invested in stocks, but because a quarter of it was on the *other* side of the seesaw, gaining exactly when stocks fell. The haven sleeves didn't just sit there — they actively offset the loss. **The intuition to keep: real diversification means owning the other side of the regime, not just owning more names on the same side.**

## The canonical risk-on and risk-off baskets

With the foundations in place, here is the map every macro trader carries in their head: the two baskets, and what gets bought and sold as money rotates between them. The single most valuable thing about this map is that it lets you *predict* a dozen assets at once. If you know it's a risk-off day, you already know — before you look — that stocks are down, the dollar is up, the yen is up, Treasuries are up, gold is probably up, credit spreads are wider, emerging markets are down, and crypto is down. They are not independent; they are choreographed.

**The risk-on basket — what gets bought when confidence rises:** equities (especially high-beta tech, small-caps, and cyclical sectors like industrials and energy), high-yield corporate credit, emerging-market stocks and currencies, industrial commodities like copper ("Dr. Copper," whose price tracks global growth), the Australian and New Zealand dollars and other commodity/EM currencies, and crypto. The common thread: these all *need* a confident, liquid, growing world to do well, and they offer a premium for bearing that cyclical risk.

**The risk-off basket — what gets bought when fear rises:** the US dollar (especially cash and T-bills), US Treasuries (the longer the maturity, the more they rally on a flight to safety), the Japanese yen, the Swiss franc, gold, and — within equities — defensive sectors like utilities, consumer staples and healthcare that people need regardless of the cycle. The common thread: liquidity, low or negative beta, and a structural global bid.

The figure below lays out the rotation explicitly — the two columns, what flows out of one and into the other, and the *direction* of money. The mental shortcut is that the two baskets are roughly *mirror images*: in risk-on, the top column is bought and the bottom sold; in risk-off, the flow reverses. A trader's whole job, much of the time, is to figure out which way the arrow is pointing today.

![Rotation map with a risk-on column and a risk-off column showing what is bought and sold in each regime](/imgs/blogs/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates-3.png)

A crucial subtlety: these baskets are about *flows*, not fundamentals, over short horizons. On a risk-off day, a fantastic company with rising profits can fall 4% while a mediocre one falls 4% too — because in a flight to safety, traders aren't selling *bad* companies, they're selling *risk*, and they raise cash by selling whatever they can, especially the liquid winners. This is why, in a sharp risk-off wave, you often see the *best* stocks fall hardest at first: they're the ones people can sell without a discount. The regime overwhelms the idiosyncratic story. That's the double edge of the risk-on/risk-off lens — it's enormously powerful for positioning the whole book, but it also means that in a true panic, stock-picking skill temporarily stops mattering and the *only* thing that matters is which side of the seesaw you're on.

There's a deeper "why" behind the baskets that's worth making explicit, because it tells you which assets *belong* in each column even for instruments you've never seen before. Every risk asset, underneath, is a claim on a *future* stream of cash or growth — a stock is a claim on future profits, a high-yield bond is a claim on a shaky company paying you back, an EM currency is a claim on that economy staying stable, crypto is a claim on future adoption. All of those futures are *more* valuable when the world is confident and *less* valuable when it's frightened, and they all get *discounted* harder when rates and risk premia rise in a panic. The havens, by contrast, are claims on the *present and the certain* — cash is spendable now, a Treasury is a near-certain repayment, gold is a tangible thing you hold today. So the seesaw is really *future-and-uncertain* on one side versus *present-and-certain* on the other, and fear is simply the market re-pricing the future downward and the present upward all at once. When you meet a new instrument and wonder which basket it's in, ask: *does this pay me for an uncertain future, or does it shelter me in the certain present?* That question sorts almost everything correctly, and it's why even brand-new asset classes slot neatly into the old risk-on/risk-off choreography the first time a real panic tests them.

Notice too that the *magnitude* of an asset's move within its basket is roughly its beta to the regime. In a −3% S&P day, a high-beta tech index (beta ~1.4) falls ~4.2%, an EM equity index (beta ~1.2) falls ~3.6%, high-yield credit (beta ~0.4) falls ~1.2%, and a defensive utilities sector (beta ~0.5) falls ~1.5% — while on the haven side, long Treasuries (beta ~−0.3 in a growth panic) might rise ~0.9% and gold its own ~1.5%. You can *pre-estimate* a risk-off day's damage across your whole book just by knowing each sleeve's beta and the size of the S&P move. That's not a parlor trick; it's the actual arithmetic risk managers run to size books, and it's why beta is the hinge between the conceptual seesaw and the dollar P&L it produces.

#### Worked example: the VIX as your position-sizer, at 15 versus 35

Here is the most practical use of everything so far: letting the VIX *size* your book, automatically, so you take less risk exactly when risk is most dangerous. The principle is that the *dollar risk* of a position is roughly its size times the market's volatility. If volatility doubles, the same-sized position is twice as risky. So to keep your *risk* constant, you should *shrink your position* as volatility rises — this is called **volatility targeting**, and it's how serious risk books are run.

Suppose your target is to risk about \$10,000 of daily volatility on your equity book — that is, you want your typical daily P&L swing to be around \$10,000. The S&P 500's expected daily move is roughly the VIX divided by 16 (because there are about 16 square-roots-of-trading-days in a year, and the VIX is an *annual* vol figure: 252 trading days, √252 ≈ 15.9). So:

```
When VIX = 15:  daily move  ~= 15 / 16  = 0.94% per day
When VIX = 35:  daily move  ~= 35 / 16  = 2.19% per day
```

To risk \$10,000 of daily swing, your position size is target-risk divided by daily-percent-move:

```
VIX = 15:  position = 10,000 / 0.0094 = ~1,064,000   (full size)
VIX = 35:  position = 10,000 / 0.0219 =   ~457,000   (43% of full size)
```

When the VIX rises from 15 to 35, you should cut your equity exposure to **about 43% of normal** — *less than half* — just to keep your risk constant. If you *don't* shrink, you're not holding the same trade; you're more than doubling your risk at the worst possible moment. This is why disciplined traders mechanically de-gross (reduce exposure) as the VIX spikes, and it's why risk-off feeds on itself: rising vol forces vol-targeting funds to sell, which pushes prices down and vol up, which forces more selling. **The intuition to keep: the VIX doesn't just tell you the regime; it tells you exactly how much to shrink, and at 35 you should be running well under half your calm-market size.**

## Why havens are havens: the dollar bid, Treasuries, gold, and the yen carry-unwind

Now let's go one level deeper and dismantle *why* each haven behaves the way it does, because once you understand the mechanism, the moves stop looking mysterious and start looking inevitable.

**The dollar's haven bid.** When global risk-off hits, the dollar almost always rises — and the reason is not patriotism but plumbing. Recall the dollar's three jobs: it's the world's invoicing currency, its dominant funding (borrowing) currency, and its top reserve asset. The funding-currency job is the key to the haven bid. The world has borrowed roughly \$13 trillion in dollars it has to pay back. In a risk-off scramble, three things happen at once. First, leveraged players who borrowed dollars to buy risk assets get *margin calls* and have to buy back dollars to repay loans and post collateral — a forced, price-insensitive bid. Second, exporters and investors abroad, frightened, repatriate into the safest, most liquid thing they know — Treasuries, which they must buy *dollars* to own. Third, traders simply raise cash, and the world's cash is dollars. All three are dollar *demand* surging exactly when fear surges. The result is what we see in the data: through the 2022 "everything down" year, the dollar didn't fall with stocks — it **rallied to a 103.5 year-end close, with a 114.8 intraday peak in September**, even as the S&P fell ~19% and bonds had their worst year in a century.

![Dollar index DXY yearly close bar chart with the 2022 risk-off rally to 103.5 highlighted](/imgs/blogs/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates-4.png)

#### Worked example: the dollar's haven bid in 2022, year by year

Let's trace the dollar through the 2022 risk-off year against its neighbors, using the year-end DXY closes, to see the haven bid in numbers. The DXY entered 2022 around its 2021 close of **95.7**. Over the year, the Federal Reserve hiked rates from near zero to 4.5% to fight 9% inflation — a *US-boom-meets-global-fear* setup that is the dollar's best regime (the "dollar smile": the dollar rises in both a US boom *and* a global panic, and 2022 was both at once). The DXY climbed relentlessly:

```
2021 close:        95.7   (starting point)
2022 Sep peak:    114.8   (intraday high, +20.0% from the 2021 close)
2022 close:       103.5   (+8.2% on the year)
2023 close:       101.3   (-2.1% as the Fed paused and fear eased)
2024 close:       108.5   (+7.1% as US growth re-accelerated)
```

Now price what that did to a non-American holding dollar debt. Imagine a European company with a \$10,000,000 dollar loan, earning revenue in euros. As the dollar rose 8.2% against the euro over 2022, the euro-cost of repaying that fixed \$10,000,000 debt rose by roughly **8.2%, or about €820,000 of pure currency loss** — with no change in the loan itself. Multiply that across \$13 trillion of global dollar debt and you see why a rising dollar *tightens conditions everywhere*: it's a tax on every borrower outside the US, which forces them to buy still more dollars to cover, which lifts the dollar further. **The intuition to keep: the dollar rises in risk-off because the world is short dollars, and a panic forces the short to cover — the haven bid is a margin call writ planetary.**

**Treasuries: the flight-to-quality engine.** US Treasury bonds are the haven's haven for two reasons. They're the deepest, most liquid market on Earth (you can sell size instantly), and they're backed by the US government's ability to tax and, ultimately, print — so in *nominal* terms they cannot default. When stocks crash, money floods into Treasuries, pushing their *prices up* and their *yields down*. A falling 10-year yield in a risk-off wave is the textbook flight-to-quality signal. The longer-dated the bond, the more its price moves for a given yield change (its **duration** is higher), so long bonds rally hardest in a flight to safety — which is exactly why the classic hedge against a stock crash is to be long *long-dated* Treasuries. (The mechanics of yield and duration are unpacked in [interest rates: the price of money, the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable); for our purposes the key fact is *Treasury price up, yield down, in risk-off*.) The one giant asterisk — when this hedge *failed* in 2022 — gets its own section below, because it's the most important exception in the whole framework.

**Gold: the no-one's-liability haven.** Gold is the oldest haven, and its logic is unique: it is the only major asset that is *no one's liability*. A Treasury is a promise from the US government; a dollar is a liability of the Fed; a stock is a claim on a company. Gold is just *gold* — it can't default, can't be printed, and isn't anyone's IOU. So in a crisis of *trust* — when people fear that institutions, currencies, or governments might fail — gold shines. But here's the subtlety that trips up beginners, and which we'll dismantle in the misconceptions section: gold does *not* always rise in risk-off. Gold has a complicated relationship with two things — the dollar (gold is priced in dollars, so a soaring dollar can push gold *down* even in a panic) and *real* interest rates (gold pays no yield, so when real yields rise, gold's opportunity cost rises and it falls). In 2022, gold *fell* during a risk-off year because the dollar and real yields both soared. Gold is a haven against *some* risk-off events (currency/inflation crises) but not all (dollar-and-rates-driven sell-offs).

**The yen: the carry-unwind haven.** This is the most mechanical and most fascinating haven, and it's the one that drove our opening Aug-2024 example. The yen is a haven not because Japan is uniquely safe but because of the **carry trade**. For years, Japan kept interest rates near zero (even negative) while the rest of the world paid much higher rates. So traders worldwide ran a simple, enormously popular trade: **borrow yen cheaply, sell the yen for dollars (or pesos, or whatever), and invest in higher-yielding assets** — pocketing the interest-rate difference (the "carry"). This borrowing-and-selling of yen *weakens* the yen and pushes USD/JPY *up* (more yen per dollar). The data shows the carry-on regime plainly: USD/JPY climbed from **108.7 at end-2019 to 157.2 at end-2024**, with intraday peaks of **151.9 in October 2022** and **161.9 in July 2024** — the yen getting weaker and weaker as the carry trade got bigger and bigger.

![USD JPY yearly close line chart rising from 108 to 157 with the 2024 carry unwind marked](/imgs/blogs/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates-6.png)

But a borrowed-yen position is a *short* on the yen, and shorts have to be *covered* in a panic. When risk-off hits and traders dump their risk assets, they have to *buy back yen* to repay their yen loans — all at once. That covering bids the yen violently *higher* (USD/JPY *down*), which is the **carry unwind**. The terrifying part is the feedback loop: a stronger yen makes the carry trade lose money on the FX leg, which forces *more* unwinding, which strengthens the yen further. That is exactly what detonated in August 2024: the yen snapped stronger, USD/JPY fell hard from its 161.9 July peak, the unwind forced selling across every risk asset funded by cheap yen, and the VIX hit 65.7. The yen is a haven because it's the world's funding short, and a panic is a giant margin call on that short — structurally identical to the dollar's haven bid, just in a different currency.

This reveals the unifying mechanism behind *all* the funding-currency havens, and it's worth stating plainly because it demystifies the whole haven side of the seesaw. A funding currency is a currency the world *borrows and sells short* to finance risk positions — the yen and franc historically, and in its own way the dollar through the offshore eurodollar system. Borrowing-and-selling a currency in calm times *weakens* it (which is why these havens drift lower for years during risk-on, the exact opposite of what "safe" suggests). But every borrowed-and-sold currency is a *short position*, and the iron law of short positions is that they must be covered. A panic is precisely the event that forces every short to cover at once — margin calls, blown stops, risk limits breached — and that synchronized covering is what makes the haven bid so violent and so reliable. So the dollar bid and the yen bid are not two separate phenomena; they're the *same* phenomenon (a forced short-cover) in two currencies that the world happens to be short. Once you see havens as "the things the world is short, that snap back when the shorts cover," their behavior stops being a list to memorize and becomes a single principle to apply.

The carry trade's profitability also tells you *when* an unwind is most dangerous, which is tradeable intelligence. A carry trade earns the interest-rate gap *as long as the funding currency doesn't strengthen faster than the gap pays*. So the trade is most crowded — and most fragile — exactly when it's been working *longest and smoothest*, because calm, profitable conditions pull in ever more capital and leverage. By July 2024, with USD/JPY at its 161.9 peak after years of one-way yen weakness, the carry trade was about as crowded as it had ever been, which is *why* a small BoJ policy nudge could trigger such an outsized unwind: the position was enormous and the exits were narrow. The lesson for the playbook is counterintuitive but vital — *the smoother and more profitable a carry-style trade has been, the closer it is to a violent unwind*, because crowding is invisible in the calm and only revealed in the snap. We come back to spotting that crowding in [following the flows: positioning, COT, and dealer hedging](/blog/trading/macro-trading/following-the-flows-positioning-cot-dealer-hedging).

## Correlations rise in a crisis: when diversification fails on cue

This is the section that, if you internalize nothing else, will save you the most money. The promise of diversification is that holding many different things protects you, because they won't all fall at once. That promise is *true in calm markets and false in a panic*, and the reason is that **correlations are not stable — they rush toward +1 precisely when fear strikes.**

Why does this happen? Because in a risk-off event, the dominant force moving every risk asset is no longer its own fundamentals — it's the regime. When a vol-targeting fund de-grosses, a margin call forces a sale, or a panicked investor raises cash, they don't sell *one* thing; they sell *everything liquid* at once, indiscriminately. Stocks, credit, EM, commodities, crypto — all get sold by the same hands for the same reason (raise cash, cut risk) at the same time. So their returns, which in calm markets are driven by their own separate stories and therefore weakly correlated, suddenly all get driven by a *single common factor*: the flight from risk. When one factor dominates everything, everything correlates. A portfolio that had correlation +0.3 across its sleeves in calm times can see those correlations leap to +0.8 or +0.9 in a crash — and a +0.9-correlated portfolio is barely diversified at all; it behaves like one concentrated bet.

The figure below shows the mechanism: in normal times the risk assets are spread out, their returns scattered, diversification working; in a crisis they collapse onto a single line, all falling together. This is sometimes called "correlation goes to one," and it is the mathematical reason that risk-off feels so much worse than any single asset's drawdown would suggest. Your *whole book* moves as one, so your losses compound across positions you thought were independent.

![Two scatter panels showing risk asset returns spread out in calm markets and collapsing to one line in a crisis](/imgs/blogs/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates-5.png)

There's a second, more insidious failure, and 2022 is its case study: the correlation between the two halves of the classic hedge — stocks and bonds — can *itself* flip. For most of the past three decades, stocks and Treasuries had *negative* correlation: when stocks fell, bonds rose, so a 60/40 portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds) was beautifully cushioned. That negative correlation is the entire reason 60/40 was the default "balanced" portfolio for a generation. But it depends on a regime: it holds when the dominant fear is *growth* (a recession scare sends money from stocks into bonds). It *breaks* when the dominant fear is *inflation* — because high inflation is bad for *both* stocks and bonds at once (it forces the central bank to hike rates, which crushes bond prices *and* stock valuations together). In 2022, inflation hit a 40-year high of 9%, the Fed hiked aggressively, and the stock-bond correlation flipped *positive*. Both fell together. The cushion didn't just thin — it inverted.

#### Worked example: the 60/40 portfolio when stock-bond correlation flips positive in 2022

Let's price the 60/40 disaster of 2022 to see what a correlation flip does. A 60/40 portfolio holds 60% in the S&P 500 and 40% in US Treasuries (say a 10-year, duration ~8). In 2022, the S&P 500 fell about **−18%** (total return), and the 10-year Treasury yield rose from **1.52% to 3.88%** — a 236 bp rise. With a duration of ~8, that's a bond *price* loss of roughly **−8 × 2.36% ≈ −18.9%** (cushioned somewhat by coupon income; the actual long-Treasury total return that year was about **−17%**). Run the portfolio:

```
S&P 500 sleeve:    60% x -18%  = -10.8%
Treasury sleeve:   40% x -17%  =  -6.8%
-----------------------------------------
60/40 total 2022                = -17.6%
```

Compare that to how 60/40 was *supposed* to behave in a normal risk-off year. In 2008 — a *growth* panic, not an inflation panic — the S&P fell ~37% but long Treasuries *rose* ~+20%, so a 60/40 lost only about (0.6 × −37%) + (0.4 × +20%) = **−14.2%**, with the bonds doing exactly their cushioning job against a much *worse* stock drop. In 2022, with a *smaller* stock drop (−18% vs −37%), 60/40 lost *more* (−17.6% vs −14.2%), because the bond sleeve, instead of cushioning, *added* to the loss. The hedge didn't underperform — it reversed sign.

```
2008 (growth panic):  stocks -37%, bonds +20%  -> 60/40 = -14.2%
2022 (inflation panic): stocks -18%, bonds -17% -> 60/40 = -17.6%
```

**The intuition to keep: your hedge only hedges in the regime it was built for; when the fear changes from growth to inflation, the stock-bond correlation flips positive and your "balanced" portfolio becomes a leveraged bet on the same thing twice.**

## Common misconceptions

The risk-on/risk-off framework is so intuitive that beginners over-apply it, and a handful of specific myths reliably cost money. Each one is corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "Gold always rises in risk-off, so it's the perfect crash hedge."** False, and 2022 proves it. Gold *fell* about 0.3% in 2022 (and was down ~10% from its March peak by year-end) during one of the worst risk-off years for stocks and bonds in a generation. Why? Gold is priced in dollars and pays no yield. When the dollar soared (DXY to 103.5) and *real* yields soared from −1% to +1.7% (the 10-year TIPS real yield), gold's two headwinds — a strong dollar and a high opportunity cost of holding a zero-yield asset — overwhelmed its safe-haven bid. Gold is a haven against *currency-and-trust* crises (a falling dollar, fears of money-printing), not against *dollar-and-rates* risk-off. Knowing *which kind* of risk-off you're in tells you whether gold helps or hurts.

**Myth 2: "Correlations are roughly stable, so I can trust my backtested diversification."** Dangerously false. As we just saw, correlations are *regime-dependent* and spike in a crisis. A portfolio whose sleeves had correlation +0.3 in the calm 2017 market (VIX ~11) saw those correlations leap toward +0.8 in the March 2020 crash (VIX 82.7), when everything liquid was sold at once. Any risk model that assumes constant correlation *understates your true crash risk*, often by a factor of two or more, because it credits you with diversification that evaporates exactly when you need it. Always stress-test your book assuming correlations go to 1 in a panic — that's the realistic risk-off scenario, not the average-times scenario.

**Myth 3: "Cash is not a position — sitting in cash means I'm doing nothing."** False, and this is one of the most expensive beginner errors. Holding cash *is* a position: it's a *long* on the dollar and a *short* on every asset you're not holding. When you sit in cash during a risk-off wave, you are *winning relative to everyone who's long risk* — and because the dollar itself *rises* in risk-off, cash isn't even flat; it's a quiet positive-carry haven that gains purchasing power as risk assets fall. The decision to be in cash is an *active macro call* that you're in or near a risk-off regime, and it's frequently the single best "trade" available. The trader who says "I'll wait in cash for a better setup" is not on the sidelines — they're positioned for risk-off and earning the T-bill rate (around 4-5% in 2023-2024) while they wait.

**Myth 4: "The yen and Swiss franc are havens because Japan and Switzerland are safe."** Mostly false — it's mechanics, not virtue. The yen is a haven because it's the world's biggest *funding short* (the carry trade), so a panic forces short-covering that bids it up; the franc is similar (and the Swiss National Bank has historically intervened to *cap* its strength, which tells you the haven bid is real enough to fight). The proof is in the direction: in calm risk-on times these currencies *weaken* steadily (USD/JPY climbed from 108.7 to 157.2 over 2019-2024) precisely *because* they're being borrowed and sold — the opposite of what you'd expect from a "safe" currency. Their haven status is a *positioning* fact, not a *safety* fact.

**Myth 5: "Risk-off means sell everything and go to cash, full stop."** Too blunt. The skill is in *which* haven, because they behave differently across risk-off types. In a *growth/recession* panic, long Treasuries are the best haven (they rally hard). In an *inflation* panic, Treasuries *fail* and the dollar/cash and sometimes commodities are the havens. In a *liquidity/dash-for-cash* event (March 2020), even Treasuries and gold briefly *fall* as everything is sold for dollars, and *only* cash dollars work — for a few days. Reading risk-off correctly isn't "sell everything"; it's matching the haven to the *flavor* of the fear.

**Myth 6: "Risk-on and risk-off alternate on some regular rhythm I can time."** False, and it leads to over-trading. Regimes are *persistent but irregular*: risk-on can grind on for years (2017 was a year so calm the VIX barely left the low teens), and risk-off can be a single terrifying day (Aug 2024) or a months-long grind (2022). There is no calendar. What you can rely on is *asymmetry*: risk-on tends to build *slowly* — the VIX drifts lower, the dollar softens, spreads tighten over weeks and months — while risk-off arrives *fast* — the VIX can triple in days, as it did in Feb 2018 and Aug 2024. "Up the stairs, down the elevator" is the cliché, and it's true. The practical consequence is that you don't need to *predict* the turn; you need to *react fast* when the dials flip, because the risk-off side moves far quicker than the risk-on side, and a day's delay in de-grossing can cost you a month's gains.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three episodes show the framework operating live, each a different *flavor* of risk-off, which is exactly the nuance the playbook needs.

**March 2020 — the dash for cash.** The pandemic crash was the most extreme risk-off event in modern history, and it had a strange twist that teaches the deepest lesson. As COVID lockdowns hit, the VIX exploded to an all-time intraday high of **82.7**, the S&P 500 fell 34% in 33 days, and oil briefly went *negative*. Classic risk-off. But for about ten days in mid-March, something broke the textbook: even *Treasuries and gold fell*. Why? Because the panic was so total that everyone needed *cash dollars specifically* — to meet margin calls, fund redemptions, and hoard liquidity — so they sold *everything*, including their havens, to raise dollars. This is the **dash for cash**, the most violent form of risk-off, where the only haven that works is physical dollar liquidity. The dollar (DXY) spiked toward 103 in days. It ended only when the Fed flooded the system with trillions in liquidity and opened dollar swap lines to foreign central banks. The lesson: in the most extreme risk-off, the haven hierarchy collapses to *one* — cash dollars — for a brief, terrifying window.

**2022 — everything down.** This was the rare year when *both* risk assets and the traditional bond hedge fell together, because the fear was *inflation* (9% CPI, a 40-year high), not growth. Stocks fell ~18%, Treasuries fell ~17% (their worst year in a century), gold fell, and crypto fell ~65%. The *only* major asset that rose was the **dollar**, which rallied to a 103.5 close and a 114.8 intraday peak — the haven of last resort when even bonds fail. 2022 is the canonical proof that "risk-off" is not one thing: an inflation-driven risk-off breaks the 60/40 hedge and leaves *cash and the dollar* as the only refuge. Any trader running a backtested stock-bond hedge got a brutal education in regime-dependence.

**August 2024 — the carry unwind.** Our opening example, and the purest *positioning-driven* risk-off. Nothing fundamental broke; the Bank of Japan merely hiked rates a little and signaled more, which made the cheap-yen funding of the global carry trade suddenly less cheap. The trade began to unwind, traders scrambled to buy back yen, USD/JPY fell hard from its 161.9 July peak, the yen-funded risk positions everywhere got liquidated at once, and the VIX spiked to **65.7** — the third-highest ever — on a day with no recession, no war, no bank failure. Within weeks, as the unwind exhausted itself and the BoJ soothed markets, the VIX collapsed and stocks recovered. The lesson: the *biggest* risk-off spikes can come from *positioning* (a crowded trade unwinding), not fundamentals, and they can reverse as fast as they came — which makes them tradeable for those who recognize the mechanism.

#### Worked example: reading the regime off three dials on the August 2024 morning

Put yourself at that August 5, 2024 open and read the four dials of the playbook in real time, to see how one regime read sizes the whole book. The dashboard: **VIX, the dollar, credit spreads, and breadth.**

```
Dial 1 - VIX:            spiked from ~16 to 65.7 intraday   -> SCREAMING risk-off
Dial 2 - Dollar/yen:     USD/JPY crashed ~5% (yen surging)  -> carry unwind, risk-off
Dial 3 - Credit spreads: high-yield spreads gapping wider   -> risk-off confirmed
Dial 4 - Breadth:        >90% of S&P stocks falling at open -> indiscriminate selling
```

All four dials point the same way: full risk-off. Now size the book to *one* decision. If you'd been running a \$1,000,000 equity-heavy book at full size, the vol-targeting rule says: with the VIX at 65 versus a normal 16, your same position is now **~4x as risky** (65/16 ≈ 4), so to hold risk constant you cut exposure to roughly **25% of normal** — sell three-quarters of the gross. A trader who read the four dials at the open and de-grossed kept a 12% intraday equity drop from becoming a portfolio catastrophe; one who held full size, reasoning "nothing fundamental changed," took the full hit. **The intuition to keep: when all four dials agree, you don't analyze forty positions — you make one regime call and let it set the size of the entire book.**

## How to trade it: the risk-regime playbook

This is the payoff. Everything above collapses into a single operating procedure: *read the regime, then size and shape the whole book to it.* You are not trying to predict the future; you're trying to correctly identify which of the two phases you're in *right now* and position accordingly, updating as the dials move.

**Step 1: read the regime off the dashboard.** Four dials, checked in this order, tell you the regime at a glance. The figure below is the dashboard.

![Risk regime dashboard panel showing VIX, dollar, credit spreads and breadth gauges](/imgs/blogs/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates-7.png)

- **The VIX (fear).** Below ~15 = risk-on/complacent; 15-20 = neutral; 20-30 = caution; above 30 = risk-off; above 50 = crisis. It's the fastest, most sensitive dial. Watch the *direction* and *speed* as much as the level — a VIX rising fast from 14 to 22 is a regime *changing*, which is more actionable than a VIX sitting flat at 25.
- **The dollar (DXY) and USD/JPY (havens).** A rising dollar and a *falling* USD/JPY (yen strengthening) confirm risk-off; a falling dollar and rising USD/JPY confirm risk-on. The dollar is the slower, more structural dial — it filters out VIX head-fakes. When the VIX *and* the dollar both move risk-off together, the signal is real.
- **Credit spreads (the smart-money dial).** The extra yield that risky corporate bonds pay over Treasuries. Credit traders are conservative and well-informed, so *widening* high-yield spreads is a high-quality risk-off confirmation, and *tightening* spreads confirm risk-on. Spreads tend to lead equities at major turns — a stock rally on *widening* spreads is suspect.
- **Breadth (participation).** What share of stocks are rising versus falling. Broad participation (most stocks up) is healthy risk-on; *narrowing* breadth (a few mega-caps holding the index up while most stocks fall) is a classic late-cycle warning that risk-on is getting fragile.

When all four agree, you have a high-confidence regime read. When they *diverge* — say the VIX is low but credit spreads are quietly widening and breadth is narrowing — you're at a *transition*, the most valuable and most dangerous moment to read correctly.

**Step 2: size the whole book to the regime.** This is the one-decision payoff. Use the VIX as your vol-targeting dial (Worked example above): the higher the VIX, the smaller your gross risk exposure, scaled by roughly VIX-normal ÷ VIX-now. In a clean risk-on regime (VIX in the low teens, dollar soft, spreads tight, breadth broad), you run full size on the risk basket. As the regime tilts risk-off, you mechanically de-gross — and you don't agonize position-by-position, because in a panic correlations go to 1 and the *whole book* is one trade anyway. One regime call sets the size of everything.

**Step 3: shape the book — rotate from the risk basket to the haven basket, matched to the flavor.** Beyond sizing, you rotate *what* you hold:

- **Tilting risk-off:** reduce equities (cut high-beta first — tech, small-caps, EM), reduce high-yield credit, reduce crypto; add the haven basket — but *match the haven to the fear*. Growth/recession fear → long *long-dated Treasuries* (the duration rallies hardest). Inflation fear → *cash and the dollar*, not bonds (2022's lesson). Liquidity/dash-for-cash → *cash dollars only*, briefly. A trust/currency crisis → *gold*.
- **Tilting risk-on:** the mirror image — add high-beta equities, cyclicals, EM, credit, and commodity currencies; reduce or *fund* the position by shorting the havens (e.g., the carry trade itself: short yen, long risk).

**Step 4: define the invalidation.** Every regime call needs a line that says "I was wrong." For a risk-off positioning, the invalidation is the dashboard reversing: the VIX collapsing back below 20, the dollar rolling over, credit spreads tightening sharply, breadth broadening. Risk-off events — *especially* positioning-driven ones like Aug 2024 — can reverse violently and fast, so a haven-heavy book held too long after the VIX has crashed back is its own mistake. The discipline is symmetric: get defensive *fast* when the dials turn risk-off, and get *back on risk* fast when they turn back, rather than fighting the regime out of stubbornness.

**The one-line version of the whole playbook:** find the regime on four dials (VIX, dollar/yen, credit spreads, breadth), let the VIX set your gross size (shrink hard as it rises), rotate from the risk basket to the *flavor-matched* haven basket as you tilt risk-off, and define your invalidation as the dashboard flipping back. Do that, and you've turned the bewildering daily noise of forty correlated positions into one clean macro decision — which is the entire reason risk-on/risk-off is the most valuable lens a macro trader owns. To go deeper on the dial that filters all the others, read [the dollar system and the DXY](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy); on what actually moves the currencies you're reading as havens, [what moves exchange rates: rates, flows, and carry](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-moves-exchange-rates-rates-flows-carry); and on spotting crowded trades like the carry trade *before* they unwind, [following the flows: positioning, COT, and dealer hedging](/blog/trading/macro-trading/following-the-flows-positioning-cot-dealer-hedging).
