> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A rate change moves stocks through two channels — the discount-rate channel (higher rates lower the present value of future earnings, hitting long-duration growth stocks hardest) and the earnings channel (tighter policy slows the economy and its profits) — and the sector dispersion this creates is enormous.
>
> - A stock is the **present value of its future earnings**. Raise the rate you discount those earnings at, and the price falls even if the earnings never change. This is the discount-rate channel, and it is mostly arithmetic, not opinion.
> - **Equity duration** is the key word: a growth stock's value sits far in the future, so a higher discount rate compounds against it brutally. The present value of \$100 received in ten years drops from \$74.41 at a 3% rate to \$55.84 at 6% — a **25% haircut from rates alone**.
> - The second channel is **earnings**: tighter policy slows the economy, so cyclical profits fall too. Most stocks get hit by *both* channels at once, which is why a hiking cycle feels so violent.
> - The one number to remember: in 2022 the Fed hiked from a 0.25% ceiling to 5.50%, and S&P 500 sectors split from **Energy +65.7%** to **Communication Services -39.9%** — a 105-point gap that was almost entirely a story of rate sensitivity, not company quality.

In 2022 the Federal Reserve did something it had not done in forty years: it raised its policy rate hard and fast, from a target range topping out at 0.25% in March to 4.50% by December, on its way to 5.50% the following summer. Five and a quarter percentage points of tightening in about sixteen months. And the stock market did not fall evenly. It *split apart*.

If you owned the S&P 500 energy sector, you had one of the best years of your life: a total return of **+65.7%**. If you owned communication services — Alphabet, Meta, Netflix, the telecoms — you lost **-39.9%**. Consumer discretionary, the home of Amazon and Tesla, fell -37%. Technology fell -28.2%. Real estate fell -26.2%. Meanwhile utilities eked out +1.6% and consumer staples were almost flat at -0.6%. Two stocks could both be "American companies in the S&P 500" and have a hundred-percentage-point difference in outcome over a single calendar year. The market was not pricing whether these were good businesses. It was pricing one thing above all else: **how sensitive each company's value was to the interest rate.**

That is what this post is about. A rate change does not hit "the stock market" as a single blob. It travels into equity prices through two specific, identifiable channels — the **discount-rate channel** and the **earnings channel** — and because different stocks sit at wildly different points on a spectrum called **equity duration**, the same rate move that barely scratches one company can cut another in half. Understand the two channels and the duration spectrum, and the chaos of a year like 2022 resolves into something almost mechanical. We will build the whole thing from absolute zero — how a stock is even priced, what a discount rate is, why growth equals long duration — and then hand you a playbook for mapping your own book's rate sensitivity.

## Foundations: how a stock is priced, the discount rate, and equity duration

Before we can talk about how rates *move* stocks, we need four ideas, each built from scratch: what a stock price actually **is**, what a **discount rate** does, where the **risk-free rate** sits inside it, and why a growth stock has **long equity duration**. Every result in this post is a consequence of these four. If you already know discounted cash flow, skim; if you do not, this section is the whole foundation.

### A stock is the present value of its future earnings

Start with the most important idea in all of equity investing, and the one most beginners never quite internalize: **a stock is not a number on a screen that goes up and down for mysterious reasons. It is a claim on a stream of future cash.** When you own a share, you own a sliver of every dollar of profit that company will ever earn, from now until the end of time. The price of the share is what the market is willing to pay *today* for that future stream.

Here is the everyday-money version. Suppose a friend offers you a tiny vending-machine business. It will pay you \$10 in profit next year, \$10 the year after, and \$10 every year forever. How much is that worth today? Not infinity, even though the dollars add up to infinity, because **money in the future is worth less than money today.** A dollar you get in twenty years is worth less to you now than a dollar in your hand, for two reasons: you could invest the dollar you have now and grow it (opportunity cost), and the future is uncertain (the vending machine might break). So you "discount" each future dollar back to its value today, and add up all those present values. That sum is the price.

A stock is exactly that vending machine, just with more uncertain, faster-growing cash flows. The price of a share is the sum of all future earnings (or dividends, or free cash flow — the family of methods is called **discounted cash flow**, or DCF) each shrunk back to a present value. Formally, if a company earns \$E_1\$ next year, \$E_2\$ the year after, and so on, and we discount at a rate \$r\$:

The present value of the year-\$t\$ earnings is that year's earnings divided by \$(1+r)^t\$. The price is the sum of all of those. That single equation — price equals the sum of discounted future earnings — is the engine behind everything that follows. **Memorize the shape of it, not the symbols: price is future cash, shrunk for time and risk.**

Two refinements make the formula realistic, and both matter for how rates bite. First, a company does not stop earning after some number of years — it earns "forever," so the DCF includes a **terminal value**, a lump sum standing in for all the cash flows beyond the explicit forecast horizon. The standard way to compute a terminal value is to take a far-future year's earnings and divide by \$(r - g)\$, where \$g\$ is the long-run growth rate. Stare at that denominator: \$r - g\$. When the discount rate \$r\$ rises and the growth rate \$g\$ stays put, the gap \$r - g\$ widens, and dividing by a bigger number gives a smaller terminal value. For a high-growth company where \$r\$ and \$g\$ start close together — say \$r = 8\%\$ and \$g = 5\%\$, so \$r - g = 3\%\$ — even a one-point rise in \$r\$ to 9% widens the gap to 4%, and the terminal value falls by a quarter (3 ÷ 4 = 0.75). The closer a company's growth rate is to its discount rate, the more explosively its valuation reacts to a rate change. That is the algebra of why the most aggressive growth stocks are the most rate-sensitive: their value lives almost entirely in a terminal value with a razor-thin \$r - g\$ denominator.

Second, "earnings" in the formula really means *free cash flow to the owner* — the cash left after the company has reinvested to keep growing. A mature company hands most of its cash to shareholders now (as dividends or buybacks); a growth company keeps almost all of it, plowing it back to build the future earnings that justify the price. That reinvestment is exactly why a growth stock's cash flows are pushed far into the future — the company is deliberately trading near-term cash for far-term cash. The DCF formula is agnostic about which company is "better"; it simply discounts whatever cash arrives whenever it arrives. The growth company has chosen a long-duration cash-flow profile, and the discount rate will treat it accordingly.

### The discount rate: the number that does the shrinking

The \$r\$ in that formula is the **discount rate**, and it is the single most important variable for understanding how policy moves stocks. The discount rate is *the rate of return investors demand to hold this stock instead of something safer.* It answers the question: "Given everything else I could do with my money, what annual return do I need to be paid to take the risk of owning this company's future cash flows?"

The discount rate is built up in layers, like a stack:

- **The risk-free rate** is the bottom layer — the return you can earn with essentially no risk of loss, which in practice means the yield on a U.S. Treasury bond. If a ten-year Treasury yields 4%, then *no rational investor will accept less than 4% to hold a risky stock for ten years*, because they could just buy the Treasury and earn 4% for free. The risk-free rate is the floor under every other rate in the economy.
- **The equity risk premium** is the layer on top — the *extra* return investors demand for bearing the risk that a stock, unlike a Treasury, can disappoint or go to zero. Historically this premium has averaged something like 4-5 percentage points over the risk-free rate, though it moves around.
- The **discount rate for a stock** is roughly the sum: risk-free rate + equity risk premium (+ adjustments for the specific company's riskiness). So if Treasuries yield 4% and the equity risk premium is 5%, the discount rate is about 9%.

Now watch the load-bearing connection, because it is the entire post in one sentence: **the central bank's policy rate is the anchor for the risk-free rate, and the risk-free rate sits at the bottom of every stock's discount rate.** When the Fed hikes, short Treasury yields rise mechanically, longer Treasury yields usually follow, and that lifts the floor under every discount rate in the market. The Fed does not set your stock's discount rate directly — but it moves the foundation the whole stack is built on. (For exactly how the Fed pins that risk-free anchor, see [interest rates, the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable); for how the move propagates outward through markets, see [the monetary policy transmission mechanism](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets).)

It is worth being honest about the *real* risk-free rate versus the nominal one, because it changes which number you watch. A stock is a *real* asset — its earnings tend to grow with inflation over time — so the discount rate that matters most for equities is the **real** risk-free rate: the Treasury yield minus expected inflation. You can read it almost directly off the market through Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), whose yield *is* a real yield. When the real risk-free rate is deeply negative, as it was in 2020-21 at about -1%, the floor under equity discount rates is below zero in real terms, and far-future earnings are worth extraordinary amounts today — the mathematical license for sky-high growth multiples. When the real risk-free rate surges positive, as it did in 2022, that license is revoked. This is why a sophisticated equity trader watches the ten-year TIPS yield as closely as the fed funds rate: it is the cleanest read on the true discount-rate floor.

There is also a second mover hiding in the discount rate, and it sharpens the channel in stressed markets: the **equity risk premium itself is not constant.** In calm times investors demand a modest premium over the risk-free rate; in a crisis they demand much more, because risk feels scarier and they want a bigger reward to hold stocks. So a rate shock that frightens the market can raise *both* layers of the discount rate at once — the risk-free anchor rises because the Fed is hiking, *and* the risk premium rises because investors are scared — and the two compound. This is part of why selloffs overshoot: the discount-rate channel is amplified by a widening risk premium exactly when the risk-free rate is also climbing. The reverse happens in recoveries, when a falling risk-free rate and a compressing risk premium both lift multiples together, which is why rebounds off a rate-driven bottom can be so explosive.

### Equity duration: why "growth" means "long"

Here is the idea that makes sector dispersion intelligible, and the one most beginners have never heard: **equity duration.**

Duration is a word borrowed from the bond market, where it measures how sensitive a bond's price is to a change in interest rates. A 30-year bond has *long* duration — its price swings violently when rates move, because almost all of its cash (the principal) arrives far in the future, where discounting bites hardest. A 2-year bond has *short* duration — its price barely moves, because its cash comes back soon.

Stocks have duration too, and it works the same way. **A stock's equity duration is how far into the future its cash flows are weighted.** And this is where growth and value separate:

- A **value stock** — say a mature utility, an oil major, or a bank — earns most of its money *now*. It pays a fat dividend today, generates steady cash this year and next, and is not promising you a fortune in 2045. Its cash flows are front-loaded, so it has **short duration**. A higher discount rate barely touches it, because near cash flows are barely discounted.
- A **growth stock** — a young software company, a biotech, a platform reinvesting every dollar — earns little or nothing *now*. Its entire valuation is a bet on enormous profits *far* in the future: years 10, 15, 20. Its cash flows are back-loaded, so it has **long duration**. A higher discount rate is devastating, because far cash flows get discounted by \$(1+r)^t\$ with a large \$t\$, and that exponent magnifies every change in \$r\$.

This is why "the discount rate matters more for growth stocks" is not a vague slogan — it is arithmetic. The further out a cash flow sits, the more a change in the discount rate compounds against it. **Growth = long duration = maximum rate sensitivity.** Hold that equation; it is the thesis of the entire post.

With those four foundations — price as discounted earnings, the discount rate, the risk-free anchor, and equity duration — in place, let us walk down the two channels through which a rate change actually moves the price.

## The discount-rate channel: rates up, present value down

The first and faster channel is pure mathematics. When the discount rate rises, the present value of every future cash flow falls — instantly, mechanically, before the company's actual earnings change by a single dollar. This is the **discount-rate channel**, and it is the reason stocks can fall hard in a *good* economy: the earnings are fine, but the rate you discount them at went up.

To see the channel in its purest form, strip away the company and just price a single future cash flow. Suppose someone promises to pay you \$100, once, exactly ten years from now. What is that promise worth today? It depends entirely on the discount rate. The chart below computes it across every rate from 0% to 8%.

![Line chart of the present value of 100 dollars received in ten years falling as the discount rate rises from zero to eight percent](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors-3.png)

At a 0% discount rate, the promise is worth the full \$100 — no shrinking. At 3%, it is worth \$74.41. At 6%, it is worth \$55.84. The line falls away steeply and keeps falling. And notice the shape: doubling the rate from 3% to 6% did not halve the value, but it knocked off a quarter of it — \$74.41 down to \$55.84 — purely because the cash sits a decade out where the exponent does its damage. That is the discount-rate channel in one picture. Nothing about the \$100 changed. Only the rate changed, and the price moved a fifth of the way to nothing.

Here is the core calculation as code. This is the entire discount-rate channel: a function that takes a future cash flow, the number of years until you receive it, and the discount rate, and returns what it is worth today.

```
def present_value(cash, years, rate):
    return cash / (1.0 + rate) ** years   # shrink the future cash to today

future_cash = 100.0    # $100 of earnings
years = 10             # arriving 10 years out
for rate in (0.00, 0.03, 0.06, 0.08):
    pv = present_value(future_cash, years, rate)
    print(f"rate {rate:.0%}: ${pv:6.2f}")

    #   rate 0%: $100.00
    #   rate 3%: $ 74.41   <- the green dot on the chart
    #   rate 6%: $ 55.84   <- the red dot: a 25% haircut from rates alone
    #   rate 8%: $ 46.32
```

Notice what the code makes obvious: the `years` exponent is where the leverage lives. Change `years` to `2` and re-run, and the gap between the 3% and 6% values almost vanishes — the cash comes back too soon for the rate to matter. Change it to `20` and the gap becomes enormous. **The same rate move produces a small price effect on near cash flows and a huge one on far cash flows.** That single line of arithmetic *is* equity duration.

#### Worked example: a stock's value when the discount rate goes from 3% to 6%

Let us price an actual stock, not just a single payment. Imagine a growth company that the market expects to earn \$100 of profit per share in ten years (its big payoff year), and suppose for simplicity that this one far-off number anchors most of the valuation. At a 3% discount rate, that future \$100 is worth \$74.41 today. The Fed then hikes, the ten-year Treasury yield climbs, and the discount rate rises to 6%. Now the same \$100 is worth \$55.84.

The stock fell from \$74.41 to \$55.84 — a drop of \$18.57, or **-25.0%** — and the company's expected earnings *did not change at all.* The \$100 is still coming. The business is identical. Every penny of that 25% loss came from the discount rate, the rate the Fed's tightening lifted. Now contrast a value stock whose \$100 arrives in *two* years: at 3% it is worth \$94.26, at 6% it is worth \$89.00 — a drop of just **-5.6%**. Same rate move, same dollar amount of earnings, but the short-duration stock lost a fifth as much. *The intuition lands: the discount-rate channel is not about whether earnings are good — it is about how far out they sit.*

This is also why the channel is so *fast*. The earnings channel takes quarters to show up; the discount-rate channel reprices in seconds, because it is the market simply re-running the DCF arithmetic with a higher \$r\$ the instant yields move. A growth stock can fall 5% on a hot inflation print that pushes the ten-year yield up fifteen basis points, with zero change to the company's prospects. The rate did all of it.

There is one more property of the channel worth making precise, because it explains a behavior that confuses people: **the discount-rate channel is not linear.** The damage from a rate rise accelerates the further out the cash flow sits, because the exponent \$t\$ in \$(1+r)^t\$ is what amplifies the move. Concretely: a cash flow one year out loses about 1% of its value per one-point rise in the discount rate. A cash flow ten years out loses roughly ten times as much — close to 9-10% per point. A cash flow twenty years out loses roughly twenty times as much. The sensitivity scales (approximately) with the number of years until the cash arrives. That number — the sensitivity of present value to the discount rate, weighted by when the cash arrives — *is* the duration we defined earlier. Duration is not a vibe; it is the slope of the DCF with respect to the rate, and it grows with how far out the money sits.

This also explains a subtler point: a stock with *negative* near-term earnings (it is losing money now, all its value is in a far-future profit) has the *longest* duration of all, because there is no near-term cash to anchor it — 100% of its value is back-loaded. That is precisely the profile of the speculative, profitless growth names that rose the most in 2020-21 (when the real discount rate was negative) and fell the most in 2022 (when it surged). They are pure duration: a leveraged bet on the discount rate with no near-term cash flow to cushion the move in either direction. When you hear "long-duration growth got crushed," the most extreme version of that is the company with no profits at all, whose entire stock price is a terminal value sitting a decade or more out.

## Multiple compression: the P/E does the work

There is a second, more visible way to describe the discount-rate channel, and it is the language traders actually use on a desk: **multiple compression.**

A stock's **price-to-earnings ratio** (P/E) is just its price divided by its annual earnings per share. If a stock trades at \$200 and earns \$10 per share, its P/E is 20 — investors are paying \$20 for every \$1 of current annual earnings. The P/E is shorthand for *how richly the market values a dollar of this company's earnings*, and — this is the key link — **a higher discount rate mechanically justifies a lower P/E.**

Why? Because the P/E is downstream of the discount rate. A low discount rate means future earnings are barely shrunk, so investors will pay a high multiple for them; a high discount rate means future earnings are heavily shrunk, so the same earnings are worth a lower multiple today. When rates rise and discount rates climb, P/E ratios fall across the market. That fall is called **multiple compression**: the *multiple* (the P/E) gets *compressed* (squeezed lower), even if earnings hold flat. Price = earnings × multiple, so if earnings are flat and the multiple compresses, the price falls. The discount-rate channel and multiple compression are the same phenomenon described in two dialects — one in DCF, one in P/E.

This is exactly what happened to the whole index in 2022. The S&P 500's forward P/E started the year around 21-22× and compressed to roughly 16-17× by the autumn, even though aggregate S&P earnings actually *grew* modestly that year. The index fell -18.1% almost entirely because the multiple compressed, not because earnings collapsed — and the multiple compressed because the discount rate, anchored to a ten-year Treasury yield that ran from about 1.5% to nearly 4%, rose sharply. The chart below shows that rate backdrop: the risk-free anchor surging from its COVID lows.

![Dual-axis line chart of the ten-year nominal Treasury yield and the ten-year real TIPS yield both rising from 2020 to 2023](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors-6.png)

Look at both lines. The blue line is the nominal ten-year yield, which climbed from about 0.6% in 2020 to nearly 5% in late 2023. But the line that matters most for the discount rate is the red one: the **ten-year real yield** (the nominal yield minus expected inflation, observed directly through inflation-protected Treasuries, or TIPS). In 2020-21 the real yield was *negative*, around -1% — meaning the true, inflation-adjusted risk-free rate was below zero, which is rocket fuel for long-duration growth multiples. By late 2022 the real yield had surged to +1.7% and beyond. That swing — from -1% to +1.7%, nearly three full percentage points of real discount rate — is the deepest part of the headwind. (For why the *real* yield, not the nominal one, is the master signal for risk assets, see [real vs nominal: the real-yield master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

#### Worked example: a \$500,000 position through multiple compression with flat earnings

Suppose you hold \$500,000 of a stock trading at a forward P/E of 25×, and the company's earnings are expected to be perfectly flat over the next year — no growth, no decline. Then the Fed hikes, the discount rate rises, and the market re-rates this kind of long-duration name down to a P/E of 18×. What happens to your position?

Price is proportional to the multiple when earnings are flat, so the price change is simply the multiple change: 18 ÷ 25 = 0.72. Your position is now worth \$500,000 × 0.72 = **\$360,000**. You lost **\$140,000**, or -28%, and the company earned exactly what everyone expected — not a dollar more or less. Every cent of the loss was multiple compression driven by the discount rate. If instead earnings had *grown* 10% over that year, the price would be \$500,000 × 0.72 × 1.10 = \$396,000 — still a \$104,000 loss, because the multiple compression (-28%) overwhelmed the earnings growth (+10%). *The lesson for a hiking cycle: when rates are rising, the multiple is usually a bigger force than the earnings, and good earnings cannot save a richly-valued long-duration stock from a falling multiple.*

This is the most counterintuitive thing about the discount-rate channel for newcomers. People expect a stock to fall because the *company* did something wrong. But in a hiking cycle, the most common reason a quality growth stock falls is that nothing went wrong with the company at all — the discount rate went up, the multiple compressed, and the price followed the arithmetic down.

#### Worked example: the terminal-value squeeze on a high-growth stock

Recall the terminal-value formula: a far-future year's cash flow divided by \$(r - g)\$. Take a fast-growing company whose terminal cash flow is \$50 per share, with a long-run growth rate \$g = 4\%\$. At a discount rate \$r = 7\%\$, the gap \$r - g = 3\%\$, so the terminal value is \$50 ÷ 0.03 = \$1,666.67\$ per share. Now the Fed hikes and the discount rate rises just two points, to \$r = 9\%\$. The gap widens to \$r - g = 5\%\$, and the terminal value becomes \$50 ÷ 0.05 = \$1,000\$ per share. The stock's terminal value fell from \$1,666.67 to \$1,000 — a drop of **-40%** — on a two-point rate move, with the company's cash flow and growth rate completely unchanged. Contrast a slow-grower with \$g = 1\%\$: its gap goes from 6% to 8%, terminal value from \$833.33 to \$625, a drop of just **-25%**. *The closer growth runs to the discount rate, the more violently the valuation reacts — which is the precise mathematical reason the market's most beloved high-growth compounders are also its most dangerous rate-beta bets.*

## The earnings channel: tighter policy slows growth and profits

The discount-rate channel is the numerator's problem — actually the *denominator's* problem, the \$r\$ — and it moves fast. The second channel attacks the other part of the DCF equation: the earnings themselves. This is the **earnings channel**, and it is slower, more economic, and the one most people *think* is the whole story.

The chain is the one every macro student learns: higher policy rates raise borrowing costs for households and businesses, which slows spending and investment, which slows the economy, which lowers corporate revenues and profits. A company that was going to earn \$E_t\$ in some future year now earns less, because its customers are buying less, its expansion is on hold, and its financing is more expensive. Lower future earnings mean a lower present value, even at an unchanged discount rate. (This is the demand-and-credit side of policy transmission; the mechanics of how the rate reaches household and corporate borrowing are in [the monetary policy transmission mechanism](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets).)

Two things make the earnings channel behave very differently from the discount-rate channel, and you must keep them separate in your head:

- **It is slow.** The discount-rate channel reprices the instant yields move. The earnings channel works through "long and variable lags" of roughly a year to two years — the time it takes for higher borrowing costs to actually slow hiring, cut capital spending, and dent revenues. In 2022 the discount-rate channel did almost all the damage to stocks, *because the earnings recession everyone feared had not arrived yet* — the slowdown was still in the post. This is why the timing of the two channels matters so much for trading.
- **It is selective by economic sensitivity, not by duration.** The discount-rate channel hits the longest-duration stocks hardest. The earnings channel hits the most *cyclical* stocks hardest — the companies whose profits swing the most with the economy: consumer discretionary, banks, industrials, semiconductors, homebuilders. A defensive staples company (people buy toothpaste in a recession) barely feels the earnings channel; a luxury-goods or travel company feels it acutely.

There are two amplifiers that make the earnings channel hit harder than a naive view suggests. The first is **operating leverage**. A company with high fixed costs — a factory, an airline, a chip fab — sees its *profits* swing far more than its *revenues* when the economy slows, because the fixed costs do not shrink with demand. If revenue falls 10% but fixed costs stay put, profit might fall 30% or 40%. So the earnings channel is not gentle even for a modest slowdown; for high-operating-leverage cyclicals, a small economic dip becomes a large earnings dent. The second amplifier is the **cost of capital on the income statement itself**: a company that carries floating-rate debt sees its interest expense rise the moment the Fed hikes, directly cutting net income even before the economy slows. A heavily-indebted company is hit by the earnings channel *immediately* through its interest bill, not just slowly through demand — which is part of why leveraged sectors like real estate and parts of small-cap fell so hard in 2022. (For how higher rates ripple into the *cost* of borrowing across the economy, the mechanics are in [the monetary policy transmission mechanism](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets).)

The cruelty of a hiking cycle is that for many stocks *both channels fire at once.* A long-duration *and* cyclical name — a high-growth, economically-sensitive tech or consumer company — gets the discount-rate channel compressing its multiple and the earnings channel cutting its profit forecasts simultaneously. That double hit is why the worst-performing stocks in 2022 fell 40%+ while the index "only" fell 18%: they were caught in both channels at the same time.

It is worth being precise about *which channel dominates when*, because the timing is a tradable edge. In the early phase of a tightening cycle, the discount-rate channel does almost all the work: yields are rising, multiples are compressing, but the economy is still strong and earnings are still fine. This is the "good news is bad news" phase, where a strong jobs report makes stocks *fall* (because it means more hikes, a higher discount rate) — a pattern that baffles newcomers but is pure discount-rate-channel logic. Then, with a lag of a year or two, the earnings channel arrives: the economy finally slows, earnings estimates get cut, and now stocks fall on *bad* news too. The regime flips from "rates drive everything" to "earnings drive everything," and the kind of stock that leads the decline changes from longest-duration (discount-rate phase) to most-cyclical (earnings phase). Knowing which phase you are in tells you whether to screen on duration or on cyclicality.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000,000 growth book vs a value book through a 2-point rate rise

Let us run two million-dollar books through a single, clean two-percentage-point rise in the discount rate (say from 4% to 6%), holding earnings flat so we isolate the discount-rate channel. Book A is a **growth book** with average equity duration of about 20 years (think long-dated tech). Book B is a **value book** with average duration of about 7 years (think energy, banks, staples).

A useful rule of thumb borrowed from bonds: the percentage price change ≈ −duration × change in rate. For the growth book: −20 × 0.02 = −0.40, a **-40%** hit. The \$1,000,000 falls to about **\$600,000**. For the value book: −7 × 0.02 = −0.14, a **-14%** hit. The \$1,000,000 falls to about **\$860,000**. Same rate move, same starting capital — but the growth book lost \$400,000 while the value book lost \$140,000, a \$260,000 difference produced entirely by duration. Now add the earnings channel: if the slowdown also cuts the growth book's expected earnings by another 10% (growth names are often cyclical too) while the value book's defensive cash flows hold, the growth book falls a further leg to roughly \$540,000. *The takeaway: in a tightening cycle the duration of your book, not your stock-picking, can be the dominant driver of your return — and a growth-heavy book can lose two to three times what a value book loses on the identical macro move.*

## Sector rate-betas: who wins and who loses

We now have the two channels and the duration spectrum. Put them together and you can sort the entire stock market by its sensitivity to rates — a property traders call a sector's **rate-beta**: how much a sector moves for a given move in interest rates. Negative rate-beta sectors fall when rates rise; positive (or low) rate-beta sectors hold up or even gain. The 2022 dispersion is the cleanest natural experiment in modern history for reading these rate-betas straight off a chart.

![Horizontal bar chart of 2022 S&P 500 sector total returns from Energy plus sixty-six percent to Communication Services minus forty percent](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors-2.png)

Read the chart top to bottom — it is a rate-beta ranking in disguise:

- **The winners (low or negative rate-beta).** Energy +65.7% sits alone at the top, but its 2022 was mostly an oil-price story (the Russia-Ukraine shock sent crude above \$120) layered on top of a sector that is short-duration and cash-rich. Utilities +1.6% and consumer staples -0.6% held up because they are classic **short-duration defensives**: near-term cash flows, stable demand, dividends now rather than promises later. The discount-rate channel barely touches them and the earnings channel spares them because people still need electricity and toothpaste.
- **The losers (steep negative rate-beta).** The bottom of the chart is a roll call of **long-duration and cyclical** sectors. Communication services -39.9%, consumer discretionary -37%, and technology -28.2% are the longest-duration parts of the index — their valuations are bets on far-future earnings, so the discount-rate channel hammered their multiples — and several of their biggest names (advertising, e-commerce, discretionary spending) are also cyclical, so the earnings channel piled on. Real estate -26.2% is a special case: it is a pure rate play, both because property is valued on a discount rate (the "cap rate") and because real-estate companies are heavily leveraged with floating debt that got more expensive overnight.

Two sectors deserve a closer look because their rate-betas are subtle, not obvious, and getting them wrong is a classic mistake. **Real estate** is valued on a **capitalization rate** (cap rate) — the property's annual income divided by its price, which is functionally a discount rate for buildings. When the risk-free rate rises, cap rates rise with it (a building must offer a higher yield to compete with a now-higher Treasury), and a higher cap rate means a *lower* property price for the same income. In the data series for the U.S., cap rates compressed to a record low around 4.6% in 2021 (when rates were near zero) and then repriced *up* toward 5.9% by 2024 as the discount rate climbed — and rising cap rates mechanically push property values down. Real estate is therefore one of the most rate-sensitive sectors there is: it gets the discount-rate channel through the cap rate *and* the earnings channel through floating-rate debt. **Financials** are the genuinely ambiguous one. Beginners assume "higher rates are good for banks" because banks earn a *net interest margin* — they lend at long rates and fund at short rates, so a higher rate environment can widen that margin and boost earnings. That is true and is a positive rate-beta. But banks are also exposed to the *credit* side: if higher rates tip the economy into recession, loan defaults rise and the earnings channel turns sharply negative, and banks holding long-duration bonds on their balance sheets take mark-to-market losses (exactly what sank Silicon Valley Bank in March 2023). So financials carry a *positive* rate-beta on the margin side and a *negative* one on the credit-and-duration side, which is why the sector fell a relatively modest -10.5% in 2022 — the two effects partly cancel. The lesson: a sector's rate-beta is the *net* of its discount-rate sensitivity, its cyclicality, and any direct rate benefit to its business model, and a few sectors (financials, insurance) have offsetting pieces that make the net small.

To make the duration mechanism unmistakable, plot return against duration directly. The scatter below takes the major 2022 asset returns and lays them out by approximate effective duration.

![Scatter chart of 2022 total return against approximate effective duration showing longer-duration assets fell more with a downward trend line](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors-5.png)

The pattern is almost a straight line, and the trend slopes down: the longer the duration, the worse the 2022 return. Cash and T-bills (near-zero duration) gained. The broad S&P 500 (around 20 years of effective equity duration) fell -18.1%. The Nasdaq 100 (longest-duration equity, perhaps 30 years) fell -32.5%. And — the detail that proves the mechanism is about *duration*, not about *stocks* — long Treasury bonds (20-year duration of pure fixed cash flows) fell -31.2%, almost as much as the most aggressive growth stocks. A 20-year Treasury and a long-duration tech stock have *nothing* in common as businesses, but they fell together because they share the one property that mattered in 2022: their value sits far in the future, where a rising discount rate does the most damage. That is the whole post in a single downward-sloping line. (For why even the supposedly "safe" bonds fell, which broke the classic 60/40 portfolio, the deep version is in [the monetary policy transmission mechanism](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets) and the cross-asset rotation in [asset rotation across the business cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants).)

The figure below makes the value-vs-growth split explicit: it is the same rate move applied to two different cash-flow timelines.

![Two-column comparison of a short-duration value stock barely repricing versus a long-duration growth stock collapsing when the discount rate rises](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors-4.png)

#### Worked example: a \$100,000 sector bet on Energy vs Communication Services

Suppose at the start of 2022 you had \$100,000 to deploy and you made a single sector bet. If you put it all in the S&P 500 energy sector, by year-end your \$100,000 had become \$100,000 × (1 + 0.657) = **\$165,700** — a gain of \$65,700. If instead you put it in communication services, your \$100,000 became \$100,000 × (1 − 0.399) = **\$60,100** — a loss of \$39,900. The difference between the two bets was **\$105,600** on \$100,000 of capital, in a single year, choosing between two slices of the same index.

No stock-picking inside either sector would have come close to mattering as much as that one allocation decision. You did not need to find the best energy company or avoid the worst telecom — the rate-beta of the *sector* swamped everything else. *The lesson is the one professionals live by in a regime change: in a year dominated by a macro rate shock, your sector and duration allocation is the trade, and security selection is a rounding error.*

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs sound obviously true and are the most expensive mistakes a beginner makes when rates move. Each one is corrected by the numbers we have already built.

**"Rate hikes are bad for all stocks equally."** This is the single most common error, and 2022 demolishes it. Energy returned +65.7% in the *same year* communication services returned -39.9%, under the *same* Fed, with the *same* hikes. Rate hikes are not a uniform headwind on "stocks" — they are a tax on *duration*, heaviest on the longest-duration sectors and near-zero on short-duration defensives and rate-beneficiaries. Treating the equity market as one rate-sensitive blob is how you sell your energy winners to buy "cheap" tech that then falls another 30%. The correct mental model is a *spectrum* of rate-betas, not a single market beta.

**"Earnings drive everything; the discount rate is academic."** Many investors believe a stock only falls if the company disappoints. But in 2022 aggregate S&P 500 earnings *grew*, and the index still fell -18.1%. The entire decline was multiple compression — the discount-rate channel — not an earnings collapse. The discount rate is not an academic abstraction; in a hiking cycle it is frequently the *dominant* driver of returns, larger than the earnings it is discounting. The worked example above showed a \$500,000 position losing \$140,000 to multiple compression with perfectly flat earnings. The denominator of the DCF can move the price more than the numerator.

**"Value always beats growth when rates rise."** This is *usually* true and *occasionally* very false, and the nuance is where money is made and lost. Value beats growth when rates rise *because of duration* — value is short-duration, so it suffers less from the discount-rate channel. But value sectors are often more *cyclical*, so if the rate rise tips the economy into recession, the earnings channel can hurt value (banks, industrials, materials) badly even as it spares defensive growth. And in a "good" tightening — rates up but no recession — short-duration value can win cleanly, as it did in 2022. The honest rule is: *rising rates favor short duration over long duration*, which usually means value over growth, but "value vs growth" is a proxy for "short vs long duration," and when those two things come apart (a defensive long-duration utility, a cyclical short-duration bank), follow the duration, not the label.

**"The Fed cut rates, so my growth stocks should go up."** Beginners conflate the *policy rate* with the *discount rate that matters for stocks*, and the two can move in opposite directions. The Fed sets the overnight rate at the very front of the curve; what discounts a growth stock's far-future earnings is the *long-end real yield* — the ten-year (or longer) TIPS yield. In 2023-24 there were stretches where the Fed was cutting or pausing at the front end while the long-end real yield *rose*, because the bond market worried about deficits, term premium, and sticky inflation. A growth-stock holder cheering a Fed cut while the ten-year real yield climbed got the discount-rate channel working *against* them even as the headline policy rate fell. The number that prices your equity duration is the long real yield, not the fed funds rate the headlines quote. (Why the curve's front and back ends can diverge is covered in [the monetary policy transmission mechanism](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets) and [real vs nominal yields](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

## How it shows up in real markets

The 2022 dispersion was not a one-off curiosity; it was the textbook case of a rate regime change, and it rhymes with every other one. Here is how the pattern shows up in real markets, with the dates and numbers attached.

**The 2022 repricing was a discount-rate event first, an earnings event second.** The sequence is the tell. Stocks — especially long-duration growth — peaked in late 2021 and early 2022, *right as the real ten-year yield bottomed near -1% and began to climb.* The Nasdaq topped in November 2021; the broad S&P topped in early January 2022, before the Fed had hiked even once, because the *bond market* had already started pricing the hiking campaign and the discount rate had started to rise. The selloff accelerated through 2022 as the real yield surged toward +1.7%. Throughout, corporate earnings held up — the feared earnings recession kept being forecast and kept not arriving. The whole bear market was, in effect, the discount-rate channel repricing the longest-duration assets down to match a risk-free rate that had moved three points higher in real terms. The earnings channel was the dog that did not bark.

**The growth-to-value rotation.** As rates rose, capital rotated out of long-duration growth and into short-duration value in one of the sharpest factor rotations in decades. The Russell 1000 Value index outperformed the Russell 1000 Growth index by more than twenty percentage points in 2022 — one of the widest value-over-growth years on record — and it was almost entirely a duration story. Investors were not suddenly deciding that oil majors were better businesses than software platforms; they were fleeing duration as the discount rate climbed. The rotation reversed sharply in 2023 once the market decided the Fed was near the end of hiking and real yields stopped rising — growth (the longest duration) led the rebound, because the same arithmetic that crushed it on the way up lifts it most on the way down.

**The 2018 "rate-scare" rehearsal.** This is not new. In the fourth quarter of 2018, the Fed was still hiking and the ten-year yield pushed above 3.2%; the S&P 500 fell about 14% in a few weeks, led by — you guessed it — the longest-duration tech and growth names, while defensives held up better. The instant the Fed signaled a pause in early 2019 and yields fell back, growth ripped higher. Same channel, smaller dose. Every rate scare runs the same playbook: long duration leads the fall when the discount rate rises and leads the recovery when it falls.

**The dot-com bust, 2000-2002.** The same channel wrote the script two decades earlier. Through 1999 and into 2000 the Fed was hiking to cool an overheating economy and a frothy internet-stock mania; the discount rate rose, and the longest-duration assets in the market — profitless dot-com companies whose entire valuation was a bet on far-future internet profits — were the most exposed. When the discount rate rose and then the earnings (the promised internet profits) failed to materialize, both channels fired and the Nasdaq fell roughly 78% from its March 2000 peak to its 2002 trough. The pattern is identical to 2022, just larger: long-duration growth inflated on a low discount rate, then collapsed when the rate rose and the far-future earnings were re-examined. Every great growth-stock drawdown in history has this discount-rate-channel skeleton.

**The deepest version: Volcker, 1980-82.** The most extreme rate shock in modern history was Paul Volcker's campaign to break inflation, with the fed funds rate driven toward 20%. A 20% risk-free rate makes the discount rate on equities astronomical, which is why the stock market's P/E multiple was crushed to single digits by 1980-82 — the great "de-rating" of equities. It is no accident that the multi-decade bull market in stocks began precisely when that rate shock ended and yields began their forty-year decline: a falling discount rate is a multi-decade tailwind for equity multiples, and a rising one is a headwind. (The Volcker episode and its mechanics are detailed in [Paul Volcker's 1980 rate shock](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation).)

**The other direction: 2020's everything-rally.** Run the channels in reverse. In 2020-21 the Fed cut to zero and the real yield went *negative*, around -1%. That is the discount-rate channel firing in the bullish direction: a near-zero (even negative) discount rate makes far-future earnings worth a fortune today, so the longest-duration assets — unprofitable tech, SPACs, crypto, meme stocks — went up the most. The 2022 collapse was simply that process unwinding when the real yield went from -1% to +1.7%. The same dial that inflated the 2020-21 growth bubble deflated it. Long duration is a leveraged bet on the discount rate in *both* directions.

## How to trade it / The playbook

Everything above earns its keep here. The discount-rate and earnings channels are not just an explanation of the past — they are a positioning framework. Here is how to actually use them.

**Step 1 — Map your book's rate-beta (its duration).** Before you have a view on rates, know your exposure to them. Go holding by holding and ask: *where do this company's cash flows sit in time?* A profitable, dividend-paying, mature business is short duration (low rate-beta). An unprofitable or barely-profitable company whose valuation is a bet on far-future earnings is long duration (high rate-beta). Tag every position. Then look at the aggregate: is your book secretly a giant long-duration bet? A portfolio of "great companies" that are all high-multiple growth names is, in rate terms, one concentrated bet that the discount rate will not rise. Most beginners are far longer duration than they realize. The figure below is the map.

![Matrix of equity sectors by rate-beta against what to do when rates rise versus fall](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors-7.png)

**Step 2 — Form a view on the discount rate, not just "the Fed."** What moves stocks is the *discount rate*, anchored to the real ten-year yield. So watch the right variable: the ten-year real (TIPS) yield, not just the fed funds rate. The front-end policy rate can be falling while the long-end real yield rises (as in parts of 2023-24), and it is the long-end real yield that sets equity duration's headwind. The trigger to act is a *change in the path* of real yields, which the bond market prices off inflation data (CPI, PCE), the labor market, and Fed guidance. (For reading the rate path itself, see [interest rates, the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable).)

**Step 3 — Rotate by duration as the path turns.** This is the core trade, and it is symmetric:

- *When real yields are rising (tightening regime):* shorten the duration of your equity book. Overweight short-duration value, energy, financials, and defensives; underweight long-duration growth and rate-sensitive real estate and utilities. This is the 2022 trade: Energy +65.7%, Comm Svcs -39.9%.
- *When real yields are falling (easing regime, or the market pricing the end of hikes):* lengthen duration. Overweight long-duration growth and rate-sensitive sectors, which rally hardest when the discount rate drops. This is the late-2023 and 2020 trade.

**Step 4 — Separate the two channels in your thesis.** When you expect rates to rise, ask *which channel* you are betting on. If it is a pure discount-rate event (rates up, economy fine), favor short-duration over long-duration regardless of cyclicality — and remember good earnings will not save a long-duration multiple. If it is also an earnings event (rates up *and* recession), add a second screen: avoid the most *cyclical* names too, because the earnings channel will hit them on top of the duration hit. The double-hit names (long-duration *and* cyclical) are the ones to be most underweight; the safe harbor is short-duration *and* defensive.

**Step 6 — Size it with a duration budget, not gut feel.** The professional version of this is to treat your equity book like a bond portfolio and run a *duration budget*. Estimate an effective duration for each holding (short-duration value ≈ 7-10 years, broad market ≈ 15-20, long-duration growth ≈ 25-35, profitless hyper-growth ≈ 40+), weight by position size, and compute a portfolio-level equity duration. Now you can quantify your rate exposure: a book with an average duration of 25 years will lose roughly 25% for every one-point rise in the real discount rate (using the −duration × Δrate rule of thumb), while a 10-year-duration book loses 10%. If you believe the real yield could rise a point and you are uncomfortable with a 25% drawdown from rates alone, you do not need a market call — you need to *shorten your book's duration* until the rate exposure fits your risk tolerance. This turns a vague worry ("I'm nervous about rates") into a concrete, sizeable adjustment ("cut book duration from 25 to 15 years by rotating a third of the growth sleeve into value and defensives"). Most retail investors never compute this number and are stunned to discover, after the fact, that their "diversified" portfolio of great companies was a 30-year-duration bet on the discount rate.

**Step 7 — Know your invalidation.** The duration trade is a bet on the *direction of the real yield*. It is wrong when the real yield reverses against you: if you are short duration (positioned for rising rates) and the real ten-year yield rolls over and starts falling — because inflation collapses, the Fed pivots, or growth scares the market into Treasuries — then long duration will start to lead and your short-duration tilt becomes a drag. So the invalidation is concrete: *the trade is on while the real ten-year yield is rising or flat; it is off, and you flip toward duration, when the real yield turns decisively down.* Watch that one line. It is the headwind or the tailwind for every long-duration stock you own, and it is the single variable that turned a hundred-percentage-point sector dispersion in 2022 into the opposite rotation in 2023.

The whole discipline reduces to a sentence you can say at your desk: **rates do not move "stocks" — they move duration, so know your book's duration, watch the real yield, and rotate from long to short as it rises and back as it falls.**
