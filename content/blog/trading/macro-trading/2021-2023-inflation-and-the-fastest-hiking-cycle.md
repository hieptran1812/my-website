> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 2021-2023 episode was a regime break: a pandemic flood of money and stimulus collided with broken supply chains, inflation hit 9.1% (a 40-year high), and a Fed that had called it "transitory" was forced into the fastest hiking cycle in four decades, which made stocks and bonds fall together for the first time in a generation.
>
> - Inflation did not return for one reason. Roughly \$5 trillion of fiscal stimulus and a 40% surge in the money supply met snapped supply chains and a reopening demand wave — money and goods both moved the wrong way at once.
> - "Transitory" was the most expensive word in modern central banking. By reading every driver as a one-off that would fade, the Fed held rates at zero into 2022 and let inflation broaden from goods into rent and wages, forcing a far harder catch-up.
> - The Fed went from 0.25% to 5.50% — plus 525 basis points in sixteen months, the fastest tightening since the early 1980s. That single variable repriced every asset on the planet.
> - The number to remember: in 2022 a standard 60/40 portfolio lost **16.1%** because stocks AND bonds fell together — the bond hedge failed because the shock was inflation, not growth.

On 10 June 2022, the Bureau of Labor Statistics released a number that broke a forty-year-old assumption. US consumer prices had risen 8.6% over the prior year, and the next month's reading would print 9.06% — the highest inflation rate the United States had seen since 1981. For an entire generation of traders, portfolio managers, and central bankers, inflation had been a museum piece. It was the thing your grandparents complained about, the villain of the 1970s, a problem that modern policy had supposedly solved. Two-percent inflation, give or take, had been the background hum of markets for so long that most risk models treated it as a constant.

Then it wasn't. The June 2022 print did not just embarrass the forecasters who had spent a year insisting the surge was "transitory." It marked the moment a regime broke. A regime, in markets, is the set of relationships you can lean on: which assets hedge which, what the central bank will do when growth slows, whether bonds protect you when stocks fall. For four decades, the regime had been *disinflation* — prices rising slowly and ever-more-slowly, rates trending down, bonds reliably rallying whenever the economy wobbled. The 9.1% print said that regime was over, at least for now, and that the next two years would be governed by a single variable: how aggressively the Federal Reserve would have to tighten to drag inflation back down.

This post is the master case study of an inflation shock and the tightening cycle it forces. We will build the whole thing from zero — why inflation returned, why "transitory" was wrong and what the error cost, why the Fed then hiked faster than at any time since Paul Volcker, why stocks and bonds fell together in 2022 when every diversification textbook said they shouldn't, and how the disinflation that followed set up the soft-landing-versus-recession debate that still hangs over markets. By the end you will be able to read the next inflation scare the way a macro trader does: not as a collection of headlines, but as a reaction function that prices every asset you own.

The timeline above is the whole story in one frame, and it is worth fixing in your mind before we go deeper. A money flood in 2020-21 became an inflation surge. The Fed read that surge as temporary and held policy at emergency settings — the transitory error. Inflation peaked at 9.06% in June 2022 and the regime broke. The Fed then hiked 525 basis points in sixteen months, the fastest in forty years, which drove a cross-asset selloff in which a 60/40 book lost 16.1%. And then, slowly, disinflation arrived and the argument turned to whether the landing would be soft. Every section below is one of those boxes, expanded.

## Foundations: why inflation returned, the transitory error, and the reaction function

Before we can trade any of this, we need three building blocks defined from scratch: what inflation actually is and why it surged, what "transitory" meant and why it was a forecast rather than a fact, and what a central bank's *reaction function* is — because the reaction function is the thing that turns an inflation number into an asset-price move.

### What inflation is, in one paragraph

Inflation is the rate at which the general price level rises — how much more a basket of goods and services costs this year than last. When economists say "CPI was 9.06% in June 2022," they mean the Consumer Price Index, a weighted basket of what a typical household buys (rent, food, energy, cars, healthcare, and so on), cost 9.06% more than it did in June 2021. The number matters because inflation is a tax on cash and on any fixed stream of future money. If you are owed \$100 a year from now and prices rise 9% in the meantime, that \$100 buys what \$91 bought today. Every bond, every savings account, every pension promise is a claim on future dollars, so inflation reaches into the value of almost everything.

There is a crucial distinction between *headline* and *core* inflation. Headline CPI includes food and energy, which are volatile — a war or an OPEC decision can swing them violently month to month. Core inflation strips those two out to reveal the underlying trend, the part that is "sticky" because it is driven by wages, rents, and services rather than by a single commodity spike. The Federal Reserve actually targets a third measure, *core PCE* (Personal Consumption Expenditures inflation excluding food and energy), because it captures how households substitute between goods and updates its basket weights more flexibly. Keep these three in mind: headline is the scary number in the news, core is the trend, and core PCE is the one the Fed is actually steering toward its 2% goal.

### Why inflation returned: money plus stimulus plus supply shocks plus reopening demand

No single cause produced the 2021-2023 inflation. Four forces stacked on top of one another, which is exactly why it was so persistent and so badly forecast. Take them one at a time.

First, **money**. In response to the pandemic, the Federal Reserve cut its policy rate to essentially zero and bought trillions of dollars of bonds (quantitative easing), expanding its balance sheet from roughly \$4.2 trillion to nearly \$9 trillion. The broad money supply, M2 — currency plus bank deposits plus money-market funds, the money households and firms actually spend — jumped from about \$15.4 trillion at the end of 2019 to \$19.1 trillion at the end of 2020 and \$21.5 trillion by the end of 2021. That is roughly a 40% increase in the money supply in two years, the fastest peacetime expansion in US history. More money chasing the same goods is the oldest inflation recipe there is.

Second, **fiscal stimulus**. This is the part that distinguished the pandemic response from the post-2008 response. After the 2008 financial crisis, the Fed printed reserves but Congress was austere, and the new money mostly sat in the banking system without reaching households — which is why 2010s inflation stayed stubbornly *below* target. In 2020-21, by contrast, the government mailed money directly to people: the \$2.2 trillion CARES Act in March 2020, a \$0.9 trillion relief package in December 2020, and the \$1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan in March 2021 — roughly \$5 trillion of direct fiscal support. Stimulus checks, expanded unemployment benefits, and forgivable business loans put cash into bank accounts that got spent, not parked. Money plus fiscal delivery is far more inflationary than money alone.

Third, **supply shocks**. The pandemic broke the world's supply chains. Factories shut, ports clogged, semiconductor production fell behind, and the cost of shipping a container from Asia to the US west coast rose roughly tenfold at the peak. When the supply of goods contracts while demand is being pumped up, prices have only one direction to go. Used-car prices, for instance, soared because new cars couldn't be built without chips. Then in February 2022, Russia's invasion of Ukraine sent oil, natural gas, wheat, and fertilizer prices spiking, layering an energy-and-food shock on top of the goods shortage.

Fourth, **reopening demand**. Households that had spent 2020 stuck at home, unable to travel or eat out, emerged in 2021 with savings and a hunger to spend. That pent-up demand hit a supply-constrained economy. Airfares, hotel rates, restaurant prices, and rents all surged as the economy reopened faster than it could re-staff and re-stock.

Stack those four — a 40% money surge, \$5 trillion delivered straight to spenders, snapped supply chains, and a reopening demand wave — and you get the chart below.

![US consumer price inflation year over year from 2020 to 2026 with the 2022 peak](/imgs/blogs/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle-2.png)

Read it left to right. Inflation sat near or below the Fed's 2% target through 2020. It then climbed relentlessly through 2021, from 1.4% in January to nearly 7% by November, and kept going to the 9.06% peak in June 2022 — the highest in forty years. The shaded area above the dashed 2% line is, quite literally, the inflation overshoot: every month spent there is purchasing power being eroded. Note also the right side of the chart: after the great disinflation of 2023, prices did not simply settle at 2% — there is a 2025-26 re-acceleration that we will return to, because it is the live reminder that inflation regimes do not always end cleanly.

### What "transitory" meant — and why it was a forecast, not a fact

Through most of 2021, the Federal Reserve described the inflation surge as "transitory." This single word became the defining controversy of the episode, so it is worth being precise about what it meant. "Transitory" was a forecast: it claimed that the inflation drivers were temporary — supply-chain knots that would untangle, a one-time reopening demand pulse, base effects from comparing 2021 prices against the deflated prices of 2020 — and that inflation would therefore fade back to 2% on its own, without the Fed having to raise rates. If that forecast were right, hiking aggressively would have been a mistake, choking off a recovery to fight a problem that was already curing itself.

The trouble is that "transitory" was treated, for too long, as a fact rather than a probability-weighted bet. Each new high print was explained away as the last gasp of a fading shock. And critically, the framing led to a policy *choice*: the Fed kept its policy rate at zero and kept buying bonds (it was still expanding its balance sheet into early 2022) well after inflation had blown through any reasonable definition of transitory. The reaction function — what we will define next — was effectively switched off while the central bank waited for the data to vindicate its story.

### What a reaction function is

Here is the concept that ties this entire post together. A central bank's **reaction function** is the rule, implicit or explicit, that maps economic conditions to policy decisions. It answers the question: "given this much inflation and this much unemployment, what will the Fed do to interest rates?" The Fed has a dual mandate — stable prices (it targets 2% core PCE) and maximum employment — and its reaction function is how it trades those two off. When inflation is far above target and unemployment is low, the reaction function says: hike. When inflation is at target and unemployment is rising, it says: cut.

Why does this matter for a trader? Because *markets price the reaction function, not the data.* A 9% inflation print is not directly tradable; what is tradable is what that print forces the Fed to do, and what that policy path does to the discount rate on every asset. When you understand that the Fed has a reaction function, you stop trading inflation headlines and start trading the *expected policy response* to them. The 2021-2023 episode is the cleanest demonstration in modern history of a reaction function being suppressed (the transitory year), then activated violently (the catch-up hikes), then slowly relaxed (the disinflation). Every asset move below is a derivative of that one curve.

For a deeper treatment of how the Fed builds and signals its reaction function — including the famous "dot plot" of where each official thinks rates should go — see the companion post on [inflation and the Fed reaction function](/blog/trading/macro-trading/inflation-and-the-fed-reaction-function-dot-plot). Here we focus on the case study itself.

## The inflation surge: the drivers and the 9.06% peak

With the foundations in place, let us look more carefully at the surge itself, because the *shape* of it — slow start, broadening middle, vertical top — is what fooled forecasters and is the pattern you should recognize next time.

In early 2021, the inflation that showed up was concentrated in a handful of pandemic-distorted categories: used cars, airfares, hotel rooms, rental cars. These were the obvious reopening categories, and a reasonable person could look at them and say, "this is mechanical, it will normalize." For a few months, that view was defensible. But by mid-2021 the breadth was changing. Inflation was no longer confined to the categories that had collapsed in 2020 and were now snapping back. It was spreading into goods broadly, then — and this is the dangerous part — into services and shelter.

Shelter is the key. Rent and "owners' equivalent rent" make up roughly a third of the CPI basket, and shelter inflation is *sticky*: leases reset slowly, so once rents start rising they keep the index elevated for a year or more even after market rents flatten. When inflation moved from used cars into shelter and wages, it stopped being a supply-chain story and became a *self-sustaining* story — workers demanding higher pay because prices were rising, firms raising prices because wages were rising, the textbook wage-price spiral that 1970s economists feared. By the time CPI hit 9.06% in June 2022, the inflation was broad, embedded, and nowhere near "transitory."

There is one more subtlety worth absorbing. Headline CPI peaked at 9.06% partly because of the energy spike from the Ukraine war — gasoline prices surged in the spring of 2022. Energy is volatile and can fall as fast as it rose, so headline came down quickly in late 2022 once oil retreated. But core inflation — the part driven by wages and shelter — peaked later and fell far more slowly. This is why the Fed kept hiking even as headline rolled over: the headline number was being flattered by falling energy, while the underlying trend the Fed actually targets was still running hot. We will quantify that core-versus-headline gap when we get to the disinflation.

### Why this surge looked like the 1970s — and why it partly didn't

It is worth pausing on the historical rhyme, because the 1970s were the ghost haunting every Fed decision in this episode. The 1970s inflation had two waves: an initial surge driven by an oil shock and loose policy, then — after the Fed eased prematurely, believing the worst was over — a second, larger wave that took double-digit rates and a deep recession to finally break. The lesson that Fed officials carried into 2022 was visceral: easing too soon is how you get a second wave, and a second wave is far more damaging than the first because it convinces households and firms that high inflation is permanent. Once people *expect* persistent inflation, they bake it into wage demands and price-setting, and the inflation becomes self-fulfilling. Economists call this the de-anchoring of inflation expectations, and it is the nightmare a central bank fights hardest to avoid.

The thing that distinguished 2021-2023 from the 1970s, and the reason the disinflation ultimately proved less painful, is that inflation expectations never fully de-anchored. Surveys of households and the prices embedded in inflation-protected bonds showed that markets and the public largely believed the Fed would eventually win — long-run expectations stayed close to 2% even as current inflation ran at 9%. That credibility, hard-won by the four decades of disinflation that preceded the shock, was the Fed's most valuable asset. It meant the Fed could break the inflation without having to break the economy as severely as Volcker did, because it did not have to first re-establish that it was serious about the 2% target. The market already believed it. This is a subtle but tradable point: the *credibility* of the reaction function determines how much economic pain a disinflation requires, and a credible central bank can disinflate at lower cost than an incredible one.

### The wage-price dynamic, concretely

The wage-price spiral deserves one more pass because it is the mechanism that turns a transitory shock into an entrenched one. The chain works like this. Prices rise (from the supply shock and excess demand). Workers, seeing their real wages fall, demand raises — and in a tight labor market, with more job openings than unemployed workers, they have the bargaining power to get them. Firms, facing higher wage bills, raise prices to protect their margins. Higher prices then prompt the next round of wage demands, and the spiral feeds itself, now disconnected from the original supply shock that started it. By 2022, US wage growth was running above 5% and the labor market was historically tight — at one point there were nearly two job openings for every unemployed worker. That is the demand-side engine that the "it was all supply chains" crowd missed, and it is why the Fed concluded that hiking — cooling demand and loosening the labor market — was unavoidable. You cannot fix a wage-price spiral by waiting for shipping containers to arrive; you fix it by raising the cost of money until demand cools and the bargaining power shifts back toward employers.

#### Worked example: the inflation tax on cash, when CPI hit 9.06%

Let us make the cost of inflation concrete, because "9.06%" stays vague until it is dollars. Suppose that in mid-2021 you held \$100,000 in cash — a checking account, a savings account, money under a mattress, it does not matter — earning roughly zero interest, which is what cash earned in 2021 because the Fed had pinned rates at zero.

Over the following twelve months, prices rose 9.06%. Your \$100,000 still says "\$100,000" on the statement. But what can it buy? The basket of goods that cost \$100,000 a year earlier now costs:

```
new cost of the same basket = 100,000 x (1 + 0.0906) = 109,060
```

That same basket now costs \$109,060, so the real value of your unchanged \$100,000 — what it can actually buy — has shrunk to:

```
real value = 100,000 / 1.0906 = 91,693
```

You lost roughly \$8,307 of purchasing power in one year while doing nothing wrong and taking no risk. That is the inflation tax: a 9.06% levy on every dollar of cash, collected silently. Now scale it. A retiree holding \$500,000 in cash and short-term savings lost about \$41,500 of real purchasing power in that single year. A corporation sitting on \$10 million of operating cash lost roughly \$830,000. The single-sentence takeaway: high inflation is not a market headline you can ignore from the sidelines in cash — cash is precisely the asset that gets taxed hardest, which is why "go to cash and wait it out" is a losing move in an inflation regime.

## The transitory mistake — and what it cost

We have established that "transitory" was a forecast and that it was wrong. The more important question for a trader is: *why* was being wrong so costly, and what does that cost teach you?

The cost of the transitory error was not just the embarrassment of a bad call. It was that the error delayed the policy response, and a delayed response forced a *harder* one. Think of inflation like a fire. If you catch it when it is a small flame in one room — say, in early-to-mid 2021, when it was confined to used cars and airfares — you can put it out with a modest amount of water. If you tell yourself the flame is transitory and will burn itself out, and you wait until it has spread through the whole house — into shelter, wages, and services by 2022 — then putting it out requires far more water, far more violently, with far more damage to the structure. The transitory framing was the decision to wait, and waiting turned a containable inflation into an entrenched one.

The figure below traces the mechanics of how this happened.

![The transitory error showing the Fed reading inflation as temporary and falling behind the curve](/imgs/blogs/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle-3.png)

Follow the chain. The supply shock and the reopening demand wave were each, individually, read as one-off events that would fade. That reading produced the official call — *transitory, no hikes needed* — which produced the policy choice to hold the rate at zero and keep quantitative easing running into 2022. While policy sat at emergency settings, inflation broadened from goods into rent and wages, the sticky categories. With the policy rate at zero and inflation at 9%, the *real* policy rate — the nominal rate minus inflation — was deeply negative, around minus seven percent. A deeply negative real rate is the most stimulative possible setting; the Fed was pouring fuel on the fire it had decided was transitory. That is what "behind the curve" means: policy is looser than the economy needs, by a wide margin, for an extended period. And being that far behind the curve is precisely what forced the catch-up — 525 basis points in sixteen months — that we turn to next.

The trading lesson here is durable and it generalizes far beyond 2022. *A central bank that is behind the curve is the single most important macro setup there is.* When the policy rate is far below where inflation says it should be, you know with high confidence that the gap will close — and it will close through the rate, not through inflation suddenly vanishing. That tells you the direction of front-end yields (up), the direction of the currency (eventually up as hikes get priced), and the regime your portfolio is entering (tightening). You do not need to predict the exact CPI print. You need to recognize that the reaction function has been suppressed and must eventually reassert itself.

### The cost in real terms

It is worth quantifying the "behind the curve" condition because the number is genuinely striking. Through 2021 and into 2022, the Fed's policy rate sat at 0.25% (upper bound) while inflation ran from 5% to 9%. The real policy rate — what the rate is worth after inflation — was therefore somewhere between minus five and minus nine percent for the better part of a year and a half. To put that in historical context, even in the easy-money 2010s the real policy rate rarely went more than a point or two negative. A real rate of minus seven percent is an emergency setting deployed during a boom — the worst possible combination, monetary fuel poured onto a fire. The cost of the transitory error was every month spent in that condition, and the bill came due as the hikes that followed.

## The fastest hiking cycle in 40 years: zero to 5.5%

Once the Fed finally accepted that inflation was not transitory — Chair Powell formally retired the word in late November 2021 — the catch-up began. And because the Fed was so far behind, the catch-up had to be brutal. The result was the fastest tightening cycle in four decades.

The mechanics are worth walking through because the *pace* is the whole point. The Fed lifted off in March 2022 with a cautious 25-basis-point hike, taking the upper bound from 0.25% to 0.50%. Then, as each subsequent inflation print came in hot, it accelerated: 50 basis points in May, then an unprecedented run of four consecutive 75-basis-point hikes from June through November 2022 — moves of a size the Fed had not made since 1994. By the end of 2022 the upper bound was 4.50%, up from 0.25% at the start of the year. The cycle continued into 2023 with smaller steps, reaching a terminal upper bound of 5.50% in July 2023, where it held. From the 0.25% floor to the 5.50% terminal is 525 basis points of tightening, the bulk of it compressed into 2022.

![Fed funds target upper bound step chart from 2019 to 2024 showing the rapid 2022 to 2023 hiking cycle](/imgs/blogs/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle-4.png)

The step chart makes the speed visceral. Look at the flat zero band that runs across 2020 and 2021 — that is the transitory year, policy frozen at the emergency floor while inflation climbed. Then look at the near-vertical staircase in 2022, shaded amber: that is 525 basis points crammed into sixteen months. For comparison, the last cycle that moved this fast was Volcker's in the early 1980s; the gentle 2015-2018 cycle, by contrast, took three years to raise rates by a similar amount. The 2022 cycle did in months what prior cycles did in years.

Why does the *pace* matter so much, separate from the destination? Because markets price the speed of change, not just the level. Asset prices can adjust to a high rate if they have time; what they cannot easily adjust to is a rate that triples in a year. The convexity of the move — the sheer rate of repricing — is what overwhelmed positioning, blew up anyone short volatility, and produced the cross-asset selloff. A slow march to 5.5% would have been painful; a sprint to 5.5% was a regime break.

#### Worked example: the hiking pace, 0.25% to 5.50%

Let us put numbers on "the fastest in forty years" so the claim is not just rhetoric. The Fed's policy rate (upper bound of the target range) went from 0.25% in early March 2022 to 5.50% in July 2023. The total move is:

```
total tightening = 5.50% - 0.25% = 5.25 percentage points = 525 basis points
```

A basis point is one-hundredth of a percentage point, the unit traders use because policy moves and yield changes are usually fractions of a percent. The bulk of those 525 basis points — 425 of them — landed in the nine months from March to December 2022:

```
2022 alone: 4.50% - 0.25% = 4.25 percentage points = 425 basis points in nine months
```

Compare that to the prior cycle. From December 2015 to December 2018, the Fed raised the upper bound from 0.50% to 2.50%, a total of 200 basis points — over three years:

```
2015-2018 pace = 200 bp / 36 months = 5.6 bp per month
2022 pace      = 425 bp / 9 months  = 47.2 bp per month
```

The 2022 cycle tightened roughly **eight times faster per month** than the prior cycle. The single-sentence takeaway: when a central bank is this far behind the curve, it does not normalize gently — it slams, and the speed of the slam is what reprices every asset at once.

The 2022 cycle tightened roughly eight times faster per month, and that ratio is the part to internalize, because the *pace* is what the previous worked example proves and what no slow-cycle intuition prepares you for.

For the broader mechanics of how a change in the policy rate actually transmits through markets — through the yield curve, credit spreads, the currency, and risk assets — the companion post on [how policy moves every asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-policy-moves-every-asset-cross-asset-transmission-map) lays out the full transmission map. The 2022 cycle was that map running at maximum speed.

### The second lever: quantitative tightening

Raising the policy rate was only half of the Fed's tightening. The other half was unwinding the bond-buying it had done during the pandemic — a process called quantitative tightening, or QT, the mirror image of quantitative easing. During the pandemic, the Fed had bought trillions of dollars of Treasuries and mortgage bonds, expanding its balance sheet from roughly \$4.2 trillion to nearly \$9 trillion, which pushed cash into the financial system and held down long-term yields. Starting in mid-2022, the Fed reversed this: it let bonds mature without replacing them, shrinking the balance sheet by up to \$95 billion per month and draining liquidity out of the system.

QT matters because it works on a different part of the curve than the policy rate. The policy rate controls overnight, front-end interest rates directly. QT works on the long end and on overall financial-system liquidity — by removing the Fed as a buyer of long bonds, it pushes long-term yields up and tightens the conditions in which all risk assets trade. So the 2022 tightening was a one-two punch: rate hikes lifting the front end and QT lifting the long end and draining liquidity simultaneously. Both pushed real yields higher, and the real yield, as we are about to see, is the discount rate on everything. For the full machinery of how the balance sheet itself moves markets, see the companion on [QE versus QT](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets). For now, hold the key point: in 2021-2023 the Fed tightened with both levers at once, which is why the squeeze on asset prices was so total.

## The 2022 synchronized selloff: stocks and bonds fell together

Now we reach the part that surprised even seasoned investors, and the part that makes 2022 a permanent case study. In a normal bad year for stocks, bonds go up. That is the entire premise of the 60/40 portfolio — 60% stocks for growth, 40% bonds for protection — and of the "risk parity" strategies that dominate institutional money. The idea is that stocks and bonds are negatively correlated: when stocks fall because the economy is weakening, the Fed cuts rates, bond prices rise (yields fall), and the bond sleeve cushions the equity loss. For most of the prior two decades, that relationship held, and it made diversification feel like a free lunch.

In 2022, it broke. Stocks fell *and* bonds fell, hard, at the same time. The chart below is the scoreboard.

![Asset class total returns in 2022 showing stocks and bonds both negative](/imgs/blogs/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle-5.png)

Read the red. Long Treasuries — the supposed safe-haven, the asset you hold *for* protection — lost 31.2%, one of the worst years in the history of the US government bond market. Investment-grade corporate bonds lost 15.4%. The S&P 500 lost 18.1%, and the long-duration Nasdaq 100 lost 32.5%. The 60/40 portfolio, holding both, lost 16.1% — its worst year since 1937. The only green on the board: commodities (up 16.1%, because they *are* inflation) and the US dollar (up 8.2%, because the Fed was hiking faster than other central banks). Even gold, the classic inflation hedge, was roughly flat because rising real yields hurt it. Bitcoin, which some had marketed as "digital gold" and an inflation hedge, lost 64.3% — the opposite of a hedge.

Why did this happen? The figure below is the causal chain, and it is the single most important diagram in this post.

![Why stocks and bonds fell together in 2022 through one rate shock raising the discount rate](/imgs/blogs/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle-6.png)

The logic runs as follows. In 2022, the shock hitting markets was not a *growth* scare — it was an *inflation* scare. This distinction is everything. When the shock is weak growth, the Fed cuts rates, bonds rally, and the bond sleeve hedges the stock loss; that is the world the 60/40 was built for. But when the shock is inflation, the Fed *hikes*, which sends real yields surging — in 2022 the 10-year real yield went from about minus 1.04% to plus 1.74%, a swing of nearly 280 basis points. And the real yield is the discount rate on everything.

Here is the unifying mechanism. Every financial asset is a claim on future cash flows — a bond pays fixed coupons, a stock pays uncertain future earnings, real estate pays future rents. To value any of them today, you discount those future cash flows back to the present using the real interest rate. When the real rate rises, the present value of *every* future cash flow falls, because future money is now worth less today. So when the Fed's hikes drove the real yield up by 280 basis points, the discount rate on bonds rose (bond prices fell), the discount rate on stocks rose (stock prices fell), the discount rate on real estate rose (cap rates rose, property values fell) — all at once, all for the same reason. The stock-bond correlation flipped from negative to positive, and diversification stopped working exactly when investors needed it most.

This is the deepest lesson of 2022, and it generalizes: *the stock-bond hedge works against growth shocks and fails against inflation shocks.* If you only remember one thing about how to position for inflation, remember that bonds are not your hedge when the Fed is hiking — they are part of the same trade as stocks, because both are long-duration claims being repriced by the same rising discount rate. For a deeper dive on the master variable behind all of this, see the companion on [real versus nominal rates and real yields](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).

#### Worked example: the 2022 selloff on a \$1,000,000 60/40 book

Let us walk a real portfolio through the year, because the 60/40's 16.1% loss is the number that made institutions rethink everything. Suppose on 1 January 2022 you held a \$1,000,000 portfolio split the classic way: \$600,000 in US stocks (the S&P 500) and \$400,000 in US bonds (a broad investment-grade bond index).

The stock sleeve, down 18.1% on the year:

```
stock sleeve = 600,000 x (1 - 0.181) = 491,400 (a loss of 108,600)
```

In a normal year, the bond sleeve would have gone *up* and offset some of that. Instead, with the Fed hiking 425 basis points, the broad bond index fell roughly 13% (the aggregate index; long Treasuries fell far more, 31.2%). The bond sleeve:

```
bond sleeve = 400,000 x (1 - 0.13) = 348,000 (a loss of 52,000)
```

Total portfolio at year-end:

```
total = 491,400 + 348,000 = 839,400
total loss = 1,000,000 - 839,400 = 160,600, or -16.1%
```

You lost \$160,600 — and crucially, the part of your portfolio you held *for safety* lost \$52,000 of it. The diversification that was supposed to protect you instead doubled down on the same trade. The single-sentence takeaway: in an inflation-driven selloff the 60/40's bond sleeve does not cushion the equity loss, it compounds it, because both legs are long-duration assets repriced by the same rising real yield.

## The disinflation and the soft-landing debate

By the second half of 2022, the inflation tide began to turn. Headline CPI rolled over first, falling from 9.06% in June 2022 to 6.45% by December and below 3% by mid-2023, as energy prices retreated and supply chains healed. But headline, as we noted, is flattered by volatile energy. The number the Fed actually watches — core PCE — came down more slowly and stubbornly, which is why the Fed held rates at the 5.50% peak well into 2024 rather than cutting at the first sign of cooling.

This sets up the great debate of 2023-2024, which dominated macro trading: would the disinflation come with a recession (the historical norm — you usually have to break the economy to break inflation), or would it be a "soft landing," the rare outcome where inflation falls back to target without a meaningful rise in unemployment? For decades, the conventional wisdom held that soft landings were nearly impossible: every prior inflation fight of this magnitude had ended in recession. The bond market agreed — the yield curve inverted deeply in 2022-2023, with the 2-year yield rising far above the 10-year, the most reliable recession signal there is, reaching its deepest inversion since 1981.

And yet, through 2023 and 2024, the economy did not break. Unemployment stayed near multi-decade lows even as inflation fell. Growth held up. The soft landing that almost no one thought possible appeared to be happening. This was a genuine surprise, and it taught its own lesson: that the relationship between inflation, rates, and the labor market is not as mechanical as the models assumed, and that an inflation surge driven heavily by *supply* (broken chains that then healed) can unwind with less economic damage than one driven purely by *demand*.

Why did the soft landing happen when history said it shouldn't? Several threads came together. First, as noted, inflation expectations stayed anchored, so the Fed did not have to over-tighten to re-establish credibility — it could stop at 5.50% rather than the punishing levels Volcker needed. Second, a large chunk of the inflation really was supply-driven, and supply healing (ports unclogging, chip production recovering, shipping costs normalizing) did the disinflationary work for free, without requiring a collapse in demand. Third, the labor market cooled in an unusual way: instead of mass layoffs, the adjustment came mostly through the disappearance of excess job openings. The roughly two-openings-per-worker ratio fell back toward one, which relieved wage pressure without throwing people out of work. That distinction — fewer openings rather than more unemployed — is the technical heart of the soft landing, and it is why unemployment could stay near 3.5-4% while wage growth cooled.

For a trader, the soft-landing surprise carried a sharp lesson about positioning against consensus. The single most crowded macro trade of 2023 was "recession is imminent" — it was in the inverted curve, in the survey of forecasters, in the underweight-equities positioning of institutional money. And it was wrong, or at least early by years. Fighting that consensus — staying long risk assets while everyone waited for a recession that didn't come — was one of the best trades available, precisely because the bar for being pleasantly surprised was so low. When a recession is universally expected and priced, the economy merely *not* collapsing is bullish. That asymmetry, between what is priced and what occurs, is where macro edge actually lives.

#### Worked example: the disinflation and a \$500,000 duration position

The disinflation was not just an economic event — it was a tradable inflection, and the asset that benefited most was duration. *Duration* measures how sensitive a bond's price is to a change in yield: a bond with a duration of 8 years gains roughly 8% for every 1-percentage-point fall in its yield, and loses roughly 8% for every 1-point rise. (For the deeper mechanics see [how monetary policy moves bonds](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-bonds-duration-convexity).)

Consider a trader who, watching core PCE peak at 5.6% in February 2022 and recognizing the inflation surge, *avoided* long bonds through 2022 — the right call, since duration got crushed in the hiking cycle. Now fast-forward to late 2023. Core PCE has fallen from its 5.6% peak toward 3%, the Fed is signaling it is done hiking, and the disinflation is clearly underway. The trader puts \$500,000 into a long-duration Treasury position with a duration of about 16 years (long Treasuries), betting that as inflation falls toward the 2% target, yields will fall and bond prices will rise.

Suppose over the following year the relevant long yield falls by 0.75 percentage points as disinflation continues and rate cuts come into view. The price gain on a 16-year-duration position:

```
price gain (approx) = duration x yield change = 16 x 0.75% = +12.0%
dollar gain = 500,000 x 0.12 = +60,000
```

The position returns roughly \$60,000 in price appreciation, plus the coupon income on top. The same duration that was a wrecking ball in 2022 — when yields were rising — became the engine of returns once disinflation flipped the direction of yields. The single-sentence takeaway: duration is not "risky" or "safe" in the abstract; it is a leveraged bet on the *direction* of yields, so the entire game is reading whether you are in the rising-inflation regime (short duration) or the disinflation regime (long duration).

This is the same regime read that governs the whole post, applied to one position. The trader who understood the reaction function avoided duration when the Fed was hiking and embraced it when the Fed was done. Reading the regime, not predicting the exact CPI, was the edge.

## Common misconceptions

The 2021-2023 episode generated a set of confident, wrong explanations that are worth dismantling, because each one will lead you astray in the next inflation scare.

**Misconception 1: "It was all supply chains."** This was the most popular exculpation, and it is half-true, which is what makes it dangerous. Yes, broken supply chains and the energy shock were real and important. But if it had been *only* supply chains, inflation would have stayed concentrated in goods and faded as chains healed — and it did not. It spread into shelter, services, and wages, the sticky demand-driven categories. The proof is in the breakdown: core services inflation, which has almost nothing to do with shipping containers, ran above 5% well into 2023. The demand side — \$5 trillion of stimulus and a 40% money surge — was the other half, and it was the half that made the inflation persistent. If it had been all supply chains, the Fed would not have needed 525 basis points of hikes to bring it down. The corrective number: core PCE, which strips out the volatile supply-driven energy and food, still peaked at 5.6% — nearly three times the 2% target — and that core overshoot is the demand fingerprint.

**Misconception 2: "The Fed could have stopped it painlessly if it had just acted earlier."** Earlier action would have helped, and the transitory delay was a genuine error. But "painlessly" is wishful thinking. Bringing 9% inflation back to 2% is, historically, never painless — Volcker needed a deep recession and 19% rates to do it in the early 1980s. The honest counterfactual is that earlier hikes would have meant a *less violent* cycle and probably less risk to financial stability, not a free disinflation. The corrective: even the relatively benign 2023-2024 disinflation required holding the policy rate at 5.50% — a deeply restrictive setting — for over a year. There is no setting of policy that delivers 9%-to-2% disinflation for free.

**Misconception 3: "A recession was inevitable."** The bond market, the inverted yield curve, and most economists said a recession was coming, and for two years it did not arrive. The deepest yield-curve inversion since 1981 (the 2s10s spread hit minus 1.08%) was, for once, not followed by a near-term recession. This taught a humbling lesson: the historical playbook — that you must break the labor market to break inflation — was not iron law. A supply-heavy inflation could partly unwind on its own as supply healed, allowing inflation to fall while employment held. The corrective: unemployment stayed near 3.5-4% throughout the disinflation, contradicting every model that required it to spike. "Inevitable" is a word that should make a macro trader suspicious; the most crowded macro view of 2023 — imminent recession — was wrong for a remarkably long time.

**Misconception 4: "Inflation is dead now, so the regime is back to normal."** Look again at the right edge of the CPI chart. After bottoming, inflation re-accelerated in 2025-26. Inflation regimes do not always end cleanly with a return to a placid 2%; they can produce second waves, exactly as the 1970s did. The corrective: assuming the inflation problem is permanently solved is the same category of error as assuming it was "transitory" — both are bets that the current trend will continue without the reaction function being tested again.

## How it shows up in real markets: the regime-break lesson

Step back from the individual moves and look at what 2021-2023 *is*, structurally, because the structure is what repeats.

Every asset move in those two years was a derivative of one variable: the Fed's reaction to inflation. The stock selloff, the bond selloff, the dollar's surge, the commodity rally, the crypto crash, the cap-rate repricing in real estate, the mortgage-rate spike from 2.65% to 7.79% — all of it reduces to "the real discount rate rose because the Fed was forced to hike to fight inflation." When one variable drives everything, correlations between assets converge toward one, diversification stops working, and the only thing that matters is whether you read that variable correctly.

That is what a regime break looks like from the inside. In a normal regime, dozens of variables matter and assets move somewhat independently — stock pickers can add value, sector rotation works, bonds hedge stocks. In a regime break, one macro variable dominates and swamps everything else. 2008 was a regime break around credit and liquidity. 2020 was a regime break around a sudden stop in activity. 2022 was a regime break around inflation and the policy response to it. The skill is recognizing *when you are in one*, because the rules of the normal regime — diversify, buy the dip, bonds protect you — can be actively dangerous inside the break.

The dispersion *within* asset classes is its own tell, and 2022 illustrates it cleanly. Even inside the falling stock market, the rate shock sorted winners from losers by exactly one variable: duration. Energy stocks rose 65.7% because they *are* the inflation and their cash flows are near-term and rising. Utilities and consumer staples — defensive, dividend-paying, cash-flowing now — held roughly flat. At the other extreme, the longest-duration equities took the worst damage: consumer discretionary fell 37%, technology fell 28%, and real estate fell 26%, all because their value sits in far-future cash flows that the rising discount rate crushed. The same logic ran through the real economy: the 30-year mortgage rate leapt from a record-low 2.65% in January 2021 to 7.79% by October 2023, freezing the housing market, and commercial real-estate cap rates — which had compressed to record lows when money was free — repriced sharply higher as the discount rate rose. One variable, the real rate, sorted every asset and every sector by its duration. That is the signature of a rate-driven regime break: not just that everything falls, but that everything falls *in order of its sensitivity to the discount rate.*

Here is the practical tell. In 2021, the warning signs were all present for anyone willing to read the reaction function rather than the reassurances: money supply up 40%, \$5 trillion of stimulus delivered to spenders, inflation broadening out of the reopening categories into shelter and wages, and a policy rate frozen at zero with a real rate at minus seven percent. The trader who took those signals seriously — who concluded that the Fed was behind the curve and would have to catch up violently — could position for the regime *before* the hikes came: short duration, underweight long-duration tech, overweight cash and energy, long the dollar. The trader who believed "transitory" walked into a 16% loss on a "diversified" 60/40 book. The information was there. The edge was in the reading.

## How to trade it / the playbook

We close, as every post in this series does, on the concrete playbook — the signals, the positions, the invalidations — distilled from the 2021-2023 episode. The figure below is the summary; the text underneath is how to use it.

![Regime break playbook matrix for reading the reaction function and positioning for inflation](/imgs/blogs/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle-7.png)

**1. Read the reaction function before the data.** The single most valuable habit is to watch the *gap* between the policy rate and where inflation says it should be. When inflation is running at 5-9% and the policy rate is at 0%, the real rate is deeply negative and the Fed is behind the curve — you do not need to forecast CPI, you only need to recognize that the gap must close through higher rates. The signals to track: core PCE versus the 2% target (the Fed's actual gauge), the breadth of inflation (is it spreading into shelter and services, or still confined to a few reopening categories?), and the Fed's own dot plot of projected rates. The invalidation: if core PCE is genuinely converging on 2% with broad participation, the behind-the-curve thesis is over and you should expect the reaction function to relax.

**2. Position for the regime, not the headline.** In an inflation regime — when the dominant shock is rising prices and the Fed is hiking — the rotation is mechanical, because you are positioning for a rising real discount rate. Overweight the assets that win when the Fed hikes: energy (S&P energy was up 65.7% in 2022), broad commodities (up 16.1%), cash and short bills (which reprice up as rates rise and got crushed by nothing), and the dollar (up 8.2% as the Fed out-hiked its peers). Underweight the assets that lose: long-duration growth and tech (Nasdaq 100 down 32.5%), long Treasuries (20y+ down 31.2%), and anything whose value is a far-future cash flow being discounted at a rising rate. The invalidation: when the shock flips from inflation to growth — when the Fed pivots from fighting inflation to cushioning a slowdown — this rotation reverses, and duration becomes the asset to own.

**3. Treat duration as the master risk dial.** Duration is not inherently risky or safe; it is a directional bet on yields. In the rising-inflation, hiking regime, shorten duration ruthlessly — floating-rate instruments, short bills, anything that reprices up rather than down. In the disinflation regime, extend duration to capture the price gains as yields fall (the \$60,000 gain on a \$500,000 long-bond position in the worked example). The signal that flips the dial is the same one as everything else: the direction of the reaction function. The invalidation: a second inflation wave (as in 2025-26) that forces the Fed back into hiking turns long duration from an engine back into a wrecking ball.

**4. Stop assuming bonds hedge stocks.** This is the hardest habit to break because it is built into every standard portfolio. The stock-bond hedge works against *growth* shocks and fails against *inflation* shocks, because in an inflation shock both legs are long-duration claims repriced by the same rising real yield (the 60/40's 16.1% loss in 2022). When you identify an inflation regime, you must add an explicit inflation sleeve — commodities, energy, real assets, short duration — because the bond sleeve will not save you. The 60/40 is not dead; it just needs an inflation hedge bolted on in this regime. The invalidation: in a disinflation or growth-scare regime, the negative stock-bond correlation reasserts and the classic 60/40 works again.

**5. Respect the curve, and plan the pivot.** The yield curve inverted to its deepest level since 1981 in 2022-2023, flagging the slowdown the hikes were engineering — even though stocks kept rising for a while and the recession took years to (almost not) arrive. Read the 2s10s as a regime barometer, not a precise timer. And plan the pivot in advance: the inflation regime ends when disinflation is broad and confirmed (not on the first soft headline print), and that is when you rotate from the inflation-winner book back toward duration and growth. The meta-invalidation that governs all of this: do not fight the last war. The 2025-26 re-acceleration is the live reminder that the next shock may not look like the last one, and the trader's job is to read *this* reaction function, not to re-run the 2022 playbook on autopilot.

The deepest takeaway of the entire 2021-2023 episode is the one we opened with: when a single macro variable comes to dominate, every asset reprices off it, and the edge collapses to one question — *can you read the reaction function?* In 2022 that variable was the Fed's response to a 9.1% inflation shock, and everything from Bitcoin to long Treasuries to your mortgage rate moved as a derivative of it. The traders who profited were not the ones with the best CPI forecast. They were the ones who recognized, early, that the disinflation regime had broken, that the Fed was behind the curve and would have to catch up violently, and that in such a regime the old rules — diversify with bonds, buy the dip, sit safely in cash — were the dangerous ones. Read the regime, position for it, respect the curve, and never trust the word "transitory" — in either direction.
