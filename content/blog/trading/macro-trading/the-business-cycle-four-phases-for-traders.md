> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The economy moves in a repeating cycle of four phases — expansion, peak, contraction (recession), and trough — and each phase rewards different assets and punishes others, so reading *where you are in the cycle* is the top-down decision that sets almost everything else.
>
> - The four phases are **expansion** (growth and jobs rise), **peak** (the top, growth fast but slowing), **contraction / recession** (output falls, jobs are lost), and **trough** (the bottom, where recovery begins). The cycle is recurring but **not regular** — expansions have run from 1 year to over 10.
> - You read the phase with **leading** indicators — the **yield curve**, the **PMI / ISM** surveys, **jobless claims**, **credit spreads** — because they turn *before* GDP does. GDP itself is a **coincident-to-lagging** number reported with a delay, so it confirms the phase you are already in, not the next one.
> - The **single best real-time gauge is the ISM Manufacturing PMI** and its **50 line**: above 50 the economy is expanding, below 50 it is contracting. It ran from **63.7** at the 2021 boom to **46.0** in the 2023 contraction.
> - The one number to remember: the **2s10s yield curve inverted to -1.08% in July 2023**, the deepest since 1981, the bond market's recession bell — and the **2020 cycle compressed the whole loop (peak Feb, trough Apr) into two months**, the fastest recession and recovery on record.

In February 2020 the US economy was, by every measure, in rude health. Unemployment sat at **3.5%**, a half-century low. Stocks were at all-time highs. The expansion that began in mid-2009 had just become the longest in American history — over a decade without a recession. If you had asked a hundred economists where the economy was in the business cycle, all hundred would have said the same thing: late expansion, maybe brushing the peak, but expansion. And they would have been right — for about three more weeks.

Then a virus and the lockdowns to contain it did something no recession had ever done. The National Bureau of Economic Research, the official arbiter of US recessions, later dated the cycle **peak to February 2020 and the trough to April 2020**. Two months. The longest expansion in history rolled over into the steepest, shortest recession in history, and then into the fastest recovery in history, all inside a single year. Real GDP fell **-2.2%** for 2020 as a whole and then snapped back **+5.9%** in 2021. The entire business cycle — a loop that historically takes the better part of a decade — got compressed into a few months you could watch in real time.

That violence is exactly why the business cycle matters to a trader, and why 2020 is the perfect teacher. The cycle is the slow tide underneath every market. Most of the time it moves so gradually that it is easy to ignore and pretend each asset has its own private story. But the cycle is always running, every asset is riding it, and when it turns — slowly over years or violently over weeks — it reprices *everything* at once. This post builds the whole machine from zero: what the cycle is, why it exists, the four phases, the indicators that let you read your position in real time, and finally how a trader reads the phase off the PMI, the yield curve, and the jobs data and positions for the *next* phase before it arrives.

## Foundations: the four phases and the indicators that read them

Before any trading, we need to build a handful of ideas from absolute scratch: what the **business cycle** is, why it **exists** at all, the **four phases** and what defines each, the crucial distinction between **leading, coincident, and lagging** indicators, what the **PMI / ISM** surveys are and why the **50 line** is magic, what an **output gap** is, and what a recession **actually** is (the NBER definition, which is not the one most people think). Everything later in this post is a consequence of these foundations. Read this section slowly; the trading is easy once the map is clear.

### What the business cycle is

Start with the simplest possible picture. Draw the total output of an economy — its **GDP**, the dollar value of everything it produces — as a line over time. Over decades that line trends upward, because economies grow: more people, more capital, better technology. But it does not climb in a smooth straight line. It **oscillates around the trend** — surging ahead for a few years, then stalling and falling back, then surging again. Those repeating ups and downs around the long-run growth trend are the **business cycle**.

The everyday-money analogy: take a household's spending and earning over the years. The long-run trend is up — careers advance, income grows. But it is not smooth. There are good years (a raise, a bonus, confident spending on a car and a vacation) and bad years (a layoff, belt-tightening, postponed purchases). Zoom out and you see a rising line; zoom in and you see a wave riding on top of it. A whole economy is millions of those household and business decisions averaged together, and they tend to swing *in sync* — everyone feels confident together, then everyone gets cautious together — which is what produces the cycle.

Two things are true at once, and holding both is the first skill:

- **The trend is up.** Over the long run economies grow, which is why "buy and hold" works for stocks over decades. Betting on permanent collapse is betting against the trend.
- **The cycle is real.** Around that rising trend, the economy expands and contracts in waves that last anywhere from a couple of years to over a decade, and those waves dominate returns over any horizon shorter than a decade.

A trader's job is to read the **cycle** (the wave) without losing sight of the **trend** (the climb). Macro trading is, at its heart, the discipline of locating yourself on the wave.

### Why the cycle exists at all

Why doesn't the economy just grow smoothly along its trend? Because the forces that drive it are **self-reinforcing in both directions** — they build momentum up and then momentum down. A few of the engines:

- **Credit and confidence feed on themselves.** When times are good, banks lend freely, businesses borrow and invest, consumers spend, asset prices rise, which makes everyone feel richer and borrow and spend *more*. The boom feeds the boom. Then something cracks, credit tightens, the same loop runs in reverse, and the bust feeds the bust. (The mechanics of how credit creates and destroys money over the cycle are their own deep topic — see [how credit creates money: the lending channel and cycles](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-credit-creates-money-lending-channel-cycles).)
- **Inventories and investment overshoot.** Businesses cannot see the future, so they build factories and stock shelves based on recent demand. When demand is strong they over-build; when it softens they are caught with too much capacity and inventory, so they slam the brakes — cutting orders and jobs — which deepens the downturn.
- **Policy is always playing catch-up.** The central bank raises rates to cool an overheating boom and cuts them to revive a bust, but its tools work with **long and variable lags** (it takes 12-18 months for a rate change to fully hit the economy), so policy tends to arrive late and overshoot, adding its own wave on top.

The deep point: the cycle exists because the economy is a system of **feedback loops with delays**. Feedback creates momentum; delays create overshoot; together they create oscillation. You do not need to forecast the virus or the war that triggers the turn — you need to read the feedback loops as they build and the delays as they play out. That is what the indicators below let you do.

### The four phases

The cycle is conventionally split into four phases. Walk around the loop once, starting from the bottom.

1. **Expansion (recovery → boom).** Output is growing. GDP is positive and often accelerating. Businesses hire, unemployment falls, profits rise, confidence builds, credit flows. This is the longest phase — expansions have lasted from about a year to over a decade. Early expansion (coming off the trough) is the sweetest part for risk assets: growth is improving *and* policy is still easy.
2. **Peak.** The top of the cycle. Growth is still positive but **decelerating** — the economy is running hot, often with rising inflation, capacity stretched, the labor market tight. The central bank is usually tightening to cool things off. The peak is a *transition*, not a long phase; the danger is that it is only obvious in hindsight.
3. **Contraction (recession).** Output is now **falling**, not just slowing. GDP turns negative, businesses cut investment and lay off workers, unemployment rises, profits fall, credit tightens, asset prices drop. This is the painful phase — and historically the *shortest*: US recessions since WWII have averaged roughly 10-11 months.
4. **Trough.** The bottom. Things stop getting *worse*. The economy is still weak in absolute terms, but the rate of decline flattens and turns. The central bank is cutting aggressively, valuations are cheap, and — crucially — markets often bottom *here*, while the news is still terrible, because markets price the *next* phase. From the trough the loop rolls back into expansion and repeats.

Figure 1 above lays the four phases out left to right with the leading indicators that turn first for each, and the loop-back that makes it a cycle rather than a line. Notice the asymmetry the figure encodes: expansions are long and gradual (the slow climb), contractions are short and violent (the fast drop). Markets feel this as "up the stairs, down the elevator."

### The output gap: above or below potential

One more concept clarifies what "peak" and "trough" really mean. Economists define **potential GDP** — the level of output the economy could sustain at full employment without overheating. Actual GDP swings above and below potential, and the difference is the **output gap**.

- A **positive output gap** (actual above potential) means the economy is running hot — full employment, stretched capacity, rising inflation. This is the **peak** territory.
- A **negative output gap** (actual below potential) means slack — unused capacity, unemployed workers, weak inflation. This is **recession / trough** territory.

The output gap reframes the cycle as the economy oscillating around its potential, not just around zero growth. It also explains a subtlety: an economy can be *growing* (positive GDP) and still be in a "slowdown" if growth is below potential and the gap is closing toward negative. Traders watch the *direction and rate of change* of growth relative to potential, not just whether GDP is positive. We will return to this — "the rate of change is the signal" is one of the deepest ideas in cycle reading.

### What a recession actually is (the NBER definition)

Here is where almost everyone — including most journalists — gets it wrong. The popular rule is "two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth equals a recession." That is a useful rough heuristic, but it is **not** the official definition, and relying on it will mislead you.

In the United States, recessions are dated by a committee at the **National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER)**. Their definition: *"a significant decline in economic activity that is spread across the economy and lasts more than a few months."* They look at a basket — real income, employment, industrial production, and sales — not just GDP. Three consequences matter enormously to a trader:

- **The NBER dates recessions with a long lag**, sometimes a year or more after they begin, because they wait for confirmed data. The NBER did not officially declare the 2020 recession until June 2020 — *after* it was already over. So you can *never* rely on the official call for trading; by the time it arrives, the move is done.
- **"Two negative quarters" is neither necessary nor sufficient.** In 2022 US GDP fell for two straight quarters, and the "two-quarter rule" screamed recession — but the NBER never called one, because employment and income were still rising. The heuristic gave a false signal.
- **Breadth matters.** A recession is a *broad* decline. A single weak sector is not a recession; a synchronized decline across income, jobs, output, and sales is.

The takeaway is liberating: you are not trying to match the NBER's backward-looking academic call. You are trying to read the *leading* indicators that turn *before* the recession the NBER will eventually date — which brings us to the most important distinction in this entire post.

### Leading, coincident, and lagging: the three speeds of data

Every macro indicator can be sorted by *when* it turns relative to the economy itself. This is the master organizing principle of cycle reading.

- **Leading indicators** turn **before** the economy does. They are forecasts embedded in market prices and business decisions made ahead of production. Examples: the **yield curve** (the bond market's growth forecast), the **PMI new-orders** sub-index (orders precede production), **initial jobless claims** (firms cut hours and stop hiring before mass layoffs show up), **credit spreads** (lenders price stress early), **building permits** (housing leads the cycle), and the **stock market** itself (prices discount the future). These are where traders live, because they turn *while you can still reposition*.
- **Coincident indicators** move **with** the economy — they describe the present. Examples: **real GDP**, **nonfarm payrolls** (jobs added this month), **industrial production**, **real income**. These confirm what phase you are *in*, but they are reported with a delay (GDP is a quarter behind), so by the time they confirm, you should already be positioned.
- **Lagging indicators** turn **after** the economy. They confirm the past. Examples: the **unemployment rate** (it peaks *after* the recession ends, because firms rehire cautiously), **core inflation** (the slowest-moving series), **corporate profits** (reported a quarter late), and the **average duration of unemployment**. These are nearly useless for entries — by the time the unemployment rate confirms a recession, the *stock-market bottom is often already behind you*.

![Leading, coincident, and lagging indicators stacked into three tiers with examples](/imgs/blogs/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders-4.png)

Figure 4 stacks the three tiers. Internalize this hierarchy, because it dissolves a paradox that confuses every beginner: *"the data says the economy is fine, so why are stocks falling?"* The answer is that stocks are a **leading** indicator and the data you are reading (GDP, the unemployment rate) is **coincident-to-lagging**. They are looking at different points in time. The market is voting on the next phase while the data describes the current one. A trader who reads only coincident and lagging data is permanently driving by the rear-view mirror.

### The PMI / ISM survey and the magic of the 50 line

Of all the leading indicators, one stands out for being timely, simple, and reliable: the **PMI**, or Purchasing Managers' Index. In the US the headline version is the **ISM Manufacturing PMI**, published by the Institute for Supply Management on the *first business day of every month* — making it one of the earliest reads on the month just ended, weeks ahead of the official hard data.

Here is how it works, and why the design is brilliant. Each month, ISM surveys purchasing managers at hundreds of manufacturing firms — the people who place the orders, hire the workers, and manage the inventory, so they see demand *first*. The survey asks a simple question about new orders, production, employment, supplier deliveries, and inventories: is each one **better, the same, or worse** than last month? ISM converts the answers into a **diffusion index** — the percentage saying "better" plus half the percentage saying "the same." That construction is the key to the magic.

Because of the diffusion math, the index has a hard, meaningful threshold:

- **PMI above 50** means more firms are seeing improvement than deterioration — the manufacturing sector is **expanding**.
- **PMI below 50** means more firms are seeing deterioration — the sector is **contracting**.
- **PMI = 50** is the exact dividing line, "no change."

So the **50 line is the boom/bust threshold drawn directly onto the chart.** You do not have to interpret a level in a vacuum the way you do with GDP ("is 2% growth good or bad?"). The PMI tells you the *sign* of the change instantly: above 50 expanding, below 50 contracting. And because it is a survey of forward-looking purchasing decisions, it **leads** the hard production data by a month or two. The new-orders sub-index leads the headline. This is why many macro desks treat the ISM as their single most important monthly print. (It anchors the monthly data calendar alongside CPI, the jobs report, and the Fed — see [the macro calendar: CPI, NFP, FOMC, PMI](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi).)

![ISM Manufacturing PMI plotted against the 50 expansion line with expansion and contraction bands](/imgs/blogs/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders-3.png)

Figure 3 plots the ISM PMI through the 2020-2025 cycle against the 50 line, with the expansion zone shaded green and the contraction zone red. Watch the *cross of 50* — that crossing is the cleanest single cycle signal you will find. We will walk the exact numbers in a worked example shortly.

With the foundations built — the four phases, the output gap, the NBER reality, the three speeds of data, and the PMI's 50 line — we can now go deep on *how a trader actually reads the cycle in real time*. The rest of this post is mechanics and the playbook.

## Why traders live on the leading indicators

The single most important practical consequence of the leading / coincident / lagging split is this: **the official, coincident, and lagging data is published too late to trade on.** By the time GDP confirms a contraction, the contraction is a quarter old and the stock market — a leading indicator — has often already bottomed. A trader who waits for confirmation is structurally late.

Consider the timeline of a typical turn. The yield curve inverts (leading) maybe 12-18 months before the recession. The PMI rolls below 50 (leading) months before output actually falls. Jobless claims start creeping up (leading). Credit spreads widen (leading). *Then* GDP turns negative (coincident, reported a quarter late). *Then* the unemployment rate climbs (lagging). *Then*, finally, the NBER announces the recession (often after it has ended). The entire useful trading window is in the **leading** column, which fires first and gives you time to reposition while prices are still moving.

This is also why the cliché "the stock market predicted nine of the last five recessions" is, properly understood, a *compliment* to the stock market, not an insult. As a leading indicator, the market is *supposed* to turn before the economy and to occasionally over-anticipate a recession that does not arrive. Its job is to discount probabilities of the future, which means false alarms are the price of being early. The yield curve has a much better record — it has preceded every US recession for about fifty years — precisely because it is the bond market's collective, money-weighted forecast of where rates and growth are going.

The practical rule: **build your cycle dashboard out of leading indicators, use coincident data to confirm, and treat lagging data as history.** Specifically, a workable real-time dashboard is the **PMI** (is it above or below 50, and which way is it moving?), the **yield curve** (steep, flat, inverted, or re-steepening?), **jobless claims** (rising or falling?), and **credit spreads** (tight or widening?). Those four, read together, place you on the cycle with weeks-to-months of lead time over anyone watching GDP.

### The rate of change is the signal, not the level

A subtle but powerful refinement: in cycle reading, the **second derivative** — the *change in the rate of change* — often matters more than the level. Markets price *surprises and inflections*, not steady states.

Concretely: a PMI of 55 that is *falling* from 60 is a worse signal than a PMI of 48 that is *rising* from 45. The first is decelerating expansion (heading toward the peak); the second is improving contraction (heading toward the trough and recovery). The *level* says "55 is still in expansion" and "48 is still in contraction," but the *direction* says the first is rolling over and the second is bottoming. Markets, which price the next phase, often reward the rising 48 over the falling 55.

This is why traders talk about "growth accelerating" versus "growth decelerating" rather than just "growth high" versus "growth low." The four phases map cleanly onto a 2x2 of *growth level* (above/below trend) and *growth direction* (rising/falling): early expansion is below-trend-and-rising, peak is above-trend-and-falling, recession is below-trend-and-falling, recovery is below-trend-and-turning-up. The inflection — the turn in the rate of change — is the tradeable moment, and it shows up in the leading indicators first.

## The labor market: the slow, honest confirmer

After the PMI, the second pillar of the cycle dashboard is the **labor market** — and it deserves its own treatment because it spans all three speeds of data at once, which is exactly why beginners misread it. The labor market is the part of the economy people feel most directly, which makes it emotionally compelling and analytically treacherous.

Start with the key distinction inside the jobs data:

- **Initial jobless claims** — the weekly count of people newly filing for unemployment benefits — is a **leading-to-coincident** indicator. Firms cut hours and stop hiring *before* they do mass layoffs, and the first people let go file for benefits immediately, so claims tend to tick up *early* in a downturn, weeks before the monthly payroll report shows it. Claims are published *weekly*, which makes them one of the most timely hard-data series in the entire kit. A sustained rise in initial claims off a low base is one of the cleaner early-warning signals that the labor market is cracking.
- **Nonfarm payrolls** — the monthly count of jobs added or lost — is a **coincident** indicator. It describes hiring *this month*. It is the headline number markets react to, but it confirms the present rather than forecasting the next phase, and it is heavily revised, so any single month is noisy.
- **The unemployment rate** is a **lagging** indicator, and this is the one that fools people. The unemployment rate typically **peaks well after the recession has ended**, because firms rehire cautiously and discouraged workers re-enter the labor force only once the recovery is clearly underway. In 2020 the rate spiked to **14.8%** in April and stayed elevated for *months* into the recovery. A trader who waits for the unemployment rate to confirm a recession is reading the most lagging series in the kit — by the time it confirms, the stock-market bottom is typically behind them.

There is also a famous statistical rule worth knowing: the **Sahm rule**, which flags a recession when the three-month average unemployment rate rises half a percentage point above its low of the prior year. It is reliable historically — but notice that it is built on the *unemployment rate*, a lagging indicator, so it confirms a recession that is usually already underway rather than forecasting one. It is a useful confirmer, not a leading signal. The cycle reader uses claims (leading) to anticipate, payrolls (coincident) to confirm the present, and the unemployment rate and the Sahm rule (lagging) to verify the past — never the other way around.

The deep practical lesson: **the labor market is the economy's slow, honest confirmer, not its forecaster.** It tells you with high reliability what phase you are *in*, but by the time it speaks clearly, the leading indicators — the curve, the PMI, claims off their lows — have already moved. Use the labor market to *confirm and size* a cycle read that the faster leading indicators flagged first; do not wait for it to be your trigger.

## Credit spreads and volatility: the market's own stress gauges

The fourth corner of the dashboard is what the *market itself* is pricing about cycle risk, read through two signals: **credit spreads** and **volatility**.

A **credit spread** is the extra yield a riskier borrower must pay over a safe government bond of the same maturity. When a company issues a bond, investors demand a higher yield than on a Treasury to compensate for default risk; that gap is the spread. The crucial fact for the cycle reader: **spreads are a leading indicator of stress.** Lenders price deterioration *early* — when the cycle turns down and default risk rises, investors demand more yield to hold corporate bonds, and spreads **widen** before the defaults actually happen and often before equity markets fully react. High-yield (junk) spreads are especially sensitive; they tend to blow out at the first whiff of a contraction. Tight, grinding-tighter spreads confirm a healthy expansion; rapidly widening spreads are an early contraction warning that frequently *leads* the stock market down. (The mechanics of how lending and credit conditions drive the cycle are developed in [how credit creates money: the lending channel and cycles](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-credit-creates-money-lending-channel-cycles).)

**Volatility**, measured by the **VIX** — the market's expected 30-day stock-market volatility, often called the "fear gauge" — is the other stress signal. A low VIX (the low-to-high teens) signals a calm, complacent, risk-on market consistent with expansion; a spiking VIX signals fear and a flight to havens consistent with contraction or crisis. The VIX is more of a *coincident* stress read than a leading one — it spikes *as* the stress hits rather than before — but it is invaluable for sizing: when the VIX doubles, the prudent move is to halve gross exposure regardless of your phase view, because a volatility spike means the market's pricing of *everything* has become unstable.

Put credit and volatility together with the PMI, the curve, and the labor market and you have a five-signal dashboard that spans leading (curve, claims, spreads), coincident (PMI, payrolls, VIX), and lagging (unemployment rate, profits) data. The art, again, is confluence: tight spreads and a low VIX *confirm* a PMI-above-50 expansion; widening spreads and a spiking VIX *confirm* a sub-50 PMI contraction. When all five point the same way, the cycle read is high-conviction; when they conflict, the cycle is in transition and you size down and wait.

## The yield curve as the recession bell

If the PMI is the best timely gauge, the **yield curve** is the best *forecaster* — the bell that rings ahead of the turn. We have a whole post on the mechanics ([reading the yield curve: slope, inversion, and the recession signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/reading-the-yield-curve-slope-inversion-recession)), so here we will keep it to what the cycle reader needs.

The curve plots the yield on government bonds across maturities — 3-month, 2-year, 10-year, 30-year. Normally it slopes **upward**: longer bonds yield more, because you demand extra return to lock your money away for longer. The number traders watch most is the **2s10s** — the 10-year yield minus the 2-year yield. When that spread is positive, the curve is normal; when it goes **negative**, the curve is **inverted** — short rates above long rates, which is backwards.

Why does inversion forecast recession? Because the **short end** of the curve is pinned by where the Fed has rates *now* (high, when it is fighting inflation late in an expansion), while the **long end** reflects where the market thinks rates will *average* over the next decade. When the market expects the Fed will have to **cut** rates in the future — which happens when it expects the economy to weaken — the long yield falls below the short yield, and the curve inverts. **Inversion is the bond market saying: rates are high now, but they are coming down, because growth is going to stumble enough that the Fed will have to ease.** It is a coherent forecast of a slowdown, not a glitch.

![The 2s10s yield curve inverting below zero as the leading recession signal](/imgs/blogs/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders-5.png)

Figure 5 shows the 2s10s through the recent cycle: the inversion below zero in 2022, the deepest reading of **-1.08%** in July 2023 (the most inverted since 1981), and the **un-inversion** back above zero in late 2024. Two features matter for the cycle reader. First, inversion **leads by a long, frustrating lag** — typically 12-18 months between the inversion and the actual recession, which is why "the curve inverted but no recession came" is a perennial false-comfort headline. Second, the real trigger is often the **un-inversion**: when the curve steepens back above zero — usually because the Fed is finally cutting into a weakening economy — the recession clock is nearly up. The bell rings on inversion; the alarm goes off on un-inversion.

## Dating the cycle in real time

Put the dashboard together and you can locate yourself on the cycle without waiting for the NBER. The procedure is a simple fusion of a few leading reads:

- **Growth (PMI):** Is the ISM above or below 50, and rising or falling? Above-and-rising = expansion; above-and-falling = approaching peak; below-and-falling = contraction; below-and-rising = trough/recovery.
- **Labor (jobless claims, payrolls):** Are claims low and falling (healthy) or rising (cracking)? Is payroll growth broad or narrowing? Labor is a coincident-to-lagging read that *confirms* the PMI's lead.
- **The yield curve:** Steep (early cycle), flattening/inverting (approaching peak), inverted (late cycle, recession ahead), or re-steepening with cuts (recession/recovery)?
- **Credit and volatility:** Are credit spreads tight (calm) or widening (stress building)? Is the VIX low or spiking?

The art is in *combining* them, because any single indicator throws false signals. A PMI dip alone might be a mid-cycle slowdown; a PMI dip *plus* an inverted curve *plus* rising claims *plus* widening spreads is a coherent late-cycle picture pointing at contraction. Confluence across independent indicators is what turns noise into a read.

![The cycle clock showing the four phases positioned around a dial with what to watch in each](/imgs/blogs/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders-6.png)

Figure 6 arranges the phases as a **cycle clock**: each position on the dial carries its indicator signature — what the PMI, the labor market, and the yield curve look like in that phase — and what to watch for the *transition* to the next. The clock turns clockwise: expansion at the top rolls to peak, peak rolls down to contraction, contraction rolls to the trough, and the trough climbs back to expansion. You are *always* somewhere on this clock; the question is only where, and which way the hands are about to move. The skill is not predicting the clock from nothing — it is reading the three dials and noting which transition the leading indicators are flashing.

### The phases as an asset-rotation map

The reason all this dating effort pays off is that **each phase has a characteristic ranking of which assets win and which lose**, and the rankings are stable enough to position around. The full mechanics are their own post ([asset rotation across the business-cycle quadrants](/blog/trading/macro-trading/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants)), but the skeleton is worth seeing here because it is *why* you read the phase at all:

- **Early expansion (off the trough):** the best risk-on environment — growth improving, policy easy. Equities (especially cyclicals and small caps), high-yield credit, and industrial commodities tend to lead. This is when you want maximum risk.
- **Peak / late expansion:** growth still positive but decelerating, inflation hot, Fed tight. Late-cycle plays — energy, materials, quality and value over growth — and rising cash. You are taking chips off the table.
- **Contraction / recession:** the defensive phase. Government bonds (Treasuries) rally as the Fed cuts, the dollar and gold catch haven bids, cash earns its keep, and defensive equity sectors (staples, utilities, healthcare) hold up best. You want duration and havens, not cyclicals.
- **Trough / early recovery:** the contrarian entry. Markets bottom while the news is still terrible. This is when you *add* risk — small caps, cyclicals, credit — into the teeth of the bad data, because prices are discounting the recovery that the leading indicators are starting to flash.

Get the phase right and the asset selection mostly follows. That is the whole thesis of top-down macro: the cycle is the master variable, and asset rotation is its shadow. The rotation itself is also a *risk-on / risk-off* story — money sliding between a risk basket and a haven basket as the cycle turns — which we develop in [risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates).

## Worked examples

Theory is cheap. Let us put real numbers on the cycle and on the P&L of getting the phase right or wrong.

#### Worked example: dating the 2020 cycle from the GDP collapse and rebound

Take the cleanest, most violent cycle on record and date it from the official growth numbers. From `data.GDP_GROWTH`, US real GDP growth was **+2.5% in 2019** (healthy late expansion), then **-2.2% in 2020** (the contraction), then **+5.9% in 2021** (the rebound), then **+2.5% in 2022**, **+2.9% in 2023**, **+2.8% in 2024**, and **+2.1% in 2025**.

![US real GDP growth bar chart 2019 to 2025 showing the 2020 collapse and 2021 rebound](/imgs/blogs/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders-2.png)

Figure 2 plots those annual growth bars. The single red bar — 2020 at -2.2% — is the contraction; the towering green bar beside it — 2021 at +5.9% — is the rebound. The whole story of the fastest cycle on record sits in those two bars, and the zero line between them is the coincident dividing line between expansion and contraction in output terms.

Walk the phases:

- **2019 (+2.5%):** solid expansion. Above-trend growth, 3.5% unemployment, the longest US expansion in history. A cycle reader in early 2020 would have placed the economy at *late expansion / brushing the peak*.
- **2020 (-2.2%):** the contraction. The NBER later dated the **peak to February 2020 and the trough to April 2020** — a two-month recession, the shortest on record. The annual figure of -2.2% hides the violence: GDP collapsed at an annualized **~-30%** in Q2 2020 and then snapped back.
- **2021 (+5.9%):** the recovery, and the fastest on record — the strongest single-year growth since 1984.

Now feel the timing problem. The annual GDP print for 2020 — the -2.2% — was not *known* until well into 2021, and the quarterly numbers showing the Q2 collapse were not published until late July 2020, *after the trough had already passed in April*. The S&P 500 bottomed on **March 23, 2020** — between the February peak and the April trough — while the coincident and lagging data were still describing catastrophe. A trader waiting for GDP to confirm the recession missed both the bottom and the **+5.9%** recovery year. **The lesson: GDP dates the cycle accurately but always too late to trade; the leading indicators and the market itself turned weeks to months before the official numbers could.**

#### Worked example: the PMI cross of 50, from boom to contraction

Now read the cycle off the single best timely gauge. From `data.ISM_PMI`, the ISM Manufacturing PMI ran: **41.5** in April 2020 (deep contraction, the COVID trough), surging to **60.5** by December 2020 and **63.7** in March 2021 (a roaring boom — one of the strongest manufacturing readings in decades), then cooling to **58.8** (Dec 2021), **53.0** (Jun 2022), and crossing **below 50** to **48.4** in December 2022, bottoming at **46.0** in June 2023, and hovering in contraction at **47.1** (Dec 2023), **48.5** (Jun 2024), **49.2** (Dec 2024), and **49.0** (Jun 2025).

Read the signal at each landmark:

- **63.7 (Mar 2021):** far above 50 — a manufacturing boom. Expansion phase, risk-on. But a cycle reader notes the *level is extreme and unsustainable* — readings above 60 rarely last, so the rate-of-change watch is "when does this roll over?"
- **53.0 (Jun 2022):** still above 50 (expanding) but **falling fast** from 63.7. This is the *decelerating expansion / approaching peak* signal — exactly the rate-of-change point: the level says "still expanding," the direction says "rolling over."
- **48.4 (Dec 2022) → 46.0 (Jun 2023):** the **cross of 50** and the contraction. Below 50 means manufacturing is shrinking. The 46.0 print signals a meaningful industrial contraction.
- **49.0-49.2 (2024-2025):** still below 50 but *grinding sideways near the line* — a shallow, drawn-out contraction in manufacturing even as the broader economy avoided an official recession.

A trader watching only the PMI would have rotated *out* of cyclicals as it fell through the high-50s in 2022 (decelerating expansion), gone defensive as it crossed below 50 in late 2022 (contraction), and watched the 49-area grind in 2024-25 for the eventual cross *back* above 50 that would flag the next expansion. **The cross of 50 is the cleanest single cycle trigger you can read in real time — and the boom-to-bust swing from 63.7 to 46.0 maps the phase change directly.**

#### Worked example: a recession's P&L — a $1,000,000 portfolio positioned right vs wrong

Money makes the phases concrete. Take two traders, each running a **\$1,000,000** portfolio going into a recession, and watch what reading the phase correctly is worth.

**Trader A reads the cycle wrong** — stays positioned for expansion into the contraction. The book: **\$700,000 in equities** (cyclicals and high-beta growth), **\$200,000 in high-yield credit**, **\$100,000 in commodities**. Now the recession hits. Suppose, using round but realistic recession-grade moves: equities fall **-30%**, high-yield credit falls **-12%**, commodities fall **-20%**.

- Equities: \$700,000 × (1 − 0.30) = **\$490,000** (a \$210,000 loss)
- High-yield: \$200,000 × (1 − 0.12) = **\$176,000** (a \$24,000 loss)
- Commodities: \$100,000 × (1 − 0.20) = **\$80,000** (a \$20,000 loss)
- **Total: \$746,000 — a \$254,000 loss, down -25.4%.**

**Trader B reads the cycle right** — sees the PMI cross below 50, the curve inverted, claims rising, and rotates to the contraction playbook: **\$300,000 in long-duration Treasuries**, **\$300,000 in cash / T-bills**, **\$200,000 in gold**, and only **\$200,000 in defensive equities** (staples, utilities). In the same recession, suppose: Treasuries gain **+12%** (yields fall as the Fed cuts), cash earns **+4%**, gold gains **+10%**, defensive equities fall a milder **-12%**.

- Treasuries: \$300,000 × 1.12 = **\$336,000** (a \$36,000 gain)
- Cash: \$300,000 × 1.04 = **\$312,000** (a \$12,000 gain)
- Gold: \$200,000 × 1.10 = **\$220,000** (a \$20,000 gain)
- Defensive equities: \$200,000 × (1 − 0.12) = **\$176,000** (a \$24,000 loss)
- **Total: \$1,044,000 — a \$44,000 gain, up +4.4%.**

The gap between the two books is **\$298,000** — nearly **30% of the starting capital** — and it came entirely from *reading the same recession differently and rotating the assets accordingly*. Both traders saw identical economic data; one was positioned for the phase they were leaving, the other for the phase they were entering. **Reading the cycle is not an academic exercise — it is the difference between a -25% drawdown and a small gain through the same downturn.**

#### Worked example: the curve as the bell — the 2s10s inversion leading the turn

Finally, time the warning. From `data.SPREAD_2S10S`, the 2s10s spread went: **+1.20%** (Jun 2021, a steep, healthy expansion curve), **+0.79%** (Dec 2021), **+0.19%** (Apr 2022, flattening fast), crossing to **-0.05%** in July 2022 (the inversion begins), deepening to **-0.55%** (Dec 2022), **-0.58%** (Mar 2023), and the deepest **-1.08%** in July 2023 — *the most inverted since 1981* — then climbing back to **-0.16%** (Oct 2023), and finally **un-inverting** to **+0.10%** (Nov 2024) and **+0.33%** (Dec 2024), reaching **+0.55%** by mid-2026.

Lay it against the PMI from the previous example and the lead time jumps out:

- The curve **inverted in July 2022** (2s10s at -0.05%). The PMI did not cross below 50 until **December 2022** — about five months later. The curve led the PMI.
- The curve hit its **deepest -1.08% in July 2023**, ringing the loudest recession warning in four decades — yet the contraction in the broad economy stayed shallow and an official recession was avoided through 2024, the classic **12-18 month frustrating lag** where "the curve was wrong" headlines proliferate.
- The **un-inversion in late 2024** (back to +0.33%) is the textbook *the-clock-is-nearly-up* signal — the curve re-steepens precisely because the Fed begins cutting into a weakening economy.

A trader using the curve as a bell would have *started* reducing late-cycle risk as the curve flattened toward zero in early-to-mid 2022 — well before the PMI confirmed in late 2022 and a year before the deepest inversion. **The yield curve is the earliest reliable bell in the kit: it inverted months before the PMI crossed 50 and over a year before any contraction, giving the cycle reader the longest possible lead time to reposition.**

## Common misconceptions

A few myths cause more bad cycle trades than anything else. Each one is corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "The cycle is regular and predictable in length."** It is not. The cycle *recurs*, but its length is wildly variable. US expansions since WWII have lasted from about **1 year** (the early-1980s expansion that was cut short) to **over 10 years** (the 2009-2020 expansion, the longest on record). Recessions have run from **2 months** (2020) to **18 months** (2007-09). There is no calendar that says "ten years, therefore a recession is due." Anyone telling you "we're overdue for a recession" because the expansion is old is committing the gambler's fallacy — expansions do not die of old age, they die of specific shocks and imbalances. *Read the indicators, not the calendar.*

**Myth 2: "GDP tells you what phase you're in, in time to act."** No — GDP is **coincident-to-lagging and reported with a delay**. The Q2 2020 GDP collapse was not published until late July 2020, *after the April trough*. The 2020 recession was not officially dated by the NBER until June 2020. By the time GDP confirms a phase, the market — a leading indicator — has usually already moved. The whole point of the leading-indicator dashboard (PMI, curve, claims, spreads) is to read the phase *before* GDP can.

**Myth 3: "A recession means the stock market crashes — so wait for the recession to sell."** Backwards. Markets **lead** the cycle, so the stock market typically **bottoms during the recession, while the news is at its worst**, because it is already pricing the recovery. The S&P 500 bottomed on March 23, 2020 — *before* the April economic trough and months before the data improved. Waiting for the recession to be confirmed before selling means selling near the bottom; the time to de-risk is *before* the recession, when the leading indicators flash, not after it is obvious.

**Myth 4: "Two negative GDP quarters means recession."** Not necessarily. In 2022 US GDP fell for two consecutive quarters and the "two-quarter rule" screamed recession — but the NBER never called one, because employment and real income kept rising. A recession is a *broad* decline across income, jobs, output, and sales, not a mechanical GDP rule. Trading the two-quarter heuristic in 2022 would have meant going maximally defensive into a market that kept climbing.

**Myth 5: "The PMI below 50 means the whole economy is in recession."** The ISM Manufacturing PMI measures *manufacturing*, which is only about 10-12% of US GDP. It can sit below 50 — as it did through much of 2023-2025, bottoming at 46.0 — while the much larger *services* economy keeps the overall economy growing. Watch the **services PMI** alongside the manufacturing one, and remember the manufacturing PMI is a *leading* gauge for the broad cycle, not a description of it. A manufacturing recession is not always an economy-wide recession.

## How it shows up in real markets

Two episodes show the cycle reader's edge — and its frustrations — in live markets.

### 2020: the cycle compressed into months

The 2020 cycle is the purest demonstration of every principle in this post, played at 10x speed. The economy entered 2020 at a clear **late-expansion** position — 3.5% unemployment, the longest expansion on record. Then the lockdown shock hit, and the cycle ran its entire loop in months: **peak in February, trough in April** per the NBER, GDP **-2.2%** for the year and **+5.9%** the next.

What did the indicators do, and in what order? The **stock market** (leading) bottomed on **March 23, 2020** — between the peak and the trough, while the data was still collapsing. The **ISM PMI** (leading) cratered to **41.5** in April, then began its V-shaped recovery, crossing back above 50 by mid-year and roaring to **60.5** by December — flashing the new expansion in real time. The **unemployment rate** (lagging) spiked to **14.8%** in April and stayed elevated for *months* after the recovery began — a textbook lagging indicator that would have kept a rear-view-mirror trader bearish through the entire rally. And the **NBER** (the official, lagging call) did not declare the recession until June 2020 — after it was over.

The trader's lesson from 2020: those who read the *leading* indicators and the market's own price action caught the March bottom and the explosive recovery; those who waited for the unemployment rate or the GDP print or the official NBER call to confirm the all-clear missed one of the fastest rallies in history. The cycle was the same for everyone; the *speed of reading it* separated the winners.

### 2022-2024: the soft-landing debate and the curve that "lied"

The next cycle was the opposite of 2020 — slow, drawn-out, and ambiguous — and it tortured cycle readers with the longest-running false-alarm in recent memory. The setup: the post-COVID boom overheated, inflation hit a 40-year high, and the Fed hiked aggressively. The leading indicators duly flashed:

- The **2s10s inverted in July 2022** and reached **-1.08% by July 2023** — the deepest since 1981, the loudest recession bell in four decades.
- The **ISM PMI crossed below 50 in December 2022** and bottomed at **46.0** in mid-2023 — a manufacturing contraction.

By every historical script, a recession should have followed within 12-18 months. And yet — through 2023 and 2024 the broad economy kept growing (**+2.9%** in 2023, **+2.8%** in 2024), the unemployment rate stayed near historic lows, and the services economy carried the day. The "soft landing" — slowing inflation without a recession — went from a long-shot to the base case. Pundits declared the yield curve "broken" and the PMI "no longer reliable."

The cycle reader's correct interpretation was more nuanced than either "recession imminent" or "indicators broken." The leading indicators were flashing a genuine *slowdown* and a real *manufacturing contraction* — both of which happened. What they over-anticipated was a *broad* NBER recession, which the unusually strong services economy and a still-loose fiscal stance held off. The **un-inversion in late 2024** then put the textbook "clock-is-nearly-up" signal squarely in front of the market. The episode teaches the cycle reader humility: the indicators map the *direction and risk* of the cycle with real lead time, but the *magnitude and exact timing* of the turn depend on second-order factors — fiscal policy, the services/manufacturing split, the strength of the labor market — that a single indicator cannot capture. You read the cycle as a *probability-weighted map*, not a deterministic prophecy.

## How to trade it: the playbook

Everything above resolves into a repeatable process. The goal is not to predict the cycle from nothing — it is to *read your current position* off the leading indicators and *position for the next phase* before it arrives.

![The cycle-reading playbook matrix: three dials across the four phases with the position to hold](/imgs/blogs/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders-7.png)

Figure 7 is the playbook in one grid: the three dials (PMI, labor/claims, yield curve) read down each phase column, and the position to hold in the bottom row. Work it top to bottom.

**Step 1 — Read the three dials and place yourself on the clock.**

- **PMI dial:** Is the ISM above or below 50, and rising or falling? Above-and-rising → expansion; above-and-falling → approaching peak; below-and-falling → contraction; below-and-rising → trough/recovery. The *cross of 50* and the *change in direction* are the triggers.
- **Labor / claims:** Are initial jobless claims low and stable (healthy) or rising (cracking)? Is payroll growth broad or narrowing to a few sectors? Labor *confirms* the PMI's lead.
- **Yield curve:** Steep (early cycle), flattening/inverting (approaching peak), deeply inverted (late cycle, recession warning), or re-steepening as the Fed cuts (recession/recovery)? The inversion is the bell; the un-inversion is the alarm.

**Step 2 — Require confluence before you act.** No single dial is enough. A PMI dip alone might be mid-cycle noise. A PMI cross below 50 *plus* an inverted-and-un-inverting curve *plus* rising claims *plus* widening credit spreads is a coherent late-cycle-into-contraction read. Demand that two or three independent leading indicators agree before you make a big rotation. Confluence turns noise into signal.

**Step 3 — Position for the *next* phase, not the current one.** This is the whole edge. Markets price the next phase, so you must too:

- **In expansion (PMI > 50 and rising, curve steep, claims low):** carry maximum risk — equities, cyclicals, high-yield credit, industrial commodities. But *watch for the peak*: when the PMI rolls over from a high level and the curve starts flattening, begin trimming.
- **At the peak (PMI high but falling, inflation hot, Fed tight, curve flattening/inverting):** rotate to quality and value, energy and materials, and raise cash. You are taking chips off the table *while the data still looks good*, because the leading indicators are flashing the turn.
- **In contraction (PMI < 50, curve inverted/un-inverting, claims surging, spreads wide):** go defensive — long-duration Treasuries (they rally as the Fed cuts), cash/T-bills, gold, USD, and defensive equity sectors. Cut gross risk. This is the haven phase.
- **At the trough (PMI bottoming and turning up, Fed cutting, curve re-steepening, claims rolling over):** *add* risk into the bad news — small caps, cyclicals, credit. This is the contrarian entry, buying the recovery the leading indicators are starting to flash while the coincident data is still ugly.

**Step 4 — Know your invalidation.** A cycle read can be wrong, and the discipline is to define *what would prove it wrong* before you size up. If you are positioned for contraction because the PMI crossed below 50 and the curve inverted, your invalidation is the PMI *crossing back above 50 and rising* alongside a *re-steepening curve and falling claims* — the trough/recovery signal. When the dials flip, flip the book; do not marry the recession thesis the way the 2022-24 perma-bears did when the soft landing arrived. The 2022-24 episode is the cautionary tale: the curve and PMI flagged the *direction* correctly (a slowdown and a manufacturing contraction) but over-anticipated the *magnitude* (a broad recession), and a trader who refused to update when services-economy strength kept growth positive paid for it. **Read the cycle as a living probability map, update on every print, and let the position follow the phase — not the other way around.**

**Step 5 — Size the conviction to the confluence.** When all the dials agree (PMI below 50 and falling, curve un-inverting, claims rising, spreads blowing wider), you have a high-conviction contraction read and can rotate the whole book decisively. When the dials disagree (PMI below 50 but claims still falling and the curve re-steepening — a mixed late-cycle/recovery picture), size smaller and wait for confirmation. The amount of risk you take should scale with how many independent leading indicators are pointing the same way.

That is the entire discipline: **read the phase off the PMI, the yield curve, and the labor market; require confluence; position for the next phase, not the current one; and define your invalidation in advance.** Get the phase right and the asset selection mostly follows — which is exactly why the business cycle is the top-down foundation that every other macro decision hangs on.
