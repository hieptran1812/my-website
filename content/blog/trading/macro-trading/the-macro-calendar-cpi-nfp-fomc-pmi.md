> **TL;DR** — Macro trading runs on a recurring calendar of scheduled data releases. CPI, the jobs report (NFP), the FOMC meeting, and the PMIs each land on a known date and act as a volatility event. The market moves on the **surprise** — the actual number minus the consensus forecast — not on the level of the number itself.
>
> - The level is already in the price. Weeks before a release, every desk has positioned for the consensus forecast, so a print that matches consensus moves almost nothing. Only the deviation from consensus is new information, and only new information moves price.
> - The releases rank by impact. **CPI is the #1 print**, the FOMC and the jobs report are close behind, the Fed's preferred PCE gauge and the ISM/PMI surveys sit a tier lower, and GDP, retail sales, and sentiment fill out the rest. Know which print is a market event and which is a footnote.
> - The reaction is cross-asset and fast. A single hot CPI number repriced bonds, stocks, the dollar, and gold within seconds, and the first move frequently reverses — the knee-jerk is often the wrong move.
> - The one number to remember: CPI peaked at **9.06% in June 2022**, and those mid-month inflation prints were the single biggest scheduled volatility days of the 2022-23 cycle.

At 8:30 in the morning, two minutes before a US inflation report, the screens go quiet in the way they only do before a number. Order books thin out. Spreads widen. Then the data crosses the wires: the consumer price index came in at 3.5% year-over-year, against a consensus forecast of 3.2%. Three-tenths of a percentage point hotter than expected. It does not sound like much. But in the next ninety seconds the two-year Treasury yield jumps fifteen basis points, the stock index futures drop more than one percent, the dollar rips higher against every major currency, and gold sells off. A single data point, three-tenths above a forecast, just moved trillions of dollars of assets at once.

What happened? Nothing about the *number* itself is dramatic — 3.5% inflation is uncomfortable but unremarkable. The drama is entirely in the gap. The market had already priced a world where inflation came in at 3.2%. Bonds, stocks, the dollar, and rate-cut bets were all positioned for that. When the actual print landed three-tenths higher, that gap was new information, and the whole market had to reprice the path of the Fed at once. The number did not move the market. The *surprise* moved the market.

This is the single most important idea in macro trading, and almost every beginner gets it backwards. They watch the calendar for the number, as if a "good" number is bullish and a "bad" number is bearish. But the seasoned desk does not trade the number — it trades the number *relative to what was already expected*. And those expectations arrive on a fixed schedule: a recurring monthly calendar of releases, each one a known volatility event you can prepare for in advance. Learn that calendar — what lands when, what each release means, and how to size the surprise against consensus — and the macro month stops being noise and starts being rhythm. We build every piece from zero.

## Foundations: the calendar, consensus, and the surprise

Before any trade, you need a clear picture of three things: what the economic data calendar *is*, what "consensus" means, and why the gap between the actual print and the consensus — the surprise — is the only part that moves price. We go slowly, defining every term, because the whole logic of event trading hangs on these.

### What the economic data calendar is

The **economic data calendar** (also called the economic calendar or the data calendar) is the published schedule of government and private-sector data releases that describe the state of the economy. Each release is a report — on inflation, on jobs, on growth, on manufacturing activity — produced on a fixed, pre-announced timetable. The US Bureau of Labor Statistics, the Bureau of Economic Analysis, the Census Bureau, the Federal Reserve, and private survey firms like the Institute for Supply Management all publish on dates set months or years in advance.

The key word is *scheduled*. Unlike a geopolitical shock or a surprise central-bank announcement, you know exactly when these reports land — usually down to the minute. The US jobs report comes out at 8:30am Eastern on the first Friday of the month. CPI comes out at 8:30am on a pre-set mid-month date. The FOMC announces its rate decision at 2:00pm on the second day of its meeting. That predictability is the entire reason these reports are tradeable as events: because the market knows when the information is coming, it spends the days beforehand forming a forecast and positioning for it. The release is the moment that forecast is tested against reality.

A trader's first job each week is simply to know what is on the calendar. You pull up the week's releases, you mark the ones that matter, and you clear your screen of surprises about *when* the data lands. There is no excuse for being caught off guard by a CPI print — the date was published months ago.

It helps to understand *how* a release physically reaches the market, because the mechanics shape the trade. At a fixed time — almost always 8:30am Eastern for the big government reports — the statistical agency releases the number simultaneously to the public and to the news wires. Approved news organizations get a brief "lock-up" period beforehand, where reporters receive the data in a secure room to prepare their stories, but cannot transmit anything until the embargo lifts at 8:30:00 exactly. At that instant the number hits every screen at once, and automated trading systems parse the headline figure in milliseconds and fire orders. This is why the first move on a big surprise is so fast and so violent: machines reading the headline number react before a human has finished reading the first line. The implication for a discretionary trader is sober — you are not going to beat the algorithms to the headline reaction. Your edge is in the *positioning beforehand* and in the *second move*, after the headline spike, when the details and the revisions get digested.

Revisions are the other mechanic that trips beginners. Most economic data is revised after its first release as more complete information arrives. The jobs report is notorious: the headline payrolls number for a given month is revised twice in the following two reports, and those revisions can be large — a +180,000 first print can later be revised down to +120,000, or a string of months can be revised down by hundreds of thousands in aggregate. A revision is itself a surprise that moves markets: a big downward revision to prior months, released alongside the current month's number, can flip the read of a report even if the headline looks fine. GDP gets two scheduled revisions after its first estimate; CPI is rarely revised meaningfully, which is part of why it is so clean to trade. The lesson: read the *whole* release, not just the headline, because the revision to last month is sometimes the real news.

### What consensus and expectations are

Every scheduled release comes with a **consensus** — the market's collective forecast of what the number will be. Consensus is built by polling economists at banks and research firms: each submits an estimate, and the median of those estimates becomes "the consensus forecast" you see on the calendar. If you read that "CPI is expected at 3.2%," that 3.2% is the consensus.

Consensus matters because it is, in effect, *the number that is already in the price*. When economists forecast 3.2% inflation, traders position as if 3.2% is true. They buy or sell bonds, stocks, and currencies in line with a 3.2% world. By the time the report is released, the consensus outcome has already been absorbed into market prices through weeks of positioning. The consensus is not just a guess — it is the bar the actual number has to clear or miss to matter.

There is a subtlety worth flagging early: there is the *published* consensus (the median of the economist poll) and there is the **"whisper number"** or market-implied expectation, which is where traders actually think the number will land. These can differ. Sometimes the published consensus is 3.2% but the market has quietly leaned toward 3.4% in the days before — so a 3.4% print, which looks like a miss against the published consensus, is actually no surprise at all to positioning, and the market barely moves. This is why a print can come in "above consensus" and yet the market rallies: the published consensus was not what was truly priced. We come back to this, because it is the most common way a beginner gets the direction wrong.

### What the surprise is, and why it is the trade

The **surprise** is the actual released number minus the consensus forecast. If consensus is 3.2% and the print is 3.5%, the surprise is +0.3% — a hot, or upside, surprise. If the print is 3.0%, the surprise is −0.2% — a cool, or downside, surprise. The surprise is the new information the release delivers, because the consensus part was already known and priced.

Here is why only the surprise moves price, stated precisely, because this is the mechanism the whole post rests on. The price of any asset already embeds the market's best forecast of the future. A bond's yield reflects the expected path of inflation and short-term rates; a stock's price reflects expected earnings discounted at expected rates. When a forecastable release arrives *exactly as forecast*, no new information has entered the world — the forecast was already in the price — so there is nothing for price to do. Price only moves when reality turns out *different* from the forecast, because only then does the embedded forecast need correcting. The surprise is that difference.

![Surprise versus consensus showing only the deviation from the forecast moves price](/imgs/blogs/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi-3.png)

So the trade is never "inflation is high, sell bonds." The trade is "inflation came in *higher than the market had priced*, so the market must now reprice toward a higher-for-longer Fed, sell bonds." The level tells you the state of the world; the surprise tells you how the state of the world differs from what was already in prices. A macro desk lives on the second one.

### The economic surprise index

Because the surprise is what matters, there is a tool that aggregates it: the **economic surprise index**. The best-known is the Citi Economic Surprise Index (CESI), but the concept is general. It measures, across all the recent releases, whether the data is coming in *above* or *below* consensus on balance. When the index is high and rising, data is consistently beating expectations — the economy is running hotter than the market had priced, which tends to push yields up and support cyclical assets. When the index is low and falling, data is consistently missing — the economy is cooling relative to expectations, which tends to pull yields down.

The surprise index is useful precisely because it strips out the level and keeps only the deviation. An economy can be growing at a healthy 2.5% and still produce a string of *negative* surprises if the market had priced 3% — and that string of negative surprises is what drives yields lower, even though "2.5% growth" sounds fine. The index reminds you, at a glance, that markets trade the gap, not the absolute.

### Why markets trade the surprise, not the number

It is worth holding the central idea in one clean sentence, because everything below is an application of it: **the release does not inject the number into the market — the market injected the expected number into prices in advance, and the release injects only the error in that expectation.** Your job as a trader is to estimate that error — the surprise — before and as it lands, because the surprise is the only thing that pays.

A second consequence follows, and it explains why these reactions are so violent. Because everyone forms a view from the same consensus, positioning going into a release is often *lopsided* — the whole market leaning one way. When the surprise comes the other way, all those positions have to be unwound at once, and a forced unwind moves price far more than the information alone would justify. A modest hot CPI surprise into a market positioned for a cooling trend does not produce a modest sell-off in bonds; it produces a stampede. This is why data days overshoot, why their ranges are multiples of a normal day, and why *positioning into the print* matters as much as the print itself.

## The marquee releases, ranked by market impact

Not every line on the calendar is a market event. Some reports move trillions; others are footnotes that professional desks barely glance at. A trader has to know the ranking cold, because trading a second-tier release as if it were a first-tier one — or sleeping through a first-tier one — is how you lose money or miss the move. Here is the ranking, top to bottom, by the size of the cross-asset reaction each typically produces.

![Release importance ranking from CPI to FOMC to jobs to PMI to the rest](/imgs/blogs/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi-5.png)

### CPI — the #1 print

The **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** is the single most market-moving scheduled release on the calendar, and has been since inflation became the dominant macro story in 2021. CPI measures the change in the price of a basket of consumer goods and services — what households actually pay for groceries, rent, gasoline, healthcare, and the rest. It is reported as a monthly change and, more importantly for markets, as a **year-over-year** rate: the price level today versus twelve months ago. The headline CPI includes everything; **core CPI** strips out food and energy, which are volatile, to show the underlying trend.

CPI is the top of the ranking because inflation is what the Fed is fighting, and the Fed's rate path is what prices every asset. A hot CPI print says the Fed must stay tight for longer — fewer or later rate cuts — which pushes up yields, pressures stocks (especially long-duration growth stocks, whose value sits in far-off cash flows), strengthens the dollar, and often hits gold. A cool CPI print says the opposite. CPI is released around the middle of each month at 8:30am Eastern, and it is, reliably, the biggest scheduled volatility window of the month.

There are several numbers inside the one CPI report, and a trader has to know which one drives the reaction. There is the **headline year-over-year** rate (the full basket versus twelve months ago), the **core year-over-year** rate (the same, excluding food and energy), and the **month-over-month** changes for both. Markets often react most to the *core month-over-month* number, because it is the freshest read on the underlying trend without the noise of energy prices and base effects. A report can show a cooling headline year-over-year rate — which looks like good news — while the core month-over-month comes in hot, telling the Fed that underlying inflation is still sticky. In that situation yields can *rise* on a report whose headline fell, because the part the Fed cares about surprised to the upside. This is why "CPI came in at 3.5%" is never the whole story: you have to know which of the four numbers surprised, and in which direction, relative to its own consensus.

Within core inflation, traders further split **goods** from **services**, and within services they watch **shelter** (housing costs, the largest single component) and a category nicknamed **supercore** — core services excluding shelter — which the Fed has flagged as the stickiest, most labor-sensitive piece of inflation. When the Fed says it is watching whether inflation is "entrenched," it is largely watching supercore. A trader who can read past the headline to the supercore trend is reading the report the way the Fed reads it, which is the report that sets rates.

![CPI year-over-year 2020 to 2026 with the 9.06 percent peak marked](/imgs/blogs/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi-2.png)

The chart above is the CPI story of the era, with each dot a scheduled monthly release. Inflation sat below the Fed's 2% target through 2020, climbed relentlessly through 2021, and peaked at **9.06% in June 2022** — a forty-year high. Each of those mid-2022 prints was a market earthquake. Then disinflation pulled it back toward 3%, and the 2025-26 re-acceleration back above 4% shows the story is never finished. Every one of those dots was a date a trader knew in advance and a volatility event they had to be positioned for.

### NFP — the jobs report

The **jobs report**, officially the Employment Situation report and known to traders by its headline line, **nonfarm payrolls (NFP)**, is released at 8:30am Eastern on the first Friday of each month. It is produced by the Bureau of Labor Statistics and contains two headline numbers traders watch: the change in nonfarm payrolls (how many jobs the economy added or lost last month, "nonfarm" meaning it excludes farm work) and the **unemployment rate** (the share of the labor force without a job and looking). It also reports **average hourly earnings**, a wage-growth gauge that feeds directly into inflation worries.

The jobs report ranks just below CPI and the FOMC because employment is the other half of the Fed's mandate (the Fed targets both stable prices and maximum employment) and because the labor market drives consumer spending, which drives the economy. A blowout payrolls number — far more jobs than expected — says the economy is hot and the Fed can stay tight, which pushes up front-end yields and the dollar. A weak number says cooling, which pulls yields down and can stoke rate-cut bets. Wage growth matters because rising wages can sustain inflation. The jobs report is famous for its *whipsaw*: the headline payrolls number, the unemployment rate, and the wage number can disagree, so the first reaction often reverses as traders digest the full report.

There is a structural reason the jobs report whipsaws, and it pays to understand it. The report is built from *two separate surveys*. The payrolls number comes from the **establishment survey** — a poll of businesses asking how many people are on their payroll. The unemployment rate comes from the **household survey** — a poll of households asking who is employed and who is looking for work. These two surveys measure overlapping but different things, with different sample sizes and different noise, so they routinely disagree. You can get a strong establishment number (jobs added) alongside a weak household number (rising unemployment) in the same report, and the market lurches one way on the headline payrolls, then reverses when the unemployment rate from the other survey tells a different story. A trader who knows the report is two surveys stapled together expects the disagreement and is less likely to chase the first move.

One household-survey number carries outsized weight: the unemployment rate, because of a recession signal called the **Sahm rule**. The rule observes that historically, once the three-month-average unemployment rate rises half a percentage point above its low of the prior year, a recession has already begun. So when the unemployment rate ticks up even a tenth, the market checks it against the Sahm threshold, and a print that crosses or approaches it can trigger a sharp risk-off move — bonds rally, stocks fall, rate-cut bets surge — far beyond what a one-tenth change would otherwise justify. This is a case where a tiny surprise in a single number has a coiled, rule-based reaction waiting behind it.

![US unemployment rate 2020 to 2026 from the COVID spike to the slow drift up](/imgs/blogs/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi-6.png)

The unemployment rate, charted above, ships inside that same 8:30am jobs report. It spiked to **14.8% in April 2020** — the jobs report that broke every chart — then fell back to a multi-decade-low 3.4% by early 2023 as the economy reopened, and has drifted slowly up toward 4.3% by 2026. In a soft-landing watch, every one-tenth move in that rate is a tradeable surprise, because the Fed and the market are scrutinizing whether the labor market is cracking or merely normalizing.

### The FOMC — the Fed

The **FOMC** (Federal Open Market Committee) meeting is where the Fed sets its main interest rate. It meets eight times a year on a fixed calendar, announcing its rate decision at 2:00pm Eastern, followed by the chair's press conference at 2:30pm. It ranks at the very top alongside CPI for impact, but it is a different *kind* of event: the rate decision itself is usually fully priced in advance (telegraphed for weeks precisely so it does not shock), so the surprise comes from the *guidance* — the wording of the statement, the dot plot of projected future rates, and the chair's tone at the podium. Because the FOMC is its own deep topic — the statement diff, the dots, the presser whipsaw — we cover it in full in [Trading the FOMC: the statement, the dot plot, and the press conference](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-fomc-statement-presser-dot-plot), and we lean on that here rather than re-deriving it. For the calendar, the thing to know is: the FOMC is a scheduled volatility event whose surprise lives in the guidance, not the rate number.

### The PMIs — the leading regime gauge

The **PMIs** (Purchasing Managers' Indexes) are monthly surveys of business managers. The most-watched in the US is the **ISM Manufacturing PMI** from the Institute for Supply Management, released on the first business day of the month, with the ISM Services PMI a couple of days later. A PMI asks purchasing managers whether activity — new orders, production, employment, deliveries — is rising or falling. The index is constructed so that **50 is the dividing line**: above 50 means the sector is expanding, below 50 means it is contracting. A reading of 55 is solid growth; 45 is a meaningful contraction.

PMIs rank a tier below CPI, the FOMC, and jobs in raw market impact, but they have a special property: they are **leading** indicators. Because they survey managers about current conditions, they turn before the hard data (GDP, payrolls) confirms a shift. The 50-line cross is a scheduled regime signal — when the PMI crosses below 50, it flags that the factory cycle has tipped from expansion to contraction, often months before a recession shows up in growth figures. Traders watch PMIs not for a single explosive reaction but as an early read on where the cycle is heading, which feeds directly into the business-cycle framework covered in [The business cycle: four phases for traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders).

![ISM Manufacturing PMI versus the 50 line as a scheduled regime gauge](/imgs/blogs/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi-4.png)

The chart shows the ISM Manufacturing PMI against that 50 line. It ran hot above 50 through the 2021 post-COVID boom, peaking near 64, then ground steadily lower and **crossed below 50 in late 2022** — flagging the factory sector slipping into contraction while the broader economy was still expanding. That cross is exactly the kind of scheduled regime flag a trader uses to lean cautious on cyclical assets well before the lagging data confirms it.

### PCE, GDP, retail sales, and the rest

Below the marquee names sit the rest of the calendar, useful but lower-octane:

- **PCE / Core PCE** — the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index is the Fed's *preferred* inflation gauge (it weights the basket differently from CPI and is broader). Core PCE strips food and energy. It ranks below CPI for one structural reason: it is released *later* in the month, by which point its inputs are already partly known from CPI and the producer price index, so the surprise is smaller. It matters because it is what the Fed actually targets (the 2% goal is a PCE goal), but as a trading event it is a confirmer, not a shock. We cover how the Fed reads inflation in [Inflation and the Fed's reaction function](/blog/trading/macro-trading/inflation-and-the-fed-reaction-function-dot-plot).
- **GDP** — Gross Domestic Product, the broadest measure of the economy, released quarterly and revised twice. It is a *lagging* and backward-looking figure (the first estimate covers a quarter that already ended), so it moves markets less than the timely monthly data, though a big miss or beat in the first estimate can still jolt.
- **Retail sales** — a monthly read on consumer spending, a second-tier but genuinely watched print, because the consumer is most of the US economy.
- **Jobless claims** — a weekly count of new unemployment filings; high-frequency and useful as a real-time labor pulse, but each individual print is small.
- **Sentiment surveys** (University of Michigan, Conference Board) — soft data on how households feel; situational, occasionally important when an inflation-expectations component spikes.

The skill is triage. On a week with CPI and the jobs report, the retail sales number is a footnote you note and move past. On a quiet week, retail sales might be the biggest scheduled event and worth real attention. The calendar is relative, and reading it well means knowing which print is the event *this* week.

## What each release tells you, and what it moves

Knowing the ranking is half the job; the other half is knowing the *content* — what each release tells you about the economy and which assets it moves. This is the translation layer between a data point and a position.

**CPI tells you about inflation, and it moves rates first.** A hot CPI surprise pushes the market to expect a tighter Fed: it sells off the front end of the yield curve (the two-year Treasury, the most policy-sensitive maturity), strengthens the dollar (higher rates attract capital), pressures stocks (higher discount rates lower the present value of future earnings, hitting long-duration growth names hardest), and often weighs on gold (which competes with interest-bearing assets). A cool CPI surprise does the reverse across the board. The cleanest single instrument to watch is the two-year yield: it is the market's read on the average policy rate over two years, and CPI repriced it instantly through 2022-23.

**The jobs report tells you about labor-market strength, and it moves the front end and the dollar.** A hot payrolls or wage surprise says the economy can take more tightening, lifting front-end yields and the dollar. A cool surprise pulls them down and can fuel rate-cut bets, lifting stocks and bonds together. The unemployment rate carries special weight because a rising rate, even by a tenth, can trigger recession-rule alarms that move markets sharply.

**The FOMC tells you the Fed's intended path, and it moves everything.** Because the decision is priced, the surprise is in the guidance, and a hawkish or dovish shift in the dots or the press conference repriced the entire rate path at once — front end, long end, dollar, stocks, gold. The two-year yield is again where the surprise lands hardest.

**PMIs tell you the cycle's direction, and they move cyclical assets.** A PMI surprise — and especially a 50-line cross — shifts the market's read on whether the economy is accelerating or decelerating, which moves cyclical equities (industrials, energy, materials), commodities, and the curve's shape. PMIs are a regime read more than a single-print shock.

**PCE confirms the inflation trend; GDP confirms the growth regime; retail sales reads the consumer.** These are the confirmers — they tell you whether the picture the leading data painted is holding, and they move markets most when they *diverge* from what the timely data already showed.

There is a deeper point that separates good calendar traders from beginners: the market's *focus* rotates across the calendar depending on which mandate is in play. When inflation is the dominant worry — as it was in 2021-23 — CPI is everything and the jobs report matters mostly for its wage component. The market is watching the inflation half of the Fed's mandate, so inflation surprises move the most. But when the worry shifts to growth and recession — as it did when the unemployment rate began drifting up in 2024-25 — the jobs report takes over as the marquee print, because now the market is watching the *employment* half of the mandate, scrutinizing every tenth of the unemployment rate for signs the labor market is cracking. The same release can be the most important number of the month in one regime and a secondary print in another. Knowing *which mandate the market is trading* tells you which release will produce the biggest surprise reaction, and it is the single most useful piece of context to carry into any data day. A hot CPI in a growth-scare regime moves less than the same CPI in an inflation-scare regime, because the market's attention is elsewhere.

This rotation also explains why the same surprise produces different cross-asset reactions at different times. Early in a hiking cycle, a hot inflation print sells off both stocks and bonds together (the "everything-down" days of 2022), because higher rates hurt both. Late in a cycle, a hot inflation print might sell off bonds but barely move stocks, if the market has decided the Fed is nearly done and a little more inflation does not change the endgame. The *instrument that reacts most* to a given surprise depends on where the cycle is, which is why a trader maps not just the calendar but the regime the calendar is landing into.

## The surprise, not the level, is the trade

We have said it; now we prove it with numbers, because this is where beginners lose money and where the whole post earns its keep. The recurring error is to trade the *level* of a number — "inflation is high, that's bad, sell" — when the market trades the *surprise*. A few worked examples make the mechanism concrete.

#### Worked example: a hot CPI surprise and the cross-asset P&L

Consensus for the year-over-year CPI is **3.2%**. The print lands at **3.5%** — a +0.3% upside surprise. Walk through what happens to a trader's positions.

Suppose you went into the print holding a position that profits if the Fed cuts rates sooner: you are long ten-year Treasury futures (you own duration, you profit when yields fall) with a position sized so that a 10-basis-point move in the ten-year yield is worth \$50,000 to you. You hold a notional position where each basis point of yield is worth \$5,000.

The hot CPI surprise repriced the Fed path: rate cuts get pushed further out, and yields jump. Say the ten-year yield rises **18 basis points** on the print. Your position loses 18 × \$5,000 = **\$90,000** in the first minutes. Meanwhile the two-year yield, more policy-sensitive, rises even more — perhaps 22 basis points. If you had instead been positioned for a cool print in stock index futures, long the S&P at \$10,000 per index point with the index down 1.2% (say 58 points on a 4,850 index), that leg loses another 58 × \$10,000... but you held bonds, so focus there. The point lands hard: a *three-tenths* surprise in a single inflation print cost you \$90,000, not because 3.5% inflation is catastrophic, but because the market had to reprice the entire Fed path off that gap. The surprise, not the level, is the trade.

#### Worked example: an NFP beat and the rate reaction

The consensus for nonfarm payrolls is **+180,000** jobs. The print lands at **+330,000** — a blowout, +150,000 above consensus. The unemployment rate, expected to hold at 3.9%, instead *falls* to 3.7%, and average hourly earnings come in 0.1% hotter than expected. Every component points the same way: the labor market is hotter than priced.

The market reaction: front-end yields spike, because a hot labor market lets the Fed stay tight. Say the two-year yield jumps **14 basis points** immediately. If you were short two-year futures going into the print — positioned for rising yields — and each basis point is worth \$4,000 to your position, you make 14 × \$4,000 = **\$56,000** on the move. The dollar rallies, stock futures dip on the higher-rate read, and rate-cut bets in fed funds futures get trimmed. Notice that "good news for the economy" (more jobs) was *bad* news for stocks and bonds, because it meant a tighter Fed. A beginner who thought "strong jobs, that's bullish, buy stocks" would have been run over. The reaction keys off the surprise relative to consensus and what it implies for the Fed, not off whether the news is "good."

#### Worked example: pricing a \$10,000,000 position into a CPI print — the consensus gap

You run a \$10,000,000 macro book and you have a view going into a CPI release. Consensus is **3.2%**. Your own model and the channel checks you trust say the number is more likely to print **3.4%** — you think the market is too sanguine. How do you size the trade?

First, the surprise you expect is +0.2% (your 3.4% versus the 3.2% consensus). But the *whisper* — where the market is actually leaning — may already sit near 3.3%, so the true surprise the market would react to is closer to +0.1%, not +0.2%. This is the subtlety from the foundations: you trade against what is *priced*, not against the published consensus.

Suppose you decide to express the view by shorting two-year Treasury futures, betting yields rise on a hotter print. You want to risk no more than **1%** of the \$10,000,000 book — \$100,000 — on this single event, because data-day ranges are violent and you can be wrong. You estimate that if you are wrong (a cool print, yields *fall* 12 basis points against you), you lose. If each basis point is worth \$8,000 to the position you are contemplating, a 12-basis-point adverse move costs 12 × \$8,000 = \$96,000 — just inside your \$100,000 risk budget. So you size to roughly that position and no larger. If your print is right and yields rise 15 basis points, you make 15 × \$8,000 = **\$120,000**. The discipline: you sized the position off *the risk if you are wrong* and *the surprise the market has not priced*, not off the headline number. Pricing a position into a print means sizing the surprise against what is priced and capping the loss if the surprise goes the other way.

#### Worked example: a PMI cross of 50 as a scheduled regime signal

The ISM Manufacturing PMI has been drifting down: 52.0, then 50.5, and the next print lands at **48.4** — a cross *below* the 50 line. Consensus was 49.8, so it is also a downside surprise of 1.4 points, but the bigger signal is the regime flag: the factory sector has tipped from expansion to contraction.

You treat this not as a single-day scalp but as a regime input. You hold a cyclical-equity position — \$200,000 in an industrials basket — that does well when the factory cycle is expanding. The sub-50 cross tells you the cycle has turned, so you trim. You cut the position in half, taking \$100,000 off the table, and you rotate some of it into defensives or duration, which tend to outperform as the cycle slows. If the cycle-slowdown read is right and the industrials basket falls 8% over the following weeks while it would have cost you 8% of \$200,000 = \$16,000 of drawdown, trimming to \$100,000 halves that to \$8,000 of exposure on the falling leg, and the rotated duration leg gains as yields ease. The PMI did not give you a single-print P&L; it gave you a scheduled, calendar-known regime signal you used to reposition the whole book. A 50-line cross is the calendar handing you a regime change on a known date.

#### Worked example: a "good" CPI headline that sells off on the core surprise

This is the one that catches everyone, so it is worth its own walkthrough. The CPI report lands and the *headline* year-over-year rate falls from 3.2% to **3.0%** — a cooler headline than last month, and it even matches the consensus of 3.0%. A casual reader sees "inflation fell, matched expectations" and assumes stocks should rally and yields should fall. They position long stocks: \$300,000 in an S&P index fund going into the number, expecting a relief rally.

But inside the report, the *core month-over-month* number printed **0.4%**, against a consensus of 0.3% — a hot core surprise. And the supercore (core services ex-shelter) accelerated. The Fed cares about that core trend, not the headline that fell partly on cheaper gasoline. So the market reprices toward a stickier-inflation, higher-for-longer Fed: the two-year yield rises 9 basis points, and the S&P *falls* 0.7% rather than rallying. Your \$300,000 long loses 0.7% × \$300,000 = **\$2,100** on a report whose headline number fell and matched consensus. The trader who read only the headline got the direction exactly wrong, because the surprise that mattered was in the core, not the headline. A falling headline can sell off when the part the Fed watches surprised hot.

These five examples share one spine: in every case the trade keyed off the *surprise versus what was priced* and the *implication for the Fed or the cycle*, never off the raw number being "good" or "bad."

## Common misconceptions

A handful of myths cause most of the avoidable losses around the calendar. Each one is corrected by a number.

**Myth 1: "A good number is bullish, a bad number is bearish."** This is the deepest error, and it is wrong because the market trades the surprise, not the goodness. A strong jobs report (good news) can crush stocks if it means the Fed stays tight — in 2022-23 hot data repeatedly sold off equities, because "good for the economy" meant "bad for rate cuts." A weak number can rally stocks if it fuels rate-cut hopes. The +330,000 payrolls beat in the worked example was "good news" that sent yields up 14 basis points and stocks down. Direction is set by the surprise and its policy implication, not by whether the news sounds good.

**Myth 2: "The calendar is just noise — the data is random, so why trade it?"** The individual prints are noisy, true, but the *reactions* are structured and the events are scheduled, which is exactly what makes them tradeable. CPI in 2022 was not noise: it was the single most important scheduled event of each month, with the June 2022 print at **9.06%** marking the inflation peak that defined the whole rate cycle. Dismissing the calendar as noise is how you get blindsided by the one print that resets the entire macro regime.

**Myth 3: "You can predict the print if you do enough homework."** You can sharpen your estimate of the surprise, but you cannot reliably predict the exact number — and you do not need to. The edge is not in forecasting CPI to the decimal; it is in knowing *what is priced*, sizing the surprise against it, and managing the reaction. Desks that blow up on data days usually do so by betting big on a *predicted* number, not by trading the *gap* with controlled risk. The unemployment rate drifting from 3.4% to 4.3% over 2023-26 was a slow, surprising grind that no one called precisely; the money was in reading each tenth's surprise, not in predicting the path.

**Myth 4: "The first move is the right move — trade it instantly."** The first reaction to a release is frequently the *wrong* move, especially on the jobs report and the FOMC, where the headline and the details disagree. A payrolls number can pop the market on the headline, then reverse when the unemployment rate or the revisions tell a different story. Chasing the first tick into a reversal is a classic way to get stopped out at the worst price. Data-day ranges run two to three times a normal day precisely because of these reversals.

**Myth 5: "PCE is more important than CPI because the Fed targets PCE."** The Fed *does* target PCE (the 2% goal is a PCE goal), but as a *trading* event CPI dominates, because CPI comes out earlier and PCE's inputs are already largely known by the time it lands. The bigger surprise — and therefore the bigger market move — is almost always on CPI day. Targeting and trading are different questions.

## How it shows up in real markets

The cleanest demonstration that the calendar runs the macro tape is the 2022-23 inflation cycle, when CPI days became the single biggest scheduled volatility events of the era.

Through 2022, as CPI climbed toward and past its **9.06% June peak**, each monthly inflation print was a market-wide event. The market would coil for days beforehand, implied volatility would bid up into the 8:30am release, and the number would detonate or relieve the whole complex in seconds. The mid-month CPI was, for many desks, the most important data point of the month — more than the FOMC, because the FOMC's decision was increasingly *driven by* the CPI prints, so CPI was upstream of everything. A hot CPI in 2022 did not just sell bonds; it repriced the expected peak of the entire hiking cycle, and you could watch the two-year yield (which ran from roughly 0.7% to a 5.05% peak across the cycle) lurch on each release.

The reactions were genuinely cross-asset and genuinely violent. On the hottest 2022 prints, the S&P could drop several percent intraday, the two-year yield could jump 20-plus basis points, the dollar would spike, and rate-cut bets that had crept into fed funds futures would get violently repriced out — all on a number three- or four-tenths above consensus. The size of the move was wildly out of proportion to the size of the surprise, for the reason from the foundations: positioning was lopsided, everyone leaning the same way on the consensus, so the surprise forced a stampede of unwinds.

The jobs report told its own version of the story. The unemployment rate's grind from a 3.4% low in early 2023 up toward 4.3% by 2026 turned every first-Friday print into a soft-landing referendum: was the labor market cracking (recession) or merely normalizing (soft landing)? Each tenth of a surprise in that rate moved the rate-cut odds and the whole curve. And the PMIs ran underneath it all as the leading tell — the ISM Manufacturing index crossing below 50 in late 2022 flagged the factory contraction months before it showed up in the lagging growth data, exactly the early-warning role the survey is built for.

The mandate rotation showed up cleanly in real time. In 2022 and into 2023, CPI day was the biggest day of every month, full stop — the market was trading the inflation half of the mandate, and every other release was secondary. By mid-2024, with inflation grinding lower and the unemployment rate beginning to climb, the *jobs report* started to take over as the marquee event. A single soft payrolls report in the summer of 2024, landing alongside a tick up in the unemployment rate, triggered a violent global risk-off episode: equity volatility spiked dramatically, the yen-funded carry trade unwound, and rate-cut bets surged — all because one scheduled jobs print landed weak into a market that had been positioned for a soft landing. The same number a year earlier, when the market was trading inflation, would have moved far less. The release did not change; the *mandate the market was trading* changed, and so the release's importance changed with it.

It is worth sitting with how disproportionate these reactions are to the size of the surprises. A payrolls number missing consensus by 70,000 jobs — a rounding error in a labor force of 160 million — can move the two-year yield 15 basis points and the stock market more than 1%. A CPI print three-tenths above consensus can erase weeks of equity gains in an hour. The reactions are this large because of positioning: when the whole market has leaned into the consensus, the surprise forces a synchronized unwind, and synchronized unwinds move price violently. This is the deepest practical lesson of the real-market record — the *size* of the move is governed less by the size of the surprise than by how crowded the positioning was going in. A small surprise into lopsided positioning produces a big move; a big surprise into balanced, two-sided positioning produces a surprisingly small one. Reading the positioning into a print is therefore as important as forecasting the print itself, which is exactly why macro desks watch flows and positioning data alongside the calendar.

The throughline: across 2022-26, the macro tape was not driven by some continuous flow of news. It was driven by a discrete, *scheduled* sequence of releases — CPI mid-month, jobs first Friday, FOMC every six weeks, PMIs at the turn of the month — each one a known volatility event, each one moving the market on the gap between the print and the consensus, and each one amplified or muted by how the market was positioned going in. The calendar was the metronome of the entire cycle.

## How to trade it: the playbook

This is the payoff. Trading the macro calendar is not about predicting numbers — it is a repeatable process you run on every marquee release. Here is the playbook, in order.

![Calendar trading playbook of price the consensus, trade the surprise, survive the whipsaw](/imgs/blogs/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi-7.png)

**1. Know the calendar cold.** Each week, pull the schedule and mark the marquee releases by date and exact time — CPI, jobs, FOMC, PMIs, and any second-tier print that is the biggest event of a quiet week. Clear your screen of surprises about *when* the data lands. There is no excuse for being caught off guard by a scheduled release whose date was published months ago. Map the whole month: PMIs at the turn, jobs the first Friday, CPI mid-month, FOMC every six weeks, PCE and GDP late.

**2. Read what is already priced — before you have any view on the number.** Find the published consensus, then sense where the market is *actually* leaning (the whisper, the recent surprise-index drift, how the curve and the dollar are positioned into the print). The priced expectation is the bar the number has to clear or miss to matter. If a hot print is already half-expected, a hot print does little; the surprise is measured against what is priced, not against the published consensus. This is the step beginners skip, and it is why they trade the wrong direction.

**3. Size the surprise, not the number.** Your trade thesis is "the print will come in *above/below what is priced*, which forces a repricing toward a tighter/looser Fed (or a faster/slower cycle)." Translate that into the cleanest instrument: the two-year yield for inflation and Fed surprises, front-end rates and the dollar for jobs surprises, cyclical assets and the curve's shape for PMI surprises. Only the deviation from the priced expectation moves price, so that deviation is the trade.

**4. Respect the whipsaw — the first move is often the wrong move.** On the jobs report and the FOMC especially, the headline and the details disagree, so the initial reaction frequently reverses. You have two honest choices: wait for the dust to settle (let the first spike and its reversal play out, then trade the durable level), or trade the first move *with a hard stop* small enough to survive a reversal. What you must not do is chase the knee-jerk into a fade with no stop.

**5. Size for the range, not your conviction.** Data-day ranges run two to three times a normal day, and the tail can gap straight through a stop. So you risk *less* on a print day, not more — the opposite of what excitement tempts you to do. In the \$10,000,000-book example, the position was capped at a 1% (\$100,000) loss-if-wrong, sized off the adverse move, not the hoped-for win. The surprise can go either way; size so that the wrong side is a planned, survivable loss.

**6. Invalidation: when the view is wrong, you are out.** Your view is invalidated the moment the print lands on the other side of what you priced — a cool number when you bet hot, a sub-consensus jobs print when you bet strong. There is no "averaging in" on a data surprise; the information arrived and it disagreed with you. Take the planned loss and reset. The whole edge of calendar trading is that the risk is *defined and scheduled* — you know the date, you know the instrument, you know your loss-if-wrong before you put it on. Squander that by removing the stop or doubling down, and you have given back the only structural advantage the calendar offered.

**The pre-print checklist.** To make this repeatable, reduce the loop to a checklist you run in the hour before any marquee release. First, confirm the *exact* release time and which sub-numbers matter (for CPI, the core month-over-month; for jobs, the unemployment rate and the revisions; for PMI, the level relative to 50). Second, write down the consensus and your best read of what is actually priced — if you cannot state the priced expectation in one sentence, you are not ready to trade the print. Third, note which mandate the market is currently trading, because that tells you whether this is a first-tier event today or a secondary one. Fourth, decide your instrument and your loss-if-wrong *before* the number lands, and make sure the loss is a small, fixed fraction of your book. Fifth, decide in advance whether you are trading the first move with a hard stop or waiting for the reversal — make that choice cold, not in the heat of the spike. A trader who can answer those five before 8:30am is trading the calendar; a trader who cannot is gambling on it.

There is one more habit that separates the durable from the blown-up: most of the time, the right trade on a data day is *no trade*. If the print lands near consensus and the market barely moves, there was no surprise and there is nothing to do — forcing a trade into a non-event is how you bleed the account between the prints that actually matter. The calendar gives you a dozen scheduled chances a month; you do not have to swing at all of them. You wait for the prints where you have a genuine read on the priced expectation and where the positioning is lopsided enough that a surprise will produce a real move, and you sit out the rest. Patience between events is part of the edge.

Run that loop on every marquee print and the macro month stops being a wall of scary headlines and becomes a known sequence of defined-risk events. You are not predicting the economy; you are pricing the consensus, sizing the surprise against it, and managing the whipsaw — week after week, on a calendar you knew months in advance. That discipline is the weekly rhythm of every macro desk, and it is built entirely on the one idea this post keeps returning to: the market moves on the surprise, never on the number alone.
