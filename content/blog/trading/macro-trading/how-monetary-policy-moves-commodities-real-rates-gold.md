> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Monetary policy moves commodities through two separate channels, and you have to know which one you are looking at before you can position. Gold trades on the real-rate channel; oil and copper trade on the growth-and-demand channel. The same rate hike can hammer one and barely touch the other.
>
> - **Gold is a real-rate trade.** It pays no yield, so its only rival is the real return on cash. When real interest rates rise, the opportunity cost of holding a yield-less metal rises and gold struggles; when real rates fall, gold soars. In 2022 the Fed pushed the 10-year real yield from about **-1%** to **+1.7%** and gold went nowhere (about **-0.3%** for the year) *despite* 40-year-high inflation.
> - **Oil and copper are growth trades.** Their demand comes from factories, construction, trucks, and grids. Tightening slows the economy and saps that demand, so all else equal a hiking cycle is a headwind for growth commodities.
> - **2022 was the exception that proves the rule.** Broad commodities returned **+16.1%** while the S&P fell **-18.1%**, long Treasuries fell **-31.2%**, and a 60/40 portfolio fell **-16.1%** — because a war-driven *supply shock* overwhelmed the policy-driven demand drag.
> - **The one number to remember:** in the year of the worst inflation since 1981, gold returned about **-0.3%**. That single fact kills the "gold is an inflation hedge" story and tells you what gold really trades against — the real rate.

In the first week of 2022, an ounce of gold cost about \$1,800, roughly where it had sat for a year and a half. The story everyone told about gold was simple and comforting: inflation was running hot — the Consumer Price Index would peak that June at **9.06%**, a 40-year high — and gold was supposed to be the classic hedge against inflation. If ever there were a year for gold to shine, this was it. The dollars in your wallet were losing purchasing power at the fastest rate since 1981. Surely the ancient store of value would protect you.

It did not. By the end of 2022, gold was worth about \$1,824 an ounce — essentially flat, a total return of roughly **-0.3%** for a year in which prices across the economy rose nearly 7%. Measured against the inflation it was supposed to hedge, gold *lost* purchasing power. The asset whose entire reputation rests on protecting you from inflation failed its single most important test in the exact year the test mattered most.

What went wrong? Nothing went wrong — the premise was wrong. Gold was never really an inflation trade. It is a **real-rate** trade, and in 2022 the Federal Reserve was raising real interest rates at the most violent pace in four decades, pushing the 10-year real yield from about **-1%** in early 2022 to **+1.7%** by autumn. A yield-less metal cannot compete with cash that suddenly pays a positive real return, so gold sat dead in the water *while* inflation raged, because the thing that actually drives gold — the real cost of holding it instead of cash — was moving hard against it. Meanwhile, a different set of commodities — oil and copper — was being pulled by a completely different string: the demand-sapping effect of a slowing economy, partly offset, in oil's case, by a war. This post is about those two strings, why they are separate, and how to trade each one.

![Two-channel flow showing the real-rate channel feeding gold and the growth channel feeding oil and copper](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one picture. Policy splits into two channels. The real-rate channel runs through the opportunity cost of holding a yield-less asset and lands on gold. The growth channel runs through the demand for raw materials and lands on oil and copper. By the end of this post you will be able to look at any policy move, sort it into the right channel, and know which commodity it should move and in which direction — and you will know the one big exception, a supply shock, that can override the whole framework. Let us build it from absolute zero.

## Foundations: real assets, gold versus growth commodities, and the real rate

Before we can connect policy to commodities, we need four ideas defined precisely: what a commodity actually is, the deep difference between a yield-less store of value and a growth commodity, what a real interest rate is, and why a yield-less asset's worst enemy is a rising real rate. We will define every term as we go and lean on everyday-money analogies before any arithmetic.

### What a commodity is, and why it is a "real" asset

A **commodity** is a raw, standardized, physical good — a barrel of crude oil, a pound of copper, an ounce of gold, a bushel of wheat. The defining feature is that one unit is interchangeable with any other unit of the same grade, which is what lets it trade on a global exchange at a single world price.

The phrase you will hear repeatedly is that commodities are **real assets**. A *real* asset is one whose value is anchored in a physical thing, not in a promise to pay money. A government bond is a *financial* asset: it is a piece of paper that promises a stream of dollars. A stock is a financial asset: it is a claim on a company's future dollar profits. A commodity is different — it is the physical stuff itself. When you own an ounce of gold or a barrel of oil, no one owes you anything; you simply own a thing.

This distinction matters enormously for how policy hits them. Financial assets are *claims on future dollars*, so their value depends heavily on the interest rate used to discount those future dollars back to today — a rate the central bank controls. A real asset has no contractual dollar stream to discount, which is precisely why people reach for real assets when they distrust the dollar stream itself. But "no dollar stream" cuts two ways, as we are about to see: it is what makes gold a haven, and it is also gold's central weakness.

### The two species: store-of-value versus growth commodity

The single most important split in this entire post is between two kinds of commodity that behave like completely different animals.

A **store-of-value commodity** is held not because it gets consumed in production but because it preserves wealth. It is scarce, durable, and trusted to hold purchasing power when paper money or financial assets are in doubt. **Gold** is the archetype. Almost all the gold ever mined still exists, sitting in vaults, central-bank reserves, and jewelry. Its industrial use is tiny. Nobody holds gold because the world's factories need more of it; they hold it as a parking spot for wealth — an alternative to holding cash or bonds. The key feature, the one that drives everything in the real-rate channel, is this: **gold pays no yield.** A bar of gold sitting in a vault throws off no interest, no dividend, no coupon. It just sits there. Hold that thought — it is the hinge of the whole argument.

A **growth commodity** is one whose demand is driven by economic *activity* — by factories running, by buildings going up, by trucks rolling, by people consuming. Industrial metals (copper, aluminum, nickel) and energy (oil, natural gas) live here. When the world economy speeds up, demand for these rises and so do their prices; when it slows, demand falls and prices sag. They are, in effect, *long the business cycle*. **Copper** is the purest example — it is wiring, motors, plumbing, power grids, and electric vehicles, with no meaningful safe-haven or store-of-value story to muddy the signal. Traders nicknamed it "Dr. Copper," the metal with a PhD in economics, because its price reads global industrial demand in real time.

Oil sits a little awkwardly across the line. Its demand is a growth signal — a booming economy burns more fuel — but because energy is an input to making and moving nearly everything, oil also feeds directly into the price level, giving it an *inflation* dimension that pure copper lacks. For the policy-transmission story, though, the cleanest way to think about oil is as a growth commodity with a supply-shock wildcard, and we will treat it that way. For a deeper read on how the three commodities work as a regime dashboard, see [commodities as macro signals: reading oil, copper, and gold](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold).

The reason this split is load-bearing: **gold and the growth commodities respond to policy through entirely different mechanisms.** Gold responds to the real rate. Oil and copper respond to growth. A single rate hike can be bullish-neutral for gold's *cause* (if it is just keeping pace with inflation) yet bearish for copper (if it is slowing the economy), or it can be brutal for gold (if it is a *real* tightening) while a war keeps oil bid. You cannot lump "commodities" together and expect to read policy off them. You have to split the species first.

### What a real interest rate actually is

Now the most important definition in the post: the **real interest rate**.

Start with the **nominal interest rate** — the headline rate you actually see quoted, the number on your savings account or on a Treasury bond. If a one-year Treasury bill yields 5%, that 5% is the nominal rate. It tells you how many more *dollars* you will have in a year.

But dollars are not the point; *purchasing power* is the point. If you earn 5% on your money but prices rise 3% over the same year, your money buys only about 2% more stuff than it did before. That roughly 2% — the part of your return left over after inflation eats its share — is the **real interest rate**. The clean definition:

> Real interest rate ≈ Nominal interest rate − Expected inflation

That is the entire formula, and it is worth committing to memory because it explains more cross-asset behavior than almost any other single relationship in macro. The real rate is the *true* reward for parking money in a safe asset — the extra purchasing power you actually pocket. An everyday way to feel it: if your bank pays 5% but the prices at the grocery store also climb 5%, the bank is paying you *nothing* in real terms — you can buy exactly the same cart of groceries next year. The real rate is zero even though the nominal rate is a healthy-looking 5%.

Two crucial points. First, it is *expected* inflation, not last year's inflation, because the return is about the future. Second, the real rate can be *negative*. If cash pays 1% and inflation runs at 7% — roughly the 2021 setup — the real rate is about **-6%**: holding cash *guarantees* you lose purchasing power. Markets watch a clean read of this directly: the yield on **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS)**, government bonds whose principal adjusts with inflation, so their quoted yield *is* a real yield. The 10-year TIPS yield is the market's headline real rate, and it is the single variable we will track against gold. For a full treatment of why the real yield sits beneath every asset price, see [real versus nominal: inflation, real yields, and the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).

### Why a rising real rate is a yield-less asset's worst enemy

Put the last two ideas together and the real-rate channel for gold falls out almost automatically.

Gold pays no yield. Cash, increasingly, does. So the question any holder of gold faces is: *what am I giving up by holding this metal instead of holding cash?* The answer is the real return on cash — the real interest rate. That forgone real return is gold's **opportunity cost**: the reward you walk away from when you choose the yield-less asset.

When the real rate is *negative* — when cash loses purchasing power just sitting there — holding gold costs you nothing. You are giving up a negative return, which is to say you are giving up nothing, or even coming out ahead. Gold's opportunity cost is low or negative, and gold tends to do well. This is exactly the 2020-21 setup: real yields around **-1%**, gold firm near record highs.

When the real rate is *positive and rising* — when cash suddenly pays you a real, after-inflation return — the calculus flips. Now holding a yield-less lump of metal means walking away from a genuine real return you could have earned in T-bills. Gold's opportunity cost has gone up. At the margin, money leaves gold and goes to cash, and the gold price struggles or falls. This is exactly the 2022 setup: the Fed drove real yields from about **-1%** to **+1.7%**, gold's opportunity cost surged, and gold went flat even as inflation hit a 40-year high.

That is the entire real-rate channel in one paragraph: **gold competes with the real return on cash, so when the real rate rises, gold's opportunity cost rises and gold struggles; when the real rate falls, gold's opportunity cost falls and gold rallies.** It has nothing directly to do with the level of inflation. Inflation matters only through its effect on the *real* rate — and as 2022 showed, inflation and the real rate can move in opposite directions when the central bank is hiking nominal rates faster than inflation. Now let us go deep on each channel.

## The real-rate channel: gold and the opportunity cost of holding it

The real-rate channel is the cleaner and more surprising of the two, so we start there. The claim is precise: **gold's price moves inversely to the real interest rate**, because the real rate is gold's opportunity cost.

### Why the relationship is inverse, mechanically

Think of every saver in the world as constantly choosing between two safe ways to hold wealth: cash-like instruments (T-bills, money funds) that pay the real rate, and gold, which pays nothing but is also a hard, dollar-independent asset. The flow of money between these two buckets is governed by the relative reward.

When the real rate rises, the cash bucket becomes more attractive: it now pays a real return that gold cannot match. Money flows out of gold and into cash, pushing the gold price *down*. When the real rate falls — especially when it goes negative, so cash is *guaranteed* to lose purchasing power — the gold bucket becomes relatively more attractive: at least gold cannot be inflated away. Money flows into gold, pushing the price *up*. The relationship is inverse by construction: gold up when real rates down, gold down when real rates up.

There is a second, related way to see it that connects gold to the rest of the asset universe. Every asset's value is the present value of its future payoffs, discounted by a rate. The deeper that discount rate — essentially the real rate — the less those future payoffs are worth today. Gold is the extreme case of a **long-duration** asset: its "payoff" (the wealth-preservation it offers) sits infinitely far in the future and never arrives as cash, so it behaves like a bond with an enormous duration. Long-duration assets are the most sensitive to the discount rate. A rise in the real rate clobbers the longest-duration assets hardest, and gold, with no cash flow at all, is about as long-duration as it gets. This is why gold so often falls alongside long bonds and unprofitable tech stocks — they are all long-duration assets repricing to the same rising real rate.

#### Worked example: gold's real-rate beta in 2024-26

Let us put numbers on the inverse relationship and measure how *much* gold moves per unit of real-rate move — what a quant would call gold's **real-rate beta**.

Take the move from 2022 into the 2024-26 surge. At the end of 2022, the 10-year real yield was about **+1.57%** and gold averaged about **\$1,802**. By early 2026, as the market priced an easing cycle and real-rate expectations fell back toward **+1.99%** off their 2023 peak near **+2.48%**, while debasement fear ran hot, gold averaged about **\$5,414** at its January 2026 spike. The cleanest leg to isolate the beta is the fall in real yields from the 2023 peak: real yields dropped from about **+2.48%** (October 2023) toward **+1.99%**, a fall of roughly **0.49 percentage points**, while gold climbed from roughly **\$1,943** (2023 average) to **\$2,388** (2024) and onward.

Hold the supply-fear part aside and look at just the cleanest historical leg — the 2020-21 era. The 10-year real yield sat near **-1%** and gold averaged about **\$1,770** to **\$1,799**. As real yields then ripped from **-1%** to **+1.7%** through 2022 — a rise of about **2.7 percentage points** — gold went from roughly **\$1,799** to **\$1,802**, essentially flat. The historical rule of thumb traders carry is that a **1 percentage point** rise in the 10-year real yield is worth roughly an **8% to 12%** headwind for gold, all else equal. So a 2.7-point rise "should" have knocked gold down 20% or more — and it nearly did intraday (gold fell from over \$2,000 in March 2022 to about \$1,620 by autumn, a roughly 20% drop) before fiscal and geopolitical fear pulled it back to flat by year-end. **The takeaway: gold's price falls when the real yield rises by a fairly stable amount per point of real-rate move, so to forecast gold you forecast the real yield, not inflation.**

Here is the picture that pairs gold against the real yield, with the real-yield axis inverted so the inverse relationship reads as two lines tracking each other:

![Dual axis chart of gold price and the inverted 10 year real yield from 2020 to 2026](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold-2.png)

Read the chart left to right. In 2020-21, real yields sit near **-1%** (high on the inverted axis) and gold is firm near \$1,800. In 2022, real yields jump to **+1.7%** (the amber line dives) and gold goes nowhere — the opportunity cost surged and capped the metal. Then in 2024-26, real-rate expectations ease and a wave of debasement fear hits, and gold goes near-vertical, surging past \$2,388 toward \$5,414. The two lines tell one story: gold is the mirror image of the real yield. When you can read that mirror, you have the gold trade.

### Measuring the real-rate beta in code

The rule of thumb above — "roughly 8% to 12% of gold per point of real yield" — comes from a regression. It is worth seeing exactly how the number is produced, because once you can compute it you can update it as the relationship drifts. Here is the core calculation: regress the percent change in gold on the *change* in the 10-year real yield, and read the beta off the slope.

```
import numpy as np

    real_yield = np.array([-0.93, -1.04, 1.57, 1.72, 2.20, 1.99])   # 10Y TIPS, % (year-end)
    gold       = np.array([1770, 1799, 1802, 1943, 2388, 5414])     # gold, USD/oz (annual avg)

    d_yield = np.diff(real_yield)                 # change in real yield, pct points
    g_ret   = np.diff(gold) / gold[:-1] * 100.0   # gold return, percent

    beta, intercept = np.polyfit(d_yield, g_ret, 1)   # slope = pct gold move per +1pt yield
    resid = g_ret - (beta * d_yield + intercept)
    r2 = 1.0 - resid.var() / g_ret.var()

    print(f"real-rate beta: {beta:+.1f}% gold per +1.0 pt real yield")
    print(f"R-squared:      {r2:.2f}")
```

Run that on the clean pre-surge window and the slope comes out negative — gold falls as the real yield rises — in the ballpark of the 8-to-12%-per-point rule, though the 2024-26 debasement surge weakens the fit because that leg was driven by fiscal fear on top of the real-rate signal. That is itself the lesson: the real rate is gold's dominant driver *most* of the time, and when the regression's fit (the R-squared) drops, it is usually telling you a second force — debasement fear, central-bank buying — has entered the picture. The beta is a tool for sizing the trade, not an oracle; when it stops fitting, ask what new force arrived.

#### Worked example: the opportunity cost on a \$50,000 gold position

Make the opportunity cost concrete and personal, because this is the mechanism in its rawest form. Suppose you hold **\$50,000** of gold — say 28 ounces at roughly \$1,800. The gold throws off no income at all: no interest, no dividend, nothing. It just sits.

Now suppose T-bills pay a **5%** nominal yield, as they did across 2023-24, against expected inflation of about **2.5%**, so the *real* yield on cash is about **2.5%**. By choosing to hold gold instead of T-bills, you are forgoing the cash return. In nominal terms, you walk away from **5% of \$50,000 = \$2,500 per year**. That \$2,500 is the price of holding the yield-less metal — money you simply do not earn. In real terms, after the 2.5% inflation that erodes both choices equally, you forgo about **2.5% of \$50,000 = \$1,250 per year** of *real* purchasing power that the T-bill would have handed you and gold will not.

Compare that to 2021, when T-bills paid about **0.05%**. Holding the same \$50,000 of gold cost you a forgone **\$25 a year** — basically nothing — and with inflation near 5%, the *real* yield on cash was about **-5%**, so by holding gold you actually *avoided* a guaranteed real loss. Same gold, same vault, wildly different opportunity cost: \$25 forgone in 2021 versus \$2,500 forgone in 2023. **The takeaway: gold's "cost" is the cash yield you give up to hold it, so a high real rate makes holding gold genuinely expensive and a negative real rate makes it nearly free — which is exactly why gold sags when the Fed hikes into positive real territory and rallies when real yields fall.**

### Why this looks like an inflation hedge but is not one

The reason "gold is an inflation hedge" is such a persistent myth is that it is *usually* approximately true, for an indirect reason. Historically, when inflation rose, central banks were often *slow* to raise nominal rates to match, so real rates *fell* during inflationary periods, and falling real rates lifted gold. The inflation and the gold rally were correlated, but inflation was not the cause — the falling real rate was. The two usually move together, so the lazy story survived.

2022 broke the correlation cleanly because the Fed did the opposite of slow: it raised nominal rates *faster* than inflation, so the real rate *rose* even as inflation hit a 40-year high. The hidden cause (the real rate) and the apparent cause (inflation) finally diverged, and gold sided with the real rate, exposing the myth. This is the single most important thing to internalize about gold: it is a real-rate trade wearing an inflation-hedge costume.

The figure lays the myth and the reality side by side. On the left, the inflation-hedge story walks into 2022 — the worst inflation since 1981 — predicts gold should soar, and is contradicted by a roughly **-0.3%** return. On the right, the real-rate story explains it cleanly: the Fed pushed the real rate from **-1%** to **+1.7%**, gold's opportunity cost surged, and the flat year falls right out. Same facts, one framework that fails the test and one that passes it. We will revisit this in the misconceptions section because it is the most expensive mistake retail traders make in this corner of the market.

### The second leg: debasement fear and central-bank buying

The real rate is gold's *primary* driver, but it is not gold's *only* driver, and the 2024-26 surge is the case where the second force took over. Gold has a second job beyond competing with the real return on cash: it is the asset of last resort when people lose faith in the *currency itself* — not just in its short-term real return, but in the long-run integrity of the dollars a bond will eventually pay you. Call this the **debasement** channel: the fear that governments, facing unpayable debts, will inflate or devalue their way out, eroding the value of the paper claims (bonds and cash) that compete with gold.

This is why the regression fit weakens after 2023. From late 2023 into 2026, the United States ran deficits near 6% of GDP with total federal debt climbing past **\$35 trillion** and net interest outlays nearly tripling since 2020 to roughly **\$1 trillion** a year — a fiscal trajectory that made the *long-run* value of holding Treasuries and dollars look shaky even when the *short-run* real yield was still positive. At the same time, central banks — especially in emerging markets wary of dollar sanctions after 2022 — bought gold at a record pace, adding a steady, price-insensitive bid that has nothing to do with the real rate at all. The result: gold detached from the real-rate signal and went vertical, rising from **\$1,943** (2023) through **\$2,388** (2024) and **\$3,000** (2025) to a **\$5,414** spike in early 2026, even as real yields stayed positive near **+2%**.

The trading lesson is not that the real-rate framework broke; it is that it has a known second term. Gold ≈ a real-rate trade *plus* a debasement-fear premium. Most of the time the real rate dominates and the debasement premium is small and stable, so tracking the real yield is enough. But in a regime of fiscal stress — runaway deficits, sovereign-debt worries, heavy central-bank reserve diversification out of dollars — the debasement premium swells and can overwhelm the real-rate signal entirely, sending gold higher *despite* positive real yields. When you see gold rallying while real yields are flat or rising, do not conclude the framework is wrong; conclude that the debasement term has woken up, and size accordingly. For how sovereign-debt stress feeds this premium, see [asset rotation across the business cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants).

## The growth channel: how policy moves oil and copper through demand

Now the second channel, which works through an entirely different mechanism: the **demand effect** of policy on economic activity. Growth commodities like copper and oil are inputs to *making things*, so their demand rises and falls with the business cycle — and monetary policy is the lever that speeds the cycle up or slows it down.

### How tightening reaches commodity demand

Walk the chain. The central bank raises its policy rate. Higher rates ripple through the economy via the channels covered in [monetary-policy transmission: how rate changes reach markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets): borrowing gets more expensive, so households buy fewer houses and cars, businesses delay expansion and capital spending, and financial conditions tighten broadly. Less building, less manufacturing, less freight, fewer big-ticket purchases. That is, by design, the central bank cooling the economy to bring down inflation.

Now connect that to commodities. A house that does not get built uses no copper wiring, no copper plumbing, no steel. A factory expansion that gets shelved orders no industrial metals. A consumer who delays buying a new car removes the roughly 50-plus pounds of copper that car would have contained and the gasoline it would have burned. A construction slowdown drops demand for diesel and energy. Every one of these is a withdrawal of demand for a *growth commodity*. So the growth channel is mechanical and direct: **tighter policy slows activity, slower activity means less stuff gets made and moved, and less stuff made and moved means less demand for the raw materials that go into it.** All else equal, a hiking cycle is a demand headwind for oil and copper.

This is the opposite of gold's channel in a subtle but important way. Gold does not care whether the economy is growing or shrinking; it cares only about the real return on the cash it competes with. Copper does not care about the real rate per se; it cares whether factories are running. So the same hike can simultaneously be bad for gold (real rate up) *and* bad for copper (growth down) — which is why a sharp, demand-driven tightening can sink both gold and the growth commodities at once. But the *reasons* are different, and that difference is what lets you predict the rare cases where they diverge.

### Copper as the purest read on the growth channel

Copper is the cleanest instrument for watching the growth channel because it has no inflation story and no safe-haven story to confuse the signal. Nobody buys copper as a hedge against the dollar; nobody flees to copper in a panic. Its price is almost purely a referendum on global industrial demand — which is exactly what policy is trying to steer. When the cycle accelerates, copper rises; when policy slows the cycle, copper softens. It tracks global manufacturing surveys, China's construction pulse, and the broad business cycle with little of the noise that clouds gold or even oil.

This is why a macro trader treats copper as a *confirming* indicator for the growth channel. If you believe policy is biting and the cycle is rolling over, copper should be telling the same story; if copper is climbing while you believe the economy is slowing, one of your two readings is wrong. The price *is* the leading indicator, weeks ahead of the official growth data. The [metals: copper, silver, and the economy's pulse](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) angle is worth holding alongside this.

#### Worked example: copper and the cycle on a \$100,000 position

Put real money on the growth channel. Suppose you hold a **\$100,000** position in copper — through a futures position or a miner ETF — and you are trying to read what the cycle will do to it.

Look at the actual path. In 2021, with the post-COVID reopening booming and policy still at zero, the ISM Manufacturing PMI — the survey where a reading above **50** means expansion and below 50 means contraction — sat near **58.8**, deep in expansion, and copper averaged about **\$4.23 per pound**. Then the Fed hiked aggressively through 2022 into 2023; the PMI fell *through* 50 to about **48.4** at the end of 2022 and **47.1** at the end of 2023 — outright contraction. Copper softened in step, from \$4.23 to about **\$4.00** (2022) to **\$3.85** (2023), a drop of roughly **9%**. On your \$100,000 position, that 9% slide is a **\$9,000** loss, driven not by anything specific to copper but by the policy-induced contraction in manufacturing demand that the PMI was flagging in real time.

Then watch the other side. As the cycle stabilized and easing came into view in 2024-25, the PMI firmed back toward **49**, still soft but no longer collapsing, and copper recovered to about **\$4.20** (2024) and **\$4.30** (2025) — roughly an **+12%** rebound off the 2023 low, or **\$12,000** on the position. The lesson is that your copper P&L was a near-direct function of where the manufacturing cycle sat relative to the expansion line, and that cycle was being steered by policy. **The takeaway: copper is long the business cycle, so to position it you read the growth backdrop policy is engineering — PMIs through 50, China, global manufacturing — not the real rate that drives gold.**

The chart overlays copper's annual average (the amber bars) on the ISM Manufacturing PMI (the blue line, with the red 50-line marking the expansion-contraction boundary). When policy pushed the PMI under 50 in 2022-23, copper softened from \$4.23 to \$3.85; when the cycle firmed back toward the line, copper recovered. Copper is reading the growth cycle that policy steers — which is precisely why it is a different trade from gold, with a different driver and a different signal.

### Oil: the growth commodity with a supply wildcard

Oil belongs to the growth channel too — a stronger economy burns more fuel, a recession crashes demand (in April 2020, WTI briefly settled *negative* on a demand-and-storage collapse). But oil carries a wildcard that copper lacks: its supply is concentrated in a handful of producers and geographies, so a war, an OPEC+ production cut, a sanction, or a refinery outage can spike the price for reasons that have nothing to do with demand or policy.

This matters for the policy story because it means oil can *defy* the growth channel. In a normal slowdown, tightening saps oil demand and the price falls. But if a supply shock hits at the same time, oil can rip *higher* even as the economy weakens and policy tightens — the worst of both worlds, a stagflationary tax that raises inflation and slows growth at once. That is exactly what happened in 2022, and it is the bridge to the next section: the case where the two channels conflict and a supply shock overwhelms the policy drag.

There is a second reason oil sits awkwardly in the policy framework, and it is the feature that makes oil unique among commodities: its price *feeds back into the very inflation the central bank is fighting*. When oil spikes, it raises the price of gasoline, diesel, and freight almost immediately — and because energy is an input to making and moving nearly everything, that crude move ripples into the whole goods basket within a month or two. So a supply-driven oil spike does not just defy the growth channel; it *forces the central bank to tighten harder*, because the oil-driven inflation shows up in the CPI the Fed targets. This creates a vicious loop in a stagflationary shock: the oil spike raises inflation, the higher inflation forces more hikes, the hikes slow growth further, and yet the oil price stays high because its cause was supply, not demand. The 1970s — covered in [case study: 1970s stagflation, commodities win](/blog/trading/macro-trading/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants) — was the archetype, and 2022 was its modern echo. The takeaway for a trader is that oil's response to policy is non-linear and sometimes perverse: a demand-driven oil rally cooperates with the growth channel and fades when the Fed tightens, but a supply-driven oil rally fights the Fed, feeds the inflation, and can persist straight through a hiking cycle. Always classify the oil move by its cause before you assume policy will sink it.

### Why short-run inelasticity makes growth commodities so violent

One more mechanical point that applies to both copper and oil and explains why the growth channel produces such large price swings: short-run **inelasticity** of both supply and demand. In the short run, you cannot quickly build a new copper mine (it takes a decade) or open a new oil field, so supply is nearly fixed; and in the short run, you still drive to work, heat your home, and run your factory even when prices rise, so demand is sticky too. When both sides are inelastic, a small change in the balance produces a large change in price. A 2% shortfall in supply against stubborn demand can move the price 20% or more to clear the market — and a 2% drop in demand in a recession can crash the price just as violently, as oil's plunge to *negative* prices in April 2020 showed.

This is why the growth channel hits commodities harder than it hits, say, a consumer-staples stock. A modest policy-driven slowdown in demand, against inelastic supply, can swing copper or oil far more than the underlying demand change would suggest. It is also why supply shocks are so explosive: take 2% of the world's oil offline against demand that barely flexes, and the price detonates. Inelasticity is the amplifier on both channels, and it is the reason commodity moves so often overshoot what a smooth demand model would predict.

## When the channels conflict: the 2022 commodity exception

Most of the time, the two channels and the rest of the asset universe broadly agree about direction. A dovish, easing central bank lowers real rates (good for gold) and supports growth (good for copper and oil), and risk assets rally too. A hawkish, tightening central bank raises real rates (bad for gold) and slows growth (bad for copper and oil), and risk assets fall. The channels point the same way as everything else, and "commodities" loosely track the policy cycle inversely — tightening down, easing up.

2022 was the rare and instructive exception, and understanding *why* is the deepest lesson in this post.

### What made 2022 unique

In 2022 the Federal Reserve executed the fastest hiking cycle in 40 years, taking the policy rate from near zero to over 4% and pushing the 10-year real yield from about **-1%** to **+1.7%**. Through the framework, this *should* have been bad for everything: bad for gold (real rates up), bad for copper and oil (growth slowing), and bad for stocks and bonds (discount rate up). And it was bad for nearly everything. The S&P 500 fell **-18.1%**. The Nasdaq 100 fell **-32.5%**. Long Treasuries fell **-31.2%** — one of the worst bond years in history. A standard 60/40 portfolio fell **-16.1%**. Investment-grade corporate bonds fell **-15.4%**. Bitcoin fell **-64.3%**. It was a synchronized rate-shock selloff: the one rising real rate hammered every long-duration financial asset at once, and there was nowhere to hide in the usual stock-bond mix.

Except one place. **Broad commodities returned +16.1%** (the Bloomberg Commodity Index), and the **US dollar rose +8.2%**. In a year when a balanced portfolio lost a sixth of its value, the commodity complex *gained* a sixth. Gold, sitting between the channels, landed almost exactly flat at **-0.3%** — its real-rate headwind (which should have sunk it) was offset by surging geopolitical and inflation fear (which supported it), netting to roughly nothing, a perfect illustration of gold caught between its real-rate driver and its haven role.

![Horizontal bar chart of 2022 total returns by asset with commodities highlighted as the exception](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold-4.png)

The figure ranks the 2022 returns. Almost the entire chart is red — the rate shock sank stocks, bonds, the 60/40, and crypto together. Two green bars stand out: the dollar at **+8.2%** and broad commodities at **+16.1%**, the lone real-economy exception. Gold sits at the seam, almost exactly flat. This single chart is why "tightening always sinks commodities" is wrong, and why you must know *which* channel and *which* shock you are dealing with before you position.

### Why the supply shock overwhelmed the policy drag

Here is the resolution. The policy drag in 2022 was real: tightening *was* sapping demand through the growth channel, and on its own that would have pulled commodities down. But a far bigger force was acting on the *supply* side. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 disrupted a major source of the world's oil, gas, wheat, fertilizer, and industrial metals. The supply of these physical goods was suddenly constrained, and constrained supply against still-substantial demand sends prices up regardless of what policy is doing to demand. WTI crude averaged about **\$94.53** for the year and spiked well above \$120 in the spring. Natural gas, wheat, and a basket of industrials soared.

The supply shock *overwhelmed* the demand drag. Yes, policy was slowing demand; but supply was constrained far harder and faster than demand was falling, so the net result was rising commodity prices. This is the general principle: **a price is set by the balance of supply and demand, and policy only controls the demand side. When a supply shock hits hard enough, it can swamp the policy-driven demand effect entirely, and commodities rise even as the central bank tightens and the rest of the economy reels.** Commodities became the one asset class that benefits when the *problem* is a supply-driven inflation shock, because they *are* the thing whose scarcity is causing the inflation. That is also exactly the regime in which commodities are the best diversifier — see [case study 2022: stocks and bonds both fell](/blog/trading/macro-trading/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants) for how a real-asset sleeve rescued an otherwise brutal year.

#### Worked example: the 2022 exception on a \$1,000,000 book

Put the exception in dollars on a real book. Imagine a **\$1,000,000** portfolio held in a conventional 60/40 mix entering 2022: **\$600,000** in stocks (call it the S&P 500) and **\$400,000** in bonds (a mix leaning toward longer Treasuries and IG credit). Run the year's actual returns through it.

The stock sleeve at **-18.1%** loses about **\$108,600**, ending near **\$491,400**. The bond sleeve, which was supposed to *cushion* the stock loss, instead got crushed by the rate shock: blend long Treasuries (**-31.2%**) and IG corporates (**-15.4%**) and call the sleeve down roughly **-20%**, a loss of about **\$80,000**, ending near **\$320,000**. Total portfolio: about **\$811,400** — a loss of roughly **\$188,600**, near the 60/40's actual **-16.1%**. The diversification that 60/40 promises *failed*, because both legs were long-duration financial assets repricing to the same rising real rate.

Now suppose this investor had carved out just **\$100,000** — 10% of the book — into broad commodities entering the year, funded by trimming the bond sleeve. That \$100,000 at **+16.1%** *gains* about **\$16,100**, ending near **\$116,100**. The \$300,000 left in bonds loses about **\$60,000** instead of \$80,000. Re-tally: stocks about \$491,400, bonds about \$240,000, commodities about \$116,100 — total about **\$847,500**, a loss of roughly **\$152,500** instead of \$188,600. The 10% commodity sleeve saved about **\$36,100** — nearly four points of the entire portfolio — in the one year a stock-bond mix needed it most. **The takeaway: in a supply-driven inflation shock, commodities are the diversifier that works precisely because they rise on the same force (constrained real goods) that sinks both stocks and bonds, so a real-asset sleeve earns its keep in the exact regime when 60/40 breaks.**

## Common misconceptions

Three myths cause more bad commodity trades than anything else. Each one is corrected with a number from the data above.

**Myth 1: "Gold is an inflation hedge."** This is the big one, and 2022 is its tombstone. In the year inflation hit a 40-year high of **9.06%**, gold returned about **-0.3%** — it failed to protect against the very thing it is famous for hedging. Gold is a *real-rate* trade. It rallies when real yields fall and struggles when they rise, *regardless* of the inflation level. The reason the myth persists is that inflation and falling real rates usually coincide (central banks are usually slow to react), so gold usually does rise during inflation — but for the wrong reason. When the central bank hikes real rates *into* an inflation, as in 2022, gold sides with the real rate and the myth shatters. Trade gold against the real yield, never against headline CPI.

**Myth 2: "Tightening always sinks commodities."** Usually true, occasionally catastrophically false. In 2022, the most aggressive tightening in four decades, broad commodities returned **+16.1%** — the *best*-performing major asset class — while stocks fell 18% and bonds fell up to 31%. The reason: policy controls only the *demand* side of a commodity's price, and a supply shock (the Ukraine war) overwhelmed the demand drag. The corrected rule: tightening is a demand *headwind* for growth commodities, but a hard enough supply shock can swamp it. Always ask whether the commodity move is demand-driven (policy bites) or supply-driven (policy is beside the point).

**Myth 3: "Oil tracks the Fed."** Oil is a growth commodity, so policy-driven demand matters — but oil's supply is so concentrated and shock-prone that the Fed is often the *minor* variable in oil's price. In 2022 oil averaged **\$94.53** and spiked over \$120 *while* the Fed hiked hardest, because a war, not the Fed, was setting the price. Conversely, in April 2020 oil went *negative* on a demand collapse the Fed could do nothing about. Oil's price is supply versus demand; the Fed moves only one of those, and supply shocks routinely dominate. Read oil through its inventories, the futures curve, and OPEC, with policy as one input among several — not as a Fed proxy.

**Myth 4: "A strong dollar always sinks commodities."** There is a real mechanism here — commodities are priced in dollars, so a stronger dollar makes them more expensive for the rest of the world and tends to weigh on demand, which is why dollar strength and commodity weakness usually correlate. But "always" is wrong, and 2022 is again the counterexample. The dollar (DXY) rose **+8.2%** in 2022 — driven by the same Fed tightening that lifted real yields — and yet broad commodities still returned **+16.1%**, because the supply shock overwhelmed the dollar drag just as it overwhelmed the demand drag. The corrected rule: a strong dollar is a *headwind* for commodities through the demand and pricing channel, but it is one force among several, and a supply shock can beat it. Note also the deeper link — Fed tightening tends to lift the dollar *and* real yields together, which is why a hawkish Fed is a double headwind for gold (real-rate channel) and growth commodities (dollar plus demand) at once, and why it took a war to break the pattern. For the dollar's cross-asset gravity, see [the dollar: cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets).

## How it shows up in real markets

Three concrete episodes show the two channels operating in the wild, with real dates and numbers.

**Gold versus real yields, 2020-2026.** The cleanest demonstration of the real-rate channel is the full arc. In 2020-21, real yields sat near **-1%** and gold was firm near **\$1,800**. Through 2022 the Fed drove real yields from **-1%** to **+1.7%** and gold went flat-to-down — its opportunity cost surged. Then from late 2023 into 2026, as the market priced an easing cycle and real-rate expectations eased off their **+2.48%** peak, and as a wave of fiscal and debasement fear hit (ballooning deficits, central-bank gold buying), gold went near-vertical, surging from **\$1,943** (2023) through **\$2,388** (2024) and **\$3,000** (2025) to a **\$5,414** spike in early 2026.

![Area chart of gold price from 2019 to 2026 showing the flat hiking years and the 2024 to 2026 surge](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold-6.png)

The figure traces gold's long climb. The red-shaded band marks the 2021-22 hiking years, when rising real yields pinned gold flat near \$1,800. The green-shaded band marks the 2024-26 surge, when falling real-rate expectations plus debasement fear sent gold vertical. The shape is the lesson: gold ground sideways exactly while the Fed was raising real rates, then exploded once that headwind reversed and a second tailwind — fear of the dollar's debasement — arrived. The real rate was the gatekeeper.

**The 2022 commodity exception.** Already dissected above, but worth restating as a live-market fact: it is the single best real-world proof that commodities are a separate animal from financial assets. In a year when *everything* a normal investor owned fell together, the commodity complex rose 16% because the inflation everyone feared was being *caused* by the scarcity of the very goods commodities represent. When the problem is a supply-driven inflation shock, commodities are not a victim of the regime — they *are* the regime.

**Copper and the growth slowdown.** As the Fed's tightening fed through the growth channel in 2022-23, the manufacturing cycle rolled over — ISM PMI fell from near **58.8** to **47.1**, outright contraction — and copper softened from **\$4.23** to **\$3.85**. This was the growth channel working exactly as designed: less activity, less metal demand, lower price. And critically, copper fell for a *different reason* than gold's flat year — copper because growth slowed, gold because the real rate rose. Two channels, two commodities, two distinct drivers, all set in motion by the same hiking cycle. Reading them apart is the whole skill.

## How to trade it: the playbook

This is the payoff. You now have a framework that sorts any policy move into the right commodity channel. Here is how to turn it into positions, with the signals to watch and the lines that invalidate each trade.

**The first move is always to read the channel.** Before you touch a commodity, ask two questions and check one override. (1) *What is the real rate doing?* — that sets your gold view. (2) *What is the growth cycle doing?* — that sets your copper and oil view. (3) *Is there a supply shock?* — that can override the growth read entirely. Get those three answers and the positions follow.

![Decision flow for the commodity policy playbook routing real rates to gold and growth to copper and oil](/imgs/blogs/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold-7.png)

The figure is the decision tree. Read the setup at the top, then branch: the real-rate question routes to the gold trade, the growth question routes to copper and oil, and the supply-shock override sits to the side as the force that can flip the growth read. Each leaf carries the position and its invalidation. Let us walk the two core trades.

**Trade 1: gold versus the real yield.** This is the cleanest trade in the post because it has a single, observable driver. The signal is the path of the 10-year real yield (the TIPS yield) and, more importantly, its *expected* path given where the Fed is in its cycle.

- *When real yields are falling or about to fall* — a Fed pivoting toward cuts, a slowing economy that will force easing, expected inflation rising faster than nominal rates — **go long gold.** Gold's opportunity cost is dropping, and the historical beta says roughly 8-12% of upside per point of real-yield decline. The 2024-26 surge is the template. *Invalidation: real yields rip higher* — if the Fed turns hawkish and the TIPS yield breaks to new cycle highs, your thesis is broken; gold's headwind is back. Cut it.
- *When real yields are rising or a hawkish Fed is hiking into positive real territory* — **underweight or avoid gold, and fade rallies into rising real yields.** This is the 2022 setup: every gold bounce was a chance to sell because the real-rate tide was running out. *Invalidation: a supply or fiscal-fear shock* — if debasement fear (runaway deficits, a currency crisis, heavy central-bank buying) overwhelms the real-rate signal, gold can rally *despite* high real yields, as it began to in 2023-24. Watch the gold price against the real yield: if gold stops falling when real yields rise, a second force has arrived and your short is in danger.

**Trade 2: copper and oil versus the cycle.** Growth commodities trade on the demand the cycle is generating, so you position them off the growth backdrop policy is engineering.

- *When the cycle is turning up or policy is easing* — PMIs climbing back through **50**, China stimulating, financial conditions loosening — **go long copper and the oil-demand story.** You are positioning for the rebuilding of industrial demand. *Invalidation: PMIs roll back over* — if the manufacturing surveys turn down again, the demand thesis is failing; copper will follow.
- *When the cycle is slowing or policy is tightening* — PMIs falling toward and through 50, credit tightening, big-ticket demand fading — **fade growth commodities;** demand drains with activity, as copper's 2022-23 slide showed. *Invalidation and the key override: a supply shock.* If a war, an OPEC+ cut, a sanction, or an outage hits, **buy the commodity anyway** — supply can swamp the policy drag, exactly as it did in 2022 when broad commodities returned **+16.1%** into the teeth of the hiking cycle. For oil especially, never short the demand story without checking the supply side first.

**Sizing and the portfolio role.** Beyond directional trades, the deepest use of this framework is as a *diversifier*. A commodity sleeve earns its keep in precisely the regime that breaks a stock-bond portfolio: a supply-driven inflation shock, where rising real rates sink both stocks and bonds while constrained real goods send commodities up. The worked example above showed a 10% commodity sleeve saving roughly **\$36,100** — about four points — on a \$1,000,000 book in 2022. That is the structural case for holding some commodity exposure even when you have no directional view: it is the insurance that pays out in the one regime your other assets cannot survive. For how this fits a full cross-asset rotation, see [asset rotation across the business cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants).

**The one habit that ties it together.** When a commodity moves, do not ask "what is the Fed doing?" and stop. Ask *which channel*. Is this gold moving on the real rate, or copper moving on growth, or oil moving on a supply shock that policy cannot touch? Sort the move into the right channel and you will know whether the Fed is the driver or a bystander — and that single discrimination, more than any forecast, is what separates traders who read commodities correctly from those who lump them into one undifferentiated "inflation hedge" and get blindsided every time the real rate and the inflation rate part ways.
