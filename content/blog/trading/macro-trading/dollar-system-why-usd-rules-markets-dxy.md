> **TL;DR** — The US dollar is the world's unit of account, its dominant funding currency, and its top reserve asset all at once, which makes the dollar's *level* a master switch on global financial conditions; when the dollar rises, the whole world tightens.
>
> - A reserve currency is one that central banks, companies, and savers everywhere hold and transact in even when no American is involved. The dollar does this on a scale no rival comes close to: around 58% of the world's disclosed FX reserves and roughly half of all trade invoicing.
> - The **DXY** (US Dollar Index) is a tradable, fixed-weight basket — 57.6% euro, then yen, pound, Canadian dollar, krona, and franc. It is really a dollar-versus-Europe gauge, not a dollar-versus-the-world measure.
> - There is an enormous pile of **dollar debt owed outside the US** — roughly \$13 trillion in the offshore "eurodollar" system. When the dollar rises, that debt gets harder to service, which is the channel that turns a strong dollar into a global wrecking ball.
> - The dollar tends to rise in *both* a global panic and a US boom — the "dollar smile" — so a rising DXY is a risk-off lens for almost everything priced against it: emerging markets, commodities, and crypto.
> - The one number to remember: **roughly \$13 trillion of dollar debt sits on non-American balance sheets**, and a stronger dollar is a tax on every dollar of it.

In late September 2022, something quietly violent happened in markets, and most retail traders never understood it as a single event. The Japanese yen had fallen to 145 per dollar, the weakest in 24 years, and Japan's central bank was selling dollars to defend it. The British pound had just crashed to an all-time low near 1.03 after a botched budget, forcing the Bank of England into an emergency bond-buying program to stop pension funds from blowing up. Emerging-market central banks from Manila to Santiago were burning through reserves. Gold, which is "supposed" to rise when things go wrong, was *falling*. Bitcoin had been cut by three-quarters from its peak. And the thing tying all of it together — the yen crash, the gilt crisis, the EM reserve drain, the gold weakness, the crypto bleed — was one variable: the US dollar was on a tear, and the DXY had just touched 114.8, a 20-year high.

That is the whole point of this post. The dollar is not just another currency in a long list. It is the *unit the rest of the world is measured against*, the *currency much of the world borrows in*, and the *asset central banks hoard for safety*. When one currency holds all three of those jobs at the same time, its price stops being a niche FX concern and becomes a master risk variable — a single dial that, when it turns, retunes the cost of money for almost everyone on earth. The diagram above is the mental model: three jobs feeding into one currency, and that currency's level feeding back out to pressure everything priced against it.

![The dollar's three jobs radiating to the world as the world's reserve currency](/imgs/blogs/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy-1.png)

Most macro is local: a country's central bank sets its rate, its bond market reacts, its currency moves. The dollar is the exception that bends the rest of the system around it. A trader who internalizes *one* thing about global macro should internalize this: **the dollar is the tide, and almost everything else is a boat.** When the tide goes out — when the dollar rises — boats that looked seaworthy start scraping the bottom. This post builds the whole picture from zero: what a reserve currency actually is, why so much of the world's debt is denominated in dollars, what the DXY measures (and what it misses), why the dollar perversely strengthens in *both* booms and panics, and — most importantly — how you read all of it as a trader.

## Foundations: the reserve currency and the dollar's three jobs

Let's start with money you already understand. In your own country, one currency does everything: you're paid in it, you price things in it, you save in it, you borrow in it. You never think about it because there's no choice and no friction. Now zoom out to the *whole planet*. There are roughly 180 national currencies. Most of them are useless outside their home borders — a shopkeeper in Brazil will not accept Vietnamese dong, and a bank in Nigeria cannot easily lend you Thai baht. So when two parties from different countries trade, save, or lend across borders, they need a *shared* currency that both sides trust and can use. For about eighty years, overwhelmingly, that shared currency has been the US dollar.

A **reserve currency** is exactly that: a currency held and used by people who are not its citizens, for transactions that may not involve the issuing country at all. When a Korean shipbuilder sells a tanker to a Greek owner, the contract is very often written in dollars, even though neither party is American. When the central bank of India wants to hold a safety buffer of foreign savings, it holds most of it in dollars — specifically in US Treasury bonds. The dollar is the world's *default*, the language money speaks when it crosses a border. The phrase "the dollar is the world's reserve currency" is a shorthand for a deeper truth: the dollar does three distinct jobs for the global economy at once, and no other currency does all three at meaningful scale.

**Job one: the unit of account — the world's price tag.** A unit of account is the ruler you measure value with. Oil is quoted in dollars per barrel. Copper, gold, wheat, and most globally traded commodities are quoted in dollars. The majority of cross-border trade is *invoiced* in dollars even when the US is not the buyer or seller — by various estimates, roughly half of all trade invoicing worldwide, and closer to 100% of oil. This matters enormously, because if the thing you import is priced in dollars and your own currency weakens, your import bill rises *mechanically*, with no change in the underlying good. When the dollar is the ruler, every other currency's weakness shows up as imported inflation.

The scale of dollar invoicing is wildly out of proportion to America's actual share of trade. The US accounts for roughly a tenth of world trade, but the dollar is used in something like *half* of all trade invoices globally — a five-to-one over-representation. A South Korean firm selling to a German buyer may well price the deal in dollars; the dollar is the neutral, liquid common denominator that two non-American parties default to because converting won-to-dollars-to-euros is cheaper and deeper than converting won directly to euros. This invoicing dominance has a sharp consequence for inflation that surprises beginners: because import prices are sticky in dollars, a country's inflation is driven less by its bilateral exchange rate with each trade partner than by its rate *against the dollar specifically*. When the dollar strengthens broadly, dozens of countries import inflation at once, even in goods they don't buy from America — which is why a US-driven dollar surge can force central banks from Indonesia to Chile to hike rates in self-defense. The dollar exports US monetary conditions to the world through the invoicing channel, whether those countries want them or not.

**Job two: the funding currency — the world's borrowing language.** This is the most underappreciated job and the one that turns the dollar into a wrecking ball. Companies and governments outside the United States borrow in dollars — a *lot* of them. A Brazilian utility issues dollar bonds. A Turkish property developer takes a dollar loan. A Chinese airline leases planes priced and financed in dollars. They do this because dollar funding is deep, liquid, and often cheaper than borrowing at home. The result is a vast pool of dollar-denominated debt sitting on the balance sheets of borrowers who *earn their income in something else*. That mismatch is the fault line. We'll come back to its exact size — it is roughly \$13 trillion — because it is the single most important number in this whole story.

**Job three: the reserve asset — the world's savings vault.** When central banks accumulate foreign-exchange reserves (the national rainy-day fund of foreign money), they need somewhere safe and liquid to park it. The dominant choice, by a wide margin, is US Treasury securities — IOUs of the US government. Treasuries are the deepest, most liquid market on the planet, and in a crisis everyone wants them. So central banks the world over hold dollars not because they love America but because, when they need to defend their own currency or pay for imports in an emergency, dollars are what *works*. Around 58% of all disclosed official reserves are held in dollars. The next currency, the euro, is under 20%.

Here is why bundling the three jobs into one currency is so consequential. Each job individually creates demand for dollars. Together, they create a *structural, always-on, planet-wide* demand for dollars that has almost nothing to do with the US economy on any given day. The dollar is not just America's currency that foreigners happen to use; it is global plumbing. And plumbing has a property worth respecting: when the pressure in the pipes changes, every faucet in the building feels it at once. A rising dollar is rising pressure in the world's financial plumbing, and the faucets that feel it first are the weakest ones — emerging markets, leveraged risk assets, and anything bought with borrowed dollars.

One more foundational point before we go deeper, because beginners trip on it constantly. **"The dollar going up" and "the dollar being strong" are statements about a *ratio*, not an absolute.** A currency has no price by itself — only a price relative to something else. "The dollar rose 10%" always means "the dollar rose 10% against *some other currency or basket*." So when we say "the dollar," we have to immediately ask: against *what*? That question is exactly what the DXY answers — and, as we'll see, the answer is narrower than most people assume.

### How the dollar got the job (and why it's stickier than it looks)

It helps to know that the dollar's role was not the inevitable order of the universe — it was built, deliberately, in a hotel in New Hampshire. In July 1944, with World War II still raging, delegates from 44 nations met at **Bretton Woods** and designed the postwar monetary system around the dollar. The deal was simple: other currencies would be pegged to the dollar, and the dollar would be pegged to gold at \$35 an ounce, redeemable by foreign governments. The US held most of the world's gold and was the only large economy left standing, so the dollar became the hub and everything else a spoke. That system formally died in 1971 when the US ended gold convertibility (the "Nixon shock") and currencies began to float — which is exactly why the DXY was created in 1973, to measure a dollar that now floated freely instead of being fixed.

But here's the thing that confuses people: when the gold anchor was cut, the dollar's *dominance did not end* — it arguably deepened. Why? Because by then the dollar's three jobs had become self-reinforcing in a way that no longer needed gold. The world already priced oil and commodities in dollars, already borrowed in dollars, already held reserves in dollars, and already had the deepest, most liquid markets in dollars. Each of those facts makes the others stickier. You invoice trade in dollars because everyone else does, which means you hold dollar reserves, which means you want a deep dollar bond market to hold them in, which makes dollars the natural thing to borrow — round and round. Economists call this a **network effect**, the same force that keeps everyone on the same messaging app even when better ones exist: the value of using the dollar comes mostly from *everyone else also using it*. A network is brutally hard to dislodge, because to switch you need everyone to switch at once, and no one wants to go first. That is the real reason de-dollarization is glacial, and we'll quantify it later.

This network has a second consequence that matters for trading: it makes the dollar **reflexive**. Because so many actors *must* hold or obtain dollars, dollar strength tends to *create the conditions for more dollar strength*, and dollar weakness begets weakness. When the dollar rises and squeezes borrowers, they scramble to buy more dollars to cover, pushing it higher still. When it falls and liquidity floods out into the world, that liquidity buys risk assets that attract yet more capital out of dollars. The dollar doesn't just *reflect* global conditions — it *amplifies* them. That reflexivity is why dollar moves trend so hard and so long, and why they're so dangerous to fade early.

Notice also what this means about *control*. The Federal Reserve sets the price of *domestic* dollars — the federal funds rate — but it has only indirect, partial control over the vast pool of *offshore* dollars that the rest of the world depends on. No single institution governs the eurodollar system; it is a privately created, lightly regulated web of dollar credit spanning every major financial center. So when that offshore system seizes up, the Fed has to reach *outside* its normal mandate to fix it — through emergency dollar swap lines to foreign central banks, which we'll see in the 2020 episode. The mechanics of how the Fed steers the domestic side of all this are covered in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates); for our purposes, the key foundational insight is that the dollar is *bigger than the Fed* — a global system that America launched but does not fully command.

## What the DXY actually measures (and what it misses)

The **DXY**, or US Dollar Index, is the single most-quoted number for "the dollar" on financial TV, in news headlines, and on trading screens. It was created by the Federal Reserve in 1973, right after the world abandoned the gold-backed Bretton Woods system and currencies began floating freely. Its job was to give one number for the dollar's value against a basket of major trading partners' currencies. It started at a base of 100. So when you see "DXY at 105," that literally means the dollar is about 5% stronger, against this specific basket, than it was at the 1973 starting line.

The crucial, widely-misunderstood fact is *what is in the basket*. The DXY is a **fixed-weight** index of just six currencies, and those weights were set in 1999 (when the euro launched and replaced the legacy European currencies) and have never been rebalanced since. The weights are:

![DXY basket weights with the euro at 57.6 percent dominating the index](/imgs/blogs/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy-3.png)

- **Euro (EUR): 57.6%** — by far the dominant component
- **Japanese yen (JPY): 13.6%**
- **British pound (GBP): 11.9%**
- **Canadian dollar (CAD): 9.1%**
- **Swedish krona (SEK): 4.2%**
- **Swiss franc (CHF): 3.6%**

Stare at that for a second. The euro is **57.6%** of the index. That means the DXY is much closer to a "dollar versus Europe" gauge than a "dollar versus the world" measure. The euro alone moves the DXY more than the other five currencies *combined*. If you only watch the DXY, you are, to a first approximation, watching the EUR/USD exchange rate flipped upside down.

#### Worked example: how a 10% euro move drives the index

Suppose the euro strengthens 10% against the dollar — say EUR/USD goes from 1.10 to 1.21. How much does that move the DXY, all else equal? The euro's weight in the index is 57.6%, so a 10% move in the euro contributes roughly its weight times the move to the index. The mechanics of the DXY use a geometric (multiplicative) weighting, but the linear approximation is close enough to reason with: a 10% EUR appreciation pushes the DXY *down* (a stronger euro means a weaker dollar) by roughly 0.576 × 10% ≈ **5.8%**. So if the DXY started at 100, a 10% euro rally drops it to about 94.2 *on the euro alone*, before any of the other five currencies move at all. Now flip it: if the yen — weight 13.6% — moves 10%, it shifts the index by only about 1.4%. The takeaway: when you trade the DXY, you are mostly trading your view on the euro, and a "dollar" move that is really a euro move can fool you about the dollar's strength against the currencies that actually matter for global debt and trade.

That last point is the DXY's blind spot, and it's a big one. Look at what is *not* in the basket: there is **no Chinese yuan**, no Mexican peso, no Korean won, no Indian rupee, no Brazilian real — none of the emerging-market and Asian currencies that dominate today's actual trade flows and hold most of the world's dollar debt. The basket reflects the world of 1973 and 1999, not 2026. China is the world's largest trading nation and is entirely absent. This is why the DXY and "the dollar's true strength" can diverge: the DXY can be flat or falling against Europe while the dollar is *ripping* higher against the yuan and the peso, doing real damage to the parts of the world that the DXY simply doesn't see.

Traders who want a truer read use the **Fed's broad trade-weighted dollar index**, which includes the yuan, the peso, the won, and dozens of other partners weighted by actual trade. It's less famous and not directly tradable, but it's the better gauge of the dollar's pressure on the real global economy. The DXY's advantage is purely practical: it is liquid, it has deep futures and options markets, it is quoted on every screen, and you can put on a position in it in seconds. So we use it — but we use it knowing it is a *liquid proxy with a European tilt*, not a measurement of the dollar against the whole world.

#### Worked example: when the DXY and the real dollar disagree

Suppose over a quarter the euro *rallies* 4% against the dollar — maybe the European Central Bank turned hawkish — while the dollar simultaneously *rises* 6% against the Chinese yuan and 8% against a basket of emerging-market currencies, because global growth is wobbling and money is fleeing to dollars. What does the DXY show? The euro is 57.6% of the index, so the 4% euro rally drags the DXY *down* by roughly 0.576 × 4% ≈ 2.3%, and the other five rich-country currencies (yen, pound, and so on) barely move. So the **DXY falls about 2%** on the quarter. A trader watching only the DXY concludes "the dollar is weakening — risk-on, buy EM." But the dollar just rose 6–8% against the yuan and EM — it's *tightening the screws* on exactly the borrowers and economies that hold the world's dollar debt. The DXY said "weak dollar"; the real dollar said "tightening." A trader who bought EM off the falling DXY would have walked straight into a strengthening dollar where it actually mattered. The intuition: the DXY is a euro-cross in disguise, so always cross-check it against the yuan, the peso, and EM FX before you call the dollar's true direction.

A practical habit follows immediately. When the DXY moves, ask: *is this a euro story or a dollar story?* If the DXY rose because the European Central Bank turned dovish and the euro sank, that's a narrow EUR event that may say little about global dollar funding stress. But if the DXY rose *and* the yen, peso, won, and rand all fell *and* EM bond spreads widened, that's a broad dollar event — the kind that tightens conditions everywhere. Same DXY print, two completely different meanings. Reading which one you've got is half the skill.

## The eurodollar system: where offshore dollars actually live

Now for the deepest and most important piece of foundation — the one that explains *why* a rising dollar is dangerous rather than merely interesting. It has an unfortunately confusing name: the **eurodollar system**. Forget the "euro" part entirely; it has nothing to do with the European single currency. A "eurodollar" is simply a **US dollar held or created outside the United States, in the offshore banking system, beyond the direct reach of the Federal Reserve.** The name is a historical accident — the first such dollars pooled in European banks in the 1950s — but the concept is the engine of global finance.

Here's the everyday-money version. Imagine US dollars are a product, like a currency-shaped commodity. The Federal Reserve is the only entity that can print the *physical* base of them. But banks outside the US can *lend* dollars they hold, and when a bank makes a dollar loan, it creates a new dollar deposit somewhere else — a dollar that exists as a ledger entry in a London or Singapore or Hong Kong bank, never having touched US soil. These offshore dollars get lent, re-lent, borrowed against, and used to fund trade and investment all over the world. It is a sprawling, lightly mapped, privately created network of dollar credit that operates *on top of* the Fed's official money but largely *outside* its control. Estimates of the dollar credit extended to non-bank borrowers outside the US run to around **\$13 trillion**, and the gross eurodollar system is far larger.

Why does this matter for trading? Because that \$13 trillion is **debt that must be serviced in dollars by borrowers who do not earn dollars.** A Turkish company that borrowed dollars earns Turkish lira from its customers. As long as the lira is stable and dollars are easy to get, no problem — it converts some lira to dollars and pays its loan. But the moment the dollar strengthens, two things happen at once, and they compound viciously:

1. **The debt gets bigger in local-currency terms.** If the lira falls 30% against the dollar, then every dollar of debt now costs 30% more lira to repay, even though the dollar amount never changed. The borrower's balance sheet just deteriorated through no action of its own.
2. **Dollars get scarcer and more expensive to obtain.** A rising dollar is usually a sign that dollars are in demand and offshore dollar funding is tightening — exactly when the borrower needs to roll its loan, the lender is less willing to lend, and the price (the interest rate on dollars) goes up.

This is the mechanism. A rising dollar is a *margin call on the world's borrowers*. It simultaneously inflates their liabilities and chokes their access to the currency they owe. And because there are trillions of these positions held by companies and governments who never hedged them, a sharp dollar rally forces a scramble — borrowers and their banks rush to buy dollars to cover, which pushes the dollar *even higher*, which deepens the squeeze. It is a self-reinforcing feedback loop, and it is why "strong dollar" episodes so often turn into emerging-market crises. The dollar isn't strong because EM is in trouble; EM is in trouble *because* the dollar is strong.

There is a subtle but vital reason this is so much more dangerous than a regular debt problem: a **currency mismatch** removes the borrower's natural defense. Normally, if a company's home economy weakens, its central bank can cut rates and let the currency fall to cushion the blow — a weaker currency makes exports cheaper and the economy more competitive. But if that same company owes dollars, a falling home currency is *not* a cushion; it's an accelerant, because it makes the dollar debt bigger. The usual policy escape valve becomes a trap. The central bank that wants to cut rates to help the economy *can't*, because cutting would weaken the currency further and blow up the dollar borrowers — so it's often forced to *hike* into a slowdown to defend the currency, which strangles growth at exactly the wrong moment. This is why dollar-strength crises are so brutal for emerging markets: the dollar takes away their main tool for managing a downturn.

You can also see why the squeeze is *systemic* rather than isolated. When dollars get scarce offshore, it isn't just one borrower in trouble — it's all of them at once, because they all draw on the same pool of offshore dollar funding. A bank that lent dollars to a Turkish developer, a Brazilian utility, and an Indonesian miner finds all three under stress simultaneously, so it pulls back from *all* dollar lending to protect itself, which starves even healthy borrowers. The funding stress propagates through the network faster than any single fundamental story can explain. This is the difference between a *credit* problem (one borrower can't pay) and a *liquidity* problem (everyone needs dollars at once and there aren't enough to go around) — and a rising dollar manufactures the second kind, which is the kind that breaks markets. For a fuller treatment of how short-term dollar funding markets seize, the post on [shadow banking and the repo market](/blog/trading/finance/shadow-banking-and-the-repo-market) maps the plumbing in detail.

#### Worked example: an EM borrower when the DXY rises 10%

Let's make the wrecking-ball concrete. Take an emerging-market company — call it a Brazilian utility — that has borrowed **\$100 million** in dollar bonds. It earns Brazilian reais. At the start of the year, the exchange rate is 5.00 reais per dollar, so its debt is worth 500 million reais on its books. Now a global dollar surge hits: the DXY climbs 10%, and the real, being a high-beta EM currency, falls *harder* than the DXY basket — say it drops 18%, to about 6.10 reais per dollar.

The dollar debt is still exactly \$100 million — not one dollar more was borrowed. But in reais, that same debt is now worth 6.10 × \$100M = **610 million reais**, up from 500 million. The company's liabilities just grew by **110 million reais — a 22% balance-sheet hit — without the company doing anything at all.** Its revenue is in reais and hasn't risen; if anything, the import costs of its equipment (priced in dollars) just went up too. Meanwhile, when it goes to refinance, dollar funding is tighter and the interest rate it's quoted is higher. Now multiply this single company by thousands of borrowers across dozens of countries holding a chunk of that \$13 trillion, and you can see how a 10% move in an index of six rich-country currencies detonates across the developing world. The intuition: dollar debt held by non-dollar earners is a short position on their own currency, and a rising dollar is the squeeze.

The petrodollar arrangement — the long-standing practice of pricing oil in dollars and recycling oil-export revenues into dollar assets — is one more pillar reinforcing all of this; if you want the full story of how oil welded the dollar to the global system, see the deep dive on [the petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance). And because so much of the world *must* transact in dollars, control of the dollar payment rails becomes geopolitical leverage — the reason sanctions work at all — which is its own story in [SWIFT and the weaponization of payments](/blog/trading/finance/swift-and-the-weaponization-of-payments).

## The DXY in motion: a decade of dollar regimes

Theory is fine; let's look at what the dollar has actually done. The chart below is the DXY's year-end level from 2014 through 2025, with the September 2022 intraday peak marked. Notice that the dollar doesn't drift randomly — it moves in *regimes* that last years, driven by the relative growth and interest-rate path of the US versus everyone else.

![DXY year-end close from 2014 to 2025 with the 2022 peak marked](/imgs/blogs/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy-2.png)

Trace the path. In 2014–2015, the dollar surged from about 90 to nearly 99 as the Fed wound down its post-crisis bond-buying ("the taper") while Europe and Japan were still easing — the US was tightening while everyone else loosened, so capital flowed toward dollar yields. The dollar then chopped sideways at a high level through the late 2010s. It spiked briefly in March 2020 as the COVID panic sent the world scrambling for cash dollars (a classic risk-off dollar grab), then faded through 2020–2021 as the Fed slashed rates to zero and flooded the system with liquidity, weakening the dollar and fueling the everything-rally in stocks, EM, and crypto.

Then came 2022 — the year the dollar became a wrecking ball. As the Fed hiked rates faster than at any time in 40 years to fight inflation, while the rest of the world lagged, the DXY rocketed to a year-end close of 103.5 and an intraday peak of **114.8 on September 28**, a 20-year high and roughly +18% on the year at its top. That single move is the through-line connecting the yen crisis, the gilt blowup, the EM reserve drain, and the brutal year for commodities and crypto. After that, the dollar gave back ground in 2023, surged again to 108.5 by end-2024 on resilient US growth, then weakened materially through 2025 to around 97 as growth converged and the Fed eased. Each of those swings was a *regime* — and in each, the same assets responded the same way to the dollar's direction.

#### Worked example: the 2022 dollar move, peak to close

Let's quantify the 2022 wrecking-ball year using the numbers on the chart. The dollar entered 2022 with the DXY around 95.7 (its 2021 close). By **September 28, 2022, the DXY had hit 114.8** at its intraday peak — a rise of about (114.8 − 95.7) / 95.7 ≈ **+20%** from the start of the year. That is an enormous move for a major-currency index in nine months; the DXY normally moves a handful of percent in a *year*. It then pulled back to a **year-end close of 103.5** — still up about +8% on the year, but well off the peak. So a trader who was long the dollar from January and held through September captured roughly a 20% move in the index itself (and far more in leveraged FX terms, since the underlying currency pairs moved more), while a trader who faded the dollar at any point before September got run over. The pullback from 114.8 to 103.5 — about −10% off the high into year-end — is itself the *other* side of the trade: when a parabolic dollar finally turns, the relief rally in everything it was crushing (EM, commodities, risk) can be just as violent. The intuition: the dollar trends hard and long in its regimes, and the most dangerous trade is fading a strong dollar early.

## The dollar smile: why USD rises in both booms and panics

Here is the property of the dollar that confuses almost everyone and is the most useful thing to understand for trading it. Most assets have a clean directional story: stocks like growth and dislike recession, gold likes fear and dislikes calm. The dollar is weirder. **The dollar tends to be strong in two *opposite* environments — a global panic *and* a US-led boom — and weak only in the contented middle.** Plotted against the state of the world, dollar strength makes a U-shape. This is the famous **dollar smile**.

![The dollar smile showing USD strong in panic and in a US boom and weak in the middle](/imgs/blogs/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy-6.png)

Walk through the three regions of the smile, because each one is a different trade.

**The left side — global risk-off panic.** When something breaks and markets crater — 2008, March 2020, a banking scare, a war shock — investors everywhere sell risky assets and run to the safest, most liquid thing on earth: US dollars and US Treasury bills. This is the *safe-haven* dollar. It rises not because the US economy is doing well — it often isn't — but because in a fire, everyone wants the fire exit, and the dollar is the fire exit. The eurodollar mechanism turbocharges this: a panic makes offshore dollar funding seize up, borrowers scramble to buy dollars to cover their debts, and the dollar grabs higher. So when the world is on fire, the dollar goes up. Left side of the smile.

**The right side — a US-led boom.** When the US economy is growing faster than the rest of the world and the Fed is hiking rates to keep up, dollar assets offer the best combination of growth and yield on the planet. Capital flows *toward* the dollar to chase those returns. This is the *high-yield magnet* dollar — 2014–2015 and 2022 are textbook examples. It rises because America is outperforming and paying you more to hold dollars. Right side of the smile.

**The bottom — muddle-through.** In between — when the world is growing steadily, no one is panicking, US growth is unremarkable relative to everyone else, and the Fed is on hold or cutting — the dollar is *weak*. Why hold low-yielding, fairly-valued dollars when global growth is humming and you can earn more by putting capital into emerging markets, commodities, and risk assets abroad? In the muddle-through middle, capital *leaves* the dollar to chase returns elsewhere, the dollar softens, and EM and risk assets rally. The bottom of the smile is the "risk-on, dollar-down" world that bulls love.

The practical genius of the smile is that it tells you *what kind of dollar strength you're looking at*, which tells you what else to expect. A dollar rising on the **left** (panic) comes *with* falling stocks, widening credit spreads, and a VIX spike — it's confirming a risk-off regime, and you position defensively across the board. A dollar rising on the **right** (boom) comes *with* rising US yields and often resilient US stocks even as the rest of the world struggles — it's a relative-strength story, and you favor US assets over international ones. A dollar *falling* into the **middle** is the green light for the reflation trade: long EM, long commodities, long risk. Same dollar, three completely different playbooks, and the smile tells you which one is live. To go deeper on the rate-and-flow forces that bend the dollar around the smile, see the companion piece on [what moves exchange rates: rates, flows, and carry](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-moves-exchange-rates-rates-flows-carry), and on why the level of US rates sits underneath all of it, [interest rates: the price of money and the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable).

## Reserve share and de-dollarization: the slow, overstated trend

You will read, constantly, that the dollar's reign is ending — that "de-dollarization" is here, that the BRICS bloc is launching a rival currency, that the dollar is about to be dethroned. It makes for great headlines and terrible trading. Let's look at the actual data, because the gap between the narrative and the numbers is enormous.

The dollar's share of disclosed global FX reserves has indeed declined — but look at the *pace*.

![USD share of global FX reserves declining slowly from 2001 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy-4.png)

In 2001, the dollar was about **71.5%** of disclosed reserves. By 2024 it was about **57.8%**. That's a fall of roughly 13–14 percentage points — over **23 years**. That works out to about **0.6 percentage points a year**, a slow structural drift, not a collapse. And the share that the dollar has lost has *not* gone to the euro, the yen, or the Chinese yuan in any dramatic way. The euro's share has been roughly flat-to-down. The yuan, despite all the noise, sits at around 2% of reserves — a rounding error. Where did the lost dollar share actually go? Mostly into a scattering of *smaller* currencies — the Australian and Canadian dollars, the Swedish krona, the Korean won — central banks diversifying at the margins, plus a steady trickle into gold. It is diversification, not replacement.

#### Worked example: reserve-share math, 72% to 58%

Let's turn the percentages into dollars to feel the scale. Global disclosed FX reserves total roughly **\$12 trillion**. At the 2001 share of about 72%, that's 0.72 × \$12T = **\$8.64 trillion** held in dollars. At the 2024 share of about 58%, that's 0.58 × \$12T = **\$6.96 trillion** held in dollars. So even after two decades of "de-dollarization," central banks still hold nearly **\$7 trillion** in dollars — more than three times what they hold in the next currency, the euro, and more than every other reserve currency *combined*. The slope of decline, projected forward at 0.6 points a year, wouldn't take the dollar below a 50% majority until roughly the **2030s–2040s**, and that's assuming the trend continues in a straight line, which trends rarely do. The intuition: the dollar's reserve dominance is eroding at the speed of a glacier, and a glacier is not a trade.

Now look at the full 2024 reserve mix and the picture gets even clearer. The dollar isn't *a* leader; it's *the* leader by a margin that dwarfs everyone else.

![Global FX reserve currency mix in 2024 with the US dollar far ahead](/imgs/blogs/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy-5.png)

The dollar at ~58% towers over the euro at ~20%, the yen at ~6%, the pound at ~5%, and the yuan at a mere ~2%. The next *five* reserve currencies combined are smaller than the dollar alone. There is, today, simply no rival that can do the dollar's three jobs at scale. The euro is a serious second but is hamstrung by the eurozone's lack of a single, deep, safe-asset market (there is no pan-European Treasury) and by recurring existential doubts about the union itself. The yuan can't be a reserve currency while China maintains capital controls — you can't be the world's reserve asset if foreigners can't freely move money in and out of you. Gold is rising as a reserve asset and is a genuine signal worth watching, but gold can't be lent into the global system to fund trade and pay debts the way dollars can; it's a store of value, not a working currency.

None of this means the dollar is invincible forever — reserve currencies *have* been dethroned (the British pound lost the crown to the dollar across the first half of the 20th century, a process that took *decades*). The point for a trader is one of *time horizon*: de-dollarization is a real, slow, structural trend that matters on a multi-decade view, and it is *almost never* the right explanation for what the dollar did this week, this month, or this year. Trading the daily dollar off a de-dollarization thesis is like trying to time the tide by watching continental drift.

## Common misconceptions

Four beliefs about the dollar are widespread, intuitive, and wrong. Each one, corrected with a number, sharpens how you trade.

**Misconception 1: "The DXY measures the dollar against the world."** No — it measures the dollar against *six rich-country currencies, 57.6% of it the euro*, with no yuan, peso, won, or rupee anywhere in the basket. The DXY can fall against Europe while the dollar is *rising* hard against the currencies that hold most of the world's dollar debt. When you need the dollar's true global pressure, look at the Fed's broad trade-weighted index, not the DXY. The DXY is a *tradable proxy*, not a measurement — use it for execution, cross-check it for analysis.

**Misconception 2: "De-dollarization is imminent — the dollar is about to be dethroned."** The dollar's reserve share fell from ~72% to ~58% over **23 years** — about 0.6 points a year — and the lost share went mostly to small currencies and gold, *not* to the euro or the yuan (which sits at ~2%). At that pace, the dollar holds a majority of reserves into the **2030s–2040s** even if the drift continues unbroken. De-dollarization is a multi-decade glacier, not a this-cycle event. Positioning short the dollar on de-dollarization headlines has been a money-loser for a decade.

**Misconception 3: "America's trade deficits and debt will sink the dollar."** The intuition — a country that imports more than it exports and runs huge deficits "should" have a falling currency — is exactly backwards for the reserve currency. The US runs persistent trade deficits *precisely because* the world wants to accumulate dollars and dollar assets; foreigners send the US goods and take back dollars and Treasuries, which they *want* to hold. The trade deficit is the *mechanism* by which the world gets the reserve dollars it needs (economists call the resulting bind the "Triffin dilemma"). The dollar surged to 20-year highs in 2022 with the US running record deficits. Deficits and debt are real long-run risks, but they have repeatedly coincided with a *strong* dollar, not a weak one.

**Misconception 4: "A strong dollar is good — it means America is winning."** A strong dollar is good for American *tourists* and *importers* and signals US relative strength, but for markets it is usually a *tightening* — it pressures US multinational earnings (foreign revenue translates back into fewer dollars), it crushes emerging markets and commodities, and it drains global liquidity. The 2022 super-strong dollar coincided with one of the worst years for global stocks, bonds, and crypto on record. "Strong dollar = good" confuses the dollar being a *winner* with the dollar being *easy*; for risk assets, a rising dollar is almost always a headwind regardless of *why* it's rising.

## How it shows up in real markets

Mechanisms are abstract until you see them detonate. Here are the dollar's three jobs and the dollar smile playing out in real episodes, with real numbers.

**The 2022 dollar surge as a global wrecking ball.** This is the canonical case, and it ties together everything above. As the Fed hiked from near 0% to 4.5% in a single year — faster than any tightening in four decades — while Europe and especially Japan lagged badly, the DXY rocketed +20% to its 114.8 peak. The right side of the dollar smile, on steroids. The damage radiated exactly along the transmission channels: the **yen** collapsed past 145 and then 150 per dollar (Japan's first FX intervention since 1998), the **pound** crashed to a record low near 1.03 and triggered a UK pension crisis and emergency BoE intervention, **emerging-market** currencies fell and their central banks hiked into weakness and burned reserves to defend their pegs, **commodities** rolled over (a stronger dollar makes dollar-priced oil and copper more expensive in every other currency, denting demand), **gold** fell despite being a "safe haven" because the *real* safe haven — the dollar with rising yields attached — outcompeted it, and **crypto** completed its bear market with Bitcoin down roughly 75% from its peak. None of these were independent stories. They were one story: the dollar going up, transmitting through trade, debt, and reserves.

![How a rising dollar transmits into a global headwind across markets](/imgs/blogs/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy-7.png)

**Dollar funding squeezes — March 2020 and the swap lines.** When COVID hit, the world didn't sell dollars — it *desperately bought* them. Everyone with dollar liabilities scrambled for dollars at once, offshore dollar funding markets seized, and the DXY spiked violently in a matter of days even as the US economy was collapsing. This is the left side of the smile and the eurodollar mechanism in pure form: a panic *is* a dollar shortage. The squeeze was so severe that the Federal Reserve had to step in as the world's de facto central bank, reopening "swap lines" — emergency facilities that lend dollars to foreign central banks (the ECB, BoJ, and others) so they can pass dollars to their own banks and relieve the shortage. The fact that *only the Fed can ultimately make the dollar shortage go away* is the most vivid possible proof of the dollar's centrality: in a global dollar crisis, every central bank on earth waits for the Fed to open the spigot. Watch the swap-line balances and the cross-currency basis (the premium foreigners pay to borrow dollars) — when they blow out, the dollar is the most important thing in markets and nothing else matters until they normalize.

**The 2014–2015 taper-tantrum dollar.** A gentler version of 2022, and instructive precisely because there was no crisis. The Fed merely *signaled* it would slow its bond-buying while Europe and Japan eased further, and the DXY climbed from ~90 to ~99. The right side of the smile without the panic. The consequences were still real: commodities entered a brutal bear market (oil fell from over \$100 to under \$30 by early 2016), emerging-market currencies and equities underperformed for years, and capital rotated decisively into US assets. The lesson: the dollar doesn't need a crisis to do damage; it just needs to be *rising* because the US is out-tightening the world.

**2020–2021 weak dollar and the everything rally.** The mirror image, and the bottom of the smile. With the Fed at zero and flooding the system with liquidity, the dollar slid through 2020–2021, and *everything* priced against it ripped: emerging markets recovered, commodities rallied, and crypto went on one of its largest bull runs ever, with Bitcoin running from under \$10,000 to nearly \$69,000. It was not a coincidence that the wildest risk-on melt-up in a generation happened during the weakest-dollar stretch in years. A falling dollar is liquidity flooding *into* the global system; a rising dollar is liquidity draining *out*. The tide, again.

**2025's dollar give-back.** After the 2024 surge to 108.5 on resilient US growth, the dollar weakened materially through 2025 toward ~97 as global growth converged toward the US, the Fed eased, and the extreme US-outperformance premium faded. The smile slid from the right edge back toward the middle — and, predictably, the assets that had struggled under the strong dollar got their relief: EM currencies stabilized, commodities found a floor, and risk appetite improved. The reflexive lesson of every cycle: the dollar's regime shifts are *the* signal for when to rotate into or out of the risk trade, and the turn off a strong-dollar peak is one of the most reliable risk-on catalysts there is.

## How to trade it / The playbook

Everything above collapses into one operating principle: **the dollar is the global liquidity tide, and the DXY is your risk dial.** You do not need to trade the dollar directly to use it. You read it as the master variable that tells you whether the wind is at your back or in your face for almost every other position you hold. Here is the playbook.

**Make the DXY a permanent overlay on every risk position.** Before you size up any long in equities, EM, commodities, or crypto, look at the dollar's direction and regime. A rising DXY is a headwind for *all* of them simultaneously — it's the single most efficient "risk-off lens" you have. The rule of thumb that survives contact with reality: **DXY up = global conditions tightening = lean defensive; DXY down = liquidity flooding in = lean risk-on.** When the dollar is trending higher, demand more of everything from your risk longs — better setups, smaller size, tighter stops — because you're swimming against the tide. When the dollar is trending lower or breaking down, the tide is with you and risk longs work easier.

**Diagnose *which* dollar you've got using the smile.** A rising dollar is not one signal — it's two opposite ones depending on the regime, and confusing them is the most common error. Cross-check the dollar's move against equities, credit spreads, and the VIX:

- Dollar up **+** stocks down **+** credit spreads widening **+** VIX spiking → you're on the **left of the smile** (panic). Get defensive across the board, raise cash, expect correlations to go to one. Watch for a dollar-funding squeeze (the cross-currency basis and Fed swap lines).
- Dollar up **+** US stocks resilient **+** US yields rising **+** the *rest* of the world underperforming → you're on the **right of the smile** (US boom). Favor US assets over international, fade EM and commodities on rallies, and respect that this regime can run for *years*.
- Dollar *falling* **+** EM outperforming **+** commodities firm → you're sliding toward the **bottom** (muddle-through reflation). This is the green light for the risk trade: long EM, long commodities, long high-beta risk including crypto.

**Watch the funding plumbing, not just the level.** The most dangerous dollar moves are funding squeezes, and they show up in the plumbing before the headlines. The **cross-currency basis** (how much extra foreigners pay to borrow dollars via swaps) blowing out, and **Fed swap-line usage** rising, are the tells that an offshore dollar shortage is building — the eurodollar mechanism cranking up. When those stress, the dollar is about to become the only thing that matters; flatten risk and wait for the Fed to relieve it. When they're calm, the dollar's moves are "normal" and you can trade the regime.

To turn that into a concrete dashboard, here is what actually belongs on the screen next to the DXY. First, the **DXY's level *and* its rate of change** — a slow grind has a very different meaning than a parabolic spike; the spikes are what break things. Second, **EUR/USD and USD/JPY**, the two biggest basket components, so you can immediately tell a euro story from a yen story from a true broad-dollar story. Third, a **proxy for the dollar against China and EM** — USD/CNH (the offshore yuan) and an EM-currency index — because that's where the DXY is blind and where the real funding damage shows first. Fourth, the **funding-stress gauges**: the three-month cross-currency basis for euro and yen, and any reporting on Fed swap-line drawings; when the basis goes sharply negative, dollars are scarce offshore. Fifth, the **risk cross-checks** — the S&P 500, high-yield credit spreads, and the VIX — to place yourself on the dollar smile. A trader who glances at those six things knows, in about ten seconds, whether the dollar is the story today, which *kind* of dollar move it is, and whether to be leaning into risk or out of it. That ten-second read is worth more than most multi-page macro notes, because it's the master variable that conditions everything else.

**Use EM and commodities as the dollar's amplifier — and your early warning.** Because EM currencies and dollar-priced commodities are the *most* sensitive to the dollar (high beta to the DXY through the trade and debt channels), they often move *first* and *more*. A grinding selloff in EM FX and a roll-over in copper while the DXY is still calm can be an early signal that a broad dollar move is starting. Conversely, EM FX stabilizing and copper basing while the dollar is still strong can flag that a dollar peak is forming and the relief rally is near.

**Respect the trend; never fade a strong dollar early.** The dollar moves in multi-year regimes driven by *relative* US growth and rate paths, and those regimes trend hard — recall the +20% melt-up into September 2022. The graveyard of macro traders is full of people who called the dollar's top three times on the way up. Wait for the *turn* — a clear break of trend, a Fed that's now easing relative to the rest of the world, a peak in the US-outperformance story — before betting against a strong dollar. The turn, when it comes, is violent and tradable on the *other* side (the 114.8-to-103.5 give-back into year-end 2022, and the 2025 fade, were both monster relief rallies in everything the dollar had crushed). But early is wrong.

**Anchor on the one number.** When the headlines scream that the dollar is finished, that de-dollarization is here, that the BRICS currency is coming — anchor on the math: the dollar's reserve share is drifting down about **0.6 points a year**, it still sits near **58%** (nearly \$7 trillion), the yuan is **~2%**, and roughly **\$13 trillion of dollar debt** sits on non-American balance sheets that a rising dollar squeezes. The structural decline is real and worth a multi-decade allocation thought; it is *not* a this-cycle trade. For this cycle, the dollar is still the tide, the DXY is still your dial, and the rule still holds: **when the dollar rises, the world tightens — so read the dollar first, and position everything else around it.**
