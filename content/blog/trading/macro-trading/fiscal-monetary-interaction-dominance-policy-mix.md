> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Fiscal policy (the government's spending and taxing) and monetary policy (the central bank's interest rate) are two separate levers that almost never get pulled by the same hand — and when they point in opposite directions, the "policy mix" that results defines the whole market regime.
>
> - The **policy mix** is a 2x2: loose-or-tight fiscal crossed with loose-or-tight money. Both loose is a boom; both tight is a squeeze; the off-diagonal cases are where the levers *fight*, and those are the confusing, dangerous regimes traders get wrong.
> - In **2022-24** the United States ran the rarest and tensest mix in the box: the Fed slammed the brakes to a 5.50% policy rate while the Treasury floored the gas with deficits near \$1.8 trillion a year. Money and fiscal were openly pulling apart.
> - **Fiscal dominance** is the regime risk that matters most for the next decade: once government debt is huge and short-dated, every rate hike feeds straight back into the budget as interest, until the central bank can no longer tighten without blowing up the deficit it ultimately answers to.
> - The one number to remember: US federal **net interest** nearly tripled from \$345 billion in 2020 to about \$1 trillion in 2025 — in 2024 the interest bill passed the entire defense budget for the first time. That rising bill is the leash on the central bank, and watching it is the core of the trade.

In the autumn of 2023, two of the most powerful institutions on the planet were standing on the same patch of economic ground and pushing in exactly opposite directions, and almost nobody outside a macro desk was framing it that way.

On one side was the Federal Reserve. It had spent eighteen months executing the most aggressive interest-rate hiking campaign in four decades, dragging its policy rate from near zero up to a 5.50% ceiling by July 2023 and then holding it there. The entire point of that campaign was to *tighten* — to make money expensive, cool demand, and choke off the inflation that had spiked to a 9% annual rate in mid-2022. Tight money is the brakes.

On the other side was the United States Treasury, the borrowing arm of the federal government. While the Fed was jamming on the brakes, the government was running a budget deficit of roughly \$1.7 trillion in fiscal 2023 and \$1.83 trillion in fiscal 2024 — spending vastly more than it taxed, pumping that gap directly into the economy as income for households, contractors, and bondholders. A big deficit is *loose* fiscal policy. Loose fiscal is the gas.

![Policy-mix 2x2 with fiscal on one axis and monetary on the other, four labeled quadrant regimes](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix-1.png)

So you had one foot flooring the accelerator and the other stamping the brake. That is not a footnote — it is the single most important reason the US economy refused to slow the way every textbook said it should. The Fed's brake was real, but the Treasury's gas pedal was partly cancelling it out, which is why growth stayed firm, why "higher for longer" became the phrase of the cycle, and why the soft landing surprised so many forecasters. And lurking underneath that tug-of-war was a slower, scarier question that this entire post builds toward: *how long can a central bank keep hiking when every hike makes the government's own debt more expensive to carry?* That question has a name — **fiscal dominance** — and it is, in my view, the defining macro risk of the next ten years.

This post assumes you have never thought about either lever before. We will define fiscal policy and monetary policy from zero, build the policy-mix 2x2 brick by brick, walk through what each of the four regimes does to growth, inflation, and the dollar, and then go deep on fiscal dominance: what it is, the famous piece of arithmetic that proves it can happen, how monetary financing and the inflation tax fit in, and — most importantly — how a trader reads the mix, spots the dominance tripwire, and positions around it. The mechanism is not academic. It is already showing up in the price of gold, in the steepness of the yield curve, and in how much the world trusts a long-dated government bond.

## Foundations: the two levers, the policy mix, and what each piece means

Everything in this post rests on cleanly separating two things that beginners — and plenty of professionals — blur together. Let us define every piece from the ground up.

### Lever one: monetary policy (the central bank's rate)

**Monetary policy** is what a country's central bank does to control the price and quantity of money. In the United States that central bank is the Federal Reserve ("the Fed"). Its main tool is the **policy interest rate** — the rate at which banks lend to each other overnight, which the Fed steers within a target range. When the Fed *raises* that rate, borrowing across the whole economy — mortgages, car loans, business credit, margin loans — gets more expensive, which cools spending and, eventually, inflation. That is **tight** (or "restrictive") money: the brakes. When the Fed *cuts* the rate, borrowing gets cheaper, spending picks up, and risk-taking is encouraged. That is **loose** (or "accommodative") money: the gas. (If you want the nuts and bolts of how the Fed actually moves that rate, the companion post on [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) walks through the plumbing.)

The Fed has a second tool too — its **balance sheet**. By buying bonds (quantitative easing, or QE) it adds money to the system; by letting bonds roll off (quantitative tightening, or QT) it drains money. We cover that machinery in depth in [QE vs QT: how balance-sheet policy moves markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets) and the [central-bank toolkit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance). For our purposes the key idea is simple: the central bank controls *money* — its price (the rate) and its quantity (the balance sheet).

The crucial institutional fact: in most developed economies the central bank is, by law and by tradition, **independent** of the elected government. The Fed sets rates without needing the President's or Congress's permission. That independence is the whole reason the policy mix can be a *conflict* in the first place — if one hand controlled both levers, they would never fight.

### Lever two: fiscal policy (the government's spending and taxing)

**Fiscal policy** is what the elected government does with its budget: how much it spends and how much it taxes. The gap between the two is the **deficit** (spending greater than taxes) or, rarely, the **surplus** (taxes greater than spending). In the US, fiscal policy is set by Congress and the President; it is executed by the Treasury, which raises the cash to cover any deficit by selling government bonds at auction. (How that bond selling itself moves markets is the subject of [deficits, debt, and bond supply](/blog/trading/macro-trading/deficits-debt-bond-supply-why-issuance-moves-yields).)

When the government runs a *big* deficit — spending far more than it taxes — it is injecting net new money into the private economy. Every dollar of deficit is a dollar of income for someone: a defense contractor, a Social Security recipient, a hospital paid by Medicare, a bondholder collecting interest. A big deficit is **loose** (or "expansionary") fiscal policy: the gas. When the government runs a surplus or sharply cuts spending and raises taxes, it pulls money *out* of the private economy. That is **tight** (or "contractionary") fiscal policy — austerity — the brakes.

Here is the mental model to lock in: **monetary policy controls the price of money; fiscal policy controls the flow of net new money from the government into the economy.** They are different levers, held by different hands, aimed at overlapping targets (growth, inflation, employment). And because the hands are different, the levers can agree or disagree.

### The policy mix: the 2x2 that defines the regime

Now cross the two. Fiscal can be loose or tight. Money can be loose or tight. Two choices on each axis give four combinations — the **policy mix**. This is the master frame of the whole post, and it is worth carrying in your head like a compass:

- **Both loose** — fiscal gas + monetary gas. Maximum stimulus. Growth surges, inflation builds, the currency tends to weaken. This is the 2020-21 pandemic response: trillions in stimulus checks *and* a zero policy rate.
- **Both tight** — fiscal brakes + monetary brakes. Maximum squeeze. Growth slows hard, inflation falls, recession risk is high, the currency can swing either way. This is the rarest quadrant in modern times — almost nobody runs austerity *into* a hiking cycle, because it courts a deep recession.
- **Loose fiscal + tight money** — gas + brakes, the levers fighting. Big deficits keep demand alive while high rates try to kill it. The result is sticky growth, slow disinflation, "higher for longer" rates, and a *strong* currency (we will see why). This is 2022-24, and it is the quadrant where fiscal dominance starts to bite.
- **Tight fiscal + loose money** — brakes + gas. Low deficits give the central bank room to keep rates easy. This was much of the late 1990s, when budget surpluses let the Fed run a relatively relaxed policy — often remembered as a "good mix" because disciplined budgets and cheap money coexisted without runaway inflation.

The diagonal cases (both loose, both tight) are **coordination**: the levers reinforce each other and the macro signal is loud and easy to read. The off-diagonal cases (one loose, one tight) are **conflict**: the levers partly cancel, the signal is muddy, and the trade becomes a question of *which lever wins*. Most of the interesting — and most of the dangerous — macro happens off the diagonal.

### Two more terms we need: fiscal dominance and monetary financing

Two pieces of vocabulary will carry the back half of this post, so let us plant them now and define them properly later.

**Fiscal dominance** is the regime in which the government's budget situation — specifically, the size of its debt and interest bill — becomes so large that it constrains what the central bank can do. In a fiscally dominant world, the central bank is no longer fully free to set rates wherever inflation demands; it has to keep one eye on whether its rate decisions are bankrupting the Treasury. Monetary policy bends to serve fiscal needs. The opposite, healthier state is **monetary dominance**, where the central bank sets rates purely for inflation and the government adjusts its budget to live with whatever rate results.

**Monetary financing** (also called "monetizing the debt") is the most extreme way fiscal dominance shows up: the central bank effectively prints money to fund the government's deficit, buying the bonds the Treasury issues so the government never has to find real savers to lend to it. Done at scale, this is the classic road to high inflation — and the **inflation tax**, which we will define carefully, is the mechanism by which it quietly erodes the real value of debt and savings.

### Monetary dominance vs fiscal dominance — who adjusts to whom

The cleanest way to hold the two regimes in your head is to ask a single question: *when the budget and the inflation target collide, which one gives way?*

Under **monetary dominance** — the healthy, post-Volcker norm in developed economies — the central bank gives no ground. It sets rates wherever inflation requires, and the fiscal authority is forced to adjust: if higher rates make the debt more expensive, the government must cut spending, raise taxes, or accept a smaller deficit to live within its means. The inflation target is the anchor; the budget bends around it. Paul Volcker's brutal 1980-82 rate shock — taking the policy rate above 19% to break double-digit inflation, with no regard for the recession or the fiscal pain it caused — is the textbook image of monetary dominance in action. (We tell that whole story in [Paul Volcker's 1980 rate shock](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation).) The Fed won because, ultimately, the government accepted the cost.

Under **fiscal dominance**, the arrow reverses. The budget is the anchor — the deficits are treated as politically fixed, untouchable — and the central bank is the one that must bend. When tight money threatens the budget, it is *monetary* policy that gives way: rates stay lower than inflation wants, the balance sheet stays larger, and the inflation target quietly becomes negotiable. The central bank's mandate is still inflation on paper, but in practice it is subordinated to keeping the government solvent.

The reason this distinction is the whole ballgame: the *same* tight-money policy has opposite consequences depending on which regime you are in. Under monetary dominance, a hike crushes inflation and the budget absorbs the hit. Under fiscal dominance, that same hike inflates the deficit, forces more issuance, and — per the unpleasant arithmetic we will reach shortly — can *raise* future inflation rather than lower it. You cannot trade the central bank's hike without first knowing which regime you are standing in.

Hold those two terms loosely for now. The point of the foundations is the frame: two levers, four regimes, and a back door — fiscal dominance — through which the budget can reach into the central bank's job. Now we go deep.

## The four quadrants: what each mix does to growth, inflation, and the dollar

Let us walk the four corners of the box one at a time, because each one produces a recognizable market regime, and recognizing the regime is half the trade.

**Both loose (boom).** When the government floods the economy with deficit spending *and* the central bank holds rates on the floor, you get the most powerful possible push on demand. Households have stimulus income *and* cheap credit. Businesses have customers *and* low borrowing costs. The result is fast nominal growth — and, if the push outruns the economy's ability to produce real goods, **inflation**. Because cheap money and a flood of new currency make the unit itself less scarce, the currency tends to *weaken* in this regime. Risk assets — stocks, credit, crypto, gold — usually love it, at least until inflation forces the central bank to take the punchbowl away. The canonical example is 2020-21: roughly \$5 trillion in pandemic fiscal packages (the \$2.2 trillion CARES Act, \$0.9 trillion in December 2020, the \$1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan) landing on top of a zero policy rate and massive QE. The boom was real, and so was the 9% inflation that followed.

**Both tight (squeeze).** Flip everything. The government cuts spending or raises taxes while the central bank hikes hard. Demand gets hit from both sides. Growth slows sharply, unemployment rises, inflation falls fast — and recession risk is high enough that almost no government deliberately chooses this corner. When it happens it is usually forced: a country in a debt crisis pushed into austerity *while* its central bank defends the currency with high rates. Risk assets suffer; the safest government bonds can rally as growth fears dominate.

**Loose fiscal + tight money (the fight).** This is the quadrant that defines 2022-24 and deserves the most attention, because it is the least intuitive. The government keeps pumping demand into the economy through big deficits while the central bank tries to drain it with high rates. The two effects partly cancel, so growth stays stubbornly firm and inflation falls only slowly — which forces the central bank to keep rates *higher for longer* than it otherwise would. Now the surprising part: the currency usually gets *stronger* in this mix, not weaker. Why? Because high rates attract foreign capital chasing yield, and a firm economy plus tight money makes the currency a magnet. The US dollar index (DXY) climbed from around 95.7 at the end of 2021 to a multi-decade high near 114.8 in September 2022 precisely as this mix took hold. So the "fight" regime reads as: sticky growth, slow disinflation, a strong dollar, and a steady grind higher in the government's interest bill — the last of which sets up fiscal dominance.

**Tight fiscal + loose money (the good mix).** The government runs disciplined budgets — small deficits or surpluses — which gives the central bank room to keep rates low without stoking inflation. Demand comes from the private sector (cheap credit) rather than the public sector (deficits). This is widely seen as the most sustainable corner: the late-1990s US ran budget surpluses while the Fed kept policy fairly easy, and you got strong growth with contained inflation. The currency tends to be firm (sound budgets inspire confidence) and bonds behave well (low supply, credible inflation). The catch is that it requires political willingness to run tight budgets — which, as we will see, is exactly what is in short supply.

#### Worked example: the 2022-24 conflicting mix in numbers

Let us make the "fight" quadrant concrete with the real settings. On the monetary side, the Fed took its target range upper bound from 0.25% (where it sat in early 2022) all the way to **5.50%** by July 2023 — a 525-basis-point tightening, the steepest since the early 1980s. That is the brakes, slammed.

On the fiscal side, over the same window the federal government ran deficits of about **\$1.38 trillion** (fiscal 2022), **\$1.70 trillion** (2023), and **\$1.83 trillion** (2024) — roughly 5.4%, 6.2%, and 6.3% of GDP. That is the gas, floored, at a level of deficit normally seen only in recessions or wars — except the economy was *growing* the whole time.

Put the two together: imagine the economy as a car. The Fed's foot is on the brake hard enough to stop a normal car cold. But the Treasury's foot is on a gas pedal feeding the engine roughly 6% of GDP in extra fuel every year. The car does not stop — it just gets harder to control, burns more fuel, and the engine (the interest bill) runs hot. The point lands in one line: **when one lever pushes 525 basis points of tightening and the other pushes 6% of GDP of stimulus, the economy does not respond like the textbook says, because the textbook assumes only one lever is moving.**

![Dual-axis chart of Fed funds rate stepping up while the federal deficit stays large from 2021 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix-2.png)

The figure above is the whole "fight" in one picture. The blue step path is the Fed funds rate marching from the floor to 5.50% — tight money. The red bars are the federal deficit running at 5-6% of GDP the entire time — loose fiscal. Two policy hands, opposite directions, on the same economy. The reason "higher for longer" became the cycle's defining phrase is sitting right there: the brake had to stay clamped because the gas would not let up.

## Fiscal dominance: when the interest bill ties the central bank's hands

We have now seen that the levers can fight. The deeper question is whether the *fight itself* can disable one of the levers. It can — and the mechanism is fiscal dominance.

### The core mechanic

Start from a government with a large stock of debt. The US, at the time of writing, owes about **\$37 trillion** in total federal debt — roughly 123% of GDP. Crucially, a big chunk of that debt is *short-dated*: bills and short notes that mature within a year or two and have to be rolled over — refinanced by issuing fresh bonds — constantly. (The mechanics of that rolling-over are in [Treasury issuance: bills, coupons, and the liquidity drain](/blog/trading/macro-trading/treasury-issuance-bills-coupons-liquidity-drain).)

Now the central bank hikes rates to fight inflation. Here is the feedback loop that creates dominance: when rates rise, all that maturing short debt rolls over at the *new, higher* rate. The government's **net interest** outlay — the cash it pays bondholders each year — climbs fast. The higher and longer rates stay, the bigger the interest bill, the wider the deficit, and the more debt the Treasury must issue *just to pay the interest on its existing debt*. The central bank's anti-inflation tool has become a deficit-*widening* tool.

At some point the bill gets large enough that hiking further does more fiscal damage than it does inflation good — and political and financial pressure mounts on the central bank to *stop tightening*, even though inflation may not be fully tamed. When the central bank yields to that pressure — keeping policy looser than pure inflation-fighting would dictate, in order to protect the budget — that is fiscal dominance. The budget has reached into the central bank's reaction function and turned a dial.

![Pipeline of fiscal dominance from huge short-dated debt through hikes raising interest to the central bank getting boxed in](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix-3.png)

The figure traces the loop step by step: huge, short-dated debt at the top; an inflation flare-up that demands hikes; those hikes rolling onto maturing debt and ballooning the interest bill; the bill exploding past the defense budget; the bind where more tightening only worsens the deficit; and finally dominance, where fiscal needs override the inflation target. Notice that nothing in this chain requires anyone to *announce* a policy change. Dominance does not arrive with a press conference. It arrives quietly, as a central bank that finds itself cutting "a little early" or holding "a little easier" than the inflation data alone would justify — because the interest bill is screaming.

### Why short maturity makes it worse

The speed at which dominance bites depends heavily on how *fast* the debt reprices. If a government had locked in all its debt at fixed low rates for 30 years, a central-bank hike would barely touch the interest bill for decades — the dominance risk would be minimal. But the more short-dated the debt, the faster higher rates flow through into interest costs, and the tighter the leash on the central bank.

This is why the maturity structure of the debt is a genuine financial-stability variable, not an accounting footnote. A Treasury that funds heavily in short bills to spare the long end from supply (as the US did in 2023) is, as a side effect, *shortening the leash* — making the budget more sensitive to the very rate the Fed controls. There is a real tension here between issuing short (cheap now, fast-repricing) and issuing long (locks in cost, slow-repricing). The more a government leans short, the closer fiscal dominance is to the surface.

#### Worked example: the interest-bill constraint

Let us put numbers on the leash. In fiscal 2024, US federal **net interest** ran about **\$880 billion** — already enough that interest exceeded the entire national defense budget (~\$850 billion) for the first time in history. By 2025 it crossed roughly **\$1 trillion**.

Now ask the dominance question directly: suppose inflation flares again and the market debates whether the Fed should hike enough to add, say, one percentage point to the *average* rate the government pays. With about \$37 trillion of debt outstanding and a meaningful share rolling over each year, the steady-state effect of a 1-point rise in the average funding rate is on the order of **1% × \$37 trillion = \$370 billion** of *additional* annual interest once it fully flows through. That would take the interest bill from ~\$1 trillion toward ~\$1.37 trillion — adding roughly a third of a trillion dollars to the deficit *every year*, purely from the cost of carrying old debt.

Compare the two paths a central bank faces. Hold rates and the interest bill grows slowly with the debt. Hike another point and you bolt an extra ~\$370 billion onto the deficit annually — which the Treasury must then *borrow*, issuing yet more debt, on which it then pays yet more interest. The takeaway is uncomfortable and exact: **past a certain debt size, the central bank's anti-inflation hike becomes a deficit-expansion machine, and that is the precise moment its hands start to be tied.**

![Bar chart of federal net interest outlays nearly tripling from 345 billion in 2020 to about 1 trillion in 2025](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix-4.png)

The bar chart shows the leash tightening in real time. Net interest sat at \$345 billion in 2020, when rates were near zero. By 2022 it was \$475 billion, by 2023 \$658 billion, by 2024 \$880 billion — and through \$1 trillion in 2025. ## Monetary financing and the inflation tax

If a government cannot or will not close its deficit, and lenders start demanding punishing rates to keep buying its debt, there is an escape hatch — and it runs straight through the central bank. Understanding that hatch is essential, because it is the ultimate destination of unchecked fiscal dominance.

### Monetizing the debt

Normally, when the government runs a deficit, it must borrow *real* savings — it sells bonds to households, pension funds, banks, and foreign governments who hand over existing money in exchange for the IOU. That is non-inflationary: no new money is created, money just changes hands from saver to government to spender.

**Monetary financing** breaks that discipline. Instead of finding real savers, the government leans on its central bank to buy the bonds — and the central bank pays for them with newly created money. (For the deep mechanics of how a central bank conjures money to buy bonds, see [how money is created by banks and central banks](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier).) Now the deficit is funded not by redirecting existing savings but by *adding new currency* to the system. The Treasury gets to spend; the central bank's balance sheet absorbs the bond; fresh money enters circulation. This is "monetizing the debt" — turning debt that should have been funded by savings into money instead.

In small, temporary doses during a crisis, this can be benign — much of pandemic-era QE looked like this and did not immediately ignite hyperinflation because the new money was hoarded, not spent. But done *systematically*, to fund ongoing deficits a government cannot otherwise finance, monetary financing is the textbook recipe for high and accelerating inflation. The currency is being printed faster than the economy grows, so each unit buys less. This is the failure mode at the far end of fiscal dominance: a central bank so captured by the budget that it becomes a money-printer for the Treasury.

### The inflation tax

Here is the subtle, important part. A government drowning in nominal debt has a quiet way out that requires no default and no explicit tax hike: it can let inflation run. This is the **inflation tax**.

The logic: government debt is denominated in *nominal* dollars — a \$1,000 bond pays back \$1,000 regardless of what \$1,000 is worth at maturity. If inflation is high, the dollars the government pays back are worth less in real terms than the dollars it borrowed. The *real* value of the debt shrinks. Inflation, in effect, transfers wealth from bondholders (who get repaid in debased dollars) to the government (which repays its debt with cheaper money). It is a tax on everyone holding nominal claims — bonds, cash, savings — collected silently, without a vote.

This is why fiscal dominance and inflation are joined at the hip. A government with an unmanageable nominal debt and a captured central bank has a powerful incentive to *tolerate* inflation, because inflation is the one "tax" that erodes the debt without anyone having to legislate it. The central bank that should be fighting that inflation is the same one being pressured to keep money loose. The inflation tax is not a bug of fiscal dominance — for an indebted government, it is the feature.

There is one defense bondholders try, and understanding why it only partly works tells you a lot about where the risk lands. If lenders *expect* inflation, they demand a higher yield up front to compensate — so newly issued bonds price in the inflation the market foresees, and on those bonds the inflation tax is largely neutralized. The catch is twofold. First, it only protects against *expected* inflation; an inflation *surprise* — a few points above what the market priced — is collected in full from existing bondholders, because they are locked into yields set before the surprise. Second, the government still benefits enormously from inflating away the *stock* of debt issued in the past at low yields — the trillions in long-dated Treasuries sold during the 2020-21 zero-rate era pay back fixed, low coupons in dollars now worth far less. This is the deep reason a fiscally dominant government quietly *wants* an inflation surprise rather than steady, expected inflation: the surprise is what transfers real wealth, and the larger the stock of cheap legacy debt, the larger the prize. For a trader, the implication is that the dominance trade is really a bet on *unexpected* inflation — which is exactly what conventional bonds cannot hedge and inflation-linked bonds (TIPS) and real assets can.

#### Worked example: inflating away a slice of the debt

Take the real number: about **\$37 trillion** in federal debt. Suppose the central bank, under fiscal pressure, quietly tolerates inflation running 2 percentage points above its 2% target — so 4% instead of 2% — for three years.

Each year, that extra 2 points of inflation erodes roughly 2% of the *real* value of the outstanding nominal debt. Over three years, compounding, that is about a **6% real reduction**. On \$37 trillion, 6% is roughly **\$2.2 trillion** of real debt burden quietly erased — wealth transferred from everyone holding Treasuries, cash, and fixed nominal savings to the government's balance sheet, with no legislation, no default, and no headline tax increase.

Now see why a heavily indebted government finds this tempting. Closing a \$2.2 trillion gap through actual taxes or spending cuts would be a political bloodbath. Letting inflation run a couple of points hot for a few years achieves the same real result and is nearly invisible to most voters, who feel it only as "prices are higher" rather than "the government just taxed my savings." The takeaway is blunt: **for a government that owes \$37 trillion in nominal dollars, a few years of above-target inflation is the cheapest tax it will ever collect — which is exactly why a captured central bank is so dangerous.**

The chart shows the pile the inflation tax works on: total federal debt climbing from \$22.7 trillion in 2019 to \$37.4 trillion in 2025, and the red slice marking the roughly \$2.2 trillion of real value that a multi-year inflation overshoot can quietly erode. ## The unpleasant arithmetic: why a big deficit can force inflation

The argument above — that an unsustainable fiscal path eventually forces the central bank's hand — is not just a story. It was formalized in 1981 by economists Thomas Sargent and Neil Wallace in a paper with one of the great titles in economics: *"Some Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic."* Their argument is worth understanding because it is the analytical backbone of the entire fiscal-dominance case.

The standard view in the 1970s was that inflation is *always and everywhere* a monetary phenomenon — control the money supply and you control inflation, full stop, independent of what the government does with its budget. Sargent and Wallace showed that this is only true under one condition: that monetary policy gets to go *first* and fiscal policy adjusts to live with the consequences (monetary dominance). Reverse the ordering — let the fiscal authority commit to a path of deficits that monetary policy must somehow accommodate — and the conclusion flips.

The arithmetic runs like this. Suppose the government is committed to a permanent stream of large primary deficits (spending beyond taxes, before interest). Someone has to fund those deficits forever. There are only three ways: raise taxes, issue more debt, or print money. If raising taxes is off the table politically, the government must either keep issuing debt or print. Now suppose the central bank tries to be tough today — it refuses to print, forcing the government to fund the deficits by issuing bonds instead. That works for a while, but each bond issued adds to the debt *and* to the future interest bill, so the debt compounds. Eventually the debt grows so large relative to the economy that no more real buyers can be found at any reasonable rate. At that point the central bank has no choice but to monetize — to print after all — and because it waited, it must print *more* than if it had monetized gradually from the start.

The punchline is genuinely unpleasant: **tight money today, in the face of unsustainable deficits, can mean *higher* inflation tomorrow.** By refusing to monetize early, the central bank lets the debt compound to a point where it is forced into a bigger monetization later. Inflation is not avoided — it is deferred and amplified. Monetary policy, in a fiscally dominant world, cannot durably control inflation on its own; the fiscal path has the last word.

You do not have to believe the arithmetic holds exactly to take the lesson. The lesson is that **the central bank's power over inflation is conditional on the fiscal authority behaving** — on deficits being sustainable. When they are not, monetary policy is pushing against a fiscal current that will, sooner or later, win. That is the theoretical reason fiscal dominance is a real risk and not just a scare story, and it is why a trader has to watch the *budget*, not just the central bank, to forecast inflation and rates over a multi-year horizon.

![Before-and-after comparison of coordination where levers align versus conflict where they oppose](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix-5.png)

The figure contrasts the two states the unpleasant arithmetic hinges on. On the left, **coordination**: both levers pull the same way, the push adds up, and the macro signal is clean and directional — like 2020-21, when stimulus and zero rates lit a boom together. On the right, **conflict**: the levers oppose, the push partly cancels, the regime is muddled, and the trade becomes a question of which lever ultimately wins. Sargent and Wallace's point is that in a sufficiently indebted world, the fiscal lever wins the long game — which is why the right-hand column eventually collapses back into inflation if the deficits never close.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about fiscal-monetary interaction are widespread, comforting, and wrong. Each one will cost you money if you trade on it.

**Misconception 1: "The central bank is fully independent, so the budget can't touch monetary policy."** Legally, yes, the Fed sets rates without government permission. *Practically*, independence is conditional. A central bank operates inside a political and financial system, and when its rate decisions threaten to bankrupt the very government that grants it its mandate, the pressure is enormous and the independence frays at the edges. History is full of central banks that were independent on paper right up until the fiscal situation forced their hand. The 2024 reality check: with net interest at \$880 billion and rising, the cost of "fully independent" tight policy is now a line item bigger than defense. Independence is real, but it is not unconditional — it holds only as long as the fiscal path lets it.

**Misconception 2: "Fiscal and monetary policy always coordinate — they're on the same team."** They are run by different institutions with different mandates and, often, different parties in charge. They coordinate during genuine crises (2008, 2020) when everyone agrees the priority is to avoid collapse. But in normal times they frequently *conflict*: an elected government wants to spend (it gets re-elected for spending), while an independent central bank wants to control inflation (it gets criticized for letting prices rise). The 2022-24 mix — Fed at 5.50% while the Treasury ran \$1.8 trillion deficits — was not coordination; it was open conflict, and pretending otherwise would have had you forecasting a recession that the fiscal gas pedal kept postponing.

**Misconception 3: "Fiscal dominance is only an emerging-market problem."** This is the most expensive misconception, because it lulls developed-market investors into complacency. Yes, the classic cases are emerging markets — Argentina, Turkey, Brazil in various decades — where weak institutions monetized deficits into high inflation. But the *mechanism* is universal: it is arithmetic, not geography. A developed country with debt at 123% of GDP, a near-\$1-trillion interest bill, persistent 6%-of-GDP deficits, and no political appetite to close them is on the same road, just earlier along it. The advantages of a reserve currency and deep institutions buy *time* — they do not repeal the arithmetic. The number that should puncture the complacency: US net interest tripled in five years to ~\$1 trillion, and the deficits funding it show no sign of closing. "It can't happen here" is exactly what every country said before it happened there.

**Misconception 4: "If the government just controls the money supply, it controls inflation."** This was the pre-Sargent-Wallace orthodoxy, and it is incomplete. As the unpleasant arithmetic shows, monetary control of inflation is conditional on the fiscal path being sustainable. If deficits are permanent and unsustainable, no amount of monetary toughness durably suppresses inflation — it only defers and amplifies it. Inflation is a monetary phenomenon *and* a fiscal one; ignoring the fiscal half is how you get blindsided by a central bank that "should" be able to crush inflation but somehow can't.

## How it shows up in real markets

The policy mix and fiscal dominance are not chalkboard theory. They have been pricing themselves into markets in plain sight. Here is where to look.

### The 2022-24 conflicting mix and the strong dollar

The single cleanest real-world illustration is the one we opened with. From 2022 through 2024, the US ran the loose-fiscal-tight-money quadrant: a Fed funds rate that climbed to 5.50% against deficits of 5-6% of GDP. The market consequences were exactly what the "fight" regime predicts and what surprised consensus forecasters who modeled only the monetary lever:

- **Growth refused to break.** Every recession call from 2022 and 2023 was wrong, because the fiscal gas pedal was offsetting the monetary brake. Real GDP grew 2.5% in 2022, 2.9% in 2023, and 2.8% in 2024 — robust, not recessionary, *despite* the most aggressive hiking cycle in forty years.
- **Disinflation was slow and incomplete.** CPI fell from its 9.06% peak but got sticky, and by 2025-26 it was *re-accelerating* (CPI back above 4% by mid-2026 in the data). Sticky inflation against firm growth is the off-diagonal signature: the deficit keeps demand alive, so prices won't fully cool.
- **The dollar got strong, then wobbled.** DXY surged to a multi-decade high near 114.8 in September 2022 as high US rates pulled in global capital — the classic tight-money-plus-firm-economy magnet. (Why the dollar behaves this way is the subject of [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).) But by 2025 the dollar softened as the interest-bill story and dominance fears began to weigh — the early tremor of the regime tipping.

A trader who held the policy-mix frame fading recession calls and staying long the dollar through 2022-23 made money precisely because they did not treat the Fed's brake as the only force in the room.

### The rising-interest-bill constraint and the price of gold

The longer-horizon signal is the interest bill itself, and the asset quietly pricing it is **gold**. Gold has no cash flow — it pays no coupon and no dividend, so its price is set largely by what it costs to hold an alternative and by how much investors fear the debasement of currencies. (We unpack this fully in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock).) As the fiscal-dominance story has built — net interest tripling, deficits refusing to close, the inflation-tax temptation growing — gold has gone on a historic run, from an annual average near \$1,800 in 2022 to roughly \$2,388 in 2024 and onward to multi-thousand-dollar prints in 2025-26.

That is not random. Gold is, among other things, a bet *against* the central bank's ability to keep policy tight in the face of an exploding interest bill — a bet on fiscal dominance and the inflation tax. When the market starts to believe that the budget, not the central bank, will set the long-run inflation rate, it buys the asset that cannot be printed. The gold tape over 2022-26 is, in part, the fiscal-dominance thesis being voted on with real money.

### The yield curve and "term premium"

The third place it shows up is in the *shape* of the yield curve — specifically in **term premium**, the extra yield investors demand to hold a long bond rather than rolling short ones. (For the full grammar of the curve, see [reading the yield curve](/blog/trading/macro-trading/reading-the-yield-curve-slope-inversion-recession).) When investors fear fiscal dominance — that the government will lean on the central bank and tolerate inflation to manage its debt — they demand more compensation to lend *long*, because long bonds are where the inflation tax bites hardest. That pushes up long yields relative to short ones, *steepening* the curve from the long end. A curve that steepens because long yields are rising on fiscal-risk fears (a "bear steepener") is a market that is starting to price the dominance regime. Watching whether the curve steepens for *growth* reasons (healthy) or *fiscal-risk* reasons (ominous) is one of the sharpest reads available on where the regime is heading.

### Why the regime is so hard to escape once you are in it

The most important — and most under-appreciated — feature of fiscal dominance is that it is *self-reinforcing*. Once you are deep in it, the exit gets harder every year, for a chain of reasons worth spelling out.

Start with the math of compounding interest. The interest bill is itself a line in the deficit, so a large interest bill *widens* the deficit, which forces more borrowing, which grows the debt, which grows next year's interest bill. This is a feedback loop with positive feedback: debt begets interest begets more debt. Once net interest is a fifth of revenue and the deficit is 6% of GDP with no primary surplus in sight, the debt-to-GDP ratio climbs on autopilot even if the government changes nothing — and *especially* if the central bank keeps rates high. The arithmetic does not need a recession or a crisis to deteriorate; it deteriorates in the base case.

Layer on the political economy. Closing a 6%-of-GDP deficit means some combination of large tax increases and large spending cuts — to defense, to Social Security, to Medicare, to the programs voters most fiercely defend. No democratic government volunteers for that while it can borrow instead, and as long as the bond market keeps lending at tolerable rates, it *can* borrow instead. So the deficit persists not because anyone wants dominance but because every individual budget decision points toward more spending and lower taxes, and no one is rewarded for the austerity that would break the loop. The deficits are, in Sargent and Wallace's framing, treated as politically fixed — which is precisely the assumption that flips the arithmetic toward inflation.

Now add the reflexive market dimension. The moment bond investors *believe* dominance is coming, they demand a higher term premium to lend long — which raises the government's funding cost — which widens the deficit — which makes dominance more likely. The fear becomes partly self-fulfilling. A country can tip from "expensive but fine" to "dominant" not because the fundamentals changed overnight but because the market's *belief* about the fundamentals changed, and that belief raised the very funding cost it feared. This reflexivity is why dominance can arrive suddenly after years of apparent calm: the arithmetic builds quietly, then sentiment snaps. For a trader, that asymmetry — slow build, fast repricing — is exactly the kind of setup worth positioning *ahead* of, because the move when it comes is violent.

The one circuit-breaker is the reserve-currency privilege. Because the world needs dollars and US Treasuries are the planet's reserve asset, the US can sustain debt levels and deficits that would have already broken a smaller country — global demand for Treasuries keeps funding costs lower than the fundamentals alone would justify. (The mechanics of that privilege are in [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).) But "lower than otherwise" is not "immune." The privilege buys time and lowers the funding cost; it does not repeal the compounding loop. The danger is that the privilege breeds complacency — the belief that the rules that bind everyone else do not apply — right up until the interest bill and the deficits force the issue anyway.

## How to trade it: the policy-mix playbook

Everything above converges here. The job is to read the two levers, name the regime they produce, let the regime select the trade, and keep one tripwire armed for fiscal dominance — the regime change that overrides every other signal. Here is the operating procedure.

### Step 1: Read the mix

Establish where you are in the 2x2, every quarter:

- **Fiscal lever** — track the deficit as a percent of GDP (is it expanding or contracting?) and the trend in Treasury issuance via the Quarterly Refunding Announcement. Rising deficit and rising issuance = loose and getting looser. (Details in [deficits, debt, and bond supply](/blog/trading/macro-trading/deficits-debt-bond-supply-why-issuance-moves-yields).)
- **Monetary lever** — track the policy-rate path, whether the balance sheet is in QE or QT, and the level of *real* (inflation-adjusted) yields, which tell you how genuinely tight money is. A 5% nominal rate with 5% inflation is not tight; a 5% rate with 2% inflation is.

Plot yourself in the box. That single placement tells you the regime's default behavior for growth, inflation, and the dollar.

### Step 2: Trade the regime

Each quadrant has a default playbook:

- **Both loose (boom):** long risk assets, long gold, lean short the currency. The gas-on-gas regime inflates nominal asset prices — ride it until inflation forces the central bank to turn.
- **Loose fiscal + tight money (the fight):** the 2022-24 trade. Fade recession calls and premature rate-cut bets (the fiscal gas keeps the economy alive), lean long the currency (high rates plus firm growth), but stay alert because *this is the quadrant where dominance is born*.
- **Both tight (squeeze):** defensive. Long high-quality duration (bonds rally as growth fear dominates), reduce risk, watch for the recession the double-brake usually causes.
- **Tight fiscal + loose money (good mix):** the benign regime — own risk and duration both; low supply and easy money is a tailwind for almost everything. Rare, so enjoy it while it lasts.

### Step 3: Arm the fiscal-dominance tripwire

This is the part most traders skip, and it is the most important. Independent of the quadrant, keep a set of tripwires that signal the regime is tipping into dominance:

- **Net interest as a share of revenue.** When interest consumes a large and rising slice of federal revenue — say, climbing toward and past 20% — the leash on the central bank is getting short. (Worked below.)
- **Cuts despite firm inflation.** Watch for a central bank that eases or stops hiking even though inflation is still above target and growth is fine. That is the behavioral fingerprint of dominance — policy looser than inflation alone would justify.
- **A bear steepener on fiscal news.** Long yields rising on deficit/issuance headlines (not on growth) means the market is demanding a fiscal-risk premium — pricing dominance directly.
- **Gold and real assets outrunning real yields.** When gold rips higher *even as* real yields rise — breaking its usual inverse relationship — the market is buying debasement insurance, a classic dominance tell.

#### Worked example: the dominance threshold on revenue

Put the tripwire in numbers. Suppose federal revenue is roughly **\$5 trillion** a year. A common rule of thumb is that when interest payments consume around **20%** of revenue, a sovereign's fiscal flexibility is seriously impaired — every fifth dollar of tax goes just to servicing old debt, leaving that much less for everything else and that much more pressure on the central bank to keep funding costs down.

Twenty percent of \$5 trillion is **\$1 trillion**. And we have already crossed it: net interest hit ~\$880 billion in 2024 and ~\$1 trillion in 2025. In other words, the US is *at* the rule-of-thumb dominance threshold right now — interest is consuming on the order of a fifth of federal revenue. The trade implication is direct: position for a central bank whose freedom to stay tight is structurally constrained, which over a multi-year horizon means a bias toward real assets (gold, TIPS, commodities), a steeper curve, and skepticism of long nominal bonds and the currency. Here is the whole post in one sentence: **once interest eats a fifth of revenue, the question is no longer whether the central bank wants to keep money tight — it is whether the budget will let it.**

If the tripwire fires — interest through ~20% of revenue *and* a central bank easing into firm inflation — the dominance trade overrides the quadrant playbook: own what cannot be printed (gold, TIPS, real assets), position for a steeper curve, and be short long-dated nominal bonds and the currency, because in a fiscally dominant world the inflation tax is the government's exit and those instruments are what it taxes.

![Decision graph of the policy-mix playbook from reading the levers to the regime trade and the dominance tripwire](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix-7.png)

The playbook figure ties the whole procedure together: read fiscal and monetary on the left, sort into the boom / conflict / squeeze regime in the middle, and — critically — route the conflict regime through the dominance tripwire on the right, where a firing tripwire flips you into the real-assets trade that overrides everything else. The discipline is to do this *every quarter*, mechanically, so that when the regime tips you are already positioned rather than scrambling.

### The invalidation

Every view needs a kill switch. The policy-mix/dominance trade is *wrong*, and you should cut it, if: the government credibly closes the deficit (real fiscal tightening — the deficit-to-GDP ratio falling sharply toward balance), the interest bill stabilizes or shrinks as a share of revenue, and the central bank demonstrably stays tight against inflation without political flinching. Any durable move *back* toward the "good mix" (tight fiscal, loose money with low deficits) invalidates the dominance thesis — that is the healthy regime, and it deserves the opposite positioning. Watch the deficit trajectory and the interest-to-revenue ratio as your primary invalidation signals: if they turn down and stay down, the leash loosens and the trade is over.

It is worth picturing what a *genuine* invalidation looks like, so you do not mistake noise for it. A one-quarter dip in the deficit because tax receipts came in strong is noise — the structural deficit is still 6% of GDP. A real invalidation is a multi-year fiscal consolidation: a credible, legislated path that takes the deficit from ~6% of GDP toward ~3% or below and holds it there, alongside an interest bill that flattens as a share of revenue rather than climbing. That is the 1990s template — the kind of disciplined budget that hands the central bank back its freedom and returns the economy to monetary dominance. If you see that path begin and stick, you flip the whole book: the real-asset and steepener trades come off, and the benign "good mix" positioning (own both risk and duration) goes back on. The discipline is symmetric — you arm the dominance tripwire *and* the consolidation tripwire, and you let the data, not your prior, decide which one fires.

The deeper point to carry away is that you cannot forecast rates, inflation, or the dollar over a multi-year horizon by watching the central bank alone. The central bank is one of two hands on the controls, and increasingly it is the constrained one. Read *both* levers, name the regime, and keep the interest bill in the corner of your eye at all times — because the most important macro story of the next decade is not what the Fed wants to do, but what the budget will let it do.
