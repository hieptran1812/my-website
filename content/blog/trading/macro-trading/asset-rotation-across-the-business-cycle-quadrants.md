> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Different assets lead in different macro regimes, and the cleanest map of those regimes is a simple 2x2: whether **growth** is rising or falling, crossed with whether **inflation** is rising or falling. Those two questions carve the world into four "seasons," and each season has its own winning asset — get the quadrant right and you own the right thing.
>
> - The four quadrants are **reflation** (growth up, inflation down → stocks win), **overheating** (growth up, inflation up → commodities win), **stagflation** (growth down, inflation up → cash and gold win), and **disinflationary slowdown** (growth down, inflation down → bonds win).
> - The economy rotates through these quadrants roughly clockwise over a cycle, which is why the framework is often drawn as an **"investment clock"** — and leadership rotates with it, from bonds to stocks to commodities to cash and back.
> - The reason each asset wins is mechanical, not magic: stocks need growth *and* easy policy, commodities are literally made of inflation, long bonds rally when rate cuts come, and cash and gold are the only things that hold up when stocks and bonds fall *together*.
> - The number to remember from 2022: real GDP still grew **2.5%** while CPI peaked at **9.06%** — a stagflation-flavored quadrant where the S&P fell about 19% and long bonds fell *with* it, while WTI crude averaged **\$94.53** and short-term cash quietly became the year's best risk-adjusted asset.

In January 2022, a trader sitting on a "balanced" portfolio — some stocks, some bonds, a sensible 60/40 split that had worked beautifully for forty years — was about to discover that the rules had changed. Over the next ten months almost everything they owned fell at the same time. The S&P 500 dropped roughly 19% on the year. Long-dated US Treasuries, the thing that was *supposed* to rise when stocks fell, instead had their worst year in modern history, with the long bond down more than 30%. Investment-grade corporate bonds fell. Gold, the supposed inflation hedge, finished the year roughly flat. The classic diversified book — the one designed to never have all its pieces fall together — had all its pieces fall together.

Almost nothing worked in 2022. But two things did. The first was **commodities**: WTI crude oil averaged **\$94.53** for the year and spiked well above \$120 in the spring, energy stocks were the single best sector by a mile, and a broad commodity index posted one of its best years in a decade. The second was the most boring asset on Earth — **cash**, specifically short-term Treasury bills, which went from yielding almost nothing to yielding over 4% as the Federal Reserve hiked rates at the fastest pace since the 1980s. The trader who had quietly held T-bills and a sleeve of commodities sailed through the year that wrecked the balanced book.

This was not luck, and it was not unknowable in advance. It was a *quadrant*. 2022 was a regime where growth was slowing while inflation was still raging — the corner of the macro map where stocks and bonds both bleed and only real assets and cash survive. The entire point of this post is to give you the map that tells you, ahead of time, which corner you're standing in and therefore what you should own. The map is a single 2x2 grid, and once you can read it, dozens of separate "should I buy stocks or bonds or gold?" questions collapse into one: *which quadrant are we in?*

![Two by two grid of growth versus inflation with the winning asset labeled in each of the four boxes](/imgs/blogs/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants-1.png)

The figure above is the whole framework on one page, and we'll spend the rest of the post earning the right to use it. We'll build it from absolute zero — what asset rotation even means, why growth and inflation are the two axes that matter, what each of the four quadrants feels like, and *why* a specific asset wins in each one for concrete, mechanical reasons. Then we'll go a layer deeper: how leadership rotates *inside* the stock market between cyclical and defensive sectors, the old "investment clock" that ties it all together, the myths that get beginners hurt (no, the quadrant is not a vending machine), how the framework actually played out in 2021, 2022 and 2023-24, and finally the playbook — how to locate the quadrant, own its winner, and rotate when the regime flips. This connects directly to the broader regime lens in [risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates), but where that post asks "is fear rising or falling?", this one asks the deeper structural question: "which way are growth and inflation heading?"

Why these two axes, and not interest rates, the dollar, or volatility? Because growth and inflation are the two forces that determine what *every* asset is worth, and they act in opposite directions for different assets. Growth drives corporate earnings and the appetite for risk; inflation drives the cost of money and the value of real things. A stock is a bet on growth that *hates* inflation, because inflation raises the discount rate on its future earnings. A commodity is the mirror image — a bet on inflation that *needs* growth to sustain the demand for it. A long bond dislikes both growth and inflation, since either one pushes yields up and its price down, and it thrives in their absence. Cash and gold are what remain standing when growth and inflation move in the one combination that hurts stocks and bonds at the same time. Every other macro variable you could name — the policy rate, the dollar, the shape of the yield curve — is *downstream* of these two: rates are the central bank's response to growth and inflation, the dollar reflects relative growth and rate differentials, the curve encodes expected growth and inflation. That is why the growth-inflation grid is not one framework among many. It is the two-dimensional space in which all the other macro indicators are merely coordinates, and mastering where growth and inflation are heading answers most of the questions the other gauges are only trying to ask.

## Foundations: the two axes, the four seasons, and why assets take turns

Let's build every term from the ground up, because the framework is only as good as your grip on its pieces.

**Asset rotation** is the simple observation that capital does not sit still. Money is constantly flowing *out* of one kind of asset and *into* another as the macro environment changes. When investors collectively decide the economy is recovering, money rotates out of safe bonds and into growth-sensitive stocks. When they decide inflation is the bigger threat, it rotates into commodities and real assets. When they decide a recession is coming, it rotates into long government bonds and cash. "Rotation" is just the name for this sloshing of capital between the major asset classes — and the whole game of macro trading is anticipating *which way* the money is about to slosh, before it does.

To anticipate rotation you need a map of *why* it happens, and here is the central claim of this entire post: **the two forces that drive asset rotation more than any other are the direction of economic growth and the direction of inflation.** Not the *level* — the *direction*. Markets are forward-looking; they price the second derivative, the change in the change. What matters for an asset's next move is not whether growth is high or low today but whether it is *accelerating or decelerating*, and likewise whether inflation is *rising or falling*. Almost every macro regime that matters can be summarized by the answers to just those two yes/no questions.

Why these two and not, say, interest rates or the dollar or unemployment? Because growth and inflation are the two *root* variables, and almost everything else is downstream of them. Interest rate policy is the central bank's *response* to growth and inflation. The dollar's strength depends heavily on relative growth and inflation across countries. Unemployment is a lagging shadow of growth. Corporate earnings are a function of growth. Bond yields are a function of expected growth plus expected inflation. If you know the *direction* of growth and the *direction* of inflation, you have effectively pinned down the macro weather, and every other variable is a consequence you can reason about. That is why the cleanest regime map uses exactly these two axes and nothing else.

Now cross them. Put **growth** on the vertical axis (rising at the top, falling at the bottom) and **inflation** on the horizontal axis (rising on the right, falling on the left). Two binary questions give you four boxes, and each box is a distinct macro *season*:

**Quadrant 1 — Reflation (growth rising, inflation falling).** This is the sweet spot, sometimes called "Goldilocks": the economy is recovering and accelerating, but inflation is still low or falling because there's slack in the system (unemployed workers, idle factories). Policy is easy — the central bank is keeping rates low to nurse the recovery. Corporate profits are rising. Risk appetite is high and there's no inflation problem forcing the central bank to take the punchbowl away. This is the best possible world for **stocks**, which need exactly two things at once: rising growth (so earnings climb) and easy money (so those earnings get a high valuation multiple). Reflation delivers both.

**Quadrant 2 — Overheating (growth rising, inflation rising).** The boom matures. Growth is still strong, but now the economy is running hot enough that prices and wages start climbing. Capacity gets tight, commodity demand surges, and inflation accelerates. The central bank shifts from friend to foe — it starts raising rates to cool things down. This is the late-cycle quadrant, and its winner is **commodities** (oil, copper, energy, materials). Why? Because commodities *are* inflation in its rawest form — when the economy overheats, the price of the stuff economies are built from goes up first and fastest. Stocks can still grind higher here, but they wobble as rates rise; commodities lead.

**Quadrant 3 — Stagflation / slowdown (growth falling, inflation rising).** The ugly quadrant, and the one we opened with. Growth is rolling over — often *because* the central bank tightened too much in the previous quadrant — but inflation is still elevated and sticky. This is the trap: the central bank can't cut rates to rescue growth because inflation is still too high, so the cushion that normally saves stocks isn't available. Both stocks (hurt by slowing growth) and bonds (hurt by high inflation and rising rates) fall *together*. The only winners are the assets that don't depend on either growth or low rates: **cash** (which now pays a real yield as rates stay high) and **gold** (the hedge against monetary disorder). This is the quadrant where diversification across stocks and bonds fails completely.

**Quadrant 4 — Disinflationary slowdown (growth falling, inflation falling).** The cleansing quadrant. Growth is weak, often recessionary, but the good news is inflation is finally *falling* — demand has cooled enough to break the price pressure. Now the central bank is free to *cut* rates to support the economy, and it does. The winner here is **long-duration government bonds**. When the central bank cuts and growth is weak, yields fall across the curve, and falling yields mean *rising* bond prices — especially for long-dated bonds, which are the most sensitive to rate changes. This is the bond quadrant, the mirror image of overheating.

That is the entire framework in four sentences. Whether each of growth and inflation is rising or falling sorts the world into reflation, overheating, stagflation, or disinflation — and each season hands the leadership baton to a different asset.

One more foundational point before we go deeper: **how do you actually measure which way growth and inflation are pointing?** You don't get to read "growth is now falling" off a single official number — GDP is reported with a lag of weeks and revised for years, so by the time it confirms a turn the market has long since moved. Instead you triangulate from faster, forward-looking indicators. For the *growth* axis, the workhorses are the **purchasing managers' indices (PMIs)** — monthly surveys of business activity where a reading above 50 means expansion and below 50 means contraction, and the *direction* of the PMI is one of the cleanest reads on growth momentum. You add monthly payrolls (the trend in job creation), jobless claims (rising claims warn of a slowdown), and credit conditions (is lending expanding or tightening?). For the *inflation* axis, you watch CPI and the central bank's preferred core PCE, but you lean on *leading* inflation signals too: commodity prices (which move first), wage growth, shipping and input costs, and market-implied inflation breakevens. None of these is perfect alone; the skill is reading several together and asking "which way is the weight of evidence pointing?" That triangulation — not any single print — is how you locate the quadrant in real time.

It's also worth naming the *fifth* state that lurks at the center of the grid: **the transition zone**, where growth or inflation is flat or the signals conflict, and you genuinely can't tell which quadrant you're in. This is not a failure of the framework; it's a feature of reality, and it's where most of the year actually lives. The quadrants are clearest at the extremes (a raging stagflation, a clean reflation) and fuzziest at the boundaries. The correct response to the transition zone is not to force a call but to *reduce conviction and size* — hold a more balanced book, keep more cash, and wait for the signals to resolve. The traders who blow up are usually the ones who insist on a confident quadrant call when the data is genuinely ambiguous.

#### Worked example: positioning each of the four quadrants with a \$1,000,000 book

Let's make this concrete by allocating a \$1,000,000 portfolio four different ways — once for each quadrant — so you can see what "owning the quadrant's winner" actually looks like in dollars. These are illustrative tilts, not advice, but they capture the *direction* the framework points you.

In **reflation**, you lean hard into risk and growth. A reasonable tilt: \$650,000 in equities (with a cyclical bias — tech, financials, small caps), \$150,000 in high-yield credit, \$100,000 in commodities, and only \$100,000 in cash and bonds combined. The book is built to capture rising growth with easy policy behind it.

In **overheating**, you keep growth exposure but rotate toward real assets and shorten your bond duration because rising rates are now the enemy. A tilt: \$350,000 equities (now energy and materials heavy), \$300,000 commodities, \$200,000 cash and short bills, \$150,000 gold, and essentially *zero* long-duration bonds — they're about to get hurt.

In **stagflation**, you play defense hard. A tilt: \$400,000 cash and T-bills (now earning a real yield), \$200,000 commodities/energy, \$200,000 gold, and only \$200,000 in equities concentrated in defensive, pricing-power names. Long bonds: zero. This is the "preserve capital and earn the risk-free rate" quadrant.

In **disinflation**, you pivot to the bond trade. A tilt: \$450,000 in long-duration government bonds (set to rally as the central bank cuts), \$200,000 in rate-sensitive defensive equities (utilities, REITs), \$200,000 cash, \$150,000 gold. The book is positioned for falling yields and the equity recovery that follows.

Notice what changed across the four books: the *same dollar* of capital was sluiced from stocks to commodities to cash to bonds as we walked around the grid — that is asset rotation, and the quadrant is the steering wheel.

### Why markets price the *direction*, not the level

Before we go further, it's worth dwelling on the single most counterintuitive feature of the framework: it cares about whether growth and inflation are *rising or falling*, not whether they're high or low. A beginner's instinct is the opposite — surely high growth is good for stocks and low growth is bad? But that instinct loses money, and understanding why is what separates a trader from a tourist.

The reason is that markets are *discounting machines*. A stock's price today already reflects whatever everyone currently knows about the level of growth. If the economy is booming and everyone knows it, that boom is already in the price. What moves the price *next* is new information — and new information is, by definition, a *change*: growth coming in faster or slower than the level already priced. So the variable that drives the next move is the *direction* and *rate of change*, not the level. A market with 5% growth that's decelerating toward 2% will often fall, while a market with −2% growth that's accelerating toward 0% will often rally. The first looks objectively healthier and the second objectively sicker, but the *direction of change* points the other way, and direction is what gets priced.

This is why the most powerful signals in macro are *second-derivative* signals — the change in the change. Inflation falling from 9% to 6% is wildly bullish for stocks and bonds even though 6% is still a terrible level, because the *direction* has flipped and the central bank's hand is loosening. That's exactly what happened in late 2022 and 2023: inflation was still high in absolute terms, but the moment it *peaked and started falling*, the disinflation rally began, even though inflation wouldn't reach target for another two years. The quadrant axes are drawn as "rising/falling" rather than "high/low" precisely because the direction is the tradable variable. When you locate the quadrant, you are reading the direction of travel, not taking a snapshot of the level.

A second consequence: the quadrant boundaries aren't fixed at specific numbers. There's no universal "inflation above 3% means overheating" line. What matters is whether inflation is rising *relative to what the market and the central bank expected and have already priced*. In an economy used to 1% inflation, a move to 3% and rising is an overheating shock; in one used to 8%, a move to 5% and falling is a disinflationary relief. The map is about *direction relative to expectations*, which is why two economists can look at the same CPI print and disagree about the quadrant — they disagree about the trend and the baseline, not the number. Keep this in mind: you are always reading momentum against a backdrop of what's already priced.

## What wins in each quadrant, and the mechanical reason why

The framework is only useful if you understand *why* each asset wins its quadrant, because the "why" is what lets you reason about edge cases and transitions instead of memorizing a table. Let's take each asset in turn and find the mechanism. The unifying idea: **every asset is a claim whose value depends on some combination of growth and the discount rate, and the quadrants simply sort which assets get tailwinds and which get headwinds.**

**Stocks win in reflation.** A stock is a claim on a company's future earnings. Its value is, roughly, those future earnings divided by a discount rate (the rate of return investors demand, which rises and falls with interest rates). So a stock loves two things: *rising earnings* (the numerator goes up) and a *low discount rate* (the denominator goes down). Reflation delivers both simultaneously — growth is accelerating so earnings climb, and inflation is low so the central bank keeps rates low and the discount rate stays cheap. That is a double tailwind, and it is why stocks, especially longer-duration growth stocks whose value is concentrated in far-future earnings, perform best in the reflation quadrant. The moment inflation starts rising and the central bank starts hiking (overheating), the discount-rate tailwind turns into a headwind, and stocks lose their clean lead.

**Commodities win in overheating.** A commodity — a barrel of oil, a pound of copper, a bushel of wheat — has no earnings and no coupon. Its price is set purely by physical supply and demand *right now*. When the economy overheats, demand for physical inputs surges (factories running hot, construction booming, consumers spending), and supply is slow to respond (you can't drill a new oil well overnight). So prices spike. Crucially, commodities are not just *correlated* with inflation — for the energy and food components, they substantially *are* inflation; a rising oil price feeds directly into the CPI. This makes commodities the one asset class that *benefits* from the very thing (rising inflation) that hurts both stocks and bonds. That is why they're the overheating quadrant's champion, and why they're the only reliable hedge when inflation is the problem. We go deep on this in [commodities as macro signals: oil, copper and gold](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold).

**Bonds win in disinflationary slowdown.** A bond is a stream of fixed payments. Its price moves *inversely* to interest rates: when rates fall, the fixed payments a bond promises become more valuable relative to newly issued bonds paying less, so the old bond's price rises. The magnitude of that price move depends on **duration** — roughly, how many years until you get your money back. A long-duration bond (a 30-year Treasury, duration around 17) moves about 17% in price for a 1% change in yield; a short bond barely moves. So in the disinflation quadrant — growth weak, inflation falling, central bank cutting rates hard — yields fall across the curve, and long-duration government bonds rocket higher. This is also the quadrant where bonds reclaim their role as the stock-portfolio hedge: as the recession bites, money flees stocks *into* bonds, pushing yields down further. The link between rate cuts and the bond rally is the subject of [reading the yield curve: slope, inversion and recession](/blog/trading/macro-trading/reading-the-yield-curve-slope-inversion-recession).

**Cash wins in stagflation (alongside gold).** Cash — specifically short-term Treasury bills — is the asset everyone underrates. In three of the four quadrants it's the boring laggard. But in stagflation it becomes the hero, for a precise reason: when inflation is high, the central bank holds rates high, which means the yield on T-bills is high *and* nearly risk-free. In 2022, T-bills went from yielding about 0% to over 4% with zero price risk, while every other asset fell. Cash also has a second, subtler virtue in stagflation: it gives you *optionality*. When stocks and bonds are both falling and you can't tell when the bottom is, holding cash that pays you 4-5% to wait is enormously valuable — you earn a real return while keeping the dry powder to buy the eventual disinflation rally. We make the full case for this in [cash and money markets: the underrated asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/cash-money-markets-the-underrated-asset) and explain *why* the rate gets set where it does in [interest rates: the price of money, the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable).

**Gold is the cross-quadrant wildcard.** Gold doesn't fit neatly into one box, which is exactly why it confuses people. Gold has no yield and no earnings; its price is driven mainly by **real interest rates** (the interest rate minus expected inflation) and by demand for a monetary hedge. The cleanest rule: gold tends to do well when *real* yields are low or falling, and when there's stress in the monetary system. It shines in stagflation (high inflation eats real yields, and there's monetary fear) and in disinflation if the central bank cuts aggressively (driving real yields down). It struggles in overheating *if* the central bank hikes faster than inflation rises (pushing real yields up, as in most of 2022's middle), and it's an also-ran in clean reflation when risk assets are the obvious play. We come back to gold's quadrant in detail later, and unpack the real-yield mechanism in [real vs nominal: inflation, real yields, the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).

#### Worked example: the 2022 stagflation quadrant, and why commodities and cash won

Let's anchor the framework in the hardest, most instructive year. In 2022, where were growth and inflation heading? Real GDP growth for the full year came in at **2.5%** — positive, but *slowing* sharply from 2021's red-hot **5.9%**, and the economy actually contracted in the first two quarters. So growth: *falling*. Meanwhile CPI inflation was screaming higher, peaking at **9.06%** in June 2022, the highest in forty years, and averaging about **8.0%** for the year. So inflation: *rising* (and far above target). Falling growth plus rising inflation lands you squarely in **quadrant 3 — stagflation**.

Now apply the framework. Stagflation says: stocks and bonds both lose, cash and commodities and gold win. Check it against what actually happened:

```
Asset                 2022 result        quadrant prediction
S&P 500               about -19%         stocks lose  (correct)
Long Treasuries       about -30%         bonds lose   (correct)
WTI crude (avg)       $94.53, +39% YoY   commodities win (correct)
T-bills / cash        ~0% -> 4%+ yield   cash wins    (correct)
Gold (avg)            $1,802, ~flat      gold mixed   (partly)
```

The framework nailed the two big calls: own commodities and cash, avoid the stock-bond core. WTI averaging **\$94.53** versus **\$68.17** the year before was a roughly 39% jump, and energy was the only S&P sector that rose. Cash went from worthless to paying over 4% risk-free. Gold was the one wrinkle — it finished roughly flat near **\$1,802** because for most of 2022 the central bank was hiking *faster* than inflation, driving real yields sharply higher (the 10-year real yield went from about **−1.0%** in early 2022 to **+1.6%** by year-end), which is a headwind for gold even in a high-inflation year. The lesson: the quadrant told you to avoid the 60/40 core and own real assets and cash, and that single call was the difference between a brutal year and a flat one.

The data path makes this visible. The chart below plots the US economy's actual position year by year, with inflation on the horizontal axis and growth on the vertical, so you can watch it travel through the quadrants.

![Scatter path of US growth versus inflation by year from 2020 to 2025 moving through four labeled quadrants](/imgs/blogs/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants-2.png)

Trace the path. **2020** sits in the bottom-left (growth deeply negative at −2.2%, inflation low at about 1.3%) — a deflationary slump. **2021** swings up and to the right into reflation-becoming-overheating (growth surging to +5.9%, inflation climbing to about 4.4%). **2022** pushes hard to the right and starts falling (growth slowing to +2.5%, inflation peaking near 8% average) — the stagflation push. Then **2023, 2024 and 2025** walk back down and to the left (growth steady around 2-3%, inflation cooling from 3.75% to 2.69%) — the disinflation glide. The economy quite literally rotated clockwise around the grid, and the leading asset rotated with it: bonds and cash in 2020, stocks in 2021, commodities and cash in 2022, then stocks again as disinflation took hold in 2023-24.

#### Worked example: the 2020-21 reflation trade and the stock-commodity surge

Now the happy quadrant, to balance the ugly one. Coming out of the COVID crash, 2020-21 was the textbook reflation-into-overheating run, and it minted fortunes for anyone positioned for it.

Start in mid-2020. Growth had cratered (full-year 2020 real GDP was **−2.2%**), but it was about to *accelerate* violently off the bottom as the economy reopened, and inflation was *low* — CPI averaged just **1.28%** in 2020, with massive slack in the labor market. Growth turning up, inflation low: **reflation**, the stock quadrant. And policy was the most stimulative in history — the central bank had slashed rates to zero and was buying bonds by the trillion, while the government sent out stimulus checks. A reflation quadrant *with* the most aggressive easy-money backdrop ever assembled.

The result was exactly what the framework predicts, on steroids. Through 2021, real GDP roared to **+5.9%** — the fastest annual growth since 1984 — and the S&P 500 returned about 27% for the year on top of its 2020 recovery, led by exactly the high-growth, long-duration tech names that thrive when growth rises and rates stay low. And as the recovery matured into overheating in the back half of 2021, the baton passed to commodities: WTI crude went from **\$39.68** average in 2020 to **\$68.17** in 2021, copper jumped from **\$2.80** to **\$4.23** a pound, and the inflation that would define 2022 began to build (CPI averaged **4.43%** in 2021, up from 1.28%). Even Bitcoin, the highest-beta reflation asset of all, exploded from a **\$28,990** close in 2020 to **\$46,306** at the end of 2021.

The reflation playbook — own stocks and let the cyclical, high-beta names lead, then rotate toward commodities as overheating sets in — was the single best trade of the decade, and the quadrant map called it cleanly: rising growth plus easy policy equals own risk, with the leadership rotating from stocks toward commodities as you climb from reflation into overheating.

## Sector rotation inside the stock market: cyclicals versus defensives

The quadrant map works at the asset-class level — stocks vs bonds vs commodities vs cash. But there's a second, finer rotation happening *inside* the stock market, and a good macro trader reads both. Even in quadrants where stocks aren't the headline winner, *which* stocks lead tells you where in the cycle you are.

The key distinction is **cyclical versus defensive sectors**. A **cyclical** sector is one whose earnings swing hard with the economic cycle — when growth booms its profits soar, when growth slumps its profits collapse. Think technology, consumer discretionary (cars, travel, luxury), financials (banks), industrials, materials, and energy. A **defensive** (or "non-cyclical") sector is one whose earnings barely move with the cycle because it sells things people need no matter what — consumer staples (food, soap, toothpaste), healthcare, utilities (electricity, water), and telecoms. People keep buying toothpaste in a recession; they postpone buying a new car. Defensives are the steady, boring, "always-on" businesses.

Here's the rotation. **Early in the cycle (recovery/reflation),** cyclicals lead. Growth is accelerating off a low base, and the businesses most leveraged to that acceleration — tech, discretionary, financials, small caps — post the biggest earnings gains and the biggest stock moves. High beta wins. **In the overheating phase,** leadership tilts toward the inflation-beneficiary cyclicals: energy, materials, industrials — the sectors with pricing power that profit directly from rising commodity prices. **As growth peaks and rolls over (slowdown),** cyclicals roll over *first*, often before the headline economy even confirms a slowdown, because the stock market is forward-looking. Money rotates *defensive*: into staples, healthcare and utilities, whose stable cash flows hold up when earnings expectations everywhere else are getting cut. **In the disinflation quadrant,** the rate-sensitive defensives shine — utilities and REITs behave like bond proxies, so when yields fall they re-rate higher alongside bonds, and long-duration growth tech recovers as the discount rate drops.

![Matrix of sector leadership by cycle phase showing cyclical and defensive sectors across four rows](/imgs/blogs/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants-5.png)

The figure lays out the full sector rotation: which sectors lead, by phase. The practical value is enormous, because **the cyclical-versus-defensive ratio is a leading indicator of the quadrant itself.** When you see defensive sectors (utilities, staples) suddenly outperforming cyclicals (tech, discretionary) while the headline market is still rising, that's an early warning that growth is peaking and you're rotating from overheating toward slowdown — often weeks before the economic data confirms it. The internal rotation of the stock market is one of the most reliable real-time reads on where the quadrant cursor is heading next.

There's a deeper reason the sector rotation is so reliable, and it ties straight back to the discount-rate logic from earlier. Different sectors have different *durations*, just like bonds. A high-growth tech company's value is concentrated in earnings far in the future, so it behaves like a long-duration asset — exquisitely sensitive to the discount rate, soaring when rates fall and cratering when they rise. A utility or a consumer-staples company earns steady cash today, so it behaves like a short-duration, bond-like asset. This is why, in the overheating quadrant where rates are rising, long-duration tech struggles while energy and materials (whose earnings rise *with* inflation) lead; and why, in the disinflation quadrant where rates are falling, long-duration tech re-rates violently higher and rate-sensitive utilities and REITs rally like the bonds they resemble. The sector rotation isn't a separate phenomenon from the asset rotation — it's the *same* growth-and-discount-rate machine operating one level down, inside the equity market. Once you see that, you can read the sector tape as a finer-grained version of the quadrant map.

#### Worked example: reading the sector tape to anticipate the quadrant flip

Suppose it's mid-cycle and you're trying to figure out whether you're still in the "growth rising" half of the map or about to drop into the "growth falling" half. The headline index isn't much help — the S&P can keep grinding to new highs even as the cycle quietly turns, because a handful of mega-caps mask the rot underneath. So you watch the *internal* rotation instead.

Construct a simple ratio: a basket of cyclical sectors (say, equal weights of technology, consumer discretionary, financials and industrials) divided by a basket of defensives (staples, healthcare, utilities). Call it the **cyclical/defensive ratio**. When the economy is in reflation or overheating and growth is rising, this ratio trends *up* — cyclicals are beating defensives. The moment it rolls over and starts trending *down* while the broad index is still flat-to-up, that divergence is the tell: institutional money is rotating defensive ahead of a growth slowdown.

Concretely: imagine the cyclical basket is up 2% over a month while the defensive basket is up 6%. The ratio fell about 4%. If that's a one-month blip, ignore it; if it persists for two or three months and the yield curve is also flattening and the manufacturing PMI is sliding toward 50, you have three independent signals all pointing the same way — *you are rotating from the top half of the grid (growth rising) into the bottom half (growth falling)*. That's your cue to start trimming high-beta cyclicals, extending bond duration, and raising cash *before* the headline data makes it obvious. The single most useful habit here: watch what leadership *inside* the market is doing, because the stock market rotates defensive before the economy admits it's slowing.

## The investment clock: tying the quadrants into a cycle

So far we've treated the four quadrants as static boxes. But the deeper truth is that the economy doesn't jump randomly between them — it *rotates* through them in a fairly consistent order, like a clock's hand sweeping through the hours. This is the famous **"investment clock,"** a framework popularized by Merrill Lynch in the 2000s, and it's the dynamic version of our quadrant map.

The logic of the rotation is causal, not coincidental. Start at the bottom of a recession (**disinflation**: growth weak, inflation falling). The central bank, free to act because inflation is low, slashes rates. Low rates and cheap money eventually revive the economy: growth turns up while inflation is still low — you've rotated into **reflation**. Stocks lead. As the recovery matures and the economy runs hot, capacity tightens and inflation starts rising — you rotate into **overheating**. Commodities lead, and the central bank begins hiking to cool things off. Those rate hikes eventually bite: growth rolls over while inflation is still sticky and high — you rotate into **stagflation/slowdown**. Cash and gold lead, stocks and bonds suffer. Finally the slowdown deepens enough to break inflation, which falls — and you're back in **disinflation**, where the central bank starts cutting again and bonds lead. Then the cycle repeats.

![Circular clock diagram showing the economy rotating clockwise through four quadrants with the leading asset in each](/imgs/blogs/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants-3.png)

The figure shows the clock turning clockwise through the four phases, with leadership rotating from bonds to stocks to commodities to cash and back. This is the single most important *dynamic* insight in the whole framework: **the assets don't just have a favorite quadrant — they hand the baton to each other in a predictable sequence.** Bonds lead at the bottom (rate-cut anticipation), stocks take over in the recovery, commodities take the late-cycle boom, cash and gold survive the slowdown, then bonds lead again. If you know which phase you're in, you not only know what to own *now* — you know what to rotate *into* next.

A crucial caveat that we'll hammer in the misconceptions section: the clock does *not* tick on a fixed schedule. Some phases last six months, others last three years. The 2020-2022 cycle compressed all four quadrants into about two and a half years — an unusually fast sweep driven by the pandemic shock and the policy response. Other cycles, like the 2010s expansion, sat in the reflation/Goldilocks quadrant for the better part of a *decade* because growth was modest and inflation stubbornly refused to rise. The clock tells you the *order* of rotation and the *direction* of travel; it tells you nothing reliable about the *timing*. Get the order right and stay humble about the schedule.

## Common misconceptions

The quadrant framework is powerful, which is exactly why it's dangerous when misused. Three myths in particular get traders hurt.

**Misconception 1: "The quadrant is a vending machine — put in the regime, get out the guaranteed winner."** This is the most seductive and most wrong reading. The framework gives you *probabilistic tilts*, not deterministic outputs. In any given stagflation, cash and commodities are *favored*, but commodities can still fall if a supply glut hits, and there will be weeks within the quadrant where stocks rally hard. The quadrant shifts the odds in your favor; it does not remove risk. Treat it as a tilt — "overweight the quadrant's winner, underweight its loser" — not as a switch that turns one asset on and another off. The 2022 example proves the point: gold was *supposed* to win the stagflation quadrant and instead finished flat, because real yields rose. The framework is a base rate, not a guarantee.

**Misconception 2: "Stocks beat everything over the long run, so rotation is a waste of time — just buy and hold equities."** It's true that over a multi-decade horizon, equities have historically had the highest returns. But this misconception confuses the *very long run* with the horizons people actually invest over, and it ignores the math of drawdowns. A buy-and-hold stock investor who rode through 2022 was down 19% in a year and didn't recover for over a year; one who recognized the stagflation quadrant and sat in T-bills earning 4% not only avoided the loss but had dry powder to buy the bottom. Over a 30-year horizon stocks may win, but you don't live and trade on a 30-year horizon — you live through the quadrants, and avoiding the quadrant where stocks get crushed dramatically improves your compounding. Avoiding a −19% year and earning +4% instead is a 23-point swing; you'd need a huge subsequent rally just to make buy-and-hold catch up. Rotation isn't about beating stocks over 30 years; it's about not getting carried out in the years stocks lose.

**Misconception 3: "The framework predicts the timing — it tells me *when* to rotate."** It does not, and believing it does is how traders rotate far too early and bleed. The quadrant map tells you the *direction* and the *order* of rotation; it is silent on the *schedule*. Knowing that overheating eventually gives way to stagflation does not tell you whether that transition is three months or two years away. Markets can stay in a quadrant far longer than seems reasonable — the reflation quadrant of the 2010s lasted the better part of ten years. The correct use is to identify the quadrant you're *in* (from current data), position for it, and watch the transition signals — *not* to front-run a quadrant change because you "know it's coming." It's coming, but maybe not for a long time, and being early is indistinguishable from being wrong in your P&L.

**Misconception 4: "Bonds always hedge stocks, so a 60/40 portfolio is always safe."** This is the assumption 2022 detonated. The stock-bond hedge — bonds rising when stocks fall — *only holds reliably in the disinflation and reflation quadrants*, where inflation is low and the central bank can cut rates to support both. In the *stagflation* quadrant, the stock-bond correlation flips *positive*: both fall together, because the thing hurting stocks (slowing growth) coexists with the thing hurting bonds (high inflation and rising rates), and the central bank can't ride to the rescue. The 60/40 isn't a law of nature; it's a bet that you're in the low-inflation quadrants. The number that ended the myth: in 2022, stocks fell about 19% and long bonds fell about 30% *in the same year* — the hedge didn't just fail, it doubled the damage. We unpack this correlation flip in [risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates).

## How it shows up in real markets

Enough theory — let's walk the framework through three consecutive real regimes, 2021 through 2024, and show it earning its keep in real time with real numbers.

**2021 — Reflation into overheating.** Coming out of the COVID recovery, the US economy was in the reflation quadrant turning toward overheating: full-year real GDP growth hit **+5.9%** (growth strongly rising) while inflation, though climbing, was still being dismissed by policymakers as "transitory" (CPI averaged **4.43%** and was rising). The framework said: own stocks while reflation lasts, then rotate toward commodities as overheating sets in. That's exactly what worked. The S&P returned about 27% for the year. And the late-2021 commodity surge — WTI rising to a **\$68.17** annual average, copper to **\$4.23**/lb — was the overheating quadrant announcing itself. A trader watching the quadrant cursor climb from reflation into overheating would have stayed long stocks but begun adding energy and commodities into the back half of the year, right before they became 2022's only winners.

**2022 — Stagflation.** As covered in detail above, the cursor pushed hard into the stagflation quadrant: growth slowing to **+2.5%** (and contracting in H1) while inflation exploded to a **9.06%** peak. The framework's verdict — avoid the stock-bond core, own commodities and cash — was the trade of the year. WTI averaged **\$94.53**, energy was the only green sector, and T-bill yields marched from near-zero past 4%. The chart below isolates this: WTI crude by year, with 2022's **\$94.53** peak standing out against the disinflation years on either side.

![Bar chart of annual average WTI crude oil from 2019 to 2025 with the 2022 peak of about 95 dollars highlighted](/imgs/blogs/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants-4.png)

The bars tell the stagflation story at a glance: oil at **\$94.53** in 2022 was up roughly 39% from 2021's \$68.17 and towered over the \$77.64 (2023), \$75.83 (2024) and \$65.39 (2025) of the disinflation years that followed. While the balanced portfolio was having its worst year in decades, the overheating-stagflation quadrant's asset — commodities — was having one of its best. That is the framework's whole promise: in the quadrant where almost everything loses, it pointed you at the one thing that won.

**2023-24 — Disinflation.** Then the cursor walked back down and to the left. Growth held up better than feared (real GDP **+2.9%** in 2023, **+2.8%** in 2024 — a soft landing rather than a recession), but the headline story was *falling inflation*: CPI dropped from a 3.75% average in 2023 to 2.95% in 2024, and core inflation cooled steadily toward target. Falling inflation with decent growth is the benign disinflation/reflation corner, and the winner rotated back to **stocks** — the S&P returned about 26% in 2023 and 25% in 2024, led once again by long-duration tech as the discount-rate headwind from 2022 reversed. This was a slightly unusual disinflation (growth stayed too strong for bonds to be the clean winner, so the rate cuts came slowly and stocks led instead), which is a perfect reminder that the quadrants are a *map*, not a deterministic script — but the directional call, "inflation is falling so the equity-killing headwind of 2022 is reversing, rotate back into stocks," was exactly right.

#### Worked example: gold's quadrant, real yields, and the 2024-25 surge

Gold deserves its own worked example because it's the asset the framework handles most subtly. The cleanest driver of gold is not inflation directly but the **real yield** — the interest rate minus expected inflation, which is what you give up by holding a metal that pays no interest. When real yields are high, gold is unattractive (you're forgoing a fat risk-free return to hold a rock); when real yields are low or falling, gold shines.

Watch this play out. In **2022**, gold finished roughly flat at a **\$1,802** average *despite* 9% inflation, which baffles people who think "gold = inflation hedge." The reason is real yields: the 10-year real yield rocketed from about **−1.0%** in early 2022 to **+1.6%** by year-end as the central bank hiked faster than inflation, and that surge in real yields capped gold. The inflation tailwind was overwhelmed by the real-yield headwind.

Now watch the reversal. As inflation fell through **2023 and into 2024-25** while the central bank stopped hiking and began cutting, real yields rolled over from their peak (the 10-year real yield eased from about **+2.5%** in late 2023 back toward **+2.0%**), and *that* — combined with central-bank gold buying and rising geopolitical hedging demand — lit the fuse. Gold's annual average went from **\$1,943** in 2023 to **\$2,388** in 2024 to roughly **\$3,000** in 2025, a historic breakout after years of going nowhere near \$1,800.

![Area and line chart of annual average gold price rising from about 1400 dollars to a surge above 3000 dollars by 2025](/imgs/blogs/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants-6.png)

The chart shows the shape of gold's regime story: a long, flat base near \$1,800 from 2019 through 2022 (when real yields were rising), then the powerful surge from 2023 onward as real yields rolled over and monetary-hedge demand returned. The one-line takeaway: gold doesn't track inflation, it tracks *real yields* — so it wins the quadrants where real yields are falling (late stagflation into disinflation, as the central bank pivots from hiking to cutting), which is exactly the corner it caught in 2024-25.

## How to trade it: the playbook

You now have the full map. Here is how to actually use it — the concrete loop of locating the quadrant, owning its winner, sizing by conviction, watching the transition, and rotating on the flip.

![Five step pipeline showing locate the quadrant own its winner size by conviction watch the transition rotate on the flip](/imgs/blogs/asset-rotation-across-the-business-cycle-quadrants-7.png)

**Step 1 — Locate the quadrant.** Answer two questions from current data. *Is growth rising or falling?* Read it from the direction of the manufacturing and services PMIs (above 50 and rising = expanding; below 50 or falling = slowing), the trend in monthly job gains, and the path of GDP nowcasts — you care about the *direction* and *momentum*, not the absolute level. *Is inflation rising or falling?* Read it from the direction of CPI and core PCE (the central bank's preferred gauge), and from forward signals like commodity prices, wage growth, and inflation breakevens. Two directions give you the quadrant. Don't overthink it; the framework's power is in the binary.

**Step 2 — Own its winner, underweight its loser.** Translate the quadrant into a tilt: reflation → overweight stocks (cyclical/high-beta tilt), underweight cash; overheating → overweight commodities and energy, shorten bond duration to near zero; stagflation → overweight cash and gold and commodities, underweight the stock-bond core; disinflation → overweight long-duration bonds and rate-sensitive defensives, then rotate into stocks as the recovery firms. These are *tilts away from a neutral allocation*, not all-or-nothing bets.

**Step 3 — Size by conviction, not by certainty.** When growth and inflation directions are *clear and confirmed by multiple indicators*, size the tilt large. When you're in a *fuzzy zone* — near the dividing lines, or in a transition where the signals conflict — size small and stay hedged. A "clearly stagflation, every dial confirms it" read justifies a big defensive tilt; a "might be rotating from overheating to slowdown but it's early" read justifies trimming risk modestly while you wait for confirmation. **Remember the framework sets direction, not timing** — never lever up on a quadrant call as if it were a certainty.

**Step 4 — Watch the transition signals.** The money in this framework is made on the *rotations*, so you watch the leading indicators of a quadrant flip. The best ones: inflation *peaking and rolling over* (the single most important transition — it frees the central bank to cut, flipping overheating/stagflation toward disinflation), the cyclical/defensive sector ratio rolling over (growth peaking), the yield curve and PMI turning, and commodity prices breaking trend. When several of these turn together, the quadrant is flipping.

**Step 5 — Rotate on the flip, slightly early.** When the transition signals align, rotate *before* the consensus confirms it, because the biggest moves happen at the inflection. Sell the old quadrant's winner and buy the next quadrant's: when inflation peaks in overheating/stagflation, rotate *out* of commodities and *into* bonds ahead of the disinflation move; when growth bottoms in disinflation, rotate *out* of bonds and *into* stocks ahead of the reflation recovery. The invalidation is built in: if the data reverses and the quadrant *doesn't* flip, you cut the new position and step back. Rotate early enough to catch the move, but small enough that being wrong about the timing doesn't hurt.

**The invalidation, stated plainly.** Your quadrant thesis is wrong, and you should cut, when the two axes you're betting on stop pointing the way you thought. If you're positioned for stagflation (long commodities, heavy cash) and inflation suddenly collapses *and* growth holds up, you're actually in disinflation/reflation — exit the defensive book and rotate to stocks. If you're positioned for reflation (long stocks) and inflation reignites while growth stalls, you've slipped into stagflation — cut the stock-bond core and raise cash. The discipline is simple: you're betting on the *direction* of growth and inflation; the day that direction changes, your quadrant changes, and your book has to change with it. That single rule — re-locate the quadrant whenever growth or inflation changes direction, and rotate the whole book to match — is the entire playbook in one sentence.
