> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — "Money" is not one thing. There is the small **base money** a central bank actually prints (cash plus bank reserves) and the far larger **broad money** that commercial banks create when they lend. Tracking both — and the gap between them — is how a trader reads liquidity, anticipates inflation, and understands why asset prices move.
>
> - The central bank prints **base money (M0)** — about \$5.6 trillion in the US at the end of 2024. Commercial banks create **broad money (M2)** — about \$21.5 trillion. Broad money is roughly **4x** the base.
> - "Printing money" usually means the central bank adding *reserves*, which is base money. That does **not** automatically become spendable broad money in the economy. The two can move in opposite directions.
> - US broad money (M2) jumped **+24% in 2020** (from \$15.40T to \$19.13T). About 18 months later, CPI inflation peaked at **9.06% in June 2022**. Then M2 *contracted* through 2023 — its first sustained drop since the 1930s — and inflation came down.
> - The one number to watch: **M2 year-over-year growth, adjusted for inflation (real M2).** Rising real M2 is a liquidity tailwind for risk assets; falling real M2 is a headwind. Use it as a slow regime dial, not a timing trigger.

In March 2020, as the world locked down, the US Federal Reserve did something that looked, to most people, like turning on a printing press. Its balance sheet exploded. Headlines screamed about "money printing." Congress sent checks. And over the next two years, the number that economists call **M2** — the broad measure of money sitting in the economy — rose from about \$15.4 trillion to over \$21 trillion. That is roughly \$6 trillion of new money in two years, the fastest expansion of the US money stock in the postwar era.

Then came the inflation. By June 2022, the prices Americans paid for goods and services were rising at **9.06% a year** — a 40-year high. The connection seemed obvious: print money, get inflation. Case closed.

Except it is not that simple, and the gap between "the Fed printed money" and "M2 surged" is exactly where most people — and a surprising number of traders — get confused. The Fed prints one *kind* of money. The economy ran on a *different, larger* kind. They are linked, but loosely, and the link breaks in ways that matter for every position you hold. The trader who understands the two layers of money saw the 2020 liquidity flood coming, sized the 2022 inflation, and read the 2023 drain. The trader who thought "money is money" got whipsawed by all three. Let us build the whole picture from zero.

## Foundations: The layers of money

Before any trading signal, you need to be able to answer one deceptively hard question: *when you say "money," which money do you mean?* Almost every macro argument that goes nowhere is two people using the same word for different things. So we define every term from the ground up, in plain language, before we touch a single chart.

### Fiat money and legal tender

Start with the dollar in your pocket (or, more likely, in your banking app). A modern dollar is **fiat money** — money that has value because a government declares it does and because everyone agrees to accept it, not because it is backed by gold or any physical commodity. "Fiat" is Latin for "let it be done." The government decrees that this paper *is* money, and so it is.

**Legal tender** is the legal status that forces a creditor to accept that money to settle a debt. If you owe me \$100 and you hand me a hundred-dollar bill, I cannot legally refuse it and demand gold instead. That legal backing is part of what gives fiat money its staying power: taxes must be paid in it, debts can be discharged with it, and the entire legal-economic machine runs on it.

The everyday-money intuition: fiat money is a *shared agreement*, written down and enforced. It works exactly as long as the agreement holds. That is why "confidence" and "credibility" — words that sound vague — are actually the bedrock of a currency. A trader who watches inflation expectations or the dollar index (DXY) is, at bottom, watching the strength of that agreement.

### The thing almost nobody realizes: most money is not printed

Here is the fact that reorganizes everything. **The physical cash a central bank prints is a tiny slice of all the money in the economy.** The overwhelming majority of "money" is just *numbers in bank databases* — the balance in your checking account, in your savings account, in a company's deposit account. That number is a promise from your bank to pay you cash on demand. It spends like money, it *is* money for every practical purpose, but no central bank printed it. A commercial bank created it.

So already we have two species of money:

1. **Money the central bank creates** — physical cash and electronic reserves that banks hold at the central bank. Call this **base money** (also called M0, high-powered money, or the monetary base).
2. **Money that commercial banks create** — the deposits in your accounts, which appear when banks make loans. Call this **broad money** (the bigger M-aggregates: M1, M2).

The cover figure above shows the relationship as a pyramid: a narrow base layer of central-bank money at the bottom, supporting much larger layers of bank-created broad money on top. The whole rest of this post is about how those layers relate, who controls each, and what their movements mean for prices and positions.

### Central-bank reserves: the money banks use with each other

When a commercial bank — say, JPMorgan — needs to settle a payment with another bank, it does not ship cash across town. It moves **reserves**: electronic balances that every bank holds in an account at the central bank. Reserves are base money. They are, in a sense, the banks' own checking account at the Fed.

Two crucial properties of reserves:

- **They never leave the banking system.** A reserve balance can move from one bank's account at the Fed to another's, but it cannot be spent at a grocery store. You and I cannot hold reserves. Only banks (and a few other institutions) can. This is the single most under-appreciated fact in all of monetary policy, and we will come back to it repeatedly.
- **They are the raw material of the payment system.** Every wire, every ACH transfer, every check ultimately settles in reserves between banks. Reserves are the plumbing.

So when the Fed "creates money" by adding reserves, it is adding to the *plumbing layer* — the layer banks use to settle with each other. Whether that ever reaches your wallet is a separate question.

### Bank deposits: the money you actually use

A **bank deposit** is the balance you see in your account. Legally, it is a liability of the bank — a promise to pay you cash on demand. Economically, it functions as money: you can transfer it, spend it, and it settles your debts. The vast majority of what we casually call "money in the economy" is bank deposits.

The pivotal insight, which sounds wrong the first time you hear it: **banks create deposits when they lend.** A bank does not take \$1,000 of someone else's deposit and lend it to you. When it approves your \$1,000 loan, it simply *types \$1,000 into your account* and records a matching \$1,000 loan as an asset. New deposit, new money, created from nothing but a creditworthy borrower and a keystroke. (We unpack this fully in the companion piece on [how credit creates money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-credit-creates-money-lending-channel-cycles), and the classic mechanics in [how money is created by banks and the money multiplier](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier).)

This is why broad money is so much bigger than base money. The base is the foundation; bank lending builds the skyscraper on top.

### The money aggregates: M0, M1, M2

Economists count money in nested buckets, from narrowest to broadest. The exact definitions vary by country and have shifted over time, but the US versions you need are:

- **M0 / the monetary base** — physical currency in circulation **plus** bank reserves at the Fed. This is base money, the only layer the central bank directly controls. US year-end 2024: about **\$5.6 trillion**.
- **M1** — the most liquid spendable money: physical currency held by the public, plus checking-account deposits, plus other easily-spent deposits. This is "money you can spend today."
- **M2** — M1 **plus** savings deposits, small time deposits, and retail money-market fund balances. This is the standard "broad money" measure macro traders watch. US year-end 2024: about **\$21.5 trillion**.

Notice that M1 and M2 are *broad money* — they are dominated by bank deposits, which banks created. M0 is *base money* — it is what the central bank printed. The aggregates are concentric circles: M0 is mostly *not* inside M2 (reserves are not deposits), but currency is in both, and the deposit layers of M1 and M2 sit on top of the base like floors of a building.

> A clean mental shortcut: **M0 is the money the central bank makes. M2 is the money the banking system makes.** They are different things produced by different institutions, and a trader needs to track both because they tell you different stories.

### Inside money versus outside money

One more pair of terms that will sharpen your thinking. Economists distinguish:

- **Outside money** — money that is a net asset to the private sector as a whole, with no offsetting private liability. Base money is outside money: the cash and reserves the central bank issues are an asset to whoever holds them and are not anyone in the private sector's debt. It comes from "outside" the private system.
- **Inside money** — money created *inside* the private sector that is simultaneously someone's asset and someone else's liability. Bank deposits are inside money: your deposit is your asset and your bank's liability; they net to zero across the private sector. It is created "inside" the system, by the act of lending.

Why does a trader care about an academic-sounding distinction? Because **inside money can vanish.** When loans are repaid or written off, the deposits they created disappear — broad money *shrinks*. Outside money does not vanish that way; the central bank decides its quantity. So broad money is inherently more volatile and more cyclical than base money: it expands in credit booms and contracts in credit busts. That cyclicality is the liquidity wave traders surf.

### A short history of how we ended up with two layers

The two-layer structure is not an accident of modern policy; it grew out of centuries of banking, and the history makes the mechanics click.

For most of history, money *was* the base layer — gold and silver coins with intrinsic value. The first "broad money" appeared when goldsmiths and early banks discovered they could issue **paper receipts** for the gold they stored. Those receipts circulated as money because they were claims on real gold in the vault. Crucially, the goldsmiths noticed that depositors rarely all came for their gold at once, so they could issue *more* receipts than they had gold — lending the receipts out at interest. That was the birth of **fractional-reserve banking**: claims on money (broad money) growing larger than the underlying base (gold). The whole two-layer pyramid is implicit in that one trick.

Through the 19th and early 20th centuries, the base layer was gold and the broad layer was bank deposits and notes built on top of it. The constraint was the gold supply: a country could only have so much base money. This is why the gold standard was both stabilizing (it disciplined money creation) and brutal (it could force deflationary contractions when gold was scarce). The Great Depression's monetary collapse — broad money shrinking by roughly a third as banks failed and deposits vanished — happened partly because the gold-standard base could not be expanded to offset the collapse in the broad layer. That trauma is *why* central banks were eventually given the power to expand the base at will.

The decisive break came in 1971, when the US ended dollar convertibility into gold (the "Nixon shock"), completing the move to pure **fiat money**. From that point, base money was no longer constrained by any commodity — the central bank could create reserves without limit, in principle. But notice what did *not* change: broad money is *still* created by commercial-bank lending on top of the base, exactly as in the goldsmith days. The base went from gold to fiat reserves; the upper floors of the pyramid are built the same way they always were. Understanding that continuity is what lets you reason about money without getting lost in the policy-of-the-week. (The deeper story of how the dollar became the world's base layer is in [the petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance).)

### How M2 is actually measured — and why the definition matters

When you read "M2 rose," you should know *what* rose, because the boundaries are arbitrary and occasionally redrawn. M2 in the US is, concretely:

- **Currency** in the hands of the public.
- **Demand and checkable deposits** — your checking account.
- **Savings deposits** — including, after a 2020 reclassification, most accounts that used to be capped at six withdrawals a month.
- **Small-denomination time deposits** — retail CDs under \$100,000.
- **Retail money-market mutual fund** balances.

What is *excluded* from M2 is just as telling: large institutional money-market funds, large time deposits, repo, and the eurodollar market — much of the so-called "shadow banking" plumbing where enormous money-like balances live. (That hidden layer is its own deep topic in [shadow banking and the repo market](/blog/trading/finance/shadow-banking-and-the-repo-market).) The lesson for a trader: **M2 is a useful proxy, not the full measure of liquidity.** In 2020, a regulatory reclassification mechanically shifted some balances *into* savings deposits, and the broader point — that the official line between "money" and "not-money" is drawn by committee — means you should treat M2 as a strong signal but not a precise instrument. Watch the *trend and the rate of change*, where definitional quirks wash out, not the third decimal of the level.

#### Worked example: which bucket does each form of money fall into

Sort a household's wealth into the aggregates to cement the nesting.

- \$300 cash in a wallet → counts in **M0** (base, as currency) **and** in **M1 and M2** (broad). Cash is the one form that lives in both the base and the broad layer.
- \$4,000 in a checking account → **M1 and M2** (broad money, a bank deposit). Not in M0 — no central bank printed it.
- \$25,000 in a savings account → **M2 only** (broad). It is in M2 but not M1, because savings are slightly less liquid than checking.
- \$50,000 in a retail money-market fund → **M2 only** (broad).
- \$200,000 in stocks → **none of the above.** Stocks are not money; they are an asset you must *sell for money* first. This is why "asset wealth" and "money" are different — and why a broad-money surge can inflate stock prices without those stocks ever counting as money.

The intuition: **the aggregates are nested liquidity buckets, and only cash straddles both the base and the broad layer** — everything spendable above it is bank-created broad money. That single household's \$79,300 of money is almost entirely broad money the banking system made; only the \$300 of cash touches the base.

### "Printing money" almost never means what people think

Put it together and you can finally translate the most abused phrase in finance. When a newspaper says the central bank is "printing money," it almost always means the central bank is **creating reserves** — adding to base money — usually by buying bonds (quantitative easing). That is *not* the same as putting spendable money into the economy. Reserves sit in the banking-system plumbing. They become spendable broad money only if banks lend them out or if the bond was bought from a non-bank (more on that mechanism later).

This is why, as we will see, the central bank can quintuple base money while broad money barely moves — or, conversely, why broad money can surge for reasons that have little to do with the central bank's printing press and everything to do with bank lending and government deficits. The phrase "printing money" papers over the single most important distinction in monetary economics. A trader who keeps the two layers separate has an edge over the crowd that does not.

## The money pyramid: base under broad

Let us make the size relationship concrete, because the *ratio* between the two layers is itself a tradable signal.

![M2 broad money versus the monetary base in dollars, 2018 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/what-money-really-is-base-money-broad-money-traders-2.png)

The chart plots two lines from 2018 to 2024: the upper blue line is **M2 broad money**; the lower amber line is the **monetary base (M0)**. Two things jump out. First, broad money is always far larger than the base — the blue line rides well above the amber one. Second, both jumped in 2020, but they did not jump by the same amount, and after 2021 they diverge: M2 keeps climbing and then dips, while the base falls back. The *gap* between the lines — that shaded band — is the money the banking system created on top of the central bank's base. Reading the behavior of that gap is the whole game.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000 deposit, base versus broad

You walk into a bank and deposit \$1,000 in physical cash that you had under your mattress. What just happened to each layer of money?

- **Before:** the \$1,000 was physical currency in public hands. Currency in circulation is part of *both* M0 (base) and M2 (broad), because cash counts in both. So before the deposit: base money includes your \$1,000 cash; broad money includes your \$1,000 cash.
- **After you deposit it:** the bank takes your \$1,000 of cash. That physical cash is now a bank's vault cash, which still counts as base money. In exchange, the bank credits your account with a \$1,000 deposit. That deposit is broad money (it is in M1 and M2). The cash is no longer "currency in circulation"; it became vault cash / reserves.
- **Net effect:** base money is unchanged — \$1,000 of cash simply moved from your pocket into the bank's vault, both forms of base money. Broad money is *also* unchanged in total — your \$1,000 of currency (broad money) became a \$1,000 deposit (broad money). Nothing was created. You converted one form of money into another.

Now the magic. Suppose the bank is required to hold only \$100 against that deposit and is free to lend the rest. It lends \$900 to a borrower by **crediting the borrower's account with a new \$900 deposit.** *Now* broad money has grown: there is still your \$1,000 deposit, plus a brand-new \$900 deposit the borrower can spend. Broad money rose from \$1,000 to \$1,900 — while base money did not change at all. The lesson: **base money is the seed; bank lending is what multiplies it into broad money.** The central bank planted \$1,000 of base; the banking system grew \$1,900 of broad money on top.

#### Worked example: the broad-money multiple (M2 ÷ base)

The ratio of broad money to base money tells you how much money the banking system has built on each dollar of central-bank base. Using year-end 2020 data:

- M2 (broad money) = \$19.13 trillion.
- Monetary base (M0) = \$5.21 trillion.
- Broad-money multiple = 19.13 ÷ 5.21 = **3.67**.

So at the end of 2020, every \$1 of base money supported about \$3.67 of broad money. Compare that to the end of 2019, *before* the Fed flooded the system with reserves:

- M2 = \$15.40 trillion; base = \$3.44 trillion.
- Multiple = 15.40 ÷ 3.44 = **4.48**.

Here is the counterintuitive part: after the Fed "printed money" in 2020, the multiple *fell* from 4.48 to 3.67. The central bank added base money so fast that broad money — which depends on bank lending — could not keep pace. The pyramid got a much bigger base but the upper floors did not grow proportionally. **A falling multiple is the fingerprint of reserves piling up faster than banks lend them out** — exactly what QE does, and exactly why "printing money" did not produce 1970s-style hyperinflation in 2020. The base ballooned; broad money grew much less.

That single ratio — broad money divided by base — is one of the cleanest ways to see whether the central bank's actions are actually reaching the real economy or just sloshing around the banking plumbing.

## Who creates each kind of money

We have asserted the two-creator model. Now let us trace it step by step, because the *sequence* is what determines whether central-bank action becomes economy-wide money.

![Flow from central bank to reserves to bank lending to deposits](/imgs/blogs/what-money-really-is-base-money-broad-money-traders-3.png)

The flow figure walks the chain: the **central bank** issues reserves and cash (base money); those reserves sit in the banking system; a **commercial bank makes a loan**, which *creates a new deposit*; that deposit is **broad money**; and the borrower spends it into the **real economy**. Read left to right, it is a relay race — and the baton can be dropped at the third stage. If banks do not lend, the chain stops at reserves and broad money never grows. This is the crux: the central bank controls the first two boxes; *commercial banks control the third*, and the third is where broad money is born.

### What the central bank actually controls

The central bank has near-total control over **base money**. It can:

- **Print physical currency** in whatever quantity the public demands (this is demand-driven and not really a policy lever).
- **Create reserves** by buying assets. When the Fed buys a \$1 million bond, it pays by crediting the seller's bank with \$1 million of new reserves. Reserves appeared from nothing; this is the literal mechanism of "money creation" by a central bank. (Quantitative easing, covered in depth in [QE explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money), is just this at enormous scale.)
- **Set the price of reserves** — the interest rate banks earn on reserves and the rate they pay to borrow them overnight. That policy rate is the master price that ripples through every other interest rate, a topic we develop in [interest rates: the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable) and [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).

What the central bank does *not* control directly is **broad money**, because that depends on bank lending decisions, which depend on loan demand, bank capital, risk appetite, and regulation. The central bank can make lending cheaper (low rates) or dearer (high rates), and it can stuff banks full of reserves, but it cannot force banks to lend or borrowers to borrow. It can lead the horse to water.

### What commercial banks actually control

Commercial banks create **broad money** every time they extend credit. The mechanics, stripped to the bone:

1. A creditworthy borrower wants a \$200,000 mortgage.
2. The bank approves it and simultaneously creates a \$200,000 deposit in the borrower's (or the home seller's) account and books a \$200,000 loan asset.
3. New broad money — \$200,000 — now exists that did not exist before. No prior depositor's money was used.

The constraint on this is not "how much base money the bank has lying around." The constraints are (a) finding profitable, creditworthy borrowers, (b) having enough capital to absorb the loan's risk, and (c) being able to obtain the modest reserves needed to settle the resulting payments. In normal times banks acquire those reserves easily. So **broad-money creation is paced by credit demand and bank willingness, not by the literal stock of reserves.** This is why a flood of reserves (base money) does not mechanically produce a flood of deposits (broad money). The connection between [credit creation and the economic cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-credit-creates-money-lending-channel-cycles) is the lending channel, and it is where most of the action is.

#### Worked example: how a \$200,000 mortgage creates broad money but not base money

Walk the mortgage through both layers.

- The bank creates a **\$200,000 deposit** for the home seller. Broad money (M2) rises by \$200,000. The central bank did nothing; a commercial bank just made money.
- The seller's deposit is at, say, Bank B (a different bank than the lender, Bank A). To settle, Bank A must transfer \$200,000 of **reserves** to Bank B. Reserves are base money — but notice they only *moved between banks*. Total base money in the system is **unchanged**. Bank A's reserves fell \$200,000; Bank B's rose \$200,000.
- So the loan created \$200,000 of broad money and zero new base money. The base money already existed; it merely shifted from Bank A to Bank B to settle the payment.

The intuition to carry forward: **broad money is created by lending and is constrained by capital and credit demand, not by the existing pile of base money** — which only gets shuffled around to settle the resulting payments.

## How QE floods reserves but not always broad money

Now we can demolish the biggest source of confusion in all of macro: the idea that quantitative easing (QE) "prints money into the economy." It prints *base* money. Whether that becomes *broad* money — money in the real economy — depends entirely on *who the central bank bought the bond from* and *what the banks do next*.

![QE mechanics showing reserves rise but broad money rises only on some branches](/imgs/blogs/what-money-really-is-base-money-broad-money-traders-5.png)

The diagram branches on the buyer. The Fed buys a bond. Two cases:

- **Bought from a bank.** The bank swaps a bond (an asset) for reserves (another asset). The bank's total assets are unchanged in size — it just holds reserves instead of a bond. No new deposit was created. **Base money up, broad money flat.** This is the dominant case in much of QE, and it is why early QE rounds (2008–2014) added trillions of reserves with surprisingly little broad-money growth.
- **Bought from a non-bank** (a pension fund, an investment fund, a household). The Fed cannot credit a pension fund with reserves — only banks hold reserves. So the Fed credits the *pension fund's bank* with reserves, and that bank credits the *pension fund's deposit account*. Now there is a brand-new deposit. **Base money up AND broad money up.** This is why the 2020 QE, much of which bought bonds from non-banks (and coincided with a flood of government-deficit money landing in household accounts), *did* lift broad money sharply.

And even after reserves rise, broad money only keeps growing if banks then *lend the room out*. If banks sit on the reserves — as they largely did after 2008 — broad money stays flat. If banks lend aggressively, broad money rises. The lower-right branches of the figure capture exactly this fork: reserves up either leads to "banks sit on reserves, broad money flat" (red) or "banks lend, broad money rises" (green).

#### Worked example: QE that adds reserves but no broad money

The Fed buys \$10 billion of Treasury bonds from a large bank.

- The bank hands over \$10B of bonds and receives \$10B of new reserves. Base money (M0) rises by \$10B.
- The bank's balance sheet: assets were "\$10B bonds," now "\$10B reserves." Same size, different composition. No new deposit was created for any household or firm.
- Broad money (M2): **unchanged.** Not one dollar reached the real economy. The \$10B sits in the banking-system plumbing as reserves.

This is the 2008–2014 experience in miniature. The Fed's balance sheet grew by trillions, reserves exploded, and a chorus warned of imminent hyperinflation. It never came, because broad money — the money that actually chases goods and services — grew only modestly. The base ballooned; the broad layer did not follow. **Reserves are not spending power until they are lent or unless the bond was bought from a non-bank.** Watch broad money, not the central bank's balance sheet, to gauge real liquidity.

Contrast that with 2020, where a large share of asset purchases effectively put money into non-bank and household hands (directly via fiscal transfers, indirectly via non-bank bond sales), so broad money *did* surge. Same tool, opposite outcome for broad money — because the buyer and the lending behavior differed. That is the whole point: **QE's effect on the money that matters is contingent, not automatic.**

## Why the base↔broad gap matters for inflation and assets

We now have the machinery. Time for the payoff: *why does a trader care about all this?* Because broad money — not base money — is the quantity that, loosely and with a lag, drives inflation and asset prices. And the *gap* between base and broad tells you whether central-bank action is reaching the economy.

### The crude but durable link: too much money chasing goods

The oldest idea in monetary economics is the quantity theory: if the amount of (broad) money in the economy grows much faster than the amount of goods and services, you tend to get inflation — more dollars bidding for the same stuff. It is crude. The link is loose, lagged, and breaks for years at a time. But over long horizons and at extremes, it holds enough to be useful. The 2020–2022 episode is the textbook case: a once-in-a-generation surge in *broad* money, followed about 18 months later by the highest inflation in 40 years.

The reason it is *broad* money and not *base* money that matters: only broad money is spendable in the real economy. Reserves cannot buy groceries. When base money surges but broad money does not (2008–2014), inflation stays quiet. When broad money surges (2020–2021), inflation tends to follow. **The base↔broad gap is, in effect, a filter that tells you whether the central bank's printing is reaching the part of the economy that sets prices.**

### Why asset prices move with broad money even when CPI does not

Consumer prices (CPI) are not the only place money goes. When broad money grows faster than the economy can absorb it into goods spending, the excess tends to pool in **asset prices** — stocks, bonds, real estate, crypto. This is the "asset inflation" of the 2010s: broad money grew steadily, goods inflation stayed near 2%, but stock and house prices marched up for a decade. A trader reads broad-money growth as a liquidity tide that lifts asset valuations — sometimes long before it shows up in CPI, sometimes instead of ever showing up in CPI.

The practical takeaway: **broad money is a liquidity gauge for risk assets, and it is a (lagged, imperfect) inflation gauge for goods.** Which one it shows up in depends on the regime — where the money flows. In the 2010s it flowed into assets; in 2020–2021 it flowed into both, and goods inflation followed because supply was simultaneously constrained.

### The missing variable: velocity

There is a reason a careful trader never reads a money number in isolation, and its name is **velocity** — the speed at which a given stock of money changes hands. The quantity theory is usually written as an identity: money times velocity equals price level times real output (M × V = P × Y). In plain words: total spending in the economy equals how much money there is *times* how fast each dollar gets spent. Inflation depends on *spending*, and spending is money *times* velocity — so the same stock of money produces wildly different outcomes depending on whether it sits still or circulates fast.

This is the single most important caveat to "money causes inflation," and it is why the 2010s defied the doom-mongers. From 2008 to 2019, broad money grew steadily, yet inflation stayed low — because **velocity fell**. The new money piled up in savings, in corporate cash, and in asset markets rather than chasing goods. A dollar that sits in a money-market fund or a stock portfolio is not bidding up the price of groceries. Velocity was the relief valve that let broad money grow without consumer inflation.

Then 2020–2021 flipped it. The new broad money did *not* sit still — stimulus checks and pent-up demand sent it racing through the economy as lockdowns lifted. Money growth *and* a rebound in velocity, hitting a supply-constrained economy, is precisely the recipe for the inflation that followed. The two-layer model tells you *how much* money was created; velocity tells you whether it will *move*. You need both.

For a trader, velocity is hard to forecast but its *direction* is readable from context: stimulus that lands directly in spenders' hands (the bottom of the income distribution, who spend a high fraction of each dollar) raises velocity; money parked with savers and asset-holders lowers it. So when you see a broad-money surge, ask the second question immediately: *is this money going to people who will spend it, or to balance sheets where it will sit?* That single follow-up separated the analysts who called 2021–2022 inflation from those who repeated "money grew in the 2010s and nothing happened" — true, but only because velocity collapsed, which was not going to repeat when the money was being mailed to households.

### The global angle: every country has the same two layers

The two-layer model is not a US quirk; it is how *every* modern monetary system works. The European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, the Bank of England, the People's Bank of China — each prints base money and watches commercial banks create broad money on top of it. The mechanics are identical; only the labels and the policy choices differ. Japan ran enormous QE for two decades and saw base money explode while broad money and inflation barely moved — a multi-decade demonstration that base-money printing alone does not create inflation, because the broad layer and velocity stayed flat. China, by contrast, runs its broad-money creation largely through directed bank lending and credit quotas, so its broad money grows fast and deliberately — a different control method, same two-layer structure. (Vietnam's version, where the central bank steers broad money with an explicit **credit ceiling** on banks, is a vivid example of directly controlling the upper layer: see [Vietnam's monetary policy and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).)

Why does the global view matter to a trader? Two reasons. First, **relative broad-money growth drives exchange rates** over the medium term: a currency whose broad money is expanding much faster than its trading partners' tends to weaken, all else equal, because more of it is being created. Second, **global liquidity is a sum**. When the major central banks are all expanding broad money at once (2020–2021), the tide lifts risk assets *everywhere*; when they all drain at once (2022–2023), the headwind is global. A trader who tracks broad-money growth across the US, euro area, Japan, and China gets a read on the *world* liquidity tide, not just the domestic one — and asset markets are global. The same dial, summed across the big four, is one of the most powerful slow signals in macro.

## Common misconceptions

The two-layer model immediately corrects several beliefs that cost traders money. Each myth, with the number that kills it.

### Myth 1: "Printing money causes instant inflation."

Reality: from 2008 to 2014, the Fed *quadrupled* the monetary base — from roughly \$0.85 trillion to over \$4 trillion — and core inflation spent most of that period *below* the Fed's 2% target. Base-money printing did not produce inflation because it did not become broad money; banks parked the reserves. Inflation tracks *broad* money's path through the real economy, not the size of the central bank's balance sheet, and even then with a lag of a year or more. The myth confuses the layers.

### Myth 2: "Reserves are money in the economy."

Reality: reserves are base money that lives *only inside the banking system* and can never be spent on goods. At the end of 2024, banks held trillions in reserves at the Fed, yet that pile is not "money chasing goods." It is settlement plumbing. Counting reserves as spending power is the single most common error in armchair macro. The \$5.6 trillion base is mostly reserves; the \$21.5 trillion that actually circulates is broad money. A 4x difference in the wrong direction.

### Myth 3: "M2 always predicts CPI."

Reality: the M2→CPI link is real at extremes but *unreliable for timing*. Through the 2010s, M2 grew ~5–7% a year while CPI sat near 2% — money grew but inflation did not, because the money pooled in assets and velocity (how fast money changes hands) fell. The relationship reasserted itself violently in 2020–2022, then M2 *contracted* in 2023 and inflation cooled. So M2 is a *regime dial*, not a *trade trigger*. Treating a single month's M2 print as a CPI forecast will get you run over.

### Myth 4: "The central bank controls the money supply."

Reality: the central bank controls *base* money tightly but *broad* money only loosely, because broad money is created by commercial-bank lending the central bank cannot command. In 2020, broad money surged partly because of *fiscal* policy (government deficits crediting household accounts) and a lending boom — not purely because the Fed willed it. And in 2023, broad money *fell* despite the Fed not "destroying" base money, because bank lending slowed and deposits ran off into money funds. The money supply is a *system* outcome, not a central-bank dial.

### Myth 5: "More base money means a weaker currency."

Reality: currencies trade on *relative* base and broad money, real interest rates, and growth — not absolute base levels. The Fed expanded its base enormously in 2020, yet the dollar (DXY) closed 2022 at 103.5, *up* from 89.9 at the end of 2020, because the Fed then hiked rates faster than peers. Base-money size alone does not set the exchange rate; the *whole policy stance* does.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Let us run the actual 2018–2024 tape through the two-layer lens and watch the model earn its keep.

![M2 year-over-year growth bar chart showing the 2020-21 spike and 2023 contraction](/imgs/blogs/what-money-really-is-base-money-broad-money-traders-4.png)

The bar chart shows **M2 growth year-over-year**: a small bar in 2019, an enormous green bar in 2020 (+24.2%), a still-large bar in 2021 (+12.3%), then *red* bars in 2022 (−0.6%) and 2023 (−2.3%), and a return to modest green in 2024 (+3.3%). The two red bars are historic: a sustained M2 contraction had not happened in the United States since the Great Depression. That swing — from the fastest expansion in postwar history to the first contraction since the 1930s — *is* the macro story of the decade, and it is invisible to anyone watching only the Fed's balance sheet.

#### Worked example: the 2020 M2 surge as a percentage

Quantify the flood precisely, using the year-end figures.

- End of 2019: M2 = \$15.40 trillion.
- End of 2020: M2 = \$19.13 trillion.
- Increase = 19.13 − 15.40 = **\$3.73 trillion** of new broad money in one year.
- Percentage growth = 3.73 ÷ 15.40 = **+24.2%**.

To feel the scale: \$3.73 trillion is more broad money created in twelve months than the *entire* US money stock added in the prior several years combined. Nothing in the postwar record comes close. **A +24% one-year jump in broad money is a five-alarm liquidity signal** — and a trader who saw it in real time had every reason to expect both an asset melt-up (which came in 2020–2021) and an inflation problem on a 12–24 month lag (which came in 2022). The number told the story before the story was in the headlines.

### The inflation that followed

![CPI inflation year over year with the June 2022 peak at 9.06 percent annotated](/imgs/blogs/what-money-really-is-base-money-broad-money-traders-6.png)

Now overlay the consequence. The CPI chart climbs from near-zero in mid-2020, through the Fed's 2% target band, and up to a **9.06% peak in June 2022**, before falling back toward 3% through 2023. Line up the two charts and the sequence is stark: broad money surged through 2020–2021; goods inflation peaked roughly 18 months after the money surge began, in mid-2022; and as M2 *contracted* through 2023, inflation cooled. The lag is the key feature — money growth does not hit prices instantly; it works through spending, supply, and expectations over many months.

#### Worked example: the lag from money growth to the 2022 inflation peak

Measure the lag explicitly.

- The M2 surge *began* in earnest around March–April 2020, when year-over-year growth rocketed past 20% (full-year 2020 growth was +24.2%).
- CPI inflation peaked in **June 2022** at **9.06%** (the dataset's `CPI_PEAK`).
- Lag from the *start* of the money surge (≈ April 2020) to the *peak* of inflation (June 2022) ≈ **26 months**, or a little over two years. Measured from the *peak* of M2 growth (around early 2021) to the inflation peak, the lag is closer to **15–18 months**.

The trading lesson is about *timing expectations*: when you see a broad-money surge, the inflation it implies is **a year-plus away**, not next quarter. Traders who shorted bonds the instant M2 spiked in mid-2020 were early by a year and a half; those who faded "transitory" calls in *late 2021*, after the surge had clearly fed through, timed the 2022 bond rout far better. **The signal is real but slow — size it as a regime, not a catalyst.** And note the symmetry on the way down: M2's 2023 contraction preceded the 2023 disinflation by a similar lag, which is exactly what the model predicts.

### The control case: 2008–2014, when printing did *not* inflate

To trust the model, you need to see it work in *both* directions — the dog that barked (2020) and the dog that did not (2008–2014). The earlier episode is the more important lesson because it is the one that fools people.

After the 2008 financial crisis, the Fed launched the first rounds of QE. Its balance sheet went from under \$1 trillion to over \$4 trillion. The monetary base quadrupled. Every commentator who equated "base money" with "money" predicted runaway inflation; some predicted hyperinflation and bought gold accordingly. They were wrong for years, and the two-layer model explains exactly why:

- **The reserves stayed in the banks.** Most of the QE bought bonds *from banks*, swapping one bank asset (bonds) for another (reserves). No deposits were created. Base money soared; broad money did not.
- **Banks did not lend the room out.** Scarred by the crisis, facing weak loan demand and tighter capital rules, banks parked the reserves rather than expanding credit. Broad money (M2) grew at a modest ~6% a year, not the explosive pace that would signal trouble.
- **Velocity collapsed.** What little new money there was changed hands more slowly each year. Spending — and therefore inflation — stayed subdued.

Net result: the largest base-money expansion in history up to that point, and core inflation that spent most of a decade *below* target. The gold bugs who shorted the dollar on "money printing" lost money for ten years. **The trader who watched broad money instead of the Fed's balance sheet correctly stayed long bonds and long equities through the 2010s** — the regime of "lots of base money, modest broad money, low velocity, asset inflation but no goods inflation." The same QE tool produced almost no broad-money growth in 2008–2014 and a flood in 2020, because the *transmission* differed. Watching the tool tells you nothing; watching the broad layer tells you everything.

### The 2023 drain that almost nobody priced

The under-told sequel: in 2022–2023, M2 *contracted* (−0.6% then −2.3%). Deposits ran off as savers chased higher yields in money-market funds and Treasury bills, bank lending slowed under the weight of rate hikes, and the Fed shrank its balance sheet (quantitative tightening). Broad money actually fell. The two-layer model said this should drain liquidity from risk assets and cool inflation on a lag — and 2023's regional-bank stress, the disinflation, and the choppy-then-narrow equity market all rhyme with a shrinking broad-money tide. A trader watching *only* the still-large pile of reserves would have missed it; a trader watching *broad money* saw the liquidity headwind building.

## How to trade it / The playbook

Everything above collapses into one practical instrument: **the broad-money dial.** Here is how to read it and position around it.

![Before and after panels showing how a trader reads falling versus rising M2 growth](/imgs/blogs/what-money-really-is-base-money-broad-money-traders-7.png)

The two-panel figure is the whole playbook in one picture. On the left (the "before"/headwind regime): **M2 falling or negative in real terms** → liquidity is draining → asset multiples compress as fewer dollars chase assets → lean **defensive** (trim risk, raise cash, favor short-duration and quality). On the right (the "after"/tailwind regime): **M2 rising in real terms** → liquidity is expanding → multiples re-rate as more dollars chase assets → lean **risk-on** (add duration, equities, long-beta). Same indicator, two regimes, opposite stances.

### The signal: real M2 year-over-year

The single most useful number is **M2 year-over-year growth, deflated by CPI** — "real M2 growth." Why real, not nominal? Because \$1 trillion of new money matters less when prices are rising 9% than when they are flat. Real M2 strips that out and isolates the genuine change in purchasing-power liquidity.

- **Real M2 strongly positive and accelerating** → liquidity tailwind. Historically supportive of risk assets (equities, credit, crypto) and, on a 12–24 month lag, a warning that goods inflation may build.
- **Real M2 negative or sharply decelerating** → liquidity headwind. Historically a drag on risk multiples and, on a lag, disinflationary. This was the 2022–2023 setup.
- **The base↔broad gap** as a cross-check: if the central bank is adding base money (QE) but broad money is *not* growing (a falling broad-money multiple), the "stimulus" is not reaching the economy — discount the inflation impulse and the risk-asset tailwind. If broad money is growing *with* the base, the impulse is real.

### How to actually position

This is a **slow, structural** signal — a regime dial, not a day-trade trigger. Use it to set your *baseline lean*, then let faster signals time entries.

- **In a real-M2 tailwind regime** (rising broad money): bias toward being *long risk and long duration early in the cycle*. More dollars are chasing assets; multiples expand. Pair it with the rate cycle from [interest rates: the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable) — easy money plus expanding broad money is the strongest tailwind combination.
- **In a real-M2 headwind regime** (falling broad money): bias defensive — raise cash, shorten duration, favor quality and balance-sheet strength, and treat rallies with suspicion. Falling broad money in 2022–2023 was a structural reason to fade the "soft landing means buy everything" narrative until the tide turned.
- **At regime turns:** the most valuable moments are when real-M2 growth *crosses zero* or clearly inflects. The 2023 inflection from contraction back toward growth in 2024 (+3.3% nominal, and positive real as inflation fell) was an early tell that the liquidity headwind was easing.

### What invalidates the view

A disciplined trader writes down what would prove the thesis wrong. For the broad-money dial:

- **Velocity shifts.** If money is growing but *velocity* (how fast it changes hands) is collapsing — as in the 2010s — broad money can rise without inflation or even strong asset gains. Money sitting still is not the same as money spending. Watch whether the money is actually moving.
- **Supply shocks.** The 2021–2022 inflation was money-fueled *and* supply-constrained (lockdowns, supply chains, energy). Pure-money models under- or over-shoot when a big supply shock hits. Money is one input, not the only one.
- **Regime in where the money flows.** Broad money that flows into assets (2010s) versus goods (2021) produces very different outcomes. The same M2 growth can be "asset inflation only" or "CPI inflation too." Cross-check with the inflation and rate prints, not M2 alone.
- **The lag is long and variable.** Anywhere from ~12 to ~24 months. Anyone selling you a precise M2→CPI timing model is overfitting. Size the position to survive being early.

### The companion signals: what to watch beside M2

The broad-money dial is most powerful when you triangulate it with a few cheap, related reads. None replaces M2; together they tell you whether its signal is real and which way it will flow.

- **The broad-money multiple (M2 ÷ monetary base).** Already introduced — it tells you whether central-bank action is reaching the economy. A *rising* multiple means broad money is outgrowing the base (banks lending, money circulating); a *falling* multiple means the base is outgrowing broad money (reserves piling up, transmission blocked). In 2008–2014 the multiple fell hard; in a healthy lending boom it rises.
- **Bank lending growth.** Since banks *are* the broad-money creators, the growth rate of total bank loans is the leading edge of broad money. Accelerating lending → broad money will rise; a credit crunch → broad money will stall or fall, as it did in 2023. Loan-officer surveys that show tightening standards are an early warning that the broad layer is about to contract.
- **Money-market fund flows.** When savers yank deposits out of banks and into money funds chasing higher yields — as they did in 2022–2023 — broad money (M2) can fall even though the money has not been destroyed, just relabeled. Large inflows to money funds are a tell that M2 is understating available liquidity, and a reason to cross-check the headline.
- **Real rates and the dollar.** Falling real broad money usually travels with rising real interest rates and a firmer dollar (the 2022 regime: DXY closed at 103.5, up from 89.9 in 2020). Rising real broad money travels with the opposite. If your money signal and your rate/FX signal disagree, distrust the position size until they reconcile.

The point of the companions is *confirmation and direction*. If real M2 is rising, the multiple is rising, lending is accelerating, and real rates are easing, you have a clean, mutually-confirming risk-on regime — lean into it. If the signals fight each other, the regime is ambiguous and you should size down, not force a view.

#### Worked example: reading the four signals at the end of 2022

Apply the dashboard to a real moment — year-end 2022, when the headlines were a confusing mix of "still high inflation" and "recession coming."

- **Real M2:** nominal M2 *fell* 0.6% in 2022 while CPI ran ~6.5% year-end — real M2 was deeply *negative*, roughly −7%. Strong headwind signal.
- **The multiple:** M2 ÷ base = 21.36 ÷ 5.39 = **3.96**, down from the 2020–2021 peak — base shrinking, broad money shrinking, transmission tightening.
- **Lending and money funds:** bank lending standards were tightening sharply and deposits were fleeing into money-market funds — broad money draining further.
- **Real rates and dollar:** real 10-year yields had swung from about −1% to over +1.5%, and DXY had spiked to a multi-decade high near 114.8 in September 2022 — a textbook tightening regime.

Every signal pointed the same way: a hard liquidity headwind. The mutually-confirming read in late 2022 was **defensive** — and it correctly anticipated the 2023 disinflation and the rolling stress in rate-sensitive corners (regional banks, commercial real estate, long-duration tech). When all four dials agree, the regime read is high-conviction; that is when the slow signal is worth acting on.

### The one-paragraph operating procedure

Each month, pull M2 and CPI. Compute M2 year-over-year, subtract CPI year-over-year to get *real M2 growth*. Check the broad-money multiple (M2 ÷ monetary base) to confirm whether central-bank action is reaching the economy. If real M2 is positive and rising with the multiple stable or rising, set a structural *risk-on* lean and expect inflation pressure 12–24 months out. If real M2 is negative or falling, set a structural *defensive* lean and expect disinflation on a lag. Re-check at regime turns, and never let the slow dial override your risk management or your faster timing signals. That is the entire playbook — and it is more than most market participants, who watch the Fed's balance sheet and miss the layer that actually matters, ever do.
