In the spring of 2021, a remarkable thing happened to the price of a struggling video-game retailer's stock. GameStop — a company most analysts had left for dead — rocketed from a few dollars to over \$80 in a matter of weeks. Bitcoin doubled. Used cars, of all things, became an inflation story. Trading apps like Robinhood reported record sign-ups; Coinbase went public into a frenzy. Something was washing through every corner of the market at once, lifting things that had nothing in common except that they could be *bought*.

The trigger was not a new technology or an earnings cycle. It was a check. On March 11, 2021, the American Rescue Plan was signed into law, and within days the U.S. Treasury began depositing **\$1,400 per person** into roughly 160 million bank accounts. It was the third such round in a year. Most of that money went where it was supposed to — rent, groceries, catching up on bills. But a meaningful slice did not go to Main Street at all. It went into brokerage accounts. People who had never owned a single share opened an app, funded it with their stimulus money, and started buying. The "everything rally" was, in part, a fiscal event wearing a market costume.

Here is the thing a trader needs to extract from that episode, and it is the thesis of this entire post. Fiscal stimulus is not some vague "good for the economy" slogan. It is money — created by the government, injected into the system, and partly redirected into asset prices through a chain you can trace step by step. And the single most useful way to read it is not "how much is the government spending?" but "is the spending *accelerating or decelerating?*" That change — the **fiscal impulse** — is what actually moves growth and prices at the margin. Markets do not trade the level of the deficit. They trade its second derivative. By the end of this post you will know exactly what that means, why it matters, and how to position around it.

## Foundations: stimulus types, the fiscal impulse, and the multiplier

Before we can trade any of this, we have to be precise about what fiscal stimulus actually *is*, the different forms it takes, and the two concepts that do all the heavy lifting later: the **fiscal impulse** and the **multiplier**. We build everything from zero here, with no assumed background.

### What fiscal stimulus is, from zero

"Fiscal" simply means *related to government spending and taxation*. It is the half of macro policy run by the legislature and the treasury — Congress and the U.S. Treasury in America — as opposed to **monetary policy**, which is run by the central bank (the Federal Reserve) through interest rates and the money supply. The two are cousins, and they interact constantly, but they are different levers pulled by different hands.

**Fiscal stimulus** is the government deliberately injecting demand into the economy by spending more than it collects in taxes, usually during or after a downturn. The defining feature is that the government does *not* fully pay for the spending with current tax revenue. It borrows the difference. That gap between what the government spends and what it takes in is the **budget deficit**, and stimulus is, almost by definition, a deficit-financed injection.

Why would the government do this? The classic reason traces back to the economist John Maynard Keynes. In a recession, households and businesses pull back — they save instead of spend, they fear for their jobs, they cancel investments. Everyone trying to spend less at the same time makes the downturn worse, a vicious circle Keynes called a "paradox of thrift." The government, the argument goes, can break the circle by stepping in as the spender of last resort: it borrows money that private savers are too scared to spend, puts it into the economy, and props up demand until confidence returns. Stimulus is, in this telling, a way to put idle money back to work.

The key mental hook for a trader: **stimulus is fresh purchasing power dropped into the economy that nobody had to earn first.** That is what makes it so potent — and, as we will see, so inflationary when it is overdone.

It is worth pausing on why fiscal stimulus and monetary stimulus, though often discussed together, are fundamentally different in how they reach the economy. When the central bank cuts interest rates or buys bonds, it is making money *cheaper and more available* — but somebody still has to choose to borrow and spend it for that to translate into demand. Monetary policy works through the *willingness* of banks to lend and households and firms to borrow, which is why it can "push on a string" in a deep recession: rates can be at zero and nobody wants the loan. Fiscal stimulus has no such problem. When the Treasury credits \$1,400 to your account, the money is *in the economy* whether or not anyone felt like borrowing. It bypasses the banking-and-credit channel entirely and lands demand directly. This directness is why fiscal stimulus is the more powerful tool when the economy is frozen — and why it is the more dangerous one when the economy is already running hot, because there is no "string" to push on; the money goes in regardless.

### The three flavors of stimulus: transfers, direct spending, and tax cuts

Not all stimulus works the same way, and the differences matter for how fast and where the money shows up. There are three main channels.

**Transfers** are direct payments from the government to people or businesses, with nothing required in return. Stimulus checks are the purest example: the Treasury simply credits your bank account. Unemployment insurance top-ups, child tax credit payments, and small-business grants are also transfers. Transfers are fast — the money can be in your account in days — and they go straight to households, who then choose what to do with it. This is the channel most likely to leak into asset prices, because once the cash is in a bank account, the government has no control over whether it buys groceries or Bitcoin.

**Direct government spending** is the government itself buying goods and services or building things: infrastructure, defense contracts, vaccine purchases, payments to hospitals, aid to state and local governments. This is slower — you have to plan the project, award the contract, pour the concrete — but it is more *targeted*: the money is spent on the thing the government chose, and it tends to support employment in specific sectors. A road does not end up in a brokerage account.

**Tax cuts** are the government collecting *less* rather than spending more. Lower income-tax rates, payroll-tax holidays, business-investment incentives. Economically this also expands the deficit and puts money in private hands, but it tends to be slower-acting and more skewed toward whoever pays the most tax (often higher earners and corporations), who are more likely to save the windfall than spend it. That makes the demand "bang per buck" of a tax cut generally lower than a transfer to a cash-strapped household.

The distinction is not academic. A package weighted toward **transfers** hits demand fast and leaks into markets; a package weighted toward **infrastructure** hits demand slowly and supports it for years; a package weighted toward **tax cuts** may barely move demand at all if the recipients just save it. When you read that a stimulus bill is "\$2 trillion," your very next question should be: *what kind of \$2 trillion?*

#### Worked example: the total stimulus scale

Let us make the \$5 trillion concrete, because the scale is the whole story. The three major COVID-era packages, by headline size, were:

```
CARES Act (March 2020)              $2.2 trillion
December 2020 relief bill           $0.9 trillion
American Rescue Plan (March 2021)   $1.9 trillion
-----------------------------------------------
Total                               $5.0 trillion
```

Now put that against the size of the economy. U.S. GDP in 2020 was roughly \$21 trillion. So in the space of about twelve months, the federal government injected stimulus equal to nearly **a quarter of annual GDP**. To feel the scale a different way: spread \$5 trillion across roughly 330 million Americans and you get about **\$15,000 per person** — every man, woman, and child — in a single year, on top of normal government spending. No peacetime economy had ever seen a fiscal injection remotely this large this fast. The takeaway: when you hear "over \$5 trillion," the right reaction is not "that's a big number" but "that is a quarter of the entire economy, dropped in twelve months — of course it moved everything."

### The fiscal impulse: the change in the deficit, not the level

Now the single most important concept in this post, and the one that separates a trader who understands fiscal policy from one who just reads headlines.

The **budget deficit** is a *level* — how much the government is borrowing in a given year, often expressed as a percent of GDP so we can compare across time. A 5% deficit means the government borrowed an amount equal to 5% of the economy that year.

The **fiscal impulse** is the *change* in that deficit from one year to the next — more precisely, the change in the **cyclically-adjusted deficit** (we will unpack that qualifier in a moment). It answers a different question: is fiscal policy adding *more* demand this year than last, or *less*?

Why does the change matter more than the level? Because the economy adjusts to whatever the level is. If the government runs a steady 5% deficit year after year, the economy bakes that 5% of spending into its baseline — it is already in the GDP, already in employment, already in prices. It stops being "new" demand. What actually pushes growth up or drags it down at the margin is whether the deficit is *expanding* (more new demand than last year — an expansionary impulse, a tailwind) or *contracting* (less new demand than last year — a contractionary impulse, a headwind).

This is exactly analogous to acceleration in physics. Your *speed* is the deficit level. Your *acceleration* is the impulse. You can be driving very fast (a huge deficit) but if you are taking your foot off the gas (a shrinking deficit), you are decelerating, and the car feels it. A trader who only watches the speedometer — "the deficit is still enormous, so policy is still stimulative!" — misses that the economy is being pressed against the brakes.

The "cyclically-adjusted" qualifier matters because part of every deficit moves automatically with the economy, not because politicians decided anything. In a recession, tax revenue falls (people earn less) and safety-net spending rises (more unemployment claims), so the deficit widens *on its own* — these are called **automatic stabilizers**. The cyclically-adjusted (or "structural") deficit strips those automatic moves out to isolate the part that reflects *deliberate policy choices*. When economists talk about the fiscal impulse, they mean the change in this structural, policy-driven part — the part that genuinely represents a decision to push harder or pull back. For trading purposes you will often use the headline deficit change as a rough proxy, but know that the cleaner signal adjusts for the cycle.

![Bar chart of the US federal deficit as percent of GDP from 2019 to 2025 showing a spike to 15 percent in 2020 and a collapse to 5.4 percent in 2022 with the impulse marked](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-stimulus-and-markets-how-impulses-move-prices-3.png)

#### Worked example: the fiscal impulse from 2020 to 2022

This is the example that makes the whole concept click. Look at the federal deficit as a percent of GDP across the COVID era:

```
Fiscal year    Deficit (% of GDP)
2019           4.6
2020          15.0   <- the bazooka
2021          12.3
2022           5.4   <- still large, but collapsing
2023           6.2
2024           6.3
```

Now compute the *impulse* — the year-over-year change — for the key transitions:

```
2019 -> 2020:  15.0 - 4.6  = +10.4 pts   (huge POSITIVE impulse: foot slams the gas)
2020 -> 2021:  12.3 - 15.0 =  -2.7 pts   (already turning: smaller deficit than 2020)
2021 -> 2022:   5.4 - 12.3 =  -6.9 pts   (huge NEGATIVE impulse: foot off the gas)
```

Read those numbers carefully. In 2022 the deficit was still **5.4% of GDP** — by any normal standard, an enormous, "stimulative-looking" deficit. A trader watching only the level would have said fiscal policy was still wildly supportive. But the *impulse* was a brutal **negative 6.9 points of GDP** — one of the largest fiscal contractions in modern history, even though the deficit never actually went to surplus. The government did not have to *cut spending below normal* to create a headwind; it just had to spend *dramatically less than the year before*. That collapse in the impulse — not the level — is what made 2022 such a hostile year for risk assets, on top of the Fed's rate hikes. The lesson: a deficit can be large and shrinking at the same time, and the shrinking is what the economy feels.

### The multiplier: how one dollar of stimulus becomes more than a dollar of demand

The last foundational piece is the **fiscal multiplier** — the answer to "if the government spends one dollar, how much does total economic output rise?" The multiplier is the leverage in fiscal policy.

The mechanism is a chain of re-spending. Suppose the government sends you \$1,000 and you spend \$800 of it at local businesses (saving \$200). Those businesses now have \$800 of extra income; they spend, say, \$640 of *that* (saving the rest), which becomes someone else's income, who spends \$512 of it, and so on. Each round is smaller than the last, but they add up. If the total of all those re-spending rounds comes to \$1,500, the multiplier was 1.5 — one dollar of stimulus produced a dollar-fifty of total spending.

Multipliers are not fixed. They depend on a few things a trader should internalize:

- **Who gets the money.** A transfer to a cash-strapped household, who spends almost all of it, has a high multiplier. A tax cut to a wealthy person or corporation, who saves most of it, has a low one. This is the "marginal propensity to consume" — the fraction of an extra dollar that gets spent rather than saved.
- **The state of the economy.** In a deep recession with idle workers and factories, stimulus can have a multiplier well above 1 because it puts genuinely unused capacity to work. In a fully-employed, capacity-constrained economy, the same stimulus has a *lower* multiplier and a higher chance of leaking into inflation instead — there are no idle workers to hire, so the extra demand just bids up prices and wages.
- **Leakage.** Money spent on imports, or saved, or used to pay down debt, or routed into asset purchases, does not circulate as domestic demand. It "leaks" out of the multiplier chain. The 2021 leak into stocks and crypto is exactly this kind of leakage — money that boosted asset prices instead of GDP.

The practical upshot: the *same* headline stimulus number can produce wildly different economic effects depending on its composition and timing. A \$1.9 trillion package of transfers into a recovering, near-fully-employed economy (which is roughly what the American Rescue Plan was) is a different animal from \$1.9 trillion of infrastructure into a deeply depressed one. The first is far more likely to overheat demand and leak into markets and inflation — which is exactly what happened.

### Timing is everything: the right stimulus at the wrong time

There is a final foundational idea that ties the multiplier and the impulse together, and it is the most under-appreciated by people who only read headlines: the *timing* of stimulus relative to the economic cycle determines whether it helps or harms. Stimulus delivered into a deeply depressed economy with millions of idle workers is genuinely productive — it puts unused capacity back to work, has a high multiplier, and produces real output rather than just higher prices. The CARES Act in March 2020, dropped into an economy that had just shut down overnight, was close to this ideal: it cushioned a free-fall.

The same stimulus delivered into an economy that has *already recovered* is a different story. By the time the American Rescue Plan passed in March 2021, the economy was reopening fast, unemployment was falling, and much of the spare capacity had already been re-absorbed. Pouring \$1.9 trillion of transfers into that environment meant the multiplier was lower (less idle capacity to activate) and the leakage into inflation and assets was higher (the extra demand met a supply that could not expand fast enough). This is the core of the "the Fed and Congress did too much" critique of 2021: not that stimulus is bad, but that the *quantity* delivered exceeded what the recovering economy could absorb in real output, and the excess spilled into prices. For a trader, the lesson is to always ask not just "how big is the stimulus?" but "how much spare capacity does the economy have left to absorb it?" — because that ratio determines whether the impulse shows up as growth or as inflation.

## From a stimulus check to an asset price: the transmission chain

We now have the vocabulary. Let us trace, link by link, how a dollar of stimulus actually reaches a stock price, because seeing the chain is what lets you anticipate where the money goes.

**Link 1 — Congress authorizes, Treasury funds.** A stimulus bill passes. The Treasury needs the cash, so it sells bonds (Treasuries) to raise it. During the COVID era the Federal Reserve was simultaneously buying enormous quantities of those bonds through **quantitative easing**, which is why this was not just fiscal policy in isolation — it was fiscal stimulus *financed by a money-printing central bank*. The two reinforced each other. (We have a whole companion piece on the bond-buying side of this; see the cross-links at the end.)

**Link 2 — The money is injected.** The Treasury sends transfers to households and makes direct payments to states, hospitals, and contractors. For transfers specifically, the cash lands in personal bank accounts within days. This is the moment "new" purchasing power enters the system — money that was not there before and that nobody had to produce anything to receive.

**Link 3 — Households decide what to do with it.** Here is the fork in the road. A household with the new cash can: (a) spend it on goods and services, (b) pay down debt, (c) save it in the bank, or (d) invest it in assets. The split depends on who got the money and how secure they feel. A family behind on rent spends it immediately on necessities — pure demand. A household that was already financially fine and got a check anyway is far more likely to save or invest it, because their immediate needs are met. In 2020-21, with much of normal spending (travel, restaurants, events) shut down by the pandemic, an unusually large fraction of the money piled up as savings and investment rather than getting spent on services that were unavailable.

**Link 4a — The demand path.** The portion that gets spent on goods becomes demand. Because services were partly shut, an outsized share went to *physical goods*: home gym equipment, furniture, electronics, cars. This is where the supply problem bit. Factories and shipping were disrupted by the pandemic at the exact moment demand for goods surged. Too much money chasing too few goods — the textbook recipe for inflation, which we examine in detail next.

**Link 4b — The asset path.** The portion that gets invested goes into brokerage accounts and crypto exchanges. This is the leak into markets. It is "extra" buying pressure on stocks and digital assets that has nothing to do with those companies' fundamentals — it is just fresh cash looking for a home. When millions of people do this at once, with money they did not have to earn, you get the everything-rally: prices rise across the board, valuations stretch, and the most speculative corners (meme stocks, small-cap crypto) run the hottest because that is where new, price-insensitive money tends to flow.

![Channel showing a 1400 dollar check landing in a bank account moving to a trading app and buying stocks and crypto which lifts paper wealth and feeds back into more spending](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-stimulus-and-markets-how-impulses-move-prices-6.png)

The asset path is the link most macro commentary misses, because it is the one that connects a *fiscal* program to a *market* move with no obvious intermediary. There is no line item in the stimulus bill labeled "buy stocks." The leak happens entirely at the household's discretion, in the gap between the cash landing and it being spent. And the design of the 2021 environment made that gap unusually leaky: services were shut, so the usual outlets for spare cash (vacations, dining, concerts) were closed; commission-free trading apps had removed every friction from opening a brokerage account; and a culture of online retail trading was at its peak. So an unusually large slice of an unusually large transfer found its way to the only thing that was open twenty-four hours a day and frictionless to buy — financial assets. That is why the figure above routes the check through a bank account and a trading app and into stocks and crypto: every arrow is a real step that millions of people actually took.

#### Worked example: a \$1,400 check times 160 million recipients

Let us size the asset leak directly. The American Rescue Plan sent \$1,400 per eligible person, and roughly **160 million** payments went out. Start with the gross flow:

```
$1,400 per recipient  x  160,000,000 recipients  =  $224 billion
```

That is about a quarter-trillion dollars dropped into household bank accounts from this one provision in a matter of weeks. Now apply a plausible split. Suppose households spent 70% on goods and services, used 15% to pay down debt, and invested or saved the remaining 15%. The investment-and-savings slice alone is:

```
15%  x  $224 billion  =  $33.6 billion
```

Even if only a third of *that* slice went into markets rather than a savings account, you get roughly **\$11 billion** of fresh, price-insensitive buying flowing into stocks and crypto from a single round of checks — and there were three rounds. To feel why that matters: \$11 billion of indiscriminate buying concentrated into the smallest, most speculative corners of the market (where a few billion dollars moves prices a lot) is more than enough to send meme stocks parabolic and double the price of Bitcoin. The takeaway: you do not need *most* of a stimulus check to leak into markets for the effect to be enormous — a small percentage of a quarter-trillion-dollar flow, aimed at the frothiest assets, is a tidal wave.

**Link 5 — The feedback loop.** Rising asset prices make people feel richer (the "wealth effect"), which makes them more willing to spend, which feeds back into demand. And rising prices attract more buyers chasing the move, which pushes prices higher still. Both loops are self-reinforcing on the way up — and, crucially, they run in reverse when the stimulus stops. This reversibility is the part traders must respect: the same wealth-effect loop that amplified the 2021 rally turned into an accelerant on the way down in 2022, as falling asset prices made households feel poorer, pulled back spending, and drained the speculative bid that had been refreshed monthly by stimulus and was now gone.

That is the full chain: package → transfer → wallet → (goods demand **or** asset purchase) → (inflation **and** higher asset prices) → wealth effect feedback. Every link is a real, observable step, and every link is where you can look for the money.

## Demand-pull inflation: too much money chasing too few goods

The inflation that followed the 2020-21 stimulus is the cleanest real-world example of **demand-pull inflation** you will ever see, so it is worth understanding the mechanism precisely.

Economists split inflation into two broad types by cause. **Cost-push** inflation comes from the supply side — a shock that makes things more expensive to produce, like an oil embargo or a crop failure, pushing prices up even if demand is flat. **Demand-pull** inflation comes from the demand side — too much spending power chasing a limited supply of goods, pulling prices up. The phrase that captures it perfectly is "**too much money chasing too few goods.**"

Walk through it with the stimulus. The \$5 trillion injection put an enormous amount of new spending power into household hands. At the same time, the supply of goods was *constrained* — factories throttled by the pandemic, ports clogged, semiconductors scarce, workers out sick. So you had a record surge of demand colliding with a hobbled supply. When a lot of buyers compete for a limited number of goods, sellers raise prices because they can. That is demand-pull inflation, and the 2021-22 episode was almost a laboratory demonstration of it.

Two subtleties a trader should hold onto. First, **inflation is a flow with a lag.** The money landed; it took time to circulate, to be spent, to bid up prices, and for those price increases to show up in the official statistics. The checks went out in spring 2021; inflation did not peak until mid-2022. If you waited for the inflation print to confirm the problem, you were a year late. The smart read was to see the demand surge coming *from the stimulus itself* and anticipate the inflation, rather than wait for it.

Second, **once it starts, it can feed on itself.** When workers see prices rising, they demand higher wages; when businesses pay higher wages, they raise prices to cover them; and the cycle can become self-sustaining — a "wage-price spiral" — even after the original demand shock fades. This is why central banks are so terrified of letting demand-pull inflation get embedded: the initial cause (stimulus) may be long gone, but the dynamics it set off can persist, which is exactly why the Fed had to hike so aggressively in 2022-23 to break it.

There is a useful diagnostic buried in the cost-push versus demand-pull distinction, and it is one a trader can apply in real time. If inflation is broad — showing up across goods *and* services, in things that have nothing to do with oil or supply chains, especially in sticky categories like rent and wages — it is demand-pull, and it will not fade on its own; it needs the central bank to crush demand. If inflation is narrow — concentrated in energy and a few supply-disrupted goods, with the rest of the basket calm — it is cost-push, and it may well mean-revert once the supply shock passes, requiring less aggressive policy. The 2021-22 episode was unmistakably the *broad* kind: by 2022 inflation had spread well beyond used cars and gasoline into rent, restaurant meals, and services, which is precisely what told the Fed (belatedly) that this was a demand problem requiring a demand solution, not a supply blip that would resolve itself. When you decompose an inflation print, the breadth is the tell: broad and sticky means the stimulus-driven demand-pull dynamic is in control, and the policy response — and the headwind for risk assets — will be correspondingly larger.

![Line chart of CPI inflation year over year from 2020 to 2026 with a green shaded stimulus window in 2020 to 2021 and a peak of 9.06 percent in June 2022 marked](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-stimulus-and-markets-how-impulses-move-prices-5.png)

#### Worked example: the lag from stimulus to the 9.06% inflation peak

Let us time the chain precisely, because the lag is where most people get the trade wrong.

```
2021-03    American Rescue Plan signed; $1,400 checks begin landing
2021-05    CPI year-over-year                4.99%
2021-11    CPI year-over-year                6.81%
2022-01    CPI year-over-year                7.48%
2022-06    CPI year-over-year                9.06%   <- 40-year peak
```

From the moment the third round of checks landed (March 2021) to the inflation peak (June 2022) is about **fifteen months**. That is the lag from a demand injection to its full price effect in this episode. Notice the path: inflation was already at 5% when the checks went out — it had started accelerating — and it kept climbing for more than a year afterward, more than *doubling* from 4.99% to 9.06%. The money was working its way through the system the entire time. A trader who understood the transmission chain in early 2021 could see that a \$1.9 trillion transfer into a supply-constrained economy that was *already* running 5% inflation was pouring gasoline on a fire. The Fed, infamously, called it "transitory" and waited; the data made fools of that call by the summer of 2022. The intuition to carry: a demand injection shows up in prices with a lag of roughly a year, so the time to position is when the *stimulus* is announced, not when the *inflation* prints.

## The fiscal impulse as the tradeable variable: trading the second derivative

We return now to the central claim, armed with everything above. The reason a trader cares about all this machinery is that the fiscal impulse — the change in the deficit — is one of the most reliable *leading* inputs to growth and risk appetite, and it is largely *knowable in advance* because fiscal policy is set by legislation with published schedules.

Think about what makes a variable tradeable. It has to (a) move prices, (b) be predictable enough to position around, and (c) not already be fully priced in. The fiscal impulse scores well on all three:

- **It moves prices** because it changes the rate at which new demand is entering the economy, which feeds growth, inflation, and corporate earnings.
- **It is predictable** because stimulus packages have known sizes and, crucially, known *expiration dates*. When a one-year transfer program is set to end, you can see the negative impulse coming months ahead. The 2022 fiscal cliff was visible on a calendar in 2021.
- **It is under-watched.** Most market participants react to the *level* of the deficit ("the government is spending a lot, that's stimulative") and to the inflation prints after the fact. Far fewer track the *change* in the structural deficit and position ahead of it. That gap is the edge.

The "second derivative" framing is the heart of it. If the deficit (the level) is the first derivative of the government's fiscal stance, then the impulse (the change in the deficit) is the second derivative. Markets, almost universally, trade the second derivative of macro variables — not "is the data good?" but "is the data getting *better or worse* than it was, and than expected?" An economy where growth is bad but improving (positive second derivative) is a buy; an economy where growth is great but decelerating (negative second derivative) is a sell. The fiscal impulse is precisely the second derivative of fiscal policy, and it behaves the same way: a *rising* impulse is a tailwind even if the deficit is moderate, and a *falling* impulse is a headwind even if the deficit is still large.

Why do markets trade the second derivative rather than the level? Because a market price is not a verdict on how things *are* — it is a bet on how things will *change* relative to what is already priced in. By the time a deficit has been large for a year, that largeness is in the data, in earnings, in valuations; it is old news, fully discounted. What is *not* yet discounted is the turn — the moment the largeness starts to fade or grow. Asset prices move when the *rate of change* surprises, because that is the new information. This is the same reason a stock can fall on "good" earnings that merely met expectations, or rally on a smaller-than-feared loss: the level was known, the change versus expectation was the news. Fiscal policy obeys the identical logic. The market had priced a huge 2021 deficit; what it had to *re-*price in 2022 was the deficit collapsing faster than the support could be replaced. The level was discounted; the negative impulse was the surprise that moved prices. Train yourself to look one derivative further out than the headline, and you are looking where the market actually trades.

![Two column comparison of the deficit level versus its change showing that a shrinking deficit is still a headwind because growth responds to the impulse not the level](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-stimulus-and-markets-how-impulses-move-prices-4.png)

This is why the figure above frames it as a before/after of two *readings* of the same data. On the left, the naive read: "the 2021 deficit was 12.3% and the 2022 deficit was 5.4% — both huge, so fiscal policy is still supportive." On the right, the correct read: "the deficit shrank by 6.9 points of GDP, an enormous negative impulse, so fiscal policy flipped from a tailwind to a headwind." Same numbers, opposite conclusions. The trader who reads the right column saw the 2022 risk-asset drawdown coming as a *fiscal* event, not just a Fed event. The two reinforced each other: the Fed was hiking *and* the fiscal impulse was collapsing, a double tightening that crushed everything from long-duration tech stocks to crypto.

#### Worked example: comparing two countries with identical deficits but opposite impulses

To cement that the impulse, not the level, is what matters, here is a stylized but instructive comparison. Imagine two economies, both running a deficit of exactly **6% of GDP** this year — identical levels.

```
                 Last year    This year    Impulse        Read
Country A          3%            6%         +3 pts (up)    EXPANDING: tailwind
Country B          9%            6%         -3 pts (down)  CONTRACTING: headwind
```

Both countries have a 6% deficit right now. A trader looking only at the level would treat them identically — "both running big deficits, both stimulative." But Country A is *accelerating* its spending (deficit doubled from 3% to 6%), pumping a positive +3-point impulse into demand: a growth tailwind, good for cyclical stocks and risk. Country B is *decelerating* (deficit fell from 9% to 6%), delivering a negative −3-point impulse: a growth headwind, a reason to fade risk. Identical levels, opposite trades. This is the entire reason the second derivative is the variable: the *direction of change* determines whether fiscal policy is helping or hurting at the margin, and the level alone tells you almost nothing about that. Whenever you read a deficit number, your reflex should be to immediately ask "compared to what last year?"

## The fiscal cliff: when stimulus rolls off

The flip side of a stimulus boom is the **fiscal cliff** — the moment when temporary stimulus programs expire and the impulse turns sharply negative. This is one of the most reliably tradeable setups in macro, because cliffs are scheduled.

The term comes from the shape of the chart: government support that ramped up steeply and then, when the program's authorization runs out, drops off a "cliff" back toward zero. Because so much COVID stimulus was explicitly temporary — one-time checks, time-limited unemployment top-ups, expiring tax credits — its expiration was baked in from the start. The enhanced unemployment benefits expired. The expanded child tax credit lapsed at the end of 2021. The checks stopped coming. Each of these was a scheduled withdrawal of demand.

The mechanism of the headwind is straightforward and follows directly from the impulse concept. When a transfer program is running, it adds, say, 2% of GDP of demand each year. The economy adjusts to that. When the program ends, that 2% does not just stop growing — it *disappears* from the flow of new spending, so the change in the deficit (the impulse) swings hard negative. Households who were receiving the support now have less to spend; the multiplier runs in reverse as the re-spending chain contracts; demand softens. The cliff is, in impulse terms, the steepest possible negative second derivative.

For markets, a fiscal cliff is a headwind on several fronts at once. Slower demand growth means softer corporate revenue, which pressures earnings and equity prices. The wealth-effect feedback loop runs backward: as the speculative money that flowed in during the boom stops being replenished, the most stimulus-sensitive assets (meme stocks, speculative crypto, unprofitable growth names) deflate fastest. And if the cliff coincides with a central bank that is *also* tightening — as it did in 2022 — the two reinforce into a powerful drag.

The crucial trading insight is that **the cliff is visible in advance.** You do not have to guess. Stimulus legislation specifies expiration dates. When you see that a major support program is scheduled to end in, say, Q3, you know a negative impulse is coming, and you can position for it before the data confirms it. This is the rare macro setup where the timing is on a published calendar rather than buried in a forecast.

The term "fiscal cliff" entered the mainstream vocabulary in late 2012, when a bundle of expiring tax cuts and automatic spending cuts was set to hit all at once on January 1, 2013 — a combination the Congressional Budget Office warned could push the still-fragile post-2008 economy back into recession. Markets spent the back half of 2012 trading the cliff: every headline about a congressional deal moved equities, because participants understood that a sudden, large negative fiscal impulse would hit growth. That episode is the template. Whenever a large tranche of fiscal support is scheduled to expire on a hard date, the months leading up to it become a fiscal-impulse trade, with risk assets sensitive to whether the cliff is softened (programs extended, impulse stays positive) or allowed to bite (programs lapse, impulse goes negative).

A subtlety worth flagging: a cliff does not require any *new* austerity to be a headwind. Nobody has to vote to cut spending. The support simply *stops being renewed*, and because the economy had adapted to its presence, the absence is felt as a contraction. This is exactly the impulse logic again — the level of support can stay positive while its *change* goes sharply negative — and it is why traders who watch only for explicit spending cuts miss the most common form of fiscal tightening, which is simply the quiet expiration of yesterday's stimulus. The 2022 cliff was not legislated as austerity; it was just the 2021 programs running out their clocks. The headwind was identical to a deliberate cut.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of intuitive-sounding beliefs about fiscal stimulus are wrong in ways that cost traders money. Here are the most important, each corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "Stimulus only helps Main Street, not markets."** The cleanest refutation is the 2021 everything-rally. A measurable slice of the \$1,400 checks went straight into brokerage and crypto accounts — Robinhood and Coinbase reported record sign-ups in exactly the weeks the checks landed, and the most speculative assets ran the hottest precisely because price-insensitive new money flows to the frothiest corners. Bitcoin roughly doubled in early 2021; meme stocks went vertical. Stimulus does not stop at the grocery store. Whatever is not spent on goods leaks into assets, and in a world of shut-down services and online trading apps, that leak was enormous. Markets are downstream of fiscal policy whether or not the policy intended it.

**Misconception 2: "The level of the deficit is what matters."** This is the single most common and most expensive error, and the entire post is a refutation of it. The 2022 deficit was still a large 5.4% of GDP, yet 2022 was a brutal year for risk assets — because the *change* in the deficit was a punishing −6.9 points of GDP. A large but shrinking deficit is a headwind; a small but growing one can be a tailwind. The economy and markets respond to the impulse — the second derivative — not the level. Anyone who looked at the still-elevated 2022 deficit and concluded "policy is still supportive" got the regime exactly backwards.

**Misconception 3: "Stimulus is free."** Stimulus is borrowed, and the bill arrives later. The COVID packages helped push federal debt from about \$22.7 trillion in 2019 to over \$35 trillion by 2024, and — critically — when interest rates rose to fight the inflation the stimulus helped cause, the cost of *servicing* that debt exploded. Federal net interest outlays roughly tripled from around \$345 billion in 2020 to roughly \$880 billion in 2024, the first year interest exceeded the entire national defense budget. The "free money" of 2020-21 is now an \$880-billion-a-year interest bill that crowds out other spending and pressures the bond market. Stimulus is a transfer of demand from the future to the present, financed by debt, and the future eventually shows up.

**Misconception 4: "More stimulus is always better for stocks."** Only up to a point. Stimulus that overheats a supply-constrained economy produces inflation, and inflation forces the central bank to hike rates, and rate hikes are poison for the very assets the stimulus initially inflated. The 2021 stimulus boosted stocks; the inflation it helped cause forced the Fed to hike from zero to over 5%, which then crushed stocks in 2022. Beyond the level that uses up an economy's spare capacity, additional stimulus buys you inflation and a hawkish central bank, not higher asset prices. The relationship is a hump, not a straight line.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Here is how the fiscal-impulse framework actually played out across the COVID cycle, with real dates and numbers — the case study every macro trader should have memorized.

**The 2020-21 surge.** The positive impulse was the largest in modern history: the deficit went from 4.6% of GDP in 2019 to 15.0% in 2020, a +10.4-point impulse, then stayed elevated at 12.3% in 2021. With the Fed simultaneously pinning rates at zero and buying bonds, this was the most stimulative fiscal-plus-monetary combination ever assembled in peacetime. Markets responded exactly as the transmission chain predicts: the S&P 500 staged a historic recovery and pushed to new highs, the most speculative assets ran the hardest (Bitcoin from under \$29,000 to over \$46,000 across 2021, meme stocks parabolic), and the leak into brokerage accounts was visible in record retail-trading activity. This was the impulse working through the asset path.

**The 2022 inflation.** The demand path caught up with a lag. CPI, already at 4.99% when the spring-2021 checks landed, climbed relentlessly to a 40-year peak of **9.06% in June 2022**. The "transitory" framing collapsed. This forced the second act: the Fed pivoted to the fastest hiking cycle in four decades, taking the policy rate from near zero to over 5%. The inflation was the bill for the demand surge, arriving fifteen months after the injection — exactly the lag the transmission chain implies.

**The 2022 fiscal-cliff drag.** Simultaneously, the fiscal impulse went violently negative. The deficit collapsed from 12.3% in 2021 to 5.4% in 2022 — a −6.9-point impulse — as the temporary programs expired and the checks stopped. So 2022 delivered a rare *double* tightening: the fiscal impulse was the most negative in modern history *at the same time* the Fed was hiking the most aggressively in forty years. Both legs of policy slammed shut at once. The result was one of the worst years on record for a balanced portfolio — both stocks and bonds fell hard — and the speculative assets that had led the 2021 rally led the 2022 collapse (Bitcoin from over \$46,000 to roughly \$16,500). A trader reading the fiscal impulse alongside the rate path saw this coming: the same engine that drove the everything-rally went into reverse, and the most stimulus-sensitive assets felt it first and worst.

The through-line: the impulse drove the boom, the lagged inflation forced the policy response, and the impulse rolling off compounded the drag. One framework — the change in the deficit — explained the entire arc.

**The contrast with 2008-09.** It is instructive to compare the COVID episode with the previous big stimulus, the response to the 2008 financial crisis, because the difference explains why one produced an inflation and the other did not. The 2009 American Recovery and Reinvestment Act was roughly \$800 billion — large for its day, but a fraction of the COVID packages — and it was weighted heavily toward infrastructure and tax provisions rather than direct cash transfers, so it reached the economy slowly and with a lower bang-per-buck. Critically, it landed into an economy with an enormous *hole* to fill: unemployment hit 10%, households were deleveraging from a housing bust, and banks were not lending. The stimulus mostly filled the hole rather than overflowing it, which is why the 2010s were defined by *persistently below-target* inflation, not above it. The COVID stimulus was the mirror image: far larger, far faster, far more weighted to transfers, and dropped into an economy whose hole closed quickly. Same tool, opposite outcome — and the difference was entirely about the *size of the impulse relative to the spare capacity it was filling*. For a trader, the comparison is the clearest possible proof that the headline word "stimulus" tells you almost nothing; the size, speed, composition, and economic context are everything.

## How to trade it: the fiscal-impulse playbook

Everything above converges on a concrete, repeatable playbook. The goal is not to predict the exact level of any market but to read whether the fiscal wind is at your back or in your face, and to position accordingly.

![Pipeline of the fiscal impulse playbook track the change in the deficit read the sign map the calendar go risk on into a rising impulse and defensive into a fiscal cliff](/imgs/blogs/fiscal-stimulus-and-markets-how-impulses-move-prices-7.png)

**Step 1 — Track the impulse, not the level.** Build the habit of always computing the *change* in the deficit as a percent of GDP, year over year (and ideally the change in the cyclically-adjusted deficit, which the CBO and IMF publish). Your dashboard number is not "the deficit is 6%" but "the deficit went from 9% to 6%, a −3-point impulse." The Congressional Budget Office, the IMF Fiscal Monitor, and the Hutchins Center's "fiscal impact" measure all publish versions of this — use them. The sign of the impulse is your single most important fiscal read.

**Step 2 — Read the sign and size the move.** A *positive and rising* impulse (deficit expanding) is a growth tailwind: lean toward risk — equities, cyclicals, credit, and the most stimulus-sensitive assets when the impulse is large and the economy has spare capacity. A *negative and falling* impulse (deficit contracting, a fiscal cliff) is a growth headwind: lean defensive — trim high-beta exposure, favor quality over speculation, watch market breadth for the speculative leaders rolling over first. The bigger the impulse in absolute terms, the bigger the position conviction.

**Step 3 — Map the calendar and front-run the cliff.** This is the edge that fiscal policy uniquely offers. Stimulus programs have *expiration dates written into law*. When a major transfer program is scheduled to end, mark the date and recognize that a negative impulse is coming months ahead of the data. Position for the fiscal cliff *before* the demand softening shows up in the prints. Conversely, when a new package is being negotiated, the positive impulse is often telegraphed by the legislative process long before the money lands. Trade the announcement and the schedule, not the lagging economic data.

**Step 4 — Combine with the monetary impulse.** Fiscal and monetary policy are two engines, and the most powerful setups are when they point the same way. 2020-21 was both stimulative (huge positive fiscal impulse *and* zero rates plus QE) — maximum tailwind. 2022 was both restrictive (huge negative fiscal impulse *and* aggressive rate hikes) — maximum headwind. When they align, conviction is highest. When they conflict — fiscal loosening while the central bank tightens, or vice versa — they partly cancel, and the net impulse is what matters; size down and respect the ambiguity.

This combination view also resolves a puzzle that confuses many traders: why didn't large continuing deficits *after* 2022 keep markets propped up? The deficit stayed elevated (6.2% in 2023, 6.3% in 2024), so on a level basis fiscal policy still looked supportive. But the *impulse* had flattened — the deficit was roughly stable year to year, so it was no longer adding new demand at the margin — and the Fed was holding rates at multi-decade highs. A flat fiscal impulse combined with a restrictive monetary stance nets out to neutral-to-tight, which is why the post-2022 environment was a grind rather than a repeat of the 2021 melt-up, despite deficits that would have looked enormous in any earlier decade. The level was a red herring; the impulse and the rate path told the real story. Always read the two engines together, and always read each one as a change, not a level.

**Step 5 — Know your invalidation.** A fiscal-impulse view is invalidated by exactly the things that change the impulse: a surprise new stimulus package (flips a negative impulse positive), a central-bank pivot that *offsets* the fiscal stance (the Fed cutting hard into a fiscal cliff can cushion it), or a supply shock that swamps the demand picture. The discipline is to hold the view only as long as the impulse you measured is intact; the moment a new package passes or the central bank changes course, recompute the net impulse and re-position. The framework is robust precisely because it is mechanical: you are tracking a measurable number, not a narrative.

One more practical note on execution. Because the fiscal impulse is a slow-moving, low-frequency variable — it updates with the budget cycle and legislative calendar, not minute to minute — it is best used to set your *bias and positioning regime*, not to time entries to the day. Think of it as the wind: it tells you which way to lean and how hard, while your shorter-term signals (price action, the rate path, positioning data) tell you when to actually put the trade on. A trader who knows the fiscal impulse is turning negative does not short the open on the day a program expires; they carry a defensive bias into that window, fade rallies rather than chase them, and let the slower fiscal drag work in their favor over weeks and months. The impulse is a tide, not a wave. You do not trade the tide tick by tick — you make sure you are swimming with it, and you build that into every position you size while it is running your way.

The deepest point to carry away is the one this post is built around. Fiscal stimulus is real money injected into the economy and, through a traceable chain, into markets — but the question that prints the move is never "how big is the deficit?" It is "is the deficit getting bigger or smaller, faster or slower, than before?" Markets trade the second derivative. Read the fiscal impulse, watch the calendar for the cliff, line it up with the monetary impulse, and you are reading the fiscal wind before most of the market has even noticed which way it is blowing.
