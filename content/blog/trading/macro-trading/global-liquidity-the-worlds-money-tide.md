In the spring of 2020, the financial world did something it had never done at that speed. As economies locked down and markets seized up, the world's major central banks opened the spigots all at once. The US Federal Reserve bought trillions of dollars of bonds. The European Central Bank launched a €1.85 trillion emergency program. The Bank of Japan kept its balance sheet pinned wide open. China's central bank eased. Within two years, the Fed's balance sheet alone had ballooned from about \$4.3 trillion to nearly \$9 trillion — and the other three were expanding in parallel.

Then something strange happened, or rather, something that looks strange until you understand it. *Everything* went up. US stocks roared to record highs. Bitcoin went from under \$5,000 to nearly \$69,000. Junk bonds rallied so hard their yields hit record lows. Emerging-market equities surged. Even assets with no earnings, no cash flow, and no obvious connection to lockdown economics — meme stocks, unprofitable tech, digital art — flew. It was called the "everything rally," and the name is a clue. When *everything* rises together, the cause is usually not any one company's fundamentals. It is something flowing underneath all of them at once.

That something is **global liquidity** — the worldwide tide of money the central banks were creating. And when the tide reversed in 2022, the same logic ran in reverse: stocks, crypto, and credit fell together in the worst cross-asset drawdown in decades. The trader who was watching the tide saw both moves coming as one move. The trader who was analyzing individual companies saw a hundred unrelated stories. This post is about how to be the first trader. We will build the whole idea from zero.

## Foundations: What global liquidity is and who creates it

Before any signal, we need to define the thing precisely, because "liquidity" is one of the most overloaded words in finance. People use it to mean at least three different things, and confusing them is the fastest way to get lost.

### Three meanings of "liquidity" — and which one we mean

When a trader says "liquidity," they could mean any of these:

- **Market liquidity** — how easily you can buy or sell an asset without moving its price. A stock with tight bid–ask spreads and deep order books is "liquid." This is a *microstructure* property of one market.
- **Funding liquidity** — how easily a bank or fund can borrow short-term cash to fund its positions. When funding liquidity dries up, leveraged players are forced to sell. This is about the *plumbing* of credit.
- **Monetary / macro liquidity** — the total quantity of money-like balances the central banks and banking system have created and pushed into the financial system. This is the *tide* — the ocean of money in which all assets float.

This post is about the **third** meaning: macro liquidity, the global money tide. The other two matter (a liquidity *crisis* is usually all three failing at once), but the slow, powerful force that drives multi-year moves in risk assets is the macro tide. When we say "global liquidity" from here on, we mean the combined money created by the world's major central banks plus the credit the offshore dollar system layers on top.

### What "money created by a central bank" actually means

To understand the tide you have to understand its source. A central bank creates money by **expanding its balance sheet** — and that mechanic is simpler than it sounds.

A central bank, like any institution, has a balance sheet with assets and liabilities. When it wants to inject money into the system, it *buys assets* — typically government bonds — and pays for them by *creating new reserves*, which are electronic money it conjures into existence. The bonds go on the asset side; the new reserves go on the liability side. The balance sheet grows on both sides at once. Those new reserves are base money that did not exist a moment before. (We unpack this mechanic fully in [quantitative easing explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money), and the deeper question of *what counts as money at all* in [what money really is](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-money-really-is-base-money-broad-money-traders).)

So the **size of a central bank's balance sheet** is, to a first approximation, a measure of how much money it has created. A bigger balance sheet means more reserves in the system. This is why the single most useful proxy for liquidity is a central bank's *total assets*: it is a direct, public, weekly-updated number that tracks how much money the bank has pumped out.

### The four major central banks

Most of the world's macro liquidity comes from four institutions:

- **The Federal Reserve (Fed)** — the US central bank, issuer of the dollar, the world's reserve currency. Its balance sheet runs around **\$6.7 trillion** as of mid-2025 (down from a \$8.96T peak). Because the dollar is the world's funding currency, the Fed is the single most important liquidity provider on Earth. (Why the dollar dominates is its own deep topic in [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).)
- **The European Central Bank (ECB)** — issuer of the euro, balance sheet around **€6.4 trillion**.
- **The Bank of Japan (BoJ)** — issuer of the yen, balance sheet around **¥760 trillion**, which is enormous relative to Japan's economy. The BoJ has been the most aggressive money-printer in the developed world for over a decade.
- **The People's Bank of China (PBoC)** — issuer of the yuan, balance sheet around **¥45 trillion**. China's liquidity creation works differently (more through bank lending quotas than outright asset purchases), but it is a major source.

These four together control the bulk of the global money tide. There are smaller players — the Bank of England, the Swiss National Bank, the Reserve Bank of Australia — but the big four set the level.

### The shadow layer: the eurodollar system

Here is the part most retail traders never learn. On top of the official central-bank money sits a vast, lightly-regulated layer of **offshore dollar credit** — the **eurodollar system**. "Eurodollars" are dollar-denominated deposits and loans held *outside* the US banking system, originally in Europe (hence the name), now everywhere. A bank in Singapore lending dollars to a company in Brazil is creating eurodollar liquidity that no central bank directly printed.

This shadow layer is genuinely huge — by many estimates larger than the official monetary base — and it expands and contracts on its own credit cycle. When offshore banks are confident, they extend dollar credit freely and the effective global money supply swells beyond what the Fed's balance sheet shows. When they get scared, they pull dollar credit, and the world experiences a *dollar shortage* even if the Fed has not changed anything. (The repo market is the visible tip of this iceberg; see [shadow banking and the repo market](/blog/trading/finance/shadow-banking-and-the-repo-market).)

The practical takeaway: global liquidity is **central-bank money plus shadow credit**. The central-bank part is measurable and public. The shadow part is harder to see, but it shows up indirectly — in the dollar's exchange rate, in cross-currency basis swaps, in funding stress indicators. We will use the measurable part as our anchor and treat the dollar and volatility as windows onto the shadow part.

### Why you convert everything to dollars

Now we hit the wrinkle that trips people up. The four central banks measure their balance sheets in *four different currencies*. The Fed in dollars, the ECB in euros, the BoJ in yen, the PBoC in yuan. You cannot simply add ¥760 trillion to €6.4 trillion to \$6.7 trillion — those are different units, like adding meters to gallons.

To get one global number, you **convert each balance sheet into a common currency**, and by overwhelming convention that currency is the **US dollar**, because the dollar is the unit in which global trade, debt, and reserves are denominated. So you take the ECB's €6.4 trillion, multiply by the EUR/USD exchange rate, and get its dollar value. Same for the BoJ's yen and the PBoC's yuan. Then you add the four dollar figures into one global liquidity total.

This conversion introduces something crucial: **the exchange rate now affects the total.** If the euro weakens against the dollar, the ECB's balance sheet *shrinks in dollar terms* even though it has not changed in euros. A strong dollar mechanically shrinks the foreign legs of global liquidity; a weak dollar inflates them. This is one reason the dollar's level is so important to risk assets — it is partly a *liquidity* signal, not just an FX signal. We will come back to this.

There is a second, deeper reason the dollar matters, and it is worth slowing down for because it is where the FX channel and the shadow channel meet. The world has borrowed an enormous quantity of dollars *outside* the United States — companies, governments, and banks in Europe, Asia, and the emerging world owe trillions of dollars they did not earn in dollars. By common estimates, dollar-denominated debt owed by borrowers outside the US runs to the tens of trillions. When the dollar *rises*, every one of those borrowers faces a heavier debt burden in their home currency: they owe the same number of dollars, but each dollar now costs more euros, yen, or reais to buy. That is a *tightening* of global financial conditions with no central bank lifting a finger. Borrowers scramble for dollars, funding costs rise, and risky positions get unwound. So a strong dollar drains global liquidity *twice over*: it mechanically shrinks the foreign balance-sheet legs in the sum, *and* it squeezes the vast offshore-dollar borrower base. A weak dollar does the reverse, loosening conditions and effectively adding liquidity. This is why old market hands say "the dollar is the world's master variable" — and why a falling DXY is one of the most reliable tailwinds for risk assets, while a surging DXY is one of the most reliable warnings. ### How the shadow layer multiplies liquidity through collateral

To really understand why global liquidity is bigger and more volatile than the four balance sheets, you need one more mechanic: **collateral**. In the modern funding system, a dollar of safe collateral — a Treasury bond, say — does not sit still. It gets *reused*. A hedge fund pledges a Treasury to a bank to borrow cash; the bank pledges that same Treasury onward to borrow from someone else; and so on. Each reuse, called *rehypothecation*, creates a new layer of funding off the same underlying bond. The number of times a piece of collateral is reused is the **collateral multiplier**, and in good times it is high — a single high-quality bond might support several dollars of funding up and down the chain.

This is why liquidity can swell far beyond what any central bank printed: the *velocity and reuse of collateral* expand the effective money supply during confident times and collapse it during scared times. When confidence is high, the multiplier rises, collateral chains lengthen, and effective liquidity balloons. When something breaks — a counterparty looks shaky, a price gaps — everyone demands their collateral back at once, the chains unwind, the multiplier collapses, and effective liquidity *vanishes* even though no bonds were destroyed. That collapse *is* a liquidity crisis. It is why crises arrive so suddenly: the shadow tide can go from full to empty in days when the collateral multiplier resets. The Fed's balance sheet barely twitches; the *effective* tide drains in a flash. This collateral plumbing is the engine room beneath the eurodollar system, and it is why funding-stress indicators (the VIX, cross-currency basis swaps, repo rates) can warn you before the slow balance-sheet proxies move at all.

### Why the tide is cyclical, not random

One more foundational point, because it underpins the entire cycle section later. The tide *cycles* rather than drifting randomly because two of its biggest inputs are inherently cyclical. The first is **bank credit**, which is "inside money" — money created when banks lend and destroyed when loans are repaid or written off. Credit expands in booms (more lending, more deposits, more effective money) and contracts in busts (lending pulls back, defaults destroy deposits, money shrinks). That gives liquidity a natural boom-bust rhythm tied to the credit cycle. (The full mechanics are in [how credit creates money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-credit-creates-money-lending-channel-cycles).) The second is **policy**: central banks ease into weakness and tighten into strength, producing a multi-year easing-then-tightening cycle. Layer the credit cycle, the policy cycle, and the debt-refinancing rhythm on top of one another and you get the composite, recurring wave we will measure in months. Understanding *why* it cycles is what lets you trust the cycle framework instead of treating it as numerology.

### The anchor proxy: the Fed balance sheet

For most of this post we will use the **Fed's balance sheet** as our working anchor proxy for global liquidity, for three honest reasons. First, it is the largest and most important single source, denominated in the world's reserve currency. Second, it is published weekly, cleanly, by the Fed (the series is called WALCL — "Wednesday level, all reserve banks"). Third — and this is a real limitation I want to flag up front — *clean, aligned, dollar-converted series for all four banks plus the eurodollar system are not in the dataset behind this post.* I have the Fed's series, the reverse-repo, and the Treasury account. I do not have the ECB, BoJ, and PBoC series here in usable form.

So the charts in this post show the **Fed balance sheet and Fed net liquidity** as proxies for the global tide, and I am telling you plainly that they are a *partial* view — the US slice of a global ocean. In practice, a serious global-liquidity tracker (the kind sold by research firms like CrossBorder Capital, whose founder Michael Howell popularized the ~65-month cycle) sums all four banks plus shadow-credit estimates. The Fed proxy captures most of the *direction* and *turns*, because the banks tend to move together and the dollar leg dominates, but it understates the *level* and occasionally misses a divergence (for example, when the BoJ is flooding while the Fed is draining). Treat the Fed series as the tide gauge nearest your boat, not the whole sea.

![Fed total assets from 2019 to 2025 showing the COVID flood peak and the QT drain](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-the-worlds-money-tide-2.png)

The chart makes the tide visible. From a pre-COVID level of about \$3.8 trillion, the Fed's balance sheet exploded through 2020–2021 as it bought bonds (the green "tide rising" QE band), peaking at **\$8.96 trillion in April 2022**. Then it reversed: the Fed began *quantitative tightening* (QT), letting bonds roll off and draining reserves (the red "tide ebbing" band), pulling the balance sheet down toward \$6.7 trillion by mid-2025. That single line — up then down — is the macro story of the everything-rally and the everything-selloff in one picture.

## Net liquidity: the proxy that strips out parked cash

The raw balance sheet is a good first approximation, but a sharper proxy has become the standard trader's tool, and understanding it will make you genuinely better at reading the tide. It is called **net liquidity**, and it adjusts the Fed's balance sheet for two big pools of cash that are *created* but *not deployed into markets*.

### Two drains: the reverse-repo and the Treasury account

Not all the money the Fed creates reaches the financial markets. Two large accounts can absorb it:

- **The Overnight Reverse Repo (ON RRP) facility** — a place where money-market funds and banks park spare cash *at the Fed* overnight, earning interest. Think of it as a giant parking lot for idle cash. When the RRP balance is high, a lot of money is sitting in the lot, *not* chasing stocks or bonds. The RRP launched in 2021, swelled to a staggering **\$2.55 trillion by December 2022**, then drained to near zero by 2025.
- **The Treasury General Account (TGA)** — the US government's checking account at the Fed. When the Treasury collects taxes or sells bonds, cash flows *into* the TGA and *out* of the banking system. When the government spends, cash flows back out. A rising TGA drains liquidity; a falling TGA adds it.

Both of these are *subtractions* from effective liquidity. Money in the RRP parking lot or the TGA checking account is money that exists on the Fed's books but is not in the markets. So traders compute:

**Net liquidity = Fed total assets − ON RRP − TGA**

This is the amount of central-bank money actually available to slosh around the financial system. It is a better proxy for the tide that lifts risk assets than the raw balance sheet, because it captures the *deployment* of the money, not just its creation.

### Why net liquidity can diverge from the headline

This distinction is not academic — it produced one of the most important head-fakes of the cycle. Look closely at the timing: the Fed's *gross* balance sheet peaked in April 2022 at \$8.96 trillion. But **net liquidity was already draining** months before that, because the RRP parking lot was filling up fast. Cash the Fed had created was flowing into the RRP instead of into markets. So even while the headline "Fed balance sheet" was still near its peak, the *effective* tide had already turned. Traders watching only the gross number were late; traders watching net liquidity caught the turn earlier. The chart below shows this divergence directly.

![Net liquidity equals Fed assets minus reverse repo minus the Treasury account, with the gross and net lines diverging](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-the-worlds-money-tide-4.png)

The amber band between the two lines is the cash that is *parked, not deployed* — the RRP plus TGA. Notice how it bulges in 2022, when the RRP was ballooning: the gross line (dashed) stays high while the net line (blue) sinks to its trough. That gap is the early-warning signal. When the parking lot fills, the tide is going out even if the headline says it is still high.

#### Worked example: the COVID liquidity surge and the everything-rally

Let us anchor the whole thesis in numbers. Take the Fed's balance sheet as our tide gauge across the COVID flood.

- **Starting level (March 2020):** Fed total assets ≈ **\$4.31 trillion**.
- **Peak level (April 2022):** Fed total assets ≈ **\$8.96 trillion**.
- **Increase:** \$8.96T − \$4.31T = **\$4.65 trillion** of new central-bank money in about two years. That is a **+108%** expansion — the balance sheet more than doubled.

Now line that up against what risk assets did over roughly the same window (early 2020 trough to late-2021 peak):

- The S&P 500 roughly **doubled** from its March 2020 low (~2,237) to its January 2022 high (~4,800).
- The Nasdaq 100 rose even more, well over **+130%** from trough to peak.
- Bitcoin went from under **\$5,000** in March 2020 to nearly **\$69,000** in November 2021 — a move of more than **13x**.
- High-yield credit spreads collapsed from over 1,000 basis points of panic in March 2020 to under 300 basis points — meaning the riskiest borrowers could suddenly borrow cheaply.

Four completely different assets — large-cap stocks, tech, crypto, junk credit — all melted up *in lockstep* with the \$4.65 trillion liquidity flood. **The intuition:** when the central bank creates trillions of new dollars, that money has to go somewhere, and it bids up the price of every asset it can reach — the higher the beta, the bigger the move.

#### Worked example: net liquidity at the 2022 trough versus the 2021 peak

Now let us do the sharper, net-liquidity version, because it explains the *turn*. Using the Fed assets, ON RRP, and TGA series (aligned to the nearest available observation), compute net liquidity at two points.

**The 2021 net-liquidity peak (mid-2021):**

- Fed assets ≈ **\$8.08T**
- ON RRP ≈ **\$0.79T** (parking lot still modest)
- TGA ≈ **\$0.46T**
- Net liquidity = 8.08 − 0.79 − 0.46 = **\$6.83 trillion**

**The 2022 net-liquidity trough (end-2022):**

- Fed assets ≈ **\$8.55T** (still high — QT had barely started)
- ON RRP ≈ **\$2.55T** (the parking lot exploded)
- TGA ≈ **\$0.46T**
- Net liquidity = 8.55 − 2.55 − 0.46 = **\$5.54 trillion**

So between the 2021 peak and the 2022 trough, *gross* Fed assets actually **rose** by about \$0.5 trillion — yet *net* liquidity **fell** by about \$1.3 trillion (from \$6.83T to \$5.54T, roughly **−19%**), almost entirely because \$1.76 trillion drained into the reverse-repo parking lot. **The intuition:** the headline balance sheet said "still easy," but the money had been quietly siphoned off into a vault where it could not bid up stocks — and 2022 was a brutal year for exactly the high-beta assets that had flown in 2021.

#### Worked example: the dollar's drag on global liquidity

Now let us quantify the FX leg, because it is the piece the Fed-only proxy misses entirely. Suppose, for round numbers, that the three *foreign* central banks hold a combined balance sheet worth **\$15 trillion** when converted at the start of a year. Over that year the dollar strengthens — say the DXY rises **10%** — and, to keep it simple, the euro, yen, and yuan each fall roughly 10% against the dollar.

- **Start-of-year foreign legs in USD:** \$15.0 trillion.
- **A 10% stronger dollar** shrinks each foreign leg by roughly 10% when re-converted: 15.0 × (1 − 0.10) = **\$13.5 trillion**.
- **Liquidity lost purely to FX:** \$15.0T − \$13.5T = **\$1.5 trillion** — gone from the global total, with *no central bank changing its balance sheet at all*.

To put that \$1.5 trillion in scale: it is about a third of the entire \$4.65 trillion COVID flood, *erased by the exchange rate alone.* This is exactly what happened in 2022, when the DXY surged from ~96 to a peak of ~114.8 (about +20%) while the Fed was also draining — a double squeeze. **The intuition:** the dollar is a liquidity lever in its own right, and a Fed-only tracker that ignores it will systematically *overstate* global liquidity in a strong-dollar year and *understate* it in a weak-dollar year. Always read the DXY alongside the balance sheets.

## Why Bitcoin and the Nasdaq are high-beta liquidity proxies

We have established that there is a tide and how to measure it. Now the central trading insight: **certain assets ride the tide with amplified sensitivity.** They behave as if they are *leveraged long* on global liquidity. The two cleanest examples are Bitcoin and the Nasdaq.

### What "long liquidity" means

An asset is "long liquidity" if it rises when the money tide rises and falls when it drains, more than fundamentals alone would justify. The mechanism has several reinforcing parts:

- **The discount-rate channel.** An asset's value is its future cash flows discounted back to today. When liquidity is abundant, interest rates and discount rates are low, which inflates the present value of *long-dated* cash flows the most. Tech stocks and growth assets have most of their value far in the future, so they are the most sensitive to the discount rate — hence "long duration." (The real-yield version of this channel is the master signal in [real versus nominal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)
- **The portfolio-rebalancing channel.** When the central bank buys safe bonds, it takes them out of investors' hands and forces that money to find a new home. Investors rotate out of bonds and into riskier assets — stocks, credit, crypto — pushing their prices up. This is the "there is no alternative" dynamic.
- **The animal-spirits channel.** Rising liquidity loosens funding, lowers volatility, and emboldens leverage. Traders borrow more and chase more speculative bets. This is why the *most* speculative assets — unprofitable tech, meme stocks, crypto — show the *highest* beta to liquidity. They have no fundamental anchor, so they are pure liquidity plays.

Bitcoin is the purest case. It has no earnings, no coupon, no cash flow — its price is *almost entirely* a function of how much speculative money is sloshing around looking for a home. That makes it the highest-beta liquidity proxy in the major asset universe.

To see the discount-rate channel concretely, picture two companies. Company A is a mature utility paying \$100 a year forever, with most of its value coming from near-term cash. Company B is a growth firm expected to pay nothing for a decade, then \$100 a year far in the future — almost all of its value sits in distant cash flows. Now change the discount rate, which is what abundant or scarce liquidity does. Cut the rate from 6% to 3% and Company A's value rises meaningfully, but Company B's value *explodes*, because discounting distant cash flows by a smaller number inflates them dramatically — the further out the cash, the more sensitive it is to the rate. Liquidity drives rates, rates drive discount factors, and discount factors hit *long-duration* assets the hardest. That is the precise mechanism behind "tech is long duration": the same liquidity tide that nudges the utility sends the growth stock flying — and sends a zero-cash-flow asset like Bitcoin, whose entire value is a bet on future adoption, flying hardest of all. The beta ranking is not arbitrary; it falls out of where each asset's cash flows sit on the timeline.

### Beta: the amplification factor

In finance, **beta** measures how much an asset moves relative to a benchmark. A beta of 1.0 means it moves one-for-one. A beta of 2.0 means it moves twice as much. Risk assets have a beta *to global liquidity*:

- **Bitcoin: roughly 2–3x.** When global liquidity rises 10%, Bitcoin has historically tended to rise something like 20–30% (very roughly, with enormous noise). It is the leveraged tip of the spear.
- **The Nasdaq: roughly 1.5x.** High-growth tech is sensitive but anchored by real (if distant) earnings, so its liquidity beta is lower than crypto's but well above 1.
- **The broad S&P 500: roughly 1x.** Big, diversified, earnings-heavy — it tracks the tide but with less amplification.
- **Credit spreads: inverse.** Spreads *tighten* (the inverse of price falling) as liquidity rises, because abundant money chases yield and bids up risky bonds.

A caution before you over-trust these numbers: liquidity beta is **not a precise, stable coefficient.** It is a loose, regime-dependent tendency with months of lead/lag and plenty of exceptions. Treat "Bitcoin ~2–3x, Nasdaq ~1.5x" as a *mental ordering* of sensitivity, not a formula you can lever into blindly. The point is the ranking and the direction, not the third decimal.

#### Worked example: a liquidity-beta calculation

Make the beta idea concrete with simple arithmetic. Suppose your global-liquidity proxy is rising and you expect a **10% increase** in the tide over the coming year (say, the combined four-bank dollar total goes from \$27T to \$29.7T). What does that imply, using rough historical betas?

- **Bitcoin (beta ≈ 2.5x):** expected move ≈ 10% × 2.5 = **+25%**. If Bitcoin starts at \$60,000, that points toward roughly **\$75,000** — purely from the liquidity tailwind, before any crypto-specific catalyst.
- **Nasdaq (beta ≈ 1.5x):** expected move ≈ 10% × 1.5 = **+15%**. From 18,000 that points toward roughly **20,700**.
- **S&P 500 (beta ≈ 1.0x):** expected move ≈ 10% × 1.0 = **+10%**.

Now flip it. Suppose instead you expect the tide to *drain* 10% (QT plus a rising dollar shrinking the foreign legs):

- Bitcoin: 10% × 2.5 = **−25%**, from \$60,000 toward **\$45,000**.
- Nasdaq: 10% × 1.5 = **−15%**.
- S&P 500: **−10%**.

**The intuition:** the same underlying tide produces a fan of outcomes, and the higher the beta, the bigger your gain *and* your loss — which is exactly why you size high-beta positions *smaller* for the same dollar risk. The beta is a magnifier in both directions.

## The global liquidity cycle (~65 months)

Liquidity does not rise forever or drain forever. It **cycles** — and the cycle has a surprisingly regular period. This is the most actionable structural fact in the whole topic.

### Why liquidity cycles at all

Liquidity oscillates because the forces creating and destroying it are themselves cyclical. Central banks ease when the economy weakens and tighten when it overheats — a multi-year policy cycle. The credit cycle (banks lending freely, then pulling back) runs on a similar rhythm. And critically, the **global financial system carries a mountain of debt that must be constantly refinanced.** Most debt is short-to-medium term and rolls over every few years. That refinancing need forces central banks and the credit system to keep liquidity flowing — and the bunching of refinancing creates a recurring rhythm.

Researchers who track aggregate global liquidity (most prominently Michael Howell of CrossBorder Capital) have identified a recurring cycle of roughly **65 months** — about **5.5 years** — from one liquidity peak to the next. It is not a precise clock; it is a tendency, like a tide that comes in and out on a rough schedule but with weather pushing it around. The figure below shows the shape.

![The global liquidity cycle drawn as a wave running about 65 months from peak to peak with rising and falling phases](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-the-worlds-money-tide-5.png)

The wave has a clear structure. A **rising phase** (green) where liquidity expands, funding is easy, and risk assets rally — the "risk-on" half of the cycle. Then a **peak**. Then a **falling phase** (red) where liquidity drains, funding tightens, and risk assets struggle — the "risk-off" half. Then a **trough**, and the cycle begins again. Each full peak-to-peak span is roughly 65 months.

### Why ~65 months and not something else

The period is not magic; it falls out of the debt-refinancing structure. A large share of global debt has a maturity in the three-to-five-year range. When a cohort of debt comes due, it must be rolled over, and the system must provide the liquidity to do so — which tends to come in waves. Combine that refinancing rhythm with the typical length of a central-bank easing-then-tightening cycle and a business cycle, and you get a composite wave that has empirically clustered around 5.5 years. Treat the exact number as an *observation*, not a law. The robust, tradeable claim is the *qualitative* one: liquidity moves in multi-year waves, it does not trend forever in either direction, and after a long drain a turn back up becomes increasingly likely.

#### Worked example: the cycle timing and where 2026 sits

Let us locate ourselves on the wave. Suppose we take a recognized liquidity *peak* around **2021** (the COVID-flood top, when net liquidity crested near \$6.83 trillion and risk assets topped). Apply the ~65-month rule:

- **Prior peak:** ~2021.
- **Add 65 months (≈ 5.4 years):** 2021 + ~5.4 years ≈ **late 2026 to 2027** for the *next* projected peak.
- **The trough between them** typically falls roughly in the middle — call it **late 2022 into 2023**, which is exactly when net liquidity bottomed (the \$5.54T trough) and risk assets put in their cycle lows (the S&P bottomed October 2022; Bitcoin bottomed near \$16,000 in November 2022).

So as of **2026**, the cycle framework places us on the **rising leg of a new liquidity upswing** — past the 2022–23 trough, climbing toward a projected 2026–27 peak. The lavender marker on the wave chart is roughly where 2026 sits: early-to-mid in a new expansion, with the tide flowing in. **The intuition:** if the ~65-month rhythm holds, the second half of the 2020s should be a liquidity-tailwind environment for risk assets until the next peak — which is a top-down reason to keep a structural risk-on lean and a date to start watching for the next turn. (As always: this is a *bias*, not a guarantee, and the cycle has been wrong before.)

How do you *use* the cycle without overtrusting it? Treat it as a slow positioning bias, not a trade. On the **rising leg**, the cycle says lean into risk and treat dips as opportunities rather than the start of a bear market — the structural wind is at your back. Approaching the **projected peak**, the cycle says start trimming beta and tightening risk *even while everything still feels great*, because the late stage of a liquidity expansion is exactly when speculation is most extreme and the turn is closest. On the **falling leg**, the cycle says respect the drain — keep cash, fade rallies, and resist the urge to "buy the dip" until the proxies actually turn back up. And near the **projected trough**, the cycle says begin watching for the turn, because after a long drain the odds of a fresh expansion climb. The cycle never tells you the *exact* day; it tells you which *phase* you are in, and the phase is enough to set your posture. Pair the cycle (the slow clock) with the net-liquidity trend (the fast confirmation), and you have both the strategic map and the tactical signal.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of plausible-sounding beliefs about global liquidity are wrong, and each one is expensive. Let us correct them with numbers.

### Misconception 1: "Only the Fed matters"

It is tempting to watch only the Fed, since the dollar dominates. But the *global* total is what risk assets ride, and the other banks can offset the Fed. The clearest example: through 2022–2024 the Fed ran QT, draining dollars — yet the **Bank of Japan kept printing**, holding its balance sheet near ¥760 trillion and suppressing yields, while at times China eased. That foreign liquidity partially cushioned the Fed's drain and helped fuel the 2023 stock rally that surprised Fed-only watchers. The fix: track the **four-bank total in dollars**, not just the Fed. The Fed is the biggest gauge, not the only one. (And the dollar's level — which converts the foreign legs — is its own lever; see [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).)

### Misconception 2: "QE money flows directly into stocks"

People assume the Fed buying bonds sends the new money straight into the stock market. That is not the mechanism. When the Fed buys a bond, the new reserves go to a *bank*, and reserves **cannot leave the banking system** — you and I cannot hold them; they are not spent at a grocery store or a brokerage. What QE actually does is *indirect*: it removes safe assets from circulation, lowers yields, and pushes investors to rebalance *their own* portfolios out of bonds and into risk. The liquidity "flows into stocks" only through the portfolio-rebalancing and discount-rate channels, not as a literal pipe. This is why the link is loose and lagged, and why net liquidity (which captures where the money actually sits) beats the headline.

### Misconception 3: "QE is the only source of liquidity"

QE — central-bank asset purchases — is the most visible source, but it is far from the only one. Three others matter enormously:

- **The Treasury account (TGA) and reverse-repo (RRP).** As we saw, draining the RRP or running down the TGA *adds* effective liquidity without any QE at all. Much of the 2023–24 risk rally was funded by the RRP draining \$2 trillion+ back into the system — a stealth liquidity injection with no new QE.
- **The eurodollar / shadow system.** Offshore dollar credit can expand or contract independently of the Fed, swelling or shrinking the effective tide.
- **The dollar's exchange rate.** A falling dollar inflates the dollar value of foreign balance sheets and eases global funding — a *de facto* liquidity injection with no central-bank action.

The fix: liquidity is **central-bank assets, plus the RRP/TGA swings, plus shadow credit, plus the FX leg.** QE is one input among several.

### Misconception 4: "Liquidity is a precise timing tool"

Global liquidity is a *regime dial*, not a *timing trigger*. The lead/lag between a liquidity turn and an asset turn can be anywhere from weeks to many months, and the beta is unstable. Anyone selling you a precise "liquidity says buy on Tuesday" signal is overfitting. The honest use is slow and directional: *which way is the tide flowing, and how strongly?* — and then leaning your structural exposure with it. Use it to set your *bias and your size*, and let faster tools handle entries.

### Misconception 5: "Rising liquidity always means rising prices"

Usually, but not when liquidity is rising *because something is breaking*. A panicky surge in liquidity during a crisis (emergency QE, a bailout) coincides with falling prices in the short run, because the liquidity is *responding* to a crash, not causing a rally. March 2020 is the example: the Fed's biggest, fastest injection happened *during* the crash, not before the rally. The signal is the *sustained, proactive* tide — not the panic response. Watch the trend, and watch volatility (the VIX) to tell a calm tide from a crisis flood.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Let us walk the actual cross-asset record through the cycle, because the same tide explains move after move that looked unrelated at the time.

### The 2020–21 surge: the everything-rally

We covered the numbers above, but the *pattern* is the lesson. From the March 2020 trough, the Fed's balance sheet ran from \$4.31T toward \$8.96T, the other three banks expanded in parallel, the RRP was still small (money was deployed, not parked), and the dollar *weakened* (DXY fell from ~103 in March 2020 to ~90 by year-end, inflating the foreign legs). **Every input to the tide was rising at once.** The result was the broadest, most indiscriminate risk rally in modern history: stocks, crypto, credit, EM, commodities, collectibles. When *everything* with a price went up, the cause was not a thousand good stories — it was one rising tide.

### The 2022 drain: the everything-selloff

Then the inputs reversed, hard. The Fed started hiking and announced QT. Net liquidity rolled over (the \$6.83T → \$5.54T drain, mostly via the ballooning RRP). The dollar *surged* (DXY ran from ~96 to a peak of ~114.8 in September 2022), shrinking the foreign legs of global liquidity in dollar terms. **Every input to the tide reversed at once.** The result was a near-perfectly correlated cross-asset bear market: the S&P fell ~25%, the Nasdaq ~33%, Bitcoin ~65% (from \$48k to \$16k), and even "safe" long bonds had their worst year in history. The high-beta assets fell the most — exactly as their liquidity beta predicted. Diversification failed because everything was the *same trade*: long liquidity, in a year the tide went out.

### The 2023 stealth rally: liquidity without QE

Here is the case that humbles people who watch only the Fed's headline balance sheet. Through all of 2023, the Fed was running QT — its gross balance sheet *fell* from about \$8.55T to \$7.68T. The textbook says risk assets should have struggled. Instead, the Nasdaq rose over **40%** and Bitcoin more than doubled off its lows. How?

Two stealth liquidity sources did the work that QE was no longer doing. First, the **reverse-repo parking lot drained**: the RRP fell from \$2.55T at the end of 2022 to about \$1.0T by the end of 2023 — roughly **\$1.5 trillion** of cash flowing *out* of the Fed's vault and *back into* the financial system, where it could chase assets. Second, the **Treasury managed its issuance** so that much of its borrowing was funded by that draining RRP cash rather than by pulling reserves out of the banking system. The net effect: even with QT shrinking the gross balance sheet, *net* liquidity held up far better than the headline implied, and risk assets rallied. The Fed-only headline screamed "tightening"; the net-liquidity proxy and the draining RRP whispered "actually still fine." The traders who watched the *net* number were positioned for the rally; the ones watching the *gross* number were baffled by it. This single episode is the strongest argument for using net liquidity, not the headline, as your anchor.

### The China divergence: when the banks disagree

One more real-market wrinkle. The four central banks do not always move together, and when they diverge, the *global* total can tell a different story than any single bank. Through parts of 2022–2024, the Fed was draining while China's PBoC was easing — cutting reserve requirements and pushing bank lending to support a wobbly property sector. A trader watching only the Fed saw pure tightening; a trader watching the global total saw a partial offset, with Chinese liquidity cushioning the dollar drain in Asian and emerging markets. The lesson is not that China rescues every selloff — it often does not, and Chinese liquidity leaks abroad less freely than the Fed's. The lesson is that **the global total is the object of interest**, and a single-bank view can mislead you in either direction when the banks disagree. When you read a divergence — one bank flooding while another drains — ask which one dominates the assets *you* hold, and weight accordingly.

### The volatility lens: VIX as a stress gauge

There is one more instrument that helps you read the tide in real time: the **VIX**, the market's "fear gauge," which measures expected stock-market volatility. The connection is tight: **abundant liquidity suppresses volatility** (easy funding, calm markets, low VIX), while a draining or *crisis* tide spikes it (forced selling, funding stress, VIX surges). So the VIX is a window onto funding liquidity in particular — the plumbing layer of the tide.

![VIX year-end levels with intraday panic spikes in 2018, 2020, and 2024 marking liquidity stress events](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-the-worlds-money-tide-6.png)

The chart shows the pattern. In calm, liquidity-rich years (2017 at 11, 2019 at 14, 2021 at 17, 2023 at 12) the VIX closes low. But the *spikes* mark the moments the tide went out fast: **37 in the February 2018 "volmageddon,"** **82.7 in the March 2020 COVID crash** — the highest in history — and **65.7 in the August 2024 yen-carry unwind**, when a sudden BoJ shift and a strengthening yen triggered a global funding scare. Each spike is a liquidity-stress event: a moment when funding seized and leveraged players were forced to sell. The long-run VIX average sits near 19.5; sustained readings *below* it signal a liquidity-rich, risk-on tide, and sharp breaks *above* 30 signal the tide is going out fast. A trader uses the VIX as the high-frequency confirmation of what the slow liquidity proxies imply.

### The 2024 carry-unwind: a shadow-liquidity event

The August 2024 episode deserves a closer look because it shows the *shadow* layer biting. For years, traders had borrowed cheap yen (funded by the BoJ's flood) and bought higher-yielding assets worldwide — the "yen carry trade," a giant source of effective global liquidity. When the BoJ unexpectedly hiked and the yen surged, that funding evaporated almost overnight. The forced unwind crashed Japanese stocks (the Nikkei fell ~12% in a day), spiked the VIX to 65, and rattled everything from tech to crypto — *even though no major central bank had drained its balance sheet that week.* The tide went out because the *shadow* layer (carry-trade dollar/yen funding) contracted. It is a vivid reminder that global liquidity is more than the four balance sheets: it includes the leveraged plumbing on top.

## How to trade it / The playbook

Here is the payoff: how to turn the global liquidity tide into actual positions. The edge is top-down and slow — it sets your *bias and size*, not your entries — but it is one of the most durable edges in macro, because it explains the *common factor* behind moves that look unrelated.

![The global-liquidity playbook from tracking proxies to reading the trend to leaning exposure to invalidation](/imgs/blogs/global-liquidity-the-worlds-money-tide-7.png)

### Step 1 — Track the proxies

Build a simple liquidity dashboard you check weekly:

- **Fed net liquidity** = Fed assets (WALCL) − ON RRP − TGA. This is your anchor, updated weekly and free from FRED. It is the single most useful number.
- **The four-bank total in dollars** — Fed + ECB + BoJ + PBoC balance sheets, each converted to USD. This is the true global tide; if you cannot build it yourself, follow a research provider that does (the ~65-month-cycle trackers).
- **The dollar (DXY)** — a *falling* dollar inflates the foreign legs and eases global funding (liquidity tailwind); a *rising* dollar drains them (headwind). Read it as a liquidity signal, not just FX.
- **The VIX** — your high-frequency stress confirmation. Sustained sub-19 = calm, risk-on tide; spikes above 30 = the tide is going out fast.

### Step 2 — Read the trend, not the level

Do not obsess over the absolute level; read the **direction and rate of change**. A practical method: track the **13-week (one-quarter) change** in net liquidity. Rising over the quarter = tide flowing in. Falling = tide draining. The slope matters more than the number. This smooths out weekly noise (a single tax-date TGA swing, for instance) and isolates the regime.

### Step 3 — Lean your risk exposure with the flow

This is the core of the playbook:

- **Tide rising (net liquidity climbing, dollar soft, VIX calm):** lean **risk-on**. Favor the high-beta liquidity proxies — Nasdaq/tech, Bitcoin, high-yield credit, emerging markets. Be willing to hold beta. This is the environment the cycle framework projects for 2026 (rising leg toward a 2026–27 peak).
- **Tide falling (net liquidity draining, dollar strong, VIX rising):** lean **risk-off**. Cut beta, raise cash, favor the dollar and short-duration safety. The high-beta assets that led the rally will lead the decline.

You are not trying to call exact tops and bottoms. You are tilting your *structural exposure* — how much beta you carry — to match the tide.

### Step 4 — Size by beta

Because high-beta assets amplify the tide, **size them smaller for the same dollar risk.** If Bitcoin moves ~2.5x the tide and the Nasdaq ~1.5x, a position in Bitcoin needs to be proportionally smaller to carry the same risk as a Nasdaq position. The beta is your magnifier; respect it in your position sizing, not just your direction. (The discipline of sizing to risk rather than to conviction is the heart of survival; the mechanics live in position-sizing and risk-of-ruin work.)

#### Worked example: reading the dashboard on a single day

Let us run the playbook on a concrete (illustrative) snapshot, so the abstract steps become a checklist you could actually execute. Imagine you sit down on a Monday and your four gauges read:

- **Fed net liquidity:** \$6.10T this week, up from \$5.85T a quarter ago. The 13-week change is **+\$0.25T (+4%)** — *rising*.
- **The dollar (DXY):** 97, down from 103 three months ago — *falling*, which inflates the foreign legs and eases offshore funding. **Tailwind.**
- **The VIX:** 14, comfortably below the 19.5 long-run average and nowhere near 30 — *calm.* **Risk-on confirmed.**
- **The four-bank trend (from your research feed):** the BoJ is still easy and China is adding; the global total is rising faster than the Fed slice alone. **Tailwind.**

Reading them together: net liquidity rising, dollar falling, volatility calm, foreign banks adding. Every gauge points the same way — the **tide is flowing in**. The playbook says lean *risk-on*: carry beta, favor the high-beta proxies (Nasdaq, Bitcoin, high-yield credit, EM), and size the highest-beta positions smallest. You are not predicting a specific price; you are setting a *bias and a size* that match the tide.

Now suppose three months later you re-check and net liquidity's 13-week change has flipped to **−\$0.30T**, the DXY has broken up to 106, and the VIX has spiked to 32 on a funding scare. Three of your four gauges have reversed at once. That is your **invalidation firing** — the tide has turned. The playbook says cut beta, raise cash, and step off, *regardless of what any individual stock's story is telling you.* **The intuition:** the dashboard is not a crystal ball; it is a regime detector, and its whole value is telling you which way to lean and when to stop leaning that way.

### Step 5 — Define your invalidation

Every macro view needs a kill switch. For a liquidity-driven risk-on lean, the invalidation is concrete:

- **Net liquidity turns down** on the 13-week trend (the tide is now draining), **or**
- **The VIX spikes above ~30** and holds (funding stress — the tide is going out fast), **or**
- **The dollar breaks out sharply higher** (foreign legs shrinking, global funding tightening).

Any of these flips the bias from risk-on to risk-off. Pre-committing to the invalidation is what separates a disciplined macro trader from someone who rode the tide up and then drowned holding the same high-beta bag when it went out. The tide that lifted you is the same tide that can strand you — so you watch the gauges, lean with the flow, and step off when they turn.

### Honest limits — what the tide does not tell you

A playbook is only as good as its self-awareness, so here are the failure modes, stated plainly. **First, the tide sets the average, not the dispersion.** Rising liquidity lifts the *index*, but it does not tell you *which* stock or coin wins — a rising tide can still leave a badly-run company on the rocks. Use liquidity for your beta exposure; use bottom-up work to pick *what* you express the beta in. **Second, the lead/lag is real and variable.** A liquidity turn can precede the asset turn by months, or coincide with it, or even briefly lag. If you treat the proxy as a same-day trigger, you will get chopped up; treat it as a slow regime dial. **Third, the proxies are incomplete.** The Fed net-liquidity number I used throughout these charts is the US slice; the true global tide needs the four banks in dollars plus shadow-credit estimates, which I have flagged repeatedly. **Fourth, crises invert the sign briefly** — the biggest liquidity injections happen *during* crashes, so a sudden surge in liquidity amid a VIX spike is a falling tide responding to a crash, not a rising one to buy. Respect all four limits and the tide becomes a durable edge; ignore them and it becomes a way to be confidently wrong.

### How it fits with the rest of your process

Global liquidity is the *top* of a top-down process, not the whole process. The clean hierarchy: **liquidity sets the regime** (risk-on or risk-off, and how much beta to carry); **relative value and fundamentals choose the expression** (which assets, sectors, or names to hold within that regime); and **technicals and risk management time the entries and size the positions**. The liquidity tide answers "should I be leaning into risk at all right now, and how hard?" — the single most important question, and the one most traders skip while they argue about a chart pattern on a five-minute candle. Get the tide right and you are swimming with the current; get it wrong and the best stock-picking in the world is swimming against it. That is why a top-down macro trader checks the liquidity gauges *first*, before anything else touches the book.

The deepest reason this works: in a financialized world drowning in debt, the price of nearly every risk asset is, at the margin, a question of *how much money is available to chase it.* Earnings, narratives, and technicals all matter — but they fluctuate around the level set by the tide. Learn to read the tide, and a hundred unrelated market stories collapse into one.
