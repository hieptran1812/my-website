> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 2020 COVID episode is the master case study of policy-driven markets. A liquidity panic — the March "dash for cash" that briefly broke even the US Treasury market — was met with the most aggressive policy response in history: open-ended "infinite" QE plus a fiscal bazooka of over \$5 trillion. The result was the fastest crash and the fastest recovery ever, an "everything rally" that was pure liquidity, and the inflation that followed eighteen months later.
>
> - **The crash was a liquidity event, not an earnings event.** In a true panic everyone needs dollars at once, so they sell whatever they can — including the world's safest asset. In mid-March 2020 even US Treasuries fell, and the Treasury market itself briefly stopped functioning. That is the tell that you are watching a *funding* crisis, not a *valuation* one.
> - **The response was open-ended.** On March 23, 2020 the Fed said it would buy bonds **"in the amounts needed"** — no cap. Its balance sheet went from \$4.31T (March 2020) to \$7.36T (December 2020), and on top of that came over \$5T of fiscal spending that put cash directly into households' hands.
> - **The everything rally was the liquidity finding the riskiest assets.** A wall of cash that earns nothing in safe assets gets pushed down the risk ladder until the most speculative things on earth — unprofitable tech, junk credit, crypto — lead. The economy was collapsing the whole time; that is the point.
> - **The one number to remember:** the 2020-21 flood fed inflation that peaked at **9.06% in June 2022** — a 40-year high. Liquidity moved markets first and the real economy second, and the lag was about eighteen months.

On **March 23, 2020**, with the COVID panic at its worst and even the market for US government bonds seizing up, the Federal Reserve released a short statement containing two words the modern market had never heard together. It would buy Treasury and mortgage bonds **"in the amounts needed."** No cap. No fixed program size. Open-ended. The financial press instantly nicknamed it **infinite QE.**

Look at what had just happened and what happened next. The S&P 500 had peaked at an all-time high on February 19, 2020 with the economy still humming. Over the following five weeks it fell **34%** — the fastest descent from an all-time high into a bear market in the history of the index. Volatility, measured by the VIX, spiked to **82.7**, higher than at any point in the 2008 financial crisis. And then, on the very day of that "amounts needed" statement, the market bottomed. It did not just stabilize; it went on to nearly **double** over the next two years. By the end of 2021 the Nasdaq had risen over 130% from the low, Bitcoin had gone from under \$5,000 to nearly \$69,000, and the riskiest, longest-duration, most speculative assets in the world had been the biggest winners of all. It was, almost literally, an **everything rally.**

Here is the puzzle that should bother you, because it is the whole lesson of this post. The economy in mid-2020 was a catastrophe. Over twenty million Americans lost their jobs in a single month. Whole industries shut down. Corporate earnings collapsed. GDP fell at an annualized pace not seen since the Great Depression. And yet asset prices *soared*. The standard story — "the stock market reflects the economy" — fails completely here. It does not bend; it breaks. The thing that explained the rally was not the economy. It was **liquidity**: the trillions of dollars the Fed and Congress poured into the financial system. By the end of this post you will understand exactly why 2020 is the cleanest demonstration ever recorded that liquidity, not fundamentals, is what moves markets — and how a trader reads and positions around that reality. We build everything from the ground up; no finance background is assumed.

## Foundations: the dash for cash, infinite QE, and the fiscal bazooka

Before the story makes sense, you need a clean mental model of four ideas: what a "liquidity panic" actually is and why it is different from a normal selloff, what the Fed does when it intervenes, what the government does with fiscal policy, and why all of that ends up in asset prices. We define every term from zero.

### What "liquidity" means, in one paragraph

Most people use "liquidity" loosely. For a trader it has a precise meaning: **liquidity is the ease of turning something into spendable cash, right now, without moving its price.** Cash is perfectly liquid. A US Treasury bond is normally extremely liquid — there is always a deep market of buyers, so you can sell a billion dollars of it in seconds at a price barely different from the last trade. A house is illiquid: selling it takes months and a price concession. A panic is what happens when everyone tries to convert assets into cash *at the same time* and discovers the buyers have vanished. When that happens, even normally liquid assets become temporarily unsellable except at a steep discount. Hold this definition — it is the spine of the entire 2020 story. We unpack it at length in [What liquidity means: market, funding, and global liquidity for traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-liquidity-means-market-funding-global-traders).

The everyday analogy: think of liquidity as the water level in a swimming pool of assets. When the level is high, every asset floats easily — you can get in and out without a splash. When the level drops fast, assets scrape the bottom; trades that used to be effortless now grind. A "liquidity crisis" is the pool draining all at once because every swimmer is simultaneously trying to climb out. The 2020 crash was that pool draining in a matter of days.

### Two kinds of market crash: valuation versus liquidity

This distinction is the single most useful thing in this post, so let it land. There are two fundamentally different reasons asset prices fall, and they call for opposite responses.

A **valuation crash** is when the market decides assets were worth less than it thought. Earnings disappoint, a bubble pops, a sector was overpriced. The dot-com bust of 2000-2002 was a valuation crash: tech stocks were priced for fantasies that never materialized, so they were repriced lower, and there was no quick fix because nothing was *broken* — the prices were simply wrong and got corrected. In a valuation crash, the right assets fall and the wrong assets hold up; it is *discriminating*.

A **liquidity crash** is different. Here the problem is not that assets are worth less; it is that everyone needs cash at the same moment and there are not enough buyers, so prices gap down regardless of value. The defining symptom is that *everything* falls together — even the assets that should be safe havens — because people sell what they *can*, not what they *want* to. A liquidity crash is *indiscriminate*. And crucially, it has a fix: someone with infinite cash steps in and becomes the buyer of last resort. That someone is the central bank. The March 2020 crash was a textbook liquidity crash, which is exactly why a central-bank intervention could reverse it almost overnight. A valuation crash cannot be fixed by liquidity; a liquidity crash can be fixed by nothing else.

### The dash for cash

In a liquidity panic, the asset everyone wants is **cash** — and in the global system, "cash" overwhelmingly means **US dollars**, because the dollar is the currency in which the world's debts and trades are denominated (we cover why in [The dollar system: why the USD rules markets and the DXY](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy)). When the COVID shock hit, a scramble for dollars erupted worldwide. Leveraged funds faced **margin calls** — demands from their lenders to post more cash collateral as positions moved against them. Investors yanked money out of funds, forcing managers to sell holdings to raise cash for redemptions. Companies drew down their credit lines and hoarded dollars in case revenue stopped. Foreign central banks needed dollars to support their own banks. Everyone, everywhere, needed dollars *now*.

This is called the **dash for cash**, and it produces a result so counterintuitive it tells you immediately what kind of crisis you are in.

### Why even Treasuries fell

In a normal selloff, money flees stocks and rushes into US Treasury bonds — the safest asset on earth — pushing Treasury prices *up*. That is the classic "flight to safety," and it is so reliable that a 60/40 stock-bond portfolio leans on it for protection. But in the third week of March 2020, something almost unheard of happened: Treasuries fell *alongside* stocks. The world's safe asset was being dumped.

Why would anyone sell the safest asset in a panic? Precisely *because* it was the safest. When you desperately need cash and your risky positions have no buyers, you sell what *can* still be sold — and in mid-March 2020, the only thing with any buyers left was Treasuries. Leveraged players sold their most liquid holding to raise dollars, and the sheer volume of forced selling overwhelmed the market's capacity to absorb it. The bid-ask spread on Treasuries — the gap between what buyers offered and sellers asked — blew out to multiples of normal. For a few days in March 2020, the most liquid market on the planet, the \$20-trillion-plus US Treasury market, the literal foundation of the global financial system, **stopped functioning normally.** Dealers could not move bonds fast enough; prices on supposedly identical securities diverged. This is the financial equivalent of the bedrock cracking.

That is the tell. When even Treasuries are falling, you are not watching a debate about value — you are watching a pure scramble for dollars. And a pure scramble for dollars has exactly one cure.

### Quantitative easing, and the meaning of "infinite"

The Fed's basic tool in normal times is the **policy interest rate** — the federal funds rate, the rate at which banks lend each other money overnight. To stimulate, the Fed cuts it; to cool things down, it hikes (we cover the mechanics in [How the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates)). But that rate has a floor at roughly **zero**, because no one will lend at a negative rate when they could just hold cash at 0%. In early 2020 the rate was already low, and on March 15, 2020 the Fed cut it all the way to the **zero-to-0.25% range** in an emergency Sunday-night move. The conventional tool was now exhausted. The thermostat was jammed against the floor and the room was still freezing.

So the Fed reached for its other lever: the **balance sheet**, through **quantitative easing (QE)**. QE is the central bank buying large quantities of bonds — mostly Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities — in the open market, paying for them with newly created money. "Quantitative" because the lever is now a *quantity* (how many bonds, how much money) rather than a *price* (the interest rate). When you cannot cut the price of money any lower, you pump the *quantity* of money higher. We have a full primer in [Quantitative easing explained: is it really printing money?](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money), and a deep dive on the mechanics in [QE vs QT: how balance-sheet policy moves markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets).

What made 2020 different from previous QE programs (2008-2014) was the word **infinite**. In every prior round, the Fed announced a *fixed* size — "we will buy \$600 billion of bonds," for example — and the market would then debate whether that was enough. On March 23, 2020, the Fed threw the fixed number away. It pledged to buy "in the amounts needed to support smooth market functioning" — open-ended, uncapped, however much it took. This was the Fed becoming the explicit buyer of last resort for the entire bond market. The dash for cash met an entity with a literally unlimited supply of dollars. There is no contest there. The panic ended within days because the Fed had announced it would, in effect, print as many dollars as anyone needed.

### Fiscal policy and the fiscal bazooka

The Fed handles *monetary* policy — money, rates, the balance sheet. But there is a second, entirely separate lever: **fiscal policy**, which is what the government (Congress and the Treasury) does with **spending and taxation**. Where monetary policy works *through* the financial system, fiscal policy can put money *directly* into people's hands — stimulus checks, expanded unemployment benefits, loans and grants to businesses. We cover this engine in [Fiscal policy for traders: spending, deficits, and demand](/blog/trading/macro-trading/fiscal-policy-for-traders-spending-deficits-demand) and how it hits markets in [Fiscal stimulus and markets: how impulses move prices](/blog/trading/macro-trading/fiscal-stimulus-and-markets-how-impulses-move-prices).

In 2020, the fiscal response was the largest peacetime spending program in American history. The headline package, the **CARES Act** of late March 2020, was about \$2.2 trillion. It was followed by a roughly \$0.9 trillion relief bill in December 2020 and the \$1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan in March 2021. Together with smaller measures, the total exceeded **\$5 trillion**. This is what people mean by the **fiscal bazooka**: a single, overwhelming blast of government money aimed straight at the economy. The combination — infinite QE *and* a fiscal bazooka, monetary and fiscal cannons firing in the same direction at the same time — is what makes 2020 unique. We have never before, and have not since, seen both levers pulled to the maximum simultaneously. The interaction of the two is its own deep subject, covered in [Fiscal–monetary interaction: dominance and the policy mix](/blog/trading/macro-trading/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix).

### Why it was a global dollar shortage, not just a US event

One more foundation, because it explains the *scale* of what was needed. The dash for cash was not confined to American funds; it was a worldwide scramble for *US dollars* specifically. Banks and companies outside the United States hold an enormous amount of dollar-denominated debt — they borrowed in dollars because the dollar is the world's trade and funding currency. When the panic hit, all of those foreign borrowers needed dollars to service their debts at the same instant, and they could not get them, because the offshore market where the world borrows dollars (the so-called eurodollar market) froze. This showed up as the dollar *spiking* even as US stocks crashed — the opposite of what you would expect if the crisis were "about America."

To plug a global dollar shortage, the Fed did something only it can do: it opened **dollar swap lines** with other major central banks (the European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, the Bank of England, and others). A swap line is a facility where the Fed lends dollars to a foreign central bank, which then lends them on to banks in its own country. In effect, the Fed became the dollar lender of last resort *for the entire world*, not just the United States. By late March 2020, hundreds of billions of dollars were flowing out through these lines. This is why the response had to be measured in trillions: the Fed was backstopping the global dollar system, which is many times larger than the US economy alone. Once those dollars reached the world, the dollar's spike reversed and it sank through the rest of 2020 — the tell that the shortage had become a glut. We unpack the dollar's central role in [The dollar system: why the USD rules markets and the DXY](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).

That is the foundation: a liquidity crash (the dash for cash that broke Treasuries), the monetary cure (infinite QE), the fiscal cannon (over \$5T), and the global dollar backstop (the swap lines). Now we walk through how it actually unfolded.

## The crash: the fastest 34% drop ever, even Treasuries breaking

Let us reconstruct the five weeks that took the market from euphoria to the edge of a systemic breakdown, because the *speed* is the part that matters.

On **February 19, 2020**, the S&P 500 closed at an all-time high. The economy was, by most measures, fine — unemployment was near a 50-year low, the VIX (the market's "fear gauge," measuring expected volatility) sat around 14, which is calm. The virus was still mostly a foreign news story to US markets. There was no warning in the price.

Then, over the next 23 trading days, the index fell **34%**. To put that in context: the 2008 crash, which felt apocalyptic at the time, took *months* to fall that far. The 1929 crash that began the Great Depression took longer still. The 2020 crash compressed a full bear market into about five weeks. It was the fastest fall from an all-time high into a 20%-plus bear market that the S&P 500 had ever recorded.

The VIX, which had been at 14, spiked to a closing high of **82.7** on March 16, 2020 — *higher than its 80.9 peak during the worst of the 2008 crisis.* A VIX of 82 implies the market expected daily moves of around 5% in either direction, every day, for the foreseeable future. That is not a market processing news; that is a market that has stopped functioning as a price-discovery mechanism and become a pure liquidation.

And then came the symptom that told professionals this was a *funding* crisis: in the week of March 16-20, **Treasuries fell with everything else.** The flight-to-safety bid that should have rescued bonds was overwhelmed by forced selling. The Treasury market's plumbing — the dealers who normally stand ready to buy and sell — was swamped. Bid-ask spreads on Treasuries widened to roughly ten times normal. The market for the safest asset on earth had become disorderly. This is the moment the Fed cannot ignore, because if the Treasury market breaks, *everything* priced off Treasuries breaks, and that is the whole financial system. The crash had become an existential plumbing emergency. The deeper mechanics of the funding markets that seized are in [Money market plumbing: repo, collateral, and SOFR](/blog/trading/macro-trading/money-market-plumbing-repo-collateral-sofr).

#### Worked example: how a margin call forces a Treasury sale

Walk through one leveraged fund to see *why* the safe asset got sold. Suppose a fund runs a \$100 million portfolio but, like most such funds, uses **leverage** — it has borrowed an additional \$300 million from its prime broker, so it controls \$400 million of assets against \$100 million of its own capital (4-to-1 leverage). The broker requires the fund to keep at least \$80 million of equity as a cushion. The COVID shock hits and the portfolio drops 6% — a \$24 million loss — so the fund's equity falls from \$100 million to \$76 million, below the \$80 million floor. The broker issues a **margin call**: post \$4 million in cash immediately or we liquidate your positions. The fund has no spare cash; it is fully invested. So it must *sell something to raise \$4 million in dollars today*. Its corporate bonds have no buyers — the market is frozen. Its small-cap stocks are gapping down with no bids. The only thing it owns that *anyone* will still buy is its US Treasuries. So it sells \$4 million of Treasuries — not because it wants to, but because they are the only liquid asset left. Multiply this by thousands of leveraged players doing the same thing in the same week, and the safest asset in the world gets dumped into a market that cannot absorb it.

The intuition: in a dash for cash, you do not sell what you want to sell — you sell what you *can* sell, and what you can sell is the safe asset, which is exactly why the safe asset breaks first.

## The policy response: infinite QE plus over \$5 trillion in fiscal support

The response was overwhelming, fast, and came in two channels at once. Understand both, because their *combination* is what makes 2020 the master case study.

### The monetary cannon: infinite QE

The Fed had already cut rates to zero on March 15. It had relaunched QE with a fixed \$700 billion target on the same day. But fixed numbers were not stopping the dash for cash — the market kept demanding more dollars than any cap could supply. So on **March 23, 2020**, the Fed removed the cap entirely with the "amounts needed" language. In the first weeks after that, it bought Treasuries at a staggering pace — at the peak, on the order of **\$75 billion *per day*** of Treasuries and \$50 billion per day of mortgage bonds. To grasp the scale: in a single week the Fed bought more Treasuries than it had in entire *years* of the 2008-era programs. It was, in effect, standing in the Treasury market with an unlimited checkbook, buying whatever sellers needed to offload. The forced selling met an infinite buyer, the bid-ask spreads snapped back to normal within days, and the panic in the plumbing was over.

Beyond plain QE, the Fed also opened an alphabet soup of emergency **facilities** — programs to backstop specific markets that had frozen: commercial paper (short-term corporate IOUs), money-market funds, municipal bonds, and, for the first time ever, **corporate bonds**, including some high-yield ("junk") bonds. The mere *announcement* that the Fed stood ready to buy corporate bonds caused credit spreads to collapse before the Fed bought almost anything — the promise of the backstop was enough. This is a recurring theme: the backstop works through *expectations*, often without the central bank spending a dollar.

The cumulative effect on the balance sheet was the steepest expansion in Fed history. The chart below shows the Fed's total assets. The near-vertical line in 2020 is the story.

![Line chart of Fed total assets in trillions of dollars from 2019 to 2025 showing the steep 2020 jump from 4.31 trillion to 7.36 trillion and the peak at 8.96 trillion in April 2022](/imgs/blogs/2020-covid-crash-infinite-qe-and-the-everything-rally-2.png)

#### Worked example: the scale of infinite QE

Put real numbers on it. In March 2020 the Fed's total assets stood at **\$4.31 trillion** (data point: data.FED_ASSETS, March 2020). By December 2020 they had reached **\$7.36 trillion**. That is **\$3.05 trillion** added in nine months — the Fed created and injected new reserves equal to over three trillion dollars in three quarters of a year. For perspective, the *entire* 2008-2014 QE program, spread over six years, took the balance sheet from under \$1 trillion to about \$4.5 trillion — roughly \$3.5 trillion over six years. In 2020 the Fed nearly matched that total in *nine months*. And it did not stop in December: the buying continued at \$120 billion a month through 2021, pushing the balance sheet to its peak of **\$8.96 trillion** in April 2022 (data.FED_ASSETS_PEAK). From \$4.31T to \$8.96T, the Fed's balance sheet **more than doubled** in two years.

The intuition: when you hear "infinite QE," picture \$3 trillion of new central-bank money entering the system in nine months — a flood that has to go somewhere, and it cannot go into Treasuries (the Fed is hoovering those up), so it goes into everything riskier.

### The fiscal cannon: over \$5 trillion

While the Fed worked the financial plumbing, Congress aimed money straight at households and businesses. The **CARES Act** (signed March 27, 2020) was about **\$2.2 trillion**: direct \$1,200 checks to most adults, a \$600-per-week boost to unemployment benefits on top of normal state benefits, hundreds of billions in forgivable loans to small businesses (the Paycheck Protection Program), and aid to large corporations and hospitals. A second package in **December 2020** added roughly **\$0.9 trillion** (more checks, more unemployment support). The **American Rescue Plan** of March 2021 added **\$1.9 trillion** (another \$1,400 per person, extended benefits, state and local aid, an expanded child tax credit). Add smaller measures and the total topped **\$5 trillion** in under a year.

The crucial difference from QE: this money went *directly into the real economy.* QE creates reserves that live inside the banking system and never reach a household's checking account (we explain exactly why in the [QE deep dive](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets)). Fiscal transfers are the opposite — a \$1,200 check lands in your bank account and you can spend it at a store *today*. Much of it was spent; a large chunk, with much of the economy locked down and nothing to spend on, was *saved* and *invested* — into stocks, into crypto, into the very assets that were rallying. The fiscal bazooka did not just support the economy; it put cash into the hands of people who then put it into the market.

![Horizontal bar chart of the three main COVID fiscal packages in trillions of dollars showing CARES Act 2.2 trillion, December 2020 relief 0.9 trillion, and American Rescue Plan 1.9 trillion totaling over 5 trillion](/imgs/blogs/2020-covid-crash-infinite-qe-and-the-everything-rally-4.png)

#### Worked example: the fiscal bazooka, package by package

Sum it directly. CARES Act = **\$2.2T**. December 2020 relief = **\$0.9T**. American Rescue Plan = **\$1.9T** (data points: data.STIMULUS_PACKAGES). That is **\$5.0 trillion** in just those three packages, and with the smaller programs layered on top, the real total ran **above \$5 trillion**. To feel the size: \$5 trillion is roughly *a quarter of an entire year's US GDP* (about \$21 trillion in 2020), deployed in twelve months. Spread across the roughly 130 million US households, it is on the order of **\$38,000 per household** in gross federal support flowing through the system. Some households got direct checks worth a few thousand dollars; the rest flowed through benefits, business loans, and aid — but the aggregate firehose was that large.

The intuition: \$5 trillion is not a "stimulus" in the usual sense of a gentle nudge — it is a flood roughly a quarter the size of the whole economy, and floods of that size do not vanish; they show up later, first in asset prices and then in consumer prices.

## The everything rally: liquidity floods to the riskiest assets

Now the central mechanism of the post. Why did asset prices soar while the economy was in ruins? Because of a chain reaction that always follows a liquidity flood, and 2020 ran it in its purest, fastest form ever.

Start with the wall of cash. The Fed's QE *removed* safe assets — Treasuries and mortgage bonds — from the market and replaced them with reserves and cash. The fiscal transfers *added* cash directly to households and businesses. Now the system is awash in dollars. But here is the trap built into that cash: with the policy rate at zero and the Fed buying up the safe bonds, **cash earns nothing, and the safe yielding alternatives have been taken away.** A pension fund, an insurer, an individual sitting on a stimulus check — none of them can afford to leave money earning 0% if they need a return. The cash *must* find somewhere to go.

So it cascades down the **risk ladder**. First it bids up Treasuries (whatever the Fed left), crushing their yields toward zero — so there is no return there. Then it moves into investment-grade corporate bonds, slamming their spreads tighter — still not enough yield. Then into equities, where the big-cap tech names that had clear pandemic tailwinds led first, then the whole index. Then into the riskier corners: high-yield junk bonds, speculative growth stocks, SPACs (blank-check shell companies), meme stocks. And finally into the absolute riskiest assets on earth: far-out-of-the-money options, unprofitable concept stocks, and crypto. Each rung of the ladder gets bid up until its return is no longer attractive, which pushes the cash to the next, riskier rung. This is the **forced rebalancing** or **portfolio-balance** channel, and when the flood is large enough, it lifts *every* rung at once. That is the **everything rally**: not a judgment that every asset is a good business, but the mechanical consequence of too much cash chasing too little yield.

![Flow chart showing infinite QE and fiscal transfers creating a wall of cash that is pushed down the risk ladder from Treasuries to investment grade to equities to junk and finally to crypto and speculative assets](/imgs/blogs/2020-covid-crash-infinite-qe-and-the-everything-rally-5.png)

Notice the order of the rally is the *reverse* of the order of safety. The safest assets rallied first and least (they were already near the ceiling), and the riskiest assets rallied last and most. By the end of 2021, the biggest winners were precisely the things with the least connection to current cash flows: unprofitable tech trading on dreams of distant revenue, meme stocks driven by online crowds, and cryptocurrencies with no earnings at all. **The riskier and more speculative the asset, the better it did.** That is the fingerprint of a liquidity-driven rally, and it is the opposite of what a fundamentals-driven rally looks like (in which quality and profitability lead). We trace how money rotates through these regimes in [Risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates).

There is a second reason the flood landed on long-duration, speculative assets specifically, and it is worth a sentence because it makes the playbook sharper. A stock is, in finance terms, a claim on *future* cash flows, discounted back to today at an interest rate. When the Fed crushes interest rates to zero, the discount rate collapses, and the *present value of distant future cash flows rises the most.* A profitable utility throwing off cash today barely moves; a speculative tech company whose profits are imagined for 2035 becomes worth dramatically more, because those far-off dollars are now discounted at almost nothing. Zero rates are mathematically a tailwind for exactly the longest-duration, most speculative assets — which is why they led. We work through this discounting mechanism in [How monetary policy moves stocks: discount rates and sectors](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors).

#### Worked example: a \$100,000 bet on the riskiest assets while the economy collapsed

Make the divergence concrete. Imagine two people each put **\$100,000** to work at the March 23, 2020 bottom. Person A invests in "the economy" — they buy a basket tied to current conditions: airlines, hotels, energy producers, small businesses, exactly the things being crushed by lockdowns. Through 2020 the *economy* those assets track had its worst year since the Depression; real GDP fell **2.2%** for the year (data.GDP_GROWTH, 2020), unemployment hit **14.8%** in April (data.UNRATE), and many of those specific businesses lost money or went bankrupt. Person B ignores the economy entirely and buys the *riskiest financial assets*: the Nasdaq, speculative growth stocks, and a slug of Bitcoin. By the end of 2021, Person B's Nasdaq exposure was up over 130% from the low; their Bitcoin, bought near \$5,000-\$6,000 in March 2020, had touched nearly \$69,000 — more than a **10x** — turning a \$20,000 crypto allocation into roughly \$200,000+ at the peak. Person B, who bet *against* the economy and *with* the liquidity, multiplied their money. Person A, who bet on the real-world businesses, mostly waited years just to break even on the worst-hit names.

The intuition: in 2020 the trade that worked was to ignore the collapsing economy entirely and position for the liquidity flood, because the flood — not the economy — set the prices.

## The inflation aftermath: the bill comes due in 2022

Liquidity moves markets first. It moves the *real economy* second, with a long lag — and that lag is where the 2020 story turns from triumph to hangover.

Here is the chain. The fiscal transfers put cash directly into households' pockets. With much of the service economy locked down, people could not spend that cash on restaurants, travel, or experiences, so they spent it on *goods* — home offices, exercise equipment, electronics, cars — and saved or invested the rest. Demand for physical goods surged at the exact moment global supply chains were snarled by the pandemic (factories shut, ports backed up, shipping containers stranded). Too much money chasing too few goods is the textbook definition of **inflation**. On top of that, broad money — the total stock of money in the economy, measured as M2 — exploded: it went from about \$15.4 trillion at the end of 2019 to **\$19.1 trillion** by the end of 2020 and **\$21.5 trillion** by the end of 2021, a roughly 40% increase in two years (data.M2_YEAREND). That is an enormous expansion in the actual quantity of spendable money in the real economy.

For about a year and a half, inflation stayed quiet — it was still under 2% in early 2021. Both the Fed and most economists called the early rise **"transitory,"** arguing it was just supply-chain bottlenecks that would resolve. They were wrong about the magnitude and the persistence. By the time the bottlenecks eased, the wall of money had worked through the system, and inflation took off. The chart shows the path. Inflation crossed 5% by mid-2021, 7% by January 2022, and peaked at **9.06% in June 2022** (data.CPI_PEAK) — a **40-year high**, the worst inflation since the early 1980s. The lag from the peak of the 2020-21 money flood to the peak of inflation was roughly **eighteen months.**

![Line chart of CPI inflation year over year from 2020 to 2026 showing the rise from under 2 percent to the 9.06 percent peak in June 2022 with the 2020 to 2021 liquidity flood window marked](/imgs/blogs/2020-covid-crash-infinite-qe-and-the-everything-rally-6.png)

And inflation is what *ended* the everything rally. Because once inflation is running near 9%, the Fed has no choice: it must reverse everything. It hiked the policy rate from zero to over 5% in 2022-2023 — the fastest hiking cycle in decades — and switched from QE to QT (quantitative tightening, draining the balance sheet). The discount rate that had been crushing toward zero, lifting all those long-duration speculative assets, now shot *up*. The exact mechanism that had inflated crypto, junk, and unprofitable tech ran in reverse. In 2022 the Nasdaq fell about 33%, long-dated Treasuries fell over 30%, and Bitcoin fell **64%**. The everything *rally* became, for one brutal year, the everything *selloff* — and it was caused by the bill from 2020 finally coming due. The full 2022 episode, where stocks and bonds fell together, is its own case study, but the cause traces straight back to the 2020 flood.

Sit with the symmetry for a moment, because it is the strongest single piece of evidence for the entire thesis of this post. In 2020-2021, the ranking of *winners* went from least risky (Treasuries, up a little) to most risky (crypto, up enormously). In 2022, the ranking of *losers* went in almost exactly the same order — the most speculative, longest-duration assets fell the hardest, while the least risky fell the least. If fundamentals had driven the cycle, you would not see this mirror image, because companies' actual businesses do not improve and then deteriorate in lockstep with the Fed's balance sheet. Earnings did not double in 2021 and halve in 2022. What doubled and halved was the *liquidity*, and the assets repriced up and then down in proportion to how sensitive they were to that liquidity. The cycle is, in effect, a controlled experiment: the same set of assets, run forward (flood) and then backward (drain), producing mirror-image results. There is no cleaner demonstration anywhere in market history that the liquidity tide — not the economy — sets the prices of risk assets.

It is also worth naming the cost in human terms, because the inflation tail was felt directly in households' budgets. The same rate hikes that ended the rally pushed the 30-year mortgage rate from a record-low **2.65%** in January 2021 to **7.79%** by October 2023 (data.MORTGAGE_30Y) — more than tripling the monthly interest on a new home loan and effectively freezing the housing market. Savers who had held cash through 2020-2021 watched 9% inflation quietly erase a tenth of their purchasing power in a single year. The 2020 response averted a depression, which was the right call; but "averted a depression" and "had no cost" are different claims, and the inflation tail is where the cost showed up.

#### Worked example: the inflation lag from money flood to 9.06%

Trace the timeline with numbers. The money flood peaked over 2020-2021: M2 rose from \$15.4T to \$21.5T (a roughly \$6 trillion, ~40% increase), the Fed balance sheet went from \$4.31T to \$8.76T, and over \$5T of fiscal transfers hit. Inflation in 2020 itself was *low* — CPI ran around 1.2-1.4% through mid-2020 (data.CPI_YOY), because the money had not yet circulated into spending and supply was still keeping up. Then the lag elapsed. Eighteen months after the bulk of the flood, CPI hit **9.06%** in June 2022. Run a rough check: \$6 trillion of new M2 against a roughly \$21 trillion economy is a ~28% bulge in the money stock over two years; some of that was matched by recovering output, but the excess showed up as a multi-year burst of inflation that cumulatively raised the price level by roughly 20% from 2020 to 2024. The money did not disappear — it converted, with a delay, into higher prices.

The intuition: a liquidity flood prices assets up immediately and prices goods up about eighteen months later, so the inflation print is the *delayed receipt* for the rally you already enjoyed.

## Common misconceptions

The 2020 episode generated more durable myths than almost any market event in history. Each one, corrected, sharpens the trading lesson.

**Myth 1: "The 2020-2021 rally reflected an improving economy."** No — the rally happened *while* the economy was collapsing and led the recovery by a year or more. Real GDP fell 2.2% in 2020; unemployment hit 14.8% in April 2020; tens of thousands of businesses failed. Yet the S&P doubled off its low and the Nasdaq rose 130%. If the rally reflected the economy, it would have *waited* for the economy to recover. It did not wait, because it was not tracking the economy — it was tracking the liquidity. The proof is in *which* assets won: the most speculative, least profitable, most economically disconnected assets led. An economy-driven rally rewards quality and current earnings; a liquidity-driven rally rewards risk and duration. 2020 was unambiguously the latter.

**Myth 2: "QE directly funded the stimulus checks — the Fed printed the money the government sent out."** This conflates two separate things. The fiscal stimulus was funded by the **Treasury issuing bonds** (government borrowing). QE was the **Fed buying bonds** in the secondary market. The two are related — heavy Treasury issuance plus heavy Fed buying meant the Fed effectively absorbed a large share of the new government debt, which kept borrowing costs low and is sometimes loosely called "monetizing the debt." But the Fed did not write the stimulus checks, and the reserves QE created cannot be spent at a store. The checks came from Treasury borrowing; QE made that borrowing cheap and kept the bond market orderly. Different cannons, same direction. We separate these channels carefully in [Fiscal–monetary interaction: dominance and the policy mix](/blog/trading/macro-trading/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix).

**Myth 3: "It was free — the policy response cost nothing because markets recovered and the economy was saved."** It was not free; the bill arrived as inflation. The over-\$5T fiscal flood and the doubling of the Fed's balance sheet fed the worst inflation in 40 years, peaking at 9.06%. That inflation cut the real purchasing power of every wage and every dollar of savings, and forced the Fed into the fastest rate-hiking cycle in decades, which then caused the 2022 selloff and squeezed mortgage borrowers (30-year mortgage rates went from a record-low 2.65% in January 2021 to 7.79% by October 2023). The 2020 response was the right call to stop a systemic collapse — but "right call" is not "free." Every large liquidity injection has a delayed inflation tail; pretending otherwise is how traders get blindsided by the reversal.

**Myth 4: "The crash was about COVID deaths and lockdowns, so the market was just pricing the pandemic."** The *trigger* was COVID, but the *crash* was a liquidity mechanism. The proof: the market bottomed on March 23, 2020 — at the *start* of the pandemic, before the worst death tolls, before vaccines, with the economic data still getting worse for months. If the market were pricing the pandemic's severity, it would have kept falling as the news worsened. Instead it bottomed the day the Fed went infinite and then rose *through* the worst of the pandemic. The market was pricing the *policy response*, not the virus.

**Myth 5: "Stocks always go up, so the lesson of 2020 is to just buy and hold."** The lesson is narrower and more useful: stocks go up *when liquidity is expanding* and struggle *when it is draining.* The same playbook that said "buy" in March 2020 (liquidity flooding in) said "reduce risk" in 2022 (liquidity draining via QT and rate hikes). A trader who learned only "buy and hold" from 2020 got run over in 2022. The real lesson is to read the *direction* of the liquidity tide, not to assume it only ever rises.

## How it shows up in real markets

Beyond the headline crash and rally, the 2020 episode left fingerprints across every asset class — and recognizing these patterns is how you spot the *next* policy-driven move.

**The bottom was a policy date, not a fundamental date.** The S&P 500 bottomed on March 23, 2020 — the day of the "amounts needed" statement. Not when COVID cases peaked, not when lockdowns lifted, not when earnings stabilized. The single most important price low of the decade was set by a Fed press release. This is the recurring shape of policy-driven markets: the inflection points cluster on *policy* dates (FOMC meetings, emergency statements, fiscal-bill signings), not on economic data. A trader who was watching the policy calendar caught the turn; one watching the case counts did not. We cover reading these dates in [The macro calendar: CPI, NFP, FOMC, and PMI](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi).

**Credit spreads turned on the announcement, before any buying.** When the Fed announced (March 23) it would buy corporate bonds, high-yield credit spreads — the extra yield junk bonds pay over Treasuries, a direct measure of default fear — peaked at **5.69 percentage points** in July 2020 and then collapsed back toward 3% by year-end, even though the Fed bought only a tiny fraction of the corporate bond market. The *promise* of the backstop did the work. This is one of the most important lessons in the post: a credible central-bank backstop reprices markets through expectations, often without the bank spending much at all. Front-running a credible backstop is close to free money, and 2020 proved it twice (Treasuries in March, credit in March-April).

**The dollar spiked, then sank — the liquidity tell.** During the dash for cash (March 2020), the US Dollar Index (DXY) spiked as everyone scrambled for dollars; the dollar is the asset everyone needs in a panic, so it *rallies* in a liquidity crisis. Then, as the Fed flooded the world with dollars (through QE and dollar swap lines to foreign central banks), the DXY fell from the March spike to a close of **89.9** by the end of 2020 — a weak dollar is what a dollar flood produces. The dollar's round trip — spike on the panic, sink on the flood — is itself a clean reading of the liquidity regime, and we cover it in [The dollar system: why the USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).

**Gold, the no-yield asset, soared on zero real rates.** Gold pays no coupon, so its main competitor is the *real* (inflation-adjusted) yield on cash and bonds. When the Fed crushed nominal rates to zero while inflation expectations rose, *real* yields went deeply negative — the 10-year real yield hit about **-1.0%** in 2021 (data.UST10Y_REAL). A negative real yield means holding cash *loses* purchasing power, which makes a zero-yield asset like gold look great by comparison. Gold's annual average rose from \$1,393 in 2019 to \$1,770 in 2020 (data.GOLD). The mechanism is general: negative real rates are rocket fuel for assets with no cash flow, and it explains gold *and* crypto in the same breath. We work it out in [How monetary policy moves commodities: real rates and gold](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold).

**The reversal mirrored the rally, asset for asset.** When the bill came due and the Fed hiked in 2022, every winner of 2020-2021 became a loser, in proportion to how speculative it was. In 2022: the S&P fell 18%, the Nasdaq 33%, long Treasuries 31%, and Bitcoin 64% (data.ASSET_RETURNS_2022). The ranking of losses in 2022 was almost exactly the *reverse* ranking of gains in 2020-2021 — because the same liquidity mechanism that lifted them ran backward. This symmetry is the deepest evidence that liquidity, not fundamentals, was driving the whole cycle: fundamentals do not reverse symmetrically on a Fed pivot, but liquidity-priced assets do.

## How to trade it: the policy-over-fundamentals playbook

Everything above earns its keep here. The 2020 episode is not just history; it is a repeatable template for how to position when policy takes over the market. Four moves, in order, with the signals and the invalidation for each.

**1. Respect the panic — do not catch the knife before policy arrives.** The first signal of a liquidity crash (as opposed to a normal selloff) is *correlation going to one*: everything falls together, including the assets that are supposed to hedge. The specific tell is **even Treasuries falling** and the VIX spiking above ~50-60 (it hit 82.7 in 2020). When you see that, understand you are in a *funding* crisis, and a funding crisis can overshoot wildly before policy responds — the bottom is not set by value, it is set by whenever the central bank decides to intervene. Do not try to pick the low on fundamentals; in a dash for cash, fundamentals are irrelevant and "cheap" gets cheaper. **The position:** raise cash, reduce leverage (you do not want to be the fund getting the margin call), and wait. **The invalidation of waiting:** a credible, open-ended policy backstop — that is your cue to flip.

**2. Front-run the backstop the instant it goes open-ended.** This is the highest-conviction trade in the whole playbook, and 2020 proved it. The exact signal is *open-ended* language — "in the amounts needed," "whatever it takes," "unlimited," "no cap." A *fixed* program ("we will buy \$X") can fail if X is too small; an *uncapped* program cannot, because the central bank has infinite dollars and the market knows it. The moment the Fed went uncapped on March 23, 2020, the bottom was in — that day. **The position:** buy risk aggressively, and buy the *most beaten-down, highest-quality* version of risk (the assets that fell hardest in the panic but are not going bankrupt — they snap back the most). Buy the credit backstop too: when the Fed says it will buy corporate bonds, spreads collapse on the announcement. **The invalidation:** the backstop being *capped or conditional* rather than open-ended — a fixed-size program is far weaker, and you size down accordingly.

#### Worked example: sizing the front-run

Put numbers on the front-run trade. Suppose on March 23, 2020 you have **\$50,000** to deploy and you split it: \$30,000 into a broad equity index and \$20,000 into the most beaten-down high-quality risk (large-cap tech that fell 30%+ but had pandemic tailwinds). The equity index roughly doubled off the low into 2021, taking the \$30,000 toward \$60,000. The beaten-down tech sleeve, which fell hardest and snapped back hardest, more than doubled, taking the \$20,000 past \$45,000. The combined \$50,000 grew toward \$105,000 in under two years — and the *signal* that triggered the whole trade was four words in a press release ("in the amounts needed"), not a single piece of economic data. The risk you accepted: if the backstop had been capped and failed, the beaten-down sleeve could have fallen another 20-30% first.

The intuition: the cleanest, highest-conviction macro trade is to buy risk the instant a central-bank backstop goes open-ended, because the market reprices on the *promise* before the buying even starts.

**3. Ride the liquidity while the balance sheet expands.** Once you are positioned, the holding signal is simple and mechanical: **the Fed's balance sheet and broad money are rising.** Rising liquidity is a tailwind for every risk asset; you stay long and you favor the *riskiest* rungs of the ladder (the higher-beta, longer-duration assets capture the most upside in a flood), as long as the tide is coming in. The everything-rally fingerprint — the riskiest, most speculative assets leading — is the *confirmation* that the liquidity channel is the dominant driver. **The position:** stay long risk, tilt toward duration and speculation while liquidity expands, and do not be talked out of it by bad economic data — in a liquidity-driven rally, the economy can be terrible and the rally continues. **The invalidation:** the liquidity *trajectory* flattening or turning — when QE slows ("tapers") or the balance sheet starts shrinking, the tailwind becomes a headwind, and the riskiest assets you favored on the way up will fall the most on the way down.

**4. Watch the inflation tail the entire time — it is the regime's expiration date.** Every large liquidity flood carries a delayed inflation bill, arriving roughly twelve to eighteen months later. This is your single most important *forward-looking* signal, because the inflation is what forces the policy reversal that ends the rally. **The signal to monitor:** broad money growth (M2) and the inflation prints (CPI, core PCE). When inflation breaks decisively above the central bank's ~2% target and keeps rising, the clock is ticking — the Fed will eventually have to hike and drain, and the everything rally will reverse into an everything selloff. In 2020-2022, the inflation tail ran from a low base to **9.06%** over about eighteen months, and the moment the Fed pivoted to fighting it (early 2022), the 2022 bear market began. **The position as the tail arrives:** reduce risk, cut duration and speculation hardest (they fall most when rates rise), and rotate toward the assets that *benefit* from the reversal — cash (which finally yields again), short-dated bonds, and inflation beneficiaries like energy and commodities. **The invalidation of staying long:** inflation accelerating *and* the central bank turning hawkish — that combination is the explicit end of the liquidity regime, and you do not fight it.

The meta-lesson that ties all four together — the reason 2020 is the master case study — is this: **in a policy-driven market, the policy is the fundamental.** When liquidity is the dominant force, the economy, earnings, and the news are second-order; the first-order question is always "what is the central bank's balance sheet doing, and what is the government's fiscal stance?" 2020 demonstrated this with a clarity no other episode matches, because the economy was *as bad as it has ever been* while markets had *one of their best runs ever* — the two facts can only be reconciled by liquidity. Learn to read the liquidity tide, front-run the backstop, ride the flood, and respect the inflation tail, and you have the core of macro trading in a single case study. The same lens applies to building any macro view, which we assemble in [Building a macro thesis: from data to a trade](/blog/trading/macro-trading/building-a-macro-thesis-from-data-to-a-trade).
