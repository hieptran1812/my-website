> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Almost every macro view you can hold ends up expressed, directly or indirectly, through a dollar position, which is why learning to trade the DXY and the major pairs is the core skill of applied macro.
>
> - The dollar **smiles**: it tends to be strong in a global panic (the safe-haven left side) *and* strong in a US-led boom (the high-yield right side), and weak only in the contented muddle in between. Knowing which side you're on tells you the whole trade.
> - The dollar is the hub of the global **carry trade** — borrow a low-yield currency, hold a high-yield one, pocket the gap — and the carry's fat tail is a violent unwind when the funding currency snaps back. The 2024 yen carry going from 161.9 toward 145 erased years of carry in days.
> - The level the dollar actually follows is the **real-rate differential** — the US inflation-adjusted yield versus the rest of the world — not the nominal rate and not the size of America's deficits.
> - The one rule to remember: **size every dollar/carry position for the unwind, not the average day.** Carry earns a little most of the time and loses a lot all at once.

In the first week of August 2024, a position that had quietly printed money for years detonated. For most of 2023 and 2024, the single most crowded trade on earth had been the yen carry: borrow Japanese yen at essentially zero, sell them for dollars earning more than 5%, and collect the difference while the yen kept sliding to multi-decade lows. By July, USD/JPY — the number of yen it takes to buy one dollar — had pushed to **161.9**, the weakest yen since 1986. The trade had become a one-way escalator. Then the Bank of Japan nudged its policy rate up, a soft US jobs number landed, and the escalator reversed into a trapdoor. Within days USD/JPY collapsed toward 145 — a roughly 10% move in a "stable" major currency in about three weeks. Carry books that had levered the trade got margin-called, dumped everything they owned to raise cash, and the shock jumped the rails of FX entirely: Japanese stocks had their worst single day since 1987, US tech sold off, and the VIX briefly spiked above 65. A trade about *one exchange rate* became, for forty-eight hours, the most important thing in every market on the planet.

That is the lesson of this entire post, compressed. The dollar is not just one more line on your FX screen. It is the **hub** of the world's carry trade, the **master barometer** of risk-on versus risk-off, and the asset that perversely **smiles** — rising at both ends of the macro spectrum. Because of those three roles, expressing almost any macro view runs through a dollar position whether you mean it to or not. Long emerging markets? You're implicitly short the dollar. Long commodities? Short the dollar. Running a carry book? Long the dollar against your funding currency, and short volatility besides. The companion piece on [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) explains *what* the dollar is and *why* it rules — the three jobs, the eurodollar plumbing, the reserve share. This post assumes you've got that and answers the next question: **how do you actually trade it?**

![The dollar smile with strong dollar in panic and in a US boom and weak in the muddle](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile-1.png)

The figure above is the map we'll spend the post filling in. The dollar's strength, plotted against the state of the world, makes a U — a smile. We'll learn to locate ourselves on it, because each region of the smile is a *different trade with a different instrument and a different way to size it*. By the end you'll have a concrete playbook: read the smile regime, pick whether to express the view through the DXY or a specific pair, and size the position for the carry-unwind tail that can wipe out a year of patient gains in a single bad week.

## Foundations: the DXY, carry, real rates, and the smile

Let's build the four ideas this whole post stands on, each from the ground up, before we go deep on positioning. If you've read the dollar-system piece, this is a fast refresher; if not, it's everything you need.

**The DXY, in one paragraph.** The **DXY** (US Dollar Index) is a single, tradable number for "the dollar," but it measures the dollar against only six rich-country currencies, and it is **57.6% the euro**, with the yen, pound, Canadian dollar, Swedish krona, and Swiss franc making up the rest. The weights were fixed in 1999 and never rebalanced, so the DXY contains no Chinese yuan, no Mexican peso, no Korean won — none of the currencies that hold most of the world's dollar debt. For trading purposes this means two things. First, the DXY is liquid: deep futures and options, quoted everywhere, you can be in a position in seconds. Second, it's really a *dollar-versus-Europe* gauge, so a DXY move is mostly a euro move in disguise. When you trade the DXY you are, to a first approximation, trading your view on EUR/USD flipped upside down. We'll use the DXY constantly — as an instrument *and* as a risk dial — but always knowing it's a liquid proxy with a European tilt, not a measurement of the dollar against the whole world.

**Carry, in one paragraph.** A **carry trade** is the simplest idea in macro and one of the most dangerous. You *borrow* in a currency with a low interest rate (the **funding currency**), convert the proceeds into a currency with a high interest rate (the **target currency**), and park the money there earning the higher rate. As long as the exchange rate doesn't move against you, you pocket the *interest-rate difference* every single day. The dollar, for most of 2022-2024, was the great target currency: US short rates were above 5% while Japan's were near zero and Switzerland's were barely positive, so "borrow yen or francs, hold dollars" was the carry trade of the era. The catch — and it's the whole game — is that you are short the funding currency, so if it suddenly rallies, your FX loss can dwarf years of accumulated interest. Carry is a strategy that **earns a little most of the time and loses a lot all at once**. That payoff shape — many small wins, rare huge losses — is the defining feature, and sizing for it is most of the skill.

**Real-rate differentials, in one paragraph.** Currencies are pulled toward the country that pays the highest *real* (inflation-adjusted) return on safe assets, because that's where global capital wants to sit. The **real rate** is roughly the nominal interest rate minus expected inflation — what you actually earn after inflation eats its share. A country can have a high nominal rate and a *negative* real rate if inflation is higher still, and capital sees through the nominal number to the real one. So the dollar doesn't follow US rates in the abstract; it follows the **US real rate relative to everyone else's**. When US real yields rise above the rest of the world's, capital flows toward dollar assets and the dollar strengthens. This is the deep driver underneath the smile's right side, and we'll quantify it. For the full machinery of real versus nominal, the dedicated post on [real yields as the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal) is the reference; here we just need the link from real-rate differential to dollar direction.

**The smile, in one paragraph.** Put those together and you get the dollar's signature behavior. The dollar is strong in a **panic** (left of the smile) because when markets crater, everyone runs to the safest, most liquid asset on earth — dollars and US Treasury bills — and because a panic makes offshore dollar funding seize, forcing borrowers to scramble for dollars. The dollar is *also* strong in a **US boom** (right of the smile) because when America out-grows and out-yields the world, capital flows toward dollar assets to chase the return. The dollar is **weak** only in the muddle (the bottom) — when the world is growing calmly, no one is panicking, and US growth is unremarkable, so capital happily leaves low-yielding dollars to chase higher returns abroad. Strong at both ends, weak in the middle: a U, a smile. Each region is a distinct regime with a distinct trade, and the single most useful diagnostic skill in dollar trading is figuring out which region you're standing in.

One more foundational reminder, because beginners trip on it: **"the dollar" is always a ratio.** A currency has no price by itself, only relative to something else. "The dollar rose 5%" always means "against some currency or basket." So whenever you form a dollar view, you must immediately ask *against what?* — the DXY (mostly the euro), the yen, the peso, an EM basket? The answer changes the trade completely, and we'll see episodes where the dollar was simultaneously *falling* against the euro and *ripping* against the yuan. Holding "the dollar" as a single direction in your head, without specifying the cross, is the most common rookie error in macro.

## The dollar smile in depth: three regimes, three trades

The smile is worth slowing down on, because it is the organizing idea of the whole post. Most assets have a clean directional story — stocks like growth, gold likes fear. The dollar is weird: it likes *both* fear and a particular kind of growth, and dislikes only the comfortable middle. Plotted against the world's state, dollar strength is a U-shape, and the practical genius of the smile is that it tells you *what kind* of dollar move you're looking at, which tells you what every other asset should be doing and how to position.

**The left side: the panic bid.** When something breaks and markets crater — 2008, March 2020, a banking scare, a war shock — investors everywhere sell whatever is risky and run to the safest, most liquid thing on earth. That thing is the US dollar and the US Treasury bill. The dollar rises here not because America is doing *well* — it usually isn't; in March 2020 the US economy was collapsing — but because in a fire, everyone sprints for the same exit, and the dollar is the exit. There's a second, mechanical reason the panic bid is so violent. So much of the world's debt is borrowed in dollars (roughly \$13 trillion sits on non-American balance sheets) that when funding markets seize, every dollar borrower scrambles to buy dollars at once to cover their obligations. A panic *is* a dollar shortage. So on the left of the smile, a rising dollar comes packaged with *falling stocks, widening credit spreads, and a spiking VIX*. The trade: get defensive across the board, and if you want to express it through the dollar, long USD against the high-beta currencies (EM, commodity currencies, and the yen if it's not the funding panic itself).

**The right side: the high-yield magnet.** When the US economy is growing faster than the rest of the world and the Fed is hiking to keep up, dollar assets offer the best combination of growth and yield on the planet. Capital flows *toward* the dollar to chase those returns, and the dollar strengthens. 2014-2015 (the "taper tantrum" dollar) and 2022 (the inflation-fighting dollar) are textbook right-side episodes. The crucial difference from the left side: here the dollar rises *with* resilient US stocks and *rising* US yields, even as the rest of the world struggles. It's a *relative-strength* story, not a panic. The trade: favor US assets over international ones, and if you express it through FX, long the dollar against the world — and respect that this regime can run for *years*, because the rate-path gap that drives it doesn't close overnight.

**The bottom: the weak-dollar muddle.** In between — the world growing steadily, no one panicking, US growth unremarkable relative to everyone else, the Fed on hold or cutting — the dollar is *weak*. Why hold low-yielding, fairly-valued dollars when global growth is humming and you can earn more in emerging markets, commodities, and risk assets abroad? In the calm middle, capital *leaves* the dollar to chase returns elsewhere, the dollar softens, and the risk complex rallies. 2020-2021 (Fed at zero, liquidity flooding out) and 2025 (growth converging, the Fed easing) are the recent examples. The bottom of the smile is the "risk-on, dollar-down" world bulls love. The trade: this is the green light for the reflation book — short the dollar, long EM, long commodities, long high-beta risk including crypto.

Here is why the smile is such a powerful diagnostic. A *rising* dollar is not one signal — it is two opposite signals depending on which side you're on, and confusing them is the most expensive mistake in macro. A dollar rising on the left (panic) screams "get out of risk." A dollar rising on the right (boom) says "favor US risk over the rest." Same dollar direction, opposite playbooks. So you never read the dollar in isolation; you cross-check it against the risk cross-asset tape — equities, credit spreads, the VIX — to place yourself on the smile. The post on [risk-on, risk-off rotation](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates) is the companion that maps how money moves between the two states; the dollar is the cleanest single read on which state you're in.

#### Worked example: the dollar smile in three years of data

Let's locate three real years on the smile using the DXY year-end levels. In **2020**, the DXY closed at **89.9**, near a multi-year low — the bottom of the smile. The Fed had cut to zero and flooded the system with liquidity, the panic of March had passed, and capital was streaming out of dollars into the everything-rally; risk assets ripped. In **2022**, the DXY closed at **103.5** and had touched **114.8** intraday on September 28 — hard on the right side of the smile, the inflation-fighting boom dollar, as the Fed hiked faster than anyone else while the world lagged. That same year, the *panic* dynamics were also live underneath: when the September gilt crisis hit and the yen broke 145, the dollar got an extra left-side bid on top of the right-side trend. So 2022 sat on the right edge with intermittent left-side spikes — the most dangerous configuration for anyone short the dollar. By **2025**, the DXY had slid back to about **97.0**, sliding off the right edge toward the muddle as US outperformance faded and the Fed eased — and, predictably, the assets the strong dollar had crushed got their relief rally. Reading the level alone — 89.9, 103.5, 114.8, 97.0 — and asking "which side of the smile?" would have told you the regime, and the regime is the trade.

![DXY 2014 to 2025 line chart with the 2022 peak 114.8 marked across regimes](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile-2.png)

The chart above is the DXY's path through these regimes from 2014 to 2025. Notice it doesn't drift randomly — it *trends in regimes that last years*, driven by the relative growth and rate path of the US versus everyone else. The 2014-2015 surge (taper plus Fed liftoff while Europe and Japan eased), the 2020-2021 weak-dollar trough (Fed at zero), the 2022 wrecking-ball peak (the fastest hiking cycle in forty years), and the 2024 surge back to 108.5 on resilient US growth are all visible as multi-year moves, not noise. This is the single most important fact for trading the dollar: **it trends hard and long inside a regime, and the most reliably losing trade is fading a strong dollar early.** We'll return to that as the cardinal rule of the playbook.

## Carry: borrow low-yield, hold high-yield, and the unwind

Now the engine that makes the dollar the hub of global macro: the carry trade. We met it in the foundations; let's go deep, because carry is where the dollar's three roles fuse, and where the biggest blow-ups live.

Start with the mechanic. The interest-rate **parity** that textbooks teach says that a high-yield currency *should*, in theory, depreciate by exactly the rate differential, so that carry earns nothing on average — the higher interest is supposed to be exactly offset by the currency falling. In the real world, this is *systematically false* over long stretches. High-yield currencies tend to stay strong or even *appreciate* for years before they snap, so the carry trade earns the rate gap *plus* often a currency gain — until it doesn't. This persistent gap between theory and reality is called the **forward premium puzzle**, and it's the reason carry is one of the most reliably profitable strategies in finance *and* one of the most dangerous: the profit is real and shows up most of the time, but it's compensation for taking a fat, rare, catastrophic risk — exactly the shape that lures traders into over-sizing.

Here's the dollar version that ran 2022-2024. US short rates were above 5%; Japan's were near zero. So the trade was: borrow yen, sell them for dollars, hold the dollars in T-bills or money-market funds earning ~5.3%, and collect roughly the 5-point gap. As a bonus, the yen kept *weakening* (USD/JPY rose from 115 in 2021 to 161.9 by mid-2024), so carry traders earned the rate gap *and* a currency gain on top. It felt like free money for two years. That feeling is the danger signal, not the green light.

![The carry trade mechanic from borrowing yen to the forced unwind](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile-3.png)

The figure traces the full life of the trade: borrow the low-yield funding currency, convert to the high-yield target, hold and collect the gap, pocket the carry — and then, when the shock hits, the forced unwind that buys back the now-expensive funding currency at a loss that erases the patient gains. Walk the chain once and the asymmetry is obvious: the left half of the figure is slow and pleasant, the right half is fast and brutal. That asymmetry — slow gains, fast losses — is *the* property to internalize. Let's put numbers on both halves.

#### Worked example: the carry P&L — borrow yen, hold dollars, on a \$1,000,000 book

Suppose at the start you put on a yen-funded dollar carry of **\$1,000,000** notional. You borrow the yen equivalent at the Japanese funding rate of about **0.1%** a year, sell the yen for dollars, and hold the \$1,000,000 in US cash earning the Fed-funds-anchored money rate of about **5.3%** (the Fed's target-range upper bound sat at 5.50% from mid-2023 through September 2024; call your deposit rate a touch under that). The carry you earn is the *difference* between the two legs:

```
Earn on USD leg:   5.3% x $1,000,000  =  $53,000 / year
Pay on JPY leg:    0.1% x $1,000,000  =     $1,000 / year
Net carry:                              =  $52,000 / year  (a ~5.2% rate gap)
```

So you bank about **\$52,000 a year**, or roughly **\$1,000 a week**, *as long as the exchange rate doesn't move against you.* If, on top of that, the yen weakens while you hold — as it did, USD/JPY climbing toward 161.9 — you also earn an FX gain on the position, stacking on top of the carry. Two years of this and you've collected over \$100,000 in carry plus a chunk of currency appreciation, and the trade feels bulletproof. The intuition: carry pays you a steady drip for being short the funding currency, and the drip is real — but you're being *paid to insure someone against a yen rally*, and the premium feels free right up until you have to pay the claim.

Now the other half. The funding currency that's been sliding for two years can reverse in *days*, and because the whole point of carry is leverage — you borrowed to put it on — a sharp reversal triggers margin calls that force everyone out the same exit at once. This is the **carry unwind**, and it's the single most important tail risk in FX.

#### Worked example: the carry unwind — USD/JPY from 161.9 to 145

Take the same \$1,000,000 yen-funded dollar position, put on when USD/JPY was at its **161.9** peak in July 2024. Then the unwind hits: the Bank of Japan hikes, a soft US payrolls print lands, and USD/JPY collapses toward **145** — call it a move from 161.9 to 145.0. Your position is *long dollars, short yen*, so a stronger yen (a *lower* USD/JPY) is a loss. The FX move is:

```
Entry:  161.9 yen per dollar
Exit:   145.0 yen per dollar
Move:   (145.0 - 161.9) / 161.9  =  -10.4%   (yen strengthens ~10%)
FX loss on a $1,000,000 position:  ~ -$104,000
```

That **~\$104,000 loss** in roughly three weeks erases **two full years of the \$52,000-a-year carry** — and that's *before* leverage. A carry book levered five times would face a loss five times as large on its equity, which is why these unwinds cascade into forced selling of everything: to meet the margin call on the yen leg, funds dump stocks, bonds, and EM, and a single-pair shock becomes a cross-asset event (exactly the August 2024 episode in the hook). The intuition: carry's payoff is a coiled spring — you collect small, steady gains in exchange for a rare, violent loss, so the *only* number that matters when sizing it is how much you lose in the unwind, not how much you earn on an average day.

![USD/JPY 2019 to 2025 line chart with peaks at 151.9 and 161.9 marking the carry](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile-4.png)

The chart shows the full yen-carry arc: USD/JPY grinding higher from 103 in 2020 (yen strong, carry asleep) to the 151.9 peak in October 2022 (the first BoJ intervention since 1998) and on to the 161.9 peak in July 2024 — the most crowded the trade ever got — before the unwind. Notice the *shape* of the line: long, smooth, persistent climbs (the carry earning its drip) punctuated by sharp vertical drops (the unwinds). That signature — a slow ramp and a cliff — is the visual fingerprint of every carry trade in every currency. When you see a pair that has only gone one way for two years on a wide rate differential, you are not looking at a free trade; you are looking at a coiled spring, and your job is to be sized to survive the snap.

Why is the *dollar* specifically the hub of the carry complex, rather than just one carry currency among many? Because the dollar is on one side of almost every carry trade that matters. When US rates are high, the dollar is the great *target* currency — the world borrows yen, francs, and euros to hold dollars, so the carry complex is *long the dollar*. When US rates are low (2020-2021, the Fed at zero), the dollar flips to being a *funding* currency — traders borrow cheap dollars to buy higher-yielding EM and commodity currencies, so the complex is *short the dollar*. Either way, the dollar is the pivot, which means a sharp dollar move detonates carry in *both* configurations: a dollar rally crushes the dollar-funded EM carry (the borrowed dollars get dearer), and a dollar selloff can crush the dollar-target carry from the other direction. The practical consequence is that the dollar's direction is the master switch on the entire global carry book, and that's a second, independent reason the DXY belongs on your screen even when you're trading a pair that doesn't contain it: the DXY tells you whether the carry complex as a whole is loading up or unwinding.

A few more deeper points about carry that separate survivors from victims. First, **carry and volatility are joined at the hip.** Carry trades make money when volatility is *low and falling* (the funding currency stays asleep) and lose catastrophically when volatility *spikes*. Being long carry is, in disguise, being **short volatility** — you're collecting a premium for selling insurance against a big move. So when implied volatility is historically cheap and everyone is piling into the same carry, that's the most dangerous moment, not the safest: cheap vol means the insurance you're selling is underpriced, and the position is most crowded right before it breaks. Second, **carry crashes are correlated across pairs.** When the yen carry unwinds, the peso carry, the real carry, and the lira carry often unwind *together*, because the same leveraged players hold all of them and de-risk all at once. So "diversifying" across several carry pairs gives you far less protection than it looks like — in the crash, the correlations all go to one. Third, **the unwind is faster than the build.** It took two years to grind USD/JPY from 115 to 162 and three weeks to give a big chunk back. You cannot wait for confirmation to exit a carry trade; by the time the move is obvious, the gap-down has already happened. This is why carry traders watch *positioning and volatility* obsessively — they're trying to get out *before* the crowd, because there's no liquidity to get out *with* the crowd.

There's one more carry mechanic worth knowing, because it's a leading indicator of stress: the **cross-currency basis**. When non-US institutions want dollars without selling assets, they borrow dollars in the swap market, and the extra premium they pay above the "fair" interest-rate difference is the cross-currency basis. In calm times this premium is tiny. When dollars get scarce — a left-smile panic, a funding squeeze — the basis blows out *negative*, meaning foreigners are paying up sharply to get dollars. That widening basis is often the *first* sign that a carry unwind and a risk-off dollar grab are starting, before the spot exchange rate has moved much. A carry trader who watches the basis can see the funding stress building and cut exposure ahead of the gap-down; one who watches only spot finds out too late. The basis is the plumbing gauge that turns "the dollar is about to squeeze" from a hunch into a number.

## The dollar as the risk-on / risk-off master barometer

Step back from any single pair and look at what the dollar *is* to a macro trader: the single most reliable barometer of global risk appetite. When the dollar is rising broadly, money is leaving risk and seeking safety and funding; when it's falling, money is flooding into risk. This is so dependable that many macro desks treat the DXY's direction as their *first* read on the whole tape, before they look at any individual position.

![The dollar as the risk barometer with a rising DXY radiating risk-off to all assets](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile-5.png)

The figure shows why. A rising DXY transmits, through the dollar's three jobs, into a headwind across every risk asset at once. Equities fall as global liquidity tightens and the VIX rises. Commodities, priced in dollars, get more expensive for every non-dollar buyer, so demand and prices soften. Emerging-market stocks and currencies fall as the \$13 trillion of dollar debt gets heavier to service. Credit spreads widen and the most speculative corners — high-yield, crypto — feel the funding squeeze first. And carry trades unwind as the funding dollars they're short get scarcer and dearer. Every arrow points the same way: **DXY up = risk-off, across the board.** The dashed reverse arrow at the bottom is the mirror image — a falling dollar reverses all of it, the reflation green light. This is why the dollar deserves a permanent spot on your screen even when you never trade it directly: it conditions the wind direction for everything else you own.

The reason the dollar is such a clean barometer comes back to its plumbing. Because so much of the world *must* hold or obtain dollars — to pay for dollar-invoiced imports, to service dollar debt, to hold as reserves — dollar demand is structurally always-on, and it *intensifies* precisely when stress rises. A risk-off shock makes funding dollars scarcer at the exact moment everyone needs them most, so the dollar's rise and the risk selloff are not two separate events that happen to coincide — they're the *same* event seen from two sides. The dollar rising and risk falling are mechanically linked through the funding channel, which is why the correlation is so tight and so trade-able. The deeper plumbing of how short-term dollar funding seizes up is mapped in the dollar-system post; for trading, the takeaway is that the dollar leads, confirms, and amplifies risk-off, all at once.

There's a subtlety that makes the dollar *better* than the VIX as a risk read in one important way: the dollar tells you not just *that* risk is off but often *why*, through the smile. A VIX spike just says "fear is up." The dollar's behavior tells you whether the fear is a *panic* (left smile: dollar up *with* the VIX, EM cratering, a flight to safety) or a *US-outperformance* tightening (right smile: dollar up *with* US yields, US stocks relatively resilient, the rest of the world lagging). Those demand different defensive trades — in a panic you raise cash and buy Treasuries; in a right-smile tightening you rotate from international into US assets rather than going to cash. The VIX can't make that distinction. The dollar can. That's why, of all the single indicators a macro trader could put on one screen, the dollar's level and rate of change is arguably the most information-dense.

A practical refinement: watch the dollar's **rate of change**, not just its level. A slow grind higher in the DXY is a normal, tradeable regime. A *parabolic spike* — the DXY adding several points in days — is the tell that a funding squeeze is underway and that something is breaking. The spikes are what detonate carry trades and trigger EM crises; the grinds are what you position around. So the trader's dollar dashboard has two numbers, not one: where the DXY is, and how *fast* it's moving. A fast move is a different animal from a slow one, and treating them the same is how people get run over.

## Reading rate differentials and the path

We've said the dollar follows the US real-rate differential. Now let's make that operational, because "rate differentials drive FX" is true but useless until you know *which* rates, *real or nominal*, and *the level or the path*.

**Real, not nominal.** The mistake beginners make is watching the *nominal* rate gap — US 5% versus Japan 0% — and concluding the dollar must rise. But capital cares about the *real* return: nominal minus expected inflation. A country with a 10% nominal rate and 12% inflation has a *negative* 2% real rate, and capital flees it despite the eye-catching nominal yield (this is the trap in many EM carry trades — the high nominal rate is compensation for inflation and currency risk, not free money). So the dollar's driver is the **US real yield relative to the rest of the world's real yields**. When US real yields rise — because the Fed hikes faster than inflation, or inflation falls while rates stay up — dollar assets become more attractive in real terms and the dollar strengthens. The single cleanest market read on the US real yield is the **10-year TIPS yield** (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities), which prices the real rate directly.

![DXY and US 10-year real yield from 2020 to 2024 on a dual axis tracking together](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile-6.png)

The chart overlays the DXY on the US 10-year real yield from 2020 to 2024, and the relationship jumps out: when the US real yield was *negative* (around -1% in 2020-2021, the Fed pinning rates below inflation), the dollar was *weak* — the bottom of the smile, capital fleeing negative-real-yield dollars for anything that paid more. Then in 2022 the real yield flipped sharply *positive* (to +1.57% by year-end as the Fed hiked into falling inflation), and the dollar *surged* with it — the right side of the smile. By 2024 the real yield was up near +2.2% and the DXY was back at 108.5. The dollar followed the real-rate differential, not the nominal one and not the deficit headlines. This is the structural anchor: **to forecast the dollar's regime, forecast the US real-yield path relative to the world's.**

**The path, not just the level.** Here's the part that separates good FX traders from the rest: currencies move on the *change* in the expected rate path, not the level of rates today. If US rates are 5% and *everyone already knows it and expects them to stay there*, that 5% is fully priced into the dollar — it provides ongoing carry but no fresh directional fuel. The dollar moves when the *expected path shifts* — when the market suddenly prices the Fed cutting sooner, or hiking more, than it did yesterday. So the dollar reacts violently to data and Fed communication that *changes the path*: a hot CPI print that makes the market price fewer cuts sends the dollar up; a weak jobs number that prices more cuts sends it down. The level of rates is the carry; the *change in the path* is the trade. This is why FX traders live and die by the economic calendar — CPI, payrolls, FOMC — because those are the events that move the path, and the path is what moves the currency.

The operational version of "trade the path-change" is that you position against the *market's already-priced expectation*, not against the raw data. Before an FOMC meeting or a CPI release, the market has a consensus baked into prices — say, "three Fed cuts this year." The dollar barely reacts if the data merely confirms that consensus, even if the headline number is dramatic; it reacts hard when the data *moves the consensus*. So the tradeable question is never "is inflation high?" but "is inflation higher than what the dollar is already priced for?" A CPI print of 3% is dollar-bullish if the market expected 2.7% and dollar-bearish if it expected 3.3%, *for the exact same 3% number*. This is why a beginner watching the headline gets whipsawed — the dollar sells off on a "high" inflation print because it came in *below* the whisper — while the trader watching the *surprise versus pricing* gets the direction right. The discipline: know what the dollar is priced for before the event, and trade the gap between the outcome and that pricing, not the outcome itself. The rate path is a forecast the whole market holds; your edge is in anticipating *revisions* to that forecast, and the dollar is the cleanest, most liquid way to express a view on a path revision.

#### Worked example: a real-rate-differential trade using the 10-year real yield

Suppose you're positioning the dollar in early 2022 and you watch the US 10-year real yield, which has been deeply negative — about **-1.0%** at the end of 2021. Your macro view: inflation is high and sticky, the Fed has been far too slow, and it's about to hike aggressively while *inflation expectations* stay anchored — which means the *real* yield is set to rise sharply, because the nominal rate will climb faster than expected inflation. If you're right, dollar assets are about to offer a positive real return where they offered a negative one, and global capital will rotate toward them. You go **long the dollar** — say long DXY, or long USD against the lowest-real-yield majors (the yen and the euro, whose central banks were nowhere near hiking).

Over 2022 the US 10-year real yield does exactly that, climbing from about **-1.0%** to roughly **+1.6%** — a swing of about **2.6 percentage points** into positive territory. The DXY rises from about 95.7 (end-2021) to a peak of **114.8**, a **+20%** move, and closes the year at 103.5. The real-yield differential trade *was* the dollar trade: you didn't need a view on deficits, de-dollarization, or the trade balance — you needed a view on the US real yield relative to the world's, and the dollar followed it. The intuition: the dollar is a bet on the US real-rate differential, so when you can forecast the real-yield path better than the market, you can forecast the dollar's regime — and that single variable beats every louder narrative about the dollar's doom.

A caution that pairs with this: real-rate differentials drive the dollar in the *right-side* (US-outperformance) and *bottom* (muddle) regimes cleanly, but in a *left-side panic* the differential can break down, because the flight to safety overwhelms it. In March 2020 US real yields fell hard, which should have weakened the dollar — but the dollar *spiked* anyway, because the panic bid for liquidity swamped the rate signal. So real-rate differentials are your primary dollar model in normal regimes, and you override them with the safe-haven logic when the VIX is spiking and funding is seizing. Knowing *which model is live* — the rate-differential model or the safe-haven model — is, once again, a matter of reading which side of the smile you're on. The companion piece on [what moves exchange rates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-moves-exchange-rates-rates-flows-carry) develops the rate, flow, and carry forces in full; here the point is to make the real-rate read your default and the safe-haven read your panic override.

## Common misconceptions

Four beliefs about trading the dollar are widespread, intuitive, and wrong. Each one, corrected with a number, sharpens the playbook.

**Misconception 1: "A strong economy means a strong currency, always."** It's intuitive — a booming country *should* have a strong currency — and it's right often enough to be dangerous. But it fails in two big ways. First, what matters is *relative* strength and the *real-rate path*, not the absolute level of growth: a country can boom and still see its currency fall if its central bank is *cutting* while others hike. Second, the safe-haven left side of the smile breaks the rule entirely — the dollar surged in March 2020 *while the US economy was collapsing*, because a panic is a dollar shortage regardless of US growth. The dollar rose 20% in 2022 partly on US strength (right smile) and partly on global stress (left smile) at the same time. The corrected rule: the currency follows the *relative real-rate path and the risk regime*, and a strong economy is only one input — sometimes outweighed by the safe-haven bid or the rate path.

**Misconception 2: "Carry is free money."** The carry trade earns the rate gap most of the time, so it *looks* free — the yen-dollar carry paid roughly 5.2% a year (about \$52,000 on a \$1,000,000 book) for two straight years. But that income is *compensation for a rare catastrophic loss*: the 2024 unwind took USD/JPY from 161.9 toward 145, a ~10% move that erased two years of that carry in three weeks. Carry isn't free money; it's *selling insurance against a currency crash*, and the premium feels free right up until you pay the claim. The corrected rule: carry's expected return is real but its risk is a fat left tail, so the only honest way to hold it is sized for the unwind, not the average day.

**Misconception 3: "The DXY is the dollar versus everything."** The DXY is **57.6% the euro** and contains no yuan, peso, won, or rupee — none of the currencies that hold most of the world's dollar debt. So the DXY can be *flat or falling* against Europe while the dollar is *ripping* against the yuan and EM, doing real damage where the DXY can't see it. A trader who reads "DXY down, risk-on" off a euro rally while the dollar tightens the screws on EM has misread the dollar entirely. The corrected rule: use the DXY for execution because it's liquid, but cross-check it against USD/CNH and an EM-currency basket before you call the dollar's true direction — the DXY is a tradable euro-cross in disguise, not a measurement of the global dollar.

**Misconception 4: "America's deficits and debt will sink the dollar, so stay short."** Persistent shorts on the dollar based on the deficit have been a money-loser for over a decade. The US ran record deficits *while* the dollar surged to 20-year highs in 2022, because the dollar follows the real-rate differential and the risk regime, not the deficit on any tradeable horizon. The reserve-currency country runs deficits *precisely because* the world wants to accumulate its assets. The corrected rule: deficits and debt are a real multi-decade structural risk worth an allocation thought, but they are almost never the right explanation for what the dollar did this cycle — trading the dollar short off the deficit is like timing the tide by watching continental drift.

## How it shows up in real markets

Mechanisms are abstract until you watch them detonate. Here is the smile, the carry, and the real-rate driver playing out in real episodes with real numbers.

**The 2022 dollar surge — the right smile on steroids.** As the Fed hiked from near 0% to 4.5% in a single year — the fastest tightening in four decades — while Europe and especially Japan lagged badly, the US real yield swung from about -1% to +1.6% and the DXY rocketed **+20%** to its **114.8** peak on September 28. This was the right side of the smile, the high-yield-magnet dollar, and it transmitted exactly along the channels in the barometer figure: the yen collapsed past 145 and then 150 (Japan's first FX intervention since 1998), the pound crashed to a record low near 1.03 and triggered a UK pension crisis, EM currencies fell and their central banks hiked into weakness, commodities rolled over, gold fell despite being a "safe haven" (because the *real* safe haven — dollars with rising real yields attached — out-competed it), and crypto completed its bear market with Bitcoin down ~75%. The dollar wasn't reacting to those crises; it was *causing* them, through the real-rate and funding channels. A trader who read the rising real-yield differential and the right-smile regime and *stayed long the dollar through September* captured the whole move; the graveyard is full of those who called the top in June.

**The 2024 yen carry and its August unwind — the carry tail in pure form.** This is the hook, and it's the canonical carry blow-up. For two years the yen carry paid its ~5% drip and the yen kept weakening to **161.9** by July 2024 — the trade had become a one-way escalator and the most crowded position on earth, with implied volatility crushed to multi-year lows (the danger signal, not the safety signal). Then the BoJ hiked, a soft US payrolls print landed, and USD/JPY collapsed toward **145** in roughly three weeks. Leveraged carry books got margin-called and dumped everything: Japan's Nikkei had its worst day since 1987, US tech sold off, and the VIX briefly spiked above 65. A trade about one exchange rate became, for forty-eight hours, the most important thing in every market — exactly because the dollar sits at the hub of the carry complex and a yen-leg margin call forces selling across all assets. The lesson that actually generalizes: when a carry pair has gone one way for years on a wide differential and volatility is dirt cheap, you are looking at a coiled spring, and *position size is the only thing that determines whether the snap is a scratch or a catastrophe.*

**The 2020-2021 weak dollar — the bottom of the smile and the everything-rally.** The mirror image. With the Fed at zero, US real yields deeply negative (~-1%), and liquidity flooding out, the DXY slid to its **89.9** trough, and *everything* priced against the dollar ripped: EM recovered, commodities rallied, and crypto went on one of its largest bull runs ever, with Bitcoin running from under \$10,000 toward \$69,000. It was not a coincidence that the wildest risk-on melt-up in a generation happened during the weakest-dollar stretch in years. A falling dollar is liquidity flooding *into* the global system; the reflation playbook (short dollar, long EM, long commodities, long high-beta risk) was the trade, and the falling DXY was the green light that confirmed it.

**The 2025 give-back — sliding off the right edge toward the muddle.** After the 2024 surge to **108.5** on resilient US growth, the dollar weakened materially through 2025 toward **97.0** as global growth converged toward the US, the Fed eased, and the US-outperformance premium faded — the real-rate differential narrowing as the world caught up. The smile slid from the right edge back toward the middle, and the assets the strong dollar had crushed got their relief: EM stabilized, commodities found a floor, risk appetite improved. The recurring lesson of every cycle: the dollar's *regime turn* — off a strong-dollar peak, when the Fed starts easing relative to the world — is one of the most reliable risk-on catalysts there is, and trading the turn (on the *other* side, long the assets the dollar had crushed) is where the cleanest money is, *once the turn is confirmed.*

## How to trade it / The playbook

Everything above collapses into one operating principle: **read the smile regime, choose the instrument, and size for the carry-unwind tail.** Direction comes from the regime, the *how* comes from the instrument choice, and the *how big* comes from the tail. Here is the concrete sequence.

**Step 1 — Place yourself on the smile before you do anything.** Every dollar decision starts with locating the regime, and you do that by cross-checking the dollar against the risk tape:

- Dollar up **+** stocks down **+** credit spreads widening **+** VIX spiking → **left of the smile (panic).** Get defensive across the board, raise cash, expect correlations to go to one. If you express it through the dollar, long USD against high-beta currencies (EM, commodity FX). Watch for a funding squeeze (the cross-currency basis blowing out, Fed swap-line usage rising).
- Dollar up **+** US stocks resilient **+** US real yields rising **+** the *rest* of the world underperforming → **right of the smile (US boom).** Favor US assets over international; long the dollar against the world; respect that this regime can run for years, so add on dips rather than fading.
- Dollar *falling* **+** EM outperforming **+** commodities firm **+** US real yields low or falling → **bottom of the smile (muddle / reflation).** Green light for the risk trade: short the dollar, long EM, long commodities, long high-beta risk including crypto.

The single most expensive error is treating a rising dollar as one signal. It's two opposite signals, and the smile is how you tell them apart. Get the regime wrong and every downstream decision is backwards.

**Step 2 — Choose the instrument: DXY or a specific pair.** Once you have a direction, decide *how* to express it. Use the **DXY** (via futures or ETFs) when your view is a *broad* dollar move and you want liquidity and simplicity — but remember it's mostly a euro bet, so the DXY is the right vehicle when your thesis is really "dollar versus Europe and the developed world." Use a **specific pair** when your view is sharper or the DXY is blind to it: long **USD/JPY** or **USD/CHF** to express a US-rate-differential / carry view against a low-yield funder; long **USD/CNH** or **USD/MXN** or an EM basket to express a global-funding-stress view that the DXY can't see; **EUR/USD** when your view genuinely is about Europe. The discipline: match the instrument to the *channel* your thesis runs through. A funding-stress view expressed through the DXY (which has no EM in it) is a thesis-instrument mismatch that will fool you.

**Step 3 — Size for the carry-unwind tail, not the average day.** This is the step that keeps you alive, and it's where most traders fail. If your position has *any* carry component — long USD against a low-yield funder, long EM for the yield, anything short volatility — you are exposed to a gap-down unwind, and you must size assuming the unwind happens. Concretely: assume the funding currency can rally ~10% in days (USD/JPY did 161.9→145), assume volatility can triple, assume correlations across your carry pairs go to one in the crash, and assume there's no liquidity to exit *with* the crowd — you have to be out *before*. So your position size is set by "how much do I lose in the unwind and can I survive it," *not* by "how much carry do I earn on a calm day." A useful sanity check: if the carry-unwind loss would be more than a small fraction of your capital, you're too big, no matter how attractive the daily carry looks. The carry is the bait; the tail is the trade.

The matrix above is the whole playbook on one screen: each smile regime maps to a direction, an instrument, and a sizing/invalidation rule, with a dedicated carry-tail row at the bottom because the tail check applies in *every* regime. Read it left to right within a row and you have a complete trade: where you are (regime), which way (direction), through what (instrument), and how big plus what kills the thesis (sizing/invalidation).

**Step 4 — Respect the trend; never fade a strong dollar early.** The dollar moves in multi-year regimes driven by the *relative* US real-rate path, and those regimes trend hard — recall the +20% melt-up into September 2022. The graveyard of macro traders is full of people who called the dollar's top three times on the way up. Wait for the *turn* — a clean break of trend, a Fed now easing relative to the world, a real-rate differential that's narrowing, a peak in the US-outperformance story — before betting against a strong dollar. The turn, when it comes, is violent and tradable on the *other* side (the 114.8→103.5 give-back into year-end 2022 and the 2025 fade were both monster relief rallies in everything the dollar had crushed). But early is wrong, and "early" in a trending dollar is indistinguishable from "wrong" until the trend actually breaks.

**Step 5 — Build the one-screen dashboard and use the path, not the level.** Keep six things next to the DXY: (1) the DXY's level *and* its rate of change (a parabolic spike means something is breaking; a grind is a tradeable regime); (2) **EUR/USD and USD/JPY**, so you can instantly tell a euro story from a yen story from a true broad-dollar move; (3) a **dollar-vs-EM proxy** (USD/CNH and an EM-FX index) for the channel the DXY is blind to; (4) the **US 10-year real yield** (TIPS), the structural driver of the dollar's regime — and watch the *change in the expected rate path* around CPI, payrolls, and FOMC, because the path-change is what moves the currency, not the level; (5) the **funding-stress gauges** (the cross-currency basis, swap-line usage) that flag a left-smile squeeze before the headlines; (6) the **risk cross-checks** (S&P, high-yield spreads, VIX) that place you on the smile. A trader who glances at those knows in about ten seconds whether the dollar is the story today, *which kind* of dollar move it is, and whether to be leaning into risk or out of it — and that ten-second read conditions every other position on the book.

**Anchor on the one rule.** When the daily carry feels free and the dollar trend feels eternal and the headlines scream the dollar is finished — anchor on the discipline that survives every cycle: **the dollar smiles, carry coils, and the only sustainable way to trade either is to size for the unwind, not the average day.** Read the smile to get the direction, match the instrument to the channel, and let the carry-tail set the size. Do that and the dollar stops being the thing that blows up your book in a single bad week and becomes what it should be: the master variable you read first and position everything else around.
