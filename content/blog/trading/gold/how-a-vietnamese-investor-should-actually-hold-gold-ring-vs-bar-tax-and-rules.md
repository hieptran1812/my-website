>

In May 2024 a Vietnamese saver who walked into an SJC shop to buy one luong of gold — the standard 37.5-gram unit your grandmother would recognize — paid about 92.4 million VND for it. That same morning, the same shop would sell you 37.5 grams of a plain 9999 gold ring, identical metal, 99.99% pure, for roughly 78 million VND. The gold inside was the same. The difference — about 14.4 million VND, or \$566 at the exchange rate of the day — was the price of four stamped Vietnamese characters: **SJC**.

That gap is the single most expensive habit in Vietnamese household finance. Millions of families instinctively buy the gold bar with the famous brand on it, the one the evening news quotes, the one the shop displays behind glass. They are paying a brand premium on a metal whose entire purpose is to be a store of pure value — and worse, they are buying the one form of gold whose price is hostage to a government monopoly that, by 2024-25, the State Bank itself was openly preparing to dismantle.

This post is the practical capstone of the Vietnam track. We have already explained *why* the SJC premium exists ([the SJC premium decomposed](/blog/trading/gold/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price)) and *how* the monopoly was built ([Decree 24 and the SJC monopoly](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly)). Here we answer the only question a saver actually has at the counter: **given all that, what should I actually buy, and how do I hold it?** Ring or bar or jewelry; what purity; what spread; where to buy it safely; what the tax man wants; and what changed in the 2024-25 reforms. By the end you will be able to walk into a shop and not overpay.

## Foundations: the forms, the purity, and the units

Before any recommendation, you need three vocabularies straight: the **forms** gold comes in, the **purity** that decides how much metal you actually own, and the **units** Vietnam sells it in. None of this is complicated, but the whole "ring vs bar" decision is invisible until you have these clear.

### The three forms

A Vietnamese saver meets gold in three shapes:

1. **The SJC bar (vang mieng SJC).** A stamped, serial-numbered bar produced by the Saigon Jewelry Company, which since Decree 24 of 2012 has been the *only* legal gold-bar brand in the country. It comes in fixed sizes — 1 chi, 5 chi, 1 luong, and larger — sealed in a plastic-and-paper jacket with a serial number. It is 9999 pure gold. Its price is the one the news quotes every evening, and it is the form the government's anti-goldization policy is built around.

2. **The plain gold ring (nhan tron 9999, sometimes "nhan tron tron" or "vang nhan").** A simple, unembellished ring or small disc of 99.99% pure gold, sold by weight. "Tron" means *round/plain* — the point is that there is no design, no gemstone, no craftsmanship to pay for. It is investment gold that happens to be ring-shaped so it is easy to handle and store. Crucially, **it is not a "bar," so it is not the SJC monopoly form** — which is exactly why its premium is small.

3. **Gold jewelry (vang trang suc / nu trang).** Rings, necklaces, bracelets made to be worn. Usually alloyed below pure gold (18K, 14K) for hardness, and always carrying a **making charge (tien cong)** — the labor and design cost — on top of the metal. This is the worst store of value, for reasons we will quantify.

The single most important fact in this entire post: **the metal inside a 9999 ring and the metal inside an SJC bar is the same.** Both are 99.99% pure gold. The bar costs more not because it is purer or better gold, but because it carries a brand the state made artificially scarce. You are not buying better gold; you are buying a more expensive sticker.

### Purity: 9999 means 24K means pure

"Purity" (or *fineness*) is the fraction of a piece that is actually gold, the rest being harder alloy metals like copper or silver. Two scales describe the same thing:

- **The four-nines scale (9999).** "9999" means 99.99% pure — the investment standard. "999" is 99.9%. This is what you want for storing value.
- **The karat scale (K).** Pure gold is **24 karats (24K)**. Each karat is 1/24th. So 24K = 100% (in practice 99.9-99.99%), **18K = 18/24 = 75% gold**, **14K = 14/24 ≈ 58.3% gold**, **10K ≈ 41.7%**.

Both SJC bars and reputable 9999 rings are 24K / four-nines pure. Jewelry is usually 18K or 14K — meaning a quarter to over 40% of what you are holding is not gold at all. If a "gold" necklace is 18K, then for every 100 grams you own only 75 grams of gold; the other 25 grams is cheap alloy you paid a gold-ish price for and will recover almost nothing on at resale.

#### Worked example: what 18K jewelry actually costs you

Suppose you buy an 18K gold necklace weighing 10 chi (37.5 grams) and the shop charges you as if it were worth 7 million VND per chi, a total of **70 million VND (\$2,750 at 25,450 VND/\$)**. How much *gold* did you buy? At 75% purity, 37.5 grams of 18K contains 28.1 grams of gold. The world-price value of that gold (at, say, 78 million VND per luong of pure metal) is 78 × (28.1/37.5) ≈ **58.4 million VND (\$2,295)**. You paid 70 million for 58.4 million of metal — an **11.6 million VND (\$456) gap**, and that is *before* the making charge that is also baked into the price. When you sell, the shop pays you for the gold content only, minus its own margin. The intuition: with jewelry you pay a gold price for an alloy, and the alloy is worth almost nothing back to you.

### The units: chi, luong, gram, troy ounce

Vietnam does not price gold in grams or ounces in daily speech. It uses traditional units, and the headline quote is always *per luong*:

- **1 luong** (also called **cay** or, in English, a **tael**) **= 37.5 grams = 10 chi.** This is the quote unit: when the news says "gold is 92 million," it means 92 million VND per luong.
- **1 chi = 3.75 grams = 0.1 luong.** This is the practical small unit — a modest ring is bought and sold in chi.
- **1 phan = 0.375 grams = 0.1 chi.** The smallest unit you will see.
- For the bridge to world prices: **1 luong = 1.20565 troy ounces**, and the world price is quoted in US dollars **per troy ounce** (1 troy oz = 31.103 grams).

That last conversion is the whole reason this series keeps anchoring VND prices in dollars. The world gold price lives in \$/troy oz; the Vietnamese price lives in VND/luong; the bridge is `VND per luong = (world \$/oz) × 1.20565 × (VND per \$) + premium`. Every time you compare an SJC bar to a ring to the world price, you are walking across that bridge.

![Units ladder from phan to chi to luong to troy ounce alongside purity tiers from 9999 to 18K to 14K](/imgs/blogs/how-a-vietnamese-investor-should-actually-hold-gold-ring-vs-bar-tax-and-rules-3.png)

Keep this figure as a reference. The left column is **weight** — what you pay for; the right column is **purity** — what you actually own. The trap is to confuse them: a heavy 18K necklace can weigh more than a light 9999 ring while containing less gold.

### Premium and spread: define them from zero

Two more words decide whether you are getting a fair deal.

**Premium** is how much *more* than the world-equivalent price you pay. If 37.5 grams of pure gold is worth 78 million VND at the world price (converted into VND) and a shop sells you an SJC bar for 92 million, the premium is 14 million VND, or about 18%. The premium is the brand-plus-policy tax. For an SJC bar it has been large and volatile; for a plain ring it is small and stable. We will see exactly how large in the next two sections.

**Bid-ask spread** (in Vietnamese, the gap between *gia mua* — the buy price the shop pays you — and *gia ban* — the sell price you pay the shop) is the shop's cut on a round trip. Buy at the sell price, immediately sell at the buy price, and the spread is your instant loss. A wide spread means gold has to rise a lot just to break even.

These two are different costs and you pay both. The premium is paid *once* relative to the world price; the spread is paid *every round trip*. The genius of the plain ring is that it tends to charge you less of **both**.

### How to read a Vietnamese gold quote

When you look at a dealer board or a price app, you will see two numbers side by side for each product — for example "SJC 188.3 / 190.3." The first, lower number is **gia mua** (the buy price — what the shop pays *you* if you sell to them). The second, higher number is **gia ban** (the sell price — what *you* pay the shop to buy). The gap between them is the spread. Both numbers are quoted in million VND per luong, and they move through the day with the world price and the dong.

A few habits make you a sharper reader of that board. First, **always compare the right products.** The SJC-bar line and the 9999-ring line are quoted separately; do not compare an SJC sell price to a ring buy price and conclude the ring is "cheaper" — compare like to like. Second, **watch the spread, not just the level.** A dealer can quote an attractive sell price and quietly widen the buy price, so the round trip is expensive even though the headline looks fair; the spread is where shops hide their margin in volatile periods. Third, **convert to the world price in your head.** If a luong of pure gold is worth about `world \$/oz × 1.20565 × VND-per-\$` and the SJC bar is quoted well above that, the excess is the premium you are about to pay. A saver who can do that rough sum at the counter is a saver who never overpays by surprise.

### Where the units came from, and why they still matter

The luong, chi, and phan are not arbitrary — they descend from the old East Asian weight system (the Chinese *tael* and *mace*), carried into Vietnam over centuries of trade. That heritage is why gold here is sold by a 37.5-gram luong rather than a 31.1-gram troy ounce, and why a saver thinks in chi rather than grams. It matters for one very practical reason: **the unit mismatch is where conversion errors and small rip-offs hide.** The world price is per troy ounce; the Vietnamese price is per luong; and 1 luong is 1.20565 troy ounces, about 20% heavier than an ounce. A saver who forgets that — who mentally compares a "per ounce" world price to a "per luong" Vietnamese price as if they were the same weight — will misjudge the premium by a fifth. Keeping the units straight is not pedantry; it is the difference between knowing what you paid and guessing.

## The SJC bar: a brand premium and a policy risk

Let us be fair to the SJC bar first, because for decades it was the obvious choice. It is the most *recognized* gold in Vietnam. Every shop, every bank counter, every news ticker quotes the SJC bar price. It is serial-numbered and sealed, so its authenticity is easy to verify. For a large saver who wants a single, instantly-recognized, instantly-resellable lump of value, the SJC bar has a real convenience.

But that recognition is exactly what you overpay for, and it sits on top of a fragile policy.

### Where the premium comes from

The SJC premium is not a market accident; it is engineered. We decomposed it fully in [the SJC-premium post](/blog/trading/gold/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price), but the short version: Decree 24 of 2012 banned all private gold-bar production and made SJC the sole brand, while the State Bank tightly controlled gold imports. Demand for *the brand* kept rising; the legal supply of the brand was frozen. Scarcity plus monopoly equals premium. On top of that sits the FX cost of getting gold into the country and the simple fact that, in a panic, Vietnamese savers reach for the most famous gold first.

![SJC bar premium over the world price in million VND per luong from 2023 to 2026](/imgs/blogs/how-a-vietnamese-investor-should-actually-hold-gold-ring-vs-bar-tax-and-rules-2.png)

Look at the size of it. Through 2023 the SJC premium sat around 12 million VND per luong. In the panic of early-to-mid 2024 it blew out to roughly 18-18.5 million VND — about **a quarter of the metal's value, paid for the sticker.** This is the bar's defining flaw: the premium is not only large, it is *unstable*. You can buy at a 25% premium and watch it collapse to single digits, losing money on the brand even as the world gold price holds.

#### Worked example: the brand premium on one luong of SJC

At the May-2024 peak the SJC bar sold for about **92.4 million VND** per luong. The world-equivalent value of that 37.5 grams of pure gold — world price about \$2,350/oz × 1.20565 oz × 25,450 VND/\$ — was roughly **72-74 million VND**. So the premium was on the order of **18.5 million VND (\$727)** on a single luong. Convert the whole thing to dollars for a clean comparison: the bar cost **\$3,631**; the metal inside was worth about **\$2,900**; you paid roughly **\$727** — a fifth of your money — for the SJC stamp. The intuition: at a blow-out premium, a quarter of what you hand over buys you no extra gold at all, only a brand.

### The policy risk nobody prices

Here is the part savers routinely miss. The SJC bar is not just expensive — it is **the specific instrument the government's gold policy targets.** Decree 24 created the monopoly precisely so the state could manage it. By 2024 the State Bank was running direct-sale interventions through four state banks and SJC to *crush* the premium ([the 2024 intervention](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly)), pulling it from ~18M down toward 6M within months. And through 2024-25 the policy direction became explicit: **end the SJC monopoly**, allow other licensed producers and more import quota, and let the premium converge toward the world price.

For the metal in your hand that is good news. But for the *brand premium you already paid*, it is a slow bleed. If you bought an SJC bar at a 20% premium and the reform succeeds in collapsing the premium to 3-5%, you lose that gap relative to someone who held plain rings the whole time — even if world gold went straight up. **You took policy risk on a monopoly that the policymaker had announced it would dismantle.** That is the opposite of what a store of value is supposed to do.

To see why this is a genuine loss and not just an accounting curiosity, separate the two things you bought when you bought an SJC bar. You bought **the metal** — 37.5 grams of 9999 gold, whose value tracks the world price and the dong. And you bought **the premium** — the extra VND you paid because the brand was scarce. The metal's value is yours to keep; it rises and falls with the world. The premium's value depends entirely on the brand *staying* scarce. The moment policy makes SJC bars less scarce — by licensing competitors, by importing more, by direct State Bank sales — the premium deflates, and that deflation comes straight out of *your* purchase price. A ring holder never owned that fragile second component, so they have nothing to lose when it evaporates. This is the cleanest way to understand the whole post: the ring buyer owns only the durable thing (metal); the bar buyer owns the durable thing *plus* a perishable thing (a policy-dependent premium) they did not need.

### Who the SJC bar is actually right for

Fairness demands a clear answer to "is the bar ever the right choice?" Yes, narrowly. The SJC bar makes sense when **instant, no-questions resale recognition of a single large lump** is worth more to you than the premium — for example, a large holder who may need to liquidate a big position fast, in a hurry, at any counter in the country, with zero haggling over authenticity. The sealed serial-numbered bar is the most frictionless large unit to resell, and for someone moving 50 luong that frictionlessness can matter. It can also make sense for a *small* slice of a large, diversified holding purely for that liquidity optionality. What does *not* make sense is the default behavior — a small or mid saver reflexively buying SJC bars because they are "the official gold" — because that saver pays the maximum brand premium and policy risk to solve a liquidity problem they do not have. The bar is a specialist tool, not the everyday store of value most Vietnamese families need.

## The gold ring: same metal, smaller premium, fully divisible

Now the case for the plain 9999 ring — which, for most savers, is simply better.

### It tracks the world price

Because nhan tron is *not* the SJC monopoly form, its supply was never frozen the way SJC bars were. Reputable jewelers (PNJ, DOJI, SJC's own ring products, Bao Tin Minh Chau, Phu Quy, and others) produce and sell 9999 rings freely. That means the ring's price is set much closer to the actual cost of the metal plus a normal craft-and-margin markup. Its premium runs a few percent — and even when the SJC-bar premium blew out to 18-25% in 2024, ring premiums stayed in low single digits. The ring is the form that lets a Vietnamese saver own gold at close to the world price.

![SJC bar sell price in million VND per luong from 2020 to 2026 with the 2024 peak intervention floor and 2026 record](/imgs/blogs/how-a-vietnamese-investor-should-actually-hold-gold-ring-vs-bar-tax-and-rules-4.png)

This is the price the premium rides on. The SJC bar ran from ~56 million VND per luong in 2020 to a 92.4M peak in May 2024, was forced back to a ~77M floor by State Bank direct sales, then surged with the world price to a ~190M all-time high in January 2026. The metal's journey is the steep line. The *premium* is the extra distance the SJC bar sits above where a ring would sit — and the ring saver captures the same metal move without that extra, wobbling distance.

#### Worked example: one luong of rings vs one SJC bar

Go back to the May-2024 morning. An SJC bar: **92.4 million VND = \$3,631**. One luong of plain 9999 rings that same day: about **78 million VND = \$3,065** (a low single-digit premium over the ~74M world-equivalent). Same 37.5 grams, same 99.99% purity. The ring buyer saved **14.4 million VND = \$566** up front — about 16% — and, just as important, did not buy the brand the State Bank was about to deflate. A year later, with the world price far higher, both holdings rose with the metal, but the ring buyer never paid the brand tax in the first place. The intuition: for the same gold, the ring buyer simply starts richer by the size of the premium they refused to pay.

### It is divisible and liquid for ordinary amounts

A working family does not save in 92-million-VND lumps. They save the way they earn — a chi at Tet, a couple of chi after a good month, half a luong before a wedding. The ring is built for that. You can buy gold rings in 1-chi (3.75 g), 2-chi, 5-chi increments and build up a luong over time, then sell exactly as much as you need for a hospital bill or a deposit without breaking a large bar.

An SJC bar is coarse by comparison. The smallest convenient bar is 1 chi but the *recognized, liquid* unit is the 1-luong bar; selling part of your savings means selling a whole bar. Divisibility is not a luxury — it is the difference between gold that fits a household's actual cash needs and gold that forces you to liquidate in big, lumpy steps.

#### Worked example: a 5-chi ring purchase (half a luong)

A saver wants to put aside half a luong — **5 chi = 18.75 grams**. In early 2026, with the world price near its record and a ring premium of a few percent, half a luong of rings runs about **91 million VND = \$3,460** (the ~182M-per-luong ring level vs the ~190M SJC bar). She pays in one visit, walks out with five small 9999 rings, and three months later — when she needs cash for tuition — sells exactly **2 chi** back, keeping the other 3. Try that with a single 1-luong SJC bar: she would have to sell the whole bar and re-buy. The intuition: the ring lets you save and spend gold in the same small denominations you live your financial life in.

### The resale reality: how rings are verified

The one honest objection to rings is verification. A sealed SJC bar carries a serial number and a tamper-evident jacket, so a buyer trusts it on sight. A ring is loose metal, so how does a shop know it is really 9999? The answer is that **reputable rings come with the same trust infrastructure, just in a different shape.** A 9999 ring from PNJ, DOJI, Bao Tin Minh Chau, Phu Quy, or SJC's own ring line is sold with a dealer invoice stating the weight and the 9999 purity, often a small certificate or branded packet, and the dealer's own stamp. When you bring it back, that dealer pays its best buyback price for its own branded gold with paperwork — the same fast, no-assay transaction an SJC bar gets. The friction only appears if you try to sell *unbranded, no-invoice* ring gold to a shop that did not make it; then it gets weighed, assayed, and discounted. The lesson is not "rings are hard to sell" — it is "**buy branded rings with invoices, and sell them back through that brand's counters.**" Do that and the ring's resale is just as smooth as the bar's, at a smaller spread.

This is also why the SJC bar's last remaining advantage is shrinking. Brand recognition at resale used to be a real SJC edge, because for years SJC was the *only* serial-numbered, instantly-trusted form. But the major jewelers now stamp, brand, and document their 9999 rings, and as the post-Decree-24 reforms license more producers and standardize ring products, the trust gap between "a sealed SJC bar" and "a branded PNJ 9999 ring" keeps narrowing. The bar is racing to keep an advantage that policy is deliberately eroding.

### Why the ring usually wins

Put the two together. The ring holds the same pure metal, at a premium a fraction of the bar's, in denominations a household can actually use, and it sits *outside* the monopoly the State Bank is reforming. The only edge the SJC bar retains is brand recognition at resale — and as more licensed producers stamp and serial-number their rings, even that edge is shrinking. For the overwhelming majority of Vietnamese savers, the plain ring is simply the better store of value.

There is a deeper point hiding in all this. The reason the ring tracks the world price and the bar does not is that the ring is *closer to being just metal*, while the bar is *metal wrapped in a policy*. The whole Vietnam track of this series has been about how policy — Decree 24, the import controls, the monopoly, the interventions — drives a wedge between what gold is worth in the world and what a Vietnamese saver pays for it. The plain ring is the form that minimizes that wedge. When you choose a ring over a bar, you are not picking a cheaper product; you are choosing to own gold as *gold* rather than as a bet on Vietnamese gold policy. For an asset whose entire job is to be neutral, durable, apolitical value, that is exactly the right choice.

## Jewelry: the worst store of value

It feels like saving. A gold necklace is gold, it is beautiful, and grandmother gave it as a wedding gift. But as an *investment*, jewelry is a trap, for two compounding reasons.

First, **purity.** As we saw, most jewelry is 18K or 14K — 75% or 58% gold. You are holding a quarter to nearly half non-gold alloy that you paid a gold price for and will recover almost nothing on.

Second, and worse, **the making charge (tien cong).** Jewelry's price includes the labor and design — the craftsmanship that makes it wearable and pretty. That charge runs **10-30%** of the price and it is **non-recoverable**: when you sell, the shop pays you for the gold content at the buyback price and pays you *nothing* for the labor. The artistry that made it valuable to wear makes it worthless to resell.

#### Worked example: the round trip on a jewelry piece

Buy an 18K gold bracelet, 5 chi (18.75 g), priced at **45 million VND = \$1,768**. Of that, the gold content (75% of 18.75 g = 14.1 g) is worth maybe 29 million VND; the rest is alloy markup plus a making charge of, say, 6 million VND. Sell it the next week: the shop weighs it, assays the 14.1 grams of actual gold, and pays you the *buyback* price for 18K scrap — perhaps **27-28 million VND = \$1,080**. You lost roughly **17 million VND (\$680), about 38%**, in a week with no move in the gold price. The intuition: jewelry is a consumer good wearing a gold costume; buy it to wear, never to store wealth.

The rule is simple and absolute: **wear jewelry, do not save in it.** If you want gold as savings, you want bars or — usually better — rings.

## Where and how to buy: the invoice is the whole game

Owning gold safely in Vietnam comes down to one discipline: **buy from a licensed dealer and keep the dated invoice.** Everything else follows from that.

![Buying flow from a licensed dealer through the dated invoice to holding and resale](/imgs/blogs/how-a-vietnamese-investor-should-actually-hold-gold-ring-vs-bar-tax-and-rules-5.png)

Walk the flow left to right. You buy from a **licensed dealer** — SJC, PNJ, DOJI, Bao Tin Minh Chau, Phu Quy, or a licensed bank counter — never from an unlicensed shop or a stranger, where fakes and tungsten-cored bars circulate. For an SJC bar you show **ID**, and under the 2024-25 rules larger purchases are logged. You **pay the price plus the premium** (small for a ring, large for a bar). You get a **dated invoice (hoa don)** listing weight, purity (9999), and — for an SJC bar — the serial number. You **hold** it at home or in a bank safe-deposit box. And when you sell, the invoice plus the matching brand gets you the **top buyback price** with the least haggling.

Why the invoice matters so much:

- **It proves authenticity and provenance.** A sealed SJC bar with a matching invoice and serial resells fastest. A ring with a reputable dealer's invoice stating 9999 and the weight is trusted at any of that dealer's counters.
- **It is your protection on resale spread.** Dealers pay their best buyback price for gold they sold, with paperwork. Gold with no invoice gets re-assayed, discounted, and sometimes refused.
- **It is increasingly required.** The 2024-25 reforms pushed transactions onto invoices and bank channels (more below). Cash-and-no-paper gold buying is being squeezed out.

Three practical rules: **(1)** buy 9999 for savings (rings or bars), never alloyed jewelry; **(2)** keep every invoice in the same place you keep the gold's documentation, because mismatched or missing paper costs you real money at resale; **(3)** for storage, a bank safe-deposit box costs a modest annual fee and removes home-theft risk — for a large holding it is worth it, though note it reintroduces a mild version of the counterparty/access question we covered in [allocated vs unallocated and counterparty risk](/blog/trading/gold/physical-gold-bars-coins-allocated-vs-unallocated-and-counterparty-risk) (a box is *your* metal, but access depends on the bank being open).

### Avoiding fakes and the assay process

Gold's value density makes it a target for fraud, and Vietnam has seen its share — gilded tungsten bars (tungsten is almost exactly as dense as gold, which is why it is the classic counterfeit core), under-karat "9999" that is really 99.0% or alloyed, and short-weight pieces. The defenses are mostly about *where* you buy, not clever home tests:

- **Buy only from licensed dealers and bank counters.** The single biggest protection. SJC, PNJ, DOJI, Bao Tin Minh Chau, Phu Quy and the like stake their brand on every piece; they have no incentive to sell you a fake and every incentive to authenticate what they buy back. The cheap "gold" from an unlicensed shop or a private seller is where the tungsten and the under-karat metal live.
- **Insist on the invoice, weight, and purity statement** — and for a bar, the intact serial-numbered jacket. A broken seal on an SJC bar means it must be re-assayed before anyone trusts it again.
- **Understand what a shop does when you sell.** A reputable dealer **weighs** the piece on a calibrated scale (to the phan) and, for unbranded gold, **assays** it — historically by acid testing on a touchstone, increasingly by non-destructive X-ray fluorescence (XRF) that reads the metal composition in seconds. You are then paid for `grams × purity × buyback price`. For its own branded gold with an invoice, a dealer usually skips the assay and pays the standard buyback immediately, which is exactly why provenance saves you money and time.

The practical upshot is comforting: **a saver who only ever buys branded 9999 from licensed dealers, with invoices, and sells it back through the same channels essentially never touches the fake-gold problem.** Fraud is a risk of the gray market and the bargain that seems too cheap — not of the disciplined ring buyer.

### Storage: home vs the bank box

Once you own the metal you have to keep it somewhere, and the choice is the classic store-of-value tradeoff in miniature. **Home storage** is free, instantly accessible, and private — but it carries theft and loss risk, and a large pile of gold at home is a meaningful security exposure. **A bank safe-deposit box** costs a modest annual fee, removes the home-theft risk, and keeps your holding discreet — but it reintroduces a soft counterparty/access dependency: your gold is genuinely *yours* (a box is allocated, specific metal, unlike a bank deposit, which is a claim), yet you can only reach it when the bank is open and operating. For a few chi of rings, home storage is fine. For a large holding, the box is usually worth the fee. This is the same allocated-vs-claim distinction from the global [counterparty-risk post](/blog/trading/gold/physical-gold-bars-coins-allocated-vs-unallocated-and-counterparty-risk), just applied to a Vietnamese household's safe.

## Tax and reporting: the rules and the 2024-25 changes

Here is the reassuring part for an ordinary saver, followed by the part you should actually watch.

**For a typical household, buying and selling physical gold has not been a directly taxed personal event.** Vietnam has not levied a personal capital-gains tax on a family selling its gold rings or bars the way some countries tax investment gains. The dealer's spread is your main cost. So a saver accumulating a few luong of rings over the years and selling some for a wedding or a house deposit has historically faced **fees and spread, not a gold income tax**.

What you actually pay and watch:

- **The bid-ask spread**, every round trip — the real, recurring cost (quantified below).
- **The premium**, paid once relative to the world price — large for SJC bars, small for rings.
- **Safe-deposit-box fees**, if you store at a bank — a modest annual amount.
- **Reporting and source-of-funds rules.** This is the live area. Large cash gold transactions are increasingly logged; banks and dealers must report sizable transactions under anti-money-laundering rules, and the 2024-25 reforms pushed gold buying toward **invoices, ID, and bank-transfer payment** rather than anonymous cash. For an honest saver this is paperwork, not tax — but it means "buy a bar with a bag of cash and no name" is going away.

### The 2024-25 rule changes — what actually shifted

This is the most important regulatory window in a decade for a Vietnamese gold saver, and it cuts in the ring's favor:

1. **State Bank direct-sale intervention (2024).** To crush the blown-out SJC premium, the SBV sold gold directly through four state-owned banks and SJC, dragging the premium from ~18M down toward 6M VND per luong within months ([SBV gold policy and the 2024 intervention](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly)). The signal: the state will not let the SJC brand premium run wild — and will spend reserves against it.

2. **The move to end the SJC monopoly (2024-25).** The policy direction shifted decisively toward amending Decree 24 to **allow other licensed gold-bar producers and more import quota.** As the monopoly opens, the SJC-specific premium is expected to converge toward the world price. The whole point of buying the SJC brand — its scarcity — is being legislated away.

3. **Tighter transaction rules.** Invoicing, ID, electronic payment for larger purchases, and AML reporting all tightened. The gray, cash-only corner of the market is shrinking.

For a saver, the through-line is clear: **the reforms reduce the value of holding the SJC brand specifically, and reduce the friction of holding plain 9999 gold.** They are, almost by design, an argument for the ring.

### Vietnam in international context

It helps to see how light Vietnam's gold-tax regime is by comparison, because it shapes how Vietnamese savers behave. In many Western countries physical gold is taxed as a *collectible* or an investment asset: the United States taxes long-term gains on physical gold at a 28% collectibles rate; several European countries apply capital-gains tax on gold sold within a holding period, and some apply VAT on certain forms (investment gold is VAT-exempt in the EU, but coins and jewelry rules vary). Against that backdrop, the Vietnamese household — facing **no direct personal capital-gains tax on selling its gold** — pays remarkably little in tax to hold the metal. The costs that *do* bite a Vietnamese saver are the **premium** and the **spread**, not the tax authority. That is precisely why minimizing the premium (choose rings) and the spread (buy branded, sell branded) is the whole game here: in a market with no gains tax, the brand premium and the dealer's cut *are* your tax, and you have direct control over both.

### What the reporting rules actually require of you

"Reporting" sounds ominous; for an honest saver it is mundane. Under anti-money-laundering (AML) rules, dealers and banks must report transactions above certain thresholds and keep records — name, ID, amount. The 2024-25 reforms reinforced this by pushing larger purchases toward **ID, invoices, and electronic (bank-transfer) payment** instead of anonymous cash. None of this taxes you. What it does is end the era of buying a stack of bars with a bag of cash and no name attached. For a saver with legitimately-earned money, the practical effect is simply: be ready to show ID and pay by transfer for sizable purchases, and keep your invoices. The reporting regime is a friction on the *gray market and money-laundering*, not on a family saving in gold.

#### Worked example: the all-in cost of saving in rings for a year

Tie the costs together. A mid saver buys 5 luong of 9999 rings in early 2026 at, say, 182 million VND per luong — **910 million VND = \$34,600** (at 26,300 VND/\$). The premium baked in is a few percent, perhaps 3 million VND per luong = 15 million VND total (\$570) — versus the ~40 million VND (\$1,520) premium they would have paid buying SJC bars at the same time. There is **no capital-gains tax** to set aside. If she stores in a bank box, the annual fee is small, call it 1-2 million VND. And the spread is a cost only when she sells. So her all-in cost of *holding* gold for the year is essentially the small premium she paid once plus a tiny storage fee — a fraction of what the bar buyer paid in premium alone. The intuition: in Vietnam's gold market the saver's real "tax" is the premium and the spread, and the ring saver pays the lowest version of both.

## A practical playbook by saver size

What you should actually buy depends on how much you are saving. The principle is constant — minimize the premium and the spread, stay divisible, avoid jewelry — but the optimal mix shifts with size.

![Decision flow recommending gold forms by saver size from small to large with a shared avoid box](/imgs/blogs/how-a-vietnamese-investor-should-actually-hold-gold-ring-vs-bar-tax-and-rules-6.png)

Follow the branches:

- **Small saver (under ~2 luong, under ~155 million VND).** Buy **9999 rings in chi and luong.** This is the great majority of Vietnamese savers, and for them the ring is simply correct: low premium, divisible to a single chi, liquid at any reputable counter. Do not touch SJC bars; the brand premium is pure deadweight at this size.

- **Mid saver (~2-10 luong, ~155-780 million VND).** **Mostly rings, with perhaps a few SJC bars** if you specifically value the instant, no-questions resale recognition of a sealed bar for part of the holding. Keep the invoices. The bulk should still be rings to keep the average premium down and stay divisible.

- **Large saver (over ~10 luong, over ~780 million VND).** Now operational risk matters more than the premium. **Spread across dealers** so you are not exposed to one counterparty, use a **bank safe-deposit box** for storage, and consider holding part of your hard-currency reserve in **USD deposits** rather than all in gold — diversification across the gold/USD/VND triangle we covered in [the household allocation post](/blog/trading/gold/gold-vs-the-dong-real-estate-and-savings-the-vietnamese-allocation). A large holder can absorb a few SJC bars for liquidity, but rings should still anchor the position.

- **Everyone, every size: AVOID** the two destroyers of value — **jewelry as savings** (the making charge) and **chasing the SJC-bar premium** when it has blown out. If the SJC premium is 18M and the ring premium is 2M, buying the bar is setting 16M VND on fire for a sticker.

### A concrete accumulation plan

The playbook above is a snapshot; saving in gold is a habit. Here is how an ordinary household actually builds a gold position over years without overpaying:

1. **Buy in chi, regularly, in rings.** Rather than waiting to amass 92 million VND for a luong, buy a chi or two whenever you have surplus — after Tet, after a bonus, after a good harvest. Buying small and often is a form of cost-averaging: you buy some gold cheap and some dear, and your average price smooths out the market's swings. The ring's divisibility is what makes this possible.
2. **Pick one or two reputable brands and stick with them.** Concentrate your buying at, say, PNJ and SJC's ring line. Holding mostly one brand's documented 9999 rings means your eventual resale is fast and at that brand's best buyback price, with no assay friction.
3. **Mind the premium when you buy, the spread when you sell.** Do not buy gold the week the SJC premium has blown out in a panic — that is the most expensive moment to enter. And when you sell, sell back to a counter that recognizes your brand and invoice, to capture the tightest spread.
4. **Graduate to bars and a bank box only as size demands it.** A household crossing into large-holder territory can add a few SJC bars for liquidity and move storage to a bank safe-deposit box. But the core of the holding — the part that is pure store of value — should stay in branded rings.
5. **Keep the gold's job in mind.** This is insurance against the dong losing purchasing power, not a get-rich trade. Size it as the part of your savings you want to be currency-proof, rebalance occasionally against your other savings, and do not let a record-high headline lure you into overpaying the premium for the privilege of buying at the top.

Follow that and a Vietnamese family ends up, a decade later, holding several luong of pure gold bought at close to the world price, fully divisible, fully documented, and entirely outside the monopoly's policy risk — which is precisely what a store of value is supposed to be.

#### Worked example: the round-trip spread on a ring vs an SJC bar

Spread is the cost you pay *every* time you trade, so it compounds for an active saver. Take one luong in early 2026. An SJC bar might quote a sell price of 190.3M and a buy-back price of about 188.3M — a **~2 million VND (\$76) spread** per luong, sometimes much wider in stress. A plain 9999 ring might quote a sell price of 182M and a buy-back of 180.7M — a **~1.3 million VND (\$50) spread**. The ring not only starts ~8M cheaper (the premium it never charged), it also costs less to round-trip. Over a saver who buys and sells several times across a decade, that spread difference alone — \$50 vs \$76 each round trip — quietly adds up to real money. The intuition: the ring is cheaper to *own* and cheaper to *trade*, on the same metal.

## Common misconceptions

Three myths drive Vietnamese savers into the wrong gold. Each is wrong, and each can be corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "SJC bars are purer or better gold than rings."** False. Both are 9999 — 99.99% pure, 24K. The metal is identical. The SJC bar costs more because of its monopoly brand, not its quality. At the 2024 peak you paid ~14.4 million VND more per luong for *the same purity* — \$566 for nothing extra in the metal.

**Myth 2: "Jewelry is an investment because it's gold."** False, and expensively so. Jewelry is usually 18K (75%) or 14K (58%) and carries a 10-30% making charge you never recover. Our worked example lost ~38% on a one-week round trip with no move in the gold price. Wear it; do not save in it.

**Myth 3: "Bigger bars are always cheaper per gram, so buy the biggest you can afford."** Mostly false in Vietnam. The relevant cost is the *premium plus spread*, and the SJC 1-luong bar — the most recognized size — has historically carried the *largest, most volatile* premium, precisely because it is the headline monopoly unit. A big SJC bar is not a bulk discount; it is the maximum dose of the brand premium and the policy risk. The cheaper-per-gram intuition from buying rice in bulk does not transfer.

A fourth, quieter myth deserves a line: **"the SJC bar is safest because it's the official one."** The opposite is closer to true for a *store of value*. The SJC bar is the one form whose premium the government actively manages and whose monopoly the government has announced it will dismantle. Holding the official brand means holding the policy risk.

Why do these myths persist so stubbornly? Because the SJC bar is the form Vietnamese savers *see* — it is the price on the evening news, the gold behind the glass at the famous shops, the unit older relatives bought and recommend. Familiarity reads as safety. A saver buys the bar for the same reason people buy the most-advertised brand on the shelf: it feels like the default, the responsible choice, the one nobody gets criticized for picking. The whole point of this post is to break that reflex with arithmetic. The bar is not safer; it is more expensive and more policy-exposed. The plain ring is the quieter, smarter default — it just does not have a nightly news ticker telling you so.

## How it shows up in real markets

These are not abstractions. The Vietnamese gold market has spent 2024-26 demonstrating every point above with real prices and real dates.

**The May-2024 premium blow-out.** As global gold surged and Vietnamese savers piled into the most famous form, the SJC-bar premium tore out to roughly 18-18.5 million VND per luong — about 25% over the world price — while the bar itself hit 92.4M (\$3,631). Ring buyers that same week paid low-single-digit premiums. Anyone who bought SJC bars at that peak premium overpaid by hundreds of dollars per luong for the brand, on top of the metal.

**The SBV intervention.** Within months the State Bank's direct-sale program through four state banks and SJC dragged the bar back toward a ~77M floor and the premium down toward 6M. The lesson for savers was stark: **the SJC premium is policy-dependent and can be deliberately destroyed.** A ring's small premium had no such cliff.

![SJC bar premium as a percent of the world price from 2023 to 2026 with ring premiums noted as running well below](/imgs/blogs/how-a-vietnamese-investor-should-actually-hold-gold-ring-vs-bar-tax-and-rules-7.png)

The percentage view tells the whole arc. The SJC-bar premium ran ~18% in 2023, spiked to ~24-25% in early-mid 2024, then collapsed under intervention and the reform direction to single digits — ~8% in late 2024, ~5% in 2025, ~4% by early 2026. A plain 9999 ring's premium ran *well below* this line the entire time, in the low single digits. Read the chart as a story about the *brand*, not the metal: the wild swings are the price of the SJC sticker, and the reform program is squeezing that sticker toward zero.

**The 2025-26 record run, in rings vs bars.** As world gold rocketed to its January-2026 all-time high and the SJC bar printed ~190M VND per luong, the saver who had been quietly accumulating 9999 rings captured essentially the same metal move — because gold is gold — but did it without ever paying the 2024 brand premium and with smaller round-trip spreads along the way. Two savers, same gold price exposure; the ring saver simply kept more of the gain.

**A tale of two savers, 2024 to 2026.** Make it concrete with two families who each set aside the same money in early 2024. Family A bought one SJC bar at the May-2024 peak: 92.4 million VND, of which roughly 18 million was premium. Family B bought one luong of 9999 rings the same week: about 78 million VND, premium just a couple of million. By January 2026 the world price had soared; the SJC bar quoted ~190 million and the equivalent rings ~182 million. Both families' gold rose enormously with the metal — gold did its job. But Family A had paid ~16 million VND more at the start for the brand, and as the reform program ground the SJC premium from ~25% down toward ~4%, that brand premium partly bled away even as the metal climbed. Family B captured the same metal gain, paid almost nothing for a sticker, and carried no policy risk on a monopoly being dismantled. Same gold, same era, same direction — and Family B simply kept more. That is the post in one comparison.

The pattern repeats because the structure is fixed: the metal moves with the world price and the dong; the *premium* moves with monopoly policy. The ring lets you own the first and skip the second.

## The takeaway

Step back to the spine of this whole series: **gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability.** A Vietnamese saver buys gold for exactly that reason — distrust of the dong's purchasing power over decades, a hedge against devaluation and crisis. If that is the job, then the *form* you hold it in should do that job as cheaply and cleanly as possible.

The SJC bar fails that test in a specific, fixable way. It does insure you against currency debasement — but it loads on top a second, unwanted exposure: a bet on a government monopoly's brand premium, a premium that is large, volatile, and politically targeted for demolition. You wanted insurance against the dong; you also bought a side wager on Decree 24. That is not what a store of value is for.

The plain 9999 ring strips the wager away. Same metal, same insurance against the dong, at a premium a fraction of the bar's, in denominations a household actually uses, sitting outside the monopoly the State Bank is reforming. For the overwhelming majority of Vietnamese savers — anyone not specifically paying for instant brand-recognition on a large lump — **the ring is the better way to hold the same gold.**

Notice that this conclusion is not a Vietnamese quirk; it is the universal logic of physical gold, applied to a particular market. Everywhere in the world, the rule for owning the metal is the same — minimize the premium over spot, minimize the round-trip spread, hold a recognized form with clean provenance, and avoid paying for craftsmanship or branding you cannot resell. The [global physical-gold post](/blog/trading/gold/physical-gold-bars-coins-allocated-vs-unallocated-and-counterparty-risk) makes exactly that case for bars and coins; an American saver who overpays for a fancy "collectible" coin is making the same mistake as a Vietnamese saver who overpays for the SJC brand or a jeweled 18K necklace. Vietnam just supplies an unusually vivid version, because Decree 24 manufactured an unusually large and visible premium on one specific form. The fix is the same everywhere: own the metal as close to the metal as you can get. In Vietnam, that form is the plain 9999 ring.

So the practical rule, the one to walk into the shop with: **buy plain 9999 rings in chi and luong, from a licensed dealer, with the invoice; avoid jewelry as savings and avoid chasing a blown-out SJC premium; store large amounts in a bank box; and remember that the metal is your insurance, while the SJC sticker is a tax you do not have to pay.** The single most expensive mistake a Vietnamese saver makes is buying the brand when they could buy the metal — and now you know how not to.
