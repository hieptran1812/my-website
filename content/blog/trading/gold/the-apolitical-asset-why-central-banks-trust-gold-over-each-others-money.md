> **TL;DR** — Every reserve asset except gold is somebody's liability — a Treasury is a claim on the US government, a euro deposit a claim on a European bank, an SDR a claim on the IMF — and the issuer can freeze, default on, or inflate any of them. Gold is the only major reserve asset that is no one's promise and no state's to debase or seize, which is exactly why central banks who fundamentally distrust each other still all accept it.
>
> - A reserve asset that is somebody's "liability" can be switched off by that somebody. In 2022 the world watched roughly \$300bn of one country's reserves get frozen with a keystroke — because those reserves were claims on Western institutions.
> - Gold has **no counterparty**: holding a bar is not a promise from anyone, so there is no one to default, no one to inflate it away, no one to deny you access. That single property is the whole argument.
> - Because no rival state can veto a gold settlement, gold is the **money of last resort between enemies** — the asset that still clears when sanctions and defaults block every other rail.
> - The number to remember: central banks hold roughly **36,000 tonnes** of gold, and the United States, Germany, Italy, and France keep around **70%** of their reserves in it — a vote, in metal, that no one's paper is fully trusted.

In February 2013, Germany's central bank, the Bundesbank, announced something that sounded almost antique: it was going to bring its gold home. Over the next four years it physically shipped **674 tonnes** of gold — worth tens of billions of dollars — out of the vaults of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York and the Banque de France, packed it onto insured, armored transport, and moved it across the Atlantic and across Europe into its own vault in Frankfurt.

Step back and notice how strange this is. Germany is a rich, peaceful, founding member of NATO. The gold was sitting in the vaults of its closest allies — the United States and France — institutions Germany trusts with its security, its trade, and its diplomacy. Nobody had threatened to take it. There was no crisis, no war, no sanction. And yet the Bundesbank decided it was worth the cost, the logistics, and the political awkwardness to haul metal home from an ally's basement.

Why? Because even an ally's vault is a **counterparty**. Gold sitting in New York is, in the end, a *claim* on the New York Fed — a promise that when you ask for your bars, you will get them back. A promise from a friend is still a promise, and the entire history of money is the history of promises being broken. The Bundesbank's repatriation was not an accusation against America. It was a recognition of a deeper truth about what gold *is* — and about why, in a world of states that do not fully trust one another, gold remains the one form of money they all accept. This post is about that truth: gold as the **apolitical asset**, the politically neutral money in a world with no trusted referee.

![Liability ladder showing Treasury euro deposit and SDR as claims while gold is no ones liability](/imgs/blogs/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money-1.png)

This series argues a single thesis, and this post is its political-economy heart: **gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability.** Today's installment is about *who* casts the most consequential votes — the central banks themselves — and *why* they cast them for a metal that pays no interest, costs money to store, and does absolutely nothing. A companion post in this series, [Central Banks: The Structural Buyer That Changed Gold After 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022), covers how that buying *moves the price*. Here we go beneath the price to the politics: why an asset's neutrality is its most valuable feature when the holders are sovereigns who would, in a genuine crisis, happily freeze each other out.

## Foundations: reserves, liabilities, and the trust problem between states

Before we can explain why gold is special, we need three ideas built from zero: what a **reserve asset** is, what it means for an asset to be somebody's **liability**, and why states fundamentally do not trust each other. None of these require finance background. They are about promises, and what happens when promises can be broken.

### What is a reserve asset?

Start with you. You probably keep a cash buffer — money you don't invest, money that just *sits there* so that if you lose your job, or your car dies, or a medical bill lands, you can pay without selling your home or borrowing at a bad moment. That buffer is your personal reserve. Its job is not to make you rich. Its job is to be *there*, liquid and reliable, on the worst day.

A country has the same need on a vastly larger scale. A nation earns foreign money by exporting (Vietnam sells coffee and electronics; Germany sells cars; Saudi Arabia sells oil), and it spends foreign money by importing (oil, food, machinery, medicine). It also has debts denominated in foreign currencies. When trade is lumpy, when a crisis hits, when its own currency is under attack, a country needs a stockpile of *internationally accepted money* it can deploy. That stockpile is its **foreign exchange reserves** — managed by its central bank.

What goes into that stockpile? Whatever the world will reliably accept. In practice, a modern central bank's reserves are some mix of:

- **US Treasury securities** — IOUs from the US government, the single largest holding for most central banks because the dollar is the world's dominant trade and reserve currency.
- **Deposits and bonds in other major currencies** — euros (claims on European banks and governments), Japanese yen, British pounds, and a small but growing slice of Chinese renminbi.
- **Special Drawing Rights (SDRs)** — a synthetic reserve unit issued by the International Monetary Fund (IMF), itself a claim on a basket of those same major currencies.
- **Gold** — physical bars, the oldest reserve asset of all.

A typical reserve manager's whole job is to hold this pile so it is safe, liquid, and accepted. And here is where the trouble starts, because four of those five categories share a feature that the fifth does not.

### What does it mean for an asset to be a "liability"?

This is the load-bearing concept of the entire post, so let's make it concrete.

When you own a US Treasury bond, what do you actually own? Not a thing. You own a *promise*. The US government has written, in effect: "We owe you \$1,000 plus interest, payable on this date." Your asset is the government's **liability** — its obligation, its debt. Every financial asset that is a claim on someone else has this two-sided nature: it is your asset and simultaneously their liability. Your bank deposit is your asset and the bank's liability. A corporate bond is your asset and the company's liability. A euro in a German bank is your asset and that bank's (and ultimately the ECB's) liability.

The crucial implication: **the value and the very accessibility of a liability depend on the issuer.** The issuer is a *counterparty* — the other party to the promise — and a promise is only as good as the party making it. A counterparty can do three damaging things to you:

1. **Default** — refuse or fail to pay. A government can decline to honor its bonds; a bank can fail; a country can repudiate its debts.
2. **Inflate** — pay you back in money worth far less. The US can always pay its dollar debts because it prints dollars, but it can print so many that the dollars you get back buy a fraction of what they did. This is debasement by another name.
3. **Freeze** — block your access entirely. The issuer (or its government) can simply refuse to let you use, sell, or move the asset. This is the modern weapon, and it is the one the Bundesbank was quietly insuring against.

We call the danger that the counterparty does any of these **counterparty risk**. It is the risk that the person on the *other side* of your asset breaks the promise. And it is everywhere in a reserve portfolio — because almost everything in that portfolio is a promise.

#### Worked example: the \$10bn that can be frozen vs the \$10bn that cannot

Imagine you run a central bank with two ways to park \$10bn of reserves.

**Option A — \$10bn in US Treasuries.** These are extraordinarily safe in the ordinary sense: the US has never missed a payment, the market is the deepest on earth, and you earn interest. But your \$10bn is an entry in a system the US government controls. The bonds settle through US-supervised infrastructure; the dollars they pay live in accounts at US-supervised banks. If your country and the United States fall out badly enough, that \$10bn can be rendered unusable — not stolen exactly, but *frozen*: you still "own" it on paper, but you cannot spend a cent of it. Your asset has become a hostage.

**Option B — \$10bn in gold bars, in your own vault.** No interest. Storage and insurance cost you perhaps \$5–10 million a year (around 0.05–0.1% of value). But there is no issuer. No government's keystroke can freeze a bar that sits in your basement. No default, because there is no promise to break. No inflation of *the gold itself*, because no one can print more gold than the mines dig up.

The intuition: Option A pays you a yield in exchange for accepting that a rival controls the off-switch; Option B pays you nothing but hands you the only off-switch yourself. **For most days, Option A is smarter. For the day that actually matters — the crisis day — Option B is the only one that still works.** That asymmetry is the whole reason gold survives in modern reserves.

### Why don't states trust each other?

The final foundation is the one people raise least and that matters most. Individuals can trust each other because there is a referee above them: courts, police, contracts, the state itself. If your bank steals your deposit, you sue, and the legal system — backed by the government's monopoly on force — makes it right.

Between sovereign states there is **no referee**. There is no world court with the power to seize a nation's assets and hand them to another against its will. There is no global police force. When the United States freezes another country's reserves, there is no higher authority that country can appeal to and expect enforcement. International law exists, but it is enforced only by the willingness of states to comply — which evaporates exactly when stakes are highest.

This is what political scientists call **anarchy** in the international system — not chaos, but the absence of a governing authority above states. In that world, every nation's holdings of *another* nation's money are, ultimately, held at that nation's pleasure. You can trust your ally today. But reserves are held for the day your ally is no longer your ally — the war, the sanction, the regime change, the trade rupture you cannot foresee. A reserve manager who only plans for friendly weather is not doing the job.

Gold's answer to the trust problem is radical in its simplicity: it removes the counterparty entirely. If there is no issuer, there is no one to distrust. That is the bridge from "what gold is" to "why states converge on it," and it is where we go next.

The picture below captures the whole dynamic. Three rival states — call them the USA, China, and Russia — each hold their own currency and each refuse to fully trust the others' money; the dashed bars between them are mutual distrust, blocked rails. But every one of them will accept gold, because gold is no one's liability. The solid arrows all converge on the same neutral hub. This is the structural fact that organizes the rest of the post: **in a triangle of distrust, the one node every side points to is the asset that none of them controls.**

![Three rival states distrust each others money but all accept neutral gold as a hub](/imgs/blogs/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money-4.png)

There is a subtle point hiding in that diagram worth drawing out, because it is *why* the convergence happens rather than some other outcome. You might think rivals would simply refuse to settle with each other at all, or insist on barter — oil for wheat, machines for metals. But barter is wildly inefficient: it requires a "double coincidence of wants," where each side happens to want exactly what the other is selling, at the same time, in the same amounts. Money exists precisely to break that bottleneck — it is the thing everyone accepts, so no one needs a coincidence of wants. Between rivals, the question becomes: *which* money will everyone accept when no one trusts anyone's currency? The answer has to be an asset that is no one's liability, because any liability is a lever in its issuer's hand. Gold is the Schelling point — the obvious focal answer that rivals converge on without needing to coordinate — for the same reason it has been money for five thousand years: it is the one valuable thing whose acceptance does not depend on trusting its issuer, because it has no issuer. The hub in the figure is not an accident of history. It is the unique solution to a problem every adversarial monetary system faces.

## The no-counterparty property: gold is no one's IOU

Here is the single sentence that the rest of finance dances around and gold answers head-on: **gold is the only major reserve asset that is not simultaneously somebody's liability.**

Walk back through the list. A Treasury is the US government's liability. A euro deposit is a European bank's liability. A yen bond is the Japanese government's liability. An SDR is a claim on the IMF, which is itself a claim on member governments. Every one of them appears on someone *else's* balance sheet as a debt. Look at a gold bar's balance-sheet treatment and you find the opposite: it is an asset to its owner and a liability to *no one*. There is no entry, anywhere on Earth, that reads "we owe this gold to someone." The chain of obligation simply ends at the metal.

This is sometimes phrased as gold being **"no one's liability"** or having **"no counterparty risk,"** and reserve managers say it constantly because it is the entire pitch. Recall the three things a counterparty can do — default, inflate, freeze. Run gold against each:

- **Can gold default?** There is no issuer to fail. A bar cannot refuse to pay you because it never promised to pay you anything. It just *is* gold.
- **Can gold be inflated away?** No central bank can conjure more gold. Total above-ground gold grows by only about 1.5–2% a year from mining — the so-called stock-to-flow stability that makes gold's "monetary base" almost fixed compared to fiat money, where the supply can double in a crisis. (For how that compares to a money supply that genuinely can balloon, see this series' [debasement and the fiscal story](/blog/trading/gold/debasement-debt-and-the-fiscal-story-gold-as-an-anti-currency) post and the broader [petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance) primer.)
- **Can gold be frozen?** Only if someone else is holding it for you. A bar in *your* vault cannot be frozen by a foreign government — there is no account to suspend, no transfer to block, no message to refuse. (The catch, which we'll get to, is that gold "held abroad" *re-introduces* a counterparty, and that is exactly what repatriation is about.)

The figure at the top of this post is the whole argument in one image: a ladder of reserve assets, each connected by "is a claim on" to the entity that can break the promise — and gold at the bottom, its arrow ending in nothing, because there is no entity there.

It is worth pausing on how unusual this is, because almost nothing else in modern finance shares the property. Cash in your wallet? A liability of the central bank that printed it. A bank deposit? A liability of the bank — which is why deposit insurance exists, to paper over the counterparty risk you'd otherwise bear when the bank fails. A money-market fund, a Treasury bill, a "risk-free" government bond — all liabilities, all promises, all dependent on an issuer staying solvent and willing. Even the assets we casually call "as good as cash" are good *only as long as the issuer is good.* The financial system is, almost in its entirety, a vast web of interlocking IOUs: my asset is your liability, your asset is someone else's liability, and the chains can stretch through dozens of intermediaries. In 2008 the world discovered how fragile those chains are when the promises at the bottom — subprime mortgages — turned out to be worth far less than the layers of claims built on top of them. Gold sits entirely outside that web. It is one of the very few assets that is not a link in anyone's chain of obligation. The chain doesn't pass through gold; it simply *ends* there.

This is why some analysts describe gold as the **base of the monetary pyramid** — the one layer with no layer beneath it. Currencies are claims on central banks; bank deposits are claims on banks that hold central-bank money; derivatives are claims on those; and so on up the pyramid, each layer a promise referencing the layer below. Gold is the bedrock the pyramid's logic implicitly rests on, the asset that is valuable without referencing anything else. In normal times nobody thinks about the bedrock; the upper layers of promises work fine and are far more convenient. It is only when the upper layers crack — when a default cascades, when a currency is debased, when reserves are frozen — that holders scramble down the pyramid toward the one layer that cannot break because it was never a promise in the first place. That scramble is what a gold rally in a crisis actually *is.*

#### Worked example: the counterparty-risk discount

Why would anyone hold an asset that yields *nothing*, when a Treasury yields, say, 4%? Because the yield is partly *compensation for counterparty risk* — and gold's price already bakes in the value of not having any.

Suppose you compare two ways to hold \$1,000 of reserves for a decade. The Treasury pays 4% a year; gold pays 0%. Naively the Treasury wins by a mile: at 4% compounded, \$1,000 becomes about \$1,480 over ten years, while the gold just sits there. But that 4% is the *price of the promise* — the market pays you to accept that the issuer might default, inflate, or freeze. Now imagine you assign even a 2% annual probability to a scenario where the Treasury becomes unusable to you (frozen, restructured, paid in debased dollars). Over ten years, the cumulative probability of hitting that scenario at least once is roughly 1 − (0.98)^10 ≈ **18%**.

If that scenario wipes out, say, half the real value of the bond position, the expected loss is about 0.18 × 0.5 = **9%** of the position — which quietly eats most of a decade's yield advantage. The gold has no such tail. **The intuition: gold's "missing" yield is the premium you*don't* pay for a promise that can be broken — and for a reserve held against exactly the day promises break, that trade can be worth it.** This is why a rational reserve manager holds *some* gold even though it never pays a coupon: it is buying off the tail.

## The neutrality argument: no state can freeze, debase, or default it

The no-counterparty property has a political twin, and it is the one that makes gold uniquely suited to *states*: gold is **neutral**. Because it is no one's liability, it is also no one's *instrument*. No single government can weaponize it, because no single government issues it.

Think about what the dollar gives the United States. Because the world's reserves and trade run on dollars, and because dollar payments ultimately clear through US-supervised systems, the United States can reach into the global financial system and switch off a target's access. This is an extraordinary, almost unique power — a power that flows directly from the dollar being *America's* liability. The euro gives Europe a smaller version. The renminbi would give China the same, if it were widely held. Every national currency is, by construction, a lever in its issuer's hand.

Gold has no such lever, because it has no such hand. There is no "gold authority" that can sanction a country, no central issuer that can debase the global gold stock to fund a war, no committee that can vote to suspend your access. Gold is neutral in the precise sense that **its usefulness does not depend on the goodwill of any government** — including the government that happens to be the strongest at the moment. For a reserve manager in a country that might one day find itself on the wrong side of Washington, Brussels, or Beijing, that neutrality is not a philosophical nicety. It is the product.

Let's be precise about the three neutralities, because each maps to one of the counterparty dangers:

1. **No state can default it.** Gold isn't a promise, so no sovereign can repudiate it. Compare: Argentina has defaulted on its sovereign debt nine times; Russia defaulted in 1998; even the US technically defaulted on gold convertibility in 1971 (more on that below). An ounce of gold has defaulted on no one, ever.
2. **No state can debase it.** A government can print its currency to dilute the holders — which is what "debasement" means — and the dollar has lost over 98% of its purchasing power since 1913. No government can print gold. The most any state can do is mine more, slowly, at real cost.
3. **No state can freeze it** (when self-held). A government can freeze foreign-currency reserves, block SWIFT messages, sanction a central bank — all of which act *on the rails the asset travels*. Gold in your own vault travels on no foreign rails. It is, as reserve managers put it, **sanction-proof money**. (The sibling post [Sanctions, Reserve Freezes, and the 2022 Turning Point for Gold](/blog/trading/gold/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold) develops this in full.)

This is why the data look the way they do. Across the world's official holders, gold sits at the top of the reserve pile — and the institution that owns the third-most is not even a country.

![Official gold reserves by holder in tonnes United States Germany IMF leading](/imgs/blogs/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money-2.png)

The United States holds about **8,134 tonnes**, more than the next two holders combined. Germany has **3,352 tonnes** — the gold the Bundesbank fought to bring home. Then the IMF itself, with **2,814 tonnes**, sitting between Germany and Italy: even the multilateral referee-that-isn't keeps a giant pile of the neutral asset. Italy and France hold around 2,450 each; Russia and China have built to roughly 2,300 each, deliberately, over the past two decades. These are not relics gathering dust. They are positions, held on purpose, by the most sophisticated money managers on the planet — for the neutrality reason above all.

There is a tell in the ownership data that confirms the political reading. Notice that the heaviest holders cluster into two groups with opposite stories. The first group — the US, Germany, Italy, France, the Netherlands, Switzerland — is the *old monetary core*, the countries that ran or anchored the gold-standard and Bretton Woods systems and simply never sold their inheritance down. Their gold is a fossil of the era when paper money was a *claim on gold* rather than the other way around. The second group — Russia, China, and a long tail of emerging-market central banks (Turkey, India, Poland, Kazakhstan, and others) — is the *new accumulator*, building gold reserves deliberately and rapidly over the past two decades from a low base. The first group holds gold out of *legacy*; the second buys it out of *strategy*. And the strategy is transparent: these are precisely the countries most exposed to a dollar-centric system they do not control and might one day be locked out of. When Russia spent a decade quadrupling its gold reserves before 2022, it was not making a price bet. It was building the one part of its reserve book that could not be frozen — and when the freeze came, that part was the only part that still worked. The ownership map is a map of who runs the system (the legacy holders) and who is hedging against it (the accumulators), drawn in tonnes of metal.

#### Worked example: gold's share of a \$3 trillion reserve book at 6% vs 20%

Consider a large emerging-market central bank — China is the cleanest example — with roughly \$3 trillion of total reserves. Suppose its gold sits at about **6%** of reserves today (China's official figure is around there). What would moving to a Western-style **20%** require, and what would it signal?

- At 6%, gold is worth about 0.06 × \$3,000bn = **\$180bn**.
- At 20%, gold would be worth about 0.20 × \$3,000bn = **\$600bn**.
- The gap is **\$420bn** of gold to acquire — at, say, \$3,000 per ounce, that's about 4,350 tonnes, *more than Germany's entire stockpile*, and well over three years of total global central-bank buying at the recent ~1,000-tonnes-a-year pace.

The intuition cuts two ways. First, it shows why de-dollarization is *slow*: even a determined giant cannot swing 14 percentage points of a \$3 trillion book quickly without moving the gold price against itself. Second — and this is the political punchline — **the very fact that emerging-market banks sit at 6% while Western banks sit at 70% tells you the West's gold is a legacy of the gold-standard era, and the East's low share is a multi-decade buying *opportunity* it is now, visibly, taking.** The direction of travel is the story, and it points one way.

That East–West gap is stark enough to deserve its own picture.

![Gold as a share of reserves by country West around seventy percent emerging markets much lower](/imgs/blogs/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money-3.png)

The United States, Germany, France, and Italy all park around **68–72%** of their reserves in gold. Russia sits near 29%, India around 11%, China around 6%, Japan around 5%. The split is not economic — gold is the same metal for everyone. It is *political and historical*: the West inherited its gold from the era when the dollar was literally backed by it, and never sold most of it down, while Asia accumulated dollars during its export boom and is only now diversifying into the neutral asset. Read the chart as a map of *who already insured themselves against the dollar and who is still buying the policy.*

## The exorbitant-privilege problem: why holding a rival's currency is a vulnerability

To see why neutrality matters so much, you have to understand the strange, lopsided deal at the center of the global monetary system — what a French finance minister in the 1960s famously called America's **"exorbitant privilege."**

The dollar is the world's reserve currency. Roughly 58% of disclosed global reserves are held in dollars; most cross-border trade is invoiced in dollars; most international debt is issued in dollars. This gives the United States privileges no other country has: it can borrow cheaply and almost without limit in its own currency, it can run persistent deficits the rest of the world finances, and — the privilege that matters here — it sits at the *control point* of the system everyone else depends on. (For the full mechanics of how the dollar came to rule, see [The Dollar System: Why USD Rules Markets and the DXY](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) and the cross-asset companion [The Dollar: Cross-Asset Gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity).)

For everyone *else*, the privilege is a vulnerability. If most of your reserves are dollars, then the United States holds a switch on your economic life. Most of the time the switch is never touched, and the dollar is simply convenient. But "most of the time" is not what reserves are for. A reserve held in a rival's currency is a reserve held *at the rival's discretion* — and the more the relationship sours, the closer the rival's hand drifts to the switch.

The mechanism by which the switch works is worth spelling out, because it is more total than most people realize. The United States does not need to physically seize anything. Because dollars ultimately exist as entries at US-supervised banks and clear through US-supervised systems, the US can simply *declare* a target's dollars off-limits — instruct American institutions and, through secondary sanctions, foreign institutions that want continued access to the US market, to stop transacting with the target. The dollars don't move; they just become radioactive. No bank will touch them for fear of losing its own access to the system, and so the target's "reserves" become numbers on a screen that buy nothing. The genius and the menace of this power is that it costs the US almost nothing to use and is nearly impossible to resist, because the entire world's financial plumbing routes through American territory. Holding dollars is convenient for exactly the same reason it is dangerous: everyone uses them, which means the issuer controls the chokepoint everyone passes through.

This is the asymmetry that makes "exorbitant privilege" sting from the outside. The issuer of the reserve currency enjoys the privilege; everyone else bears the corresponding *risk*. And there is no purely financial way out, because the obvious substitutes are other people's currencies, each of which simply relocates the chokepoint to a different capital. Hold euros and Brussels gets the switch. Hold yen and Tokyo gets it. Hold renminbi and Beijing gets it — and Beijing's willingness to use such a switch is, if anything, less constrained than Washington's. The only escape from *being switched* is to hold an asset with no switch, because it has no issuer to flip it. That is the corner the logic backs you into, and it is the corner gold occupies alone.

For decades, the answer to this was: hold a *little* less dollar, a *little* more euro, a *little* more yen. But the euro is the EU's liability, the yen is Japan's, the pound is Britain's — every "alternative" was just *another rival's switch*. Diversifying among national currencies is like fleeing one landlord by renting from his cousin. The only asset that escapes the entire structure of national control is the one with no nation behind it. And the data show the slow recognition of this: the dollar's share of global reserves has been eroding for two decades.

![USD share of global FX reserves declining from over seventy percent to under fifty eight percent](/imgs/blogs/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money-6.png)

The dollar was about **71.5%** of disclosed global reserves in 2001. By 2024 it had drifted to roughly **57.8%** — a slow, grinding decline of more than thirteen percentage points. This is not a collapse; the dollar remains dominant by a wide margin, and no rival currency has taken its place. The striking thing is *where the share has gone*: not mainly into the euro or yen (those have been flat or shrinking too), but into a long tail of "other" currencies and — increasingly — into gold. The chart is the quiet sound of a hundred reserve managers each deciding, independently, to lean a little less on a rival's promise.

#### Worked example: the cost and logistics of repatriating 300 tonnes

Make the vulnerability concrete from the *other* direction — the cost of fixing it once you realize your gold is sitting in someone else's vault. Suppose a mid-sized central bank decides to bring **300 tonnes** of gold home from foreign storage, as several have in the 2010s and 2020s.

- 300 tonnes is 300,000 kilograms, or about 24,000 standard **"Good Delivery" bars** of roughly 12.5 kg each.
- At, say, \$3,000 per troy ounce, 300 tonnes is worth about 300,000 kg × 32.15 oz/kg × \$3,000 ≈ **\$28.9bn**. That is the value you are physically moving.
- Transport, insurance, security, and re-assay typically run on the order of **0.3–1% of value** — call it \$90–290 million all-in — spread over multiple secret, staggered flights because no one ships \$29bn of metal in one plane.
- It takes *years*. Germany's 674-tonne program ran from 2013 to 2017. Every bar is weighed, assayed, and serial-checked on arrival, because the whole point of repatriation is to verify that the gold you were *promised* is the gold you actually get.

The intuition: repatriation is expensive and slow precisely because it is the act of converting a *claim* (gold "held for you" abroad) back into *possession* (a bar in your own basement). **A central bank willing to spend hundreds of millions and several years to remove the counterparty is telling you, in the most expensive language available, that it values neutrality over convenience.** No one pays that bill for an asset they consider a barbarous relic.

## Gold as the settlement asset of last resort between rivals

Now combine the two properties — no counterparty, full neutrality — and you arrive at gold's most dramatic role: it is the **money that still works between enemies.**

To understand this, think about how international payments normally happen. When Country A pays Country B, the money does not teleport. It moves along *rails* — the dollar clearing system, the SWIFT messaging network that banks use to instruct each other, correspondent-banking relationships, securities-settlement systems. Every one of these rails has an **operator**, and almost every operator is, directly or indirectly, subject to the United States or its allies. That is the deep meaning of the dollar's exorbitant privilege: the US doesn't just issue the currency, it sits astride the *plumbing*.

Which means the plumbing can be turned off. Cut a bank out of SWIFT, and its payment instructions stop being delivered. Freeze a central bank's correspondent accounts, and its dollars become unspendable. Sanction a country's financial system, and the ordinary rails seize up. We saw a sweeping version of this in 2022, when a major economy's reserves — roughly \$300bn held in Western institutions — were frozen essentially overnight, and its banks were cut from SWIFT.

What still works when the rails are off? Gold. Because a gold settlement does not travel on anyone's rails. To settle a debt in gold, you do not send a message that a sanctioning party can block; you do not move a balance through an account a foreign government supervises. You *deliver the metal.* Bars change hands — vault to vault, or country to country — and the debt is extinguished, with no third party able to veto the transaction. This is what people mean when they call gold the **settlement asset of last resort**: when sanctions, defaults, or capital controls block every other channel between two parties who don't trust each other, gold is the channel that remains.

![When dollar and SWIFT rails are blocked gold still settles between rivals](/imgs/blogs/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money-7.png)

The figure makes the logic stark. A country that needs to settle with a rival or a sanctioned counterparty has three rails. The dollar/SWIFT rail needs the US and its banks to *allow the message* — and in a sanctions scenario, that rail is blocked. The "hold the counterparty's bonds" rail needs the issuer to *honor the claim* — and a default or capital controls block that too. Only the gold rail — deliver physical bars, no gatekeeper needed — clears: the bars change hands, the debt is settled, and no one can veto it.

This is not theoretical. Sanctioned and isolated states have used gold to buy what they could not buy with frozen dollars — paying for imports, settling bilateral balances, moving value across borders the dollar system had closed to them. The amounts are modest relative to global trade, and gold is clumsy as everyday money (you cannot ping a bar across an ocean in milliseconds). But "clumsy money that always works" beats "elegant money that can be switched off" on exactly the day that matters. A reserve manager does not hold gold to settle Tuesday's invoices. They hold it for the geopolitical Tuesday when the elegant rails are dark.

It helps to be precise about *why* the gold rail cannot be switched off, because the reason is structural, not a matter of anyone's permission. The dollar rail can be blocked because it is, at heart, a *messaging and ledger* system: a dollar payment is an instruction ("debit this account, credit that one") that travels through institutions a sanctioning power supervises, landing as an updated entry in a ledger that same power can freeze. Block the message or freeze the ledger, and the payment never happens — the money was never a physical thing, only a permission to update a record. Gold settlement has no message and no shared ledger. The "ledger" is the location of the metal: whoever physically holds the bar owns it, full stop. To settle, you move the bar. There is no instruction for a third party to intercept, no account for a third party to freeze, no record for a third party to refuse to update. The settlement *is* the physical transfer. This is the deep reason gold is unblockable and dollars are not — and it is also, not coincidentally, why gold is slow and inconvenient. The same physicality that makes it impossible to freeze makes it impossible to wire. You cannot have the unblockability without the clumsiness; they are the same property viewed from two sides.

This is also why gold's "last resort" role does not require gold to be *common* settlement money. Most international trade will keep running on dollars and euros precisely because they are fast and convenient, and most of the time the convenience is worth the dependence. Gold's job is to exist as the *fallback layer* — the rail that two parties can always reach for when the convenient rails are closed to them, and, just as importantly, the rail whose mere existence shapes behavior. A country that knows it holds an unblockable settlement asset negotiates differently, takes risks differently, and depends on the dollar system less anxiously than one that knows it could be cut off with nothing to fall back on. The option has value even when it is never exercised, the way a fire exit has value even in a building that never burns.

#### Worked example: pricing the "always clears" option

How much is "still settles when everything else is blocked" worth? Treat it like an insurance option and you can sanity-check why central banks pay for it.

Suppose a central bank holds \$200bn of reserves and judges there is a **3% chance over the next decade** of an event — sanction, war, financial blockade — that renders its dollar-system reserves unusable for, say, two critical years. In those two years, the ability to *still transact* might be worth avoiding an economic loss of, conservatively, **\$40bn** (imports it could not otherwise pay for, contracts it could not honor, a currency crisis it could not defend). The expected value of having a working settlement rail is then about 0.03 × \$40bn = **\$1.2bn** over the decade, or roughly \$120 million a year.

Now compare the cost of holding, say, \$30bn of that \$200bn in gold instead of Treasuries. The "cost" is the foregone yield — at 4%, about \$1.2bn a year — *minus* whatever gold appreciates, which over the long run has roughly tracked the debasement of fiat money. **The intuition: a central bank holds gold not because it expects to use it as settlement money next year, but because the expected value of having an unblockable rail — even at a few percent probability — justifies giving up some yield on a slice of the book.** Gold is the premium on a policy that only pays out in catastrophe, which is precisely what makes it a *reserve* asset rather than an investment.

## Repatriation: bringing the gold home

Return now to where we began — Germany hauling 674 tonnes across the Atlantic — because repatriation is the clearest physical expression of everything above. It is the moment a country acts on the realization that **gold held abroad is not the same as gold held.**

When a central bank stores its gold in a foreign vault — and historically *most* gold was stored abroad, for sound reasons we'll cover — it does not actually hold metal. It holds an *entry*: the foreign vault's record that says "we are holding X tonnes for you." That entry is a claim. And a claim, as we have established, has a counterparty — the host central bank. In normal times this is fine and even useful: gold stored at the New York Fed or the Bank of England can be lent, swapped, or sold in the deep London and New York markets without ever physically moving, which is *why* so much gold ended up there in the first place. Convenience and liquidity pulled the world's gold into a handful of trusted vaults.

But convenience and counterparty risk are two sides of one coin. The gold that is easy to trade abroad is also the gold a foreign government could, in extremis, freeze, delay, or "fail to deliver." Repatriation is the deliberate choice to trade convenience for *certainty of possession*.

![Repatriation flow gold stored abroad shipped home to remove the counterparty Germany case](/imgs/blogs/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money-5.png)

The figure traces the logic: gold stored abroad (NY Fed, Bank of England, Banque de France) is a *claim, not a bar*; that claim *exposes* the owner to counterparty risk — the host could freeze, lend, or refuse access in a crisis; so the owner audits and airlifts it home, at a logistics cost of roughly 0.3–1% of value, checking each bar's serial number; and the gold ends up *at home* — possession, no IOU. Germany's 2013–17 program, moving 674 tonnes from New York and Paris so that around half its gold now sits in Frankfurt, is the textbook case.

Germany is far from alone. The Netherlands moved a large share of its gold back from New York in 2014, explicitly citing the goal of holding a *visible, on-soil* stockpile to anchor confidence. Austria, Belgium, Hungary, Poland, and others have repatriated or audited their foreign-held gold in the past decade. Venezuela, in a more desperate version of the same logic, fought a years-long battle to bring its gold home and, later, to wrest its gold *back from the Bank of England*, which declined to release roughly 31 tonnes amid a dispute over which government to recognize — a vivid, real-world demonstration that "your gold" in someone else's vault can become *contested* exactly when you need it. The motive runs along a spectrum: from Germany's calm institutional prudence, to Venezuela's crisis-driven scramble, to the strategic accumulation of states bracing for a more confrontational world. But the underlying insight is identical. **A bar in your own vault is yours; a bar in someone else's vault is theirs to deliver — and the gap between those two sentences is the entire reason to bring it home.**

It is worth naming the cost honestly, because it sharpens the choice. Repatriated gold is *less useful*: it is harder to lend, harder to swap, harder to sell into the deep markets that cluster around London and New York. A country that brings all its gold home gives up the liquidity and the modest lending income that foreign storage provides. That a sophisticated, profit-conscious institution like the Bundesbank chose certainty over that income tells you how it weighs the tail risk. Repatriation is the central bank equivalent of paying to keep your emergency cash in a safe at home rather than in a bank account that earns interest — irrational if you only fear an ordinary Tuesday, entirely rational if you are insuring against the day the bank's doors are locked.

## Common misconceptions

The neutrality argument attracts a lot of sloppy thinking, in both directions. Here are the myths most worth correcting with numbers.

**Myth 1: "Gold pays no interest, so a rational central bank wouldn't hold it."** The premise is true; the conclusion does not follow. Gold's zero yield is not a flaw the holder overlooked — it is the *price of having no counterparty*, as the discount worked example showed. A reserve held against the day promises break should not itself be a promise. The 8,000+ tonnes the US holds and the 1,000-tonnes-a-year central banks have bought since 2022 are not a mistake by people who forgot bonds pay interest. They are a deliberate purchase of tail insurance by the world's most sophisticated money managers. The yield gold "lacks" is the premium it doesn't charge.

**Myth 2: "Central banks could just hold a more neutral currency instead of gold."** There is no such thing. Every national currency is its issuer's liability and its issuer's lever — the euro is Europe's switch, the yen is Japan's, the renminbi would be China's. The "neutral basket" idea, the SDR, is a claim on those same currencies and is allocated and governed by the IMF's weighted vote, where the US holds an effective veto. Diversifying among currencies spreads counterparty risk; it does not *escape* it. Only an asset with no issuer escapes the structure, and gold is the only liquid one that qualifies.

**Myth 3: "Gold in the New York Fed or Bank of England is perfectly safe — repatriation is paranoia."** It is safe *until it isn't*, and the "isn't" is precisely the scenario reserves exist for. The Bank of England's refusal to release roughly 31 tonnes of Venezuela's gold during a political dispute is not a hypothetical — it happened, and it converted "your gold, safely stored" into "contested gold you cannot touch." Repatriation isn't an accusation that allies are thieves; it's a recognition that custody is a *relationship*, and relationships between states can break.

**Myth 4: "Gold is too clumsy to be real settlement money, so the 'last resort' idea is a fantasy."** Clumsy, yes — you cannot wire a bar. But "clumsy and unblockable" beats "fast and freezable" on the only day the asset is held for. Sanctioned states have, in fact, used gold to pay for imports and settle balances the dollar system closed to them. The point was never that gold is *convenient* money. It is that gold is the money that *still functions* when the convenient money is switched off — which is exactly the definition of a last resort.

**Myth 5: "De-dollarization means the dollar is about to collapse and gold will replace it."** No. The dollar's reserve share fell from ~71% to ~58% over twenty years — a slow drift, not a collapse, and no currency has dethroned it. Gold is not *replacing* the dollar as everyday money; it is functioning as the *neutral reserve and ultimate settlement layer* underneath a system the dollar still dominates day to day. The realistic picture is not "gold instead of the dollar" but "more gold as insurance *against* over-dependence on the dollar." The trend is real; the apocalypse is not.

## How it shows up in real markets

Strip away the theory and watch the neutrality argument play out in concrete events, dates, and tonnages.

**The Bundesbank repatriation (2013–2017).** Germany's program is the cleanest case study because it was done in peacetime, by an ally, with no crisis forcing its hand. The Bundesbank brought home 674 tonnes — 300 from New York, 374 from Paris — so that roughly half its 3,352-tonne stockpile now sits in Frankfurt, with the rest split between New York and London. The stated rationale was about *trust and verifiability*: a desire to hold a substantial share of the nation's gold on its own soil, auditable bar by bar. The unstated rationale was the one this post is about. A country does not spend years and hundreds of millions converting a claim into possession unless, somewhere in its risk models, it has priced the possibility that an ally's vault is still a counterparty.

**Gold at the NY Fed and the Bank of England — who stores where.** The geography of stored gold is itself a map of the trust problem. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York holds, in its bedrock vault beneath Manhattan, around 6,000 tonnes — *almost none of it American*. It is the gold of other nations, stored in the US because, historically, New York was the safest and most liquid place to keep it and the most convenient place to settle international balances. The Bank of England's vaults hold a comparable hoard for the world. London and New York became the planet's gold custodians for the same reason they became its financial centers: deep markets, strong institutions, and — until recently — unquestioned trust. The repatriation wave is the sound of that last assumption being re-examined.

**The 2022 reserve freeze and the structural break.** When roughly \$300bn of one major economy's reserves were frozen in 2022, every reserve manager on earth learned the same lesson at the same moment: *dollar and euro reserves are usable only with the issuer's permission.* The response shows in the data — central-bank gold buying jumped to over **1,000 tonnes a year** in 2022, 2023, and 2024, the highest sustained pace in modern history, led by exactly the countries with the most reason to fear the switch. This is the demand story that the companion post [Central Banks: The Structural Buyer](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022) covers in price terms; here the point is the *motive*. The buying is not a bet that gold will go up. It is a flight to the only asset that cannot be frozen — neutrality, purchased by the tonne.

**The slow erosion in the COFER numbers.** None of this is loud. The dollar's reserve share grinds down a few tenths of a percent a year; gold's share of reserves ticks up; a few hundred tonnes get repatriated; a sanctioned state settles a few billion in metal. There is no single dramatic day. But the *direction* is unmistakable across two decades, and it all points the same way: toward more of the neutral asset and less dependence on any single state's promise. Markets price the trend, not the apocalypse — and the trend has been one long, quiet vote of partial no-confidence in everyone's money.

## The takeaway: gold is the trust that survives when states stop trusting

Here is what to carry away, and it reframes how you should think about gold itself.

Most people argue about gold as an *investment* — will it beat stocks, does it hedge inflation, is it in a bubble. Those are real questions, covered elsewhere in this series. But the central banks who hold 36,000 tonnes of the stuff are not asking them. They hold gold for a reason that has almost nothing to do with return and everything to do with *trust* — specifically, the absence of it between sovereign states.

In a world with no referee above nations, every reserve asset except gold is held at someone else's discretion. A Treasury is held at America's discretion; a euro at Europe's; an SDR at the IMF's weighted vote. Most of the time that discretion is benign and the assets are simply convenient. But reserves are not held for the convenient times. They are held for the rupture — the war, the sanction, the freeze, the default, the day an ally is no longer an ally. And on that day, the only asset that is still *yours* — not yours-by-permission, but yours-in-fact — is the bar in your own vault that is no one's promise to keep.

That is what it means to call gold the **apolitical asset.** It is not neutral because it is wise or fair or above the fray. It is neutral because it is *no one's* — and in a system where every alternative is somebody's lever, being no one's is the rarest and most valuable property an asset can have. Gold is the money that two enemies will both accept precisely because neither of them controls it. It is the trust that survives when states stop trusting each other.

So when you see a central bank quietly buying gold, or hauling it home across an ocean, do not read it as a forecast that the metal will rise. Read it as what it is: a sophisticated institution paying real money to remove a counterparty — buying, by the tonne, the only insurance policy that still pays out when the world's promises stop being kept. The price of gold, in the end, is the price of that insurance. And the more the world's referee is in doubt, the more that insurance is worth.
