> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Both the bull case and the bear case for gold over the next decade are serious, and a fair reading of the evidence does not produce a price target — it produces a *position size*.
>
> - **The bull case is structural, not emotional.** Central banks have bought over 1,000 tonnes a year since 2022, the dollar's share of global reserves has slid from 71% to 58%, US debt is near \$37 trillion with \$1 trillion a year in interest, and every crisis still drags real interest rates back toward zero. None of these is a slogan; each is a measurable, persistent force.
> - **The bear case is just as concrete.** When the real yield on a 10-year inflation-protected bond is +2%, gold's zero coupon competes against a guaranteed, government-backed 2% real return — and historically that has capped gold. Over the full post-1971 era gold has returned only about 3% per year after inflation, barely beating cash and badly trailing stocks.
> - **The swing variables are real rates, central-bank flows, and the dollar — and they are not independent.** A debt-driven monetary pivot can push all three the bull's way at once; a resilient real-rate world does the reverse.
> - **The one fact to remember:** gold near \$5,400 is *insurance whose premium has risen*. You do not buy more fire insurance because you are sure the house will burn; you buy the right amount because you cannot rule it out. Own gold sized — roughly 5-10% — not as a directional bet on either column.

On 28 January 2026, gold printed an all-time high near \$5,589 an ounce, and the spot price has hovered around \$5,400 since. To one camp, this is the early innings of a monetary regime change: the world's central banks are quietly voting against the dollar, fiat currencies are being debased by debt that can never be repaid in real terms, and gold — the one form of money that is no one's promise — is being re-monetized for the first time in fifty years. To the other camp, this is a crowded, late-cycle trade. Real interest rates are positive and high. Cash pays. Bonds pay. Gold pays nothing, costs money to store, and over the long sweep of history barely keeps pace with inflation. A thing that has tripled in three years, they argue, is not the start of something — it is the part of the story right before the mean reversion.

Here is the uncomfortable truth that this post is built on: **both of those people are looking at real evidence, and both of them are partly right.** The gold debate is usually conducted as a shouting match between perma-bulls who think every dollar is about to become wallpaper and perma-bears who think gold is a "barbarous relic" with no business in a portfolio. That framing is useless, because it forces you to pick a tribe instead of weighing forces. This post does the opposite. It steel-mans each side — builds the strongest, most honest version of both cases, with the numbers — and then asks the only question that actually has an answer: *given that both cases are serious and the future is genuinely uncertain, how should a thinking person hold gold?*

This is the second-to-last post in the Gold series, and it deliberately leans on everything that came before. Where the bull case rests on central-bank buying, [central banks: the structural buyer that changed gold after 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022) does the deep mechanics; where the bear case rests on real rates, [real interest rates: the master variable behind the gold price](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price) builds the model from zero. The de-dollarization thread runs through [the multipolar money order](/blog/trading/gold/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback), and the sizing conclusion connects to [gold's job in a portfolio](/blog/trading/gold/golds-job-in-a-portfolio-sizing-rebalancing-and-the-permanent-portfolio). What this post adds is the synthesis: putting the cases side by side, naming what would prove each wrong, and landing on a position rather than a prediction.

## Foundations: the four drivers, in one page

Before we argue, we need a shared vocabulary. Gold has no earnings, no coupon, and no dividend; you cannot value it the way you value a stock or a bond, by discounting its future cash flows, because it produces none. So what *does* move the price? The whole series boils down to four drivers, and both the bull and the bear case are just different bets on how those four behave. Let me define each from scratch, because the rest of the post is built on them.

**1. Real interest rates — the master variable.** The "nominal" interest rate is the headline number on a bond — say 4.5% on a 10-year US Treasury. The "real" rate is that number *minus expected inflation*: it is what you actually earn in purchasing power after prices rise. The market gives us a clean read on this through **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS)**, government bonds whose payout is adjusted for inflation; the yield on a 10-year TIPS *is* the market's real interest rate. Here is why it rules gold: gold pays no yield, so its only "competition" is whatever you could safely earn elsewhere in real terms. When the real yield is +2%, holding gold means giving up a guaranteed, government-backed 2% real return every year — a steep opportunity cost. When the real yield is negative, cash and bonds *lose* purchasing power, and gold's zero suddenly looks great. The historical relationship is therefore inverse: **real rates down, gold up; real rates up, gold down.** Hold that, because it is the single biggest hinge in the whole debate.

**2. Central-bank demand.** Central banks hold reserves — the national rainy-day fund — mostly in foreign currencies and bonds, but also in gold. For two decades they were net *sellers* of gold. Then, abruptly, after 2022 they became enormous net *buyers*, taking down more than 1,000 tonnes a year. Why this matters: central banks are price-insensitive, strategic, multi-decade buyers. When they buy, they take supply off the market and don't sell it back on a dip. A persistent official bid acts like a floor under the price.

**3. The dollar.** Gold is priced in dollars, so mechanically a weaker dollar tends to mean a higher gold price (and vice versa) — the same ounce just costs more dollars when the dollar is worth less. But the deeper story is gold's role as the dollar's *rival*. The dollar is the world's reserve currency: the default money for trade, debt, and central-bank reserves. To the extent the world trusts the dollar less and diversifies away from it, some of that flight lands in gold. So the dollar matters twice: as the pricing unit, and as the incumbent gold is competing against.

**4. Debasement — debt and money printing.** "Debasement" is the slow erosion of a currency's value as governments run large deficits, accumulate debt, and (directly or indirectly) finance it with newly created money. Gold is the classic "anti-currency": you cannot print it, no government controls its supply, and it is no one's liability — it is not a promise that can be broken. When the credibility of paper money is in question, gold is the asset that has, for 5,000 years, been the alternative.

Every argument that follows is a claim about how these four drivers will behave over the next ten years. The bull thinks drivers 2, 3, and 4 have entered a new structural regime and driver 1 will keep cycling back to gold's favor. The bear thinks driver 1 is back in charge — and at high positive real rates, that is fatal. Now let us make each case as strong as it can possibly be.

## The bull case, at its strongest

The serious bull case is not "the dollar is going to zero." It is four specific, measurable, persistent forces, each of which has already shown up in the data. Take them one at a time.

### Bull pillar 1: central banks have become a structural buyer

For most of the post-gold-standard era, central banks were sellers. Under the Central Bank Gold Agreements of 1999-2009, European central banks dumped gold — net official sales ran to *negative* 477 tonnes in 1999, negative 663 in 2005. Gold was seen as a relic, a non-yielding embarrassment on the balance sheet next to interest-bearing reserves. Then the regime flipped, and it flipped hard.

![Central banks flipped from net sellers in 1999 to 2009 to record buyers above 1000 tonnes per year after 2022](/imgs/blogs/the-bull-case-vs-the-bear-case-for-gold-over-the-next-decade-2.png)

Since 2022, central banks have bought more than 1,000 tonnes of gold per year — 1,082 tonnes in 2022 (the most since 1950), 1,037 in 2023, 1,045 in 2024, and 863 in 2025. Compare that to the 2010-2021 average of about 473 tonnes a year, itself already a positive number. The post-2022 pace is roughly *double* the prior decade's, which was itself a reversal of the selling era. This is the single most important new fact in the gold market, and it is why [central banks are the structural buyer that changed gold after 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022) is the load-bearing post of the whole series.

Why does this matter so much to the bull? Because of *who* is buying and *why*. A retail investor buys gold because they expect the price to rise, and sells when it does — they are momentum, they are fickle, they leave. A central bank buys gold because of a strategic decision about national reserve composition, made over a multi-decade horizon, often explicitly *because* the price is rising (it confirms the thesis), and it essentially never sells back into a rally. The People's Bank of China, the central banks of Turkey, Poland, India, and a long tail of emerging-market reserve managers are not trading gold; they are *accumulating* it as a permanent reserve asset. That is a different kind of demand. It does not need a falling real rate to show up. It is, in the bull's framing, a standing bid — a price floor that did not exist before 2022.

The bull also points to *how far this still has to run*, which is where the demand looks structural rather than spent. Gold's share of total reserves is wildly uneven: the United States, Germany, France, and Italy hold roughly 70% of their reserves in gold, a legacy of the gold-standard era. But the central banks that have been *adding* — China at about 6%, India at 11%, Russia at 29% — are starting from a fraction of that. If China alone decided to lift gold from 6% toward even 20% of its enormous reserves, that is years of four-figure-tonne buying by one institution. The bull's argument is not that the catch-up is certain; it is that the *room* to catch up is vast, the *incentive* (apolitical, unfreezable reserves) is fresh and strong, and the *direction* has been one-way for four straight years. A demand source with that much runway is not a flash in the pan — it is the early-to-middle innings of a reserve-rebalancing that could span the entire decade.

There is a second-order effect the bull leans on too. Official buying is not just *demand*; it is a *signal*. When a private investor sees that the most informed, most strategic, most patient buyers on the planet are accumulating gold year after year through a +2% real-rate environment that "should" deter them, that itself draws in private flows — Western ETF investors, Asian households, sovereign wealth funds. The central-bank bid can therefore be self-reinforcing: it puts a floor under the price, the floor attracts momentum, and the momentum validates the original thesis. That feedback loop is exactly what a structural regime change looks like from the inside, and it is why the bull treats 2022 as a genuine break rather than a blip.

> #### Worked example: what a permanent 1,000-tonne bid does to the float
>
> Annual mine production runs about 3,661 tonnes and recycling adds roughly 1,370, for total new supply near 5,030 tonnes a year. If central banks take 1,045 tonnes of that off the market and lock it in vaults, they are absorbing about **21% of all annual supply** before jewelry, technology, bars, coins, and ETFs get to bid for the rest. Now suppose that pace simply *continues* for the decade: 1,000 tonnes a year × 10 years = 10,000 tonnes permanently removed from circulation, against an above-ground stock of about 216,000 tonnes. That is roughly **4.6% of all the gold ever mined**, moved from the tradable float into reserves that will not come back. When a price-insensitive buyer removes a fifth of yearly supply every year, the price required to clear the *remaining* supply against private demand has to rise — that is just supply and demand. The intuition: a permanent official bid does not predict a price; it raises the floor under every price.

### Bull pillar 2: de-dollarization is real and it is slow-moving

The dollar's dominance in the global financial system rests on trust — trust that the United States will not weaponize the currency, that US assets are safe, that the dollar will hold its value. In 2022, that trust took a measurable hit when the reserves of a major economy were frozen as a sanction. Every reserve manager on Earth drew the same lesson: dollar reserves are only "safe" until you are on the wrong side of Washington, at which point they can be switched off. Gold, sitting in your own vault, cannot be frozen by anyone. [Sanctions, reserve freezes, and the 2022 turning point](/blog/trading/gold/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold) and [the multipolar money order](/blog/trading/gold/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback) trace this in full; here is the headline evidence.

![The US dollar share of disclosed global FX reserves fell from about 71 percent in 2001 to 58 percent in 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-bull-case-vs-the-bear-case-for-gold-over-the-next-decade-5.png)

The dollar's share of disclosed global FX reserves has fallen from about 71.5% in 2001 to 57.8% by 2024 — roughly 14 percentage points in two decades. That money did not all go to gold; much went to the euro, the yen, and a basket of smaller currencies. But the *direction* is unmistakable: the world is, slowly, diversifying away from the single-currency reserve system, and gold — the one reserve asset that is no government's liability — is a natural beneficiary. The bull's point is not that de-dollarization is fast (it is glacial) but that it is *structural and one-directional*. A 14-point move over 20 years is not a trade that reverses next quarter; it is a tectonic shift, and gold is sitting on the right side of it.

This is the crucial difference between the bull's de-dollarization argument and the cartoon version ("the dollar is dying tomorrow"). The serious version says: the dollar will remain the dominant reserve currency for the foreseeable future, *and* its share will keep grinding lower, *and* a meaningful slice of the diversification will keep flowing into gold. All three can be true at once. You do not need the dollar to collapse for gold to win; you only need the diversification trend to continue at the pace it has already shown for 20 years.

### Bull pillar 3: fiscal debasement is no longer a tail risk

For decades, "the government will debase the currency" was a fringe worry. The numbers have moved it into the mainstream. US federal debt is near \$37 trillion — about 123% of GDP, with debt held by the public near 99% of GDP. The federal deficit has run between 5.4% and 6.3% of GDP every year since 2022, in an *expansion*, with no recession to explain it. And the line that makes even sober analysts nervous: net interest on the debt has nearly tripled, from \$345 billion in 2020 to roughly \$1 trillion in 2025 — in fiscal 2024, the government spent more on interest than on national defense for the first time. [Debasement, debt, and the fiscal story](/blog/trading/gold/debasement-debt-and-the-fiscal-story-gold-as-an-anti-currency) does the deep version.

Here is the bull's logic, which is really an argument about *constraints*. A government with debt at 123% of GDP and \$1 trillion in annual interest has a problem: it cannot let real interest rates stay high indefinitely, because every percentage point of higher rates adds hundreds of billions to the interest bill, which widens the deficit, which forces more borrowing — a doom loop. The political path of least resistance, every time in history, is to let inflation run a little hot and keep nominal rates a little too low, so the debt is slowly inflated away in real terms. That is *financial repression*: keeping real rates below where they "should" be to ease the debt burden. And financial repression is gold's best friend, because it means negative-leaning real rates *by policy*, not by accident.

> #### Worked example: the arithmetic that pins real rates down
>
> Take a government with \$37 trillion of debt. Suppose the average interest rate it pays rises by just one percentage point as old low-rate debt rolls over into new higher-rate debt. The added annual interest cost is \$37 trillion × 1% = **\$370 billion a year** — more than the entire 2020 interest bill, added on top. To stabilize the debt-to-GDP ratio at a 6%-of-GDP deficit, the economy has to grow in *nominal* terms (real growth plus inflation) faster than the debt compounds at the interest rate. With real growth around 2%, that math gets much easier if inflation runs at 3-4% rather than 2% — higher inflation erodes the real debt while nominal GDP catches up. The intuition: a government this indebted has a powerful, permanent incentive to keep real rates low and let inflation do the quiet work — and a world of low real rates and persistent inflation is the world gold is built for.

### Bull pillar 4: every crisis drags real rates back toward zero

The bear's strongest weapon is high positive real rates. The bull's reply is: *they never last.* Look at the record. In 2020-2021, the 10-year real yield was deeply negative — around −1.0% — because a crisis forced the Federal Reserve to slam rates to zero and buy bonds. The economy could not tolerate positive real rates, so it did not have them. Every modern crisis — 2008, 2020, and the next one, whenever it comes — produces the same response: emergency rate cuts, money creation, and real yields pinned below zero to keep a debt-soaked system afloat. [How monetary policy moves commodities, real rates, and gold](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold) walks through the transmission.

The bull's claim is therefore probabilistic and structural: over a ten-year horizon, *the base rate of a recession or financial accident is high* — historically the US has a recession roughly every 6-8 years — and every one of those events forces real rates back down, which is rocket fuel for gold. You do not have to predict the timing. You only have to accept that in a decade, at least one and probably two negative-real-rate episodes are nearly certain, and that gold's biggest historical rallies (2008-2011, 2019-2020) happened in exactly those windows. The bull does not need real rates to be low *today*; they need them to *visit zero again at least once* in the next ten years, which is close to a sure thing.

Put the four pillars together and you have the serious bull case: a price-insensitive structural buyer (central banks), a slow one-directional reserve shift (de-dollarization), a debt load that all but guarantees financial repression (debasement), and the near-certainty of at least one negative-real-rate crisis (the rate cycle). None of these is a slogan. Each is in the data. That is why the bull case deserves a fair hearing — and why it is not enough on its own.

## The bear case, at its strongest

Now flip it. The serious bear case is not "gold is a useless rock" — that is the cartoon, and the cartoon is wrong, because a rock that has held value for 5,000 years is clearly doing *something*. The serious bear case accepts that gold has a role and still argues it is a poor bet *at this price, in this rate environment, over this horizon.* It rests on four pillars of its own.

### Bear pillar 1: positive real rates are gold's natural predator

Start with the master variable. For most of the last forty years, when the real yield on a 10-year TIPS rose, gold fell, and when it fell, gold rose. The mechanism is the opportunity cost we defined in the Foundations: gold pays nothing, so its competition is the real return on safe assets. When that real return is +2%, the bar gold has to clear is high — every year you hold gold instead of TIPS, you forgo 2% of guaranteed, inflation-protected, government-backed purchasing power.

![Gold versus the 10-year real yield with the real-yield axis inverted, showing the historical inverse link and the 2022 to 2026 decoupling](/imgs/blogs/the-bull-case-vs-the-bear-case-for-gold-over-the-next-decade-3.png)

The chart shows the relationship, with the real-yield axis inverted so that, historically, the two lines should track. From 2013 to 2021 they did, beautifully: real yields fell, gold climbed; real yields rose, gold stalled. The 2020-2021 gold high coincided exactly with the −1% real yield trough. Then look at the shaded band: from 2022 onward, real yields surged to +2% — territory that should have crushed gold — and instead gold *ripped to new highs.* This is the famous decoupling, and the bear has a sober reading of it: **the relationship has not been repealed; it has been suspended.** The central-bank bid is currently overpowering the real-rate signal. But if the central-bank bid ever fades — if reserve managers slow down, or turn sellers in a funding crunch — the gravity of +2% real rates is still there underneath, and it pulls down. The bear says: you are paying a record price for an asset whose oldest, most reliable headwind is currently at its *strongest level in over a decade*, and you are betting that an unusually intense new buyer keeps overriding it forever. That is a fragile bet.

### Bear pillar 2: opportunity cost compounds — and gold's doesn't

This is the bear's deepest and least emotional point, and it is worth slowing down for. Gold's defining feature is that it produces no income. A stock pays dividends and grows earnings; a bond pays coupons; cash pays interest. Gold pays *nothing* — and worse, it costs something to hold (storage, insurance, or an ETF's expense ratio, roughly 0.4% a year). Over short windows this is invisible. Over a decade, the foregone yield compounds into a mountain.

> #### Worked example: the opportunity-cost mountain
>
> You have \$100,000 to deploy for ten years. Option A: buy gold. Option B: buy 10-year TIPS yielding a +2% real return, with coupons reinvested. Ignore price changes in the gold and just measure the *yield you gave up.* The TIPS grow at 2% real, compounded: \$100,000 × (1.02)^10 = **\$121,899** in real, inflation-adjusted purchasing power — a guaranteed \$21,899 gain, backed by the US government, with zero price risk if held to maturity. The gold, meanwhile, has paid you \$0 in income and cost you roughly 0.4% a year to store — call it about \$4,000 over the decade. So before any price move, gold starts the race **about \$26,000 behind** the bond on a \$100,000 stake. For gold merely to *match* the boring TIPS, its price has to rise about 26% over the decade just to cover the yield it didn't pay and the cost it did. The intuition: gold's zero coupon is not free — it is a 2%-a-year handicap that compounds, and at high real rates that handicap is brutal.

This is why the bear is unmoved by "gold went up a lot." The question is never the nominal price; it is the price *relative to what the same money would have earned safely.* At zero or negative real rates, the handicap vanishes and gold is competitive. At +2% real, the handicap is a structural drag that the gold price has to outrun every single year just to break even against a Treasury bill.

### Bear pillar 3: the dollar has no credible rival

The bull's de-dollarization story is real, but the bear has a powerful rejoinder: *diversifying away from the dollar is not the same as replacing it.* Yes, the dollar's reserve share has fallen 14 points. But to what? The euro is the currency of a union with no common fiscal authority and a recurring sovereign-debt fragility. The yen belongs to an economy with debt at 250% of GDP and decades of stagnation. The renminbi sits behind capital controls — China will not let money flow freely in and out, which is precisely what a reserve currency must allow — so it remains barely 2% of reserves despite China being the world's second-largest economy. There is no incumbent-in-waiting. [The dollar system: why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) lays out why the network effects are so sticky.

The bear's conclusion: the dollar will remain the dominant reserve and trade currency for the entire next decade, simply because there is nothing to replace it. And a dominant, resilient dollar is a headwind for gold both mechanically (a strong dollar means a lower gold price in dollars) and structurally (the flight-from-the-dollar trade has no urgency if the dollar keeps working). The de-dollarization trend is real but slow, and "slow" over a ten-year horizon may not be enough to overcome +2% real rates and a 26% opportunity-cost handicap. The bull is betting on a trend that moves at the speed of geology.

### Bear pillar 4: over the long run, gold barely beats inflation

This is the bear's anchor, and it is the hardest fact in the whole debate for the bull to answer. Strip away the recent fireworks and look at gold's *entire* track record since the dollar left the gold standard in 1971.

![Real annual returns since 1971 with US stocks at 7 percent, gold at 3 percent, bonds at 2.5 percent, and gold's 1971 to 2000 sub-period negative](/imgs/blogs/the-bull-case-vs-the-bear-case-for-gold-over-the-next-decade-7.png)

Over roughly 1971-2024, gold returned about **3% per year after inflation.** That beats cash (about 0.4% real) and edges out bonds (about 2.5% real), but it is *less than half* the roughly 7% real return of US stocks. Over 50 years, that gap is staggering: a dollar in stocks compounding at 7% real becomes about \$29 in today's purchasing power; a dollar in gold at 3% becomes about \$4.4. The bear's point is blunt: **gold is, over the long run, a wealth *preserver*, not a wealth *builder*.** It roughly tracks the purchasing power of money. That is genuinely valuable — but it is not a reason to own a large position, and it is certainly not a reason to chase it at a record high.

And there is a sharper version hiding in that chart. Look at the two gold sub-periods. From 1971 to 2000, gold's real return was *negative* — about −0.5% per year. From 2001 to 2024, it was about +7.5% per year, roughly matching stocks. The entire 50-year "3% real" average is the blend of one terrible 30-year stretch and one great 24-year stretch. This is the bear's most damning observation: **gold's returns are not a steady compounding stream; they are a few explosive regimes separated by long, brutal winters.** [The twenty-year winter, 1980 to 2000](/blog/trading/gold/the-twenty-year-winter-1980-to-2000-when-gold-did-nothing) is the cautionary tale — two decades in which gold went *nowhere* while stocks compounded relentlessly. If you buy gold at a record high and the next regime is a winter, you could wait 20 years to get your money back in real terms. That has happened before, in living memory. The bear is not saying it *will* happen; they are saying you are not being paid enough, at +2% real rates and a record price, to take that risk in size.

The bear sharpens the knife with a point about *volatility and timing*. Gold has roughly the same year-to-year volatility as the stock market — it can fall 20-30% in a bad year — but, unlike stocks, it has no earnings growth underneath to pull the price back up over time. So you are taking equity-like risk for bond-like average returns, and the *path* matters enormously: gold went from \$850 in January 1980 to under \$300 by 1999, a *64% nominal loss* over nineteen years (and far worse after inflation). An investor who bought the 1980 top did not just earn a mediocre return; they sat on a deep, grinding, decades-long loss while watching every other asset class compound. The bear's warning is precise: buying a high-volatility, zero-yield asset *at a record high* exposes you to exactly that path-risk, and the only thing that has rescued gold buyers historically is a subsequent monetary crisis — which is to say, you are implicitly betting on the bull's scenario just to avoid a loss. If the bull is wrong, the bear's worst case is not "flat returns"; it is a multi-year drawdown in size.

One more bear observation deserves airtime because it cuts at the bull's psychology. The bull case has become *consensus* — central-bank buying is on every strategist's slide, de-dollarization is a magazine cover, and gold at a record high is the trade everyone agrees with. Markets have a long history of punishing the comfortable consensus, because by the time a thesis is universally accepted, the buyers it would attract have largely already bought. The bear does not need the bull's facts to be wrong; they only need the facts to be *fully priced*. At \$5,400, after a near-tripling, the bear argues a great deal of the structural-demand story is already in the price — which means the *surprises* from here are asymmetric: a continuation merely confirms what everyone expects, while any disappointment (central banks pause, a recession is avoided, real rates stay high without a crisis) lands on a crowded, expensive position with a long way to fall.

Put the four bear pillars together: the master variable (real rates) is at its most hostile level in over a decade, the opportunity cost compounds into a 26% handicap, the dollar has no replacement, and the long-run return barely beats inflation with a track record of decades-long droughts. That is a serious case. It is not a sneer at gold; it is a sober argument that gold at this price, in this environment, is an expensive insurance policy — which is exactly the frame we will land on.

## The swing variables that decide it

Notice what just happened. The bull and the bear are not really disagreeing about *facts* — they agree central banks are buying, they agree real rates are high, they agree the dollar is sticky, they agree gold's long-run return is modest. They are disagreeing about *which forces win over the next ten years.* And when you lay the two cases side by side, three variables do almost all the work. Everything else is commentary.

![Three swing variables — real rates, central-bank flows, and the dollar — each tilting gold toward the bull or the bear depending on their setting](/imgs/blogs/the-bull-case-vs-the-bear-case-for-gold-over-the-next-decade-4.png)

**Swing variable 1: real interest rates.** If the 10-year real yield stays at +2% or climbs higher, the bear's opportunity-cost gravity wins, and gold struggles to justify a record price. If real yields fall back toward or below zero — which every crisis produces — the bull wins, because gold's handicap vanishes and its biggest historical rallies are unlocked. This is the master switch.

**Swing variable 2: central-bank flows.** If official buying stays above 1,000 tonnes a year, the bull has a standing price floor that can override even high real rates (it has, for three years). If buying fades back toward the old 400-tonne normal — or, worse, if a funding crunch turns some central banks into sellers — the floor disappears and the real-rate gravity reasserts itself.

**Swing variable 3: the dollar.** If the dollar stays strong and no reserve rival emerges, the bear's headwind persists and de-dollarization stays too slow to matter on a decade horizon. If the dollar weakens and its reserve share keeps slipping below 58%, the bull's flight-from-the-dollar trade keeps feeding gold.

Here is the insight that the side-by-side framing reveals and that neither tribe likes to admit: **these three variables are not independent, and they tend to move together.** Consider the bull's nightmare-scenario-for-the-bear: a debt crisis or a sharp recession forces the Federal Reserve to cut rates and resume bond buying. That single event drives real rates *down* (variable 1 to the bull), tends to weaken the dollar (variable 3 to the bull), and — because a crisis is exactly when reserve managers want apolitical, unfreezable reserves — sustains or accelerates central-bank gold buying (variable 2 to the bull). All three tilt the same way at once. The bull case is, at its core, a bet that the next decade contains at least one such event — and given a debt load near 123% of GDP and a recession base rate of one every 6-8 years, that is not a wild bet.

Now run it the other way — the bear's dream. The US grows steadily, inflation is genuinely tamed back to 2%, the Fed holds real rates at a healthy +2%, the dollar stays king because the alternatives keep disappointing, and central banks — having rebuilt their gold allocations — slow their buying back toward normal. All three variables tilt to the bear, and gold spends the decade in a long, grinding winter, barely beating inflation while stocks compound. This, too, is not a wild scenario; it is roughly what happened from 1980 to 2000.

The honest position is that **you cannot know which scenario the next ten years will deliver**, because it depends on the path of inflation, politics, war, and policy — none of which is forecastable with any reliability. What you *can* do is watch the three swing variables and notice that they are correlated, so the realistic outcomes are not a smooth bell curve but two clusters: a "bull regime" where all three line up for gold, and a "bear regime" where all three line up against it, with a muddier middle. That structure — bimodal, correlated, genuinely uncertain — is exactly the kind of risk you manage with sizing, not with prediction. Hold that thought; it is where we are going.

## What would prove each side wrong: the falsifiers

A thesis you cannot disprove is not a thesis — it is a religion. The fastest way to tell a serious analyst from a tribal one is to ask: *what would change your mind?* The perma-bull who says "gold is going to \$10,000 and any decline is just manipulation" has built an unfalsifiable belief; so has the perma-bear who calls every gold rally a bubble on its way to bursting. Let us do better and name, concretely and in advance, the observations that would kill each case.

![Two columns of concrete falsifiers — what would kill the bull case and what would kill the bear case — feeding into a base-case verdict](/imgs/blogs/the-bull-case-vs-the-bear-case-for-gold-over-the-next-decade-6.png)

**What would kill the bull case:**

- *Central banks stop buying.* If net official purchases fall below roughly 400 tonnes a year for two straight years — back to the pre-2022 normal — the bull's central pillar is gone. The "new regime" would have been a three-year reaction to one geopolitical shock, not a structural shift.
- *Real rates stay high and gold finally cracks.* If the 10-year real yield holds above +2% *and* gold falls more than 30% from its high and stays down, the decoupling will have been temporary, and the old master-variable relationship will have reasserted itself. The bull's "this time is different" will have been wrong.
- *The fiscal scare reverses.* If US deficits shrink below 3% of GDP and the debt-to-GDP ratio turns down for several years, the debasement thesis loses its engine. Gold's monetary-insurance premium would deflate as the thing it insures against recedes.

**What would kill the bear case:**

- *Gold keeps rising with high real rates.* If real yields stay above +1.5% and gold makes new highs for years — if the 2022-26 decoupling simply *holds* — then the opportunity-cost model the bear leans on has been structurally overridden, and "high real rates cap gold" is no longer true.
- *Central-bank buying becomes permanent.* If the >1,000-tonne pace persists for a full decade, then the standing official bid is not a phase; it is the new market structure, and it absorbs new supply at any reasonable real rate. The bear's assumption that the bid fades will have been refuted.
- *The dollar's reserve role keeps eroding.* If the USD reserve share falls below 50% and gold's share of official reserves keeps climbing, the de-dollarization trend the bear dismissed as "too slow" will have crossed into a self-reinforcing shift, and gold's re-monetization will be a fact, not a forecast.

Now look at what this exercise does. Each side's falsifiers are **observable, dated, and specific** — you can check them against real data as the decade unfolds. And crucially, *most of them have not fired yet.* Central banks are still buying. Real rates are high but gold has not cracked. The fiscal picture is deteriorating, not improving. The decoupling is holding. The dollar's share is still sliding. As of mid-2026, the data is, if anything, leaning toward the bull's falsifiers *not* triggering — but the bear's strongest pillar (the long-run return and the risk of a winter) is a statement about the *future distribution*, which no current data point can confirm or deny. The honest reading: the bull case is, so far, surviving its falsification tests, while the bear case rests on a structural prior that gold reverts to its modest long-run norm. Neither has been disproven. That is precisely why the answer is not a side but a size.

## A balanced base case

So what do you actually expect? Not a point forecast — anyone who gives you a single number for gold in 2036 is selling something. But you can build an honest base case as a *distribution of outcomes*, weighting the bull and bear regimes by how plausible each looks.

Here is a fair framing. Over the next decade, there are roughly three regimes:

1. **The bull regime (call it ~35-40% likely):** at least one significant negative-real-rate crisis hits, debasement continues, central banks keep buying, the dollar slips. Gold compounds well above inflation — think mid-to-high single digits real, with the price potentially well above today's level. This is the 2001-2024 experience repeating.
2. **The muddy middle (~35-40% likely):** real rates stay positive but not punishing, central-bank buying continues but moderates, the dollar drifts. Gold roughly holds its real value — preserves purchasing power, beats cash, lags stocks. A 1-3% real return, choppy, neither hero nor villain.
3. **The bear regime (~25-30% likely):** inflation is genuinely tamed, real rates stay high, the dollar stays king, central-bank buying fades. Gold enters a winter — flat to down in real terms for years, the 1980-2000 experience repeating. You bought at a record high and waited a long time to be made whole.

> #### Worked example: the probability-weighted expectation
>
> Assign rough real-return numbers to each regime and weight them. Suppose the bull regime (38% likely) delivers +7% real per year, the middle (37%) delivers +1.5% real, and the bear regime (25%) delivers −2% real (a long winter). The probability-weighted expected real return is: (0.38 × 7%) + (0.37 × 1.5%) + (0.25 × −2%) = 2.66% + 0.56% − 0.50% = **about +2.7% real per year.** That is — strikingly — almost exactly gold's long-run historical average of ~3% real. The intuition: a fair, balanced base case does not say "gold to the moon" or "gold to zero"; it lands right back on gold's multi-decade norm of *barely beating inflation, with a wide spread of outcomes around it.* The expected return is mediocre; the *value* is in the shape of the distribution — gold pays off in regime 1, exactly when your stocks and bonds are suffering.

That last sentence is the whole game. Gold's expected return is unexciting. But its *payoff is concentrated in the bad states of the world* — the negative-real-rate crisis, the debasement scare, the dollar wobble — which are precisely the states where the rest of your portfolio is bleeding. An asset with a mediocre average return whose good years align with everyone else's bad years is not valued for its return. It is valued as **insurance.** And that reframes the entire decision.

It is worth being precise about why a mediocre *expected* return does not kill the case, because this is where most people's intuition fails them. In standard investing logic, an asset that is expected to return only ~2.7% real — barely above bonds, far below stocks — looks like a clear "skip." That logic is correct *for an asset whose returns are uncorrelated with timing or independent of your other holdings*. Gold is neither. Its return is back-loaded into the exact scenarios (regime 1) where your stocks and bonds are down 20-40%, and that timing is worth far more than the average return suggests. The technical name is that gold has *negative covariance with your portfolio in the left tail* — it pays you when payment is most valuable. A dollar of gain in a year your portfolio fell 30% is worth much more, in utility and in your ability to rebalance, than a dollar of gain in a year everything rose. So the right way to read the +2.7% expected real return is not "a poor investment" but "a fairly priced insurance premium": you expect to roughly break even on the metal over the decade, and the *real* payoff is the protection it provides in the bad regime, which the average return does not capture.

The muddy middle deserves its own honest look, because it is the most *likely* single regime and the one the shouting tribes both ignore. In the middle, nothing dramatic happens: inflation hovers around 3%, real rates stay positive but not punishing, the Fed neither slams rates to zero nor holds them brutally high, central banks keep buying but at a calmer pace, and the dollar drifts sideways. Gold in this world is *boring* — it roughly tracks inflation, gives you a low-single-digit real return, chops around in a range, and quietly lags the stock market over the decade. This is the regime in which the bear "wins" on returns but the bull "wins" on the metal-doesn't-collapse point. For a sized 5-10% holder it is a non-event: the sleeve does its diversification job, you rebalance the wiggles, and the modest drag versus stocks is invisible against the rest of your portfolio. The middle is precisely why *sizing* beats *conviction* — it is the outcome that makes a giant bet either way look foolish in hindsight and a small sized position look exactly right.

## Common misconceptions

Two beliefs poison the gold debate from opposite ends. Both feel sophisticated. Both are wrong, and the numbers say so.

### Misconception 1: "It's obviously going to \$10,000 — the dollar is dying"

This is the perma-bull error. It takes the real bull pillars — central-bank buying, debasement, de-dollarization — and inflates them into a certainty and a price target. The problem is twofold. First, the de-dollarization trend, as we saw, moves at the speed of geology: 14 percentage points in 20 years. At that pace the dollar is still the dominant reserve currency in 2046. "The dollar is dying" is not supported by the data the bull's own case rests on. Second, and more important, *a price target is a category error.* Gold has no cash flows, so there is no fundamental anchor that says it is "worth" \$10,000 or \$3,000. The only honest statement is conditional: *if* real rates go to −2% and central banks keep buying and the dollar slips, *then* gold is much higher. Stating the conditional outcome as an unconditional certainty is exactly the overconfidence that gets people to put 40% of their net worth into a single non-yielding asset at a record high — the worst version of the bull's own thesis.

> #### Worked example: why "obviously going to \$10,000" is overconfidence
>
> A \$10,000 target from today's ~\$5,400 is an 85% gain. For that to be "obvious," you would need the bull regime to be near-certain. But we just estimated the bull regime at roughly 38% likely, the muddy middle at 37%, and the bear winter at 25%. Even granting the bull's own most optimistic inputs, the *probability* of the \$10,000 outcome is well under half. Sizing a position as if a 38%-likely outcome were certain is the textbook definition of betting too big. If you put \$200,000 (40% of a \$500,000 portfolio) into gold on a \$10,000 conviction and the bear winter arrives instead, you could sit on a real loss for a decade with two-fifths of your wealth — a catastrophic, unforced error. The intuition: the bull case can be *right* and a 40% position can still be *reckless*, because the case is probabilistic, not certain.

### Misconception 2: "It's obviously a bubble about to pop"

This is the perma-bear error, and it is just as overconfident in the other direction. The argument: gold has tripled in three years, it pays no yield, real rates are high, therefore it is a mania that must mean-revert. The flaw is that a "bubble" requires the price to be untethered from any fundamental driver — and gold's rise is *tethered* to a real, measurable, persistent force: more than 1,000 tonnes a year of price-insensitive central-bank buying that did not exist before 2022. A price driven by a structural new buyer is not a bubble; it is a repricing. The bear who calls it a bubble is making the same mistake as the bull who calls it a regime change — confusing a possibility with a certainty. The fact that gold *could* enter a winter (it could) does not mean it is in a bubble *now* (it need not be). And calling a top because the price went up a lot is how bears missed the entire 2001-2011 and 2019-2026 advances.

### Misconception 3: "Real rates are positive, so gold must fall"

A subtler version, and the most defensible of the three — but still a mistake when stated as a law. It is true that high positive real rates are gold's historical predator. But the 2022-2026 decoupling is now four years long, which is too long to dismiss as noise. The honest statement is not "real rates are positive, so gold falls" (the data refutes it) but "real rates are positive, so gold faces a *headwind* that is currently being *overpowered* by central-bank demand — and if that demand fades, the headwind wins." That is a conditional, and conditionals are what serious analysis trades in. Treating a strong tendency as an iron law is how the bear got run over for the last four years.

## How it shows up in real markets

Strip the theory away and watch how this debate actually played out in prices, because the recent record is the cleanest test we have of both cases.

**2020-2021: the bull's textbook.** Real yields hit −1.0%, the Fed printed money, the dollar weakened, and gold ran to a then-record \$2,075 in August 2020. Every bull driver fired at once. This is the regime-1 experience, and it is what the bull is betting recurs.

**2022: the bear's textbook — almost.** Real yields surged from −1% to +1.6% in a single year as the Fed hiked at the fastest pace in 40 years. By the old playbook, gold should have been crushed. It finished the year at −0.3% — essentially *flat*. Note carefully what this does to *both* cases. It is bad for the simple bear ("real rates up, gold down" failed), but it is also not a roaring bull year — gold did not rise, it just refused to fall. What held it up? The first full year of >1,000-tonne central-bank buying. This single year is the hinge of the whole modern debate: it is the moment the central-bank bid demonstrably overrode the real-rate signal.

**2023-2026: the decoupling extends.** Real yields stayed high — +1.7%, +2.2%, +2.0% — and gold did not just hold; it *soared*, from ~\$2,000 to ~\$5,400, more than doubling while its supposed master headwind sat at a decade-high. The bull points to this as proof the regime has changed. The bear points out that no decoupling lasts forever and that the higher gold goes against +2% real rates, the more violent the eventual reversion if the central-bank bid ever falters. Both are looking at the same chart and drawing opposite lessons — which is the entire reason this post exists.

> #### Worked example: sizing under genuine uncertainty — a \$500,000 portfolio
>
> You have \$500,000 and you accept that the bull and bear cases are *both* serious and you genuinely cannot tell which decade you are about to live through. What position do you take? Run the three options.
>
> **0% gold (the perma-bear).** If the bull regime arrives — a debasement crisis, real rates to −2%, gold doubling while stocks fall 30% — you own zero of the one asset built for that world. Your \$500,000 of stocks and bonds takes the full hit with no offset. You were *right* about gold's mediocre average return and *catastrophically exposed* in the one regime where that didn't matter.
>
> **30% gold (the perma-bull).** \$150,000 in gold. If the bear winter arrives — high real rates, strong dollar, gold flat-to-down for a decade — nearly a third of your wealth sits dead, earning less than cash, while stocks compound at 7% real without you. Over ten years the opportunity cost on \$150,000 versus stocks compounding is enormous — well over \$150,000 of foregone real wealth. You were *right* that gold could rise and *recklessly concentrated* in an asset that might not.
>
> **7.5% gold (the sized middle).** \$37,500 in gold, \$462,500 in stocks and bonds. In the bull regime, the gold sleeve surges — say it doubles to \$75,000 — adding a \$37,500 cushion exactly when your other \$462,500 is suffering; you rebalance, selling some gold high. In the bear winter, the \$37,500 underperforms, but it is *less than a thirteenth* of your portfolio, so the drag is a rounding error against your compounding stocks. In every regime you are fine. The intuition: a 5-10% gold sleeve is the only choice that is not catastrophic in *either* tail — it costs you a little in the bear world and helps you a lot in the bull world, which is the literal definition of a good insurance premium.

That worked example is the answer to the entire post. The 0% and 30% positions are both *bets* — bets that you know which decade is coming. The 7.5% position is *insurance* — an admission that you don't. And the data we have says you genuinely don't: the bull's falsifiers haven't fired, the bear's structural prior hasn't been disproven, and the swing variables could break either way.

## The takeaway: sizing over conviction

Here is the spine of this whole series, and it lands hardest in exactly this debate: **gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability.** Read the bull and bear cases through that lens and the apparent contradiction dissolves.

The bull case is, at bottom, the argument that *the premium on that insurance policy has risen and will stay elevated*, because the things it insures against — debasement, reserve-currency risk, financial repression — have genuinely gotten worse and more structural since 2022. The bear case is the argument that *insurance you don't end up needing is just a cost*, and at +2% real rates and a record price, the premium is expensive and the long-run return is mediocre. Notice that these are not contradictory. They are both *true*. The premium has risen (the bull is right) *and* an expensive premium drags your return if the disaster doesn't come (the bear is right). The resolution is not to pick a side. It is to recognize that you are pricing insurance under genuine uncertainty, and the correct response to that is never "all in" or "none" — it is **the right size.**

What is the right size? For almost everyone, the honest answer the evidence supports is **roughly 5-10% of a long-term portfolio** — enough that the gold sleeve meaningfully cushions you in the bull regime (a doubling of 7.5% adds a real 7.5% to your whole portfolio precisely when stocks are down 30%), and small enough that a bear-regime winter is a rounding error rather than a wound. [Gold's job in a portfolio](/blog/trading/gold/golds-job-in-a-portfolio-sizing-rebalancing-and-the-permanent-portfolio) does the full allocation math; the point here is that the bull-bear debate, taken seriously, *delivers you to a sizing decision, not a price forecast.*

So the next time someone tells you gold is "obviously" going to \$10,000 or is "obviously" a bubble, you now know what to say: *both of you are looking at real forces, and neither of you knows which decade is coming.* The structural bull drivers are real and measurable. The bear's gravity — high real rates, opportunity cost, a sticky dollar, a modest long-run return — is real and measurable. The swing variables that decide it are correlated and unforecastable. The falsifiers for each side are sitting there, mostly untriggered. In that situation, conviction is not insight; it is overconfidence wearing the costume of insight. The insight is humility expressed as position size.

Gold near \$5,400 is insurance whose premium has risen because the world has more to insure against than it did a decade ago. You don't refuse fire insurance because you're sure the house won't burn, and you don't insure a \$500,000 house for \$5 million because you're sure it will. You buy the right amount. Own gold sized, not as a directional bet — and let the people shouting price targets argue with each other while you collect the diversification, rebalance the sleeve, and sleep.
