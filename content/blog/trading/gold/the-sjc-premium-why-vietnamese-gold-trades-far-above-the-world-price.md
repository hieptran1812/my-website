> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The SJC premium is the gap between the price of a gold bar inside Vietnam and the same metal's world-equivalent value in dong; for years it was enormous because the law forbids importing the gold that would close it.
>
> - Convert the world price into dong terms — \$/oz times 1.20565 (ounces per luong) times the USD/VND rate — and you get the world-equivalent price. The SJC premium is everything the domestic price sits *above* that.
> - Four forces stack the premium: an import wall (no legal supply to arbitrage in), an SJC brand monopoly, FX pressure as the dong weakens against the dollar, and captive local demand.
> - In mid-2024 the premium blew out to roughly 18-19 million VND per luong — about 25%, or around \$750 — before the State Bank crushed it back to single digits.
> - The one number to remember: a wide premium is not mostly about gold. It is a thermometer of dong-versus-dollar stress, and it reads hottest exactly when Vietnamese savers most distrust their own currency.

In the second week of May 2024, a luong of SJC gold — the standard Vietnamese unit, a bar weighing 37.5 grams — sold for about 92 million VND. At the exchange rate of the day, roughly 25,400 dong to the dollar, that was about \$3,620. The very same quantity of gold, bought as a plain bar in Singapore or Hong Kong, cost about \$2,879. A Vietnamese buyer was paying roughly \$740 more — about 19 million VND — for an identical lump of metal, simply because it was being bought inside Vietnam.

In any normal market, that gap would not survive a single afternoon. Someone would buy the cheap bar abroad, carry it across the border, sell it dear at home, and pocket the difference. They would keep doing it until the flood of imported gold pushed the domestic price back down to the world price. That is the machine — called arbitrage — that keeps the price of a fungible commodity roughly the same everywhere on earth. The price of a barrel of oil, an ounce of silver, a bushel of wheat: all kept in line by people who get rich closing gaps.

The SJC premium survived not for an afternoon but for years. The reason is the most important fact in the whole Vietnamese gold market: **the trade that would close the gap is illegal.** Private gold imports were effectively banned. The arbitrage machine was unplugged. And once you understand that one fact, the entire premium — its size, its persistence, its sudden collapse in 2024 — falls into place.

Why would a country deliberately wall off its gold market and accept a premium that taxes its own savers? The answer reaches back to the early 2010s, when Vietnam's gold market looked very different. Gold was freely imported and several brands of bar competed; households held enormous quantities of it; and in the inflationary, currency-wobbling years around 2011, Vietnamese savers were converting dong into gold and dollars on a scale that drained the country's foreign reserves and undermined the State Bank's control of the currency. The policy response, codified in Decree 24 of 2012, was to "anti-goldize" the economy — to break gold's role as a parallel currency by monopolizing the bar, banning private imports, and discouraging gold as a savings instrument. The wall, in other words, was not an accident or an oversight; it was a deliberate trade. The State Bank accepted a chronic domestic premium as the price of stopping gold demand from continuously bleeding the nation's dollars. The premium we are about to decompose is the standing cost of that bargain — and the 2024 blowout was the moment the cost came due all at once.

![Decomposing the SJC premium into the world price plus four surcharges](/imgs/blogs/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price-1.png)

This post is the analytical core of the Vietnam track. We are going to take the premium apart piece by piece: define exactly what it is, do the arithmetic that turns a dollar-per-ounce world price into a dong-per-luong domestic benchmark, identify the four forces that drive the gap, walk through the 2024 blowout with real numbers, and then make the argument that matters most — that the premium is less a story about gold than a live readout of how much pressure the dong is under. The whole series rests on one idea: gold is not an investment that compounds, it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money. The SJC premium is that vote, cast by ninety million people who have lived through currency they could not trust, and priced in the one market where they were locked in.

## Foundations: what a premium is, and the conversion that defines it

Start with the word. A **premium**, in any market, is the amount by which one price sits above a reference price. If a concert ticket has a face value of 100 dollars and scalpers sell it for 160, the scalper's premium is 60 dollars, or 60%. The premium exists because the official channel cannot supply everyone who wants in, so a parallel price forms above the official one.

Gold has a single world reference price, set continuously in the deep, liquid London and New York markets and quoted in **US dollars per troy ounce**. (A troy ounce is the historical unit for precious metals, about 31.1 grams — heavier than the ordinary kitchen ounce. The mechanics of how that world price is set are covered in [How gold is priced: spot, the London fix, futures, and the troy ounce](/blog/trading/gold/how-gold-is-priced-spot-the-london-fix-futures-and-the-troy-ounce).) Every local gold market on earth quotes some version of that same number, translated into local currency and local weight units, plus whatever local frictions apply.

The **SJC premium** is the gap between the price of an SJC-brand gold bar inside Vietnam and the **world-equivalent price** — what that same gold *should* cost in dong if Vietnam's market were perfectly connected to the world's. To compute it, you have to translate the world price into Vietnamese terms, and that translation has exactly two steps.

**Step one: fix the weight unit.** Vietnamese gold is priced not per ounce but per **luong** (also spelled *cay* or, in the old colonial term, *tael*). One luong is 37.5 grams. Since a troy ounce is about 31.1035 grams, one luong is `37.5 / 31.1035 = 1.20565` troy ounces. That constant — **1.20565 ounces per luong** — is the single most-used number in this post. Whenever you want to turn an ounce price into a luong price, you multiply by it.

**Step two: fix the currency.** The world price is in dollars; the domestic price is in dong. So you multiply by the **USD/VND** exchange rate — the number of dong it takes to buy one dollar, around 25,000-26,000 in recent years. Put the two steps together and you have the master conversion of the entire Vietnamese gold market:

> **World-equivalent price (VND per luong) = (world price in \$/oz) × 1.20565 × (USD/VND rate)**

That formula is the spine of everything that follows. The SJC premium is simply the domestic SJC price minus that world-equivalent number. If the SJC bar trades right at the world-equivalent, the premium is zero and the market is well-connected. If it trades far above, the premium is large and something is blocking the connection.

#### Worked example: turning the world price into dong

Take the 2024 average world gold price of \$2,388 per troy ounce. Convert it:

- Ounces per luong: `2,388 × 1.20565 = 2,879` — so a luong of gold is worth about **\$2,879** at the world price.
- Now into dong, at 25,400 VND per dollar: `2,879 × 25,400 = 73,130,000` VND.

So the world-equivalent price of a luong in mid-2024 was about **73 million VND**, or **\$2,879**. That is the benchmark. Any Vietnamese gold price above 73 million VND that day carried a premium; the SJC bar at 92 million VND carried a big one. The intuition: the world price and the exchange rate together set the floor, and the premium is the surcharge stacked on top.

Notice what the conversion already tells you. The world-equivalent number moves for two reasons that have *nothing to do with the premium*: when the world gold price rises, and when the dong weakens (USD/VND climbs). A Vietnamese gold price can go up purely because gold went up in dollars, or purely because the dong fell — and neither of those is a premium. The premium is only the *extra* gap beyond what those two global forces explain. Keeping that distinction clean is the whole discipline of reading this market.

To see why the distinction matters, decompose a single move. Suppose the dong price of an SJC luong rises by 10 million VND over a quarter. A naive observer screams "the premium is exploding." But suppose that over the same quarter the world gold price rose 8% in dollars and the dong fell 3% against the dollar. Run those through the conversion and most of the 10-million-VND rise is simply the world-equivalent moving up; the *premium* — the residual after you strip out gold and FX — might have barely budged, or even shrunk. Conversely, a quarter where the dong price of gold is flat can hide a *widening* premium if the world price fell in dollars at the same time. The headline dong price is the sum of three things — world gold, the exchange rate, and the premium — and only the third is the Vietnam-specific story. A reader who cannot separate them will mistake global gold rallies for local dysfunction and vice versa.

This is also why quoting the premium in *percent* is usually more honest than quoting it in dong. Twelve million VND of premium sounds the same in 2020 and in 2026, but against a 50-million-VND world-equivalent it is 24%, while against a 172-million-VND world-equivalent it is 7%. As the world price has soared, a *constant* dong premium has become a *shrinking* percentage premium. The charts in this post show both the dong figure and the percentage for exactly this reason: the dong number tells you how much extra cash leaves a buyer's pocket, but the percentage tells you how dysfunctional the market actually is.

A practical caution on the conversion itself: which exchange rate you use matters. The State Bank publishes a managed "central rate," commercial banks quote a slightly higher rate, and the gray market quotes higher still. In calm times these differ by under a percent and the choice is immaterial. But in an FX-stress episode — exactly when the premium blows out — the gray-market dong/dollar rate can run several percent above the official one, because dollars are scarce. If you compute the world-equivalent with the *official* rate, you will overstate the premium (you under-converted the world price); if you use the *gray-market* rate, part of what looks like a smaller gold premium is really hidden FX premium. The cleanest read uses the freely-traded market rate, and treats any wedge between official and gray-market FX as part of the same currency-stress signal the gold premium is telling you about.

### Why arbitrage normally erases a premium — and why it cannot here

Here is the mechanism that makes premiums temporary in normal markets. Suppose gold trades at \$2,879 per luong-equivalent abroad but 92 million VND (\$3,620) inside Vietnam. A trader does this: buy a luong abroad for \$2,879, import it, sell it domestically for \$3,620, and bank about \$740 of risk-free profit. That trade is so attractive that *everyone* does it. The buying abroad nudges the world price up a touch; the selling at home, and the flood of new supply onto the domestic market, pushes the domestic price down hard. The gap shrinks. Traders keep going until the profit disappears — until the domestic price has fallen back to roughly the world-equivalent. This is the **law of one price**: a single fungible good, freely tradable across a border, must cost about the same on both sides, because any gap is a money-machine that arbitrageurs run until it closes.

The phrase that does all the work is *freely tradable across a border*. In Vietnam, gold is not. Under Decree 24 of 2012, private and commercial gold-bar imports were effectively shut off, and the right to produce the standard SJC bar was concentrated in the state (the full machinery is the subject of [SJC and Decree 24: how Vietnam built a gold-bar monopoly](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly)). With imports blocked, the arbitrageur's first step — *buy a luong abroad and bring it in* — is illegal. The money-machine cannot be switched on. So the gap does not close. The premium becomes not a temporary anomaly but a standing feature of the market, as durable as the law that creates it.

![Why arbitrage cannot close the premium because the import wall blocks the trade](/imgs/blogs/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price-6.png)

It is worth dwelling on *why* this is so unusual, because it runs against the instinct of anyone who has watched commodity markets. In nearly every traded good, a persistent 20% gap between two locations is impossible — it would be devoured in days. If New York crude were 20% cheaper than London crude, tankers would redirect; if silver were 20% dearer in Mumbai than Zurich, dealers would ship metal east until the gap closed. The cost of closing such a gap is just freight, insurance, and financing — typically a fraction of a percent for gold, which is dense, durable, and cheap to move (a luong is the size of a small chocolate bar). So the *natural* premium for Vietnamese gold, in a free market, would be perhaps 0.5-1%: the cost of getting bars physically into the country and refined to the local standard. Every percentage point above that is the price of the legal wall, not the price of logistics. When the premium hit 26%, roughly 25 of those points were pure regulatory scarcity — a number that says nothing about gold and everything about the law.

There is a second subtlety that the import ban creates: a *one-way valve*. Gold cannot legally come in, but gold inside Vietnam can be sold within Vietnam freely. So the domestic market can absorb selling pressure (holders dumping bars push the price *down* toward the world-equivalent) but cannot absorb buying pressure (no new bars can arrive to meet it). That asymmetry is why the premium spikes upward violently during demand surges but drifts down only slowly during calm — the wall lets the price fall back to fair value on its own time but slams shut against any force that would do so quickly. A market that can deflate but not inflate its supply will always carry a premium biased to the upside.

A high enough premium does, eventually, summon a shadow version of the blocked trade. When the gap is wide enough to clear the risk, gold gets smuggled — carried across borders outside the legal channel — and that gray-market flow is the *illicit* arbitrage that a legal market would have done in the open. But smuggling is capacity-constrained and risky, so it nibbles at the premium rather than erasing it: it caps how absurd the gap can get without ever closing it to the logistics-only level a free market would reach. The size of the legal premium is therefore also a rough measure of how much smuggling profit is on offer, and how hard the gray market is working — a link explored in the series' post on [smuggling, the gray market, and the gold-dollar-FX triangle](/blog/trading/gold/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam). The premium and the smuggling are two readings of the same pressure: one legal and visible, one illegal and hidden.

That is the foundation. A premium is a price above a reference; the reference here is the dong-converted world price; and the premium persists because the one trade that would erase it is forbidden. Everything else in this post is detail on *how big* the gap gets and *why* it moves.

## The four causes of the premium

Saying "the import ban causes the premium" is true but incomplete. The ban is the necessary condition — it is what lets a gap exist at all — but the *size* of the gap on any given day is set by four forces working together. Think of the import ban as the dam: it is what makes a reservoir possible. The other three forces decide how high the water rises behind it.

![The four causes of the SJC premium feeding into the gap](/imgs/blogs/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price-5.png)

**Cause one: the import wall.** This is the structural cause, the one without which none of the others could matter. With no legal channel to import gold, domestic supply is fixed — it is whatever bars already exist inside the country, plus recycled scrap and the trickle the State Bank chooses to release. When Vietnamese demand rises, that fixed supply cannot expand to meet it. In a normal market, rising demand pulls in imports and the price barely moves; in a walled market, rising demand has nowhere to go but straight into price. The wall converts every surge of demand into a surge of premium.

The economics here is the textbook picture of inelastic supply. Picture a supply curve: in an open market it is nearly flat, because at any price meaningfully above the world level, imports flood in and hold the price down — supply expands almost without limit at the world price. The import ban turns that flat curve vertical: above the world-equivalent, no extra gold can appear at *any* price, because the law forbids the imports that would supply it. When you slide a rising demand curve along a flat supply curve, the price barely moves and the quantity rises. When you slide that same rising demand along a *vertical* supply curve, the quantity cannot rise at all, so the entire adjustment falls on price. The premium is the vertical distance the price is forced to climb to choke demand back down to the fixed supply. This is not a Vietnam quirk; it is what happens to *any* good whose supply is capped while demand grows — concert tickets, rent-controlled apartments, a famine's grain. Gold just makes it unusually visible because there is a world price sitting right next door to compare against.

**Cause two: the SJC brand monopoly.** Decree 24 did not merely ban imports; it made the SJC bar — produced by the state-owned Saigon Jewelry Company — the only nationally recognized gold-bar brand, and it stopped new SJC bars from being minted for years. So the most trusted, most liquid form of gold in the country was also the most supply-constrained. Vietnamese savers came to treat the SJC bar as *the* gold, the one a pawnshop or bank would always take back at the best price. That trust premium, layered on top of a frozen supply of bars, meant SJC gold traded above not only the world price but above other domestic gold like rings and jewelry. A meaningful slice of the headline premium was specifically an *SJC-brand* premium, not a gold premium.

You can actually *see* this brand premium by comparing SJC bars to nhan — plain gold rings of the same 9999 purity, sold by jewelers, made of identical metal but not carrying the protected SJC brand. During the worst of the blowout, SJC bars traded several million dong per luong *above* gold rings, even though the gold inside was the same. That spread is pure brand-and-monopoly premium: it is what people paid for the liquidity and recognized resale value of the one bar the whole country trusts. It is also why the 2025 reforms, which aim to break SJC's exclusivity and let more bars qualify as standard, attack the premium at its source — widen the set of trusted bars and you dilute the brand monopoly that propped up part of the gap. (Which form of gold a Vietnamese investor should actually hold — bar versus ring versus jewelry — is the subject of the series' practical-playbook post.)

**Cause three: FX pressure.** This is the cause most people miss, and the one this post cares about most. When the dong weakens against the dollar, two things happen at once. First, the world-equivalent price mechanically rises (more dong per ounce), which is *not* premium. But second — and separately — a falling dong frightens savers. People who fear their currency is losing value rush into the assets that hold value: dollars, and gold. That rush of fearful buying, hitting a walled market with fixed supply, pushes the domestic gold price *above* even the FX-adjusted world-equivalent. So FX stress widens the premium twice over: once through the conversion math, and once through the panic it triggers. We will return to this; it is the key to reading the gap.

The reason FX fear flows so strongly into gold specifically — rather than spreading evenly across dollars, property, and bank deposits — is that Vietnam's other escape hatches are also partly closed. Holding large amounts of physical US dollars is discouraged and the formal FX market is rationed by the State Bank, so the dollar door is half-shut. Property is illiquid and lumpy; you cannot move a fifth of your savings into an apartment overnight. Bank deposits pay interest but are denominated in the very dong you are trying to escape. Gold, by contrast, is liquid, divisible down to a fraction of a luong, universally accepted, and — crucially — culturally pre-approved as the thing you flee into. So when fear of the dong rises, gold catches a disproportionate share of the flight, and because the gold market is walled, that concentrated demand has nowhere to vent but into the premium.

**Cause four: captive demand.** Vietnam has one of the highest rates of household gold ownership on earth — gold is the wedding gift, the Tet (lunar new year) tradition, the savings vehicle of a population that has lived through real currency collapses. (The cultural and historical roots are covered in the series' opener on gold in Vietnam.) That demand runs roughly 45-60 tonnes a year. Crucially, this demand is *captive*: a Vietnamese saver who wants gold cannot easily buy it abroad and cannot legally import it, so all that buying pressure lands inside the walled domestic market. Strong, structural, trapped demand pressing against fixed supply is a recipe for a persistent premium, and a recipe for the premium to spike whenever fear adds a demand surge on top.

These four are not independent additive bricks so much as forces that *reinforce* one another. The import wall makes supply inelastic; the brand monopoly concentrates trust into the single most supply-constrained instrument; FX fear injects demand surges; and captive demand guarantees those surges land entirely inside the wall. Remove any one and the premium shrinks: open imports and supply becomes elastic; break the brand monopoly and trust spreads across more bars; stabilize the dong and the fear surges stop; let demand escape abroad and the pressure vents elsewhere. The 2024-25 policy response, as we will see, worked precisely by attacking two of these legs at once — supplying gold (relieving the wall) and stabilizing expectations (relieving the fear) — which is why the premium fell so fast.

#### Worked example: how a demand surge becomes a premium in a walled market

Imagine domestic supply is fixed at, say, the equivalent of 50 tonnes a year of SJC bars and savers want exactly that much at a premium of 12 million VND per luong (the rough 2023 level). Now fear strikes — a wobble in the dong, a banking scare — and savers want 20% more gold. In an open market, dealers would import the extra 10 tonnes and the price would barely move. In the walled market, the extra 10 tonnes *cannot arrive*. The only thing that can give is price: the premium must rise until enough buyers are priced out and enough holders are tempted to sell that the fixed supply clears. Say that clearing point is a premium of 18-19 million VND per luong — the mid-2024 level. The premium rose by roughly 6-7 million VND (about \$250-270), *not* because gold changed but because trapped demand met a wall. The intuition: in a market that cannot import, the premium is the pressure-release valve, and it hisses loudest exactly when people are most afraid.

## The 2024 blowout

For most of the 2010s the SJC premium was uncomfortable but tolerable — often a few million dong per luong, occasionally more. Then in early-to-mid 2024 it exploded.

![The SJC premium in million dong per luong over time](/imgs/blogs/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price-2.png)

By April and May 2024 the SJC bar was selling for as much as 92.4 million VND per luong, while the world-equivalent — using the world price and the exchange rate of the day — sat near 73-74 million VND. The premium had blown out to roughly **18-19 million VND per luong**. As a percentage of the world price, that was about **25%**: a quarter again on top of fair value, for an identical metal.

![The SJC premium as a percent of the world-equivalent price](/imgs/blogs/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price-3.png)

Three things converged to produce that peak. The world gold price itself was surging — central-bank buying and a fragile macro backdrop drove the world price from under \$2,000 toward \$2,400 (the global story is told in [From the 2011 top to the 2024 breakout](/blog/trading/gold/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime)). That global surge lit the fuse on Vietnamese demand: when gold is visibly rocketing worldwide, captive savers pile in even harder. Meanwhile the dong was under pressure, sliding past 25,000 to the dollar, which both raised the world-equivalent and frightened savers into gold. And the SJC bar's frozen supply meant none of that demand could be met with new metal. The dam, the fixed supply, the FX fear, and the global rally hit at once. The premium did what a pressure valve does.

The blowout also fed on itself through a behavioral channel that any walled market eventually develops: the premium became a story. Vietnamese news ran daily gold-price headlines; queues formed outside SJC shops; the bar that "always goes up" became a one-way bet in the public mind. That narrative pulled in marginal buyers who would not normally hold gold — people buying *because the price was rising*, the classic momentum dynamic. In an open market, dealers would meet that frenzy with imports and the price would calm. In the walled market, the frenzy met the same fixed supply and pushed the premium higher still, which made better headlines, which pulled in more buyers. The premium did not just *reflect* demand; for a few months it manufactured its own, the way a bank run manufactures the insolvency it fears. This reflexivity is why walled-market premiums tend to overshoot — they climb past the level fundamentals alone would justify, until something (here, the State Bank) breaks the loop.

It is worth pausing on the human meaning of a 26% premium, because the abstraction hides it. A Vietnamese family buying a few luong of gold for a wedding — a completely ordinary, culturally expected purchase — was, in May 2024, handing over roughly a quarter again on top of fair value, several hundred dollars per luong, to a market structure they had no part in designing and no way around. They could not order the bar from Singapore; they could not wait for imports that would never come; they could either pay the premium or go without the gold their tradition called for. The premium was, in the most literal sense, a regressive tax on the savings habits of ordinary people, collected by the gap between a managed market and the world. That is why its political salience was so high, and why a central bank chose to spend reserves to bring it down.

#### Worked example: the May 2024 premium, in dong and dollars

Pin it down with the conversion. In early May 2024:

- World price ≈ \$2,388/oz. World-equivalent per luong = `2,388 × 1.20565 × 25,400 ≈ 73,130,000` VND ≈ **73 million VND** (≈ **\$2,879**).
- SJC sell price ≈ 92.4 million VND ≈ `92,400,000 / 25,400 ≈ ` **\$3,638**.
- Premium = `92.4 − 73.1 ≈ 19.3` **million VND**, which is `19,300,000 / 25,400 ≈ ` **\$760**.
- As a percent of the world price: `19.3 / 73.1 ≈ 26%`.

So a Vietnamese buyer in May 2024 was paying about **\$760** — roughly 19 million VND — over the world price for one luong of gold, a 26% surcharge. The intuition: the premium was not a few percent of friction; it was a quarter of the asset's value, a tax on being locked inside a walled market at the exact moment everyone wanted out of the dong.

The chart that makes this most visible plots the domestic SJC price against the computed world-equivalent line, year by year. The vertical distance between the two lines *is* the premium — and you can see it open up dramatically in the early-2020s and stay open.

![Domestic SJC price against the computed world-equivalent line](/imgs/blogs/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price-4.png)

(One technical note on that chart: it uses annual-average figures, so the 2024 gap it shows is the year's average, which is narrower than the May intraday peak of ~19 million VND. The peak premium and the average premium are different numbers; the worked examples above use the peak, because that is the figure that made headlines and forced the policy response.)

## Why the premium narrowed in 2024-25

A 26% premium is politically intolerable. It is a visible, daily tax on ordinary households, a flashing sign that the gold market is broken, and — because it tends to spike alongside FX stress — an advertisement that the dong is wobbling. So the State Bank of Vietnam acted, and the premium collapsed.

The mechanics belong to the next post — [SBV gold policy: auctions, the 2024 intervention, and anti-goldization](/blog/trading/gold/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization) — but the logic is exactly the arbitrage logic, run by the state instead of by private traders. The premium exists because nobody can legally bring gold into the walled market. So the State Bank did the importing itself. Through a direct-sale scheme run via several state-owned banks and SJC outlets, it sold gold to the public at a price deliberately set close to the world-equivalent. In effect, the State Bank became the arbitrageur that the law had forbidden everyone else to be: it supplied the metal that demand was screaming for, at a price near fair value, and the gap had no choice but to close.

It is worth appreciating the elegance and the cost of this maneuver. The State Bank had tried the obvious tool first — auctions, in which it sold gold to the highest bidders. But auctions, by design, *clear at the elevated price*: if you sell gold to whoever bids most in a market where buyers are desperate, you simply confirm the high price rather than break it. The 2024 pivot was subtler. Instead of auctioning to the highest bidder, the State Bank set a *fixed sell price* close to the world-equivalent and let the four state banks supply gold to ordinary buyers at that level. By choosing the price rather than letting the auction discover it, the State Bank put a ceiling on the SJC quote: no dealer could hold the price far above a level at which the state itself was selling unlimited gold. The premium was capped by competition with an unlimited-pocketed seller.

The cost is real, though. To supply that gold, the State Bank had to source it — partly from its own reserves, partly by quietly importing on the state's account, the one party legally permitted to. Defending the gold price this way *spends* reserves or FX, the same scarce resource the bank is trying to protect on the currency front. This is the deep tension of the whole policy: the import wall was erected to *stop* gold demand from draining the country's dollars, but crushing the resulting premium requires spending dollars to import gold. The wall converts a continuous FX leak into a periodic, state-managed one — it does not abolish the underlying demand, it just decides who satisfies it and when. The premium is the pressure; the intervention is the state bleeding that pressure off on its own terms, at its own cost.

The premium fell from roughly 18-19 million VND (about 25%) in mid-2024 toward 4-6 million VND (single-digit percent) by the autumn, and a 2025 rule change aimed to keep it in a 3-5% band durably by broadening which bars count as standard and loosening the SJC exclusivity that had concentrated the brand premium. The dam had not been removed — imports were still controlled — but the State Bank had opened a sluice gate and let just enough water through to drop the level. The lasting question, taken up in the next post, is whether a sluice gate operated by hand can keep the reservoir low forever, or whether the only durable fix is to lower the dam itself by reconnecting the market to world supply.

#### Worked example: what closing the premium was worth to a buyer

Compare buying one luong in May 2024 versus after the intervention, holding the world price roughly constant for the comparison:

- **Before**, premium ≈ 26%: a buyer paid the ~73 million VND world-equivalent plus ~19 million VND premium = ~92 million VND ≈ **\$3,620**.
- **After**, premium ≈ 5%: the same buyer paid ~73 million VND plus ~3.7 million VND premium = ~76.7 million VND ≈ **\$3,020**.
- The intervention saved the buyer about **15 million VND**, roughly **\$600**, on a single luong — the difference between a 26% surcharge and a 5% one.

The intuition: the premium is not an abstraction. It was \$600 of real money per luong taken out of, or left in, the pockets of millions of Vietnamese savers — which is precisely why a central bank was willing to spend reserves to crush it.

## The premium as an FX-stress signal

Now the argument that matters most, the one that elevates this from market trivia to a genuine macro signal. **The SJC premium is, to a large degree, a thermometer of dong-versus-dollar stress.** When it runs hot, the deepest reason is usually not gold — it is fear of the currency.

![The premium as an FX-stress thermometer comparing calm and pressured regimes](/imgs/blogs/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price-7.png)

Trace the causal chain. A Vietnamese household that loses faith in the dong wants to move savings into something that holds value. The two classic stores are US dollars and gold. But buying and holding dollars is itself restricted, and the official FX market is managed by the State Bank, which leans hard against a falling dong by selling reserves and rationing dollars. So a chunk of the flight-from-the-dong demand that *would* go into dollars gets diverted into gold instead. That gold demand lands in the walled domestic market, where supply cannot expand — and the premium widens. The premium becomes the place where suppressed FX pressure surfaces. You can cap the dollar price by managing the FX market; you cannot as easily cap the gold market, so the stress leaks out there.

This is why the premium and the exchange rate so often move together. In 2024, as the dong slid past 25,000 to the dollar, the gold premium blew out to 25%. The two were not a coincidence; they were two faces of the same thing — a population trying to get out of a currency it distrusted, with the dollar door half-shut, so the pressure piled up against the gold door. A central bank watching the premium is, in part, watching its own currency's credibility priced in real time by its own citizens.

The thermometer analogy is worth taking literally, because it explains *why* the premium is such a useful signal: it measures the pressure the official channels are hiding. A managed exchange rate is, by design, a censored price. The State Bank can hold the official dong/dollar rate steadier than free-market forces alone would, by selling reserves and rationing dollars. So the FX rate *understates* the true stress on the currency — it shows you the rate the authorities are willing and able to defend, not the rate the market would clear at. The gold premium has no such censor. The State Bank cannot easily defend a gold price the way it defends an FX rate, because it would have to sell physical gold continuously to do so (which, as we saw, is exactly the intervention it eventually resorted to). Until it does, the premium runs free, and so it captures the stress that the managed FX rate suppresses. In a country with a managed currency, the premium on a freely-priced safe asset is often a *truer* gauge of currency fear than the currency's own quoted price.

This generalizes well beyond Vietnam. Wherever a government simultaneously pegs or manages its currency *and* restricts access to safe foreign assets, a parallel premium pops up somewhere to vent the suppressed demand for safety. In Argentina it is the "blue dollar" — the black-market peso/dollar rate that runs far above the official one. In various capital-controlled economies it shows up as a premium on dollar-denominated bonds, or on Bitcoin, or on imported goods. Vietnam's version is the gold premium, because gold is the culturally-blessed safe asset and the one the import wall happens to fence in. The specific asset differs; the mechanism is identical. A premium on a safe store of value, in a market the authorities cannot fully arbitrage, is the universal pressure gauge of a managed currency under strain. The SJC premium is Vietnam's blue-dollar — denominated in metal instead of greenbacks.

The link runs the other way too, which is why the authorities take the premium so seriously. A widening gold premium is not only a *symptom* of FX stress; it can *worsen* it. When the premium is screaming, it advertises to the whole population that gold inside Vietnam is scarce and the dong is suspect — which encourages still more savers to convert dong into gold and dollars, draining confidence and reserves. A central bank that lets the premium run risks a feedback loop: fear widens the premium, the wide premium broadcasts fear, the broadcast deepens the fear. Crushing the premium is therefore partly an exercise in breaking that loop — restoring the visible signal that gold is available at fair value and the currency is under control. The premium is both a thermometer and, if ignored, a small fire of its own.

#### Worked example: reading the premium as an FX gauge

Suppose you are an analyst with no FX data, only gold quotes. You observe the SJC premium widening from 5% to 20% over a few months while the world gold price is roughly flat in dollars. What can you infer?

- The world price is flat, so the move is *not* a global gold story.
- The premium is the domestic price minus the world-equivalent, and the world-equivalent already includes the exchange rate. So a premium widening *on top of* the FX-adjusted benchmark means domestic buyers are paying up beyond what FX explains.
- That extra payment is the signature of fear-driven, captive demand surging into a walled market — which is what happens when savers are fleeing the dong faster than the official channels let them.

You would conclude, correctly, that the dong is under stress and that flight-to-safety demand is building — and you would have learned it from gold quotes alone. The intuition: in a market with controlled FX and a gold import wall, the gold premium becomes the uncensored price of currency fear, the one number the authorities find hardest to manage down.

#### Worked example: decomposing a dong-price move into its three parts

Take the move in the dong price of an SJC luong from end-2023 to the May-2024 peak — from about 74 million VND to about 92.4 million VND, a rise of roughly 18.4 million VND. Split it into the three drivers using the conversion:

- **World gold:** the world price rose from about \$2,063 to about \$2,388 per ounce. Holding FX and the premium fixed at their end-2023 values, that alone lifts the world-equivalent by `(2,388 − 2,063) × 1.20565 × 24,270 ≈ 9,510,000` VND — about **9.5 million VND**, or **\$374** per luong.
- **The dong:** USD/VND rose from about 24,270 to about 25,400. Holding gold at \$2,388, that adds `2,388 × 1.20565 × (25,400 − 24,270) ≈ 3,250,000` VND — about **3.3 million VND**, or **\$128** per luong.
- **The premium:** the residual. Total move 18.4M, of which gold explains ~9.5M and FX ~3.3M, leaving about **5.6 million VND** — roughly **\$220** — as the genuine widening of the premium itself.

So of an alarming-looking 18.4-million-VND jump in the price of gold, well over half was the world price and the exchange rate doing exactly what the conversion predicts, and only about a third was the Vietnam-specific premium widening. The intuition: most of what looks like local gold mania is global gold plus a sliding dong; the premium is the smaller, sharper residual — and it is the only part that a Vietnamese policy maker can actually fix without changing the world.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The premium reflects purer or better gold."** No. SJC bars are 9999 fineness — the same essentially-pure gold sold worldwide. A luong of SJC gold and a luong of plain bullion in Singapore are physically interchangeable. The premium is paid for the *brand and the locked market*, not for any difference in the metal. The proof is in the math: convert the world price with the standard 1.20565 and the day's USD/VND, and the world-equivalent for identical 9999 gold comes out tens of millions of dong below the SJC quote. The gap is institutional, not metallurgical.

**Misconception 2: "The premium is pure manipulation."** It is tempting to assume someone is rigging the price for profit. But the premium does not require a conspiracy — it is the *predictable* output of a market design. Ban imports, monopolize the bar, layer on captive demand and FX fear, and a large premium emerges mechanically, the way water rises behind any dam. The State Bank's own intervention proves the point: when it simply *supplied gold near fair value*, the premium collapsed, with no villain to punish. A structural premium is a policy result, not a crime.

**Misconception 3: "The premium will always be there, so it doesn't matter."** Wrong on both counts. The 2024 collapse, from ~25% to single digits in months, shows the premium is a policy variable, not a constant — open the supply sluice and it falls. And it matters enormously: at 26% it was a ~19 million VND (~\$760) surcharge per luong, real money extracted from savers, and a flashing macro warning sign. Treating it as permanent background noise is exactly the mistake that lets it blow out unnoticed.

**Misconception 4: "A high premium means gold is a great investment in Vietnam."** Dangerously backwards. Buying *into* a high premium means overpaying for the metal; if the premium later normalizes — as it did in 2024-25 — you lose that premium even if the world gold price is flat. A buyer who paid the 26% premium and then watched it fall to 5% lost roughly 15 million VND of premium per luong (about \$600) on the spread alone. The premium is a cost of entry to be minimized, not a feature to be chased.

**Misconception 5: "The premium can be abolished by decree."** Tempting, but it confuses the symptom with the disease. You can *suppress* the premium by force — set a maximum price, punish dealers who quote above it — but with the import wall still up and demand still captive, all that does is push the real transaction price into the gray market, where bars trade above the official cap and the premium reappears as an unobservable spread. The premium is the market's way of clearing a fixed supply against excess demand; outlaw the high price and the supply still does not grow, so the shortage simply changes its address. The only ways to genuinely shrink the premium are to add supply (what the 2024 direct sales did) or to reduce captive demand (what stabilizing the dong does). A price ceiling without one of those is a thermometer you have painted over — the fever is still there, you just can no longer read it.

## How it shows up in real markets

The premium's history is the clearest evidence for everything above. Three episodes stand out.

**2013, the first auctions.** After Decree 24 walled the market in 2012, the premium ran high — often around 3-5 million VND per luong, sometimes much more — and the State Bank ran a series of gold-bar auctions through 2013 to inject supply. Over dozens of auctions the bank sold tens of tonnes of gold, and the premium eased but never fully closed, because the auctions were episodic and the import wall stayed up. The deeper flaw, in hindsight, was the auction format itself: selling to the highest bidder in a hungry market tends to *validate* the elevated price rather than break it, and the moment the auctions paused, the premium drifted back up. The 2013 experience is the control experiment that makes the 2024 direct-sale approach look so effective by contrast — same wall, same captive demand, but a price-setting mechanism that actually targeted the gap instead of clearing at it. Lesson: a sluice opened occasionally, and at the wrong price, is not the same as a connected market.

**2023-2024, the great blowout.** As the global gold rally accelerated and the dong weakened, the premium climbed through 2023 (around 12 million VND, roughly 18%) and detonated in spring 2024 to ~18-19 million VND, about 25%. This is the episode in the charts above. It combined every cause at maximum: a global rally lighting demand, a sliding dong stoking fear, a frozen SJC supply, and a hard import wall. The premium became front-page news and a political problem precisely because it had grown into a quarter of the asset's value.

**Mid-2024 onward, the crush.** The State Bank's pivot from auctions to *direct sales near the world-equivalent* worked where the 2013 auctions had not, because it supplied gold continuously and priced it to kill the gap rather than to raise revenue. The premium fell to single digits within months. A 2025 rule change institutionalized the goal of a 3-5% band. By early 2026, with the world gold price at record highs above \$5,000/oz and the SJC bar at all-time highs near 190 million VND per luong, the premium sat near 4% — meaning almost the entire eye-watering rise in the dong price of gold was the *world* price and the exchange rate doing their work, with only a thin premium on top. That is what a managed-but-connected market looks like.

#### Worked example: the all-time-high luong, decomposed

Take early 2026, with the world gold price near \$5,414/oz, USD/VND near 26,300, and the SJC bar near 190 million VND per luong.

- World-equivalent per luong = `5,414 × 1.20565 × 26,300 ≈ 171,700,000` VND ≈ **172 million VND** (≈ **\$6,529**).
- SJC at ~190 million VND ≈ `190,000,000 / 26,300 ≈ ` **\$7,224**.
- Premium = `190 − 172 ≈ 18` **million VND** ≈ **\$685**, or about `18 / 172 ≈ 10-11%` of the world price.

So even at record nominal prices, the *percentage* premium is far below the 2024 peak — the huge dong price simply reflects a soaring world price and a higher exchange rate, exactly what the conversion predicts. The intuition: once you decompose the price, a scary-looking 190-million-dong gold bar is mostly the world price translated into dong, not a runaway domestic premium.

## The takeaway

Strip away the specifics and the SJC premium teaches one durable lesson about gold and about money. Gold's world price is set by the planet's collective verdict on real yields, paper currencies, and political risk. When you wall off a national market from that world price, you do not escape the verdict — you just create a second, local price that carries an *extra* charge for the wall itself, and that extra charge becomes a readout of local stress.

For a Vietnamese saver, the practical lessons are concrete. Always decompose the price before buying: take the world \$/oz, multiply by 1.20565 and the day's USD/VND to get the world-equivalent, and look at how far the SJC quote sits above it. A small premium (a few percent) is the cost of a connected market; a large one (double digits) means you are paying a tax to be locked in, and that you are buying at the moment of maximum fear — historically the worst time to overpay. The premium is a cost to minimize on entry, never a feature to chase.

A few corollaries follow from treating the premium as a cost rather than a feature. First, *when* you buy matters as much as *that* you buy: accumulating gold steadily in calm periods, when the premium is thin, beats lump-buying in a panic when the premium is fat — the discipline of dollar-cost-averaging applies doubly in a walled market, because you are averaging the premium as well as the price. Second, *what form* you buy matters: when the SJC-brand premium over plain gold rings is wide, a saver who only wants exposure to the metal can sidestep part of the gap by buying nhan or jewelry-grade 9999 gold instead of the branded bar, accepting slightly worse liquidity for a materially lower entry premium. (The full bar-versus-ring-versus-jewelry trade-off, including tax and resale frictions, is the subject of the series' Vietnam playbook post.) Third, the premium cuts both ways on exit: if you buy into a wide premium and the market later normalizes, you eat the compression; but if you happen to *hold* gold when the premium widens, you can sell into the spike and capture a gap that has nothing to do with the world price. The premium is volatility you can be on either side of — which is all the more reason to be deliberate about which side.

For an investor thinking about gold globally rather than locally, the SJC premium is a cautionary illustration of a general principle: *the price you can transact at is not always the world price.* A frozen market, capital controls, a brand monopoly, a tax wedge — any of these can open a gap between the screen price of an asset and the price a real buyer in a real jurisdiction actually pays. Vietnam's gold market is an unusually clean and large example, but the lesson travels. Whenever you hold an asset through a constrained channel — a country's only legal exchange, a single custodian, a restricted share class — ask what would happen if everyone tried to use that channel at once. The premium that emerges is the price of the constraint, and it always comes due at the worst possible moment, because the very fear that drives everyone to the channel at the same time is what makes the channel too narrow to handle them. Gold's world price is the world's verdict on paper money; the SJC premium is the surcharge for hearing that verdict from inside a locked room.

For anyone reading Vietnam's macro economy, the deeper lesson is that the premium is a thermometer. It runs hot not mainly when gold is interesting but when the dong is frightening — when captive demand, blocked from the dollar, piles against a walled gold market. Watch the premium and you are watching, in real time and through a channel the authorities cannot fully censor, how much the country's own savers trust their currency. That is the series' thesis in its sharpest local form: gold is monetary insurance, the premium is the price of being trapped while you buy it, and the size of that premium is a vote of no-confidence in the dong — cast in dong, priced in gold, and impossible to hide.
