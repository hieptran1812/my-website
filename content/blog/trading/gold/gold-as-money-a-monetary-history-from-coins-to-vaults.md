> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold became money by winning a 5,000-year selection contest for the six properties of good money; it lost its formal monetary role in 1971 but never lost its monetary identity, which is exactly why central banks still vault it.
>
> - Money is not decreed into being — it is *selected*. Across millennia, traders converged on whatever best held value, divided cleanly, travelled well, and could not be faked or printed. Gold scored highest of any natural substance, so it won.
> - The gold standard was a contract: a paper note was a claim on a fixed weight of metal. That contract imposed brutal discipline on governments — which is precisely why every government eventually broke it.
> - On 15 August 1971, Richard Nixon "closed the gold window," ending the dollar's last link to gold. Gold was demonetized on paper. Its price, frozen at \$35 for decades, then floated and is now near \$5,400.
> - Gold today is **money without a state** — the only major reserve asset that is no one's liability. That single property is why central banks bought over 1,000 tonnes a year after 2022 and hold roughly 36,000 tonnes in vaults.
> - The number to remember: an ounce of gold cost **\$35 in 1971** and roughly **\$5,400 in 2026** — a ~150-fold rise that is not gold getting more valuable so much as paper money losing its grip.

On the morning of 15 August 1971, a Sunday, Richard Nixon went on national television and interrupted *Bonanza* to tell Americans that the dollar would no longer be convertible into gold. He framed it as a temporary defensive measure against "international money speculators." It was neither temporary nor defensive. In that single sentence, the United States severed the last formal link between paper money and metal that had governed the world economy, in one form or another, for the better part of three thousand years. Economists call it the Nixon Shock. It is arguably the most consequential monetary event of the twentieth century, and almost nobody watching that night understood what had just happened.

What had happened was this: money had been cut loose from gold. From that Monday forward, the value of a dollar — and, because the dollar anchored the system, the value of nearly every currency on Earth — would rest on nothing but trust in the institutions that issued it. No metal in a vault stood behind it. The price of gold, which had been pinned at \$35 an ounce since 1934, was free to find its own level. It would take just nine years to climb to \$850, and forty-five years to reach \$5,400. That trajectory is the single most important chart in this entire series, and it is the spine of everything we will discuss: gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's running tally of no-confidence in paper money.

But to understand why 1971 mattered, and why gold still sits in vaults more than half a century after it was officially demonetized, you have to go back much further — about 2,600 years further, to a riverbank in what is now western Turkey, where a king named Croesus struck the first pure-gold coins and, without knowing it, formalized a contest that gold had already been quietly winning for thousands of years.

![Six properties of good money scored across gold silver cattle seashells and paper](/imgs/blogs/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults-1.png)

The whole journey we are about to take runs along a single thread, and it helps to see the shape of it before we dive into the detail. Gold's monetary life moves through six turning points: the first state-stamped coins struck by Croesus around 600 BC; the long bimetallic era when gold and silver circulated together at a fixed mint ratio; the disciplined classical gold standard of 1870 to 1914; the dollar-anchored Bretton Woods system built in 1944; the Nixon Shock of 1971 that cut the last link; and the strange present, in which gold is officially demonetized yet sits by choice in tens of thousands of tonnes inside the world's central-bank vaults. Each of those moments taught the same lesson from a different angle, and the timeline below lays them out end to end so you can keep your bearings as we work through them one at a time.

## Foundations: The six properties of good money

Before we can talk about *why* gold became money, we have to be precise about what money even *is*. This is where most discussions of gold go wrong from the first sentence, because people treat "money" as something a government simply declares into existence. That is true of *modern* money, and it is exactly the thing that is new and strange about the world we live in. For almost all of human history, money was not declared — it was discovered. It emerged from the bottom up, from millions of individual trades, as people gravitated toward whichever good best solved the problem money exists to solve.

That problem is the **double coincidence of wants**. Imagine a world of pure barter, with no money at all. You are a fisherman, and you want a pair of sandals. To trade directly, you must find a sandal-maker who happens, at that exact moment, to want fish. If she wants bread instead, you are stuck: you must first find a baker who wants fish, trade for bread, then carry the bread to the sandal-maker before either the fish or the bread spoils. Barter does not scale. The number of trades you might have to chain together grows impossibly fast, and perishable goods rot before the chain completes.

Money solves this by inserting a universally accepted intermediate good between every buyer and seller. You sell your fish *for money*, and money buys your sandals. Nobody has to want fish at the same time you want sandals. But this only works if everyone agrees on what counts as money — and that agreement is not arbitrary. Over thousands of years of trial and error, traders converged on goods that share a specific cluster of physical and economic properties. There are six, and they are worth committing to memory, because the entire history of money is just the story of different substances scoring high or low on this scorecard.

**Durability.** Money must survive being held, carried, buried, and passed between thousands of hands over decades or centuries. A medium of exchange that rots, rusts, evaporates, or dies is useless as a *store* of value — and storing value across time is half of money's job. Wheat rots. Cattle die. Iron rusts. Gold does not. Gold is chemically inert: it does not react with oxygen, water, or almost any acid. A gold coin pulled from a 2,000-year-old Roman shipwreck looks the same as the day it was struck. This is not a poetic flourish; it is a hard chemical fact that no other monetary candidate can match.

**Divisibility.** Money must split cleanly into the size of the transaction. You need to be able to buy a loaf of bread *and* a house with the same substance, just in different quantities. A diamond fails badly here: cut a one-carat diamond in half and you do not get two half-carat diamonds worth half as much — you get two chips worth a tiny fraction of the whole, because a diamond's value is wildly non-linear in size. A cow fails completely: half a cow is not money, it is meat. Gold passes perfectly. You can melt an ounce into ten tenths, or hammer it into gold leaf one-ten-thousandth of a millimetre thick, and every fragment is worth exactly its proportional weight.

**Portability.** Money must pack a lot of value into a small, movable form, because trade and the storage of wealth both require moving value across space. This is really a measure of *value density* — value per unit of weight and volume. Cattle are the worst possible case: enormously valuable, but you cannot put a herd in your pocket or carry it across a border. Gold is extraordinarily value-dense. At \$5,400 an ounce, a single briefcase of gold the size of a few bricks holds millions of dollars. A man can carry a fortune; a country's entire monetary base, historically, fit in a few rooms.

**Fungibility.** Every unit of money must be interchangeable with every other unit of the same denomination. One ounce of pure gold is identical to, and worth exactly the same as, any other ounce of pure gold anywhere on Earth. This is why you can quote a single global price for gold. Seashells fail: no two are alike in size or condition. Real estate fails spectacularly: no two parcels are the same, which is exactly why a house is an investment but not money. Gold's atomic uniformity makes it perfectly fungible.

**Scarcity, or "hardness."** This is the subtle one, and the one that matters most for everything that follows in this series. Money must be *hard to produce* — it must be impossible, or at least very costly and slow, to create more of it. If anyone could manufacture money cheaply, they would, and its value would collapse toward the cost of production. The technical measure is the **stock-to-flow ratio**: the total existing above-ground supply (the stock) divided by the new amount produced each year (the flow). Gold's stock-to-flow ratio is the highest of any physical commodity. Roughly 216,000 tonnes of gold have ever been mined, and annual mine production adds only about 3,500 tonnes — an increase of around **1.7% per year**, and that rate has held remarkably steady for over a century because gold is genuinely difficult and expensive to dig out of the ground. By contrast, a central bank can expand the supply of paper dollars by 20% in a year with a few keystrokes, which is the whole story of why fiat money loses purchasing power over time.

**Verifiability.** People must be able to confirm that what they are accepting is the real thing, not a counterfeit. This is gold's one genuine weakness as money — verifying purity historically required an assay or a touchstone, which is friction. But here gold has a clever physical defense: it is one of the densest common metals, so a "gold" coin or bar that has been adulterated with cheaper metal will weigh wrong or displace the wrong amount of water. The famous story of Archimedes leaping from his bath shouting "Eureka!" is, at its core, a story about *verifying gold*: the king suspected his goldsmith had cut the crown with silver, and Archimedes realized he could detect the fraud by measuring the crown's density. Gold's extreme density is, in effect, a built-in anti-counterfeiting feature.

Now run every candidate substance through these six tests, and the result is the scorecard in the figure above. Cattle are durable-ish but undividable and unportable. Seashells are portable but not scarce and not fungible. Silver is gold's nearest rival — durable, divisible, portable, fungible — but it tarnishes, is mined in larger and more variable quantities (so it is "softer"), and is less value-dense. Paper fiat scores high on divisibility, portability, and verifiability precisely because a *state* engineers those properties into it — but it fails catastrophically on scarcity, because the state can print it at will, which is the entire reason fiat currencies lose value while gold does not.

Gold is the only natural substance that never fails a single property. That is not an opinion or a tradition; it is the conclusion of a multi-millennium, decentralized, trial-and-error experiment run by every trading civilization that ever existed, all independently arriving at the same answer. **So what does this tell you?** It tells you that gold's monetary status was never granted by any government — it was earned in the market, before any government existed to grant anything. Keep that in your pocket, because it is the key to understanding why gold's monetary role survived 1971 even after every government on Earth officially walked away from it.

## From barter to coinage

The six properties explain why gold rose to the top of the monetary heap. But raw gold — nuggets, dust, rough lumps — is awkward money. Every transaction requires weighing and assaying, which is slow and invites fraud (the seller shaves the weights, the buyer's gold is secretly alloyed). The great leap was **coinage**: stamping a standardized, state-guaranteed weight and purity into the metal so that it could be accepted by count rather than re-weighed every time.

The first coins appeared in the kingdom of Lydia, in western Anatolia, around 600 BC. They were made of *electrum*, a naturally occurring alloy of gold and silver found in the silt of the Pactolus River. The problem with electrum was that its gold-to-silver ratio varied from nugget to nugget, so its value was uncertain — a verifiability failure. The Lydian king Croesus (the source of the phrase "rich as Croesus") solved it around 550 BC by refining the metal and minting the first coins of *pure gold* and *pure silver* separately, each at a guaranteed weight and fineness. With that, a merchant could accept a coin at face value without an assay. The friction of the verifiability problem collapsed, and the velocity of trade exploded.

This is the crucial insight about coinage, and it is one people consistently miss: the coin did not give gold its value. Gold already had value — that is *why* it was being stamped into coins rather than, say, lead. What the coin added was a layer of **trust and convenience** on top of the metal's intrinsic worth. The state's stamp was a certification: "I, the sovereign, vouch that this disc contains exactly this much pure gold." The value lived in the metal; the stamp merely made the metal *spendable without re-verification*. Hold this distinction carefully, because the entire later history of money is the story of that thin layer of state-provided trust slowly inflating until, in 1971, it became the *only* thing left and the metal underneath was removed entirely.

#### Worked example: Why coins traded near melt value

Consider the U.S. \$20 gold "Double Eagle," minted under the classical gold standard. It contained 0.9675 troy ounces of pure gold. Under the standard, the official price of gold was \$20.67 per ounce, so the metal in the coin was worth:

```
metal value = 0.9675 oz  x  $20.67/oz  =  $19.99
```

The coin's face value was \$20.00. So a \$20 gold piece contained almost exactly \$20 of gold — the tiny gap (about one cent, plus a small seigniorage charge for minting) was the cost of the state's stamp. This is the defining feature of **commodity money**: the coin trades at (or just above) the value of the metal it contains, because if it ever traded for much *less* than its metal, people would melt it down for the gold, and if it traded for much *more*, people would mint more coins. The face value and the melt value are chained together. The one-sentence intuition: under a true gold standard, a coin is not a *token* representing money held elsewhere — it *is* the money, and you carry your purchasing power around in your pocket as physical metal.

Coinage spread from Lydia to Greece, to Persia, to Rome, and eventually across the entire trading world, because it worked. For roughly the next 2,500 years — from Croesus to Nixon — the basic architecture of money was some version of this: precious metal, certified by a sovereign, circulating as coin, sometimes supplemented by paper claims on metal held in a vault. The metal was the foundation. Everything else was a convenience built on top of it.

## The bimetallic era and Gresham's law

For most of monetary history, gold did not work alone. It worked alongside silver in a system called **bimetallism**, in which both metals circulated as legal money at an official fixed exchange ratio set by the mint. Silver coins handled everyday transactions — bread, wages, rent — because silver is cheaper per ounce and so divides down to small values without producing absurdly tiny coins. Gold handled large transactions and the storage of serious wealth, because its value density makes it efficient for big sums. For centuries the mint ratio between them hovered around **15 to 16 ounces of silver per ounce of gold**, and this 15:1 figure is one of the most stable numbers in all of economic history.

The problem with bimetallism is that a government can *legally fix* the ratio at 15:1, but it cannot *force the market* to agree. Silver and gold are mined, consumed, and traded by the whole world, and their relative scarcity drifts over time. When a big new silver deposit is discovered, silver becomes more abundant and its market value falls relative to gold — the market ratio might drift to 16:1 even while the mint still insists on 15:1. That gap between the *official* ratio and the *market* ratio is a free profit waiting to be harvested, and harvesting it is the mechanism behind one of the oldest laws in economics.

That law is **Gresham's law**, usually stated as "**bad money drives out good.**" It sounds like a paradox, but it is just arbitrage. When the law forces two monies to be accepted at a ratio that differs from their true market value, people spend the *overvalued* money (the "bad" money the law props up) and hoard or melt the *undervalued* money (the "good" money the law underprices). The good money disappears from circulation — into mattresses, melting pots, and foreign trade — leaving only the bad money in everyday use.

#### Worked example: Bimetallic arbitrage and Gresham's law

Suppose the mint's official ratio is **15:1** — by law, 15 ounces of silver coin exchange for 1 ounce of gold coin — but the world market price has drifted to **16:1**, because silver has become relatively more plentiful. Here is the trade:

```
Step 1: Take 1 oz of gold coin to the mint.
Step 2: At the official 15:1 ratio, exchange it for 15 oz of silver coin.
Step 3: Take those 15 oz of silver to the open market.
Step 4: At the market ratio of 16:1, it now takes 16 oz of silver
        to buy 1 oz of gold -- so 15 oz of silver buys 15/16 = 0.9375 oz gold.
```

That round trip *loses* you gold (you started with 1 oz and ended with 0.9375 oz), so run it the *other* direction — the profitable way:

```
Step 1: Start with 16 oz of silver (worth 1 oz gold at the market ratio).
Step 2: Take 15 oz of that silver to the mint, get 1 oz of gold coin.
Step 3: You have spent 15 oz of silver and have 1 oz gold PLUS 1 oz silver left over.
Profit:  1 oz of silver, free, on every cycle -- about a 6.7% gain.
```

Rational traders repeat this until the mint runs out of gold coins entirely: gold (the undervalued "good" money) is pulled out of circulation and into the arbitrage, leaving only silver (the overvalued "bad" money) actually changing hands. The one-sentence intuition: whenever a law forces two monies to trade at the "wrong" price, the market quietly hoards the underpriced one and dumps the overpriced one — which is why a fixed mint ratio is always at war with the floating market ratio, and the market always wins.

This tension is exactly why bimetallism eventually collapsed and the world drifted toward a pure **gold standard** in the nineteenth century. Britain went onto gold *de facto* in 1717 (famously, Isaac Newton, as Master of the Mint, set the silver-to-gold ratio in a way that drove silver out of England), and *de jure* in 1821. As the world's dominant trading and financial power, Britain pulled the rest of the world onto gold behind it. By the 1870s, the major economies had abandoned silver and adopted the gold standard, and the gold/silver ratio — no longer fixed by any mint — was free to float. And float it did. The ancient 15:1 relationship is now ancient history: the modern market ratio sits around **80 to 90 ounces of silver per ounce of gold**, as the chart below shows.

![Gold to silver ratio over time with the 15 to 1 bimetallic reference line](/imgs/blogs/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults-3.png)

**So what does this tell you?** It tells you something profound about the difference between a *decree* and a *price*. For two thousand years, governments tried to legislate the relationship between gold and silver, and Gresham's law punished every attempt by quietly emptying their mints. The moment they stopped decreeing and let the market price the two metals freely, the "stable" 15:1 ratio promptly drifted to five or six times that level — revealing that the old ratio had been a political fiction propped up by law, not a natural constant. This is your first lesson in a pattern that will recur throughout gold's history and throughout this series: **governments can fix prices by decree, but only for as long as they can defend the decree against the market — and they can never defend it forever.** Remember this when we get to 1971.

## The classical gold standard, 1870–1914

The four and a half decades before the First World War are the high-water mark of gold as the world's money — a period economic historians call the **classical gold standard**, and it is the system gold-standard nostalgics are usually picturing when they say we should "go back to gold." It is worth understanding precisely, because it was a genuinely remarkable monetary machine, and because its mechanics explain both why it worked and why it could not survive the modern world.

Under the classical gold standard, each participating country defined its currency as a fixed weight of gold and committed to **convertibility**: any holder of paper notes could walk into the central bank and exchange them for that fixed weight of gold on demand, no questions asked. The British pound was defined as roughly 0.2354 troy ounces of fine gold; the U.S. dollar as 0.04837 troy ounces (which works out to the famous \$20.67 per ounce). Because every currency was just a different-sized claim on the same metal, exchange rates between countries were effectively fixed — the pound/dollar rate was simply the ratio of their gold contents, around \$4.87 to the pound. Money was, in a real sense, the same thing everywhere: gold, denominated in different national units.

The genius of the system was that it was **self-correcting**, through a mechanism the philosopher David Hume described back in 1752 called the **price–specie–flow mechanism** ("specie" just means metallic money). Suppose Britain imports more than it exports — it runs a trade deficit. To pay for the excess imports, gold flows out of Britain and into its trading partners. Now here is the automatic part. The amount of money in an economy under a gold standard is tied to the amount of gold it holds. So as gold drains out of Britain, the British money supply shrinks; with less money chasing the same goods, British prices *fall*. Meanwhile, gold piles up in the surplus countries, their money supplies *expand*, and their prices *rise*. Suddenly British goods are cheap and foreign goods are expensive — so the world buys more from Britain and less from everyone else. The trade deficit reverses, gold flows back, and the system returns to balance. No central planner, no policy committee, no intervention: the metal flows do the work.

That self-correcting property came at a price, and the price was **discipline**. A government on the gold standard cannot print money to fund a war, paper over a recession, or buy votes, because every note it prints is a promise redeemable in metal it does not have. If it over-issues, citizens and foreigners line up to convert paper into gold, the central bank's vault empties, and the government is forced to either stop printing or default on convertibility — which is to say, leave the standard in disgrace. The gold standard, in other words, is a *constraint on state power*. It takes the printing press out of the politician's hands and chains the money supply to a metal that no government can manufacture. To its admirers, that discipline produced the price stability of the late nineteenth century — over the entire classical period, the U.S. and U.K. price levels were roughly flat, with prices in 1914 close to where they had been in 1870. To its critics, that same rigidity made the system unable to respond to crises and crippled it during downturns, when a shrinking gold supply forced a shrinking money supply at exactly the moment the economy needed *more* liquidity, not less.

![Convertible gold standard money versus pure fiat after 1971](/imgs/blogs/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults-4.png)

The figure above captures the single most important difference between the gold-standard world and our own. Under convertibility (the left side), a paper note was a *claim check* — a legally enforceable promise to deliver a specific weight of gold on demand. The note had value because the gold behind it had value; the paper was just a more convenient way to carry and transfer the metal. The supply of money was anchored to the supply of gold, and the government could not break that anchor without breaking its own promise. After 1971 (the right side), the claim check became a claim on *nothing*. Take a modern dollar bill to the Federal Reserve and ask to redeem it, and you will receive... another dollar bill. The note's value rests entirely on the collective expectation that other people will accept it tomorrow at roughly the value it has today — an expectation backed by the taxing power, economic strength, and credibility of the issuing state, but backed by no metal whatsoever.

#### Worked example: The cost of leaving the standard

Here is the most vivid way to feel what the gold standard actually meant. Under the U.S. gold standard, the dollar was convertible at roughly \$20.67 per ounce in the classical era, and at \$35 per ounce after the 1934 devaluation. Take that \$35 figure and ask: how much gold did \$1,000 in cash represent under the Bretton Woods system?

```
gold per dollar = 1 oz / $35 = 0.02857 oz
gold for $1,000 = $1,000 x 0.02857 oz/$ = 28.57 oz
```

So in 1971, a \$1,000 bill was, legally, a claim on **28.57 ounces of gold**. Now price that same gold at today's level of roughly \$5,400 per ounce:

```
value today = 28.57 oz x $5,400/oz = $154,300
```

The gold that a single \$1,000 note represented in 1971 is worth about **\$154,000 today**. Put differently: anyone who, in 1971, held \$1,000 in cash instead of converting it to its gold equivalent and holding the metal has watched that purchasing power shrink to less than one percent of what the gold would now be worth. The one-sentence intuition: the gold standard's "cost" was the discipline it imposed on governments — and the cost of *abandoning* it has been borne by everyone who held the paper instead of the metal, because once the anchor was cut, the paper was free to sink and the metal was free to rise.

## Breakdown: WWI, the interwar mess, and Bretton Woods

The classical gold standard did not collapse because of an economic flaw. It collapsed because of a war. When the First World War broke out in 1914, the combatant nations needed to spend on a scale that gold-backed money made impossible — you cannot fight a total war on a budget chained to the metal in your vault. So one by one, the belligerents suspended convertibility, printed money to fund the war, and let their currencies inflate. The discipline of the gold standard, which had been its entire point, was the first casualty of the war.

The interwar attempt to rebuild the gold standard in the 1920s was a half-hearted, brittle affair — a "gold-exchange standard" in which countries held some reserves in gold and some in the currencies of the major powers, with several currencies (notably the British pound) restored at pre-war gold parities that no longer matched economic reality. It was rigid where it needed to be flexible and loose where it needed to be firm, and it transmitted the Great Depression around the world: as the slump deepened, the gold standard forced contracting economies to contract their money supplies further, deepening the spiral. Country after country was forced off gold in the early 1930s — Britain in 1931, the U.S. effectively in 1933 — and the economic historian Barry Eichengreen has shown a stark pattern: the *sooner* a country abandoned the gold standard, the *sooner* its recovery began. By the late 1930s the interwar gold standard was dead, and it had helped kill the world economy on its way out.

Out of the wreckage of the Second World War came the system that would govern money for the next quarter-century. In July 1944, delegates from 44 Allied nations gathered at a resort hotel in **Bretton Woods**, New Hampshire, to design the postwar monetary order. The system they built was an ingenious — and, in hindsight, doomed — compromise between the discipline of gold and the flexibility nations now demanded. Here is how it worked:

- The **U.S. dollar** was pegged to gold at **\$35 per ounce**, and the United States committed to convert dollars into gold at that price *for foreign governments and central banks* (not for ordinary citizens, who had been barred from owning monetary gold since 1933).
- **Every other currency** was pegged to the dollar at a fixed rate.

The result was a two-tier pyramid. Gold sat at the apex as the ultimate anchor; the dollar was a claim on gold; and every other currency was a claim on the dollar. The world was on a *gold-exchange standard* with the dollar as the intermediary — what the French finance minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing would famously resent as America's "**exorbitant privilege**," because it let the U.S. issue the world's reserve currency and run deficits in its own money. This is the architecture that made the dollar the center of the global financial system, and it is the deep root of the dollar dominance we still live with today, a story we trace in detail in [the petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance).

For about two decades it worked. But it carried a fatal flaw, identified in the 1960s by the economist Robert Triffin and now called the **Triffin dilemma**. The world needed ever more dollars to grease the growing volume of global trade — the dollar was the world's money, and a growing world economy demands a growing money supply. But the United States could only supply those dollars by running persistent deficits, sending more dollars abroad than it took back. The more dollars piled up overseas, the more they exceeded the \$35-an-ounce gold sitting in Fort Knox. At some point, foreign holders would do the arithmetic, realize the U.S. could not possibly honor all its outstanding dollars in gold at \$35, and rush to convert before the vault emptied. The dollar's role as the world's currency and its promise of gold convertibility were on a collision course. It was only a matter of time.

## The Nixon Shock of 1971 and demonetization

By the late 1960s the math had become undeniable. The United States had printed far more dollars than it had gold to back at \$35 an ounce — funding the Vietnam War and the Great Society programs without raising taxes enough to pay for them. Foreign central banks, led by France under Charles de Gaulle (who pointedly sent French warships to ferry gold home from New York), began converting their dollar holdings into American gold, and the U.S. gold stock drained from over 20,000 tonnes after the war to around 8,000 tonnes — close to the level it holds today. By the summer of 1971, a full-blown run on the dollar's gold was underway. Britain reportedly requested conversion of \$3 billion into gold, an amount that would have gutted what remained of the U.S. reserves.

Nixon's options were brutal. He could honor convertibility and watch the gold vanish entirely; he could raise interest rates savagely and slash spending to defend the dollar, throwing the economy into recession before an election year; or he could break the promise. On 15 August 1971, he broke the promise. He "closed the gold window" — suspending the dollar's convertibility into gold — along with imposing wage and price controls and an import surcharge, a package his team had hammered out in secret over a weekend at Camp David. The convertibility suspension was sold as temporary. It was permanent. An attempt to patch the fixed-rate system later that year (the Smithsonian Agreement, which devalued the dollar to \$38 and then \$42 an ounce) failed within months, and by 1973 the major currencies were floating freely against one another, their values set by markets rather than pegged to gold or to each other. The metallic age of money was over.

This is the moment of **demonetization** — the point at which gold formally ceased to be the foundation of the monetary system. It was finalized internationally in 1976, when the IMF's Jamaica Accords amended the Articles of Agreement to abolish gold's official role and embrace floating exchange rates. From that point on, the world's money was, for the first time in human history, *pure fiat* — money that is money solely because a government declares it legal tender and the population accepts it, with no commodity backing of any kind. ("Fiat" is Latin for "let it be done"; fiat money is money by decree.)

The chart of the gold price across this break is the most eloquent summary of what happened, and it is worth studying carefully.

![Gold price from 1970 to 2026 on a log scale with a 1971 regime marker](/imgs/blogs/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults-5.png)

Notice the shape. For the entire left edge of this chart up to 1971, the price is a flat line pinned at \$35 — not because that was gold's "true" value, but because the U.S. government *decreed* it and defended the decree with its gold reserves, exactly the way the mints once decreed the 15:1 silver ratio. The instant the decree was abandoned, the price did what every artificially suppressed price does when the suppression ends: it exploded. Gold ran from \$35 to \$850 by January 1980 — a 24-fold increase in nine years — as the world repriced the metal in the new fiat reality and as the inflation of the 1970s gathered force. The chart uses a logarithmic scale (each gridline is ten times the one below it) precisely because the rise is so enormous that a normal scale would compress the entire pre-2000 history into a flat smear at the bottom.

**So what does this tell you?** The same lesson the silver ratio taught, now written in dollars: when a government fixes the price of gold and then can no longer defend the fix, gold does not get "more valuable" so much as the truth gets revealed. The \$35 peg was a measure of how much the government was lying to itself and the world about what its paper was worth. The post-1971 ascent is the market's repricing of that lie, decade after decade. Which raises the obvious question, the one that drives this entire series: if gold was officially fired as money in 1971, why does it keep behaving like money, and why is its price now near \$5,400?

## Gold today: money without a state

Here is the central paradox of modern gold, and resolving it is the whole point of this post. Gold was demonetized in 1971. No currency is backed by it. No mint stamps it as legal tender. By the strict definition — money is what a government declares money — gold is not money anymore. It is, officially, a commodity, like copper or wheat. And yet:

- The world's central banks — the very institutions that issue the fiat currencies that replaced gold — collectively hold roughly **36,000 tonnes of it** in their vaults, worth trillions of dollars at current prices.
- After 2022, those same central banks bought gold at the fastest pace in modern history: over **1,000 tonnes a year in 2022, 2023, and 2024**, against an average of under 500 tonnes a year in the preceding decade.
- Gold trades 24 hours a day in a global market, is quoted against every currency, and rises in *all* of them over time — as we will see in a moment.

No one mandates that central banks hold gold. They are not required to; many other "safe" assets exist. They hold it by choice, with their own money, in enormous quantities, and they have been *increasing* their holdings. Why?

The answer is a single phrase, and it is the key that unlocks everything: gold is **money without a state**. More precisely, gold is the only major reserve asset that is **no one's liability**. To see why that matters, you have to understand that almost everything else we call "money" or a "safe asset" is, on closer inspection, somebody's *promise*. The cash in your wallet is a liability of the central bank. The deposits in your bank account are a liability of your commercial bank. A government bond is a liability of the issuing government. Every one of these instruments is an IOU — it has value because some institution promises to honor it, and that institution can default, freeze your access, inflate the value away by printing more, or simply cease to exist. The figure below makes this hierarchy explicit.

![Money pyramid showing gold at the base as no one's liability with credit at the apex](/imgs/blogs/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults-6.png)

This is often called the **money pyramid** or the hierarchy of money. At the apex sits the riskiest, most abstract money: credit, bonds, and shadow-banking claims, which are promises by borrowers and funds. Below that are bank deposits — the numbers in your account, which are promises by your commercial bank. Below that is central-bank money — physical cash and the reserves banks hold at the central bank, which are promises by the state. And at the very base sits gold. Gold is different in kind from everything above it: it is not a promise by anyone. It does not pay interest, it cannot default, it cannot be frozen by a foreign government, and no one can create more of it with a keystroke. It is **final settlement** — the bottom of the pyramid, the asset that extinguishes a debt completely rather than merely transferring a claim. The deep mechanics of how the layers above gold are spun into existence are the subject of [how money is created](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier); here, the point is simply that gold sits *underneath* all of it.

This is why central banks — institutions whose entire job is to manage *other people's* fiat liabilities — choose to hold the one asset that is nobody's liability. It is their insurance against the failure of the very system they run. If trust in the dollar, the euro, or any other reserve currency erodes; if a country's foreign reserves can be frozen by the issuing nation in a geopolitical dispute (a possibility that became vividly real in 2022, when much of Russia's dollar and euro reserves were frozen after the invasion of Ukraine — a turning point we explore fully in Track C); if the whole architecture of paper claims comes under stress — gold keeps its value because it does not depend on any of that architecture. It is the asset of last resort precisely because it answers to no state.

**So what does this tell you?** It tells you that gold's demonetization in 1971 was real but partial. Gold lost its *formal*, legal monetary role — its job as the literal backing of currencies. But it never lost its *monetary identity* — its function as the bedrock store of value that exists outside and beneath any state's control. Governments fired gold from the official payroll, but the market, and the central banks themselves, quietly kept it on retainer as the ultimate insurance policy. That is the thesis of this entire series in one idea: gold is monetary insurance, and its price is the premium the world is willing to pay for protection against the failure of paper money. When that fear rises, the premium rises, and gold goes up — which is exactly what the next chart shows, in every currency at once.

## Common misconceptions

The monetary history of gold is fertile ground for myths, partly because it sits at the intersection of economics, politics, and ideology. Three misconceptions in particular distort how people think about gold's place in the modern world, and each can be corrected with the history we have just walked through.

**Misconception 1: "We're going to go back to the gold standard."** This is a perennial prediction in certain corners of finance, usually offered as either a hope or a threat. The history says it is extraordinarily unlikely, for a specific structural reason: a true gold standard *removes the printing press from the government's hands*, and no modern government will voluntarily surrender that power. The entire reason the gold standard was abandoned — in 1914 to fund a war, in the 1930s to fight a depression, in 1971 to fund deficits without taxing — is that elected governments need the flexibility to print money in a crisis, and a gold standard forbids it. The discipline that gold-standard advocates *prize* is exactly the discipline that politicians *flee*. Could a country re-link a currency to gold? In principle, yes. Will the major economies voluntarily re-chain themselves to a metal that strips them of monetary sovereignty? The 5,000-year pattern says no — governments always abandon gold under pressure, never the reverse. Note the number that makes a return arithmetically brutal: to back even the U.S. monetary base with gold at the existing 8,133 tonnes would require a gold price many times higher than today's, which is itself a backdoor admission that the dollar has been vastly expanded relative to the metal.

**Misconception 2: "Gold has no monetary role after 1971."** This is the opposite error, and it confuses *formal* demonetization with *functional* demonetization. Yes, gold no longer legally backs any currency. But "no role" cannot be squared with the facts: central banks hold ~36,000 tonnes of it, they bought over 1,000 tonnes a year after 2022, and the United States still holds 8,133 tonnes — more than any other nation — making gold roughly 72% of U.S. official reserves. Institutions do not park trillions of dollars in an asset that has "no role." Gold's role changed from *backing* money to being the *base-layer reserve and insurance asset* beneath the fiat system. That is a different role, not no role.

**Misconception 3: "Fiat money is backed by nothing."** This is the gold-bug's favorite line, and it is half right in a way that obscures the truth. It is correct that fiat money has no *commodity* backing — there is no metal behind a dollar. But "backed by nothing" is too glib. Fiat money is backed by something real, just something less tangible than gold: the taxing power of the state (you must pay your taxes in the national currency, which creates baseline demand for it), the legal-tender laws that compel its acceptance, and the productive capacity and credibility of the issuing economy. A dollar is, in effect, a claim on the future output of the U.S. economy as mediated by the U.S. government's monopoly on legal tender. The accurate critique of fiat is not that it is "backed by nothing" but that it is backed by a *promise that can be diluted* — the issuer can always print more, which is why fiat currencies lose purchasing power over time and gold does not. The problem is not the absence of backing; it is the *softness* of the backing.

## How it shows up in real markets

History is only useful if it tells you something about the world you actually trade and live in. Two patterns from the monetary history above show up directly in modern markets, and recognizing them is the practical payoff of everything we have covered.

**The 1971 regime break is the single most important feature of the long-run gold chart.** Look again at the price history: a flat line at \$35 for decades, then an explosive, multi-decade ascent. Any analysis of gold that uses pre-1971 data as if it were the same asset is making a category error — before 1971, the gold "price" was a government-set number, not a market price, and it told you about policy, not about supply and demand. The *real* price history of gold-as-a-traded-asset begins on 16 August 1971. From that starting line, gold has compounded at a rate that has roughly tracked the long-run debasement of fiat currencies — not steadily, but in great surging waves that coincide with periods of monetary stress (the 1970s inflation, the 2000s debasement and financial crisis, the post-2022 era of fiscal dominance and reserve weaponization).

#### Worked example: Purchasing power across the 1971 break

The cleanest way to see what "money without a state" means in practice is to compare what an ounce of gold and a stack of dollars have done since the regime broke. In 1971, gold was \$35 an ounce. In 2026 it is roughly \$5,400. So:

```
ounce of gold in 1971:  $35
ounce of gold in 2026:  ~$5,400
multiple:               5,400 / 35 = ~154x
```

Run it the other way to feel it as the dollar's loss rather than gold's gain. One dollar in 1971 bought 1/35 = 0.0286 ounces of gold. In 2026, one dollar buys 1/5,400 = 0.000185 ounces. So measured *in gold*, the dollar has lost about **99.4%** of its value since the gold window closed:

```
dollar's gold value, 1971:  0.0286 oz
dollar's gold value, 2026:  0.000185 oz
retained value:             0.000185 / 0.0286 = 0.0065 = ~0.65%
```

The same ounce of metal that bought you \$35 of goods in 1971 commands \$5,400 of goods today — it has roughly preserved its purchasing power across two generations, while the paper dollar that was its equal in 1971 has been ground down to less than one percent of its gold value. The one-sentence intuition: gold did not get 154 times more *useful* — it is the same inert metal it always was; what changed is the measuring stick, because the dollar shrank by ~99% against the one form of money that cannot be printed.

**Gold rises in every currency, which means it is the currencies that are falling.** This is the deepest practical lesson of "money without a state," and the final chart makes it impossible to miss. We instinctively think of gold's price as a dollar number — "gold is \$5,400" — as if the dollar were the fixed thing and gold the variable. But a citizen of the eurozone, Japan, or Vietnam experiences gold in their *own* currency, and the striking fact is that gold has risen against *all* of them. Since 2015, gold is up many-fold in dollars, euros, yen, and Vietnamese dong alike, as the indexed chart shows.

![Gold indexed to 2015 in US dollars euros yen and Vietnamese dong all rising](/imgs/blogs/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults-7.png)

When something rises against every currency simultaneously, the parsimonious explanation is not that the thing is getting more valuable in some absolute sense — it is that *all the currencies are losing value against it*. Gold is the fixed point; the currencies are the variables. This is the modern echo of the bimetallic story: just as the "stable" 15:1 silver ratio turned out to be the silver and gold drifting apart once the decree was lifted, the "stable" value of money turns out to be paper drifting downward against the one money that cannot be diluted. For a saver in a country with a weaker currency — and we will devote an entire track of this series to Vietnam, where this dynamic is lived rather than theorized — gold is not an exotic speculation. It is the natural unit of account for anyone who intuitively distrusts the durability of their national paper. This is the same force that makes gold a portfolio's monetary insurance line, a framing we develop in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock).

#### Worked example: A Vietnamese saver's view of "money without a state"

Make this concrete with the dong. From the data, gold indexed to 100 in 2015 reached about 520 in VND terms by 2026 — a 5.2x rise in dong. A saver who, in 2015, kept their wealth in 100 million VND of bank deposits versus the same 100 million VND converted to gold experienced two very different decades:

```
held as 100m VND in deposits: still ~100m VND of principal
                              (plus modest interest, eroded by inflation
                               and the dong's slide from ~22,500 to ~26,300 per USD)
held as gold (indexed):       100 -> ~520, i.e. ~520m VND of value
```

The dong saver preserved the *number* but lost purchasing power; the gold saver's holding multiplied roughly five-fold in the same dong terms. The one-sentence intuition: in a currency that the world is slowly voting against, holding gold is not a bet on gold going up — it is a refusal to hold the thing that is going down, which is exactly the "money without a state" insight applied to a household balance sheet.

## The takeaway

Strip away the 5,000 years of detail and the monetary history of gold reduces to a single, clarifying idea that should reshape how you think about the metal sitting in central-bank vaults today.

Gold did not become money because a king decreed it, and it did not stop being money because a president undecreed it in 1971. Gold became money the way a language becomes dominant or a standard becomes universal: through a vast, decentralized, multi-millennium selection process in which it simply out-performed every rival on the six properties that money requires — durability, divisibility, portability, fungibility, hardness, and verifiability. It won that contest before any state existed to grant or withhold its blessing. And that is precisely why the contest's verdict survived the state's later attempt to overturn it. Croesus did not give gold its value; he certified it. Nixon did not take gold's value away; he merely removed gold's *job title*, while the market — and the central banks themselves — quietly kept gold on as the insurance policy underneath the entire fiat system.

What 1971 actually changed was not gold but *paper*. Before 1971, paper money was a claim check on metal, and its supply was disciplined by the metal in the vault. After 1971, paper money became a claim on nothing but the issuer's restraint — and issuers, being governments facing wars, recessions, and elections, have never been able to sustain restraint for long. That is why the dollar has lost roughly 99% of its gold value since the window closed, why gold rises against every currency at once, and why the institutions that *print* the paper are the ones quietly hoarding the metal. The price of gold is not, fundamentally, a forecast about gold. It is a thermometer reading the world's confidence in paper money, real yields, and the political stability of the states that issue currency. When that confidence falls, the thermometer rises.

So here is how the history should change the way you hold gold. Do not treat it as an investment that is supposed to compound and pay you an income — it is not, it pays nothing, and judging it that way will always disappoint you. Treat it as what the central banks do: **money without a state, the base layer of the money pyramid, the one asset that is no one's liability and cannot be printed.** You hold it not because you expect it to go up, but because it is the form of money that survives when the other forms fail — and the entire 5,000-year record, from a Lydian riverbank to the vaults beneath modern central banks, is the evidence that it does.
