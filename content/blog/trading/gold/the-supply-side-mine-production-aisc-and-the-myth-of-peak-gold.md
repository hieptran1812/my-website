> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — For almost every commodity, supply is half the price story. For gold it is nearly irrelevant, because gold is a *stock* (almost all of it ever mined still exists), mine output is *inelastic* (a new mine takes ~10 years to build), and the cost of mining sets a soft *floor* under the price but never the *ceiling*.
>
> - From 2019 to 2026 the gold price roughly **tripled** (about \$1,393 → \$5,400/oz) while world mine production barely moved — from ~3,300 to ~3,661 tonnes a year. That is the whole post in one fact.
> - **All-in sustaining cost (AISC)** — the cash cost to keep a mine running, ~\$1,390/oz in 2024 — is a *floor*: it tells you where mining gets unprofitable, not where the price stops rising.
> - **Recycling** (~1,370 t in 2024) is the only fast-responding supply, and even a price-driven scrap surge is a few hundred tonnes against a **216,000-tonne** above-ground hoard.
> - **"Peak gold"** — the idea that mine output has plateaued — is probably real, and it barely matters: a stock that grows ~1.7% a year is unmoved by whether that figure is 1.7% or 1.5%.
> - **The one fact to remember:** a gold thesis is a demand-and-real-rates thesis, never a supply thesis. You can almost ignore the supply side and still get gold right.

Gold tripled from 2019 to 2026, yet mine production barely moved — and that is the most revealing fact about how gold is priced. Sit with how strange that is. In the spring of 2024, gold averaged **\$2,388 an ounce**, roughly **\$1,000 an ounce above** what it cost the average miner to dig it out of the ground. In January 2026 it spiked to an all-time high near **\$5,589/oz**. A gross profit margin of forty percent and then far more, sustained year after year, on a product the whole world wants — in almost any other industry that is a starting gun. New factories. Extra shifts. Competitors piling in until the extra supply competes the price back down.

Gold did none of that. Mine output in 2024 was about **3,661 tonnes**, essentially flat against the prior several years and only a hair above the all-time production high set back in 2018. The miners booked spectacular margins and the world's gold supply just sat there. A year later, with the price more than doubled again, the supply side answered with a shrug.

That non-response is not an accident or a failure. It is the deepest structural fact about the gold market, and it has a clean consequence: **for gold, supply is almost irrelevant to price.** For oil, copper, wheat, lithium, or pork bellies, you cannot forecast the price without forecasting supply — a new shale basin, a copper strike, a drought, a glut. For gold you can mostly ignore the supply side and still get the price right, because the thing that moves gold is on the *demand* side: real interest rates, the dollar, central-bank buying, and the world's confidence in paper money. This post is about *why* that is true — and why even the much-discussed idea of "peak gold" changes the conclusion barely at all.

![Demand and price both tripled from 2019 to 2026 while mine production stayed flat](/imgs/blogs/the-supply-side-mine-production-aisc-and-the-myth-of-peak-gold-1.png)

A companion post in this series, [where gold comes from](/blog/trading/gold/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock), walked through the supply *chain* — ore grades, refining into a Good Delivery bar, recycling, and the giant above-ground stock. This post is about the *price consequences* of all that: inelasticity, the cost curve, AISC as a floor, recycling as the only fast supply, peak gold, and the bottom line that a gold thesis is a demand thesis. We will lean on that earlier post for the mechanics and go deep here on what it all means for price.

## Foundations: stock, flow, cost, and elasticity

Four ideas do all the work in this post. Let us define each from zero, because the conclusion — "supply barely matters" — is a direct logical consequence of the four, not a vibe.

### Stock versus flow (the one A3 established)

A **flow** is how much of something is produced or used *per period* — barrels of oil per day, bushels of wheat per harvest. A **stock** is how much of it *exists in total* at a point in time — all the oil in all the tanks, all the wheat in all the silos. For most commodities the flow dwarfs the stock: oil and wheat are *consumed*, burned and eaten, so the world holds only a few weeks or months of supply at any moment. Price is therefore set by this year's flow against this year's use.

Gold is the opposite. Gold is not consumed. Almost every gram ever refined still exists — in a ring, a bar, a coin, a vault. So gold's **stock is enormous and its flow is tiny.** As the [supply-chain post](/blog/trading/gold/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock) laid out, the above-ground stock is about **216,000 tonnes**, while a record year of mining adds only about **3,661 tonnes**. That ratio — stock divided by annual flow — is gold's celebrated **stock-to-flow ratio**, near **59**. It would take roughly fifty-nine years of full-tilt global mining just to re-dig the gold that already exists. Copper's stock-to-flow is barely a month; oil's is measured in weeks. Hold that number; it is the reason everything else in this post is true.

The practical difference this makes is hard to overstate. When a copper mine in Chile floods or an oil field in the Gulf goes offline, a meaningful slice of *this year's available supply* vanishes overnight, and the price jumps — because the world holds only a few weeks of buffer. The flow *is* the market. But when a gold mine floods, the world still has its 216,000 tonnes sitting in vaults and jewellery boxes, and the lost few tonnes of new production are invisible against that ocean. There is no "supply scare" in gold because there is always a 59-year buffer sitting above ground, owned by people who could, in principle, sell. The flow is *not* the market; the stock is. And the stock's owners decide whether to part with it based not on this year's mine output but on real interest rates and their confidence in paper money — which is to say, on demand. The whole architecture of "supply doesn't matter" is sitting inside that one ratio.

#### Worked example: the stock-to-flow ratio, and why doubling mine output barely registers

Start with the published figures: above-ground stock ≈ **216,000 t**, 2024 mine production ≈ **3,661 t**. The stock-to-flow ratio is:

```
stock / flow  =  216,000 t / 3,661 t  =  59.0
```

So one year of mining grows the total pile by `3,661 / 216,000 = 1.69%`. Now suppose a miracle: the world somehow *doubles* mine output overnight to 7,322 t — physically impossible given lead times, but indulge it. The stock would then grow by `7,322 / 216,000 = 3.39%` instead of 1.69%. The extra supply added to the market in that miraculous year is one extra 1.69% of the stock. **Even a doubling of mine production — the most violent supply shock imaginable — adds under 2% to the standing pile of gold in a single year.** That single fact is why supply cannot drive gold's price the way it drives oil's: the lever is just too small.

### A 90-second refresher on how an ounce is actually made

The earlier [supply-chain post](/blog/trading/gold/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock) covers this in depth; here is just enough to make the cost curve make sense. Gold mining is, stripped to its essence, three steps: find rock with a tiny amount of gold in it, dig up an enormous quantity of that rock, and use chemistry and heat to separate the gold from the dirt. A modern open-pit mine might process tens of thousands of tonnes of ore a day to recover a few tens of kilograms of gold. The recovered gold is poured into rough *doré* bars at the mine, then shipped to a refiner who purifies it to the 99.5%-plus standard of an LBMA Good Delivery bar.

The reason this matters for *price* is that every step costs money, and the cost depends on things the miner cannot control: how deep the ore sits, how rich it is, how hard the rock is, the price of diesel and electricity, and the wage and regulatory environment of the country it's in. A shallow, rich, open-pit deposit in a cheap-power jurisdiction produces gold for a fraction of what a deep, low-grade underground mine in a high-cost country spends. That spread in extraction cost — mine by mine — *is* the cost curve, and it is why the question "at what price do mines shut?" has no single answer. It depends entirely on *which* mine you mean.

### What "AISC" actually is

**AISC** stands for **all-in sustaining cost** — the gold-mining industry's standard measure of what it costs to produce an ounce and *keep producing it.* It rolls together the cash cost of digging and processing the ore, plus the sustaining capital a mine must spend just to stand still: maintenance, equipment replacement, ongoing exploration to replace mined-out reserves, site administration, and royalties. It deliberately *excludes* the big upfront cost of building a brand-new mine (that is "growth" capital, a separate decision) and excludes interest and taxes.

Think of AISC as the mine's *break-even running cost*: at a gold price above its AISC, the mine makes money on every ounce; below it, the mine bleeds cash to keep the lights on. In 2024 the industry-average AISC was about **\$1,390/oz**. The single most important thing to understand about AISC is what it is *not*: it is not a price target, not a fair value, and not a ceiling. It is a soft floor, and a soft one at that — we will see exactly why miners keep running below it.

### The cost curve

Not every mine costs the same to run. Rank the world's gold mines from cheapest to most expensive to operate, and you trace out the industry **cost curve**: a rising staircase from the lowest-cost producers (rich ore, open pits, cheap power) on the left to the highest-cost producers (deep shafts, low grades, old infrastructure) on the right. The cheapest mines might run at an AISC near \$900/oz; the most marginal, near \$1,900/oz. The cost curve is the central object for thinking about supply, because it tells you *which* mines make money at a given price and *which* would lose money — and therefore which, in theory, should shut when the price falls. The punchline, which we will earn below, is that even the losers rarely shut quickly.

### Price elasticity of supply

The last piece of vocabulary is the one economists reach for: **price elasticity of supply.** It answers a simple question — when the price rises by 1%, by how many percent does the quantity supplied rise? A *high* (elastic) elasticity means supply jumps to chase a higher price; a *low* (inelastic) elasticity means supply barely budges no matter how high the price goes. Formally:

```
elasticity  =  (% change in quantity supplied) / (% change in price)
```

Wheat is fairly elastic: a good price gets more acres planted next season. Gold is almost perfectly *inelastic* — and the rest of this post is, in effect, an explanation of *why* that number is so close to zero, and what follows from it.

#### Worked example: the (near-zero) elasticity of gold supply, 2019-2026

Plug the real numbers into the formula. Gold's annual-average price went from about **\$1,393/oz in 2019** to roughly **\$5,400/oz in 2026** — a rise of:

```
(5,400 - 1,393) / 1,393  =  +287.6%   (call it "about +200% to +290%")
```

Over the same window, mine production went from about **3,300 t (2019)** to **3,661 t (2024, the recent peak)** — a rise of:

```
(3,661 - 3,300) / 3,300  =  +10.9%
```

So the elasticity of gold mine supply over this stretch was roughly:

```
+10.9% / +287.6%  =  0.038
```

An elasticity of **about 0.04** means a near-tripling of the price summoned only an eleven-percent increase in output — and even that eleven percent was largely the result of mine plans set in motion *years before* the price moved, not a response to it. For comparison, a textbook elastic good has an elasticity well above 1. Gold's ~0.04 is, for practical purposes, a vertical supply curve: **price can do whatever it likes and the quantity barely moves.** That is the analytical heart of this entire post.

With the four ideas in hand — gold is a stock, AISC is a running cost, the cost curve ranks producers, and supply elasticity is near zero — every other claim follows. Let us build them out.

## Why mine supply is inelastic

The single number above (elasticity ≈ 0.04) demands an explanation. *Why* can't gold miners respond to a tripling of the price? It is not laziness or a cartel. It is four hard constraints — physical, geological, financial, and structural — stacked on top of each other, each of which would slow the supply response on its own and which together make it nearly inert.

Before the list, it's worth fixing the intuition for *why this is even possible*, because in a normal market it would not be. In wheat, oil, or copper, a high price reliably calls forth more supply on a horizon of months to a couple of years — plant more acres, drill more wells, run an extra shift. Those markets have short, flexible supply chains and producers who can ramp quickly. Gold has none of that flexibility: its supply chain is the slowest in the entire commodity world, its raw material is getting physically scarcer, and its producers are scarred into caution. The result is a supply curve that, on any horizon a trader or investor cares about, simply does not move. Here are the four reasons it stays put.

### It takes about a decade to build a mine

The first and biggest reason is *time.* You cannot conjure a gold mine in response to a price signal. The pipeline from "we think there's gold here" to "metal reaches the market" runs roughly **ten years**, and often longer. Walk the stages:

![Timeline from discovery through permitting and construction to first gold production over about ten years](/imgs/blogs/the-supply-side-mine-production-aisc-and-the-myth-of-peak-gold-5.png)

First you have to *find* the gold — years of geological surveying and drilling to prove an ore body actually exists and is big enough to bother with. Then a **feasibility study**: can this rock be mined profitably at a plausible AISC? Then **permitting** — environmental review, water rights, community agreements, government licences — which in many jurisdictions takes longer than the mining itself and can stall for a decade. Then **financing and construction**: raising hundreds of millions or billions of dollars and physically building the pit, the mill, the tailings facility, the roads and power. Only then does the first ounce reach the market.

The consequence is brutal and clean: **the gold price today can do absolutely nothing to supply for roughly ten years.** When gold spiked in 2024-2026, the mines producing that year had been planned, permitted, and funded back in the 2010s — when gold was sometimes under \$1,200. Conversely, the high prices of 2024-2026 might, *maybe,* nudge a few extra projects to start now — and they will deliver gold in the 2030s, into a world whose price nobody can forecast. By the time a miner can "respond" to a price, the price that prompted the response is ancient history. Supply and price are decoupled in time, and a decade of lag is a decade of irrelevance.

One natural objection: surely a high price funds a *boom in exploration* that shortens the lag? It funds the boom, yes — but it does not shorten the lag, and often it makes the bottleneck worse. Exploration is the *first* step of the ten-year pipeline, not a shortcut around it. A flood of exploration dollars in 2024 buys more drilling, more geologists, more junior-miner share issuance — and a discovery in 2025 still faces feasibility, permitting, financing, and construction before it produces an ounce in the mid-2030s. Worse, the discovery rate for large, high-grade gold deposits has been *falling* for decades despite rising exploration budgets: the easy, near-surface "elephants" have largely been found, and what's left is deeper, smaller, and more remote. So pouring money into exploration during a price spike is like flooring the accelerator on a car stuck in first gear with a ten-year transmission. It feels like a response. It changes this decade's supply not at all.

### Ore grades are falling, so the same effort yields less gold

The second constraint is geological and it runs against the miners. Humanity, sensibly, mined the *best* gold first — the richest, shallowest, easiest deposits. What remains is, on average, lower grade and harder to reach. A century ago, mined ore commonly graded 10-20 grams of gold per tonne of rock; today the global average is closer to **1-1.5 grams per tonne**. (As the [supply-chain post](/blog/trading/gold/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock) noted, a gram is about the mass of a paperclip — and a typical modern mine moves a *tonne* of rock to recover one or two of them.)

Declining grade means that even running flat-out, the industry has to move *more* rock, burn *more* energy, and spend *more* per ounce just to hold output steady. It is a treadmill: the miners run faster every year and the output curve stays roughly level. This is also why AISC drifts *up* over time even when the price of diesel and labour is stable — the rock itself is getting stingier. A rising real cost of extraction is the geological counterweight to a rising price.

### The capital cycle makes miners cautious exactly when they should expand

The third constraint is psychological and financial, and it is the cruel one. Mining companies are run by people who remember the *last* cycle. Gold miners spent the 2011-2015 bust writing off tens of billions of dollars of value — they had built and bought aggressively near the 2011 top at \$1,900, then watched the price collapse to \$1,060 by 2015, turning those shiny new projects into money-losing albatrosses. The scars from that episode taught the entire industry a doctrine: *capital discipline.* Pay down debt, return cash to shareholders, do not chase the price with reckless expansion.

So in 2024-2026, with margins the fattest in the industry's history, the major gold miners largely **did not** plough that cash into a wave of new mines. They paid dividends, bought back stock, and bought *each other* (it is faster and cheaper to acquire an existing mine than to permit a new one). This is the well-documented **capital cycle**: producers over-invest at the top, get burned, then under-invest for years afterward — which means the supply response to a price is not only slow but actively suppressed by the memory of the last crash. The behaviour that would flood the market is exactly the behaviour the survivors swore off. We will return to the live 2019-2026 version of this in the real-markets section.

### No single producer can swing the market

There is a fourth, quieter reason mine supply can't respond: it is *spread out.* In oil, a single decision by OPEC — a few governments coordinating around one table — can add or remove millions of barrels a day and move the price in a week. Gold has no equivalent swing producer. Mining is geographically diffuse, scattered across dozens of countries with different geology, politics, and ownership, and not one of them is large enough to flood or starve the market by choice.

![Top gold-mining countries in 2023 by tonnes of production](/imgs/blogs/the-supply-side-mine-production-aisc-and-the-myth-of-peak-gold-2.png)

Look at the league table. The biggest single producer — China — mines about **370 tonnes a year**, roughly **10% of world output**. Russia and Australia follow near 290-310 t; Canada, the United States, and a long tail of others fill out the rest. There is no producer with the market share OPEC commands in oil, no cartel, no swing barrel. The diffuse, fragmented structure of gold mining is one more reason the supply curve sits there inert: no one is holding the dial, and even the largest hand on it is too small to turn it. This stands in sharp contrast to the demand side, where a handful of central banks *can* move the market with a coordinated shift, as we will see.

#### Worked example: even the biggest producer can't move the stock

Push the "swing producer" idea to its limit. Suppose China — the world's number-one gold miner — decided to *halve* its output overnight for strategic reasons, the most dramatic supply cut any single nation could make. China mines about 370 t/yr, so the cut removes:

```
supply removed  =  370 t × 50%  =  185 t / yr
as a share of annual flow  =  185 t / 5,031 t  =  3.7%
as a share of above-ground stock  =  185 t / 216,000 t  =  0.086%
```

The single largest unilateral supply cut on Earth shaves under 4% off the *annual flow* and under one-tenth of one percent off the *stock that actually clears the market.* Compare an oil market where OPEC routinely adjusts output by 1-2 million barrels a day — several percent of *global* daily supply — and moves prices within a week. Gold has no such lever because there is no swing producer and, more fundamentally, no flow worth swinging against the stock. **There is no hand on the gold-supply dial big enough to matter** — which is exactly why traders don't bother looking for one.

#### Worked example: the 2024 margin that did not summon supply

Put the margin in dollars and feel its size. In 2024 the average mined ounce sold for the annual-average price of **\$2,388** and cost an AISC of **\$1,390** to produce and sustain:

```
margin per ounce  =  2,388 - 1,390  =  $998 / oz
margin as % of price  =  998 / 2,388  =  41.8%
```

Now compare 2015, the trough: price ≈ **\$1,160**, AISC ≈ **\$900**, so the margin was `1,160 - 900 = $260/oz`, about **22%** — and in real terms many specific mines were near break-even or below. Between 2015 and 2024 the per-ounce profit on gold **quadrupled** (from \$260 to \$998). In steel, chips, or soybeans, a quadrupling of unit margins would unleash a torrent of new capacity. In gold, mine output over those same years rose by roughly a tenth, most of it from projects already under construction. The lesson in one line: **gold's profit signal is loud and its supply response is nearly mute** — exactly what an elasticity of 0.04 means in dollars.

## AISC as a soft floor, not a ceiling

We now have the inelasticity story for *rising* prices. The mirror-image question is about *falling* prices: if the gold price drops below what it costs many mines to operate, shouldn't supply collapse, pulling production offline and putting a hard floor under the price? The answer — *sort of, eventually, and far less than you'd think* — is the second pillar of "supply doesn't drive price." AISC is a *floor* on the price in the loosest sense, and emphatically not a ceiling.

### Why AISC is a floor at all

The floor intuition is straightforward. If the gold price sits below the AISC of a large slice of the industry, those mines are losing cash on every ounce. Over time some will defer spending, high-grade their best ore to cut costs, idle their worst pits, and eventually close. That removal of supply *should* help the price recover. So in a rough, multi-year sense, the cost curve does put a soft cushion under gold: prices that stay deeply below the bulk of the industry's AISC are not sustainable, because the industry slowly shrinks until they recover. This is the kernel of truth in "AISC is the floor."

It is worth noting that AISC is also a *slippery* number, which makes "the floor" even softer than it first appears. AISC is a metric the industry largely defines for itself, and miners have real discretion over what they include — which costs count as "sustaining" versus "growth," how they treat by-product credits (a gold mine that also produces copper or silver can net those revenues against its gold cost, reporting a dramatically lower AISC), and how they allocate corporate overhead. The headline industry-average AISC you read — about \$1,390/oz in 2024 — masks an enormous spread across mines and a fair amount of accounting flexibility. So even the *level* of the supposed floor is fuzzy, mine by mine, year by year. A trader who treats "industry AISC" as a precise line gold cannot cross is leaning on a number that is part geology, part accounting convention.

### Why it is a *soft* floor — miners keep running at a loss

But the floor is mushy, and understanding why is the whole point. Picture the cost curve as a ladder of producers, and slide an imaginary "current price" line across it.

![A four-tier cost curve showing low to high cost mines, their margins, and whether they shut when price falls](/imgs/blogs/the-supply-side-mine-production-aisc-and-the-myth-of-peak-gold-6.png)

When the price falls below a high-cost mine's AISC, the mine does *not* simply switch off the next morning, for several stubborn reasons:

- **Sunk costs are sunk.** The billions spent building the mine are gone whether it runs or not. The relevant decision is at the margin: does running cover the *cash* costs of running? AISC includes sustaining capital that can be deferred, so a mine bleeding against AISC may still be cash-positive on a stripped-down basis, and will keep running.
- **Closing and reopening is enormously expensive.** Mothballing a mine means severance, flooding control, environmental remediation, and the loss of a skilled workforce that may never come back. Reopening later costs a fortune and takes years. Faced with a temporary dip, managers rationally choose to *endure* rather than close, betting the price recovers before they run out of cash.
- **Contracts, hedges, and obligations.** Miners often have forward sales, offtake agreements, debt covenants, and government or community commitments that make idling costly or contractually impossible.
- **High-grading buys time.** A struggling mine can temporarily mine only its richest ore, slashing its effective cost per ounce — at the price of sterilizing the lower-grade ore around it. This keeps the mine alive through a downturn without "responding" to price in the way an economist's supply curve predicts.

The net effect: supply is *sticky downward* as well as inelastic upward. Mines do not flood in when the price soars, and they do not vanish when the price sags. The 2013-2015 bust is the proof: gold fell from \$1,669 (2012 average) to \$1,160 (2015), AISC for many producers was near or above the price, the industry took huge write-downs — and yet **mine production rose every single one of those years**, from ~2,900 t in 2012 to over 3,200 t in 2015. Output went *up* into a brutal bear market, because mines already built keep running, and projects already started get finished. There could be no cleaner demonstration that AISC is not a hard floor on supply.

#### Worked example: the high-cost mine that bleeds but doesn't shut

Take a marginal mine with an AISC of **\$1,600/oz** and suppose the gold price drops to **\$1,200/oz**. On paper the mine loses:

```
loss per ounce (full AISC basis)  =  1,200 - 1,600  =  -$400 / oz
```

A \$400 loss on every ounce sounds like an obvious shutdown. But AISC of \$1,600 might break down as, say, \$1,150 of true cash cost (labour, fuel, reagents) plus \$450 of sustaining capital and exploration that can be *deferred.* On a deferred-capital basis the mine's cash position is:

```
cash margin  =  1,200 - 1,150  =  +$50 / oz
```

So the mine is *still cash-positive* if it stops investing in its own future — it generates \$50 an ounce by running down its remaining good ore. Mothballing would cost tens of millions upfront and forfeit any chance of recovery. The rational choice is to keep mining, harvest the cash, and pray the price recovers. **That is why a price below AISC removes supply only slowly, over years, and not in the clean way a textbook supply curve implies.** The floor exists, but it is made of foam, not concrete.

### And why it is never a ceiling

If a soft floor is the downside story, the *ceiling* question is even more lopsided, and it ties straight back to inelasticity. When the price runs far *above* the entire cost curve — as in 2024-2026, when even the highest-cost mine was wildly profitable — nothing on the supply side stops the rally, because new supply cannot arrive for a decade and existing mines are already running flat out. There is no mechanism by which "miners make too much money" caps the price. The price is set by what buyers will pay, full stop. **AISC tells you where mining gets painful, not where the rally ends.** This asymmetry — soft floor below, no ceiling above — is exactly what you would expect from a vertical supply curve, and it is why charts of gold-price-versus-AISC show the price wandering hundreds or thousands of dollars away from the cost line in both directions, never anchored to it.

## Recycling: the only fast-responding supply

If mine supply is a decade-lagged, inelastic boulder, is there *any* part of gold supply that responds to price quickly? Yes — exactly one — and it is small. It is **recycling**: old jewellery, dental gold, scrap from electronics, and the contents of "cash for gold" shops, melted down and refined back into bars. Recycling is the genuinely price-sensitive sliver of supply, and seeing both *that* it responds and *how little* that response is settles the supply question for good.

![Annual gold supply for 2024 split into mine production and recycling](/imgs/blogs/the-supply-side-mine-production-aisc-and-the-myth-of-peak-gold-4.png)

In 2024, total gold supply was about **3,661 t of mine production plus 1,370 t of recycling**, for roughly **5,031 t** in all. Recycling was about **27%** of supply — meaningful, but the junior partner. Crucially, recycling behaves the opposite way from mining: it has *no lead time.* The moment the price jumps, people in Mumbai, Istanbul, Ho Chi Minh City, and Phoenix decide their old necklace is suddenly worth selling, and scrap flows to the refiners within weeks. Recycling rose sharply in the high-price years of 2011-2012 and again in 2024-2026; it falls when prices are low and people hold on to their gold. It is the swing component — the one part of supply that actually obeys the price signal.

There is a nice irony in *where* recycling comes from. The biggest recycling responses tend to come from the very countries — India, Turkey, the Gulf, Vietnam, Southeast Asia — where households hold the most physical gold as savings and where a price spike is felt most personally. A family that bought gold jewellery as a store of value will, at a high enough price, monetize some of it: sell the old bangle, take the profit, perhaps buy it back later. This is gold doing its job as money — convertible back to cash when needed — and it is also the *only* mechanism by which the gigantic above-ground stock feeds back into supply at all. Every recycled ounce is an ounce that was already counted in the stock; recycling does not add new gold to the world, it merely *moves* gold from the "held" pile into the "for sale" pile and back again.

And yet it changes the conclusion not at all, for two reasons. First, recycling is bounded by the simple fact that people are *reluctant* to sell heirloom gold, religious gold, and wedding gold even at a high price — much of the above-ground stock is held for reasons (sentiment, tradition, insurance against catastrophe) that resist any price. The gold in a wedding necklace or a central bank's vault is not "supply" in any practical sense; it is removed from the market by the holder's intent. Second, and decisively, even a big recycling surge is tiny against the stock.

#### Worked example: how much can a price spike pull from the scrap drawer?

Suppose a dramatic price spike pulls an *extra* 300 tonnes of recycled gold into the market in a year — a large response, in the ballpark of the jump seen in 2024 over a normal year. Set that against the above-ground stock:

```
extra recycled supply  /  above-ground stock  =  300 t / 216,000 t  =  0.14%
```

A *huge* recycling response — hundreds of tonnes of grandma's necklaces hitting the refiners — amounts to about **one-seventh of one percent** of all the gold in the world. Even adding it to the entire normal flow of new supply, total supply moves from ~5,031 t to ~5,331 t, a 6% bump in the flow and a rounding error against the stock. So yes, recycling is the fast supply — and "fast" still cannot move a stock this large. **The only price-responsive supply gold has is far too small to set the price.** That is the last brick in the wall: neither slow mine supply nor fast scrap supply has the size to be the marginal price-setter. Demand is.

## Peak gold: the real plateau and why it barely matters

No discussion of gold supply is complete without "**peak gold**" — the idea, much repeated since the late 2010s, that world mine production has reached or is reaching a permanent plateau and will soon decline. It is the gold version of "peak oil." Is it true? Probably, roughly. Does it matter for the price? Almost not at all. Both halves of that answer are important, and they sit together more comfortably than people expect.

### The plateau is real

The case for peak gold is solid and follows directly from the inelasticity story. Mine production has *flattened*: from the 2010s into the mid-2020s, annual output has hovered around 3,500-3,700 t, setting marginal new records (2018, then 2024) but no longer growing the way it did from 2000 to 2010, when it climbed steadily. The reasons are exactly the constraints we have catalogued: the best deposits have been found and largely depleted; new discoveries are smaller, deeper, and lower grade; permitting is slower and stricter than ever; and the capital cycle has made the industry allergic to aggressive expansion. Many serious analysts believe annual mine output will *gently decline* over the coming decades. So "peak gold," interpreted modestly as *mine output has plateaued and may slowly fall,* is likely correct.

### Why it barely matters

Here is the twist that the headlines miss. **Peak gold is a story about the flow, and gold's price is a story about the stock.** Go back to the stock-to-flow arithmetic: mining adds about 1.7% to the above-ground pile each year. Suppose peak gold is real and mine output drifts down so that new supply adds only 1.4% a year instead of 1.7%. The pile still *grows* — just a hair more slowly. The difference between a 1.7% and a 1.4% annual increment to a 216,000-tonne stock is a few hundred tonnes a year against a 200,000-tonne-plus base: invisible to the price, which is being whipped around by central banks buying a *thousand* tonnes a year and by real interest rates moving the entire 216,000-tonne hoard's appeal.

There is a deep irony here. "Peak gold" is usually invoked as a *bullish* argument — *output is falling, so the price must rise!* But the entire thesis of this post is that mine output is too small a lever to set the price in *either* direction. If a tripling of the price couldn't summon new supply (the inelasticity we measured), then a gentle decline in supply cannot, by itself, propel the price. Peak gold and "supply doesn't matter" are not in tension; they are the *same fact* viewed from two sides. Supply is so inelastic and so tiny relative to the stock that whether it ticks up or down is a sideshow. The main event — always — is demand and the real cost of holding a non-yielding asset, the subject of the rest of this series.

It is instructive to remember how "peak oil" played out, because gold-bugs often borrow the framing. For decades, "peak oil" predicted that declining production would send oil prices ever higher. What actually happened was that high prices *and new technology* (shale, deepwater drilling) unlocked vast new supply, and the peak kept receding. The peak-oil thesis failed largely *because oil supply is elastic* — high prices summon new barrels. Peak gold inverts this in a way few notice: gold supply is *inelastic*, so high prices will *not* unlock a flood of new metal the way they did for oil — the peak-gold plateau is more likely to be real *because* the supply response is so weak. But that same inelasticity is exactly why the plateau doesn't help the price: the lever is too small in both directions. Peak oil was wrong about supply and therefore wrong about price; peak gold is probably right about supply and *still* says almost nothing about price. The asset's defining feature — a supply that cannot respond — quietly defeats the bullish supply argument it is usually recruited to support.

#### Worked example: peak gold's price effect is a rounding error

Make the bullish peak-gold case as strong as honesty allows and watch it shrink. Suppose mine supply falls 20% over a decade, from 3,661 t to ~2,930 t a year — a steep decline few forecasters expect. Recycling roughly fills part of the gap (it rises as the price rises). But take the worst case and assume total *new* supply to the market falls by, say, 500 t a year. Against the stock:

```
lost annual supply  /  above-ground stock  =  500 t / 216,000 t  =  0.23%
```

A decade of dramatic supply decline removes about **a quarter of one percent** of the world's gold from the *annual increment* — it does not remove a single ounce from the existing 216,000-tonne stock, which is what people actually hold and value. Set that against demand swings: central banks alone shifted from net *selling* ~400 t/yr before 2010 to net *buying* over 1,000 t/yr after 2022 — a roughly 1,400-tonne annual swing in demand, dwarfing any plausible supply decline by an order of magnitude. **The biggest supply story of our era is, at most, a rounding error next to a single demand regime change.** That is why a serious gold analyst spends almost no time on peak gold.

## Common misconceptions

Supply is where gold mythology runs thickest, because people import their intuitions from normal commodities. Here are the four most common, each corrected with a number.

### Myth 1: "High prices will bring a flood of new supply that crashes the price."

This is the most natural mistake — it is true for nearly every other good — and it is wrong for gold. We measured it: from 2019 to 2026 the price roughly tripled and mine output rose about 11%, an elasticity of **~0.04**. A new mine takes a *decade* to build, so today's price cannot create today's supply, and the capital cycle suppresses even the delayed response. The "flood" never comes because the spigot has a ten-year delay and a frightened hand on the valve. High gold prices persist *because* supply cannot punish them.

### Myth 2: "We're going to run out of gold."

The flip side, beloved of the doom-and-asteroid crowd, is equally wrong. You cannot "run out" of a stock that does not get consumed. Even if every mine on Earth closed tomorrow, the **216,000 tonnes** already above ground would still be here — in vaults, rings, and coins — available to be traded, lent, and recycled forever. As the [supply-chain post](/blog/trading/gold/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock) put it, gold is not used up; it is *accumulated.* "Running out" is a flow concept applied to an asset whose whole identity is its stock. New mining could cease entirely and the gold market would barely notice for years.

### Myth 3: "A new technology — or a big discovery, or asteroid mining — will crash gold."

Periodically someone predicts that seawater extraction, a giant new discovery, or mining a metal-rich asteroid will flood the market and collapse the price. Run the arithmetic on the most dramatic version. Even an enormous one-off injection — say a fantastical 5,000 tonnes, more than a year's entire mine output dumped at once — would add `5,000 / 216,000 = 2.3%` to the stock. A 2.3% bump to supply against a stock that the world re-prices daily on real rates and confidence is simply not a price-crashing event; markets absorb larger demand swings routinely. Asteroid mining is also wildly uneconomic for the foreseeable future, but even granting it, the *stock-to-flow* shield means no realistic supply surprise can break gold the way a new oil basin breaks oil. The thing that makes gold valuable — that its stock cannot be quickly inflated — is exactly the thing that immunizes it against supply shocks.

### Myth 4: "AISC is the floor, so gold can't fall below ~\$1,400."

A popular trading heuristic says gold "can't" fall far below industry AISC because mines would shut and choke off supply. We saw why this is only loosely true: in 2013-2015 the price fell well into the cost curve and production *rose anyway*, because sunk costs, deferred capital, and high-grading keep loss-making mines running for years. AISC is a *soft, multi-year* floor, not a hard line a trader can lean on. Gold absolutely can — and historically did — trade below the AISC of a large slice of the industry for an extended stretch. Treat AISC as a slow gravitational pull, not a trampoline.

### Myth 5: "Miners are colluding to keep supply tight."

When you tell someone that gold output stayed flat through the richest margins in history, a tempting conclusion is that the miners must be *conspiring* to restrain supply and keep prices high, like an OPEC of gold. There is no such cartel, and none is needed — which is the more interesting truth. We saw that mine production is geographically fragmented across dozens of countries with no dominant producer; coordination on the scale OPEC manages is simply not possible. What looks like collusion is just thousands of independent companies all facing the *same* constraints at the *same* time: a ten-year lead time, falling grades, slow permitting, and the shared scar tissue of the 2011-2015 crash that turned the whole industry toward capital discipline. Restraint that emerges from physics and memory does not require a smoke-filled room. The flat supply curve is structural, not conspiratorial — and that is exactly why it is so reliable.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstractions are cheap; let us watch the supply story play out in the actual market of 2019-2026, the most extreme natural experiment the gold market has ever run.

### 2019-2026: the price tripled and supply shrugged

The headline experiment is the one we opened with, and it is worth stating precisely. Over 2019-2026:

- **Price:** annual-average gold ran from about **\$1,393/oz (2019)** to roughly **\$5,400/oz (2026)** — a near-tripling, with the January 2026 intraday all-time high near **\$5,589**.
- **Mine supply:** from about **3,300 t (2019)** to **3,661 t (2024)** — up roughly 11%, and essentially at a plateau thereafter.
- **Recycling:** rose with the price, as expected of the swing component — the only part of supply that visibly responded.

If supply set gold's price, a near-tripling would have brought a wall of new metal and competed the price back down. Instead, supply did almost nothing while the price ran. The driver was unambiguously on the demand side: collapsing then volatile *real* interest rates, a structural surge in *central-bank* buying after 2022, and waves of safe-haven and debasement demand. The [macro-trading take on commodities](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) frames why gold trades on monetary variables while oil and copper trade on the economic cycle — gold's inelastic, stock-dominated supply is precisely what frees its price to be a pure monetary signal.

### The margin expanded enormously without a supply flood

Now look at the miners' profitability through the same window, because it is the cleanest refutation of the "high prices bring supply" myth.

![Miner margin per ounce and as a percent of price from 2013 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-supply-side-mine-production-aisc-and-the-myth-of-peak-gold-7.png)

The per-ounce margin (price minus AISC) went from around **\$231/oz in 2013** to **\$998/oz in 2024**, and as a share of the price climbed from the high teens to about **42%**. By 2025-2026, with the price doubled again and AISC rising only modestly, margins were the fattest the industry had ever recorded. And the supply response to the richest margins in history was — again — essentially flat output, plus a wave of dividends, buybacks, and merger activity. The 2024-2026 episode is the capital cycle in real time: producers harvested the windfall and pointedly refused to repeat the 2011-2015 mistake of expanding into the top.

### 2013-2015: production rose into a crash

The mirror-image episode is the bust that taught the industry its discipline. Gold fell from a 2012 average near **\$1,669** to **\$1,160 in 2015** — a 30% drop that pushed the price into or below the AISC of many specific mines. The textbook says supply should contract. Instead, mine production *rose* every year of the bust, from roughly 2,900 t (2012) to over 3,200 t (2015), as projects greenlit during the 2010-2012 boom finished construction and came online into a falling market. The industry took enormous write-downs and still produced more gold. There is no clearer real-world proof that AISC is not a hard floor and that supply, in gold, marches to a decade-old drumbeat rather than to today's price.

### 2001-2011: a decade-long bull, and supply that crept

Zoom out to the great 2001-2011 bull, when gold rose roughly sevenfold from around \$270 to the September 2011 peak near \$1,920. This is the one window where supply *did* grow meaningfully — but it is the exception that proves the rule, because look at *how* it grew and *when.* World mine production actually fell or stagnated through the early 2000s even as the price climbed, then rose gradually from the late 2000s onward as the high prices of the mid-2000s finally — after the requisite decade of lag — funded a wave of new projects that came online around 2008-2014. The supply response to the *early-2000s* price rise didn't show up until *ten years later,* by which point the price had already done most of its work. And even that response was a gentle climb, not a flood: from roughly 2,600 t (2008) to 3,000 t (2014), a ~15% increase spread over the back half of a sevenfold price move. The 2001-2011 decade is not a counterexample to inelasticity; it is inelasticity in slow motion, with the ten-year lag stamped clearly on the timing.

### Central banks: the demand that dwarfs every supply story

Finally, the scale check that makes the whole supply debate feel small. After Western governments froze Russia's foreign-exchange reserves in 2022, central banks around the world accelerated their buying of the one reserve asset that is no one's liability. Official-sector net purchases ran **1,082 t (2022)**, **1,037 t (2023)**, and **1,045 t (2024)** — versus a 2010-2021 average near **473 t/yr**. That is an extra ~570 t/yr of demand from one buyer category, appearing in a single regime shift. Put it beside the entire peak-gold debate — which concerns whether mine output drifts down by a few hundred tonnes over a *decade* — and the asymmetry is stark. A demand change arrived in two years that is larger than any supply change forecast over twenty. When you understand the scale, you understand why a gold analyst watches central banks and real yields, and barely glances at the mines. The [structural-buyer post](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022) takes that demand story apart in full.

## How to use it: a gold thesis is a demand thesis

So what do you *do* with all this? The practical payoff is liberating, and it is the opposite of how most people approach a commodity.

For oil, copper, or wheat, half your work is supply: rig counts, OPEC quotas, mine strikes, weather, inventories. **For gold, you can put the supply side down almost entirely.** Mine production is a slow, inelastic, decade-lagged boulder that adds a steady ~1.7% to the stock no matter what the price does; recycling is a small fast sliver that cannot move the stock; and "peak gold," even if real, is a rounding error against demand. None of these is your variable. If you find yourself building a gold thesis around "supply is tightening" or "miners will flood the market," you are looking at the wrong half of the equation.

Where, then, should your attention go? **Everywhere demand and the cost of holding gold are set.** A serious gold framework is built from:

- **Real interest rates** — the opportunity cost of holding a metal that pays no yield, and the single most important driver. (See [real interest rates, the master variable](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price).)
- **The dollar** — the currency gold is priced in; a weaker dollar generally lifts the dollar price.
- **Central-bank buying** — the structural demand floor that re-formed after 2022.
- **Safe-haven and debasement demand** — the crisis and money-printing flows that spike the price when confidence in paper money wobbles.

Every one of those is a *demand-side* or *monetary* variable. That is not an accident; it is a direct consequence of everything in this post. Gold's supply is so inelastic and so dominated by its giant stock that supply removes itself from the equation, leaving the price to be set almost purely by demand and the real rate. This is what makes gold genuinely different from every other commodity, and it is why this series treats gold as **monetary insurance** rather than as a thing that trades on next year's output.

### The one place supply *does* matter: if you own the miners

A fair caveat keeps this honest. "Ignore supply" is advice for someone forecasting the *gold price* or holding *bullion.* It is the opposite of advice for someone who owns gold *miners.* For a mining company, supply economics are the entire business: AISC, ore grade, reserve life, the cost curve, and the capital cycle are exactly what determine whether a given producer turns a rising gold price into rising profit or squanders it. A miner whose costs creep up as fast as the gold price earns nothing extra from a rally; one with disciplined costs and long reserve life turns a 50% gold move into a far larger move in earnings. That operating leverage — and the industry's long, sad history of fumbling it — is a story in its own right, and it is why gold-mining stocks have so often *disappointed* relative to the metal even in roaring bull markets. The [gold-miners post](/blog/trading/gold/gold-miners-the-leveraged-and-disappointing-bet-on-the-gold-price) takes that apart. The neat division to carry away: **supply is nearly irrelevant to the price of gold, and nearly everything to the value of a gold miner.** Confusing the two is one of the most expensive mistakes in the sector.

### The bottom line

A commodity's price is a tug-of-war between supply and demand. Gold's price is a one-sided referendum — the world voting, through demand, on its confidence in paper money and real yields — with supply standing mute on the sidelines, unable to cast a meaningful vote either way. Mine output is a decade-lagged boulder; recycling is a small fast sliver; peak gold is a rounding error; AISC is a floor made of foam. Every one of them is too slow, too small, or too sticky to be the marginal price-setter, and that is *by design* — the very inelasticity that makes gold useless as a normal commodity is precisely what makes it useful as money. A thing whose supply could be flooded in response to its price could never serve as a store of value; gold's mute supply side is the source of its monetary power, not a footnote to it. So spend your analysis where the votes are counted: ignore the mines, and watch the money.
