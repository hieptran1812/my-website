In April 1933, six weeks into his presidency, Franklin Roosevelt signed a one-page document that did something no peacetime American government had done before or has done since: it made it a federal crime for ordinary citizens to own gold. Executive Order 6102 ordered Americans to hand in their gold coins, gold bullion, and gold certificates to the Federal Reserve by May 1, 1933, in exchange for paper dollars at \$20.67 per troy ounce. The penalty for refusing was a fine of up to \$10,000 — more than ten years' wages for a typical worker at the time — or up to ten years in prison, or both.

Most people complied. Some buried coins in their backyards. A few sued. And then, less than a year later, the punchline arrived: the Gold Reserve Act of 1934 revalued gold to \$35 per ounce. The government had bought the nation's gold at \$20.67 and, with the stroke of a pen, declared that same gold to be worth 69% more. The difference — billions of dollars of newly conjured "profit" — flowed to the Treasury, which used it to seed a fund for stabilizing the dollar. Citizens who had surrendered their gold held paper dollars that now bought roughly 41% less gold than before. They had been, in the most literal sense, on the wrong side of a state-run trade.

This is the uncomfortable flip side of everything good about gold. Across this series we keep returning to one idea: gold is monetary insurance — a non-yielding metal whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability. The reason central banks hold it, the reason it survives every currency, is that it is **no one's liability**: it has no issuer who can default, inflate it away, or freeze it. But that same independence is exactly what makes a desperate government want it. A state that cannot tax, borrow, or print its way out of a crisis can still do one more thing — it can come for the apolitical asset its own people are using to escape the consequences of the state's decisions.

## Foundations: what confiscation and capital controls actually are

Before the history, three plain definitions. None of this requires any finance background — these are just words for things governments do.

**Confiscation** is the state taking your property. In its rawest form it is outright seizure: soldiers at the door, the gold goes in a truck. But the version that actually happens to gold is more polite and more effective: a *forced sale* at a price the state sets. You are not robbed; you are "compensated." You hand over an ounce of gold and receive paper money worth, on that day, the same amount. The theft, if there is one, is hidden in what happens to the price *afterward* — which is precisely the trick EO 6102 pulled.

**Capital controls** are rules that limit how money — and money-like assets such as gold — can move into, out of, or around a country. They come in many flavors: a ban on importing gold, a limit on how much foreign currency you can take abroad, a tax on converting your local currency into dollars, a requirement to register large gold purchases. A country imposes them when its currency is under pressure and its leaders fear that, left free, citizens would convert their savings into harder assets and ship their wealth out the door. Gold, being the oldest and most portable store of value, is almost always near the top of the list of things capital controls target.

**Revaluation and devaluation** are the two sides of changing the official link between a currency and gold. When a government on a gold standard declares that a dollar is now worth less gold than before, it has *devalued the dollar*; equivalently, it has *revalued gold higher*. In 1934, raising gold from \$20.67 to \$35 was a 41% devaluation of the dollar (the dollar bought 41% less gold) and a 69% revaluation of gold (an ounce of gold commanded 69% more dollars). Both numbers describe the same act from opposite ends. Keeping them straight matters, because the headline you read — "gold up 69%" or "dollar down 41%" — depends entirely on which side the writer chose.

It helps to see these as a *ladder of intensity* rather than separate things. At the bottom rung is **taxation** — a duty on imports, a levy on transactions, a capital-gains tax on profit. Tax takes a slice and is the most socially accepted, because everyone already accepts that the state taxes. One rung up is **registration and reporting** — the state does not take anything yet, but it builds the list of who holds what. A step further is **restriction** — capital controls that make moving or buying gold harder without forbidding ownership. Higher still is **prohibition** — outright bans on holding the investment forms, as in 1933 America or 1962 India. At the very top is **seizure with revaluation** — the state takes the gold and then changes its price, capturing the gain. The crucial thing to notice is that a government rarely jumps to the top rung. It climbs the ladder as desperation grows, and each rung is sold to the public as something milder than it is. A 15% import duty does not feel like confiscation, but over enough purchases it transfers as much wealth as a one-time levy would. Watching which rung a country is on tells you how worried its government is — and how worried a gold holder there should be.

There is one more distinction worth drawing, because it dissolves a lot of confusion. Confiscation of *monetary* gold — coins and bars held as savings — is what states do; confiscation of *cultural* gold — jewelry worn and inherited — is what states almost never do, because it is unenforceable and politically toxic. Every regime in this post drew that exact line. The state is not squeamish about taking your wealth; it is squeamish about taking your wedding ring on camera. That line is the single most reliable feature of gold confiscation across a century and three continents, and a holder who understands it understands most of the practical defense.

### Why states do it

Strip away the legal language and there are only three reasons a government reaches for its citizens' gold.

The first is **to get the gold itself.** Gold is real reserves. A government that has run out of credible ways to back its currency or pay foreign debts can solve the problem instantly by pooling the nation's privately held gold. This was a live motive in the 1930s, when a country's gold stock literally determined how much money it was allowed to issue.

The second is **to stop capital flight.** When citizens lose faith in the local currency, they convert savings into gold and dollars and try to move them somewhere safer. That capital flight drains the central bank's reserves and accelerates the very currency collapse people are fleeing. Banning gold imports or private gold holding is an attempt to plug the leak. This is India's motive across three decades, and Vietnam's today.

The third is **to capture the revaluation profit.** If the state owns all the gold and then revalues it higher, the gain belongs to the state. This is the cleverest and least understood motive, and it is the engine of the 1933–34 episode. We will work the arithmetic in a moment.

### The apolitical-asset paradox

Here is the paradox at the center of this entire post, stated as plainly as possible. The property that makes gold valuable as insurance — that it answers to no government — is the same property that makes governments want to control it.

A government bond is the *opposite* of gold in this respect. A bond is a promise *from* the state; the state can always honor it by printing the currency it is denominated in, and it can always tax the interest it pays you. The state has no reason to confiscate its own bonds, because it already controls them. Gold it does not control. When you hold gold, you have stepped outside the state's monetary system. In good times the state shrugs. In a crisis — when the state most needs everyone *inside* its system, holding its currency, funding its deficits — your standing outside it looks like desertion, and the state may legislate you back in.

That is why the timeline of gold confiscation maps almost perfectly onto the timeline of monetary crises. It is not random cruelty. It is what cornered governments do.

## Executive Order 6102 (1933): the order, the revaluation, the trick

Let us reconstruct exactly what happened, because the mechanics are more interesting — and more devious — than the popular "FDR stole everyone's gold" version.

In early 1933 the United States was in the depths of the Great Depression. A third of banks had failed or were failing. People were hoarding gold coins and gold certificates because they trusted metal more than a banking system that kept collapsing. Under the gold standard of the era, the Federal Reserve was legally required to hold gold equal to at least 40% of the dollars it issued. Every gold coin hoarded in a mattress was gold the Fed could not count toward that backing — which meant the hoarding directly *shrank* the money supply the country could legally have, deepening the deflationary spiral. The government's diagnosis, right or wrong, was that private gold hoarding was strangling the recovery.

EO 6102, signed April 5, 1933, ordered the surrender of essentially all privately held gold above a small exemption. The exemptions are worth noting because they foreshadow how every confiscation regime works: you could keep up to about \$100 of gold coin (roughly five ounces at the official price), gold used in industry and the arts, and "rare and unusual" collectible coins. In other words, the *jewelry-and-collectibles loophole* existed from the very first modern confiscation. The state went after monetary gold — coins and bars held as savings — not the wedding ring on your finger or the coin in a collector's display case.

Surrendered gold was paid for at the statutory \$20.67 per ounce. For about ten months, nothing else happened. Then, in January 1934, the Gold Reserve Act revalued gold to \$35 per ounce. The government now held the nation's gold, bought at \$20.67, and had declared it worth \$35.

How thoroughly was the order enforced? Less brutally than the legend suggests, and that itself is instructive. There was no mass program of door-to-door searches; the government relied overwhelmingly on voluntary compliance backed by the threat of ruinous penalties, plus banks reporting depositors who had recently withdrawn gold. Famously, almost no one was actually prosecuted under EO 6102. The handful of cases that did arise tended to involve people who flaunted large hoards or tried to ship gold abroad. The point of the law was never to fill prisons; it was to change behavior, and a credible threat changes behavior far more cheaply than enforcement does. This is a pattern worth remembering: confiscation works through the *expectation* of consequences far more than through their delivery. A law that ninety-five percent of people obey out of fear has succeeded completely while jailing almost no one.

Resistance, where it happened, mostly took the two forms that resistance always takes. Some people simply did not comply — they kept coins buried or hidden and quietly held them for decades, which is why pre-1933 gold coins in original condition still surface in estates today. Others used the exemptions deliberately, converting bullion into "rare and unusual" collectible coins or into jewelry that fell outside the order's reach. The exemptions, meant as humane carve-outs, became the legal escape valve — exactly as they would in India and Vietnam later. A confiscation regime, it turns out, almost always contains the seeds of its own circumvention in the very exceptions it grants to seem reasonable.

#### Worked example: the \$14.33 the Treasury booked on every ounce

Suppose, in April 1933, you were a moderately prosperous American holding 100 troy ounces of gold coins — a substantial but not extraordinary nest egg, the kind a successful shopkeeper might have accumulated.

- You surrender the 100 oz under EO 6102 and receive paper at \$20.67/oz: 100 × \$20.67 = **\$2,067** in dollars.
- In January 1934 the government revalues gold to \$35/oz. Your former 100 oz are now worth, on the government's own books, 100 × \$35 = **\$3,500**.
- The difference, 100 × (\$35 − \$20.67) = 100 × \$14.33 = **\$1,433**, did not come to you. It accrued to the Treasury as a "revaluation profit." Roughly \$2 billion of such profit across all the gold the government held was used to capitalize the Exchange Stabilization Fund.
- Your \$2,067 in paper dollars now buys 2,067 / 35 = **59.1 ounces** of gold at the new price, down from the 100 ounces you started with. You can buy back only 59% of what you handed in.

The single sentence to carry away: you were paid "fairly" at the price on the day you surrendered, and then the price was changed against you — so the entire 69% gain on your former gold was harvested by the state rather than by you.

### The gold-clause cases: closing the escape hatch

There was one more move, and it is the part legal historians find most striking. For decades, American bonds and private contracts had included "gold clauses" — promises to pay not just *dollars* but *a fixed weight of gold*, precisely so that lenders would be protected if the dollar were ever devalued. These clauses were the private market's own insurance against exactly what the government was about to do.

So in June 1933, Congress passed a joint resolution declaring all gold clauses void and unenforceable. A bond that promised to repay in gold would now be repaid in paper dollars at face value, devaluation be damned. Bondholders who had specifically contracted to be protected against devaluation were stripped of that protection by statute.

The cases challenging this reached the Supreme Court in 1935 (the *Gold Clause Cases*). In a 5–4 decision the Court upheld the government's power to abrogate the clauses on public contracts and, in effect, on private ones too — reasoning that Congress's authority over the currency overrode private agreements to the contrary. The dissent was scathing; Justice McReynolds reportedly said from the bench, "This is Nero at his worst." But the law stood. The lesson for any holder of paper claims to gold is permanent: a contractual promise of gold is only as good as the government's willingness to let that promise survive a crisis. The metal in your hand has no such dependency. The IOU written *in terms of* the metal does.

Private gold ownership in the US stayed illegal for forty-one years. It was not until December 31, 1974 — after the dollar's last formal tie to gold had been severed in 1971 — that Americans could legally own bullion again. The chart below picks up the price story from 1970, just before re-legalization, and shows the world gold price has compounded enormously since the state stopped forbidding its citizens to own it.

## Why FDR did it: deflation and the gold standard's straitjacket

It is tempting to read 1933 as simple villainy, but the more useful reading is that the gold standard itself had become a trap, and confiscation was the lever the administration used to escape it.

Under a strict gold standard, a country's money supply is chained to its gold stock. That is the whole point — it stops governments from printing — but the same discipline becomes a noose in a deflationary collapse. By 1933, prices in the US had fallen by roughly a quarter from their 1929 peak; debts contracted in 1928 dollars had to be repaid in much scarcer, more valuable 1933 dollars, which crushed farmers, homeowners, and businesses. The textbook cure for deflation is to expand the money supply. But the gold standard forbade it: more dollars required more gold, and the country could not conjure gold.

Devaluation broke the chain. By revaluing gold from \$20.67 to \$35, the government made each ounce of its gold "back" more dollars — \$35 worth instead of \$20.67 — which legally permitted a roughly 69% larger money supply on the same physical gold. That was the macroeconomic goal: reflation, the deliberate raising of prices to relieve debtors and restart growth. To get there, the government needed to *own* the gold first, which is why confiscation came before revaluation. The two acts are a single policy: confiscate at the old price, revalue, and capture both the expanded money supply and the paper profit.

The international context matters too, because the US was not acting alone or first. Britain had abandoned the gold standard in September 1931, letting the pound fall — which gave British exporters an advantage and effectively exported deflation to countries still pegged to gold, including the US. Through 1931 and 1932 the world fractured into a "currency war," each country trying to devalue against the others to grab a larger share of shrinking global trade. The US revaluation of gold in 1934 was, in part, America's entry into that war: by raising the dollar price of gold, the US devalued the dollar against the currencies of countries still on the old parity, clawing back competitiveness. Seen this way, confiscation was the *domestic* enabling step for an *international* devaluation. The state needed to own the gold before it could reprice it as a weapon in the currency war. That linkage — between what a government does to its own citizens' gold and what it is trying to do to other countries' currencies — recurs whenever the global monetary order is under strain, and it is one reason gold confiscation clusters in periods of international monetary breakdown rather than appearing at random.

Economists still argue about whether it worked. Many credit the 1933–34 gold moves and the abandonment of the strict gold standard with ending the worst of the deflation and beginning the recovery; the economy did grow strongly in 1934–36, and the timing — recovery beginning almost exactly when the gold link was loosened — is hard to dismiss as coincidence. But "it worked as macroeconomic policy" and "it was a one-time wealth transfer away from gold holders" are both true at once. That is the recurring shape of state action on gold: it is usually defensible as crisis policy *and* costly to the specific people who were holding the metal. The two are not in tension. The cost to gold holders is often exactly the mechanism by which the policy works — the revaluation that reflated the economy *is* the same revaluation that left surrendering citizens with paper worth 41% less gold. You cannot have the macroeconomic benefit without the distributional cost, because they are two descriptions of one act.

#### Worked example: how revaluation expanded the money supply

Keep the numbers simple to see the lever clearly.

- Suppose the US Treasury holds 6,000 tonnes of gold. At \$20.67/oz, and with about 32,151 troy ounces per tonne, that gold is worth roughly 6,000 × 32,151 × \$20.67 ≈ **\$3.99 billion**.
- The 40% gold-backing rule means \$3.99 billion of gold can legally back up to \$3.99B / 0.40 ≈ **\$9.97 billion** of currency.
- Now revalue gold to \$35/oz. The *same physical gold* is now worth 6,000 × 32,151 × \$35 ≈ **\$6.75 billion**.
- That revalued gold can now back \$6.75B / 0.40 ≈ **\$16.9 billion** of currency — a 69% increase in the legal money supply, created without mining a single new ounce.

The takeaway: revaluation is money-printing performed through the accounting value of gold rather than the printing press — and confiscation is the step that ensures the printer, not the citizen, owns the gold being repriced.

## India's long war on gold

If 1933 America shows confiscation as a sharp, one-time event, India shows the slow-burn version: not seizure, but a decades-long attempt to ban private gold ownership outright — and a vivid demonstration of why such bans fail at their stated goal while creating large unintended consequences.

India is, culturally, the most gold-loving large nation on earth. Gold is dowry, savings, religious offering, and inflation hedge all at once, especially for rural households with no access to banks. It is also, for the same reason, a perennial headache for India's policymakers, because every tonne of gold the country imports is foreign currency spent on a metal that just sits in a vault or around a neck, worsening the trade deficit and pressuring the rupee.

So in 1962, in the aftermath of the border war with China and amid a foreign-exchange crisis, India passed the **Gold Control Act**, which over the following years made it illegal for private citizens to hold gold in the form of bars and prohibited the production of gold bullion. Gold could be held as jewelry, and licensed jewelers could make it, but the investment forms — bars, biscuits, new coins — were banned. The motive was textbook capital control: cut gold imports, conserve scarce foreign exchange, defend the rupee.

It did not work, and the way it failed is the most instructive part.

Because legal demand could not be supplied legally, an enormous **smuggling economy** grew to meet it. Gold flowed in from the Gulf by dhow and by air, financed through the informal *hawala* money-transfer network that moved value across borders without moving currency through banks. By some estimates, smuggling supplied the majority of India's gold during the control era. Whole criminal organizations — the figures who later became Mumbai's notorious underworld — were built on gold smuggling. The state had not eliminated the gold trade; it had handed it to outlaws and lost both the import duties it might have collected and any control over the FX leakage it was trying to stop. Meanwhile households simply bought *jewelry* instead of bars, since jewelry was legal — exactly the loophole EO 6102 had left thirty years earlier.

There is a second-order cost worth naming, because it generalizes. A prohibition does not just create smugglers; it degrades the *quality* and *safety* of the market for ordinary people. With no legal bullion market, an Indian buyer in the control era could not be sure of purity, could not get a fair, transparent price, and had no recourse against fraud — every transaction happened in a shadow market with shadow-market rules. The poorest, least sophisticated buyers bore this worst, paying the widest gray-market spreads and most exposed to adulterated metal. This is the quiet tragedy of well-intentioned gold bans: aimed at conserving the nation's foreign exchange, they end up taxing the financially vulnerable through worse prices and worse protection, while the wealthy and well-connected access cleaner supply through the smuggling networks they can afford to use.

The Gold Control Act was finally repealed in 1990, amid the liberalization that opened India's economy. The state switched from prohibition to taxation: instead of banning gold, it allowed imports and slapped a customs duty on them. That is the modern Indian approach — duties that rise and fall (as high as 15% in 2022, cut to 6% in 2024) to nudge import volumes without the smuggling explosion an outright ban produces. India today is a top-two gold consumer and holds roughly 876 tonnes in official reserves. The decades of prohibition changed almost nothing about how much gold Indians wanted; they changed only who supplied it and at what cost.

Britain offers a quieter parallel that is easy to forget. Under post-war exchange controls, the UK restricted residents from holding gold coins from 1966, and earlier exchange-control rules made it difficult for Britons to acquire gold freely — all in service of defending the pound's value and the country's reserves during the strained Bretton Woods years. The controls were not as draconian as America's outright ban, but the motive was identical: a currency under pressure, a government wanting to keep capital from leaking into hard assets. The UK relaxed these restrictions only in 1979, the same broad era in which the US re-legalized bullion. The cluster is not coincidental. Across the late-1970s, as the post-war monetary order gave way to floating currencies, the rich democracies *simultaneously* relaxed gold controls — because a government that has given up trying to defend a fixed currency parity no longer needs to police its citizens' gold. Free-floating money and free gold ownership tend to arrive together, just as fixed parities and gold controls did.

#### Worked example: the import duty as a soft confiscation

A duty is not a seizure, but it transfers wealth from the buyer to the state in much the same way — quietly, on every transaction. Suppose an Indian saver buys 50 grams of gold when the world price works out to about 6,000 rupees per gram.

- Pre-duty cost of the metal: 50 g × 6,000 = **300,000 rupees**.
- At a 15% import duty (the 2022 rate), the duty adds 0.15 × 300,000 = **45,000 rupees**, so the saver pays **345,000 rupees** for gold worth 300,000 at the world price.
- After the 2024 cut to 6%, the same purchase carries 0.06 × 300,000 = **18,000 rupees** of duty, total **318,000 rupees**.
- The duty cut saved this one buyer 45,000 − 18,000 = **27,000 rupees** — and, predictably, India's official gold imports jumped after the cut as demand that had been smuggled or suppressed moved back into the legal, taxed channel.

The takeaway: a high enough duty is confiscation by installment — it does not take your gold, it takes a slice of every purchase, and like an outright ban it pushes the marginal buyer toward the gray market.

## Modern capital controls and "soft" confiscation

The crude 1933 model — criminalize, surrender, revalue — is rare today, partly because no major currency is on a gold standard anymore, so there is no backing ratio to manipulate and far less reason to pool the nation's gold. But the *impulse* is alive and well; it has simply gone soft. Modern states rarely take your gold. They make it expensive, illiquid, or inconvenient to own, sell, or move — which achieves much of the same effect without the optics of soldiers at the door.

The modern toolkit:

**Import bans and licensing.** The most common control. If gold cannot legally enter the country, the supply for domestic buyers is capped, which depresses imports' drain on FX reserves. Vietnam runs exactly this: since 2012, only the State Bank may import gold, and private gold-bar production is banned.

**State production monopolies.** Rather than ban gold, the state monopolizes the *trusted form* of it. Vietnam designated a single brand — SJC — as the only legal gold bar, and made the State Bank the sole producer. Citizens can still own gold, but the bar everyone trusts is a state-controlled product, which lets the state manage its supply and price.

**Punitive duties and taxes.** As in India: tax the import, tax the transaction, tax the capital gain. High enough, a tax is a slow confiscation that never requires the word.

**FX limits.** Restricting how citizens convert local currency to dollars (and thus, indirectly, to gold priced in dollars) chokes the channel through which capital flees. Gold and the dollar are substitutes for a citizen fleeing a weak currency, so controlling one usually means controlling both.

**Reporting and registration.** Requiring dealers to report large purchases, or requiring holders to register gold above some threshold, does not take anything — but it builds the *list* that any future confiscation would use. A government that knows who holds gold has already done the hard part.

The common thread is that none of these is dramatic enough to trigger the panic that "the government is seizing gold" would cause, yet together they accomplish the state's two real aims: keep capital from fleeing, and keep the gold trade inside the perimeter the state can see and tax. The price signal that gives the game away is the **premium** — when controls choke legal supply below domestic demand, the local price of gold rises *above* the world price, and that gap is the cleanest measure of how binding the controls are.

Why is soft confiscation so much more common than the hard 1933 version today? Three reasons, all worth understanding. First, the gold standard is gone, so there is no backing ratio for a state to exploit and no legal requirement to pool the nation's gold — the *get-the-gold* and *capture-the-revaluation-profit* motives have largely evaporated, leaving mainly the *stop-capital-flight* motive, which soft controls serve well. Second, optics and globalization make hard confiscation costly: a modern government that criminalized gold ownership would trigger capital flight in everything else, spook foreign investors, and make global headlines, whereas a quiet import duty barely registers abroad. Third, soft controls are *reversible and tunable* in a way a seizure is not. A duty can be raised in a crisis and cut in calm; a monopoly's supply can be tightened or loosened. Governments prefer dials to switches, and soft confiscation is a dial.

The premium that controls create is, for a macro-watcher, one of the most useful signals an economy emits — because it is a thermometer for currency stress that the government cannot easily fake. When a country's gold trades far above the world price, it is telling you that citizens are so eager to convert local money into gold that they will pay a large markup, and that the official channels are too choked to satisfy them. That is, almost by definition, a vote of no confidence in the local currency — the same no-confidence vote that, in this series' spine, the world gold price expresses against fiat money in general, but concentrated and amplified by the controls. A widening domestic gold premium often *leads* an official devaluation: the gray market is pricing the weak currency before the central bank admits it. A trader or saver who watches the local premium is, in effect, front-running the policymaker's eventual capitulation. The premium is where the controls and the currency stress they are trying to hide meet the price system that refuses to be hidden.

The same logic links gold controls to *currency* controls, because in a stressed economy gold and the dollar are near-perfect substitutes for the citizen trying to preserve value. A government that limits dollar conversion but leaves gold free will see demand pour into gold; one that controls gold but leaves dollars free will see capital flee into the greenback. To actually stem the bleeding a state usually has to control *both* — which is why episodes of gold restriction and episodes of FX restriction tend to arrive together, two faces of the single project of keeping savings trapped inside the domestic currency. The gold↔dollar↔local-currency triangle is the real object the controls are trying to clamp, and squeezing one corner just bulges another.

#### Worked example: how a control regime manufactures a premium

This is the math that turns into the Vietnam story, and it shows precisely how a "soft" control becomes a real cost to holders.

The world gold price and a country's domestic gold price should differ only by the legitimate cost of getting the metal there: shipping, insurance, refining into the local bar, plus any import duty. Call that fair gap a few percent. When the state bans imports, domestic supply can no longer respond to demand, so the only way the market clears is for the domestic price to rise until enough people are unwilling to buy.

- Suppose the world price implies a "fair" domestic value of 76 million VND per *lượng* (a *lượng*, or tael, is the Vietnamese unit, 37.5 grams or about 1.206 troy ounces).
- In early-to-mid 2024, with imports banned and demand surging, the SJC bar traded as high as about 92.4 million VND per *lượng*.
- The premium was therefore roughly 92.4 − 76 = **16.4 million VND per lượng**, or about 16.4 / 76 ≈ **22%** above the world-equivalent price.
- A buyer of one *lượng* at the peak paid about 16 million VND — roughly **640 US dollars at the ~25,450 VND/USD rate** — purely for the privilege of buying gold inside a controlled market. That premium is not the metal; it is the price of the control.

The takeaway: a premium of 20% or more is the market's invoice for capital controls — the holder pays it on the way in, and a sudden official move to crush the premium (as Vietnam's State Bank did in mid-2024, dragging the bar back toward 77 million) can hand a real loss to whoever bought near the top.

This is the bridge to the Vietnam track of this series: Vietnam's gold regime is the modern, living cousin of India's old Gold Control Act — an import ban plus a state bar monopoly, producing a premium, a gray market, and a recurring policy headache. The deep dives on [Decree 24 and the SJC monopoly](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly) and on the [State Bank's broader monetary toolkit](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling) trace it in full.

## How holders respond: the confiscation-resistant forms of gold

If the state's reach varies by *how* gold is held, then a thoughtful holder can think about gold the way an insurer thinks about correlated risk: the point of insurance is to not lose it in the exact scenario you bought it for. Gold held in a form the state can seize with a single decree is poor insurance against the state itself. So holders sort gold along a spectrum of confiscation resistance — and, as with everything in finance, resistance is not free.

At the most exposed end sit the *paper and electronic* forms: a gold ETF, an unallocated gold account at a bank, a futures position. These are claims inside the financial system the state most directly controls. An account can be frozen, an ETF can be subjected to forced redemption rules, a domestic custodian can be ordered to hand over what it holds. They are the most convenient and liquid forms of gold and the easiest for the state to reach. (The counterparty-and-custody question is its own deep topic, treated in the companion post on [physical bars, coins, and allocated vs unallocated gold](/blog/trading/gold/physical-gold-bars-coins-allocated-vs-unallocated-and-counterparty-risk).)

In the middle sit *physical bars and coins held at home or in a private vault*. Off the banking system's books, harder to seize by a single order — but illegal to hold during an outright ban (as in 1933 America or 1962 India), reachable by search, and dependent on the holder simply not complying. This is the form most "buy physical" advocates mean, and it is genuinely more resistant than an account, but it is not magic: it failed in 1933 for everyone who obeyed the law.

Near the resistant end sits *jewelry*. This is the dark joke that runs through every confiscation in history: the form the state almost never bans is the one worn on the body and embedded in culture. EO 6102 exempted it; India's Gold Control Act exempted it; Vietnam's controls target bars, not necklaces. Jewelry carries a large making-charge premium and is an inefficient way to store value ounce-for-ounce, but it has survived every crackdown precisely because banning a billion wedding rings is politically impossible.

At the most resistant end sits *gold held offshore*, in a vault in a different country and a different legal jurisdiction from the one whose government you are worried about. A home government cannot easily order a foreign vault to surrender your metal. But this introduces a new counterparty, a new jurisdiction whose own government could act, real storage costs, and the practical difficulty (and now, often, the legal reporting requirement) of getting the gold home when you need it.

The deep structure of the spectrum is a single, unavoidable trade-off: **confiscation resistance moves opposite to liquidity and convenience.** The forms easiest for *you* to use are easiest for the *state* to reach, because both you and the state operate most easily inside the formal financial system. An ETF you can sell with a click; a regulator can freeze it with a memo. Gold buried in your garden is hard for the state to find and hard for you to spend at a fair price on short notice. This is not an accident of the current rules — it is structural. Liquidity *means* being connected to the system, and being connected to the system *means* being reachable by the entity that runs the system. There is no form of gold that is simultaneously instantly spendable, perfectly private, and beyond sovereign reach, for the same reason there is no door that is both wide open and perfectly locked. Accepting that trade-off honestly is more useful than chasing the imaginary form that escapes it.

A second structural point: confiscation resistance is *jurisdiction-relative*, not absolute. Offshore gold is resistant to *your* government but fully exposed to the *host* government — Venezuela learned this when its gold held at the Bank of England became entangled in a sovereignty dispute and London simply would not release it. You have not escaped sovereign risk by going offshore; you have *swapped* one sovereign for another, and the swap only helps if you judge the new sovereign less likely to act against you than the old one. For a citizen of a stable country that is usually a bad trade; for a citizen of an unstable one it can be the whole point. The right question is never "is this gold safe from confiscation?" but "safe from *which* government, and do I trust *that* government's restraint more than my own's?"

#### Worked example: offshore storage cost vs confiscation risk

Confiscation resistance has a running price, and it is worth sizing it against the risk it hedges.

- Suppose you hold \$100,000 of gold and store it in an offshore allocated vault charging 0.5% per year.
- Annual storage cost: 0.005 × \$100,000 = **\$500 per year**, or \$5,000 over a decade (ignoring price changes).
- Now estimate the risk you are hedging. Suppose you judge the probability of a confiscatory event in your home country over that decade at 2%, and that in such an event a domestically held position would lose, say, 30% of its value (forced sale at an unfavorable price, or a punitive levy). The expected loss avoided is 0.02 × 0.30 × \$100,000 = **\$600 over the decade**.
- On those assumptions the offshore cost (\$5,000) far exceeds the expected loss avoided (\$600) — so offshore storage as *pure* confiscation insurance only pays if you judge the probability or severity much higher, or if you value the protection against a low-probability catastrophe more than its expected value (which is a perfectly rational thing to do, and is the whole logic of insurance).

The takeaway: confiscation-resistant storage is insurance with a real annual premium, so size it to a genuine assessment of political risk in your jurisdiction, not to a generic fear — for most holders in stable democracies the premium is not worth it, while for someone in a country with a history of seizures it plainly is.

## Common misconceptions

**"It could never happen again."** The most comforting and most dangerous myth. It is true that the *exact* 1933 mechanism is obsolete: no major currency is gold-backed, so there is no backing ratio to exploit and far less reason to pool the nation's gold. But that is an argument against the *form*, not the *impulse*. The impulse — a desperate state reaching for citizens' hard assets — recurs constantly in softer dress. Vietnam imposed a gold-bar monopoly in 2012. India ran punitive duties into the 2020s. Capital controls reappear in every emerging-market currency crisis. The honest statement is: classic confiscation is unlikely in a stable, gold-demonetized economy, and some form of control is near-certain in an economy whose currency is collapsing. Which describes your gold's jurisdiction?

**"Allocated gold at my bank is safe from the state."** Allocated gold — specific, numbered bars set aside in your name — protects you from your *bank's* insolvency far better than an unallocated claim does. It does not protect you from your *government*. A bank operates under a banking license the state grants and can revoke; a state that ordered gold surrendered in 1933 had no trouble reaching gold inside the banking system, and a modern state can compel a domestic custodian by the same authority. Allocation is a hedge against counterparty default, not against sovereign decree. The two risks are different, and conflating them is how people end up holding the right asset in the wrong place.

**"My collectible and jewelry gold proves bans don't work."** Half right. Bans consistently exempt jewelry and collectibles, and demand consistently flows into those exempt forms — so in that narrow sense bans "don't work" at killing demand. But the exempt forms carry large premiums (making charges on jewelry, numismatic markups on rare coins) that are a real cost, and they are illiquid to sell at full value. "The ban didn't stop me owning gold" is true; "the ban cost me nothing" is not.

**"Owning physical at home makes me immune."** Physical-at-home is more resistant than an account, but 1933 is the counterexample that should end the overconfidence: the law demanded surrender, and most people — law-abiding citizens facing a \$10,000 fine and prison — complied. Resistance to confiscation ultimately depends on either non-compliance (with legal risk) or on the gold being somewhere the law cannot easily reach (offshore, with its own costs). There is no form of gold that is both perfectly liquid, perfectly convenient, and perfectly seizure-proof. The spectrum is a spectrum precisely because the corners don't exist.

**"Gold is the apolitical asset, so politics doesn't touch it."** This is the misconception this whole post exists to correct. Gold is apolitical in the sense that it is no government's *liability* — no one can inflate or default it away. It is intensely political in the sense that governments *want to control it* for exactly that reason. The price of gold even contains a political-risk term: part of why gold trades at a premium in countries with capital controls is the market pricing in the difficulty of getting metal in and out. Apolitical money living inside a political jurisdiction is never fully free of that jurisdiction's politics.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three concrete episodes, with real dates and numbers, show the full range — from hard 1933-style confiscation to modern soft control to outright state predation in a failed economy.

**The United States, 1933–1974.** The textbook case, already worked above. Confiscation in April 1933 at \$20.67, revaluation to \$35 in January 1934, the gold-clause cases in 1935, and a full forty-one-year prohibition on private bullion ownership that ended only on December 31, 1974. The market consequence visible in the data is what happened *after* re-legalization: once Americans could legally own gold again and the dollar floated freely, the price — \$159/oz on average in 1974 — embarked on the long compounding climb that, by January 2026, reached an all-time high near \$5,589/oz intraday. The metal the state had spent four decades forbidding became, once freed, one of the great long-run stores of value of the fiat era. The state could ban ownership; it could not abolish the demand that ownership expressed.

**India, 1962–1990 and after.** The Gold Control Act banned private bars for nearly three decades to defend the rupee's reserves. The market's answer was a vast smuggling economy supplying most of the country's gold, a permanent gray-market premium, and the entrenchment of the *hawala* network. When India repealed the ban in 1990 and switched to import duties, the smuggling did not vanish but it shrank toward the level the duty made worthwhile — and when the duty was cut from 15% to 6% in 2024, official imports surged as gray-market demand moved back into the legal channel. The data point that matters: across the entire ban-and-duty saga, Indian household appetite for gold barely budged. Prohibition changed the *channel*, not the *demand*.

**Vietnam, 2012–present.** The living modern case. Decree 24 of 2012 banned private gold-bar production, made the State Bank the sole importer, and designated SJC as the only legal bar brand — explicitly to fight "goldization," the tendency of Vietnamese savers to hold gold instead of dong. The control produced exactly the textbook artifact: a premium of SJC gold over the world price that blew out to roughly 22–25% in early 2024, with the bar hitting about 92.4 million VND per *lượng* in May 2024. The State Bank then intervened — selling gold directly through four state banks — and dragged the premium down to single digits and the bar back toward 77 million by mid-2024, before the global surge pushed SJC to a fresh record near 190 million VND per *lượng* by January 2026. Through all of it, Vietnamese consumer demand stayed near 50 tonnes a year. The chart below makes the central point of every control episode in one picture: controls move the price and the channel; they do not extinguish the want.

![Vietnam consumer gold demand 2019 to 2025 staying near 50 tonnes despite the import ban](/imgs/blogs/when-states-take-gold-confiscation-capital-controls-and-the-1933-order-7.png)

**Venezuela, 2010s–present.** The grimmest version — confiscation not of citizens by a functioning state, but of a nation's gold by a collapsing one. As Venezuela's economy imploded under hyperinflation and sanctions, the government turned to the country's gold reserves as the asset of last resort, selling and shipping tonnes abroad (and fighting in foreign courts over gold held at the Bank of England) to fund itself when no one would lend it dollars. Meanwhile ordinary Venezuelans mined gold by hand in the Orinoco arc and used it as money when the bolívar became worthless. Venezuela is the limiting case that proves the spine of this series: when the currency dies completely, gold is what is left — for the state reaching for reserves and for the citizen reaching for something, anything, that holds value. Both are clutching the same metal for the same reason.

## The takeaway: the political risk of the apolitical asset

Pull the episodes together and a single, slightly uncomfortable conclusion emerges, and it sharpens rather than contradicts everything else in this series.

Gold is monetary insurance against your *currency* — against inflation, devaluation, banking collapse, the printing press. On that score the historical record is overwhelming: gold has outlived every fiat currency that has tried to replace it, and in every country whose money has failed, gold has held value when paper did not. That is the case for owning it, and nothing here weakens it.

But gold is *not* automatic insurance against your *government*. The very feature that makes it work — that it answers to no state — is what makes states want it when they are cornered. A government that has exhausted taxing, borrowing, and printing has one asset class left to reach for, and it is sitting in its citizens' safes. The history is not ancient: 1933 is within living memory of people's grandparents, India's ban ended in 1990, and Vietnam's controls are operating right now.

So the practical lesson is not "don't own gold" — it is the opposite, sharpened. *Own gold, and treat the form and the jurisdiction as part of the decision, not an afterthought.* The relevant questions are political as much as financial: How stable is the currency I'm hedging, and therefore how desperate might its government become? Is my gold in a form a single decree can reach — an account, a domestic ETF — or in one it cannot? Is it in a jurisdiction with a history of seizures or one without? For a saver in a stable, gold-demonetized democracy, the political-risk discount on holding domestically is small, and the convenience of accounts and ETFs is worth it. For a saver in a country with capital controls and a weak currency, the calculus flips, and the premium for resistant forms — physical, jewelry, offshore — starts to look like the point rather than the cost.

Notice how cleanly this maps onto the spine of the whole series. Gold's price is a vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability — and confiscation is what happens when that vote becomes *threatening enough to the state that issued the paper.* The currency-debasement that drives people into gold and the political instability that drives governments to seize it are not two separate risks; they are the same crisis at two stages. Early in a monetary breakdown, gold protects you, rising as the currency falls. Late in one, when the government is cornered, that protection is exactly what attracts the state's attention. The holder who understands the spine therefore understands the confiscation risk as part of the same picture: you are buying insurance against a process whose final act, if it goes far enough, can include the insurer's own jurisdiction reaching for the insurance. Sizing *where* you hold gold is just extending the insurance logic one step further — insuring the insurance against the specific party most motivated to take it.

And there is a final, almost philosophical point that the history forces on us. Every confiscation episode in this post ended with the state's goal slipping through its fingers in the long run. America banned gold for forty-one years and then watched it compound into one of the great stores of value of the fiat era. India banned bars for three decades and remained, throughout, one of the most gold-hungry nations on earth. Vietnam monopolized the bar and watched demand hold near fifty tonnes a year while the premium it created broadcast the dong's weakness to anyone watching. The deep reason is that confiscation and control can change the *form*, the *channel*, and the *price* of gold ownership, but they cannot change the *want* — the multi-millennium human reflex to hold something outside the state when trust in the state's money runs low. States can tax that reflex, restrict it, drive it into jewelry or the gray market, even criminalize it for a generation. They cannot legislate it away, because it predates them and, on the evidence of five thousand years, will outlast them.

The deepest way to hold the idea is this: gold is the apolitical asset, but you do not get to hold it apolitically. It always sits inside *some* jurisdiction, subject to *some* government, and the price you pay — in premiums, in storage, in the small probability of a decree — is the price of keeping monetary insurance just slightly outside the reach of the institution you bought the insurance against. The metal is no one's liability. Holding it, in a particular place under a particular flag, never quite is.
