> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold is a *stock*, not a flow: almost every ounce ever mined still exists, so a year of frantic global mining adds only about 1.7% to the pile — and that is exactly what makes gold "hard money."
>
> - About **216,000 tonnes** of gold have ever been mined. It would fit in a cube roughly **22 m on a side** — about four Olympic swimming pools.
> - A whole year of world mining is only **~3,661 t**, plus **~1,370 t** of recycled scrap: total annual supply **~5,031 t**, a thin sliver against the hoard.
> - That gives gold a **stock-to-flow ratio near 59** — it would take ~59 years of full-tilt mining just to dig up the gold that already exists. Copper's stock is barely a month of use.
> - Mining costs (all-in sustaining cost, **AISC**, ~\$1,390/oz in 2024) put a soft *floor* under the price but never the *ceiling* — supply is too slow and too inelastic to cap a rally.
> - **The one fact to remember:** no discovery, no price spike, no asteroid can flood the gold market. Scarcity that cannot be debased is the whole point of gold as monetary insurance.

In the spring of 2024, the gold price did something that, on its face, should have unleashed a wall of new supply. Gold tore through one record after another, averaging **\$2,388 an ounce** for the year — roughly **\$1,000 an ounce above what it costs the average miner to dig it out of the ground**. In almost any other industry, a margin like that — gross profit of more than 40% on every unit sold — would summon a flood of new product. Factories would run extra shifts; competitors would pile in; prices would get competed back down.

Gold did not do that. Mine production in 2024 was about **3,661 tonnes** — essentially flat against the prior several years, and only a hair above the previous all-time high set back in 2018. The miners earned spectacular margins and yet the world's gold supply barely budged. A year later, in January 2026, gold spiked to an all-time high near **\$5,589/oz**, and *still* the response from the supply side was a shrug.

That non-response is the single most important fact about gold supply, and it is the subject of this post. To understand it you have to flip the way you think about almost every other commodity. For oil, copper, wheat, or pork bellies, what matters is the *flow* — how many barrels or bushels are produced this year against how many are consumed this year. Gold is different. Gold is not consumed. Almost every gram ever pulled from the earth is still here, sitting in a vault, a ring, a coffer, or a coin. Gold is a *stock*, and the annual flow of new metal is a rounding error against it. That, and nothing fancier, is why gold is "hard money" — and it is why the rest of this series treats gold as monetary insurance rather than as a commodity that trades on next year's harvest.

![Gold above-ground stock as a huge block beside a tiny annual mining sliver](/imgs/blogs/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock-1.png)

Look at that contrast for a moment, because it does the heavy lifting for everything below. The big block is the hoard — all the gold humanity has ever refined. The thin amber strip at the bottom, drawn to the same scale, is a record year of mining. The miners can dig as hard as they like; what they add each year is a sliver. Now let us build the whole picture from the rock up.

## Foundations: how gold is actually mined

Before we can talk about why supply doesn't move the price, we need to know how an ounce of gold gets made in the first place. Strip away the jargon and gold mining is a brutally simple proposition: find rock that contains a tiny, tiny amount of gold, dig up enormous quantities of it, and use chemistry and heat to separate the gold from the dirt. Everything else — the cost curve, the inelasticity, the cost floor — falls out of that one sentence.

### Ore grade: gold is measured in grams per tonne, not percent

The first thing to internalize is *how little* gold is in "gold ore." When you mine copper, a decent deposit might be 0.5–2% copper by weight — so a tonne of ore contains 5 to 20 kilograms of copper. Gold is nothing like that. A typical modern gold deposit grades **1 to 5 grams of gold per tonne of ore**. Not percent. *Grams*. A gram is about the mass of a paperclip.

Let that sink in. At a grade of **1.5 g/t**, you must dig, crush, and process a full tonne of rock — a thousand kilograms — to liberate a piece of gold the size of a small pea. Higher-grade underground mines might hit 5–10 g/t; spectacular ones reach 20+; but the great bulk of the world's gold today comes out of low-grade, high-volume open pits running at a couple of grams a tonne. The "ore grade" — grams of gold per tonne of rock — is the master number of a mine's economics, because it determines how much rock you must move for each ounce sold.

#### Worked example: how much rock for one good-delivery bar?

> Let us turn ore grade into something concrete. The standard wholesale gold bar — the **LBMA Good Delivery bar** we will meet shortly — weighs about **400 troy ounces**, which is **12.44 kilograms** (1 troy ounce = 31.103 grams; 400 × 31.103 ≈ 12,441 grams).
>
> At an ore grade of **1.5 grams per tonne**, the rock you must process to get 12,441 grams of gold is:
>
> ```
> ore needed = gold mass / grade
>            = 12,441 g / 1.5 g per tonne
>            = 8,294 tonnes of ore  (about 8,300 t)
> ```
>
> Roughly **8,300 tonnes of rock** — call it 300 dump-truck loads — crushed and chemically treated to produce **one** bar of gold. And recovery is never perfect: if the mill recovers 90% of the gold in the ore, you need ~9,200 t of rock. **The intuition:** gold supply is physically slow because the metal is fantastically dilute — you cannot "just dig faster" without first building bigger pits, mills, and haul fleets, which takes years.

### Open-pit versus underground

There are two broad ways to get at gold ore. **Open-pit (surface) mining** carves a giant terraced bowl into the ground and hauls the ore out by truck. It is cheap per tonne and handles enormous volumes, which is why it dominates low-grade deposits — when the gold is only 1–2 g/t, you need volume, and open pits supply it. The trade-off is that you move colossal amounts of waste rock for each ounce, and the pit's environmental and rehabilitation footprint is large.

**Underground mining** sinks shafts and tunnels to chase higher-grade veins too deep or too narrow to reach from the surface. It is far more expensive per tonne — narrow tunnels, ventilation, ground support, the constant cost of bringing rock up a shaft — so it only pays when the grade is high enough that each tonne carries a lot of gold. South Africa's legendary Witwatersrand mines, some of which reach kilometres underground, are the classic case: high grade, terrifying depth, brutal cost.

The choice is not aesthetic. It is dictated by the deposit's grade and geometry, and it feeds straight into the cost number that governs everything: AISC.

There is a third extraction style worth knowing, because it sits at the cheap end of the cost curve: **heap leaching**. Instead of crushing ore finely and processing it in a mill, low-grade ore is piled into vast heaps and sprayed with a dilute cyanide solution that trickles down, dissolving the gold, which is then collected at the base of the heap. Heap leaching is slow and recovers a smaller fraction of the gold, but it is so cheap per tonne that it makes *very* low-grade deposits — sometimes under 0.5 g/t — economic. It is a big part of why "peak grade" hasn't yet meant "peak gold": as the rich ore runs out, cheaper extraction methods bring ever-poorer rock into play. But each step down the grade ladder requires moving more rock for the same ounce, which is the deep reason mining is a business of relentlessly rising costs.

### The extraction chemistry, briefly

You do not need to be a metallurgist to understand gold supply, but one chemical fact explains both the cost and the controversy of mining. Gold is chemically *inert* — it does not rust, tarnish, or react with most substances, which is exactly why it survives for millennia and makes good money. The flip side is that getting it out of rock is hard precisely *because* it won't react. For over a century the workhorse has been **cyanidation**: a weak cyanide solution is one of the few cheap reagents that will dissolve gold, forming a soluble gold-cyanide complex that can be separated from the worthless rock and then recovered (historically by adding zinc, or with activated carbon). Cyanide is toxic, which is why gold mining carries real environmental risk — tailings dams holding cyanide-laced sludge have failed catastrophically — and why the *regulatory and rehabilitation* portion of mining cost has grown so much. This matters for our story in a concrete way: tightening environmental standards are one of the forces steadily pushing AISC higher, tightening the cost floor under gold over time.

### AISC: the all-in cost of keeping the gold coming

If ore grade is the master *geological* number, **all-in sustaining cost (AISC)** is the master *financial* one. AISC is the gold industry's standard measure — adopted across the sector around 2013 — of what it actually costs to produce an ounce of gold *and keep producing it*. The "all-in" and "sustaining" parts matter:

- It includes the obvious **cash operating costs**: mining the rock, crushing and milling it, the chemicals and energy to extract the gold, the labour, the on-site administration.
- Crucially, it also includes **sustaining capital** — the money you must spend just to keep the existing mine running at its current rate: replacing worn-out trucks, developing the next stretch of ore to keep the mill fed, ongoing mine-site rehabilitation, royalties.
- It deliberately *excludes* the big one-off cost of building a brand-new mine from scratch ("growth capital"), so AISC measures the cost of *sustaining* output, not of expanding it.

Think of AISC as the answer to: "At what gold price does this mine roughly break even on a continuing basis?" If gold trades below a mine's AISC for long, the mine bleeds cash and eventually shuts; if gold trades well above, the mine throws off profit. The industry-wide average AISC has climbed steadily — from around **\$900/oz in the mid-2010s to roughly \$1,390/oz in 2024** — pushed up by falling ore grades (the easy, rich ore gets mined first), labour and energy inflation, and the rising cost of meeting environmental and regulatory standards. We will return to that rising floor; for now, just hold the definition.

One more nuance about AISC that trips people up: it is an *average*, and the gold industry is a **cost curve**, not a single cost. Some mines produce gold at \$700/oz; others, at the high-cost margin, need \$1,800/oz just to break even. When people quote "AISC of ~\$1,390," they mean the production-weighted industry average. What sets the *floor* under the price in a downturn is not the average but the **marginal** producer — the most expensive mine still operating. As the price falls, the highest-cost mines shut first, output shrinks from the top of the cost curve down, and that withdrawal of supply is what arrests the decline. Keep that picture — a sloped cost curve, not a flat line — because it is the right mental model for how the cost floor actually works.

With those ideas — grade in grams per tonne, the open-pit/underground split, the cost curve, and AISC — you have the entire foundation. Everything else in this post is consequence.

## Refining and the LBMA Good Delivery bar

Raw mine output is not the gold you picture. What comes off the mine site is **doré** (pronounced "dor-AY") — a rough, impure alloy bar, typically only **70–90% gold**, with the rest a mix of silver and base metals. Doré is the mine's product; it is *not* tradeable as money. To become the gold that backs an ETF or fills a central bank's vault, doré must be **refined** to high purity and cast into a standardized bar that the wholesale market will accept on sight. That standard is the **LBMA Good Delivery bar**.

![Gold supply chain pipeline from ore to refinery to LBMA bar to vault](/imgs/blogs/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock-5.png)

The diagram traces the whole chain. Let us walk it stage by stage:

1. **Ore in the ground**, 1–5 g/t. Almost all of it is worthless rock.
2. **Mine and mill.** The ore is dug, crushed to a powder, and the gold is chemically extracted — historically by cyanide leaching, which dissolves the gold so it can be collected. The output is concentrated, then smelted on-site into doré.
3. **Doré bar**, ~80% gold. This is the mine's saleable product. It is shipped — under heavy security — to a refinery, often on a different continent.
4. **Refinery.** Here the doré is purified to **at least 99.5% fine** (995 parts per thousand) for a Good Delivery bar — many refiners go to 99.99% ("four nines") for retail products. The two classic methods are the **Miller process** (chlorine gas bubbled through molten gold, which removes base metals to ~99.5%) and the **Wohlwill process** (electrolysis, which reaches 99.99%). Switzerland refines a huge share of the world's gold; so do refineries in the UAE, China, and the US.
5. **LBMA Good Delivery bar.** The refined gold is cast into the wholesale standard: a bar of **roughly 400 troy ounces (about 12.44 kg)**, at least **99.5% pure**, stamped with the refiner's mark, a serial number, the fineness, and the year. To carry the **LBMA Good Delivery** stamp, both the *bar* and the *refiner* must meet the London Bullion Market Association's specifications — the refiner has to be on the LBMA's accredited list, which is the global benchmark for trustworthy bullion.
6. **Vault, ETF, or coin.** From there the bar enters the monetary world: stored in a London or New York vault, allocated to a gold ETF, held in a central bank reserve, or melted down and re-cast into smaller bars and coins for investors.

Why does the 400-oz, 99.5% standard matter so much? Because it is what makes gold **fungible** — every Good Delivery bar is interchangeable with every other, so the wholesale market can trade gold by paper title without inspecting each lump. A bar accepted into the London "loco London" system can change hands hundreds of times without ever physically moving. That standardization is the plumbing that lets gold function as money at industrial scale, and it is why "where gold comes from" doesn't end at the mine — it ends at the refinery door, with a stamped bar the whole world trusts.

There is one more link in the chain that turns a refined bar into *monetary* gold: the **chain of custody** that keeps a bar's "Good Delivery" status intact. Once a bar leaves an accredited vault and enters someone's private possession, it falls out of the wholesale chain — to re-enter, it may have to be re-assayed or re-refined, which costs money. So the system carefully keeps Good Delivery bars *inside* a network of LBMA-approved vaults, where they can be traded by title without ever being physically inspected again. This is why the wholesale market distinguishes **allocated** gold (specific, serial-numbered bars that are legally yours, sitting in a vault with your name on them) from **unallocated** gold (a paper claim on a pool of gold owned by a bullion bank, where you are an unsecured creditor of the bank rather than the owner of any specific metal). Most "paper gold" that trades day to day is unallocated — and that distinction, glossed over here, becomes a central question for anyone who actually wants to *own* the metal, which a later post in this series takes up in detail.

For our purposes the point is narrower but important: the refining-and-vaulting stage is where *mine output becomes part of the monetary stock*. A doré bar in a mine's strongroom is industrial product; a Good Delivery bar in a London vault is money. The supply chain's job is to convert the former into the latter — and once it does, the gold rarely leaves. It is added to the hoard, where it will sit, in one form or another, for centuries.

### Where the world's monetary gold physically sits

It is worth pausing on the geography, because it surprises people. The deepest, most liquid pool of wholesale gold sits in **London** vaults (the Bank of England alone holds over 5,000 tonnes, much of it on behalf of other nations) and in **New York**, where the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's vault holds gold for dozens of foreign governments, and COMEX-approved vaults hold the metal that backs US futures. **Zurich** is a third great hub, reflecting Switzerland's dominance in refining. This concentration is not an accident: gold gravitates to where the trust, the auditing, and the legal protections are strongest, because the whole value of a 12.4 kg brick of inert metal rests on the certainty that it is real, pure, and yours. That, too, is part of "where gold comes from" — not just the mine, but the institutions that vouch for it.

#### Worked example: the value of a single Good Delivery bar

> A 400-oz bar at the 2024 average price of **\$2,388/oz** is worth:
>
> ```
> bar value = 400 oz x $2,388/oz = $955,200
> ```
>
> Just shy of a million dollars in a brick you can hold in two hands. At the January-2026 high near **\$5,589/oz**, the same bar is worth **400 x 5,589 = \$2,235,600** — over two million dollars. **The intuition:** because each standardized bar packs roughly a million dollars of value into 12.4 kg, gold is the densest portable store of value humans have ever made — which is exactly why it has crossed borders, wars, and centuries when paper money could not.

## Recycling: the swing supply that responds to price

So far we have only counted *new* gold — metal freshly dug from the earth. But every year a large second stream of supply enters the market: **recycled gold**, mostly old jewellery and, to a lesser extent, scrap from electronics and industrial use. Because gold never corrodes or degrades, a 2,000-year-old Roman coin and a melted-down wedding ring yield exactly the same metal as a fresh mine bar. Recycling is, in effect, the above-ground hoard handing some of itself back to the market.

In 2024, recycling supplied about **1,370 tonnes** — roughly **27%** of total supply. Combine it with mine production and you get the full picture of where a year's gold comes from:

![Stacked bar of 2024 gold supply split between mine production and recycling](/imgs/blogs/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock-3.png)

Two-thirds mined, one-third recycled, totalling **~5,031 tonnes** for the year. But the *behaviour* of the two streams could not be more different, and this difference is the key insight.

**Mine supply is sticky.** It barely responds to price in the short run, because changing it requires years — permitting a new pit, building a mill, developing new ore zones. A miner cannot conjure extra ounces this quarter because the price jumped; the mine plan was set years ago.

**Recycled supply is elastic.** It responds to price almost immediately, because the "deposit" is sitting in people's drawers and safes. When gold spikes, more people decide to sell Grandma's necklace; when it falls, they hold on. Recycling is therefore the market's *swing supply* — the fast-acting valve that brings extra metal when prices are high and dries up when they are low.

You can see this in the historical record. In **2009**, in the depths of the financial crisis with gold prices rising and households strapped for cash, recycling surged to around **1,700 tonnes** as people cashed in their gold. The **2020** COVID spike and the **2024** record-price run both drew out fresh waves of scrap selling, especially across Asia and the Middle East where households hold large stocks of investment-grade jewellery and watch the price closely. Recycling is the part of supply that "feels" like a normal commodity: high prices call forth more of it.

It is worth being precise about *where* recycling comes from, because it shapes how the swing supply behaves. The bulk of recycled gold is **old jewellery** sold back by households — and that flow is concentrated in markets where gold doubles as savings: India, China, the Middle East, Turkey, and Southeast Asia. In those places, a high local gold price is a *signal to sell*, so recycling is highly price-sensitive. A smaller slice of recycling comes from **industrial scrap** — chiefly gold recovered from discarded electronics, where circuit boards, connectors, and chips contain tiny amounts of gold. Industrial recycling is less price-sensitive (it runs on the flow of e-waste, not the gold price) but it is also a reminder that gold is *recoverable* even from its most dispersed uses, which is why almost none of the metal is ever truly lost.

The behavioural asymmetry is the thing to remember. Recycling can *add* supply quickly when prices are high, but it can also *withhold* supply when prices are low or when people expect prices to rise further — households sit on their gold, waiting. In a true monetary panic, this can flip the usual logic: instead of selling jewellery for cash, people may *hoard* it harder, so the very environment that should call forth scrap (high prices) coincides with reluctance to part with it (fear of holding paper money). That is one reason recycling, for all its elasticity, never reliably caps a fear-driven rally — the sellers and the buyers are often the same households changing their minds about which they trust more, metal or money.

But here is the trap the next section springs. Even at its most responsive, recycling moves in the *hundreds* of tonnes. And the hoard it is drawing from is measured in the *hundreds of thousands*. So however dramatic a recycling surge looks in the supply tables, it is a teaspoon scooped from a swimming pool. To see why, we have to size the pool.

## The above-ground stock: the ~216,000-tonne hoard

Here is the number that reframes everything. The **total above-ground stock of gold** — every ounce ever mined and still in human hands, in any form — is estimated by the World Gold Council at about **216,000 tonnes**. (The estimate creeps up each year as mining adds to it; end-2023 was around 212,600 t.)

How big is 216,000 tonnes, really? Gold is extraordinarily dense — **19.3 grams per cubic centimetre**, nearly twice the density of lead. So 216,000 tonnes, despite being a staggering quantity of money, occupies a startlingly small *volume*. Run the arithmetic and it comes to roughly **11,200 cubic metres** — a cube about **22.4 metres on each side**. That is the famous image: *all the gold ever refined in human history would fit inside a cube the size of a modest house, about four Olympic swimming pools' worth of water.* Every gold ring, every coin, every central-bank bar, the gold in your phone — all of it, in one cube you could walk around in a minute.

Where does that hoard actually sit? Not in one place, and not all as "investment." It is spread across four broad pools:

![Horizontal bar showing where the above-ground gold stock sits by share](/imgs/blogs/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock-6.png)

- **Jewellery — about 45%** (roughly 97,000 tonnes). The single largest pool. Much of the world, especially India and China, holds savings as wearable gold. This is "investment in disguise": high-purity jewellery that doubles as a store of value and is sold back (recycled) when cash is needed.
- **Private investment — about 22%** (roughly 48,000 tonnes). Bars, coins, and ETF holdings — gold held explicitly as financial insurance rather than adornment.
- **Central bank reserves — about 17%** (roughly 37,000 tonnes). The official sector: the Federal Reserve's 8,133 t, Germany's 3,352 t, and so on down the list. This pool has been *growing* since 2022 as central banks turned into structural buyers — a story this series tells in its own post.
- **Other and industrial — about 15%** (roughly 32,000 tonnes). Gold in electronics, dentistry, aerospace, and lost or unaccounted-for metal. A slice of this is genuinely consumed and hard to recover.

The crucial property of this hoard is **persistence**. Unlike oil (burned), copper (mostly buried in cables and buildings), or wheat (eaten), gold is not destroyed by use. The ring melts back into a bar; the bar re-casts into coins; the coins fund an ETF. The stock only ever grows, and it grows slowly. That persistence is what turns the next idea — stock-to-flow — from an accounting curiosity into the deepest fact about gold's monetary nature.

### How the hoard accumulated

It helps to see how 216,000 tonnes came to exist, because it underlines just how slow the process is. For most of human history, the annual addition to the gold stock was a *trickle*. Estimates suggest that as of around **1900, only about 30,000 tonnes** of gold had ever been mined — meaning **roughly 85% of all the gold that exists today was mined in the last ~125 years.** The acceleration came with industrial-scale mining: the great South African Witwatersrand discoveries from the 1880s, the cyanide process that made low-grade ore economic, and later the giant open-pit operations across the Americas, Australia, and Asia. Even so, the *total* still only grows about 1.7% a year, because each year's addition — large in absolute terms — is small against an ever-larger base. The hoard is the compounded result of millennia of slow accumulation, and that long, steady history is itself part of gold's monetary credibility: it has never been suddenly and massively inflated, because it physically cannot be.

### A closer look at the four pools

The four-way split is worth dwelling on, because each pool behaves differently and that matters for the price.

**Jewellery (~45%)** is the great reservoir, but it is not a passive one. In Western markets, jewellery is mostly consumption — bought as adornment, rarely sold back. In India, China, the Middle East, and much of Asia, by contrast, jewellery is *savings in wearable form*: high-purity (often 22K or 24K) pieces bought by weight, with the maker's margin kept small, explicitly so the gold can be sold back near its melt value when cash is needed. This Asian jewellery pool is the swing reservoir that feeds recycling — it is, in effect, the part of the hoard that quietly re-enters the market when prices are high.

**Private investment (~22%)** is the most "monetary" pool: bars, coins, and the gold held by ETFs on behalf of investors. This is the slice that grows when the world wants insurance — it expands in fear and debasement, and contracts (gold flowing back to the market) when confidence returns and investors rotate into yielding assets.

**Central bank reserves (~17%)** is the official sector — the gold held by governments and the IMF as the ultimate reserve asset. For two decades up to about 2009, central banks were net *sellers* of gold; since 2022 they have been aggressive net *buyers*, adding over a thousand tonnes a year. Because this pool moves on policy and geopolitics rather than price, its post-2022 buying has put a new structural floor under demand — a theme this series develops in its own dedicated posts.

**Other and industrial (~15%)** is the only pool where a meaningful fraction of gold is genuinely difficult to recover — gold dispersed in electronics, used in dentistry and aerospace, or simply lost. Even here, rising prices make recovery more economic, so much of it eventually recirculates. This is the closest gold comes to being "consumed," and it is small.

The takeaway from the breakdown is that the hoard is not an inert lump; it is a set of reservoirs that exchange gold among themselves and with the market depending on price and confidence. But — and this is the point that survives all the nuance — the *total* barely changes from year to year, because the only true inflow is the tiny mine-plus-recycling flow, and there is essentially no outflow at all.

#### Worked example: how fast does mining grow the hoard?

> If the hoard is **216,000 t** and a record year of mining adds **3,661 t**, the annual growth rate of the *total stock* from mining is:
>
> ```
> stock growth = new mine supply / existing stock
>              = 3,661 t / 216,000 t
>              = 0.0169 = about 1.7% per year
> ```
>
> Even a flat-out, record-breaking year of global mining grows the world's gold by only **~1.7%**. For comparison, broad money supply in major economies routinely grows **5–10%+** a year, and in a crisis the central bank can create *trillions* in months. **The intuition:** gold's supply grows slower, and far more steadily, than any government's printing press — which is precisely the property a "store of value" needs, and precisely the property fiat money lacks.

## Stock-to-flow: why gold is "hard money"

We now have the two numbers that define gold's monetary character: a vast, near-permanent **stock**, and a small, slow annual **flow**. Divide one by the other and you get the metric that, more than any other, explains why humanity chose gold as money: the **stock-to-flow ratio (S2F)**.

> **Stock-to-flow** = existing above-ground stock ÷ annual new production. It answers: "How many years of current production would it take to dig up an amount of metal equal to everything that already exists?"

A *high* stock-to-flow ratio means the existing pile is enormous relative to what can be added each year — so new supply, even at full tilt, cannot meaningfully dilute the holders. A *low* stock-to-flow ratio means the commodity is consumed about as fast as it is produced; there is no real "hoard," and a production surge can swamp the market. The whole concept of "**hardness**" in money is just this ratio: a hard money is one whose stock-to-flow is so high that no producer can inflate the supply. Gold's S2F is the highest of any element on earth.

#### Worked example: gold's stock-to-flow, and why it means "hard"

> ```
> S2F (gold) = above-ground stock / annual mine supply
>            = 216,000 t / 3,661 t
>            = about 59
> ```
>
> A stock-to-flow near **59** means it would take roughly **59 years** of all-out mining just to dig up a second pile equal to the gold that already exists. Now compare copper. The world holds only a few weeks of copper consumption in usable above-ground stock — call it **~30 days**, or about **0.08 years** of supply — because copper is *consumed* (locked into wiring, pipes, and buildings, not stored as a hoard). Its stock-to-flow is therefore well under **1**.
>
> ```
> S2F (copper) = roughly 30 days / 365 days = about 0.08
> ```
>
> So gold's stock-to-flow is something like **700 times** copper's. **The intuition:** "hard" money is money no one can flood. A copper producer who doubled output could crater the copper price in a year; a gold producer who doubled output (impossible in practice) would still only nudge the stock from +1.7% to +3.4% a year. Gold cannot be debased by its own miners — and that, not its shine, is why it became money.

![Stock-to-flow ratio bars comparing gold silver and copper](/imgs/blogs/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock-7.png)

The figure lays the three side by side. Gold's bar runs almost the full width. Silver's is meaningfully high but lower — silver has a large above-ground stock too, but it is also *consumed* industrially (solar panels, electronics), so its hoard is constantly being drawn down, and its S2F sits well below gold's. Copper barely registers: a stub, because there is almost no copper "saved" relative to how much the world uses each year.

This is the chain of logic that the entire gold thesis hangs on, so let us state it plainly. Gold is dilute and slow to mine → so the annual flow is tiny → and gold is not consumed → so the stock just accumulates → so stock dwarfs flow → so the stock-to-flow ratio is enormous → so no one can inflate the supply → so gold holds value across centuries while currencies that *can* be inflated lose it. The supply side of gold is not a boring backwater of the story; it *is* the story. The metal's scarcity is structural and un-fakeable, and that is the property that makes it a credible vote of no-confidence in money that can be printed at will.

### Why silver, despite its hoard, is "softer" than gold

Silver is the instructive comparison because it is *also* a precious metal with a long monetary history, yet it has never held value the way gold has — and stock-to-flow explains why. Silver does have a large above-ground stock, so its S2F is meaningfully higher than copper's. But silver has a critical difference: a large and growing share of it is **consumed** industrially. Silver is the best electrical conductor of any metal, and it is essential to solar panels, electronics, and countless industrial processes — applications that lock the metal into products and disperse it so finely that recovery is often uneconomic. So silver's stock is constantly being *drawn down* by industry, which keeps its stock-to-flow far below gold's and makes its price swing with the industrial cycle, not just with monetary fear. That dual nature — part money, part industrial commodity — is why silver is more volatile than gold and why the gold-to-silver ratio is itself a watched signal. The deeper lesson: *being precious is not enough to be hard money; you also have to not be consumed.* Gold's uselessness for industry — long mocked as proof it is "just a shiny rock" — is in fact the source of its monetary supremacy, because it means almost nothing is ever destroyed.

### The same logic, applied to money itself

Stock-to-flow is not only a gold concept; it is the right lens for *any* would-be store of value, and applying it to fiat currency is clarifying. A national currency's "stock" is the money supply, and its "flow" is how fast new money is created. When a central bank expands the money supply by 10% in a year, the currency's stock-to-flow is effectively 10 — and in a hyperinflation, when money is printed faster than it can be spent, stock-to-flow collapses toward zero. That is exactly the condition under which a currency becomes worthless: its supply can be inflated without limit. Gold's S2F of ~59 is the polar opposite — a supply schedule that no authority controls and no one can accelerate. This is the precise, measurable sense in which gold is "harder" than any fiat currency: not because it shines, but because its stock-to-flow cannot be manipulated downward. (The same framework, incidentally, is why some treat Bitcoin's fixed, programmatically halving supply as "digital gold" — it is an explicit attempt to engineer a gold-like, un-inflatable stock-to-flow. Whether code-enforced scarcity earns the same trust as 5,000 years of geological scarcity is a separate debate, but the *concept* they are reaching for is exactly the one in this section.)

## The cost floor: why AISC supports but does not set the price

If supply can't move the price upward by flooding the market, can the *cost* of supply at least set a floor underneath it? Partly — and understanding exactly *how partly* is the difference between a naive and a sophisticated view of gold.

The intuitive story is simple: if it costs the average miner ~\$1,390/oz to produce gold (the 2024 AISC), then gold "can't" fall much below that, because miners would shut down, supply would shrink, and the price would be supported. There is real truth here. When gold dipped toward the low \$1,000s in 2015, with AISC around \$900, marginal high-cost mines did curtail or close, and that withdrawal of supply helped put a floor under the market. AISC is a genuine *support* level.

![All-in sustaining cost line 2013 to 2024 with the 2024 gold price and margin](/imgs/blogs/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock-4.png)

But notice what the chart shows. Yes, AISC has climbed — from ~\$900 in the mid-2010s to ~\$1,390 in 2024 — and it does sit beneath the price like a floor. But look at the *gap*. In 2024 the gold price (dashed blue) sat roughly **\$1,000 an ounce above** the cost floor (amber). The floor was nowhere near the price. AISC explains where gold *won't* go (much below cost, for long); it explains nothing about where gold *will* go on the upside.

#### Worked example: the miner's margin, and why it doesn't summon supply

> At the 2024 average gold price of **\$2,388/oz** and average AISC of **\$1,390/oz**:
>
> ```
> gross margin = price - AISC
>              = $2,388 - $1,390
>              = $998 per ounce
> margin %     = $998 / $2,388 = about 42%
> ```
>
> A **~42% gross margin** — the kind of number that, in any normal industry, would trigger a building boom of new capacity until competition competed the margin away. In gold, it does not, for three reasons. **(1)** New mines take **5–10+ years** from discovery to first production — permitting, financing, construction — so today's margin can't pull forward today's supply. **(2)** The best, richest ore is mined first, so each new tonne of supply tends to come from *lower-grade, higher-cost* deposits, which raises AISC and self-limits how much new supply the margin actually justifies. **(3)** The annual flow is so small relative to the stock that even a heroic supply response wouldn't dent the price. **The intuition:** sky-high mining margins make miners' *shareholders* rich, but they do **not** unleash enough new gold to cap the price — which is why the cost floor supports gold without ever setting it.

There is a subtle but important consequence: because supply is so inelastic, the cost floor itself *drifts upward over time* and tends to follow the price rather than anchor it. As grades fall and the easy ore is exhausted, AISC rises; as the gold price rises, miners high-grade less and chase more marginal ore, which also raises AISC. So the floor and the price move loosely together, with the floor trailing well below. This is why you should be deeply skeptical of any analyst who argues "gold is cheap because it's only X% above cost" or "gold is expensive because the margin is huge." The margin tells you about *miner profitability*, not about gold's *value as money*. A 42% margin in 2024 and a notionally enormous margin in 2026 coexisted with gold being, by most monetary measures, fairly or even cheaply priced relative to the money supply it competes with. Cost is a floor, not a valuation.

So who *does* set the gold price, if not supply? Demand — and specifically *investment and central-bank* demand, the topics of the companion posts in this series. Gold's price is set by how much the world wants to *hold* the stock, not by the trickle of new flow. When real interest rates fall, when the dollar weakens, when central banks buy, or when fear spikes, the world bids up the price it will pay to hold the existing 216,000 tonnes — and the miners, with their fixed slow output, simply collect the windfall. Supply sets the floor; the *monetary demand to hold a debasement-proof asset* sets everything above it.

To put the asymmetry in the sharpest possible terms: on any given day, the amount of gold that *could* trade is the entire 216,000-tonne stock, because every holder is a potential seller at the right price; the amount of *new* gold that arrives is 1/365th of ~5,000 tonnes, or about 14 tonnes. The daily flow is a thousandth of the stock. So when the price moves, it is overwhelmingly because holders re-priced what they will accept to part with their share of the hoard — not because the trickle of new metal overwhelmed or starved the market. Gold is the one major commodity where, on any normal day, *the flow is irrelevant to the price*. That single sentence, properly absorbed, separates people who understand gold from people who keep waiting for a supply story that never comes.

## Common misconceptions

The supply side of gold breeds a particular crop of myths, each of which evaporates once you hold the stock-versus-flow distinction in your head. Here are the big ones, each answered with a number.

### Myth 1: "We're going to run out of gold" / "peak gold means the price must soar"

It is true that the *rate* of new mine supply is plateauing — the era of discovering giant, rich, easy deposits is largely over, and grades are falling. This is the "peak gold" idea: that annual mine output may have topped out. But "peak gold" is a statement about the *flow*, and the flow is the part that barely matters. Even if mine production fell to zero tomorrow, the world would still hold **216,000 tonnes** of perfectly good gold, plus over a thousand tonnes a year of recycling recirculating it. Gold is not a fuel that gets burned; we don't "run out." Peak gold might gently support prices over decades by trimming the +1.7% annual stock growth toward +1.5% or +1.0% — but it is not the supply cliff the headlines imply. The hoard is the supply.

### Myth 2: "High prices will bring a flood of new gold that crashes the price"

This is the commodity intuition applied wrongly. For oil or copper, a price spike really does call forth new supply that eventually caps the rally. For gold it cannot, for the reasons in the margin example above: new mines take a decade, the marginal ore is worse and costlier, and the flow is tiny against the stock. The 2024 margin of ~\$998/oz (≈42%) did *not* produce a supply flood — mine output was roughly flat. Recycling rose somewhat, but as the next myth shows, that too is a rounding error. High prices make miners profitable; they do not make gold abundant.

### Myth 3: "A 10% price jump will pull out so much scrap it caps the rally"

Recycling is the elastic part of supply, so this myth has a kernel of truth — but the scale is wrong by orders of magnitude.

#### Worked example: a price jump and the scrap response

> Suppose gold jumps **10%** and, in response, recycling supply rises by a punchy **15%** — from ~1,370 t to ~1,576 t, an extra **~206 tonnes** of scrap coming to market. That sounds like a lot. Now weigh it against the hoard:
>
> ```
> extra scrap as a share of the stock = 206 t / 216,000 t = 0.00095 = about 0.1%
> ```
>
> An aggressive recycling surge adds about **one-tenth of one percent** to the world's gold stock — a rounding error. **The intuition:** recycling can smooth a price spike at the margin (it brings *some* metal when prices are high), but it has no power to *cap* a true monetary repricing of gold, because the pool it draws from is a thousand times larger than the surge.

### Myth 4: "Gold from asteroids or seawater will eventually crash the price"

A favourite of futurists. It is true that there is dissolved gold in seawater and vast quantities locked in asteroids and the earth's core. But the binding constraint is *cost*, not existence. Seawater holds gold at concentrations of parts per trillion — you would process unimaginable volumes of water for a single gram, at a cost thousands of times the gold's value. (People have literally tried; it has never been remotely economic.) Asteroid mining is generations away and would cost orders of magnitude more than the gold is worth for the foreseeable future. The relevant supply is the gold that can be extracted at or below the market price, and that supply is the slow, inelastic mine flow we have been describing. Theoretical abundance that costs more to retrieve than the metal is worth is not supply at all.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap; let us ground all of this in real episodes and numbers, because the supply mechanics show up in the tape in concrete, recurring ways.

### Where new gold actually comes from

The flow of fresh metal is geographically spread, with no single country dominating the way Saudi Arabia once dominated oil. Here is the 2023 mine-production map:

![Horizontal bar of top gold mine producing countries in 2023](/imgs/blogs/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock-2.png)

China leads at ~370 t, followed by Russia (~310 t), Australia (~290 t), Canada, the United States, and a long tail of producers from Kazakhstan to Peru. Two features matter for the market. First, the **diversity**: with the top producer supplying only ~10% of mine output, no single nation can manipulate supply the way an oil cartel can — another structural reason gold supply is "boring." Second, notice that **South Africa**, once by far the world's largest producer (it mined over 1,000 t/yr at its 1970 peak), is now down near 100 t as its deep mines age and grades fall — living proof of "peak gold" at the *country* level, and yet the global market never noticed, because the lost flow was tiny against the stock.

There is a second-order point worth drawing out here. Notice that two of the three largest producers are **China and Russia** — both of which are also among the most aggressive central-bank *buyers* of gold and, not coincidentally, the two powers most motivated to reduce their dependence on the US dollar. China, in particular, mines a great deal of gold and exports almost none of it, channelling domestic production toward domestic demand and official reserves. So the production map is not just a geology lesson; it is a quiet geopolitical map of who is accumulating the apolitical asset. That intertwining of mine supply, central-bank demand, and great-power rivalry is a thread the geopolitics posts in this series pick up — but it starts here, at the mine.

There is also a meaningful gap between where gold is *mined* and where it is *refined and vaulted*. A great deal of mine output flows to Switzerland — which mines essentially no gold of its own yet refines a large share of the world's supply — and increasingly to the UAE and China. The metal's journey from a low-grade pit in Australia or Peru, to a Swiss refinery, to a London or New York vault, to a central-bank reserve in Beijing, is a small illustration of why "where gold comes from" is really a question about a *global trust network*, not a single hole in the ground.

### Miners' margins at record prices

We have already done the margin math, but it is worth seeing it as a market event. Through 2024, with gold averaging **\$2,388** against an industry AISC near **\$1,390**, gold miners enjoyed gross margins around **\$998/oz (≈42%)** — among the fattest in the sector's history. And yet, as a group, mine output did not surge, and (a point the dedicated miners post in this series develops) gold-mining *equities* notoriously *underperformed* the metal even in this windfall, dogged by cost inflation, capital discipline, and operational stumbles. The supply side's inelasticity cuts both ways: it props the gold price up, but it does not reliably translate even spectacular margins into either a supply flood or soaring miner returns.

### Recycling surges in 2009, 2020, and 2024

The swing-supply role of recycling shows up cleanly in crisis windows. In **2009**, with the financial crisis squeezing households and gold rising, recycled supply jumped toward **~1,700 t** as people sold scrap for cash. The **2020** COVID spike — gold hitting a then-record ~\$2,075 — drew a fresh wave of scrap, especially across Asia. And the **2024** record run pulled recycling up again, with notably heavy scrap selling in price-sensitive markets like India and the Middle East where households hold large jewellery stocks and watch the local gold price daily. Each surge was real and visible in the supply data — and each was, as the worked example showed, a fraction of a percent of the hoard. Recycling is the part of gold supply that behaves like a commodity; the rest behaves like money.

### The 2026 spike: a stress test of the whole thesis

The cleanest recent test came in January 2026, when gold spiked to an all-time high near **\$5,589/oz** — pushing the implied gross margin on average-cost production toward **~\$4,200/oz**, a margin no normal industry could sustain without a supply avalanche. The supply side's response was, once again, essentially nothing in the short run: mines cannot retool a decade-long development pipeline in a quarter, and recycling, while it picked up, could not move the needle against a 216,000-tonne stock. The price was free to do whatever monetary demand dictated, exactly because supply was structurally incapable of answering. That is the stock-to-flow thesis confirmed in real time.

## The takeaway: scarcity that cannot be debased

Step back from the ore grades and refinery processes and the single, durable insight is this: **gold's supply is designed by physics and geology to be un-floodable, and that is the entire point.** Every property we have walked through — the dilute grades, the decade-long mine lead times, the AISC floor that supports but never caps, the elastic-but-tiny recycling stream, the persistence of a hoard that only ever grows ~1.7% a year — converges on one conclusion: **no one can inflate the supply of gold.** Not a miner with a record margin, not a new discovery, not a price spike, not (economically) an asteroid or the sea.

Hold that next to the alternative. A central bank can create a year's worth of new currency in an afternoon; a government can run deficits that expand the money stock by 10% or more in a crisis; a fiat currency's "stock-to-flow" can collapse to near zero when the printing presses run. Gold's cannot. Its stock-to-flow near **59** is not a marketing slogan — it is a measured, physical fact about a metal that is rare, indestructible, and slow to produce. *That* is why gold is the asset the world reaches for when it stops trusting paper money: it is the one form of money whose quantity no committee, no algorithm, and no panic can debase.

This reframes how you should read gold's price entirely. A rising gold price is not a story about supply running short — supply is as boring as ever, plodding along at +1.7% a year. It is a story about *demand for un-debasable money rising*, which is to say a story about falling confidence in the alternatives: in real yields, in the dollar, in the fiscal solvency of governments, in political stability itself. The supply chain we traced is the constant in the equation; the variable is the world's faith in paper. When that faith falls, the price of the one asset that can't be printed rises to meet it. Gold's flat, immovable supply is precisely what lets its *price* function as a clean barometer of monetary fear — there is no supply noise to muddy the signal.

And notice the deeper irony that closes the loop on this whole post. The properties that make gold seemingly *useless* — it does nothing, it pays no yield, it can't be eaten or burned or wired into a grid, it just sits there — are the very properties that make it supreme as money. Its inertness is why it doesn't corrode; its industrial uselessness is why it isn't consumed; its dilute scarcity is why it can't be flooded. A metal that was useful would be consumed, and a money that can be consumed cannot be a reliable store of value. Gold is hard money *because* it is, in every practical sense, good for nothing but being money.

So when you read the next supply headline — a mine strike, a new discovery, a "peak gold" warning, a recycling surge — you now have the right reflex. Ask: how big is this against the *stock*? Almost always, the answer is "a sliver against the cube," and the price impact is roughly nil. Gold does not trade on its flow. It trades on the world's willingness to hold its enormous, un-debasable stock — which is to say, gold trades on confidence in everything *else*. The supply chain we have traced, from a paperclip of gold in a tonne of rock to a million-dollar bar in a vault, is ultimately the machinery that guarantees gold's defining promise: scarcity you can count on when nothing else can be counted on. That promise is what you are really buying when you buy gold, and the supply side is the proof that the promise can be kept.
