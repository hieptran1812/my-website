> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold attracts more confident myth than almost any asset because it has no cash flow to argue with and strong tribal priors, so people fill the vacuum with stories. Almost all of those stories are wrong or half-true, and the data says so.
>
> - It is **not** a month-to-month inflation hedge: CPI hit 9% in 2022 and gold went from a \$1,799 average to \$1,802. It answers to **real interest rates**, on a decade scale, not to the latest CPI print.
> - It is **not** a useless rock: it is insurance. In 2022 gold returned **−0.3%** while a 60/40 portfolio fell **−16%**. The "useless" 0% yield *is* the price of the insurance.
> - It is **not** systematically rigged: spot, the London fix, COMEX and ETFs arbitrage to one price, and the fines that get headlines hit intraday spoofing, not the multi-year trend.
> - It is **not** a get-rich asset and **not** Bitcoin's poorer cousin: gold's real return since 1971 is ~3%/yr (stocks ~7%), and in the 2022 crash gold was −0.3% while Bitcoin was −64%.
> - **The one number to remember:** in 2022, the year that broke the 60/40 portfolio, gold was −0.3%. That single figure dismantles four myths at once — it is not a useless rock, not only-for-doomers, not the same trade as Bitcoin, and it earned its place without anyone predicting a price.

On 28 January 2026, gold printed an all-time high near \$5,589 an ounce. Within hours, the internet did what it always does with gold. One camp declared vindication for a 5,000-year-old truth and warned that fiat money was finally collapsing. Another camp called it a bubble that central banks were secretly inflating, or quietly suppressing, depending on the poster. A third camp shrugged that a rock that pays no interest and does nothing useful had simply gone up because gold is irrational, and you should buy Bitcoin instead. Every one of these takes was delivered with total confidence. Almost every one of them was wrong, or true in a way so narrow that the confident version is misleading.

This is the strange thing about gold. It is one of the most analyzed assets on Earth, with continuous price records going back to the 1970s and price *fragments* going back millennia, and yet the average smart person's beliefs about it are a museum of misconceptions. People who would never claim to "just know" how a bond is priced will tell you with a straight face that gold tracks inflation, that the price is fixed by banks, that it is for paranoid survivalists, or that it is heading to \$10,000 for certain. The beliefs are sticky because gold sits at the intersection of money, politics, and identity — and because, unlike a stock or a bond, it has no cash flow to anchor an argument. There is no coupon, no dividend, no earnings call. Into that vacuum rushes a flood of stories.

This post takes the six biggest stories and corrects each one with a number. Not with a vibe, not with a chart cropped to make a point, but with the actual record. The goal is not to make you a gold bull or a gold bear. It is to give you the calibrated, slightly-boring truth, because the calibrated truth is the only thing that lets you size a position you can actually live with.

## Foundations: why gold breeds myths

Before we correct any single myth, it is worth understanding *why* gold is such fertile ground for them. Two structural facts about the asset do almost all the work, and once you see them, every myth in this post becomes easier to dismantle.

**The first fact: gold has no cash flow.** A share of stock entitles you to a slice of a company's future earnings. A bond entitles you to a stream of coupon payments and a return of principal. These cash flows give you something concrete to argue about. You can disagree about whether a company will earn \$5 or \$8 a share next year, but you are at least arguing about a number that will eventually be revealed. Gold has nothing like this. A one-ounce coin you buy today will be a one-ounce coin in fifty years. It will pay you no interest, issue you no dividend, and grow into nothing. Its entire return comes from the price someone else is willing to pay for it later.

This sounds like a weakness, and in one narrow sense it is — we will deal with the "useless rock" version of that complaint as Myth 2. But for now, notice what it does to the *discourse*. When an asset has no cash flow, there is no fundamental number to discipline the conversation. You cannot value gold with a discounted-cash-flow model, because there are no cash flows to discount. So the question "what is gold worth?" has no clean answer, and nature abhors that vacuum. People fill it with narratives: gold is "real money," gold is "a hedge against the apocalypse," gold is "a barbarous relic." Each narrative feels complete, and none can be falsified by a quarterly earnings report. That is the engine of myth.

**The second fact: gold is tribal.** Because there is no anchoring cash flow, beliefs about gold tend to bundle with beliefs about everything else — politics, the trustworthiness of governments, the future of the dollar, even personality. "Goldbugs" are a recognizable type; so are the people who sneer at them. Once a belief about gold becomes part of your identity, contradicting evidence does not update the belief, it threatens the self. A goldbug who sees gold fall for a year decides the price is being suppressed. A gold skeptic who sees gold rise for a decade decides it is a bubble about to pop. Both have a story ready that protects the prior. This is not a moral failing unique to gold investors — it is how humans treat any belief that has fused with identity. Gold just happens to be unusually good at fusing.

**A third fact compounds the first two: gold has a very long, very selective memory.** Markets that trade continuously produce a fresh data point every day, and over a decade you accumulate thousands of them — enough to test almost any claim statistically. Gold has those daily prints too, but the *narratives* about gold are anchored to a handful of dramatic episodes that everyone remembers and almost no one weights correctly. The 1970s, when gold went from \$35 to \$850. The 1980 blow-off top. The 2008 crisis. The 2011 peak at \$1,921. The 2020 COVID record. The 2026 all-time high. These are the data points people carry in their heads, and they are precisely the *least representative* moments in the series — the tails, not the body. Ask someone what gold "does" and they will describe a crisis or a mania, because those are the episodes that made the news. The twenty boring years from 1980 to 2000, when gold did almost nothing and lost real value, are simply not part of the folk memory. A belief built from remembered tails and forgotten middles will always overstate how dramatic and how reliable gold is. The corrections in this post are, in large part, just the act of putting the forgotten middle back into the picture.

> [!note]
> **The spine of this whole series, stated once:** gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability. Almost every myth in this post is a failure to take that sentence seriously. People expect gold to behave like a growth investment (so they are disappointed by its returns), or like a yield instrument (so they call it useless), or like a perfectly predictable index (so they assume any gap must be manipulation). Hold the insurance framing and the myths mostly dissolve on their own.

One more framing note before the myths, because it will save you from a category error that runs through all six. There are really only two coherent ways to think about gold, and most confused beliefs come from secretly switching between them. The first is gold as a **commodity** — a physical thing with supply, demand, mining costs, and industrial uses, whose price should be analyzed like copper or oil. The second is gold as a **monetary asset** — a store of value, a near-currency, whose price is really a statement about the alternatives (paper money and real yields) rather than about the metal itself. The truth is that gold is *mostly* the second and only marginally the first: its annual mine supply is tiny relative to the enormous above-ground stock, so industrial supply-and-demand barely moves the price, while shifts in the monetary story move it enormously. Almost every myth below is a case of applying commodity logic to what is fundamentally a monetary asset, or vice versa. Keep the monetary lens primary and the commodity lens secondary, and you will already be ahead of most of the confident voices online.

With those two facts in hand — no cash flow, strong priors — let us take the myths one at a time. For each, I will state the myth as people actually believe it, then correct it with the record.

## Myth 1: gold is a perfect inflation hedge

This is the most widespread belief about gold, and it is the one that does the most damage, because it is *almost* true and people therefore trust it past its breaking point. The myth, in its strong form, is: "When inflation goes up, gold goes up. Gold protects your purchasing power, dollar for dollar, in real time."

The correction is the entire reason this series exists, and it is worth stating bluntly. Over short horizons — months, even a year or two — gold's correlation with inflation is weak and unreliable. The cleanest natural experiment of our lifetime is 2021 and 2022. Consumer prices in the United States accelerated from under 2% in early 2021 to a 40-year high of **9.06%** in June 2022. If the strong inflation-hedge myth were true, gold should have soared. It did almost nothing. The annual average gold price was **\$1,799** in 2021 and **\$1,802** in 2022 — a move of less than two-tenths of one percent, while the cost of living was rising at the fastest pace since 1981.

![Gold annual price versus CPI inflation 2020 to 2026 showing gold flat while inflation spiked](/imgs/blogs/gold-myths-corrected-with-numbers-hedge-bubble-manipulation-and-more-2.png)

Look at the chart and you can see the myth break in real time. The amber line — CPI — rockets to nearly 9% in 2022. The blue bars — gold — sit there. If you had bought gold in 2021 *specifically* as protection against the inflation everyone could feel coming, you spent eighteen months watching the thing you were supposed to be hedged against eat your real wealth while your hedge did nothing.

So why does the myth persist? Because over *long* horizons it contains a real truth. Across decades, gold has roughly preserved purchasing power: an ounce of gold buys about as much today as it did generations ago, which you cannot say of a dollar bill stuffed in a mattress. The 1970s, the formative decade for the entire goldbug worldview, saw gold rise from \$35 to a peak of \$850 while inflation ran hot. So the believer remembers the 1970s and the multi-decade purchasing-power story, and forgets that *within* those long stretches, the month-to-month relationship is mostly noise.

The deeper correction — the one that makes this series worth reading — is that gold does not respond to inflation at all, directly. It responds to **real interest rates**: the nominal interest rate minus expected inflation. Real rates are what you actually earn on safe money after inflation eats its share. When real rates are deeply negative — when cash and bonds are guaranteed to lose purchasing power — the opportunity cost of holding a zero-yield asset like gold disappears, and gold tends to rise. When real rates are high and positive — when a Treasury bond pays you 2% *above* inflation — holding a metal that pays nothing is expensive, and gold tends to languish.

This is why 2021–22 looks paradoxical only to someone watching the wrong variable. Inflation was high, yes, but the Federal Reserve was raising interest rates faster than at any time in decades, dragging the 10-year real yield from about **−1.04%** at the end of 2021 to **+1.57%** by the end of 2022. The thing that actually matters to gold — the real return on the safe alternative — was rising sharply. Gold, watching the variable it actually cares about, correctly went sideways.

It helps to see why the 1970s and 2021–22 produced opposite outcomes despite both featuring high inflation, because the contrast *is* the correction. In the 1970s, inflation ran into double digits while the Fed, under-reacting for most of the decade, kept nominal interest rates *below* the inflation rate. That meant deeply negative real rates: if inflation was 12% and you could only earn 7% on a Treasury bond, you were losing 5% of your purchasing power every year just for holding the "safe" asset. In that world, gold's 0% yield was actually *better* than the bond's −5% real yield, and money poured into the metal. In 2021–22, by contrast, the Fed reacted hard and fast, hiking rates above the level the market expected inflation to settle at, pushing real rates from deeply negative to clearly positive. Same high headline inflation, opposite real-rate path, opposite gold outcome. The headline number — CPI — was nearly identical in spirit across the two episodes. The variable that actually drives gold — the real yield on the safe alternative — moved in opposite directions, and gold followed the real yield, not the CPI, both times.

This also explains the otherwise-baffling 2024–26 stretch, when gold surged to new records even as real yields stayed *positive* — a apparent violation of the real-rate rule. The resolution is that real rates are the dominant driver but not the only one. When a second force, like the structural central-bank buying we will meet in Myth 4, becomes large enough, it can override the real-rate headwind. The lesson is not that the real-rate model is wrong; it is that gold answers to a small set of monetary variables — real rates first, then the dollar, then official-sector demand — and never to the single headline number that the inflation-hedge myth fixates on.

#### Worked example: the 2021–22 inflation hedge that wasn't

Suppose at the start of 2021 you held \$100,000 and feared inflation. You put \$50,000 into gold as a "hedge" and left \$50,000 in cash. Over 2021–22, inflation cumulatively ran roughly 15% (about 7% then about 6.5%, compounding). Your \$50,000 of cash lost about 15% of its purchasing power — it could now buy what about \$42,500 bought before. And your gold? It went from a 2021 average of \$1,799 to a 2022 average of \$1,802, a nominal gain of about 0.2%. In real terms, after that same 15% inflation, your \$50,000 of gold had the purchasing power of about \$43,500. **Both halves of your "inflation-protected" \$100,000 lost roughly 14–15% of their real value.** The hedge did not hedge. The takeaway: gold protects purchasing power across decades, not across the exact inflation spike you are worried about today — for that horizon, watch real rates, not CPI.

And for the real-rate machinery underneath it, the cross-asset series treats [real yields as the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything).

## Myth 2: gold is a useless rock that yields nothing

The skeptic's favorite. Warren Buffett gave it its most quotable form: gold just sits there, it doesn't pay you anything, you have to pay to store and insure it, and a productive asset like farmland or a business will compound while your shiny metal does nothing. The implied conclusion is that owning gold is irrational — a triumph of psychology over arithmetic.

The factual core is correct: gold yields nothing. A bar of gold will never send you a dividend or a coupon. If you store it in a vault, you pay a fee; if you keep it at home, you pay in risk. On a pure cash-flow basis, gold is a cost center, not an income source. We covered the deep version of this in [the no-yield problem: how a metal that pays nothing can be worth anything](/blog/trading/gold/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything).

But "yields nothing" is not the same as "useless," and the slide between the two is where the myth lives. Consider the things in your life that yield nothing and that you pay to maintain. Your home insurance yields nothing. Your fire extinguisher yields nothing. The spare tire in your trunk yields nothing, and it costs you fuel to carry it around. You do not call them useless. You understand that they have a *convenience yield*: a non-cash return that shows up precisely when everything else is going wrong. You hold them not because they compound, but because they pay off in the one state of the world where your other assets fail.

That is exactly what gold is. Its yield is not paid in dollars; it is paid in **portfolio insurance** — a payoff that arrives in the rare, ugly states of the world when stocks and bonds fall together. And we can put a number on that payoff, because 2022 handed it to us.

It is worth being precise about the term "convenience yield," because economists use it for a real and measurable thing, not just a rhetorical dodge. The convenience yield of an asset is the non-cash benefit you get from holding it directly rather than holding a claim on it or a substitute. For a commodity a factory needs, it is the value of having the input on hand so the production line never stops. For gold, the convenience yield is the value of holding an asset that is liquid, no one's liability, and negatively correlated with your other holdings in exactly the moments you most need liquidity and safety. That benefit is real even though it never shows up as a coupon — and crucially, it is *highest* precisely when markets are most stressed, which is why gold's "return" is so lumpy and concentrated in crisis years. A bond pays you a smooth coupon every period regardless of the state of the world. Gold pays you nothing in the calm and a great deal in the storm. Averaging that lumpy payoff and complaining that it is "only 3% a year" misses that you are not buying it for the average; you are buying it for the storm.

![2022 total returns by asset showing nearly everything fell while gold was flat](/imgs/blogs/gold-myths-corrected-with-numbers-hedge-bubble-manipulation-and-more-3.png)

In 2022, almost every financial asset fell, and they fell *together*, which is the thing the textbook 60/40 portfolio is supposed to make impossible. The S&P 500 returned **−18.1%**. The Nasdaq 100, **−32.5%**. Long-dated Treasuries — the thing you own *specifically* to cushion a stock crash — fell **−31.2%**, because rising rates clobber long bonds. The classic 60% stocks / 40% bonds portfolio had its worst year in modern history at **−16.1%**. And gold? **−0.3%.** Essentially flat, in the exact year that the rest of the portfolio betrayed its owner.

That −0.3% is not a triumphant return. It will never make headlines. But it is the entire point. The "useless rock" did its job in the one year of the decade when everything else broke at once. A fire extinguisher that "does nothing" 364 days a year is not useless; it is doing exactly what you bought it for on the 365th.

#### Worked example: the convenience yield, priced in 2022

Two investors start 2022 with \$1,000,000. Investor A holds a standard 60/40 portfolio. Investor B holds the same 60/40, but carves out 10% into gold (so 54% stocks, 36% bonds, 10% gold). Run the 2022 numbers. Investor A's 60/40 fell −16.1%, ending at about \$839,000 — a loss of \$161,000. Investor B's stock-and-bond sleeve (90% of the portfolio, at the same −16.1% blend) lost about \$145,000, while the 10% gold sleeve (\$100,000) returned −0.3%, losing just \$300. Investor B ends at about \$854,700 — a loss of about \$145,300. **The gold sleeve saved Investor B roughly \$15,700 in a single year, and the metal "yielded nothing" the entire time.** The takeaway: gold's yield is not a coupon, it is the loss you *did not* take in the year your other assets fell together — call it useless only if you call insurance useless.

The cross-asset series frames this allocation question directly in [gold: money, insurance, or just a rock?](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock) — the portfolio-math home of the "insurance, not investment" argument.

## Myth 3: the gold price is rigged and manipulated

This is the myth with the most emotional charge, and the one where you have to be most careful, because the honest answer is "mostly no, with an important asterisk," and both the true-believer and the dismissive skeptic get it wrong.

The believer's version: powerful banks, sometimes with central-bank blessing, systematically suppress the gold price to protect confidence in fiat currencies. Every sharp drop is "a raid." Every failure of gold to rise as much as the believer expected is "the cartel capping it." The price you see is fake; the "real" price is much higher and is being hidden from you.

Start with what is actually true, because there *is* a real basis for suspicion. Traders at major banks have, in fact, been caught and fined for manipulating precious-metals prices. The technique is called **spoofing**: placing large orders you never intend to execute, to create a false impression of supply or demand, nudging the price for seconds or minutes so you can profit on a real trade in the other direction. JPMorgan paid a settlement of about \$920 million in 2020 over years of spoofing in metals and Treasuries; other banks and individual traders have been fined and, in some cases, jailed. So manipulation of gold prices is not a conspiracy theory in the literal sense — it has been proven in court.

But notice the *scale* of what was proven. Spoofing moves the price for seconds to minutes. It is a way to skim a few basis points on a single trade. It is real, it is illegal, and it has nothing to do with the multi-year trend. Nobody was convicted of holding gold down from \$1,000 to \$300 for a decade, because that is not a thing a trading desk can do. To understand why, you have to understand how the gold price is actually formed.

![Why the gold price is not rigged showing four arbitraged venues converging to one price](/imgs/blogs/gold-myths-corrected-with-numbers-hedge-bubble-manipulation-and-more-4.png)

Gold does not have one price set by one authority. It has at least four major venues that all trade the same underlying metal: the **London OTC spot market** (the largest, trading roughly \$200 billion a day), the twice-daily **London auction** (the "fix," where a transparent price is struck by matching buy and sell orders), **COMEX futures** in New York (standardized, deliverable contracts), and the **physically-backed ETFs** like GLD, whose shares can be created and redeemed for actual bars. These venues are connected by arbitrage: if COMEX futures got meaningfully cheaper than London spot, traders would buy futures, take delivery, and sell the metal in London until the gap closed. If GLD shares traded below the value of the gold they represent, authorized participants would buy the cheap shares, redeem them for bars, and sell the bars, until the discount vanished.

This is the asterisk to the whole manipulation myth. To *durably* suppress the gold price, you would not have to fool one venue — you would have to defeat all four at once, against the combined capital of every arbitrageur on the planet who would gleefully buy underpriced gold and sell it where it was dear. And you would have to do it while the largest holders of physical gold — central banks, who own thousands of tonnes — declined to take advantage of the cheap price by buying. The mechanism does not exist. What exists is a deeply liquid, multiply-arbitraged market in which a trading desk can nudge the price for ninety seconds and get fined for it, but in which no one can hold a 5,000-year-old globally-traded asset off its trend for years.

There is a second, subtler confusion bundled into the manipulation myth that is worth separating out, because it sounds like manipulation but is actually just how the market works. Believers point out that there are far more "paper" claims on gold — futures contracts, unallocated accounts at bullion banks — than there is physical metal to back them all simultaneously. This is true, and it is presented as proof that the price is fake: if everyone demanded delivery at once, the system would break, so the screen price must be a fiction propped up by paper. But this is just fractional-reserve mechanics, the same thing that is true of every financial market. There are vastly more dollar bank deposits than physical dollar bills; there are more shares promised through securities lending than exist in some moments; this is not fraud, it is the normal structure of a market where most participants want price exposure, not physical possession. The price is "real" in the only sense that matters: it is the price at which you can actually buy or sell, and at which physical metal actually changes hands every day. The fact that not everyone can take delivery at once is a feature of leverage, not evidence of a suppressed "true" price hiding behind the screen. Where paper-versus-physical genuinely matters — the basis, contango, delivery mechanics — is a real and interesting topic, just not a manipulation one; the series covers it in the futures post.

It is also worth being precise about *who* would even want to suppress gold and whether the incentive holds up. The believer's story usually casts central banks or governments as the suppressors, wanting to hide the "true" weakness of fiat money. But central banks have spent the last three years doing the exact opposite — buying gold by the thousand tonnes, as Myth 4 will show. An institution suppressing gold to protect its currency would not simultaneously be the largest buyer of the thing it is suppressing. The incentive story is incoherent: the actors with the means to move the market are, by their own disclosed transactions, on the *buy* side. The simplest explanation — that gold trades at a fair, arbitraged, occasionally-spoofed-for-seconds price set by a global market — fits the evidence far better than a multi-decade conspiracy that the supposed conspirators keep undermining with their own purchases.

For the plumbing of how these venues actually connect — spot versus the fix versus futures, and the troy-ounce conventions underneath — see [how gold is priced: spot, the London fix, futures, and the troy ounce](/blog/trading/gold/how-gold-is-priced-spot-the-london-fix-futures-and-the-troy-ounce).

#### Worked example: how arbitrage erases a "suppression"

Imagine a cartel manages to push COMEX gold futures down to \$5,000 while physical gold in London still clears at \$5,400 — a \$400 "suppression." What happens next? An arbitrageur buys the \$5,000 futures contract, stands for delivery to receive 100 ounces of real gold, and simultaneously sells that gold in London at \$5,400. That is \$400 per ounce, \$40,000 per contract, of essentially riskless profit. Every trading desk on Earth does the same thing the instant the gap appears, buying the cheap futures (pushing them up) and selling physical (pushing it down) until the \$400 gap collapses to the cost of carry — a few dollars. **A suppression that creates a \$400 arbitrage cannot survive contact with a market that has thousands of participants able to capture it.** The takeaway: a price gap across arbitraged venues is not evidence of successful manipulation, it is the profit that destroys the manipulation.

## Myth 4: only doomers and goldbugs own gold

The cultural myth. Gold, in this telling, is for the bunker crowd — people stockpiling canned food and ammunition who think the financial system is about to collapse. If you own gold, you are signaling that you expect catastrophe, and since catastrophe rarely comes, you are mostly just being paranoid and missing out on the stock market.

The correction is the most quantitative in this post, and it comes from the most boring, least doom-coded institutions on Earth: central banks. These are not survivalists. They are the conservative, committee-driven managers of national reserves, whose entire job is to *not* be wrong. And since 2022, they have been buying gold at a pace not seen in modern history.

The numbers are stark. From 2010 through 2021, central banks bought a respectable but unremarkable average of about **473 tonnes** of gold a year. Then in **2022 they bought 1,082 tonnes** — the highest annual total since 1950 — and followed it with **1,037 tonnes in 2023** and **1,045 tonnes in 2024**. Three consecutive years above a thousand tonnes, more than double the prior decade's pace. The buyers were not preppers; they were the central banks of China, Turkey, India, Poland, and a long list of others, deliberately diversifying their reserves away from dollars and into the one reserve asset that is no one else's liability.

Why does this dismantle the doomer myth? Because the entire point of a diversifier is that you hold it *whether or not* you expect catastrophe. A central bank holding gold is not betting on the apocalypse. It is doing exactly what a sensible portfolio manager does: holding an asset whose returns are uncorrelated with the rest of its book, so that when one thing fails, the whole reserve does not fail with it. The 2022 chart from Myth 2 is the proof — gold was −0.3% while everything else fell. An asset that behaves *differently* from your other holdings is the textbook definition of a diversifier, and diversification is the least doom-laden idea in all of finance. It is just not putting all your eggs in one basket.

There is a structural story here too, and it is the most important development in the gold market of the last decade. The freezing of Russia's foreign-exchange reserves in 2022 taught every central bank on Earth a lesson: dollar reserves held in the Western financial system can be switched off by political decision. Gold sitting in your own vault cannot. That lesson turned central-bank gold buying from a cyclical trade into a structural one — a new, price-insensitive floor under demand. The full treatment is in [central banks: the structural buyer that changed gold after 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022).

This points at the property that makes gold uniquely suited to the conservative reserve manager, and it is the deepest reason the doomer myth is backwards. Every other reserve asset is somebody's *liability*. A U.S. Treasury bond is a promise from the U.S. government; a euro deposit is a claim on a European bank; a dollar in your reserve account is an entry on someone else's ledger that can be frozen, defaulted on, or inflated away by the issuer. Gold is the only major reserve asset that is no one's liability — it does not depend on any counterparty keeping a promise, because it simply *is*. For a central bank whose entire mandate is to hold reserves that will still be there and still be spendable in a crisis, an asset with zero counterparty risk is not a doomer's toy; it is the most conservative holding available. The doomer myth gets the psychology exactly inverted: gold is not the bet of someone expecting collapse, it is the holding of someone whose job is to be prepared for a counterparty failure they hope never comes — which is, again, the definition of insurance. The series develops this in [the apolitical asset: why central banks trust gold over each other's money](/blog/trading/gold/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money).

#### Worked example: the diversifier math, not the doomer bet

Take a simple two-asset portfolio: \$900,000 in a 60/40 stock-bond blend and \$100,000 in gold, total \$1,000,000, entering 2022. The 60/40 sleeve fell −16.1%, losing about \$144,900. The gold sleeve was −0.3%, losing \$300. Total portfolio loss: about \$145,200, or **−14.5%** — versus −16.1% if the whole thing had been 60/40. The gold did not require you to believe the world was ending. It simply moved differently from your stocks and bonds in the year they all fell together, and that difference is worth 1.6 percentage points of avoided loss on the whole portfolio. **You do not have to be a doomer to want one holding that zigs when the rest zags.** The takeaway: gold's case is diversification, a tool every conservative institution uses, not a forecast of collapse.

## Myth 5: it's a bubble and it's going to \$10,000

After a price runs to an all-time high, two opposite myths appear simultaneously, and they share a hidden assumption. The bear says "this is a bubble, it's going to crash." The bull says "this is just the start, it's going to \$10,000." Both are confident *price predictions*, and the data's answer to both is the same: you cannot reliably predict the price, so stop trying, and size the position instead.

Start with the bear's "bubble" claim, because it has a specific, testable meaning. A bubble is a price detached from any reasonable basis, driven by the greater-fool dynamic, destined to collapse. Is gold's run a bubble? The honest answer is that gold has *no* cash-flow basis to be detached *from* — that is Myth 2's whole point — so "bubble" is harder to define for gold than for a stock. But we can at least ask whether the recent run is unprecedented mania. It is large, but central banks buying a thousand tonnes a year and real yields gyrating is a coherent macro story, not obvious tulip-mania. Calling it a bubble is a prediction, and predictions about gold have a poor track record in both directions.

Now the bull's "\$10,000" claim, which is the more seductive and the more revealing. The hidden premise is that gold makes you rich if you just hold on. The long-run record says otherwise.

![Real after-inflation returns 1971 to 2024 showing gold modest versus stocks](/imgs/blogs/gold-myths-corrected-with-numbers-hedge-bubble-manipulation-and-more-5.png)

Over the whole post-gold-standard era, from 1971 to 2024, gold's **real** (after-inflation) return has been roughly **3% per year**. Compare that to U.S. stocks at about **7%** and even bonds at about **2.5%**. Three percent real is not nothing — it beats cash and roughly matches bonds — but it is not a wealth machine. A 3% real return doubles your purchasing power in about 24 years. A 7% real return from stocks doubles it in about 10. Over a 40-year working life, that gap is the difference between roughly tripling and roughly fifteen-fold-ing your real wealth. Gold is the slow lane.

And the chart hides a second, crucial truth: gold's return is wildly **regime-dependent**. From 1971 to 2000, its real return was about **−0.5% per year** — three decades of losing purchasing power, including the famous twenty-year winter from the 1980 peak. From 2001 to 2024, it was about **+7.5% per year** — a generational bull market. The 3% full-sample figure is the average of a brutal bear and a roaring bull, and *which one you got depended entirely on when you bought.* The person who bought at the 1980 top waited 28 years just to break even in nominal terms, and far longer in real terms. The person who bought in 2001 looked like a genius.

This is the deepest correction in the entire post, so let me state it plainly. **Because you cannot reliably predict which regime you are entering, you should not bet on a price target. You should size a position.** A \$10,000 price call requires you to be right about both the timing and the magnitude of an asset whose own history shows decade-long swings in both directions. A 5–15% portfolio sleeve, rebalanced, requires you to predict nothing. It pays off as insurance when you need it, it costs you little when you don't, and the act of rebalancing — trimming gold after it spikes, adding after it slumps — harvests a bonus from gold's own volatility without any forecast at all.

![Sizing beats prediction showing the fragile price target bet versus the robust sized sleeve](/imgs/blogs/gold-myths-corrected-with-numbers-hedge-bubble-manipulation-and-more-7.png)

The figure lays the two approaches side by side. The prediction bet on the left is fragile: it needs the timing and the magnitude both right, and one wrong decade (1980–2000) can wipe it out. The sizing rule on the right is robust: the position is small enough that being wrong costs little, rebalancing turns volatility into a small tailwind, and it survives every price path because it never required a forecast in the first place. This is not a counsel of timidity. It is the recognition that for an asset whose real returns swing from −0.5% to +7.5% depending on the era, *position sizing is the only thing you actually control.*

The rebalancing point deserves more than a passing mention, because it is the mechanism that turns gold's biggest apparent weakness — its volatility and its lack of a clear trend — into a quiet advantage. Suppose you commit to a 10% gold sleeve and rebalance once a year back to that 10%. When gold spikes, it grows past 10% of your portfolio, so rebalancing forces you to *sell* some gold and redeploy into whatever lagged — you are systematically selling high. When gold slumps, it shrinks below 10%, so rebalancing forces you to *buy* more with proceeds trimmed from whatever rose — you are systematically buying low. Over a full cycle of gold's large swings, this disciplined trimming and adding harvests a "rebalancing bonus" that can add a meaningful amount to your realized return *relative to* gold's own buy-and-hold path, precisely because gold is volatile and mean-reverting around the monetary story. The volatility that makes gold scary to a price-target bettor is exactly what makes it valuable to a rebalancer. You do not need gold to go up to benefit; you need it to *move*, and to move differently from your other holdings.

There is a final piece of the "bubble or moon" debate worth naming, because it traps smart people. The fact that gold's full-era real return is only 3% does not mean any *particular* decade will deliver 3% — it almost never does. You get −0.5% or +7.5%, not the average. So the bull pointing at the 2001–2024 surge and the bear pointing at the 1980–2000 winter are both showing you real data, and both are committing the same error: extrapolating one regime into the future. The honest position is that you do not know which regime is next, the monetary variables (real rates, the dollar, central-bank demand) will decide it, and your job is not to guess the regime but to hold a position size that is fine in either. That is what "sizing beats prediction" really means: it is humility about an unknowable, expressed as a number you choose in advance.

#### Worked example: the price-target bet versus the sized sleeve

Investor A, the predictor, is sure gold goes to \$10,000 and puts \$200,000 — most of his liquid savings — into gold at \$5,400/oz, about 37 ounces. Investor B, the sizer, puts 10% of a \$1,000,000 portfolio, \$100,000, into gold and rebalances annually. Now suppose gold instead falls 30% over two years to \$3,780, as it has done before. Investor A is down \$60,000 on a \$200,000 position — a 30% hit to most of his savings — and faces years of waiting, exactly the 1980-style trap. Investor B's gold sleeve falls from \$100,000 to \$70,000, a \$30,000 loss that is just 3% of his \$1,000,000 portfolio, and his rebalancing rule now has him *buying* more gold cheaply with gains trimmed from whatever rose. **The predictor's outcome hinges on a number no one can know; the sizer's outcome is survivable on every path.** The takeaway: the durable edge in gold is not calling the price, it is choosing a position size you can hold through both regimes.

## Myth 6: Bitcoin replaced gold

The newest myth, and the one with the most cultural momentum. "Digital gold" is a brilliant piece of marketing: Bitcoin is scarce (capped at 21 million coins), it is not controlled by any government, and it is portable in a way physical gold never can be. The conclusion that gold is the obsolete, analog version — "boomer gold" versus "digital gold" — feels intuitive to a generation that trusts code more than vaults.

The correction is not that Bitcoin is bad or that gold is better in every way. It is that the two assets behave completely differently in the one situation that defines a safe haven: a crisis. A haven is not the asset that goes up the most in a bull market. It is the asset you can rely on when everything else is falling. And on that test, the data is unambiguous.

![Gold versus Bitcoin as a haven scorecard across crisis record, volatility, counterparty, and track length](/imgs/blogs/gold-myths-corrected-with-numbers-hedge-bubble-manipulation-and-more-6.png)

Go back to 2022, our recurring stress test. Gold was **−0.3%**. Bitcoin was **−64.3%**. In the year that broke the 60/40 portfolio and tested every asset's claim to be a haven, Bitcoin fell harder than the Nasdaq, harder than long bonds, harder than almost anything. It behaved like a high-beta technology stock, not like a safe haven — it went *down* with risk assets precisely when a haven is supposed to hold. This is not a one-off. Bitcoin's defining behavior in every risk-off episode of its short life has been to fall *with* equities, often more, because it is the most speculative thing in most portfolios and the first to be sold when investors raise cash.

The scorecard captures the four dimensions on which a haven is judged. **Crisis record:** gold −0.3% in 2022, Bitcoin −64%. **Volatility:** gold runs at roughly 15% annualized, bond-like; Bitcoin at 60–80%, equity-plus, which alone disqualifies it as a stable store of value. **Counterparty risk:** physical gold in your possession is no one's liability, while Bitcoin, though decentralized on-chain, is in practice held on exchanges and in custody arrangements that *have* failed catastrophically — the collapse of FTX in 2022 vaporized billions in customer crypto. **Track length:** gold has been money across roughly 5,000 years and dozens of monetary regimes; Bitcoin has existed for about fifteen years and one macro cycle, and has never been tested through a sustained high-inflation, high-rate environment as a mature reserve asset.

There is a deeper reason the two assets diverge in a crisis, and it is about *who holds them and why*. Gold's holders are dominated by central banks, long-term reserve managers, and conservative allocators who hold it precisely as insurance and are therefore the least likely to dump it in a panic — the marginal seller of gold is not a leveraged speculator. Bitcoin's holders, by contrast, still skew heavily toward leveraged traders, momentum chasers, and recent buyers with no through-the-cycle conviction. When risk-off hits, the leveraged Bitcoin holder gets a margin call and is *forced* to sell, which pushes the price down, which triggers more margin calls — a liquidation cascade. Gold's holder base does not produce that dynamic. The asset's crisis behavior is downstream of its ownership, and Bitcoin's ownership is still structured like a high-beta speculation, not like a reserve. Until that holder base matures — until Bitcoin is held mostly by people who will sit through a 50% drawdown without selling — its crisis behavior will keep looking like the Nasdaq's, not gold's.

It is also worth granting Bitcoin its genuine advantages honestly, because the goal here is calibration, not a hit piece. Bitcoin is more portable than gold — you can carry a fortune across a border in your memory — which matters enormously for someone fleeing a collapsing regime, a use case where physical gold's weight is a real liability. Bitcoin's supply schedule is fixed and transparent in a way gold's is not. And its track record, though short, is not zero. The honest summary is that Bitcoin may be developing into a *different* kind of monetary asset with its own merits, but it has not yet earned the specific title "safe haven," because a safe haven is defined by crisis behavior and Bitcoin's crisis behavior is, so far, that of a risk asset.

None of this means Bitcoin has no future or that it cannot become a haven someday. It means it is not one *yet*, on the only evidence that matters — its behavior when investors are frightened. Calling Bitcoin "digital gold" is a claim about a future that has not happened, dressed up as a description of the present. The crisis data says gold and Bitcoin are not the same trade — and an investor who treats them as interchangeable will discover the difference at the worst possible moment.

#### Worked example: the haven test, run on 2022

An investor wants a \$100,000 crisis hedge and is deciding between gold and Bitcoin, persuaded by the "digital gold" pitch. He splits the test: say he had put \$100,000 in each at the start of 2022. The gold position ended the year at about \$99,700 — down \$300, having done its job as a haven. The Bitcoin position ended at about \$35,700 — down \$64,300, having amplified the crisis instead of cushioning it. **The asset marketed as gold's replacement lost two-thirds of its value in the exact year a hedge was supposed to prove itself.** The takeaway: an asset earns the word "haven" by what it does in a crash, and on that test gold and Bitcoin are not interchangeable.

For how gold actually behaves across crises — including the important exception where gold *first* falls in a liquidity squeeze before soaring — see [fear and the safe-haven trade: how gold behaves in a crisis](/blog/trading/gold/fear-and-the-safe-haven-trade-how-gold-behaves-in-a-crisis).

## How it shows up in real markets

Myths are not just internet arguments; they cost real money when they drive real decisions. The whole reason to correct a belief about gold is that the belief becomes a position, and a position has a profit-and-loss. Here is how each correction shows up in the behavior of actual investors and markets, with dates and numbers attached — and in every case, notice that the people who got hurt were not unintelligent. They were operating a confident, internally-consistent model of gold that happened to be wrong, which is the most dangerous kind of error because it feels like knowledge.

**The inflation-hedge believers of 2021.** A wave of retail and even institutional money flowed into gold and gold ETFs in 2020–21 explicitly as an inflation hedge, as everyone could see stimulus and supply shocks coming. Global gold-backed ETF holdings peaked at about **3,909 tonnes** in late 2020. Then inflation actually arrived in 2022 — and gold went sideways while the Fed hiked, and ETF holdings *drained* to about 3,473 tonnes by year-end 2022 as the believers, confused that their "inflation hedge" wasn't working, sold. They had the right asset for the wrong reason and the wrong horizon, and they sold at exactly the moment the real-rate story was about to turn back in gold's favor. The myth — "gold tracks inflation month to month" — cost them the trade.

**The manipulation believers and the perpetual bottom-call.** A subculture of gold investors spent the 2013–2018 grind insisting the price was being suppressed and that "real" gold was worth multiples of the screen price. The screen price was real. Gold fell from its 2011 average of about \$1,572 to about \$1,160 in 2015 because real yields rose and the post-crisis fear trade unwound — a coherent macro story, not a conspiracy. Investors who believed the suppression myth refused to size their positions sensibly (why diversify out of an asset that's "secretly" worth triple?) and rode a 26% drawdown with no risk management, because the myth told them the loss wasn't real. The market, as always, did not care what they believed.

**The 2022 vindication of the diversifier.** Allocators who held gold as a boring 5–10% diversifier — not as a doom bet, not as an inflation play, just as the one line in the portfolio that moves differently — had the best year of their professional lives relative to peers in 2022. While the 60/40 crowd posted −16% and explained to clients why the "safe" bond sleeve had fallen 31%, the diversified allocator pointed to a gold sleeve that was flat and a total portfolio that lost meaningfully less. No prediction was required. The diversification did the work.

**The 2022 Bitcoin haven test, run live.** Plenty of investors entered 2022 believing Bitcoin was a hedge against the very monetary debasement they feared. They learned otherwise in real time: as the Fed hiked and risk assets fell, Bitcoin fell 64% and the FTX collapse in November wiped out those who held their "digital gold" on the wrong exchange. The investors who had treated gold as their crisis hedge and Bitcoin as a speculative growth bet — two different jobs, two different sleeves — were the ones who came through with their hedge intact.

The pattern across all four is the same. The myth is not a harmless misbelief. It is a wrong model of the asset, and a wrong model produces a wrong position, held for a wrong reason, sold at a wrong time. The corrections in this post are not pedantry. They are the difference between holding gold as insurance you understand and holding it as a story you will abandon the moment it stops flattering your prior.

## The takeaway

Step back and notice what all six corrections have in common. Every myth in this post is a failure to take one sentence seriously: gold is monetary insurance, not a compounding investment. The "perfect inflation hedge" myth expects insurance to track CPI; it doesn't, it answers to real rates over a decade. The "useless rock" myth expects insurance to pay a yield; it doesn't, it pays off in the crash. The "rigged market" myth expects an arbitraged global asset to be controllable; it isn't. The "doomer asset" myth mistakes a diversifier for a forecast of collapse. The "\$10,000 sure thing" myth expects an asset with −0.5% to +7.5% regime-dependent returns to be predictable; it isn't, so you size it instead. And the "Bitcoin replaced gold" myth judges a haven by its bull-market returns instead of its crisis behavior, where gold was −0.3% and Bitcoin was −64%.

So what do you actually *do* with this? Three things.

First, **stop expecting gold to behave like something it isn't.** It is not a growth asset, a yield instrument, a CPI tracker, or a price-target lottery ticket. It is the one line in your portfolio that is no one else's liability and that tends to hold when everything else falls together. Judge it by that job, and the disappointments and the conspiracy theories both evaporate.

Second, **hold it small and hold it on purpose.** The single most robust decision in gold is not a market call, it is a sizing call: a 5–15% sleeve, rebalanced, requires you to predict nothing and survives every regime. The myths that cost the most money — the inflation bet, the suppression belief, the \$10,000 dream — all flow from treating gold as a thing to be *right about* rather than a thing to be *positioned in*. Sizing beats prediction, every time, for an asset this regime-dependent.

Third, **remember the one number.** In 2022, the year that broke the 60/40 portfolio and tested every asset's claim to be a haven, gold was −0.3%. That figure is the calibrated truth at the center of this whole post. It is not exciting. It will never trend on social media. But it dismantles four myths at once — gold is not a useless rock, not only for doomers, not the same trade as Bitcoin, and it earned its place in the portfolio without anyone having to predict a price. The world is full of confident gold stories. The number is more useful than all of them.

Gold's price is, in the end, the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability. That vote is loud, emotional, and tribal — which is exactly why it breeds so much myth. The discipline is to ignore the noise, hold the insurance framing, and let the numbers, not the vibes, set the size of your bet.
