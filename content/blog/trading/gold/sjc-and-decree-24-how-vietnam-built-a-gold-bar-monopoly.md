> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In 2012 Vietnam passed Decree 24, which made the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) the sole producer of gold bars, anointed SJC as the only legal bar brand, and banned banks from taking gold deposits or making gold loans. The goal was to "de-goldize" the economy so gold would stop competing with the dong. It largely worked, and it created a captive, distorted market whose signature is the SJC premium.
>
> - **Before 2012, gold was a parallel currency.** Vietnamese banks paid interest on gold deposits and lent gold; households priced houses in gold; a weak, high-inflation dong was constantly dumped for gold and US dollars. Decree 24 set out to end all of that.
> - **The decree's core move was a monopoly.** Only the state may make gold bars, and only one brand (SJC) is legal. Every legal bar in the country now passes through one producer and one brand before it reaches a saver, which lets the state control supply and, indirectly, the price.
> - **It stabilized the financial system and killed the macro problem** of gold competing with the currency, but the monopoly cut domestic gold off from the world market. The result is a structural gap between the SJC price and the world price, the premium, which blew out to about 25% in 2024 before the SBV intervened.
> - The one number to remember: at the 2024 peak, one luong of SJC gold cost about **92.4 million VND (roughly \$3,630)** while the same gold valued at the world price was about **73 million VND (\$2,870)** — a gap of nearly **19.4 million VND (\$762)** that a Vietnamese buyer paid simply for the privilege of buying inside the monopoly.

On the morning of 10 May 2024, the screens at gold shops across Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City flashed a number that made even seasoned dealers wince: a single luong of SJC-branded gold bar was selling for **92.4 million VND**. Translate that into the world's money — at the prevailing exchange rate of about 25,450 dong to the dollar — and you get roughly **\$3,630** for one luong, or about **\$3,010 per troy ounce**. That same morning, gold on the world market traded around **\$2,380 per ounce**. Vietnamese buyers were paying nearly a *quarter more* than the rest of the planet for the identical, fungible, internationally-priced metal. There was no quality difference. There was no shortage of gold on Earth. The gap was pure policy.

How does a country end up with gold that is the most expensive on the planet? The answer is not greed, smuggling, or a quirk of taste. It is a single piece of legislation — **Decree 24/2012/ND-CP**, signed in April 2012 — that rewired the entire Vietnamese gold market on purpose. Before that decree, gold in Vietnam behaved like a second currency: banks took it on deposit and paid interest, lent it out, and let households save in it instead of the perpetually-shrinking dong. Decree 24 set out to end that arrangement. It handed the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) a monopoly on bar production, made one company's brand the only legal bar in the country, and banned the gold credit system outright. The macro goal was clean and defensible: stop gold from competing with the national currency. The side effect was a captive market whose distortions are still being unwound more than a decade later.

![Before and after Decree 24 showing many brands and gold lending replaced by an SJC monopoly](/imgs/blogs/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly-1.png)

This post is the policy architecture behind Vietnam's strange gold market. We will build it from zero: what the State Bank of Vietnam actually is, what "goldization" (in Vietnamese, *vang hoa*) means and why a government fears it, the pre-2012 chaos the decree was written to fix, exactly what Decree 24 did, the goals it pursued, what it achieved, the distortions it created, and the long-running pressure to reform it that came to a head in 2024 and 2025. The spine of this whole series is that **gold is monetary insurance — its price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money.** Vietnam's story is what happens when a state decides it can no longer afford to let its own citizens buy that insurance freely, and builds a wall around the metal instead.

## Foundations: the SBV, "goldization," and why a state fears gold

Before any of the policy makes sense, we need three ideas defined from scratch: what a central bank is and what the SBV in particular does, what it means for an economy to be "goldized," and why a government would treat its own citizens' love of gold as a problem worth legislating against.

### What the State Bank of Vietnam is

Every country with its own currency has a **central bank** — an institution that issues the national money, sets the cost of borrowing (interest rates), and tries to keep the currency stable and the banking system solvent. In the United States it is the Federal Reserve; in the eurozone, the European Central Bank; in Vietnam, it is the **State Bank of Vietnam (SBV)**.

There is one structural difference worth flagging up front, because it shapes everything that follows. The US Federal Reserve is *independent* of the elected government by law. The SBV is not. It is a **ministry-level agency of the Vietnamese government** — its governor sits in the cabinet, and its policy is an arm of state economic planning, not an independent counterweight to it. This matters enormously for gold. In the US, no agency has the legal authority to declare one private company the only legal maker of gold bars; here is a government body that *does* have exactly that kind of power, and in 2012 it used it.

The SBV's job, like any central bank's, is to defend the value and credibility of the national currency, the **dong** (abbreviated VND). In 2012, that job was extremely hard. To see why, we have to understand the thing the SBV was fighting.

### What "goldization" (vang hoa) means

A currency works only because people *agree to use it*. You accept dong for your labor because you trust you can spend them tomorrow at roughly the same value. The instant that trust frays — when people expect the dong to lose value faster than they can spend it — they start reaching for something that holds value better. In many countries that "something" is the US dollar; economists call a flight into dollars **dollarization**. In Vietnam, with its deep cultural attachment to the metal, it was also, and especially, gold. The Vietnamese term is *vang hoa* — literally "goldization."

A useful way to understand goldization is by what it does to the *functions of money*. Economists say money does three jobs: it is a **medium of exchange** (you pay with it), a **unit of account** (you quote prices in it), and a **store of value** (you save in it). In a healthy economy the national currency does all three. In a goldized economy, gold quietly takes over the jobs the currency is failing at. By the late 2000s in Vietnam, large transactions — apartments, land, sometimes even cars — were routinely *quoted and settled in luong of gold*, not dong. Savings were held in gold. The dong was demoted to small everyday purchases. The currency was being hollowed out from the inside.

A luong, recall, is Vietnam's traditional gold unit: **37.5 grams**, equal to **1.20565 troy ounces**. (The series' [pricing post](/blog/trading/gold/how-gold-is-priced-spot-the-london-fix-futures-and-the-troy-ounce) does the full unit grid; we lean on it here.) When a Hanoi family agreed to sell an apartment "for 50 luong," they were pricing the largest transaction of their lives in gold because they did not trust what 50 luong's worth of dong would be worth by the time the deal closed. That is goldization in one sentence.

It helps to compare goldization with its more familiar cousin, **dollarization**, because the two ran side by side in Vietnam and the government feared both. Dollarization is what happens in places like Argentina or Lebanon, where citizens lose faith in the local currency and start holding, pricing, and transacting in US dollars. Goldization is the same flight, but into metal rather than another country's paper. The two are not mutually exclusive — a 2010-era Vietnamese saver might keep some wealth in USD deposits (dollarization) and some in gold bars (goldization), with the dong reserved for the groceries. From the SBV's chair, both looked identical: large pools of national savings sitting in instruments the central bank could neither print, control, nor steer with interest rates. A currency that has lost the store-of-value job to *two* rivals at once is in genuine danger of losing the other two jobs as well, which is how a slow erosion of trust becomes a fast currency crisis.

The reason gold, specifically, is such a potent rival deserves a moment. Unlike a foreign currency, gold is no government's liability — it cannot be inflated away by *any* central bank, sanctioned, or frozen. For a population with living memory of war, hyperinflation, and multiple currency redenominations, that "answers to no one" quality is not a theory; it is the lesson of the twentieth century. So when the dong wobbled, the reach for gold was not speculation — it was a deeply rational, culturally reinforced flight to the one asset that had outlived every Vietnamese regime change. A government trying to rebuild trust in its paper money was therefore not fighting a fad. It was fighting five thousand years of accumulated reason to distrust paper, embodied in a metal its own citizens trusted more than the state's promises.

### Why a government fears gold competing with its currency

Here is the part that feels counterintuitive to a Western reader: why would a government *care* if its citizens save in gold? Isn't that their business? Three reasons, and they are the entire motive behind Decree 24.

**First, it strips the central bank of control.** A central bank steers the economy by adjusting the supply and price of its own money — cutting rates to stimulate, raising them to cool inflation. But that lever only works on money people actually use. If a large slice of the nation's savings sits in gold, which the SBV cannot print, cannot tax easily, and cannot set an interest rate on, then a chunk of the economy is simply *outside the central bank's reach*. Monetary policy becomes a lever attached to only part of the machine.

**Second, it drains foreign reserves.** Vietnam does not mine meaningful quantities of gold; almost every gram is imported. Every time a household converts dong savings into a gold bar, that demand ultimately pulls **US dollars** out of the country to pay for imported metal (or pulls dollars into the black market to fund smuggling, which we'll come to). A central bank with limited foreign-currency reserves cannot tolerate an open-ended drain of dollars into gold imports. Gold demand and FX-reserve defense are directly opposed.

**Third, it is a vote of no-confidence that becomes self-fulfilling.** When citizens flee the dong for gold and dollars, the dong weakens, which validates the flight, which weakens it further. A goldized, dollarized economy is one perpetually one shock away from a currency crisis. The SBV in 2011–2012 was staring at exactly that spiral.

So the state's logic was: *gold is acting as a rival currency, and a rival currency that I cannot control is a threat to monetary stability.* The question was what to do about it. The answer was Decree 24.

## The pre-2012 chaos: when gold was a second currency

To appreciate how radical Decree 24 was, you have to feel how wild the prior system was. Three features defined Vietnamese gold before 2012, and each was a slow-motion threat to the dong.

### Gold deposits and gold loans

The strangest feature, to modern eyes, is that Vietnamese commercial banks ran a full **gold banking** business. You could walk into a bank, hand over physical gold, and open a **gold deposit account** that paid *interest in gold*. The bank, in turn, could *lend gold* to borrowers — businesses and speculators who wanted gold exposure or who were betting on the gold price. There was a functioning interbank gold market and gold-denominated credit.

Think about what this means. Gold was not just a thing people hoarded; it was a financial asset *inside the banking system*, with its own yield curve and its own credit risk. A jewelry business might borrow 100 luong of gold, sell it for dong to fund operations, and owe 100 luong back — a naked short position on the gold price embedded in the banking system. When gold rose sharply (as it did into the 2011 peak), these borrowers were crushed, banks faced losses, and the SBV had to manage a tangle of gold-denominated liabilities sitting next to its dong-denominated ones. It was, in effect, a second monetary system bolted onto the first, with the central bank responsible for the stability of both.

The mechanics of why this was dangerous are worth spelling out, because the danger is exactly the kind that builds quietly and detonates at the worst moment. A bank that takes a gold deposit owes that gold back. If the bank simply vaults the gold, it earns nothing on it but still has to pay the depositor interest — a losing trade. So banks did what banks always do with a deposit: they *put it to work*. They sold the deposited gold for dong, lent the dong out, and ran an open obligation to return gold they no longer held. As long as the gold price was stable and depositors did not all want their metal back at once, the carry was profitable. But this is a textbook **maturity-and-currency mismatch**: the bank's liability was in gold, its assets were in dong loans, and the price of gold could move violently against it. When gold spiked into 2011, the dong value of the gold these banks owed ballooned far faster than their dong assets, opening losses that threatened solvency. The SBV found itself backstopping a shadow gold-banking system it had never fully intended to guarantee — a system that could transmit a *gold-price* shock straight into a *banking* crisis. Dismantling that linkage, not the monopoly, was arguably the most consequential thing Decree 24 ever did, and it is the part economists most consistently judge a success.

#### Worked example: the interest a saver gave up when gold deposits were banned

Picture a Ho Chi Minh City family in 2011 holding **5 luong** of gold — a typical mid-sized household stash. At the time they could deposit it at a bank earning, say, **5% per year in gold**. At a gold value of about 74 million VND per luong, that holding was worth **370 million VND (about \$14,500)** at a 25,450 dong/dollar rate. The interest stream was therefore roughly:

- 370 million VND × 5% = **18.5 million VND per year**, or about **\$727**.

After Decree 24 banned gold deposits, that 18.5 million VND (\$727) of annual yield simply vanished. The family's gold still sat in a safe, but it now paid *nothing* — exactly as physical gold pays nothing everywhere else in the world. The decree did not lower the gold price; it abolished the *yield* gold used to earn, which is one of the quiet ways it pushed savers back toward interest-bearing dong deposits.

### Multiple bar brands and a fragmented market

Before 2012 there was no single official gold bar. Several companies — SJC, PNJ, Bao Tin Minh Chau, AAA, and others — each minted and sold their own branded bars. SJC (the Saigon Jewelry Company, a state-owned enterprise under the Ho Chi Minh City government) was the largest and most trusted, holding an estimated 90%-plus of the bar market by reputation, but it was not a *legal* monopoly. The market was fragmented, with each brand trading at slightly different prices and liquidity, and quality assurance varied.

A fragmented, multi-brand market sounds chaotic, but notice one thing about it: because brands *competed*, and because gold could be freely imported and fabricated, domestic prices stayed close to the world price. Competition is what keeps a premium small. Remember that, because the monopoly that replaced this fragmentation is precisely what let the premium balloon.

### A weak dong and chronic instability

The backdrop to all of this was a genuinely fragile dong. In 2011, Vietnamese inflation ran above **18%**. The dong was repeatedly devalued. Foreign reserves were thin. The economy was both heavily dollarized (large deposits and prices in USD) and heavily goldized. The trade deficit was wide, and capital was prone to flight. In that environment, the rational move for any Vietnamese saver was exactly what the government feared: get out of dong, into gold and dollars. The currency was losing the store-of-value contest to the metal, in the open, every day.

This is the crucial framing of the entire story, and it ties straight back to the series' spine. **A high SJC premium, a goldized economy, and a weak dong are all the same phenomenon: the world (and Vietnam's own citizens) voting against the paper currency.** Decree 24 was an attempt to suppress the visible symptom — gold competing with the dong — by law.

## What Decree 24 actually did

Decree 24/2012/ND-CP, "on the management of gold trading activities," took effect in 2012. Strip away the legal language and it did four concrete things.

![The monopoly chain from the State Bank to SJC to dealers to households](/imgs/blogs/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly-3.png)

**1. The SBV became the sole producer of gold bars.** The decree declared the *production of gold bars* a state monopoly. Private companies could no longer mint bars. The State Bank of Vietnam alone holds the right to organize bar production and to manage the import and export of raw gold for that purpose. In one stroke, the supply of new legal gold bars came under direct state control.

**2. SJC became the only legal bar brand.** Of all the existing brands, the SBV chose **SJC** as the national gold-bar brand. From 2012 onward, the only gold bar with full legal standing — the one banks and shops would freely buy back at the headline price — was the SJC bar. The bars of every other former brand were demoted overnight: still gold, still valuable, but no longer the official instrument. Many holders converted their non-SJC bars to SJC (paying a conversion fee and queuing to do so), which cemented SJC's dominance from ~90% by reputation to ~100% by law.

It is worth dwelling on a subtlety here. The SBV owns the *brand and the monopoly*; SJC the company became, in effect, the state's contracted manufacturer. The bars say "SJC," but the right to make them flows from the central bank. This is why people talk about "the SBV monopoly" and "the SJC monopoly" interchangeably — they are two faces of the same arrangement, as the chain above shows: one state producer, one brand, then the dealers and households.

The conversion period that followed was its own small drama and reveals how brand monopoly translates into market power. Owners of the now-demoted brands — the PNJ, Bao Tin, and AAA bars that had been perfectly good gold the week before — faced a choice: keep a bar that shops would now buy back only at a *discount* (because it was no longer the freely-tradable instrument), or pay to convert it into an SJC bar. Conversion meant queuing, a fee, and sometimes weeks of waiting, and during periods when SJC fabrication capacity was constrained, the conversion premium itself widened. A holder of a non-SJC bar could watch their gold trade several percent below the SJC price for no reason other than the letters stamped on it. That spread — same metal, different brand, different price — was the first visible sign of what a brand monopoly does: it makes the *label*, not the metal, the scarce thing. Every distortion that came later is a larger version of that same effect.

**3. Gold deposits and gold loans were banned.** The decree (alongside related SBV circulars phased in over 2011–2013) dismantled the entire gold-banking system. Banks were ordered to stop taking gold on deposit and to stop lending gold, and to wind down their existing gold-denominated positions. Gold was forcibly *demonetized within the banking system* — pushed out of the credit machine and back to being an inert lump in a safe. This is arguably the most important part of the decree for financial stability, even though the monopoly gets more attention.

**4. The physical metal trade was licensed and constrained, while jewelry stayed freer.** Buying and selling *gold bars* was restricted to licensed institutions; ordinary shops could no longer freely trade bars. Crucially, though, the decree drew a line between *bars* (tightly controlled) and *jewelry and gold rings* (much more loosely regulated). This distinction matters enormously in practice — it is why a Vietnamese saver today often buys *nhan* (plain gold rings) or jewelry as a back-door way to own near-pure gold without touching the monopoly bar, a theme the [practical-ownership post](/blog/trading/gold/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price) and its siblings develop. And critically, **private import of gold was effectively shut off**: only the SBV could authorize bringing raw gold into the country. That import valve, closed, is the technical root of the premium.

Put those four moves together and you have a market that looks nothing like a normal one. One producer. One brand. No gold credit. A closed import valve. The state holding every important lever. The chart of the SJC bar price over the years that followed is the story of that machine under pressure.

## The goals: de-goldization and a stable dong

Why go this far? Decree 24 was not an accident or an overreach by an unaccountable agency; it was a deliberate strategy with named objectives. The umbrella term the government used was **anti-goldization** (*chong vang hoa*) — a campaign to drive gold out of its role as a rival currency and reassert the dong as the country's only money. Underneath that umbrella sat three concrete goals.

![The three goals of de-goldization feeding into a stable dong](/imgs/blogs/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly-5.png)

**Goal 1: stop gold competing with the dong.** This is the headline. By ending gold deposits and gold loans, the state cut gold out of the financial system entirely. You could still *own* gold, but you could no longer *earn* on it inside a bank, and you could no longer easily use it as a financing instrument. The deliberate effect was to make holding gold *boring* — a yieldless brick — while making dong deposits attractive by comparison (they paid real interest, and the dong was being actively stabilized). Over the following years, large-value transactions migrated back to dong, and the practice of pricing apartments in luong faded. On this measure, the decree largely succeeded.

**Goal 2: channel savings into the banking system.** A modern economy needs savings to flow *through banks* so they can be lent to businesses and fund investment. Gold sitting in a household safe is "dead" capital — it funds nothing. By making gold yieldless and dong deposits attractive, the state nudged the nation's savings out of inert metal and into the banking system, where it could be intermediated into productive credit. This is the same logic behind why goldization is a development problem, not just a monetary one: a country that saves in gold is starving its own banks of deposits.

**Goal 3: protect FX reserves.** Because gold is imported with dollars, an open-ended gold-buying frenzy is a steady leak of foreign reserves and downward pressure on the dong. By closing the import valve and making the SBV the only legal importer, the state could *throttle* how much hard currency leaked out to buy metal. The cost of this — and it is the central trade-off of the whole policy — is that throttling imports while domestic demand stays strong is exactly what forces the domestic price above the world price.

All three goals point at one target: **a stable, trusted dong.** That is the thread connecting every part of Decree 24. The decree was never really about gold for its own sake; gold was collateral damage in a war to rehabilitate the national currency.

#### Worked example: the FX a single luong of imported gold pulls out of the country

To make Goal 3 concrete, follow the dollars behind one luong. The metal in one luong is 1.20565 troy ounces. At a world gold price of, say, **\$2,380/oz**, the raw metal alone costs:

- 1.20565 oz × \$2,380 = **\$2,869** of foreign currency per luong imported.

If Vietnamese households wanted to buy, hypothetically, an extra **10 tonnes** of gold in a year through legal imports — modest against the country's ~50 tonnes of annual demand — that is about **267,000 luong**, costing roughly:

- 267,000 × \$2,869 ≈ **\$766 million** of foreign reserves, drained to import inert metal.

For a central bank guarding a finite reserve buffer, that is a serious number — and it is *recurring*, year after year, as long as the goldization habit persists. Closing the legal import valve is how the SBV stopped that bleed; the premium is the price the domestic market pays for the closed valve.

## What it achieved, and the distortions it created

Judged against its own goals, Decree 24 was, for its first decade, a qualified success — and it is important to give the policy its due before cataloguing its costs, because the costs are easy to see and the benefits are the kind that show up as *crises that didn't happen*. The gold-banking system was dismantled without a banking crisis. The dong stabilized markedly: headline inflation fell from above **18% in 2011** to the low single digits through much of the mid-2010s, the currency's annual depreciation against the dollar slowed to a managed crawl, and the practice of pricing apartments and land in luong faded from common life. Financial-system goldization — gold sitting *inside* banks as deposits and loans — was genuinely killed and never came back. If you measure the policy by "did gold stop being a systemic rival to the dong," the answer is largely yes.

That success is the reason the decree survived as long as it did despite its obvious distortions. A government weighing the visible cost (a fat premium on a luxury-cum-savings good) against the invisible benefit (no repeat of the 2011 currency-and-gold spiral) reasonably concluded, year after year, that the trade was worth it. The premium was a price the state was willing to make its citizens pay for monetary stability — a quiet, regressive tax justified by a macro emergency. The argument for *keeping* the wall only weakened when the emergency receded and the wall's own side effects (the gray market, the FX leakage it caused) started to threaten the very stability it had bought.

But every wall has a far side. The very mechanism that achieved stability — a monopoly with a closed import valve — produced a set of distortions that grew more glaring as the global gold price climbed. Three stand out.

### Distortion 1: the SJC premium

This is the headline distortion and the signature of the whole system. In a normal, open gold market, the domestic price tracks the world price closely, separated only by import duties, fabrication, and a thin dealer margin — typically **1–2%**. The arbitrage is simple: if the domestic price rose above the world price plus costs, importers would bring in cheap world gold and sell it domestically until the gap closed.

To see why arbitrage is the thing that normally keeps domestic and world gold glued together, run the trade in your head. Imagine domestic gold is trading 10% above the world price in an *open* market. A trader buys gold abroad at the world price, ships it in, pays the import duty and a fabrication cost, and sells it domestically at the 10% premium. As long as the premium exceeds those costs, the trade is pure profit, so traders do it again and again — importing until the extra domestic supply pushes the domestic price back down toward the world price plus costs. The act of arbitrage *is* the mechanism that closes the gap. It is automatic, self-correcting, and it requires only one thing: the freedom to import.

Decree 24 *broke that arbitrage* by closing the legal import valve. With no legal way to bring world gold in and sell it domestically, the self-correcting trade simply cannot run. Domestic SJC supply became fixed by SBV decisions, while domestic demand kept rising with the world gold price and with every bout of dong anxiety. Fixed supply meeting rising demand has only one outcome: the domestic price detaches from the world price and floats *above* it, and nothing pulls it back. The gap is the **SJC premium** — and it is precisely as large as the arbitrage that *would* have closed it, if only the arbitrage were legal. By 2023 it sat around **18%**; in early-to-mid 2024 it blew out to roughly **25%** — the widest in the world — before the SBV's intervention crushed it. The premium, in other words, is a direct read-out of how much the closed valve is worth: it measures the size of the trade the law forbids.

![The SJC premium in cash showing the 2024 blow-out and the post-intervention narrowing](/imgs/blogs/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly-4.png)

#### Worked example: the premium as a hidden tax on a single luong

This is the most important calculation in the post, because it converts an abstract policy into the dong a real buyer paid. Take the 10 May 2024 peak.

- **SJC bar price:** 92.4 million VND per luong. At 25,450 dong/dollar, that is **\$3,631 per luong**, or **\$3,011 per troy ounce** (divide by 1.20565).
- **World-equivalent price:** world gold was about \$2,380/oz. One luong of world gold is therefore 1.20565 × \$2,380 = **\$2,869**, which at 25,450 dong/dollar is about **73 million VND per luong**.
- **The premium:** 92.4 − 73.0 = **19.4 million VND per luong**, which is about **\$762**.

So a Vietnamese buyer at the peak paid an extra **19.4 million VND (\$762)** on top of what that exact gold was worth anywhere else on Earth. That is not a fee, a tax line, or a quoted spread — it is an *invisible* surcharge baked into the only legal price, the direct consequence of a closed-import monopoly. A family buying a luong as a wedding gift was, without anyone telling them, paying a roughly **27% markup over the world price** for the privilege of buying inside the system. The premium is the tax the monopoly levies on every bar, and it falls hardest on exactly the ordinary savers the rest of the policy claimed to protect.

### Distortion 2: a captive market

The second distortion is structural. Because there is one brand, one producer, and (until reform) no legal way to import, Vietnamese gold buyers are a **captive market**. They cannot vote with their feet by buying a cheaper brand or importing their own gold; the only legal bar is SJC, at whatever price the constrained domestic supply produces. Competition — the force that keeps premiums small everywhere else — was legislated away.

A captive market is wonderful for whoever sits at the chokepoint and terrible for the people downstream of it. The state captured the chokepoint to control the macro picture; households inherited the higher price. This is the deep irony of the decree: a policy framed as protecting savers from a collapsing currency ended up charging those same savers a steep, permanent premium to access the very asset they were fleeing to.

#### Worked example: a saver's all-in cost of the monopoly on one luong

Add up what the monopoly actually costs a household that buys, holds, and later sells one luong of SJC gold, separate from any move in the gold price itself. Take a buyer at a moderate premium of, say, 18% (the 2023 norm), on a world-equivalent luong worth about 73 million VND:

- **Premium paid on entry:** 73 million × 18% = **13.1 million VND**, about **\$515** at 25,450 dong/dollar.
- **Bid-ask spread on the bar:** SJC bars carry a buy-sell spread that widens with stress — call it a further **2 million VND (\$79)** round-trip in normal conditions, much more in a panic.
- **Forgone deposit interest:** because the metal is yieldless and dong deposits paid, say, 5%, holding 73 million VND in gold for one year instead of a deposit gives up about 73 million × 5% = **3.65 million VND (\$143)** of interest per year of holding.

So before gold moves a single dong, a one-year round trip in one luong of SJC costs the saver on the order of **13.1 + 2.0 + 3.65 ≈ 18.8 million VND, about \$735**, of which the lion's share is the monopoly premium. That is the true price of buying monetary insurance *inside the wall*. A saver who instead bought the same 9999 gold as rings near the world price would skip most of the 13.1 million VND premium entirely — which is exactly why so many Vietnamese households do.

### Distortion 3: policy risk baked into the bar

The third distortion is the subtlest and, for a sophisticated saver, the most important. When the state holds every lever, **the price of SJC gold carries political risk that physical gold elsewhere does not.** The whole point of gold, per this series' spine, is to be no one's liability and no one's policy variable. But an SJC bar's *premium* is entirely a creature of SBV policy — and the SBV can, and did, decide to crush it.

In June 2024, the SBV began selling gold directly to the public through four state banks and SJC at a price designed to drag the premium down. The SJC bar fell from the 92.4 million VND peak to around **76.98 million VND** within weeks — a drop of about **15.4 million VND (\$606) per luong, roughly 17%** — even as the *world* gold price barely moved. Anyone who had bought at the peak watched a sixth of their money evaporate not because gold fell, but because policy changed.

![What Decree 24 changed for a Vietnamese saver across yield brand price and policy risk](/imgs/blogs/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly-6.png)

#### Worked example: the policy-risk discount embedded in an SJC bar

Suppose you bought one luong at the 92.4 million VND peak, expecting it to track world gold. Within weeks, SBV intervention compressed the premium and the SJC price fell to about 76.98 million VND, while world gold was roughly flat.

- **Your loss from policy alone:** 92.4 − 76.98 = **15.4 million VND**, about **\$606 per luong**, or **−17%**.
- **What world gold did over the same span:** essentially nothing.

Your entire loss came from the *premium* compressing, not from gold. That gap between "what I paid" and "what the metal is worth at the world price" is a **policy-risk discount**: the part of the SJC price that exists only because of the monopoly and that the state can choose to remove at will. The lesson for a Vietnamese saver is sharp — when you buy SJC at a fat premium, a slice of your money is a bet that the SBV will *not* intervene. That is precisely the kind of policy dependence gold is supposed to free you from.

### Distortion 4: the premium summons a gray market

The fourth distortion is the one the policy could least afford, because it quietly undoes Goal 3. A fat, persistent premium is not just an annoyance to buyers — it is a **profit signal flashing across the border**. When domestic gold trades 20–25% above the world price, anyone who can get world-price gold *into* the country and sell it domestically pockets that gap. The legal import valve is closed, so this arbitrage cannot happen through the front door. It happens through the back door: smuggling.

The arithmetic that drives it is irresistible. Bring in one kilogram of gold bought at the world price and sell it into a domestic market trading 20% richer, and the gross arbitrage is one-fifth of a kilo of gold's value — tens of thousands of dollars per kilogram, before the smuggler's costs and risk. That margin funds a sophisticated cross-border trade in metal carried over land borders, and — this is the part that matters for the dong — it has to be *paid for in US dollars on the black market*. To buy world gold abroad, smugglers need dollars, and they get them by bidding for dollars in Vietnam's parallel FX market. So the premium created by closing the import valve, intended to *protect* FX reserves (Goal 3), ends up generating black-market dollar demand that pressures the dong from a different direction. The wall did not stop the dollars from leaving; it just rerouted them through a channel the central bank could see even less clearly. (The full gold-dollar-dong triangle and the border flows get their own treatment in a sibling post; here the point is simply that *a premium is a smuggling subsidy*, and a smuggling subsidy is an FX leak — the exact thing Goal 3 set out to plug.)

This is the trap at the heart of the whole design. The premium is simultaneously the *evidence* that the monopoly is binding and the *force* that erodes one of the monopoly's own goals. The wider the premium, the stronger the incentive to route around the wall, and the more the wall's protection of reserves leaks away. By 2024 this feedback loop had become loud enough that the government concluded the cure had grown more expensive than the disease.

## The pressure to reform: 2024 and 2025

By 2024 the contradictions had become impossible to ignore. The world gold price was surging toward record highs, dragging the SJC price up with it; the premium had blown out to the widest on the planet; and the gap was fueling a thriving gold smuggling trade and putting fresh pressure on the dong through the black-market dollar channel (the gold-dollar-dong triangle that a sibling post explores in detail). The decree that had stabilized the dong in 2012 was, by 2024, *destabilizing* it through the very premium the monopoly created. The cure had developed its own disease.

The government's response came in two waves. **First, the 2024 direct-sale intervention.** Rather than reopening imports, the SBV initially tried bullion auctions (echoing a 2013 episode), then switched to selling gold directly to the public through four big state-owned banks (Vietcombank, VietinBank, BIDV, Agribank) and SJC at a controlled price. The aim was to flood enough supply at a capped price to drag the premium down without burning through reserves. It worked, partially and temporarily: the premium fell from ~25% to single digits within months, as the price chart's mid-2024 trough shows. (The mechanics of this intervention get their own [SBV-policy post](/blog/trading/gold/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization).)

**Second, the structural reform of Decree 24 itself.** Through 2024 and into 2025, the government signaled and then moved to *amend* the decree's core. The direction of travel: break the SJC monopoly by allowing additional licensed producers and brands, permit qualified businesses to import gold (reopening the valve under quota), and shrink the premium toward a policy-target band of roughly **3–5%** rather than the double-digit norm. The logic of reform is exactly the inverse of the logic of the original decree: *competition and a reopened import valve are how you keep a premium small.* What 2012 closed to stabilize the dong, 2025 began to reopen — because by then the dong was strong enough that the cure's side effects had become the bigger problem.

Notice what made reform *possible*, because it is the whole point. In 2012 the dong was fragile, inflation was near 20%, and goldization was a live systemic threat — the wall was a defensible emergency measure. By 2024–25 the situation had inverted: inflation was tamed, the dong was far steadier, and the gold-banking system was long dismantled. The macro emergency the decree was written for had passed. What remained was the *cost* of the wall — the premium, the captive market, the smuggling subsidy — with the emergency that justified it gone. Reform is what you do when a temporary defense outlives the attack it was built to repel. The hard question the reform must answer is one of *sequencing*: reopen imports too fast and you risk reigniting an FX drain; reopen too slowly and the premium and its gray market persist. The whole craft of unwinding a market intervention is calibrating that valve so the premium deflates without the dollars stampeding out — which is exactly why the SBV chose controlled direct sales and quota-bound imports rather than simply flinging the door open.

![The SJC premium as a percent over the world price](/imgs/blogs/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly-7.png)

The percentage chart above is the clearest single picture of the whole arc: a structurally elevated premium (well above the 1–2% a competitive market would show), a violent 2024 spike to ~25%, and a deliberate policy-driven compression back toward single digits. Every wiggle in that line is a policy decision, not a market one. That is the defining feature of a managed market — and the reason a Vietnamese gold buyer has to think about *policy*, not just *price*.

## Common misconceptions

The Vietnamese gold market generates more folk wisdom than almost any other corner of finance. Three myths in particular are worth correcting with numbers, because believing them costs real money.

**Misconception 1: "The monopoly protects buyers."** The framing that the SJC monopoly guarantees quality and protects ordinary buyers is exactly backward. A monopoly with a closed import valve *raises* the price buyers pay — that is the entire mechanism of the premium. At the 2024 peak, the "protection" cost buyers about **19.4 million VND (\$762) per luong** versus the world price. Monopoly does not protect a buyer; it captures them. What protects buyers is *competition*, which is precisely what the decree removed and what the 2025 reform aims to restore.

**Misconception 2: "SJC gold is purer or better gold."** It is not. An SJC bar is 9999 (four-nines, 99.99%) gold — the same purity as a Good Delivery bar in London, a Maple Leaf coin, or a 9999 gold ring sold across the street. There is no metallurgical difference. The SJC premium pays for *legal status and brand monopoly*, not quality. This is why savvy Vietnamese savers often buy plain gold rings (*nhan*), which are the same 9999 gold but trade at a far smaller premium because they sit outside the bar monopoly. You are paying up for a brand the law made special, not for better metal.

**Misconception 3: "Decree 24 killed gold demand in Vietnam."** Also false. Vietnamese consumer gold demand stayed robust right through the decree era — roughly **50–60 tonnes per year** across jewelry, bars, and coins, keeping Vietnam among the highest per-capita gold consumers on Earth. The decree did not extinguish the *appetite* for gold; the cultural and economic drivers behind it (covered in the [Vietnam culture post](/blog/trading/gold/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal)) are far too deep for a decree to erase. What it changed was the *form*: demand shifted from gold-banking and free bars toward jewelry, rings, and the gray market. Suppress the legal channel and demand does not vanish — it reroutes. This is a general law of capital controls worth carrying out of this post: prohibitions rarely destroy the demand they target; they relocate it to a channel that is harder to see and usually more expensive for the citizen.

**Misconception 4: "The premium means SJC gold is a better investment, because it's worth more."** This one inverts cause and effect, and it can ruin a saver. The premium does not make SJC gold *worth* more in any economic sense — it makes it *cost* more to buy and is the part of the price most likely to evaporate. A buyer who pays a 25% premium has handed over a quarter of their money for something that exists only by decree and that the SBV crushed by ~17% in weeks in mid-2024. The premium is a liability you carry, not an asset you own. The correct mental model is the reverse of the myth: *the higher the premium, the worse the entry, because you are paying the most for the part of the price with the least durability.* Buy gold for its world-price metal value; treat any premium as a cost to minimize, never a feature to pay up for.

## How it shows up in real markets: the premium history

The cleanest way to *see* Decree 24 at work is to watch the premium over time, because the premium is the market's running scorecard of how binding the monopoly's supply constraint is at any moment.

There was an early dress rehearsal worth noting. Shortly after the decree took effect, in 2013, the SBV ran a series of **gold-bar auctions** — selling state gold into the market to narrow the gap that the new monopoly had opened. It was the first admission, barely a year in, that the closed valve produced a premium the state would have to actively manage rather than ignore. The 2013 auctions trimmed the gap temporarily but did not resolve the structural cause, and over the following decade the premium settled into a persistently elevated range. The 2024 direct-sale program was, in effect, the 2013 playbook run again at far larger scale and under far more public pressure — which is itself a tell that the distortion was structural, not a one-off.

In the relatively calm years after 2012, with the world gold price range-bound and the dong stabilizing, the premium was elevated but manageable — gold buyers grumbled about paying a few percent over the world price, but the gap rarely became a national issue. The story changed when the world gold price began its powerful 2023–2026 surge (the [post-2011 regime post](/blog/trading/gold/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime) covers the global drivers — central-bank buying, real-rate dynamics, de-dollarization). A rising world price is the stress test of a capped-supply market: when the underlying climbs and demand intensifies but legal supply cannot expand, the premium is the pressure gauge that spikes. That is exactly what 2024 was — the monopoly's supply constraint meeting the strongest global gold bull market in a generation.

As world gold climbed, Vietnamese demand surged with it, but legal supply stayed throttled by the closed import valve. The premium, which had hovered around 18% in 2023, exploded to roughly **25% by May 2024** — the SJC bar hitting 92.4 million VND while the world-equivalent sat near 73 million. That ~19 million VND (\$762) gap per luong became front-page news and a genuine policy embarrassment: the country's gold was the most expensive on the planet, the gap was funding smuggling, and the black-market dollar demand behind the smuggling was leaking back into pressure on the dong.

Then came the intervention. From June 2024, the SBV's direct-sale program dragged the SJC price down to ~77 million VND and compressed the premium to single digits — about **6%** by September 2024 and as low as **4–5%** through 2025 as the reform direction firmed up. Note the crucial detail in the price chart: the SJC price *fell sharply* in mid-2024 while the world price did **not** fall. The entire move was premium compression — the monopoly's distortion being squeezed out by policy, not a change in the value of gold. By the 2026-01 all-time high, the SJC bar reached about **190.3 million VND per luong** (roughly \$7,240, or about \$6,000/oz at a 26,300 dong/dollar rate), but now riding a *narrow* premium on top of a genuinely sky-high world price, rather than the fat monopoly premium of 2024.

#### Worked example: how to read the SJC price into a world price

A practical skill for any Vietnamese gold watcher is to translate the SJC headline number into its world-price equivalent, so you can see the premium yourself. Take the 2026-01 record of **190.3 million VND per luong**, with the dong at about 26,300 to the dollar:

- **In dollars per luong:** 190.3 million ÷ 26,300 = **\$7,236 per luong**.
- **In dollars per troy ounce:** \$7,236 ÷ 1.20565 = **\$6,002 per ounce**.
- **Compare to world gold** of roughly \$5,500–5,600/oz at the time: the implied premium is about **\$400–500/oz, or 7–9%** — elevated, but a fraction of 2024's ~25%.

Run that arithmetic on any day's SJC quote and you have an instant read on how binding the monopoly is right now. When the implied premium widens, the supply constraint is biting and policy pressure is building; when it narrows toward a few percent, the reform and intervention are working. The premium is the single most informative number in the entire Vietnamese gold market — and it exists only because of Decree 24.

## The takeaway: a monopoly is a policy bet wearing a gold bar

Strip away the detail and Decree 24 is a study in trade-offs that every reader of this series should internalize, because it generalizes far beyond Vietnam.

The decree achieved something real and hard: it broke a dangerous goldization spiral, dismantled a precarious gold-banking system without a crisis, and helped rehabilitate a currency that was losing the store-of-value contest to a metal. Those are serious accomplishments, and they came from the state seizing the one chokepoint that mattered — the production and import of gold bars. If you only measure "did the dong stabilize," the policy worked.

But it worked by *converting a market problem into a policy problem*, and that conversion never fully disappears — it just changes shape. The premium, the captive market, the gray-market FX leak, and the policy risk baked into every SJC bar are all the same thing viewed from different angles: the price of replacing a free market with a managed one. A market problem is self-correcting — arbitrage closes a gap whether anyone is watching or not. A policy problem requires someone to keep making the right decision, forever, with the political will to bear the costs of each choice. That is the hidden burden Decree 24 took on. The moment the state captured the chokepoint, it also assumed permanent responsibility for the price, the premium, and every distortion they spawn — a burden it is only now, through reform, trying to hand back to the market. The deep lesson connects straight to this series' spine. **Gold's entire value proposition is that it is no one's liability and no one's policy variable** — the insurance you hold precisely *because* you do not trust paper money and the institutions behind it. An SJC bar inverts that. Its premium is a pure creature of state policy; a sixth of its peak-2024 value could be, and was, erased by an SBV decision while world gold sat still. The Vietnamese saver who buys SJC at a fat premium is, in a quiet way, *re-introducing the very policy dependence that gold is supposed to escape.*

That is why the smartest response to the monopoly, for a Vietnamese household, has been to route around it — to hold the same 9999 gold as rings and jewelry that trade near the world price, capturing gold's monetary insurance without paying the monopoly's tax or wearing its policy risk. And it is why the 2025 reform matters beyond Vietnam: reopening imports and breaking the single-brand monopoly is, at bottom, an admission that you cannot permanently wall a market off from the world price without making your own citizens pay for the wall. The premium was always the bill for the wall. Decree 24's real legacy is the proof that when a state tries to suppress gold's vote of no-confidence by decree, it does not erase the vote — it just moves it into a premium, a gray market, and a number every gold shop in the country prints on the wall each morning.
