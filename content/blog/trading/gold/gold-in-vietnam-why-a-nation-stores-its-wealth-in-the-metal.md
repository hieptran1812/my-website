> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnam's devotion to gold looks like tradition but is actually one of the world's most rational savings cultures, forged by a century of currency trauma; gold (vang) is the household's hard currency.
>
> - Vietnamese gold is measured in luong (tael) — a fixed weight of 37.5 grams, equal to 1.20565 troy ounces — and in chi, one-tenth of a luong. People save in it, gift it at weddings, and queue to buy it on Than Tai day.
> - The devotion is rooted in memory, not myth: war, the post-1975 hyperinflation that wiped out savers, and a dong that has slid from roughly 23,170 to about 26,300 per dollar in just seven years taught families that paper money is the thing that fails.
> - Gold does four jobs at once for a household — long-term savings, dowry and inheritance, emergency liquidity you can sell at any shop, and visible status — in one portable, divisible asset that is nobody else's promise to pay.
> - Vietnam buys roughly **50 tonnes of gold a year** for a population of ~100 million, putting it among the highest per-capita private gold demand on earth.
> - **The one number to remember:** at the 2024 peak of about 92.4 million VND per luong — roughly **\$3,600** of stored value in a coin you can hold in your palm — a household's modest 10-luong nest egg was worth around 920 million VND, or about **\$35,000**.

Every year, on the tenth day of the first lunar month, something happens in Vietnamese cities that bewilders foreign visitors. Hours before dawn, lines form outside gold shops. By the time the shutters roll up, the queues stretch around the block — office workers in suits, grandmothers with handbags, young couples, all waiting patiently to buy a small amount of gold. They are not investors timing a trade. Many will buy just one chi — about 3.75 grams, a sliver the size of a coat button. They are observing Than Tai day, the day of the God of Wealth, when buying gold is believed to bring a prosperous year.

To an outsider, it looks like superstition dressed up as a shopping holiday. A nation lining up to buy a non-yielding metal because a folk deity supposedly smiles on it — what could be less rational? But spend any time with the families in those queues and a different picture emerges. The grandmother holding her place in line lived through years when the money in her pocket lost half its value before she could spend it. The young couple are starting the savings habit their parents drilled into them: a little gold, bought steadily, kept at home, trusted more than any bank. The ritual is the wrapper. Inside it is one of the most clear-eyed savings cultures on the planet, built by people who learned the hard way that paper money is the thing that fails.

This post is about that culture — what it is, where it came from, and why it is rational rather than quaint. It is the foundation of this series' Vietnam track. Before we get into the policy machinery (the SJC monopoly, the State Bank's interventions, the premium, the gray market) that the later posts cover, we need to understand the demand that all of it is built on: why, in a fast-modernizing, smartphone-saturated, increasingly middle-income country, so many households still keep a meaningful slice of their net worth in metal stored in a drawer.

![Why Vietnamese households store wealth in gold trust ladder from dong to property to gold](/imgs/blogs/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal-1.png)

The figure above is the whole argument in one picture, and we will spend the rest of the post filling it in. A Vietnamese family trying to store its wealth has tried the obvious paper options and watched each one fail in its own way. Cash dong erodes as the currency slides. A bank deposit often pays less than true inflation, and trust in the banking system is thin after generations of instability. Property holds value but is enormous, illiquid, and impossible to divide — you cannot sell half a house to pay for a wedding next month. At the bottom of the ladder sits the asset that solved all of those problems before any of them existed: gold, held in luong, portable, divisible into chi, owing nothing to anyone. That is the destination this whole culture climbs down to, and it got there for reasons, not by accident.

This series' spine runs straight through Vietnam more cleanly than almost anywhere else on earth. The thesis is that gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability. In most rich countries that "vote" is cast by reserve managers and macro funds. In Vietnam it is cast by tens of millions of ordinary households, one chi at a time, and the no-confidence is in their own currency. If you want to see the spine of this entire series operating not as an abstraction but as a lived, daily, household-level practice, Vietnam is the place to look.

## Foundations: the luong, the dong, and why a country distrusts its own paper money

Let me build this from zero. To understand Vietnamese gold you need three things: the unit it is measured in, the currency it is measured against, and the history that set the two against each other in people's minds.

### The luong (tael) and the chi

In most of the world gold is quoted in troy ounces and grams. In Vietnam — and across much of East Asia — the traditional unit is the **luong** (also romanized *luong*, and called *cay* in the south), known in English as the **tael**. A luong is a fixed weight: **37.5 grams**, which works out to **1.20565 troy ounces**. That conversion factor matters and we will use it repeatedly, because the world quotes gold in dollars per troy ounce while Vietnam quotes it in millions of dong per luong, and to compare the two you have to bridge both the currency and the weight.

A luong divides into ten **chi** (*chi*), so one chi is 3.75 grams — about the weight of a small ring. The chi is the everyday retail unit: a wedding gift might be a few chi, a Than Tai day lucky purchase is typically one chi, and a serious saver accumulates luong over years. Finer divisions exist (a *phan* is a tenth of a chi) but in practice chi and luong are the units that matter.

![The luong tael unit and the gold buying occasions Tet weddings Than Tai day](/imgs/blogs/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal-3.png)

The reference figure above lays out the unit on one side and the buying occasions on the other; keep it in mind as we go, because the whole market runs on these few weights and these few dates. The genius of the luong/chi system, from a household's point of view, is divisibility. A 37.5-gram luong is a serious store of value, but if you need cash you do not have to sell the whole thing — you sell one chi, then another, the way you might withdraw from a savings account. Gold is the rare physical asset that is both compact enough to hide and divisible enough to spend in small pieces. Property has the first quality and not the second; cash has the second and not the first. Gold has both.

#### Worked example: what one luong is worth, in dong and in dollars

Take the 2024 peak price for an SJC gold bar: about **92.4 million VND per luong**. To translate that into a number a global reader recognizes, do two conversions. First, currency: at roughly **26,300 dong per US dollar**, 92.4 million VND ÷ 26,300 ≈ **\$3,510** — call it **\$3,600** with rounding and shop spread. Second, sanity-check it per ounce: 92.4 million VND ÷ 1.20565 troy oz ≈ 76.6 million VND per ounce, and 76.6 million ÷ 26,300 ≈ **\$2,915 per troy ounce**. The world gold price in mid-2024 averaged around \$2,388 and peaked near \$2,790, so the Vietnamese price implied roughly a few hundred dollars per ounce *above* world — that gap is the famous SJC premium, which the [next post in this track](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly) is entirely about. The intuition: one luong of gold is a single coin you can close your fist around, worth about the price of a decent used motorbike or a few months' salary — a meaningful chunk of wealth in portable form.

### The forms gold takes: bars, rings, and jewelry — and why purity matters

A foreign reader hears "gold" and pictures one thing. A Vietnamese saver distinguishes carefully between several forms, because the form determines the price you pay above the metal, the price you get back when you sell, and how the law treats it. Getting this right is half of holding gold sensibly, and the final post in this track is a [practical playbook](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly) on exactly that choice. The foundations belong here.

The first axis is **purity**, expressed two ways that mean the same thing. The international convention is *karat*: pure gold is 24 karat (24K), and lower karats are alloys — 18K gold is 18/24 = 75% gold mixed with other metals for hardness and color. The Vietnamese convention is a *fineness* number: **9999** means 99.99% pure (four-nines, effectively pure 24K gold), and **999** means 99.9%. When a Vietnamese saver says they want *vang 9999* (gold four-nines), they mean investment-grade pure gold, the kind held for value rather than fashion. Lower-purity gold — 18K, 14K — is for jewelry where durability and design matter and where you are paying partly for the craftsmanship, not just the metal.

The second axis is **form**, and there are three that matter:

- **SJC bars** (*vang mieng SJC*). These are the stamped, serial-numbered gold bars produced under the state monopoly brand SJC, in denominations measured in luong and chi. They are the closest thing Vietnam has to a "standard unit of investment gold," and for years they commanded the highest premium and the most policy attention — the entire [Decree 24 monopoly story](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly) is about these bars. An SJC bar is bought and sold purely on its gold content plus a brand and policy premium; nobody buys one for its looks.
- **Plain gold rings** (*nhan tron*, literally "round ring"). This is high-purity (often 9999) gold formed into a simple, unadorned ring with little or no design markup. The nhan tron is the clever Vietnamese saver's workhorse: it is investment-grade pure gold, divisible into small amounts, but because it is technically "jewelry" rather than a "bar" it historically sat outside the tightest SJC-bar regulation and often traded at a *smaller* premium than SJC bars. Many households accumulate savings specifically in nhan tron for this reason — same metal content, lower markup, more flexibility.
- **Jewelry** (*trang suc*). Necklaces, bracelets, and ornate pieces, often 18K, bought for wearing and for status. The markup over metal content is much larger here because you are paying for design and craftsmanship, and you will not recover that markup when you sell — so jewelry is the worst form for pure savings even though it is the most visible form of status.

#### Worked example: the markup you pay for form

Suppose the pure metal content you want is **1 chi** — 3.75 grams of gold — and the underlying metal value at the time is **9.24 million VND** (one chi at 92.4 million per luong), which converts at 26,300 dong per dollar to about **\$351**. Now compare what you actually pay across forms. A plain nhan tron ring of 9999 gold might cost roughly the metal value plus a thin few-percent shop spread — say **9.5–9.7 million VND**, about **\$360–\$369** — and you would recover most of that metal value on resale. An ornate 18K jewelry piece containing the same 3.75 grams of *pure* gold equivalent might be tagged at **13–15 million VND** (about **\$494–\$570**) because you are paying for design and labor — and on resale you would be quoted only the metal content, losing the entire craftsmanship markup. The intuition: for *savings*, the Vietnamese rule of thumb is to buy the form closest to pure metal at the thinnest spread (nhan tron or a bar), and to treat ornate jewelry as a status purchase whose markup is a cost, not an investment.

This distinction between forms is not a side detail — it is central to how Vietnamese gold policy works, because the state's monopoly applies to *bars* and the premium attaches to the *brand*, so savers route around it through rings and jewelry. We flag the mechanics here and develop them fully in the policy posts; for now the foundation is simply: in Vietnam, "gold" is not one thing, and the form you choose determines how much of your money is actually buying metal.

### The dong, and the case against trusting it

The other half of the story is the currency the gold is measured against. The Vietnamese **dong** (VND) is the national currency, and for the average saver it has one defining historical property: it loses value. Not catastrophically every year — Vietnam today runs a managed, relatively stable currency by emerging-market standards — but persistently, in one direction, for as far back as living memory reaches.

The exchange rate tells the recent chapter of the story plainly. In 2019 it took about 23,170 dong to buy a US dollar. By 2026 it took about 26,300. That is a slide of roughly 14% in seven years against the dollar alone — and the dollar itself was losing purchasing power to inflation over the same period, so the dong's loss of *real* purchasing power was larger still. A saver who held dong cash through that window watched it quietly bleed value the entire time, while doing nothing wrong.

![The dong long slide USD VND exchange rate 2019 to 2026](/imgs/blogs/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal-5.png)

That chart is the modern, mild version of a much older and much more violent pattern, and the violence is what wrote the cultural rule. To understand why Vietnamese trust metal over paper at a gut level, you have to go back further than the exchange rate table.

### Why the distrust runs deep: war, the post-1975 collapse, and serial devaluation

Three layers of history stacked the distrust.

The first is **war and its aftermath**. Vietnam spent much of the twentieth century at war, and war is the ultimate test of what money is. When a regime can fall, when borders can move, when banks can be nationalized or closed, paper claims on institutions become only as good as the institutions, and the institutions were not reliable. Gold, by contrast, kept working. A family that fled — and millions did, across decades and in multiple directions — could carry gold sewn into clothing or hidden in luggage; they could not carry a bank balance denominated in a currency that might not exist next year. Gold became the asset that survived regime change, the thing you could take with you and turn into local money anywhere you landed. Several of the later posts in this series, especially [gold in war and collapse](/blog/trading/gold/gold-in-war-and-collapse-flight-capital-and-the-asset-of-last-resort), are about exactly this property of the metal; Vietnam is one of its clearest case studies.

The second layer is the **post-1975 economic collapse and hyperinflation**. After reunification in 1976, the centrally planned economy struggled badly. Currency reforms wiped out savings — in 1985 a currency conversion forcibly exchanged old dong for new at a punitive rate, instantly destroying the value of cash holdings for ordinary people. Inflation in the second half of the 1980s ran into the hundreds of percent a year; estimates for the worst stretch put annual inflation around 300% to 700%. For a saver, that is not a slow erosion — it is a fire. Money you set aside in spring was worthless by autumn. An entire generation learned, viscerally and at the household level, that the government's paper could be reset or inflated away on a politician's signature, and that the only savings that survived were the ones the government could not print, recall, or convert: gold and, secondarily, US dollars.

It is worth dwelling on the 1985 currency reform specifically, because it is the kind of event that imprints a financial culture permanently. A currency conversion of that sort is not a market event you can hedge against — it is an administrative act. One day your cash is worth what it says; the next, by decree, old notes are exchanged for new ones at a ratio that confiscates a large fraction of their value, often with a cap on how much any one person may convert. There is no warning you can act on and no way to opt out: if your savings are in dong, the state can rewrite their value overnight. Now contrast the family that, instead of holding cash, had quietly converted its surplus into gold. The conversion decree does nothing to gold. A luong is still a luong; 37.5 grams is still 37.5 grams; it is still worth the world price translated into whatever the new currency is. The gold-holding family walked through the reform with its purchasing power intact while the cash-holding family was wiped out. That single contrast, repeated across millions of households and burned into the memory of everyone who lived through it, is arguably the most powerful single source of the modern Vietnamese gold habit. People do not need to be told to distrust paper money when they have personally watched it be cancelled.

And the lesson compounds across generations precisely because of the inheritance mechanism described later in this post: the grandparents who survived 1985 by holding gold did not just keep their wealth — they kept their *story*, and they handed both down. The metal carried the memory.

The third layer is the **long, managed devaluation of the modern era**. The 1986 *Doi Moi* reforms stabilized the economy and put Vietnam on a remarkable growth path, and today's macro picture is far healthier — moderate inflation, real growth, a functioning banking system. But the currency still slides against the dollar year after year as part of a deliberate policy to keep exports competitive. So even the optimistic, modern, post-reform experience confirms the old lesson in a gentler key: the dong loses ground over time, and the family that wants to preserve purchasing power over a generation cannot do it by holding dong.

Put those three layers together and you get a culture-wide prior that an economist would call *rational expectations about currency depreciation*, and that a grandmother would call *common sense*: paper money is for spending this month; gold is for keeping. This is not folklore. It is the correct empirical generalization from a century of data, encoded as habit. For the broader macro mechanics of why a developing-country currency tends to depreciate, this series leans on the macro track — see [the dollar system and why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) — but the Vietnamese household reached the practical conclusion without needing the theory.

## The cultural role: weddings, dowry, Tet, and the God of Wealth

If the foundation is distrust of paper, the visible expression of it is a set of deeply embedded cultural practices in which gold is the medium. These are not optional flourishes. For most Vietnamese families they are the structure through which gold enters and leaves the household across a lifetime.

![The four jobs gold does in a Vietnamese household savings dowry liquidity status](/imgs/blogs/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal-6.png)

The figure above is the map for this whole section and the economic one that follows. One stack of household gold does four jobs at once — it is the family's long-term savings, its dowry and inheritance vehicle, its emergency liquidity (sell a chi today, get cash today), and its visible status — and the cultural practices below are the channels through which it fills each of those roles. A bank deposit is savings but not a wedding gift; a house is a store of value but not liquidity; cash is liquid but not a store of value. Gold, uniquely, does all four, which is why the culture organizes so much of family life around acquiring and passing it on.

### Weddings and dowry

The most important is the wedding. At a traditional Vietnamese wedding, gold is the central gift. Parents, grandparents, and close relatives give the couple gold — often in the form of jewelry that the bride wears during the ceremony, sometimes as small bars or rings. This is not symbolic in the way a Western wedding gift is symbolic. It is a literal transfer of wealth, a portable nest egg handed to the new household to start its married life with a cushion of stored value. The amount signals the family's standing and its commitment to the couple, but it is also, quite practically, the couple's first savings.

#### Worked example: a wedding gift of 5 chi

Suppose a relative gives the couple **5 chi** of gold — that is half a luong, 0.5 × 37.5 = 18.75 grams. At the 2024 peak of about 92.4 million VND per luong, five chi is 0.5 × 92.4 = **46.2 million VND**. Converting at 26,300 dong per dollar: 46.2 million ÷ 26,300 ≈ **\$1,757**, call it **\$1,800**. So a single mid-range wedding gift transfers roughly \$1,800 of hard, portable, instantly sellable value to the couple. A well-attended wedding in a comfortable family can see the couple receive several luong in total across all the gifts — many thousands of dollars of gold, handed over in an afternoon. The intuition: the wedding is not just a celebration, it is the moment a Vietnamese family capitalizes a new household, and it does it in gold because gold is the one gift that will still be worth something at any point in the marriage to come.

The dowry tradition reinforces this. Gold given at marriage stays with the couple (in many cases specifically with the bride) as a personal reserve, separate from joint spending money. It is the emergency fund, the thing you do not touch unless you must. Because it is held as physical metal rather than as a bank deposit, it is psychologically and practically walled off from day-to-day finances — you have to physically decide to take it to a shop and sell it, which is a higher bar than tapping a debit card. That friction is a feature: it makes the gold sticky savings.

There is a gender dimension worth naming, because it makes the savings even stickier. In many Vietnamese families the wedding gold and dowry gold are understood to belong to the *wife* specifically, as her own reserve within the marriage. This is partly tradition and partly practical insurance: it gives the woman a measure of financial independence and a fallback that does not depend on her husband's earnings or decisions. Gold is uniquely suited to this role because it is private, portable, and does not require a bank account or a co-signer — a woman can hold her own wealth in a form that is entirely hers, hidden if need be, and convertible to cash on her own initiative. In a society where formal financial independence was historically harder for women to achieve, the dowry gold was a quietly powerful institution, and it remains one. The metal in the drawer is, in many households, *her* metal.

### Inheritance and the intergenerational chain

Beyond the wedding, gold is the medium through which Vietnamese families pass wealth down the generations. An inheritance is often literally a quantity of gold — luong handed from parents to children, sometimes accumulated specifically as a bequest over decades. This matters for understanding why the habit is so durable: each generation does not just *hear about* gold in words, it *physically receives* gold from the one before, along with the instruction to keep saving in it. The transmission mechanism is the metal itself.

Think about what this does to the persistence of the behavior. A financial habit taught only in words can fade in a generation as circumstances change. A habit transmitted as a physical object — here is the family gold, your grandmother saved it through the war and the inflation, now you keep adding to it — is far harder to break, because abandoning it means consciously deciding to sell or stop adding to a tangible inheritance loaded with family meaning. The gold carries the memory of *why* the family saves in gold. That is why the practice survives modernization that has swept away many other traditions: the asset and the lesson are the same physical thing, passed hand to hand. This intergenerational transmission is the single best explanation for the "younger people haven't moved on" fact we will return to in the misconceptions section.

### Tet and Than Tai day

The second great occasion is the calendar. **Tet**, the lunar new year, is the most important holiday in Vietnam, and gold features in it as gifts, as lucky money for children (often the year's first deliberate savings act), and as the traditional moment to set aside wealth for the year ahead. Buying gold around Tet is partly about luck and partly about the natural rhythm of an agricultural and bonus-driven economy: this is when households have accumulated some surplus and convert it into the form they trust.

Then comes the spectacle this post opened with. **Than Tai day** — the tenth day of the first lunar month, the day of *Than Tai*, the God of Wealth — has become, in the modern era, the single busiest gold-buying day of the Vietnamese year. The belief is straightforward: buying gold on this day invites prosperity for the coming year. The practice is a national event. Shops open before dawn; people queue for hours; the most common purchase is a small, affordable amount — a single chi, or even smaller commemorative pieces — bought precisely so that as many people as possible can participate regardless of means.

#### Worked example: a Than Tai day one-chi purchase

A typical Than Tai buyer purchases **1 chi** — 3.75 grams, one-tenth of a luong. At 92.4 million VND per luong, one chi costs 92.4 ÷ 10 = **9.24 million VND**, which at 26,300 dong per dollar is 9.24 million ÷ 26,300 ≈ **\$351**, call it about **\$350**. (On Than Tai day itself shops typically charge a wider spread, so the buyer pays a bit more and would get a bit less if selling back the same day.) Now multiply by participation: if even a few million households each buy a chi or two on that one morning, the country moves *tonnes* of gold in a single day. The intuition: Than Tai day looks like a luck ritual, but functionally it is a synchronized national savings event that converts a slice of the year's surplus into metal — the most rational thing a population with Vietnam's monetary history could do, wrapped in a story about a god.

What is striking, and what undercuts the "younger people have moved on" claim we will address later, is how thoroughly Than Tai day has *grown* in the modern era. It is not a fading folk custom; it is a contemporary phenomenon, amplified by media coverage and by gold shops marketing themed small bars. The ritual has modernized rather than died, which tells you the underlying demand — the desire to hold a little metal — is alive and well in the smartphone generation.

## The economic role: informal savings, property priced in gold, and the unbanked

Strip away the culture and there is a hard economic engine underneath, and it is worth seeing on its own terms because it explains why the gold habit persists even as Vietnam modernizes.

### Gold as the informal savings account

For a large share of Vietnamese households, gold has historically been the primary long-term savings vehicle — the functional equivalent of a savings account or a pension, held in physical form. The logic is the logic of the trust ladder in the cover figure. Bank deposits in dong pay interest, but in years when inflation runs hot the real return can be negative, and there is a residual distrust of the banking system that predates the current, healthier era. Gold pays no interest at all — and this series spends a lot of time on the fact that gold is a [non-yielding asset](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock) whose lack of a coupon is its central feature — but for a saver who believes the dong will lose more than the deposit pays, a 0% asset that holds its purchasing power beats a positive-rate asset denominated in a depreciating currency. The household is making an implicit real-return comparison, and gold wins it more often than an outsider would guess.

### Property historically priced in gold

One of the most telling facts about the depth of the gold habit is that, for decades, large transactions in Vietnam — especially real estate — were quoted and settled in gold rather than dong. A house or a plot of land would be priced in luong: "this property costs 800 luong of gold." The reason is the same distrust translated into a unit-of-account problem. Over the months it takes to negotiate and complete a property sale, a price fixed in dong could be eroded by inflation or devaluation, so buyer and seller agreed on a price in the one unit both trusted to hold its value: gold. The dong was the medium of small daily exchange; gold was the unit of account for serious, durable value. This practice has faded as the currency has stabilized and as regulation has pushed transactions into dong, but its very existence tells you how completely gold functioned as the real money of the economy for the things that mattered most.

### Gold as collateral and the down-payment

The economic role goes beyond passive storage. Gold is also *working capital* for the household, in two specific ways.

First, it is collateral. A family that needs cash quickly but does not want to sell its gold can pawn it — hand the gold to a pawnshop or a moneylender, receive cash against it, and redeem it later by repaying with interest. Because gold's value is transparent and universally agreed (the price is quoted publicly every day), it is the ideal collateral: the lender can size the loan precisely against the metal and faces almost no valuation risk. This makes gold a source of *liquidity without sale* — the family can raise cash in an emergency and still get its gold back if things recover, rather than permanently liquidating its savings. For a small business owner facing a cash crunch or a family facing a sudden expense, the gold in the drawer is a standing line of credit that requires no bank approval.

Second, gold has historically been the **property down-payment**. When large transactions were priced in gold, the deposit that locked a deal was paid in gold, and even today the savings a family draws on to make a house down-payment are frequently held in gold and sold at the moment of purchase. The gold is the bridge between the household's long-term savings and its single largest lifetime purchase. This connects the gold story directly to the property story, and the [Vietnamese allocation post](/blog/trading/gold/gold-vs-the-dong-real-estate-and-savings-the-vietnamese-allocation) traces the full gold-versus-property-versus-deposit decision that sits at the center of a Vietnamese household's balance sheet.

### The unbanked and the cash economy

Vietnam has modernized its financial system rapidly, but a meaningful slice of economic life still runs in cash, and financial inclusion — while improving fast — is not universal, especially in rural areas and among older people. For a household without easy, trusted access to interest-bearing, inflation-protected savings products, gold is the available technology. It requires no bank account, no paperwork, no credit history, and no trust in an institution. You can buy it at a shop two streets away, store it yourself, and sell any fraction of it at any time at a transparent, publicly quoted price. For the unbanked or under-banked, gold is not a primitive choice — it is often the *best available* savings instrument, and it has been for generations.

There is a deeper point here about what a savings instrument actually has to do, and gold quietly does all of it. A good store of household value needs to be: durable (it must not rot, rust, or expire); divisible (you must be able to use a little without destroying the rest); portable (you must be able to move it and hide it); fungible (any unit must be as good as any other); recognizable (a counterparty must be able to verify it); and scarce (no one must be able to manufacture more of it cheaply). Economists list these as the textbook properties of money — and gold scores well on every one, which is precisely why it became money five thousand years ago, a story this series tells in [gold as money](/blog/trading/gold/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults). What the Vietnamese household demonstrates is that when the *official* money fails several of these tests — a dong that is not a reliable store of value, that gets reset or inflated away — people fall back on the asset that passes all of them. They are not being primitive or superstitious. They are applying the definition of money correctly and noticing that, by that definition, gold is better money than the dong for the specific job of *keeping* value over time.

## The scale: one of the highest per-capita gold cultures on earth

All of this culture and economics adds up to a number, and the number is large. Vietnam, a country of roughly 100 million people that is still middle-income by GDP per head, is consistently one of the largest consumer gold markets in the world relative to its size.

![Vietnam consumer gold demand 2019 to 2025 in tonnes per year](/imgs/blogs/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal-2.png)

The chart shows annual Vietnamese consumer gold demand — jewelry plus bars and coins — running at roughly 50 tonnes a year across the recent period, with a dip in 2020 (the pandemic year, when shops closed and incomes fell) and a recovery afterward. Fifty tonnes is the headline, and it is worth dwelling on what it means. At a world gold price of, say, \$2,400 an ounce, 50 tonnes is about 1.6 million troy ounces, or roughly **\$3.9 billion** of gold absorbed in a single year by private Vietnamese households — for a country whose entire GDP is a few hundred billion dollars. As a share of household income, that is an enormous, sustained allocation to a single non-yielding asset, year after year, decade after decade.

#### Worked example: a household's 10-luong nest egg

Consider a comfortable-but-ordinary household that has accumulated **10 luong** of gold over the years — a realistic lifetime savings goal, built up chi by chi at Tet and Than Tai and topped up at weddings. Ten luong is 10 × 37.5 = 375 grams of gold, about 12 troy ounces. At the 2024 peak of 92.4 million VND per luong, that holding is worth 10 × 92.4 = **924 million VND** — call it **920 million VND**. Converting at 26,300 dong per dollar: 924 million ÷ 26,300 ≈ **\$35,100**, round to about **\$35,000**. So a fairly typical, hard-saved family reserve is on the order of \$35,000 of gold sitting at home — not in a bank, not earning interest, not visible to any tax authority or creditor, instantly convertible to cash at any shop in the country. The intuition: this is the household's true balance sheet, and a foreign analyst looking only at the family's bank deposits would dramatically understate its real net worth.

When you multiply that household behavior across tens of millions of families, you get the per-capita scale that makes Vietnam stand out globally. Cumulatively, the gold stored in Vietnamese homes is estimated to run into the hundreds of tonnes — possibly more than the official reserves of many mid-sized central banks — sitting in drawers, safes, and jewelry boxes rather than in any vault the government controls. This is "the gold in the mattress" as a national-scale phenomenon, and it is one of the central facts the State Bank of Vietnam has to reckon with, as the [policy posts in this track](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly) explore.

## The trust thesis: gold as the household's hard currency

Now we can state the thesis directly, because the foundations are all in place. **In Vietnam, gold is the household's hard currency.** It is the money that the household trusts more than the national money, and it plays, at the family scale, exactly the role that a reserve currency plays at the national scale.

Recall the four jobs from the figure at the start of the cultural section — savings, dowry and inheritance, emergency liquidity, and status. The reason gold can do all four simultaneously, when no paper instrument can, comes down to a single property. Jewelry you bought purely for fashion is status but a poor store of value because of the markup; a deposit is savings but not a gift you can hand across a wedding table; cash is liquid but melts in real terms. Gold — held as bars, rings, or high-purity jewelry priced close to its metal content — is all of them at once because it is, in the end, a single asset that is no one else's liability.

That phrase is the whole series in three words. The defining property of gold, repeated across every track of this series, is that it is **no one's promise to pay**. A bank deposit is the bank's promise; a bond is the issuer's promise; the dong in your wallet is the State Bank's promise. Every one of those promises can be broken, devalued, frozen, or redenominated — and in Vietnam's history, many of them were. Gold cannot be devalued by a central bank because no central bank issues it; it cannot be defaulted on because no one owes it; it cannot be inflated away because no one can print more of it at will. For a household whose entire monetary memory is a catalog of broken paper promises, an asset that makes no promise — and therefore can break none — is the safest thing imaginable. That is the trust thesis. Vietnamese families hold gold for the same reason central banks do, which the series covers in [the apolitical asset](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022): it is the one form of money that does not depend on trusting anyone.

This reframes the Than Tai queues entirely. The people in line are not gambling on a folk belief. They are doing, at the household level and wrapped in a cultural ritual, precisely what the most sophisticated reserve managers on earth have been doing in record volumes since 2022 — moving wealth out of paper promises and into the one asset that is nobody's liability. The grandmother in the queue and the central-bank governor in Beijing or Warsaw are running the same trade. The grandmother just figured it out first, because her currency taught her the lesson earlier and more painfully.

## Common misconceptions

The Vietnamese gold culture attracts a few confident dismissals from outsiders, and each one falls apart on contact with the numbers and the history.

**Misconception 1: "It's just tradition — sentimental, not financial."** This is the most common and the most wrong. Tradition is the *wrapper*, not the *content*. The content is a correct, data-backed conclusion: in a country where the currency lost value violently in the 1980s and continues to slide today (23,170 to 26,300 per dollar in seven years, a ~14% depreciation against a dollar that was itself inflating), holding wealth in a depreciating paper currency is the irrational choice and holding it in gold is the rational one. The ritual makes the rational behavior automatic and social, which is exactly what good financial habits should be. Calling it "just tradition" mistakes the cultural delivery mechanism for the financial logic it delivers.

**Misconception 2: "Younger, urban, banked Vietnamese have moved on from gold."** The data say otherwise. Than Tai day, the most visible expression of household gold demand, has *grown* into a major modern event, not faded into a quaint relic — its queues and media coverage expand year by year. National consumer gold demand has held around 50 tonnes annually right through the period when Vietnam was urbanizing, digitizing payments, and building a modern banking system. Younger Vietnamese have certainly added stocks, crypto, and bank products to their menu — but they have added them *alongside* gold, not instead of it. The base allocation to metal has proven remarkably sticky, which is what you would expect from a behavior rooted in deep, intergenerational memory rather than fashion.

**Misconception 3: "It's irrational to hold an asset that pays no interest."** This is the most financially sophisticated-sounding objection and it is still wrong in context. Yes, gold pays no coupon — and this series takes that seriously as gold's defining feature in [the no-yield problem](/blog/trading/gold/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything). But "no interest" is only a cost relative to a *safe, real, positive* alternative. For a Vietnamese saver, the relevant alternative is a dong deposit whose interest may not keep up with inflation and currency depreciation. If the deposit's real, currency-adjusted return is roughly zero or negative — which it has been in plenty of years — then a 0% asset that preserves purchasing power is not irrational; it is the *better* choice. The interest you forgo is small if the alternative was going to be eaten by depreciation anyway. We will put numbers on this in the next section.

**Misconception 4: "All that home gold is dead money the economy can't use."** There is a grain of truth here — gold in a drawer is not financing factories — and it is precisely the concern that drives Vietnamese gold policy and the State Bank's "anti-goldization" agenda, which the [policy posts](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly) cover in depth. But from the *household's* point of view, the gold is not dead at all: it is doing the four jobs in the figure above — savings, dowry, liquidity, status — that the household cannot reliably get elsewhere. "Dead money" is the policymaker's frame; "the only savings I trust" is the saver's frame, and the saver is not wrong about their own situation.

## How it shows up in real markets: gold versus the dong through the devaluation

The cleanest way to see that the gold habit is rational is to put it through the test that matters: what would a Vietnamese saver actually have ended up with, holding gold versus holding the alternative, through the currency's slide? Here the data series in this post do real work.

![Gold value in major currencies indexed to 100 in 2015 with VND highlighted](/imgs/blogs/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal-4.png)

Start with the chart of gold's value in different currencies, indexed to 100 in 2015. The first thing it shows is that gold has risen in *every* major currency — this is the [gold-rises-in-every-currency](/blog/trading/gold/gold-and-the-dollar-the-inverse-relationship-and-when-it-breaks) point the dollar post makes: gold going up is partly gold strengthening and partly paper money weakening, everywhere at once. The second thing it shows is the ordering. Gold's gain in dong (the highlighted line) tracks above its gain in dollars and roughly alongside the euro, because the dong depreciated against the dollar over the window — so a Vietnamese saver measuring their gold in their own currency saw an even larger nominal gain than a dollar-based saver did. The weaker your home currency, the more gold "goes up" in it, which is the same fact as saying gold preserved purchasing power while the currency lost it.

#### Worked example: gold versus a dong deposit over a decade

Make it concrete with two savers in 2015, each starting with the dong equivalent of one luong of gold. Indexed to 100 in 2015, the gold-in-dong line reaches about **520 by 2026** — a 5.2x increase in nominal dong terms over roughly a decade, driven by the world gold price rising and the dong depreciating against the dollar at the same time. The saver who held the metal turned their starting value into about 5.2 times as many dong.

Now the saver who instead put the money in a dong bank deposit. Vietnamese deposit rates over the decade averaged very roughly 6% a year (they have ranged widely, often 5–8%). Compounding even a generous 6% for ten years gives 1.06^10 ≈ **1.79x** — the deposit not quite doubled in nominal dong terms. So in nominal dong: gold ~5.2x versus the deposit ~1.8x. In dollars, the gap is even starker, because the dong the deposit is denominated in lost ~14%+ of its dollar value over the period: the depositor's roughly 1.8x of dong converts to materially less than 1.8x of dollars, whereas the gold holder's dong gain partly *came from* that same depreciation and converts to a real dollar gain. The intuition: across this particular window, the gold-holding household did not just beat the deposit — it beat it by a multiple, and exactly the depreciation that punished the depositor is what rewarded the gold holder. That is the trust thesis paying off in numbers, not sentiment.

![SJC gold bar price in million VND per luong 2020 to 2026 with the 2024 intervention](/imgs/blogs/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal-7.png)

The final chart shows the domestic SJC gold-bar price in million VND per luong, and it is a preview of everything the rest of this track will dig into. Notice the shape: a steady climb from about 56 million VND per luong in 2020, an acceleration into the 2024 blow-off near 92.4 million, then a sharp pullback to around 77 million when the State Bank intervened by selling gold directly through banks to compress the premium, and finally a powerful surge to a fresh all-time high near 190 million VND per luong in early 2026 as the world gold price exploded. Two lessons sit in that line. First, the *direction* over time is relentlessly up in dong terms — which is the depreciation/inflation story playing out in real time and validating every household that held metal. Second, the 2024 pullback is the fingerprint of *policy*: the Vietnamese gold price is not a free price, it is a managed one, shaped by an import ban, a state monopoly on bars, and active central-bank intervention. That managed character is the entire subject of the [SJC and Decree 24](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly) post and the [premium](/blog/trading/gold/gold-vs-the-dong-real-estate-and-savings-the-vietnamese-allocation) posts that follow this one.

### The 2024 episode, from a household's seat

It is worth replaying the 2024 spike from inside a Vietnamese household, because it shows the whole system — culture, economics, and policy — colliding in real time. Through late 2023 and into 2024, the world gold price was climbing and the dong was sliding, and the domestic SJC price did what it always does in such moments: it ran. By May 2024 an SJC bar hit about **92.4 million VND per luong** — roughly **\$3,600** — a record that dominated the news. But something unusual was happening underneath the record. The *premium* — the gap between the domestic price and the world-equivalent price — had blown out to extreme levels, around 18 million VND per luong, perhaps 20–25% above world. Vietnamese were paying a quarter more than the global price for the same metal, because the import ban and the SJC monopoly had throttled supply while household demand surged. The very policies meant to discourage gold hoarding had instead created a scarcity premium that made the gold Vietnamese already held even more valuable, and made new buyers pay through the nose.

The household response was textbook. Some long-time holders, sitting on gold bought years earlier at 40 or 50 million per luong, sold into the spike and took a large dong gain — exactly the payoff the insurance policy is for. Others kept buying, premium be damned, because the cultural and precautionary motives do not switch off just because the price is high; if anything a rising price and a falling currency *intensify* the urge to hold metal. And then the state stepped in. To crush the premium without burning through its reserves defending the dong, the State Bank of Vietnam began selling gold directly through a handful of state banks at a controlled price, pulling the SJC quote down toward about **77 million VND per luong** within weeks — the sharp dip you can see in the price chart. That intervention, and the policy logic behind it, is the entire subject of the [SBV gold-policy and Decree 24 posts](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly); the point for *this* post is what the episode reveals about the demand. Even a determined, well-resourced central bank, willing to sell metal into the market, could only dent the price temporarily: by 2025–2026, with the world price exploding, the SJC quote tore past its old record to a fresh all-time high near **190 million VND per luong**. The household bid is that strong and that persistent. You can manage the premium; you cannot manage away the underlying desire to hold gold. That desire is the bedrock the entire Vietnamese gold market — and this whole track of the series — is built on.

It is worth being precise about a caveat, because honesty about gold is this series' whole posture. The decade I just used, 2015–2026, was an exceptionally strong one for gold globally. Run the same comparison across the long bear market of, say, 1990–2000 and gold in any currency went sideways or down while a dong deposit, even net of depreciation, would have done fine. Gold is not a magic guaranteed winner over the dong — it is an *insurance policy* whose payoff is concentrated in exactly the episodes Vietnam's history is full of: high inflation, sharp devaluation, and instability. The Vietnamese household is not claiming gold always beats a deposit. It is buying protection against the scenarios where the deposit gets destroyed, and accepting that in calm years it gives up some yield for that protection. That is a thoroughly rational insurance decision, and it is the same logic the [allocation framing](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock) applies at the portfolio level.

## The takeaway: what Vietnam teaches you about gold

Here is what I want you to walk away with, because it changes how you should think about gold everywhere, not just in Vietnam.

Most explanations of gold demand in the West are abstract: real interest rates, the dollar, central-bank diversification, tail-risk hedging. They are correct, and the rest of this series is built on them. But they are *intermediated* — the demand reaches the gold price through funds, ETFs, and reserve managers, and it is easy to treat it as a financial-market phenomenon disconnected from anything real. Vietnam strips the intermediation away and shows you the raw thing underneath. Here, gold demand is not a position in a portfolio model. It is a grandmother who lost her savings to hyperinflation teaching her granddaughter to buy a chi every Than Tai day. It is a family capitalizing a marriage in metal because metal is the gift that will still be worth something in thirty years. It is a population that has run the experiment — *trust the paper or trust the metal* — across a century of war, collapse, and devaluation, and reached a verdict.

The verdict is the series' spine, stated by an entire nation rather than by an analyst: **gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy against the failure of paper money, and its price is a vote of no-confidence in that paper.** In most countries the no-confidence vote is cast quietly, by institutions, and shows up as a line on a chart. In Vietnam it is cast loudly, by tens of millions of households, and shows up as queues outside gold shops before dawn. Same vote. Same asset. Same logic. The only difference is that the Vietnamese household has held the position longer and understands *why* it holds it better than most professional investors do, because it learned the lesson the expensive way.

So the next time you see the Than Tai day photos and feel the urge to file them under "exotic superstition," do the opposite. File them under "a rational savings culture, fully priced for currency risk, executing the oldest and most reliable monetary-insurance trade in human history — and doing it more disciplined than your hedge fund." The luong in the drawer is not a relic. It is the household's central bank, and its reserve asset is the only one that has never defaulted in five thousand years.

If you want to understand gold, do not start with the COMEX futures curve. Start with the queue outside the gold shop in Hanoi at 5 a.m. on Than Tai day. Everything else in this series is a more complicated way of explaining what those people already know.
