> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — For a Vietnamese household, the real allocation question is not stocks vs bonds; it is gold vs dong deposits vs real estate vs US dollars, and each has done a different job in a different era.
>
> - **Gold** is the devaluation-and-crisis hedge: it has roughly quintupled in dong terms since 2015 while paying nothing and never defaulting.
> - **Dong deposits** look "safe" and pay ~5-6% a year, but inflation plus a slowly sliding dong have quietly eroded their real purchasing power.
> - **Real estate** is the leveraged status play that built most Vietnamese household wealth — and the one asset that can fall hard and lock your money up for years.
> - **US dollars** are the cleanest hard-currency hedge against the dong, but holding them is legally constrained inside Vietnam.
> - The one number to remember: a 100 million VND dong deposit compounding at ~5.5% became ~171 million VND over a decade — about **\$6,500** at today's exchange rate — while the same money in gold became roughly **\$19,000**.

A family in Ha Noi sells a small plot of inherited land in 2016 and ends up with 1 billion VND — call it about **\$39,000** at the time. The grandmother's instinct is gold: turn it into bars at the SJC shop, lock them in the cabinet. The son, an engineer, thinks that is old-fashioned — gold pays nothing, just sits there. He wants the money working: a fixed-term dong deposit at the bank paying over 6%, or a deposit on a second apartment that, everyone agrees, "only goes up." The daughter, who studied abroad, quietly keeps a sleeve in US dollars because she has watched the dong slide year after year and does not trust it.

Ten years later, in 2026, every one of them turns out to have been partly right and partly wrong. The grandmother's gold has roughly **quintupled** in dong terms. The son's bank deposit grew — but so slowly that, measured in what it can actually buy, or converted back into dollars, it barely moved. The apartment did very well for a while, then the property market wobbled and the money was stuck. The daughter's dollars protected her from the dong's slide but earned almost nothing.

This is the uniquely Vietnamese version of the allocation problem. In a US or European personal-finance article, the debate is "stocks vs bonds." For a Vietnamese saver, the four real choices on the table are **gold, a dong bank deposit, real estate, and US dollars** — and the "safe" default, a dong savings account, has quietly lost ground to gold and property for most of the last two decades. This post is the practical decision, built from zero, with dong worked examples anchored in dollars.

Why does the menu look so different from the Western one? Because the Vietnamese saver lives inside a different monetary reality. The Vietnamese stock market is younger, smaller, and more volatile than the developed-market equity that a Western "stocks vs bonds" debate assumes; the corporate bond market has had real stress; and the local-currency government bond is denominated in the very dong whose slide is the problem. So the assets that dominate Western retirement advice are, for a Vietnamese household, either thin, risky, or denominated in the wrong unit. What is left — what generations of Vietnamese families have actually used to protect wealth — is the four-way menu above: a metal, a deposit, a building, and a foreign banknote. Each one answers the same question in a different way: *how do I store value in a country where the currency is designed to slowly weaken?* That question, not "stocks vs bonds," is the real one, and the rest of this post answers it.

## Foundations: the Vietnamese saver's menu

Before any allocation, you need to know what is on the menu and what each thing does. We will define every term from scratch, because the whole argument hinges on a few words that get used loosely.

### The four assets

**Gold (vang).** Physical gold, usually held in Vietnam as SJC-branded bars or as gold jewellery and rings. The standard unit is the **luong** (also written *cay* or *tael*) — a weight of **37.5 grams**, equal to **1.20565 troy ounces** (a troy ounce is the global gold unit, about 31.1 grams). Gold pays no interest and no dividend; you make money only if its price rises. Its job is to **hold value** when the currency around it loses value, and to be the asset you can sell in any crisis.

**A dong deposit.** Money you put in a Vietnamese bank in a fixed-term savings account (*so tiet kiem*). The bank pays you a fixed **interest rate** — for a 12-month term, recently around **5-6% a year**. Your balance grows in dong, and the deposit is backed by the bank (and, up to a limit, by deposit insurance). Its job is **yield and liquidity**: steady, safe-looking growth you can access easily.

**Real estate.** Land, a house, or an apartment — by far the largest store of Vietnamese household wealth. It is usually bought partly with **leverage**: a bank mortgage, where you put down, say, 30% and borrow the rest. Property can pay a small **rental yield**, but most Vietnamese buyers hold it for **capital gains** and status. Its job is **leveraged growth** — and it is the least liquid of the four.

**US dollars (USD).** Holding hard foreign currency — physical dollars or a dollar-denominated savings account where permitted. Its job is a **hard-currency hedge**: a direct bet against the dong, in the world's reserve currency. Crucially, Vietnam restricts how freely residents can hold and trade foreign currency, so this is the most legally constrained option.

### The forces acting on all four

Three forces decide which of these wins over time. Define them carefully — they are the engine of the whole post.

**Inflation** is the rate at which prices rise, so each dong buys a little less than it did last year. If a bowl of pho cost 30,000 VND last year and 33,000 VND this year, that is 10% inflation on pho. Vietnam's official consumer inflation has mostly run in the **3-4% a year** range in recent years, though households experience the prices that matter to them — food, rent, school fees, medicine — which often rise faster than the official basket suggests. Inflation is the quiet thief: it never sends a statement, it never asks permission, and it works on every dong you hold, whether that dong sits in cash, in a deposit, or under the mattress. The longer you hold a fixed number of dong, the more of its buying power inflation removes. Compounded at even a modest 4% a year, prices roughly **double in eighteen years** — so a dong saved today buys half as much by the time a child finishes school.

**Devaluation** of the dong is the rate at which the dong loses value *against other currencies*, chiefly the US dollar. We measure it with the exchange rate **USD/VND** — how many dong it takes to buy one dollar. When that number rises, the dong has weakened. It went from roughly **22,790** dong per dollar at the end of 2021 to about **26,300** by 2026 — a slide of more than 15% in five years, on top of a long, steady downward grind over decades. Devaluation is not an accident; it is largely a *policy*. The State Bank of Vietnam runs a managed exchange rate, letting the dong slide gradually to keep Vietnamese exports competitive and to absorb pressure when the dollar is strong globally. For a saver, the direction of travel has been remarkably consistent for thirty years: the dong weakens, slowly, almost every year. That consistency is exactly why hard-money assets — gold and dollars — have a structural tailwind in Vietnam that they do not have in a country with a stable or strengthening currency.

**The deposit (interest) rate** is what a dong savings account pays you, around 5-6% a year recently. Here is the trap built into the foundation, and the single most important idea in this post: a deposit rate is a **nominal** number — it is growth measured in dong, before you subtract inflation. What you actually care about is the **real** rate: the deposit rate *minus* inflation. If your deposit pays 5.5% and inflation is 4%, your real return is only about **1.5%** — and if you also measure your wealth against the dollar, the dong's devaluation eats into it again. The distinction between nominal and real return is the hinge on which this entire post turns. A saver who looks only at the nominal number — "my money grew 5.5%!" — feels richer while quietly getting poorer in everything that matters: groceries, dollars, gold. A saver who thinks in real terms sees the truth, which is that a low-single-digit real return, repeatedly eroded by devaluation, barely keeps pace with the cost of living.

There is one more force worth naming, because it sits behind both inflation and devaluation: **money growth**. When a central bank lets the money supply expand faster than the economy produces goods, each unit of money is chasing a slice of a pie that grew more slowly — so prices rise and the currency tends to weaken. Vietnam's broad money supply has grown rapidly for decades as the economy developed and credit expanded. That long expansion is the deep current beneath the dong's slide, and it is the reason a fixed quantity of gold — which no one can print — tends to hold its value against a currency that is steadily being created in greater quantity. (The global version of this story is told in [quantitative easing explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money); the Vietnamese version runs through the SBV's credit ceiling.)

> The Vietnamese saver's central problem in one line: a dong deposit pays a nominal rate that looks fine, but inflation and devaluation are two quiet leaks in the bucket, and over a decade they can drain most of what the interest poured in.

Now we can take each of the four assets in turn, see what job it really does, and then put them together.

## Gold: the devaluation and crisis hedge

Start with gold, because it is the asset most misunderstood by outsiders and most trusted by Vietnamese families.

Gold's defining feature is that it pays **nothing**. No coupon, no dividend, no rent. A luong of gold sitting in a cabinet for ten years produces exactly one luong of gold. So why has it been the bedrock of Vietnamese savings for generations? Because gold's job is not to *grow* — it is to *hold*. Gold is best understood as a **monetary insurance policy**: an asset whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money. When confidence in the dong (or any currency) sags, the price of gold *in that currency* rises to compensate. You do not earn a yield; you earn protection.

This is worth dwelling on, because the "gold pays nothing" objection is the most common reason people dismiss it — and it misunderstands what gold is *for*. A bond pays interest because you are lending money and bearing the risk that you might not be repaid. A stock pays dividends because you own a slice of a business that earns profits. Gold does neither, because gold is not a claim on anyone — it is not a promise, an IOU, or a share of a company. It is just a fixed quantity of an element that no government can print and no company can dilute. The "no yield" is the flip side of "no counterparty": there is no one who owes you anything, so there is no one who can fail to pay you, default on you, freeze your account, or inflate away what they owe. In a country where the currency reliably weakens, the absence of a counterparty is not a weakness — it is the entire point. (We unpack the no-yield puzzle in depth in the series' [no-yield problem](/blog/trading/gold/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything).)

The data make this concrete. Priced in dong and indexed to 100 in 2015, gold rose to roughly **460** by early 2026 — it more than quintupled. That is not because the metal "did" anything; gold just sat there. It is because the dong slid against the dollar *and* against gold, and global gold demand surged. Two separate engines drove that dong-terms gain, and it is worth separating them: first, the **world price of gold in dollars** rose enormously over the decade as central banks bought, real interest rates fell, and geopolitical anxiety mounted; second, the **dong weakened against the dollar**, so each dollar of gold value translated into more and more dong. A Vietnamese gold holder captured *both* engines at once — the global bull market *and* the currency slide — which is why gold's dong-terms return has so dramatically outpaced what a dollar-based saver earned. The chart below is the single most important picture in the post: it is what "holding value in dong" actually looks like.

![Gold priced in dong indexed to 100 in 2015 rising to about 460 by 2026](/imgs/blogs/gold-vs-the-dong-real-estate-and-savings-the-vietnamese-allocation-2.png)

The flat dashed line at 100 is what your money would have done if it had merely *kept its nominal value* — a banknote in a drawer. Gold did the opposite of a banknote in a drawer: it climbed as the dong's purchasing power fell.

#### Worked example: 100 million VND in gold over a decade

A saver puts **100 million VND** — about **\$3,900** at the 2016 exchange rate of roughly 25,600 dong per dollar (using a mid-decade rate for the comparison) — into SJC gold bars and locks them away for ten years.

- Gold in dong rose roughly **5x** over that span (the dong-indexed series went from about 104 to 520 on the 2015 base).
- So 100 million VND in gold became roughly **500 million VND** by 2026.
- Converted back to dollars at the 2026 rate of 26,300 dong per dollar, that is about **\$19,000**.

The saver paid no interest, watched no statements, and did nothing — and turned **\$3,900** of stored value into roughly **\$19,000** of stored value. The intuition: gold did not make her rich; it refused to let the dong make her poor, and then global demand did the rest.

The catch — and it is a real one — is that gold is **lumpy and illiquid in the small**. You buy and sell at a dealer's spread (the gap between buy and sell prices), and in Vietnam there is an extra wrinkle: the SJC-branded bar trades at a **premium** to the world gold price, a domestic markup that has at times blown out to a quarter of the price before the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) intervened. We cover that premium in depth in [the SJC premium](/blog/trading/gold/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price); for allocation purposes, just know that gold's "holding" job comes with a transaction cost and a policy overhang that a bank deposit does not have.

A few practical points the allocation depends on. Vietnamese households hold gold in two main forms, and they behave differently. **SJC gold bars** are the standard investment vehicle — a state-recognised brand, easy to buy and sell at dealers, but carrying that domestic premium and, since the 2012 rules, subject to a state monopoly on production that is itself part of why the premium exists. **Gold jewellery and rings** (often "9999" gold, meaning 99.99% pure) are how a great deal of household gold is actually held, especially gifted at weddings; they trade closer to the world price but carry a workmanship cost and are less standardised to sell. For pure store-of-value purposes the bar is cleaner; for everyday savings and gifting the ring is the people's instrument. Either way, the key allocation fact is that gold is something you can hold *outside the banking system entirely* — in your own home, in your own hands, legally — which no other asset on this menu can claim.

The second-order effect that matters most for allocation: gold's price does not move smoothly with the dong's slide. It moves in **long, lumpy regimes**. For most of 1980-2000, gold did essentially nothing while the dong still weakened — a saver who went all-in on gold in that window waited two decades for a payoff. Then, from 2001 to 2011 and again from 2019 to 2026, gold ran hard. This regime-dependence is why gold is a *hedge to size and hold*, not a momentum trade to chase: you hold it through the quiet years precisely so you own it when the loud years arrive, and the loud years are unpredictable.

## Dong deposits: real yield, and how inflation erodes it

Now the asset most Vietnamese savers treat as the safe, sensible default: the bank deposit.

A dong savings account is genuinely useful. It is **liquid** (you can get the money), it is **safe** in nominal terms (the bank owes you your balance, and deposit insurance covers a chunk of it), and it pays a real interest rate of around 5-6% a year on a 12-month term. For an emergency buffer and for money you will need soon, nothing beats it.

The problem is what "safe" means. A deposit is perfectly safe at protecting the **number of dong** you hold. It is not safe at protecting the **purchasing power** of those dong. Two leaks drain the bucket:

1. **Inflation** eats the dong's domestic buying power at ~3-4% a year. A 5.5% deposit rate minus 4% inflation is a real return of only ~1.5%.
2. **Devaluation** eats the dong's value against the dollar at, recently, ~3% a year on average. Measured in dollars — which matters for anyone who imports, travels, sends children to study abroad, or simply thinks in hard money — the deposit's growth nearly vanishes.

It is worth being precise about how these two leaks interact, because savers routinely double-count or miss one. Inflation is the leak you feel at home: your deposit grew 5.5%, but the price of everything you buy rose ~4%, so your *domestic* purchasing power grew only ~1.5%. Devaluation is the leak you feel abroad: your deposit grew 5.5% in dong, but the dong fell ~3% against the dollar, so your *dollar* purchasing power grew only ~2.5%. A saver who imports goods, pays international school fees, or simply wants to preserve hard-money wealth suffers *both* leaks at once, and the combination is brutal: a nominal 5.5% can collapse to a real, hard-money return barely above zero. Over one year that is invisible; over a decade it is the difference between \$6,500 and \$19,000.

There is a deeper reason the deposit so reliably loses the long race, and it is structural rather than bad luck. A bank deposit rate in a managed-currency economy tends to be set just high enough to keep savers from fleeing the currency, but not so high that it chokes off the credit the economy runs on. In practice that means the *real* deposit rate hovers near zero or slightly positive in calm times and goes sharply **negative** whenever inflation spikes — exactly when savers most need protection. In 2008 and 2011, when Vietnamese inflation ran into the high teens, deposit rates lagged behind, so real returns were deeply negative: savers in "safe" deposits were losing perhaps 5-10% of purchasing power a year while their balances grew on paper. The deposit is safest precisely when you need safety least (low-inflation calm) and fails precisely when you need it most (an inflation shock). That asymmetry is why it cannot be the whole of a long-term store of value.

#### Worked example: 100 million VND in a deposit vs in gold

Take the same **100 million VND** (~**\$3,900**) and put it in a 12-month dong deposit, rolled over for a decade at an illustrative **5.5% a year**.

- After ten years of compounding: 100 x (1.055)^10 = about **171 million VND**.
- That *looks* like a 71% gain. But convert it to dollars at the 2026 rate of 26,300: 171 million / 26,300 = about **\$6,500**.
- The same 100 million VND in gold became roughly **500 million VND**, or about **\$19,000**.

So the "safe" deposit turned **\$3,900** into about **\$6,500** of hard money, while gold turned it into about **\$19,000**. In dong the deposit grew; in real, dollar-anchored purchasing power it crawled, because inflation and the dong's slide quietly clawed back most of the interest. The intuition: a dong deposit is a leaky bucket that the bank keeps topping up with interest — over a decade the leaks can win.

The chart makes the gap visceral: the same 100 (2015 = 100) in gold versus in a 5.5% deposit, both in dong terms.

![Gold in dong versus a dong deposit compounded at 5.5 percent both indexed to 100 in 2015](/imgs/blogs/gold-vs-the-dong-real-estate-and-savings-the-vietnamese-allocation-7.png)

None of this means deposits are bad — they are the right tool for liquidity and near-term spending. It means a deposit is a **cash-management** tool, not a long-term **store of value**, and treating it as the latter is the most common and most expensive mistake a Vietnamese saver makes.

The right way to think about the deposit is as the *liquidity engine* of the household, not the *wealth engine*. Money you will need in the next year or two — an emergency fund, a planned purchase, a buffer against losing a job — belongs in a deposit, where its nominal stability is exactly the feature you want and where the slow real erosion does not have time to matter. Money you are storing for a decade or a generation does not belong there, because over those horizons the erosion compounds into the dominant effect. The skill is not "deposits or gold" — it is matching the *time horizon* of the money to the *job* of the asset: short horizon to the deposit's stability, long horizon to gold's and property's inflation protection.

## Real estate: leverage, status, illiquidity, and the boom's risk

If gold is the grandmother's instinct and the deposit is the cautious default, **property is where most Vietnamese household wealth actually lives**. Land and apartments have been the great wealth-builder of the modern Vietnamese economy, and for good reasons — and one dangerous one.

The good reasons. Property is a **real asset**: like gold, its value tends to rise with inflation rather than be eroded by it, because the land and the building cost more to replace as prices climb. It can produce a **rental yield**. And — uniquely among the four — it is bought with **leverage**. If you put 30% down and borrow 70%, and the property rises 20%, your *equity* (your own money) rises far more than 20%, because the gain accrues to your slice while the loan stays fixed. Leverage is the rocket fuel that turned modest down payments into large fortunes during Vietnam's property booms.

To make the leverage concrete: suppose you buy a 2 billion VND apartment with a 600 million VND down payment (30%) and a 1.4 billion VND mortgage. If the apartment rises 20% to 2.4 billion VND, the gain is 400 million VND — but that gain is *all yours*, because the loan is still 1.4 billion VND. Your equity went from 600 million to 1 billion VND, a **67% gain on your own money** from a 20% move in the property. That multiplication is why Vietnamese families who bought property early and held through the booms ended up so far ahead of their deposit-holding neighbours: leverage turned the property's gain into a much larger gain on their cash.

The dangerous one. Leverage works exactly as hard in reverse. If the property *falls* 20% to 1.6 billion VND, the 400 million VND loss again falls entirely on your equity — which drops from 600 million to 200 million VND, a **67% loss on your own money**, while you still owe the bank the full 1.4 billion VND and must keep paying the mortgage out of your income. A 30% fall would wipe your equity out entirely and leave you "underwater" — owing more than the apartment is worth. Property is also brutally **illiquid**: you cannot sell a third of an apartment to cover an emergency, and in a downturn there may be no buyers at any price you will accept for months or years, while the mortgage payment falls due every single month regardless. Vietnam's property market has had real wobbles — periods where prices stalled or fell, developers stumbled, projects stalled half-built, and owners found their "safe" wealth frozen and their mortgages still due.

There is also a quieter risk that Vietnamese buyers know well: the **legal and developer risk** specific to off-plan and project property. Much Vietnamese residential buying is of apartments in projects still under construction, where the buyer pays in instalments before the building exists. If the developer runs into trouble — a financing squeeze, a bond default, a legal hold on the land — the project can stall for years, and the buyer's money is trapped in a half-finished tower with an uncertain title. This is a category of risk that gold and deposits simply do not carry, and it is why "property is safe" deserves more scepticism than it usually gets.

#### Worked example: a down payment, priced in luong of gold

Here is how Vietnamese families have long *felt* the price of property — not in dong, but in **luong of gold**, because gold is the stable yardstick.

Suppose a modest apartment requires a **720 million VND** down payment (30% of a 2.4 billion VND price). In **2020**, when SJC gold was about **56 million VND per luong**, that down payment cost the equivalent of **720 / 56 = about 12.9 luong** of gold — roughly **\$28,000** at the 2020 exchange rate.

By **early 2026**, with SJC gold at about **190 million VND per luong**, the *same* 720 million VND down payment is worth only **720 / 190 = about 3.8 luong** of gold.

In other words, measured in gold, property got dramatically *cheaper* — because gold ran far ahead of property prices over those years. The family that held gold instead of rushing into a down payment in 2020 could, by 2026, buy the same down payment with less than a third of their gold. The intuition: in a hard-money yardstick, the "property only goes up" story is far less obvious than it looks in dong.

Property's job in the allocation is **leveraged growth and a roof over your head** — genuinely powerful, but concentrated, illiquid, and the one asset whose downside can be worse than zero because of the mortgage.

One more honest point about property, because it is where Vietnamese households over-concentrate most. For most families, the home is not really an "allocation choice" at all — it is a life necessity that also happens to be an investment. That dual role is fine, even good: owning your home removes rent, provides stability, and gives inflation protection. The mistake is treating *additional* property — the second apartment, the speculative plot of land bought purely to flip — as if it carried the same safety as the home you live in. A second leveraged property is a concentrated, illiquid, leveraged bet on one local market, with developer and legal risk on top. It can be a fine bet for a household with surplus income to service the debt and a long horizon to ride out a downturn; it is a dangerous bet for a household that has poured its entire net worth into it and would be forced to sell at the bottom. The discipline is to recognise which one you are doing.

## US dollars: the hard-currency hedge and its legal limits

The fourth choice is the quietest: holding **US dollars**.

The dollar's appeal is simple and direct. The dong's slow slide — from ~22,790 per dollar in 2021 to ~26,300 in 2026 — *is* the dollar's gain. Every dong you hold loses a little against the dollar each year; every dollar you hold captures that exactly. Where gold hedges devaluation *indirectly* (gold rises in dong because the dong falls and because global demand rises), the dollar hedges it **directly and precisely**. If your worry is specifically "the dong will keep weakening," dollars are the cleanest expression of that worry.

![The dong's slide USD per VND from about 22790 in 2021 to 26300 in 2026](/imgs/blogs/gold-vs-the-dong-real-estate-and-savings-the-vietnamese-allocation-3.png)

But the dollar has two weaknesses for a Vietnamese saver. First, **it pays almost nothing** if held as physical cash, and Vietnamese banks pay little to no interest on dollar deposits by policy — so unlike a US saver who can earn money-market yields, the Vietnamese dollar-holder gets the hedge but not the yield. This is a deliberate policy choice: the SBV has long kept dollar deposit rates near zero precisely to discourage "dollarisation," the tendency of citizens to abandon the local currency for the dollar. The result is that holding dollars in Vietnam is almost purely a *currency hedge* — you are betting on the dong falling, and earning nothing while you wait, which is a real cost if the dong happens to be stable for a stretch.

Second, and decisively, **the law constrains it**. Vietnam runs foreign-exchange controls: residents cannot freely buy, hold, and move large amounts of foreign currency, contracts inside Vietnam must be priced in dong, and the official channel for converting is regulated. You cannot legally walk into a shop and pay in dollars, you cannot freely accumulate large dollar balances, and moving money in and out of the country is tightly governed. The dollar is a hedge you can use only within limits — which is exactly why gold, which is legal to own freely and behaves like hard money, became the people's substitute for the dollar. Gold is, in effect, the dollar that the law lets you keep: a hard, non-dong store of value you can hold in your own hands, in any quantity, without a bank, without a counterparty, and without running afoul of foreign-exchange rules. Understanding this is the key to understanding why a developing economy with a sliding currency stores so much of its wealth in a metal — it is not superstition, it is a rational response to the legal landscape. (For the broader machinery of how the SBV manages the dong, see [Vietnam's monetary policy](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling); the relationship between gold and the dollar globally is in [gold and the dollar](/blog/trading/gold/gold-and-the-dollar-the-inverse-relationship-and-when-it-breaks).)

#### Worked example: the dong's slide and gold's offset

Imagine two savers in 2021, each holding **100 million VND** (about **\$4,390** at 22,790 dong per dollar).

- **Saver A** keeps it in dong cash. By 2026, the 100 million VND is still 100 million VND, but at 26,300 per dollar it is worth only about **\$3,800** — she lost roughly **13%** of her dollar purchasing power simply by holding the dong, before even counting domestic inflation.
- **Saver B** converts to gold. By 2026 her gold is worth far more in both dong and dollars (the gold-in-dong index roughly tripled over 2021-2026), so the dong's slide that hurt Saver A *helped* Saver B, because gold rises as the dong falls.

The dollar would have done the same protective job as gold against the *currency* leg — turning Saver A's \$4,390 into a preserved \$4,390 rather than a shrunken \$3,800 — but without gold's extra upside from global demand, and with the legal friction of holding foreign currency. The intuition: dollars hedge the dong precisely; gold hedges the dong *and* tends to pay you for the world's broader anxiety, which is why Vietnamese savers reach for the metal.

## How each has performed by era

The reason this allocation is genuinely hard is that **each of the four has won in a different era** — there is no permanent champion, which is the whole case for holding more than one.

- **Through the 1990s and 2000s**, dong deposits often paid double-digit nominal rates, and in the calmer stretches a saver could earn a decent real return while gold did comparatively little. Property in the big cities began its long climb.
- **The 2007-2011 stretch** was a gold and property era: global gold soared, Vietnamese inflation spiked into the high teens and even higher in 2008 and 2011, the dong was devalued repeatedly, and savers who held gold and land were protected while dong cash was savaged.
- **The 2012-2019 stretch** was the deposit's better era relative to gold: global gold fell from its 2011 top and drifted, inflation came down, the dong was more stable, and a 6-7% deposit looked smart against a falling gold price.
- **The 2020-2026 stretch** has been, emphatically, a **gold and hard-asset era again**: gold roughly quintupled in dong terms, the dong slid steadily, property had a boom and then a scare, and dong deposits — for all their apparent safety — lost the real-purchasing-power race badly.

The lesson is not "gold always wins." It is that the *winner rotates with the monetary regime* — high inflation and currency stress favour gold, property, and dollars; calm, disinflationary periods with positive real rates favour deposits. Since you cannot reliably predict the regime, you hold a **mix** sized to which job you need done.

This regime-rotation is the single most important reason not to chase whatever just won. Consider the temptation each era created. In 2011, after gold had soared and inflation had savaged dong cash, the obvious move felt like "all gold, all the time" — and a saver who did that watched gold drift sideways and downward for the next several years while a 7% deposit quietly compounded. In 2019, after years of stable dong and a falling gold price, the obvious move felt like "deposits are fine, forget gold" — and a saver who did that missed gold's quintupling and watched their deposit lose the real race badly through 2020-2026. The asset that has just delivered spectacular returns is often the one about to disappoint, because by then its regime is mature and the next regime is forming. The household that holds all four does not need to call the turn: it always owns the asset that the next regime will favour, and it rebalances *into* the laggard and *out of* the winner, which is precisely the discipline that buys low and sells high without forecasting.

It also helps to see *why* the regimes rotate, because it demystifies the whole thing. The deep driver is the **real interest rate** — the deposit rate minus inflation, or globally, the bond yield minus inflation. When real rates are high and positive, cash and deposits pay you handsomely to wait, and gold (which pays nothing) looks unattractive, so it languishes — the 2012-2019 story. When real rates are low or negative, holding cash is a guaranteed slow loss, so the appeal of a no-yield hard asset like gold *rises* — there is no longer any yield being given up — and gold runs, as in 2001-2011 and 2020-2026. The dong saver feels this through the deposit rate; the global gold price feels it through US real yields. The two are connected, and the master variable behind both is laid out in [real interest rates and the gold price](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price).

## A sensible household allocation

So how should a Vietnamese household actually split, say, **1 billion VND** (about **\$39,000**)? There is no single right answer — it depends on age, wealth, and risk tolerance — but the *framework* is clear, and it follows directly from the jobs we defined.

Start from the jobs, not the percentages:

- You need a **liquidity sleeve** for emergencies and near-term spending — that is the **dong deposit**, regardless of its weak long-run return. Roughly **6-12 months of expenses**.
- You need a **store-of-value sleeve** that survives devaluation and crisis — that is **gold**, the legal, liquid hard-money asset for a Vietnamese saver.
- You need a **growth sleeve** if you have the income to service debt and the stomach for illiquidity — that is **real estate**, ideally your own home first.
- You may want a **hard-currency sleeve** as a direct dong hedge — a modest **dollar** position, within what the law allows.

#### Worked example: splitting 1 billion VND four ways

A balanced mid-career allocation of **1 billion VND** (~**\$39,000**) might look like:

- **Real estate 40%** — 400 million VND (~\$15,600) of equity in a home, often levered with a mortgage so the *property value* is larger.
- **Gold 25%** — 250 million VND (~\$9,750), about **1.3 luong** at the 2026 price of ~190 million VND per luong, as the devaluation-and-crisis store of value.
- **Dong deposit 25%** — 250 million VND (~\$9,750) for yield and liquidity, knowing this sleeve loses the real-return race but wins on access.
- **US dollars 10%** — 100 million VND (~\$3,900) as a direct hard-currency hedge, within legal limits.

The intuition: each slice is sized to a *job*, not to a forecast — you are buying a liquidity job, a hedging job, a growth job, and a hard-currency job, so that no single regime can ruin you.

The percentages are a starting point, not a prescription, and the *order* in which you fill the sleeves matters more than the exact weights. A sensible build order for a Vietnamese household goes roughly like this. First, build the **liquidity sleeve** — six to twelve months of expenses in a dong deposit — before anything else, because without it any shock forces you to sell a long-term asset at the worst possible time. Second, secure the **home**, because owning where you live removes rent and gives inflation protection, and it is the one form of leverage that also delivers a place to sleep. Third, build the **gold sleeve** steadily over time, ideally by buying a fixed dong amount on a regular schedule so you average through the price swings rather than trying to time the lumpy regimes. Fourth and last, add the **dollar sleeve** and any *additional* property only with genuine surplus. The mistake is to invert this order — to pour everything into a second leveraged apartment before the liquidity buffer exists — which is exactly how households end up forced sellers in a downturn.

#### Worked example: the saver who held only deposits

To see the cost of getting the mix wrong, follow a saver who did the "safe" thing and held **everything** in dong deposits. Start with **1 billion VND** (~**\$39,000**) in 2016, rolled at ~5.5% a year.

- By 2026 it compounded to roughly **1.71 billion VND** — a comfortable-looking 71% gain in dong.
- But at the 2026 exchange rate of 26,300 per dollar, that is about **\$65,000** — and adjusting for the cumulative inflation over the decade, its real domestic purchasing power grew only modestly.
- Had the same saver split it into the four-way mix above — with 25% in gold that roughly quintupled — the gold sleeve alone (250 million VND becoming ~1.25 billion VND) would have nearly matched the *entire* all-deposit outcome, before counting the property and the rest.

The intuition: the "safe" all-deposit saver was not safe at all — they made the single largest allocation bet available, a 100% bet on the one asset structurally designed to lose the real-purchasing-power race, and a modest gold sleeve in a diversified mix would have rescued the result.

And the mix should **shift with the saver's stage of life**: a young saver with a small base and a long horizon can tilt hard toward leveraged property and growth; a near-retirement household should tilt toward gold, deposits, and liquidity, because preserving capital matters more than compounding it. The young household can ride out a property downturn because it has decades of income ahead to service the mortgage and rebuild; the near-retirement household cannot, so it trades the higher expected return of leverage for the certainty of capital preservation. This is not timidity — it is matching risk to the length of the runway you have left.

This is the same logic behind the global "own every regime" portfolios — see [gold's job in a portfolio](/blog/trading/gold/golds-job-in-a-portfolio-sizing-rebalancing-and-the-permanent-portfolio) for the rebalancing mechanics — translated into the assets a Vietnamese household can actually hold.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs do the most damage to Vietnamese savers. Each is corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "A dong deposit is the safe choice."** It is safe in *nominal* dong and unsafe in *real purchasing power*. A 100 million VND deposit compounding at 5.5% for a decade became ~171 million VND — but only about **\$6,500** at the 2026 exchange rate, versus roughly **\$19,000** for the same money in gold. Safe in dong, eroded in real terms. A deposit is a cash tool, not a long-run store of value.

**Misconception 2: "Property only goes up."** Priced in gold, property has often gone *down*: a 720 million VND down payment cost about **12.9 luong** of gold in 2020 but only about **3.8 luong** in 2026, because gold ran far ahead. And property is leveraged and illiquid — a 20% price fall can wipe out a 30%-down buyer's equity while the mortgage stays due in full. Property is powerful but it is the asset that can go *below* zero for you.

**Misconception 3: "The prudent move is to put everything in gold."** Gold pays nothing and is lumpy to transact, with an SJC premium and policy overhang on top. An all-gold household has no liquidity sleeve for emergencies and no leveraged growth engine — it has over-insured and under-invested. Gold is the *hedge* in the portfolio, typically a meaningful minority (say 20-30%), not the whole thing.

**Misconception 4: "Dollars are the obvious hedge, so just hold dollars."** Dollars hedge the dong directly, but they pay you almost nothing in Vietnam and are legally constrained — you cannot freely accumulate and transact large foreign-currency balances onshore. That legal reality is precisely why gold became the Vietnamese saver's dollar-substitute: it behaves like hard money but is legal to own freely. (More on why a nation reaches for the metal in [gold in Vietnam](/blog/trading/gold/gold-in-vietnam-why-a-nation-stores-its-wealth-in-the-metal).)

**Misconception 5: "Gold's recent quintupling means it will keep soaring — buy now."** This is the most dangerous misconception precisely because it feels like the lesson of the post. It is not. Gold's dong-terms return came in lumpy regimes, and it has spent long stretches — most of 1980-2000 — going *nowhere* while savers waited. The 2024 SJC blow-out is a live warning: savers who panic-bought into a 25% premium spike paid far above the world value and then watched the SBV compress the premium. The right way to own gold is as a *steadily accumulated, long-held hedge sized to a job* — not as a momentum trade chased after it has already run. The case for gold in this post is structural (a sliding currency, a legal hard-money asset, an insurance role in a mix), not a prediction that the recent run continues; treating it as the latter is how savers buy tops.

**Misconception 6: "Diversifying is for the rich; I have too little to split four ways."** The opposite is true. The smaller your savings, the *less* you can afford a single concentrated bet that goes wrong, because you have no cushion to recover. A household with 100 million VND can still hold a sensible mix — a deposit buffer, some gold (even a fraction of a luong, or gold rings), and its home equity — and adding even a modest gold sleeve to an otherwise all-deposit position would have dramatically improved the decade's outcome, as the worked examples show. Diversification is not a luxury for large portfolios; it is the cheapest insurance available to small ones.

## How it shows up in real markets

These are not abstractions; they showed up in dated, specific Vietnamese market events.

**2008 and 2011 — inflation savages dong cash.** Vietnamese consumer inflation spiked into the high teens around 2008 and again in 2011, while the dong was devalued repeatedly. A saver in a 12% deposit watched real purchasing power fall as prices rose ~20%; gold, which roughly tripled globally from 2007 to its 2011 top, was the asset that held. This is the historical memory that makes Vietnamese families instinctively reach for gold when inflation flares.

**May 2024 — the SJC gold blow-out.** Domestic SJC gold spiked to about **92.4 million VND per luong** (roughly **\$7,000** per luong, far above the world-price equivalent) as Vietnamese savers piled into the metal amid currency and confidence anxiety. The premium over the world price blew out to roughly a quarter — meaning a Vietnamese buyer was paying about 25% more than the global gold value for the same metal, purely because domestic demand overwhelmed the constrained domestic supply of SJC bars. The SBV then intervened with direct gold sales through the state-owned banks, pulling the SJC price back toward a ~77 million VND floor and compressing the premium toward single digits. The episode is the clearest modern proof of three things at once: that Vietnamese households treat gold, not the dollar, as the panic hedge of first resort; that the domestic premium is a real, sometimes large cost the allocator must account for; and that gold in Vietnam carries a *policy* risk — the state can and does intervene in its price — that the global metal does not. For the allocation, the takeaway is not to avoid gold but to buy it steadily over time rather than panic-buying into a premium spike, and to size it as a long-term hedge rather than a short-term trade.

![SJC gold bar price in million VND per luong from 2020 to 2026 with the 2024 spike and 2026 high](/imgs/blogs/gold-vs-the-dong-real-estate-and-savings-the-vietnamese-allocation-5.png)

**2021-2026 — the slow dong slide and the gold record.** Over this stretch the dong weakened from ~22,790 to ~26,300 per dollar while global gold made repeated records, carrying SJC gold to an all-time high near **190 million VND per luong** in January 2026 (about **\$6,000** per troy-ounce equivalent). The dong-cash saver lost ~13% of dollar purchasing power on the FX move alone; the gold and dollar holders were protected. This is the era that re-taught a generation of Vietnamese savers the lesson their grandparents already knew.

**The property scare.** Across the same window, Vietnam's property market ran hot and then stumbled — developer stress, a corporate-bond squeeze that hit several large developers, stalled projects, and stretches where owners simply could not sell at the prices they expected. Households that had concentrated everything into a single leveraged apartment discovered the illiquidity risk in real time: their net worth was frozen in an asset they could not sell and could not partly liquidate, while the mortgage payment still fell due each month. Those who had *also* held gold had a liquid hedge they could actually access — they could sell a few luong to cover a cash need without touching the apartment, exactly the role the gold sleeve is supposed to play. This is the diversification argument made painfully concrete: the value of holding more than one asset is not abstract, it is the ability to raise cash from the *liquid* sleeve when the *illiquid* sleeve is frozen.

**The everyday version.** Step back from the dramatic dates and the same pattern runs through ordinary Vietnamese household finance every year. The family that kept its long-term savings entirely in a dong deposit watched its real wealth quietly erode. The family that bought gold at weddings and held it for decades found, often to its surprise, that the "unproductive" metal had outpaced the "sensible" deposit. The family that bought a home early and held it built real wealth through leverage — and the family that over-extended into a second speculative property at the top learned the cost of illiquidity and debt. None of these are extreme scenarios; they are the median Vietnamese financial experience of the last two decades, and they all point the same way: the assets that protect against a sliding currency have, era after era, beaten the asset that *is* the sliding currency.

## The takeaway

The Vietnamese allocation question is not "stocks vs bonds." It is **gold vs the dong deposit vs property vs dollars**, and the deep insight is that each one does a *different job* and *wins in a different monetary regime* — so the saver's task is not to pick the winner but to size the jobs.

Hold the **deposit** for liquidity, and never mistake it for a store of value: it is the leaky bucket the bank keeps topping up, and over a decade the leaks of inflation and devaluation can win. Hold **gold** as the legal, liquid, hard-money hedge that protects you when the dong slides and the world panics — the asset that quintupled in dong terms while doing nothing, because it is monetary insurance, not an investment that compounds. Hold **property** for leveraged growth and a home, with clear eyes about its illiquidity and the fact that leverage can take your equity below zero. And hold a modest sleeve of **dollars** where the law allows, as the cleanest direct bet against the dong.

If there is a single mental adjustment this post is asking for, it is this: **stop measuring your wealth only in dong.** A saver who tracks only the dong number on a bank statement is using a ruler that quietly shrinks every year — of course the number always seems to grow, because the unit it is measured in keeps getting smaller. Measure your wealth in something stable: in dollars, in gold, in the number of bowls of pho or square metres of housing it can buy. Seen through that lens, the "safe" deposit stops looking safe and the "unproductive" gold stops looking unproductive, and the real shape of the allocation problem comes into focus. The dong is a fine medium of exchange — you spend in it, you keep your near-term money in it — but it is a poor long-term store of value, and the household that understands the difference holds its spending money in dong and its *stored* wealth in things the dong cannot erode.

The corollary is that you do not need to be a market forecaster to get this right. You do not need to predict the next gold cycle, call the property top, or guess where the dong settles. You need only to recognise that you cannot predict any of those things, and to build a mix robust to all of them: liquidity you can reach, a hard-money hedge you hold through the quiet years, a home and perhaps leveraged growth sized to your income, and a hard-currency sleeve within the law. Then you rebalance occasionally — trimming whatever has run far ahead, topping up whatever has lagged — and let the regimes rotate around a portfolio that always owns the asset the next regime will favour.

The grandmother in our opening was not being old-fashioned. She understood, without the vocabulary, the one truth this whole series keeps returning to: **gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money.** In a country whose currency slides a little every year, that insurance has, era after era, quietly out-earned the "safe" choice. The sophisticated move is not to copy her entirely — it is to keep her gold *and* add the liquidity, the growth, and the hard-currency hedge she never had, each sized to the job only it can do.
