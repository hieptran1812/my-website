> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Since 2022, central banks have bought more than 1,000 tonnes of gold a year, and unlike investors, they do not care what it costs — that price-insensitive bid is the single biggest change in the modern gold market.
>
> - For most of the 2000s and 2010s central banks were a sideshow or net *sellers*; gold's price was set by Western investors reacting to real interest rates.
> - In February 2022 the West froze roughly \$300 billion of Russia's central-bank reserves overnight. Every reserve manager on earth learned the same lesson: dollars and bonds can be switched off; gold cannot.
> - Central banks buy gold because it is no one else's liability — sanction-proof, default-proof, and impossible to inflate away. They buy it whatever the price.
> - This put a floor under gold and let it rise in 2022-24 even as real yields climbed to +2%, breaking the old rule that high real rates sink gold.
> - **The one number to remember:** central banks absorbed about **29% of all newly mined gold in 2024** (1,045 of 3,661 tonnes) — up from roughly 13% before 2022.

On the morning of 26 February 2022, two days after Russian tanks rolled into Ukraine, the finance ministers of the G7 published a short joint statement. Buried in the diplomatic language was a sentence that would reshape the gold market for a decade: they would "impose restrictive measures that will prevent the Russian Central Bank from deploying its international reserves." In plain English, they switched off Russia's money.

The Central Bank of Russia had spent years building a war chest — roughly \$630 billion of foreign-exchange reserves, the fourth-largest stockpile on the planet. About half of it sat in the form most reserve managers had always treated as the safest assets in the world: US Treasury bonds, euro-denominated bonds, deposits at Western banks, and balances at the Bank for International Settlements. Overnight, around \$300 billion of that — the part held in dollars, euros, pounds, and yen — became unusable. Not seized, exactly. Just frozen. The bonds still existed; Russia simply could no longer sell them, spend them, or move them. A central bank's most liquid assets had been rendered as useful as a locked safe to which someone else holds the key.

Every other central bank watched this happen, and almost all of them quietly reached the same conclusion. The dollar and the bonds denominated in it are not unconditionally safe. They are *safe until the issuer decides you are an enemy.* And there is exactly one reserve asset in the world that no foreign government can switch off, because it is no one's promise to pay: a bar of gold sitting in your own vault. What followed was the largest sustained wave of official gold buying since the gold standard. This post is about that buying — why it happened, who is doing it, and why it changed how the gold price itself behaves.

![February 2022 turning point: before and after central banks shifted from paper reserves to gold](/imgs/blogs/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022-1.png)

In the [demand map of who owns gold](/blog/trading/gold/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry), we introduced central banks as one of four buckets of demand and floated the idea of the "marginal buyer" — the participant whose decision to buy or sell one more tonne actually moves the price. That post named central banks; this one goes after the *why*, the *data*, and the *consequences*. By the end you will understand why a price-insensitive government buyer is a fundamentally different animal from a hedge fund, and why this single shift is, in my view, the most important regime change in gold since the United States severed the dollar's link to the metal in 1971.

I want to be precise about the claim, because it is easy to overstate and easy to dismiss, and the truth sits in between. I am *not* claiming central banks are the only thing that matters for gold — real rates, the dollar, inflation expectations, and crisis flows all still move the price day to day. What I *am* claiming is that the *baseline* has changed: there is now a large, persistent, price-blind buyer underneath the market that was not there before, and its presence changes how every other force translates into price. It is the difference between a swimmer in a calm pool and a swimmer in a rising tide — the strokes are the same, but the water level is no longer where it used to be. That rising water level is the official sector, and the rest of this post is about where it came from, who is pouring it in, and why it is unlikely to drain any time soon.

## Foundations: what reserves are and why gold is the one asset no one can switch off

Before we can talk about why central banks buy gold, we need three plain-language building blocks: what foreign-exchange reserves are, why a central bank holds *any* assets at all, and what makes gold structurally different from everything else in the vault.

### What "reserves" actually means

Imagine a country as a household that earns and spends in a foreign currency. Vietnam, for instance, earns dollars when it exports phones and coffee, and needs dollars to import oil, machinery, and chips. The State Bank of Vietnam keeps a stash of foreign money — dollars, euros, gold — so that the country can always pay for imports, service its foreign debts, and defend the dong if it comes under attack. That stash is the country's **foreign-exchange reserves**: the national rainy-day fund, held by the central bank on behalf of the whole economy.

Crucially, reserves are not piles of physical banknotes in a basement. A central bank does not want \$630 billion sitting idle as paper — it wants the money to be *safe*, *liquid* (sellable instantly), and ideally to *earn a little interest*. So historically it parks the reserves in the safest, most liquid interest-bearing instrument on earth: short- and medium-term government bonds, overwhelmingly US Treasuries. A "Treasury" is simply an IOU from the US government — a printed promise that says "the United States will pay you \$1,000 on this date, plus interest along the way." It is considered the global risk-free asset because the US has never defaulted and can always print the dollars it owes.

That last clause is the catch, and it is the whole story. A Treasury is safe *as an investment* — but it is still someone else's promise, recorded on someone else's ledger, settled through someone else's banking system. Its safety depends entirely on the goodwill (or at least the indifference) of the country that issued it.

It helps to be concrete about what "switched off" really means, because it is more subtle than confiscation. When a central bank holds US Treasuries, it does not have the bonds in a drawer. It has an *entry on a ledger* — usually at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, or at a custodian bank, or recorded in an account at a clearing system. The "asset" is fundamentally a database row that says "this institution is owed this much." A sanctions order does not take the bonds away; it instructs every institution under Western jurisdiction to refuse to act on that database row. The custodian will not transfer the bonds. The bank will not release the cash. The clearing system will not settle the trade. The bonds still legally belong to you, the coupons still technically accrue, and none of it does you any good, because every door you would use to turn that ledger entry into spendable money has been locked from the outside. This is what makes the modern reserve so fragile in a way the gold-standard-era reserve was not: it is access-dependent, and access is granted by your potential adversary.

### Why a central bank holds assets at all

It is worth pausing on *why* a central bank bothers holding reserves, because the purpose dictates the asset choice. A central bank holds reserves for three jobs. First, **to pay for essential imports** when private dollar earnings dry up — a country that cannot buy oil or food or medicine in a crisis faces a real catastrophe, so it keeps a buffer. Second, **to defend its currency**: if the dong or the lira or the ruble starts collapsing, the central bank can sell dollars and buy its own currency to slow the fall — but only if it has dollars to sell. Third, **to service foreign debt**: loans denominated in dollars must be repaid in dollars, whatever the exchange rate.

Notice that all three jobs are about *crises* — the import crunch, the currency attack, the debt-repayment squeeze. Reserves are, by their entire reason for existing, a crisis tool. And here is the uncomfortable realization that 2022 forced: the very crises in which a country most needs its reserves — a war, a sanctions confrontation, a geopolitical rupture — are precisely the crises in which dollar reserves are most likely to be frozen. The asset designed to save you in an emergency turned out to be most vulnerable in exactly the emergencies that matter. That is a devastating flaw in the logic of paper reserves, and gold is the obvious patch.

### What makes gold different: it is no one's liability

Here is the single most important sentence in this entire post. **Gold is the only major reserve asset that is not simultaneously someone else's liability.**

Unpack that. A dollar in a bank account is the bank's liability — a promise to pay you. A Treasury bond is the US government's liability. A euro deposit is a European bank's liability. Every form of paper money and every bond is, by construction, a claim on some institution, and that institution can fail, freeze your account, inflate the value away, or be ordered by its government to deny you access. Gold has no such issuer. An ounce of gold in your own vault is not a promise from anyone — it is the thing itself. There is no counterparty who can default on it, no central bank that can print more of it to dilute you, and no foreign government that can press a button and make it disappear from your balance sheet.

This is why we keep returning to the spine of this whole series: gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy. And insurance, by its nature, is the asset you want most precisely when the institutions around you are failing or turning hostile. A reserve manager who buys gold is not chasing a return. They are buying the one asset that survives the failure of trust itself.

For most of modern history, that insurance felt unnecessary. Trust in the dollar system was so total that holding a 0%-yielding metal instead of an interest-paying Treasury looked like leaving money on the table. The events of 2022 changed the price of that trust — and once you re-price trust, you re-price gold.

There is one more property of gold worth nailing down here, because it is the quiet workhorse of the whole thesis: **fungibility and universal acceptance.** A bar of London Good Delivery gold is identical to every other bar of London Good Delivery gold, recognized and priced the same in Zurich, Shanghai, Dubai, and Mumbai. It does not need a functioning SWIFT connection, a friendly correspondent bank, or a sanctions waiver to be worth what it is worth. If you are a central bank that has just been cut off from the Western financial system, your frozen Treasuries are worth nothing *to you*, but your gold can still be sold to a non-aligned buyer, pledged as collateral, or shipped physically across a border. Gold is the only reserve asset that retains its full value and full liquidity *even after you have been excommunicated from the dollar system.* For a country that fears excommunication, that is not a nice-to-have. It is the entire point.

## The old regime: when central banks were the *sellers*

To feel how big the post-2022 turn is, you have to understand what came before it. For roughly a quarter-century, central banks were not the heroes of the gold story. They were the villains — or at least the steady, relentless sellers who capped the price.

In the late 1990s, the prevailing wisdom in Western finance ministries was that gold was a *barbarous relic*, an embarrassing leftover from the gold-standard era that earned no interest and just sat there costing money to store and insure. Real interest rates were high and positive, inflation was tamed, the dollar reigned supreme, and the "Great Moderation" made financial crises look like history. Why hold a sterile yellow metal when you could hold US Treasuries paying 5%? So the European central banks — which between them held the largest stockpiles outside the US — started selling.

The selling got so disorderly that it threatened to crash the price and destabilize the market, so in 1999 the major holders signed the **Central Bank Gold Agreement (CBGA)** — a pact to *cap and coordinate* their sales so they didn't trample each other on the way out. Read that again: the central banks of Europe formally agreed on how much gold they would be allowed to dump each year. That is the mindset of the old regime in a single fact.

The most famous episode of the era was Britain's. Between 1999 and 2002, the UK Treasury, under Chancellor Gordon Brown, sold roughly 395 tonnes — about *half* of the nation's gold reserves — at an average price near \$275 an ounce, and it announced the auctions in advance, which drove the price down before each sale. Gold bottomed at around \$252 in 1999, almost exactly when Brown started selling. The metal then rose more than tenfold over the following two decades. The episode is now known, with no affection whatsoever, as **"Brown's Bottom."** It is the canonical case study in selling insurance at the exact moment you should have been buying it.

What is fascinating, looking back, is how *rational* the selling felt at the time. This was the height of the Great Moderation — inflation conquered, the dollar dominant, the internet booming, the [Dow/gold ratio](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock) screaming that stocks were the future and metal was the past. In 1999 it took about 42 ounces of gold to buy the Dow Jones index; in 1980 it had taken barely more than one. By that measure gold had lost something like 97% of its value relative to equities over two decades. A reserve manager in 1999 was not being stupid by selling gold — they were extrapolating the most powerful trend of their careers. The trouble with extrapolating a trend is that trends are loudest right before they reverse, and "everyone agrees gold is useless" is precisely the sentiment that marks a generational bottom. The official sector sold the bottom not because the people running it were foolish, but because the consensus they belonged to had become so total that the contrarian trade looked insane. Keep that dynamic in mind, because the post-2022 buying may be the mirror image: an official sector that, having been burned, may keep buying past the point of obvious value, for the same herd-and-narrative reasons that made it sell past the point of obvious value a generation earlier.

![Central bank net gold purchases by year, net sellers before 2010 turning to a record buyer after 2022](/imgs/blogs/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022-2.png)

The chart above tells the whole arc in one shape. Look at the red bars on the left: from 1999 to 2009, central banks were *net sellers*, disgorging hundreds of tonnes a year — a peak of 663 tonnes of net selling in 2005 alone. During this decade, the official sector was a drag on the gold price, a permanent supply overhang. If you were trying to forecast gold in 2005, "what will central banks dump this year?" was a real and bearish question.

#### Worked example: the cost of Brown's Bottom

Let us put a number on selling insurance at the wrong time. Britain sold roughly 395 tonnes between 1999 and 2002 at an average of about \$275/oz. There are about 32,151 troy ounces in a metric tonne, so 395 tonnes is roughly 12.7 million ounces. The proceeds were therefore about 12.7 million × \$275 ≈ **\$3.5 billion**.

Now value that same 395 tonnes at the 2024 average price of \$2,388/oz: 12.7 million × \$2,388 ≈ **\$30.3 billion**. And at the January 2026 all-time high near \$5,589/oz, it would be worth roughly **\$71 billion**.

So the UK swapped an asset now worth \$30-71 billion for \$3.5 billion in cash, which it largely used to buy interest-bearing bonds. Even compounding that \$3.5 billion at the bond yields of the era, the gold leg ran away from the cash leg by a factor of several. **The lesson reserve managers absorbed: gold is the asset you regret selling, because you only need it in the world where everything else has gone wrong.**

## The turn: emerging markets start accumulating, then 2022 detonates

The old regime did not end all at once. It ended in two stages, and only the second stage is the one this post is really about.

**Stage one (2009-2021): the emerging-market accumulation.** After the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, a different set of central banks started doing the opposite of Europe. China, Russia, India, Turkey, and a long tail of emerging-market central banks began *buying*. Their motives were already partly political — Russia in particular accelerated its buying after the 2014 Crimea sanctions, an early, smaller rehearsal of what would come — but mostly it was straightforward diversification. These countries had accumulated enormous dollar reserves from trade surpluses and commodity exports, and holding 100% of the national savings in the currency of a single, distant, sometimes-adversarial superpower started to look imprudent. By 2010 the official sector flipped from net seller to net buyer, and it stayed there, averaging about 473 tonnes a year from 2010 to 2021 — the amber dashed line on the chart above. Respectable, steady, but not yet a market-defining force.

**Stage two (2022 onward): the freeze, and the acceleration.** Then came February 2022, and the buying roughly *doubled*. Look at the green bars on the right of the chart: 1,082 tonnes in 2022 (the highest annual total since at least 1950), 1,037 in 2023, 1,045 in 2024. Three consecutive years above 1,000 tonnes — something that had simply never happened in the modern era. The pre-2022 average of 473 tonnes a year became the new *floor*, not the ceiling. A steady diversification trend turned, almost overnight, into a structural stampede.

The catalyst was not a change in interest rates, inflation, or the dollar. It was a change in *fear* — specifically, the fear of one's reserves being weaponized. And that distinction matters enormously, because fear of weaponization does not go away when interest rates rise. It is a one-way ratchet. Once you have seen that your reserves can be frozen, you cannot un-see it, and no level of bond yield fully compensates you for that risk again.

It is worth dwelling on *why* the acceleration was a doubling and not a 10% bump, because the size is the tell. If 2022 had merely been another year of normal diversification, you would expect the trend to continue at its existing pace — maybe 500 tonnes, maybe 550. Instead it leapt to 1,082. A change of that magnitude does not come from a marginal adjustment to existing thinking; it comes from a *category shift* in how reserve managers classify the risk. Before 2022, "my dollar reserves get frozen" sat in the same mental bucket as "an asteroid hits the bank" — theoretically possible, not worth planning around. After February 2022, it moved into the bucket of "things that demonstrably happen to large, sophisticated central banks." When a tail risk migrates from the unthinkable bucket to the observed bucket, the rational response is not a small hedge — it is a wholesale rebalancing. The doubling is the fingerprint of that reclassification happening across dozens of institutions at once.

And note what the buying did *not* respond to. Across 2022-2024, the conditions that normally govern gold were almost uniformly hostile: real interest rates rose hard, the dollar was strong, and the Fed was running the most aggressive tightening cycle in forty years. Every one of those is, historically, a reason for gold to fall. The official sector bought through all of it. That is the single most important behavioral fact in this post — a buyer that ignores the entire traditional sell signal — and we will return to its market consequences at length below.

## Why they buy: sanction-proofing, de-dollarization, and diversification

Reserve managers are not romantics. When they pivot \$100 billion of a nation's savings, they have hard institutional reasons. Post-2022, three of them dominate, and the freeze sits underneath all three.

### Sanction-proofing: the asset that cannot be confiscated

This is the new motive, and it is the loudest. The 2022 freeze demonstrated, in a way no white paper ever could, that holding your reserves in dollars and euros means holding them at the pleasure of Washington and Brussels. For any country that is *not* a firm Western ally — and that is most of the world's population — this is now an existential risk to the national savings. China, with over \$3 trillion in reserves, looked at Russia and saw a live demonstration of what could happen to its own war chest in a confrontation over Taiwan.

Gold solves this problem completely, *if you hold it physically at home.* A gold bar in a vault in Beijing or Mumbai or Ankara cannot be frozen by a US sanctions order, because no US institution touches it. There is no account to switch off, no bank to lean on, no settlement system to deny. This is why we are seeing not just gold *buying* but gold *repatriation* — countries physically shipping their bullion home from the traditional storage hubs in London and New York. An ounce of gold stored in the Bank of England is sanction-vulnerable in a way that the same ounce in your own basement is not.

The repatriation point is subtle but important, and it sharpens the whole thesis. For decades, central banks were perfectly happy to store their gold abroad — much of the world's official gold has historically sat in the vaults of the Bank of England and the New York Fed, because London and New York are the deepest, most liquid trading hubs, and gold stored there can be lent and traded without the cost and risk of shipping bars around. Germany famously stored a large share of its reserves in New York and Paris throughout the Cold War, on the logic that if the Soviets ever invaded, the gold would be safer on the other side of the Atlantic. The post-2014, post-2022 wave of repatriations — Germany, Austria, the Netherlands, Hungary, Poland, and others bringing bullion home — reflects a different fear. The threat is no longer a foreign invasion that might overrun your domestic vault; it is your *custodian* turning hostile. Gold held in London is only sanction-proof until London decides you are the adversary. So the logic of sanction-proofing pushes not just toward owning gold, but toward owning gold *you physically control on your own soil* — which is why the buying and the repatriating are two faces of the same impulse.

### De-dollarization: trimming a dangerous concentration

The second motive is the slow, structural drift away from over-reliance on the dollar. As covered in [why the dollar rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) and the [petrodollar system](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance), the US dollar still dominates global reserves — but its share has been quietly slipping, from over 70% at the turn of the century toward the high 50s today. Central banks are not abandoning the dollar; they are *diversifying around* it. Gold is the natural diversifier because, unlike switching from dollars to euros or yen (just trading one Western liability for another), gold escapes the entire liability-and-sanctions system at once.

### Diversification and the inflation worry

The third motive is the oldest and least political: gold is genuinely uncorrelated with the bonds and currencies that make up the rest of the reserve portfolio, and it has historically held its purchasing power through episodes of inflation and currency debasement. After the inflation spike of 2021-22, when the supposedly "safe" long-dated Treasury portion of reserves lost enormous value as yields rose, gold's appeal as a non-correlated store of value got a second, powerful boost. A reserve manager who watched their bond holdings drop while gold held firm in 2022 needed no further argument.

This diversification motive deserves more weight than it usually gets, because 2022 was a genuinely traumatic year for the "safe" part of a reserve portfolio. The whole point of holding long-dated government bonds is that they are supposed to be the ballast — the thing that holds its value when everything else falls. But in 2022, as central banks hiked rates to fight inflation, long-dated Treasuries fell about 31% — one of the worst years for "safe" government bonds in recorded history. A reserve manager holding a conventional portfolio watched their supposedly conservative bond allocation deliver an equity-sized loss, while gold ended the year essentially flat (down a fraction of a percent in dollar terms, and *up* in most other currencies). For an institution whose mandate is capital preservation, that was a five-alarm fire. The bond ballast had failed in exactly the way it was never supposed to, and the 0%-yielding relic had done the one job — *not losing value* — that the bonds were paid to do. Add the sanction story on top of that, and the case for gold went from "a sensible 5% diversifier" to "the asset we were most wrong about."

There is a fourth, quieter motive worth naming: **gold has no issuer who can devalue it by printing more.** A central bank holding euros is exposed to the European Central Bank's printing press; a central bank holding dollars is exposed to the Fed's. Over the 2020-2022 period, the major Western central banks expanded their balance sheets enormously — the Fed roughly doubled its assets — and reserve managers around the world watched the issuers of their reserve currencies create trillions of new units of those currencies. You do not have to be a goldbug to find that unsettling: if the thing you hold can be diluted at the issuer's discretion, then your reserve is only as sound as the issuer's restraint. Gold's supply grows by roughly 1.7% a year no matter what any government wants, and that hard cap is its own quiet argument. We develop this debasement angle fully in [gold as an anti-currency](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money); here it is enough to note that it stacks on top of the sanction and diversification motives rather than competing with them.

![Reserve manager checklist comparing a US Treasury against a gold bar across liability, freezability, inflation, yield, and settlement](/imgs/blogs/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022-5.png)

The figure above is the reserve manager's decision in a single table. Notice that for *almost* every property, the thing that makes a Treasury convenient is exactly the thing that makes it controllable by someone else. It is someone's IOU (can default), it can be frozen (sanctions), it can be inflated away (the issuer prints), and it settles through the issuer's banking system (which can deny you). Gold reverses all of these — at the cost of one thing: it pays no yield. The entire post-2022 story is reserve managers deciding that, after the freeze, *sovereignty is worth more than yield.*

#### Worked example: the freeze, in incentive terms

Put yourself in the seat of a non-aligned central banker in March 2022. You watch \$300 billion of Russia's reserves get frozen. Your own country holds, say, \$200 billion, of which \$120 billion is in dollar and euro bonds.

What is the expected cost of *not* diversifying? If there is even a 10% chance over the next decade that your country ends up on the wrong side of a Western sanctions decision, your expected loss is 10% × \$120 billion = **\$12 billion** of suddenly-unusable reserves. What is the cost of diversifying into gold? Gold yields 0% while Treasuries yield roughly 4.5%, so moving \$120 billion into gold costs you about \$120bn × 4.5% = **\$5.4 billion a year** in forgone interest.

Now the calculation is no longer obvious — it is a live tradeoff between a recurring yield cost and a catastrophic tail risk. But here is the asymmetry that tips it: the forgone yield is a known, survivable cost, while the frozen reserves are an unrecoverable loss in the exact scenario where you need your reserves most. **For a manager who now believes the tail risk is real, paying the yield cost is cheap insurance — and that is the precise mental shift that drove the post-2022 buying.**

## Who is buying — and the "room to buy" that makes this durable

So who, concretely, is doing all this buying? The honor roll of the post-2022 era is dominated by exactly the countries you would expect from the sanction-proofing logic.

![Official gold reserves by holder in tonnes, United States largest with emerging market accumulators highlighted](/imgs/blogs/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022-3.png)

The chart of who *holds* the most gold still looks like the old world: the United States sits on top with 8,134 tonnes, followed by the legacy European holders — Germany (3,352), Italy (2,452), France (2,437) — relics of the Bretton Woods era when these nations ran trade surpluses with America and took payment in metal. But notice the bars highlighted in blue, which are the post-2022 accumulators: **Russia** (2,336 tonnes), **China** (2,280 official tonnes, almost certainly an understatement), **India** (876), **Turkey** (595), and **Poland** (448). These are the countries adding fastest. Poland's central bank, sitting on NATO's eastern flank and watching the war next door, has been one of the single most aggressive buyers in the world, explicitly framing gold as a hedge against geopolitical catastrophe.

A word on China's number, because it is a story in itself. The official figure of around 2,280 tonnes is widely believed to *understate* China's true holdings, possibly by a large margin. China reports its gold reserves only sporadically, and there are persistent indications that it accumulates gold through state-controlled channels and the State Administration of Foreign Exchange without immediately reporting it to the IMF, then "announces" jumps in official holdings later. Analysts have for years tracked discrepancies between China's gold imports, its domestic mine output (the world's largest, at about 370 tonnes a year), and its reported reserve growth — the metal seems to go in faster than the official number rises. Whatever the precise figure, the direction is unambiguous: China has been a steady, deliberate accumulator, and it has every strategic incentive — given the Taiwan tension and the live demonstration of what happened to Russia — to keep buying. The opacity itself is informative: a country that wanted to *reassure* markets about its dollar commitment would not quietly stockpile the one asset that is a hedge against the dollar.

Turkey and India illustrate two other flavors of the same trend. Turkey's buying has been more erratic — it buys aggressively, occasionally sells to manage a domestic currency crisis, then resumes — reflecting an economy under chronic inflationary and FX stress, where gold serves as both reserve diversifier and domestic-market shock absorber. India, by contrast, has been a patient, consistent buyer, methodically lifting its gold share and repatriating bullion from London, motivated less by acute sanction fear than by a long-term strategic judgment that a multipolar world calls for a more neutral reserve mix. Different countries, different precise motives — but all of them pulling in the same direction, which is what makes the aggregate so powerful.

But the holdings chart is the *past*. The chart that tells you about the *future* is the next one — the share of each country's reserves that is actually in gold.

![Gold as a share of total reserves, Western holders near 70 percent versus China 6 percent and India 11 percent](/imgs/blogs/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022-4.png)

This is the "room to buy" argument, and it is why the structural bid is likely to persist for years rather than fade. The United States, Germany, France, and Italy already hold roughly 68-72% of their reserves in gold — they are *done* diversifying; they cannot meaningfully add. But the big emerging-market reserve holders are barely started. China holds only about **6%** of its reserves in gold. India about 11%. Even Russia, an aggressive buyer for a decade, is only at 29%. These countries hold trillions of dollars of reserves and a tiny sliver of it in the one asset that is sanction-proof. If they merely drift toward the level the West considers normal, the buying has years to run.

#### Worked example: if China moved gold from 6% to 20% of reserves

Let us size the "room to buy" with China, the biggest case. China holds roughly \$3.2 trillion in total reserves. At 6%, its gold holding is worth about 0.06 × \$3.2 trillion = **\$192 billion**. Suppose China decided to move gold to 20% of reserves — still below where Russia already sits, and far below the Western ~70%. That target is 0.20 × \$3.2 trillion = **\$640 billion**, so China would need to *buy* about \$640bn − \$192bn = **\$448 billion** of additional gold.

At \$2,400 an ounce, \$448 billion buys about \$448bn ÷ \$2,400 ≈ 187 million ounces, which at 32,151 ounces per tonne is roughly **5,800 tonnes**. That is about **1.6 times the entire world's annual mine production** of 3,661 tonnes. Spread over five years to avoid moving the price against itself, it is about **1,160 tonnes a year — from a single country.**

In other words, just one central bank, executing a modest reallocation, could match the *entire* recent record of global official buying *by itself, for years.* **That is the scale of latent demand sitting in the gap between a 6% gold share and a 20% one — and it is why the post-2022 bid is structural, not a one-year spasm.**

This is also why the structural bid is partly self-limiting in a healthy way, which is worth understanding so you do not over-extrapolate. The "room to buy" is enormous *because* gold is currently a small share of EM reserves, but every tonne bought, and every rise in the gold price, mechanically lifts that share toward the Western norm. As China's gold share climbs from 6% toward 20%, the marginal urgency to add fades — the insurance is increasingly bought, and the remaining gap is smaller. So the trend is not infinite; it is a multi-year *convergence* toward a higher equilibrium gold share, not a permanent one-way escalator. The bid is durable on the horizon that matters for an investor (years, not weeks), but it is not a perpetual-motion machine, and the day to start worrying about it is the day the big EM holders approach the gold share the West already considers normal. We are, in 2026, nowhere near that day — but it is the right thing to watch for, and we return to it in the playbook section.

## How this changes price dynamics: a buyer who ignores the price

Now we get to the part that matters most for anyone trying to understand the gold *price*, not just gold *politics*. A central bank is not just a big buyer. It is a *different kind* of buyer, and the difference rewrites the rules of how gold trades.

Think about the two types of gold buyer. A Western investor — a hedge fund, an ETF holder, a wealth manager — buys gold as a *trade*. They are weighing it against alternatives: if real interest rates rise, holding a 0%-yielding metal becomes more expensive relative to holding a 4.5%-yielding Treasury, so they sell gold and buy bonds. This is the textbook inverse relationship between gold and [real yields](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything) that has governed the gold price for decades: rates up, gold down. The investor is, by definition, *price-sensitive and rate-sensitive.* They will sell if the price gets too high or the opportunity cost gets too steep.

A central bank buying for sanction-proofing is the opposite. It is *price-insensitive.* It is not comparing gold's return to the return on Treasuries — it is buying gold *because of* a risk that Treasuries carry and gold does not. It does not have a target price at which it stops; it has a target *share of reserves.* If gold goes up, that does not make it sell — if anything, a rising gold price *raises* the value of the diversification it already holds and confirms the thesis. This buyer keeps buying through a rising market, through high real rates, through every signal that would make an investor pause.

![The structural floor diagram, central bank buying overrides the real rate seesaw that used to set the gold price](/imgs/blogs/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022-7.png)

The figure above is the mechanism. The old gold price was a *seesaw* balanced on real interest rates: rates up tipped the price down (Western funds sold), rates down tipped it up. The post-2022 central-bank bid is not on the seesaw at all — it is a *floor* placed underneath it. When real rates rise and Western investors sell, the central banks are there to buy what the West dumps, at any price, in size. The seesaw still wobbles, but it can no longer push the price through the floor. That is what it means for a market to acquire a price-insensitive structural buyer: the downside is capped by someone who will catch every falling knife on principle.

To see why "price-insensitive" is such a powerful property, contrast the two buyers' demand curves. An ordinary investor has a *downward-sloping* demand curve for gold: the higher the price, the less they want, and a rising opportunity cost (higher real yields) shifts that whole curve left. This is the normal economics of a discretionary asset, and it is what makes the price mean-revert — when gold gets expensive relative to its drivers, demand falls and the price comes back. A central bank buying to a reserve-share target has, in effect, a near-*vertical* demand curve over the relevant range: it wants a roughly fixed quantity (its target tonnage) almost regardless of price. Add a vertical demand curve to a market and you do something specific to the price: you raise the level at which supply and demand clear, and you blunt the mean reversion, because the price can no longer fall far enough to choke off the price-blind buyer. The market does not stop reacting to real rates; it reacts *from a higher base*, and it stops reverting all the way back down. That is the precise microeconomic signature of what happened to gold after 2022, and it is why "the floor" is the right mental model rather than "more demand."

There is a second-order effect that compounds this. Because the central-bank bid is known to be persistent and price-blind, it changes the behavior of the *other* participants too. A Western fund deciding whether to sell gold into a real-rate spike now has to reckon with the fact that there is a wall of sovereign buying underneath, which means the downside is shallower than the rate model alone would suggest — so the fund sells less, or buys the dip. Speculators front-run the floor. The structural buyer thus exerts an influence larger than its own tonnage, by anchoring everyone else's expectations about how far the price can fall. This is how a buyer that takes "only" a third of new supply ends up reshaping the entire price distribution: it doesn't just add demand, it rewrites the risk that every other holder is pricing.

#### Worked example: a reserve manager's choice, in dollars

Here is the choice in concrete terms, to feel why it overrides the price. A reserve manager has two ways to deploy a slug of reserves: buy **100 tonnes of gold**, or hold **\$8 billion of US Treasuries**.

At \$2,400/oz, 100 tonnes (≈ 3.2 million ounces) is worth about 3.2 million × \$2,400 ≈ **\$7.7 billion** — call the two options roughly equal in size. The Treasuries pay about 4.5%, which is \$8 billion × 4.5% = **\$360 million a year** in interest. The gold pays nothing. So on a pure-return basis, the Treasury wins by \$360 million a year — and a hedge fund would take the Treasury every time, especially when the gold price already looks high.

But the reserve manager is pricing a different risk. The \$360 million a year is the premium on a sanction-insurance policy. The "claim" they are insuring against is the entire \$8 billion being frozen in a geopolitical rupture. **A buyer who values that insurance more than \$360 million a year of yield will buy the gold no matter what the chart says — and when enough buyers think that way, the gold price stops obeying the yield math that used to govern it.**

## Common misconceptions

This is a topic dense with half-truths, so let me correct the most stubborn ones with numbers.

**"Central banks are a small part of the gold market, so this is overhyped."** False, and increasingly so. In 2024, central banks bought 1,045 tonnes against total mine production of 3,661 tonnes — they absorbed roughly **29% of all newly mined gold.** Before 2022, that figure was around 13%. When one buyer takes nearly a third of new supply and does so without caring about the price, that buyer *is* the market's swing factor, not a footnote.

**"They are buying because gold is going up — it's just performance-chasing."** Backwards. The clearest tell that this is structural, not momentum, is that the buying *led* the price and continued *through* conditions that should have suppressed it. Central banks bought a record 1,082 tonnes in 2022 — a year when real interest rates *rose* sharply (10-year TIPS went from −1% to +1.6%) and gold "should" have fallen. A momentum chaser sells when the macro turns against them; these buyers did not.

**"This is just China secretly trying to back the yuan with gold."** Overstated. There is no evidence anyone is moving to a formal gold standard, and a gold-backed yuan would require China to give up monetary control it has no intention of surrendering. The motive is far simpler and more durable: *insurance and diversification*, not monetary reform. Reading it as a yuan-gold-standard conspiracy actually *underestimates* the trend by making it sound like a fringe ambition rather than a near-universal risk-management response.

**"If gold gets expensive enough, central banks will take profits like everyone else."** Unlikely, and this is the crucial point. They are not return-maximizing investors with a sell discipline; they are sovereigns buying insurance to a target *share* of reserves. A rising price does not trigger profit-taking — it *raises the value of the protection they want to hold.* The buyer who would sell into strength is the Western ETF holder, not the central bank.

**"Western ETF outflows cancel out the central-bank buying, so it's a wash."** They partially offset on *flow*, but they are not the same animal, and that is the whole point of the next section.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Here is where the structural bid is visible in the actual market data of 2022-2025.

### The decoupling from real rates

The cleanest evidence is the broken seesaw. For two decades, gold and real interest rates moved inversely with near-mechanical reliability — you could practically price gold off the 10-year TIPS yield. Then in 2022-24 the relationship *snapped*. Real yields rose from around −1% in 2021 to +2.2% by the end of 2024 — a brutal headwind that, under the old rules, should have crushed gold by hundreds of dollars. Instead, gold rose from about \$1,800 to \$2,625, and then exploded higher into 2026. An asset that "should" have fallen with rising real rates rose *with* them. The thing that changed between the old behavior and the new behavior is the arrival of a buyer who does not look at real rates at all. This decoupling is, to me, the single most important chart in the modern gold market, and it is covered in depth from the rate side in [real interest rates, the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold).

### The marginal buyer moved from West to East

![Dual axis chart, central bank purchases rising while Western ETF holdings fell from 2020 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022-6.png)

The figure above captures the handoff that defined the era. The green bars are central-bank purchases, surging past 1,000 tonnes a year. The red line is Western gold-ETF holdings — the gauge of how much gold price-sensitive Western investors are holding through funds like GLD. From its 2020 peak near 3,909 tonnes, that ETF stockpile *fell* steadily to about 3,219 tonnes by 2024, as Western investors sold gold to chase the high yields that bonds and cash were finally offering. In the old world, that wave of Western selling would have tanked the price. It didn't — because the central banks were on the other side of every trade, absorbing the metal the West was dumping. The *marginal buyer*, the one setting the price at the edge, migrated from the rate-sensitive West to the price-insensitive East. When the marginal buyer changes, the price behavior changes, and that is exactly what we observed.

#### Worked example: who set the price in 2024?

Let us tally the 2024 flows to see who was actually in control. On the demand side, central banks bought 1,045 tonnes. On the supply side, mine production was 3,661 tonnes and recycling added 1,370, for total supply of about 5,031 tonnes. Central banks alone took **1,045 ÷ 5,031 ≈ 21% of all the gold available in the world that year** — and about 29% of the freshly mined portion.

Meanwhile, Western ETFs were net *sellers*, shedding metal as the red line shows. So the price-insensitive sector was adding more than 1,000 tonnes while the price-sensitive sector was subtracting. The fact that gold rose strongly in 2024 despite ETF outflows tells you the answer directly: **the price was being set by the buyer who didn't care about the price, not by the seller who did.** That is the structural bid made visible in a single year's arithmetic.

### The 2024-26 surge: the floor lets the price run

The structural bid does not just cap the downside; once the macro stops fighting it, it lets the price *run*. That is the story of 2024 into early 2026. For two years (2022-23) the central-bank floor mostly held gold *up* against a brutal rate headwind — the price chopped sideways in the \$1,800-2,000 range while real yields punished it. Then in 2024, as the Fed pivoted toward cutting and real yields stopped rising, the headwind eased — and with the floor still firmly in place, gold did not just recover, it broke out, climbing from around \$2,000 to \$2,625 over the year, then accelerating to an all-time high near \$5,589 by January 2026. The mechanism is worth stating plainly: the structural buyer set the floor, and when the cyclical seller (the rate-sensitive Western fund) stopped pressing on the other side, the floor became a launchpad. A market with a price-blind buyer underneath does not behave symmetrically — it grinds when fought and rips when unopposed, because there is no comparable price-blind *seller* to cap the upside.

This asymmetry is the practical payoff of understanding the structural bid. In the old regime, you could reasonably expect gold to give back most of a rate-driven rally once the rate move reversed, because the marginal holder was a trader who would take profits. In the new regime, the rallies have a stickier floor under them, because a large slice of the buying never sells. That does not make gold a one-way bet — it can and will have vicious corrections, especially when a liquidity crisis forces leveraged holders to dump everything (a dynamic we cover in the [safe-haven post](/blog/trading/gold/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry)). But it does change the *shape* of the distribution: a higher floor, stickier rallies, and a price that no longer reliably round-trips back to where the rate model says it "should" be.

### The geopolitical thread

It is no accident that the biggest buyers — Russia, China, Turkey, India, Poland — are precisely the countries most exposed to either Western sanctions or a Western-adjacent war. This is where the gold story stops being about interest rates and becomes about geopolitics, which is the subject of the next track in this series. The freeze of Russia's reserves was not just a financial event; it was the moment the world's monetary order visibly fractured into "money you control" and "money someone else controls." Gold is the asset on the right side of that line. The full story of the freeze, what it taught every reserve manager, and where it leads the multipolar money order is taken up in [sanctions, reserve freezes, and the 2022 turning point for gold](/blog/trading/gold/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold).

## How to use this: tracking central-bank demand as a structural signal

So what does a reader actually *do* with all this? Central-bank buying is not a short-term trading signal — you cannot buy gold on Monday because a central bank bought on Friday. It is a *regime* signal: it tells you what kind of market you are in, which in turn tells you how to weigh every other signal.

Here is the practical framework I use.

**First, recalibrate your real-rate intuition.** If you grew up on the rule "real rates up, gold down," you must now treat that rule as *necessary but no longer sufficient.* As long as central banks are buying over 1,000 tonnes a year, rising real rates will hurt gold *less* than the historical relationship predicts — possibly not at all. Don't short gold purely because real yields rose; check whether the structural bid is still there.

**Second, watch the World Gold Council's quarterly data as your dashboard.** The WGC publishes *Gold Demand Trends* every quarter, with official-sector purchase figures. The numbers to track: is quarterly central-bank buying still running near or above 250 tonnes (a >1,000-tonne annual pace)? Is the buying broad (many countries) or concentrated (one or two)? Broad buying is more durable. A *sustained drop* below the post-2022 pace — back toward the old 400-500 tonne range — would be the single most important bearish signal for the structural thesis, and worth more than any move in rates or the dollar.

**Third, read the gold share of reserves as your fuel gauge.** The room-to-buy argument says the bid persists as long as the big emerging holders are far below the Western ~70% norm. China at 6%, India at 11% — these are the gauges of how much demand is still in the tank. When they approach 20-25%, the structural tailwind starts to weaken. Until then, assume it is intact.

**Fourth, understand what would break the thesis.** Intellectual honesty demands knowing the disconfirming evidence. The structural bid would weaken if: the geopolitical temperature genuinely cooled and reserve-freezing came to look like a one-off rather than a permanent tool; or if the emerging holders reached their target gold shares; or if a gold price spike got so extreme that even sovereigns balked at adding at the margin. None of these looks imminent in 2026, but they are the things to watch for, not the daily wiggle in real yields.

The deeper takeaway is this. For most of the modern era, gold's price was a referendum on Western monetary policy — a bet on real rates and the dollar, run by Western investors. Since 2022, a second, more powerful voter has entered the room: the world's central banks, voting not on interest rates but on *whether they trust the system that issues the dollar.* And unlike the rate trade, that vote does not reverse when the Fed changes course. It is a vote of no-confidence in the safety of paper reserves, cast by the very institutions that issue paper money — which is the most damning verdict gold has ever received, and the deepest foundation its price has ever stood on. Gold did not become more valuable because it changed. It became more valuable because the world decided, all at once, that it needed more insurance — and there is only one policy that no one else can cancel.
