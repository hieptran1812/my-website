> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold pays no yield, and that is not a flaw to apologize for — it is the whole reason the price moves the way it does. With no cash flow to discount, gold is valued by its *opportunity cost*: what you give up by holding inert metal instead of an interest-bearing safe asset. That opportunity cost is the **real** (inflation-adjusted) interest rate.
>
> - A bond is worth the present value of its coupons; a stock, the present value of its earnings. Gold has neither, so the only thing left to price it is the discount rate itself — and the real discount rate is the real interest rate.
> - When real rates fall, holding gold costs almost nothing, so its price rises. When real rates surge, cash and TIPS out-compete it, so its price falls. This is gold's single most reliable relationship.
> - The cleanest mental model: gold is a **perpetual, zero-coupon, zero-default real bond** with a floating "anti-yield." It never pays you and never matures — but it is no one's promise, and that is what you are buying.
> - The number to remember: from 2013 to 2021 the inverse held tightly — but in 2022-26 real yields rose to multi-year highs *and* gold tripled. That break is not a refutation; it is the next driver (central banks, geopolitics) arriving on top of the opportunity-cost engine.

Warren Buffett has spent decades dismissing gold with a line that always gets a laugh: it "just sits there and looks at you." He has a sharper version, too. He once asked his audience to picture all the gold ever mined melted into a single cube — about 22 metres on a side, small enough to sit in the middle of a baseball infield. For the same money, he said, you could buy all the farmland in America plus a dozen ExxonMobils and still have a trillion dollars of walking-around cash. One pile grows crops and pays dividends forever. The other pile you can fondle. Which would you rather own?

It is a devastating argument, and on its own terms it is correct. Gold produces nothing. It pays no coupon, no dividend, no rent. Bury an ounce in your garden and dig it up in a hundred years and you have exactly one ounce — no interest has accrued, no offspring have been born. By the logic that values every other financial asset, gold should be worth roughly the cost of digging it out of the ground, and not a penny more.

And yet. This inert, unproductive, non-compounding metal has had a continuous human bid for five thousand years. Central banks — the most sophisticated financial institutions on earth, run by people who understand Buffett's argument perfectly well — hold roughly 36,000 tonnes of it and in 2022, 2023, and 2024 bought more than a thousand tonnes a year, the heaviest accumulation since records began. In January 2026, the metal that "just sits there" printed an all-time high near \$5,589 an ounce. Either tens of thousands of years of buyers, and the world's monetary authorities, are collectively stupid — or Buffett's argument, while internally consistent, is answering the wrong question. This post is about the second possibility. The resolution of the paradox is a single idea, **opportunity cost**, and once you have it, you hold the key to the entire rest of this series.

## Foundations: What "yield" is, and how everything else gets priced

Before we can explain why gold's *lack* of yield matters, we have to be precise about what yield is and how it gives every other asset its value. Skip nothing here; the whole argument rests on these definitions.

**Yield** is the income an asset throws off, expressed as a percentage of what you paid for it. If you lend a company \$1,000 and it pays you \$40 a year in interest, the yield is 4%. If you buy a stock for \$100 and it pays a \$3 dividend, the dividend yield is 3%. If you buy a rental apartment for \$500,000 and collect \$25,000 a year in rent after costs, the rental yield is 5%. Yield is the asset *paying you to own it*. It is the reward for parting with your money — the compensation a borrower hands a lender, a company hands a shareholder, a tenant hands a landlord.

Now, how does yield translate into a *price*? Through one of the most important ideas in all of finance: **discounting cash flows**. The value of any income-producing asset is the sum of all the cash it will ever pay you, with each future payment shrunk down — "discounted" — to reflect the fact that money tomorrow is worth less than money today. Why is a dollar tomorrow worth less than a dollar today? Because today's dollar can be put to work and earn interest. A dollar you will receive in a year is only worth, today, the amount you would need to invest now to *have* a dollar in a year.

The shrinking is done with a **discount rate**, and the formula for one future payment is mechanical:

```
present value = future cash flow / (1 + discount rate) ^ years away
```

A \$1,000 payment due in one year, discounted at 5%, is worth \$1,000 / 1.05 = \$952 today. The same \$1,000 due in ten years is worth \$1,000 / 1.05^10 = \$614 today. The further out the cash, and the higher the discount rate, the smaller its present value. To value a whole bond, you discount every coupon and the final principal, then add them up. To value a stock, you discount every future dividend or every dollar of future earnings the business will generate. The price you are willing to pay *is* that sum.

Two consequences of this machinery matter enormously, and we will lean on both:

1. **The discount rate is the gravity that holds all asset prices down.** Raise the discount rate and every future cash flow shrinks, so every price falls. This is why a single sentence from a central banker about interest rates can move trillions of dollars across stocks, bonds, and property in an afternoon. The cash flows did not change; the rate at which we shrink them did.

2. **An asset with no cash flow cannot be valued this way at all.** The entire discounting apparatus needs a stream of payments to chew on. Hand it an asset that pays nothing and the formula returns zero, every time, no matter the discount rate. Zero coupons discounted at any rate is still zero.

And that is the problem with gold, stated as sharply as it can be. Gold has no cash flow. Plug it into the discounted-cash-flow machine — the machine we use to value literally everything else — and the machine says gold is worth nothing. The machine is not broken. Gold is simply not the kind of thing the machine was built to value. We need a different tool.

#### Worked example: pricing a bond, then trying to price gold

Take a 3-year bond with a \$1,000 face value paying a 4% coupon — that is \$40 a year, plus the \$1,000 back at maturity. Suppose the market discount rate is 4%. Its value is:

- Year 1 coupon: \$40 / 1.04 = \$38.46
- Year 2 coupon: \$40 / 1.04² = \$36.98
- Year 3 coupon + principal: \$1,040 / 1.04³ = \$924.56
- **Total present value = \$1,000.00**

The bond is worth exactly \$1,000 because its coupon equals the discount rate — a clean, satisfying answer the machine produces in seconds. Now point the same machine at one ounce of gold. Its cash flows are: \$0 in year 1, \$0 in year 2, \$0 in year 3, \$0 every year thereafter, forever. Present value: \$0 / 1.04 + \$0 / 1.04² + … = **\$0.** Discounting says gold is worthless. Since gold demonstrably is not worthless, the lesson is not "gold is overpriced" — it is "discounting cash flows is the wrong lens for an asset that has none."

## Valuing a thing with no cash flow: the opportunity-cost model

If gold cannot be valued by what it *pays*, it must be valued by what it *costs you to hold*. This is the pivot of the entire argument, so slow down here.

Every choice in finance is a comparison. When you decide to hold gold, you are simultaneously deciding *not* to hold the next-best safe thing you could have held instead — typically cash in a savings account, or a short-term government bond, or inflation-protected government bonds (TIPS, which we will meet properly in a moment). That foregone alternative has a return. By choosing gold, you give that return up. Economists call the value of the path not taken the **opportunity cost**, and for a non-yielding asset, the opportunity cost *is its true cost of ownership*.

Think about what this means. Gold's headline cost — storage, a little insurance — is tiny, a few tenths of a percent a year. But its real cost is invisible: it is the interest you *would have earned* on a safe asset, which you forfeit the moment you convert your dollars into metal. If a safe government bond pays 2% after inflation, then holding gold "costs" you 2% a year in foregone safe return. If that safe bond pays nothing after inflation — or worse, *loses* you 1% a year to inflation — then holding gold costs you nothing, or even saves you money relative to the alternative.

This flips the whole question. We do not ask "what does gold earn?" — it earns nothing, and always will. We ask "what does the *competition* earn?" Gold competes, dollar for dollar, with cash and with safe bonds for a place in your portfolio. When the competition is paying well, gold is an expensive thing to hold and its price tends to fall as money flows toward the yield. When the competition is paying poorly — or paying you to lose purchasing power — gold's permanent zero suddenly looks attractive, money flows toward it, and its price rises.

![Three columns showing a bond valued by discounted coupons, a stock by discounted earnings, and gold by opportunity cost only](/imgs/blogs/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything-3.png)

The figure above lays the three side by side. A bond and a stock share a column structure: an instrument at the top, a cash-flow stream in the middle, a valuation method at the bottom — and for both, that method is "discount the cash flows." Gold's column breaks the pattern at the middle row: its cash flow is *none*. So its valuation row cannot say "discount the cash flows." It says something completely different — "opportunity cost only, the real interest rate" — and that single difference is what makes gold behave unlike anything else you own.

There is a subtle but crucial point hiding here. For a bond, the discount rate is a *drag* — raise it and the bond's value falls because its fixed coupons get shrunk harder. For gold, the discount rate is *the entire story*, but it acts through opportunity cost, not through shrinking cash flows (there are none to shrink). The higher the safe real return available elsewhere, the more painful it is to sit in metal that returns nothing, so the less anyone will pay for it. Same variable — the real interest rate — but it reaches gold by a different road than it reaches a bond. Hold that thought; it is why gold and bonds do not always move together even though both answer to rates.

It helps to make the "competition" concrete by lining up the three things a cautious saver actually chooses between. **Cash** (a bank deposit or money-market fund) pays a short-term nominal rate and carries essentially no price risk, but it is a bank's or a fund's liability. **A safe government bond** pays a longer-term nominal rate, fluctuates in price as rates move, and is the government's liability. **Inflation-protected government bonds (TIPS)** pay a contractual *real* rate, are the government's liability, and explicitly defend purchasing power. Gold is the fourth option: it pays nothing, fluctuates in price, defends purchasing power over long horizons, and — uniquely — is *nobody's* liability. Every day, capital weighs gold against those three, and the deciding question is always the same: how much real return am I giving up by choosing the metal over the best safe alternative? The answer to that question is the real interest rate, and it is why the entire gold market can be summarized as a referendum on one number.

Notice what this does to the usual intuition about "demand." For most goods, price is set by physical supply and consumption: more coffee drinkers, higher coffee prices. Gold barely works that way, because almost none of it gets consumed — nearly every ounce ever mined still exists, sitting in vaults and jewelry boxes. The price is therefore set not by how much new gold is dug up each year (a tiny flow against an enormous stock) but by whether the *existing* holders want to keep holding versus rotate into yield, and whether new buyers want to rotate *out* of yield into gold. That rotation is governed by opportunity cost. So when you read that "investment demand drove gold up," translate it: the opportunity cost of holding gold fell, so the existing pile became more attractive to keep and more attractive to buy. Demand for gold is, at bottom, demand for a zero real yield *relative to* the real yield on offer elsewhere.

#### Worked example: the headwind of a positive real rate

Suppose you hold \$100,000 in gold and the safe alternative — say, 10-year TIPS — yields **+2% real**. Every year you hold gold instead of those TIPS, you forgo \$2,000 of real, inflation-protected return (\$100,000 × 2%). That \$2,000 is invisible — no bill arrives, no money leaves your account — but it is just as real as a fee. For gold merely to *break even* against the TIPS, its price has to rise more than 2% in real terms that year. If gold is flat, you have lost \$2,000 of opportunity relative to the safe alternative. A high positive real rate is therefore a structural headwind: gold has to swim hard just to stand still. The intuition: when safe money pays you well, the metal that pays nothing has to fight uphill.

#### Worked example: the tailwind of a negative real rate

Now flip it. It is 2021. Inflation is running near 5% and a 1-year safe deposit pays barely 0.5%, so its **real** return is roughly **−1%** (you nominally gain 0.5% while prices rise 5%, so your purchasing power shrinks). Put \$100,000 in that "safe" deposit and a year later your money buys about \$1,000 less than it did — the safe option is a guaranteed slow bleed. Gold's "yield" is zero, but in a world where cash yields *minus* 1% real, zero is the *better* number. By holding gold you avoid the \$1,000 real loss the deposit would have inflicted. Suddenly the metal that "just sits there" is out-competing cash simply by not going backward. This is precisely why gold ran from roughly \$1,500 to over \$2,000 across 2020-21: the opportunity cost of holding it had gone *negative*. The intuition: when safe money loses you purchasing power, gold's stubborn zero wins by default.

#### Worked example: the three-way race between cash, TIPS, and gold

Line all three competitors up over one year with \$100,000 each, and watch how the *real* rate decides the winner before gold's price even moves. Take two scenarios.

- **Scenario A — real rate +2%.** Cash and TIPS each deliver about +2% real, so \$100,000 becomes \$102,000 of purchasing power. Gold, if flat in real terms, stays at \$100,000. Gold *loses the race by \$2,000* before any price action — it must rise more than 2% in real terms just to draw level. The deck is stacked against it.
- **Scenario B — real rate −1.5%.** Cash and TIPS each deliver about −1.5% real, so \$100,000 erodes to about \$98,500 of purchasing power. Gold, flat in real terms, holds \$100,000. Now gold *wins the race by \$1,500* without doing anything but refusing to lose. The deck is stacked in its favor.

Same gold, same zero yield, opposite outcomes — and the only thing that changed was the real rate, the height of the bar gold has to clear. This is the seesaw expressed as a stopwatch: gold's runner never speeds up or slows down, but the finish line moves toward or away from it depending on the real rate. The intuition: you do not need a view on gold to have a view on gold — you need a view on the real return of its competitors.

## Real versus nominal rates: why it has to be the *real* rate

We keep saying "real" interest rate, and the word is doing heavy lifting. If you take one technical idea from this post, take this one, because it is the master variable for the rest of the series.

A **nominal** interest rate is the number on the tin — the 4% your bond advertises, the 5% your savings account quotes. A **real** interest rate is that number *after subtracting inflation* — what your money actually earns in purchasing power, in terms of how many groceries or litres of petrol it will buy. The relationship is, to a close approximation:

```
real rate  ≈  nominal rate  −  expected inflation
```

A 5% nominal yield when inflation is 2% is a +3% real yield — a genuine 3% gain in what your money can buy. The same 5% nominal yield when inflation is 6% is a **−1%** real yield — you are nominally richer but actually poorer, because prices outran your interest. The headline number lied; the real number told the truth.

Why must it be the real rate that prices gold, not the nominal one? Because gold's entire job is to preserve *purchasing power* — it is a claim on real value, a hedge against the erosion of money. So the question a gold-holder implicitly asks is not "what nominal interest am I giving up?" but "what *real* return am I giving up — what purchasing power could safe assets have earned me?" If a bond pays 5% nominal but inflation eats 6%, that bond is *destroying* purchasing power; gold's flat-in-real-terms profile beats it. The nominal rate can be high and screaming "sell your gold," while the real rate is negative and quietly saying "hold it." The real rate is the one that tells the truth, so the real rate is the one gold listens to.

This is also why a single market instrument — the **10-year TIPS yield** — is the most-watched number in the gold world. TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) are US government bonds whose principal rises with inflation, so their quoted yield *is*, directly and without any subtraction, the market's real interest rate. When you hear a strategist say "real yields are at 2%," they almost always mean the 10-year TIPS yield is 2%. That single number is the opportunity cost of holding gold, quoted live, all day, on a screen. Watch it and you are watching the seesaw's other end.

One refinement makes the real rate even more powerful as a gold signal. The inflation that matters for pricing assets today is not last month's CPI print — it is *expected* inflation over the life of the asset, because that is what determines the real return an investor is locking in. The bond market prices this expectation continuously as the **breakeven inflation rate**: the gap between a regular Treasury's nominal yield and a TIPS' real yield of the same maturity. A 4.5% nominal 10-year yield against a 2% real 10-year yield implies the market expects about 2.5% average inflation over the decade. This decomposition — nominal yield equals real yield plus breakeven inflation — is the cleanest way to see *why* gold sometimes ignores a hot inflation report. If inflation surprises higher but the bond market believes the central bank will respond, nominal yields can rise *more* than breakevens, so the real yield actually goes *up* — and gold falls even as the inflation headline screams. Gold did not "fail to hedge inflation"; it correctly tracked the real yield, which is the variable that prices it. The inflation print is noise; the real yield is the signal.

There is a second-order channel worth flagging now and developing later in the series: the **US dollar**. Real yields and the dollar are tightly linked — when US real yields rise relative to the rest of the world, capital flows toward dollar assets and the dollar strengthens. Since gold is priced in dollars, a stronger dollar mechanically pushes the dollar price of gold down (it takes fewer of a more valuable dollar to buy the same ounce), reinforcing the direct opportunity-cost effect. So a rising real yield hits gold twice: directly, by raising its opportunity cost, and indirectly, by strengthening the currency it is priced in. This is why the real-yield relationship, the dollar relationship, and the gold price all tend to rhyme — they are three views of the same underlying force. Track B has dedicated posts on each; for now, just file it as "real yields are the trunk, and the dollar is a thick branch off the same tree."

The scatter plot above is the opportunity-cost model meeting the data. Each dot is one year: the 10-year real yield on the horizontal axis, gold's year-end price on the vertical. For the blue dots — 2013 through 2021 — the relationship is exactly what the model predicts: a clear downward slope. The fitted line says that across that era, every extra one percentage point of real yield was associated with roughly \$371 lower gold. When real yields cratered to around −1% in 2020-21, gold sat near \$1,900; when they were a healthy +1% in 2018, gold languished near \$1,280. Higher opportunity cost, lower gold. The seesaw works.

Then look at the orange dots — 2022 through 2026 — sitting defiantly *above and to the right* of where the line says they should be. Real yields rose to +1.5%, +2%, and beyond, which the model says should have crushed gold. Instead gold went to records. We will come back to that break; it is the most important caveat in this post. But first, let us nail down the cleanest way to hold the whole idea in your head.

## Gold as a perpetual, zero-coupon, real bond

Here is the mental model that, once it clicks, makes everything about gold's price behavior obvious. **Gold is a perpetual, zero-coupon, zero-default, real bond.** Take that phrase apart word by word, because every word is load-bearing.

- **Zero-coupon** — it pays no interest along the way. Just like a zero-coupon bond, all of its value is locked in its eventual sale price; you collect nothing in the meantime.
- **Perpetual** — it never matures. A normal bond hands back your principal on a fixed date; gold never does. It is a claim with no end date, which you redeem only by selling to the next holder.
- **Zero-default** — it cannot fail to pay, because it never promised to pay anything *and* it is no one's liability. A corporate bond can default; a government bond can be inflated away; gold owes nothing to anyone, so there is no promise that can be broken.
- **Real** — its value is in purchasing power, not in a fixed number of dollars. It is a claim on real value, which is why the *real* rate, not the nominal one, is what competes with it.

![A coupon bond cash-flow timeline with coupons and a maturity, beside a gold timeline with zero cash flows and no maturity](/imgs/blogs/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything-5.png)

The two timelines above show the surgery. The top row is an ordinary 3-year coupon bond: you pay \$1,000 at t=0, collect \$40 coupons (green inflows) along the way, and get \$1,040 back at maturity. Its value is the sum of those discounted inflows — the machine has plenty to chew on. The bottom row is gold: same \$1,000 outlay, then \$0 at t=1, \$0 at t=2, \$0 forever — and no maturity at all, just an open-ended hold until you choose to sell. Strip the coupons off a bond and strip away its maturity date, and the thing you are left with *is* gold.

Now, here is the payoff of the analogy. For a normal bond, the present value is dominated by the coupons and principal, and the discount rate adjusts that value at the margin. But imagine a bond with *zero* coupons that pays one lump sum very far in the future. Its present value becomes extraordinarily sensitive to the discount rate, because there is nothing but the discount factor between you and a distant payoff. This is the formal version of "long-duration assets are interest-rate-sensitive." Gold, as a perpetual zero-coupon claim, sits at the extreme end of that spectrum: maximally sensitive to the *real* discount rate, because the real discount rate is essentially the *only* thing pricing it. A small move in real rates moves gold a lot, for the same mathematical reason a small move in rates moves a 30-year zero-coupon bond far more than a 2-year coupon bond.

#### Worked example: the perpetual-bond sensitivity to real rates

Model gold crudely as a claim on \$1 of real value, redeemed far in the future — say 30 years out — and discount it at the real rate. At a +2% real rate, the discount factor is 1 / 1.02³⁰ = 0.552, so the claim is worth about 55 cents. Now let the real rate fall to +1%. The discount factor becomes 1 / 1.01³⁰ = 0.742, and the claim is worth about 74 cents. A one-percentage-point fall in real rates lifted the present value by (0.742 − 0.552) / 0.552 ≈ **34%**. The same one-point move on a 2-year claim would move its value only about 2%. The lesson in one line: because gold has no coupons to anchor it, its value is *all* discount factor, so it swings hard when the real rate moves — exactly the leverage to real yields that we see in the market. (This is a deliberately simplified illustration of the duration effect, not a literal valuation of gold — but the direction and rough magnitude are the point.)

## Gibson's paradox and the cost of carry

Two older ideas round out the picture and pre-empt two common objections.

The first is **Gibson's paradox**, a relationship John Maynard Keynes named in the 1920s after observing that, under the gold standard, the general price level moved with the *level* of nominal interest rates, not — as you might expect — with their rate of change. Economists puzzled over it for decades. The modern resolution, due in large part to work by Robert Barsky and Lawrence Summers, reframed it in exactly our terms: under a gold standard, the "price level" is just the inverse of the real price of gold, and the real price of gold is governed by the real interest rate. When real rates are low, gold is dear (so goods priced in gold are cheap, i.e. the price level is low); when real rates are high, gold is cheap. Gibson's paradox, stripped of its 1920s clothing, is simply our opportunity-cost model observed a century early. It is reassuring: the idea that gold's real price tracks the real interest rate is not a recent rationalization invented to explain the 2010s — it is a relationship economists have been tripping over for a hundred years.

There is a practical corollary of the perpetual-zero-coupon framing that traders live by: gold has the *longest duration of any asset*. Duration, in bond language, measures how much a price moves for a given change in yield, and it rises with how far away an asset's cash flows are. A bond that pays you back next year has short duration and barely moves when rates change; a 30-year zero-coupon bond has long duration and lurches. Gold, whose "cash flow" is an open-ended sale at some indefinite future date, behaves like an *infinite*-duration real bond — which is exactly why a seemingly small move in the 10-year real yield, from say +1% to 0%, can be worth hundreds of dollars an ounce. When a strategist says "gold is the ultimate long-duration asset," this is the precise, non-mystical meaning: it is all discount factor and no coupon, so it carries maximum sensitivity to the real discount rate. It is not that gold is mysterious; it is that it sits at the mathematical limit of an idea every bond trader already knows.

The second idea is the **cost of carry**, and it sharpens "gold yields nothing" into "gold yields slightly *less* than nothing, before opportunity cost." Holding physical gold is not quite free. Allocated bars in a professional vault cost roughly 0.1% to 0.3% a year to store, plus around 0.1% to insure against theft and loss — call it ~0.4% a year of genuine carry, on top of the foregone real interest that is the big invisible cost. So gold's full "anti-yield" is: the real interest rate you give up, *plus* a small storage-and-insurance drag. (This is also why the futures market for gold is almost always in *contango* — distant-delivery contracts trade above spot by roughly the interest rate plus storage — because someone holding metal to deliver later must be compensated for the carry. The futures basis is, in effect, the carry made into a tradeable number; a later post in the series takes this apart.)

![A ledger comparing the costs of holding gold against the convenience yield of owning a no-counterparty asset](/imgs/blogs/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything-6.png)

But — and this is the resolution — a holder is willing to pay that carry for a reason, and the reason has a name: the **convenience yield**. In commodity finance, a convenience yield is the non-monetary benefit of holding the physical thing rather than a paper claim on it. For gold, the convenience yield is *safety with no counterparty*. Gold is the one major asset that is no one's IOU. A bond can default; a bank deposit can be frozen or bailed-in; a currency can be inflated away or sanctioned out of the system; even a stock depends on a company continuing to exist. Gold depends on no one. It cannot be printed, cannot be defaulted on, cannot be frozen by a foreign government, and is accepted across every border and every regime. The ledger figure above is the trade: on the left, the costs you pay (foregone real interest, ~0.4% carry); on the right, what you buy with them (a claim that is nobody's promise, insurance that pays off precisely when the rest of the financial system is failing). When you understand gold as paying a *convenience yield of safety*, the "no yield" objection dissolves. Gold does have a yield — it is just paid in security rather than dollars, and it shows up only in the states of the world where everything else is breaking.

The convenience yield is not a hand-wave; it is the missing term that closes the valuation. The full statement of gold's "anti-yield" is: real interest rate forgone, *plus* the carry (storage and insurance), *minus* the convenience yield of no-counterparty safety. When the financial system feels solid, the convenience yield is small — safety is cheap, no one is worried — so gold's net cost of holding is roughly the real rate plus carry, and the seesaw runs clean against real yields. But when the system feels fragile — a banking scare, a sovereign-debt wobble, a war, a wave of sanctions — the convenience yield spikes, because suddenly being no one's liability is worth a great deal. A rising convenience yield offsets, and can more than offset, a rising real rate. That single term is what lets us say with a straight face that gold rose in 2022-26 *despite* high real yields: the safety the metal provides was being repriced upward faster than its opportunity cost was rising. The convenience yield is the bridge from "gold tracks real yields" to "gold is monetary insurance" — and it is why the spine of this entire series is that gold is the price of monetary insurance, with real yields as the premium and the convenience yield as the payout the market expects from that insurance.

#### Worked example: what the carry actually costs

Put \$50,000 into allocated gold bars. Storage at 0.3% is \$150 a year; insurance at 0.1% is \$50 a year; total carry ≈ **\$200 a year**, or 0.4%. That is the literal, out-of-pocket price of holding the insurance — about the cost of a single nice dinner per year per \$50,000 protected. Set that against the foregone real interest: if safe real yields are +2%, the opportunity cost is \$1,000 a year (\$50,000 × 2%), five times larger than the carry. So when people say "gold has a cost," the storage they are picturing is the small part; the real interest you give up is the big part — and the big part swings with the real rate, which is the whole reason the price moves. The intuition: the carry is the small, fixed insurance premium; the real rate is the large, variable one.

## Why the direction is easy but the level is hard

Here is the honest limit of everything we have built, and it is worth stating plainly because it is where most gold commentary quietly cheats. The opportunity-cost model is superb at telling you which *direction* gold should move: real rates down, gold up; real rates up, gold down. That directional call is reliable enough to trade around, and it is most of what a sensible gold-watcher needs. What the model is *not* good at is telling you the right absolute *level* — whether "fair value" for gold today is \$2,000 or \$3,500 or \$5,000. And the reason traces straight back to the term we just introduced: the convenience yield. Direction is governed by the real rate, which you can read off a screen. Level is governed by the real rate *and* the convenience yield, and the convenience yield is not quoted anywhere. It is the market's shifting, unobservable estimate of how badly the world wants no-counterparty insurance — and you only find out what it was after the fact, by looking at where gold actually traded.

This is why two honest analysts, looking at the *same* 10-year real yield, can produce wildly different "fair values" for gold and both be defensible. They are not disagreeing about the discount rate; they are disagreeing about how much insurance the world should be willing to pay for. A stock has the same problem in principle — its value depends on an unobservable equity risk premium — but a stock at least has earnings to anchor the estimate. Gold has no earnings, so *the entire valuation rests on two numbers, one of which is unobservable*. That is not a flaw in the analysis; it is a true property of the asset. Pretending otherwise — quoting a precise model price for gold to the dollar — is the single most common tell of someone who does not understand what they are pricing.

#### Worked example: the same real yield, two fair values

Suppose the 10-year real yield is +1.0%, and two analysts both model gold as a long-duration real asset. Analyst A assumes the world's demand for monetary insurance is "normal" — a convenience yield of, say, 1.5% — so in her model gold's net holding cost is roughly 1.0% (real rate) + 0.4% (carry) − 1.5% (convenience) = **−0.1%**, a slight *net benefit* to holding, and she pencils in a fair value around \$2,400. Analyst B, watching central banks hoard over 1,000 tonnes a year and sanctions reshape the reserve system, assumes the convenience yield has repriced to 3.0%: his net holding cost is 1.0% + 0.4% − 3.0% = **−1.6%**, a much larger net benefit, and his fair value lands near \$3,800. Same real yield, same carry, same math — a \$1,400 gap, driven entirely by a single unobservable judgment about how much the world wants insurance. Neither is "wrong." The intuition: in gold, the discount rate sets the *direction* and the convenience yield sets the *level*, and only one of the two is on a screen.

The practical discipline that falls out of this is liberating rather than discouraging. Stop trying to compute gold's "correct" price; you cannot, because half the inputs are a vote on fear that has not been counted yet. Instead, do the thing the model *can* do well: watch the real yield to know which way the wind is blowing, and watch the convenience-yield proxies — central-bank buying, sanctions and reserve-freezing news, banking and sovereign-debt stress, the gold price's own refusal to fall when real yields say it should — to gauge whether the insurance bid is quietly rising. When gold climbs even as real yields climb, you are not watching the model fail; you are watching the convenience yield being repriced in real time, and that is itself one of the most valuable macro signals there is. The level being hard to pin down is not a bug in gold analysis. It is gold telling you, in the only language it has, exactly how nervous the world has become.

## When the opportunity-cost model breaks

A model you cannot break is a model you do not understand. So let us deliberately break this one, because the break is where the next driver in the series enters.

From 2013 through 2021 the opportunity-cost model was almost embarrassingly reliable — the blue dots on our scatter, the tight inverse relationship, the seesaw working exactly as advertised. Then came 2022-26. The Federal Reserve raised rates at the fastest pace in forty years; the 10-year real yield climbed from around −1% to +2% and stayed there. The model's verdict was unambiguous: with the opportunity cost of holding gold rising to its highest level in over a decade, gold should fall, probably hard. Instead, gold went from roughly \$1,800 to a January-2026 record near \$5,400. Real yields high *and* gold high — the two things the seesaw says cannot happen together, happening together for years.

![Bars showing high real yields alongside large positive gold returns each year from 2022 to 2026, the anomaly years](/imgs/blogs/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything-7.png)

The bar chart makes the anomaly impossible to miss. In the shaded 2022-26 band, the amber bars (the real yield, the headwind) are the *highest* of the whole sample, while the green bars (gold's yearly return) are large and positive — including a roughly 27% gain in 2024 and a doubling into the 2026 spike. By the opportunity-cost model alone, those green bars should have been red. So is the model wrong?

No — it is *incomplete*, and the way it is incomplete is the most important thing in modern gold analysis. The opportunity-cost model is a statement about **investor demand for gold as a financial substitute for safe yield.** That demand is real and it still obeys the seesaw. But in 2022 a second, structurally different buyer arrived in size: **central banks**, buying gold not as a yield substitute but as a *political* asset — a reserve that cannot be frozen or sanctioned, in a world that had just watched Russia's dollar reserves get frozen overnight. Central banks do not care about the 10-year TIPS yield when they buy gold; they care about whether their reserves can be confiscated by a rival power. That is a price-insensitive, geopolitics-driven bid layered *on top of* the rate-sensitive investor bid. When it is large enough, it can overwhelm the opportunity-cost headwind entirely.

The right way to hold this is not "the model failed" but "the model is one engine, and a second engine has been added." Real yields still matter — they are still the master variable for the *marginal investor*. But the level of the gold price now also reflects a structural floor from official-sector demand and a risk premium from geopolitical fragility. The dual-axis chart below shows both engines at once: gold and the (inverted) real yield moving together through 2021 — the seesaw — and then visibly decoupling from 2022, where gold climbs even as real yields rise.

![Dual-axis time series of gold against the inverted 10-year real yield showing co-movement then the 2022-26 decoupling](/imgs/blogs/the-no-yield-problem-how-a-metal-that-pays-nothing-can-be-worth-anything-4.png)

This is exactly why A6 is the keystone of the series. The opportunity-cost model is the foundation — but Track B (the drivers) and Track C (geopolitics) are the additional engines that, in the current regime, sit on top of it. Real interest rates remain the variable you watch first. Just do not be surprised when, in a world questioning the safety of paper money itself, gold ignores them for a while.

## Common misconceptions

**"No yield means no value."** This is Buffett's argument, and it confuses two different valuation questions. Discounted cash flow values an asset by what it *pays*; opportunity cost values it by what it *costs to hold relative to the alternative*. Gold scores zero on the first and can score very well on the second — specifically, whenever the safe alternative is paying a low or negative real return. A thing can have no yield and still have value if it provides a service (here, no-counterparty safety) that people will pay a price to hold. The correct statement is not "gold has no value" but "gold has no *cash-flow* value, and is therefore priced entirely by opportunity cost and convenience yield."

**"Gold should track inflation one-for-one."** It does not, and the opportunity-cost model tells you why. Gold answers to the *real* rate, which is nominal yield *minus* inflation. Inflation rising does not automatically help gold; what helps gold is inflation rising *faster than nominal yields*, so that real yields fall. In the early 1980s inflation was high but Paul Volcker pushed nominal rates even higher, driving real yields sharply positive — and gold collapsed for two decades despite elevated inflation. Over a *decade or more* gold has preserved purchasing power well, but month to month it tracks real yields, not the CPI print. (The series has a whole post on this; the short version is: inflation hedge by the decade, not by the month.)

**"High interest rates always kill gold."** Usually, but not always — and the exception is precisely the real-versus-nominal distinction plus the second-engine point above. *Nominal* rates can be high while *real* rates are low or negative (the 1970s), in which case gold soars despite high headline rates. And even high *real* rates can be overwhelmed by a large enough geopolitical or central-bank bid (2022-26). The accurate rule is "high real rates are a headwind for the rate-sensitive investor bid," not "high rates kill gold."

**"Gold's price is irrational because it has no fundamentals."** It has a very precise fundamental — it just is not a cash flow. Its fundamental is the real interest rate (its opportunity cost) plus the value the world places on no-counterparty insurance (its convenience yield). Both are measurable. The 10-year TIPS yield is on a screen; central-bank purchases are reported quarterly. Gold is not unanchored; it is anchored to a *different* anchor than stocks and bonds, and people who only know the cash-flow anchor mistake "different" for "none."

**"If gold pays nothing, holding it is pure speculation."** Holding cash also pays nothing in real terms when real rates are negative, yet no one calls a checking account speculation. Gold is the same trade with an upgrade: a zero-real-yield asset that, unlike cash, is no government's liability and cannot be inflated. Calling that speculation is like calling a fire-insurance policy speculation because it pays nothing in the years your house does not burn down.

## How it shows up in real markets

**2011-2020: the era of free money, and gold's golden decade.** After the 2008 crisis and through the 2010s, central banks held policy rates near zero and bought bonds by the trillion, driving real yields down and, for stretches, negative. With the opportunity cost of holding gold near zero or below, the seesaw tilted hard in gold's favor. Gold rose from around \$870 in 2008 to a then-record \$1,921 in September 2011 as real yields collapsed. The whole move is the opportunity-cost model in its purest form: nothing about gold changed — it still paid nothing — but the *competition* (safe real yield) had stopped paying, so capital rotated into the asset that at least would not be inflated away. When you read that gold "loves easy money," this is the mechanism: easy money is low real yields, and low real yields are a low opportunity cost of holding gold.

**2013: the taper tantrum, the model's cleanest win.** In mid-2013 the Fed merely *hinted* it would slow its bond-buying. Real yields jumped — the 10-year TIPS yield swung from near zero toward +0.8% over the year — and gold fell roughly 28%, one of its worst years on record, from around \$1,675 at the start of 2013 to about \$1,200 by year-end. No crisis, no inflation surprise, no change in gold itself; just a sharp rise in the real return available on safe assets, which raised gold's opportunity cost and sent money the other way. The taper tantrum is the textbook case: when real yields spike and there is no overriding geopolitical bid, gold falls, fast.

#### Worked example: sizing the 2013 opportunity-cost shift

Suppose at the start of 2013 you held \$200,000 in gold while the real yield was roughly 0%. Your opportunity cost of holding gold was essentially nil — fine. By late 2013 the real yield had risen to about +0.8%, meaning the safe alternative now offered \$1,600 a year of real return you were forgoing (\$200,000 × 0.8%). That shift in opportunity cost — from zero to a positive, growing drag — is exactly the kind of change that prompts the marginal investor to sell. Multiply that decision across every rate-sensitive holder and you get a 28% drawdown. The price did not fall because gold got worse; it fell because the *alternative* got better. The intuition: gold's worst years are usually years when safe real yields *rose*, not years when anything happened to gold.

**2020-21: real yields go deeply negative, gold makes a record.** In the COVID response, the Fed cut to zero and inflation expectations rose, driving the 10-year real yield to roughly −1.1% — the most negative on record. Holding cash or safe bonds was now a *guaranteed* loss of purchasing power. Gold's permanent zero became the best safe number available, and it surged to an all-time high above \$2,075 in August 2020. Again, pure opportunity-cost mechanics: when the safe alternative pays you to lose money, the asset that merely breaks even in real terms wins.

**2022-26: the anomaly that defines the new regime.** Then the model "broke," and understanding *how* it broke is the whole point. The Fed hiked aggressively; real yields went to +2% and stayed there; by the seesaw, gold should have fallen for years. Instead it tripled. The reason, as we covered, is the arrival of a second buyer: central banks adding over 1,000 tonnes a year from 2022, motivated not by yield but by the lesson of frozen reserves and a desire for sanction-proof, no-counterparty money. The opportunity-cost engine was running in reverse (high real yields, a headwind), but the geopolitical-insurance engine was running so hot that gold rose anyway. The takeaway is not that real yields stopped mattering — they remain the master variable for the investor flow — but that in a regime where the *safety of paper money itself* is in question, the convenience yield of gold (its no-counterparty insurance) can be repriced upward fast enough to swamp a rising opportunity cost. The series' spine, made concrete: gold is the price of monetary insurance, real yields are the premium on that insurance, and in 2022-26 the world decided it wanted a lot more insurance regardless of the premium.

## The takeaway: what the no-yield problem actually teaches you

The instinct to dismiss gold because "it just sits there" is not wrong about the facts — gold genuinely produces nothing — it is wrong about the question. Gold was never trying to be a productive asset that compounds. It is trying to be the one asset that is no one's promise, and the only sensible way to price such a thing is by what you give up to hold it. That cost is the real interest rate, and so the single most useful thing you can do as a gold-watcher is stop asking "what does gold yield?" and start watching the **10-year real yield** as the live quote of gold's opportunity cost.

Internalize the seesaw and the rest of the series clicks into place. Real rates down — because of crisis cuts, money-printing, or inflation outrunning nominal yields — and gold's opportunity cost falls, so its price rises. Real rates up — because a central bank is fighting inflation with genuinely positive real rates — and gold's opportunity cost rises, so its price tends to fall. That is the engine. Everything else in this series is either an input to the real rate (Track B: inflation, the dollar, monetary policy) or a *second engine* that can override it (Track C: geopolitics, central-bank de-dollarization, the search for sanction-proof reserves).

And here is the deeper point to end on, because it reframes the whole Buffett objection. The years when gold "does nothing" — when it sits there paying you zero — are the years the financial system is working: real yields are healthy, paper money is trusted, productive assets compound, and you should mostly own those. Gold's flat, boring, yield-less stretches are a feature, not a bug; they are gold quietly charging you a small premium for an insurance policy you hope never pays out. The years gold explodes are the years that insurance pays out — when real yields go negative, or when the world stops trusting that paper money and government bonds are truly safe. A non-yielding asset that costs you a little in good times and protects you in catastrophic ones is not an investment that failed to compound. It is insurance, correctly priced by its opportunity cost, doing exactly the job it was bought to do. The no-yield "problem" was never a problem. It is the entire point.
