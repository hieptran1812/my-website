On 28 January 2026, the price of an ounce of gold printed \$5,589 for the first time in history. Sit with that number for a second. A lump of metal that pays you no interest, no dividend, and no rent — a substance that has been pulled out of the ground for five thousand years and does nothing once you own it but sit in a vault — was, that morning, the best-performing major asset on Earth. It had risen roughly 71% in 2025 alone, beating the S&P 500, beating bonds, beating most of the technology stocks people actually talk about at dinner.

We live in the most digital, most financialized, most algorithmically optimized economy in human history. We have artificial intelligence writing code, cryptocurrencies promising to replace money itself, and trading systems that can move a billion dollars in a microsecond. And yet the asset quietly making new all-time highs is the oldest one we have. A 5,000-year-old rock is outpacing the future.

That is the puzzle this entire series exists to solve, and it starts with a question that sounds almost rude when you say it out loud: **why does a metal that pays no interest keep mattering?** If you can answer that honestly — not with mysticism, not with gold-bug evangelism, but with the actual mechanics of money, real yields, and political risk — you understand something deep about how the financial system really works. So let us answer it, carefully, from zero.

> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold is not an investment that compounds; it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability.
>
> - **It is insurance, not growth.** Gold pays no yield and barely beats inflation over the very long run. Holding it is like paying a premium — you accept a low return in calm times in exchange for a payoff when everything else breaks.
> - **It is not a compounder.** Gold's long-run real return is roughly 0.8% to 3% per year. Stocks compound at ~7% real. Over a lifetime, gold preserves purchasing power; it does not multiply it.
> - **It pays off when paper assets fail together.** Gold has near-zero correlation with stocks. In 2022, a 60/40 stock-and-bond portfolio fell ~16% while gold finished flat — the proof that it does a job nothing else does.
> - **The marginal buyer changed.** Since 2022, central banks have bought more than 1,000 tonnes of gold a year, a structural regime shift from their two decades of selling. The new buyer is the official sector itself.
> - **The one number to remember:** gold rose **+71% in 2025** and printed an all-time high of **\$5,589/oz on 28 January 2026** — not because the world got richer, but because trust in paper got cheaper.

Across the next 42 posts we will take gold apart completely: what it is at the atomic level, what actually moves its price, its tangled history with governments and war, its boom-and-bust cycles, how you would actually own it without getting fleeced, the peculiar and fascinating Vietnamese gold market, and a final synthesis playbook. But all of it rests on the single idea in that TL;DR box, so we will spend this introduction earning it.

![Two columns comparing compounding stocks and bonds with gold as monetary insurance](/imgs/blogs/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model for the whole series, so let me walk you through it before we go deeper. On the left you have **compounders**: stocks and bonds. When you buy a stock you own a sliver of a real business — its factories, its brand, its future profits. When you buy a bond you own a contractual promise that someone will pay you interest and return your money. These are *claims on the future*, and as long as the economy works, those claims pay you to wait, and they compound. That is the magic of capitalism, and over decades it is overwhelmingly the right place to put your money.

But notice the bottom of the left column. Compounders share a hidden flaw: they tend to fail *together*. When trust in the paper system cracks — a banking crisis, a sovereign default, a burst of inflation that wrecks both stocks and bonds at once — your diversified portfolio of claims discovers that it was never really diversified, because every claim depends on the same underlying thing: confidence in the system that backs them.

On the right is gold, and it is a fundamentally different kind of object. You do not own a promise; you own a *thing*. It pays you nothing to wait — that is its cost. It barely beats inflation over the long haul — that is its disappointment. But it has one property that no stock and no bond can ever have: it is **no one's liability**. There is no company that can go bankrupt on you, no government that can default on it, no central bank whose printing press can dilute it. And so, in exactly the regimes where the left column fails together, the right column does its job. That asymmetry — useless most of the time, indispensable at the worst time — *is* gold. Everything else in this series is a footnote to it.

## Foundations: what gold actually is, from the atom up

Before we can talk about gold as money or insurance, we have to be brutally literal about what it physically is, because almost every property that makes it valuable flows directly from its chemistry. Let me build this from zero, defining each idea as we go.

**Gold is a chemical element.** Its symbol is Au (from the Latin *aurum*), and it sits at atomic number 79 on the periodic table. That matters more than it sounds. Being an element means gold cannot be manufactured by any chemical process — you cannot mix two cheaper substances in a lab and produce gold the way you can produce, say, plastic or fertilizer. The only ways to create new gold atoms are inside dying stars and inside particle accelerators, and the accelerator route costs vastly more than the gold is worth. For all practical human purposes, the total amount of gold is fixed by what already exists on the planet plus the trickle we dig out of the ground each year. *Scarcity is built into the physics*, not decreed by any authority.

**Gold is nearly indestructible.** It does not rust, corrode, tarnish, or react with oxygen or water. A gold coin pulled from a 2,000-year-old shipwreck comes up gleaming. This is why we say the gold ever mined still essentially exists: the wedding ring on someone's finger today may contain atoms that were a Roman coin, then a medieval chalice, then a Victorian sovereign, then melted and recast a dozen times. Nothing is consumed. That permanence is the literal physical basis of the phrase "store of value" — gold *stores* because it physically cannot decay.

**Gold is rare but not impossibly rare.** Here is a fact that genuinely surprises people. Every ounce of gold ever mined in all of human history — every coin, every bar, every ring, every electrode — would fit, if melted together, into a cube about 22 metres on each side. That is roughly the volume of about four Olympic swimming pools. The total above-ground stock is estimated at around 216,000 tonnes. The entire 5,000-year accumulation of the most coveted metal on Earth would fit comfortably under the roof of a single large building. That tells you something the price alone does not: gold is not valuable because there is a lot of it doing useful work. It is valuable precisely because there is so little of it, and because no one can make more in a hurry.

**Gold pays no yield.** This is the single most important fact in the entire series, so let me be exact about what "yield" means. Yield is the income an asset throws off just for owning it: a bond pays you a coupon, a stock pays you a dividend, a savings account pays you interest, a rental property pays you rent. That income is the reward for tying up your money. Gold pays none of it. A bar of gold you buy today and hold for thirty years will, at the end, be exactly one bar of gold — no bigger, no richer, generating not a single cent of income along the way. In the language of finance, gold is a *zero-coupon, non-cash-flowing asset*. Its entire return, whatever it turns out to be, comes only from the change in its price.

That single fact has an enormous consequence, and it is worth stating plainly because we will return to it in every post: **the cost of holding gold is the income you gave up by not holding something that pays.** Economists call this *opportunity cost*. If a safe government bill pays you 4.5% interest, then holding gold instead of that bill costs you 4.5% per year in forgone income. Gold has to rise by more than that just to break even against the boring, safe alternative. We will work that number out with real dollars in a moment.

#### Worked example: the opportunity cost of holding gold for a year

Suppose you have \$10,000 and two choices. Choice A: buy a one-year Treasury bill (a short-term loan to the US government, about as safe as anything exists) paying 4.5%. Choice B: buy \$10,000 of gold.

If you pick the T-bill, at the end of the year you have \$10,000 × 1.045 = **\$10,450**. You earned \$450 risk-free, simply for waiting. If you pick gold, your \$10,000 of gold is worth... whatever gold is worth in a year, and it paid you nothing in the meantime. For gold to leave you as well off as the T-bill, it must rise enough that your gold is worth at least \$10,450 — that is, it must climb by at least 4.5% just to *match* the safe alternative. Anything less and you would have been better off in the boring bill.

So the \$450 of forgone interest is the real, measurable price of owning gold for that year. The intuition: gold is never "free" to hold — every year you own it, you are quietly paying the interest you could have earned elsewhere, and that premium is exactly why gold struggles when safe yields are high and shines when they collapse.

Now hold that thought, because it is the seed of the deepest idea in gold investing. The thing gold competes against is not stocks. It is *cash that earns interest*. When that interest is high, gold is expensive to hold and tends to languish. When that interest is low — or, crucially, when it is *negative after inflation* — the opportunity cost of holding gold vanishes, and gold tends to soar. The whole drama of the gold price is, at bottom, a fight between gold's zero yield and the yield on safe money. We will give that fight its own full post (Track B's "real interest rates, the master variable"), and you will see that almost everything about gold's price comes back to it.

### Stock versus flow: why gold's price barely cares about mining

There is one more foundational idea that trips up almost everyone who comes to gold from a normal commodity background, and getting it right will save you from a hundred confused headlines. For most commodities — oil, wheat, copper — the price is set by the balance between this year's *production* and this year's *consumption*. If oil producers pump more than the world burns, the oil price falls; the *flow* dominates because the stuff gets consumed and has to be continuously replaced.

Gold is the opposite, and the reason is that permanence we discussed. Because gold is never consumed, essentially all the gold ever mined still exists — the ~216,000-tonne above-ground *stock*. Against that enormous standing pile, annual mine production is tiny: the world digs up only about 3,600 tonnes of new gold a year, which is roughly **1.7% of the existing stock**. Add in recycling of about 1,300 tonnes, and total new supply is on the order of 2% to 3% of what already exists. That ratio — the existing stock divided by the annual new flow — is called the *stock-to-flow ratio*, and gold's is by far the highest of any commodity, around 60 years' worth of production sitting above ground.

Why does this matter for the price? Because it means mining barely moves the needle. If a copper miner discovers a giant new deposit and floods the market, copper crashes, because new supply is large relative to the standing inventory. But if a gold miner doubles its output overnight — which is impossible, but suppose — it would add a rounding error to a stock that took five thousand years to accumulate. The gold price is therefore set almost entirely by *demand for the existing stock*: by whether the holders of those 216,000 tonnes want to hold more or less of it, given real yields, the dollar, and fear. This is why, in the chapters ahead, we will spend almost no time on mine-supply forecasts (Track B's supply post explains exactly why "peak gold" stories rarely move the price) and almost all our time on the *monetary* demand for the metal. Gold is a monetary asset wearing a commodity's clothes, and the stock-to-flow ratio is the seam where you can see it.

### What "store of value" actually means

People throw the phrase "store of value" around as if it were obvious, but it is worth defining precisely because gold is the canonical example. A store of value is something that lets you move purchasing power across time without it leaking away. If you do real work this year and convert your effort into something, will that something still buy a comparable basket of goods in ten, fifty, or a hundred years?

Money in a checking account is a *terrible* store of value, because inflation steadily erodes what each unit buys. A dollar in 1970 buys roughly what eight cents bought then — most of its purchasing power has quietly evaporated. A stock is an *excellent* store of value over long horizons because it grows faster than inflation, but it is volatile and can be cut in half in a bad year. Gold sits in a peculiar middle: it does not grow your purchasing power the way stocks do, but it has, over very long arcs, *held* it remarkably well. The classic, almost folkloric illustration is the suit.

#### Worked example: an ounce of gold and a good suit, a century apart

In the 1920s, a fine bespoke men's suit on Savile Row cost roughly the price of one ounce of gold — call it the \$20 to \$35 range that an ounce traded at under the gold standard. A century later, in 2024, a fine bespoke suit still costs roughly the price of one ounce of gold: with gold around \$2,400 an ounce, that buys you a genuinely high-end tailored suit.

The dollar price of both the gold and the suit rose by a factor of roughly a hundred over the century — that is inflation eating the dollar. But measured *against each other*, an ounce of gold bought one excellent suit in 1920 and still buys one excellent suit in 2024. The purchasing power was preserved almost perfectly across a century that included two world wars, the Great Depression, the end of the gold standard, and the birth of the internet.

The intuition is subtle and it is the whole point: gold did not make you *richer* over that century — you could not buy two suits in 2024 where you bought one in 1920. It kept you *exactly as rich*. The constancy is the product. Gold is not a machine for growing wealth; it is a machine for not losing it.

## The 5,000-year track record: why gold survived every currency

Here is a thought experiment. Name a paper currency that has been in continuous use for five hundred years. You cannot, because none exists. The historical batting average of paper money is, frankly, catastrophic. One survey of currencies found that of the hundreds of paper currencies ever issued, the overwhelming majority no longer exist — they were inflated to worthlessness, replaced after revolutions, or redenominated out of existence. The average lifespan of a fiat currency is measured in decades, not centuries. The Roman denarius, the German papiermark, the Zimbabwean dollar, the Venezuelan bolívar — the graveyard is enormous.

Gold has outlived all of them. It was money in ancient Egypt, in Lydia where the first coins were struck around 600 BC, in Rome, in imperial China, in the great Islamic caliphates, in Renaissance Florence, in the British Empire, and it is being accumulated by central banks today in 2026. No king, no parliament, no central bank ever had to *declare* gold valuable. Its value was discovered independently by civilizations that had never heard of each other, on every inhabited continent, which is about as strong a signal as economics ever gives you that something is responding to a real and durable human need rather than to fashion or decree.

Why gold specifically, and not silver, copper, seashells, salt, or cattle — all of which have served as money somewhere? That question gets a full post of its own (Track A's monetary history), but the short version is that gold won a kind of evolutionary contest for the properties money requires. It is durable (does not rot or rust). It is divisible (you can split it into tiny amounts without destroying its value, unlike a cow). It is portable relative to its value (a small bag holds a fortune). It is fungible (one ounce is interchangeable with any other). It is hard to counterfeit (its density gives it away). And, decisively, it is *scarce in a way that cannot be gamed* — you cannot print more gold the way a government can print more paper. Other commodities fail at least one of these tests. Gold passes all of them, and so it kept being rediscovered as money for five thousand years.

This is the historical backbone of the series spine. **Gold's price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money.** Every currency that ever failed did so for the same underlying reason: the people who issued it eventually found it irresistible to issue more, and the more they issued, the less each unit was worth. Gold cannot be issued at will, which is exactly why it has outlasted every currency that could be — and why, in 2026, with global debt at records and the dollar's reserve role being quietly questioned, gold is making new highs.

It is worth being precise about the *mechanism* of currency failure, because it is the same every time and gold is the mirror image of it. A government that controls its own currency faces a permanent temptation: it can pay its bills by printing new money rather than by taxing or borrowing, and printing is politically painless in the short run because no one votes against it directly. But each new unit of currency dilutes the value of every existing unit, exactly as issuing new shares dilutes existing shareholders. In small doses this is mild inflation; in large doses, when a government finances itself almost entirely by the printing press, it becomes hyperinflation and the currency dies. Weimar Germany, where prices doubled every few days in 1923, and Zimbabwe and Venezuela in our own century, are the extreme cases, but the *same arithmetic* operates gently in every fiat currency all the time — which is why the US dollar has lost more than 90% of its purchasing power since 1970. Gold is the asset that cannot be diluted, because no one holds the printing press. That single asymmetry — diluteable paper versus undiluteable metal — is why gold keeps reappearing as the refuge whenever the dilution accelerates. The metal is not doing anything; it is simply standing still while the paper around it loses value, and standing still is the entire job.

![Gold price 1970 to 2026 on a log scale with the 1980, 2011 and 2026 peaks marked](/imgs/blogs/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value-2.png)

The chart above shows the modern price record, from 1970 — just before the US severed the dollar's last link to gold in 1971 — to the 2026 record. I have plotted it on a *logarithmic scale*, which means equal vertical distances represent equal *percentage* moves rather than equal dollar moves. That is essential here, because gold went from \$35 to nearly \$5,400, and on a normal scale the early decades would be an invisible flat line crushed against the bottom. On a log scale you can see the real shape of the history, and the shape tells the whole story.

Notice it is not a smooth upward march. It is *bursts separated by long flat stretches*. There is the explosive 1970s bull that took gold from \$35 to a January 1980 peak of \$850. Then the shaded "winter" — twenty years, from 1980 to roughly 2000, in which gold did *nothing*, drifting sideways and slightly down while stocks went on the greatest bull run in history. Then a second great bull from 2001 to the \$1,920 peak of September 2011, driven by the dot-com bust, the financial crisis, and money-printing. Then another long grind, and finally the violent breakout from 2024 onward to the 2026 record. This is the single most important visual fact about gold, and it is why the spine insists gold is not a compounder: it does not compound steadily. It sleeps for years, even decades, and then it *moves* — and it moves precisely when the monetary world is in trouble.

What did the up-bursts have in common, and what did the winter have in common? This is the question Track D answers in full, but the pattern is visible even from this one chart. The two great bull markets — the 1970s and 2001-2011 — were both periods when the *real* return on safe money was low or negative: in the 1970s, inflation ran into double digits while interest rates lagged behind it, so cash holders were losing purchasing power every year; in the 2000s, the Fed held rates low through two crises and then printed money outright. In both regimes, the opportunity cost of holding yieldless gold collapsed, and gold soared. The long winter, by contrast, was the opposite regime: after 1980, the Fed chairman Paul Volcker crushed inflation by jacking interest rates to nearly 20%, which made cash and bonds wildly attractive in real terms and made holding gold ruinously expensive in opportunity-cost terms. Gold did nothing for twenty years not because the world ran out of fear, but because *safe money paid you handsomely to wait*, and gold could not compete with that. Keep this in your head as the single best predictor in the series: gold tends to win when the real yield on safe money is falling toward zero or below, and to lose when it is high and rising. The bursts are not random. They are a regime.

## What gold is NOT: the modest truth about returns

We have to puncture a myth right now, because half of all bad gold decisions come from believing it. **Gold does not make you rich.** It is not a wealth-multiplication machine, and anyone who tells you it is — usually while trying to sell you some — is either confused or dishonest. Let me prove it with the longest example in this post.

#### Worked example: \$35 to \$5,400 over fifty-six years — the real return

In 1970, gold averaged about \$35.94 per ounce. In early 2026 it printed \$5,589 and was averaging well over \$5,000. So a single ounce went from ~\$36 to ~\$5,400 — it multiplied roughly 150-fold in *nominal* (not-inflation-adjusted) dollars over fifty-six years. Sounds spectacular, right?

Let us turn that into an annual growth rate. A 150-fold increase over 56 years is a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of about 150^(1/56) − 1 ≈ **9.4% per year** in nominal terms. Respectable. But now we have to subtract inflation, because dollars in 2026 buy far less than dollars in 1970. US consumer prices rose roughly 8-fold over that same span, an inflation rate of about 3.8% per year. The *real* return — what gold earned you in actual purchasing power, after inflation — is approximately 9.4% − 3.8% ≈ **5.6% per year** if you measure from the 1970 low, but that flatters gold badly because 1970 was a generational bottom and 2026 is a generational top.

Measure from a more neutral starting point and the picture is harsher. The long-run real return on gold across the whole post-1971 floating era is usually estimated at roughly **1% to 3% per year**, depending on the exact window. Over the same period, US stocks returned about **7% per year in real terms**. Compounded over fifty years, that gap is staggering: \$1 in stocks grows to about \$29 in real purchasing power; \$1 in gold grows to maybe \$2 to \$4. Same starting dollar, an order-of-magnitude different ending.

The intuition is the entire thesis in one line: **gold preserved your purchasing power; stocks multiplied it.** If your goal is to be wealthier in thirty years, gold is the wrong tool. If your goal is to still have your wealth at all after a monetary disaster, gold is one of the very few right tools.

![Real annual returns of stocks bonds cash and gold with gold split into two eras](/imgs/blogs/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value-5.png)

The chart above makes the point harder still, and it carries a second lesson that is easy to miss. On the left are the long-run real (after-inflation) annual returns of the four basic assets: US stocks at about +7%, US bonds at +2.5%, cash (Treasury bills) at a meagre +0.4%, and gold at about +3% across the full ~1971-2024 window. So gold beat cash and roughly matched bonds, but it was left in the dust by stocks. That is the "not a compounder" verdict, in data.

But now look at the two bars on the right, where I have split gold's record into two sub-eras. From 1971 to 2000, gold's real return was approximately **−0.5% per year** — it actually *lost* purchasing power over three decades, the long winter you saw on the price chart. From 2001 to 2024, gold's real return was approximately **+7.5% per year** — it matched or beat stocks. Same metal, two utterly different outcomes, separated by which monetary regime you happened to live in. This is what "gold works in bursts" means in numbers. Gold's lifetime average return is a kind of statistical fiction: in reality it does almost nothing for long stretches and then delivers all of its return in concentrated explosions, and *those explosions cluster in exactly the periods when paper assets are in trouble.* Which is the perfect bridge to the question of what gold is actually *for.*

## The four jobs gold does in a portfolio

If gold barely beats inflation and gets demolished by stocks, why would any sane investor own it? The answer is that gold is not bought for its average return. It is bought for *what it does in the bad years* — and "the bad years" come in several distinct flavors. Gold does four separable jobs, each one a hedge against a different way the paper world can break.

![Four jobs of gold matched to the regime each one wins](/imgs/blogs/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value-4.png)

The grid above lays them out. Let me take each in turn, because keeping them distinct is what separates a clear thinker about gold from a vague one.

**Job one: crisis insurance.** When a financial crisis hits, the thing that fails is *trust between counterparties*. Banks stop trusting banks, lenders stop trusting borrowers, and assets that depend on that trust — stocks, corporate bonds, even some government bonds — can fall together. Gold has no counterparty. There is no one whose failure can make your gold worth less. So in genuine systemic crises, money flees toward the one asset that cannot default, and gold tends to hold or rise while equities crater. We will see the 2008 numbers shortly, and they are stark.

**Job two: dollar hedge.** Gold is priced in dollars, so mechanically, when the dollar weakens against other currencies, it takes more dollars to buy the same ounce, and the gold price (in dollars) rises. But there is a deeper version of this: gold is a hedge against the *debasement of the dollar itself*, against the dollar losing purchasing power over time. In regimes where the dollar is structurally weak — 2002 to 2011, the 2020 money-printing era, the 2025 slide — gold tends to climb. The cross-asset series covers the dollar's gravitational pull on everything in detail; here it is enough to know that gold and the dollar are, most of the time, two ends of a seesaw.

**Job three: crisis liquidity.** This is the most underrated job and the most human. Gold is the most liquid asset on the planet in a true emergency — it is recognized, valued, and sellable in literally every country on Earth, by anyone, without needing a functioning banking system, an internet connection, or a government's permission. In a war, a hyperinflation, a regime collapse, or a bank holiday, your brokerage account and your local currency may be frozen or worthless, but a gold coin sewn into a coat lining is still money everywhere you flee to. This is why refugees throughout history have carried gold, and why it is called the asset of last resort.

**Job four: debasement hedge.** This is the slow-motion version of the dollar hedge, and it is arguably the dominant driver of the 2020s bull market. When governments run large deficits and central banks print money to finance them, the supply of currency expands while the supply of gold stays fixed by physics. Over time, more currency chasing the same fixed quantity of gold means a higher gold price. Gold is the natural "anti-currency" — it is the asset that wins precisely when fiat is being created in volume. With global debt at records and deficits structurally large, this job has rarely looked more relevant.

Notice the unifying thread: each job is a payoff in a *different failure mode of paper*. That is why gold's correlation with stocks is so low — it is not responding to the same things stocks respond to. It is responding to the breakdown of the system that stocks depend on. And that brings us to the cleanest single piece of evidence in this entire post.

It is worth pausing on a subtlety that separates the four jobs, because lumping them together is the most common analytical mistake. Notice that they do not all fire in the same kind of crisis. Job one (crisis insurance) and job three (crisis liquidity) reward you in *acute* events — a market crash, a war, a bank run — sharp episodes that arrive and resolve over weeks or months. Jobs two and four (the dollar hedge and the debasement hedge) reward you in *chronic* ones — the slow grind of a weakening currency and an expanding money supply over years. A given holder of gold might value it overwhelmingly for one job and barely care about the others. A Vietnamese household sewing gold rings into a safe deposit box is buying jobs three and four — protection against a devaluing dong and an asset that works without trusting a bank. A pension fund adding a 5% sleeve is buying jobs one and two — crash insurance and a dollar hedge for a portfolio of dollar assets. A central bank rotating out of Treasuries is buying jobs one and four — sanction-proof insurance and a debasement hedge against the very currencies it is selling. The metal is the same; the job it is hired to do depends entirely on what the buyer is afraid of. When you read that "gold is rising," the useful question is never just *how much* but *which job is the market buying* — and the rest of this series is largely about learning to tell.

## How gold's low correlation showed up in 2022

Correlation is a statistical measure of whether two things move together. A correlation of +1 means they move in lockstep; 0 means they are unrelated; −1 means they move in perfect opposition. The reason gold earns a place in a portfolio is that its correlation with stocks is close to zero over long periods — it marches to its own drummer. But correlation is an abstraction, so let me show you the year it became concrete and unforgettable.

![Total returns of seven assets in 2022 with gold flat and everything else down](/imgs/blogs/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value-3.png)

2022 was the year the textbook broke. For forty years, the bedrock of conservative investing had been the *60/40 portfolio* — 60% stocks for growth, 40% bonds for safety, the idea being that when stocks fall, bonds rise and cushion the blow. That relationship held through crash after crash. Then inflation hit a 40-year high, the Federal Reserve raised interest rates at the fastest pace in modern history, and stocks *and* bonds fell at the same time, for the same reason. The hedge failed exactly when it was needed.

Look at the bars. The Nasdaq 100 fell 32.5%. Long-dated Treasury bonds — the supposedly safe ballast — fell 31.2%, an almost unheard-of loss for "safe" government debt. The S&P 500 fell 18.1%. Investment-grade corporate bonds fell 15.4%. The classic 60/40 portfolio fell 16.1%, its worst year in a generation. Every line in a conventional diversified portfolio was red. And gold? Gold finished the year at **−0.3%** — essentially dead flat.

#### Worked example: the 60/40 versus a 60/35/5 with a gold sleeve

Let us turn that chart into a portfolio you might actually hold. Take \$100,000. The classic mix is \$60,000 in stocks (call it the S&P 500) and \$40,000 in bonds (call it a broad bond index that fell roughly in line with the asset classes above; we will use the 60/40's −16.1% as the blended outcome). After 2022, that \$100,000 became about **\$83,900** — a loss of \$16,100.

Now build a portfolio that carves out a small gold sleeve: \$60,000 in stocks (−18.1% → loses \$10,860), \$35,000 in bonds (using the IG bond −15.4% → loses \$5,390), and \$5,000 in gold (−0.3% → loses \$15). Total loss: \$10,860 + \$5,390 + \$15 = **\$16,265**. Hmm — that is actually a hair *worse* than the pure 60/40, because in this slice we funded the gold out of stocks, and we used slightly different sub-indices.

The honest lesson is the important one, and it is why I did not rig the example: a *5%* gold sleeve does not transform a bad year into a good one — it is too small for that. What it does is reduce the *worst-case dispersion* and, crucially, give you a liquid, gainful asset to *rebalance from* — to sell high and buy your beaten-down stocks low at the bottom. In years when gold actually rises during the stress (2008, 2020), even a 5% sleeve meaningfully cushions the fall; in 2022 it merely stopped bleeding while everything else hemorrhaged. The intuition: gold's job is not to win the portfolio, it is to not lose when the rest of the portfolio loses together — and that uncorrelated flatness is worth paying for, even at the price of a zero yield.

That is the deepest reason gold survives in serious portfolios despite its mediocre average return. Its value is not in its mean; it is in its *behavior in the tail*, the rare extreme events where everything correlated fails at once. You buy gold the way you buy fire insurance: not because you expect the house to burn, but because if it does, nothing else in your possession will help.

## The marginal buyer changed: enter the central banks

Here is the development that has rewired the gold market in the 2020s, and it sets up most of Track B and Track C of this series, so I want to plant the seed carefully. In any market, the price is set at the margin — by the *last* buyer and seller who transact, not by the giant pile of people sitting still. For decades, the marginal buyers of gold were jewelry consumers in India and China and a fickle crowd of Western investors who piled in during scares and dumped it during calm. That made gold's price jumpy and sentiment-driven.

Then, around 2022, something structural happened. The official sector — the central banks of the world's governments — became massive, persistent, price-insensitive buyers of gold. And once you understand *why*, you understand the floor under the current bull market.

![Central bank net gold purchases per year from 1999 to 2025 showing the post 2022 surge](/imgs/blogs/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value-7.png)

The chart above is one of the most important in the series. Each bar is the net amount of gold (in tonnes) that the world's central banks bought or sold in a given year. Look at the left side, the red bars: from 1999 to about 2009, central banks were net *sellers*, dumping 400 to 600 tonnes a year. The conventional wisdom of that era was that gold was a "barbarous relic," a yieldless embarrassment on a modern balance sheet, and central banks were selling it to hold income-producing dollars and bonds instead. (Britain famously sold half its gold reserves at the very bottom of the market around 1999, an episode now bitterly remembered as "Brown's Bottom.")

Then watch the bars cross zero around 2010 and climb. And then watch the three green bars: in 2022, 2023, and 2024, central banks bought **1,082, 1,037, and 1,045 tonnes** respectively — more than a thousand tonnes a year, every year, the highest sustained official buying since the 1950s. The 2010-2021 average had been about 473 tonnes; the post-2022 pace more than doubled it. The marginal buyer of gold is no longer a nervous Western investor or a wedding in Mumbai. It is the People's Bank of China, the central banks of Russia, India, Turkey, Poland, and a long list of others.

#### Worked example: why a central bank would rather hold gold than dollars

Put yourself in the seat of a central bank reserve manager outside the Western alliance — say, a country sitting on \$400 billion of reserves. Historically you would have held the bulk of it in US Treasury bonds, because they are deep, liquid, and *they pay you interest*: at 4.5%, that \$400 billion throws off about \$18 billion a year. Gold, by contrast, pays you nothing. On a pure income basis, the dollars win every time, and that is exactly why central banks held dollars and sold gold for decades.

Now reprice the *risk*. In 2022 the world watched roughly \$300 billion of one country's reserves get frozen overnight by the issuers of those very currencies. Ask yourself: what is the expected cost of holding a sanction-able reserve, once you assign even a small probability to being on the wrong side of a geopolitical dispute? If you judge there is, say, a 5% chance over a decade that a meaningful slice of your dollar reserves becomes inaccessible, that tail risk can easily dwarf the \$18 billion of annual interest. Gold's zero yield suddenly looks like a *reasonable insurance premium* to escape that tail. The intuition: for a sovereign, gold's lack of yield is not the cost it once was — it is the price of owning the one reserve asset that no foreign government can switch off, and after 2022 that price looked cheap.

That recalculation, run in dozens of central banks at once, is the engine behind the green bars on the chart. And it explains the trigger directly: in 2022, after Russia invaded Ukraine, the United States and its allies *froze* roughly \$300 billion of Russia's foreign-currency reserves — dollars and euros that Russia thought it owned but discovered it could not access. Every central bank on Earth drew the same lesson: reserves held as someone else's currency are reserves someone else can switch off. Gold cannot be frozen, sanctioned, or defaulted on, because it is no one's liability and it sits in your own vault. The official sector responded by quietly rotating toward the one reserve asset that is sanction-proof. That is a *structural* source of demand — driven by geopolitics, not by the gold price — and it puts a kind of floor under the market that simply did not exist in the 1999 selling era. This is the single biggest reason a 5,000-year-old rock is at all-time highs in a digital age: the people who issue the digital money are themselves buying the rock.

## Common misconceptions

Before we go further in the series, let me clear away the three most damaging myths about gold, each corrected with a number, because believing any of them will lead you astray.

**Myth 1: "Gold is an inflation hedge — when inflation goes up, gold goes up."** This is true over decades and badly false over months. The correlation between monthly inflation surprises and the monthly gold price is weak to nonexistent. The proof is right in our data: in 2022, US inflation hit a 40-year high of 9.06% — and gold finished the year *down* 0.3%. If gold were a tight month-to-month inflation hedge, 2022 should have been one of its best years ever; instead it was flat, because the *real interest rate* (the inflation-adjusted yield on safe bonds) was rising hard, and that mattered more. Gold preserves purchasing power across decades; it does not track the monthly CPI print. We devote a whole Track B post to this distinction.

**Myth 2: "Gold is useless because it pays no yield — only an idiot holds a non-cash-flowing rock."** This confuses *yield* with *value*. Cash also pays little or nothing in real terms (a real return of about +0.4% per year), yet no one calls cash useless, because its value is in what it does in a portfolio, not in its yield. Gold's "yield" is its insurance payoff in the tail: in 2008, when the S&P 500 fell about 37% in a year, gold *rose* about 5.5%. That counter-move, in the year you most needed it, is worth far more than the missing coupon — and the people who sneered at the yieldless rock in 1999 watched it then rise more than sevenfold over the following twelve years.

**Myth 3: "Gold is only for doomsday preppers and conspiracy theorists."** Tell that to the People's Bank of China, the Reserve Bank of India, and the central banks of Poland, Turkey, and Singapore, who together bought more than 1,000 tonnes a year in 2022, 2023, and 2024. The largest, most sober, most risk-averse financial institutions on the planet — the ones whose entire job is to hold safe reserves — have been the marginal buyers of gold for three straight years. Gold is not a fringe asset; it is, increasingly, a *sovereign* one. The doomers were early, but the institutions arrived.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Let me close the case with three real episodes — specific years, specific numbers — where gold did exactly the job the thesis predicts. Keep the spine in mind as you read each one: in every case, gold paid off in a regime where conventional assets failed together.

**2008 — the Global Financial Crisis.** The banking system nearly collapsed. The S&P 500 fell about 37% over the calendar year (and far more peak-to-trough), the worst year for US stocks since the Great Depression. Credit markets froze; even "safe" assets were sold to raise cash. And gold? For the full year 2008, gold *rose* about 5.5%. (It is worth noting the texture: in the worst weeks of the panic, even gold dipped briefly as funds sold everything liquid to meet margin calls — the "liquidity squeeze" exception we will study in Track B — but it recovered fast and ended the year up, then kept climbing through the recovery. This is job one, crisis insurance, working as designed.)

**2020 — the COVID shock and the money flood.** Markets crashed in March 2020 as the pandemic hit, then central banks and governments unleashed the largest monetary and fiscal flood in peacetime history — the Fed's balance sheet roughly doubled, and trillions in stimulus hit the system. Gold's response was textbook job four: with safe real yields driven deeply negative and the money supply exploding, gold rose about 25% in 2020 and set a then-record above \$2,075 an ounce in August. When the printing press runs hot, the anti-currency wins.

**2022 — the synchronized rate shock.** We have already dissected this one. Inflation at a 40-year high, the fastest Fed hiking cycle in modern history, and the 60/40 portfolio's worst year in a generation at −16%. Stocks fell, bonds fell *with* them, and the comfortable diversification of a generation evaporated. Gold finished flat. In a year when the two pillars of conventional investing collapsed together, the uncorrelated rock was the only thing in the room that did not break. That is the single cleanest demonstration of why gold exists in a portfolio, and it happened in living memory.

Three crises, three different flavors — a credit freeze, a money flood, a rate shock — and in each one gold did its specific job while the correlated paper world struggled. None of these made you rich. All of them kept you from being wiped out in the regime that did the wiping. That is the entire pitch, demonstrated three times in fifteen years.

## The takeaway: hold gold the way you hold insurance

So let me bring it all home, because the *way you think about gold* determines whether it helps you or hurts you.

If you walk away from this post believing gold is a get-rich asset, you will buy it at the top of a mania, expect it to keep soaring, and then suffer through a twenty-year winter wondering why your "investment" went nowhere while your friends in stocks got wealthy. That is the trap, and it has snared generations of latecomers, because the bursts are seductive and the winters are long. The data is unambiguous: gold's long-run real return is low single digits, far below stocks, and it delivers that return in violent, unpredictable spurts separated by years of dead money.

But if you walk away believing gold is *monetary insurance* — a non-yielding, no-counterparty asset whose price is the world's real-time vote of confidence in paper money, real yields, and political stability — then everything snaps into focus. You will size it like insurance: a modest sleeve, often cited in the 5% to 15% range, not your whole portfolio. You will not panic when it does nothing for years, because insurance is *supposed* to do nothing in the good years; that is what you are paying for. You will not chase it at the top of a fear spike. And you will be quietly grateful you held it in the one year when your stocks and your bonds fell together and the only green line on your statement was the rock.

There is one more practical reason a modest gold sleeve earns its keep, and it is the most counterintuitive: the *rebalancing bonus*. Because gold is uncorrelated with stocks, it tends to be up (or merely flat) in exactly the years stocks are down. If you hold a fixed target — say 10% gold, 90% stocks — and you rebalance back to that target once a year, then after a stock crash your gold sleeve has grown as a *share* of the portfolio, and rebalancing forces you to sell some of the relatively expensive gold and buy the relatively cheap stocks at the bottom. You are mechanically buying low and selling high, without having to predict anything, purely because the two assets zig and zag at different times. Over a full cycle, that disciplined rebalancing can add more to a portfolio's return than gold's own meagre standalone return would suggest — the diversification is, in the famous phrase, the closest thing to a free lunch in finance. This is the rigorous version of "why hold an asset that barely beats inflation," and it is why allocators who care only about the math, not about gold's mystique, still find room for it. We give the sizing-and-rebalancing decision a full quantitative treatment in Track E, and the cross-asset companion post linked below builds the free-lunch argument from scratch.

The deepest insight — the one this whole series will keep returning to — is that the gold price is not really telling you about gold at all. It is telling you about *everything else*. When gold is rising hard, it is the market whispering that it trusts paper a little less than it did: that real yields are too low to compensate for the risk, or that the dollar is being debased, or that the political and monetary order feels less stable. Gold at \$5,589 in January 2026 is not a story about a shiny metal. It is a story about a world of record debt, structurally large deficits, a questioned reserve currency, and central banks quietly buying the one asset they cannot be cut off from. The rock is the messenger. The message is about us.

That is why a 5,000-year-old store of value still matters in a digital age — and why it will matter long after this year's headlines fade. Now that you have the thesis, the rest of the series is the deep mechanics behind it. Let us begin.
