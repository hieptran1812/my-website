> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Over a century, gold is a preservation asset, not a growth asset: its real return is modest, but its ratios to stocks and oil are powerful valuation gauges that strip out currency debasement.
>
> - From 1971 to 2024 gold returned roughly **+3% per year after inflation** — far below stocks (~7%), but the figure hides two opposite eras: **−0.5%/yr in 1971–2000** and **+7.5%/yr in 2001–2024**.
> - The **Dow/gold ratio** — how many ounces of gold buy the Dow — swung from about **1 in 1980** (gold dear, stocks cheap) to about **42 in 1999** (stocks dear, gold cheap). Each extreme told you exactly which asset to own next.
> - The **gold/oil ratio** — barrels of oil per ounce — mean-reverts around the low-30s, so it flags when an ounce buys unusually little or unusually much energy.
> - The one fact to remember: an ounce of gold bought a fine set of formal clothes in ancient Rome and still does today. **Gold did not get richer; the dollar got poorer** — and that is the whole story behind a price that climbed from \$20 to over \$3,000.

## The ounce that does not change

On 21 January 1980 the London gold fix printed \$850 an ounce — a record that, in inflation-adjusted terms, would not be beaten for another forty-four years. If you had bought that day, you spent the next two decades watching gold fall, the Dow scream higher, and every dinner guest tell you that gold was a relic. By 1999 the press had a name for your asset: a "barbarous relic," and a "pet rock." Gold sat near \$280; the Dow had multiplied roughly thirteenfold.

And yet. The ounce you bought in 1980 still bought, in 1980, a good men's suit. The ounce still bought a good men's suit in 1999. It buys a fine tailored suit today, at over \$3,000. The number on the price tag exploded — but what the ounce *purchases* barely moved. That is the secret hiding inside every gold cycle, and it only becomes visible when you stop measuring gold in dollars and start measuring it in *things*: suits, the Dow, barrels of oil, real baskets of goods.

Every other post in this series zooms *in*: on a single cycle, a single driver, a single crisis, a single decade. Each of those views is true and useful, but each is also a fragment, and fragments mislead — the 1980–2000 winter looks like proof gold is a dead asset; the 2001–2024 bull looks like proof it is a wealth machine; the 2026 record looks like proof it is a bubble. Hold any one fragment up to the light and you draw the wrong lifetime conclusion. The only way to see gold for what it actually is, is to stand far enough back that all the fragments resolve into a single shape.

This post does the one thing that no single cycle can do: it zooms all the way out. We will look at gold's real return over half a century and longer, and we will price gold not in a currency that is constantly being debased but in *ratios* — the Dow/gold ratio and the gold/oil ratio — that divide the dollar out entirely. Seen this way, gold stops looking like a wild speculation and starts looking like what it has always been: a constant ruler against which the value of paper money, stocks, and energy can be measured. The figure below is the whole arc on one chart.

![Gold price 1970 to 2026 on a log scale with peaks and bottoms marked](/imgs/blogs/gold-over-the-long-arc-real-returns-the-dow-gold-ratio-and-gold-oil-1.png)

Notice that the chart is drawn on a *log* scale — each gridline is a multiple, not an addition — because gold ran from \$35 to over \$5,000 and on a normal scale the entire first thirty years would be a flat line on the floor. The log view reveals the real shape: a great surge in the 1970s, a twenty-year winter into 1999–2000, and a long secular bull from 2001 onward. Hold that shape in mind. The rest of this post explains what it means once you take the dollar out of it.

A quick word on why the log scale is not a cosmetic choice but a conceptual one. On a normal (linear) scale, a move from \$1,000 to \$2,000 looks ten times bigger than a move from \$100 to \$200, even though both *doubled* your money — both are +100%. For an asset that has risen a hundredfold, a linear chart crushes all the early action into an invisible sliver near the axis and makes the recent dollar moves look apocalyptic by comparison. A log scale plots *percentage* moves at equal heights, so a doubling looks the same whether it happens at \$100 or \$5,000. That is the only fair way to compare gold's behavior across eras separated by a factor of a hundred — and it quietly makes the same point the ratios will make explicitly: what matters is proportional change, not the dollar count, because the dollar count is contaminated by the shrinking dollar.

## Foundations: real returns, and why a ratio is a deflator

Before we can read the long arc, we need three ideas from zero. None requires any finance background.

**Nominal vs real.** A *nominal* return is the change in the number of dollars you have. A *real* return is the change in what those dollars can *buy*. If your money grows 5% in a year but prices also rose 5%, your nominal return is +5% and your real return is **0%** — you have more dollars and exactly the same purchasing power. Real return is the only number that matters for whether you got richer, and it is *always* lower than nominal return whenever there is inflation. Over long spans the gap is enormous, because inflation compounds. A dollar in 1913 buys about three cents of goods today; it lost roughly 97% of its purchasing power. So a price quoted in dollars across a century is measuring two things at once — the asset *and* the shrinking dollar — and you cannot tell them apart without deflating.

**Why deflate, and what a ratio does instead.** The textbook way to find a real return is to subtract inflation (or divide the price by a price index like the CPI). That works, but it depends on which index you trust, and indices are revised and disputed. There is a cleaner trick that requires no index at all: **price one real asset in units of another real asset.** How many ounces of gold does it take to buy the Dow Jones Industrial Average? How many barrels of oil does one ounce buy? Both numerator and denominator are quoted in dollars, so when you divide, *the dollar cancels out*. A ratio of two real things is automatically inflation-adjusted. It is the cheapest, most honest deflator there is — and it is the heart of this post.

**What the two famous ratios measure.** The **Dow/gold ratio** measures gold against the broad US stock market — the productive, dividend-paying, compounding economy. When the ratio is low, gold is expensive relative to stocks (or stocks are cheap); when it is high, stocks are expensive relative to gold (or gold is cheap). The **gold/oil ratio** measures gold against energy — the input to almost all economic activity. It tells you how much real energy an ounce of gold commands. Both ratios mean-revert, because the dollar drift that pushes raw prices ever higher has been divided away, leaving only the genuine, bounded relationship between two assets that both retain real value.

**The arithmetic of compounding, briefly.** It is worth being precise about *why* a couple of percentage points of annual return matter so much over a century, because the gap between gold and stocks is the whole reason this post insists on the words "preservation" versus "growth." Money that grows at rate r per year multiplies by (1 + r) each year, and over n years it multiplies by (1 + r) raised to the power n. That exponent is merciless. At 7% real, money doubles roughly every ten years (the "rule of 72": 72 divided by the rate gives the doubling time); over a century that is about ten doublings, or a factor of roughly a thousand. At 0% real — gold's rough 20th-century figure — there are *zero* doublings, and a century later you have exactly what you started with in real terms. This is not a small difference that compounding magnifies; it is the difference between a thousand-fold gain and break-even. Any asset that produces no income to reinvest is structurally locked out of that exponential, and gold produces no income by design.

**Why "no income" is the whole story.** A stock is a claim on a stream of future profits; a bond is a claim on a stream of future interest payments; a rental property is a claim on a stream of future rents. Each of those streams can be *reinvested* — last year's dividend buys more shares, which pay more dividends — and reinvestment is the engine of compounding. Gold has no stream. It is a pure, sterile asset: a lump of metal that will be the same lump, the same weight, the same purity, a thousand years from now. That sterility is simultaneously its great weakness (no compounding) and its great strength (no counterparty who can default on you, no earnings that can collapse, no dividend that can be cut). When you hold gold you are not lending to anyone or owning a piece of anyone's promises; you are holding the asset itself, and the asset cannot go bankrupt. The price of that safety is the foregone compounding — economists call it the *opportunity cost* of holding gold, and it is exactly the real yield you could have earned elsewhere. That single trade-off — safety bought with foregone yield — is the master variable behind the gold price, and it is why gold does best precisely when real yields are low or negative and the foregone return is small.

One more foundational point, and it is the spine of this whole series: **gold is not an investment that compounds.** It pays no coupon, no dividend, no rent. An ounce in your vault is still one ounce a century later. Stocks reinvest earnings and grow; bonds pay interest; gold just *sits there and keeps its value*. That single fact is why its long-run real return is modest, and why the right yardstick for gold is preservation — holding your place — rather than growth. Keep that distinction sharp; the misconceptions section is full of people who forgot it.

## Gold's long-run real return: modest, and wildly era-dependent

Now the central, slightly deflating number. Over the post-gold-standard era — call it 1971 (the year the dollar's last link to gold was cut) to 2024 — gold's real return was about **+3% per year.** Positive, real, after inflation. But put it next to the alternatives and it looks pedestrian.

![Real returns by asset 1971 to 2024 with gold split into two eras](/imgs/blogs/gold-over-the-long-arc-real-returns-the-dow-gold-ratio-and-gold-oil-2.png)

US stocks returned roughly **+7%/yr** in real terms over the same span; bonds about **+2.5%**; cash (T-bills) a thin **+0.4%**. So gold beat cash and roughly matched bonds, but stocks left it far behind. Over 53 years, compounding makes that gap brutal.

#### Worked example: a real dollar in each asset, 1971–2024

Start with \$1 of *real* (today's) purchasing power in 1971 and let it compound at each asset's real rate for 53 years:

```
real-growth(rate, years) = (1 + rate/100) ^ years

stocks  @ 7.0%  ->  1.07^53  =  ~$36.1   of today's purchasing power
gold    @ 3.0%  ->  1.03^53  =  ~$4.79
bonds   @ 2.5%  ->  1.025^53 =  ~$3.73
cash    @ 0.4%  ->  1.004^53 =  ~$1.24
```

Your real dollar in stocks became almost \$36; in gold, under \$5. Gold roughly **quintupled your purchasing power over half a century** — real preservation with a little growth on top — while stocks multiplied it thirty-six-fold. The lesson is not that gold is bad; it is that gold is *not the growth engine of a portfolio.* It held and slightly extended your wealth while paying you nothing to wait.

But the +3% headline hides the most important fact on the chart: the two amber-and-red bars on the right. Split gold's history at the millennium and you get two opposite assets. From **1971 to 2000**, gold's real return was about **−0.5% per year** — it actually *lost* purchasing power across an entire generation. From **2001 to 2024**, it returned about **+7.5% per year** in real terms — out-running stocks over that window.

#### Worked example: the two gold eras, in real dollars

```
era 1971-2000 (29 years) @ -0.5% real:  1.0^29 with -0.5% -> ~$0.86
   a real dollar of gold SHRANK to 86 cents over 29 years.

era 2001-2024 (24 years) @ +7.5% real:  1.075^24 -> ~$5.67
   a real dollar of gold GREW to $5.67 over 24 years.
```

The same metal, the same vault, the same "store of value" — and in one generation it quietly bled 14% of your real wealth, while in the next it nearly sextupled it. The intuition: **gold's real return is not a constant; it is a payoff for being early to monetary distress.** It does nothing for decades and then does everything in a compressed window, which is exactly why an *average* return badly misrepresents it.

**What actually drove each era.** The two halves are not random; each was governed by the master variable, real yields. From the early 1980s through the late 1990s, the United States ran *high and rising real interest rates*. Paul Volcker had crushed inflation with brutal rate hikes by 1981–82, and for the next two decades you could earn a fat, safe, inflation-beating return in Treasury bonds and even in cash. When the safe alternative pays 4–5% real, the opportunity cost of holding a metal that pays nothing is enormous, and gold did exactly what theory predicts: it fell, year after year, for twenty years. The 1971–2000 figure of −0.5% real per year *is* that opportunity cost made visible. Then the regime flipped. From 2001 onward — through the dot-com bust, the 2008 crisis, the zero-rate decade, the COVID money-printing, and the 2020s fiscal blowout — real yields collapsed, repeatedly going *negative*. When cash and bonds lose purchasing power, the opportunity cost of holding gold vanishes, and the metal's lack of yield stops being a handicap. Gold's +7.5% real per year in 2001–2024 *is* that vanished opportunity cost made visible. The era split is not two coincidences; it is one mechanism — real yields — running in two directions.

**Why the average is a lie of a number.** Quoting gold's "+3% real per year, 1971–2024" is technically true and practically misleading, in the same way that a person with one foot in ice water and one in boiling water is "on average comfortable." The +3% is the blend of a −0.5% generation and a +7.5% generation, and you experienced one or the other, not the blend, depending on when you were born and when you bought. This is the single most important statistical fact about gold: its returns are not drawn from a stable, bell-shaped distribution that an average summarizes well. They are *regime-dependent* — long, flat, even negative stretches punctuated by violent multi-year repricings. An investor who buys gold expecting "+3% real, smoothly" will be wrong every single year; the return arrives lumpy or not at all. That is why a serious gold framework leans on the ratios, which read the *regime*, rather than on the long-run average, which hides it.

That era-dependence is the bridge from "how much does gold return" to "when is gold worth owning" — and the cleanest answer to *when* lives in the ratios.

## The Dow/gold ratio: the great valuation gauge

Here is the single most useful long-arc chart for anyone who owns both stocks and gold. The Dow/gold ratio asks: *how many ounces of gold would it take to buy one share of the Dow?* (Take the Dow's point level, divide by the gold price.) It is a tug-of-war between the two great pillars of wealth — the productive economy and the monetary metal — with the dollar removed from both sides.

![Dow to gold ratio from 1980 to 2026 with the 1980 and 1999 extremes marked](/imgs/blogs/gold-over-the-long-arc-real-returns-the-dow-gold-ratio-and-gold-oil-3.png)

Read the extremes. In **1980**, at the top of gold's mania and the bottom of stocks' decade-long malaise, the ratio fell to about **1** — one ounce of gold bought the entire Dow. In **1999**, at the peak of the dot-com bull and the depths of gold's winter, the ratio reached about **42** — it took forty-two ounces to buy the Dow. By the 2008–2011 crisis it had collapsed back toward **6–9**, and today it sits in the high single digits to mid-teens.

Why does this particular ratio carry so much information? Because the Dow is the cleanest available proxy for *the productive economy* — a basket of large, profitable, dividend-paying companies that compound over time — while gold is the cleanest proxy for *monetary insurance*. Dividing one by the other gives you the market's running verdict on a single deep question: how much do people trust paper claims on future corporate earnings versus a sterile metal that promises nothing but its own permanence? When confidence in the future is sky-high, capital floods into stocks and abandons gold, the numerator soars, the denominator sags, and the ratio screams toward 40. When confidence collapses, capital flees productive risk for the metal, and the ratio crashes toward 1. The ratio is therefore not really about gold or stocks individually; it is a barometer of *collective faith in the financial system*, and it happens to be quoted in ounces.

Each extreme was a once-a-generation signal — and crucially, it told you what to do *next*, not what had already happened.

#### Worked example: Dow/gold at 1 (1980) vs 42 (1999) — what to own

Picture two investors with the same simple rule: own whichever asset is *cheap* in ratio terms.

```
1980: Dow/gold ~= 1  (one ounce buys the whole Dow)
   reading: gold is as expensive as it has ever been vs stocks
   action:  SELL gold, BUY stocks
   what happened next: the 1980-2000 stock bull (Dow up ~13x);
                       gold fell for 20 years.

1999: Dow/gold ~= 42 (forty-two ounces to buy the Dow)
   reading: stocks are as expensive as they have ever been vs gold
   action:  SELL stocks, BUY gold
   what happened next: gold rose ~7.5%/yr real for two decades;
                       stocks had a "lost decade" into 2009.
```

The investor who simply bought the *low-ratio* asset at each extreme caught both the great stock bull and the great gold bull — by doing the opposite of the crowd at the precise moments the crowd was most sure. The ratio did not predict the timing to the day; the 1999 reading flashed "gold is cheap" a little early and "stocks are dear" a little early. But over the *long arc* it was right in a way no dollar price ever could be, because a dollar price of gold tells you nothing about whether gold is cheap relative to the thing you'd actually switch into. The intuition: **the Dow/gold ratio is a relative-value thermostat for your entire long-term portfolio** — and the colder or hotter it reads, the more you should listen.

**How the ratio is actually built — and why that makes it honest.** The construction is almost embarrassingly simple, and the simplicity is the point. Take the level of the Dow Jones Industrial Average — say 35,000 points — and divide by the price of an ounce of gold — say \$2,500. The answer, 14, is the Dow/gold ratio: it would take fourteen ounces of gold to buy "one Dow." Both numbers are quoted in dollars, so the dollar units cancel in the division and you are left with a pure ratio of two real assets. There is no model, no assumption, no fudge factor, no inflation index to argue about. That is why the ratio has been used by long-arc investors for over a century while fancier valuation models come and go: it cannot be gamed, because it is just arithmetic on two market prices that anyone can look up.

#### Worked example: computing the Dow/gold ratio from raw prices

```
inputs (illustrative, ~2026):
   Dow Jones Industrial Average level  = 45,000 points
   gold price                          = $3,200 / oz

Dow/gold ratio = 45,000 / 3,200 = ~14 ounces

now run the dollar OUT explicitly:
   Dow level is in "dollars per Dow-unit"
   gold price is in "dollars per ounce"
   45,000 [$ / Dow] / 3,200 [$ / oz] = 14.06 [oz / Dow]
   -> the $ cancels; the unit is ounces of gold per Dow.

read it: 14 is mid-range (history runs ~1 to ~42).
   gold is neither historically cheap nor dear vs stocks here.
```

The dollar literally cancels in the units, leaving ounces per Dow — a number no amount of money-printing can inflate, because printing money tends to push *both* the Dow and gold up together, leaving the ratio roughly unchanged. The intuition: the Dow/gold ratio measures the *relative* health of the productive economy versus the monetary metal, with the noise of currency debasement filtered out, which is exactly why it survives a century of inflation without trending.

**A century of swings, not just two points.** The two extremes — ~1 in 1980, ~42 in 1999 — are the headline, but the full history is a series of grand oscillations that map almost perfectly onto the alternating regimes of US economic confidence. The ratio reached an earlier peak of about 18 in 1929 just before the Crash (stocks euphoric, gold fixed and cheap), bottomed near 2 in 1932 at the Depression low and again near 1 in 1980, climbed back to its all-time high of ~42 at the millennium, collapsed to ~6.5 by 2011, and has chopped in the high single digits to high teens since. Read across the century, the pattern is unmistakable: the ratio is high when society is confident in paper claims on the future (1929, 1999) and low when it has lost faith and fled to the metal (1932, 1980, 2011). It is, quite literally, a hundred-year chart of confidence in the financial system, denominated in gold.

A practical note on how to use it. The ratio is a *valuation* tool, not a *timing* tool. A reading of 42 did not mean "sell all stocks tomorrow"; it meant "the long-run odds have shifted hard toward gold, size your allocations accordingly, and expect the turn within a few years, not a few weeks." Extremes can persist and even widen — 1999 felt insane and then 2000 was even higher for a few months. The ratio sets the *direction* of the next decade, not the date of next quarter. It is also a *mean-reverting* gauge with no fixed mean to revert to: the center of its range drifts as the economy's mix of real productivity and monetary distress changes, so the right reading is always relative to its own history ("this is near the high end of the last century"), never against a sacred number.

## The gold/oil ratio: energy purchasing power

If the Dow/gold ratio measures gold against capital, the gold/oil ratio measures it against the most fundamental input to the real economy: energy. The calculation is just as simple — divide the gold price by the oil price — and the unit is intuitive: **barrels of crude oil that one ounce of gold can buy.**

Where the Dow/gold ratio is a barometer of financial confidence, the gold/oil ratio is a barometer of the *physical* economy. Oil is not a financial claim or a sentiment-driven asset; it is the literal fuel of industry, transport, and agriculture, and its price reflects real supply (wells, OPEC quotas, war) and real demand (factories running, planes flying, the global growth cycle). Pricing gold in oil therefore tells you something the Dow/gold ratio cannot: how the monetary metal is valued against the thing that *powers everything*. When an ounce buys a lot of oil, either energy is in a glut or gold has run hot on monetary fears that the physical economy has not validated. When an ounce buys little oil, energy is scarce and gold is, by comparison, the bargain. Two ratios, two lenses — one on the financial economy, one on the physical — and together they triangulate where gold really sits.

![Gold to oil ratio from 2000 to 2026 in barrels per ounce with its mean](/imgs/blogs/gold-over-the-long-arc-real-returns-the-dow-gold-ratio-and-gold-oil-4.png)

Across this sample the ratio averages about **32 barrels per ounce**, but it swings widely around that mean: down to **9–11 barrels** when oil is booming (2000, 2008), out to the **mid-40s** in the 2020 oil crash, and to extreme highs when gold has run far ahead of energy. Because both gold and oil are real assets that hold value through inflation, the ratio has no long-run trend — it oscillates. And an oscillating series that keeps returning to a center is the definition of something you can read for "cheap" and "dear."

#### Worked example: the ounce that buys 9 barrels vs 45 barrels

Take two real readings and translate them into something physical:

```
2008: gold/oil ~= 9 barrels per ounce
   one ounce of gold buys ~9 barrels of oil.
   reading: an ounce buys unusually LITTLE energy
            -> gold is cheap relative to oil (oil is expensive).

2020: gold/oil ~= 45 barrels per ounce
   one ounce of gold buys ~45 barrels of oil.
   reading: an ounce buys unusually MUCH energy
            -> gold is dear relative to oil (oil collapsed below $20).

mean ~= 32 barrels. Both 9 and 45 are far from 32,
so both were "stretched" readings that later snapped back toward the middle.
```

If, in 2008, you could trade an ounce of gold for only nine barrels of oil, energy was historically expensive in gold terms — a hint that either oil would fall or gold would rise to restore the usual ~30:1 relationship. If, in 2020, an ounce fetched forty-five barrels, energy was historically cheap in gold terms — a hint the ratio would compress, which it did as oil recovered. The intuition: **the gold/oil ratio is a single number that tells you whether gold or energy is the bargain right now**, with no inflation adjustment required because the dollar has already cancelled out.

**Why gold and oil are linked at all.** It can seem arbitrary to price a monetary metal in barrels of crude — they are different markets driven by different things. But there is a real economic tether, which is why the ratio mean-reverts rather than wandering off forever. Three threads bind them. First, both are *real assets priced in dollars*, so over long spans both rise with inflation and currency debasement, keeping the ratio range-bound even as both prices climb. Second, energy is a major input cost of producing gold: digging ore, hauling it, and crushing and processing it are enormously energy-intensive, so a sustained rise in oil prices raises gold's all-in production cost and, with a lag, supports its floor. Third, both are classic "real economy" hedges that investors crowd into during inflationary scares — sometimes together, which compresses the ratio, sometimes out of step, which stretches it. The tether is loose enough that the ratio swings widely, but real enough that it always comes home.

**What pulls the ratio to its extremes.** The ratio gets stretched high (an ounce buying *many* barrels) in two situations: an oil *crash* (2020, when crude briefly traded *negative* on the front-month future as storage filled up) or a gold *melt-up* that runs ahead of energy (2024–2026). It gets compressed low (an ounce buying *few* barrels) during an oil *boom* (1973–74, 2008, when crude spiked toward \$150) while gold lags. Crucially, the two cases have different implications: a high ratio driven by collapsing oil often resolves by oil *recovering* (so you'd lean toward energy, not gold), while a high ratio driven by surging gold can resolve by gold *correcting* (so you'd trim gold). Reading the ratio well means asking *which side* moved to create the extreme — the same number can be a signal to buy oil or to sell gold depending on the cause.

One caution that keeps the ratio honest: it is *not* a mechanical buy/sell switch, and the "mean" itself drifts slowly over decades as the energy economy changes (more efficiency, different supply, the long renewables transition that may structurally lower oil's real price). Read it as a gauge of stretch — how far from normal the relationship has wandered — not as a precise trigger. The picture below collects both ratios into a single map of what each regime is telling you.

![Map of cheap and expensive regimes for the Dow gold and gold oil ratios](/imgs/blogs/gold-over-the-long-arc-real-returns-the-dow-gold-ratio-and-gold-oil-5.png)

The common thread, shown in the right-hand column, is the whole reason ratios work: a ratio is a price with the dollar divided out, so it cannot be moved by printing money — only by a *real* change in how the two assets are valued. That is why ratios mean-revert and dollar prices march endlessly higher. The currency drifts; the relationship returns. Buy the cheap side, sell the dear side, and let the dollar do whatever it likes.

It is worth dwelling on *why* a ratio mean-reverts while a raw price does not, because the asymmetry is the entire engine of this approach. A dollar price has an unbounded upward drift built into it: as long as central banks expand the money supply faster than the economy grows, the dollar loses value and *every* real asset's dollar price tends to rise over time. There is no ceiling, because there is no limit to how much money can be printed. A ratio of two real assets has no such drift, because the money-printing that lifts the numerator lifts the denominator too — the effect cancels. What is left after the cancellation is only the *genuine* relationship between two things that both hold real value, and a genuine relationship between two persistent real assets is bounded: stocks can only get so expensive relative to gold before capital rotates, and gold can only get so cheap relative to oil before it is the obvious buy. Bounded series oscillate; unbounded ones trend. That single difference is why you can confidently call a ratio "high" or "low" relative to its own history but can never confidently call a dollar price "high" or "low" — there is no fixed reference, because the ruler itself is melting.

## Gold vs other assets over 50–100 years

Stretch the window from decades to a full century and the picture sharpens further. Over the 20th century, gold's real return was close to **zero** — it spent the first seven decades pinned to fixed official prices under the gold standard and Bretton Woods, and only floated freely after 1971. Stocks, over that same century, compounded at roughly **6–7% real**, turning a real dollar into thousands. By the brute arithmetic of compounding, gold *cannot* compete with productive assets over very long, stable spans, because it produces nothing to reinvest.

This is where many people quietly give up on gold, and the logic seems airtight: if stocks turn a real dollar into thousands and gold turns it into a handful, why hold gold at all? The answer requires distinguishing two completely different jobs an asset can do. The first job is *to grow your wealth* — to take a dollar and make it many dollars over decades — and for that job, productive, compounding, cash-flowing assets win, always, over any long calm period. The second job is *to keep your wealth from being destroyed* in the rare, violent episodes when the financial system itself fails: a hyperinflation, a default, a confiscation, a war that closes the markets. For *that* job, the very feature that makes gold a poor grower — its lack of any counterparty, any promise, any cash flow that can be reneged on — makes it supreme. You do not hold gold to win the long calm; you hold it to survive the rare catastrophe with your purchasing power intact. Asking gold to beat stocks over a century is asking the fire extinguisher to also cook dinner.

So why hold it at all? Because the century also contained two world wars, the Great Depression, the 1933 US gold confiscation, the 1971 default on Bretton Woods, the 1970s stagflation, and 2008. In each, the "compounding" assets did not compound — they crashed, defaulted, or were inflated away — and gold did its one job: it held real value when the system did not. Gold is insurance, and insurance is *supposed* to have a low expected return; you do not buy fire insurance hoping the house burns down.

**Why the 20th-century real return is so close to zero — and why that figure is partly an artifact.** For most of the century gold's price was not free to move: under the classical gold standard and then Bretton Woods, the United States *fixed* the dollar price of gold (\$20.67/oz before 1934, then \$35/oz from 1934 to 1971). A fixed nominal price during decades of inflation means a *falling real price* by construction — gold was legally prevented from repricing while the dollar lost value around it. So gold's flat 20th-century real return is partly the story of a price that was held down by law, then released in 1971 to play catch-up. When it was finally freed, it did exactly that, surging in the 1970s to recover the real value the fixed era had suppressed. This is a subtle but important point: "gold returned nothing real over the 20th century" is true of the *measured price*, but the metal's underlying purchasing power — what an ounce actually bought in goods — was far steadier than the leashed price suggested. The freeing of the price in 1971 is why the post-1971 window (the one we keep using) is the honest one for measuring what gold does when it is allowed to move.

**The shape of the return matters as much as the size.** A 0% real return that arrives as "flat the whole time" is useless as insurance; a 0% real return that arrives as "flat in good times, sharply *up* in crises" is priceless, because the gains land exactly when your other assets are bleeding. Gold's century is the second kind. It went nowhere real through the calm 1950s and 60s, the booming 1980s and 90s, and then delivered explosive real gains in the crisis-soaked 1970s and post-2008 years. That timing — payoffs concentrated in the worst environments for stocks and bonds — is what makes a low-return asset valuable in a portfolio. The technical name is *negative correlation in the left tail*: gold tends to rise when everything else falls hardest, so a slice of it cushions the drawdowns that do the most damage to long-run compounding. You hold gold not for its return but for *when* its return shows up. You can see the asymmetry in gold's real peaks.

![Gold real peaks 1980 versus 2024 in nominal and inflation adjusted dollars](/imgs/blogs/gold-over-the-long-arc-real-returns-the-dow-gold-ratio-and-gold-oil-6.png)

The 1980 peak of \$850 nominal is worth roughly **\$3,300 in 2024 dollars** once you deflate it by the CPI. The 2024 average price was about **\$2,388 nominal** — meaning that, in *real* terms, gold spent **forty-four years** failing to make a new high after January 1980. Stocks made dozens of new real highs over the same span. That single bar comparison is the most honest one-line summary of gold's character: a long, flat, occasionally explosive real-value plateau, not a rising staircase.

#### Worked example: the ounce → fine suit, across a century

Here is the constancy that survives every cycle, priced in clothes rather than dollars:

```
ancient Rome (~1st c.): ~1 oz of gold -> a fine toga + accessories (a full formal outfit)
around 1900: gold ~$20/oz -> ~1 oz bought a quality tailored men's suit
1980 peak:   gold ~$850/oz -> ~1 oz still bought a good men's suit
today:       gold ~$3,000+/oz -> ~1 oz still buys a fine tailored suit

dollar price of the same outfit:
   1900: ~$20      2026: ~$3,000+      -> the DOLLAR price rose ~150x
gold price of the same outfit:
   1900: ~1 oz     2026: ~1 oz         -> the GOLD price was ~unchanged
```

The dollar needed to grow 150-fold to keep buying the same suit; the ounce needed to grow not at all. **Gold did not appreciate against the suit; the dollar depreciated against both.** That is the entire mechanism behind gold's thousand-fold rise in dollar terms — and the reason its real return is nonetheless close to zero over the very long run. The intuition: a metal that holds its purchasing power for two thousand years is doing something no currency has ever done, and *that*, not price appreciation, is the product you are buying.

#### Worked example: why a low-return asset can raise a portfolio's real outcome

A modest allocation to a non-compounding asset can *improve* a portfolio if it cushions the worst drawdowns. Compare two simple paths over a crisis-and-recovery cycle:

```
Portfolio A: 100% stocks
   year 1 (crisis): -40%   ->  $100 -> $60
   year 2 (recovery): +50% ->  $60  -> $90
   net over the cycle: -10%

Portfolio B: 85% stocks + 15% gold (gold +30% in the crisis year)
   crisis year stock leg:  85 x (1-0.40) = 51
   crisis year gold  leg:  15 x (1+0.30) = 19.5  ->  total $70.5
   recovery year (stocks +50%, gold flat 0%):
      stock leg 51 x 1.50 = 76.5 (on the 85-weight base it is
      proportional; using post-crisis values: 51 -> 76.5)
      gold leg 19.5 x 1.00 = 19.5  ->  total ~$96
   net over the cycle: roughly break-even to slightly positive
```

The all-stock portfolio is still underwater after the round trip; the blended one, helped by gold rising *when stocks fell*, is roughly whole — even though gold's standalone long-run return is far lower than stocks'. The intuition: in a portfolio, *when* an asset pays off can matter more than *how much* it pays on average, and gold's payoff lands in exactly the years that do the most damage to compounding.

## Common misconceptions

Almost every popular claim about gold is a fragment of the long arc mistaken for the whole. The bulls quote the 2001–2024 window and call gold a wealth machine; the bears quote the 1980–2000 window and call it a dead rock; the doom-sellers quote the nominal record and call it the only safe asset; the skeptics quote the zero real return and call it useless. Each is reasoning from one slice of a regime-dependent, century-long series — and each lands on a confidently wrong lifetime conclusion. The corrections below all share a single move: replace the cherry-picked slice with the full arc, and replace the dollar price with a real or ratio measure.

**Myth 1: "Gold is a great long-term investment."** It is a great long-term *store of value* and a mediocre long-term *investment* — the words matter. From 1971 to 2024 it returned about +3% real per year, beating cash but trailing bonds plus the equity risk premium; over the full 20th century its real return was roughly zero. A great long-term investment compounds; gold preserves. If your goal is to grow capital over decades, gold is the wrong primary tool. If your goal is to *not lose* capital when currencies, governments, or markets fail, it is one of the best tools that has ever existed. Calling it an "investment" sets you up to be disappointed for the 70% of the time it does nothing.

**Myth 2: "Gold beats stocks."** Only across carefully chosen windows. Buy at the 1999 ratio extreme and gold trounced stocks for two decades; buy at the 1980 extreme and stocks trounced gold for two decades. Over the full 1971–2024 span, a real dollar in gold became ~\$4.79 while a real dollar in stocks became ~\$36 — stocks won by **roughly sevenfold.** Anyone who tells you gold "beats stocks" is quoting a window that starts at gold's cheapest and ends at its dearest. The Dow/gold ratio is precisely the tool that exposes this trick: it shows you that "who wins" depends entirely on the *ratio* you started at.

**Myth 3: "The ratios are mystical / they always revert to a magic number."** The ratios are not mystical and there is no sacred level. The Dow/gold ratio does not have to return to 1; the gold/oil ratio does not have to sit at exactly 30. What is real is the *mechanism*: because both sides are real assets quoted in the same currency, the dollar cancels, so the ratio cannot be inflated upward forever the way a raw price can. That makes the ratios *bounded and mean-reverting*, which is genuinely useful — but the center of the band drifts slowly as economies change (more services, more energy efficiency, different equity earnings power). Treat a ratio reading as "how stretched is this relationship," not "the cosmos demands a return to 1.5."

**Myth 4: "Gold protects you month to month."** Over the long arc gold preserves purchasing power; over any given month or year it can be brutally volatile, and it can fall *during* an inflation scare if real yields are rising at the same time (as in 2013 and 2022). The hedge works by the decade, not the month — which is the entire argument of the inflation post in this series. Confusing the long-arc constancy with short-arc stability is how people buy gold for protection and then panic-sell it in a drawdown.

**Myth 5: "A new nominal high means gold is in a bubble."** Because the dollar shrinks relentlessly, gold makes new *nominal* highs as a matter of routine — a new dollar record is the baseline expectation, not evidence of mania. The 1980 nominal high of \$850 was not exceeded in nominal terms until 2008, and not in *real* terms until the 2020s; for decades, headlines breathlessly reported "gold near record" using nominal prices that were, in real terms, far below 1980. The only honest way to judge whether gold is stretched is to deflate it (the real-price chart) or to price it in ratios (Dow/gold, gold/oil). A nominal record tells you about the dollar; a real or ratio extreme tells you about gold. Mistaking the first for the second has cost people both ways — buying nominal "records" that were real bargains, and selling nominal "records" that had years left to run.

**Myth 6: "Gold has no fundamental value, so the ratios are meaningless."** The opposite is true. Precisely *because* gold pays no cash flow and can't be valued by discounting future earnings, the cleanest way to value it is *relatively* — against other real assets via ratios — rather than absolutely. A stock has an intrinsic value you can model from its profits; gold's "fair value" is inherently a comparison ("how much stock, how much oil, how many goods does it buy versus history?"). Far from making the ratios meaningless, gold's lack of cash flow is exactly *why* the ratios are the right valuation tool for it. The mistake is to demand a discounted-cash-flow number from an asset that has no cash flow, conclude it is "unvaluable," and then either avoid it entirely or treat its price as pure sentiment. Ratios give gold the valuation discipline that DCF gives a stock.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 1980 top, read three ways.** In January 1980 the dollar price said "gold is at a record — buy"; the Dow/gold ratio said "gold has never been more expensive relative to stocks — *sell*"; the real-price chart later confirmed it was a 44-year high. Three lenses, one of them — the ratio — gave the correct, contrarian, long-arc signal while the headline price screamed the opposite. The investor watching the ratio rotated into the cheapest US stocks in a generation and rode the 1980s–90s bull.

**The 1999–2000 bottom.** The financial press declared gold dead; central banks were *selling* (the UK famously auctioned half its reserves near the lows, the "Brown Bottom"); the Dow/gold ratio hit ~42. Every dollar-based and sentiment-based signal said avoid gold. The ratio said the opposite: stocks had never been more expensive relative to gold. What followed was a decade-plus secular gold bull (~7.5%/yr real) and a "lost decade" for the S&P 500. The ratio was the only widely-available gauge pointing the right way *before* the turn.

**2008–2011, the ratio compresses.** As the financial crisis hit and then central banks flooded the world with liquidity, the Dow/gold ratio collapsed from the high teens back toward 6–7 by 2011 — gold roughly tripled while stocks first crashed and then recovered. An investor who had been adding gold since the 1999 extreme was now sitting on the dear side of the ratio, and the gauge was quietly telling them the easy gains in gold were behind them. Gold then went sideways-to-down for the next four years, exactly as a stretched-cheap-stocks ratio would predict.

**2020–2026, the dollar prices go vertical — read the ratios instead.** Gold ran from ~\$1,500 to over \$5,000, a move that, in dollars, looks like a once-in-history bubble. But price it in ratios and it is far less extreme: the Dow/gold ratio fell only to the high single digits (gold getting *less* cheap vs stocks, not absurdly dear), and the gold/oil ratio jumped to ~80 barrels per ounce — a genuine extreme that says gold has, for now, run well ahead of energy. The two ratios disagree about how stretched gold is, which is itself the signal: against stocks gold is mid-range; against oil it is historically rich. A long-arc investor reads that as "gold is fairly valued against the productive economy but expensive against the real economy's fuel" — a far more textured read than the dollar chart's blunt "all-time high."

**The central-bank tell underneath the 2020s move.** What makes the 2020s especially worth reading through ratios is *who* was buying. The post-2022 surge was driven not by retail mania (the classic late-cycle, high-ratio-dollar-price tell) but by *central banks* buying gold at the fastest pace since the 1950s, after the freezing of Russia's dollar reserves showed every government that "safe" reserve assets held in someone else's currency can be switched off. That is a structural, price-insensitive buyer accumulating for monetary insurance, not a speculative crowd chasing momentum. In ratio terms it helps explain why the Dow/gold ratio did *not* blow out to an extreme even as the dollar price tripled: the buying reflected a genuine repricing of monetary risk, not a bubble in sentiment. When the marginal buyer is a central bank de-risking its reserves rather than a taxi driver chasing a chart, a high dollar price carries a very different meaning — another reason the dollar tag alone never tells you enough.

**The reflexive trap the ratios protect you from.** The deepest reason to price gold in ratios is psychological. A rising dollar price *feels* like value being created, and a falling one *feels* like value being destroyed — but for an asset whose entire job is to hold constant while the currency moves, those feelings are exactly backwards. The dollar price of gold can rise simply because the dollar is falling, which is *bad news about your money* dressed up as *good news about your gold*. Ratios strip the feeling out. When you ask "how many ounces buys the Dow" instead of "what is gold worth," you stop reacting to currency noise and start reacting to genuine changes in relative value. Every major gold mistake in the historical record — selling at the 1999 lows, buying the 1980 top, panicking out of the 2013 drawdown — was a reaction to the dollar price. The ratios are the antidote.

## The takeaway: gold as the constant measuring stick

Step back and the long arc resolves into one idea. **Gold is the fixed ruler in a world of rubber-band currencies.** It does not compound, it does not innovate, it does not pay you — and over a full century its real return rounds to roughly nothing. By the standards of a growth asset, that is a failing grade. But that is the wrong report card. Gold's job is not to grow; it is to *not change*, so that everything else can be measured against it.

![Gold as the constant measuring stick across a century with the dollar shrinking](/imgs/blogs/gold-over-the-long-arc-real-returns-the-dow-gold-ratio-and-gold-oil-7.png)

That constancy is what makes the ratios so powerful, and it is the most actionable thing in this post. Stop asking "is gold going up?" — in dollars it almost always eventually is, because the dollar is almost always eventually going down. Ask instead: **how many ounces does the Dow cost, and how many barrels does an ounce buy?** When the Dow/gold ratio is low (single digits), gold is expensive relative to the productive economy and you should favor stocks; when it is high (30+), stocks are expensive relative to gold and you should favor the metal. When the gold/oil ratio is far below its center, gold is cheap against energy; far above, it is dear. These are not magic numbers and they are not market-timing triggers — they are valuation thermostats that tell you which great asset class is the bargain at the start of the next decade.

So how should a long-arc holder think about gold? Hold *some* of it not because it will outperform — over decades it usually will not — but because it is the one asset whose real value has survived every monetary regime, every war, every confiscation, and every default of the last several thousand years. Size it for insurance, not for growth. Add to it when the ratios say it is cheap relative to stocks and energy, and trim it when they say it is dear. And whenever the dollar price makes a frightening new high or a frightening new low, do the one thing this entire post is built to enable: divide the dollar out, look at the ratio, and ask not "what is gold worth in money?" but "what is money worth in gold?" The ounce that bought a fine toga in Rome is still on the table, unchanged, waiting to tell you the answer.

**A practical long-arc checklist.** The framing only helps if it changes what you do, so here is the whole post compressed into a routine you can run in five minutes. First, *never react to a nominal price.* When a headline says "gold hits record," that is a statement about the dollar, not about gold; ignore it until you have deflated. Second, *check the Dow/gold ratio against its century-long range* (~1 to ~42): single digits mean gold is dear relative to the productive economy — favor stocks and trim gold; high twenties and above mean stocks are dear relative to gold — favor the metal. Third, *check the gold/oil ratio against its band* (roughly the low-30s center, single-digits-to-80s range), and ask which side moved to create any extreme — a high ratio from an oil crash points to buying energy, a high ratio from a gold melt-up points to trimming gold. Fourth, *size the position as insurance, not as a bet* — a slice large enough to matter in a crisis (commonly cited ranges run from a few percent to low double digits of a portfolio) but small enough that its zero-real-return drag does not cripple your long-run compounding. Fifth, *rebalance mechanically* — when gold's run pushes it above your target weight, the ratios are usually flashing "dear" at the same time, so trimming back to target is both a discipline and a valuation signal working together.

**The deeper reframe.** What the long arc ultimately teaches is humility about the unit of measurement. We instinctively treat the dollar as the fixed thing and every asset's price as the variable — but over a century it is the dollar that has been the wildly moving target, losing 97% of its value, while gold has been the closest thing to a fixed point. Almost every confused argument about gold ("it went up, so it's a great investment"; "it went up, so it's a bubble"; "it does nothing, so it's useless") dissolves the moment you flip the measuring stick and ask what the *dollar* did against *gold*. The metal didn't soar a hundredfold; the currency sank to a hundredth. Holding that inversion in your head is the entire payoff of zooming out: gold is not a thing that goes up and down, it is the still ruler against which we can finally see how much our money, our stocks, and our energy are really moving. Own a little of the ruler — and use it to read everything else.
