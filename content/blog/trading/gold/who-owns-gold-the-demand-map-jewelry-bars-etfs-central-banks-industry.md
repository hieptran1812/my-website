> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold has four very different buyers — jewelry, private investment (bars, coins, ETFs), central banks, and technology — and the one buying *at the margin* sets the price, not the biggest one.
>
> - Jewelry is the largest buyer by volume (~1,877 t in 2024) but the *least* powerful at the margin: it is price-sensitive and shrinks when gold rallies.
> - Private investment — bars, coins, and ETFs — is the West's swing buyer. ETF holdings *fell* from a ~3,909 t peak in 2020 through 2024, and that is exactly when the old model "broke."
> - Central banks became the new marginal buyer after 2022, buying >1,000 t a year at *any* price for monetary insurance, not for yield. They never chase the price; they set the floor.
> - The one number to remember: in 2023, gold ETFs shed roughly 248 tonnes while central banks bought ~1,037 tonnes. The price-insensitive buyer dwarfed the price-sensitive one — and the price rose even as real yields climbed.

In the spring of 2024 something happened that, by the textbook, was not supposed to happen. The price of gold tore through one record after another — \$2,100, then \$2,300, then \$2,400 an ounce — while the single most-watched gold gauge on every trading desk was flashing *sell*. Holdings in the giant Western gold exchange-traded funds, the vehicles through which most American and European investors own the metal, were *falling*. They had been falling for three straight years. And the variable that is supposed to govern gold above all others — the real (inflation-adjusted) interest rate you can earn on a safe government bond — was not collapsing toward zero, the condition under which gold "should" rally. It was *high* and *rising*. Cash was paying you more than it had in fifteen years.

For a generation of traders who learned that gold is a simple inverse bet on real yields, the chart had stopped making sense. The model was broken. Strategists wrote notes with titles like "Gold's puzzling resilience" and "What is gold telling us?" The honest answer was uncomfortable: *the model was never the whole story.* It described one buyer — the price-sensitive Western investor toggling between gold and Treasury bills — and treated that buyer as if he were the entire market. He was not. While he was selling, somebody else was buying with both hands, and that somebody did not care about real yields at all.

This post is a map of who actually buys gold. It turns out there are four very different buyers, and they could not be more different from one another. One buys gold to wear at a wedding. One buys it to flip when the price rises. One buys it because it is the only money no government can freeze. One buys it because nothing else conducts electricity in a microchip as reliably. They have different motives, different geographies, and — the single most important fact in this entire essay — different *price-sensitivities*. And because the price of anything is set at the margin, by whoever is willing to transact at the current price, understanding gold means understanding which of these four buyers is sitting at the margin right now. After 2022, that seat changed hands.

## Foundations: the four demand buckets, defined from zero

Before we can talk about who sets the price, we have to be precise about what we are even counting. When the gold industry's statisticians (chiefly the World Gold Council, which aggregates data from the refiner-and-mint network) tally up a year of demand, they sort every ounce that leaves the wholesale market into four buckets. Let us define each one as if you have never heard the terms before, because the whole argument hangs on the differences between them.

**Bucket one — jewelry.** This is gold fabricated into rings, necklaces, bangles, and earrings and sold to households. It is by far the largest bucket by weight. In 2024 it absorbed about **1,877 tonnes** of gold. (A *tonne*, throughout this essay, is a metric tonne — 1,000 kilograms, or 32,151 troy ounces. A *troy ounce*, the unit gold trades in, is 31.103 grams, slightly heavier than the ounce on your kitchen scale.) Jewelry sounds frivolous — adornment, vanity — and in the West it largely is. But in the two countries that dominate the bucket, India and China, a gold necklace is not just decoration. It is a savings account you can wear, a form of household wealth that is portable, divisible, and trusted in a way the local banking system often is not. Hold that thought; we will return to it, because "jewelry is investment in disguise" is the key to understanding the largest buyer.

**Bucket two — private investment.** This is gold bought purely as a financial asset, with no intention of wearing it or using it for anything. It splits into two very different sub-forms. The first is **physical bars and coins** — a one-kilogram bar in a Swiss vault, a one-ounce Krugerrand in a German saver's safe, a tael of gold under a Vietnamese family's mattress. In 2024 bars and coins took about **1,186 tonnes**. The second is **gold-backed ETFs** (exchange-traded funds) — a share you buy in a brokerage account that represents a claim on real bullion sitting in a London or New York vault on your behalf. ETFs are how the modern Western investor "owns gold" without ever touching it, and they are the great swing factor in this bucket: in some years they add hundreds of tonnes of demand, and in others they *subtract* it as investors sell. In 2024 the ETF line was actually slightly *negative* — about **−7 tonnes** of net outflow on the year — which is why, when you add it to bars and coins, total private investment was roughly 1,180 tonnes.

**Bucket three — central banks.** This is the official sector: the State Bank of this country, the People's Bank of that one, buying gold to hold in the national reserves alongside their dollars, euros, and government bonds. For most of the late twentieth century this bucket was a net *seller* — central banks were dumping a "barbarous relic" they no longer thought they needed. Then it flipped. Since 2010 central banks have been net buyers every single year, and after 2022 the numbers exploded: about **1,082 tonnes** in 2022, **1,037** in 2023, and **1,045** in 2024 — each year more than double the 2010–2021 average of roughly 473 tonnes. This is the bucket that changed everything, and it is the reason the old model broke.

**Bucket four — technology.** This is gold used in industry: the microscopic bonding wires inside every semiconductor, the contacts in connectors and switches, a little dentistry, a little aerospace. Gold earns its place here because it is the most reliable conductor that does not corrode — your phone's logic board needs connections that will still work in twenty years. The bucket is small, about **326 tonnes** in 2024, roughly grams per device spread across billions of devices. But it has a property no other bucket has, which we will get to: it is almost perfectly *price-inelastic*. A chipmaker needs its few milligrams of gold whether the metal costs \$1,000 or \$5,000 an ounce, because there is no cheaper substitute that works.

![Bar chart of 2024 gold demand by buyer in tonnes](/imgs/blogs/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry-2.png)

There is the whole market in one picture: jewelry on top, then bars-and-coins, then central banks, then technology, with the ETF line dipping below zero. Two things should already feel surprising. First, the four buckets are *not* wildly different sizes — jewelry is the biggest, but central banks now buy almost as much as bars and coins, and far more than they did a decade ago. Second, and this is the trap the whole post is built to spring: **size does not equal power.** The biggest buyer by tonnage is not the one that sets the price. To see why, we first have to separate two pictures that everyone confuses: where the gold *is*, versus where the new gold each year *goes*.

## Where the stock sits versus where the annual flow goes

Here is a distinction that, once you see it, you cannot unsee — and most commentary about gold gets it wrong by conflating the two.

There is **the stock**: all the gold that has ever been mined and still exists, sitting in vaults, jewelry boxes, and central-bank reserves around the world. And there is **the flow**: the new gold that comes to market in a given year — fresh mine output plus recycled scrap — and the new demand that absorbs it. The stock is enormous and slow-moving. The flow is tiny and fast-moving. Almost everything dramatic that happens to the gold *price* happens in the flow, but almost everything people say about gold "demand" is really about the stock.

Let us get the magnitudes straight, because they are genuinely astonishing. The total above-ground stock of gold — every ounce ever mined, from the pharaohs' tombs to last week's pour at a Nevada mine — is about **216,000 tonnes**. If you melted all of it into a single cube, it would measure roughly 22 metres on a side. Every bar of gold humanity has ever produced would fit comfortably inside a few Olympic swimming pools. Against that 216,000-tonne hoard, the world mines only about **3,661 tonnes** of new gold a year and recycles roughly **1,370 tonnes** more. So annual new supply is on the order of 5,000 tonnes — barely **2%** of the existing stock. Gold is the opposite of a crop or a barrel of oil, most of which is consumed within the year it is produced. Almost no gold is ever destroyed; it is just re-melted and moved. The stock-to-flow ratio — stock divided by annual new supply — is around 60, the highest of any commodity, and that single number is the deepest reason gold behaves like money rather than like a commodity.

Why does that ratio matter so much for who can move the price? Because it tells you how easily supply can respond to a surge in demand — and the answer for gold is "barely at all." When the demand for copper or oil jumps, producers can, over a year or two, dig faster and bring more to market, and that extra supply caps the price. Gold cannot do this. Mine output is set by geology and by mines that took a decade to permit and build; it does not jump because the price did. So when a determined new buyer arrives, there is no cavalry of fresh supply to meet it. The only sources of extra gold are recycling — old jewelry melted down, which itself only rises when the price is *already* high — and the existing stock, which is overwhelmingly held by people who do not want to sell (central banks hold for insurance, Indian households hold for the next wedding). A 216,000-tonne stock sounds like an ocean that could absorb any buyer, but almost all of it is *held, not for sale*. The float — the gold actually willing to change hands at the current price — is a thin slice of the annual flow, which is why a single price-insensitive buyer taking a few hundred extra tonnes a year can dominate the marginal price of a 216,000-tonne metal. The vastness of the stock is exactly what makes the metal stable as *money*; the thinness of the willing-to-sell flow is exactly what lets the marginal buyer dominate the *price*. Both facts come from the same high stock-to-flow ratio, and holding them together is the key to the whole demand map.

Now look at *where* that giant stock sits, and compare it to where the annual flow went.

![Donut chart of where the above-ground gold stock sits by share](/imgs/blogs/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry-3.png)

The stock breaks down roughly as: **45%** in jewelry (~97,000 tonnes — the cumulative result of millennia of weddings and dowries), **22%** in private investment bars, coins, and ETFs (~48,000 tonnes), **17%** in central-bank reserves (~37,000 tonnes), and the remaining **15%** in other and industrial uses (~32,000 tonnes, much of it locked inside electronics that will eventually be recycled). That is the *stock* picture, and it is heavily skewed toward jewelry, because jewelry has been accumulating for five thousand years.

But the *flow* picture — who is buying the new gold this year — is completely different in its balance of power. Jewelry is still the biggest single flow bucket, but it is no longer dominant the way it dominates the stock, and crucially, jewelry demand *shrinks when the price rises*, which means it cannot be the buyer pushing the price up. Central banks, which hold only 17% of the stock, were buying a *quarter to a third* of all the new gold mined in 2024. That is the difference between stock and flow in one sentence: **central banks are a minority of the gold that exists, but a plurality of the gold being bought.** When you read "China holds only 6% of its reserves in gold, so it has plenty of room to buy," that is a stock argument with a flow implication — and it is, as we will see, one of the most important sentences in modern macro.

#### Worked example: central banks ate a third of mine supply

> Let us nail the flow point with arithmetic. In 2024 the world's gold mines produced about **3,661 tonnes** of new metal. In that same year, central banks bought a net **1,045 tonnes**. Divide: 1,045 ÷ 3,661 = **0.285**, or roughly **29%**. Almost three out of every ten ounces dug out of the ground in 2024 went straight into a central-bank vault and, in practice, will never come back to the market. Now compare that to the pre-2022 era, when central banks bought around 473 tonnes a year: 473 ÷ 3,661 ≈ **13%**. The official sector roughly *doubled* its claim on new mine supply. Mine output, meanwhile, barely budged — it is one of the most price-insensitive supply curves in any commodity, because opening a new mine takes a decade. So you had a new buyer absorbing an extra ~570 tonnes a year out of a supply that could not grow to meet it. **When a price-insensitive buyer takes a third of an inflexible supply, the price has to rise until someone else is squeezed out — and the someone who got squeezed out was the price-sensitive Western jewelry buyer and ETF investor.**

That worked example is, in miniature, the entire thesis of this post. Hold it in mind as we walk through each buyer in turn, because the question we are always really asking is: *how does this buyer behave when the price moves?*

## Jewelry: investment in disguise

Start with the biggest bucket by weight, and the most misunderstood. In the West, jewelry is a luxury good — you buy a gold chain because you like how it looks, and you would never count it as part of your savings. Western jewelry demand behaves accordingly: it is a small, stable, discretionary category that rises gently with consumer confidence.

But the West is a rounding error in global jewelry demand. The bucket is dominated by **India and China**, and in those cultures gold jewelry is something the Western frame completely misses: it is the household's savings, denominated in a form the household trusts more than the banking system. In rural India, a farmer who has a good harvest does not open a brokerage account; he buys his wife a gold bangle. It is wealth that cannot be inflated away by the rupee, cannot be frozen by a bank, can be pawned in an afternoon if a medical bill arrives, and is socially sanctioned to wear at every wedding and festival. The purity convention tells you it is savings, not adornment: Indian "gold jewelry" is typically **22-carat**, meaning 91.6% pure gold, far higher than the 14-carat (58%) or 18-carat (75%) alloys sold in Western jewelry stores. Nobody makes a 22-carat necklace because it is the most durable or the prettiest setting — 22-carat gold is soft. They make it because the point is to *store gold*, and they want as much gold per gram as a wearable piece allows. It is, almost literally, **wearable bullion**.

#### Worked example: a 22-carat necklace is mostly a savings deposit

> An Indian family buys a 50-gram, 22-carat gold necklace for a daughter's wedding. How much of that is actually gold, and what is it worth? Purity 22-carat = 91.6%, so the pure-gold content is 50 g × 0.916 = **45.8 grams**. Convert to troy ounces: 45.8 g ÷ 31.103 g/oz = **1.473 oz**. At a gold price of \$2,388 an ounce (the 2024 average), the metal value is 1.473 × \$2,388 ≈ **\$3,517**. The family paid a little more than that — there is a making charge for the craftsmanship, typically 8–15% — but the *gold* in the necklace is worth about \$3,500, and that value tracks the world gold price day by day. If the rupee weakens or India inflates, the necklace's rupee value rises to compensate. **The necklace is not really jewelry that happens to be valuable; it is a \$3,500 savings deposit that happens to be wearable** — which is exactly why Indian gold demand is so large and so culturally entrenched.

So why, if jewelry is the biggest and most culturally durable buyer, does it *not* set the price? Because of how it behaves when the price moves: jewelry demand is **price-elastic and even price-perverse**. When gold gets expensive, jewelry buyers buy *less* of it, not more. The Indian wedding still happens, but the family buys a lighter bangle, or waits for a price dip, or recycles an old piece into the new one. This shows up clearly in the data: in years when gold spikes to records, jewelry tonnage *falls*. In 2024, as the dollar gold price jumped roughly 23%, global jewelry volume actually *declined* compared to prior years even as the dollar *value* of jewelry demand rose. The buyer is reacting to price the way you react to the price of beef: when it gets dear, you buy less.

There is a second, subtler reason jewelry stabilizes rather than drives the price, and it lives on the *supply* side of the same bucket. The jewelry already sitting in the world's stock — that ~97,000 tonnes — is also the world's largest reservoir of *recyclable* gold. When the price spikes, two things happen at once in places like India: new jewelry buying slows (the elastic demand we just described), and old jewelry comes *out* of drawers and dowry chests to be sold or melted down, because high prices are exactly when a household decides to cash in grandmother's bangle. That recycling flow — roughly 1,370 tonnes in 2024 — is itself price-sensitive in the stabilizing direction: it rises when the price rises and dries up when the price falls. So the jewelry bucket damps the price from both ends: as a buyer it retreats into a rally, and as a source of recycled supply it leans *into* a rally, adding metal exactly when the price is high. A buyer-and-recycler that does both is the textbook definition of a mean-reverting cushion.

A buyer who buys less as the price rises is, by definition, *not* the buyer pushing the price up. Jewelry is a cushion, not an engine. It absorbs gold when the price is low and quietly releases gold (through recycling) when the price is high — a stabilizing, mean-reverting force. The "so what" is counterintuitive but important: **the biggest buyer of gold is the one you can most safely ignore when you are trying to forecast the price.** Strong Indian wedding-season demand is a perennial bullish headline, and it is almost always noise. It does not move the marginal price; it just determines how much physical metal flows to households at whatever price the *other* buyers have already set.

One nuance worth keeping, because it complicates the "ignore jewelry" rule at the edges: India and China, the two giants of the bucket, do not behave identically. Indian demand is the more purely price-elastic and savings-driven — it tracks the monsoon, the harvest, and the wedding calendar, and it recycles aggressively into rallies. Chinese demand has a larger *investment* character: Chinese households often buy higher-purity 24-carat pieces and small gold bars as a savings vehicle, blurring the line between the jewelry and bar-and-coin buckets, and in years of domestic anxiety — a wobbling property market, a weakening yuan — Chinese buying can turn briefly *pro*-cyclical, chasing gold up rather than retreating from it. So the cushion is mostly Indian; the occasional engine is Chinese. But even Chinese household buying, large as it is, has never been the marginal price-setter at the global level — it is a regional swing factor layered on top of a price the official sector and the ETF complex have already set. The rule survives the nuance: jewelry, taken as a whole, is the buyer to watch *last*, not first.

## Private investment: bars, coins, and the Western swing buyer

Now we reach the buyer that the broken model was actually about — and the buyer whose disappearance created the 2023–24 puzzle. Private investment is gold bought as a pure financial bet, and within it the **ETF** is the instrument that matters for price, because it is the most liquid, the most visible, and the most fickle.

First, the mechanics, because "ETF" hides a lot. A gold ETF — the largest is the SPDR Gold Shares, ticker GLD — is a trust that holds physical bullion in a vault (GLD's sits in London) and issues shares, each representing a fractional claim on that bullion. When you buy a share of GLD in your brokerage account, you are not buying a derivative or an IOU from a bank; you are buying a slice of a specific pile of real gold bars. The clever part is the *creation and redemption* mechanism: a handful of large institutions called Authorized Participants can hand the trust a block of physical gold and receive new shares to sell, or hand back shares and receive physical gold. That arbitrage is what keeps the ETF's price glued to the spot price of gold — if shares trade above the metal, an AP creates new ones and sells them until the gap closes; if below, an AP redeems shares for metal and pockets the difference. The upshot is that **when investors pour money into GLD, the trust must go out and buy real, physical gold to back the new shares** — and when they sell, the trust must deliver real gold out the door. ETF flows are not paper games; they are physical demand and supply.

This is what makes the ETF such a potent — and such a *fast* — buyer relative to the others. A jewelry buyer's decision plays out over a wedding season; a central bank's purchases are deliberate and announced quarters in advance. But an ETF buyer can change his mind in a single trading session. A wave of fear or a sharp move in real yields can send billions of dollars into or out of GLD within days, and because every dollar in or out must be matched by physical metal created or redeemed, that sentiment translates almost instantly into real demand pressure on the wholesale London market. The ETF, in other words, is the *transmission belt* that turns Western investor sentiment into physical gold flow at high speed — which is precisely why, in the era when the Western investor was the marginal buyer, the gold price was so tightly readable from the ETF holdings line. The belt still works; it is just that since 2022 it has been turning in the *opposite* direction from the price, because the hands on the wheel of the price moved elsewhere. A mechanism designed to make the Western investor's sentiment legible kept doing its job faithfully — it told you, correctly, that the Western investor was selling — while a different, slower, louder-when-you-look-for-it buyer set the price over the top of it.

The deeper point about ETFs versus bars and coins is worth a sentence, because they sit in the same bucket but behave differently. A saver who buys a one-kilogram bar and locks it in a vault tends to *hold* — physical bar-and-coin demand is stickier, more "monetary insurance" in flavour, and turns over slowly. The ETF investor is more of a *trader*: liquid, fast, performance-chasing, in and out. So within private investment, bars and coins lean toward the patient, sticky end of the spectrum (closer in spirit to the central bank), while ETFs are the hot, fickle, marginal end. When you read a "private investment demand" figure, it is worth knowing which half is moving: a swing driven by bars and coins is a quieter, more durable shift in saver sentiment; a swing driven by ETFs is the fast money repositioning, and it is the half that shows up in the price within the week.

#### Worked example: why a 100-tonne ETF inflow moves the spot price

> Suppose investors get bullish and pour enough money into gold ETFs to add **100 tonnes** to their holdings over a quarter. How much physical gold does that require? One tonne is 32,151 troy ounces, so 100 tonnes is 100 × 32,151 = **3,215,100 ounces** — about **3.2 million ounces** of physical bullion that the funds' Authorized Participants must source from the wholesale market and lock in the vault. To put that in scale, the entire world mines roughly 3,661 tonnes a year, or about 305 tonnes a month. A sudden 100-tonne ETF inflow over three months is, on its own, comparable to a tenth of global mine supply over that period — *additional* demand on top of everything else. **That is why the daily ETF holdings number is on every gold trader's screen: it is the most immediate, most visible source of new physical demand, and it can swing by hundreds of tonnes a year in either direction.**

And swing it did — the wrong way. Look at the holdings line over the last decade.

![Line chart of global gold ETF holdings 2015 to 2026 in tonnes](/imgs/blogs/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry-4.png)

The story is right there. ETF holdings climbed through the 2010s, then *surged* during the 2020 pandemic as terrified investors piled into gold — holdings peaked around **3,909 tonnes** in the autumn of 2020. That was the textbook working perfectly: real yields had collapsed to deeply negative levels, cash paid nothing, fear was everywhere, and Western investors bought gold ETFs by the truckload, and the price made an all-time high. So far, so model-compliant.

Then the model's main character left the building. From the 2020 peak, ETF holdings *drained* — down through 2021, 2022, 2023, and into 2024, shedding hundreds of tonnes. Western investors were *selling* gold. And why wouldn't they? Real yields were climbing back to positive territory; by 2023–24 you could earn 2% real on a Treasury bond, the highest in over a decade. The textbook says: rising real yields, falling gold. The price-sensitive Western ETF investor did exactly what the textbook predicted — he sold his zero-yielding gold to go earn real yield in bonds. **By the old logic, with the swing buyer in full retreat, gold should have fallen hard.** It did the opposite. It made record after record. The buyer the model described was selling, and the price went *up* anyway. Something not in the model was buying.

The "so what" of the ETF bucket is this: it is the most *visible* buyer, which fools people into thinking it is the *only* buyer. For two decades the financial press treated ETF flows as a proxy for "what gold is doing," because ETF holdings are published daily and Western journalists cover Western investors. That worked while the Western ETF investor *was* the marginal buyer. The lesson of 2021–24 is that you can be the loudest, most-tracked buyer in the market and still not be the one setting the price.

## Central banks: the price-insensitive accumulator

So who was buying while the West sold? The buyer that broke the model is the central bank — and the reason it broke the model is that the central bank breaks the model's central assumption. Every line of the real-yield framework rests on one premise: that the marginal gold buyer is an *investor* comparing gold's zero yield to the real yield on a bond, and choosing whichever pays more. A central bank does not make that comparison. It is not buying gold to earn a return. It is buying gold for something a bond can never provide: **monetary insurance — a reserve asset that is no one else's liability.**

That phrase is the whole point, so let us unpack it slowly. When a central bank holds US Treasury bonds in its reserves — as most do, in vast quantity — it is holding an IOU from the United States government. That IOU is only as good as the United States' willingness to honor it. Normally that willingness is total. But in 2022, after Russia invaded Ukraine, the West did something that every other central bank watched very carefully: it *froze* roughly \$300 billion of Russia's foreign-exchange reserves — the dollars, euros, and bonds Russia held abroad. Overnight, a country's "safe" reserves became inaccessible, because those reserves were liabilities of governments that had just become Russia's adversaries. The lesson every reserve manager from Beijing to Brasília absorbed was brutal and simple: *the money you hold in someone else's financial system can be taken from you.*

Gold is the one major reserve asset immune to that. A bar of gold in your own vault in your own capital is **nobody's liability**. It cannot be frozen by a foreign treasury, cannot be defaulted on, cannot be inflated away by another country's printing press, and does not depend on anyone's promise to pay. It is the only reserve asset that is *purely an asset* — no counterparty, no IOU, no off-switch. For a central bank worried that its dollar reserves might one day be weaponized against it, gold is the obvious hedge, and after February 2022 a great many central banks reached the same conclusion at the same time.

It is worth pausing on *why gold specifically*, because a skeptical reader will ask: if the worry is the dollar, why not just hold euros, or yen, or Chinese government bonds instead? The problem is that every one of those alternatives is *also* someone else's liability, and a liability of a government that could plausibly join the next round of sanctions. Euros are a claim on the European Central Bank; the EU sanctioned Russia alongside the US. Yen are a claim on the Bank of Japan, a US ally. Chinese bonds are a claim on Beijing, which is fine if you are not worried about Beijing — but a Chinese reserve manager cannot diversify *out of* dollar risk *into* his own currency, and most other central banks do not want concentrated exposure to China either. Every paper reserve asset carries the political risk of its issuer. Gold is the only large, liquid reserve asset whose value depends on no government at all. That is not a small advantage at the margin; for a central bank, in a world that just demonstrated it will freeze reserves over geopolitics, it is *the* advantage. This is the same logic explored in the geopolitics track's [the apolitical asset: why central banks trust gold over each other's money](/blog/trading/gold/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money) — here we only need the demand-side consequence: a wave of reserve managers all wanting the one asset that is no one's liability, all at once.

There is also a quieter, longer-running motive layered underneath the 2022 shock: **de-dollarization**, the slow effort by countries like China, Russia, and others to reduce their dependence on a dollar-centric financial system they do not control. You do not need to believe the dollar is about to lose reserve-currency status to see why a rising power would want to hold *less* of its national savings in its strategic rival's currency and *more* in an asset that rival cannot touch. Gold is the natural destination for that diversification, because there is no deep, liquid, politically neutral alternative. So the central-bank bid has two engines: an acute one (the 2022 freeze, which lit the fuse) and a structural one (multi-decade reserve diversification, which keeps it burning). An acute shock fades; a structural reallocation does not. That is why this buyer is not a one-year spike but a *regime*, and why the post-2022 demand has persisted across 2022, 2023, 2024, and 2025 rather than reverting after the initial scare.

![Bar chart of central bank net gold purchases by year in tonnes](/imgs/blogs/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry-5.png)

The chart tells the regime change in one glance. On the left, in red, sit the years of net *selling* — 1999, 2002, 2005, 2007 — when central banks (mostly European) were dumping gold under the Central Bank Gold Agreement, having decided it was a relic. Through the 2010s the bars turn positive but modest, averaging the low hundreds of tonnes. Then look at the right edge: **2022, 2023, and 2024 tower over everything**, each above 1,000 tonnes, the highest since records began in 1950. This is not a wiggle in a noisy series. It is a structural break — a buyer who was net-negative for a decade becoming the largest single source of price-setting demand in the market.

And here is the property that makes this buyer so powerful: the central bank is **price-insensitive**. It is the mirror image of the jewelry buyer. The jewelry buyer buys less when gold rises; the central bank buys the same — or more — at any price, because its motive has nothing to do with the price. China's central bank is not going to abandon its de-dollarization strategy because gold went from \$2,000 to \$2,500. Poland's central bank governor said publicly he wanted gold to be 20% of reserves, and he kept buying as the price climbed. These buyers have a *target quantity*, not a target price. A buyer with a quantity target and no price sensitivity is the most powerful force a market can have, because there is no level at which it stops bidding. When such a buyer takes a third of inflexible supply, the price simply has to rise until the *other*, price-sensitive buyers have been rationed out — which is precisely the picture we have had since 2022.

#### Worked example: the 2023 net flow that explains the "broken" chart

> Here is the puzzle resolved in numbers. In 2023, gold ETF holdings fell by about **248 tonnes** (from ~3,473 t at end-2022 to ~3,225 t at end-2023) — the price-sensitive Western investor in full retreat, exactly as rising real yields predicted. Over the same year, central banks bought a net **~1,037 tonnes** — the price-insensitive Eastern buyer at near-record pace. Net these two against each other: −248 + 1,037 = **+789 tonnes** of net new demand from just these two players combined. The buyer the model watched was a 248-tonne drag; the buyer the model ignored was a 1,037-tonne thrust, more than *four times* larger and pulling the opposite way. **The chart was never broken. The model just had its eyes on the smaller buyer.** Gold rose in 2023 not despite the ETF outflows but because the central-bank inflow swamped them — and because central banks do not sell when the price rises, that demand put a floor under the market that the old framework had no variable for.

This is the deepest "so what" in the whole essay, and it connects straight to this series' spine: **the structural buyer of gold is reaching for monetary insurance, not for yield.** Once the marginal buyer is someone buying insurance rather than chasing return, the real-yield model — which only describes return-chasers — loses its grip on the price. We go deep on this buyer in its own dedicated post, [central banks, the structural buyer that changed gold after 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022); here the point is simply where it sits in the demand map — at the margin, with its hand on the price.

## Technology: small but inelastic

The fourth buyer is the smallest and, for price purposes, usually the least interesting — but it teaches a concept worth having, so it earns its place on the map. Technology demand — gold inside semiconductors, connectors, and the occasional dental crown or satellite — runs around **326 tonnes a year**, less than a fifth of jewelry. It rarely moves the price, because it is small and grows slowly with the electronics cycle.

But it has the cleanest example of a property we have been circling: **price-inelasticity for an essential input.** A chip designer uses gold for the hair-thin bonding wires that connect the silicon die to its package, and for plating on critical contacts, because gold is the most reliable conductor that simply does not corrode — and a connection that fails after five years in a car's engine controller or a hospital monitor is unacceptable at any savings. The quantity per device is minuscule, milligrams, so even a large jump in the gold price barely moves the cost of the finished product. A chipmaker faced with gold doubling does not stop buying gold; it shrugs, because gold is a trivial line item in a \$500 device, and there is no substitute that meets the reliability spec. So technology demand is almost a flat line with respect to price: the buyer *needs* its grams and will pay whatever it costs.

That makes technology a tiny mirror of the central bank: both are price-insensitive, both buy a target *quantity* regardless of price. The difference is scale — central banks' price-insensitivity moves the world because they buy a thousand tonnes; technology's price-insensitivity is irrelevant to the price because it buys three hundred and changes little year to year. The lesson the technology bucket cleanly isolates is the one the whole post turns on: **what matters for the price is not whether a buyer is large, but whether it is price-sensitive — and how much of it there is at the margin.** Technology is inelastic but small. Jewelry is large but elastic. Central banks are large *and* inelastic — which is why they, and not the bigger jewelry bucket or the louder ETF bucket, are the buyer that sets the price.

It is also worth noting what technology demand is *not*, because gold's industrial use is sometimes oversold by enthusiasts who want to argue gold is "really" a consumed commodity like copper. It is not. Three hundred-odd tonnes of annual technology demand against a 216,000-tonne stock is a rounding error in the metal's identity; the milligrams locked in old electronics are themselves slowly recycled back. Gold is not valuable *because* it is useful in chips — silver, which has far larger genuinely-consumed industrial demand, trades at a fraction of gold's price precisely because its monetary premium is smaller. Gold's value comes overwhelmingly from the three *monetary* buyers — jewelry-as-savings, private investment, and central banks — and technology is the small, honest reminder that even gold's purely industrial demand behaves the same way the monetary demand does at the margin: those who truly need it pay whatever it costs.

## The marginal-buyer concept — who sets the price, and the 2022 handoff

We have met all four buyers. Now let us state the organizing idea explicitly, because it is the single most useful concept in commodity markets and almost nobody teaches it cleanly.

**Price is set at the margin.** In any market, the price is not an average of what all buyers would pay or a function of total demand. It is the level at which the last willing buyer meets the last willing seller. The price clears where the *marginal* buyer — the one who is just barely willing to transact at the current level — sits. If that marginal buyer is price-sensitive, the price is anchored: push it up a little and he stops buying, so it falls back. If the marginal buyer is price-*insensitive*, the price is unanchored on the upside: there is no level at which he balks, so the only thing that stops the price rising is other sellers appearing, or the price-sensitive buyers being fully rationed out.

For roughly two decades — call it 2003 to 2021 — the marginal buyer of gold was the **Western ETF investor**. He was the swing factor, the one toggling in and out based on real yields and fear, and because his decision was the one at the margin, his calculus *was* the gold price. Real yields fell, he bought, gold rose; real yields rose, he sold, gold fell. The real-yield model worked beautifully because it correctly described the marginal buyer. The model was never wrong about its buyer; it was just silently assuming that buyer would always *be* the one at the margin.

Then came 2022, and the seat at the margin changed hands.

![Before and after comparison of the marginal gold buyer in 2022](/imgs/blogs/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry-6.png)

Before 2022 (the left column), the marginal buyer was an ETF investor in New York or London who reacted to real yields — rates up, he sells, price down — and the rule that worked was "gold falls when real yields rise." After 2022 (the right column), the marginal buyer became a central bank in Beijing, Ankara, or Warsaw, buying for monetary insurance and ignoring real yields entirely — and the rule that *broke* was exactly that old one, as gold rose *with* real yields through 2022–24. The handoff did not happen because the West's buyer changed his mind about the model. It happened because a different, larger, price-insensitive buyer stepped in front of him at the margin. As long as central banks are willing to absorb a thousand tonnes a year at any price, *they* are the last willing buyer, and the price clears at the level that rations the Western investor *out*, not in.

This reframes the famous "gold decoupled from real yields" of 2023–24. Gold did not decouple from anything. It is still tightly linked to its marginal buyer's motive — it always was. The marginal buyer's motive simply *changed*, from yield-arbitrage (which keys off real rates) to monetary insurance (which keys off geopolitical and currency risk). The relationship that broke was not gold-versus-rates; it was the assumption about who is buying. **A market's price is a faithful read on its marginal buyer; when the price stops obeying your model, the first question is not "what's wrong with gold" but "who is buying now."**

## Common misconceptions

Three myths follow directly from confusing the buckets, and each one dissolves the moment you apply the marginal-buyer lens.

**Myth 1: "Jewelry demand drives the gold price — watch the Indian wedding season."** This is the most common and the most backwards. Jewelry is the largest bucket by weight, so it *sounds* like it should dominate, but it is price-*elastic*: it buys less when gold rises, which means it cannot be the buyer pushing prices up. In 2024, with the dollar gold price up ~23%, global jewelry *tonnage fell* — the buyer retreated as the price climbed. Strong wedding-season demand is a perennial bullish headline that almost never moves the marginal price. Jewelry is a stabilizing cushion, not a price engine; treat reports of "robust Indian demand" as background, not signal.

**Myth 2: "ETF holdings ARE the gold market — if they're falling, gold is weak."** This was true enough from 2003 to 2021, when the ETF investor was the marginal buyer, which is exactly why it became a lazy reflex. It stopped being true in 2022. ETF holdings fell from ~3,909 t in 2020 to ~3,219 t in 2024 — and gold *doubled* over roughly that span. If you used ETF outflows as your sell signal in 2023–24, you fought the biggest gold bull market in a generation. ETFs are the most *visible* buyer, not the *only* one; visibility and marginal power are not the same thing.

**Myth 3: "Central banks always buy at the top — when they pile in, it's a sell signal."** The instinct is that central banks are dumb money, late and trend-chasing. The record says the opposite. Central banks were heavy *sellers* near the 1999–2001 *bottom* (the UK famously sold half its gold at ~\$275 an ounce, the so-called "Brown's Bottom," right before a decade-long bull run), and they turned into committed *buyers* from 2010 onward, well before the 2022–26 surge. Far from buying the top, the official sector has been an early, persistent, price-insensitive accumulator. And because it buys for a structural reason — insurance against its own dollar exposure — it is *less* likely to capitulate at the bottom than the emotional retail buyer, which makes it a stabilizer, not a contrarian sell signal.

## How it shows up in real markets

Concepts are cheap; let us watch the demand map play out in two episodes that look opposite but are the same lesson told twice.

**2013: the ETF crash, when the marginal buyer was the West.** In the spring of 2013, gold suffered one of its worst breaks in decades, plunging from around \$1,600 to under \$1,200 over a few months. The proximate cause was a textbook marginal-buyer event: the US Federal Reserve hinted it would "taper" its bond-buying, real yields jumped, and the Western ETF investor — the marginal buyer of that era — bolted for the exits. Gold ETFs shed an astonishing **~880 tonnes** in 2013 alone, the largest single-year outflow on record. Because that selling buyer *was* the marginal buyer, his stampede *was* the price: gold cratered. Jewelry buyers in India and China, true to form, came in heavily to buy the dip — bargain-hunting physical demand surged — but the cushion only softened the fall; it did not reverse it, because the elastic buyer never sets the marginal price. 2013 is the model in its pure form: the price-sensitive Western investor at the margin, his exit crushing the price.

**2022–24: the central-bank surge, when the marginal buyer was the East.** Now run the same play with a different cast. Real yields rose just as sharply in 2022–23 as they had in 2013 — the Fed hiked from zero to over 5%, and real yields went from −1% to +2%. The Western ETF investor did *exactly* what he did in 2013: he sold, draining hundreds of tonnes from ETFs across 2021–24. By the 2013 logic, gold should have collapsed again. Instead it climbed from roughly \$1,800 in early 2022 to records above \$2,700 by late 2024 and on toward \$5,000-plus in early 2026. The difference was the buyer at the margin. This time, behind the retreating ETF investor stood central banks buying >1,000 tonnes a year, price-insensitively, for insurance against the very reserve-freezing they had just watched happen to Russia. The same real-yield shock that crushed gold in 2013 barely dented it in 2022–24 — not because the relationship between gold and real yields changed, but because the buyer the relationship described had been replaced at the margin by one the relationship never accounted for.

**The 2023–24 decoupling, decomposed.** Put the two flows side by side for 2023, the cleanest year. ETF holdings: **−248 tonnes**. Central-bank purchases: **+1,037 tonnes**. Jewelry: large but flat-to-down as the price rose. Technology: small and steady. Net the price-setting flows and the answer is unambiguous — the price-insensitive accumulator dwarfed the price-sensitive seller by more than four to one, and pulled the opposite way. The "decoupling" pundits puzzled over was not gold detaching from reality; it was the simple consequence of the marginal buyer changing identity. Anyone holding the demand map saw it coming, because the map's whole point is that *the price obeys whoever is at the margin, and the margin had moved East.*

You can see the destination of all that official buying in the reserves table — who, after these years of accumulation, actually holds the gold.

![Horizontal bar chart of official gold reserves by holder in tonnes](/imgs/blogs/who-owns-gold-the-demand-map-jewelry-bars-etfs-central-banks-industry-7.png)

The United States still towers over everyone with about **8,134 tonnes**, a legacy of the Bretton Woods era when the dollar was gold-backed and gold flowed to America — that is a *stock* fact, frozen in place since the 1970s, and the US has not bought a meaningful ounce in decades. The interesting action is lower down: Russia (~2,336 t), China (officially ~2,280 t, and widely believed to hold more it does not report), India, Turkey, Poland — the emerging-market accumulators who drove the post-2022 *flow*. The table is the demand map's punchline made physical: the legacy holders sit on Bretton-Woods hoards they neither add to nor sell, while the marginal buyers — the ones actually setting today's price — are the rising powers methodically building reserves they were largely without a generation ago. And note China's gold is still only about 6% of its total reserves, against 70%-plus for the US and Germany. If China alone decided to lift that share toward the Western norm, it would have to buy thousands more tonnes — a price-insensitive bid that could persist for a decade. *That* is why the reserves table is, quietly, one of the most important charts in macro.

## The takeaway: ask who is buying, not just how much

If you remember one thing from this demand map, make it this: **gold has four buyers with four motives and four price-sensitivities, and the price is set by whichever one is at the margin — which is almost never the biggest one.** Jewelry is the largest by weight and the least important for price, because it is elastic and retreats as gold rises. ETFs are the most visible and the most over-watched, a faithful proxy for the gold price only during the eras when the Western investor happens to be the marginal buyer. Technology is inelastic but too small to matter. And central banks — a minority of the stock, a plurality of the flow, price-insensitive, buying for insurance rather than yield — are the buyer that since 2022 has sat at the margin with its hand on the price.

That reframes how you should read every gold story you encounter. When the price moves and the headlines reach for a single cause, ask the demand-map question: *which buyer is at the margin right now, and what is its motive?* If it is the Western ETF investor, real yields and fear will explain the price, and the old model works. If it is the central bank, real yields will *not* explain the price, and you should be watching geopolitical risk, reserve-freezing precedents, and de-dollarization instead. The model was never broken; the market was just being honest about a buyer the model forgot to include.

There is a practical discipline buried in all this for anyone who actually wants to track gold rather than just admire the charts. Keep four numbers on your dashboard, one per buyer, and read them for *direction and price-sensitivity*, not just for size. From jewelry, watch whether physical demand is rising into a rally (a regional anxiety bid, worth noting) or retreating from it (the normal cushion, safe to ignore). From private investment, watch the ETF holdings line — but treat it as a thermometer for *Western sentiment*, not a verdict on the gold price, and remember it can fall while the price climbs. From central banks, watch the quarterly net-purchase figure and the published reserve-share targets, because that is where the marginal buyer now lives and it telegraphs its intentions in advance. From technology, watch essentially nothing; it is a steady backdrop. The skill is not memorizing the levels — it is knowing which of the four is *setting* the price this cycle, because only then do you know which of the four numbers actually matters.

And this is exactly where the demand map rejoins the spine of the whole series. The buyer who flipped the market after 2022 is not buying gold as an *investment that compounds* — central banks do not expect gold to outperform stocks, and they earn no coupon on it. They are buying it as a *monetary insurance policy*: protection against the possibility that the dollars and bonds in their reserves could be frozen, debased, or weaponized. When the marginal buyer of gold is buying insurance rather than chasing return, the gold price stops being a bet on real yields and becomes something closer to a premium — the world's collective vote of no-confidence in the safety of paper money held in someone else's system. Read the demand map and you are reading that vote: it tells you not just how much gold is changing hands, but *who* is reaching for the one asset that is no one else's promise — and why that reach, once it starts, does not stop because the price went up.
