> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The gold standard was never abandoned because gold failed; it was abandoned because gold imposed a discipline governments could not tolerate when war or spending demanded more money than gold permitted.
>
> - A gold standard means your money is a claim on a fixed weight of gold, redeemable on demand. That convertibility is the whole point — and the whole problem. It caps how much money a government can create.
> - The same cycle repeats across a century: a state pegs to gold for credibility and cheap borrowing, then spends beyond what gold allows (almost always for war), then suspends convertibility and prints. Britain in 1914, the US in 1933 and again in 1971.
> - Bretton Woods (1944) tried to keep gold at the center while giving governments room to maneuver. It contained its own death sentence — **Triffin's dilemma**: the dollar had to be both abundant enough for world trade and scarce enough to stay convertible to gold. It cannot be both.
> - On August 15, 1971, Nixon "temporarily" closed the gold window. It is now permanent. **The one number to remember: gold was frozen at \$35 an ounce for 27 years, then ran to roughly \$5,400 — a 154-fold revaluation of paper against metal.**

On a Sunday night, August 15, 1971, Richard Nixon interrupted the most popular show on American television — the western *Bonanza* — to address the nation. In a flat, almost technical voice, he announced that the United States would "suspend temporarily the convertibility of the dollar into gold." For 27 years, any foreign government holding dollars could walk up to the US Treasury and demand gold at a fixed price of \$35 an ounce. As of that broadcast, the window was closed. The dollar — and with it every currency on Earth, since all of them were pegged to the dollar — became, for the first time in human history, a pure piece of paper backed by nothing but trust in the government that printed it.

He called it temporary. It has now lasted more than half a century, and it is the single most important monetary event of the modern era. Every floating exchange rate, every quantitative-easing program, every debate about money-printing and inflation traces back to that Sunday night. The world we trade in — a world of fiat currencies whose value rests entirely on confidence — was born when the last formal link between money and gold was severed.

But the Nixon Shock was not a one-off blunder. It was the latest verse of a song that had been playing for a century. To understand why it happened, and why it tells you something permanent about paper money, you have to understand what a gold standard actually *is*, why governments keep adopting it, and why they always, eventually, leave.

This post is the deep mechanics. If you want the broad sweep of why gold became money in the first place — coins, the classical standard, demonetization across five thousand years — read the companion piece, [Gold as Money: A Monetary History from Coins to Vaults](/blog/trading/gold/gold-as-money-a-monetary-history-from-coins-to-vaults). Here we go underneath the history into the machinery: convertibility, the price-specie-flow mechanism, the policy trilemma, Triffin's dilemma, and the specific politics of 1971.

## Foundations: what a gold standard actually is

Start with the everyday version of the idea, because the formal one is a dressed-up cousin of something you already know.

Imagine a coat-check at a theater. You hand over your coat and get a numbered ticket. The ticket is not your coat — it is a *claim* on your coat. You can hand the ticket to a friend, who can hand it to someone else, and whoever holds it at the end of the night can redeem it for the coat. The ticket circulates as a stand-in for the real thing. As long as everyone trusts that the coat-check will honor the ticket, the ticket is "as good as" the coat — lighter, easier to carry, and more divisible if you could tear it.

A gold standard is a coat-check for gold. The government (or a bank) holds gold in a vault and issues paper notes that are *claims* on a fixed weight of that gold. A dollar bill in the classical era was, in effect, a ticket that said "redeemable for 1/20.67th of an ounce of gold." You could carry the paper because it was convenient, but at any moment you could march into a bank and demand the metal. The paper was money because — and only because — it was convertible into gold on demand.

So the first and most important term:

**Convertibility** is the right to exchange your paper money for a fixed amount of gold at a guaranteed price, any time you want. It is the load-bearing wall of any gold standard. Take convertibility away and the gold standard is gone, no matter what the law says about reserves or backing. Convertibility is what makes the promise real — and, as we will see, it is exactly what governments under pressure cannot keep.

The second term follows immediately:

**The mint parity (or par)** is the fixed price the government promises. Classical US par was roughly \$20.67 per troy ounce of gold. Bretton Woods par was \$35. The number sounds arbitrary — and historically it was set by political choice — but once fixed it became the anchor of the whole system. Every other price in the economy floated; gold did not.

### The discipline gold imposes

Here is why governments both love and hate the gold standard. If your money is convertible into gold at a fixed price, then you *cannot print more money than your gold stock can plausibly back*. The moment you print too much, holders sense the paper is getting watered down, they line up to convert their notes into gold before everyone else does, and your vault drains. A run on the gold reserve is the gold standard's enforcement mechanism. The threat of that run is what keeps a government honest.

This is the **discipline** the gold standard imposes — and it is the spine of this entire post. Under a credible gold standard:

- A government cannot finance a war or a deficit simply by printing money, because the new money is redeemable for gold it does not have.
- A central bank cannot cut interest rates to stimulate the economy whenever it likes, because cheap money encourages people to convert paper to gold and ship it abroad where rates are higher.
- Inflation is largely impossible over the long run, because the money supply is tethered to a metal that grows only a percent or two per year.

To a saver, all of this is wonderful. Your money holds its value across decades. To a government facing a war, a depression, or a re-election campaign, all of this is a straitjacket. And the central lesson of monetary history is this: **when the straitjacket conflicts with the survival of the state, the state takes the straitjacket off.** Every single time.

> [!note]
> The gold standard's discipline is the same discipline a [household budget](/blog/trading/gold/debasement-debt-and-the-fiscal-story-gold-as-an-anti-currency) imposes: you can only spend what you have. Fiat money is a credit card with no fixed limit — convenient until the bill arrives as inflation. Gold is the cash-only economy. Governments choose the credit card for the same reason most people do.

### How fixed exchange rates fell out of convertibility for free

There is a beautiful second-order consequence of convertibility worth pausing on, because it explains why the classical era's exchange rates were so stable without any treaty fixing them. If the British pound is convertible into a fixed weight of gold, and the US dollar is convertible into a fixed weight of gold, then the exchange rate between pound and dollar is *automatically* pinned by the ratio of those two gold weights. Nobody has to legislate the exchange rate; it falls out of the two convertibility promises for free.

In the classical era a pound contained about 113 grains of fine gold and a dollar about 23.22 grains, which made the pound worth about \$4.866. The market rate could wobble around that number, but only within a tiny band, because of arbitrage. If the pound rose much above \$4.89, a trader could convert dollars to gold in New York, ship the gold to London, convert it to pounds, and pocket the difference — and the shipping plus insurance cost set the width of the band. These boundaries were called the **gold points**: the upper and lower limits beyond which it became profitable to move physical gold rather than trade currency. Exchange rates simply could not stray outside the gold points, because gold arbitrage dragged them back. This is why the classical gold standard delivered fixed exchange rates as a *byproduct* of domestic convertibility — and why it placed the world firmly in the "fixed rate plus free capital" corner of the trilemma. The stability traders prized was not designed; it emerged.

#### Worked example: how convertibility caps the money supply

Suppose a small country runs a strict gold standard. Its central bank holds 100 tonnes of gold. One tonne is 32,151 troy ounces, so the bank holds about 3,215,100 ounces. The law sets par at \$35 per ounce.

The maximum gold value the bank can back is `3,215,100 oz × \$35 = \$112.5 million`. If the country runs a "100% reserve" standard, it can issue at most \$112.5 million in convertible notes. In practice gold standards ran on *fractional* reserves — issuing, say, \$3 of notes for every \$1 of gold, a 33% cover ratio — which lets it issue roughly \$337 million.

Now the government wants to fight a war costing \$500 million. There is no honest way to print it. To create \$500 million in convertible money, the bank would need a cover ratio so thin that the first whiff of trouble triggers a run: holders convert their notes, the 3.2 million ounces walk out the door, and the standard collapses. **The math is the discipline — gold caps the money supply at a multiple of the metal in the vault, and war does not respect that cap.**

### The policy trilemma: why you can only pick two

There is a deeper structural reason the gold standard constrains governments, and it generalizes far beyond gold. Economists call it the **policy trilemma** (or the "impossible trinity"). It says a country can have at most two of these three things at once:

1. **A fixed exchange rate** — your currency is pegged to gold, or to another currency, at a set rate.
2. **Free capital flows** — money can move freely in and out of your country.
3. **An independent monetary policy** — you set your own interest rates for your own economy's needs.

You can have any two. You cannot have all three.

![Policy trilemma triangle: fixed exchange rate, free capital flows, independent monetary policy — pick two](/imgs/blogs/the-gold-standard-and-its-death-bretton-woods-to-the-nixon-shock-7.png)

A gold standard is a specific corner of this triangle: it chooses **fixed exchange rates** (your money is pegged to gold) and **free capital flows** (gold can move across borders freely — that is the whole mechanism). The price you pay is the third leg: you *give up independent monetary policy*. You cannot cut rates to fight a recession, because doing so would drain your gold to wherever rates are higher. Your interest rates are set, in effect, by the gold flows, not by your own central bank.

This is the abstract version of the discipline. The gold standard does not just constrain the *quantity* of money; it constrains the *one tool* a modern government most wants in a crisis — the ability to lower interest rates and ease conditions when its own economy is hurting. A government on a gold standard that hits a recession must simply endure it. That endurance is politically lethal, and it is the reason the standard kept being abandoned the moment domestic pain and the peg came into conflict.

Hold these three ideas — convertibility, discipline, the trilemma — because every episode that follows is a variation on them. Now let us watch the standard live and die, four times in a century.

## The classical standard, 1870–1914, and the price-specie-flow mechanism

The "classical gold standard" — the era people mean when they speak of *the* gold standard with reverence — ran from roughly 1870 to 1914. Britain had been on gold since 1821; by the 1870s most of the industrial world, including Germany, France, and the United States (de facto from 1879), had joined. For about four decades, the major currencies were all just different-sized claims on gold, which meant exchange rates between them were effectively fixed and inflation was near zero over the long run.

What made it work without a world central bank? An elegant self-correcting mechanism that the philosopher David Hume had described back in 1752: the **price-specie-flow mechanism**. ("Specie" is an old word for coined precious metal.)

![Price-specie-flow mechanism: trade deficit, gold outflow, money supply shrinks, prices fall, exports rise, trade rebalances](/imgs/blogs/the-gold-standard-and-its-death-bretton-woods-to-the-nixon-shock-4.png)

Here is the loop. Suppose Britain imports more from France than it exports — a trade deficit. Britons must pay French sellers, and under a gold standard, international balances ultimately settle in gold. So gold physically flows out of Britain and into France. But gold *is* the money supply. As gold leaves Britain, the British money supply shrinks; with less money chasing the same goods, British prices fall. Meanwhile France, gaining gold, sees its money supply expand and its prices rise. Now British goods are cheap and French goods are dear. Foreigners buy more British exports; Britons buy fewer French imports. The trade balance swings back toward zero, gold flows reverse, and the system rebalances — all without any policymaker doing anything.

This is the part that genuinely amazes people who study it. The classical gold standard had no Federal Reserve, no IMF, no policy committee adjusting things. Trade imbalances corrected themselves through gold flows and the price level, automatically. The discipline was not enforced by a person; it was enforced by physics and arithmetic.

#### Worked example: the price-specie-flow in numbers

Say Britain runs a £10 million trade deficit in a year. Under the gold standard, that imbalance settles in gold, so roughly £10 million of gold leaves the Bank of England. If Britain's total money supply was £100 million, the gold drain removes about 10% of the monetary base.

A 10% contraction in money, holding output roughly constant, pushes the price level down over time — call it a few percent of deflation. British wool that cost £1.00 now costs perhaps £0.95; the same wool in France, where gold has flowed in, has risen to the equivalent of £1.05. The price gap is now about 10%. Foreign buyers shift orders to the cheaper British wool; British buyers stop importing the now-expensive French goods. The trade deficit narrows, gold stops leaving, and equilibrium returns. **The mechanism turns a trade imbalance into a price adjustment that fixes itself — the gold standard's great gift, and the source of the deflation that made it so painful in a downturn.**

Notice the catch hiding in that last sentence. The mechanism's "fix" for a deficit is *deflation and contraction* — falling prices, falling wages, a shrinking money supply. That is fine when it is a mild trade adjustment. It is catastrophic when it collides with a depression, because the standard then forces a contracting economy to contract *further*. Hold that thought; it is the bomb under the interwar years.

In practice the mechanism was not quite as hands-off as Hume's pure model suggests, and the gap matters. Central banks — above all the Bank of England, which sat at the hub of the system — *helped* the adjustment along by raising and lowering their discount rate (the interest rate at which they lent). When gold was flowing out, the central bank would raise its rate, which both attracted foreign gold (chasing the higher return) and cooled domestic credit, speeding the price adjustment. When gold flowed in, it would lower the rate. Economists call this following the "rules of the game" — leaning into the gold flows rather than offsetting them. The Bank of England became so adept that it could shepherd the entire system with small rate moves, earning the quip that it "could pull the world by a thread." The takeaway is subtle but important: even the classical gold standard was not purely automatic. It was an automatic core *managed at the margin* by a hegemon — Britain — whose financial centrality made the whole thing cohere. When that hegemon was exhausted by war, the system lost its conductor, which is part of why the interwar restoration never worked.

The classical standard's golden age was real but it rested on conditions that would not survive the twentieth century: governments small enough that the discipline did not crush them, no expectation that the state would manage the business cycle or guarantee employment, a single dominant financial center to coordinate the system, and — crucially — no total wars. The standard worked beautifully in a world that did not yet ask much of its governments. That world ended in August 1914.

## The interwar collapse: the standard's first death

When the First World War broke out, every major combatant did the same thing within weeks: they suspended gold convertibility. The reason was exactly the discipline described above. You cannot fight an industrial total war on a gold budget. The war cost sums that dwarfed any plausible gold backing, and the only way to finance it was to print — to create money far beyond what gold could cover. Convertibility and total war are mutually exclusive, so convertibility went.

This is the pattern's first clear appearance in the modern era: **peg for credibility in peacetime, suspend the peg when war demands more money than gold allows.** Britain, France, Germany, and the rest all printed massively to fund 1914–1918. Germany's printing famously ran on into the hyperinflation of 1923, in which the mark became literally worthless — wheelbarrows of cash for a loaf of bread — a case study in what happens when the gold anchor is cut and nothing replaces the discipline.

After the war, policymakers wanted the prestige and stability of the prewar order back. So in the 1920s they tried to *restore* the gold standard. The most famous, and most disastrous, restoration was Britain's. In 1925, Chancellor of the Exchequer Winston Churchill returned the pound to gold at its *prewar parity* — the old, strong exchange rate — even though Britain had printed so much during the war that this rate now massively overvalued the pound. To defend an overvalued peg, Britain had to keep money tight and prices falling: deflation, high unemployment, and the bitter coal strikes of the 1920s. The economist John Maynard Keynes wrote a pamphlet titled *The Economic Consequences of Mr. Churchill* arguing the decision was a self-inflicted wound. He was right.

The restored "gold-exchange standard" of the 1920s was fragile in a way the classical one had not been. To economize on scarce gold, countries held part of their reserves not in metal but in the currencies of the big gold powers (mainly the pound and the dollar), treating those currencies as "good as gold." This stacked claims on claims: if confidence in the pound wobbled, holders would convert pounds to gold, draining Britain — exactly the dynamic that would later kill Bretton Woods. (Keep this gold-*exchange* idea; it is the direct ancestor of the postwar system.)

It helps to see *why* layering currency reserves on top of gold is structurally unstable, because the same flaw recurs in 1971. In a pure gold standard, every reserve is final — gold is no one's liability, so holding it carries no counterparty risk. In a gold-*exchange* standard, much of the world's reserves are claims on *another country's* gold. Those claims can be redeemed all at once, but the gold behind them cannot be produced all at once. So the system has the architecture of a bank: it works as long as not everyone redeems simultaneously, and it is vulnerable to a run the moment confidence cracks. Worse, the gold-exchange standard quietly inflated the world money supply, because the same ounce of gold in London could back both British notes *and* the foreign reserves denominated in pounds — the metal was doing double duty. When France in the late 1920s began converting its sterling reserves into actual gold, it pulled the metal out from under that double-counting and helped trigger the deflationary spiral. The reserve-currency country gets a privilege (the world finances it by holding its paper) and a curse (it is one panic away from a run on its gold). That privilege-and-curse is the exact knot Triffin would formalize thirty years later for the dollar.

Then came the Great Depression. And here the price-specie-flow mechanism's dark side detonated. As the slump deepened from 1929, the gold standard *forced* contracting economies to contract further. A country losing gold had to raise interest rates and cut its money supply to defend the peg — precisely the opposite of what a depressed economy needs. The standard transmitted deflation across borders like a contagion. Economists Barry Eichengreen and others have shown a stark empirical pattern: **the sooner a country abandoned the gold standard, the sooner it recovered.** Britain left in 1931; the US devalued in 1933–34; the "gold bloc" countries that clung on (France, the Netherlands, Switzerland) suffered the longest. Gold, in the 1930s, was not the source of stability; it was the chain dragging economies down.

So the interwar period gives us the second and third deaths of the standard — the wartime suspension of 1914 and the depression-driven abandonment of the 1930s — and a crucial nuance we will return to: the gold standard did not *cause* the Depression in the simple sense, but it powerfully *propagated* and deepened it, and leaving it was the precondition for recovery. By the late 1930s the classical dream was dead. What replaced it was designed at a New Hampshire resort in 1944.

## Bretton Woods: gold at the center, dollars in between

In July 1944, with the war's end in sight, 730 delegates from 44 Allied nations gathered at the Mount Washington Hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to design the postwar monetary order. They had watched the interwar gold standard transmit the Depression and the chaotic competitive devaluations that followed. They wanted the *stability* of fixed exchange rates without the *brutal automatic deflation* of the classical standard. The result was a clever, and ultimately doomed, compromise.

The Bretton Woods system was a **gold-exchange standard built around the dollar.** It worked in two layers:

- **The US dollar was pegged to gold** at \$35 per troy ounce. The United States promised that foreign central banks (not private citizens — this is important) could convert their dollars into US gold at \$35, on demand.
- **Every other currency was pegged to the dollar** at a fixed rate. The British pound, the French franc, the German mark, the Japanese yen — all defined their value in dollars and intervened to hold that rate within a narrow band.

So gold sat at the center, but you reached it *through* the dollar. The dollar was the world's reserve currency by design: as good as gold, because it was convertible to gold, and far more convenient. Countries held their reserves in dollars (which paid interest, unlike gold) and trusted that those dollars were a claim on metal in Fort Knox.

Why did this seem like a brilliant design in 1944? Because it gave governments back the leg of the trilemma they had lost. Under Bretton Woods, a country had a fixed exchange rate *and* — through capital controls, which were universal and accepted at the time — it could retain *some* independent monetary policy. The system deliberately restricted free capital flows so that governments could manage their economies (pursue full employment, run the welfare state) without an immediate gold run punishing them. It was the trilemma corner the classical standard had refused to occupy. Pegs were also adjustable: a country in "fundamental disequilibrium" could devalue against the dollar with IMF approval, rather than grind through years of deflation. Britain did exactly this in 1949 and again in 1967.

And for a while — a quarter century of the strongest growth the industrial world has ever seen — it worked. The "Bretton Woods era," roughly 1945 to 1971, gave Western Europe and Japan their postwar miracles, stable exchange rates, and expanding trade. But the system carried a contradiction in its DNA, and a Belgian-American economist spotted it almost from the start.

## Triffin's dilemma: the contradiction built into the design

In 1959–1960, the economist Robert Triffin testified to the US Congress and published a book laying out a problem so fundamental that it now bears his name. **Triffin's dilemma** is the reason Bretton Woods was doomed — not by mismanagement, but by its own architecture.

The argument goes like this. The Bretton Woods system needed the dollar to do two jobs that pulled in opposite directions:

1. **Supply the world with liquidity.** As global trade and economies grew, the world needed more reserves to settle transactions and hold as savings. The only reserve asset that grew with demand was the dollar — gold's supply was nearly fixed. So the world *needed an ever-growing supply of dollars held outside the United States.*

2. **Stay convertible to gold.** For the dollar to *be* a reserve asset, foreign holders had to believe they could always convert their dollars to gold at \$35. That belief required the US gold stock to be large relative to the dollars held abroad.

Here is the trap. The only way for dollars to flow out into foreign hands in growing quantity is for the United States to run **balance-of-payments deficits** — to send more dollars abroad (through imports, military spending, foreign investment) than it takes back. So supplying the world's reserves *requires* the US to run persistent deficits. But the more dollars pile up abroad, the more foreign claims there are on a roughly fixed US gold stock. Sooner or later, the dollars held abroad exceed the gold available to redeem them. And at that point, confidence cracks: holders realize there is not enough gold for everyone, and the rational move is to convert *first*, before the gold runs out. A run becomes inevitable.

![Triffin's dilemma: the reserve issuer must run deficits to supply dollars, eroding confidence in gold backing](/imgs/blogs/the-gold-standard-and-its-death-bretton-woods-to-the-nixon-shock-5.png)

The dollar was asked to be simultaneously **abundant** (enough to oil world trade) and **scarce** (no more than the gold could back). It cannot be both. That is the dilemma — a genuine logical contradiction, not a policy mistake. Whichever way the US leaned, it lost: print too few dollars and you strangle world trade; print enough dollars and you guarantee an eventual gold run. Triffin pointed this out a *decade* before the system collapsed, with a precision that reads like prophecy.

#### Worked example: Triffin's dilemma in dollars and ounces

In 1949, near the system's birth, the United States held the great bulk of the world's monetary gold — roughly 700 million ounces, about 22,000 tonnes. At \$35 an ounce, that hoard was worth about **\$24.5 billion**. Dollars held by foreigners at the time were a small fraction of that, so US gold comfortably covered foreign dollar claims. The promise was credible.

By the late 1960s the picture had inverted. Two decades of US deficits — Marshall Plan aid, overseas military bases, the Vietnam War, and growing foreign investment — had flooded the world with dollars. Meanwhile the US gold stock had *shrunk* as foreign central banks quietly converted, down toward 290 million ounces (about 9,000 tonnes). At \$35, that was worth only about **\$10 billion**. But foreign-held dollar claims had ballooned to *several times* that figure — on the order of \$40–50 billion. The arithmetic was now unforgiving: there were roughly four or five paper dollars abroad for every dollar of gold to redeem them at the official price.

**Once foreign dollar claims visibly exceed the gold backing them, the \$35 promise is a bluff — and bluffs in monetary policy get called.** Triffin had said exactly this would happen, and it did.

## The road to 1971: the gold drain, the London Gold Pool, and de Gaulle

Through the 1960s, the contradiction Triffin described turned from theory into a slow-motion crisis. The story has three threads worth pulling.

**The gold drain.** As foreign central banks grew nervous, they exercised their right to convert dollars into US gold. Year after year, gold trickled — then poured — out of Fort Knox. The US gold reserve fell from over 20,000 tonnes after the war to around 9,000 tonnes by 1971. Every conversion made the remaining promise less credible, which encouraged more conversions: a classic run dynamic, just in slow motion because it was governments, not panicked depositors, doing the converting.

**The London Gold Pool.** A second front opened in the *private* gold market. Officially the price was \$35, but private investors could buy and sell gold in London, and as confidence in the dollar eroded, they bid the market price *above* \$35. If the free-market price rose much above the official price, the bluff would be obvious and the run would accelerate. So in 1961 the US and seven European central banks formed the **London Gold Pool**, a cartel that sold gold into the London market to hold the price at \$35. For a few years it worked. But defending the price meant *selling more gold* — accelerating the very drain they feared. In March 1968, after a wave of speculation, the Pool collapsed. The authorities gave up and created a **two-tier system**: the official price stayed \$35 for central-bank settlement, but the private market price was set free to float. This was a tell. The official \$35 was now a fiction maintained only between governments; the market had already left.

**De Gaulle's challenge.** The most pointed assault came from France. President Charles de Gaulle and his economic adviser Jacques Rueff resented what they called the dollar's "exorbitant privilege" — the unique advantage the US enjoyed from issuing the world's reserve currency, able to run deficits and pay for foreign goods and assets simply by printing dollars the world had to hold. (The phrase, coined by French finance minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing, has stuck ever since; it is the throughline of the [dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) to this day.) Starting in the mid-1960s, France deliberately and provocatively converted its dollar reserves into gold, even reportedly sending French naval vessels to ferry the metal home from New York. De Gaulle was not just hedging; he was making a political statement that the dollar's privilege was illegitimate, and he was forcing the contradiction into the open by *calling the bluff in size*.

By the late 1960s the imbalance had a name traders used: the **dollar overhang** — the mass of dollars held outside the US that exceeded the gold available to redeem them. The overhang did not just sit there; it became ammunition for speculative attacks. Currency traders and corporations, seeing the contradiction, would sell dollars and buy gold or stronger currencies (especially the German mark) whenever a crisis loomed, betting that the dollar would have to devalue. Each attack forced central banks to either buy the unwanted dollars (expanding their own money supplies and importing US inflation) or let their currencies rise against the dollar. West Germany, tired of importing American inflation, finally let the mark float in May 1971 — a crack in the dam. The attacks were self-fulfilling: the more obvious the dollar's overvaluation against gold became, the larger the bets against it, and the larger the bets, the faster the gold drained. By 1971 the system was no longer being undermined by slow central-bank conversions alone; it was under active speculative assault, and the speculators had the arithmetic on their side.

#### Worked example: the gold cover ratio collapses

The cleanest single number for the run's inevitability is the **gold cover ratio** — US gold reserves divided by foreign dollar claims on them, both at the official \$35 price. A ratio above 1 means the US could honor every claim; below 1 means it is a bank that cannot pay all its depositors.

In 1949, US gold of about \$24.5 billion comfortably exceeded the modest foreign dollar holdings, so the cover ratio was well above 1 — the promise was rock solid. By 1971, US gold had fallen to roughly \$10 billion at the official price, while foreign dollar claims had grown to somewhere around \$40–50 billion. The cover ratio had collapsed to roughly `\$10B ÷ \$45B ≈ 0.22` — about 22 cents of gold for every dollar of claim.

A bank with a 22% cover that has promised to pay 100% on demand is not solvent; it is merely *not yet run upon*. **Once the cover ratio falls below 1, closing the gold window is no longer a policy choice but an accounting reality — Nixon did not break a working promise, he stopped pretending a broken one still held.**

By the summer of 1971 the situation was untenable. A run was building. In the first days of August, Britain reportedly requested conversion of \$3 billion into gold. The US gold stock could not survive a general rush. The Treasury faced a choice: devalue the dollar against gold (raise the official price), impose brutal deflation to defend the peg, or break the link entirely. Nixon chose to break it.

## The Nixon Shock and why governments always leave gold

On August 13–15, 1971, Nixon convened his top economic advisers — including Treasury Secretary John Connally and the budget director George Shultz, with a young Paul Volcker as the Treasury's monetary point man — at Camp David for a secret weekend. The decision they reached, announced that Sunday night, had three parts:

1. **Close the gold window** — suspend the dollar's convertibility into gold, ending the last formal link between money and metal.
2. **Impose a 90-day wage and price freeze** to suppress the inflation everyone knew the move would unleash.
3. **Slap a 10% surcharge on imports** to pressure other countries into revaluing their currencies upward against the dollar.

It was packaged not as a surrender but as a tough, nationalist economic offensive — "the New Economic Policy." Connally, asked about the international fallout, delivered the era's defining line to foreign finance ministers: *"The dollar is our currency, but it's your problem."* The stock market rose the next day. The public, told this would defend the dollar against foreign "speculators," largely approved.

The domestic politics are worth dwelling on, because they are the purest illustration of why governments leave gold. Nixon faced re-election in 1972. The economy had unemployment near 6% and rising inflation — the unpleasant combination later named "stagflation." Defending the \$35 peg honestly would have required exactly the medicine the gold standard always prescribes: higher interest rates and tighter money to stop the gold drain, which would have deepened unemployment going into an election year. No president chooses that. The gold window stood between Nixon and the monetary freedom to run the economy hot for the campaign — so the gold window went. The decision was not made by monetary theorists weighing Triffin's dilemma; it was made by a politician who wanted lower rates and a stronger labor market in an election year and found gold convertibility in his way. That is the mechanism behind "governments always leave gold," in its most naked form: the discipline is removed precisely when a leader's political survival requires the freedom the discipline denies.

Why "temporarily," then, if the decision was effectively permanent? Partly genuine uncertainty — many advisers did expect to renegotiate a new fixed system at a higher gold price. But partly it was presentation. "Temporarily suspend" sounds like a defensive, reversible tactic; "abolish the gold standard" sounds like a historic capitulation. The softer word kept confidence from collapsing and bought time. It is a recurring feature of these episodes: the break is always announced as temporary and emergency, never as the permanent regime change it almost always turns out to be. Britain's 1931 departure was "temporary." The 1933 US gold seizure was an "emergency." The language of impermanence is part of how the discipline gets dropped without panic.

The attempt to keep some fixed-rate system limped on for a couple of years. In December 1971 the **Smithsonian Agreement** devalued the dollar to \$38 (then \$42.22) an ounce and tried to re-peg the major currencies at new rates. Nixon hailed it as "the most significant monetary agreement in the history of the world." It lasted barely a year. By early 1973 the pegs broke for good, and the major currencies began to *float* — their exchange rates set by markets minute to minute, as they still are today. The official US gold price was eventually abolished; gold was set free to find its own price in the market.

![Gold price 1970 to 2026 on a log scale, frozen at 35 dollars until 1971 then free to rise](/imgs/blogs/the-gold-standard-and-its-death-bretton-woods-to-the-nixon-shock-3.png)

The chart above is the whole story in one image. For 27 years gold was a flat line at \$35 — frozen by law, a price that was a lie by the end. The instant the window closed, the lie ended and the metal began revealing what the market thought paper money was actually worth. Within a decade gold hit \$850. By 2026 it was around \$5,400. The line did not go up because gold suddenly became more useful; it went up because the dollar, unmoored from gold, was free to be created without limit — and gold's price is simply the inverse of the world's confidence in that paper.

Now step back and ask the question this whole post has been building toward: *why* do governments always leave gold?

Because the gold standard's discipline — the very thing that gives it credibility — is intolerable in exactly the moments a state needs flexibility most. War, depression, mass unemployment, a fiscal crisis: these are the situations where a government must spend or ease beyond what gold permits. And in every such situation, across a full century, the government has chosen its own survival over the discipline. Britain in 1914. The gold bloc in the 1930s. The US in 1933, and again, definitively, in 1971. The pattern is not about bad leaders or weak resolve. It is structural: **a state will always, eventually, value its freedom to print over its promise to redeem.** The gold standard asks a government to tie its own hands; sooner or later, every government cuts the rope.

That is the deepest reason to expect paper money to keep being debased — and gold, the asset that cannot be printed, to endure. Gold's permanence is not a claim about gold. It is a claim about governments.

> [!tip]
> This is the spine of the entire gold story: **gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money.** The Nixon Shock is the moment that vote became possible. Before 1971, the price was frozen and the vote suppressed. After 1971, the price floats — and every rise is the market marking down trust in fiat.

## Common misconceptions

The gold standard is a magnet for myths, from both the people who want it back and the people who want it buried. Here are the ones worth correcting, each with a number.

**Myth 1: "Nixon's suspension was temporary; we could go back to gold any time."** Nixon genuinely used the word "temporarily," and many at the time believed convertibility would be restored at a new, higher price. It never was. The reason is not inertia but arithmetic. To make the dollar convertible to gold today, the US would have to back the dollars in existence with its 8,133 tonnes of gold — about 261 million ounces. With a US money supply (M2) above \$20 trillion, the implied gold price would be in the *tens of thousands of dollars per ounce*. "Going back to gold" at any honest price would mean a massive devaluation of the dollar against gold. It is not coming back because the math of going back is itself a confession.

**Myth 2: "The gold standard caused the Great Depression."** This is too strong, but it points at something real. The Depression had many causes — the 1929 crash, banking panics, a collapse in trade, policy errors. The gold standard did not *start* it. But it powerfully *propagated and deepened* it: by forcing countries losing gold to tighten into a downturn, it transmitted deflation worldwide and prolonged the slump. The cleanest evidence is the recovery pattern — the earlier a country left gold, the earlier it recovered. So the accurate statement is not "gold caused the Depression" but "the gold standard turned a severe recession into a global decade-long catastrophe, and abandoning it was the precondition for recovery." The nuance matters, and we will return to the broader debate over gold's role in crises in [Fear and the Safe-Haven Trade](/blog/trading/gold/fear-and-the-safe-haven-trade-how-gold-behaves-in-a-crisis).

**Myth 3: "Under a gold standard there's no inflation."** Over the very long run, roughly true — the classical era had near-zero net inflation across decades. But it is wrong about the short and medium run. Gold standards delivered *price stability over decades at the cost of wild volatility within them*: sharp deflations (the price-specie-flow mechanism in a downturn) and bursts of inflation when new gold was discovered. The California and Australian gold rushes of the 1850s caused inflation; the Spanish silver and gold from the Americas caused the "price revolution" of the 1500s. A gold standard does not abolish inflation; it trades chronic mild inflation for occasional violent swings in both directions.

**Myth 4: "Bretton Woods failed because of mismanagement."** Better managers might have delayed the collapse, but they could not have prevented it. Triffin proved the contradiction was *structural*: the system needed the dollar to be both abundant and scarce, which is impossible. No amount of skill resolves a logical contradiction. Bretton Woods did not fail; it expired on schedule, exactly as the math required.

**Myth 5: "Leaving gold was a free lunch — we got flexibility at no cost."** The flexibility was real and valuable; floating rates and discretionary monetary policy have let central banks fight depressions in a way the gold standard forbade (2008 and 2020 are the proof). But the cost is also real and ongoing. Freed from the gold anchor, the dollar has lost the overwhelming majority of its purchasing power, and the temptation to finance deficits by printing — restrained only by the soft discipline of inflation and bond markets — has produced the debt and money-supply growth that the [debasement story](/blog/trading/gold/debasement-debt-and-the-fiscal-story-gold-as-an-anti-currency) documents. Fiat is not a free lunch; it is a flexible lunch you pay for slowly, in the currency's eroding value.

## How it shows up in real markets

The death of the gold standard is not a museum piece. It is the operating system of every market you trade today. Here is how the 1971 break shows up, concretely, in prices.

**The price after 1971: a 154-fold revaluation.** The single most direct market consequence is the gold price itself. Frozen at \$35 for a generation, gold was free after 1971 to express what the market thought of an unbacked dollar. The answer was: a lot. The annual average ran from \$40.62 in 1971 to roughly \$5,400 in 2026.

#### Worked example: what \$1,000 in 1971 gold is worth today

In 1971, with gold pegged at \$35, \$1,000 bought you `\$1,000 ÷ \$35 = 28.57 troy ounces` of gold. Set those ounces aside in a drawer and never touch them.

At the 2026 gold price of about \$5,414 an ounce, those same 28.57 ounces are worth `28.57 oz × \$5,414 = \$154,686`.

The same \$1,000 left in cash in 1971 is still \$1,000 — and after decades of inflation it buys what perhaps \$140 bought back then. The gold *held its real value and then some*; the cash lost roughly 85% of its purchasing power. **The gold did not "grow"; the dollar shrank against it by a factor of about 154, and that shrinkage is exactly the discipline the gold standard used to prevent.** This is the spine made arithmetic: gold's rise is the dollar's debasement, measured.

**Gold in every currency, not just dollars.** A subtle point that trips up beginners: gold is usually quoted in dollars, so people think of it as a dollar story. But the dollar is not special here — *every* fiat currency has lost ground to gold since the anchor was cut, because every currency is now unbacked paper. The yen, the euro, the dong all buy less gold over time.

![Gold priced in four currencies indexed to 100 in 2015, all rising over time](/imgs/blogs/the-gold-standard-and-its-death-bretton-woods-to-the-nixon-shock-6.png)

#### Worked example: purchasing power across the 1971 break, currency by currency

Index gold's price to 100 in 2015 and watch what each currency does by 2026. In USD, the index reaches about 459 — the dollar buys roughly one-fifth the gold it did in 2015. In EUR it reaches about 500; in VND about 520; in JPY a startling 690, because the yen weakened most. A Japanese saver who held yen cash from 2015 to 2026 saw it buy *one-seventh* as much gold by the end.

The lesson is not "buy gold" — it is conceptual. **After 1971, holding any government's paper is a bet that the government will not over-issue it; gold is the reference point that reveals, in every currency at once, how that bet is going.** The fact that gold rises in *all* of them simultaneously tells you the move is about paper money in general, not about one weak currency. This is why central banks themselves — the very institutions that print the paper — have become the structural buyers of gold since 2022, a theme developed in the [central-banks](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022) post.

**The trilemma is still live, every day.** The policy trilemma did not die with Bretton Woods; the world simply moved to a different corner of the triangle. Most major economies now choose **free capital flows** and **independent monetary policy**, and pay the price in the third leg: *floating, not fixed, exchange rates*. That is why the dollar, yen, and euro swing against each other daily — the float is the cost of keeping the other two legs. Countries that instead want a fixed exchange rate (a currency peg) must give up one of the other two: either capital controls (China's historical choice) or surrendering monetary independence (a currency board, or the eurozone members who gave up their own central banks). Every exchange-rate regime on Earth is just a choice of which trilemma corner to occupy. Vietnam's managed-float dong, for instance, sits in an in-between zone that requires the State Bank to lean on capital controls and reserves precisely because you cannot have all three — the same iron constraint that bound the gold standard.

**Floating rates made exchange-rate volatility a permanent asset class.** Before 1971, currencies barely moved against each other — the whole point of the pegs was stability. After 1973, exchange rates became prices like any other, set by markets and free to swing. This single change created entire industries. The foreign-exchange market, now the largest market on Earth by turnover, exists in its modern form *because* of the float; so does currency hedging, so do the FX options and futures that let a company lock in a rate. A Japanese exporter or a European importer now has to manage currency risk every quarter — a risk that simply did not exist for their grandparents under Bretton Woods. The death of the gold standard did not just change the gold price; it converted exchange rates from fixed constants into volatile variables, and an entire layer of global finance grew up to trade and hedge that volatility. When traders speak of the yen "weakening" or the dollar "strengthening," they are speaking a language that only became necessary after the anchor was cut.

**The petrodollar replaced the gold anchor.** When gold stopped backing the dollar, what kept the world demanding dollars? Partly inertia and network effects — but also a new arrangement in the 1970s whereby the world's oil was priced and settled in dollars, forcing every oil-importing nation to hold and earn dollars. The [petrodollar system](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance) became, in effect, the soft anchor that replaced the hard gold anchor — a demand for dollars rooted in commodities rather than convertibility. Understanding 1971 is the key to understanding why that arrangement was necessary at all: the dollar needed a new reason to be held once it was no longer a claim on gold.

## The takeaway: gold's permanence is a statement about governments

The history we have walked through is not really a story about gold. Gold barely changed across these two centuries — it sat in vaults, inert, doing what it has always done. The thing that changed, over and over, was the *government's willingness to be bound by it.*

That is the insight to carry away. The gold standard was abandoned four times in a century not because gold was a bad form of money — by the metrics savers care about (it held value across decades, it could not be printed, it was no one's liability) it was an *excellent* form of money. It was abandoned because that excellence is, from a government's point of view, a constraint — and constraints get discarded the moment they conflict with the state's need to spend, ease, or survive a crisis. Convertibility is a promise; the trilemma is a cage; the price-specie-flow mechanism is an automatic deflation machine. All three are intolerable to a modern state that has promised its citizens full employment and a welfare state and must finance wars and downturns. So the state leaves. Every time.

This is why I think about gold the way the spine of this series frames it: **not as an investment that compounds, but as a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's running vote of no-confidence in paper money.** The Nixon Shock is the moment that vote went live. Before 1971, the answer was fixed at \$35 and the question could not even be asked. After 1971, the answer floats — and the long climb from \$35 to \$5,400 is the cumulative tally of that no-confidence vote, paid out in the only money that no government can print.

So when you see a gold chart breaking to new highs, do not read it as gold "going up." Read it as paper going down — as the market re-pricing, in real time, the same discipline that governments keep promising and keep abandoning. The chart is not a story about a metal getting more valuable; it is a ledger of every moment a state chose the printing press over the vault, tallied in the one unit that cannot be inflated away. The deepest reason to expect that climb to continue is the most certain fact in this entire history: **governments will keep leaving gold, because they always have, and gold will keep enduring, because it is the one form of money that does not depend on a government keeping its word.**

The gold standard is dead. The reason it died is precisely why gold itself will not.
