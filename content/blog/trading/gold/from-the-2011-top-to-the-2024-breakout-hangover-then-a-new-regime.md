> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — After its 2011 peak, gold spent eight years going nowhere — a hangover driven by rising real interest rates — and then from 2019 it tripled, rising *even as* real rates climbed, because a new engine took over the price: central banks buying more than 1,000 tonnes a year.
>
> - The old regime: gold's price was set almost entirely by **real interest rates** (the inverse relationship). Real rates rose 2011-2018, so gold fell and then stalled.
> - The 2011 top to the 2015 bottom was a roughly **-45% bear market**; 2013 alone fell about **-28%** on the "taper tantrum."
> - From 2019 to 2026 gold **tripled** (about \$1,400 to \$5,400) — and it did so while the 10-year real yield rose by roughly **+2.7 to +3 percentage points**, which the old model says should have *crushed* it.
> - The reason the old model broke: after Russia's reserves were frozen in 2022, **central banks became a price-insensitive structural buyer**. The number to remember: gold tripled against a real-rate headwind that "should" have sunk it.

In the middle of the 2010s, gold was the asset nobody talked about. It had peaked in September 2011 at an intraday \$1,921 an ounce — the climax of a decade-long bull run that took it up roughly sevenfold — and then it just… deflated. By the end of 2015 it was trading near \$1,060. For the next three years it drifted in a narrow band around \$1,200, while the S&P 500 marched relentlessly to new highs and the only thing rising faster than tech stocks was the conviction that gold was a dead asset, a relic, a rock that pays no dividend and does nothing.

If you had bought at the top in 2011 and held, you spent the better part of a decade underwater while everyone around you got rich owning index funds. The financial press wrote gold's obituary on a regular schedule. "Gold is a pet rock," one famous columnist declared in 2015. The logic was airtight: gold pays no interest, the economy was recovering, real interest rates were climbing back toward normal, and a non-yielding metal cannot compete with assets that actually pay you. That logic was *correct* — for the regime that existed at the time.

Then, quietly, the regime changed. From a low near \$1,200 in 2018, gold began to climb. It cleared its 2011 record in 2020, kept going, and by January 2026 it printed an all-time high near \$5,589 — roughly **triple** its 2019 level. And here is the part that broke the textbook: it did most of that *while real interest rates were rising*, which is exactly the condition under which the old model says gold should fall. This post is the story of that transition — the most important regime change in the modern gold market — and it is the one you have to understand to make sense of where the price is today.

## Foundations: the old engine, and the setup left behind by 2011

Before we can explain why the old model broke, we have to be precise about what it actually was. If you have read the companion post on [real interest rates as the master variable](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price), this is a fast recap; if not, here is the whole idea in one paragraph.

**A real interest rate is a nominal interest rate minus inflation** — it is what your money *actually* earns in purchasing power after prices rise. If a government bond pays 3% and inflation is 2%, the real yield is about +1%: hold the bond and your wealth grows 1% a year in real terms. The cleanest market measure of this is the yield on **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS)** — bonds whose principal adjusts for inflation, so their quoted yield *is* a real yield, observable in real time. Every gold desk in the world watches one chart above all others: the 10-year TIPS real yield (the Federal Reserve publishes it as series DFII10).

Now connect that to gold. Gold pays no interest and no dividend — it just sits there. So the cost of holding gold instead of a safe inflation-protected bond is exactly that real yield: it is the **opportunity cost** of owning the metal. When real yields are high (say +2%), holding gold means giving up a guaranteed +2% real return every year — expensive, so gold tends to fall. When real yields are low or negative (say -1%), the "safe" alternative is actually *losing* purchasing power, and gold's zero looks great by comparison — so gold tends to rise. The result is one of the most reliable relationships in macro finance: **gold moves inversely to real interest rates.** For most of the 2010s the relationship was tight enough that you could turn a real-yield forecast into a gold price target with a single multiplication.

> [!note]
> **Why "real" and not "nominal" rates.** Gold is a store of purchasing power, so what competes with it is purchasing-power return, not headline interest. A 5% nominal yield with 6% inflation is a *negative* real yield — terrible for cash, great for gold. This is why gold can rise during high-inflation periods even as the central bank hikes rates: what matters is whether the hikes outrun inflation. The macro series develops this in [real vs nominal — the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).

There is a second strand to the old engine, and it is worth naming because it will matter later: **the dollar.** Gold is priced in dollars, so a stronger dollar mechanically makes gold cheaper in dollar terms (it takes fewer dollars to buy an ounce when each dollar buys more of everything), and a weaker dollar makes gold dearer. The dollar and gold tend to move inversely too — and the two strands are linked, because the same Fed policy that lifts real yields usually lifts the dollar. So the "old regime" was really one coherent story: when the Fed tightens, real yields rise *and* the dollar firms, and both push gold down. When the Fed eases, real yields fall *and* the dollar softens, and both lift gold. (The dollar's gravitational pull on every asset is the subject of [the dollar as cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity); the deeper inverse with gold specifically is [gold and the dollar](/blog/trading/gold/gold-and-the-dollar-the-inverse-relationship-and-when-it-breaks).)

Now picture the world that engine left behind at the end of 2011. Gold had just finished a spectacular run — the 2001-2011 bull covered in [the debasement bull](/blog/trading/gold/the-debasement-bull-2001-to-2011-dot-com-gfc-and-qe) — powered by the dot-com bust, the 2008 financial crisis, and three rounds of the Federal Reserve printing money (quantitative easing, or QE). Through all of that, real yields had been pushed *negative*: the Fed held interest rates near zero while inflation fear ran hot, so the real return on safe assets was below zero, and gold soared.

By late 2011 that setup was as stretched as it would get. Real yields were near their lows and had nowhere to go but up. The inflation that the gold bugs kept predicting never showed up the way they feared — all that printed money sat in the banking system rather than chasing goods. And the economy was, slowly, healing. The stage was set for the engine to run in reverse, and reverse it did, for the next seven years.

## The 2011-2015 bear: the engine runs in reverse

The turn came in 2013, and the trigger had a name: the **taper tantrum**.

For years the Fed had been buying bonds — QE — to hold long-term interest rates down and support the recovery. In May 2013, then-Chairman Ben Bernanke merely *hinted* that the Fed might begin to "taper," that is, slow down those purchases. He did not raise interest rates; he did not even stop QE; he just suggested it might end someday. Markets convulsed anyway. Bond yields jumped, real yields jumped with them, and the most rate-sensitive asset on the board — gold — fell out of bed.

#### Worked example: the 2013 taper crash

Gold entered 2013 around \$1,675 (late-2012 levels) and ended the year near \$1,205. That is a calendar-year decline of (1,205 − 1,675) ÷ 1,675 ≈ **−28%** — a 28% loss in a single year, in an asset people had been told was "safe." What changed in 2013? Not the dollar in any dramatic way, and certainly not a crisis. The 10-year TIPS real yield swung from deeply negative in 2012 to around **+0.8%** by year-end 2013 — a move of well over two full percentage points into positive territory. The opportunity cost of holding a zero-yielding metal went from "you're effectively being paid to own gold" to "you're giving up almost +1% a year, guaranteed." If you use the rough historical sensitivity of about \$370 of gold price per 1 percentage point of real yield, a +2-point swing implies roughly −\$740 of "fair value," and gold fell about \$470 — same direction, same order of magnitude. **The intuition: a 28% crash with no crisis and no dollar spike is the unmistakable signature of the real-rate engine, not of fear.**

The bear did not stop in 2013. It ground lower through 2014 and into December 2015, when gold bottomed near \$1,060. There was no single dramatic event — just the steady drip of an improving economy, a Fed that finally raised interest rates in December 2015 for the first time in nearly a decade, and real yields holding stubbornly positive. From the September 2011 intraday peak, the full drawdown was brutal.

#### Worked example: the 2011-to-2015 drawdown

From the peak of \$1,921 (September 2011) to the trough of \$1,060 (December 2015), the decline is (1,060 − 1,921) ÷ 1,921 ≈ **−44.8%**, call it −45%. Gold lost nearly half its value over four years. And the arithmetic of recovery is worse than the arithmetic of decline: to get back from \$1,060 to \$1,921, gold had to rise (1,921 ÷ 1,060) − 1 ≈ **+81%** — which it would not manage for another five years. An investor who bought the 2011 top did not see a new high until 2020, a full nine years later. **The intuition: gold is monetary insurance, not a savings account; bought at a mania top, it can leave you underwater for the better part of a decade — the price you pay for an asset that does nothing in the years the system feels safe.**

Why did this bear unfold so cleanly, so legibly? Because in this regime, *only one engine was running.* The 2011-2015 decline maps almost perfectly onto the rise in real interest rates. Inflation was quiet, often running below the Fed's 2% target, so there was no inflation-hedge bid. There was no financial crisis pulling in safe-haven money. The dollar was firm but not violent. And central banks — though they had stopped *selling* gold, as the 1990s-2000s European disposals ended — were buying it at a modest pace that did not move the price against the prevailing tide. With nothing else in the picture, gold did exactly what the textbook said: real rates up, gold down.

This is the period that taught a whole generation of analysts that gold is "just" a real-rate trade. It is a fair lesson for the data they had. It is also the period whose lesson would expire — and the analysts who never updated it would spend 2022-2026 baffled.

![Gold annual percent change 2011 to 2026 bar chart bear range then breakout](/imgs/blogs/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime-7.png)

The bar chart above turns the whole modern cycle into one shape. The angry red bars of 2013, 2014, and 2015 are the bear. Then the bars shrink almost to nothing through 2016-2018 — the range. Then they turn green and grow, year after year, from 2019 onward — the breakout. Three regimes, one picture. Hold that shape in mind; the rest of this post explains each band.

## The 2016-2018 range: the boring years

After the 2015 bottom, gold did something almost worse than fall: it went quiet. From 2016 through 2018 it traded in a tedious band, with annual averages clustered around \$1,250-\$1,270 — barely changed year to year. Look at the percent-change chart above and you see it instantly: after the red bars of 2013-2015, the 2016-2018 bars are tiny. Gold gained a little in 2016 (a bounce off the washed-out lows), did essentially nothing in 2017, and slipped a hair in 2018 as the Fed kept hiking.

These were the years that hardened gold's reputation as a dead asset, and to understand the modern breakout you have to feel how thoroughly gold was *discredited* by 2018. Consider the contrast. From the start of 2016 to the end of 2018, the S&P 500 returned roughly +30% with dividends; the U.S. economy added jobs every single month; the Fed was confidently *hiking* interest rates back toward normal, pushing real yields up toward +1%. In that environment, an asset that pays nothing and goes sideways looked not just boring but *foolish*. Why hold a pet rock when the index compounds at 9% a year?

Money voted with its feet. It flowed *out* of gold and into the assets that were working — stocks, and increasingly, a new digital "store of value" that captured the speculative imagination and absorbed a good deal of the marginal "alternative money" demand that might once have gone to gold. The narrative had shifted: the young, risk-seeking version of the gold buyer now bought crypto, and gold was left to the old, conservative holders who weren't selling but weren't adding either. Volume was thin. Volatility was low. Gold was, in market terms, asleep.

But underneath the boredom, something was beginning that almost nobody priced. **Central banks had quietly become net buyers of gold again.** As the next section's chart shows in detail, official-sector purchases ran around 350-650 tonnes a year through the mid-2010s. That was not enough to move the price against the real-rate headwind — a few hundred tonnes a year is small against a market with an above-ground stock measured in the hundreds of thousands of tonnes. But it was a foundation being poured under the floor. The buyer who would eventually overpower the real-rate engine was already in the market in 2016-2018; it was simply too small, yet, to be felt. The boring years were not nothing happening. They were the new engine warming up where no Western investor was looking.

#### Worked example: the opportunity cost of holding gold, 2016-2018

Suppose you held \$10,000 in gold from early 2016 to the end of 2018, instead of in safe assets earning the real yield available at the time, which averaged roughly +0.6% real over those three years. Gold's price went from about \$1,150 (early 2016) to about \$1,280 (end 2018), a gain of (1,280 − 1,150) ÷ 1,150 ≈ **+11.3%** over three years, or about +3.6% a year. That actually *beat* the +0.6% real on cash — your \$10,000 became about \$11,130. But it badly lagged the S&P's roughly +9% a year, which would have turned \$10,000 into nearly \$13,000. The pain of holding gold in the boring years was never that it *lost* money; it was the **regret of watching everything else compound faster.** And that regret is exactly the emotion that flushes the last marginal holders out of an asset right before its regime changes — which is precisely what happened. **The intuition: the most discredited an asset ever gets is usually just before the engine that will drive its next bull market quietly comes online.**

## The 2019-2020 surge: the old engine fires one last time

In mid-2019 the picture changed, and at first it changed in a way the old model fully explains.

The Fed, having spent 2017-2018 hiking, abruptly reversed and began *cutting* interest rates in July 2019 as growth wobbled and trade tensions bit. Real yields rolled over. Right on cue, gold woke up — it broke out of its three-year range and climbed from the low \$1,300s toward \$1,500 by year-end. This was the real-rate engine doing exactly what it always does: rates fall, opportunity cost falls, gold rises. Nothing surprising — just the textbook, in the right direction, after a long pause.

Then came 2020, and the surprise was the *magnitude*, not the direction. The COVID-19 pandemic triggered the fastest economic shutdown in modern history. In a span of weeks in March 2020, the Fed slashed interest rates to zero, restarted bond-buying on an unlimited scale (what some traders called "QE infinity"), and the federal government passed trillions of dollars in stimulus — the CARES Act alone was \$2.2 trillion. The U.S. money supply (M2) jumped from about \$15.4 trillion at the end of 2019 to roughly \$19.1 trillion by the end of 2020 — a one-year increase of nearly a quarter, unprecedented in peacetime.

Real yields plunged to record lows. The 10-year TIPS yield fell to roughly **−1.06%** — deeply negative. With safe assets now *guaranteeing* a loss of purchasing power, the opportunity cost of holding gold wasn't just low — it was negative. Owning gold was, in a real and measurable sense, *cheaper* than owning cash, because cash was contractually losing 1% a year in real terms while gold's permanent zero now looked generous. Layer on top of that the raw fear of a global pandemic and the debasement worry from all that printed money, and you had all three classic gold tailwinds firing at once: crashing real rates, a fear bid, and a flood of new money.

Gold responded by smashing through its long-standing 2011 record. In August 2020 it set a new all-time high near **\$2,075** an ounce. Nine years after the 2011 top, gold had finally made a new nominal record — and it did so for textbook reasons. There is a subtlety worth pausing on: even at \$2,075, gold had *not* made a new high in inflation-adjusted terms. The January 1980 peak of \$850 is worth roughly \$3,300 in today's money, and the 2011 peak of \$1,921 is worth roughly \$2,650 — so the 2020 record was a new *nominal* high but still below both prior *real* highs. Gold's real record would not fall until the 2025-2026 surge. (Why the metal that "preserves purchasing power" took 44 years to make a new real high is its own cautionary tale, and a reason this series insists gold is insurance, not a compounder.)

#### Worked example: the 2020 move, decomposed across all three engines

The 2020 surge is the rare moment all three classic tailwinds can be measured pulling the same way. Real yields: the 10-year TIPS yield fell from about +0.15% (end 2019) to about −1.06% (the 2020 low) — a drop of about **1.2 points**. At the rough \$370-per-point sensitivity, that alone is worth roughly +\$440 of gold price. The dollar: the dollar index slid from about 96.4 (end 2019) to roughly 89.9 (end 2020), a fall of about **−7%**, which under the inverse relationship is a further tailwind of a few hundred dollars on gold. Money supply: M2 jumped about +24% in a single year, feeding the debasement bid. Gold went from about \$1,517 (end 2019) to its August peak of \$2,075 — a move of about **+\$558, or +37%**. The real-rate leg explains the bulk of it (+\$440), the weaker dollar and the fear/debasement bid explain the rest, and the central-bank engine was still in its quiet phase — this was the *old* regime's last, perfect performance. **The intuition: 2020 is the control case — when you can account for almost the entire move with real rates and the dollar, you are still in the one-engine world; the tell that the regime changed is when you suddenly cannot.**

For the mechanics of why money-printing supports gold, see [quantitative easing explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money) and [debasement and the fiscal story](/blog/trading/gold/debasement-debt-and-the-fiscal-story-gold-as-an-anti-currency).

If the story had ended in 2020, it would be unremarkable: gold did what it always does when real rates crash. The real-rate engine was firing on all cylinders, perfectly, one more time. But it was firing for the *last* time as gold's *sole* driver. Because in 2021, the engine reversed hard — and gold, this time, refused to follow it down.

## The 2022-2026 breakout: rising against the textbook

Here is where the modern gold market becomes genuinely strange, and genuinely important. If you understand this section, you understand gold in the 2020s.

Starting in 2021 and accelerating violently in 2022, inflation roared to a 40-year high — U.S. CPI peaked above 9% in mid-2022 — and the Fed responded with the fastest rate-hiking campaign in four decades, taking its policy rate from near zero to over 5% in about eighteen months. Nominal yields surged. Critically, **real yields surged too**, and that is the part that matters for gold. The 10-year TIPS yield went from roughly **−1.0%** at the end of 2021 to **+1.57%** by the end of 2022, and kept climbing to around **+2.2%** by the end of 2024. In the language of the old model, that is a catastrophic headwind: the opportunity cost of holding gold swung from "you're paid to own it" to "you give up more than 2% a year, guaranteed."

By every rule that governed 2011-2018, gold should have collapsed. A swing of more than three percentage points into deeply positive real-yield territory is the single most bearish thing that can happen to gold under the old regime. Remember: in 2013, *half* that move produced a 28% crash. Apply the old sensitivity naively and a +2.7 to +3 point real-yield rise should have taken gold down 40% or more. The dollar, too, was strong through 2022 (the dollar index hit a two-decade high), which under the old engine is a second hammer on the price.

Instead, gold went *up*. It held roughly flat through the brutal 2022 rate shock — a year that demolished stocks and bonds simultaneously — then broke decisively higher in 2023 and 2024, cleared \$2,700 in late 2024, and then went vertical into 2025-2026, printing an all-time high near \$5,589 in January 2026. Both engines of the old regime — real rates and the dollar — were pushing *down* for much of this period, and the price went *up* anyway. When an asset rises in the teeth of the two forces that are supposed to govern it, the honest conclusion is not that the forces stopped existing. It is that a *third* force, bigger than both, has entered the market.

![Gold versus 10-year real yield dual axis showing tight inverse then 2022 decoupling](/imgs/blogs/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime-2.png)

Look hard at the chart above, because it is the single most important image in this post. The red dashed line is the 10-year real yield, plotted on an *inverted* axis so that "tracks together" visually means "moves inversely." From 2013 through 2021, gold (the blue line) and real yields move in near-lockstep — when one rises the other rises on the chart, which means they are moving inversely in reality, exactly the textbook relationship of the old regime. The two lines hug each other for the better part of a decade. Then, in the amber band from 2022 on, the lines *split violently apart*. Real yields rise (the red line drops on the inverted axis) while gold climbs to the top of the frame. **The relationship that defined gold for a generation simply stopped working in 2022, and it has not come back.**

#### Worked example: the decoupling, quantified

Let's back out the size of the anomaly in numbers. From the end of 2021 to recent levels, the 10-year real yield moved from about **−1.04%** to about **+1.99%** — a swing of roughly **+3.0 percentage points** *against* gold (and even measuring from the 2021 low to 2023's +1.72% gives **+2.7 points**; either way, a brutal headwind). Over the same span, gold went from about \$1,829 (end 2021) to about \$5,400 (2026), a gain of (5,400 ÷ 1,829) − 1 ≈ **+195%**, nearly a triple. Now put those together. Under the old model, a +2.7 to +3 point rise in real yields should have knocked gold *down* — call it a 30-50% decline based on the 2013 sensitivity. Instead gold rose about +195%. The gap between "what the real-rate model predicted" (roughly −40%) and "what actually happened" (+195%) is on the order of **230 percentage points of performance the old engine cannot explain.** That is not a rounding error or a lag; it is a different market. **The intuition: when a model is off by 230 points in the wrong direction, the model isn't wrong about its own engine — a second engine you weren't tracking has come online and is now setting the price.**

## The regime change explained: one engine became two

So what was the second engine? Two things, deeply linked: **central banks** and **geopolitics**.

The structural buyer is covered in depth in [central banks: the structural buyer that changed gold after 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022), so I will not re-derive the whole story — but the numbers are the heart of this post's argument, so look at them directly.

![Central bank net gold purchases bar chart stepping up to over 1000 tonnes from 2022](/imgs/blogs/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime-4.png)

The chart tells the whole regime change in one image. Through the 2000s, central banks were net *sellers* of gold — the red bars below zero — dumping the metal as an unfashionable relic. (This is the era of the late-1990s European sales that produced the so-called "Brown's Bottom," when the U.K. famously sold half its gold reserves near the absolute price low.) Then, around 2010, the official sector flipped to net buyers, and ran an average of about **473 tonnes a year** from 2010 through 2021. That was meaningful but modest — a steady bid that helped put a floor under the price during the bear and the range, but could not, on its own, overpower the real-rate engine.

Then 2022 happened. Central-bank net purchases leaped to **1,082 tonnes** — the highest annual figure since 1950 — and stayed above 1,000 tonnes in both 2023 (1,037 t) and 2024 (1,045 t). The buying *more than doubled* from its 2010-2021 average, and it doubled in the exact year that real yields were rocketing higher. A price-insensitive buyer of unprecedented size arrived at the precise moment the price-sensitive engine turned maximally hostile. That timing is not a coincidence, and the reason it is not a coincidence is the whole story.

The trigger was the freezing of Russia's foreign-exchange reserves in early 2022, after the invasion of Ukraine. Western governments froze roughly \$300 billion of Russian dollar and euro reserves with the stroke of a pen. Every central bank on earth absorbed the same lesson in real time: **dollars and euros held in the Western financial system are not truly *your* money — they can be switched off.** A reserve held as U.S. Treasuries is, legally, a claim on the U.S. government, payable through Western banks; it can be frozen, sanctioned, or seized. Physical gold sitting in your own national vault is different in kind. It is no one's liability — it is not a claim on any government, it has no counterparty who can refuse to pay, and it cannot be frozen by a decision made in Washington or Brussels. The full mechanics of why this single event reframed reserve management for the entire world are in [sanctions, reserve freezes, and the 2022 turning point](/blog/trading/gold/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold).

This is the difference between the two engines, and it is worth stating crisply, because everything else follows from it:

- **The real-rate engine** is *price-sensitive*. It asks one question: "What is the opportunity cost of holding gold versus a yielding bond?" When that cost rises, this engine sells gold and buys the bond. It is the engine of Western investors, traders, and ETF holders — people maximizing risk-adjusted return.
- **The central-bank engine** is *price-insensitive*. It asks a completely different question: "Do I want a reserve asset that no foreign government can freeze?" That question does not care whether gold is \$1,800 or \$5,000 an ounce. A central bank diversifying out of dollars for *strategic* reasons will buy gold across a wide range of prices, and — crucially — will *keep* buying as the price rises, because a higher price does not change the strategic answer.

When those two engines *agree* — both pulling gold up, as in 2020 when real rates crashed and fear surged — gold soars. When they *disagree*, as they did from 2022 on with real rates pushing down and central banks pushing up, the new regime's answer is that **the price-insensitive engine can win.** The Western trader sells gold because the real yield is +2% and the math says hold the bond instead; the central bank buys it anyway because a +2% real yield is irrelevant next to the risk of having your entire reserve base frozen in a geopolitical conflict. The central bank has deeper pockets, a longer horizon, and a motive that price cannot satisfy. Between a price-sensitive seller and a price-insensitive buyer, the buyer sets the price on the way up. The price went up.

![Old regime versus new regime showing the real-rate engine alone then real rate plus central bank engines](/imgs/blogs/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime-3.png)

The diagram above is the whole post in one frame. On the left, the old regime: a single engine, real interest rates, feeding the textbook rule (gold is a bet on the opportunity cost of cash), feeding a price that tracks real yields tightly. On the right, the new regime: Engine 1 (real rates) is *still running* — it never switched off — but now a second, heavier engine sits beside it: central banks and geopolitics, more than 1,000 tonnes a year of price-insensitive buying driven by reserve-freeze fear. When the two engines disagree, Engine 2 can override Engine 1, and the price rose about +180% despite a +2.7-point real-rate headwind. That override — not the disappearance of the old rule — is the regime change.

There is one more piece of evidence that nails the case, and it is the cleanest single "tell" in the entire modern gold story.

![Western gold ETF holdings falling 2021 to 2024 while the price rose](/imgs/blogs/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime-5.png)

This chart shows total holdings in gold-backed **exchange-traded funds (ETFs)** — the dominant vehicle through which Western investors own gold without holding the physical metal. (When you buy a share of a gold ETF, the fund buys and vaults a corresponding amount of physical gold; ETF tonnage is therefore a clean proxy for Western investment demand.) Holdings peaked near 3,900 tonnes in 2020, at the height of the COVID surge — Western investors piling in exactly when the real-rate engine was screaming "buy." And then, from 2021 through 2024, *they fell*: ETF tonnage dropped from about 3,570 in 2021 to about 3,219 by the end of 2024. Western investors were net **sellers** of gold for three straight years.

Sit with the contradiction, because it is the crux of the entire argument. The classic Western gold buyer — the investor responding to real yields, the ETF holder — was *selling* through 2021-2024. And yet the price *rose* sharply through the very same period. This is logically airtight: if the people who usually set gold's price were net sellers, then by simple arithmetic *someone else* was absorbing all that sold metal **and** buying enough more to drive the price up. That someone was central banks (who buy physical bars for vaults, not ETF shares, and so never appear in this data) and Asian physical buyers (savers in China, India, and across emerging Asia, who likewise buy outside the Western ETF channel). The price and the Western flow moved in *opposite* directions — and that divergence is the unmistakable fingerprint of a buyer who does not live in the West, does not trade ETFs, and does not care about the real yield.

#### Worked example: the ETF outflow against the rising price

Make the absorption concrete. From the end of 2021 to the end of 2024, ETF holdings fell from about 3,570 tonnes to about 3,219 tonnes — roughly **350 tonnes** dumped onto the market by the rate-sensitive Western crowd. Over those same three years, central banks bought about 1,082 + 1,037 + 1,045 ≈ **3,164 tonnes**. So central banks didn't merely offset the 350 tonnes of Western selling — they absorbed it *and* took down roughly nine times more on top. Gold rose from about \$1,829 to \$2,625 over that span, a gain of (2,625 ÷ 1,829) − 1 ≈ **+44%**, *while* its most price-sensitive holders were heading for the exits. **The intuition: when the price and the Western ETF flow move in opposite directions, you are no longer in a one-engine world, and any model built only on real yields will get you run over.**

#### Worked example: the 2019-to-2026 triple, as a return

Step back and measure the whole breakout as a return. Gold's annual average went from about **\$1,393** in 2019 to about **\$5,414** in 2026 — a total gain of (5,414 ÷ 1,393) − 1 ≈ **+289%**, very nearly a quadruple measured on annual averages (a clean triple measured year-end to year-end). Compounded over seven years, that is a CAGR of (5,414 ÷ 1,393) raised to the power 1/7, minus 1 ≈ **+21.4% a year**. A non-yielding metal compounded at over 21% a year for seven years — beating most major stock indices over the same span. **The intuition: gold doesn't usually compound at equity-like rates, but when both engines turn on at once (2019-2020) and the new structural engine then takes over (2022-2026), it can outrun stocks for years — which is precisely why "gold pays no dividend, so it can't compete with stocks" is a regime-specific argument, not a law of nature.**

There is a deeper, more durable reason the central-bank engine raises the *floor* under the price and not just the ceiling, and it is worth stating because it changes how you should think about gold's downside in this regime. A price-sensitive buyer sells into strength and buys into weakness — that is what makes the price oscillate around fair value. A price-insensitive buyer with a strategic mandate does the opposite of selling into strength: it keeps buying as price rises, and it does not panic-sell on dips, because its motive (avoiding reserve seizure, diversifying out of dollars) is unaffected by a 10% pullback. Each year the structural buyer takes another ~1,000 tonnes off the market and into vaults where it will sit for decades, the *float* of freely-tradable gold shrinks, and the price needed to clear the remaining supply ratchets up. This is why the new regime looks less like a spike and more like a rising staircase — a higher floor, re-set each year by a buyer who does not sell.

## The geopolitical engine and why supply can't answer it

It helps to see *why* central-bank buying has so much further to run, because the answer comes from a simple imbalance in how the world's reserves are currently allocated. Reserve managers in the developed West already hold most of their reserves in gold — the United States, Germany, Italy, and France each hold gold as roughly **70%** of their total reserves. But the large emerging-market central banks that have been *adding* gold since 2022 hold almost none: China's official gold is around **6%** of reserves, India around **11%**, Russia around **29%**. These are the countries with the most dollars to diversify and the most geopolitical reason to do it. The gap between "where they are" (single-digit gold shares) and "where the West sits" (70%) is the size of the runway. For an emerging-market reserve manager to move even partway toward the developed-world norm implies *years* of buying at the recent pace — which is exactly why this is a structural, multi-year story and not a one-year flurry.

#### Worked example: the de-dollarization runway

Take a stylized large emerging-market central bank holding \$3 trillion of reserves, with gold at just 5% of the total — so about \$150 billion in gold, and \$2.85 trillion in dollars, euros, and other paper claims that could, in principle, be frozen. Suppose, post-2022, it decides to lift gold toward a more defensive 20% of reserves. That means roughly \$600 billion of gold (20% of \$3 trillion) versus the \$150 billion it holds today — a target purchase on the order of **\$450 billion** of additional gold. At a gold price of, say, \$4,000 an ounce, \$450 billion buys about 112 million ounces, or roughly **3,500 tonnes**. One central bank, one reallocation, demands more gold than the *entire world's* official sector bought in any recent three-year stretch. **The intuition: the central-bank bid is not a fad that buys for a year and stops — it is a structural rebalancing with a multi-thousand-tonne runway, which is why the new engine's floor keeps rising.**

Now the obvious question from anyone who has studied normal commodities: if demand has exploded, why doesn't *supply* surge to meet it and cap the price? In copper or oil, a price spike pulls forth new production and the price mean-reverts. Gold is different, and the difference is fundamental to its role as money. The reason is in [the supply side: mine production, AISC, and the myth of peak gold](/blog/trading/gold/the-supply-side-mine-production-aisc-and-the-myth-of-peak-gold), but the core fact is this: annual mine production is *tiny* relative to the existing above-ground stock. All the gold ever mined — roughly 216,000 tonnes — already exists, sitting in vaults, jewelry, and reserves; annual mine output of about 3,600 tonnes adds barely **1.6%** to that stock each year. Even if miners could double output overnight (they cannot — building a mine takes a decade), they would add a rounding error to the float. Gold's "supply" that actually sets the price is not the mine; it is the willingness of *existing holders* to sell. And the marginal new holder since 2022 — the central bank — is the holder least willing to sell at any price. Supply cannot answer this demand the way it answers oil's, which is precisely what lets a structural buyer drive a multi-year re-rating.

There is even a soft floor *under* the price from the cost side: the all-in sustaining cost (AISC) of mining an ounce has risen from under \$900 in the mid-2010s to roughly \$1,390 by 2024. Below that level, marginal mines shut and supply contracts, which historically puts a floor under the price. But notice how far *above* that floor gold now trades — at \$5,000-plus, the price is more than triple the cost of production. That enormous gap is itself a measure of the monetary premium: people and central banks are paying for gold not because it is scarce to *produce* (the margin over cost is huge) but because of what it *is* — money that no one can freeze. The cost floor matters in a bear market; in this bull, it is a distant safety net far below a price set by monetary demand.

## Common misconceptions

**"The old real-rate model is dead — rates don't matter for gold anymore."** Wrong, and dangerously so. The real-rate engine never switched off — it is the same Engine 1 in the diagram above, still running underneath everything. What changed is that a *second*, more powerful engine came online beside it. In a future year where central banks slow their buying *and* real rates spike *and* the dollar surges, the old relationship can reassert itself violently, and "rates don't matter anymore" will be an expensive thing to have believed. The correct statement is not "real rates stopped mattering" but "real rates stopped being the *only* thing that matters." A trader who ignores real yields entirely because "it's all central banks now" will be blindsided the first time the official bid pauses while real yields are high — the same mistake, mirror-imaged, as the 2018 analyst who thought it was *only* real rates.

**"The 2022-2026 run is a pure bubble — it's all speculation."** The data say otherwise. A speculative bubble is characterized by *Western retail and leveraged money piling in* at the top — and the ETF chart shows the exact opposite: Western ETF holders were net *sellers* for most of the run. The buying was dominated by central banks (sovereign, strategic, slow, vaulting metal for decades) and Asian physical demand (savers buying jewelry and bars, not leveraged traders). That is close to the least bubble-like buyer base imaginable — the buyers least likely to dump in a panic. This does not mean gold cannot get ahead of itself: the 2025-2026 vertical move almost certainly carries speculative froth on top, and the gold/silver ratio and miner valuations can flash overheating. But the *core* of the move is a structural, real-money reallocation, not a margin-fueled mania like 1980, when it really was Western retail chasing a parabola. Distinguishing structural demand from froth is the difference between a correction and a crash.

**"Gold is a great inflation hedge, and that's why it rose in 2022."** Mostly wrong about the mechanism, even though it sounds right. As [gold and inflation](/blog/trading/gold/gold-and-inflation-the-hedge-that-works-by-the-decade-not-the-month) shows, gold's month-to-month correlation with inflation is poor. The proof is in 2022 itself: inflation hit a 40-year high *and gold went sideways* — if gold were a simple inflation hedge, it should have soared in the highest-inflation year in four decades. It didn't, because the Fed hiked *real* rates faster than inflation rose, and the real-rate engine pushed gold *down*, roughly cancelling the central-bank bid. Gold's big move came in 2023-2026, *after* inflation had already cooled — driven by the structural buyer, not by inflation. Inflation is a long-arc, decade-scale story for gold's purchasing-power preservation; the 2022-2026 breakout is a central-bank and geopolitics story, and conflating the two will lead you to buy gold for the wrong reason at the wrong time.

**"If central banks are buying, the price can only go up."** Also wrong, and the antidote to over-confidence. Central banks are price-insensitive *buyers*, but their *flow* is not constant — it can slow. In 2025, net purchases of about 863 tonnes came in *below* the 2022 record of 1,082 tonnes, a meaningful deceleration. When the structural bid slows *and* real yields are high *and* Western investors are not stepping back in, gold can correct sharply even within the new regime. The new engine raises the *floor* over time; it does not abolish *cycles*. The 1970s bull had a vicious 1974-1976 correction (gold nearly halved) on its way to the 1980 peak — structural bulls still have brutal pullbacks, and a holder who mistakes "higher floor" for "no drawdowns" will sell at the worst moment.

## How it shows up in real markets

The regime change is not an academic curiosity — it shows up in the tape, in specific, datable episodes you can point to.

**2022: the year gold "did nothing" was actually a triumph.** In 2022, the S&P 500 fell about −18%, long-dated Treasuries fell about −31%, investment-grade corporate bonds fell −15%, and the classic 60/40 portfolio had its worst year in a generation, down about −16%. Even Bitcoin, the supposed "digital gold," collapsed −64%. Gold finished roughly *flat* — about −0.3% on the year. To a casual observer, gold "failed": it was supposed to be the crisis hedge, and in a year of carnage it just sat there. But run it through the right model and 2022 was gold's finest hour. In a year when real yields rose more than two and a half percentage points and the dollar hit a two-decade high, the old single-engine model says gold should have fallen 30% or more. The fact that it held *flat* — that it beat stocks, bonds, the 60/40, and Bitcoin by a country mile — was the central-bank engine *exactly cancelling* the real-rate engine, in plain sight, for the first time. 2022 was not gold doing nothing. It was the two engines fighting to a perfect draw. (The cross-asset carnage of that year, and why the dollar wrecked everything at once, is detailed in [the dollar as cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity).)

#### Worked example: what real yields "should" have done to gold in 2022, and what canceled it

Take 2022 in isolation. The 10-year real yield rose from roughly −1.0% (start of year) to +1.57% (end of year) — a swing of about **+2.6 percentage points**. Using the rough 2013 sensitivity, where about +2 points of real-yield rise produced a roughly −28% gold move, a +2.6-point swing "should" have produced a decline of about −28% × (2.6 ÷ 2.0) ≈ **−36%**. Instead, gold finished about **−0.3%** — essentially flat. So the central-bank engine, buying a record 1,082 tonnes that year, had to supply roughly **+36 percentage points** of offsetting demand just to hold the price level against the real-rate hammer. **The intuition: 2022's "boring" flat gold price was, under the hood, the single most violent tug-of-war between the two engines in the modern record — the new engine fought the old one to a standstill in the old engine's most hostile year, which is exactly how you know the new engine is real and large.**

**2023-2024: the bid that wouldn't quit.** As inflation cooled and the Fed paused, real yields stayed *high* — around +2% — for the better part of two years. Under the old regime, high and persistent positive real yields are death for gold; the metal should at best tread water and more likely grind lower. Instead, gold kept making new records, clearing \$2,700 in late 2024. There was no falling-real-rate story to explain it — real rates were high the whole time. The only available explanation was persistent official-sector and Asian physical demand, exactly the buyer the ETF data revealed. This is the cleanest "new regime" stretch in the whole record: high real rates, no Fed easing, a firm-ish dollar, and gold up anyway, month after month. If 2022 was the tug-of-war, 2023-2024 was the moment the new engine simply started winning outright.

**Gold in every currency at once.** A final, often-overlooked tell: gold made new all-time highs not just in dollars but in euros, yen, British pounds, and Vietnamese dong *simultaneously* across 2022-2026. This distinction matters. When gold rises in *one* currency, it can simply be a story about that currency weakening — gold priced in a sinking currency goes up mechanically without "gold" doing anything. But when gold rises to records in *every* major currency at once, the currency-weakness explanation fails (they can't all be weakening against each other), and you are left with a story about *gold itself* — about a structural, global reallocation toward an asset that is no government's liability. That synchronized, all-currency new high is the geopolitical engine showing up in the price across the entire world at the same time. It is the market-wide vote of no-confidence that this series's spine is about, made visible.

![Breakout drivers stack from rate cuts to COVID to reserve freeze to central bank demand to geopolitics](/imgs/blogs/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime-6.png)

The figure above stacks the breakout's drivers in the order they arrived, and the order is the argument. First, 2019 Fed rate cuts restart the old real-rate engine. Second, 2020 COVID and QE crash real rates to record lows and add a fear bid — the \$2,075 record. Third, 2022's reserve freeze reframes the entire question of what a safe reserve asset is. Fourth, 2022-2025 central-bank demand exceeds 1,000 tonnes a year, a price-insensitive new buyer. Fifth, 2023-2026 geopolitics — wars, soaring government debt, de-dollarization — keeps the bid on. Each wave added a buyer who does not sell on higher prices, so the effect compounds into a *rising floor* rather than a one-off spike. The result, at the bottom of the stack: a clean triple from about \$1,500 in 2019 to about \$5,400 in 2026 — a breakout built from five stacked shocks, not a blow-off built from one.

## The takeaway: how to position in the new regime

If you take one mental model from this entire post, make it this: **gold is now driven by two engines, not one, and you have to watch both at once.**

The old single-engine view — "gold is a real-rate trade; short it when rates rise, buy it when they fall" — was correct for 2011-2018 and would have gotten you run over from 2022 onward; an investor who waited to buy gold until real yields fell, the way the playbook said in 2015, would have missed the entire breakout. The naive new view — "it's all central banks now, real rates don't matter anymore" — will get you run over the first time the official bid pauses while real yields are high and the dollar is firm. The truth is the harder, two-handed version: gold's price is the *sum* of a price-sensitive Western flow (governed by real yields and the dollar) and a price-insensitive structural flow (governed by geopolitics, de-dollarization, and the fear of having your reserves frozen). Hold both in your head, and the last fifteen years stop looking contradictory and start looking like a clean handoff between two drivers.

It is also worth naming, honestly, what would *break* this thesis — because a view you can't falsify is a religion, not an analysis. The new regime depends on the central-bank bid persisting, and that bid would fade if the geopolitical fear that started it reversed: a durable thaw between the major powers, an unwinding of the sanctions architecture, and a restoration of trust that Western-held reserves are safe again. In that world, the strategic reason to hold gold over Treasuries weakens, the official bid slows toward its old ~470-tonne pace, and gold would once again be left alone with the real-rate engine — which, at +2% real yields, points down. The deceleration to about 863 tonnes of official buying in 2025 from the 1,082-tonne 2022 record is the kind of data point to watch for whether that fade is beginning. The bull case is structural, but it is *conditional*; the condition is that the world stays distrustful enough to keep diversifying out of paper money. So far it has.

For a holder, this changes the job in three concrete ways. First, **the floor is higher than it used to be.** The structural buyer means the kind of −45% multi-year bear that followed 2011 is materially less likely *as long as* the central-bank bid persists — you are no longer fighting a one-engine market that can grind you down for a decade on rising real rates alone. That is a genuine change to gold's risk profile, not a slogan. Second, **don't expect the old inverse signal to time your entry.** The single most important practical lesson of 2022-2026 is that "real yields are rising, so avoid gold" stopped being a reliable sell signal. Watch real yields, but treat them as one input, not the input. Third, **watch the new dashboard:** central-bank purchase data (the World Gold Council reports it quarterly — the most important gold chart of the 2020s), the trajectory of de-dollarization and sanctions policy, and whether Western ETF holders are still net sellers (a sign the structural buyer is still firmly in charge) or finally returning (which would add a *third* leg to the move and is the bullish development to watch for next).

And step back to the series spine, because the modern cycle is its clearest demonstration. Gold is not an investment that compounds the way a business does — it produces nothing, pays nothing, and just sits in a vault. Its price is the world's running tally of no-confidence in paper money, in real yields, and in political stability. For most of the 2010s that vote of no-confidence was *low*: the system was healing, real rates were positive, faith in the dollar was unquestioned, and so gold languished near \$1,200, mocked as a pet rock. The 2022 reserve freeze changed the question that every central bank — and, increasingly, every thoughtful saver — was asking, from "what earns me the most yield?" to "what can never be taken from me?" Gold's price today is, more than at any time since the 1970s, the answer to that second question. The breakout from \$1,200 to \$5,000-plus is not, at its core, a bet that interest rates will fall. It is a *re-pricing of trust* — and that is a far deeper, slower, and more durable engine than the one the textbook had us all watching.
