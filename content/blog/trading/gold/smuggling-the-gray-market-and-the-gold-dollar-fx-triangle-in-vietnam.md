On a humid morning in May 2024, a queue formed outside an SJC shop on Tran Nhan Tong street in Hanoi. The official gold-bar price had just printed 92.4 million dong for one luong — the thirty-seven-and-a-half-gram bar that is Vietnam's standard unit of gold wealth. Convert that at the day's exchange rate of about 25,450 dong per dollar and you get roughly \$3,631 for a piece of metal that, at the London price, was worth about \$2,879. The same gold. A 26% markup, sitting in a glass case, fully legal, with a receipt.

That gap is the subject of this post — not because it is unfair to the buyer in the queue (it is), but because of where the gap *goes*. A 19-million-dong wedge per luong is not a number on a screen. It is a wage. Somewhere along the Cambodian border, a courier with a few kilograms of gold taped to their body is being paid, in effect, out of that gap. And to put that gold into Vietnam in the first place, someone had to buy it abroad — in dollars. The story of the SJC premium is, underneath, the story of how Vietnam's gold market leaks dollars out of the country every time the official price runs too far ahead of the world.

This is the shadow side of the market we built up over the rest of the Vietnam track. We have explained why Vietnamese households hold so much gold, how Decree 24 built the SJC monopoly, why the premium exists, and how the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) tries to manage it. Here we follow the metal underground — through the gray market, across the borders, and around the gold-dollar-dong triangle — and we end with the single most useful thing the premium can tell you: a live reading on the dong itself.

## Foundations: why bans create gray markets, and the gold-dollar-dong triangle

Before any of the mechanics, we need three ideas built from zero: what a gray market is and why a ban creates one, what the three different prices for the same gold are, and what the gold-dollar-dong triangle means. None of these requires any finance background. They are all variations on one piece of common sense: if people want something badly and you make the legal version expensive or scarce, the demand does not disappear — it finds another door.

### A ban does not destroy demand; it relocates it

Start with the most basic fact about any market. A price is just where the amount people want to buy meets the amount available to sell. If you cap the legal supply of something people still want, you have not changed how much they want. You have only changed where they can get it.

Vietnam's gold rules do exactly this. Since Decree 24 in 2012, only the State Bank, through the single SJC brand, may produce gold bars, and private companies may not import gold to make them. The stated goal was "anti-goldization" — to pull the economy off its habit of pricing apartments and savings in gold and to stop gold demand from draining the country's dollar reserves. (We covered the full policy in the Decree 24 post.) But Vietnamese households did not stop wanting gold. Weddings still happen. Tet still comes. Distrust of the dong, earned over decades of devaluation, did not evaporate because of a decree.

So demand that could not be met by legal imports went looking for another door. That other door is the **gray market**: a market that is not formally illegal in the way heroin is, but that operates outside the official, taxed, monopolized channel. Gray-market gold in Vietnam is real gold — often 9999 (99.99% pure) — sold by jewelry shops and informal dealers, frequently as rings (*nhan*) or jewelry rather than stamped SJC bars, precisely because rings and jewelry sit in a looser regulatory category than bullion. The gray market is where the demand the ban could not extinguish actually clears.

Here is the load-bearing idea for the whole post: **a ban on legal supply, against demand that does not fall, does not lower the price — it raises it, and the size of the increase is the reward for supplying the market illegally.** Economists have a dry name for this (the "smuggling rent"); we will just call it the smuggler's wage. The bigger the gap between what people will pay and what the legal channel will supply, the bigger the wage, and the more metal gets smuggled to collect it.

This is not a Vietnam-specific quirk; it is a law of prohibition that shows up everywhere from alcohol in 1920s America to cigarettes across tax borders to gold in India. The pattern is always the same three steps. One: a government restricts the legal supply of something people want, usually for a reason that sounds good (protect reserves, raise tax, control a vice). Two: because demand does not fall, the legal price rises or the legal supply runs short. Three: the gap between what buyers will pay and what the legal channel offers becomes a bounty, and a parallel market springs up to collect it. The only variables are *how big* the gap is and *how easy* it is to supply the parallel market — and for a small, high-value, easy-to-hide good like gold, sitting next to porous land borders, both variables point toward a large and durable gray market.

There is one more piece of common sense to install here, because it explains why the gray market is so resilient. The legal channel and the gray channel are *substitutes* for the buyer but not perfect ones. An SJC bar is more trusted, easier to resell, and unambiguously legal; a gray-market ring is cheaper but carries a worse spread when you sell it back and a faint legal cloud. So the buyer's choice depends on the *size of the premium*: when the official premium is small, most buyers pay up for the SJC bar's convenience and legitimacy; when the premium is huge, more buyers defect to the gray channel to save real money. The gray market does not have a fixed market share — it *breathes* with the premium, swelling when the official price runs away and shrinking when policy reins it in. Hold that idea; it is why the 2024 intervention worked and why the demand it displaced did not vanish but migrated.

### Three prices for the same metal

Once you accept that demand finds another door, you get something that confuses every newcomer to Vietnam's gold market: the *same* gold trades at *three different prices* at the same moment. They are not three qualities of gold — physically it is the same metal. They are three channels.

![Layered diagram of three price tiers world price gray market price and official SJC price for Vietnamese gold](/imgs/blogs/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam-3.png)

- **The world price.** This is gold's price everywhere that is not artificially walled off: the London spot price, quoted in dollars per troy ounce, which we covered in the pricing post. To make it comparable to Vietnam, convert it: multiply the dollar-per-ounce price by the exchange rate (dong per dollar) and by 1.20565, the number of troy ounces in one luong. In mid-2024, with world gold around \$2,388 per ounce and the dong at 25,450 per dollar, the **world-equivalent price** of a luong was about 73.3 million dong (~\$2,879). This is what gold "should" cost in Vietnam if the country had a normal open market.
- **The official SJC price.** This is the price in the glass case: the SJC-stamped bar, the only legal bullion brand, sold by licensed dealers. It carries the full weight of the monopoly and the import ban — at its May 2024 peak, about 92.4 million dong (~\$3,631) per luong.
- **The gray-market price.** This is where smuggled metal clears — below the SJC bar (because it lacks the trusted SJC stamp and the legal blessing) but above the world price (because it still had to be smuggled in, and smuggling is not free). Think of it as roughly 88 million dong (~\$3,470) per luong in that same window, a few million dong under the official quote.

The two gaps in that stack each pay someone. The gap between the world price and the gray price pays the smuggler. The gap between the gray price and the official price is the premium for the SJC stamp's legitimacy — the monopoly's own reward. The total distance from the world price to the SJC price is what we usually mean by "the SJC premium," and it is the headline number in the press.

It is worth being precise about why these three prices do not simply converge, because in a normal market they would. In London or New York, if gold traded at three prices at once, arbitrageurs would instantly buy the cheap one and sell the dear one until the gap closed. That force — arbitrage — is exactly what an import ban switches off. A Vietnamese dealer cannot legally import a tonne of London gold to sell into the 25%-richer Hanoi market and pocket the difference; the law forbids it. So the arbitrage that would normally crush the gap can only happen *illegally*, through the gray market, at the cost and risk of smuggling. The premium is, in one sentence, **the amount of arbitrage profit that the law has made illegal to capture cheaply.** The gray market is arbitrage wearing a disguise, and the disguise has a price.

![Chart of the official SJC gold price versus the implied world-equivalent price with the premium wedge shaded from 2023 to 2026](/imgs/blogs/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam-6.png)

The chart above draws the top and bottom rungs of that ladder over time — the official SJC bar price and the implied world-equivalent price — with the premium shaded between them. Two features are worth reading. First, both lines march upward together: Vietnamese gold rises when world gold rises, because the world price is the floor the premium sits on top of. The premium is a *markup*, not an independent price. Second, the shaded wedge between the lines is not constant — it ballooned in 2024 (the 18-million-dong gap) and was squeezed thin afterward. That shaded area, luong by luong, tonne by tonne, is the total pool of money the gray market and the monopoly split between them. When it widens, smuggling becomes more profitable per unit *and* there is more of it; when the SBV crushes it, the pool shrinks and the couriers' wage shrinks with it.

### The gold-dollar-dong triangle

Now the central mechanism. Why does any of this touch the dong's exchange rate? Because gold abroad is priced in dollars, and gold in Vietnam is paid for in dong.

Follow one cycle, which is exactly what the cover figure draws:

1. **Buy gold abroad in dollars.** A smuggler (or the network they work for) buys gold at the world price in a nearby hub — Phnom Penh, Bangkok, sometimes Hong Kong or Dubai upstream. They pay in **US dollars**, because that is the currency the global gold market runs on. To do step 1, they first need dollars.
2. **Smuggle it into Vietnam.** The gold crosses a land border — most often Cambodia, sometimes Laos — by courier, hidden in vehicles, or carried by "mules." This leg costs money and risk: bribes, couriers, the chance of seizure. Call this the smuggling cost.
3. **Sell it inside Vietnam for dong.** The gold is melted, re-cast, or sold as rings and jewelry through the gray market, and the buyer pays in **dong** at the gray-market price.
4. **Convert the dong back into dollars.** To run the loop again, the network must turn its dong proceeds back into dollars — and since the banking channel will not hand out dollars for this, they buy dollars on the **black FX market**, paying a few percent above the official rate.

Then back to step 1. The triangle closes. And notice what just happened to Vietnam's economy: a stream of dollars left the country to buy gold abroad, and a matching stream of dong chased dollars on the black market to refill the loop. That second leg — domestic dong hunting for scarce dollars — is *exactly* the pressure that pushes the dong's exchange rate weaker. Gold smuggling is, at its core, a machine for converting dong into a claim on dollars and shipping it abroad. That is why the government calls it "goldization" and treats it as a reserve-and-FX problem, not just a tax-evasion problem.

It is worth dwelling on *why a triangle and not a straight line*. A normal importer also spends dollars to buy a foreign good and earns dong selling it — that is just trade. What makes gold special is two things. First, gold is not consumed; it is *hoarded*. An imported phone gets used up and creates ongoing demand for more phones, but the value is consumed. A smuggled luong becomes a permanent store of dollar-denominated value sitting in a household safe — so the dollars spent to import it are, from the country's point of view, *exported and not coming back as future earnings*. Second, because the trade is illegal, the dollar leg cannot run through the banks at the official rate; it runs through the black market, where it directly bids up the dong price of a dollar. A legal importer's dollar demand is absorbed by the formal FX market under the SBV's management; the smuggler's dollar demand spills into the informal one, where it is most visible as a widening gap between the official and street exchange rates. The triangle, in short, is a leak in the capital account that the controls were specifically built to plug — and gold is the substance most able to slip through, because a fortune fits in a fist.

### The conversion that makes the premium legible

Everything in this post hinges on one small piece of arithmetic, so it is worth making it explicit and keeping it in your pocket. To compare a Vietnamese dong-per-luong price with the world dollar-per-ounce price, you need two conversion factors: the exchange rate (dong per dollar) and the unit conversion (one luong is 1.20565 troy ounces, because a luong is 37.5 grams and a troy ounce is 31.1035 grams). The world-equivalent price of a luong, in dong, is:

```
world-equivalent (VND/luong)  =  world price ($/oz)  x  1.20565 (oz/luong)  x  USD/VND
```

Plug in the May 2024 numbers — world gold \$2,388 per ounce, exchange rate 25,450 dong per dollar:

```
2,388  x  1.20565  x  25,450  =  ~73.3 million VND per luong
```

And the premium is simply the actual SJC quote minus that world-equivalent, expressed in dong or as a percentage. There is no mystery in the premium; it is a subtraction. What makes it interesting is not the formula but what the result *means* — that the difference between those two numbers is a wage, a dollar drain, and an FX signal all at once. Keep the formula; we will use it again to turn a premium into a dong-stress reading.

#### Worked example: the smuggler's gross wage on one luong

Take the May 2024 episode, where the data is richest. A smuggler buys one luong's worth of gold abroad at the world price: \$2,388 per ounce times 1.20565 ounces per luong equals about **\$2,879**, or about **73.3 million VND** at 25,450 dong per dollar. They smuggle it in and sell it into the Vietnamese gray market. Even pricing the sale conservatively at the SJC level of **92.4 million VND (~\$3,631)**, the gross spread is:

```
sell in Vietnam      92.4 million VND   (~$3,631)
buy abroad           73.3 million VND   (~$2,879)
-----------------------------------------------
gross wedge          19.1 million VND   (~$752)  per luong
```

Roughly **19 million dong — about \$750 — per luong, before any cost.** Out of that gross wedge come the smuggling costs (courier, bribes, the dollar premium on the FX leg, the occasional seizure write-off) and the gray-market discount versus the SJC bar. What is left is the net wage. The intuition: every percentage point the official price runs above the world price is, almost mechanically, a percentage point of gross reward for putting one more luong across the border.

## The smuggling channels: the borders, the mechanics, and the scale

The triangle explains *why* gold gets smuggled. The next question is *how much*, and *through where* — because the scale is what turns a curiosity into a macro problem.

![Pipeline diagram of the gold smuggling supply chain from source hub to border to gray dealer to buyer](/imgs/blogs/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam-5.png)

### The geography

Vietnam shares a long, mountainous, and porous land border with Cambodia (about 1,100 km) and Laos (about 2,300 km). These are not the heavily fenced borders of a Cold War map; they run through jungle, rivers, and remote crossings, with legitimate cross-border trade and family ties on both sides. That makes them ideal for moving a high-value, low-bulk good. A kilogram of gold — worth roughly \$130,000 at 2026 prices — is about the size of a small chocolate bar. You can carry a fortune in a backpack.

The supply chain, as the figure above lays it out, runs in stages. Gold is bought at or near the world price in a regional hub where bullion trades freely — Phnom Penh and Bangkok are the classic staging posts, fed in turn by Hong Kong, Dubai, and Singapore. From the hub it moves to the border zone, where couriers (often paid per trip, sometimes ordinary travelers recruited as mules) carry it across. On the Vietnamese side, it reaches a gray dealer — a jewelry shop or informal trader who melts and re-casts the metal, or sells it on as **9999 rings and jewelry** that look like ornaments but function as savings. Finally it reaches the buyer, who pays in dong at the gray-market price.

Why Cambodia and not, say, a direct flight of gold from London? Geography and price. Cambodia runs a far more open gold regime than Vietnam — bullion trades close to the world price in Phnom Penh's markets — and it shares hundreds of kilometers of lightly policed land border. So the cheapest gold in the region sits right next to the most expensive gold in the region, separated by a jungle and a line on a map. That juxtaposition is the entire business case. The smuggler is not a global trader; they are a *local arbitrageur* exploiting a price cliff that runs right along a national boundary. The same logic explains the upstream hubs: Hong Kong, Dubai, and Singapore are the world's great physical-gold entrepots, where kilobars move freely and anonymously, so they are the wholesale source that feeds Bangkok and Phnom Penh, which in turn feed the border.

The courier economics are brutally simple and that is why they work. A "mule" might be paid a few hundred dollars per crossing to carry one or two kilograms — a rounding error against the wedge. Recall the worked example: the gross spread was about \$750 per luong, and a kilogram is roughly 26.7 luong, so one kilogram carried across the border collected on the order of **\$20,000 of gross wedge** at the 2024 premium. Pay the courier a few hundred, bribe what needs bribing, write off the occasional seizure, and the network still keeps the lion's share. When the prize per backpack is twenty thousand dollars and the bulk is a chocolate bar, you do not need a sophisticated cartel — you need a steady supply of people willing to take a walk across a border. That abundance of willing carriers is exactly why interdiction alone never closes the trade.

The disguise leg matters. Because the regulation bites hardest on *bars* (only SJC may make them), much smuggled gold is deliberately turned into rings (*nhan tron* — plain round rings) and jewelry, which trade with thinner rules. A "wedding ring" that happens to be a one-luong slug of 9999 gold is, economically, a gold bar wearing a costume. This is why the *nhan* market exploded in 2024-2025 even as the SJC-bar premium was being squeezed: the demand simply migrated to the channel the rules left open. (We dig into the practical *nhan*-versus-bar choice in the F7 post on how a Vietnamese investor should actually hold gold.)

### The scale

How big is the gray market? By its nature, smuggled volume is not published — but we can triangulate. The World Gold Council estimates Vietnamese consumer gold demand at roughly 50 tonnes per year in recent years (about 50.0 tonnes in 2024). Vietnam has essentially no domestic mine output and the legal import channel for bullion was frozen for years. So a large share of that 50 tonnes — analysts and the Vietnamese press have at times put smuggled volume in the **tens of tonnes per year** — had to arrive *somehow*, and the only somehow available was the gray market. Periodic seizures back this up: customs and police have intercepted single shipments measured in tens of kilograms, and one widely reported network in 2024 was alleged to have moved several tonnes worth of gold across the Cambodian border.

There is a simple mass-balance argument that pins the scale even without trusting any single estimate. Vietnam consumes roughly 50 tonnes of gold a year. Where can that gold come from? Only three places: domestic mining (essentially zero — Vietnam has negligible gold production), legal imports (frozen for bullion under Decree 24 for years, with only limited, controlled flows), and recycling of gold already in the country (real, but it cannot create *net new* gold — it only re-circulates the existing stock). If domestic supply and legal imports together fall far short of 50 tonnes, the gap *must* be filled by metal that entered outside the legal channel. That is not speculation; it is arithmetic. The gray market is not the marginal fringe of Vietnam's gold market — for years it was a load-bearing pillar of it, simply because the legal channel was not allowed to do its job.

Tens of tonnes per year sounds abstract. The way to feel it is to convert it into dollars leaving the country, which is the next worked example — and the reason the SBV cares.

#### Worked example: the dollar drain from smuggling one tonne a month

Suppose the gray market pulls in one tonne of gold per month — a deliberately round, conservative figure given 50 tonnes of annual demand. One tonne is one million grams; at 37.5 grams per luong, that is about **26,667 luong**. Each luong was bought abroad at the world price of about **\$2,879**. So the dollars required to source one month's gold are:

```
26,667 luong  x  $2,879 per luong  =  ~$76.8 million per month
```

That is roughly **\$77 million of dollar demand every month** — about **\$920 million a year** — created purely to feed the gold loop, dollars that must be found *outside* the banking system because the banks will not finance smuggling. To put it in dong, sold inside Vietnam at 92.4 million per luong, that one tonne fetches about **2.46 trillion dong** a month. The intuition: smuggling is not a victimless markup; at this scale it is a steady, billion-dollar-a-year siphon on the country's dollar liquidity, which is precisely why the State Bank treats the premium as a monetary problem.

## Why smugglers need dollars: the triangle in motion

We have asserted that the loop runs on dollars. It is worth slowing down on *why*, because this is the hinge that connects a gold premium to a currency's exchange rate — the insight most casual observers miss.

Gold is a globally traded commodity priced in **US dollars**. The London spot price, the COMEX futures price, the wholesale quotes in Bangkok or Dubai — all dollars. If you want to buy physical gold anywhere in the regional supply chain, you settle in dollars (or a currency instantly convertible to dollars). A smuggler cannot walk into a Phnom Penh wholesaler and pay in Vietnamese dong; the wholesaler has no use for dong they cannot easily spend. So **step 1 of the triangle is, before it is a gold transaction, a dollar transaction.** This single fact is what elevates gold smuggling from a customs nuisance to a monetary-policy headache. A smuggler of counterfeit handbags or untaxed cigarettes also evades the law, but they do not systematically drain the country's dollars — their costs are mostly local. The gold smuggler's largest cost is denominated in the one currency the central bank is fighting to conserve. That is the difference between a trade-policy problem and a *balance-of-payments* problem, and it is why gold sits in the SBV's in-tray rather than only the customs bureau's.

That is the crucial asymmetry. The smuggler *earns* dong (selling gold in Vietnam) but *spends* dollars (buying gold abroad). To keep the loop turning, they must continuously convert dong into dollars. And here Vietnam's capital controls bite: an individual or informal network cannot simply buy \$77 million a month through the banks at the official rate. They buy dollars on the **black market** — the informal FX dealers, gold shops, and money-changers who quote a dong-per-dollar rate a few percent above the SBV's official rate.

A short primer on those controls, because they are what makes the whole thing tick. Vietnam, like China and unlike a fully open economy, runs a **managed capital account**. The SBV sets a daily central reference rate for USD/VND and allows the market rate to trade only within a band around it; banks must justify large dollar purchases; and individuals face limits on converting dong to dollars for anything other than approved purposes like travel or documented imports. The intent is to keep the dong stable and to stop capital from fleeing during stress. The side effect is that whenever the demand for dollars exceeds what the controlled channel will supply at the official rate, the excess demand pools into an *informal* dollar market that clears at a higher price. The black-market USD/VND rate is, in effect, the true market-clearing price of a dollar in Vietnam, and the gap between it and the official rate is a real-time measure of how binding the controls are.

The gold loop is one of the largest, steadiest sources of that excess dollar demand. It is not the only one — over- and under-invoicing of ordinary trade, informal remittances, and tourism all move dollars informally too — but gold smuggling is distinctive in that its appetite for dollars rises *precisely when confidence in the dong is falling*, because that is also when gold demand and the premium rise. So the gold loop is a pro-cyclical amplifier of dong stress: weak dong → more gold demand → wider premium → more smuggling → more black-market dollar bid → weaker dong still. That feedback is what makes the premium such a sensitive thermometer, and it is why the SBV cannot treat gold policy and FX policy as separate files.

So every luong smuggled in generates a matching unit of black-market dollar demand. When the gold loop is running hot — premium wide, lots of metal flowing — the black-market dollar bid is strong, and the dong is being pulled weaker against the dollar through the back door. The official exchange rate and the black-market rate diverge; the SBV burns reserves or tightens to defend the dong; and the whole thing shows up in the FX statistics as "pressure on the dong" whose root cause is, partly, a gold premium nobody connected to the currency.

#### Worked example: the gray-versus-official gap on a five-luong purchase

Put yourself in the buyer's seat for a moment, because the gray market exists to serve them. A family buying five luong for a wedding faces three prices. At the official SJC quote of 92.4 million dong per luong, five luong cost **462 million VND (~\$18,153)**. At the gray-market price of about 88 million dong per luong, the same five luong cost **440 million VND (~\$17,289)** — a saving of **22 million VND (~\$864)** for choosing the unstamped channel. And the gray price still sits about 14 million dong per luong over the world-equivalent price of ~73.9 million, so on five luong the buyer is paying roughly **70.5 million VND (~\$2,770)** more than a London buyer would for the same gold:

```
5 luong, official SJC    462.0 million VND   (~$18,153)
5 luong, gray market     440.0 million VND   (~$17,289)   <- saves ~$864
5 luong, world price     369.5 million VND   (~$14,520)   <- the gray buyer overpays ~$2,770
```

The intuition: the gray market is cheaper than the monopoly but still far above the world — the buyer captures *part* of the smuggler's discount, while the smuggler keeps the rest as their wage, and both pay the "Vietnam tax" of living behind an import wall.

## The premium as an FX-stress gauge

Here is where the post earns its title. If the gold loop runs on dollars, then the SJC premium is not only a tax on gold buyers — it is a *barometer of how hard it is to get dollars in Vietnam*. Read that way, the premium becomes one of the most honest, market-priced signals of dong stress available, precisely because it is set by people putting real money at risk in the gray market rather than by any official rate.

![Dual axis chart of USD VND exchange rate and the SJC gold premium in million dong per luong from 2023 to 2026](/imgs/blogs/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam-4.png)

The chart above puts the dong's price of a dollar (USD/VND) next to the SJC premium in million dong per luong. Two things are worth reading honestly. First, the dong has weakened steadily over the period — from about 24,270 per dollar at end-2023 to about 26,300 by 2026 — a slow, persistent loss of value that is the backdrop to everything. Second, the premium did *not* simply track that trend; it spiked violently to ~18.5 million dong in early-to-mid 2024, then was crushed back to single digits after the SBV's direct-sale intervention in mid-2024 (the F4 post tells that story). So the relationship is not a clean one-for-one correlation, and you should not pretend it is. It is a *conditional* one: when the dong is under acute pressure *and* the SBV is not actively flooding the market with cheap official gold, the premium blows out, because the gold loop's dollar demand has nowhere cheap to go.

Think of it as a thermometer with a manual override. Left alone, a wide premium reads as a hot FX environment: dollars scarce, the dong leaning weaker, smugglers paying up. But the SBV can grab the thermometer and cool the *reading* by selling official gold directly — which is exactly what it did in 2024, pulling the premium from ~25% to single digits without the underlying dollar pressure fully going away. That is why the premium is a better signal in the periods *between* interventions, and why a premium that reappears *despite* intervention is the loudest dong-stress alarm of all.

![Thermometer diagram showing the SJC premium mapped to calm watch and stress bands of dong pressure](/imgs/blogs/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam-7.png)

The figure above turns this into a usable reading. A premium under about 8% (the SBV's de facto target band, roughly 4-5 million dong per luong in 2025) reads as **calm**: the dong is steady, dollars are flowing, and the gold loop is not straining. A premium of 8-15% reads as **watch**: FX pressure is building, import demand is outrunning what the official channel supplies. A premium above 15% — the 2024 blow-out hit ~25% — reads as **stress**: the dong is under heavy dollar pressure and the gray market is paying a fortune for the dollars it needs. The premium is, in effect, the price the gray market is willing to pay for the dollars it cannot get through the front door, and that price is a number you can read every day.

How does this signal stack up against the more conventional dong-stress gauges? A macro analyst watching Vietnam usually tracks three things: the gap between the official and black-market USD/VND rates, the level of the SBV's foreign reserves (falling reserves = the central bank spending dollars to defend the dong), and the interbank dong interest rate (a spike means the SBV is draining dong liquidity to make holding dong more attractive). Each is useful, but each has a flaw for an outside observer. Reserve data is published with a lag and is often opaque. The official-black-market FX gap requires access to street quotes. Interbank rates are noisy and policy-driven. The gold premium, by contrast, is **published continuously by every gold shop**, is set by people with real money at risk, and moves on the same underlying force — dollar scarcity. It is not a *replacement* for the FX gap (the two are cousins, both expressions of the shadow price of a dollar), but it is the most accessible member of the family, and it often moves *first* because the gold market is where retail Vietnamese express their dong-distrust most quickly. When you cannot see the reserves and cannot get the street FX rate, the gold shop window is the gauge you *can* read.

#### Worked example: turning a premium into an implied dong-stress reading

Suppose you see the SJC bar quoted at 92 million dong while your phone says world gold is \$2,879 per luong and USD/VND is 25,450. The world-equivalent price is \$2,879 times 25,450 equals about **73.3 million VND**. The premium is:

```
SJC quote            92.0 million VND
world-equivalent     73.3 million VND
-----------------------------------------------
premium              18.7 million VND  =  ~25% over world  =  ~$735 per luong
```

A 25% premium is deep in the **stress** band. The reading: the gray market is paying roughly \$735 per luong of "extra" to source gold it could buy for \$2,879 in Bangkok — a markup that only makes sense if dollars are genuinely hard to get and the dong is under real pressure. The intuition: you did not need an FX desk or an official statistic; the gold shop window just told you the dong was under strain, because the premium *is* the gray market's bid for scarce dollars wearing a gold costume.

## Enforcement and the cat-and-mouse

If a 19-million-dong wedge is the bait, the state's job is to make collecting it costly enough that less metal flows. Vietnam tries on two fronts, and both are games of whack-a-mole.

**On the border**, customs, the border guard, and the police interdict shipments. Seizures happen regularly — vehicles searched at crossings, mules caught at airports with gold sewn into clothing, networks rolled up after months of surveillance. But interdiction faces the arithmetic of all smuggling enforcement: you only catch a fraction, the value-to-bulk ratio is enormous, and as long as the wedge per luong dwarfs the expected cost of getting caught (probability of seizure times the loss), the trade pays. When the premium is at 25%, the wedge is so large that it can absorb a high seizure rate and *still* be profitable on the shipments that get through. Enforcement raises the smuggling cost; it does not change the fundamental incentive, which is set by the premium.

#### Worked example: the smuggler's breakeven seizure rate

Why does interdiction fail to deter when the premium is high? Put numbers on the smuggler's decision. At the 2024 premium, one luong carried across the border collected a gross wedge of about **19 million VND (~\$750)**. Suppose the smuggler's running costs — courier pay, bribes, the dollar premium on the FX leg — eat roughly 6 million VND (~\$235) per luong, leaving a net of about 13 million VND (~\$515) per luong that *gets through*. Now suppose customs seizes a fraction of shipments, and a seized luong is a total loss of its world cost of 73 million VND (~\$2,879). The trade breaks even when expected gain equals expected loss:

```
net per luong that arrives   x  (1 - seizure rate)
   must exceed
world cost lost per seizure  x  seizure rate

13M VND x (1 - s)  >  73M VND x s
13 - 13s  >  73s
s  <  13 / 86  =  ~15%
```

So at the 2024 premium the network stays profitable even if customs seizes nearly **one shipment in seven**. Real-world seizure rates are far below that. The intuition: a fat premium does not just raise the reward per luong — it raises the *seizure rate the trade can survive*, which is why border enforcement is overwhelmed precisely when it matters most, and why crushing the premium does more than any number of checkpoints.

**On the price**, the SBV attacks the bait itself. This is the smarter lever, and the one that actually worked in 2024. By auctioning gold and then selling SJC bars directly through four state banks at a price close to the world level, the SBV collapsed the premium from ~25% to single digits. A small premium is a small wedge; a small wedge is a small wage; a small wage means less metal worth smuggling. In other words, *the most effective anti-smuggling tool is not the border guard but the premium itself.* Kill the gap and you defund the couriers. (The mechanics, costs, and limits of that intervention are the F4 post's subject; the key point here is that price policy and border enforcement are substitutes for the same goal.)

The cat-and-mouse dynamic follows directly. When the premium is high, smuggling is rampant and enforcement is overwhelmed; when the SBV crushes the premium, the bar trade quiets — but demand migrates to *nhan* rings and jewelry, which are harder to regulate, so the gray market changes shape rather than disappearing. The 2024-2025 sequence is the textbook case: squeeze the SJC-bar premium, and the *nhan* market booms. The water finds the lowest gap in the wall.

There is a deeper enforcement bind here worth naming, because it is the same trap every gold-restricting country falls into. Suppose the SBV wanted to permanently kill the gray market through price policy — keep the premium near zero forever. To do that it would have to supply Vietnamese demand at world prices, which means *importing tens of tonnes of gold a year and selling them domestically at cost*. But importing tens of tonnes of gold a year spends exactly the dollars that "anti-goldization" was meant to conserve, and it re-floods the economy with the gold the policy was meant to wean it off. So the SBV faces a genuine dilemma: a high premium means smuggling and a dollar drain through the *black* market; a low premium means a dollar drain through the *official* import channel. The controls do not eliminate the dollar cost of Vietnamese gold demand — they only choose *which door* the dollars leave through. The premium is the dial that sets the mix. This is why the SBV manages the premium to a band rather than to zero: it is trying to keep the gray market small enough to be tolerable without spending so many official dollars that it defeats the purpose.

Compare India, the world's other great gold-loving nation with a history of import restrictions. When India jacked up its gold import duty to 10% in 2013 to defend its current account, smuggling exploded — by some estimates, India's gray-market gold imports surged into the hundreds of tonnes, with gold pouring across borders and through airports, often carried by couriers from Dubai. India eventually cut the duty precisely because the smuggling it spawned was undermining the policy's goals while criminalizing a huge share of the gold trade. The lesson Vietnam's policymakers know well: a restriction's *intended* effect (less gold demand, fewer dollars spent) is partly offset by its *unintended* effect (a gray market that spends dollars anyway, untaxed and uncounted). The premium is the visible scar of that trade-off.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs about Vietnam's gold gray market are common and wrong. Each one matters because it leads people to misread the premium.

**"Smuggling is a marginal, criminal-underworld thing — not big enough to matter."** The opposite is closer to the truth. With consumer demand around 50 tonnes a year, essentially no domestic mining, and a frozen legal import channel, a large share of the gold Vietnamese households bought for years simply *had* to arrive through the gray market. As the worked example showed, even a conservative one-tonne-a-month flow implies roughly **\$77 million of monthly dollar demand**, near **\$920 million a year**, sourced outside the banking system. That is not marginal; it is a material drag on the country's dollar liquidity, which is exactly why "anti-goldization" sits in the central bank's portfolio and not just the police's.

**"The gray market is just crime — there's no economic logic to it."** The gray market is the *predictable* and arguably *rational* response to a ban placed against demand that did not fall. It is not a moral failure of Vietnamese savers; it is supply finding the door that policy left open. The metal is usually real, often 9999, and the prices form a coherent ladder — world, then gray, then official — with each rung paying a specific cost (smuggling, then the monopoly markup). Treating it as pure criminality misses that the gray price contains genuine information about both gold demand and dollar scarcity.

**"The SJC premium is just a gold thing — it has nothing to do with the exchange rate."** This is the costliest misconception, and the one this whole post is built to correct. The premium is paid, ultimately, for *dollars*: the gray market's entire reason to exist is to source dollar-priced gold for dong-paying buyers, and the spread it charges rises when dollars are scarce. A premium that blows out while the SBV is *not* intervening is, more often than not, a flashing signal that the dong is under FX pressure. Watching the gold shop window is watching the currency.

#### Worked example: the gray market's full-scale dollar footprint

Scale the one-tonne-a-month figure up to a plausible gray-market share of total demand. If, say, 30 tonnes of Vietnam's ~50 tonnes of annual gold demand were met through smuggled metal in a high-premium year, the dollars required to source it are:

```
30 tonnes  =  30,000,000 grams  /  37.5 grams-per-luong  =  800,000 luong / year
800,000 luong  x  $2,879 per luong (world cost)  =  ~$2.3 billion / year
```

Roughly **\$2.3 billion of dollar demand a year**, sourced through the black market rather than the banks — against a backdrop where the SBV is simultaneously trying to defend the dong and conserve reserves. Even if you halve the assumption, you are still talking about *over a billion dollars a year* of off-the-books dollar buying tied to gold. The intuition: at this size, the gold loop is not a footnote to Vietnam's balance of payments — it is a line item large enough to move the dong, which is the whole reason the premium and the exchange rate breathe together.

## How it shows up in real markets

The cleanest case study is the one we keep returning to, because it ran the full arc in eighteen months.

![Chart of the SJC gold premium over the world price as a percentage from 2023 to 2026](/imgs/blogs/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam-2.png)

**2023 — the wedge builds.** Through 2023 the premium ran around 18% (about 12 million dong per luong). The dong was weakening as the Fed held rates high and the dollar stayed strong globally — the same DXY-versus-gold dynamic we covered in the gold-and-the-dollar post, now refracted through Vietnam's controls. Dollars were getting harder to come by, the gold loop was running, and the premium quietly widened. The chart's amber band sits well above the SBV's comfort zone all year.

**Early-to-mid 2024 — the blow-out.** As world gold surged toward records in spring 2024, Vietnamese demand spiked into a market the legal channel could not supply, and the premium exploded — to roughly 24-25%, about 18-18.5 million dong per luong, with the SJC bar hitting 92.4 million dong on 10 May 2024. This is the **stress** reading on our thermometer, and it coincided with visible dong weakness and a widening gap between the official and black-market dollar rates. The premium and the FX pressure were the same story told in two places.

**Mid-2024 onward — the intervention.** The SBV responded by selling gold directly through the four state-owned banks at near-world prices, dragging the premium down to single digits (about 6 million dong, ~8%, by September 2024, then toward 4-5 million in 2025). The premium *reading* cooled even though the dong kept drifting weaker (toward 25,450 and beyond) — the manual-override effect. The bar trade quieted; the *nhan*-ring market boomed as demand migrated. By the January 2026 world-gold spike, the SJC bar printed a record 190.3 million dong per luong, but the premium had been held to roughly 8 million dong (~4%) — a far healthier reading than 2024, evidence that managing the *gap* (not the level) is what defunds the gray market.

**The 2013 precedent.** None of this was new. When Decree 24 first took hold in 2012-2013, the SJC premium had already blown out — at times above 4-5 million dong per luong, a large gap for that price level — as the newly monopolized market struggled to supply demand. The SBV's first response was the same lever it would reach for again a decade later: gold auctions, selling official bullion to licensed members to add legal supply and pull the premium down. It partly worked, partly did not, and the gray market hummed along underneath. The 2013 episode is the dress rehearsal for 2024; the recurrence tells you this is not a one-off crisis but a *structural* feature of a walled-off gold market — the premium wants to widen whenever dollars get tight, and policy has to keep pushing it back down.

**The persistent dong drift.** Underneath every premium spike sits the slow fact the dual-axis chart showed: the dong has lost ground against the dollar year after year — roughly 24,270 per dollar at end-2023, about 25,450 by end-2024, drifting past 26,000 toward 2026. That steady devaluation is *why* Vietnamese households want gold in the first place, and it is the deep current the premium spikes ride on top of. The premium is the fast, noisy signal; the dong's grind weaker is the slow, persistent one. Read together, they tell a single story: a population hedging a currency it does not fully trust, through a market the state has walled off, paying a premium that is really a price for dollars.

The lesson across the arc: the premium told you when the dong was under stress (2023 into mid-2024), the intervention told you when policy was actively suppressing the signal (mid-2024 on), and the migration to rings told you the gray market never died — it just changed costume. A trader or saver watching only the headline gold price would have missed all three.

## The takeaway: read the premium as a confession

Step back to the series' spine. Gold is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money. In Vietnam, that vote is taken in a rigged ballot box — the import ban and the SJC monopoly distort the price — and the distortion itself becomes information. The SJC premium is not noise to be subtracted out before you find the "real" signal. The premium *is* a signal: it is the gray market's confession, every single day, about how much it is willing to pay for the dollars it cannot get through the front door.

So here is how to actually use this. Do not read the SJC bar price in isolation; read the *premium* — the SJC price minus the world-equivalent (world dollar price times USD/VND times 1.20565). Under about 8%, the dong and the dollar are calm. Pushing past 15%, the gray market is straining for dollars and the dong is probably under real pressure. And the loudest alarm of all is a premium that *re-widens despite intervention* — because that means the SBV is spending reserves to suppress a signal that keeps wanting to scream. For a Vietnamese saver, that is the moment to take the dong-devaluation risk seriously; for an observer, it is a near-free read on the currency that no official statistic will give you as honestly.

A few practical cautions follow from the same logic. First, when you buy gold in a wide-premium environment, understand that you are paying the smuggler's wage and the monopoly's markup on top of the metal — and that if the SBV later crushes the premium, the *premium portion* of your purchase price can evaporate even if world gold holds steady. You can lose money on Vietnamese gold while world gold is flat, purely because the premium compressed; that is a Vietnam-specific risk that a London buyer never faces. Second, the premium is asymmetric: it widens fast in stress and is squeezed slowly by policy, so a sudden jump is more informative than a slow drift. Third, the *nhan*-ring market is where the live signal increasingly lives, because that is where displaced bar demand went — watch the ring premium, not only the SJC-bar premium, or you will be reading last decade's gauge. The single discipline that ties it together: treat the gold shop window as an FX screen, because in a country that walls off its gold market, that is exactly what it is.

There is also a lesson here for how to think about *any* controlled market, not just Vietnam's gold. Whenever a state puts a wall around a price — a currency peg, an interest-rate cap, a rent control, an import quota — the suppressed pressure does not vanish; it leaks out somewhere you can measure, if you know where to look. The black-market FX rate, the waiting list, the quality cut, the gray import: these are not bugs in the data, they are the data. Vietnam's gold premium is a uniquely clean example because it is published daily, set by people risking real money, and tied to the single most important variable in a small open economy — the price of a dollar. The premium is the sound the wall makes when the water pushes against it.

The deeper point is the one the ban's designers may not have fully reckoned with. You cannot abolish a price; you can only relocate it. Push gold demand underground and it reappears as a dollar drain and a black-market FX bid — the very reserve-and-currency pressures the policy was meant to prevent. Tie that back to the series' spine: gold's price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money, and in Vietnam the ballot is cast through a gray market that quietly trades dong for dollars and dollars for gold. The premium is the receipt for that trade-off, printed in the gold shop window. Learn to read it, and you are reading the dong.
