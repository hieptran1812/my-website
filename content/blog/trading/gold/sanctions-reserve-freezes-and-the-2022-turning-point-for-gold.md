In the early hours of Saturday, 26 February 2022, finance ministries in Washington, Brussels, London, Ottawa and Tokyo issued a set of coordinated statements. Buried in the legalese was a sentence with more monetary consequence than any interest-rate decision of the past half-century: the central bank of the Russian Federation would be barred from accessing its reserves held in their jurisdictions. By Monday, somewhere between \$280bn and \$330bn of what Moscow thought of as its rainy-day war chest — half of a roughly \$600bn pile — had become unusable. The dollars and euros still existed as numbers in ledgers. Russia simply could not touch them.

Notice the precise nature of what happened. No tanks rolled into a vault. No gold was confiscated. No bank was robbed. A few officials in a few capitals signed documents, and a sovereign nation's savings were neutralized over a weekend. That is the entire story, and it is why this is the geopolitical keystone of the modern gold market. Every central banker who was awake that weekend understood the implication instantly, because it applied to *them too*: if it can be done to Russia, it can be done to anyone whose reserves sit inside the Western financial system. And almost everyone's do.

This post is about the *mechanics* of that event and its *second-order consequences*. We covered central-bank demand as a price driver in [Central banks: the structural buyer that changed gold after 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022), and we covered *why* gold is the only asset central banks trust over each other's money in [The apolitical asset](/blog/trading/gold/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money). Here we go to the layer underneath both: how a reserve freeze actually works, why weaponizing the dollar is a trade-off rather than a free lunch, and why gold gained a permanent new bid that no amount of falling inflation or rising real yields has been able to dislodge.

## Foundations: what FX reserves are, how they are held, and what "sanctions" actually mean

Before we can understand a freeze, we need four plain-English definitions. None of them require any finance background, but each one hides an assumption that the 2022 freeze blew up.

### What "foreign-exchange reserves" really are

A country's central bank holds a stockpile of **foreign-exchange reserves** — call them *FX reserves* — for the same reason you keep an emergency fund: to pay for imports when exports dry up, to defend the value of its own currency, and to reassure lenders that it can meet obligations in a currency the world accepts. As of the early 2020s, the world's central banks collectively held something on the order of \$12 trillion in such reserves.

Here is the first thing almost nobody outside the plumbing knows: those reserves are *not* a pile of physical cash sitting in a national basement. A reserve of "\$300bn" is not 300 billion paper dollars. It is overwhelmingly a portfolio of **foreign government bonds and bank deposits** — chiefly US Treasury securities, German Bunds, French and Japanese government bonds — plus some deposits at foreign commercial banks. In other words, a country's dollar reserves are *claims on the United States*, and its euro reserves are *claims on European institutions*.

A claim is an IOU. When Russia "held \$300bn of dollar reserves," what it actually held was a stack of promises from the US Treasury and from banks operating under US and European law to pay it dollars and euros on demand. The reserves were entries in *foreign* ledgers, governed by *foreign* law, and reachable only through *foreign* infrastructure. That distinction is the whole game.

### Where the reserves physically live

Trace a single reserve dollar and you find it sitting at one of three kinds of institution, none of which the holder controls:

- **A custodian.** A US Treasury bond owned by a foreign central bank is held in custody — usually at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, which holds bonds and even physical gold for dozens of foreign governments, or at a securities depository like **Euroclear** in Belgium for euro-denominated bonds. The custodian is the legal record-keeper of who owns what.
- **A correspondent bank.** To actually *spend* dollars, a foreign central bank needs a dollar account at a bank with access to the US payment system. That account is a **correspondent banking** relationship: a big Western bank holds the cash and moves it on instruction.
- **The messaging layer.** To instruct a payment across borders, banks send standardized messages over **SWIFT** (the Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication), a Belgium-based cooperative that carries the instructions — "pay \$10m from account A to account B" — that make cross-border money move. SWIFT does not hold money; it carries the orders. But if your messages stop being delivered, your money cannot move even if it exists.

So a reserve sits on a foreign custodian's books, is spent through a foreign correspondent bank, and is moved by instructions over a foreign messaging network. Every one of those three is a *choke point*. Whoever has legal authority over the custodian, the bank, or the network can stand between a country and its own savings.

It helps to dwell on *why* the system is built this way, because the choke points are not accidents — they are the price of joining the global money system. A country that wants its currency to be convertible, its trade to settle smoothly, and its bonds to be held abroad *must* plug into this shared infrastructure. There is no way to hold a large dollar reserve that does *not* route through a US-supervised custodian or correspondent bank, because dollars only exist as entries on the books of banks the Federal Reserve oversees. The dollar is not a thing you can take home; it is a position in a ledger that the US ultimately backstops and supervises. To hold dollars *is* to accept a relationship with the institutions that maintain the dollar. That relationship is enormously convenient ninety-nine years out of a hundred — and it is the exact wire that a freeze trips on the hundredth.

The same is true of the messaging layer. SWIFT carries roughly 45 million financial messages a day among more than 11,000 institutions in over 200 countries. It is, in effect, the nervous system of cross-border finance — and like a nervous system, severing it does not destroy the body's organs (the money still exists) but it paralyzes them (the money cannot move). When people say a country was "cut off from SWIFT," they mean its banks can no longer send or receive the standardized instructions that make an international payment happen. The cash in the correspondent account is intact; the *order* to move it can no longer be delivered to a counterparty willing to act on it.

### What "sanctions" mean, mechanically

In ordinary speech, "sanctions" sounds like a punishment — a fine, a penalty. Mechanically, financial sanctions are something more surgical and more powerful: a **legal order that forbids institutions under your jurisdiction from doing business with a named party.** The US Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC), the EU, and the UK can each issue such orders. When they do, every bank, custodian, and clearing house in their reach is legally required to *block* the named party's assets and refuse to process its transactions, on pain of enormous fines or losing access to the dollar system themselves.

The crucial subtlety: sanctions do not take your money. They *immobilize* it. A frozen asset still belongs, on paper, to its owner. It just cannot be moved, spent, or converted. It is a bank balance you can read but never withdraw — and because the people enforcing the freeze are the very institutions holding your money, there is no counter to it.

### Why this is a *monetary* event, not just a political one

Put those four definitions together and you arrive at the uncomfortable truth that February 2022 made undeniable. A dollar reserve is a promise, the promise lives in someone else's house, and the owner of the house can refuse to honor it. As long as everyone *expected* the promise to be honored regardless of politics, dollar reserves behaved like money — fungible, reliable, "yours." The freeze proved that the promise is conditional on political alignment. The instant a reserve's usefulness depends on staying in someone's good graces, it stops being *money you own* and becomes *permission you've been granted.*

That is the spine of this entire gold series, stated in its sharpest form: gold is a monetary insurance policy whose price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money and political stability. In 2022 the no-confidence vote got a specific, dated trigger.

### The one property that makes gold different: it is no one's liability

To see why gold sits outside this entire structure, it helps to define a piece of vocabulary that quietly runs all of finance: **liability**. A liability is a promise to pay — someone else's obligation that you are counting on. Almost every financial asset you can name is, on the other side of the ledger, somebody's liability. A bank deposit is the bank's liability (it owes you the money). A Treasury bond is the government's liability (it owes you the coupons and principal). A corporate bond is the company's liability. Cash itself is the central bank's liability — a dollar bill is, literally, a "Federal Reserve note," a note being an IOU. Your "money," in nearly every form, is a chain of promises, and the value of each link depends on someone choosing to honor it.

Gold is the conspicuous exception. A bar of gold is not anyone's promise. There is no issuer standing behind it who could default, inflate it away, or refuse to honor it. It does not appear as a liability on any balance sheet on earth. Its value is intrinsic to the metal, not contingent on a counterparty's solvency or goodwill. This is what people mean by the phrase "gold has no counterparty risk," and it sounds like dry accounting until you connect it to the freeze. **A reserve freeze is a counterparty refusing to honor its liability.** The Fed, Euroclear, and the correspondent banks are the counterparties to a country's dollar and euro reserves; freezing those reserves is precisely those counterparties being ordered to stop honoring their obligations. An asset that is *no one's liability* has *no counterparty who could be ordered to stop.* The freeze did not just make gold look attractive — it revealed that gold's defining technical property is the *exact* property that immunizes it against the thing that just happened to Russia. We unpack this idea conceptually in [the apolitical asset](/blog/trading/gold/the-apolitical-asset-why-central-banks-trust-gold-over-each-others-money); here it is the load-bearing fact of the whole post.

#### Worked example: how much "money" is really a foreign IOU

Suppose Country X holds \$300bn of reserves, split the typical way: \$210bn in US Treasury bonds at the Fed, \$60bn in euro-area government bonds at Euroclear, and \$30bn in deposits at Western correspondent banks. How much of that \$300bn is in Country X's physical possession or under its own legal control? **Zero.** Every cent is a claim on a foreign institution, recorded in a foreign ledger, enforceable only through foreign courts and infrastructure. The \$300bn is real — but it is real *the way a gift card is real*: spendable only at one store, and only while that store chooses to honor it. The day the store says "blocked," the balance becomes a number you can see and nothing more.

## The 2022 freeze, step by step

Let us walk the freeze in slow motion, because the *speed* and *bloodlessness* of it are exactly what shocked the official sector.

**Friday, 25 February 2022.** Russia's reserves stand at roughly \$630bn — built up deliberately over years as a sanctions buffer after the 2014 Crimea episode. Moscow had even diversified: a chunk in gold, a chunk in Chinese yuan, the rest in dollars, euros, pounds, and yen held in the usual Western institutions. The central bank believed it had a fortress.

**Saturday-Sunday, 26-27 February.** The US, EU, UK, Canada, Japan and Switzerland announce coordinated measures. The key one is not the headline-grabbing "remove banks from SWIFT" (that came too, for selected commercial banks). It is the immobilization of the **central bank's own reserves**. Any reserve asset held in those jurisdictions — the Treasuries at the Fed, the Bunds at Euroclear, the deposits at correspondent banks — is blocked. Custodians are legally barred from acting on Russian instructions. The messages, even if sent, would hit institutions forbidden to honor them.

**Monday, 28 February.** Markets open. Russia's central bank discovers, in real time, that roughly half its fortress — the portion held in the freezing jurisdictions, around \$300bn — is inert. It still owns the bonds in a paper sense. It cannot sell them, cannot collect the coupons in usable form, cannot convert them to spendable cash, cannot use them to defend the ruble. The ruble collapses 30% in days; the central bank jacks its policy rate to 20% and imposes capital controls because the tool it built precisely for this moment has been disabled.

The figure below shows the three channels through which that disabling happened — and why blocking the *sanctioning order* at the top cascades down all three at once.

What is the precedent here? Freezing the reserves of a *commercial* entity, or even a smaller state, had happened before — Iran in 1979 and again post-2012, Afghanistan's central bank in 2021, Venezuela. But Russia was a **G20 economy, the eleventh-largest in the world, a permanent member of the UN Security Council, a nuclear power, and a major commodity exporter.** If *that* country's reserves could be neutralized over a weekend, the implicit assurance that the reserve system was politically neutral was dead. The size and status of the target is precisely what generalized the lesson.

#### Worked example: the ~\$300bn freeze in concrete terms

Convert \$300bn into something tangible. At the early-2022 gold price of roughly \$1,800 per troy ounce, \$300bn buys about **166 million troy ounces**, which is about **5,180 tonnes** of gold. For scale, that is more than twice China's *entire* official gold reserve and roughly two-thirds of the United States' 8,133-tonne hoard. In one weekend, the equivalent of *two China-sized national gold reserves* was rendered useless by signature. A central banker doing this arithmetic in March 2022 reached one conclusion: the only fraction of Russia's reserves that *survived* the freeze untouched was the gold held inside Russia's own borders. The lesson writes itself.

### What actually survived, and why it matters

It is worth being precise about *which* parts of Russia's roughly \$630bn fortress survived, because the surviving parts are a map of where un-freezable value lives. Three categories came through:

- **Gold held domestically.** Russia had spent the years after 2014 deliberately building its gold reserve to over 2,300 tonnes, and crucially it held the metal *inside Russia*, at its own central bank. No custodian abroad could be ordered to block it; no SWIFT message was needed to "use" it; it simply sat on Russian soil under Russian control. This portion was completely untouched by the freeze.
- **Chinese yuan reserves.** Russia had also shifted a meaningful share into yuan held in the Chinese system. China did not join the freeze, so these were not blocked by it — though, as we will see, holding them meant trusting Beijing instead of Washington, merely swapping one counterparty for another.
- **Reserves in non-freezing jurisdictions** more generally — a small remainder.

Everything held in the freezing jurisdictions — the dollars, euros, pounds, and yen at Western custodians and banks, roughly \$300bn — was inert. So the single asset that performed its reserve function *perfectly* in the exact scenario reserves exist for was the gold in the home vault. That is not a theoretical argument for gold; it is the recorded result of the largest real-world stress test a reserve portfolio has ever faced. Every reserve manager on earth now has a worked example, run on a peer, of which assets work when the freeze comes.

## A short history of weaponized money: the freeze was the climax, not the start

The 2022 freeze felt like a thunderbolt, but the storm had been building for a century. Understanding the lineage matters, because it shows that reserve weaponization is not a one-off aberration a country can dismiss — it is a *recurring tool* of statecraft that has only grown more powerful as the dollar system has grown more central. The arc runs roughly like this.

**The early 20th century: blocking enemy assets in wartime.** Freezing the assets of an adversary is an old idea — combatants have immobilized each other's holdings in major wars for over a hundred years. But in that era money was still tied to gold and trade was less financialized, so the reach was limited. You could seize what was physically in your territory; you could not reach into the global plumbing because the global plumbing barely existed.

**1944-1971: Bretton Woods builds the wires.** After World War II, the dollar became the anchor of the international monetary system, convertible to gold and held as the primary reserve by nearly everyone. The institutional plumbing — correspondent banking at scale, then SWIFT (founded 1973) — grew up around dollar dominance. We trace this monetary architecture's birth and death in the companion post on the gold standard; the point here is that this era *built the choke points* a future freeze would exploit. The more central the dollar became, the more powerful any future ability to deny access to it would be.

**1979: Iran and the modern template.** When revolutionaries seized the US embassy in Tehran in November 1979, Washington froze roughly \$12bn of Iranian government assets held in US institutions and their overseas branches. This was the first *modern* large-scale use of financial infrastructure — not battlefield seizure — to immobilize a state's money. It established the template: a legal order, executed through the institutions that hold the money, freezing assets that legally still belong to the target. Iran's long-run response — accumulating gold, trading outside the dollar where possible — was the first run of the playbook that 2022 would make universal.

**Post-2001 and post-2012: sanctions become a primary instrument.** After 9/11, the US dramatically expanded the use of financial sanctions as a tool of foreign policy, leaning on the dollar's central role to enforce them globally through "secondary sanctions" — penalties on *third parties* who deal with a sanctioned entity. By the 2010s, being cut off from the dollar system was a near-existential threat to any bank or company, which made US sanctions enforceable far beyond US borders. The 2012 round against Iran cut major Iranian banks from SWIFT and froze tens of billions more. The weapon was now routine, and its reach was global precisely because the dollar's reach was global.

**2021: Afghanistan rehearses the central-bank freeze.** When the Afghan government fell in August 2021, the US froze roughly \$7bn of the Afghan central bank's reserves at the New York Fed. This was a freeze of a *central bank's* reserves — the same category as Russia's — just six months before the main event, and against one of the poorest countries on earth. It drew far less attention, but it was the dress rehearsal.

**February 2022: the climax.** The freeze of roughly \$300bn of a G20 commodity superpower's reserves was not a new *kind* of action. It was the existing tool applied at a scale and against a target that made its implications impossible for any central bank to dismiss as "that could never be me." The history matters because it forecloses the comforting thought that 2022 was a fluke. It was the logical endpoint of a hundred-year trend of money becoming more weaponizable as it became more networked — and there is no reason to expect the trend to reverse. That is exactly why the gold response is structural rather than a panic that fades.

## Why it changed every central bank's calculus

A central bank reserve manager has a job that sounds boring and is, in fact, one of the most consequential in finance: keep the nation's savings *safe and available*. For decades, "safe" meant "in US Treasuries" — the deepest, most liquid, most default-proof market on earth. The entire profession was built on the premise that the *credit risk* of the US (will they pay?) was negligible, so the only real questions were yield and liquidity.

The freeze introduced a risk the textbooks barely mentioned: **political risk on the reserve itself.** Not "will the US default?" but "will the US *let me use* my own reserves if my politics turn unfriendly?" For a Western-aligned country, the answer is "of course" and the question feels paranoid. But reserve managers are paid to think about tail risks, and a huge share of the world is *not* securely Western-aligned. China, Saudi Arabia, India, Turkey, Brazil, the Gulf states, much of Africa and Southeast Asia — none of them can be certain their relationship with Washington and Brussels will look the same in ten or twenty years as it does today. And reserves are held for exactly those long horizons.

So after February 2022, the reserve manager's mental model shifted from a two-variable problem (yield, liquidity) to a three-variable one (yield, liquidity, **freezability**). And there is exactly one large, liquid reserve asset whose freezability is *zero*: physical gold held in your own vault, on your own soil. It has no issuer to receive a sanctions order. It has no custodian abroad to be told to block it. It does not move over SWIFT. It is, uniquely, **no one's liability.**

This is the hinge of the whole post. Gold's defining weirdness — that it pays no interest, that it is "just a rock," that it is "no one's promise" — had always been described as a *weakness* by mainstream finance. A Treasury bond pays you 4%; gold pays you nothing. Why hold the rock? The freeze flipped the framing. The very fact that gold is *no one's promise* means there is *no one who can break the promise.* The property that looked like a bug became the most valuable feature a reserve can have for a country that fears the freeze.

It is worth naming the precise mental error the old framing made. Conventional reserve management treated the *probability* of a freeze as effectively zero, so it dropped out of the calculation entirely, leaving only yield and liquidity. That was reasonable when no major economy had ever had its reserves frozen. But "I have never seen it happen" is not the same as "it cannot happen," and the difference between those two statements is exactly the difference between pre-2022 and post-2022 reserve management. Once the probability is *visibly nonzero* — once you have watched it happen to a peer — it re-enters the calculation with enormous weight, because the *size* of the loss in the freeze scenario is total. A risk that is small in probability but catastrophic and correlated-with-the-worst-moment in magnitude is exactly the kind of risk insurance exists to cover. The reserve managers did not become irrational gold bugs in 2022; they became *correctly* attentive to a tail they had previously, and understandably, ignored.

There is also a subtler shift in *who* the freeze frightened. You might expect only adversaries of the West to worry. But reserve managers in *neutral* and even *allied* countries took note, for a simple reason: alliances are not permanent, and reserves are held for decades. A country that is friendly with Washington today cannot be certain it will be in 2045, and the freeze proved that the cost of a future falling-out could be the instantaneous loss of its national savings. So the prudent move, even for a friend, is to hold *some* portion of reserves in the asset that survives a falling-out — to buy a little insurance against a future you cannot predict. This is why the post-2022 buying is broad rather than confined to obvious adversaries, and it is the single most important tell that the new bid is structural: when even friends hedge the abstract risk, the *property* is doing the work, not the politics of any one country.

#### Worked example: the incentive math for a non-aligned central bank

Put yourself in the seat of a reserve manager at a large non-aligned country with \$100bn of reserves you are considering holding in US Treasuries. The carry trade looks great: at a 4% Treasury yield, that \$100bn earns you **\$4bn a year**, while gold earns you exactly **\$0**. On a pure income basis, gold costs you \$4bn a year in forgone interest. That is the *visible* cost, and for fifty years it dominated the decision.

Now price the tail. Suppose you assign even a *1% annual probability* that a future geopolitical rupture freezes that \$100bn. The expected loss is 1% × \$100bn = **\$1bn a year** in *expected* terms — but that understates it badly, because the loss is not a smooth 1%; it is a 1% chance of losing *the entire \$100bn at once*, at the precise moment you need it most (a crisis). A reserve exists to be your liquidity in a crisis. An asset that vanishes exactly when crises hit has *negative* insurance value, no matter its yield. Suddenly the \$4bn of forgone interest looks like a cheap premium for an asset that *cannot* be switched off. That is the trade the entire non-aligned official sector started making in 2022, and it is why the gold bid became structural rather than cyclical.

## The gold response: the asset you can't freeze

The response was not a slogan; it was tonnage. In 2022 — the freeze year — central banks bought a net **1,082 tonnes** of gold, the highest annual figure since 1950 and the highest in the modern data era. To feel how anomalous that is: across 2010-2021, central banks averaged about **473 tonnes** of net purchases a year. The 2022 number more than *doubled* that pace. And it did not revert: 2023 came in at **1,037 t**, 2024 at **1,045 t**, and 2025 still ran hot at **863 t**. Four consecutive years near or above the four-figure mark, after a decade averaging under 500. That is a regime change, not a blip.

![Central banks net gold purchases by year 1999 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold-2.png)

Look at the left side of that chart and you see the *opposite* behavior in living memory. Through the late 1990s and 2000s, central banks were net *sellers* — dumping hundreds of tonnes a year under the Central Bank Gold Agreement, treating gold as a barbarous relic to be offloaded for yield-bearing bonds. The most infamous episode, the UK's sale of half its gold at the 1999-2002 bottom (the so-called "Brown's Bottom"), captured the mood: gold was the dead asset, and modern reserve management meant holding the *promises* — the bonds — instead. The freeze is the bookend to that era. The official sector spent the 2000s selling the un-freezable asset to buy more of the freezable one, and the 2020s reversing the trade as fast as the physical market will let it.

Critically, this buying is *price-insensitive in the way insurance always is.* You do not shop for the cheapest fire insurance the week your neighbor's house burns down; you buy it because the risk just became vivid. Central banks kept buying gold straight through 2023 and 2024 even as the price set record after record — because they were not trading the price, they were buying the *property*. This matters for anyone trying to value gold: a buyer who is indifferent to price and motivated by a structural fear is a different animal from a speculator chasing momentum. It puts a floor under the market that real yields and the dollar — gold's traditional masters, which we cover in [real interest rates: the master variable](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price) — cannot fully explain.

#### Worked example: what 1,082 tonnes is worth, and why the flow is enormous relative to supply

Convert the 2022 purchase to dollars. One tonne of gold is 32,150.7 troy ounces, so 1,082 tonnes is about **34.8 million troy ounces**. At the 2022 average price of roughly \$1,800/oz, that is about **\$63bn** of gold absorbed by the official sector in a single year (about \$83bn if valued at the 2024 average of \$2,388). Now compare to *supply*: total global mine production runs about **3,661 tonnes a year**. Central banks at 1,082 t were therefore vacuuming up nearly **30% of all newly mined gold on the planet** — on top of jewelry, technology, and private investment demand all competing for the same physical metal. The above-ground stock barely grows (we cover that in [the supply side](/blog/trading/gold/where-gold-comes-from-mining-refining-recycling-and-the-above-ground-stock)), so a buyer this size, this persistent, and this price-indifferent does not just move the market — it *re-rates* it. The intuition: when the buyer of last resort becomes a buyer of *first* resort, the price has to find a new, higher equilibrium.

## The weaponized-dollar dilemma

Here is the part that makes the freeze genuinely double-edged, and the part the most thoughtful officials inside the US Treasury worried about even as they pulled the trigger. The dollar can be used as a weapon *only because* it is the world's dominant reserve and settlement currency. The reach of US sanctions — the ability to freeze reserves, cut SWIFT access, and threaten secondary penalties on anyone who deals with the target — flows directly from the fact that almost all global trade and finance routes through dollars and dollar infrastructure. The weapon's power *is* the dollar's dominance.

But every time you fire the weapon, you give every potential future target a reason to reduce its exposure to the thing that made the weapon work. Freeze one country's reserves and you have solved one geopolitical problem today; you have also handed the other forty non-aligned countries a permanent, rational incentive to hold fewer dollars, build alternative payment rails, and stockpile the un-freezable asset. The dominance that powers the weapon is, slowly, *spent* by using it. Economists call this a self-undermining equilibrium; the diagram below shows the loop.

![The weaponized dollar dilemma feedback loop](/imgs/blogs/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold-5.png)

This is not a prediction that the dollar will collapse — it will not, and the bear case for gold rests partly on how slow and incomplete this process is. It is a description of a *direction*. The dollar's share of disclosed global FX reserves has been grinding lower for two decades, from about **71.5%** in 2001 to roughly **57.8%** by 2024 (IMF COFER data). The freeze did not start that decline, but it gave it a rationale and an accelerant. And — this is the key linkage — the reserves leaving the dollar do not all flow into the euro or the yen, which carry the *same* freezability risk because they live in the same Western system. A meaningful share flows into the one asset that escapes the entire dilemma.

![USD share of global reserves declining 2001 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold-3.png)

Notice what the chart does *not* show: a cliff. The dollar is not being abandoned; it is being *trimmed*, deliberately and slowly, by managers who still need its liquidity for day-to-day operations but no longer trust it as their *whole* rainy-day fund. That is the realistic shape of de-dollarization — not a regime collapse but a gradual rebalancing in which gold is the principal beneficiary precisely because it sidesteps the political risk that the euro, the yen, and the pound all share. We treat the broader monetary-order question in [the dollar system: why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy); the point here is narrower and sharper. The freeze converted a vague, decades-long drift into a *conscious risk-management decision*, and conscious decisions move faster than drift.

#### Worked example: the share-shift math when a central bank moves to gold

Take a single mid-sized central bank with \$500bn of reserves that decides, post-freeze, to lift gold from a token holding to 5% of its reserves. Five percent of \$500bn is **\$25bn**. At \$1,800/oz that is about **432 tonnes** of physical gold this *one* bank must buy. Set that against annual mine supply of 3,661 tonnes and you see that a single mid-sized reserve manager rebalancing toward gold demands nearly **12% of a full year's global mine output** all by itself. Now multiply by a dozen non-aligned banks all reaching the same conclusion in the same few years, and the 1,082-tonne official-sector total stops looking surprising and starts looking *inevitable*. The intuition: the gold market is tiny relative to the bond market, so even a small percentage reallocation by the official sector swamps available supply and forces the price up until private holders are bid out of the metal.

## Second-order effects: CBDCs, alternative rails, and the limits

If you are a country that just watched a peer's reserves get switched off, gold is the *defensive* move — the thing you can do unilaterally, today, with no one's permission. But gold has limits as a working reserve: you cannot easily pay for a shipment of wheat or microchips with bullion bars, and gold earns no yield. So the freeze also lit a fire under the search for *transactional* alternatives to the dollar system. Three of them get the most attention, and each has a fatal flaw that, by contrast, makes gold's role clearer.

**Central-bank digital currencies (CBDCs).** A CBDC is a digital form of a country's own money, issued directly by its central bank. China's e-CNY is the most advanced large-economy example, and projects like mBridge aim to let central banks settle cross-border payments among themselves without touching the dollar system. The appeal is obvious: a payment rail that no Western institution sits on cannot be cut by a Western order. But a CBDC is still a *state's liability* — it is the issuer's promise, recorded on the issuer's ledger, and therefore *the issuer* can freeze, reverse, or program it. It removes the *Western* counterparty only to substitute *another* one. It solves the SWIFT problem, not the trust problem.

**The Chinese yuan (renminbi).** The natural candidate to take dollar share, China has the world's second-largest economy and is pushing yuan invoicing and its own cross-border payment system, CIPS. But the yuan sits at only about 2.2% of global reserves, China runs capital controls that make the currency hard to move freely, and — decisively — holding yuan reserves means trusting *Beijing* not to freeze *you*. For a country worried about reserve weaponization on principle, swapping dependence on Washington for dependence on Beijing is a lateral move, not an escape.

**Barter and commodity settlement.** Some bilateral trade has shifted to direct goods-for-goods or local-currency arrangements (oil for goods, etc.). This genuinely routes around the dollar, but barter is clunky, illiquid, hard to value, and useless as a *store* of value between transactions. It works at the margin for specific deals; it cannot be a country's savings.

Lay these against gold on the four tests a reserve must actually pass and the picture is stark.

![Menu of sanction proof reserve alternatives compared](/imgs/blogs/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold-7.png)

Gold is the *only* option that passes all four: it is sanction-proof (no issuer to order a freeze), it has no counterparty (it is no one's liability), it is deeply liquid (gold trades on the order of \$200bn a day, the deepest of any hard asset), and it is accepted everywhere with a 5,000-year track record. Every alternative fails at least one test. The yuan fails the counterparty and acceptance tests; CBDCs fail counterparty; barter fails liquidity and store-of-value. This is *why* the de-dollarization money disproportionately found gold rather than a rival currency or a clever digital scheme: the alternatives solve the *plumbing* problem (how to move money without the West) while leaving the *trust* problem (whose promise are you holding?) untouched. Gold is the only asset that solves both at once.

But — and a thoughtful reader must hold this — gold's role is bounded. It is the perfect *store* of weaponization-proof value and a terrible *medium* of daily international payment. No central bank can run its trade settlement on bullion. So the realistic post-2022 architecture is not "gold replaces the dollar." It is "gold becomes the un-freezable *anchor* of reserves, while messy, partial, slowly-improving alternative rails handle the transactions the dollar used to monopolize." Gold is the foundation, not the whole house.

### The location problem: even gold has a "last mile"

There is one more wrinkle the freeze exposed, and it sharpens the lesson rather than weakening it. Gold is only fully un-freezable if you hold it *yourself, on your own soil.* A great deal of central-bank gold is, for historical and practical reasons, stored *abroad* — much of it at the Bank of England in London or at the New York Fed, which have long served as trusted vaults and trading hubs because selling or lending gold is easier where the market is. But gold held in a foreign vault carries a thin echo of the freeze risk: in an extreme scenario, the host country could restrict access to it, just as it can restrict access to bonds. The metal is no one's *liability*, but its *location* can still be a choke point if it sits in someone else's vault.

This is why one of the clearest tells of the post-2022 shift is logistical, not rhetorical: several central banks have moved to **repatriate** their gold — physically shipping bullion home from London and New York to vaults on their own territory. When a central bank pays the considerable cost and assumes the considerable risk of transporting hundreds of tonnes of metal across the world, it is making a statement that words could not: it wants its gold beyond *any* foreign jurisdiction's reach. The repatriation trend is the freezability lesson applied to the last mile of the supply chain. It tells you the official sector is not merely buying gold as a financial bet; it is rearranging the *physical custody* of the metal specifically to defeat the scenario that 2022 demonstrated. You do not move physical metal across oceans for a trade you intend to flip. You do it for an asset you intend to *keep*, no matter what, for a very long time.

### Why the alternatives keep failing the same test

Step back and notice the common thread running through CBDCs, the yuan, and barter. Each one attacks the *plumbing* — the SWIFT-and-correspondent-bank layer the West controls — and each one is making real, if slow, progress there. China's CIPS handles a growing volume of yuan payments; cross-border CBDC pilots are live; bilateral local-currency trade deals proliferate. If the only problem were "how do we move money without touching Western infrastructure," these would eventually solve it. But that was never the deepest problem. The deepest problem the freeze exposed was *trust*: whose promise are you holding, and can they break it? A new payment rail still moves *someone's liability*; it just changes whose. The yuan is Beijing's promise; a CBDC is its issuer's promise; even a local-currency trade deal leaves you holding a partner's currency, which is that partner's central bank's liability. The plumbing innovations relocate the counterparty risk; they do not eliminate it. Gold eliminates it, because there is no counterparty to relocate. That is why, four years into the post-freeze world, the de-dollarization money has flowed disproportionately into the oldest asset rather than the newest technology. The newest technology solves last decade's problem; the oldest asset solves the problem the freeze actually revealed.

## Common misconceptions

**"The freeze *seized* Russia's reserves."** No — and the distinction is the entire mechanism. The reserves were *frozen*, meaning immobilized; legally they still belonged to Russia. (As of the mid-2020s, the harder step of *confiscating* the frozen reserves to fund Ukraine remained legally and politically fraught precisely because seizing a sovereign's property crosses a line freezing does not.) The reason this matters for gold: a freeze proves the asset can be made *unusable* without due process, which is exactly the property a reserve must never have. You do not need outright theft to destroy a reserve's value — disabling it is enough.

**"This was unprecedented."** The *target's size* was unprecedented; the *tool* was not. The US froze Iranian assets in 1979 (the \$12bn Tehran embassy episode), again after 2012, Afghanistan's roughly \$7bn in 2021, and Venezuela's. What changed in 2022 was scope and scale: a coordinated G7 freeze of a G20 commodity superpower's reserves. The precedent existed; 2022 generalized it to "anyone."

**"Gold buying is just Russia and China; ignore it."** The data says otherwise. The post-2022 buyers are a *broad* coalition of the non-aligned: Turkey, India, Poland, Singapore, the Czech Republic, the Gulf states, and many emerging-market banks that report irregularly. China and Russia are visible buyers, but the breadth — and the buying by US-aligned countries like Poland and Singapore hedging the *abstract* risk — is what makes the bid structural. When even allies hedge freezability, the property, not the politics, is doing the work.

![Official gold reserves by country highlighting diversifiers](/imgs/blogs/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold-6.png)

**"De-dollarization means the dollar is about to collapse."** Almost certainly not, and confusing the two ruins your judgment about gold. The dollar's reserve share is *grinding* lower (71.5% → 57.8% over two decades), not crashing. The dollar remains dominant in trade invoicing, FX trading, and global debt. The realistic claim is narrow: at the *margin*, reserves are diversifying, and gold is the prime beneficiary because it alone escapes the freezability that every rival currency shares. Gold can have a permanent new bid *without* the dollar dying — those are independent facts.

**"Gold's price gain is all about real yields and the dollar."** This was true for decades and is the framework we lean on in [real interest rates: the master variable](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price). But after 2022 it broke: gold rose through 2022-2024 *even as* real yields climbed to multi-year highs (the 10-year TIPS real yield went from -1% to over +2%), a combination that "should" have crushed gold under the old model. The missing variable is the freeze-driven structural official-sector bid. When the textbook relationship decouples, look for a new buyer — and there it was, in the tonnage.

## How it shows up in real markets

The cleanest way to *see* the freeze's effect is to line up the precedents and the response.

**Iran, 1979 and post-2012.** The original modern reserve freeze. In November 1979, the US froze roughly \$12bn of Iranian assets during the embassy crisis; decades later, post-2012 sanctions immobilized tens of billions more and cut major Iranian banks from SWIFT. Iran's response over the long run was exactly the playbook 2022 would generalize: build gold reserves, trade in gold and barter where possible, and route around the dollar. Iran was the proof-of-concept; it was small enough to ignore. Russia made the same lesson impossible to ignore.

**Afghanistan, 2021.** When the Taliban took Kabul in August 2021, the US froze roughly \$7bn of the Afghan central bank's reserves held at the New York Fed. Overnight, a desperately poor country lost access to its own savings — and crucially, the gold component of Afghanistan's reserves held *abroad* was caught in the same net, while any reserves held domestically were not. Six months before the Russia freeze, Afghanistan was a quiet rehearsal of the exact mechanic.

**Russia, February 2022.** The keystone. Roughly \$300bn frozen, the largest reserve immobilization in history by an order of magnitude, against a G20 commodity power. The lesson — *if it can happen to them, it can happen to me* — propagated through every reserve-management committee on earth within weeks.

**The response, 2022-2025.** The tonnage already covered: 1,082 t (2022), 1,037 t (2023), 1,045 t (2024), 863 t (2025) — four years roughly double the prior decade's pace. And the *price* did what a price does when a giant, price-indifferent, structurally-motivated buyer enters a supply-constrained market: it broke its old models and ran. Gold rose from around \$1,800 at the time of the freeze toward record highs above \$5,000 by early 2026, climbing *through* rising real yields, a strong dollar, and every "gold should be falling now" macro signal — because the marginal buyer had changed.

**The repatriation tell.** A quieter signal confirms the motive. After 2022, several central banks accelerated *bringing their gold home* — moving bullion out of foreign vaults (the Bank of England, the New York Fed) and onto their own soil. Gold held abroad still carries a thin version of the freeze risk (the host could, in extremis, restrict access). Gold on your own soil does not. When you see central banks paying to repatriate metal they already own, you are watching the freezability lesson applied to the last mile. It is the official sector telling you, with logistics rather than words, that the 2022 lesson stuck.

**The breadth tell.** Cross-reference the buying with *who* is doing it and the structural read gets stronger. The post-2022 list is not a short roster of obvious adversaries. India lifted its reserves toward 880 tonnes; Turkey toward 595; Poland — a NATO member and staunch US ally — toward 448 and rising, explicitly framing the buying as long-term security; Singapore, the Czech Republic, and a long tail of emerging-market banks added too. When a NATO member and a Western financial hub are buying the un-freezable asset alongside Russia and China, the only coherent explanation is that they are hedging the *abstract* property of freezability rather than any specific quarrel with Washington. That is the difference between a fad and a regime: a fad is one type of buyer chasing a story; a regime is *every* type of buyer responding to a structural change in the rules.

#### Worked example: is repatriating gold worth the cost?

Make the repatriation decision concrete. Say a central bank holds 500 tonnes of gold at the Bank of England and considers shipping it home. The direct costs are real but modest relative to the value: secure transport, insurance, and new domestic vault capacity for 500 tonnes might run into the low tens of millions of dollars — call it a one-time cost on the order of 0.1-0.3% of the gold's value (500 t is roughly \$40bn at \$2,500/oz, so even \$80m of cost is about 0.2%). Against that one-time 0.2% you are buying the elimination of a tail risk: the scenario where a future rupture with the host country restricts access to \$40bn of metal at the worst possible moment. If you assign even a fraction of a percent annual probability to that scenario, the expected-loss math favors repatriation within a few years, and the *insurance* math — protection against a total, correlated-with-crisis loss — favors it immediately. The intuition: paying 0.2% once to guarantee that no foreign government can ever stand between you and your gold is, for a reserve manager who just watched the Russia freeze, one of the cheapest insurance premiums in finance.

#### Worked example: pricing the freeze's "geopolitical premium" into gold

Try to bound how much of gold's post-2022 move is the freeze versus the usual macro drivers. Under the old real-yield model, the rise in the 10-year real yield from about -1% in 2021 to about +2% by 2024 — a ~3 percentage-point tightening of gold's opportunity cost — would historically have been worth a *large decline* in gold, perhaps 20-30%. Instead gold *rose* on the order of 40% over that window (roughly \$1,800 to \$2,600 by end-2024, before the further 2025-26 surge). The gap between "what the macro model predicted" (down 20-30%) and "what happened" (up ~40%) is on the order of 60-70 percentage points of performance the old model cannot explain. That residual is the footprint of the structural official-sector bid — the freeze premium — showing up not as a line item you can read off a screen but as the *difference between the model and reality*. The intuition: when a price defies its known drivers by this much for this long, a new, large, price-insensitive buyer has entered, and in 2022-2025 that buyer was the world's central banks hedging the one risk no bond can hedge.

## The takeaway

For fifty years, the case against gold was a single sentence: *it pays you nothing, so why hold it when a Treasury bond pays 4%?* February 2022 answered that sentence permanently for a large fraction of the world. The honest reply is now: *because a Treasury bond's 4% comes with a condition — that the issuer keeps choosing to honor it — and gold's 0% comes with no condition at all.* For an entity that holds reserves precisely against the worst-case state of the world, an asset that *cannot be switched off* is worth forgoing yield to hold. The freeze did not change gold; it changed what "safe" means, and gold was the only large asset that met the new definition.

So here is how to carry this into the way you think about gold. Do not model gold as *only* an inflation hedge or *only* a real-yield play, though it is partly both. Model it as the asset that prices the *political* reliability of money — the world's running vote of no-confidence not just in paper money's purchasing power, but in the *permission* to use it. Before 2022 that political dimension was a footnote, dwarfed by real yields and the dollar. After 2022 it is a structural pillar, and it explains the otherwise-baffling fact of gold rising into the teeth of rising real rates. The single most important monetary event for gold in fifty years was not an interest-rate decision or an inflation print. It was a signature on a Saturday morning that proved foreign reserves are a permission, not a possession — and every central bank that understood the difference has been buying the one reserve that needs no one's permission ever since.

It also reframes what you are *competing with* when you, as an individual, decide whether to hold gold. You are no longer just betting against the central scenario of falling inflation and rising real yields, where gold's lack of a coupon is a pure cost. You are sitting in the same boat as the official sector, holding a small allocation to the one asset that performs when the *system itself* — not just an inflation print, but the trust that makes paper money usable — comes into question. That is a rarer and more catastrophic risk than inflation, and it is precisely the kind of risk most portfolios carry no protection against. The central banks of the world spent 2022 to 2025 buying that protection by the thousand-tonne. Whether or not you ever expect *your* country's money to be weaponized, the fact that the largest, best-informed reserve managers on earth are all buying the same insurance is a data point worth more than any forecast. They are not chasing a return. They are buying the asset that survives the one scenario their bonds cannot.

That is the deepest sense in which gold is monetary insurance. Most insurance pays out when a house burns. This insurance pays out when the *fire department* — the system you trusted to keep your money safe — turns out to answer to someone who might not be on your side. In February 2022, the world learned that such a day is possible. It has been quietly buying the policy ever since, and it has not stopped.
