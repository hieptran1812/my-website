> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When the SJC gold premium exploded to roughly 25% in mid-2024, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) did not free up imports; it became the gold dealer itself, and that choice tells you everything about how Vietnam governs gold.
>
> - The SBV's toolkit has two tools: **bullion auctions** (revived May 2024, as in 2013) and, when those failed, **direct sales** through four state-owned banks plus SJC at a price the SBV set (from June 2024).
> - Direct sales worked: the SJC price fell from a record ~92.4 million VND per luong (~\$3,630) to a managed floor near ~77 million VND (~\$3,180), and the premium collapsed from ~25% toward single digits within months.
> - It worked at a cost: every luong the SBV sold was imported gold paid for in dollars, draining foreign-exchange (FX) reserves at the same time the dong was near record lows.
> - The deeper tension never goes away: the SBV wants **anti-goldization** (less national wealth frozen in metal), yet the public keeps buying — so the 2025 reform direction is to amend Decree 24, end the single-brand monopoly, and let the premium narrow by competition instead of by hand.

In May 2024 the SJC gold-bar price in Vietnam hit a record of about 92.4 million VND per luong, and the gap between that price and what the same gold cost on the world market blew past 20%. (A *luong* — romanized; you will also see it written as *cay* or *tael* — is the Vietnamese gold unit, 37.5 grams, equal to 1.20565 troy ounces. We will use that conversion constantly.) Vietnamese households were paying a quarter more than the global price for ordinary gold, and the gap was getting wider by the week.

A government facing that has an obvious-looking fix: let people import gold. If the domestic price is 25% above the world price, importers will pour gold in until the gap closes. That is exactly what Vietnam did **not** do. Instead, the State Bank of Vietnam — the country's central bank — chose to become the gold dealer itself: first reviving an old auction mechanism, then, when that did not work fast enough, selling gold directly to the public through four state-owned commercial banks and the SJC company at a price it set day by day.

It worked. The premium that had taken two years to build was crushed in a few months. But "it worked" hides the bill, and the bill is the whole story. This post is the SBV's gold-policy toolkit, the 2024 intervention that used it, and the permanent dilemma sitting underneath: a state that officially wants its citizens to hold less gold, governing a public that has trusted gold over the national currency for generations.

![The SBV gold toolkit and the 2024 intervention sequence from premium spike to crush](/imgs/blogs/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization-1.png)

This connects straight back to the spine of this whole series: gold is not an investment that compounds — it is a monetary insurance policy, and its price is a vote of no-confidence in paper money. In Vietnam that vote is loud, the paper money is the dong, and the SBV's gold policy is the running argument between a state that wants the vote quieter and a public that keeps casting it.

## Foundations: the central bank, FX reserves, and why gold intervention is hard

Before the 2024 drama makes sense, four building blocks.

**What the SBV is.** The State Bank of Vietnam is the country's central bank. Like the US Federal Reserve or the European Central Bank, it issues the national currency (the dong, romanized; the symbol is VND), sets interest-rate and credit policy, and manages the country's foreign-exchange reserves. Unlike most Western central banks, it operates inside a managed-currency regime: the dong does not float freely against the dollar. The SBV publishes a daily *central rate* and lets the market trade in a narrow band around it. That single fact — a managed currency — is why gold policy and FX policy are joined at the hip in Vietnam, as we will see. For the broader machinery of how the SBV runs the dong and credit, the companion post on [Vietnam's monetary policy](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling) goes deeper; here we stay on gold.

**What FX reserves are, and why they are precious.** Foreign-exchange reserves are the central bank's holdings of foreign money and foreign assets — mostly US dollars and US Treasury bonds, plus some gold. They are the country's war chest. A central bank uses reserves to defend its currency (selling dollars to prop up the dong when it weakens), to pay for essential imports in a crisis, and to maintain confidence that the country can meet its external obligations. Reserves are finite and hard to rebuild — they come from running trade surpluses and attracting investment over years. So a central bank spends them carefully. **The key intuition for this whole post: gold, for the SBV, is not free. To sell gold to its own public, it must first buy that gold on the world market — in dollars, out of reserves.** That cost constraint is the invisible hand on every lever the SBV pulls.

It is worth dwelling on *why* reserves are the binding constraint and not, say, the dong. The SBV can create dong at will — it is the monopoly issuer. If gold could be bought with freshly created dong, the SBV would face no scarcity at all; it would simply print and buy. But the world does not sell gold for dong. London bullion banks, Swiss refiners, and the international gold market price and settle in dollars. So to acquire an ounce of gold, the SBV must hand over dollars — and the only dollars it has are the ones in reserves, accumulated painstakingly over years of trade surpluses. This is the asymmetry at the heart of the whole problem: the SBV has unlimited domestic currency and strictly limited foreign currency, and gold sits on the wrong side of that line. Every gold intervention is therefore, at bottom, a dollar-spending decision dressed up as a gold-market decision.

There is also a confidence dimension. Vietnam's reserves are watched by rating agencies, foreign investors, and the IMF as a measure of the country's resilience. A common benchmark is "months of imports" — reserves divided by the monthly import bill — and Vietnam has historically run a thinner cushion than it would like, often hovering near the conventional three-month adequacy floor. When reserves are already snug, spending a billion dollars to subsidize gold buyers is not a rounding error; it is a visible dent in a number that other people use to judge the country. That is the second reason the SBV cannot simply open the gold tap indefinitely: it is not just spending money, it is spending a signal.

**Why intervening in gold is genuinely hard.** A central bank can create unlimited domestic currency — it prints the dong. It cannot create gold or dollars. So when the public wants more gold than the country has, the SBV faces a real, physical scarcity it cannot paper over. It can do one of three things: (1) let prices rise until demand cools (politically painful — gold is a household savings asset, not a luxury); (2) free up imports so private traders supply the gold (gives up control and spends the nation's dollars through private hands); or (3) supply the gold itself from reserves (keeps control but burns the central bank's own dollars). Vietnam, as we will see, kept choosing option 3.

Compare this to how a central bank intervenes in its *own currency*. When the SBV wants to support the dong, it sells dollars and buys dong — and it can also raise interest rates, tighten credit, and lean on banks, a whole suite of levers. When it wants to weaken the dong, it can buy dollars with freshly created dong, effectively without limit. Currency intervention is asymmetric but rich in tools. Gold intervention is poorer: there is no "interest rate on gold" the SBV can raise, no way to make gold less attractive by decree without driving the trade underground, and — crucially — no way to *supply* gold except by spending the very dollars it is trying to conserve. The only purely domestic lever is the monopoly itself: by being the sole legal importer and the owner of the only legal brand, the SBV controls how much gold legally exists in Vietnam. But controlling the *quantity of legal gold* is not the same as controlling the *price*, and the gap between those two is precisely where the premium lives.

That is why a gold premium is such a stubborn thing. It is the market's measure of exactly how much the SBV's control of quantity diverges from what the price would be in a free market — and the SBV can only close it by giving up quantity control (free imports) or by spending dollars (direct supply). There is no third door that is both cheap and control-preserving.

**Anti-goldization, briefly.** "Goldization" (*vang hoa* in Vietnamese; we will keep the English term) is the phenomenon of a large share of a nation's private wealth being held in physical gold rather than in bank deposits, bonds, or productive investment. Vietnam has one of the world's highest rates of it. The official policy goal since the early 2010s — written into the famous Decree 24 of 2012 — has been **anti-goldization**: to discourage gold from acting as a parallel currency and to move that frozen wealth into the formal financial system. The previous post in this series, [SJC and Decree 24](/blog/trading/gold/sjc-and-decree-24-how-vietnam-built-a-gold-bar-monopoly), tells that story in full: the state made SJC the only legal gold-bar brand, banned private bar production, and made the SBV the sole importer of gold. That monopoly is the soil the 2024 premium grew in.

Why does a government care so much about its citizens holding gold? Two reasons, both monetary. First, gold held in safes is dead capital — it earns nothing, lends to no one, and funds no business. A dollar of savings in a bank deposit can be lent out several times over through the banking system, financing investment and growth; a dollar of savings buried in a gold bar finances nothing. A country with a large share of its savings in gold has effectively sterilized a chunk of its capital. Second, and more pointed, when a population trusts gold over the national currency, gold becomes a *parallel currency* — people quote big-ticket prices (houses, land) in taels of gold rather than dong, and they flee the dong into gold whenever they doubt it. That undermines the central bank's control over its own money: monetary policy works through the national currency, and it works poorly if half the country prices and saves in metal. Anti-goldization, then, is not snobbery about an old-fashioned asset; it is a central bank trying to win back monetary sovereignty from a rival store of value that happens to be a shiny metal. The bitter irony, which this whole post circles, is that the tools the SBV used to fight goldization repeatedly ended up *feeding* it.

That is the toolkit's backdrop. Now the tools.

## The 2013 auctions: the first tool

The SBV did not invent gold intervention in 2024. It had a playbook from a decade earlier.

In 2013, shortly after Decree 24 created the SJC monopoly, Vietnam's domestic gold price was also trading well above the world price, and the government wanted to close the gap and stabilize the market. The SBV's answer was a **bullion auction**: the central bank, as the sole legal importer, would import gold, mint it into SJC bars, and auction those bars to licensed banks and gold companies. Those buyers would then sell the bars on to the public.

The logic is clean. An auction is a controlled, central-bank-supplied injection of physical gold into a supply-starved market. By dribbling SJC bars in through auctions, the SBV could add supply without fully freeing imports — keeping its monopoly and its control while relieving the shortage. Over 2013, the SBV ran dozens of auction sessions and sold something on the order of 60-plus tonnes of gold this way.

Mechanically, an auction session worked like a government bond sale in reverse. The SBV announced a quantity on offer (a number of lots, each a fixed weight of SJC bars), set a *reserve price* — the minimum it would accept — and invited the seventeen-odd licensed gold-trading institutions (banks and gold companies) to bid. Bidders submitted the volume they wanted and a price at or above the reserve. The SBV allocated to the bids that cleared, the winners paid in dong, took delivery of the bars, and resold them to the public through their own counters. The state, in this design, never touches a retail customer; it is a wholesaler to a small set of licensed dealers who handle the public.

Here is the catch, and it is the same catch that returns in 2024: the auctions added supply, but they did not reliably *close the premium*. The auction price itself was set high — near the elevated domestic level — so the gold entered the market expensive. The reserve price was, by design, pegged close to the prevailing SJC market price, which meant the SBV was effectively *ratifying* the high price rather than undercutting it. A dealer who bought at the auction had paid near-market and naturally resold at a markup above that; nothing in the chain pushed the price down. The SBV's stated goal in 2013 was explicitly "stabilization and increasing the state budget," not premium elimination — and indeed the auctions generated a tidy profit for the state, because it imported gold at the world price and sold it at the inflated domestic price, pocketing the spread. The gap between domestic and world prices stayed stubbornly wide for years afterward. The auction was a supply valve, not a price cap — and arguably it was a *revenue* tool first.

That distinction — **adding supply versus capping the price** — is the hinge of the entire 2024 episode. And notice the uncomfortable incentive buried in it: an auction that ratifies a high domestic price is profitable for the state, because the state captures the premium. A direct-sale program that crushes the price *forgoes* that profit and spends reserves instead. The 2024 switch, as we will see, was a decision to stop capturing the premium and start destroying it. Hold onto that.

There is a final, sobering lesson from 2013 that frames everything after it. After the auctions wound down, the SBV declared the market stabilized and largely stepped back. For a few years that looked fine — global gold was in its long post-2011 bear market (see [the 2011 top to the 2024 breakout](/blog/trading/gold/from-the-2011-top-to-the-2024-breakout-hangover-then-a-new-regime)), domestic demand was subdued, and a quiet world price made the closed Vietnamese market look well-behaved. But the structure had not changed: SJC was still the only legal bar, the SBV was still the only importer, and supply was still frozen. So the moment the world price came alive again in 2023-24, the same trap sprung the same way, only worse. The 2013 intervention had treated a symptom and left the disease; 2024 was the disease flaring up again. That is the strongest argument for why the 2025 structural reform matters more than any intervention: a country can run the auction-then-step-back cycle forever and never escape the premium, because the premium is built into the architecture, not into the weather.

## The 2024 premium crisis: the spike to ~92 million VND

By early 2024, the SJC premium had become a national problem. Through 2023 the domestic price already carried a fat premium of around 18% over the world-equivalent. Then world gold itself began to surge — central-bank buying and falling real interest rates lifted the global price hard through 2024 (the mechanics of that global move are in [central banks, the structural buyer](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022) and [real interest rates](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price)). In a normal market, a rising world price would simply pull the domestic price up with it. But Vietnam's market is not normal: the SJC supply was frozen by the import ban, demand was rising with the price, and the premium — the gap between the trapped domestic price and the world price — exploded.

By April 2024 the premium was about 24%, roughly 18 million VND per luong. In May, SJC hit its record: about 92.4 million VND per luong, with the premium near 25% — about 18.5 million VND of pure gap.

Why did the premium *widen* as the world price rose, instead of staying constant? This is the part that surprises people, and it is worth slowing down on, because it explains why the SBV could not just wait the problem out. In a free market, a domestic price tracks the world price plus the cost of importing (shipping, insurance, a dealer's margin) — a roughly *fixed* spread. But Vietnam's domestic supply was capped by the import ban, so the domestic price was set by domestic supply and demand in isolation. As the world price rose, two things happened at once. First, rising prices in Vietnam are read by households not as "expensive, stay away" but as "gold is going up, buy before it goes higher" — gold demand is partly momentum-driven, so higher prices *attracted* buyers rather than repelling them. Second, the capped supply could not expand to meet that surge. A demand spike hitting a fixed supply does what it always does: it sends price vertical. And because the world price was the floor that the *premium* was measured against, the domestic price racing ahead of a rising world price meant the premium itself ballooned. The closed market amplified the global rally into a domestic mania.

There is a self-reinforcing loop hiding here, too. A widening premium is itself news. Vietnamese households reading that SJC gold is hitting records and that the gap to the world price is the widest ever do not conclude "I am being overcharged"; many conclude "gold is clearly the asset to be in." So the premium-driven headlines drove more demand, which drove the premium wider still. Left alone, this loop does not gently self-correct — it runs until something breaks it from outside. That "something" had to be the SBV.

![SJC gold-bar price showing the 2024 peak, the intervention dip, and the later record run](/imgs/blogs/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization-2.png)

To feel that premium, you have to convert it. The full mechanics of the conversion — world dollars per troy ounce to domestic VND per luong — are decomposed in the previous post, [the SJC premium](/blog/trading/gold/the-sjc-premium-why-vietnamese-gold-trades-far-above-the-world-price). Here is the headline number, in both currencies.

#### Worked example: how big the May-2024 premium really was

The SJC price in May 2024 was 92.4 million VND per luong. At the 2024 dong rate of 25,450 VND per dollar, that is `92,400,000 / 25,450 ≈ \$3,631` per luong. Convert to a troy ounce: a luong is 1.20565 troy oz, so the SJC price was `\$3,631 / 1.20565 ≈ \$3,012` per troy ounce.

Now compare to the world price. World gold averaged about \$2,388/oz in 2024 and was trading in the mid-\$2,300s in spring 2024. So a Vietnamese buyer was paying roughly **\$3,012 against a world price near \$2,400** — about \$600 per ounce, or 25%, in pure premium. On a single luong, the premium alone was about `18.5 million VND ≈ \$727`. The lesson: a 25% premium is not an abstraction — it is the price of a mid-range motorbike, paid on top of every luong of ordinary gold, for nothing but the privilege of buying inside a closed market.

A 25% gap on a household savings asset is politically intolerable. It also broadcasts a problem: a premium that wide is a flashing signal that the closed market is badly out of balance, and — as the post on [the gold-dollar-FX triangle](/blog/trading/gold/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam) explains — it pulls gold across the border through the gray market and pressures the dollar. The SBV had to act.

## The 2024 intervention: auctions first, then direct sales

The SBV reached for the 2013 playbook first.

**May 2024 — bullion auctions revived.** The SBV announced it would resume gold-bar auctions for the first time in eleven years. Through late April and May 2024 it ran nine auction sessions, offering SJC bars to commercial banks and gold companies, and sold on the order of 1.8 tonnes of gold. The mechanism was identical to 2013: central bank imports gold, mints SJC bars, auctions them to intermediaries, who sell on to the public.

And it failed at the one job that mattered. The auction *reserve price* was set close to the prevailing high market price, so the gold entered expensive. Worse, the very act of auctioning created a strange feedback: the auction price became a reference point that the market read as the SBV's "blessing" of a high price. Some sessions were undersubscribed. The premium did not close — in places it widened. The auction had, once again, added supply without capping the price.

So in June 2024 the SBV switched tools entirely.

**June 2024 — direct sales at a managed price.** The SBV abandoned auctions and announced it would sell gold *directly to the public* at a price *it* set. The channel: four large state-owned commercial banks — Vietcombank, VietinBank, BIDV, and Agribank — plus the SJC company itself. (These four are the "Big Four" state-controlled banks, with the densest branch networks in the country and balance sheets the SBV can lean on directly. Using them, rather than the dozens of private gold dealers, kept the entire channel inside the state's reach.) The SBV would import gold, set a daily sell price anchored close to the world-equivalent price (plus a small, defined markup), and feed that gold through the five outlets. Citizens lined up — sometimes literally, with queues outside bank branches and online registration systems — to buy at the official price.

The retail mechanics tell you how seriously the SBV meant to control the flow. Buyers typically had to register online for a slot, present national identification, and could purchase only a limited quantity per person per day — measures designed both to ration scarce gold fairly and, not incidentally, to *deter* speculative bulk buying. You could buy gold from the state, but you had to identify yourself and you could not corner the supply. This is the anti-goldization instinct showing through even inside the act of selling gold: the SBV was supplying the market while quietly making the act of accumulating gold more friction-filled and more visible to the authorities. It was, in effect, selling gold with one hand while gently discouraging it with the other.

Note also what the channel did *not* include: free imports for private dealers. The SBV remained the sole importer; it simply changed how it disposed of the imported gold — from wholesaling to dealers (auctions) to retailing to the public (direct sales). The monopoly was fully intact. What changed was the *price* at which the state was willing to be the seller.

![The four-bank direct-sale channel from the State Bank through state banks and SJC to the public](/imgs/blogs/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization-4.png)

The difference between the two tools is the whole point. The auction set a *high* reserve price and let intermediaries mark it up further — it added supply at the market price. **Direct sales set a *low* managed price and sold straight to the public — it capped the price by becoming the cheapest seller in the market.** Once the SBV was willing to sell unlimited gold at, say, ~77 million VND per luong, no one would pay 92 million VND to anyone else. The official price became the ceiling.

#### Worked example: why the managed-price channel caps the premium and the auction did not

Suppose the world-equivalent price of a luong is 74 million VND (`74,000,000 / 25,450 ≈ \$2,908` per luong). Under the **auction** tool, the SBV's reserve price was near the *market* level of ~89 million VND; intermediaries bought there and sold to the public at ~90 million VND — so the public still paid a ~22% premium (`(90 − 74) / 74 ≈ 22%`). Adding supply at the high price changed nothing for the buyer.

Under **direct sales**, the SBV set its own sell price near 77 million VND — world-equivalent plus a small markup. The implied premium is `(77 − 74) / 74 ≈ 4%`. Because the SBV was an unlimited seller at 77 million VND (~\$3,025), any dealer quoting 90 million VND lost every customer. The cap held because the *cheapest credible unlimited seller sets the price*, and the SBV made itself that seller. The lesson: in a cornered market, supply alone does not move the price — only a willing seller at a lower price does.

## How it crushed the premium

The direct-sale channel did what the auctions could not. Through June, July, and August 2024 the SBV simply kept selling at its managed price, and the market price had nowhere to go but down toward it.

By June, the SJC reference price had fallen to about 76.98 million VND per luong — call it ~77 — from the ~92.4 million peak. By September 2024 the premium over the world price had collapsed from ~25% to around 8% (about 6 million VND), and through 2025 it settled into the single-digit-percent range, with the gap in VND terms running around 4-6 million.

The speed of the collapse is the tell. A premium that took roughly two years to build from a normal few-percent spread up to 25% was undone in about three months. That asymmetry — slow to inflate, fast to deflate once the SBV committed to direct sales — is itself evidence for the marginal-seller story. Premiums build slowly because they are demand-driven: it takes time for momentum buying and capped supply to grind the gap wider. But a price ceiling, once a credible unlimited seller imposes it, bites immediately, because the arbitrage is instantaneous — the moment cheaper gold is reliably available, nobody pays more. There is no gradualism in a ceiling. The SBV did not "talk the price down" or "guide expectations"; it sold actual metal at a lower price, and the market repriced within weeks.

It is also worth noting what the SBV did *not* claim. It never said it would defend a particular SJC price level forever, and it never promised the premium would stay at any fixed number. The managed price floated daily, anchored to the world-equivalent plus a markup the SBV could adjust. This kept the policy flexible: as the world price rose through 2025, the SBV's managed domestic price rose with it, so the *premium* stayed narrow even as the *price* set records. The SBV was managing a spread, not pinning a price — a distinction that let it ride the global rally without re-triggering a premium crisis.

![The SJC premium collapsing from about 25 percent to single digits after the intervention](/imgs/blogs/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization-3.png)

#### Worked example: the premium crush in VND and dollars, on one luong

A buyer at the May-2024 peak paid 92.4 million VND for one luong (`≈ \$3,631`). A buyer a few months later, after the intervention pulled the price toward the managed floor, paid about 77 million VND (`77,000,000 / 25,450 ≈ \$3,026`).

The price fell by `92.4 − 77 = 15.4 million VND`, which at 25,450 VND/dollar is about `15,400,000 / 25,450 ≈ \$605`. That ~\$605 — about 17% off the peak price — is the buyer's saving, handed to them by the intervention. Put differently: the household that waited a quarter for the SBV to act saved roughly the cost of a high-end smartphone on a single luong of gold. The lesson: the intervention was a direct, measurable transfer from the SBV's reserves to gold buyers' wallets — which is exactly why it could not run forever.

Why did the price obey? The clean way to see it is through arbitrage and the concept of the *marginal seller*. In any market, the price is set by whoever is willing to transact at the lowest price for the quantity demanded — the marginal seller. Before the intervention, the marginal sellers were private dealers who had scarce SJC bars and could name a high price because the SBV was not competing with them. The moment the SBV announced it would sell unlimited gold through the Big Four at ~77 million VND, *it* became the marginal seller. No rational buyer would pay 92 million VND to a private dealer when the state would sell the identical SJC bar at 77 million. Private dealers either matched the official price or sold nothing. The market price was dragged down to the level of the cheapest credible unlimited seller — which was now the central bank.

This is exactly why the auctions had failed and the direct sales worked, restated in one principle: **to set a ceiling on a price, you must be willing to sell unlimited quantity at that ceiling.** Auctions sold a *limited* quantity at a *high* reserve, so they never threatened to be the marginal seller. Direct sales offered an *open-ended* quantity at a *low* managed price, so they captured the margin. The lesson generalizes far beyond Vietnam: a central bank can cap any price it is willing to supply without limit (this is how currency pegs and bond-yield caps work too), and it loses the cap the instant it runs out of ammunition — for the dong, dollars; for gold, imported metal.

Note something important the chart shows: **crushing the premium did not crush the price for long.** The SJC price dipped to ~77 in mid-2024, drifted back up to ~84 by year-end, and then — as the *world* price went vertical through 2025 into the January-2026 all-time high of about 190.3 million VND per luong — the SJC price soared with it. The intervention closed the *gap* between domestic and world prices; it did nothing to stop the world price itself. That is the difference between premium policy and price: the SBV controls the spread, not the metal.

## The cost and the dilemma

Here is the bill. Every luong the SBV sold through the four banks and SJC was gold it had **imported** — bought on the world market with US dollars, drawn from the country's FX reserves. The intervention was, in plain accounting terms, the central bank spending its dollar reserves to subsidize domestic gold buyers.

![USD to VND year-end showing a steadily weakening dong as the FX constraint on gold intervention](/imgs/blogs/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization-6.png)

The timing made the cost worse. 2024 was a year of dong weakness: the currency drifted toward record lows against the dollar, ending the year around 25,450 VND per dollar and weakening further into 2025-26. A weakening currency is exactly when a central bank most wants to *hoard* its dollar reserves — both to defend the currency and because every dollar buys more dong (so reserves are more valuable to keep). Yet the gold intervention forced the SBV to *spend* dollars at precisely that moment. The two policies — defend the dong, and crush the gold premium — competed for the same scarce dollars.

It is worth being precise about *how* spending dollars on gold weakens the currency, because the channels are not obvious. There is a direct channel and an indirect one. Directly, selling dollars to import gold is itself a source of dollar demand — the SBV is a buyer of dollars in the global market (or a depleter of its dollar pile), and that, all else equal, pushes the dong's exchange rate weaker, the opposite of what the SBV wants. Indirectly, and more subtly, when the SBV imports gold and sells it domestically for dong, it is *withdrawing dong* from circulation (people hand over dong to buy the gold). That sounds like a monetary tightening — fewer dong sloshing around — which should support the currency. But the dong taken in is offset by the dollars paid out, and the net national balance sheet has simply swapped a productive, liquid dollar asset for gold bars now sitting in households' safes, doing nothing for the economy. The country is poorer in usable foreign exchange and no richer in productive capital. That is the real cost: not just a number on the reserves line, but a conversion of the nation's most flexible asset into the most inert one.

There is also a *recovery* question people gloss over. "But the SBV sold the gold for dong — didn't it get its money back?" In *dong* terms, mostly yes; it may even have booked a small markup. But the dollars are gone. It paid dollars to the world and received dong from its citizens, and dong cannot buy gold or defend the dong on the international market. So the relevant ledger is not the dong profit-and-loss; it is the *foreign-exchange* account, and on that account the intervention is a pure, one-way outflow. This is why "the SBV got paid" is a misleading comfort: it got paid in the wrong currency for the problem it has.

#### Worked example: the FX cost of supplying the public's gold appetite

The SBV never published an exact figure, but the order of magnitude is easy to bound. Suppose, over the intervention, the SBV needed to supply 300,000 luong of gold to the market (a plausible figure given the queues and the months of sales). Each luong is 1.20565 troy oz, so that is `300,000 × 1.20565 ≈ 361,700 troy oz`, or about 11.3 tonnes.

At a world price of ~\$2,400 per troy oz, the import cost is `361,700 × \$2,400 ≈ \$868 million` — call it roughly \$0.9 billion of FX reserves spent to buy the gold the SBV then sold domestically. Even if it recovered most of that in VND sales, the *FX* leg is a one-way drain: it paid dollars out and took dong in, and dong cannot be used to defend the dong. The lesson: a few hundred thousand luong — a modest slice of Vietnam's annual gold demand of ~50 tonnes — already runs to the better part of a billion dollars in reserves. Scarcity is the binding constraint, not willingness.

Now the dilemma in full. The SBV is trying to hold three goals at once, and they pull against each other:

1. **Crush the premium** — which requires the SBV to sell gold, which requires importing gold, which spends dollars.
2. **Guard FX reserves** — which requires *not* spending dollars, especially when the dong is weak.
3. **Anti-goldization** — discourage the public from holding gold at all. But selling gold cheaply to satisfy demand does the opposite: it *feeds* the appetite it is supposed to curb.

![The gold-policy trilemma of crushing the premium versus guarding reserves versus anti-goldization](/imgs/blogs/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization-5.png)

You cannot fully have all three. Selling gold (goal 1) burns reserves (violates goal 2) and feeds gold demand (violates goal 3). Doing nothing saves reserves and starves the gold habit but leaves the premium — and the resulting smuggling and FX leakage — in place. Anti-goldization is, in the deepest sense, *in tension with the very tool used to manage the gold market*. The SBV in 2024 chose to crush the premium and pay for it with reserves, accepting the goldization cost as the price of social and FX stability. That is a defensible choice. It is not a free one, and it is not permanent.

The goldization corner of the trilemma deserves a closer look, because it is the most paradoxical. The entire legal architecture of Vietnam's gold market — the SJC monopoly, the import ban, Decree 24 — was built to *reduce* the role of gold in the economy: to nudge households out of inert metal and into bank deposits, bonds, and productive investment. The official theory was that a tightly controlled gold market would make gold less convenient and less attractive, and savings would migrate into the formal financial system, where they could be lent out and put to work. But the 2024 intervention did the opposite of what that theory wanted. By selling gold cheaply and visibly to anyone who queued up, the SBV was *validating* gold as a savings asset and *satisfying* the very demand it officially wished to suppress. A household that successfully bought an SJC bar at the official price had just been taught two lessons: gold is so important that the central bank itself will supply it, and the state will protect gold buyers from the worst of the premium. Neither lesson reduces the national appetite for gold; both deepen it.

This is the paradox in one sentence: **the tool used to manage goldization is itself a goldization subsidy.** Every intervention that crushes the premium makes gold cheaper and easier to buy, which is precisely the condition under which people buy more of it. The SBV is, in effect, fighting a fire by occasionally spraying it with gasoline at a discount. It does so because the alternative — letting the premium run, letting the market signal panic, letting smuggling and FX leakage compound — is judged worse in the short run. But it means the anti-goldization goal is structurally self-defeating as long as the chosen instrument is "sell people the gold they want, cheaper." You cannot wean a public off an asset by becoming its most reliable supplier.

There is one more layer: distributional. When the SBV spends national reserves — the whole country's hard-currency war chest — to subsidize gold buyers, it is transferring value from the public balance sheet to the specific households who managed to buy at the official price. Those tend to be people with the means and the time to register, queue, and buy gold in the first place — not the poorest. So the intervention is, quietly, a regressive transfer: everyone's reserves, spent to benefit the subset of citizens already wealthy enough to be buying gold bars. That is rarely stated out loud, but it is part of why a reserves-funded gold subsidy is not a sustainable long-run policy.

#### Worked example: the trilemma made concrete on one decision

Frame the SBV's June-2024 decision as a single trade. To pull one luong from 92 million VND down to 77 million VND, it must sell that luong into the market at 77 million VND (`≈ \$3,026`). To have that luong, it imported it at the world-equivalent of ~74 million VND (`≈ \$2,908`). So on each luong the SBV books a small VND markup of about `77 − 74 = 3 million VND (≈ \$118)` — but it paid out `\$2,908` of *reserves* to get there and took back dong. The premium fell (goal 1, achieved); reserves fell by ~\$2,908 per luong (goal 2, violated); and a household that might have kept its money in a dong deposit instead bought gold (goal 3, violated). The lesson: there is no costless cell in the trilemma — every luong sold buys premium-control with reserves and goldization.

## The 2025 reform direction: amending Decree 24

A central bank cannot be the permanent retail gold dealer of a 100-million-person country. The direct-sale scheme was a fire extinguisher, not a heating system. By 2025, the policy conversation had shifted from "how do we intervene better?" to "how do we make intervention unnecessary?"

The answer is structural, and it points at the root cause: the monopoly itself. As long as SJC is the only legal bar and the SBV is the only legal importer, the domestic supply of gold is a tap that only the state controls — and any time demand outruns the state's willingness to open the tap, a premium balloons. The fix is to widen the number of hands on the tap.

The 2025 reform direction — centered on amending Decree 24 — moves in exactly that direction: licensing more bar producers beyond SJC, allowing qualified enterprises and banks to import gold under license rather than only the SBV, and tightening the conditions and reporting around the trade. The intended result is a market where the premium narrows by **competition** — multiple importers and producers arbitraging the gap away — rather than by the SBV manually selling gold to cap it. The stated policy target after the rule change was a premium in the ~3-5 million VND range, roughly 5-7%, achieved structurally.

![Before and after view of amending Decree 24 to end the monopoly and narrow the premium by competition](/imgs/blogs/sbv-gold-policy-auctions-the-2024-intervention-and-anti-goldization-7.png)

#### Worked example: what a competitive premium would have cost the May-2024 buyer

Take the May-2024 world-equivalent price of a luong at ~74 million VND (`74,000,000 / 25,450 ≈ \$2,908`). Under the closed monopoly, the buyer paid ~92.4 million VND — a 25% premium, or about 18.5 million VND (`≈ \$727`) of pure gap. Under a competitive licensed-import market targeting a ~5% premium, the same buyer would have paid `74 × 1.05 ≈ 77.7 million VND (≈ \$3,053)` — a premium of just `3.7 million VND ≈ \$145`.

The difference between the two regimes, on a single luong, is `18.5 − 3.7 ≈ 14.8 million VND ≈ \$582`. Notice that this saving comes to roughly the *same magnitude* as what the 2024 intervention delivered — but with one crucial difference: in the competitive case, the saving is paid for by importers' arbitrage, not by the SBV's reserves. The lesson: structural competition can hand the buyer the same ~\$580 discount the intervention did, without the central bank spending a single dollar of its war chest to do it. That is the entire economic argument for amending Decree 24, in one number.

This is a real philosophical shift. The 2012-2024 model said: *control the gold market by monopolizing it, and intervene by hand when it misbehaves.* The reform model says: *let a controlled, licensed market do the price discovery, and the premium will police itself.* Whether Vietnam fully follows through — and how much it actually opens imports versus merely adding a few more licensed players — is the open question. But the direction is clear, and it is an admission embedded in policy: the monopoly that was supposed to deliver anti-goldization instead created the premium that the anti-goldization tool then had to spend reserves to fix.

The economics of why competition narrows a premium are worth spelling out, because they are the entire case for the reform. Suppose several licensed importers can each bring gold in at the world price plus a known cost of importing — shipping, insurance, financing, a margin. Call that all-in landed cost the "fair" domestic price. If the market price sits *above* that fair price (a fat premium), any importer can profit by bringing in more gold and selling just below the inflated market price; the extra supply pushes the market price down. Importers keep doing this until the premium shrinks to roughly their cost of importing — a thin, competitive spread, not a 25% gap. The premium does not vanish (importing genuinely costs something), but it collapses to the level that just compensates importers for their trouble. That self-correcting arbitrage is exactly what the import ban switched off, and exactly what licensing importers switches back on. The SBV's manual intervention was a clumsy human substitute for the arbitrage the ban had banned.

But the reform is not free of its own tension, and this is where the trilemma reasserts itself. Licensed imports mean private parties spending the country's dollars on gold — goal 2 (guard FX reserves) is still in play, just delegated to the private sector and the FX market rather than borne directly by the SBV. If a thousand licensed importers all rush to bring in gold during a price spike, the aggregate dollar outflow could pressure the dong just as the SBV's own buying did, only now harder to control. So the reform does not abolish the trilemma; it *relocates* it, trading the SBV's direct reserve drain for a market-wide one that the SBV manages with FX policy instead of gold policy. And on goal 3 (anti-goldization), a more liquid, more accessible gold market arguably makes gold *easier* to buy and hold — again cutting against the official wish to shrink gold's role. The honest reading is that the reform is less an escape from the trilemma than a bet that a competitive market manages the tension more cheaply and more transparently than a central bank improvising with its reserves. That is probably true. It is not the same as solving the problem.

## Common misconceptions

**"The SBV controls the world gold price."** No. The SBV controls the *domestic premium* — the gap between the SJC price and the world price — and only within Vietnam. It has zero influence on the global price of gold, which is set in London, New York, and Shanghai by flows the SBV cannot touch (real interest rates, central-bank buying, the dollar; see [gold and the dollar](/blog/trading/gold/gold-and-the-dollar-the-inverse-relationship-and-when-it-breaks)). The proof is in the chart above: the SBV crushed the premium in mid-2024, and the SJC price still went on to nearly triple by January 2026 — because the world price did, and the SBV could only ride it. Premium policy is local; the price is global.

**"The intervention was free — the SBV just opened a tap."** No. Every luong sold was imported gold bought with dollars out of FX reserves, at the worst possible time for spending dollars (a weak dong). As the worked example bounded, a few hundred thousand luong already runs to the better part of a billion dollars in reserves. The intervention was a deliberate spend of the nation's hardest money to subsidize domestic gold buyers — defensible, but emphatically not free.

**"The premium is gone forever now."** No. The premium narrowed; it did not vanish, and it can re-widen any time domestic demand outruns the SBV's willingness to supply. The data shows the gap settling at single digits (4-6 million VND, 4-8%) through 2025-26 — *narrower*, not *zero*. Until the structural reform genuinely opens supply, the premium is a managed quantity, not an eliminated one, and managed quantities drift the moment the manager stops pushing.

**"Auctions and direct sales are the same intervention."** No — and the difference is the lesson of 2024. Auctions add supply at a *high* reserve price and failed to close the gap. Direct sales cap the price by making the SBV the cheapest unlimited seller, and they worked. Same goal, opposite mechanism, opposite result.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The May-to-September 2024 round trip.** The clearest case study is the one this whole post traces. SJC peaks near 92.4 million VND in May 2024 with a ~25% premium; the SBV runs nine failed auctions in May (~1.8 tonnes sold, premium unmoved); switches to four-bank-plus-SJC direct sales in June at a managed price; the SJC price falls toward ~77 million VND and the premium collapses to ~8% by September and into the single digits through 2025. A reader watching the SJC-versus-world spread in real time in 2024 would have seen the auction announcement do nothing and the direct-sale announcement do everything — the market priced the *credible unlimited seller*, not the press release.

**The 2025-26 record run, premium-tamed.** By January 2026 the SJC price hit an all-time high near 190.3 million VND per luong — more than double the 2024 "crisis" peak. Yet there was no premium crisis this time, because the world price was doing the work and the post-intervention premium stayed in single digits. This is the clean demonstration that the SBV's policy targets the *spread*, not the price: gold can set records in Vietnam without a policy emergency, as long as the domestic price is tracking the world price plus a thin, stable premium.

**The FX cross-read.** Traders and macro watchers use the SJC premium as a back-door gauge of FX pressure in Vietnam. A widening premium in 2024 coincided with dong weakness and dollar demand; the gray-market gold-dollar-VND triangle (detailed in [the smuggling and FX-triangle post](/blog/trading/gold/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam)) means a blown-out gold premium and a stressed currency tend to travel together. When the SBV spent reserves to crush the gold premium, it was implicitly choosing where to deploy a scarce dollar — gold-market calm over an extra increment of currency defense. The premium, in other words, is a readable signal of how the SBV is rationing its dollars. For the global backdrop on why dollars are the binding currency everywhere, see [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).

**The smuggling pressure-valve.** A 25% premium does not just sit there — it summons gold across the border. If gold is worth 25% more inside Vietnam than outside it, the incentive to carry metal in illegally is enormous, and the gray market obliges: gold smuggled in from Cambodia, Laos, and beyond, paid for with dollars bought on the black market, which in turn pressures the dong. The wider the official premium, the louder this gray-market machine runs. So the SBV's intervention had a second, less-discussed payoff: by crushing the premium, it cut the smuggling incentive at the root. When the official price sits only a few percent above the world price, there is little margin left to justify the risk of smuggling — the arbitrage that funded the border flows simply dries up. Read this way, the 2024 intervention was as much an FX-leakage and law-enforcement measure as a consumer-price one: a narrow premium is a closed door to the smugglers, and a wide one is an open invitation. The deep mechanics of that gold-dollar-dong triangle are the subject of [the smuggling and FX-triangle post](/blog/trading/gold/smuggling-the-gray-market-and-the-gold-dollar-fx-triangle-in-vietnam).

**The 2013 precedent as a warning.** History rhymes here in a useful way. The 2013 auctions added tonnes of gold and were declared a stabilization success, yet the premium re-widened in the years that followed because the underlying structure — the monopoly, the import ban — was untouched. A reader watching the 2024 episode should hold the same skepticism: the direct-sale intervention crushed the premium, but unless the 2025 structural reform genuinely opens supply, the premium can re-inflate the next time global gold spikes and domestic demand surges. The intervention is a treatment of the symptom; the reform is the only treatment of the cause. Watching whether the post-2024 single-digit premium *holds* through the next world-price spike is the real test of whether anything structural actually changed.

## The takeaway

The SBV's 2024 intervention is the cleanest real-world demonstration of a counterintuitive truth: in a cornered market, **supply does not set the price — a willing seller does.** The 2013 and May-2024 auctions added physical gold and failed; the June-2024 direct sales added the *same kind* of gold but at a price the SBV was willing to defend, and the premium collapsed. The mechanism that worked was not "more gold" — it was "the central bank made itself the cheapest unlimited seller."

But step back to the spine of this series. Gold's price is the world's vote of no-confidence in paper money. In Vietnam that vote is cast in the SJC premium, and the SBV's whole toolkit is an attempt to manage the *expression* of that vote — to keep the gap between Vietnamese gold and world gold narrow — without being able to change what the vote is about. It cannot make the dong something people trust more than gold by decree; it can only sell gold cheaply enough to keep the premium quiet, and pay for the quiet with reserves. The 2024 intervention crushed the premium; it did nothing to crush the *reason* the premium exists, which is a public that has, for generations, trusted 37.5 grams of metal over the promises printed on a banknote.

So the way to read SBV gold policy is this: it is not a price-control program, it is a **premium-management program with a reserves budget and an anti-goldization conscience**, and those three things are permanently in tension. The 2025 reform direction — amending Decree 24 to let competition narrow the premium — is the SBV's attempt to escape the trilemma by changing the structure rather than spending the budget. If it works, the premium stays thin because the market arbitrages it, not because the central bank keeps writing dollar checks. If it doesn't, the SBV is back to being the gold dealer of last resort, and the next premium spike will find it spending reserves it would rather defend the dong with. Either way, the gold itself keeps doing the one thing it has always done: holding its value while the policymakers argue about how to manage everyone's desire to own it.
