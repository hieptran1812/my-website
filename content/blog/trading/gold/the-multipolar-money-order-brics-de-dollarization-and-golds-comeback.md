> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The world is fragmenting into monetary blocs that trust no single currency, and gold — issued by no one — is re-monetizing as the neutral reserve anchor all sides can hold.
>
> - De-dollarization is real but **slow**: the dollar's share of disclosed FX reserves fell from 71.5% in 2001 to 57.8% in 2024 — about 0.6 percentage points a year, a drift, not a collapse.
> - It is real because the *direction* is one-way and the *trigger* changed: after the West froze Russia's reserves in 2022, every central bank learned that any currency can be switched off. Gold cannot.
> - There is no credible currency replacement. Reserve managers distrust the yuan (capital-controlled, state-run) and don't want to swap one rival for another. Gold wins by being *no one's*.
> - The realistic endgame is not a dollar collapse and not a return to the gold standard — it is a multipolar order with a **bigger gold anchor**: gold as a larger slice of reserves, sitting beside a still-dominant but shrinking dollar.
> - **The one fact to remember:** for the first time since the 1990s, central banks collectively hold more of their reserves in **gold than in euros** — a quieter milestone than any summit, and a louder one.

In the autumn of 2024, the World Gold Council published a chart that drew far less attention than it deserved. It showed gold's share of total global central-bank reserves — counting both their foreign-currency holdings and their bullion — creeping past the euro's share for the first time in nearly three decades. Gold had not been the world's number-two reserve asset since the mid-1990s, back when the classical post-war monetary order still felt permanent and central banks were busy *selling* their bars, not buying them. Now, quietly, the metal had climbed back to second place, behind only the dollar.

No treaty announced this. No summit communiqué declared a new monetary era. It happened the way tectonic shifts usually do: as the sum of thousands of small, defensive, self-interested decisions by reserve managers in Beijing, Warsaw, Ankara, Mumbai, and dozens of capitals you rarely read about — each one looking at the same world and reaching, independently, the same conclusion. They were not coordinating to overthrow the dollar. They were buying insurance.

This is the story of that insurance: why the post-2022 world is fragmenting into monetary blocs that distrust each other's money, why no single currency can replace the dollar, and why the one asset all sides keep reaching for is the oldest money of all. It is also a story I want to tell *carefully*, because it sits at the center of more hype than almost any topic in finance. You have heard the breathless version — the dollar is about to collapse, the BRICS are launching a gold-backed currency next quarter, the gold standard is coming back. None of that is true, or at least none of it is imminent. The real story is slower, stranger, and in the long run more important than the cartoon. Let me make the careful case.

## Foundations: reserve currencies, de-dollarization, and why every system needs a settlement asset

Before we can judge whether gold is "coming back," we need four plain-language building blocks. None of them require any finance background — just patience to define each term properly, because the headlines abuse all of them.

### What a reserve currency actually is

When a country trades with the world, it earns and spends in money that is not its own. Vietnam sells coffee and phones and is paid in dollars; it imports oil and machinery and must pay in dollars. So the State Bank of Vietnam keeps a large stash of foreign money — a national rainy-day fund — to make sure the country can always pay for imports, service its foreign debts, and steady the dong in a crisis. That stash is the country's **foreign-exchange reserves**.

A **reserve currency** is simply the money that most countries choose to *keep* in that stash, and that most international trade and lending is *priced in*. Today that is overwhelmingly the US dollar. When a Brazilian firm buys soybeans from Argentina, the contract is usually written in dollars even though neither party is American. When Saudi Arabia sells oil, the price is quoted in dollars. When a poor country borrows from abroad, it usually borrows in dollars. The dollar is the world's common language of money — the unit everyone agrees to keep score in.

This is an enormous, almost invisible privilege for the United States. Because the whole world wants dollars, the US can borrow more cheaply, run larger deficits, and — as we will see — wield its currency as a weapon. The economist Valéry Giscard d'Estaing called it America's "exorbitant privilege," and the phrase has stuck for sixty years. Any story about de-dollarization is, at heart, a story about other countries trying to chip away at that privilege.

One more thing the term "reserve currency" hides: reserve status is not permanent, and it does not change overnight either. History keeps exactly one rhythm here. The Dutch guilder anchored global trade in the seventeenth century; the British pound took over in the nineteenth and reigned for roughly a century; the dollar succeeded the pound across the first half of the twentieth and was formally enthroned at Bretton Woods in 1944. Notice two patterns. First, transitions are *slow* — the pound's decline ran for decades, and sterling was still a meaningful reserve currency well into the 1950s, long after Britain had ceased to be the dominant economy. Second, the incumbent currency clings on far past the point where the underlying economic power has shifted, because the *network effect* — everyone uses it because everyone else does — outlives the fundamentals that created it. Both patterns matter enormously for judging the dollar today: a successor, if one is coming, arrives over a human lifetime, not a news cycle; and the dollar's persistence tells you nothing about whether its share is quietly eroding underneath. Reserve currencies do not die in a crash. They fade, and something has to be ready to absorb the savings they shed. That "something" is the question this whole post turns on.

### What "de-dollarization" means — and what it doesn't

**De-dollarization** is the gradual reduction of the dollar's role in the global financial system. But "the dollar's role" is really *three* separate jobs, and conflating them is the single most common mistake in this debate:

1. **Medium of exchange** — the currency you *invoice and settle trade in*. (Easiest to chip away.)
2. **Unit of account** — the currency you *quote prices in*, like oil priced per barrel in dollars.
3. **Store of value** — the currency you *hold your savings and reserves in* for years or decades. (Hardest to replace.)

A country can de-dollarize one job without touching the others. India and Russia can agree to settle their bilateral trade in rupees and roubles — chipping at job one — while still holding most of their reserves in dollars, because nothing has replaced the dollar for job three. As we will see, almost all the de-dollarization *moves* you read about attack jobs one and two. The genuinely hard problem — finding a trusted long-term store of value that is *not* the dollar — is exactly where gold enters the story.

### What "multipolar" means for money

For most of the last eighty years the monetary world was effectively **unipolar**: one dominant currency, one set of payment rails (the dollar-clearing system and the SWIFT messaging network that sits on top of it), one ultimate safe asset (the US Treasury bond). A **multipolar money order** is one where that single center fractures into several competing ones — a dollar bloc, a China-centered bloc, perhaps a regional bloc or two — each with its own currency, its own payment rails, its own sphere of trade. Not the dollar *replaced*, but the dollar *crowded*, with rival systems carved out alongside it.

The cover figure above shows why such a world keeps reaching for gold. Picture three groups that genuinely distrust one another: the dollar bloc (which has shown it will freeze rivals' money), the BRICS bloc (which wants out of dollar rails but whose own anchor currency, the yuan, no one fully trusts), and the large unaligned middle (emerging markets and Gulf states that hold both and trust neither). When any two of them must settle a debt and neither will accept the other's currency, there is exactly one asset both will take: the one that is nobody's promise. Gold is the hub at the center not because anyone designed it that way, but because in a low-trust world it is the only thing left that everyone trusts.

### Why any monetary system needs a settlement asset

Here is the deepest point, and the one the hype always skips. Every monetary system — gold standard, dollar standard, or whatever comes next — ultimately needs an **anchor**: a final settlement asset that everyone accepts when the music stops. Within a single country, that anchor is the central bank's own money. Between countries that trust each other, it can be a shared reserve currency like the dollar. But between countries that *don't* trust each other, the anchor has to be something neither can manipulate, freeze, or print.

For five thousand years, the default answer to "what do you settle with when you don't trust the other side" has been a metal: gold. The classical gold standard worked precisely because gold was no nation's liability — a tonne of gold owed nothing to anyone and obeyed no government. When trust between nations is high, the world economizes on gold and uses paper IOUs instead, because paper is cheaper and pays interest. When trust collapses, the world reaches back for the metal. The post-2022 world is, slowly, a low-trust world again. That is the whole thesis of this post, and of [why gold still matters as a 5,000-year store of value](/blog/trading/gold/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value), which this post sits downstream of.

## The dollar's dominance — and the cracks in it

To talk honestly about de-dollarization, you have to start by respecting how dominant the dollar still is. The hype skips this; serious analysis cannot.

### Just how dominant the dollar remains

The numbers are lopsided. As of 2024, the dollar made up about **57.8%** of disclosed global FX reserves (IMF COFER). Roughly half of all international trade is invoiced in dollars, even though the US is only about a quarter of world GDP. Around 60% of cross-border bank loans and deposits are denominated in dollars. Nearly 90% of all foreign-exchange transactions have the dollar on one side. When a crisis hits *anywhere* in the world, money does not flee *to* the next big economy — it flees *to* the dollar, which is why the dollar tends to rise in a panic even when the panic starts in America. We covered this gravitational pull in [the dollar as cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity); the short version is that the dollar's dominance is self-reinforcing, because everyone holds dollars *because* everyone else holds dollars.

The dollar is also "sticky" for reasons that have nothing to do with America's virtue. There is simply no other market deep enough to absorb the world's savings. The US Treasury market is the largest, most liquid pool of safe assets on the planet — roughly \$28 trillion and growing. A reserve manager who wants to park \$500 billion can buy and sell Treasuries all day without moving the price. Try that in the bond market of any other single country and you would crush it. Liquidity is destiny in reserve management, and on liquidity the dollar has no peer. Bears who predict an imminent dollar collapse have to explain *where the money would go*, and they never can.

There is a deep irony buried in this dominance, and it is worth naming because it both explains the dollar's strength and hints at its limit. To *supply* the world with dollars to hold as reserves, the United States has to *run deficits* — it must send more dollars abroad (by importing more than it exports, and by borrowing) than it takes back. This is the **Triffin dilemma**, named for the economist Robert Triffin, who pointed it out in the 1960s: the country that issues the world's reserve currency must perpetually run external deficits to feed global demand for its money, which over time erodes confidence in that very money. The world *needs* America to keep issuing dollar debt — and at the same time worries about how much dollar debt America keeps issuing. The dollar's dominance is therefore self-reinforcing *and* self-undermining at once. It does not resolve into a collapse; it resolves into a slow search, by everyone holding those dollars, for a little bit of insurance on the side. That search is what we are watching.

### The cracks: the slow erosion

And yet. The dollar's share of reserves has been falling for two decades, slowly but unmistakably. Figure 2 traces it.

![USD share of disclosed FX reserves fell from 71.5 percent in 2001 to 57.8 percent in 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback-2.png)

In 2001 the dollar was 71.5% of disclosed reserves. By 2024 it was 57.8%. That is a decline of 13.7 percentage points over 23 years. The trend is gentle — there is no cliff — but it is also remarkably *one-directional*. The dollar has not had a sustained period of *gaining* reserve share in this century. Every wobble up has been retraced.

#### Worked example: how fast is the dollar actually losing share?

Let's annualize the pace and resist the urge to extrapolate it into a collapse. The dollar's reserve share fell from 71.5% to 57.8% over 23 years (2001 to 2024). The total decline is \$71.5 − \$57.8 = 13.7 percentage points — read those as plain points, not dollars. Divided over 23 years, that is 13.7 ÷ 23 ≈ **0.60 percentage points per year**.

Now extrapolate *honestly*. If that exact pace continued, the dollar would still be above 50% of reserves until roughly 2037 (57.8 − 0.60 × 13 ≈ 50.0), and would not reach, say, 40% until around the **mid-2050s**. So even a straight-line continuation of the trend leaves the dollar as the dominant reserve currency for another generation. The intuition: de-dollarization is a glacier, not an avalanche — directionally certain, but measured in decades, not headlines.

What does *not* show up in COFER, crucially, is gold. The IMF's reserve survey counts only foreign *currencies*, so when a central bank sells dollars and buys bullion, the dollar's share in COFER falls but the gold does not appear anywhere in that 57.8%. This is why the headline "dollar still 58% of reserves" understates the shift: a large and growing chunk of the assets that *used* to be dollars has gone into a category the headline number doesn't even measure.

### The crack that changed everything: weaponization

The slow erosion above is the *background*. The *trigger* — the thing that turned a gentle drift into a deliberate strategy — was the weaponization of the dollar. This is the subject of the companion post [sanctions, reserve freezes, and the 2022 turning point for gold](/blog/trading/gold/sanctions-reserve-freezes-and-the-2022-turning-point-for-gold), so I will keep it brief and focus on the systemic consequence rather than re-deriving the event.

In February 2022, after Russia invaded Ukraine, the West froze roughly \$300 billion of the Russian central bank's foreign reserves — the portion held in dollars, euros, pounds, and yen. The assets still existed; Russia simply could no longer use them. For the first time, a major power's "safe" reserves were rendered useless not by default or inflation, but by political decision.

Every other reserve manager on earth watched and drew the same lesson. The safety of a reserve asset, they realized, is not just about whether the issuer can *pay* — the US can always pay, by printing — but about whether the issuer will *let you use it*. A US Treasury bond is a wonderful investment right up until the day Washington decides you are an adversary. And the list of countries that could imagine themselves on the wrong side of that decision is long: China, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, India, and a dozen others have all, quietly or loudly, concluded that *concentration risk in any single rival's currency is now a national-security problem*. This is the deepest crack in the dollar's dominance — not economic, but political. You cannot un-ring that bell.

It is worth being precise about *why* this changed the calculus so completely, because the freeze was not the first US sanction in history. The US had cut Iran, North Korea, and others off from the dollar system for years. What was new in 2022 was the *target*: a G20 central bank, holding the kind of "safe" Western government bonds that every reserve manager treats as the bedrock of the system. The message reservists heard was not "don't be Iran." It was "the rules can change, and the assets you thought were untouchable are touchable." A risk that had been treated as effectively zero — *my own reserves being frozen* — was suddenly a number greater than zero, and in reserve management even a tiny probability of total loss dominates the decision, because the whole point of reserves is to be there when you need them most. The discussion in boardrooms from Riyadh to Jakarta shifted from "how do we maximize the yield on our dollars" to "how much of our national savings do we want sitting inside a system a rival can switch off." That is a question with only one tidy answer: hold some of it in an asset no rival issues.

Notice, too, the asymmetry the freeze exposed. The countries doing the freezing were the West; the countries most exposed to *future* freezes were the non-West. So the de-dollarization impulse is concentrated precisely where reserve growth is fastest — emerging markets, the Gulf, Asia — while the West, which holds the most gold already and faces the least freeze risk, has little reason to change. That asymmetry is why de-dollarization and the gold bid both come overwhelmingly from the emerging world, and why the trend has so much structural room left, as figure 5 will make concrete.

## The de-dollarization moves: what countries are actually doing

So what *are* these countries doing about it? Here it helps enormously to separate the genuine progress from the theater. Figure 6 lays out the toolkit and — just as importantly — what each tool cannot do.

![De-dollarization toolkit local-currency invoicing CBDC bridges gold settlement and barter with limits](/imgs/blogs/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback-6.png)

### BRICS expansion: the political container for the shift

Start with the headline grabber, because it needs deflating before it can be understood. "BRICS" began as an investment-bank acronym (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) and hardened into an actual political grouping that, from 2024, expanded to include the United Arab Emirates, Iran, Egypt, Ethiopia, and others, with a long queue of applicants behind them. The expanded bloc now represents a large share of the world's population, a rising share of its GDP, and — critically for our story — a commanding share of its oil and commodity production and its central-bank gold buying.

What BRICS *is*, for the purposes of de-dollarization, is a political container: a forum where countries that share a wariness of dollar dependence can coordinate the unglamorous plumbing — payment links, local-currency agreements, a mooted multilateral development bank — without anyone having to sign up to a grand monetary union. What BRICS is *not* is a currency project. There is no BRICS currency, gold-backed or otherwise, and the bloc's own members have repeatedly said as much. Why not? Because the members are not friends. India and China are strategic rivals with a contested border; Russia and the Gulf states compete in oil; Brazil's interests overlap with none of them. A shared currency requires a shared central bank and the surrender of domestic monetary control, and a group this internally divided will never surrender that to a body the others control. So the right way to read BRICS is as the *political expression* of the multipolar money order — the place where the blocs in the cover figure actually talk — not as a unified challenger currency. The substance is in the bilateral plumbing, which is what the rest of this section covers.

### Local-currency trade invoicing

The most concrete and fastest-moving change is bilateral trade settled in local currencies instead of dollars. India buys Russian oil in roubles and rupees and UAE dirhams. China settles a growing share of its trade — with Russia, with the Gulf, with parts of Africa and Latin America — in yuan. By some estimates roughly half of China's *own* cross-border transactions are now in yuan, up from almost nothing a decade ago.

This genuinely attacks job one (medium of exchange). But it runs into a wall that explains why it cannot, by itself, dethrone the dollar: the **surplus problem**. When India pays Russia in rupees, Russia accumulates rupees it must then *spend on something Indian* — and Russia doesn't want that many Indian goods. The famous "rupee glut" of 2023 saw billions of rupees pile up in Russian accounts with nowhere to go, because the rupee is not freely convertible into anything else. Local-currency trade works beautifully when it balances; it jams the moment one side runs a surplus, because the surplus currency is not a store of value anyone else will take. This is precisely the gap gold fills, and we will get there.

### Non-dollar payment rails and CBDCs

The second move is building plumbing that does not route through the US. The dollar's power is not just the currency — it is the *rails*: the SWIFT messaging network and the dollar-clearing system, both ultimately reachable by US authorities. So China built CIPS (its own cross-border payment system), Russia built SPFS after being threatened with SWIFT exclusion, and a clutch of central banks built **mBridge** — a shared digital-currency platform connecting China, Hong Kong, the UAE, Thailand, and (as an observer) Saudi Arabia, letting them settle cross-border payments in central-bank digital currencies in seconds, entirely off the dollar system.

These rails are real and growing. But notice what they do and don't solve. A payment rail moves *whatever currency you put on it* faster and outside US reach. It does **not** make anyone trust the currency riding on the rail. mBridge can settle a yuan payment in seconds — but if the recipient doesn't want to *hold* yuan, the speed is irrelevant. The rails attack jobs one and two; they do nothing for job three.

### Gold settlement and accumulation

The third move is the quiet one, and the one this whole series is about: settling surpluses in gold, and accumulating gold reserves at home. When two countries can't agree on whose currency to hold, they can settle the *net* in gold — an asset both will accept because neither issues it. And a central bank worried about freezes can convert reserves into bullion held in its own vault, beyond any foreign government's reach. This is the only de-dollarization tool that solves the store-of-value problem, which is why it sits underneath all the others.

But be honest about gold's own limits here too, because they explain why gold complements the other tools rather than replacing them. Gold is a *clumsy* settlement medium. It is physically heavy — a tonne of it is worth hundreds of millions of dollars but must be assayed, transported under guard, and stored in a vault with real costs. You cannot settle the millions of small daily transactions of a modern economy in metal; the friction would be absurd. What gold *can* do, and does brilliantly, is settle the *large, infrequent net imbalances* between distrustful parties and *store* the value of surpluses over years. So in the toolkit of figure 6, gold occupies a specific niche: it is the store-of-value and final-settlement layer, sitting beneath the fast, light, currency-and-rails layer that handles everyday flow. The two are not competitors. A working multipolar system needs both — a fast payment layer for the flow, and a neutral anchor for the store — which is precisely why central-bank gold buying *accompanies* the rise of CIPS, mBridge, and local-currency trade instead of being made redundant by them. Gold is not trying to be the new dollar of daily commerce. It is trying to be the new neutral reserve underneath it.

#### Worked example: a bilateral trade settled in gold versus dollars

Suppose Country A sells \$10 billion of oil to Country B over a year, and B sells only \$6 billion of goods back. In the old system, B pays A \$10 billion and A pays B \$6 billion, both in dollars routed through New York — and the \$4 billion net surplus piles up in A's account as US Treasuries, freezable at the stroke of a pen.

Now settle the net in gold instead. The \$4 billion surplus, at a gold price of \$5,400 per troy ounce, is \$4,000,000,000 ÷ \$5,400 ≈ **740,740 troy ounces**, or about **23.0 tonnes** (740,740 ÷ 32,151 oz per tonne). Country A ships or allocates 23 tonnes of bullion to settle the year's imbalance. The trade flowed in local currency; the *surplus* was stored in an asset no one can freeze. The intuition: gold doesn't have to replace the dollar in *every* transaction — it only has to absorb the *net imbalances* that no rival currency can safely hold.

### Barter and swap lines

The fourth tool is the crudest: direct barter (oil for goods) and central-bank currency-swap lines. These keep trade alive under sanctions but cannot scale — barter needs a "double coincidence of wants" (each side must want exactly what the other offers), which is the very problem money was invented to solve. They are a workaround, not a system.

Put the four tools together and a pattern emerges. Local-currency trade and new rails handle the *flow* of money; only gold handles the *store* of value. That is why central-bank gold buying runs *alongside* every de-dollarization initiative rather than instead of it — and why the buying has reached the levels it has.

## Why gold, and not the yuan or the euro

Here is the question the dollar-collapse narrative always botches. If the world is moving away from the dollar, *what is it moving to*? The instinctive answer — "the next-biggest economy's currency" — is wrong, and understanding why it's wrong is the key to the whole story.

![Neutrality scorecard comparing the dollar a rival currency and gold across four reserve-manager tests](/imgs/blogs/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback-4.png)

Figure 4 runs the dollar, a rival currency (euro or yuan), and gold through the four tests a reserve manager actually applies.

**Is it anyone's liability?** A US Treasury is an IOU from Washington. A euro bond or deposit is an IOU from a European government or bank. A yuan asset is an IOU from China. Every currency reserve is *someone's promise to pay* — and a promise can be broken, inflated, or revoked. Gold alone is no one's liability: a bar in your vault owes nothing to anyone, so there is no one to default on you.

**Can it be frozen?** The dollar can — 2022 proved it. So can the euro; Europe froze its share of Russia's reserves alongside the US. And so could the yuan: Beijing could freeze the holdings of a country that crossed it just as readily as Washington did. A reserve manager fleeing the *risk of being frozen* gains nothing by swapping a dollar (freezable by Washington) for a yuan (freezable by Beijing). Gold held in your own vault is freezable by no foreign power.

**Does a rival government control its supply?** The Fed prints dollars; if the US is your geopolitical rival, your reserves' value sits in your rival's hands. But the same is true in reverse for the yuan — and *more* so, because the People's Bank of China is even more tightly state-controlled and the yuan is not freely convertible. China deliberately restricts how freely the yuan crosses its borders (capital controls), which is fatal for a reserve currency: you cannot make a currency the world's safe asset while also refusing to let it move freely. The yuan fails the reserve test on its own terms. Gold is mined, not printed; no government controls its issuance.

**Is the market deep and liquid?** Here the dollar wins outright and gold is merely adequate (deep, but pays no yield). The euro is liquid; the yuan is not. But notice: the deepest, most liquid market in the world is the *one you're trying to escape*. There is no liquid alternative currency that is also neutral.

It is worth taking the two leading "successor" candidates in turn, because the headlines treat them as plausible and they are not. **The yuan** is the one the de-dollarization narrative leans on hardest, and it has a fatal, self-imposed flaw: China does not allow money to move freely across its borders. To be the world's reserve currency, the yuan would have to be something a Saudi or Brazilian central bank could buy and sell in unlimited size, at any time, with no permission required — which would mean China relinquishing control over its capital account and its exchange rate, the two levers Beijing guards most jealously for managing its own economy. China wants the *benefits* of reserve status (prestige, cheaper borrowing, influence) without the *cost* (giving up monetary control), and you cannot have one without the other. Until Beijing chooses openness over control — which it has shown no intention of doing — the yuan is a trade-settlement currency at best, not a reserve currency. A reserve manager who cannot get her money *out* of an asset on demand will not put the nation's savings *into* it.

**The euro** is the more credible candidate — it is liquid, freely convertible, and backed by a large bloc. But it carries its own structural flaw: there is no single European safe asset. The dollar's reserve role rests on one enormous, unified Treasury market. The euro has no equivalent; instead it has German bunds, French OATs, Italian BTPs, and the rest — separate national bonds with different credit ratings and a recurring fear that the monetary union could fracture (as the 2010-2012 sovereign-debt crisis showed). You cannot park the world's savings in a "safe asset" that is really nineteen different governments' debts of varying safety. Until Europe issues a large, unified, jointly-backed safe asset — which would require a degree of fiscal union the member states have refused for decades — the euro is structurally capped as a reserve currency. Its ~20% share has barely moved in twenty years for exactly this reason.

So both candidates fail, and they fail for the *same underlying reason* gold succeeds: a reserve asset must be something every holder can trust and use without permission from a power that might become a rival. The yuan fails on permission; the euro fails on the absence of a unified asset; both fail on neutrality. Gold needs no one's permission and is no one's to fracture.

The conclusion is stark and is the heart of the post. **No rival currency can replace the dollar as the neutral reserve, because every rival currency is some other rival's to control and freeze.** A multipolar world of mutually distrustful blocs cannot agree on *whose* paper to hold — so it agrees on *no one's*. Gold doesn't win because it is better money than the dollar in normal times; it isn't (it pays no interest and is clumsy to move). Gold wins because it is the only candidate that is *neutral*, and in a low-trust order neutrality beats every other property. This is the same logic the companion post on [the apolitical asset](/blog/trading/gold/why-gold-still-matters-a-5000-year-store-of-value) draws out: gold's killer feature is the absence of a counterparty.

## Is gold re-monetizing? The evidence — and the honest limits

"Re-monetizing" is a loaded word, so let me define it precisely and then steelman both the case for and against.

To **re-monetize** does not mean "back currencies with gold again" (that is the gold standard, which we'll cover is not coming). It means something narrower: gold is reclaiming part of its old *monetary* role — as a settlement asset and a reserve store of value — rather than being treated, as it was from roughly 1980 to 2010, as just another commodity that central banks were happy to sell. Is that happening? Look at the evidence.

It helps to remember how *de*-monetized gold became in the era we are leaving. From the 1980s through the 2000s, the conventional wisdom in central banking was that gold was a barbarous relic: it paid no interest, cost money to store, and just sat there while Treasuries earned a yield. Under formal agreements, European central banks coordinated the *sale* of thousands of tonnes; the IMF debated dumping its hoard; and the smart-money consensus was that the metal's monetary days were finished. Gold had been pushed almost entirely into the "commodity" box — priced like copper or oil, driven by jewelry demand and speculative flows, with its old role as money treated as a historical curiosity. Re-monetization is the *reversal* of that fifty-year drift: gold climbing back out of the commodity box and into the monetary one, in the eyes of the institutions that matter most for that classification — the central banks themselves. The whole question is whether the post-2022 behavior is a blip or a regime change. The weight of the evidence, below, says regime change.

### The evidence for

The single clearest piece of evidence is in figure 3: central banks have flipped from net sellers to record buyers.

![Central bank net gold purchases turned from selling in the 1990s to over 1000 tonnes a year after 2022](/imgs/blogs/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback-3.png)

For most of the 1990s and 2000s, central banks were net *sellers* of gold — dumping it under the Central Bank Gold Agreements, treating it as a relic. Britain famously sold half its gold near the absolute bottom in 1999-2002 ("Brown's Bottom"). Then the regime turned. Central banks became modest net buyers through the 2010s (around 470 tonnes a year), and after the 2022 freeze, buying exploded: **1,082 tonnes in 2022** (the most since records began in 1950), followed by roughly 1,037 in 2023 and 1,045 in 2024. The companion post [central banks: the structural buyer that changed gold after 2022](/blog/trading/gold/central-banks-the-structural-buyer-that-changed-gold-after-2022) covers the *mechanics* of this demand in depth; the point *here* is what it signifies. A central bank that buys gold whatever the price is treating it as money, not as a speculative bet. That is re-monetization in action.

The second piece of evidence is the milestone in this post's hook. Counting *total* reserves — currencies plus bullion — gold has now overtaken the euro to become the world's second-largest reserve asset, a position it last held in the mid-1990s. The metal the West spent the 1990s selling is now, by market value, a bigger chunk of global reserves than the entire euro project.

The third is **repatriation**: countries physically moving their gold home. Germany brought hundreds of tonnes back from New York and Paris in the 2010s; others have quietly done the same since 2022. Gold stored in *someone else's* vault is only as safe as the host's goodwill — the same lesson 2022 taught about bonds. Bringing the bars home turns a foreign-held claim into a sovereign asset no one can freeze. The very fact that repatriation has become a *trend* — rather than a one-off quirk of German politics — is itself a signal: central banks are no longer willing to take the location of their own gold on trust, which tells you how much trust has drained out of the system.

A fourth, subtler piece of evidence is *where* the buying is concentrated. This is not a uniform global trend; it is overwhelmingly an emerging-market and Gulf-state phenomenon. Poland was among the largest buyers of the 2020s, raising gold toward a target of around 20% of reserves. China has been adding to its official holdings month after month (and is widely believed to hold more than it officially reports). Turkey, India, Singapore, the Czech Republic, and a roster of smaller central banks have all been steady accumulators. The pattern matches the freeze-risk map almost exactly: the countries buying the most are the ones most exposed to the discovery that their currency reserves could be switched off. When the *motivation* for buying lines up that cleanly with the *geopolitical* exposure, you are no longer looking at a portfolio decision — you are looking at a defensive monetary realignment.

#### Worked example: if BRICS central banks moved toward 20% gold reserves

This is the quantification that makes the "room to buy" concrete. Figure 5 shows the gap.

![Gold share of reserves is about 70 percent in the West but single digits for China and India](/imgs/blogs/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback-5.png)

The Western reserve powers already hold roughly 70% of their reserves in gold (US 72%, Germany 71%, France 70%, Italy 68%). The big emerging holders hold far less: Russia 29%, India 11%, China just 6%. That gap is the structural fuel behind the buying.

Take China. China holds officially about \$3.2 trillion in reserves, of which only ~6% — call it \$190 billion — is gold. Suppose China decided to raise gold to **20%** of reserves, still far below the Western norm. Twenty percent of \$3.2 trillion is \$640 billion; it already has \$190 billion, so it would need to *add* about \$450 billion of gold. At \$5,400 per ounce, that is \$450,000,000,000 ÷ \$5,400 ≈ 83.3 million ounces, or about **2,590 tonnes** (83.3M ÷ 32,151). That is more than China's entire current official holding, and over *two years* of total global central-bank buying at the recent pace — from one country, to reach a share the West already exceeds. The intuition: the re-monetization trend has years of structural demand left simply in the gap between what emerging markets hold today and what the developed world already considers normal.

### The honest limits — steelmanning the skeptic

Now the other side, because an honest synthesis has to give the skeptic their best shot.

**The dollar is genuinely sticky, and the trend is slow.** As the worked example on pace showed, even an unbroken continuation of the decline leaves the dollar dominant for decades. There is no liquid alternative, and liquidity is destiny.

**Gold is a *terrible* daily currency.** It pays no yield, it is physically heavy and slow to move, and it cannot be the medium of exchange for a \$100-trillion world economy. Even at the heart of the bull case, gold is settling *net imbalances* and *storing value* — not replacing the dollar in the billions of daily transactions that actually run the world. Re-monetization is partial by nature.

**Central-bank buying could slow.** The 2022-24 surge ran around 1,000 tonnes a year; 2025 came in lighter (about 863 tonnes). The price-insensitive bid is real but not infinite, and a sharp enough rally could make even committed buyers pause. Calling a one-way structural trend off three record years is exactly the kind of extrapolation this post warns against in the other direction.

**Most "BRICS currency" talk is theater.** There is no gold-backed BRICS currency. The bloc has discussed local-currency trade and shared rails, not a new gold-pegged unit — and it is internally divided (India and China are rivals, not partners). De-dollarization is a thousand bilateral defensive moves, not a coordinated assault.

Hold both truths at once. Gold *is* re-monetizing — the buying, the milestone, the repatriation are facts. And it is doing so *slowly and partially*, in its narrow ancient role as the neutral settlement-and-storage asset, not as a new global currency. The breathless version and the dismissive version are both wrong; the truth is the unglamorous middle.

## The realistic endgame

So where does this actually lead? Figure 7 lays out the three possible endgames and, importantly, assigns a sense of likelihood to each.

![Endgame spectrum dollar status quo multipolar with bigger gold anchor as central case or full gold standard](/imgs/blogs/the-multipolar-money-order-brics-de-dollarization-and-golds-comeback-7.png)

**Endgame 1: dollar status quo.** The dollar stays at 55-60% of reserves, gold remains a hedge at the margins, and not much changes. *Possible* — the dollar's stickiness is real — but the two-decade trend and the post-2022 trigger both argue against a clean reversion. What would have to happen: real yields stay high enough to make non-yielding gold expensive to hold, *and* the geopolitical blocs reconcile so the freeze risk fades. Neither looks likely today.

**Endgame 2: multipolar order with a bigger gold anchor (the central case).** The dollar slips gradually toward, say, 45-50% of reserves over a decade or two; trade fragments into overlapping blocs; non-dollar rails carry a growing share of payments; and gold climbs to 15-20%+ of global reserves as the neutral anchor every bloc holds. The dollar is *crowded*, not *replaced*. This is the outcome the data is actually pointing at, and the one I'd bet on. What confirms it: central-bank gold buying staying elevated (1,000-ish tonnes a year), the dollar's COFER share continuing its drift, and emerging markets closing the gap toward the Western 70% norm.

**Endgame 3: a full gold standard.** Currencies legally re-pegged to gold, convertibility restored. *Very unlikely.* No government wants its hands tied that way — the whole reason states abandoned gold (covered in the companion post on the gold standard's death) is that a metallic anchor strips them of the power to print in a crisis, and no modern state will voluntarily re-impose that straitjacket. A gold-backed BRICS currency would face the same wall: it would require the bloc to subordinate domestic monetary policy to a shared metal, which China and India have no intention of doing.

#### Worked example: the gold-versus-euro reserve crossover in numbers

This makes the hook concrete. Total global reserves run on the order of \$15 trillion. The euro's share of *currency* reserves is about 19.8% (IMF COFER), but COFER measures only the currency slice. Scale it to total reserves — currencies plus gold — and the euro works out to roughly 16% of the *total*. Gold's share of total reserves, by market value, has climbed to roughly 18-20% on the back of both record buying *and* a tripling of the gold price since 2019. Somewhere in 2024, gold's slice (~18-20%) crossed back above the euro's (~16%) for the first time since the mid-1990s. The intuition: gold re-took second place not through one mechanism but two at once — central banks bought more of it, *and* each ounce they already held became worth far more. The world voted with both its purchases and its prices.

The whole spectrum points one way for the asset itself. You do not need a dollar collapse or a new gold standard for gold to keep re-monetizing. You only need the central case — the slow drift into a multipolar order — which is already underway. That is what makes this, in my view, the most durable bull thesis in the gold complex: it does not require a catastrophe, only a continuation.

## Common misconceptions

This topic breeds more bad takes than almost any in markets. Here are the four worth correcting with numbers.

**Misconception 1: "The dollar is about to collapse."** No. The dollar is 57.8% of reserves, falling about 0.6 points a year. At that pace it stays above 50% into the late 2030s. There is no liquid alternative for the world's savings — the Treasury market alone is ~\$28 trillion and nothing else comes close. De-dollarization is a multi-decade *drift*, and anyone selling you an imminent collapse is selling fear. The honest claim is "slow erosion," not "collapse."

**Misconception 2: "The BRICS launched (or are about to launch) a gold-backed currency."** They have not, and almost certainly will not. What BRICS has actually discussed is *local-currency trade* and *non-dollar payment rails* — not a new gold-pegged unit. A shared gold-backed currency would force China, India, Russia, and the rest to subordinate their domestic monetary policy to a common metal, which rivals who barely trust each other will not do. The "gold-backed BRICS currency" is a recurring headline, not a plan.

**Misconception 3: "The gold standard is coming back."** No government wants it. A gold standard removes the ability to print in a crisis — exactly the power every modern state guards most jealously. Gold re-*monetizing* (as a bigger reserve and settlement asset) is real; gold becoming the *legal anchor of currencies again* is not. Don't confuse the two.

**Misconception 4: "Central-bank gold buying is a coordinated plan to dethrone the dollar."** It is the opposite — thousands of *uncoordinated*, defensive, self-interested decisions. Russia buys to escape sanctions; China buys to diversify; Poland and Turkey and Singapore buy for their own reasons. The buying *adds up* to a de-dollarization trend, but it is emergent, not directed. That actually makes it *more* durable, not less: a coordinated plan can be called off; a thousand independent decisions converging on the same conclusion cannot.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Here is where the multipolar money order leaves fingerprints you can actually see.

**The COFER releases.** Four times a year the IMF publishes the currency composition of disclosed reserves. The dollar's slow grind from 71.5% (2001) to 57.8% (2024) is the single best real-time gauge of de-dollarization — and the most sobering corrective to the collapse narrative, because it shows how *slow* the move is. Watch the trend, not any one quarter.

**The World Gold Council's quarterly demand data.** This is where the central-bank bid shows up: 1,082 tonnes in 2022, ~1,037 in 2023, ~1,045 in 2024, ~863 in 2025. When this number stays above ~1,000 tonnes, the structural floor under gold is intact; if it fell back toward the pre-2022 average of ~470 tonnes for a sustained stretch, that would be the first sign the re-monetization thesis was stalling. It hasn't yet.

**Repatriation announcements.** When a central bank announces it is bringing gold home from London or New York — as several have since 2022 — read it as a vote of no-confidence in the safety of foreign-held reserves, currency or metal. Each announcement is a small data point in the same story.

**The widening gap between official and physical gold flows.** A more technical tell, but a revealing one: in the post-2022 period, large volumes of physical gold have drained from Western vaults (London, New York) toward the East and the Gulf, even on days when the paper gold price was soft. When physical metal moves one way while the paper market shrugs, it usually means a price-insensitive official buyer is taking delivery rather than trading exposure — the signature of reserve accumulation, not speculation. You will not see this in the gold price; you see it in the vault and customs data, which is exactly why it gets missed.

**The gold price defying real yields.** The clearest market fingerprint of all. The old rule (covered in [real interest rates, the master variable behind the gold price](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price)) said high real yields should sink gold, because a non-yielding asset looks bad when bonds pay well. Yet from 2022 onward, gold rose *while* real yields climbed to +2%. The thing breaking the old rule is precisely the price-insensitive, geopolitically-motivated central-bank bid this post is about. When you see gold rally in the teeth of rising real rates, you are watching the multipolar money order reprice the metal in real time.

**Gold rising in every currency at once.** When gold makes new highs not just in dollars but in euros, yen, rupees, and dong simultaneously, that is not a dollar story — it is a *vote against paper money in general*. A purely dollar-driven gold move would leave gold flat in other currencies; a monetary-order move lifts it everywhere. The post-2022 surge has been the latter, which is the tell that something structural, not cyclical, is underway. The [dollar system post](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) and the [petrodollar post](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance) both trace why a dollar that dominates oil and trade pricing is harder to dethrone than it looks — the necessary backdrop for reading these gold moves correctly.

## The takeaway: own the drift, not the catastrophe

Step back and the whole picture resolves into one sentence. **The world is fragmenting into monetary blocs that trust no single currency, and gold — issued by no one — is the neutral anchor they are all, independently, reaching for.** BRICS expansion, local-currency trade, non-dollar rails, repatriation, and record central-bank buying are not five separate stories. They are five facets of one shift: a multipolar money order in which gold is *partially* re-monetizing, in its ancient narrow role as the settlement asset of last resort between parties who distrust each other's paper.

For how you actually think about and hold gold, three things follow.

First, **the bull case for gold no longer depends on a crisis.** The classic reasons to own gold — falling real yields, a financial panic, an inflation scare — are cyclical; they come and go. The de-dollarization bid is *structural*: a persistent, price-insensitive buyer underneath the market that exists because of geopolitics, not the business cycle. That is a different and more durable kind of support, and it is why gold has behaved differently since 2022 than the old playbook predicted.

Second, **don't trade the hype, trade the drift.** The catastrophe scenarios — dollar collapse, gold-backed BRICS currency, return of the gold standard — are unlikely and, frankly, distractions. You do not need any of them. You need only the central case: the slow, one-directional fragmentation that is already measurable in the COFER data and the WGC buying. Position for the glacier, not the avalanche. A modest, patient gold allocation captures the structural shift; betting the house on an imminent collapse captures mostly your own fear.

Third — and this is where this post rejoins the spine of the whole series — **what you are really buying is neutrality.** Gold pays no dividend and compounds nothing. It is not an investment in the ordinary sense. It is a monetary insurance policy, and what it insures against, in a multipolar world, is the discovery that *every* currency is somebody's to freeze, debase, or weaponize. The 2022 freeze taught the world's central banks that lesson in a single morning, and they responded by buying the one asset that is no one's liability. For the first time since the 1990s they now hold more of it than they hold euros. That is not a prediction of the next monetary order. It is the quiet beginning of it — and you can read it, in real numbers, right now.

If you want the practical, portfolio-sized version of that thought, it is worth being modest and concrete. The structural bid does not tell you to put everything in gold; it tells you why a *standing* allocation — the kind the [cross-asset framing of gold as insurance](/blog/trading/cross-asset/gold-money-insurance-or-just-a-rock) lays out — makes more sense in a fragmenting world than it did in the calm, unipolar 2010s. A central bank holds gold not because it expects to spend it next year, but because it wants an asset that still works on the day nothing else does. The individual saver is, in miniature, in exactly the same position: a slice of gold is the part of the portfolio that does not depend on any government keeping its promises. In a unipolar world where one government's promises ran everything, that slice could be small. In a multipolar world where the blocs in the cover figure trust each other less every year, it rationally grows. That is not a doom forecast. It is just reading the same scorecard the world's central banks are reading — and, quietly, acting on it the same way they are.

To put it one last way: the strongest evidence for this whole thesis is not an argument, it is a *behavior*. The institutions with the most information, the longest time horizons, and the most at stake — the world's central banks, including those of America's rivals — looked at the post-2022 world and voted with their vaults. They are buying gold faster than at any time in living memory, and they are no longer leaving it in each other's basements. When the people who run the monetary system itself start quietly hedging against that system, the rest of us should at least understand why. That "why" is the multipolar money order, and gold is its anchor.
