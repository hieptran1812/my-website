> **TL;DR** — A memecoin rug is not one event; it is a sequence of on-chain red flags, and every single one is visible *before* the rug, if you look.
>
> - This post walks a **representative, composite** rug from contract deployment to the liquidity pull — placeholder addresses, illustrative numbers labelled as such, grounded in the extremely common documented pattern.
> - The flags appear in order: a fresh deployer funded from a mixer, an unverified contract with live owner powers, a bundled launch faking distribution, a pump on unlocked liquidity, then the dev dump and the LP pull.
> - What you DO with it: run the check that matches each stage *before* you buy. Any one hard flag is a stop. This is pure defender framing — recognising a rug so you avoid the loss, not a how-to.
> - The one number to remember: on a permissionless launchpad only **~1.4%** of tokens ever reach a meaningful market cap (`PUMPFUN_SURVIVE_PCT`). The base rate is "goes to zero," so skipping an unscreened token is the rational default.

In a single hour on a busy day, a permissionless launchpad like Pump.fun can mint thousands of new Solana tokens. Across 2024–2025 the cumulative count climbed into the millions, and the public dashboards that track them tell the same brutal story every month: the overwhelming majority go to zero. Only on the order of **1.4%** ever reach a market cap anyone would call "meaningful." That is not a market with a few bad apples. That is a market where the bad outcome *is* the base rate, and a good outcome is the rare exception you have to actively screen for.

That makes the memecoin the prime vehicle for the rug pull — a scam where the team builds just enough of a token and a chart to attract buyers, then extracts the buyers' money and vanishes. The mechanics are old, but the chain makes them newly legible: every deploy, every funding hop, every bundled buy, and every liquidity withdrawal is a public, permanent transaction. The scammer's entire operation is written down in advance, in a ledger anyone can read. The only edge you need is the discipline to read it before you click "buy."

This post does exactly that. We will follow one **representative, composite** memecoin — call its ticker `$RUGCOIN`, deployed by a placeholder address we will write as `0xF1a2…` — from the moment it is deployed to the moment its liquidity is pulled. It is not a specific real token; the addresses are placeholders and the dollar figures are illustrative. But the *pattern* is real and documented thousands of times over, and so is every red flag. We will surface each flag in the order it appeared on-chain, name the free tool that catches it, and translate it into money. By the end you will have a stage-by-stage checklist that would have stopped the buy at any point along the way.

## Foundations: rug types, what an LP pull does, and the base rate

Before we walk the timeline, four pieces of vocabulary. If you have read [rug-pull and honeypot detection](/blog/trading/onchain/rug-pull-and-honeypot-detection) you can skim this; otherwise, build the model from zero here.

**A memecoin** is a token with no cash flows, no product, and no claim on anything — its price is pure narrative and attention. That is not automatically a scam; some memecoins are honest jokes that trade on community. But the absence of fundamentals means there is nothing to value, so the *only* thing protecting a buyer is the integrity of the contract and the people behind it. When those are absent, the memecoin becomes the perfect wrapper for a rug: cheap to deploy, easy to hype, and disposable once the money is out.

**A rug pull** is a scam where insiders extract the value buyers put in and abandon the token. There are four common shapes, and a single token can combine them:

- **Liquidity pull.** The team removes the paired asset (the ETH or SOL) from the trading pool. With nothing left to sell into, the price collapses toward zero. This is the classic "rug," and it is what `$RUGCOIN` ends with.
- **Mint-and-dump.** The owner prints new supply to their own wallet and sells it into your bids, diluting every other holder toward nothing.
- **Honeypot.** The contract lets you *buy* (so the chart looks healthy) but blocks or fully taxes your *sell*, so only the owner can exit. You are trapped from the moment you buy.
- **Slow rug.** No single dramatic event — the team bleeds the token through fees, "marketing" wallets, and quiet dribble-selling over weeks until it dies. Harder to spot precisely because there is no smoking gun.

**A liquidity pool (LP)** is the mechanism that lets you trade a memecoin at all. On a decentralised exchange (DEX) using an automated market maker (AMM) — Uniswap on Ethereum, Raydium on Solana — there is no order book. Instead there is a pool holding two assets, say the token and ETH, and a formula sets the price from the *ratio* of the two. The canonical formula is the constant product: `x · y = k`, where `x` is the amount of ETH in the pool, `y` is the amount of token, and `k` is held constant. The price of the token in ETH is roughly `x / y`. (For the full mechanics, see [reading DEX liquidity and pools](/blog/trading/onchain/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools) and the primer on [DeFi protocols](/blog/trading/crypto/defi-protocols-uniswap-aave-makerdao).)

The crucial consequence: **whoever controls the LP tokens controls the pool.** When you add liquidity you receive "LP tokens" — a receipt for your share. Whoever holds those receipts can withdraw the underlying assets. If the team holds the LP tokens (the receipts are sitting in the deployer wallet, neither locked in a time-lock contract nor burned to a dead address), then one `removeLiquidity` call empties the pool. Take the ETH side `x` to (near) zero and the token's price `x / y` goes to (near) zero with it. That single transaction is the rug. Everything before it is the setup.

**The base rate** is why none of this should surprise you, and why screening is rational rather than paranoid. The figure below is the only piece of real data in this post: of every 1,000 tokens launched on a permissionless launchpad, roughly **14** ever reach a meaningful market cap and roughly **986** rug, honeypot, slow-bleed, or simply die. When the prior probability of a bad outcome is ~98.6%, the expected value of buying an *unscreened* token is deeply negative before you have looked at anything specific. The whole point of a checklist is to move you off that base rate by ruling out the obvious traps — and to make "skip it" your default, not "ape and hope."

**Why memecoins are the prime vehicle** deserves a sentence of its own, because it explains why this pattern dominates the rug statistics rather than, say, fake lending protocols or counterfeit NFTs. Three properties stack up. First, **cost**: deploying a token on a launchpad is nearly free — a permissionless launchpad does the contract templating, the pool creation, and the bonding curve for you, so the operator needs only gas and a thin seed. Second, **narrative cover**: a memecoin is *supposed* to have no fundamentals, so the absence of a product, a team, or a roadmap raises no eyebrows — the very thing that would flag a fake "DeFi protocol" is the normal state of a memecoin. Third, **speed of attention**: memecoin culture rewards being early, which compresses the buyer's decision window from days to seconds and makes "I'll check it later" the dominant (and fatal) behaviour. Cheap to make, normal to have nothing behind it, and bought on impulse: that is the perfect substrate for a rug, and it is why the launchpad survivor rate sits near 1%.

**Pseudonymity, not anonymity** is the last foundation, and it is the one that makes everything in this post possible. The deployer `0xF1a2…` is not a name, but it is not a ghost either. Its funding source, its deploy transaction, the wallets it seeds, the buys it bundles, and the ETH it pulls are all permanent public records bound to that address and the addresses it touches. Behaviour, timing, and funding links *deanonymise* an operator over time — which is exactly why a defender can read a rug's whole shape in advance, and why a serial rugger leaves a fingerprint that flags their next launch. The scammer's reliance on you *not looking* is the only privacy they have.

![Bar chart showing about 14 of every 1,000 launched tokens survive versus about 986 that fail](/imgs/blogs/case-study-a-memecoin-rug-from-deploy-to-exit-7.png)

#### Worked example: why the base rate alone says skip

Say you ape \$1,000 into a *random* freshly launched token with no screening. Treat the base rate literally: ~1.4% reach a meaningful cap, ~98.6% go to roughly zero. Be generous and assume the survivors average a 10× return (\$1,000 → \$10,000) while the failures lose 95% (\$1,000 → \$50). The expected value of one such bet is `0.014 × \$10,000 + 0.986 × \$50 = \$140 + \$49.30 = \$189.30` — you stake \$1,000 to get back, on average, about **\$189**. Even with a flattering 10× upside assumption you lose roughly **81 cents on the dollar** per unscreened bet. The intuition: you cannot out-trade a 98.6% failure rate with entries; the only lever that moves the number is *not buying the obvious traps*, which is exactly what the rest of this post teaches.

With the model in place, here is the chronology. Each section below is one stage in `$RUGCOIN`'s life, the red flag it produced, and the free check that catches it.

## Stage 1 — Deploy: a fresh, mixer-funded wallet ships an unverified contract (flag 1)

Every token starts with a deployment transaction. Someone — an externally owned account, or **EOA**, meaning an ordinary key-controlled wallet rather than a contract — sends a transaction that creates the token contract on-chain. That deployer wallet has a *history*, and the very first thing a defender reads is that history, because the rug's first red flag is written there before a single buy is possible.

For `$RUGCOIN` the deployer is `0xF1a2…`. Pull its history on a block explorer (Etherscan for EVM, Solscan for Solana) and you see something stark: the wallet's *first-ever transaction* is the funding that paid for the deployment. There is no prior activity, no reputation, no track record — it was born minutes ago for this one purpose. That alone is a yellow flag (fresh wallets deploy plenty of honest tokens too). What turns it red is *where the funding came from.*

Trace one hop back from the funding transaction and the source is a **mixer** — on Ethereum, historically Tornado Cash; on other chains, an equivalent privacy pool — or a **fresh centralised-exchange (CEX) withdrawal** with no on-chain KYC trail you can follow. A mixer exists to break the link between a deposit and a withdrawal, so its presence in a deployer's funding path is a deliberate choice to hide where the money came from. (For how mixers work and why their *presence* is itself a signal, see [mixers, CoinJoin and obfuscation](/blog/trading/onchain/mixers-coinjoin-and-obfuscation) and [Tornado Cash and sanctioning code](/blog/trading/crypto/tornado-cash-and-sanctioning-code).)

The deployer used that ~1 ETH to deploy the contract — and the contract is **unverified**. "Verified" means the team published the Solidity source code and the explorer confirmed it compiles to the deployed bytecode, so anyone can read exactly what the contract does. An unverified contract publishes only opaque bytecode: you cannot read its rules without reverse-engineering them. A team that wants your trust verifies in thirty seconds; a team that wants to hide a trap does not.

So the first red flag is a *composite* of three cheap observations, all in block one:

1. The deployer is fresh — its first transaction is this funding.
2. It is funded from a mixer (or an untraceable fresh CEX withdrawal) — the money's origin is deliberately obscured.
3. The contract it deployed is unverified — its rules are deliberately hidden.

![Graph of a mixer and fresh CEX funding a fresh deployer that ships an unverified token contract](/imgs/blogs/case-study-a-memecoin-rug-from-deploy-to-exit-2.png)

None of these is illegal, and none alone proves a scam. But the *combination* — fresh + obfuscated funding + hidden code — is the single most common opening move of a rug, and it is fully visible before the token can even be bought. The defender's read is simple: this is flag 1, and three flags before launch is already enough to walk away.

It is worth slowing down on *why* the funding hop matters so much, because it is the least intuitive of the three. A new wallet is not suspicious on its own — every honest deployer's wallet was new once. What the funding source tells you is the operator's *intent toward being traced*. If a deployer is funded by a normal hop from a long-lived wallet with a real history, the operator either does not care about being identified or is not planning anything that requires hiding. If the deployer is funded through a mixer — a contract whose entire purpose is to break the link between who deposited and who withdrew — the operator has spent effort and gas specifically to make sure you *cannot* trace the money back to them. People do not pay to obscure the origin of capital they intend to use honestly and publicly. The mixer hop is the operator telling on themselves: it is a revealed preference for non-attribution, placed in the funding path of a token they are about to ask strangers to buy. A fresh CEX withdrawal is a weaker version of the same signal — the KYC exists, but only the exchange can see it, so on-chain the trail still goes cold at the withdrawal. Either way, the question a defender asks is not "is this wallet new?" but "did the operator take a deliberate step to be untraceable before launching a thing they want my money for?" For `$RUGCOIN`, the answer is yes, and that is the whole of flag 1.

There is a subtlety here that keeps you honest: not every mixer withdrawal is a scammer, and not every fresh CEX hop is malicious. Privacy is a legitimate need, and plenty of cautious-but-honest people route funds through privacy tools. That is why flag 1 is a *weight*, not a verdict — it raises the prior that this is a rug, and it combines with the contract powers, the clustering, and the LP status that follow. One flag tilts the odds; the *sequence* of flags is what makes the call. The mistake is treating flag 1 as either ignorable ("lots of new wallets deploy tokens") or conclusive ("mixer means scam, case closed"). It is neither: it is the first reading on a gauge you are going to read four more times.

#### Worked example: the rug's entire budget is one mixer withdrawal

`0xF1a2…` is funded with **1 ETH ≈ \$3,000** from a mixer. That is the whole capital base of the operation. Deploying an ERC-20 or an SPL token and seeding a thin pool costs a tiny fraction of it — call it a few hundred dollars in gas and seed liquidity. So the scammer is risking on the order of **\$3,000** to set a trap that, as we will see, exits with roughly **\$230,000**. That asymmetry — a \$3,000 cost against a \$230,000 take — is *why* the pattern repeats thousands of times: the expected return on running the scam is enormous, which is the mirror image of why your expected return on buying it blind is so negative. The intuition: the mixer hop is not just a privacy choice, it is the receipt for a \$3,000 bet that you will not check the funding before you ape.

## Stage 2 — The contract: mint not renounced, owner can set fees and blacklist (flags 2–3)

Suppose you ignored flag 1, or the funding was laundered cleanly enough that it looked ambiguous. The next stop is the contract itself. Even unverified, much can be inferred; and many rugs *are* verified, because verified code with dangerous powers still rugs people who do not read it. This is where flags 2 and 3 live: the privileged functions the team kept.

A token contract is just code, and the team can write into it functions that only the owner address can call. These are not bugs — they are legal, ordinary Solidity. The danger is that each privileged function is a switch the team can flip against your exit *after* you have bought. The four that matter most:

- **`mint()` not renounced.** If the mint authority is still live, the owner can print new supply to their own wallet, uncapped. Renouncing mint (handing the authority to a dead address so nobody can ever mint again) is the team's way of promising "the supply is fixed." A live mint is a loaded mint-and-dump gun.
- **`blacklist(addr)`.** Lets the owner flag any wallet as unable to send or receive the token. Aimed at a buyer, it is a honeypot on demand: your buy clears, then the owner blacklists you and your sell reverts.
- **`setFee()` with no cap.** Lets the owner change the buy/sell tax after launch, up to 100%. A 99% sell tax means every exit you attempt is confiscated.
- **An upgradeable proxy.** A proxy contract holds the token's state but points to a separate logic contract the team can *swap*. A clean, friendly contract today can be pointed at honeypot logic tomorrow, with no new deployment for you to notice.

![Matrix of dangerous owner powers — mint, blacklist, set fee, and proxy — and how each can rob a buyer](/imgs/blogs/case-study-a-memecoin-rug-from-deploy-to-exit-3.png)

For `$RUGCOIN`, the contract kept the mint authority and a `setFee()` with no upper bound, and shipped behind an upgradeable proxy. That is flags 2 and 3 — mint not renounced (flag 2) and live owner powers over fees/blacklist/upgrade (flag 3). How do you read this without being a Solidity engineer? Free token-safety scanners — GoPlus, Honeypot.is, Token Sniffer, and DEX-screener safety tabs — decompile the bytecode and report exactly these powers as plain-English yes/no flags: *mint renounced?*, *can owner set fees?*, *is it a proxy?*, *honeypot simulation result?* (We turn that into a structured score in [building a token scorecard](/blog/trading/onchain/building-a-token-scorecard).)

The mental shift here is the important one. A buyer asks "is the chart going up?" A defender asks "what can the owner do to me *after* I buy?" The chart is downstream of the contract; the contract is upstream of everything. Read the powers first.

Two of these powers deserve a closer look because buyers routinely underweight them. The **proxy** is the most insidious because it defeats a check you *did* run. Suppose you read the contract today, confirmed mint was renounced and fees were capped, and concluded the code is safe. If the contract is an upgradeable proxy, none of that is binding: the proxy holds the token's balances and state, but the *logic* — the actual rules for transfers, fees, and who can do what — lives in a separate "implementation" contract that the owner can swap with one call. The clean code you read is the implementation the owner *chose to show you*; tomorrow they point the proxy at a new implementation that taxes sells 100% or blacklists everyone but the team, and every balance carries over unchanged. A proxy is not automatically malicious — many serious protocols use upgradeable proxies, guarded by a multisig and a time-lock so upgrades are slow and visible. The danger is an *unguarded* proxy controlled by a single anonymous key, where the upgrade can happen instantly and silently. The check is not "is it a proxy?" but "if it is a proxy, who controls upgrades, and how fast can they happen?"

The **uncapped `setFee()`** is the other under-read power, because it is the honeypot that hides in a verified, mint-renounced, non-proxy contract — the kind a casual scan calls "clean." A token can have a fixed supply, no mint, no blacklist, and still confiscate your exit, simply because the owner can raise the sell tax to 99% after you are in. The reason this slips past people is that a *low* current fee looks reasonable; a 5% sell tax is common and not alarming. The flag is not the current fee, it is the *absence of a cap* — the fact that the function can set the fee arbitrarily high later. A safe fee function is one that is either renounced or hard-capped in the bytecode at, say, 10%, so the worst case is bounded. An uncapped one means the displayed fee is a courtesy, revocable the instant it is profitable to revoke. When you read the scanner output, do not stop at "sell tax: 5%" — look for whether the tax can change and whether it has a ceiling.

A practical note on *how* to read all of this when you are not a Solidity engineer: you do not decompile bytecode by hand. The token-safety scanners do it for you and surface the result as a small grid of yes/no flags. Your job is to know what each flag *means for your exit* — which is exactly what the matrix above encodes — and to treat any single dangerous power that is live, unguarded, and uncapped as a stop. The scanners are not infallible (a novel obfuscation can fool a simulator), which is why the contract check is one of five and not the only one. But for the common templated rugs that make up the bulk of the 98.6%, the scanner catches the dangerous powers in seconds, for free, before you spend a dollar.

#### Worked example: a 99% sell tax leaves \$10 of your \$1,000

Say `$RUGCOIN`'s `setFee()` has no cap and you buy **\$1,000** of the token. Right now the sell tax reads 5% — harmless-looking. The moment a meaningful pool of buyers is in, the owner calls `setFee(99)`. You try to exit your \$1,000 position. The contract takes 99% of the proceeds as "tax" (routed to the owner), so your \$1,000 sell returns about **\$10**, and the \$990 difference lands in the team's wallet. You did not get blacklisted; you were not technically blocked; the transaction even "succeeded." You simply kept 1% of your own money. The intuition: a `setFee()` with no cap means the *price* on the chart was never the real exit price — the owner sets your exit price after you commit, and a single uncapped function turns \$1,000 into \$10.

## Stage 3 — Launch: bundled buys fake distribution, snipers grab block 0 (flags 4–5)

Now the token launches: liquidity is added and trading opens. A buyer glances at the holder list, sees dozens of wallets holding the token, and concludes "distribution looks healthy — lots of independent holders, not one whale." That conclusion is exactly the illusion the launch is engineered to create, and seeing through it is flags 4 and 5.

The technique is **bundling**. In the same launch block (or the first one or two), the dev's funding source seeds a fleet of fresh wallets and has them all buy at once. On the surface the holder list shows fifteen different addresses each holding a few percent — apparent distribution. Underneath, those fifteen wallets share *one funding source* and were *born in the same block*, so they are one entity wearing fifteen masks. The "distributed" float is captured by the dev. (This is the clustering problem the whole series returns to; the heuristics live in [address clustering and heuristics](/blog/trading/onchain/address-clustering-and-heuristics) and the memecoin-specific read in [holder analysis for memecoins](/blog/trading/onchain/holder-analysis-for-memecoins).)

Alongside the bundle come **snipers** — bots that monitor the mempool (the queue of pending transactions) for new pool creations and fire a buy in block 0 or 1, ahead of every human. Some snipers are independent (and will dump on the dev as fast as on you); some are the dev's own, adding to the hidden share. Either way, the first blocks of a launch are dominated by automated, well-funded actors, and the retail buyer who "got in early" an hour later is buying *from* them.

![Graph showing one dev source funding many fresh wallets that all buy at launch alongside block-0 sniper bots](/imgs/blogs/case-study-a-memecoin-rug-from-deploy-to-exit-4.png)

For `$RUGCOIN`, the deployer source `0xF1a2…` seeded roughly fifteen fresh wallets with about \$25k total and had them all buy in the launch bundle; sniper bots filled blocks 0–1. The result: about **40% of the supply** sits with one entity plus the bots, hidden behind a holder list that looks spread out. That is flags 4 and 5 — a bundled launch faking distribution (flag 4) and sniper concentration in the opening blocks (flag 5).

How do you catch this for free? Two reads. First, **same-block, same-funder clustering**: tools like Bubblemaps draw the holder list as a graph and visibly connect wallets that share a funding source — a bundle shows up as a tight cluster all wired to one node, not fifteen unconnected dots. Second, **launch-block analysis**: on the explorer, look at *who bought in the first one or two blocks* and whether those buyers trace back to the deployer. If a third of the supply was bought by wallets funded from the same place in the same block, the holder list is theatre.

The clustering heuristics are worth understanding rather than just running, because they are the same logic an investigator uses to deanonymise any operator and the same logic that will later reassemble the scammer's wallets at the exit. The strongest link is the **common funding source**: if fifteen "independent" holders were each funded by the same wallet within minutes, they are almost certainly one operator — honest, unrelated buyers do not share a paymaster. The second is **same-block timing**: wallets that all transact in the identical launch block were coordinated, because independent humans do not synchronise to the block. The third is **lifecycle correlation**: wallets that were born together, bought together, and (you will see) sell together are one entity by behaviour even if no single transaction links them directly. Bundles trip all three at once, which is why a cluster-graph viewer renders them as an unmistakable hub-and-spoke shape — one funder in the centre, fifteen freshly minted wallets radiating out, every one of them holding the same token bought in the same block. Genuine distribution looks like noise: many unconnected dots with unrelated histories and staggered entry times. The bundle looks like a snowflake. Once you have seen the two shapes side by side you cannot unsee the difference, and that visual is the fastest read in the whole checklist.

A defender also has to handle the *false* clusters, or they will jump at shadows. Two large holders that are *not* the dev show up in every memecoin: the **pool contract itself** (the AMM pool holds a big chunk of supply by design — that is the liquidity) and **CEX hot wallets** if the token ever lists somewhere. Both will look like "whales" on a naive holder list. The fix is labelling: known addresses (pools, routers, exchange wallets, burn addresses) are tagged in every serious tool, so you subtract them before you read concentration. After you remove the labelled, expected large holders, the question is what *remains* — and if what remains is a tight same-funder, same-block cluster sitting on 30–40% of the float, that is the captured supply the bundle was built to hide. The discipline is to label first, then cluster, then read concentration — in that order — so you are measuring the dev's hidden share, not the pool's necessary one.

#### Worked example: \$25k of bundled buys captures a 40% float

The dev seeds ~15 fresh wallets with about **\$25,000** total and bundles their buys at launch. Combined with the dev's own sniper fills, that captures roughly **40%** of `$RUGCOIN`'s supply. Now do the math on what that hidden float is worth at the pump. If the token reaches a **\$200,000** market cap, 40% of supply is worth `0.40 × \$200,000 = \$80,000` on paper — captured for a \$25k cost, all of it held by one entity that controls when to sell. The retail buyers staring at "healthy distribution" are providing the exit liquidity for a position they cannot see. The intuition: \$25k of bundled buys is not a vote of confidence in the token, it is the down-payment on a \$80,000 dump that the holder list is specifically designed to hide from you.

## Stage 4 — The pump: hype, maybe wash volume, and an LP that is not locked (flag 6)

Now the chart does its job. Influencer callouts, a flurry of "wagmi" replies, a green candle that pulls in momentum traders, and volume that looks enormous. Some of that volume is real FOMO. Some of it is **wash trading** — the team trading the token between its own wallets to manufacture the *appearance* of demand, painting volume and price without any real buyer. (The full anatomy is in [detecting wash trading](/blog/trading/onchain/detecting-wash-trading) and [detecting fake volume vs organic demand](/blog/trading/onchain/detecting-fake-volume-vs-organic-demand).) The reader sees a \$200k market cap and a vertical chart and feels the fear of missing out. This is the emotional peak of the scam, and it is engineered.

But the most important fact during the pump is not on the chart at all — it is the **LP lock status**, and it is flag 6. Recall from Foundations: whoever holds the LP tokens can drain the pool. For `$RUGCOIN`, the LP tokens are sitting *in the deployer wallet* — not locked in a time-lock contract, not burned to a dead address. That means the team can pull the pool at any moment. The pump is loud precisely to keep your eyes on the green candle and off the one transaction that matters.

![Before-and-after of an unlocked liquidity pool being pulled, taking the token price from a live quote to near zero](/imgs/blogs/case-study-a-memecoin-rug-from-deploy-to-exit-5.png)

Reading the lock status is a free, decisive check. On the explorer or a DEX-screener, find the pool, find the LP token, and ask: who holds the LP supply, and is it in a recognised locker (Unicrypt, Team Finance, a time-lock) or a burn address? Three outcomes:

- **Burned** (sent to `0x…dead`): the team can never pull it. Strongest signal — though it does *not* protect you from a mint-and-dump or a honeypot tax, so it is necessary, not sufficient.
- **Locked** for a stated period in a real locker: the team cannot pull until the lock expires. Read the *duration* — a 7-day lock is theatre; the rug just waits 8 days.
- **Unlocked, held by the deployer** (what `$RUGCOIN` shows): the team can pull at will. This is flag 6, and on its own it is grounds to avoid no matter how good the chart looks.

The left panel of the figure above is `$RUGCOIN` during the pump: an unlocked pool holding about **\$80k of ETH** plus the token, quoting a live price, with holders staring at a **\$200k** cap and a green chart that "feels real." Every ingredient of the trap is in place, in public, while the hype peaks.

It helps to make the **market-cap-versus-liquidity gap** explicit, because it is the number that most distinguishes a fragile memecoin from a sound asset, and almost nobody checks it during a pump. Market cap is `price × total supply` — a notional figure that assumes you could sell every token at the current price, which you never can. Liquidity is the *actual* depth in the pool: the real money you could sell into before the price moves against you. For a memecoin the two can diverge wildly, because a thin pool plus a large supply produces a big notional cap on top of almost nothing real. `$RUGCOIN` quotes a \$200k cap on an \$80k pool — and even that \$80k overstates your exit, because the constant-product curve means the price drops as you sell, so the first sellers get a decent price and later sellers get crumbs. The cap is what the chart shows you and what the hype quotes; the pool depth is what you could actually extract. A defender reads the *ratio* of the two: a market cap many multiples of the liquidity is a token that cannot absorb its own holders trying to leave, with or without a rug. When that thin pool is *also* unlocked, the team does not even have to wait for a sell-side stampede — they can take the \$80k directly. The pump's job is to keep your attention on the \$200k cap and away from the \$80k of unlocked depth that is the only number that is real.

There is also a quieter version of this stage that does not end in a single dramatic pull: the **slow rug**. Instead of yanking the pool, the team bleeds the token through "marketing" and "treasury" wallets, a perpetual sell-fee that routes to the team, and steady dribble-selling of the bundled float over weeks. The chart does not cliff; it grinds down, with each leg blamed on "market conditions." Slow rugs are harder to call in real time precisely because there is no smoking-gun transaction — but the *setup* is identical, and the same pre-buy flags catch it: the same captured float, the same live owner fee power, the same unlocked or briefly-locked LP. Whether the operator chooses the fast pull or the slow bleed is a tactical decision made *after* you have already bought; the flags that would have stopped you are the same either way, which is why this case study screens at the deploy-and-launch stage rather than trying to time the exit.

#### Worked example: a \$200k pump sitting on \$80k of unlocked liquidity

The chart reads a **\$200,000 market cap**. The actual pool backing every quote holds only about **\$80,000 of ETH** — and that ETH is unlocked, in the deployer's wallet. So the "value" of the token is leveraged ~2.5× on top of a pool the team can empty in one transaction. If everyone tried to sell at once they would be selling into \$80k of depth at most (and far less than that as the price slips down the curve), but they cannot *all* get out before the team — because the team controls the LP and can pull it ahead of the crowd. The intuition: a \$200k market cap backed by \$80k of *unlocked* liquidity is not a \$200k asset, it is an \$80k asset the team can repossess — the cap is the bait and the unlocked \$80k is the actual, withdrawable prize.

## Stage 5 — The exit: the dev and bundle sell, then the LP is pulled (flag 7)

This is the rug. It usually comes in two motions, and `$RUGCOIN` does both.

**First, the distribution.** The dev and the bundled wallets — that hidden ~40% of supply — start selling into the FOMO bids the pump generated. They acquired the float cheaply at launch; now they dump it into real buyers at the pumped price. On-chain you see a cluster of wallets that all trace to one funding source selling in a tight window, draining the ETH side of the pool as they go. For `$RUGCOIN`, this distribution pulls roughly **\$150,000** out of the buyers and into the deployer cluster.

**Second, the liquidity pull.** With the easy sell-side profit taken, the team calls `removeLiquidity` (or burns the LP receipt to claim the underlying) and withdraws the remaining **~\$80,000 of ETH** from the pool. This is flag 7 — the rug itself, the only step in the whole sequence that needed no advance warning because *it is* the event everything else was setting up. The right panel of the before/after figure above is this moment: one withdraw call empties the ETH side, the pool is left holding ~0 ETH against all the tokens, and the constant-product price `x / y` collapses toward zero because `x` (the ETH) just went to zero.

The combined take: about **\$150,000** from the dump plus **\$80,000** from the LP pull = roughly **\$230,000** exiting the buyers and landing in the deployer cluster — against a setup cost of one \$3,000 mixer withdrawal.

The *order* of the two motions is deliberate and worth noting, because it tells you something about how the operator maximises the take. If the team pulled the LP first, the price would collapse instantly and their own bundled tokens would be worth nothing — they would have rugged themselves out of the larger prize. So they sell *into* the live pool first, while the price is still high and FOMO buyers are still bidding, converting the bundled float into ETH at the pumped price. Only once the easy sell-side liquidity is exhausted — once dumping more would crater the price faster than it is worth — do they take the remaining ETH directly by pulling the pool. The dump captures the buyers' money that flowed in *during* the pump; the pull captures the seed liquidity and whatever ETH the late buyers added. Reading this sequence backward from the chain, an investigator sees a tight window where one cluster sells aggressively, the ETH reserve in the pool drains, and then a final `removeLiquidity` empties what is left — a signature so consistent across rugs that it is itself an attribution feature.

Notice what is *not* required for any of this: no bug, no hack, no exploit. Every transaction in the exit is the contract and the AMM working exactly as written. The dump is ordinary selling. The pull is the LP holder exercising a right the protocol grants every LP holder. That is the unsettling core of the rug — it is not a failure of the code, it is the code doing precisely what the team designed it to do, against buyers who never read the design. There is nothing to patch and no one to appeal to. The only defence is the one that runs *before* the buy, which is why this entire case study is structured around the pre-buy checks rather than the post-rug recovery.

#### Worked example: a \$230,000 exit from a \$3,000 setup

Tally the scammer's economics end to end. **Cost:** ~1 ETH (\$3,000) from the mixer, most of it returned to them as seed liquidity, so the real sunk cost is a few hundred dollars in gas. **Take:** \$150,000 dumped from the bundled float into FOMO buyers, plus \$80,000 pulled from the LP, for a gross exit of **\$230,000**. That is a return of roughly **75×** on the \$3,000 staked, realised in a window measured in hours to days. Now flip it to the buyer's side of the ledger: that \$230,000 did not come from nowhere — it came out of the wallets of everyone who aped the pump. The intuition: a rug is a zero-sum transfer with a \$3,000 ticket and a \$230,000 prize, and you only avoid funding the prize by reading the flags that were public the whole time.

#### Worked example: the \$1,000 aper who rode it to \$30

Put a real person in the trade. An aper buys **\$1,000** of `$RUGCOIN` near the top of the pump, when the chart shows the \$200k cap and the green candle. They never checked the deployer's funding, never read the contract powers, never looked at the cluster graph, never checked the LP lock. Then stage 5 hits: the dev cluster dumps \$150k into the bids, the LP is pulled for \$80k, and the constant-product price collapses. The aper's position, marked against the now-empty pool, is worth about **\$30** — a **−97%** loss on the \$1,000, and even that \$30 may be unsellable because there is nothing left in the pool to sell into. They did not get unlucky on a coin flip; they bought a setup whose every red flag was on-chain before they clicked. The intuition: the −97% was not priced into the chart, it was priced into the *deployer's history, the contract powers, the cluster graph, and the LP lock* — four free checks, any one of which would have turned \$1,000 lost into \$1,000 kept.

## Stage 6 — The aftermath: price to zero, funds traced back to the cluster

After the pull, the chart is a cliff. The token quotes a price near zero, the holder chat fills with "what happened," and the deployer's social accounts go dark or pivot to the next launch. For the buyers, the position is dead. For a defender, the aftermath is where the chain's *permanence* becomes the scammer's problem instead of yours.

The stolen ETH does not vanish — it moves, and every hop is public. Trace it forward from the pool-withdrawal transaction and you watch the proceeds funnel from the bundled wallets and the deployer back toward a small number of consolidation addresses, then toward a mixer or a CEX deposit address to cash out. The same clustering that exposed the fake distribution at launch now reassembles the scammer's wallets at the exit: wallets that funded the bundle, received the dump proceeds, and received the LP-pull ETH all link back to `0xF1a2…` and its funding source. (The full methodology is in [how to trace a transaction flow](/blog/trading/onchain/how-to-trace-a-transaction-flow) and [tracing stolen funds step by step](/blog/trading/onchain/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step).)

This is the part that matters for *you* even though it comes after your loss: the trace is what makes serial ruggers identifiable. A scammer who runs the same playbook repeatedly leaves a repeated on-chain fingerprint — the same funding source, the same bundling pattern, the same proxy template, the same cash-out venue. Attribution services and community sleuths cluster those repeats into a known-bad entity, and that entity's *next* deployer wallet, funded from the same place, is a red flag before its next token even launches. The defender's loop closes: the aftermath of one rug is the flag-1 evidence for the next.

It is worth being honest about the limits. Tracing rarely *recovers* memecoin-rug money — the sums are small per victim, the cash-out is fast, and law enforcement prioritises the eight- and nine-figure hacks (see the [Ronin](/blog/trading/onchain/case-study-the-ronin-bridge-hack) and [Poly Network](/blog/trading/onchain/case-study-poly-network-and-the-returned-hack) case studies for what real recovery effort looks like). The value of the trace for a retail defender is not getting your \$1,000 back; it is *not being the next victim* of the same actor. Attribution is prevention, not restitution.

## How to read it: a stage-by-stage walkthrough on the explorer

Tie it together as a hands-on pass. Here is exactly what you click, stage by stage, before any buy — using only free tools (a block explorer, a token-safety scanner, a cluster-graph viewer, a DEX-screener). The checklist figure maps each stage to the one check that fires there.

![Pipeline mapping each rug stage to the single pre-buy check that catches its red flag](/imgs/blogs/case-study-a-memecoin-rug-from-deploy-to-exit-6.png)

1. **Deploy → check the deployer.** On the explorer, open the token's contract, find the deploy transaction, open the deployer wallet. Two questions: *how old is it* (first-ever tx?) and *where did its funding come from* (one hop back — mixer? fresh CEX? a known-bad cluster?). Fresh + obfuscated funding is flag 1.
2. **Contract → check the powers.** Is the contract verified? Run it through a token-safety scanner (GoPlus / Honeypot.is / Token Sniffer). Read the flags: *mint renounced?*, *owner can set fee / blacklist?*, *is it a proxy?*, *honeypot simulation result?* Any live dangerous power is flags 2–3.
3. **Launch → check the clustering.** Drop the token into a cluster-graph viewer (Bubblemaps) and look at the holder list as a *graph*. Are the top holders wired to one funding source? Look at the launch-block buys: same funder, same block? Captured float behind fake distribution is flags 4–5.
4. **Pump → check the LP lock.** Find the pool, find the LP token, ask *who holds it* and *is it locked or burned, for how long?* Unlocked LP in the deployer wallet is flag 6 — a stop on its own.
5. **Exit → check holders and run a sell simulation.** Before buying, check holder concentration (is one cluster holding 30–40%+?) and run a simulated sell (most safety scanners do this) to confirm you could actually exit. A failing sim or a captured float is the pre-image of flag 7.
6. **Aftermath → the rule.** Any one hard flag is a stop. Size a position only when *all* checks are clear — and even then, size it as money you are willing to lose, because "all clear" lowers the odds of a trap, it does not make a memecoin a sound investment.

The discipline is not the *knowledge* — the checks above take minutes and the tools are free. The discipline is running them *before* the FOMO, not after the rug. Every red flag in this case study was visible on-chain before the buy was possible. The buyers who lost did not lack information; they lacked the habit of looking.

A word on *how long this takes*, because the usual objection is "by the time I've run five checks the token has 10×'d and I've missed it." First, the checks are fast — deployer age and funding is one explorer page, the safety scan is one paste, the cluster graph is one paste, the LP lock is one explorer lookup, the sell simulation rides along with the safety scan. A practised defender clears all five in two or three minutes. Second, and more important, the objection has the risk backwards. The base-rate figure says ~98.6% of these tokens fail; the ones that 10× while you were "too slow" are a survivorship illusion, because for every one you "missed" there were dozens that rugged the people who *were* fast enough to ape them. Missing a winner costs you an opportunity; catching a rug saves you the principal. With a 98.6% failure base rate, the math overwhelmingly favours the discipline of checking, even at the cost of occasionally being last to a real winner. The trader who never misses a pump is, by construction, the trader who never skips a rug.

Two more practical notes make the walkthrough robust. **Re-check, do not check once.** A brand-new pool can be unlocked for a handful of blocks before the team locks it, and a sell simulation can fail transiently for liquidity reasons unrelated to a trap. So a single bad reading on the LP lock or the sim is a "wait and re-run," not necessarily a verdict — but you never buy *during* the ambiguous window, and a power or cluster flag (which does not change with time) is decisive on the first read. **Weight, then decide.** No single check except a failing sell simulation or an unlocked LP is conclusive on its own; the call comes from the *stack* of flags. A fresh wallet alone is weak; a fresh wallet funded from a mixer, deploying an unverified proxy with a live mint, whose holder list clusters to one funder, sitting on unlocked liquidity, is not a judgement call — it is `$RUGCOIN`, and every one of those readings was free.

## Common misconceptions

**"A verified contract is safe."** Verification means the *source is readable*, not that the source is *benign*. A verified contract can ship a live mint, an uncapped `setFee()`, and a blacklist in plain sight — and rug you exactly as written. Verification is necessary so you *can* read the powers; it is the reading that protects you. Plenty of \$RUGCOIN-style traps are fully verified.

**"Lots of holders means it is distributed."** A holder *count* is meaningless if the holders share a funding source. Fifteen wallets born in the same block from one funder are one entity, and the bundle exists specifically to make the count look healthy. Distribution is about *independent* holders, which you confirm with a cluster graph, not a number.

**"Locked liquidity means I am safe."** Read the *duration* and the *share*. A 7-day lock is a countdown to the rug, not protection. And a lock on the LP says nothing about a live mint (mint-and-dump), a `setFee()` (tax-trap), or a proxy (upgrade-to-honeypot). The LP lock closes exactly one of the four rug doors; check the other three.

**"The chart is pumping, so the demand is real."** Price and volume can be manufactured. Wash trading between the team's own wallets paints a green chart and fat volume with zero real buyers; the bundle's coordinated buys do the same at launch. A rising chart is downstream of a contract and a holder set you have not checked — read the upstream, not the candle.

**"If it were a scam, someone would have stopped it."** Permissionless means nobody is at the door. There is no listing committee, no underwriter, no exchange compliance desk between the deployer and your wallet — that is the whole design. The only check between `0xF1a2…` and your \$1,000 is *you*, running the free checks above.

## The playbook: what to do with it

The whole case study reduces to one if-then table you run before every low-cap buy. Each row is a stage, the on-chain read, the action, and the false-positive to keep you honest.

- **Deployer is fresh AND funded from a mixer / untraceable CEX.** → *Read:* the operator deliberately obscured the money's origin and has no track record. → *Action:* treat as flag 1; default to skip. → *False-positive:* fresh wallets and fresh CEX withdrawals are common and not always malicious — weight this *with* the other flags, not alone.
- **Contract unverified, OR verified with a live mint / uncapped `setFee()` / blacklist / proxy.** → *Read:* the team can dilute, tax, freeze, or upgrade your exit after you buy. → *Action:* any live dangerous power is grounds to avoid; this is flags 2–3. → *False-positive:* some legitimate tokens keep a *time-locked, multisig-guarded* admin for upgrades — check whether the power is renounced, time-locked, or guarded, not merely present.
- **Holder list clusters to one funder / same-block bundle / sniper-dominated open.** → *Read:* the "distribution" is one entity holding a hidden large share. → *Action:* captured float is flags 4–5; skip or treat any position as exit liquidity for the cluster. → *False-positive:* CEX hot wallets and the pool contract itself are large holders that are *not* the dev — label the known addresses before crying cluster.
- **LP unlocked / held by deployer / locked only briefly.** → *Read:* the team can pull the pool and take price to zero at will. → *Action:* unlocked LP is flag 6, a stop on its own; require burned or long-locked LP before considering a buy. → *False-positive:* a brand-new pool may be unlocked for a few blocks before the team locks it — re-check, but do not buy *during* the unlocked window.
- **A simulated sell fails, or holder concentration is extreme.** → *Read:* you may not be able to exit (honeypot), or one cluster can crush the price the instant it sells. → *Action:* a failing sim or a 30–40%+ single-cluster share is a hard stop — the pre-image of the flag-7 exit. → *False-positive:* very new tokens can briefly fail a sim for liquidity reasons unrelated to a trap — re-run it; if it keeps failing, walk.

The meta-rule sits above the table: **any single hard red flag is a stop, regardless of the chart.** You are not trying to find the one reason to buy; you are trying to find any one reason to skip — and the base rate guarantees that for ~98.6% of tokens, that reason exists and is on-chain. Run the five checks, and let "skip" be the default that the evidence has to overturn, not the other way around. That is the entire edge a defender has, and it is enough.
