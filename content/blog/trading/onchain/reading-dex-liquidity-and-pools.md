> **TL;DR** — A token's *liquidity* — not its price or its market cap — decides whether you can get in and out, and at what price. The blockchain shows you the pool's exact reserves, so you can measure that before you buy instead of finding out on the way out.
>
> - A decentralized-exchange pool is two piles of tokens and one rule, `x \* y = k`. The price you see is just the ratio of the two reserves, and every trade moves it along that curve.
> - Read the pool's two reserves and its total value locked on a DEX explorer, then estimate the *price impact* of the size you actually want to trade — a \$10k buy and a \$100k buy into the same \$500k pool are completely different events.
> - Depth can lie: liquidity can be one-sided, parked out of range in a concentrated pool, or owned by a team wallet that can pull it in one transaction. Check *who owns the LP* before you trust the depth.
> - The number to remember: **you can exit roughly a few percent of a pool's depth without wrecking the price — size to the pool, never to the market cap.**

In May 2021 a token called SafeMoon had a "market cap" north of two billion dollars and a holder count in the millions. On paper, the people in it were rich. In practice, the only place the token traded was a single PancakeSwap pool, and the moment a large holder tried to sell more than a sliver of it, the price fell out from under them — because the dollars they wanted to walk away with simply were not sitting in the pool. The market cap was a multiplication problem. The *liquidity* was a much smaller, much harder number, and it was the only one that mattered when you wanted your money back.

That gap — between what a token is "worth" and what you can actually sell it for right now — is the single most expensive lesson in crypto, and it is hiding in plain sight on the blockchain. A centralized exchange hides its order book mechanics behind a clean interface; a decentralized exchange (DEX) does the opposite. The pool is a smart contract, its balances are public, and the rule that turns those balances into a price is a single line of arithmetic anyone can read. If you learn to read a pool, you stop trusting the green number on a dashboard and start measuring the thing that actually constrains you: how much you can move, and what it will cost you to move it.

This post teaches you to read DEX liquidity from the chain. We will build the automated-market-maker (AMM) model from zero, derive why price impact explodes as your trade grows relative to the pool, learn to read reserves and total value locked on real tools, see how liquidity-provider (LP) composition decides whether the depth is trustworthy, unpack why a "two-million-dollar" concentrated pool can have fifty thousand dollars of real depth, and finish with the liquidity check you run before every single buy.

## Foundations: how an AMM pool works

Before we can read a pool, we need the vocabulary. Every term here gets defined on first use, because the whole edge of on-chain analysis is that you can verify each of these directly instead of taking a platform's word for it.

**A DEX is a smart contract, not a company.** A centralized exchange (CEX) like Binance or Coinbase runs an *order book*: a list of buy orders and sell orders that a matching engine pairs off. You trade against other people, and the exchange takes a cut. A *decentralized exchange* has no matching engine and no other people to wait for. Instead it has a pool of tokens sitting inside a smart contract, and you trade directly against that pool. The most common design is the *automated market maker* (AMM): a formula, not a human and not an order book, decides the price.

**A pool is two reserves.** A single AMM pool holds two assets — say a token (call it TOK) and a quote asset (usually a stablecoin like USDC, or wrapped ether). The amount of each asset sitting in the contract is its *reserve*. If the pool holds 1,000,000 TOK and 500,000 USDC, those are the two reserves. That is *all* a basic pool is: two balances in a contract, fully visible on the chain.

**The rule is `x \* y = k`.** The classic AMM, popularized by Uniswap v2, uses the *constant-product* formula. Call the two reserves `x` and `y`. Their product `k = x \* y` is held constant by every trade. When you buy TOK, you add USDC (raising `y`) and remove TOK (lowering `x`) — but the contract only lets you remove exactly enough TOK to keep `x \* y` equal to the same `k`. Because the product is fixed, the pool can never be fully drained: as `x` gets small, the price of the remaining TOK gets enormous, so the last token costs effectively infinite money. The pool always has *something* left.

**The price is the ratio of the reserves.** Here is the line that does all the work. The *spot price* of TOK, quoted in USDC, is simply `y / x` — the quote reserve divided by the token reserve. With 500,000 USDC and 1,000,000 TOK, the price is `500,000 / 1,000,000 = 0.50` USDC per TOK. Nobody set that price by typing it in. It *emerges* from the two balances. Change either balance and the price changes mechanically.

**A liquidity provider funds the pool, and gets an LP token.** The reserves do not appear from nowhere. People called *liquidity providers* (LPs) deposit both assets — in the example, someone deposits TOK *and* USDC in the current ratio — and in return the contract mints them an *LP token*, a receipt that represents their share of the pool. The LP token is the key object of this whole post. Whoever holds it can later *burn* it to withdraw their share of both reserves. So the LP token is, quite literally, the claim on the liquidity. We will spend a whole section on who holds it, because that answers the question that matters most: *can the depth I see actually vanish?*

**Slippage is the price moving against you as you trade.** Because price is the reserve ratio and your trade changes the reserves, the price you get is *not* the price you saw before you clicked. *Slippage* (also called *price impact*) is the difference between the quoted spot price and your actual average execution price. A small trade barely moves the ratio and gets near the quoted price; a large trade walks the price up the curve and pays a much worse average. Slippage is the cost of size, and it is the central number we will learn to estimate.

**Total value locked (TVL) is the dollar value of both reserves.** When a DEX explorer shows a pool's "liquidity" or "TVL," it usually means the combined dollar value of the two reserves. A pool with 1,000,000 TOK at \$0.50 and 500,000 USDC has \$500,000 + \$500,000 = \$1,000,000 of TVL. TVL is a useful first glance — but as we will see, it can badly overstate the depth you can actually trade against, especially in concentrated-liquidity pools.

**Market cap is not exit liquidity.** The last and most important distinction. *Market cap* is the last traded price multiplied by the total supply of tokens. *Exit liquidity* is how many real dollars you can extract by selling. These are wildly different numbers, because the act of selling moves the price that defined the market cap in the first place. A \$2B market cap sitting on a \$1M pool is a \$2B *headline* on \$1M of real money. Internalizing this gap is the point of the whole post, and we will give it its own figure later.

**The fee is the LP's pay, and a tiny tax on every trade.** One more piece. Every swap pays a fee — classically 0.30% on Uniswap v2, and a choice of tiers (0.01%, 0.05%, 0.30%, 1.00%) on v3 — that is added to the reserves and accrues to the liquidity providers in proportion to their share. That fee is *why* anyone provides liquidity: it is the yield that compensates an LP for two costs they take on. The first is *impermanent loss* — when the price moves, the constant-product rebalancing leaves an LP holding more of the asset that fell and less of the asset that rose, so they end up worse off than if they had simply held both tokens. The second is the opportunity cost of locking capital in a pool. The fee has to beat both, on average, or the liquidity leaves — and when liquidity leaves, the pool gets thinner and your exit gets worse. So even the fee, a number most traders ignore, is part of the depth story: a pool that does not pay its LPs enough is a pool whose depth is quietly draining.

**Why the trader and the defender read the same number.** It is worth saying plainly why liquidity sits at the center of both audiences this series serves. For the trader, depth is the constraint on size and the source of slippage cost — read it well and you size correctly and avoid getting filled at a price that erases your edge. For the defender, depth is the attack surface: a thin or removable pool is exactly where rugs, honeypots, and MEV bots operate, because thinness is what makes those exploits profitable. The reserves, the LP composition, the active-range depth chart — these are the same on-chain facts whether you are trying to make money or trying to not lose it. That dual use is the through-line of the whole post.

With that vocabulary, we can read any basic pool. The rest of the post is about reading them *well* — turning reserves into a price-impact estimate, judging whether the depth is real, and knowing what to do with the answer.

## The `x \* y = k` mechanic: how a trade moves the price

Let us make the curve concrete. Take the balanced pool from the figure: `x = 1,000,000` TOK and `y = 500,000` USDC. The product is `k = 1,000,000 \* 500,000 = 500,000,000,000` (five hundred billion). The spot price is `y / x = \$0.50` per TOK.

Now you buy TOK by adding USDC. Say you add `\$50,000` of USDC. The new quote reserve is `y' = 550,000`. Because `k` must stay constant, the new token reserve must satisfy `x' \* y' = k`, so `x' = k / y' = 500,000,000,000 / 550,000 = 909,090.9` TOK. You receive the difference: `1,000,000 - 909,090.9 = 90,909` TOK. You paid `\$50,000` for 90,909 TOK, an *average* price of `\$0.550` per TOK. But the *new spot* price is `y' / x' = 550,000 / 909,091 = \$0.605`. So one `\$50,000` buy moved the quoted price from `\$0.50` to `\$0.605`, about +21%, and you yourself paid an average of `\$0.55` — already 10% above the price you saw before clicking. That is slippage, and it grew straight out of the arithmetic, no order book required.

Notice the shape of it. A tiny buy of `\$500` would have barely nudged `y` from 500,000 to 500,500, moved `x` by a hair, and filled at essentially `\$0.50`. A huge buy of `\$500,000` would have *doubled* `y` to 1,000,000, forced `x` down to 500,000, and left the spot price at `\$1.00` — a 100% move from a single trade. The cost of size is not linear. It accelerates. The next figure plots exactly that acceleration.

![Curve of price impact rising steeply as trade size grows as a share of the pool](/imgs/blogs/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools-2.png)

The horizontal axis is your trade size as a share of the pool's quote reserve; the vertical axis is the approximate price impact. The curve is flat and forgiving while your trade is a small slice of the pool, then it bends sharply upward as your trade becomes a meaningful fraction of the reserves. The three marked points are the worked examples we will return to: a `\$10k` buy into a `\$500k` pool, a `\$100k` buy into the same `\$500k` pool, and a `\$10k` buy into a deep `\$5M` pool. Same dollar trade, wildly different outcomes — because impact depends on the trade *relative to the pool*, not on the dollar amount alone.

#### Worked example: a \$100k swap into a \$500k pool

Take a pool whose quote side is `\$500,000` of USDC, balanced against `\$500,000` of TOK at `\$0.50` (so `x = 1,000,000` TOK, `y = 500,000` USDC, `k = 500,000,000,000`). You market-buy with `\$100,000` of USDC — that is **20% of the quote reserve**, putting you far up the steep part of the curve.

After the trade, the quote reserve is `y' = 500,000 + 100,000 = 600,000`. Holding `k` constant, the token reserve becomes `x' = 500,000,000,000 / 600,000 = 833,333` TOK. You receive `1,000,000 - 833,333 = 166,667` TOK for your `\$100,000` — an *average* fill of `\$0.60` per TOK. But the new spot price is `y' / x' = 600,000 / 833,333 = \$0.72`, a move of about **+44%** from the starting `\$0.50`. You shoved the quoted price up almost half just by trading, and you personally paid 20% above the mid (`\$0.60` vs `\$0.50`). The intuition: once your order is a fifth of the pool, you are not *taking* the price, you *are* the price.

#### Worked example: a \$10k trade at ~0.5% impact in a deep \$5M pool

Now run the same idea on a deep pool. Suppose the pool holds `\$2.5M` of TOK and `\$2.5M` of USDC, for `\$5M` of TVL, at `\$0.50` per TOK (`x = 5,000,000` TOK, `y = 2,500,000` USDC, `k = 12,500,000,000,000`). You buy with `\$10,000` of USDC — just **0.4% of the quote reserve**, way down on the flat part of the curve.

The new quote reserve is `y' = 2,510,000`. The token reserve becomes `x' = 12,500,000,000,000 / 2,510,000 = 4,980,080` TOK, so you receive `5,000,000 - 4,980,080 = 19,920` TOK. At a mid price of `\$0.50` a "fair" fill would have been 20,000 TOK; you got 19,920, only **0.4% fewer**, for a price impact on the order of half a percent. The new spot price is `2,510,000 / 4,980,080 = \$0.504` — a move you can barely see. The intuition: the *same* `\$10,000` that detonates a thin pool is a rounding error in a deep one, which is why depth, not dollar size, is the thing to measure.

Those two examples are the whole asymmetry. To make it visceral, the next figure puts an identical trade into two pools side by side.

![A 100k trade filling near the mid in a deep pool and crashing a thin pool](/imgs/blogs/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools-3.png)

On the left, a thin `\$500k` pool: a `\$100k` buy is 20% of the quote reserve, the constant-product curve bends hard, you receive far fewer tokens than the mid would suggest, and the spot price ends near `\$0.72`. On the right, a deep `\$5M` pool of the *same token*: a `\$10k` buy is 0.4% of the reserve, `k` barely moves, you get nearly the full amount at the mid, and the price ends near `\$0.504`. Identical asset, identical mechanism — the only thing that changed is depth, and depth changed everything. This is why two tokens at "the same price" can be completely different risks, and why the very first thing to read on a DEX is not the price but the reserves.

**Slippage tolerance and why swaps fail.** When you submit a swap, you set a *slippage tolerance* — the worst price you will accept, usually expressed as a percent below the quote (the default is often 0.5%). If, by the time your transaction is mined, the pool's reserves have moved enough that your fill would be worse than that tolerance, the contract *reverts* the swap: you pay gas for the failed attempt and get no tokens. On a thin, volatile pool this happens constantly, because the price can move several percent between you clicking and your transaction confirming — other trades, including MEV bots, get there first. There is a trap built into this. Raise your tolerance to "make the swap go through" on a thin pool and you have just told the network you will accept a terrible fill — which is exactly the room a sandwich bot needs. So a string of failed swaps is itself a *signal*: the pool is too thin or too contested for the size you are pushing, and the fix is smaller size, not looser tolerance. Reading the failure correctly keeps you from paying for the privilege of being sandwiched.

**The curve is a hyperbola, and that is the whole intuition.** It helps to picture the geometry once. Because `x \* y = k` with `k` fixed, the set of valid reserve states is a hyperbola: a curve that is gently sloped near the middle (where the two reserves are balanced) and bends toward vertical and horizontal at the extremes (where one reserve is nearly drained). A trade moves you along this curve. A small trade stays in the gently sloped middle, so the price barely changes. A large trade drags you toward an extreme, where the curve is steep, so the price changes violently. *Depth* is just another word for how far you sit from the steep parts: a deep pool keeps you in the flat middle for any realistic trade, while a thin pool puts you near the cliff for even a modest one. Everything in this post — slippage, exit liquidity, why MEV loves thin pools — is a consequence of that single curve shape.

## Reading reserves and TVL on a DEX explorer

Theory is cheap; the edge is reading the real pool. Every figure we have used is something you can pull up in under a minute on a public tool. The two you will live in are **DEX Screener** (and its cousins like DEXTools) for a fast cross-chain overview, and the actual DEX's own interface (**Uniswap**, PancakeSwap, **Solscan**'s token page for Solana) for the precise pool state.

**Find the canonical pool, not a decoy.** A token can have many pools across many DEXes and chains. The number that matters is the *deepest* pool — that is where price is set and where you will actually trade. On DEX Screener, a token's page lists every pair; sort by liquidity and look at the top one. Beware tokens whose "biggest" pool is on an obscure DEX or paired against another illiquid token rather than a stablecoin or wrapped ether — that is often a setup to make depth look bigger than it is.

**Read the two reserves directly.** On the pool page you will see the two token balances and a USD figure for each. That is `x` and `y`. Confirm the displayed price equals `y / x` — if a tool shows a price that does not match the reserve ratio, treat the tool as wrong or the pool as exotic. On Uniswap's own interface, clicking into a pool shows the exact reserves; on Etherscan you can call the contract's `getReserves()` read function and see them as raw on-chain integers. This is the on-chain analyst's habit: do not trust the rendered price, reconstruct it from the reserves.

**Read TVL, then immediately discount it.** The headline "liquidity" or "TVL" is the dollar value of both reserves combined. It is your first sense of scale, but it is an *upper bound* on the depth you can use, not the depth itself, for three reasons we will hit in turn: the pool can be one-sided, the liquidity can be removable, and in a concentrated pool most of the TVL can be parked out of range. A disciplined read never stops at the TVL number.

**Estimate your own price impact.** The single most useful habit: before buying, type your *actual* intended size into the DEX's swap box and read the price-impact estimate it shows, or do the `x \* y = k` arithmetic yourself from the reserves. A swap interface will warn you with a number like "Price impact: 4.2%." If you plan to put in `\$10,000` and the impact is 0.3%, the pool is deep relative to you. If it is 18%, you are SafeMoon's large holder, and your exit will look like the left side of the figure above. Estimate impact for the size you will *exit* with, not just the size you enter with — they are often very different, and the exit is the one that hurts.

**Watch for the buy/sell asymmetry.** A swap box lets you simulate a sell as easily as a buy. Do both. A pool can be structured so buying is smooth and cheap while selling — for technical or malicious reasons — moves the price far more, or fails outright. Simulating a small sell is the cheapest honeypot test there is, and we will fold it into the playbook.

**Read the volume-to-liquidity ratio.** One more number on the explorer page earns its keep: the 24-hour volume against the pool's liquidity. Healthy organic trading turns over some fraction of the pool each day. A pool showing volume *many times* its liquidity — say `\$10M` of daily volume on a `\$200k` pool — is often wash-traded: the same funds cycling through to fake activity, not real two-sided demand. (Detecting that is its own discipline — see the dedicated walkthrough in [detecting fake volume vs organic demand](/blog/trading/onchain/detecting-fake-volume-vs-organic-demand).) Conversely, a large pool with almost no volume is *stale* depth that may not survive — LPs eventually pull capital that earns no fees. The ratio tells you whether the depth is *living* depth, not just a frozen number, and it is one click away.

#### Worked example: how much of a swap's fee reaches a small LP

You provide `\$10,000` of liquidity to a v2 pool that already holds `\$1,000,000`, so your share of the pool is `10,000 / 1,010,000 = 0.99%` — call it about 1%. The pool charges the classic 0.30% fee and does `\$500,000` of honest volume in a day. Total fees that day are `\$500,000 \* 0.0030 = \$1,500`, and your ~1% share earns `\$1,500 \* 0.0099 = \$14.85`. Annualized at that pace, your `\$10,000` earns roughly `\$5,420`, about a 54% fee yield before impermanent loss — which is why deep, high-volume pools attract liquidity, and why a token with real organic volume can keep its depth without the team holding the LP. Now run it for the thin, fake case: a `\$200k` pool wash-trading `\$10M` of fake volume "earns" `\$30,000` of fees a day, but the volume is the team's own funds round-tripping, so the headline yield is fiction and the depth can vanish the moment the wash trader stops. The intuition: real fees from real volume are what *holds* depth in place, so a pool whose volume does not look organic is depth on a timer.

## LP composition: who owns the liquidity decides whether you can trust the depth

Here is where on-chain reading separates from chart-watching. Two pools can show the *same* `\$1M` of TVL and be completely different risks, because the question that actually matters is not *how much* liquidity there is but *who can take it away*. The liquidity in a pool belongs to whoever holds the LP tokens, and what they can do with those LP tokens is fully visible on-chain.

![Matrix of LP ownership types and whether the liquidity can disappear](/imgs/blogs/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools-4.png)

The matrix above sorts LP composition into four cases, from safest to most dangerous, along with what each means for *your* exit.

**Burned LP — depth you can rely on.** The LP tokens have been sent to a dead address (commonly `0x…dead` or the zero address) that nobody controls and from which no withdrawal is possible. The liquidity is permanent: it can only ever be consumed by trading, never yanked out wholesale. This is the gold standard for a fair-launch token. With burned LP, the only thing that can stop your exit is price impact — a real, measurable cost — not a rug. On-chain, you verify a burn by checking that the LP token's holder is a known dead address and that its balance is the bulk of the LP supply.

**Time-locked LP — safe until the clock runs out.** The LP tokens are held by a *locker* contract (services like Team Finance or Unicrypt) that will not release them until a specified date, which is recorded on-chain and publicly checkable. This is meaningfully safer than team-held LP, but it has an expiry. The risk is the calendar: a 30-day lock buys you 30 days, and a sophisticated rug is sometimes timed to the unlock. The read here is twofold — confirm the lock exists *and* note the unlock date, then plan to be out well before it.

**Team-held LP — your exit is a promise.** The LP tokens sit directly in the deployer's wallet or a team wallet, with no lock and no burn. The team can call `removeLiquidity` at any block and withdraw both reserves into their own pocket — including, very often, the stablecoins *you* put in by buying. This is the classic rug setup. The depth you see is real *right now*, but it is revocable at the team's discretion, and the chain will show you the LP sitting in an externally owned account (EOA) with nothing stopping a withdrawal.

**Unlocked / removable LP — treat the depth as zero.** The catch-all dangerous case: LP tokens free to move, no lock, no burn, sitting in ordinary wallets. The pool can be drained before your transaction even confirms. For risk purposes, displayed depth on an unlocked pool should be treated as *zero* — not because it is zero today, but because it can be zero by the time you need it. The whole point of reading LP composition is to convert "the dashboard says `\$1M`" into "I can actually count on `\$X`," and for unlocked LP, `\$X` is nothing.

#### Worked example: \$1M of TVL, two very different pools

Two tokens, each showing `\$1,000,000` of pool liquidity on the explorer. Token A's LP tokens — `\$1,000,000` worth — were burned to a dead address at launch; the explorer shows 100% of LP supply at `0x…dead`. Token B's `\$1,000,000` of LP sits in the deployer's EOA, unlocked.

For Token A, your worst case on a sensible-sized exit is price impact: sell `\$20,000` (about 2% of depth) and you might give up a percent or two of slippage, call it `\$200`–`\$400` of cost — annoying, survivable, *measurable*. For Token B, your worst case is the team calling `removeLiquidity` and walking off with both reserves: your entire position, say `\$20,000`, goes to effectively `\$0` the instant the pool is pulled, regardless of how careful your sizing was. Same `\$1M` headline, same `\$20k` position — one risk is a couple hundred dollars of slippage, the other is total loss. The intuition: TVL tells you how much money is in the room, but LP composition tells you whether someone can lock the door and take it.

For the full taxonomy of how these setups are built and disguised — honeypot sell-taxes, hidden mint functions, fake locks — the dedicated walkthrough is in [rug-pull and honeypot detection](/blog/trading/onchain/rug-pull-and-honeypot-detection), and the broader pre-buy process lives in the [on-chain due-diligence checklist](/blog/trading/onchain/onchain-due-diligence-checklist).

## Concentrated liquidity: why a \$2M pool can have \$50k of real depth

The constant-product model we have used so far is Uniswap v2's. It spreads a liquidity provider's capital across *every* price from zero to infinity — most of which the price will never visit, so most of that capital sits idle. Uniswap v3 (and the many DEXes that copied it) fixed the capital inefficiency with *concentrated liquidity*: each LP chooses a *price range* — a band — and their capital only works while the price is inside that band. Inside the band they earn fees and provide depth; outside it, their capital is parked, earning nothing and providing no tradable depth at the current price.

This is brilliant for LP returns and treacherous for anyone reading TVL. Because TVL counts *all* the deposited capital regardless of where its ranges sit, a v3 pool can advertise a large headline while almost none of that capital is actually straddling the current price. The depth you can trade against — the *active liquidity* at the current tick — can be a small fraction of the TVL.

![A v3 pool showing large total value locked but only a thin band of active liquidity at the current price](/imgs/blogs/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools-5.png)

The figure shows the trap. On the left, the dashboard reports `\$2M` TVL in a v3 pool — but v3 LPs each pick a range, and most of these ranges sit *above* the current price, set by people who provided liquidity expecting a pump that never came (or who are deliberately parking capital out of range to inflate the headline). Out-of-range liquidity earns no fees and trades against nothing right now. On the right is the reality: only the band straddling the spot price is live — say `\$50k` of active range. A trade larger than that band *crosses ticks* into empty price zones, where the price jumps fast because there is little or nothing to trade against. The real depth is roughly 40× thinner than the headline.

**How to read it.** Do not read v3 TVL as depth. Read the **liquidity depth chart** that good explorers and Uniswap's own analytics show — a histogram of how much liquidity sits at each price tick. The bar *at the current price* is what you trade against first; the bars just above and below tell you how fast price will move as your trade walks through them. A pool with a tall bar at spot and tall neighbors is genuinely deep; a pool with a huge total but a tiny bar at spot is a `\$2M` mirage with `\$50k` of usable depth. This is the v3-specific version of "TVL is an upper bound" — and the gap can be enormous.

#### Worked example: \$2M of "TVL" with real depth around \$50k

A token's v3 pool shows `\$2,000,000` TVL. You inspect the liquidity depth chart and find that the active range straddling the current price holds only about `\$50,000` of liquidity; the other `\$1,950,000` is parked in ranges set well above spot. You want to buy `\$40,000`.

Against the `\$2M` headline, a `\$40,000` buy looks like 2% of the pool — trivial. Against the *real* `\$50,000` of active depth, your `\$40,000` is **80% of the tradable liquidity at the current price**. You will blow straight through the active band, cross ticks into thinly stocked price zones, and pay a brutal average — easily 30–50% impact, the kind of fill that looks like a flash crash on the chart. Meanwhile the headline TVL barely moved, so a casual observer would never know what happened. The intuition: in a concentrated pool, the number that constrains you is the liquidity *at the price*, not the liquidity *in the contract* — and confusing the two is how people get filled 40× worse than they expected.

The deliberate version of this — making a pool *look* deep while it is structurally thin, to bait buyers and mislead price oracles — is its own attack surface, covered in [fake depth, spoofing, and oracle attacks](/blog/trading/onchain/fake-depth-spoofing-and-oracle-attacks).

## Exit-liquidity reality: market cap is a multiplication, not a promise

We can now confront the SafeMoon problem head-on, because everything above feeds into it. Market cap is `last price \* total supply`. It is a multiplication. It quietly assumes you could sell every token at the last price, which is exactly the assumption the AMM curve destroys: the act of selling moves the price down against you, and a shallow pool moves it catastrophically.

![A 1M paper bag selling into a 200k pool and realizing far less than its market value](/imgs/blogs/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools-6.png)

The figure traces the gap from bag to reality. Your bag — 2,000,000 tokens at `\$0.50` — is "worth" `\$1,000,000` on paper, and the market cap makes that feel like real wealth. But the pool has only `\$200,000` of quote on the sell side. As you start selling, each token sold lowers the token reserve's counterpart and the price falls fast along the curve. You realize maybe `\$90,000` before the price is wrecked, and the rest of the bag is stranded — near-worthless, because there are no buyers left at any price that matters. The paper million was always fiction; the `\$200k` pool was the only real number, and you could only extract a fraction of *that*.

#### Worked example: selling a \$1M bag into a \$200k pool

You hold 2,000,000 tokens marked at `\$0.50` each — a `\$1,000,000` bag on paper. The pool is shallow: `\$200,000` of quote (USDC) on the sell side, balanced against 400,000 tokens (so spot is `\$200,000 / 400,000 = \$0.50`, matching your mark), giving `k = 200,000 \* 400,000 = 80,000,000,000`.

You try to dump the whole bag — add all 2,000,000 tokens to the pool. The new token reserve is `x' = 400,000 + 2,000,000 = 2,400,000`. Holding `k` constant, the new quote reserve is `y' = 80,000,000,000 / 2,400,000 = \$33,333`. You receive `\$200,000 - \$33,333 = \$166,667` — and the spot price has collapsed from `\$0.50` to `y' / x' = 33,333 / 2,400,000 = \$0.0139`, about **a 97% crash**. In practice you would never dump it all at once; selling more gradually, the *first* slug clears near the mid but the price falls off a cliff well before you are out, and a realistic realized number is closer to **half** the paper value or worse — roughly a 50% haircut on a `\$1M` mark, with the tail of the bag stranded. The intuition: your bag is worth what the *pool* can pay, not what the *price* says — and when the bag dwarfs the pool, selling is the thing that destroys the price.

This is *exit liquidity* in one picture, and it is the reason "size to the pool, not to the market cap" is the central rule of this whole post. A `\$50M` market cap on a `\$300k` pool is not a `\$50M` opportunity; it is a `\$300k` pool with a marketing department. The relationship between a token's supply, how concentrated it is in a few wallets, and how thin its pool is — the people who *can* dump on you — is the holder-side of this same coin, covered in [supply distribution and holder concentration](/blog/trading/onchain/supply-distribution-and-holder-concentration).

**Whose exit liquidity are you?** There is a darker reading of the same picture worth naming, because it reframes how you look at a hot, thin token. When insiders, the team, or early snipers hold large bags against a shallow pool, the buyers arriving late are not *trading* — they are *the exit liquidity*. The pump exists to attract enough incoming dollars that the early bags can be sold into them without crashing the price to zero. The on-chain tell is the combination this post and its siblings teach you to read together: a thin pool, supply concentrated in a few wallets, and a price chart that only goes up while those wallets quietly distribute. If you can read the pool and you can read the holders, you can usually tell whether you are the one buying the dip or the one being sold into. The arithmetic of the pool — every buy you make adds quote to the very reserve the insiders are draining — is what makes "exit liquidity" a literal mechanical role, not just a slur.

**Why the deepest pools are the safest, even at a worse entry.** A practical corollary falls out of all this. Given a choice between a token whose price is `\$0.50` in a deep `\$5M` pool and the *same* token at `\$0.48` in a thin `\$200k` pool, the deep pool is almost always the better trade despite the slightly worse entry, because your *exit* — the part that actually determines your realized return — is bounded by depth, and the deep pool's exit is dramatically better. New traders optimize the entry price they can see; experienced on-chain traders optimize the exit liquidity they have measured. The two-cent saving on entry is dwarfed by the tens of percent you would lose to slippage and MEV trying to leave the thin pool. Read depth as a feature of the trade, not a footnote.

## How MEV exploits thin pools

There is one more reason thin pools are dangerous, and it is not the team — it is the bots. *MEV* (maximal extractable value) is the profit that sophisticated actors extract by reordering, inserting, or censoring transactions inside a block. On a public blockchain, your pending swap sits in the *mempool* — the waiting room of unconfirmed transactions — visible to everyone before it is mined. Bots read it, and on a thin pool, a visible large swap is an invitation.

The classic play is the *sandwich*. A bot sees your `\$100,000` buy heading for a thin pool. It front-runs you with its own buy (pushing the price up the curve first), lets *your* buy execute at the now-worse price (pushing it up further), then back-runs by selling into the elevated price you created. You get filled high; the bot pockets the difference. The thinner the pool, the steeper the curve, and the more a bot can extract from a given trade size — which is precisely why MEV concentrates on shallow, high-impact pools. Your slippage tolerance is the bot's profit ceiling: set it loose on a thin pool and you are explicitly authorizing the sandwich.

#### Worked example: a sandwich on a \$50k swap into a thin pool

You submit a `\$50,000` buy into a thin pool with `\$250,000` of quote (so your order is 20% of the reserve — a fat, juicy target). A bot front-runs with `\$30,000`, pushing the spot price up roughly 13% before your trade lands. Your `\$50,000` then executes against the already-elevated reserves, filling you at an average maybe 9% worse than the pre-sandwich mid — call it about `\$4,500` of extra cost you would not have paid in an unsandwiched fill. The bot immediately sells its `\$30,000` of tokens into the price *you* just pushed up, and walks away with a chunk of that `\$4,500` as profit, minus gas. The intuition: in a thin pool your own market impact becomes someone else's paycheck, so the depth of the pool sets not just your slippage but how much a bot can skim on top of it.

The mechanics, the live mempool view, and the defenses (private order flow, tight slippage, splitting orders) are the subject of [MEV, sandwiches, and front-running](/blog/trading/onchain/mev-sandwiches-and-frontrunning). For our purposes the lesson is narrower and feeds straight into the playbook: **thin pools cost you twice — once in raw price impact, and again in the MEV your impact invites.**

## How to read it: a walkthrough

Let us put it all together as a single pass you run before buying anything. We will read a hypothetical token, "TOK," the way you would on DEX Screener plus the DEX's own interface. The point is the *sequence* — the order in which you ask the questions — because each answer can kill the trade before you waste time on the next.

**Step 1 — Find the deepest pool and read its reserves.** Pull up TOK on DEX Screener. It lists four pairs; the deepest, by far, is TOK/USDC on Uniswap v3 with a headline `\$2,000,000` of liquidity. The other three are dust. Good — there is one real pool, paired against a stablecoin, on a reputable DEX. Open it on Uniswap and read the reserves. The displayed price is `\$0.50`; confirm it matches the reserve ratio. So far, so normal.

**Step 2 — Discount the TVL: is it v2 or v3, and is it in range?** The pool is v3, so the `\$2M` headline is an *upper bound*, not depth. Open the liquidity depth chart. The active band straddling `\$0.50` holds about `\$50,000`; the rest is parked in ranges above the current price. Real tradable depth at spot: **~\$50k, not \$2M** — a 40× gap. Already the picture has changed: this is a thin pool wearing a thick coat. (If it had been a v2 pool, the TVL would map more directly to depth, but you would still discount for the one-sided and removable risks below.)

**Step 3 — Estimate price impact for the size you actually want.** Type your real size into the swap box, both ways. A `\$10,000` buy against `\$50k` of active depth is 20% of it — the box warns of double-digit price impact. A `\$100,000` buy is *twice* the active depth — the box flashes a near-catastrophic number or routes you across empty ticks. Crucially, simulate a `\$10,000` *sell* too: if selling moves the price far more than buying, or the simulation fails, that one-sided behavior is a honeypot flag. The cheap test — a small simulated sell — costs nothing and catches a lot.

**Step 4 — Check the LP: who owns it, and can it vanish?** Look at the LP token holders. If 100% of LP supply is at a dead address, the depth (thin as it is) is at least *permanent* — only price impact, not a rug, can stop your exit. If the LP sits in a locker, note the unlock date and plan to be out before it. If it sits unlocked in the deployer's EOA, the `\$50k` of real depth is *also revocable in one transaction* — at which point even the thin depth you measured is a fiction. For TOK, suppose the LP is time-locked for 30 more days: usable, but with a hard deadline on the calendar.

**Step 5 — Size to exit liquidity, not market cap.** TOK's market cap is `\$50M`; its real depth is `\$50k`. The cap is irrelevant. The rule: you can exit roughly a *few percent of depth* without wrecking the price, so against `\$50k` of active liquidity a sane position you could exit cleanly is on the order of `\$1,000`–`\$2,000`, not `\$10,000` and certainly not `\$100,000`. If your intended size is larger than a few percent of the depth you actually verified, the trade is too big for this pool — full stop. That single sentence — *size to the pool* — is what the entire walkthrough exists to enable.

That five-step pass takes about two minutes per token once it is habit, and it converts a marketing headline into the only two numbers that govern your outcome: the real depth, and who can take it away. For the end-to-end version that folds in contract, holders, and history, see the [on-chain due-diligence checklist](/blog/trading/onchain/onchain-due-diligence-checklist).

## Common misconceptions

**"High TVL means it is safe to trade big."** The single most expensive myth. TVL is the dollar value of the reserves, but in a v3 pool most of it can be parked out of range (the `\$2M`-headline-but-`\$50k`-real-depth case), and in *any* pool it can be one-sided or removable. High TVL is necessary for size, not sufficient. Always confirm the depth is *at the price*, *on both sides*, and *not removable* before you trust a big number.

**"Market cap tells me how much money is in the token."** Market cap is `price \* supply` — a multiplication that assumes you can sell everything at the last price, which the AMM curve makes false. The money you can actually extract is bounded by the pool, not the cap. A `\$50M` cap on a `\$300k` pool is a `\$300k` pool. Read exit liquidity; ignore the cap when sizing.

**"The price I see is the price I will get."** The quoted spot price is the price for an *infinitesimal* trade. Your real fill is the average along the curve as your order moves the reserves, and on a thin pool that average can be far worse — the difference is slippage, and it grows non-linearly with size. The price is a starting point, not a fill.

**"My trade is small, so slippage does not apply to me."** Slippage is about your trade *relative to the pool*, not its dollar size. A `\$10,000` trade is trivial in a `\$5M` pool (about 0.5% impact) and brutal in a `\$50k` active band (about 20%+). The same dollars can be either, so always judge size against the *verified* depth, not against your sense of "small."

**"If the liquidity is there now, it will be there when I sell."** Only true if the LP is burned or locked past your horizon. Team-held, unlocked LP can be removed in one transaction, and concentrated liquidity can be pulled out of range by its providers at any time. Depth is a snapshot, not a guarantee; the guarantee comes from *who owns the LP*, which is why that check is non-negotiable.

**"High 24-hour volume means there is real demand to sell into."** Volume on a DEX is just the sum of swap sizes, and on a thin pool a wash trader can manufacture an enormous number by cycling their own funds back and forth — at near-zero net cost if they also provide the liquidity and recapture the fees. High volume on thin liquidity is the *signature* of wash trading, not a sign of buyers waiting for your tokens. Real demand shows up as net inflow and a growing, two-sided pool, not as a big volume number on a small pool. Read the volume-to-liquidity ratio and the buy/sell balance, not the raw volume.

**"A v3 pool with huge TVL is deeper than a v2 pool with smaller TVL."** Not necessarily, and often the reverse. A v2 pool spreads its capital across all prices, so its TVL maps fairly directly to depth at the current price. A v3 pool's TVL can be almost entirely parked out of range, leaving a thin active band — so a `\$2M` v3 headline can be *shallower* at spot than a `\$500k` v2 pool whose capital is all in play. Comparing headline TVL across pool types is comparing two different things; only the active depth at the price is comparable.

## The playbook: what to do with it

This is the liquidity check you run before any buy, written as signal → read → action → false-positive, so it survives contact with a real screen.

**The liquidity check before any buy.**

- **Signal:** one real pool, paired against a stablecoin or wrapped ether, on a reputable DEX. **Read:** price set in one place you can verify (`y / x`). **Action:** proceed to depth. **False-positive / invalidation:** if the "deepest" pool is on an obscure DEX or paired against another illiquid token, the depth is probably staged — treat with suspicion.
- **Signal:** v3 pool with a big TVL headline. **Read:** TVL is an upper bound; open the depth chart and read the *active* liquidity at spot. **Action:** use real active depth, not TVL, for every sizing decision. **False-positive:** a tall bar at spot with tall neighbors *is* genuine depth — do not reflexively discount a v3 pool that is honestly concentrated around the price.
- **Signal:** the swap box shows your intended size's price impact. **Read:** impact for both buy *and* sell; a one-sided pool that sells far worse than it buys is a honeypot tell. **Action:** if a small simulated sell moves the price hard or fails, walk away. **False-positive:** modest asymmetry from fees or a small fee-on-transfer tax is not always malicious — confirm with the contract, but a *large* asymmetry is a hard no.
- **Signal:** LP token holders. **Read:** burned (permanent) > time-locked (safe until the date) > team-held/unlocked (revocable). **Action:** require burned or time-locked LP; no lock means no buy, full stop. **False-positive:** a "lock" through an unknown, unaudited locker contract can be fake — verify the locker, not just the word "locked."
- **Signal:** market cap vs pool depth. **Read:** market cap is a paper number; exit liquidity is bounded by the pool. **Action:** size to a few percent of *verified* depth, never to the cap. **False-positive:** a deep pool with a modest cap is the *good* case — small cap is not itself a flag, thin depth is.

![Matrix of liquidity red flags mapping each signal to its meaning and the defensive action](/imgs/blogs/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools-7.png)

The figure above is the laminated version of the playbook — four red flags you can check on a DEX explorer in under a minute each. **Thin depth versus cap:** a `\$5M` market cap on `\$80k` of pool means the cap is paper; size to the pool and assume you can sell only a few percent of depth. **One-sided pool:** almost all value on the buy side means the depth is spoofed — deep to buy into, shallow the instant you sell; simulate a sell and if it moves price hard, walk away. **LP not locked:** LP tokens in a team EOA with no lock or burn means the pool can be removed in one transaction; require burned or time-locked LP or do not buy. **TVL all out of range:** a big v3 TVL with a tiny active-range bar means the real tradable depth is a fraction of the headline; read active liquidity at the current tick before you size. Any single hard red flag is a do-not-buy. The discipline is not cleverness — it is refusing to size to a number you have not verified.

**Sizing to exit liquidity — the one rule.** Decide your position from the depth, not the price. Take the *active* depth at spot (after the v3 discount), assume you can exit a few percent of it without serious slippage, and let that cap your size. If the math says your sane size is `\$1,500` and you wanted to put in `\$15,000`, the pool — not your conviction — is telling you the trade does not fit. The whole point of reading a pool is to let the chain tell you how big you are *allowed* to be, before the exit tells you the hard way.
