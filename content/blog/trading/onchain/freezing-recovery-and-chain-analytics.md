> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Tracing tells you *where* stolen crypto went; recovery needs a separate toolkit of off-chain levers, and most stolen funds are never recovered because the people who hold those levers either cannot reach the money or are too slow.
>
> - **Tracing ≠ recovery.** The blockchain has no undo button. You can watch a thief's balance in real time and be completely unable to touch it. Recovery requires someone *off-chain* with authority — a token issuer, an exchange, or a court.
> - **The levers**: stablecoin issuers (Tether/Circle) can blacklist an address and freeze their token; KYC exchanges can freeze a deposit and unmask the casher; OFAC can sanction addresses so everyone must screen them; analytics firms (Chainalysis/TRM/Elliptic) supply the labels and the case support; law enforcement can seize keys and accounts; and sometimes the thief takes a bounty and gives it back.
> - **What you DO**: run the recovery decision tree in parallel and *fast* — trace, alert exchanges, request a stablecoin freeze, engage analytics + law enforcement, apply public pressure — because every lever has a closing window.
> - **The number to remember**: the U.S. seizure of ~\$3.6B in Bitcoin tied to the 2016 Bitfinex hack is the high-water mark for recovery — and it is famous precisely because outcomes like it are *rare*. State-actor hacks like the \$1.34B Lazarus stole in 2024 mostly launder straight past every chokepoint.

On 23 March 2022, an attacker drained roughly \$625M from the Ronin Bridge — the cross-chain bridge behind the game Axie Infinity. Within days, blockchain analysts had the exploiter's address, watched the funds, and labeled them. The FBI publicly attributed the theft to the Lazarus Group, North Korea's state hacking apparatus. Everyone could *see* the money. For most of it, seeing was all anyone could do: the funds were swapped, bridged, and pushed through the Tornado Cash mixer, and the bulk of it was never recovered.

Compare that to August 2021, when a hacker took roughly \$611M from Poly Network — at the time the largest DeFi exploit ever. Within 48 hours the attacker began *returning* the funds, eventually giving back essentially all of it in exchange for a bug-bounty offer and the absence of criminal charges. Two of the largest crypto thefts in history, separated by months. One vanished. One came back almost in full. The difference was not how good the tracing was — both were traced quickly. The difference was *which levers could be pulled, and whether anyone could pull them in time.*

That gap — between seeing the money and getting it back — is what this post is about. Tracing is the subject of the rest of this series. Recovery is a different discipline with a different toolkit, and it belongs to the defender, the compliance officer, the investigator, and the lawyer as much as the on-chain analyst.

It is worth being honest about the scale of the problem up front, because it sets the tone for everything that follows. By Chainalysis's tallies, the value stolen from crypto platforms ran to roughly \$3.3B in 2021, \$3.8B in 2022, \$1.7B in 2023, and \$2.2B in 2024 — and across all of that, the share that is ever clawed back is a minority. The headline successes you've heard of are headlines precisely *because* they are unusual. For every Poly Network or Bitfinex, there are a dozen thefts where the money was traced perfectly and recovered not at all. A defender who walks in expecting tracing to equal recovery will be disappointed and, worse, will waste the precious early minutes when the levers that *do* work still have a window. The right mindset is the opposite: assume recovery is hard, move fast on the few levers that exist, and treat each one as a window that is already closing.

## Foundations: why tracing is not recovery, and who can actually act

Before any of the specific levers make sense, you need one uncomfortable fact in your bones: **a public blockchain has no undo button, and no on-chain actor can claw a transfer back.** When you send Bitcoin or Ether, the network's only job is to confirm that you controlled the keys and that the math added up. It does not ask whether you *should* have sent it. There is no chargeback, no fraud department inside the protocol, no "reverse this transaction" call. The same property that makes the chain censorship-resistant — nobody can stop a valid transaction — also makes it un-reversible: nobody can undo one either.

So "we traced the funds" and "we recovered the funds" are two completely different sentences. Tracing is a *visibility* problem: given a starting address, where did the money go? That is what the labeling, clustering, and flow-following techniques in the rest of this series solve, and they solve it well — the chain is permanent and public, so a determined analyst can usually follow the money a long way. Recovery is an *authority* problem: who has the power to do something about it? And the answer is almost never "the chain itself."

![Before and after panels contrasting tracing which shows where stolen funds sit with recovery which needs an off-chain actor with authority to act](/imgs/blogs/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics-2.png)

Let me define the cast of characters who *can* act, because each one is a separate lever with its own rules.

**EOA vs contract.** An address on Ethereum is either an *externally owned account* (EOA) — controlled by a private key, like a person's wallet — or a *contract account*, which is code. This matters because some recovery levers act on a *contract* (a token's smart contract can be told to freeze an address) and others act on a *key holder* (a court can compel a person to hand over a private key, or seize the device holding it). You cannot "freeze" an EOA's native ETH from the outside; you *can* freeze a token balance if that token's contract has the right switch.

**Native coin vs token.** A *native coin* is the chain's own money — BTC on Bitcoin, ETH on Ethereum, SOL on Solana, TRX on Tron. No issuer controls it; that's the point. A *token* is something defined by a smart contract sitting on top of a chain — and many tokens, including the big stablecoins, give their *issuer* administrative powers over the token. This single distinction is the difference between "freezable" and "not freezable." Stolen ETH cannot be frozen by anyone. Stolen USDT *can* be frozen by Tether.

**Stablecoin.** A token designed to hold a steady value, usually \$1, backed by reserves and run by a company. The two giants are **USDT** (Tether) and **USDC** (Circle). For the recovery story, the only fact that matters is that both are *centralized*: the issuer's contract maintains a **blacklist**, and the issuer can add any address to it. A blacklisted address can no longer send or receive that token — the transfers simply revert. (For the full mechanics of how these tokens work and why they dominate, see [stablecoins, Tether, Circle, and the shadow dollar](/blog/trading/crypto/stablecoins-tether-circle-shadow-dollar).)

**Exchange / off-ramp.** An *on-ramp* turns fiat into crypto; an *off-ramp* turns crypto back into fiat (a bank transfer you can spend). A *centralized exchange* (CEX) — Binance, Coinbase, Kraken, OKX — is the dominant off-ramp, and a regulated one runs **KYC** ("know your customer"): every account is tied to a verified real-world identity. This is the crucial property. An exchange controls its own deposit addresses and its own customer database. If stolen funds land in a deposit address it controls, it can *freeze* them and it knows *who* deposited them. (Background: [centralized crypto exchanges, Binance and Coinbase](/blog/trading/crypto/centralized-crypto-exchanges-binance-coinbase).)

**OFAC and the SDN list.** The U.S. Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control maintains the *Specially Designated Nationals* (SDN) list — the sanctions list. Since 2018 it has included crypto *addresses*. Once an address (or a contract, as with Tornado Cash in 2022) is sanctioned, U.S. persons and any business that touches the U.S. financial system are legally prohibited from transacting with it. Sanctioning doesn't freeze the funds directly — the chain doesn't read the SDN list — but it makes the funds **toxic**: every compliant exchange, issuer, and counterparty must screen against the list and will refuse or freeze anything tied to a sanctioned address.

**Chain-analytics firm.** Companies like **Chainalysis**, **TRM Labs**, and **Elliptic** sell labeled blockchain data and compliance tooling. They are not law enforcement and they cannot freeze anything. What they sell is *attribution at scale* — a constantly updated map of which addresses belong to which exchange, mixer, sanctioned entity, scam, or hack — plus screening tools the exchanges and issuers use, plus investigative support to law enforcement. They are the connective tissue: the labels that let the other levers fire correctly.

**Seizure.** When law enforcement obtains a court order and gets physical or legal control of the *private keys* (or of an account at a custodian), it can move the funds to a government-controlled wallet. That is *seizure* — the only lever that actually takes possession of native coins like BTC. It usually requires identifying a person or a server, and across borders it requires **MLATs** (Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties) — slow, formal cooperation requests between governments.

**White-hat / bounty.** Sometimes the thief is reachable and rational. A *white-hat* (or "grey-hat") deal offers the attacker a **bug bounty** — a percentage of the stolen funds — plus, often, the implicit promise of no prosecution, in exchange for returning the rest. This is not a lever anyone *holds*; it is a negotiation, and it only works when the thief wants money more than they want to disappear.

There's one more piece of mental furniture worth installing: the idea that recovery is fundamentally a *race between two parties with asymmetric information and timing.* The thief usually knows the hack is coming — they planned it — and has often pre-mapped the entire laundering route before they pull the trigger: which DEX to swap on, which bridge to cross, which mixer to enter, which no-KYC venue to cash out at. The defenders, by contrast, are reacting. They learn about the hack after it happens, sometimes hours later, and must then coordinate across multiple independent companies, legal systems, and time zones to fire the levers. So the structural picture is a sprinter (the thief, running a memorized course) against a posse that first has to be assembled. Recovery, when it succeeds, almost always means the posse caught the thief at a *chokepoint* — a place where the thief had to slow down and the defenders happened to have a lever. Keep that race in mind; it explains both why the levers exist where they do and why they so often fire too late.

Hold those nine definitions and that one mental picture. Every recovery story in this post is some combination of the levers, fired in some order, against some clock.

## Lever 1: stablecoin freezes — the issuer's blacklist switch

The single most effective recovery lever in crypto is also the one that most offends crypto's founding ideals: a private company can freeze your money with one transaction.

USDT and USDC are not bearer instruments like cash. They are IOUs issued by a company, represented as tokens, and the issuing company wrote the token's smart contract. Tether's contract includes functions — historically named things like `addBlackList` — that let Tether designate an address as blacklisted. Once blacklisted, that address cannot transfer the token and cannot receive it; the contract checks the blacklist on every transfer and reverts if either party is on it. Circle's USDC has an equivalent mechanism. The funds aren't *taken* — they sit there, visible, untouchable, until the issuer decides what to do (often, later, to burn-and-reissue them to a court-designated recipient).

![Before and after panels showing a stablecoin balance that is freely transferable before the issuer blacklists the address and stranded and un-cashable after](/imgs/blogs/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics-4.png)

Why does this work so well? Because stablecoins are the *most liquid exit rail in crypto*. A thief who steals volatile tokens has a problem: to realize value, they need to convert to something stable and spendable, and the deepest, most universally accepted stable asset is USDT (especially on Tron and Ethereum). So stolen funds gravitate toward stablecoins — which is exactly where the freeze lever lives. The thief's need for liquidity walks them into the one place they can be frozen.

The tradeoff is stark and worth stating plainly: this is *centralization*. A token that one company can freeze is a token that company — or a government leaning on that company — can censor. Tether and Circle have frozen addresses at law-enforcement request, in response to hacks, and to comply with sanctions. Each freeze that recovers stolen funds is also a demonstration that these "dollars on the blockchain" are permissioned. That is a genuine cost, not a footnote; the same switch that saves a hack victim could, in principle, be used to freeze a dissident. The defender's job is to use the lever responsibly and to understand that it exists; the cypherpunk's critique of it is the subject of [Bitcoin and the cypherpunk vision](/blog/trading/crypto/bitcoin-and-the-cypherpunk-vision).

#### Worked example: Tether freezing the Poly Network USDT

When the Poly Network attacker took ~\$611M across three chains in August 2021, a chunk of it was in USDT. Tether responded by **freezing roughly \$33M of that USDT** very quickly — blacklisting the address holding it so the tokens could not move.

Walk the arithmetic of why that mattered. Say the thief held 33,000,000 USDT, each redeemable for \$1, for a frozen value of **\$33,000,000**. Before the freeze, that balance was as good as a \$33M bank wire the thief could route anywhere. The instant Tether called the blacklist function, the *spendable* value of that balance dropped to **\$0** — not because the number on-chain changed (it still read 33,000,000) but because no transfer would execute. The thief's realizable position went from \$33,000,000 to effectively \$0 in one transaction that cost Tether a few dollars of gas. That \$33M became un-cashable, and it was part of the pressure that pushed the attacker toward returning the whole haul. **The lesson: a stablecoin freeze converts a liquid balance into a frozen number, and the cost to the defender is trivial while the cost to the thief is the entire balance.**

The limit of this lever is equally important. It only works on the issuer's *own* token. The moment the thief swaps USDT for ETH on a decentralized exchange, the freeze lever evaporates — ETH has no issuer to call. Sophisticated attackers know this and convert *out* of freezable assets as fast as possible. The race, then, is whether the freeze request reaches the issuer before the thief reaches a swap. Minutes matter.

A few operational details make the freeze lever sharper than it first appears. First, issuers freeze proactively as well as reactively: Tether in particular publishes that it has frozen *hundreds of millions of dollars* of USDT over the years at the request of law enforcement, often pre-emptively flagging addresses tied to scams, sanctioned entities, and hacks before a specific victim even asks. Second, a freeze is reversible by the issuer — the funds can later be *burned and reissued* to a court-designated recipient, which is how a frozen balance eventually becomes a returned balance rather than just a stranded one. Third, the freeze applies per-chain: USDT exists as separate contracts on Ethereum, Tron, and other chains, so a freeze must be applied on whichever chain holds the funds, and the thief's chain-hopping is partly an attempt to find a chain or a moment where the issuer is slower to act. None of this changes the headline — convert out and the lever dies — but it explains why, in the window where funds *are* in a stablecoin, this is the fastest and cheapest recovery move available.

There is also a subtler strategic point. Because everyone knows stablecoins are freezable, the mere *threat* of a freeze shapes thief behavior. A rational attacker treats time-in-stablecoin as a liability and minimizes it, which means the defender's job is partly to *force* funds to dwell in freezable form — for instance, when liquidity conditions or the sheer size of a haul make it hard to swap \$100M+ out of stablecoins quickly without enormous slippage. A \$600M position cannot be dumped into ETH in one transaction without moving the market against the seller by tens of millions of dollars; that friction can keep funds parked in USDT long enough for a freeze to land. The lever and the market microstructure interact.

## Lever 2: exchange cooperation — the off-ramp chokepoint

Stolen crypto is, until it's cashed out, just numbers in a wallet. To become spendable money — rent, weapons, a yacht — it has to pass through an *off-ramp*. And the dominant off-ramp, by far, is a centralized exchange that converts crypto to fiat and sends it to a bank. That funnel is the single most important geographic fact in the whole recovery map: **the off-ramp is the chokepoint, and a regulated KYC exchange is where funds can be both frozen and de-anonymized.**

![Graph showing stolen funds routing to a KYC exchange where they can be frozen versus P2P and DEX exits where funds cash out and recovery fails](/imgs/blogs/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics-3.png)

When stolen funds hit a deposit address at a compliant exchange, two things become possible that are impossible anywhere else on-chain. First, the exchange controls that address and the customer balance behind it, so it can *freeze* the deposit — lock the account, prevent withdrawal. Second, because the exchange ran KYC on whoever owns that account, it knows the *real identity* of the casher: name, ID document, often a selfie, an IP address, a bank account. A frozen deposit plus a verified identity is the difference between "we lost the money" and "we recovered the money and here is who took it." For the trader's-eye view of why exchange flows are such a rich signal in the first place, see [exchange flows, inflows and outflows](/blog/trading/onchain/exchange-flows-inflows-and-outflows).

This is why **speed is the whole game** at the off-ramp. Exchanges receive thousands of deposits a minute. A freeze request that arrives *before* the casher withdraws to their bank can lock the funds; one that arrives an hour later finds an empty account. The professional response to a hack is to blast every major exchange's compliance and security team — usually via established channels and the analytics firms' alerting networks — within *minutes*, with the tainted addresses, so that any deposit from those addresses trips a freeze.

#### Worked example: an exchange freezing a \$2M deposit and unmasking the casher

Take a representative case (the pattern recurs across dozens of real ones). A scammer drains a victim of stolen tokens, swaps them to USDT, and sends 2,000,000 USDT — **\$2,000,000** — to a deposit address at a major KYC exchange, planning to convert to fiat and withdraw to a bank.

The victim's team had already traced the theft and sent the exchange the source addresses. The exchange's screening matched the incoming \$2,000,000 deposit to the flagged cluster and **froze the account before withdrawal**. Now run the value: the thief believed they held a spendable \$2,000,000; the freeze dropped the realizable value to **\$0** pending investigation. More valuable than the money, in the long run, was the *identity* — the KYC file showed a verified name and a bank account, which became the lead that let law enforcement build a case. Even if only \$1,400,000 of the \$2,000,000 was ultimately returned after fees and process, that is \$1.4M recovered and one casher identified — an outcome impossible on a no-KYC exit. **The lesson: at a KYC off-ramp, a fast freeze locks the money *and* hands you the human behind the address; both are levers no decentralized exit provides.**

The limit, again, defines the lever. A *decentralized* exchange (DEX) has no deposit addresses to freeze and no KYC file to reveal — it is just a smart contract. Peer-to-peer (P2P) trading desks and OTC brokers in loosely regulated jurisdictions may run little or no KYC. Every dollar that exits through a no-KYC venue is a dollar that slipped the chokepoint. The entire laundering art — explored in [how stolen funds are laundered](/blog/trading/onchain/how-stolen-funds-are-laundered) — is about reaching those leaky exits before the legitimate ones can react.

It's worth dwelling on *why* exchange cooperation works at all, because it isn't guaranteed. A regulated exchange has every incentive to cooperate: it is itself a regulated financial business that must not launder money, it does not want stolen funds on its books, and refusing a credible law-enforcement or victim request invites regulatory and reputational damage. So the major compliant venues — Coinbase, Kraken, the regulated arms of Binance and OKX — generally *will* freeze on a credible, well-documented request. The friction is rarely willingness; it is *speed and proof*. Compliance teams need enough evidence to justify freezing a customer's funds (freezing the wrong account creates its own liability), and assembling that proof and routing it to the right team takes time the thief is spending. This is the operational reason the analytics firms matter so much for this lever: their alerting networks and standardized risk scores let an exchange act on a flagged deposit in seconds rather than after a manual review. A victim with a Chainalysis-backed alert gets a far faster freeze than a victim emailing support.

The flip side is the *uncooperative* venue. Some exchanges, historically, have been slow, indifferent, or actively complicit — and a sophisticated thief will deliberately route to whichever venue is least likely to freeze. This is why the chokepoint is not a single point but a *spectrum*: a top-tier KYC exchange is a strong recovery point, a mid-tier offshore venue is a weak one, and a no-KYC DEX is no point at all. Mapping which venues are strong and which are leaky is part of an analyst's standing knowledge, and it directly shapes how a thief launders.

## Lever 3: OFAC sanctions — making funds toxic by decree

The third lever doesn't freeze anything directly. It works on *everyone else*.

When OFAC adds an address — or, in a landmark 2022 move, an entire mixing *contract* — to the SDN list, it doesn't change the blockchain. The chain still happily processes transactions to and from a sanctioned address; it has no idea what a sanction is. What changes is the legal status of touching those funds. Any U.S. person, and any business that wants access to the U.S. financial system (which is nearly all of them), is now prohibited from transacting with the sanctioned address. Overnight, the sanctioned funds become **radioactive**: every compliant exchange screens for them and will freeze or reject anything tied to them, every issuer treats them as untouchable, every market maker steers clear. The sanction doesn't seize the money; it poisons the well around it.

The defining episode is **Tornado Cash**, sanctioned by OFAC on **8 August 2022**. Tornado Cash is an Ethereum mixer — a set of smart contracts that pool deposits so withdrawals can't be linked back to specific deposits (the mechanics are covered in [mixers, CoinJoin, and obfuscation](/blog/trading/onchain/mixers-coinjoin-and-obfuscation)). By Elliptic's estimate it had processed on the order of \$7B in lifetime volume, of which roughly 30% was tied to illicit sources, including a large slice of Lazarus Group's laundering. OFAC sanctioned the *contracts themselves* — the first time code, rather than a person or company, landed on the SDN list. The compliance ripple was enormous: exchanges began rejecting deposits with any Tornado Cash history, even from people who'd used it for legitimate privacy; some users found legitimate funds frozen because their addresses had brushed against the sanctioned contracts. The legal and philosophical fight over sanctioning a tool rather than an actor is its own deep topic — see [Tornado Cash and sanctioning code](/blog/trading/crypto/tornado-cash-and-sanctioning-code).

For recovery, the practical effect of sanctions is *prevention more than retrieval*. Sanctions rarely get a specific victim's money back. What they do is shrink the set of places stolen funds can go without becoming toxic — they raise the cost and narrow the exits, which pushes thieves toward the chokepoints where freezes and seizures *can* happen. They are a structural lever, not a surgical one.

There is a second-order effect worth naming, because it cuts against the defender's intuition. Sanctioning a tool like a mixer does *not* deter the determined adversary it was aimed at — Lazarus kept laundering after the Tornado Cash sanction, simply switching to other tools and to a sanctioned-but-functional contract that the chain still executed. What the sanction reliably does is deter *everyone who has something to lose*: the compliant exchange, the legitimate privacy-seeker, the developer who didn't want trouble. So the population most affected by a sanction is, perversely, the law-abiding one — the criminal routes around it while the compliant ecosystem absorbs the friction. This is the core of the policy debate, and it's why "sanctioning code" is so contested: the lever's incidence falls hardest on the people it wasn't meant to catch. For recovery purposes the takeaway is narrow and practical — sanctions are a tool for raising the long-run cost of laundering and for justifying downstream freezes, not a tool for getting *this* victim's money back *this* week.

Sanctions also interact with the other levers in a useful way: once an address is on the SDN list, every compliant exchange and issuer is not merely *permitted* but *required* to act against it. A sanction can thus *upgrade* a freeze request from "please consider this" to "you are legally obligated to block this," which is why investigators often push for a sanctions designation precisely to harden the exchange and stablecoin levers downstream. The sanction is the legal scaffolding that makes the freezes mandatory rather than discretionary.

#### Worked example: the compliance cost of screening against a sanctioned mixer

Quantify the ripple. Suppose Tornado Cash processed ~\$7,000,000,000 over its life and ~30% was illicit — that's ~\$2,100,000,000 of illicit volume and ~\$4,900,000,000 of everything else (privacy-seekers, the merely cautious, legitimate users). After the 8 August 2022 sanction, a compliant exchange must screen *all* incoming deposits for Tornado Cash exposure, not just the illicit slice. So the sanction's enforcement burden is spread across the full **\$7,000,000,000** of historical volume and every future interaction, even though only the \$2,100,000,000 illicit portion was the target.

For one exchange, that might mean flagging, say, 5,000 customer addresses that ever touched the mixer, each requiring manual review at perhaps \$50 of analyst time — **\$250,000** of compliance cost from a single sanction, most of it spent adjudicating legitimate users. **The lesson: a sanction is a blunt instrument that taxes the whole ecosystem to deter a minority; it makes illicit funds harder to move, but the cost lands on every compliant business and many innocent users, which is exactly the centralization-versus-privacy tension at the heart of the debate.**

## Lever 4: chain-analytics firms — the labels that make the levers fire

None of the levers above can be aimed without *attribution*: which address is the thief, which is an exchange, which is a sanctioned mixer, which deposit at which venue is the cash-out. Attribution at the scale and speed real cases need is what the analytics firms sell.

**Chainalysis**, **TRM Labs**, and **Elliptic** are the big three. Each maintains a vast, continuously updated dataset that maps blockchain addresses to real-world entities — exchanges, mixers, scams, ransomware operators, sanctioned parties, darknet markets, named hacks. They build this map through a mix of techniques covered elsewhere in this series: clustering ([address clustering and heuristics](/blog/trading/onchain/address-clustering-and-heuristics)), labeling ([labeling and attribution](/blog/trading/onchain/labeling-and-attribution)), and proprietary intelligence (subpoenas they support, undercover purchases, data partnerships with exchanges). On top of the data they sell two products: *compliance screening* (the API every regulated exchange uses to check incoming deposits against the labeled risk database in real time) and *investigation tools* plus expert support for law enforcement.

What the firms are *not*: they are not law enforcement, they hold no freeze button, and their labels are *probabilistic*, not gospel. An address labeled "high risk" might be a false positive; attribution to "Lazarus" is a confidence judgment, not a courtroom certainty (though it's often very well-founded). A defender treats their labels as strong leads to verify, not facts to act on blindly — the same skepticism this series applies to every dashboard's green number.

This is also where the gap between *retail* tools and *institutional* tools is widest. A retail analyst with Etherscan, Arkham, or a Dune dashboard (the landscape is mapped in [the on-chain tooling landscape](/blog/trading/onchain/the-onchain-tooling-landscape)) can *trace* funds remarkably far — the public chain is the same chain everyone sees. What they generally cannot do is (a) screen at exchange-grade coverage and freshness, (b) access the off-chain identity data behind a KYC deposit, or (c) trigger an exchange freeze. The institutional firms sit at the junction between the public chain and the private identity data and the parties with authority. That junction is the whole value.

#### Worked example: the unit economics of buying labels versus building them

Suppose a mid-size exchange screens 50,000 deposits a day. Building a credible in-house attribution dataset — covering every major chain, every mixer, every sanctioned address, refreshed continuously — would take a team of, say, 10 analysts and engineers at a fully loaded \$200,000 each, or **\$2,000,000 a year**, before tooling. A Chainalysis or TRM enterprise subscription might run on the order of **\$100,000–\$500,000 a year** for that screening volume.

The math is obvious: paying \$300,000 for a vastly more complete, constantly maintained dataset beats spending \$2,000,000 to build a worse one. So the analytics firms aggregate the cost of attribution across the whole industry and resell it cheaply per customer — which is *why* the labels are everywhere and why the freeze and sanction levers can fire fast. **The lesson: the analytics firms are the shared backbone that makes recovery levers cheap enough to pull at scale; they don't recover funds, they make recovery *operable* — and that's why a \$300,000 subscription replaces a \$2,000,000 internal build.**

## Lever 5: law-enforcement seizure — the only lever that takes possession

Freezing strands funds; sanctioning poisons them; but only *seizure* actually takes them. Seizure is law enforcement obtaining legal control of the assets — either by getting the private keys (from a seized device, a cooperating custodian, or an arrested suspect) or by ordering a custodian holding the funds to surrender them — and moving them to a government wallet. It is the only lever that works on un-freezable native coins like BTC, and it is the one that ends with the money actually back in custody rather than merely stuck.

It is also the slowest and most jurisdiction-bound. To seize, you typically need to (a) identify a person or a server, (b) obtain a court order, and (c) reach the keys — and if any of those is in another country, you need that country's cooperation through an **MLAT**, which can take months. Against a domestic fraudster this is workable. Against a state-sponsored actor operating from a non-cooperating jurisdiction, it is close to impossible — there is no court order you can serve on North Korea.

The high-water mark — the case every recovery story is measured against — is the **Bitfinex seizure**. In 2016, ~120,000 BTC was stolen from the Bitfinex exchange. For years the funds sat, traced but untouched. In **February 2022**, the U.S. Department of Justice announced it had **seized about 94,000 BTC, worth roughly \$3.6B at the time** — then the largest financial seizure in DOJ history — and arrested a couple alleged to have laundered the proceeds. The seizure was possible because investigators eventually obtained access to a file containing the private keys (reportedly via cloud-stored credentials), which let them move the coins into government control. It is celebrated precisely because it is *exceptional*: years of patient tracing converged on a key-access opportunity, and the coins had appreciated enormously while sitting still.

The Bitfinex case is also a useful illustration of *what seizure actually requires*, because the popular telling makes it sound like the government "hacked the blockchain." It did nothing of the sort. The chain was never broken; the math held the whole time. What investigators did was patient, conventional forensic work — following the laundered funds across many addresses and venues over years, identifying the people behind the laundering, and ultimately reaching the *keys* through ordinary investigative means (a search warrant against cloud-stored data). The blockchain was the *evidence trail*, not the obstacle. This is the general shape of every successful seizure: the chain gives you the where, but possession comes from reaching an off-chain artifact — a key file, a hardware wallet, a custodial account, a confession — through legal process aimed at a person or a server you have first had to identify. Where there is no identifiable person within reach of a cooperating court, there is no seizure, no matter how clean the trace. That single requirement — *jurisdiction over a key holder* — is what separates the recoverable cases from the un-recoverable ones, and it is exactly the requirement a state actor is built to deny.

#### Worked example: the \$3.6B Bitfinex seizure as the recovery ceiling

Run the numbers that make this the high-water mark. ~120,000 BTC was stolen in 2016, when Bitcoin was around \$600, so the theft was worth roughly 120,000 × \$600 = **\$72,000,000** at the time. The coins then sat, traced, for years. When the DOJ seized ~94,000 BTC in February 2022, Bitcoin was around \$38,000, making the seized tranche worth about 94,000 × \$38,000 ≈ **\$3,572,000,000** — the ~\$3.6B headline.

Notice what actually happened: the recovery succeeded not because of a clever on-chain move but because investigators got the *keys* — an off-chain artifact — after years of work, and because Bitcoin's price had multiplied roughly 60× while the funds sat frozen-in-place by their own un-spendability. The seizure recovered the coins, but ~26,000 BTC of the original 120,000 was *not* in the seized tranche. **The lesson: seizure is the only lever that takes possession of native coins, but it hinges on reaching the keys, it is slow and jurisdiction-bound, and even the celebrated \$3.6B case left a chunk unrecovered — which is why it's the ceiling, not the norm.**

## Lever 6: white-hat and bounty negotiation — when the thief gives it back

Sometimes the most effective lever is simply asking — with leverage. A surprising share of recovered DeFi funds came back because the attacker *chose* to return them, usually in a negotiated deal: the protocol offers a **bug bounty** (a percentage of the haul) and, often, signals that there will be no criminal referral if the rest is returned. The attacker, facing frozen stablecoins, sanctioned exits, and a fully traced trail, decides that a clean 10% is better than a hunted 100%.

The archetype is **Poly Network**. After taking ~\$611M in August 2021, the attacker — quickly dubbed "Mr. White Hat" by the protocol in a deliberate face-saving move — began returning funds within a day and ultimately gave back essentially all of it, accepting a \$500,000 bounty offer (which they reportedly declined or donated) and the absence of charges. Why? Several levers were squeezing at once: Tether had frozen a chunk of the USDT, the funds were comprehensively traced and labeled, every exchange was watching, and the attacker apparently had no realistic path to cash out \$611M without being caught. Returning it was the rational exit. (The full case is the subject of [the Poly Network returned-hack case study](/blog/trading/onchain/case-study-poly-network-and-the-returned-hack); a structurally similar return happened with **Euler Finance**'s ~\$197M in 2023.)

The crucial point for the defender: white-hat returns are not luck. They are *manufactured* by making every other exit worse. The freeze, the sanctions screening, the labeling, and the public pressure are what tilt a rational attacker toward giving it back. This lever works on profit-motivated grey-hats and not at all on ideological or state actors — Lazarus is not going to take a bounty. But for the large class of DeFi exploits carried out by opportunists, negotiation backed by real pressure is one of the highest-success levers there is.

The mechanics of the negotiation are themselves instructive. The protocol almost always opens with a *public* on-chain message — a transaction carrying a text memo addressed to the exploiter — offering specific terms: keep a percentage as a "bounty," return the rest, and we will characterize this as a white-hat security disclosure rather than a theft and will not pursue charges. The face-saving framing ("Mr. White Hat") is deliberate: it gives the attacker a narrative in which returning the funds is a *choice they made as a researcher*, not a surrender. Behind the friendly language sits the implicit threat — we have traced everything, the stablecoins are frozen, the exchanges are watching, and the alternative to taking the deal is being a hunted person sitting on unspendable money. The bounty is the carrot; the fully-applied toolkit is the stick. When both are credible, the deal closes.

This lever has a dark mirror, though, and a defender must hold it honestly: paying bounties to attackers arguably *incentivizes* future attacks by establishing that exploiting a protocol and then negotiating is a viable business model. There is genuine disagreement in the industry about whether large bounty returns are a healthy norm or a moral hazard. The pragmatic defender's position is usually that recovering 90% of user funds is worth the bounty in the specific case, while the systemic question — does this encourage the next exploit? — is real and unresolved. Either way, the lever exists, it works on the right kind of attacker, and it is built entirely on the pressure the other levers create.

#### Worked example: the bounty math that makes returning rational

Put yourself in the attacker's spreadsheet after a \$611,000,000 DeFi exploit. Option A: keep all \$611,000,000 — but Tether has frozen ~\$33,000,000 of it (now un-cashable), the rest is fully traced, every KYC exchange will freeze any deposit on sight, and any large cash-out attempt risks identification and decades in prison. The *realizable* value of "keep it all" might be a small fraction of face — and the personal risk is enormous.

Option B: return the funds, take a **\$500,000** bounty (or even \$0 plus the absence of charges), and walk away free. The expected value of Option B — modest cash, zero prison risk — can easily exceed the *risk-adjusted* value of Option A, where most of the \$611M is unspendable and the downside is incarceration. So the rational move is to return it, which is exactly what happened. **The lesson: a white-hat return is engineered by the other levers; once freezing, tracing, and screening have made the stolen funds nearly unspendable, a small bounty plus amnesty becomes the attacker's best option — recovery as the outcome of well-applied pressure.**

## Why most stolen funds are never recovered

If the toolkit is this rich, why is recovery the exception rather than the rule? Because each lever has a precise defeat condition, and a sophisticated attacker — above all a state actor — satisfies all of them at once.

Look at the outcomes of the biggest documented hacks side by side and a pattern jumps out: the *fully returned* cases (green) cluster among DeFi exploits by profit-motivated grey-hats — Poly Network, Euler, Mango, Curve — where the white-hat lever worked. The *not recovered* cases (red) cluster among the largest, most sophisticated thefts — Bybit, DMM, WazirX — which share a common attribution: state actors who launder past the chokepoints. The partial cases in between (amber) are where one lever caught some of the funds before the rest leaked away. Recovery is not random; it tracks who the thief is and which levers their methods leave exposed.

![Horizontal bar chart of the biggest crypto hacks colored by outcome showing returned cases in green seized in amber and laundered or not recovered in red](/imgs/blogs/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics-5.png)

The leaderboard also debunks a comforting myth: that the size of a theft predicts whether it comes back. It doesn't. Poly Network's ~\$611M came back almost in full while Bybit's ~\$1.46B did not, even though both were among the largest ever. What predicts recovery is not the dollar amount but the *attacker's type and method* — and that is exactly what the lever-by-lever defeat table below makes explicit.

![Matrix mapping four recovery levers to their barriers, why each blocks recovery, and how a sophisticated thief defeats it](/imgs/blogs/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics-8.png)

Walk the defeats. The **stablecoin freeze** only touches the issuer's own token, so the thief swaps out of USDT/USDC into ETH or BTC — coins with no issuer to call — as fast as possible. **Exchange cooperation** only works if funds reach a KYC exchange, so the thief routes through no-KYC DEXs, P2P desks, and cross-chain bridges, splitting into many small cash-outs below the radar. **Sanctions** deter compliant businesses but not the attacker, who uses fresh wallets and venues outside any cooperating jurisdiction. **Seizure** needs jurisdiction over keys or accounts, and a state actor sits in a country that will never honor an MLAT. Defeat all four and there is no lever left to pull.

This is exactly the Lazarus Group's playbook, and it is why North Korea-attributed thefts dominate the "never recovered" column. The chart below makes the structural point: the state-actor slice of annual crypto theft is the slice that, in practice, almost never comes home, because it is engineered from the first transaction to launder past every chokepoint.

![Stacked bar chart of value stolen from crypto platforms per year with the DPRK Lazarus attributed slice shown in red as the portion that rarely returns](/imgs/blogs/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics-6.png)

Three structural forces compound the per-lever defeats:

**Speed asymmetry.** The thief acts on their own clock and can pre-plan the entire laundering route before the hack; the defenders are reacting, coordinating across companies and time zones, often discovering the hack hours after it happened. By the time freeze requests propagate, the freezable assets may already be swapped away. The thief is sprinting a route they mapped in advance; the defenders are assembling a posse.

**Decentralized off-ramps.** Every year there are more no-KYC venues — DEXs, cross-chain bridges, privacy tools, P2P networks — that have no deposit to freeze and no identity to reveal. Cross-chain tracing is hard (it's the subject of [cross-chain tracing, bridges, and the USDT rails](/blog/trading/onchain/cross-chain-tracing-bridges-and-the-usdt-rails)), and each hop across a bridge or swap adds friction for the investigator while costing the thief almost nothing.

**Cross-border friction.** Recovery's hardest levers — seizure, especially — depend on legal cooperation that stops at national borders. A thief in a non-cooperating jurisdiction is, for the seizure lever, simply out of reach. There is no court order for Pyongyang.

#### Worked example: the \$1.34B Lazarus stole in 2024, mostly gone

In 2024, Chainalysis attributed roughly **\$1,340,000,000** of crypto theft to DPRK-linked actors — part of a cumulative North Korean haul well over **\$5,000,000,000**. Trace what happens to a typical Lazarus theft: funds are stolen, swapped out of any freezable stablecoin within minutes, bridged across chains, pushed through mixers, and split into a fan of small cash-outs through no-KYC venues in non-cooperating jurisdictions, over weeks.

Now apply the toolkit. Stablecoin freeze: defeated, because the funds left USDT/USDC immediately. Exchange freeze: defeated, because the cash-outs avoid KYC venues. Sanctions: the addresses get sanctioned, which makes the funds toxic to *compliant* parties but doesn't stop Lazarus from using non-compliant ones. Seizure: no jurisdiction. White-hat deal: a state weapons program does not take a bounty. Of the \$1,340,000,000, the recovered fraction is small — often only the portion that touched a cooperating exchange before the alerts landed, sometimes a few percent. **The lesson: against an adversary that pre-engineers the laundering route to defeat every lever, the recovery toolkit largely fails; the \$1.34B figure is, for practical purposes, a measure of money that left the building — which is why "most stolen funds are never recovered" is the honest baseline.**

## How to read it: the post-hack recovery playbook, in order and fast

When a hack happens, the difference between recovery and total loss is usually measured in *minutes*, and the right move is to fire every applicable lever *in parallel* rather than in sequence. Here is the playbook a defender runs, mapped to the decision tree below.

![Decision tree for post-hack recovery branching on asset type and destination to issuer freeze, exchange freeze, analytics plus law enforcement, and white-hat bounty](/imgs/blogs/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics-7.png)

**Step 0 — Trace and alert, immediately.** The instant the theft is confirmed, identify and label the exploiter address and broadcast it to every channel that matters: your analytics provider's alerting network, the major exchanges' security/compliance contacts, and the stablecoin issuers. Every later lever depends on this list of addresses, and every minute of delay is a minute the thief uses to move. The tracing techniques in [tracing stolen funds step by step](/blog/trading/onchain/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step) and the end-to-end walkthrough in [tracing a real flow end to end](/blog/trading/onchain/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end) are the prerequisite.

**Step 1 — If the funds are (or become) USDT/USDC, request an issuer freeze.** Contact Tether and Circle with the addresses. If the thief is sitting on stablecoins, a blacklist call strands them. This window closes the moment the thief swaps out, so it is among the most time-sensitive levers.

**Step 2 — Alert every exchange the funds might reach.** Send the tainted addresses to exchange compliance teams so that any deposit from them trips a freeze and preserves the casher's KYC. This is the chokepoint lever; it only fires if the funds head to a KYC venue, but when it fires it both locks the money and unmasks a person.

**Step 3 — Engage analytics firms and law enforcement.** Bring in Chainalysis/TRM/Elliptic for attribution and case support, and file with the relevant law-enforcement body (the FBI's crypto units, IC3, national equivalents). This is the slow lever — seizure and cross-border cooperation take months — but it is the only one that ends in *possession* and the only one with subpoena power.

**Step 4 — Apply public pressure and, if the thief is reachable, offer a bounty.** A public, on-chain message offering a white-hat deal — a percentage and amnesty for the return of the rest — combined with the visible squeeze of frozen stablecoins and watching exchanges, can tip a rational attacker toward returning the funds. This works on opportunists, not on state actors.

The realistic odds, stated honestly: against a domestic opportunist who used freezable assets and a KYC exchange, recovery odds are *good* if you move fast. Against a state-sponsored actor who pre-planned the laundering route, recovery odds are *poor* regardless of how well you execute. Most of the dollar value of crypto theft falls in the second category, which is why the aggregate recovery rate is low even though the individual levers are powerful.

## Common misconceptions

**"If you can trace it, you can get it back."** No — and this is the central confusion the post exists to fix. Tracing is a visibility problem; recovery is an authority problem. You can have a perfect, real-time trace of every dollar and still recover nothing, because the chain has no undo button and the people who *can* act (issuers, exchanges, courts) may have no reach over where the funds went. The \$1.34B Lazarus stole in 2024 was largely traced; little of it came back.

**"Crypto is irreversible, so nothing can ever be frozen."** Half-true, and the half that's false is where most recovery happens. *Native coins* (BTC, ETH) genuinely cannot be frozen by anyone. But *tokens* — including the dominant stablecoins USDT and USDC — are controlled by their issuers, who can blacklist an address with one transaction. Tether freezing \$33M of the Poly funds is the counterexample. "Irreversible" applies to the protocol, not to permissioned tokens built on top of it.

**"The analytics firms can freeze funds."** They cannot. Chainalysis, TRM, and Elliptic sell *labels, screening tools, and investigative support* — the attribution that lets the real levers (issuers, exchanges, courts) fire accurately. They hold no freeze button and no seizure power. Confusing the mapmaker with the police is a common error; the firms make recovery *operable*, they don't execute it.

**"Sanctioning an address freezes the money in it."** No. OFAC sanctions don't touch the chain — the funds keep moving on-chain just fine. What a sanction does is make the funds *toxic* to every compliant party, who must then screen and refuse them. Sanctions shrink the safe exits and deter legitimate businesses; they rarely retrieve a specific victim's funds. The Tornado Cash sanction didn't recover money — it made an entire mixer radioactive going forward.

**"The Bitfinex \$3.6B seizure proves law enforcement can get crypto back."** It proves it's *possible*, not that it's *normal*. That case is famous precisely because it's the high-water mark: years of tracing converged on a key-access opportunity, against launderers within reach of U.S. jurisdiction, and the coins had appreciated ~60× while sitting still. The typical hack — fast, cross-border, state-linked — looks nothing like it. Treat \$3.6B as the ceiling, not the expectation.

## The playbook: what to do with it

This is the if-then checklist for a defender, compliance officer, or investigator the moment funds are stolen — and the realistic read on each move.

- **Signal: funds confirmed stolen.** → **Read:** the recovery clock has started and is measured in minutes. → **Action:** trace and label the exploiter address now; broadcast the addresses to analytics alerting, exchange compliance, and stablecoin issuers simultaneously. → **False-positive guard:** confirm it's actually a theft and not a known protocol movement before you blast the network — a wrong alert burns credibility you'll need next time.

- **Signal: stolen funds are in (or convert to) USDT/USDC.** → **Read:** the issuer-freeze lever is live but closes the instant the thief swaps out. → **Action:** request a blacklist from Tether/Circle immediately. → **Invalidation:** if the funds are native ETH/BTC, there is no issuer lever — skip to the exchange and seizure levers.

- **Signal: stolen funds heading toward a deposit address at a KYC exchange.** → **Read:** this is the chokepoint — a freeze here locks the money *and* unmasks the casher. → **Action:** get the tainted addresses to that exchange's compliance team before the casher withdraws. → **Invalidation:** if funds route to a DEX, P2P desk, or no-KYC venue, the exchange lever leaks — pivot to analytics + law enforcement and don't waste the window.

- **Signal: attribution points to a profit-motivated grey-hat (DeFi exploit, opportunist).** → **Read:** the thief is rational and the other levers are squeezing their exits. → **Action:** offer a public white-hat bounty plus amnesty; let the freezes and screening do the persuading. → **Invalidation:** if attribution points to a state actor (Lazarus and similar), a bounty is pointless — set expectations for low recovery and focus on sanctions + intelligence.

- **Signal: funds have crossed into a non-cooperating jurisdiction and through no-KYC exits.** → **Read:** the seizure and freeze levers are out of reach; you are now in the "mostly not recovered" regime. → **Action:** complete sanctions referrals and law-enforcement filings for the long game (deterrence, future arrests), trace for intelligence, and brief stakeholders honestly on the odds. → **Invalidation:** don't sell false hope — most of this category never comes back, and saying so is part of the job.

- **Signal: you're tempted to treat a clean trace as a recovery.** → **Read:** seeing is not touching. → **Action:** map every traced endpoint to *which lever, held by whom, could act on it* — and if the answer is "none," the trace is intelligence, not recovery. → **Rule of thumb:** the recovery question is never "where did it go?" but "who can act on where it went, and can they act in time?"

The honest summary: the recovery toolkit is real and, against the right adversary, genuinely powerful — a fast freeze can strand a thief's entire balance, and a KYC chokepoint can hand you both the money and the human. But the toolkit's reach stops exactly where decentralization, anonymity, and national borders begin, and that is precisely the territory a sophisticated thief sprints into from the first block. That asymmetry — defenders reacting, thieves pre-planning — is why most stolen funds are never recovered, and why the famous successes are famous. Read the chain to find the money; then ask, every time, who can actually pull it back.
