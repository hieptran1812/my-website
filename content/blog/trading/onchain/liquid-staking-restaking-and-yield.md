> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Liquid staking and restaking are the biggest leverage machine in DeFi, and the chain shows you exactly how much of that leverage is stacked on the same ETH and how close it is to unwinding.
>
> - **Liquid staking** turns locked, yield-earning ETH into a tradable token (Lido's stETH). **Restaking** (EigenLayer) re-pledges that staked ETH to secure other services for extra yield, and **liquid restaking tokens (LRTs)** make *that* tradable too. Each layer reuses the same ETH.
> - Read three numbers: the **peg** (stETH/ETH ratio — a discount signals forced selling and stress), the **looped leverage** sitting in lending markets, and the **real-vs-points yield split** (much advertised "restaking APR" is token emissions, not cash).
> - What you do with it: a tight peg + modest system leverage + mostly real yield is a **hold**; a wobbly peg on heavily-looped, points-funded yield is an **avoid** — that is the configuration that cascades.
> - The number to remember: in June 2022, stETH slipped to about **0.94 ETH** — a 6% discount — and that small gap was enough to force a wave of levered unwinds. The ETH was always fine; the *leverage* was not.

In the second week of June 2022, the most boring asset in crypto started behaving strangely. Lido's stETH — a token that was supposed to be worth almost exactly one ETH, because each stETH represented one ETH staked on Ethereum plus the yield it had earned — drifted off its peg. First it was 0.99. Then 0.98. By June 13 it traded around 0.94 ETH on Curve, the deepest stETH market on-chain. A 6% gap does not sound like much. For an asset that was, by construction, redeemable one-for-one for ETH the moment withdrawals turned on, it was an earthquake.

Nothing had changed about the underlying ETH. Every stETH was still backed by a real validator securing the chain and still accruing its roughly 4% staking yield. What had changed was that the biggest holders of stETH were levered, withdrawals were *not yet enabled* (they would not go live until April 2023), and the funds holding the largest looped positions — Three Arrows Capital, Celsius, and others — needed ETH *now* to meet margin calls and redemptions. They could not redeem stETH for ETH on-chain, so they sold it into a thinning order book. Each sale widened the discount, each wider discount pushed more levered positions toward liquidation, and each liquidation produced more forced selling. The chain showed all of it in real time: the Curve pool tilting toward stETH, the big wallets unwinding, the discount feeding on itself.

That episode is the whole subject of this post compressed into one week. Liquid staking and restaking are now enormous — collectively the largest category of value in DeFi — and they work by *stacking the same ETH over and over*: stake it, tokenize the receipt, re-pledge the receipt, borrow against it, stake again. Every layer adds yield. Every layer also adds a way for the structure to come apart. The good news is that, unlike a hedge fund's leverage, *this leverage is on a public ledger*. You can read the peg, you can read how much looped exposure is in the lending markets, and you can read whether the advertised yield is real cash or just points. This post teaches you to do all three.

## Foundations: staking, liquid staking, restaking, and the peg from zero

Before any of the leverage makes sense, we need four ideas, built from nothing. If you already know what stETH is, skim; if you do not, this section is the load-bearing part.

**What staking is.** Ethereum no longer runs on miners burning electricity. Since "the Merge" in September 2022 it runs on *proof of stake*: to help produce and validate blocks, you lock up ETH as a security deposit. Lock 32 ETH, run a validator, follow the rules, and the protocol pays you newly issued ETH plus a share of transaction fees — roughly 3–4% a year at current participation. Break the rules (sign two conflicting blocks, go offline for a long time), and the protocol *slashes* you: it confiscates part of your deposit. Staking is therefore a bond you post to the network. The yield is real — it is paid in ETH, by the protocol, for doing real work (securing the chain). Hold that thought, because "the yield is real" will become the single most important test we apply to everything built on top.

A little more on the mechanics, because they matter for the risk later. A validator is a piece of software holding a key, and its job is to *attest* — to vote, every few seconds, on what the current valid head of the chain is — and occasionally to *propose* a block when it is selected. Honest, online validators earn rewards for attesting correctly. Two things lose money. The mild one is an *inactivity leak*: a validator that goes offline simply misses rewards and bleeds a small penalty, recoverable when it comes back. The severe one is *slashing*: a validator that does something provably malicious — signing two conflicting blocks, or two conflicting attestations — has a chunk of its 32 ETH confiscated and is ejected, with the penalty scaled up if many validators are slashed at the same time (the "correlation penalty"). That correlation penalty is deliberate: it makes *coordinated* failure far more expensive than isolated failure, which is exactly why a single bug that trips many validators at once is the nightmare scenario. Keep that in your pocket — restaking will multiply the number of ways a correlated slash can happen.

The yield itself is not a fixed coupon; it floats with how much ETH is staked. The more validators competing for the same issuance, the thinner each one's slice — so staking yield gently *falls* as the staking ratio rises, and rises when stakers leave. On top of issuance, validators earn priority fees and the value of ordering transactions (the realm of MEV, maximal extractable value), which is lumpier and bigger when the chain is busy. The headline "3.5%" is therefore a blend of a stable issuance component and a variable fee component, and in a quiet market the real yield can be lower than the banner. None of this changes the core fact that the yield is *paid in ETH for real work* — but it does mean the durable number is a range, not a constant, and you should read it as such.

The catch with raw staking is **lockup and lumpiness**. You need 32 ETH (a large sum), you need to run reliable infrastructure, and your ETH is committed — to exit you join a *withdrawal queue* that can take days or weeks. For most people that is too much friction. Enter the next idea.

**What a liquid staking token (LST) is.** A liquid staking protocol — Lido is by far the largest — solves the friction by pooling everyone's ETH, running the validators on their behalf, and handing each depositor a *token* that represents their staked ETH plus its accruing yield. Lido's token is **stETH**. Deposit 1 ETH with Lido, receive 1 stETH. Your stETH balance (or its exchange rate, depending on the version) grows as the staking rewards accrue, so over a year your stETH is worth about 1.035 ETH. Crucially, stETH is an ordinary ERC-20 token: you can hold it, sell it, lend it, or post it as collateral *while it keeps earning the staking yield*. That is the "liquid" in liquid staking — your stake is no longer frozen; it is a receipt you can use.

Say it plainly: an LST is a tradable IOU for staked ETH. The protocol holds the real ETH and runs the validators; you hold a token that should always be worth the ETH behind it plus the yield it has earned. (Lido is the dominant LST, but the same structure describes Rocket Pool's rETH, Coinbase's cbETH, Binance's wBETH, and others.)

There are two accounting flavors of LST and it is worth knowing which you are reading, because they look different on-chain. A *rebasing* token (classic stETH) keeps a price of ~1 ETH and grows your *balance* daily as rewards accrue — you start with 100 stETH and a year later hold ~103.5 stETH each still worth ~1 ETH. A *reward-bearing* token (wstETH, rETH, cbETH) keeps your balance fixed and lets the *exchange rate* drift up — you hold 100 wstETH and a year later it is worth ~103.5 ETH. The economics are identical; the difference matters because lending markets and DeFi integrations almost always use the reward-bearing version (a balance that silently changes underfoot breaks a lot of contracts), and because when you read a "peg" you must compare the right number — the *exchange-rate* version against ETH, not a raw token price that already bakes in a year of accrual. Confusing the two makes a perfectly healthy token look like it trades at 1.03 "above peg" when it is simply a unit that represents more than one ETH by design.

Why does this matter for an investor at all? Because the LST is the base unit of the entire edifice we are about to build. Every loop, every restaking position, every LRT is denominated in, or collateralized by, an LST. If you misread the base unit's peg, its redemption path, or its yield, every layer above inherits the error. So the discipline starts here: know exactly what one stETH is a claim on, how you turn it back into ETH, and how fast.

**The peg.** Because an LST is a claim on a known amount of ETH, it *should* trade at the value of that ETH. One stETH should equal roughly one ETH (a touch more once yield has accrued). When 1 stETH trades for less than 1 ETH, we say stETH is at a **discount** — it has *depegged downward*. The peg is the LST's vital sign. It is held in place by two forces: **redemption** (once withdrawals are enabled, anyone can turn 1 stETH into ~1 ETH directly with the protocol, so the price can't stray far from par for long) and **arbitrage** (if stETH is cheap, buyers scoop it up to capture the discount plus the ongoing yield). When the peg breaks anyway, it is almost never because the underlying ETH vanished — it is because *sellers need cash faster than redemption and arbitrage can absorb*. The peg, in other words, is a stress gauge, not a solvency gauge.

**What restaking is.** Here is where the leverage genuinely begins. Restaking, pioneered by **EigenLayer**, lets you take ETH you have *already staked* and pledge it a *second time* to secure additional services — oracle networks, data-availability layers, bridges, sequencers, and so on. These services are called *actively validated services* (AVSs). In exchange for putting your staked ETH at risk for them too (they can slash you if you misbehave on their behalf), they pay you extra yield. The pitch is irresistible: the same ETH now earns the base staking yield *and* a stack of restaking rewards. The risk is exactly the flip side: the same ETH is now exposed to *every* service's slashing conditions at once. One deposit, many ways to lose it. This is **rehypothecation** — pledging the same collateral to multiple obligations — and it is the oldest source of fragility in finance, now rebuilt on-chain.

**Liquid restaking tokens (LRTs).** Just as LSTs made staked ETH liquid, *liquid restaking tokens* make restaked ETH liquid. Deposit stETH (or ETH) into a protocol like ether.fi, Renzo, or Kelp, and receive an LRT (eETH, ezETH, rsETH). The LRT is a tradable receipt for your restaked position, earning the whole yield stack while you keep a token you can trade and lend. We are now three receipts deep on the same ETH: stETH wraps staked ETH, the LRT wraps the restaked stETH, and — as we will see — people then *borrow against the LRT* to do it again.

**Looping.** The final building block, and the one that turns a moderate structure into a leveraged one. Because stETH is accepted as collateral on lending markets like Aave and Spark, you can: deposit stETH, borrow ETH against it, stake that ETH for more stETH, deposit *that*, borrow again, and repeat. Each turn of this loop adds more staked exposure on top of your original capital. The trade works because the staking yield you earn on the borrowed ETH is higher than the cost of borrowing it — a positive *carry*. With a few turns, a 3.5% base yield becomes something like 10% on your own equity. The price of that magnification is leverage in the literal sense: a small adverse move in the stETH/ETH price now wipes out a large fraction of your equity and gets you liquidated. We will read exactly how this works, and exactly how it cascades.

#### Worked example: the base layer — what real staking yield actually pays

Strip away every layer and start with plain staking. Suppose a liquid staking protocol has \$20B of ETH staked through it (Lido has been around this scale), and the network pays about 3.5% staking yield. The real cash thrown off is \$20B × 3.5% = **\$700M per year**, paid in freshly issued ETH and fee revenue, distributed to stETH holders. That \$700M is *durable* yield: it does not depend on a token price, an emissions schedule, or anyone believing a narrative — it is the protocol paying for security with the chain's own issuance. If a stETH holder owns \$50,000 of stETH, their slice is \$50,000 × 3.5% = **\$1,750 a year**, accruing automatically as the token's ETH value grows. Everything we build on top of this is an attempt to earn *more* than \$1,750 on the same \$50,000 — and every extra dollar of yield comes with an extra dollar's worth of risk.

## Reading staking flows: the validator queue and what net staking signals

The first on-chain signal is the simplest: **how much ETH is flowing into and out of staking, and how long the queues are.** Ethereum has two queues — an *entry queue* for new validators waiting to activate, and an *exit queue* for those waiting to withdraw. The protocol throttles how many can enter or leave per epoch, so when demand to stake (or unstake) spikes, a line forms. The length of that line, in ETH and in days, is public and is one of the cleaner sentiment reads in crypto.

**Net staking inflow** — new stake minus exits — is a slow, structural signal. A steadily growing entry queue means capital wants the staking yield and is willing to lock ETH up to get it; that is ETH being taken *off* the liquid market and committed long-term, a supply-tightening signal not unlike falling exchange balances. A persistent *exit* queue is the opposite: stakers are pulling ETH out, often to sell, to chase a better yield elsewhere, or to de-risk. You read these on dashboards like Dune, beaconcha.in, or Rated — they show the entry/exit queue depth, the implied wait time, and the net daily change in staked ETH.

The subtlety is *why* the flow is moving. A surging exit queue is not automatically bearish. After withdrawals were first enabled in April 2023, a large exit queue formed simply because stakers who had been locked since before the Merge finally *could* leave and rotate; much of that ETH was restaked or re-deposited within days, not dumped. So the read is: a growing entry queue with a flat exit queue is clean accumulation; a swelling exit queue *paired with* falling LST pegs and rising stablecoin outflows is de-risking under stress. The flow tells you the direction; the peg and the leverage tell you whether it is calm or forced.

For an LST specifically, watch the protocol's **net deposits**: stETH minted (new ETH staked through Lido) versus stETH burned (redemptions). Growing net deposits mean the LST is gaining share and its peg has a strong redemption backstop; shrinking deposits with a widening discount is the early shape of a confidence problem.

There is a structural reason staking flows are a slower, cleaner signal than ordinary exchange flows. Exchange inflows can mean "about to sell" or simply "moving wallets"; staking flows commit ETH to a *queue* that takes real time to clear. That throttle is itself information. When the entry queue blows out to weeks, it tells you demand to lock up ETH is outrunning the protocol's ability to onboard validators — strong, persistent conviction, not a day-trader's whim. When the exit queue blows out, it tells you a lot of stakers decided at once that they would rather hold liquid, sellable ETH than locked yield-bearing ETH — and the *reason* is the whole game. Sometimes it is benign rotation (stakers moving from one LST to a better one, or from solo staking to a pool); sometimes it is the leading edge of de-risking before a sell-off. You disambiguate by triangulating: pair the queue with the peg (is stETH still at par, or slipping?) and with stablecoin flows (is cash leaving the system, or being parked to redeploy?). One signal in isolation lies; three signals pointing the same way rarely do.

There is also a concentration angle the flows reveal. Lido alone has at times secured around a quarter to a third of all staked ETH, which raises a governance-and-decentralization question the network worries about — but for the *reader of flows* the practical point is simpler: when one LST is this large, *its* net deposits and *its* peg are a market-wide signal, not a niche one. A confidence wobble in the dominant LST is a confidence wobble in a meaningful slice of all staked ETH, and the flows show it first.

#### Worked example: reading the queue as supply on tap

Suppose the entry queue holds 400,000 ETH waiting to activate and the exit queue is nearly empty, with ETH around \$3,000. That is 400,000 × \$3,000 = **\$1.2B of ETH** that has chosen to lock itself up for the staking yield rather than sit liquid and sellable — a quiet bid being *removed* from the float for the medium term. Now flip it: an exit queue of 400,000 ETH with a thin entry queue is \$1.2B that *wants out*, and even if only a third of it is headed for the exchanges to sell, that is \$400M of latent supply you can see coming days before it lands on an order book. The queue is the rare signal that gives you lead time — but only if you also check *why* the line formed.

## Reading the peg: stETH/ETH as a stress gauge

The peg is the headline vital sign, so it gets the most careful read. In calm markets, 1 stETH trades within a fraction of a percent of 1 ETH (plus accrued yield) because both redemption and arbitrage are pinning it. The number you watch is the **stETH/ETH ratio**, read straight off the deepest on-chain market (historically the Curve stETH/ETH pool, now also balanced by direct redemptions). A reading of 1.00 is calm. A reading of 0.99 is normal noise. A reading of 0.95 or below, *sustained for hours and getting worse*, is the chain screaming that someone is selling stETH for ETH faster than the market can absorb — and forced sellers move price, informed buyers do not.

![Before and after panels comparing a stressed stETH discount of 0.94 to a calm peg at 1.00](/imgs/blogs/liquid-staking-restaking-and-yield-2.png)

Why does a discount appear at all if the ETH is fine? Three reasons, in rough order of severity. First, **redemption friction**: if withdrawals are slow or (pre-2023) disabled, you cannot instantly turn stETH into ETH, so a seller in a hurry must accept a market discount instead of redeeming at par. Second, **forced selling**: a levered holder facing a margin call or a fund facing redemptions must raise ETH immediately and will dump stETH at whatever price clears, regardless of fair value. Third, **liquidity exhaustion**: the on-chain pool that absorbs stETH selling has finite depth, and once it tilts heavily toward stETH, each additional sale moves the price more. Stack all three — as in June 2022 — and a 1% wobble becomes a 6% gash.

The flip side is that a discount is *also a signal to the patient buyer*. If you believe the ETH is sound and withdrawals will eventually clear at par, buying stETH at 0.94 is buying ETH at a 6% discount *while collecting the staking yield on top*. That is exactly the arbitrage that pulls the peg back. The 2022 discount fully closed over the following months; everyone who bought the panic and held got their ETH back at par plus yield. The peg, read correctly, is therefore two signals at once: a warning of stress for the levered, and an opportunity for the unlevered.

For LRTs the peg read is the same idea but *more dangerous*, because LRTs are newer, thinner, and many have *never had withdrawals tested under stress*. An LRT trading at 0.97 of its backing in a thin pool with no proven redemption path is a far riskier "discount" than stETH at 0.97, because the par-redemption backstop that pulls stETH home may simply not exist yet for that LRT. Read the peg *and* read whether there is a real redemption mechanism behind it.

#### Worked example: the 2022 stETH depeg forcing looped unwinds

Take a levered fund holding \$1B of stETH in a looped position at the time of the 2022 unwind, financed so that its own equity was, say, \$250M and the rest borrowed. When stETH fell to 0.94 ETH, the *collateral* lost about 6% of its ETH-denominated value almost overnight — roughly \$60M wiped off a \$1B book. For a position with \$250M of equity, a \$60M mark-down is nearly a quarter of the cushion gone from a *6% move in an asset that hadn't lost any underlying ETH*. To avoid liquidation the fund had to sell stETH for ETH and repay debt — but its selling deepened the discount, which marked down the collateral further, which forced more selling. The chain showed the Curve pool tilting past 70% stETH as wallet after wallet unwound. The lesson in dollars: leverage converts a 6% wobble in a sound asset into a solvency event, and the peg is the fuse.

## The leverage loops: how looping multiplies yield and liquidation risk

Now the centerpiece. Looping is the engine that turns liquid staking from a savings account into a leveraged carry trade, and reading *how much looped leverage is in the system* is the difference between seeing a depeg as a buying opportunity and seeing it as the start of a cascade.

![Graph of the looping loop branching into more yield and a thin liquidation cushion](/imgs/blogs/liquid-staking-restaking-and-yield-3.png)

Here is the mechanic, step by step. You start with 100 stETH of your own. You deposit it as collateral on a lending market (Aave, Spark, Morpho). Because stETH and ETH are nearly the same asset, these markets let you borrow ETH against stETH at a *high* loan-to-value (LTV) — often 90% or more, precisely because the collateral and the debt track each other. So you borrow, say, 90 ETH. You stake that 90 ETH (or buy stETH with it) to get ~90 stETH, deposit *that*, borrow ~81 ETH, and repeat. After several turns your total staked exposure approaches 100 / (1 − 0.9) = up to 1,000 stETH-equivalent on your original 100 — though in practice people stop at 2–4× to keep a safety margin.

The carry math is what makes it tempting. You earn the staking yield on *all* the stETH you hold (the looped total) and pay the borrow rate on *all* the ETH you owe. If staking pays 3.5% and ETH borrows at, say, 2.5%, every turn nets you 1% of positive carry on borrowed money, on top of the 3.5% on your own capital. Lever 3×, and your blended yield on equity climbs from 3.5% toward 10%. This is genuinely clever and, in calm markets, genuinely profitable. It is also why pegged-asset lending markets fill up with stETH collateral and ETH debt — that is the looping crowd, and you can read its size directly.

To make the magnification concrete, walk a single \$100 of equity through the turns at a 90% LTV (each turn you borrow 90% of what you just deposited): turn 1 deposits \$100 and borrows \$90; turn 2 stakes that \$90 and borrows \$81; turn 3 borrows ~\$73; and so on. The deposits sum toward \$100 / (1 − 0.9) = \$1,000 of stETH on \$100 of your own money — a theoretical 10× — while the debt sums toward \$900. In practice the LTV used is lower and the looper stops after a few turns to keep a cushion, landing at a comfortable 2–4×. But the geometry is the point: leverage in a loop is just a geometric series, and the closer the per-turn LTV is to 100%, the more terms it adds and the thinner the final cushion becomes. A loop run "to the max" against the protocol's allowed LTV is a position with almost no buffer, sitting one small peg move away from liquidation.

**How to read the looped leverage in the system.** Go to the lending market's on-chain data (Aave's dashboards, DeFiLlama, or a Dune query): look at the total stETH supplied as collateral and the total ETH borrowed against it. A market where ETH borrows are a large fraction of stETH collateral, at high utilization, is a market stuffed with loops. Then read the *health factors* — how close the aggregate of those positions is to its liquidation threshold. A pile of loops sitting at, say, 1.05 health factor (5% buffer to liquidation) is a coil of dry tinder: a 5% stETH discount lights it. The same loops at 1.30 health (a 30% buffer) are far calmer. This is the single most important leverage read in the LST world, and it ties directly to how lending and liquidations work on-chain — a topic worth studying in its own right, because the liquidation engine is what turns a price wobble into forced selling.

A few tells separate "loops" from ordinary lending activity in the data. First, the *paired* nature: looping produces a market where stETH is the dominant collateral *and* ETH is the dominant debt, in roughly matched size — because the strategy is mechanically "post stETH, borrow ETH." A market where collateral and debt are unrelated assets is not a loop. Second, *high utilization of the ETH borrow market* with rates that hug the staking yield — the loop only works while borrow cost is below staking yield, so loopers pile in until borrowing gets expensive enough to close the carry, which is itself a self-limiting signal you can watch. Third, the *recursive deposit pattern* visible in transaction histories: a wallet that deposits stETH, borrows ETH, swaps or stakes to stETH, and re-deposits in the same block or the same hour is unmistakably looping. You do not need to identify individual wallets to read the aggregate — the market-level ratio of ETH-debt to stETH-collateral and the distribution of health factors tells you how much dry tinder is stacked and how dry it is. When that ratio is high and health factors cluster near the threshold, you are looking at a system primed to cascade on a move the unlevered would barely notice.

#### Worked example: a loop turning 3.5% into 10% — and liquidating on a 5% move

Put \$1,000,000 of your own stETH to work. Loop it to 3× exposure: you now control \$3,000,000 of stETH, financed by \$2,000,000 of borrowed ETH. At a 1% net carry on the borrowed portion plus 3.5% on your equity, your yield on equity is roughly 3.5% + (2 × 1%) = **~9.5%**, call it 10%. On \$1,000,000 of equity that is **\$100,000 a year** instead of \$35,000 — a fat improvement. Now stETH depegs 5%: your \$3,000,000 of collateral loses \$150,000 of value, while your \$2,000,000 of debt is unchanged. Your equity drops from \$1,000,000 to \$850,000 — but more importantly your loan-to-value jumps, and if you were running near the liquidation threshold you are now *underwater*. The protocol liquidates you: it sells your stETH for ETH to repay the loan, often with a liquidation penalty of 5–10% on top. A 5% move in the *peg* just cost you far more than 5% of equity — and your forced sale pushed the peg down for the next looper. That asymmetry, leverage converting a small price move into a large loss plus forced selling, is the whole risk.

## Restaking and rehypothecation: the same ETH securing many things

Restaking adds a different *kind* of risk than looping. Looping stacks *price* risk (the peg) and *liquidation* risk (the lending market). Restaking stacks *slashing* risk, and it stacks it in a way that is correlated and hard to see.

![Graph showing one staked ETH pool securing several services with a correlated slash node](/imgs/blogs/liquid-staking-restaking-and-yield-4.png)

Recall the mechanic: one pool of staked ETH is pledged, via EigenLayer, to secure several actively validated services at once. Each service has its own slashing rules — its own definition of misbehavior and its own penalty. The yields add up nicely. But the *risks* do not stay independent. They share the same collateral, and several of the services may share the same operators, the same code, or the same failure trigger. A bug in a widely-used operator's software, or a single market-wide event, can trip slashing across many services *simultaneously*, all biting into the same pool. This is the on-chain version of the rehypothecation chains that amplified the 2008 crisis: the same underlying asset pledged so many times that a single shock propagates through every claim on it.

For the analyst, restaking introduces two reading problems. First, **opacity of risk**: an LRT may be restaked across a dozen services with a dozen slashing conditions, and the holder of the LRT often cannot easily enumerate what their token is actually exposed to. The yield is a single number; the risk is a basket you can't fully see. Second, **mercenary TVL and the points frenzy**: most LRT growth in 2023–2024 was driven not by people who wanted the yield but by people farming *points* — promises of a future token airdrop, scored by how much you deposit and for how long. That capital is loyal to the airdrop, not the protocol. It floods in to inflate total value locked (TVL), and it leaves the instant the airdrop lands or a better farm appears. A protocol whose TVL is mostly mercenary points-chasers has a number that looks like adoption and behaves like a hot-money balance — exactly the kind of "TVL" that needs reading honestly rather than trusted, the same way you would discount any incentive-inflated headline number.

The points dynamic deserves a closer look because it shaped on-chain flows for the better part of two years. The pattern repeated across the LRT field: a protocol launches, announces it will reward early depositors with *points* (and often multiplies them with referral bonuses and partner-protocol boosts), and TVL rockets — sometimes from nothing to billions within weeks — as farmers chase a future airdrop whose size and value are entirely unknown at the time. EigenLayer itself ran a points program that drew tens of billions of dollars of deposits before its token existed; the LRTs built on top layered *their own* points on top of Eigen's, so a single deposit could be farming two or three speculative airdrops at once. None of that capital is priced on the real yield — it is priced on a guess about a token that has not launched. When the tokens did launch, the outcomes were all over the map: some airdrops were generous and held value, rewarding the farmers handsomely; others disappointed, listed weakly, or diluted fast, and the "yield" the farmers had been booking in their heads evaporated. The flows told the story in advance — TVL that arrives the week a points program is announced and would leave the week it ends is mercenary by definition, and you can watch it slosh from one farm to the next.

A second-order risk hides inside the points frenzy: it concentrates a huge amount of restaking deposits into a handful of LRTs and a handful of operators *fast*, before the slashing machinery, the redemption paths, and the risk controls have been battle-tested. Growth driven by yield is slow and self-limiting; growth driven by an unbounded points lottery is explosive and indiscriminate. The chain therefore showed an enormous, correlated pile of rehypothecated ETH assembling in newish contracts in a very short window — the textbook setup for a stress event, because the leverage and the interconnection arrived faster than the safety. Reading restaking honestly means reading *how* the TVL got there, not just how big it is.

#### Worked example: an LRT advertising 40% APR that is 4% real and 36% points

An LRT markets a headline **40% APR**. Decompose it the way the chain lets you. The *real* component is the base staking yield (~3.5%) plus the actual restaking fees the underlying services pay (~0.5%) — call it 4%. On a \$10,000 deposit that real slice is \$10,000 × 4% = **\$400 a year** of durable, cash-like yield. The other 36% is *points*: an accrual of governance-token units that will only be worth something if and when a token launches and trades well. If the token launches and holds, that 36% on \$10,000 could be \$3,600 of value — a windfall. If the token flops, gets diluted, or never lists, those points are worth **\$0**, and your "40% APR" deposit actually earned \$400 — the same 4% you would have gotten from plain stETH, but with all the extra slashing and depeg risk of the restaking stack bolted on. The read: separate the green slice from the amber slice, and never size a position as if the points are cash.

## Real yield versus emissions: the test that survives a bear market

The previous example generalizes into the most important habit in this whole subject: **always split the yield into what is real and what is emitted.** Real yield is paid in a productive asset — ETH issuance for securing the chain, fees from services that actually have users — and it survives a token price going to zero. Emitted yield is paid in a *new token* the protocol prints, and it is only worth what the market will pay for that token, which in a downturn is often very little. The bar chart above makes the split visual: the green base is the durable part; the amber stack on top is the part that can evaporate.

This is the same test we apply to protocol fundamentals generally — fees and revenue that come from real usage are durable, while emissions that come from printing the token are not — and it is doubly important here because the restaking-and-points complex is *engineered* to make emitted yield look like real yield. A 40% banner does not say "36 of these points are a lottery ticket." You have to do that decomposition yourself, and the chain gives you the pieces: the protocol's actual fee revenue (real), versus its token emissions schedule (not). When you read an LST or LRT yield, the question is never "how high is the APR?" It is "how much of this APR is still here if the token does nothing?" For plain stETH the answer is "all of it." For a points-farm LRT the answer is often "barely a tenth of it."

A second-order point: emissions are not *worthless* — early farmers of the right protocols have made fortunes on airdrops. The discipline is not "ignore points," it is "price points as the speculative lottery ticket they are, size the position to the *real* yield, and treat any airdrop as upside." The reader who confuses a 40% banner with a 40% bond is the reader who blows up when the token underdelivers and the depeg arrives at the same time.

#### Worked example: comparing two \$100,000 allocations on durable yield

Allocator A puts \$100,000 into plain stETH at 3.5% real yield: \$3,500 a year, fully durable, with the only real risk being a temporary peg discount that closes if you are patient. Allocator B puts \$100,000 into a points-farm LRT advertising 40%, of which 4% is real: the durable cash yield is \$100,000 × 4% = **\$4,000 a year**, only \$500 more than A, while the headline 40% (\$40,000) is contingent on a token that may never list. If the token does list well, B might capture \$30,000+ of one-time airdrop value — a real prize. But B is also carrying the LRT's untested peg, its rehypothecated slashing risk, and (if looped) liquidation risk, *to earn an extra \$500 of certain yield*. The honest framing: B is making a venture bet on the airdrop dressed up as a yield trade. Size it like a venture bet — small, with money you can lose — not like a savings account.

## Systemic risk: how a depeg becomes a cascade

Put the pieces together and you can see the failure mode the whole system is wired for: a **depeg cascade**. It is the 2022 episode in mechanical form, and it is worth tracing slowly because it is the scenario every LST/LRT analyst is implicitly pricing.

![Pipeline of a depeg cascade from a trigger sale through forced liquidations to a deeper discount](/imgs/blogs/liquid-staking-restaking-and-yield-6.png)

The cascade runs like this. (1) **Trigger**: a large holder sells stETH for ETH — to meet a margin call, to exit a fund, or because a scary headline hit. (2) **Discount**: the sale outruns the on-chain liquidity and the peg slips, say to 0.95. (3) **Breach**: the most levered loops, the ones running near their liquidation threshold, cross it — their collateral (stETH) is now worth too little against their ETH debt. (4) **Forced selling**: liquidators automatically sell that stETH for ETH to repay the loans, with a penalty on top. (5) **Deeper discount**: that forced selling pushes the peg lower still, to 0.90, breaching the *next* tier of loops. (6) **Reset or settle**: either patient arbitrageurs step in to buy the discount and the spiral halts, or the leverage is deep enough and the liquidity thin enough that it keeps feeding until the levered layer is fully washed out.

What makes this genuinely systemic rather than a single protocol's problem is the *interconnection*. The looped stETH sits in the same lending markets as other collateral, so a stETH liquidation cascade drains ETH liquidity and stresses unrelated borrowers. LRTs are built *on* stETH, so a stETH depeg marks down every LRT that holds it, and LRTs that are themselves looped add their own cascade on top. Restaking ties many services to the same pool, so a slashing event during the stress could fire at the worst possible moment. The same ETH, pledged and re-pledged and borrowed against, means a single price shock arrives at many doors at once. This is precisely the shape of contagion that took down the leveraged crypto complex in 2022 — the Terra collapse, then the 3AC and Celsius failures — where one entity's forced selling became another's margin call. The instruments are more sophisticated now; the physics of correlated leverage are unchanged.

The defender's read, then, is not "is stETH safe?" (the ETH behind it almost certainly is) but "**how much levered, rehypothecated exposure is stacked on it, and how thin is the cushion?**" A system where loops sit at comfortable health factors, LRTs have proven redemptions, and yields are mostly real can absorb a 5% wobble and close it. A system where loops are maxed out, LRT pegs are untested, and yields are mostly points is one bad headline away from a cascade. The chain tells you which world you are in — if you read it.

There is a reflexive trap worth naming, because it is what makes these cascades fast. The looping carry trade is *most* crowded precisely when it has been calm and profitable for a long time — that is when the leverage is highest and the cushions are thinnest, because nothing has gone wrong to scare anyone out. So the system is most fragile at the exact moment it feels safest, and the trigger does not need to be large. It does not take a hack or a slashing event; a big holder rebalancing, a scary-but-wrong rumor, or a temporary liquidity gap in the main pool is enough to start the first liquidations, and from there the mechanism is self-sustaining. This is why reading the leverage matters more than reading the news: the news supplies the spark, but the size of the explosion is set by how much tinder was stacked beforehand, and the tinder is visible on-chain long before the spark arrives.

#### Worked example: sizing the system's tinder before a cascade

Suppose the lending markets show \$5B of stETH posted as collateral with \$3.5B of ETH borrowed against it — a 70% aggregate loan-to-value, and many individual loops sitting near the protocol's liquidation threshold. Ask the cascade question in dollars. A 5% stETH discount marks that \$5B of collateral down by \$5B × 5% = **\$250M**, against debt that does not move — enough to push the thinnest loops underwater and trigger liquidations. Say liquidations of \$500M of stETH hit the market; if the main pool can absorb only a few hundred million before tilting hard, that forced selling deepens the discount toward 0.90, marking the *remaining* \$4.5B of collateral down another \$225M and breaching the next tier. You have just sized the fuse and the powder: a 5% trigger on \$5B of looped collateral can mechanically manufacture hundreds of millions of forced selling, and the depth of the on-chain pool decides whether arbitrage catches it at 0.95 or it runs to 0.90 and beyond. Read those two numbers — looped collateral and pool depth — and you have read the system's downside before it happens.

## How to read it: a walkthrough on the four signals

Here is a concrete pass an analyst makes on any LST or LRT before sizing a position. Four reads, in order, each on public data.

**Read 1 — the peg.** Pull the stETH/ETH ratio from the deepest on-chain market (the Curve pool, or an aggregator like DeFiLlama / Curve's own analytics). For an LST: is it within ~1% of par, and what is the redemption path (is the protocol's direct withdrawal queue live and fast)? For an LRT: is there a *proven* redemption mechanism, or is the only exit selling into a thin pool? A peg at 1.00 with live, fast redemptions is the strongest possible vital sign. A peg at 0.98 in a thin pool with no proven redemption is a yellow flag even before stress arrives. Watch the *trend*, not just the level: a peg drifting from 1.00 to 0.99 to 0.985 over a day is the early shape of a problem.

**Read 2 — the net staking flow.** Check the entry/exit queue depth and the LST's net deposits. Growing entry queue and growing net deposits = healthy demand and a strengthening redemption backstop. Swelling exit queue *plus* a softening peg = de-risking under stress; treat it as a warning. The flow gives you direction and lead time.

**Read 3 — the looped leverage.** Open the lending markets (Aave, Spark, Morpho) where the LST is used as collateral. How much stETH is supplied, how much ETH is borrowed against it, at what utilization, and how close are the aggregate health factors to the liquidation threshold? A market thick with high-LTV loops sitting near 1.05 health is a coiled cascade; the same collateral at 1.30 health is calm. This read tells you how *fragile* the peg is — how big a discount it would take to start liquidations.

**Read 4 — the yield quality.** Decompose the advertised APR into real (base staking + actual service fees) and emitted (points / token rewards). For plain stETH it is ~100% real. For an LRT banner, find the real component (often 4–5%) and treat everything above it as a speculative airdrop bet. Size the position to the real yield; count the points as upside, not income.

Put the four reads on one screen and the verdict writes itself: tight peg + clean flow + low leverage + real yield → a hold you can treat as an ETH proxy with a little extra carry; wobbly peg + exit flow + maxed loops + points yield → an avoid, because that is the exact configuration that cascades.

![Risk decision matrix mapping peg health, system leverage, and yield quality to hold size or avoid](/imgs/blogs/liquid-staking-restaking-and-yield-7.png)

#### Worked example: sizing a position from the four reads

You are considering \$200,000 into a conservative LRT advertising 11% APR. Read 1: peg at 0.997, but the only fast exit is a moderately deep pool; redemption queue exists but is slow. Read 2: net deposits flat, no exit panic. Read 3: it is lightly looped — modest ETH borrows against it, health factors comfortable. Read 4: of the 11%, you estimate ~4% is real (3.5% staking + 0.5% service fees) and ~7% is points. So the *durable* income on \$200,000 is \$200,000 × 4% = **\$8,000 a year**; the points are a \$200,000 × 7% = **\$14,000-of-notional** lottery ticket that is worth that only if the token lists and holds. Your sizing decision: this earns \$8,000 of real yield for taking on an untested-under-stress peg and rehypothecated slashing risk. Reasonable as a *measured* allocation — perhaps half what you would put in plain stETH — with the points as pure upside and a hard rule to exit if the peg trends below 0.99. You have turned a marketing banner into a sized, invalidated decision.

## Common misconceptions

**"stETH is risky because it can depeg."** The peg risk is real but it is *liquidity and leverage* risk, not *solvency* risk. The ETH behind stETH is staked on Ethereum and accruing yield regardless of what stETH trades for that afternoon. In 2022 the discount fully closed and par-buyers were rewarded. The danger is not that stETH "loses its backing" — it is that *if you are levered against it*, a temporary discount can liquidate you before the peg recovers. Unlevered, a depeg is an opportunity; levered, it is a threat. Same asset, opposite meaning, depending on your leverage.

**"A 40% APR is a 40% return."** No. Most high LST/LRT yields are mostly *emitted* tokens or points, not cash. The durable part is the staking yield plus real service fees — typically single digits. The rest is a bet on a future token. Treating emitted yield as income is how farmers get blindsided when the token underdelivers. Always split the number; size to the real part.

**"Restaking is just extra free yield on the same ETH."** It is extra yield, but it is not free — it is *paid for with stacked, correlated slashing risk* on collateral that is already doing one job. The same ETH securing a dozen services means a dozen ways to be slashed, several of which can fire together. The yield is a single visible number; the risk is a basket you mostly cannot see. "Free yield" in finance is almost always a risk you haven't located yet.

**"Lido and EigenLayer are too big to fail, so the peg is safe."** Size is not safety; size is *contagion surface*. The bigger the LST/LRT complex and the more leverage stacked on it, the more forced selling a shock produces and the further it propagates. A larger system with the same leverage ratio is a *more* systemic risk, not a safer one — there is simply more to unwind. Read the leverage and the cushion, not the brand.

**"The 2022 depeg can't happen again because withdrawals are live now."** Withdrawals being enabled genuinely strengthened the stETH peg — par redemption is a real backstop that did not exist in 2022. But that backstop is *slow* (a queue), so in a fast panic forced sellers still hit the market rather than the queue, and the discount can still open before redemption arbitrage closes it. And the backstop is specific to *that* protocol: most LRTs have far weaker or untested redemption paths, so the 2022 dynamic is alive and well one layer up the stack.

## The playbook: what to do with it

The if-then checklist for an investor or risk analyst reading the LST/LRT stack.

- **Signal: stETH at par (~1.00), fast redemptions live, low looped leverage, ~100% real yield.** Read: a sound ETH proxy with a touch of extra carry. Action: hold as a core ETH-with-yield position; comfortable to size meaningfully. Invalidation: peg trends below 0.99 *and* the lending markets show loops crowding near their liquidation threshold.

- **Signal: stETH at a modest discount (say 0.97) with no special stress elsewhere.** Read: a buyable discount if you are unlevered and patient — you are buying ETH cheap plus yield. Action: if you believe the redemption backstop, accumulate the discount; do *not* lever into it. False positive: the "discount" is actually a thin-pool LRT with no real redemption — then it is not a buyable discount, it is a fair price for a worse asset.

- **Signal: peg softening + exit queue swelling + lending-market loops sitting near 1.05 health.** Read: the cascade preconditions are assembling. Action: reduce or fully exit levered exposure first (you liquidate before the forced sellers do), and stand aside from buying until the leverage washes out and the peg stabilizes. Invalidation: arbitrage steps in, the peg firms back toward par, and health factors widen — then the danger has passed.

- **Signal: an LRT advertising a yield far above the ~4–5% real range.** Read: the excess is points/emissions — a speculative airdrop bet, not income. Action: size it like a venture position (small, losable), to the *real* yield only, treating any airdrop as upside; demand a real redemption path before committing. False positive: a genuinely productive new service that actually pays high *real* fees — rare, verify the fees are real cash before believing the banner.

- **Signal: heavy mercenary/points TVL with little real usage.** Read: the headline number is hot money loyal to the airdrop, not adoption. Action: discount the TVL, expect a sharp outflow when the airdrop lands or a better farm appears, and don't be the last one holding when the incentives stop. Invalidation: TVL stays after emissions taper and real fee revenue rises to meet it — then it was sticky, durable capital after all.

A note on cadence: these reads are not one-and-done. The peg, the queues, the lending-market ratios, and the points programs all move, and the system is most dangerous when calm has let leverage build quietly. So the discipline is to glance at the four signals on a schedule — the peg and the lending-market health factors weekly when you hold a position, and immediately on any market-wide stress — rather than to check once at entry and assume the configuration holds. The whole edge of on-chain analysis is that these numbers are public and update continuously; the cost of that edge is that you have to actually keep looking. A position that was a clean hold last month can become a fragile one this month without the price of the token changing at all, simply because the leverage stacked on it grew.

The one-line version of the entire post: **read the peg, read the looped leverage, read the yield quality.** A tight peg on lightly-levered, real-yield positions is the boring, durable trade. A wobbly peg on maxed-out loops paying points is the configuration that has unwound before and will unwind again — and the chain shows you which one you are looking at, in time to act.
