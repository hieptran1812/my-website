> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A pump and dump is not a single event but three ordered phases — quiet accumulation by a few wallets, a coordinated pump plus hype to draw retail in, then distribution that sells the float onto the new buyers — and each phase leaves a distinct, readable wallet-level signature on the public ledger.
>
> - **The signal**: supply moving from many holders into a few during accumulation, a coordinated buy in a tight window during the pump, and the exact mirror — supply moving from a few back out to many — during distribution. The on-chain tells are wallet concentration, a shared funder, thin liquidity, and a holder count that spikes as price tops.
> - **How to read it**: pull the holder distribution and funding sources before you buy (Bubblemaps, Etherscan, Solscan, Nansen, DeBank, Dune); watch for a few funded wallets accumulating an illiquid token, then a coordinated volume burst, then those same wallets distributing into a rising holder count.
> - **What you DO with it**: this is a *defense* skill. When you can see that a few coordinated hands own the float and are setting up to sell into a manufactured pump, you **avoid** — you do not chase the breakout, because the catalyst that is supposed to make you money is *their* exit, paid for with *your* buy.
> - **The rule of thumb**: if a token's float is held by a few wallets that share a funder, and the price is moving on a thin order book, the rally is somebody's planned exit and you are being invited to be the liquidity.

In the spring of 2024, a particular shape kept repeating across low-cap tokens on Solana and the cheaper EVM chains. First a quiet week: a handful of fresh wallets, all funded within a day or two of each other, accumulating an obscure token on a market so thin their own buying barely moved the price. Then, in a single afternoon, a coordinated burst — several large buys inside a few minutes, a synchronized push across social channels, a chart that suddenly looked like a breakout. Retail piled in. And then, over the next few hours, the very wallets that had accumulated for a week sold into the inflow, the price collapsed, and a few hundred new wallets — bought in near the top — were left holding a token settling toward zero. None of it was hidden. Every accumulation buy, every coordinated push, every distribution sell sat on a public ledger that anyone with a block explorer could read.

That is the entire subject of this post: the **pump and dump**, the oldest manipulation in markets, executed on the most transparent ledger ever built. In a traditional market, reconstructing who accumulated before a pump and who sold into it requires subpoenas, broker records, and months of forensic accounting. On-chain, the same reconstruction is an afternoon's tracing exercise, because every position is public, permanent, and timestamped. The wallets that ran the scheme cannot un-buy their accumulation or un-sell their distribution; the moves are carved into the chain forever. The only thing separating the people who got dumped on from the people doing the dumping was that one group knew how to read those moves and the other did not.

So let us be precise about the framing before we touch a tool. This is a **defensive** post, written from the perspective of a trader and analyst who wants to *not lose money* and an investigator who wants to *recognize the pattern*. We are learning to detect a pump and dump so we can stand aside from it — not to run one. Coordinated market manipulation is illegal in most jurisdictions and is fraud everywhere; this post will never tell you how to organize a buying group, manufacture volume, or time a distribution. What it will tell you is how to read the public record so that when a token's chart starts to look like a free lunch, you can see the few wallets sitting on top of the order book, waiting for you to be the demand that lets them exit.

## Foundations: what a pump and dump is, and the three phases

Before the tooling, a small set of ideas has to be solid. Everything later is a combination of these, so we build them from zero.

**What a pump and dump is.** A pump and dump is a coordinated scheme in which a group buys an asset cheaply, drives its price up with concentrated buying and promotion, and then sells the inflated position to the new buyers that the hype attracted, collapsing the price back down. The operators profit; the people who bought during the "pump" — the retail crowd drawn in by a rising chart and a loud narrative — hold the bag. It predates crypto by a century: the term comes from boiler-room stock fraud, where salesmen would talk up a thinly-traded penny stock they already owned and dump it on the customers they called. Crypto did not invent the scheme; it just made it cheaper to run (anyone can launch a token in minutes), faster to execute (markets trade 24/7 with no circuit breakers), and — crucially for us — *visible on a public ledger*.

**The three phases.** Every pump and dump, however dressed up, runs through the same three phases in the same order. **Accumulation** comes first: the operators quietly buy the token while it is cheap and ignored, building a large position without moving the price much. **Markup** (the pump) comes second: the operators trigger a coordinated buy in a tight window, often paired with synchronized social promotion and sometimes fake volume, to make the chart look like a breakout and pull in outside buyers. **Distribution** (the dump) comes third: as retail demand floods in chasing the rising price, the operators sell their accumulated position into that demand — distributing the supply they accumulated — until the buying dries up and the price collapses. Accumulation concentrates supply into a few hands; distribution fans it back out to many; the pump is the lure in between. Hold that sequence — concentrate, lure, distribute — because the rest of this post is just learning to see each step on-chain.

**Why low-float, low-liquidity tokens are the targets.** The scheme needs two things: the operators must be able to buy a large share of the supply cheaply, and a relatively small amount of buying must be able to move the price a lot. Both point to the same kind of token — one with a small **float** (the portion of supply actually available to trade, as opposed to locked, burned, or held by the team) and shallow **liquidity** (few tokens sitting in the market ready to be bought or sold). A token with deep liquidity — say, a major asset where hundreds of millions of dollars trade daily — cannot be pumped by one group, because the buying needed to move it is more than any group can muster and the float is too widely distributed to corner. A brand-new token with a \$2 million notional market cap and only a few thousand dollars of real depth in its pool is the perfect target: a few hands can own most of the tradable supply, and a modest coordinated buy can manufacture a dramatic candle. This is why pump and dumps cluster in memecoins, micro-cap launches, and freshly-listed tokens — the thinner the market, the cheaper the manipulation.

**Who runs them.** The operators are usually some combination of a **coordinated buying group** (sometimes a paid "pump group" with hundreds or thousands of members who agree to buy a named token at a named time), one or more **influencers** with an audience to mobilize, and **insiders** — the token's deployer, early backers, or people tipped to the plan. The structure varies: at the organized end is a literal subscription group that announces a target and a time; in the middle is an influencer cabal that quietly accumulates and then promotes; at the dark end is a project team that pumps its own token to dump on its community. What unites them is *coordination* — they act together, with shared information about the plan — and coordination is exactly what leaves a fingerprint on a ledger where independent actors normally do not synchronize.

**Why it is visible.** A pump and dump is detectable on-chain for the same structural reason that all coordination is: the operators have to *do something*, and everything they do is recorded. To accumulate, they have to buy — and many wallets buying one obscure token, funded from one source, in a quiet window, is a cluster you can see. To pump, they have to push price — and a tight burst of coordinated buys on thin liquidity is a volume-and-price signature you can see. To distribute, they have to sell — and supply fanning out from a few wallets to hundreds of fresh retail wallets, while the price rolls over, is the mirror image of accumulation and is the loudest tell of all. The operators count on you watching only the price chart, which shows you the lure but hides the hands. The ledger shows you the hands.

With those five ideas — what the scheme is, the three phases, why thin tokens are the target, who runs it, and why it is visible — we can build every detection technique in this post. A sixth idea ties them together and deserves its own statement: **price is the lure, flow is the truth.** The price chart is the one thing the operators *want* you to look at, because a rising green candle is the bait. The flow — who is buying, who is selling, how many wallets, funded by whom, with what concentration — is the thing they would rather you ignore, because it gives away the game. On-chain analysis is the discipline of looking past the price at the flow underneath it. The whole edge of this post is learning to do that before you click buy, not after.

It is worth grounding why the chain you are on changes the texture of the work. On **Solana**, where the modern memecoin economy lives, tokens launch by the thousands per day, blocks are sub-second, and a pump can run its full three-phase arc in *hours*; Solscan and the Pump.fun-era dashboards expose the first buyers and the holder graph almost in real time, and the speed is the danger — by the time a pump is visible on a price screen, the distribution may already be underway. On **Ethereum and EVM L2s** (Base, Arbitrum, BSC, Polygon), the account model gives each wallet a clean running balance and a readable transaction list, so accumulation and distribution show up as straightforward fan-in and fan-out of transfers, and the deep tooling (Etherscan, Arkham, Nansen, DeBank, Dune, Bubblemaps) was built here first. On **Bitcoin**, the UTXO model and the lack of an easy token-launch primitive make classic pump-and-dump rarer, though the same concentrate-lure-distribute logic appears in some ordinals and BRC-20 episodes. The technique transposes across all of them; only the explorer and the clock speed change.

## The accumulation signature: a few wallets, one funder, an illiquid token

Start where the scheme starts. **Accumulation** is the operators quietly building their position before anyone is watching, and it has a signature precise enough to spot in advance — which is what makes it the most valuable phase to learn to read, because catching it early means you never get to the part where you lose money.

Here is the mechanism. The operators want to own a large share of a token's tradable float, but they want to acquire it *without* moving the price, because a price spike during accumulation would defeat the purpose — it would alert other buyers and raise the operators' own cost basis. So they buy patiently: small clips spread over hours or days, on a chart that stays flat and a volume profile that stays thin. To spread their buying across many wallets — which both disguises the concentration and prepares the structure for distribution — they fund a set of fresh wallets from one source, and each of those wallets does its quiet buying. The result, on-chain, is the canonical accumulation cluster: **a few fresh wallets, sharing a common funder, each buying the same illiquid token over the same quiet window.**

![Accumulation signature graph showing one funder sending capital to five fresh wallets that buy an illiquid pool](/imgs/blogs/pump-and-dump-and-coordinated-buying-2.png)

The figure shows the shape. One funder fans capital out to several fresh wallets; each wallet then buys into the same thin pool; the float quietly concentrates into a few coordinated hands while the price barely moves. Reverse the arrows in your head and the structure is an hourglass — narrow at the funder, narrow at the pool, wide only in the layer of disposable wallets in between. That hourglass is what coordinated accumulation looks like, and it is the inverse of the wide, scattered funding you would see if a real crowd had independently discovered the token. This is the same fan-in structure covered in depth in [Address clustering and heuristics](/blog/trading/onchain/address-clustering-and-heuristics) and [Early buyer and insider detection](/blog/trading/onchain/early-buyer-and-insider-detection) — the funding-source trace is the engine that collapses these many addresses into one entity.

#### Worked example: five wallets accumulating \$300k of a \$2M-cap token over a week

Say you are watching a sleepy token with a \$2,000,000 notional market cap — small, but not obviously a scam. You pull its largest recent buyers and find five wallets, all created within the same few days, each holding nothing but this token. You trace each one's first inbound transfer and they all lead back to one address, `0xF1a2…`, which funded each wallet with roughly \$60,000 in stablecoins over a single week. Five wallets at \$60,000 each is \$300,000 of buying that *looks* like five independent believers but is one actor's coordinated position — and on a \$2,000,000-cap token, \$300,000 is a meaningful slice of the real tradable float. The buying was spread over a flat week precisely so it would not move the price and tip its hand. The lesson: when one funding source explains a cluster of "independent" buyers quietly accumulating a thin token, you are not looking at organic demand — you are looking at the opening move of a scheme, and the order book is a single hand of cards held by one player who will decide when to fold it on you.

There is a structural reason accumulation is so detectable, and it tells you when the technique will and will not work. The operators face a tradeoff. To run the later phases efficiently, they want many wallets (to disguise concentration and to distribute cleanly later), and the cheapest way to provision many wallets with gas and buying capital is from one consolidated source. Building a *clean* funding structure — funding each wallet from a different origin, through different intermediaries, at different times — costs time, gas, and operational discipline, and most operators do not bother because they do not expect anyone to look at a \$2 million memecoin's funding graph. The ones who do bother leave a *peel chain* — one wallet funds three, each of those funds three more — and the branching tree still converges if you walk far enough up. And the ones who route the funding through a mixer to truly cut the link have raised a different, louder flag, because legitimate early buyers of a normal token do not launder their buying capital through a sanctioned mixer (see [Tornado Cash and sanctioning code](/blog/trading/crypto/tornado-cash-and-sanctioning-code)). When the funding trail goes cold at a mixer, you have not lost the thread; you have found a very large red flag.

The honest caveat must be stated here, at the start, because it governs everything: **not all accumulation is a pump and dump.** Sometimes a few wallets quietly buying a thin token is genuine smart money — an early, informed investor building a position in something they believe is undervalued, with a real thesis and no intention of dumping. The on-chain signature of accumulation is *identical* in its first frame whether it is a syndicate setting up a dump or a fund building a real position; both are concentration into a few hands on a quiet chart. What distinguishes them is *what happens next* — does the concentrated position get sold into a manufactured pump (a dump), or held through a real catalyst (an investment)? — and *the supporting detail*: a single aged, well-funded fund wallet with a track record reads very differently from a cluster of five fresh, single-token wallets sharing one funder. We will return to this distinction repeatedly, because the most expensive mistake in this whole domain is *not* getting dumped on; it is misreading genuine accumulation as a scam and either shorting it or, worse, dismissing the technique entirely after one false positive. Detection gives you a *probability*, sharpened by corroborating signals, never a certainty.

## The pump: coordinated buys, hype, and manufactured volume

The accumulation phase ends and the pump begins the moment the operators stop hiding and start *announcing*. The pump — also called the markup phase — is where the operators deliberately drive the price up to make the token look like a breakout and pull in the outside demand they need to sell into. It has three ingredients, and you can read all three on-chain and off.

The first ingredient is the **coordinated buy**: a burst of buying compressed into a tight window, often a few minutes. After a week of patient, flat accumulation, the chart suddenly lights up — several large buys land in quick succession, the price gaps up, and the candle that had been flat for days goes vertical. The compression is the point. A real, organic rally builds over time as independent buyers discover a token at different moments; a manufactured pump arrives all at once because it is *coordinated* — the operators (or the members of a pump group acting on a signal) all buy in the same window. On-chain, this shows up as a cluster of large buys with near-identical timing, sometimes from the same wallets that accumulated, sometimes from a fresh wave brought in to push.

The second ingredient is **hype**: synchronized promotion across social channels — coordinated posts, influencer pushes, "this is mooning" messaging — timed to land exactly when the price starts to move, so that a retail buyer who checks the token sees both a rising chart *and* a loud narrative at the same moment. The hype manufactures the *demand*; the coordinated buy manufactures the *chart*; together they create the impression of a token catching fire organically when in fact both blades of the scissors are held by the same hand.

The third ingredient, often present, is **manufactured volume** — wash trading. Wash trading is buying and selling between wallets you control to create the appearance of trading activity without any real change in ownership. On a token where genuine volume is thin, a pump looks more convincing if the volume number is large, so operators sometimes bounce the token between their own wallets to inflate the reported volume and make the pump look like a real surge of interest. Wash trading has its own detailed treatment in [Detecting wash trading](/blog/trading/onchain/detecting-wash-trading) — the key tell is volume that does not correspond to a change in the holder set or net flow, because the same tokens are circling among the same wallets.

![A pump and dump price and volume fingerprint with flat accumulation a volume burst at the pump and heavy sell volume during the dump](/imgs/blogs/pump-and-dump-and-coordinated-buying-3.png)

The chart above is illustrative — there is no curated real pump-and-dump price series in this series' data, so the numbers here show the *shape*, not a specific token — but the shape is the whole lesson. Read it left to right. The amber band is accumulation: price flat near \$0.045, volume a thin trickle. The green band is the pump: price spikes about sixty percent to a top near \$0.08 on a sudden burst of volume — coordinated buys plus wash plus the first wave of retail FOMO. The red band is distribution: the price rolls over while the volume stays *high*, because the operators are selling heavily into the remaining buyers. The gray aftermath is dead — price near \$0.02, volume gone, because there is nobody left to sell to and nothing left to pump. That high-volume *down* move in the red band is one of the most important tells in the whole picture: a healthy rally that pauses sees volume *fall* as it consolidates; a distribution sees volume *stay high while price falls*, because the high volume *is* the operators dumping.

#### Worked example: a \$200k coordinated buy moving a thin token 60%

Take the token from the previous example — \$2,000,000 notional cap, but with only about \$120,000 of tokens actually sitting in its liquidity pool. The operators (or a pump group they signal) execute a coordinated buy: roughly \$200,000 of buying compressed into a few minutes. On a market that thin, that \$200,000 does not get filled at one price — it walks up the order book, eating through the available sell orders, and by the time it is done the price has jumped about sixty percent, from \$0.045 to roughly \$0.072, then ticks to a top near \$0.08 as the first FOMO buyers chase it. A \$200,000 buy moving a \$2,000,000-cap token sixty percent feels impossible until you remember that the *cap* is fiction and the *float* is what matters: \$200,000 of buying against \$120,000 of real depth is a flood against a puddle. The lesson: the pump is not magic and it is not real demand — it is a known quantity of coordinated capital deliberately overwhelming a deliberately-chosen thin market to manufacture a candle that looks like a breakout.

Worth pausing on the legitimacy spectrum here, because "the price went up on a burst of volume" is not, by itself, proof of manipulation, and over-reading it will make you miss real moves and cry wolf at honest rallies. At the clean end, a token can spike on *genuine* news — a real exchange listing, a real partnership, a real product launch — drawing a real, *diverse* crowd of independent buyers; the volume burst there is matched by a wide spread of new, independently-funded wallets and is not preceded by a tight accumulation cluster. In the middle is a borderline case: a legitimately exciting token that *also* attracts opportunistic pumpers riding the same wave. At the dark end is the pure manufactured pump — a tight accumulation cluster, a coordinated buy from a few related wallets, wash volume padding the number, and synchronized hype, with no real catalyst underneath. You separate them with the *flow*, not the price: a real rally has many independent buyers and no shared-funder accumulation cluster sitting on top of it; a manufactured pump has a few coordinated hands that accumulated first and are now selling. The price candle looks the same in all three cases — that is exactly why the operators rely on you reading the candle and not the flow.

## Low-float mechanics: why a small buy moves a small-cap token so much

The pump only works because of the token's mechanics, so it is worth slowing down on *why* a \$200,000 buy can move a \$2,000,000-cap token sixty percent. The answer dismantles the most dangerous illusion in small-cap crypto: that market cap measures how much money is "in" a token. It does not.

A token's **market cap** is just its price multiplied by its total (or circulating) supply. It is a notional number — what the whole supply would *theoretically* be worth if every token traded at the last price. But you cannot sell the whole supply at the last price, because there are not enough buyers at that price; the moment you try, the price falls. The number that actually governs how the price moves is the **liquidity** — how many tokens (and how much stablecoin or ETH) are sitting in the market, ready to be traded right now. On a decentralized exchange, that liquidity lives in a **pool** (see [Reading DEX liquidity and pools](/blog/trading/onchain/reading-dex-liquidity-and-pools) and [DeFi protocols: Uniswap, Aave, MakerDAO](/blog/trading/crypto/defi-protocols-uniswap-aave-makerdao)): a contract holding a balance of the token and a balance of the quote asset, where the price is set by the *ratio* between them. A buy removes tokens from the pool and adds quote, shifting the ratio — and on a thin pool, a modest buy shifts the ratio a lot.

![Grid explaining why a small dollar buy moves a low-float token a large percent because the free float is tiny](/imgs/blogs/pump-and-dump-and-coordinated-buying-5.png)

The grid lays out the mechanism. The headline market cap (\$2,000,000) looks mid-size, but the real free float — the tokens actually in the pool and available to trade — might be only \$120,000. In a *deep* market, a \$200,000 buy is a drop in the ocean and barely moves the price. In a *thin* market, that same \$200,000 buy walks the price up a near-vertical order book, because each successive token costs more as the pool empties. The price impact — the **slippage** — *is* the pump: the operators are not discovering a fair higher price, they are deliberately eating a near-empty book to manufacture a move. The takeaway sits across the bottom: price reacts to order size relative to liquidity, not to market cap. A coordinated buy that would be invisible on a deep market is a sixty-percent candle on a thin one — which is precisely why the operators chose a thin one.

#### Worked example: a \$200k buy on \$120k of depth versus on \$10M of depth

Make the dependence on liquidity concrete with two markets and the same buy. Market A is the thin target: a \$2,000,000-cap token with about \$120,000 of real depth in its pool. A \$200,000 buy is larger than the entire available depth; it walks the price up roughly sixty percent and leaves the buyer with an awful average fill — exactly the manufactured pump. Market B is a deep, mature token with \$10,000,000 of depth in its pool. The same \$200,000 buy is two percent of the available depth; it moves the price perhaps one percent, fills near the quoted price, and is invisible on the chart an hour later. Same \$200,000, two completely different outcomes — because price impact scales with order size *relative to liquidity*, not with the dollar amount or the market cap. The lesson: when you see a small-cap token spike dramatically on a buy that would be a rounding error elsewhere, the move is telling you about the *thinness of the market*, not the *strength of the demand* — and thin markets are where pumps are manufactured, never where real demand lives.

This is also why the operators *need* you. Their accumulated position can only be converted to cash if there are buyers to sell it into, and on a market this thin, their own selling would crash the price before they could exit — *unless* they have first drawn in a wave of fresh demand to absorb the supply. The pump manufactures that demand. The cruel arithmetic is that the same thinness that let \$200,000 manufacture the pump means the operators' \$300,000+ position *cannot* be sold without buyers, so the entire scheme is structurally dependent on retail showing up at the top. You are not incidental to the pump and dump; you are its required final ingredient. Refusing to be that ingredient is the entire defense.

## Distribution: supply moving from few to many, the mirror of accumulation

Now the phase that completes the scheme and, conveniently, gives off the loudest signal: **distribution**, the dump. Distribution is the operators selling their accumulated position into the retail demand the pump created. Its defining property is that it is the *exact mirror* of accumulation — where accumulation moved supply from many holders into a few coordinated hands, distribution moves it from those few hands back out to many new holders, at the top of the price.

![Before and after comparison showing supply concentrating into a few wallets during accumulation then fanning out to many retail wallets during distribution](/imgs/blogs/pump-and-dump-and-coordinated-buying-4.png)

The before/after figure makes the symmetry explicit. On the left, accumulation: the float starts spread across many small earlier holders, a few funded wallets quietly buy it up, supply concentrates into a few hands, and the holder count is flat or falling even as those few wallets grow. On the right, distribution: the pump and hype draw a wave of new retail buyers in, the few sell their \$1.2 million of accumulated supply into that inflow at the top, the float fans out to hundreds of fresh wallets averaging a few thousand dollars each, and the holder count *spikes* as the price rolls over. The two panels are reflections of each other across the pump in the middle — supply many-to-few, then few-to-many — and recognizing that symmetry is the core skill, because the second half is the *consequence* of the first, and if you saw the first you should not be a participant in the second.

#### Worked example: distributing \$1.2M onto 400 retail wallets averaging \$3k each

Return to our token at its \$0.08 top. The operators' accumulated position — the \$300,000 they bought during the quiet week, plus the appreciation from the pump — is now worth around \$1,200,000 at the inflated price. To realize that, they sell into the demand the pump created. Over the next few hours, roughly four hundred fresh retail wallets buy in, drawn by the rising chart and the synchronized hype, each putting in around \$3,000 on average — and four hundred wallets times \$3,000 is about \$1,200,000 of fresh buying, almost exactly the supply the operators need to offload. The float that a week earlier had concentrated into five coordinated wallets now fans out to four hundred independent ones, at the worst possible price for the buyers. The operators walk away with roughly \$1,200,000; the four hundred retail wallets hold a token that is about to collapse. The lesson: distribution is not a separate mystery — it is accumulation run in reverse, and the \$1,200,000 that leaves the few hands is the same \$1,200,000 that arrives from the many, which is *you* if you bought the pump.

The on-chain tells of distribution are specific and worth listing, because this is the phase you are most likely to be watching in real time (price is moving, so you noticed). First, the **accumulator wallets are now sellers** — the same wallets that bought during the quiet week (or the cluster they funded) are sending tokens *to* the pool and the exchange and *out*, not in. Second, the **holder count is rising fast** — every retail buy creates a new holder, so the count of distinct holding addresses climbs steeply exactly as the operators are exiting. Third, the **average position size is shrinking** — supply is moving from a few large holders to many small ones, so the median holder gets smaller even as the total holder count grows. Fourth, and most diagnostic, **the price is rolling over while volume stays high** — that high-volume down move is the operators selling, the opposite of the low-volume consolidation a healthy uptrend shows. Put those together and distribution is unmistakable: the wallets that knew are leaving, the wallets that do not know are arriving, and the price is being handed from the former to the latter.

#### Worked example: being the buyer at the \$0.08 top who rides it to \$0.02

Now stand in the shoes of the person this whole post is trying to keep you from being. You see the token: the chart is vertical, up sixty percent on the day, social channels are loud, and it *feels* like you are early to something. You buy \$5,000 at \$0.08, near the top. What you cannot see on the price chart — but could have seen on the flow — is that the wallets selling to you are the same five that accumulated quietly a week ago, and the holder count is spiking, which means the supply is fanning out *to people like you*. Over the next several hours the operators finish distributing, the buying dries up, and with nobody left to sell to, the price collapses to \$0.02 — a seventy-five percent drop. Your \$5,000 position is now worth \$1,250; you have lost \$3,750. You were not unlucky and you were not early — you were the *exit liquidity*, the required final ingredient that converted the operators' accumulated position into cash, and the loss came directly out of your pocket into theirs. The lesson, blunt as it deserves to be: chasing a thin-token pump is not "getting in on a move," it is volunteering to be the demand a scheme was built to find.

## The fingerprint across phases: price, volume, and holder count

Step back and assemble the full fingerprint, because no single number tells the story — the diagnosis comes from how three numbers move *together* across the three phases. Those numbers are price, volume, and holder count, and each phase has a distinct signature in all three.

In **accumulation**, price is flat, volume is thin, and holder count is flat or even falling (the few accumulators are buying *out* smaller earlier holders, so distinct addresses can shrink while concentration rises). In the **pump**, price spikes, volume bursts, and holder count starts climbing as the first FOMO buyers and the pump group arrive. In **distribution**, price tops and rolls over, volume *stays high* (that is the dump), and holder count climbs steeply as the float fans out to hundreds of new wallets. In the **aftermath**, price sits near zero, volume dies, and holder count plateaus — full of bag-holders, with no new buyers because there is nothing left to pump and nobody left to sell to.

The single most useful cross-check in that table is the relationship between **holder count and price**, because it cuts straight through the price-chart illusion. Intuitively, you might expect a rising holder count to be bullish — more people own it, surely that is demand. But timing is everything: a holder count that rises *as the price falls* is the dump, because every new holder is a retail buyer absorbing the supply the operators are unloading at the top. Rising holders into a *rising* price early in a real move can be healthy; rising holders into a *falling* price is supply being handed from the informed few to the uninformed many. This is the holder-count-versus-price tell, and it is worth its own picture.

![Holder count rising while price falls during distribution shown as a dual axis chart with an illustrative low cap token](/imgs/blogs/pump-and-dump-and-coordinated-buying-6.png)

The chart above is again illustrative — the data shows the *relationship*, not a specific token — but the relationship is the lesson. The red line is the holder count; the blue line is the price. Through accumulation, holders sit flat near 110 (a few hands) while the price is quiet. Through the pump, both rise together as retail rushes in. Then comes the diagnostic moment, marked by the dashed line at the price top: the blue price line *peaks and turns down*, but the red holder line *keeps climbing*, all the way to over 500 wallets. That divergence — holders still climbing while price is falling — is the dump in a single picture. The operators are selling (price down), and the buyers absorbing the sales are new holders (count up). When you see a holder count surging into a falling price, you are watching the float move from the few who knew to the many who did not, and you do not want to be one of the many.

#### Worked example: a holder count tripling while the price halves

Quantify the tell on our token. At the price top of \$0.08, the token has about 320 holders. Over the next few hours, as the operators distribute, the price *halves* to about \$0.04 — and yet the holder count *rises* to over 500, a more than fifty percent jump in distinct holding addresses *while the price is falling*. Pull the numbers and the contradiction is stark: if the price is collapsing, why is the number of owners growing? Because each new owner is a retail wallet buying the supply the operators are dumping; the \$1,200,000 of selling has to land in *someone's* wallets, and those someones are the rising holder count. A naive holder-count dashboard would flash this as "adoption growing fast"; read against the falling price, it is "distribution in progress." The lesson: never read holder count in isolation — read it against price, and a holder count that climbs into a falling price is the loudest dump signal the chain gives you. (The deeper craft of reading holder distribution lives in [Supply distribution and holder concentration](/blog/trading/onchain/supply-distribution-and-holder-concentration) and [Holder analysis for memecoins](/blog/trading/onchain/holder-analysis-for-memecoins).)

## Detecting it early: concentration plus fresh-funder plus illiquid plus coordinated volume

Everything above converges on a practical question: how do you catch a pump and dump *before* you become its exit liquidity? The answer is a stack of four signals that, taken together, identify a token that is set up to be dumped — and the whole point is that you can read all four *before* you buy, in the few minutes of due diligence that separate a disciplined trader from a bag-holder.

The first signal is **concentration**: a few wallets hold a large share of the tradable float. Pull the holder list (Etherscan's holders tab on EVM, Solscan's holders tab on Solana, or a Bubblemaps visualization) and ask what fraction of the *non-locked, non-burned, non-LP* supply sits in the top handful of plain wallets. If five EOAs control most of the real float, the price is whatever those five decide it is. The second signal is the **shared funder**: trace those top wallets' first inbound transfers (the funding-source walk from [Address clustering and heuristics](/blog/trading/onchain/address-clustering-and-heuristics)). If they all lead back to one address, the "several holders" are one entity, and the concentration is far worse than the holder list alone shows. The third signal is **illiquidity**: check the actual depth in the pool, not the market cap. If a \$2 million-cap token has only a few thousand dollars of real depth, it is structurally pumpable — a modest coordinated buy can manufacture any candle. The fourth signal is **sudden coordinated volume**: a burst of large buys compressed into a tight window, especially from related or fresh wallets, especially paired with synchronized social hype, is the pump beginning.

Any one of these alone is weak. Concentration can be benign (locked team vesting, a CEX custodial wallet, an LP contract). A shared funder can be an exchange that pools millions of users. Illiquidity is normal for any new token. A volume burst can be real news. But *stack them* — concentration in a few fresh wallets, that all share a funder, holding a thin token, that just lit up on coordinated volume — and the probability that you are looking at a manufactured pump goes from "maybe" to "very likely." The discipline is the same as in all on-chain detection: no single clue is proof, but a convergence of independent clues that all point the same way is a signal you act on. And the action, when the stack lights up, is simple: do not buy. You cannot lose money in a pump and dump you did not enter.

#### Worked example: the four-signal stack flashing red before a \$5,000 buy

Put yourself back at the moment of temptation — the chart is vertical, the hype is loud, you are about to put in \$5,000. Now run the four-signal check first, the way this post is training you to. Signal one, concentration: the holders tab shows five plain EOAs control roughly seventy percent of the non-LP float. Signal two, shared funder: you trace those five and all five were funded by `0xF1a2…` within the same week. Signal three, illiquidity: the pool holds about \$120,000 of real depth against a \$2,000,000 headline cap. Signal four, coordinated volume: the sixty-percent candle came from a tight burst of buys minutes ago, alongside a synchronized social push. Four signals, all red, all readable in maybe ten minutes of clicking — and they tell you that the \$5,000 you were about to commit would be buying directly into the exit of five wallets that accumulated this float a week ago. You close the tab. Ten minutes of checking just saved you the \$3,750 loss from the earlier example. The lesson: the four-signal stack is cheap to run and catastrophically expensive to skip, and the entire edge of on-chain analysis for a retail buyer is the willingness to run it *before* clicking buy rather than *after* clicking sell.

The tools make this practical. On EVM, **Etherscan** gives you the holders tab and the funding trace (oldest transactions, "Funded By"); **Bubblemaps** visualizes the connection graph so a cluster of funder-linked wallets shows up as connected bubbles instead of a flat list; **Nansen** and **Arkham** label known entities (so a "whale" that is actually a labeled exchange or market maker is flagged) and automate the multi-hop funding walk; **DeBank** shows a wallet's full portfolio so you can see whether a holder is a single-token fresh wallet or a diversified real investor; **Dune** lets you query the first buyers and the holder-count-over-time series directly. On Solana, **Solscan** plus the Pump.fun-era dashboards and bubble-map tools expose the holder graph and first buyers, and the speed of the chain makes the four-signal check something you run *fast* or not at all. The broader map of which tool does what lives in [The on-chain tooling landscape](/blog/trading/onchain/the-onchain-tooling-landscape) — but the discipline matters more than the dashboard: a green number on a dashboard you did not interrogate is exactly what the operators are counting on.

## How to read it: a walkthrough of the three phases on a low-cap token

Tie it all together with a single pass through a real-feeling low-cap token, watching the three phases unfold the way you would on-chain. The token, market cap, and wallets here are illustrative composites — not a specific real token — but the *reading* is exactly what you would do live.

**Phase 1, accumulation (the quiet week).** You first notice the token not because the price is moving — it is dead flat near \$0.045 — but because a holder-distribution check flags it: five plain wallets have quietly grown to control most of the non-LP float. You pull each wallet's age (all created within a few days of each other) and each one's first inbound transfer. All five trace to one funder, `0xF1a2…`, which sent each about \$60,000 in stablecoins over the prior week — \$300,000 of coordinated accumulation. You check the pool depth: about \$120,000, thin. The price has not moved, so a casual chart-watcher sees nothing. But you have already seen the whole setup: a few fresh wallets, one funder, a thin token, supply concentrated. Your read is not "buy the quiet accumulation" — it is "this is set up to be pumped; watch, and do not touch." You add it to a watchlist with a note: *accumulation cluster, one funder, thin pool — likely pump pending.*

**Phase 2, the pump (the afternoon).** A few days later it fires. In a tight window of minutes, several large buys land, the price gaps from \$0.045 toward \$0.08 — about sixty percent — and your social feeds light up with synchronized "this is mooning" posts. The volume number is large, but when you check it against net flow and the holder set, a chunk of it does not correspond to any change in ownership: wash volume padding the figure. A chart-watcher seeing the vertical candle and the hype feels the pull to buy. You feel it too — and then you remember your watchlist note. The pump is not new information; it is the *expected* second act of the setup you already flagged. The four-signal stack you ran during accumulation is still red. You do not buy. The single hardest thing in this entire discipline is *not acting* when a chart is screaming at you to act — and the watchlist note from phase one is what makes it possible, because you decided before the adrenaline that this was a trap.

**Phase 3, distribution (the dump).** Within hours, the tell you were waiting for appears: the five accumulator wallets start sending tokens out — to the pool, to exchanges — while the holder count, which you are tracking via a Dune query, climbs steeply, past 320, past 400, toward 500. The price tops near \$0.08 and rolls over, but the volume stays high — the operators are selling into the last of the demand. You watch the holder-count line cross *above* a falling price line: the divergence, the dump in one picture. Four hundred fresh wallets, averaging a few thousand dollars each, are absorbing the \$1,200,000 the operators are unloading. The scheme completes; the price settles toward \$0.02; the holder count plateaus full of bag-holders. You did not make money on this token — and that is the *win*, because the alternative was to be one of those four hundred wallets. The walkthrough's whole lesson is that the profitable move was the trade you *did not* take, and you only had the discipline to skip it because you read the flow in phase one instead of the price in phase two.

## Common misconceptions

**"Every fast, big rally is a pump and dump."** No — and believing this will cost you, both by missing real moves and by crying wolf until you stop trusting the signal. Real tokens spike on real news: a major exchange listing, a real partnership, a genuine product launch can all draw a fast, large, *organic* rally. The difference is in the flow, not the candle. A real rally has a *wide, independent* set of new buyers and no shared-funder accumulation cluster sitting on top of it ready to sell; a pump and dump has a few coordinated hands that accumulated first and are distributing now. Judge the rally by who is buying and who accumulated before it, not by how green the candle is. The candle looks identical in both cases — which is exactly why the operators rely on you judging by the candle.

**"Market cap tells me how much money is in a token."** It does not, and this misconception is the engine of every small-cap pump. Market cap is price times supply — a notional number you could never actually realize, because you cannot sell the whole supply at the last price. The number that governs how the price moves is *liquidity*: the depth actually sitting in the pool. A \$2,000,000-cap token with \$120,000 of real depth is a puddle wearing a market cap; a \$200,000 buy floods it. Always check the pool depth, not the headline cap, before assuming a token is "too big to manipulate" — the cap is the fiction the scheme is built on.

**"A high holder count means a healthy, decentralized token."** Not by itself, and read against price it can mean the opposite. A holder count that rises *as the price falls* is distribution — every new holder is a retail wallet absorbing the supply the operators are dumping. The healthy signal is a holder count that grows on a stable or rising price with genuinely independent buyers; the dangerous one is a holder count surging into a collapsing price. And a holder count can be cosmetically inflated anyway — split one entity's stake across fifty fresh wallets and a naive count reads as "decentralized" when it is one hand. Count *entities* (via the funding and connection graph), not addresses, and always read the count against the price.

**"If I can spot the accumulation, I should buy alongside the operators and sell before the dump."** This is the dangerous misread, and it is wrong on three counts. First, you cannot reliably tell, *during accumulation*, whether you are looking at a dump-in-the-making or genuine smart money — the early frame is identical, and you would be guessing. Second, even if it is a pump in progress, you have no edge on the *timing* of the dump; the operators decide when to sell, and they will sell into your buy without warning. Third, buying alongside a manipulation scheme to ride its pump is participating in the manipulation, ethically and often legally. The signal's value is defensive: it tells you to *stand aside*, not to *join in*. Trying to surf a pump is how disciplined-sounding people end up as exit liquidity with a story about how they "almost timed it."

**"Wash volume is too sophisticated to spot."** It is one of the more readable manipulations, because the tell is structural: wash trading inflates the *volume* number without changing the *holder set* or the *net flow*, since the same tokens are circling among the same wallets. When a token shows a large volume figure but the set of holders barely changes and net flow is near zero, the volume is not real trading — it is the same coins bouncing between related wallets. You do not need to catch every wash trade; you need to notice that the volume does not correspond to a change in ownership, which is exactly what [Detecting wash trading](/blog/trading/onchain/detecting-wash-trading) teaches. Treat a big volume number on a token with a static holder set as manufactured until proven otherwise.

## The playbook: what to do with it

Pump-and-dump detection is a filter on your buy decisions — a set of checks you run *before* you commit money, plus a set of tells you watch *if* you are already in. Here is the if-then.

**Signal: a few fresh wallets sharing one funder have accumulated a large share of a thin token.**
*Read:* the accumulation phase of a likely pump and dump — the float is concentrated in coordinated hands before any hype. *Action:* do not buy the quiet; flag it on a watchlist and wait. If you want exposure, size it as a pure lottery ticket you can lose entirely. *Invalidation / false positive:* it might be genuine smart money or a labeled fund with a real thesis — check for aged, diversified, independently-funded wallets and a real catalyst before reading it as a scheme; one aged fund is not five fresh siblings.

**Signal: a sudden coordinated buy spikes the price on thin liquidity, paired with synchronized hype.**
*Read:* the pump (markup) phase — the operators are manufacturing a breakout to draw your demand in. *Action:* do not chase the candle; if you flagged accumulation earlier, this is the expected act two, not new information. *Invalidation:* a real catalyst (a genuine listing, partnership, or launch) drawing a *wide, independent* crowd of buyers with no accumulation cluster on top — judge by the breadth and funding of the buyers, not the candle.

**Signal: the accumulator wallets are selling and the holder count is climbing while the price rolls over.**
*Read:* distribution — the dump in progress; supply is moving from the few who knew to the many who did not, and you would be the many. *Action:* if you hold, exit; never average down into a high-volume down move. If you do not hold, stay out — the rising holder count into a falling price is the loudest dump tell there is. *Invalidation:* a healthy uptrend pauses on *falling* volume and a *stable* holder set, not high volume and a surging count — if volume is high and holders are spiking as price falls, it is distribution, not consolidation.

**Signal: a token's volume is large but its holder set is static and net flow is near zero.**
*Read:* manufactured volume — wash trading padding the number to make a pump look organic. *Action:* discount the volume entirely; treat the "interest" as fake and the price move as unsupported. *Invalidation:* genuine high-frequency trading on a deep, mature market produces real volume with a stable holder set too — but on a thin new token, a big volume number with no holder churn is wash until proven otherwise.

**Signal: the four-signal stack — concentration, shared funder, illiquidity, coordinated volume — all light up before you buy.**
*Read:* a token structurally set up to be dumped, with a manufactured pump in progress; buying means volunteering to be the exit liquidity. *Action:* do not buy. The cheapest, most profitable move on-chain analysis offers a retail trader is the buy you decline. *Invalidation:* if you can genuinely rule out the shared funder (independent funding), the concentration (locked/disclosed/diverse), and the manufactured volume (real, diverse buyers), the stack is not lit and the rally may be real — but require all four to be clean, not just one.

![Decision matrix mapping each pump and dump phase to its on-chain signal an action and a false positive](/imgs/blogs/pump-and-dump-and-coordinated-buying-7.png)

The matrix collapses the playbook into one picture: each phase, its on-chain signal, what it means, your action, and the false positive that keeps you honest. The through-line is the asymmetry of the trade you are being offered. When a pump and dump is running, the catalyst that is supposed to make you money — the breakout, the hype, the vertical candle — is the *exit* for people who accumulated before you on information and coordination you did not have. Detecting that is not about catching criminals; that is the exchanges' and regulators' job, with their subpoenas and their KYC. It is about recognizing, from the public record alone, that the green candle you are being shown is a few wallets' planned exit, and that the table was set before you sat down. The single most profitable thing on-chain analysis ever does for a retail trader is let you stand up and walk away from that table. The pump you do not chase is the loss you do not take.
