On August 2, 2016, the cryptocurrency exchange Bitfinex announced that it had been breached. An attacker had drained roughly **119,754 BTC** — about **120,000 coins** — out of the exchange's wallets in a single security failure. At the Bitcoin price of the day, around **\$600** per coin, that haul was worth approximately **\$72 million**. It was, at the time, one of the largest exchange hacks ever, and Bitfinex famously socialized the loss across all its customers by haircutting every account by about 36% and issuing recovery tokens. The coins themselves moved into addresses the attacker controlled, and then — to the outside world — they mostly went quiet.

What makes this case the perfect capstone for everything in this series is what happened next, and what *did not* happen. The coins did not vanish. They could not. Every one of those 120,000 stolen Bitcoin lived on a public, permanent ledger, sitting in addresses anyone could watch. For years they barely moved. When they did move, the people controlling them used a textbook sequence of obfuscation techniques — peeling, darknet hops, mixing, chain-hopping, fake accounts, and deposits chunked small to slip under reporting thresholds. And yet on February 8, 2022, the US Department of Justice announced it had seized **94,000 of those Bitcoin**, by then worth about **\$3.6 billion**, and had arrested two suspects in New York. It remains the largest financial seizure the DOJ has ever made.

This is a story about permanence beating patience. The thieves had every advantage time could give them — six years, a 50× appreciation in the asset, and a full menu of laundering tools. The investigators had one structural advantage that outweighed all of it: the ledger never forgot. Every figure, claim, and milestone below is drawn from public sources — the DOJ press release, the statement of facts filed in the case, and on-chain reporting from analytics firms. Where a specific address or transaction would be needed, this post describes the *method* and uses illustrative placeholders rather than inventing real-looking identifiers; the documented facts are the milestones, not the hex.

This is the only post in the series that *uses* every other Track-C skill at once. So we will move as an investigator would: first a brief recap of the toolkit you are about to watch in action, then the theft itself, the strange years of dormancy, the laundering pattern when the coins finally moved, how investigators followed each hop, the mechanics of the 2022 seizure, and finally the lessons that generalize to any traced flow. Read it as a forensic walkthrough, not a how-to: the point is to *recognize* these patterns and understand why they fail against a funded investigation, not to reproduce them.

## Foundations: the tracing toolkit, recapped

This is a capstone, so we are not building each tool from zero — the earlier Track-C posts do that. But you should hold the whole toolkit in your head before we watch it work, because the Bitfinex trail uses all of it. Here is the five-part kit, each defined in one paragraph, with a pointer to the post that goes deep.

**Hop-by-hop tracing** is the spine. A blockchain transaction moves value from one or more inputs to one or more outputs. To *trace* a flow is to start at a known address — here, the thief's wallets — and follow the value forward, transaction by transaction, building a directed graph of where the coins went. On Bitcoin specifically, every payment that is smaller than the input it spends produces a **change output**: the leftover coins returned to the spender, like the change you get back from a \$20 bill. Following the change output is how you keep tracking the *same owner* across many hops. The full mechanics live in [how to trace a transaction flow](/blog/trading/onchain/how-to-trace-a-transaction-flow), and the change-output idea rests on the UTXO model from [how blockchains store data: UTXO vs account](/blog/trading/onchain/how-blockchains-store-data-utxo-vs-account).

**Address clustering** is how you collapse thousands of addresses into a smaller number of *entities*. A single person or organization controls many addresses, and heuristics let you group them. On Bitcoin the strongest is **common-input ownership** (also called the co-spend heuristic): if two addresses are spent together as inputs to one transaction, the same party must control both, because one signer had to authorize all the inputs. Clustering is what turns "120,000 coins scattered across hundreds of addresses" into "one thief cluster," and later "dozens of deposit accounts" into "one cash-out cluster." The heuristics and what breaks them are in [address clustering and heuristics](/blog/trading/onchain/address-clustering-and-heuristics).

**Labeling and attribution** is how on-chain entities get real-world names. An address is pseudonymous, not anonymous: it is a number, not a name, but behavior, funding sources, and especially *interactions with regulated services* attach identity over time. When stolen coins are deposited to an exchange that runs Know-Your-Customer (KYC) checks, that exchange holds a record linking the on-chain deposit to a real person's verified identity. Labeling is the bridge from the chain to a courtroom. See [labeling and attribution](/blog/trading/onchain/labeling-and-attribution).

**Demixing** is what you do when funds pass through a mixer or a CoinJoin — a service that pools many users' coins and pays out a tangle of outputs specifically to break the input-output link. Demixing uses timing, amounts, fee fingerprints, and statistical matching to *probabilistically* re-associate inputs with outputs. It rarely gives a clean proof, but it narrows the candidates, and combined with what happens after the mixer (a known cash-out, a labeled exchange) it can reconnect the trail. The mechanics — and the honest limits — are in [mixers, CoinJoin, and obfuscation](/blog/trading/onchain/mixers-coinjoin-and-obfuscation).

**Cross-chain tracing** follows value when it leaves one chain and reappears on another — through a bridge, a swap, or a stablecoin rail. Obfuscators love chain-hops because a naive tracer who only watches Bitcoin loses the trail the moment funds convert to something else. A serious investigator watches the seams: the bridge contracts, the swap services, the USDT rails on Tron and Ethereum. That seam is covered in [cross-chain tracing: bridges and the USDT rails](/blog/trading/onchain/cross-chain-tracing-bridges-and-the-usdt-rails).

### What made this case hard — and what made it traceable

It helps to name both sides of the ledger before we start, because the whole story is a contest between them.

What made the Bitfinex trail *hard* came down to three things. First, **time**: the coins sat for years, and a cold trail is harder to staff and fund than a live chase — most investigations do not get a six-year budget. Second, **volume and fragmentation**: 120,000 coins do not move in one transaction; they were split across hundreds or thousands of addresses and moved in many small pieces, multiplying the number of hops to follow. Third, **technique stacking**: the launderers did not rely on one trick. They layered peel chains *and* darknet hops *and* mixing *and* chain-hops *and* fake exchange accounts, so that defeating any single technique still left several more in the way.

What made it *traceable* was simpler, and it is the single most important sentence in this post: **the ledger is permanent and public.** Every hop, however clever, was recorded forever and visible to anyone. Mixing and peeling do not erase the record; they *add steps* and *add noise*. A funded, patient investigator can grind through steps and filter noise. The thieves were running up a down escalator — every move they made to obscure the trail also created a fresh, permanent edge in the graph. Dormancy did not help them; it just meant the evidence sat in plain sight, aging, while investigators improved their tools.

#### Worked example: the appreciation that changed the stakes

Hold two numbers side by side. In August 2016, the **120,000 BTC** stolen were worth about `120,000 × \$600 = \$72,000,000`, roughly **\$72 million**. By the time of the seizure in early 2022, Bitcoin had appreciated enormously; the **94,000 BTC** the DOJ recovered were valued at about **\$3.6 billion**. Even on the full 120,000 coins, the 2021 peak price of roughly **\$60,000** implies `120,000 × \$60,000 = \$7,200,000,000` — over **\$7 billion** of value sitting in those addresses at the top.

So a theft that began as a **\$72M** problem became, through nothing but the asset appreciating, a multi-billion-dollar one — about a **50×** revaluation of the exact same coins. *The intuition: holding stolen Bitcoin is not like holding cash in a duffel bag; the duffel bag grew to fifty times its size while sitting still, which raised both the prize and the priority of the chase.* That appreciation is also why the case eventually justified the resources that cracked it.

![Bar chart comparing the 72 million dollar 2016 theft value with the 3.6 billion dollar 2022 seizure value](/imgs/blogs/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end-7.png)

The chart above makes the revaluation literal: the left bar is what the stolen coins were worth the day they were taken; the right bar is what the seized portion was worth the day it was recovered. The investigators did not get richer; the asset did. But that difference is exactly why a six-year-old cold case ended up as a DOJ headline.

## The theft: 120,000 BTC and a quiet exit

On August 2, 2016, the breach drained Bitfinex's wallets. The exact security failure has been described publicly in broad strokes — it involved the way the exchange's multi-signature custody arrangement with a third-party security provider was configured — but for our purposes the mechanics of the *break-in* matter less than what happened to the *coins*. Once stolen, the roughly 120,000 BTC were moved into addresses the attacker controlled. From the moment they landed there, those addresses became the **source node** of the entire flow we are going to trace.

This is the first place clustering does work. The stolen coins did not sit in one address; they were spread out. But because they were moved and consolidated using the attacker's own keys, the co-spend heuristic ties many of those addresses together: any transaction that spends two of them as inputs proves common control. An analyst watching in 2016 could draw a boundary around a **thief cluster** — a set of addresses provably controlled by one party, holding the stolen funds. That cluster is the thing investigators tagged and watched for the next six years.

It is worth pausing on what a stolen-funds cluster *is*, because it determines everything downstream. A cluster is not a guess; it is a set of addresses connected by hard heuristics, with a label attached — in this case, "Bitfinex 2016 theft." Once that label exists in the major analytics firms' databases, it propagates. Every address that ever receives funds from the cluster inherits a taint marker. Every exchange that uses one of those firms' screening feeds will see an incoming deposit flagged if it traces back to the labeled cluster, even many hops later. This is the quiet machinery that made the whole case inevitable: the moment investigators drew the boundary around the thief cluster in 2016, they planted a tripwire on every coin in it, and that tripwire stayed armed for six years no matter where the coins went.

Contrast this with how the same theft would have played out in cash. If a thief stole \$72 million in physical \$100 bills, the serial numbers might be recorded, but the bills could be spent, re-spent, exchanged abroad, and laundered through cash-intensive businesses with no central record connecting each transaction to the next. There is no permanent, public ledger of every hand a dollar bill passes through. On Bitcoin there is exactly that. The "serial numbers" — the specific coins, identified by their transaction history — cannot be erased or replaced, and the full chain of custody of every coin is published for anyone to read. The thief did not steal anonymous cash; they stole the most thoroughly documented \$72 million in financial history, and the documentation only grew more complete with every move.

There is also a notable epilogue to the theft itself that underscores the permanence point. Bitfinex did not simply absorb the loss; it issued recovery tokens to affected customers and, crucially, it never stopped considering the stolen coins *its* property. When the DOJ seized the 94,000 BTC in 2022, the question of who ultimately receives the recovered value turned on those years-old records and token commitments. The case did not end at the seizure — it had a long civil tail about restitution — precisely because the chain preserved, with perfect fidelity, the link between the 2016 theft and the 2022 coins. A six-year gap that would have muddied any cash case left the on-chain provenance crystal clear.

![Flow graph from the Bitfinex breach through the thief cluster, peel chains, darknet, and mixers to the exchange cash-outs and the seizure](/imgs/blogs/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end-2.png)

The graph above is the whole case in one picture, and we will spend the rest of the post walking through it. Read it left to right: the breach feeds the thief cluster; the cluster sits dormant; then it feeds peel chains; the peel chains feed a darknet market and mixers; those converge on exchange cash-outs; the cash-outs hit KYC and exchange cooperation; and that leads to the seizure. Every node is a documented stage of this case, and every arrow is a permanent set of on-chain edges. Notice that the flow *converges* at the cash-out: no matter how many obfuscation paths the funds took, they all had to funnel toward the same goal — turning Bitcoin into something spendable — and convergence is exactly where an investigator concentrates.

#### Worked example: how big a haul 120,000 BTC really is

To feel the scale, price it across the case's lifetime. At the 2016 theft price of about **\$600**, the full haul was `120,000 × \$600 ≈ \$72M`. By the 2017 bull run top near **\$19,000**, the same coins were worth `120,000 × \$19,000 ≈ \$2.28B`. At the 2021 cycle peak near **\$60,000**, they touched `120,000 × \$60,000 ≈ \$7.2B`. And the **94,000** coins actually seized were valued at about **\$3.6B** in February 2022, which back-implies a price near `\$3,600,000,000 ÷ 94,000 ≈ \$38,000` per coin on the seizure date.

*The intuition: a fixed pile of coins re-priced four times over the life of the case, and each repricing made the prize larger and the funds harder to launder quietly — you cannot quietly cash out billions the way you might quietly cash out tens of millions.* The sheer size is part of why the cash-out attempts had to be chunked, which, as we will see, is exactly what hung them.

## The dormant years: why sitting still is itself a signal

For a long stretch after the theft, the stolen coins barely moved. To a casual observer this looks like the safest possible strategy: do nothing, attract no attention, wait for the heat to fade. On a public ledger, it is the opposite. **Dormancy is not invisibility — it is a measurable state, and the end of dormancy is a loud, trackable event.**

Here is the mechanism. On-chain analysts track a family of metrics built on the *age* of coins. Every unspent output accumulates "coin-days": one coin sitting still for one day is one coin-day. A pile of 120,000 coins sitting untouched for years accumulates an enormous stack of coin-days. The moment those coins are finally spent, all of that accumulated age is destroyed at once — a metric literally called **Coin-Days Destroyed (CDD)** spikes. Dormant supply waking up is one of the most-watched events in on-chain analysis precisely because it is so rare and so visible. (The age-and-cost-basis family of metrics is built out in [profit, loss, SOPR, and HODL waves](/blog/trading/onchain/profit-loss-sopr-and-hodl-waves).)

![Before and after view of dormant stolen coins accumulating coin-days and then destroying them in one spike when finally moved](/imgs/blogs/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end-5.png)

The before/after figure captures the asymmetry. While the coins sit still (left), nothing on-chain happens — but nothing is lost, either; the evidence is simply aging in place, and investigators can wait at zero cost because the ledger is permanent. The instant the coins move (right), years of accumulated coin-days are destroyed in a single spike, the move is loud and conspicuous, and the trail *reopens* with fresh hops to follow. For a flagged cluster like the Bitfinex thief wallets, that waking event is the starting gun: a watcher with the cluster tagged gets an alert the moment a dormant stolen output is spent.

This is the first lesson that generalizes beyond this case. **Patience is not a defense on a permanent ledger.** In the cash economy, time is a launderer's friend — physical evidence degrades, witnesses forget, money gets spent and re-spent untraceably. On-chain, time is the *investigator's* friend: the evidence does not degrade, tools only get better, and the longer stolen coins sit, the more conspicuous they are when they finally have to move to be useful. Coins that never move are also coins that were never enjoyed; eventually a launderer has to touch them, and touching them is the event the whole defense was trying to avoid.

#### Worked example: the cost of dormancy on a permanent ledger

Suppose the thief cluster held the stolen coins untouched for **five years**. Across roughly **120,000 coins** that is on the order of `120,000 coins × 1,825 days ≈ 219,000,000 coin-days` of accumulated age sitting in flagged addresses. When even a slice of that finally moves, the destroyed-age signal is unmistakable against a normal day's background. Meanwhile the *carrying cost* of waiting, for the investigator, was effectively **\$0** — the ledger stored the evidence for free.

Contrast the launderer's side: every year of waiting, the coins appreciated, so the eventual cash-out had to move a larger dollar value. A pile worth **\$72M** in 2016 that the launderer wants to realize at 2021 prices is now several **\$billion** to move — and you cannot push billions through obfuscation quietly. *The intuition: dormancy lowered the launderer's risk of attention to zero in the short run but raised both the size and the conspicuousness of the eventual move — the bill for sitting still came due all at once, with interest.*

## When the funds moved: the laundering pattern

Eventually, portions of the stolen stash began to move. The statement of facts in the case and subsequent on-chain reporting describe a sequence of techniques layered on top of each other. We will walk each one, because together they are a near-complete catalog of how value gets laundered on a public chain — and, more importantly, of why each technique *delays* tracing rather than *defeating* it.

### Peel chains: shaving the pile down, hop by hop

The first and most characteristic pattern was the **peel chain**. A peel chain takes a large balance and, in a long sequence of transactions, repeatedly "peels" off a small amount to one address while forwarding the large remainder to a fresh address it controls. It works like a roll of bills: each hop tears off a small note and passes the rest of the roll along. Do this dozens or hundreds of times and you produce a long chain of transactions, each shedding a small, spendable chunk while the bulk keeps moving.

![Pipeline showing a peel chain shaving a small chunk off at each hop while forwarding the large remainder onward](/imgs/blogs/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end-3.png)

The pipeline figure shows one segment of a peel chain. At each hop, the wallet splits: a small **peeled output** breaks off toward a cash-out destination, and the **large remainder** forwards to the next hop, keeping the chain alive. The peeled chunks are sized to be inconspicuous when they later hit an exchange. The point of the design is to drip small, ordinary-looking amounts into the cash-out funnel over time, rather than depositing one giant suspicious lump.

Why does a peel chain fail against a tracer? Because of the change-output logic from the foundations. At each hop, the large forwarded remainder is structurally a change output — the leftover after the small peel. Following change outputs is the most basic tracing move there is, and it links every hop in the chain into one continuous, owner-consistent path. A peel chain is *long*, which makes tracing tedious, but length is not the same as anonymity. A patient analyst (or, in practice, analytics software that does this automatically) walks the change outputs hop by hop and reconstructs the entire chain. The peel chain trades a single conspicuous move for hundreds of small ones, but every one of those small moves is a permanent, followable edge.

#### Worked example: a peel chain sized to dodge thresholds

Say the launderer wants each peeled chunk to land below a deposit size that triggers extra exchange scrutiny — keep each cash-out leg around **\$30,000** to look like an ordinary retail deposit. At a Bitcoin price of, say, **\$40,000**, that is `\$30,000 ÷ \$40,000 = 0.75 BTC` peeled per cash-out leg. To move just **\$3,000,000** in laundered value at that chunk size takes `\$3,000,000 ÷ \$30,000 = 100` separate peel-and-deposit legs — and to move a meaningful slice of a **\$3.6B** stash you would need `\$3,600,000,000 ÷ \$30,000 = 120,000` such legs.

*The intuition: chunking small to dodge thresholds is self-defeating at scale — the smaller each piece, the more pieces you need, and every extra piece is one more permanent transaction the tracer can follow and one more deposit the exchange can flag.* The arithmetic of staying under a threshold is exactly what produced a sprawling, clusterable web of cash-out addresses.

### Darknet hops and chain-hops: borrowing someone else's tangle

The trail did not stay on a single straight peel chain. Documented reporting describes funds being routed through a **darknet marketplace** — AlphaBay — as a laundering layer. A darknet market runs its own internal wallets, pooling deposits from thousands of users and paying out withdrawals from a shared hot wallet, much like an exchange. By depositing stolen coins into such a service and later withdrawing, a launderer hopes to "borrow" the service's commingling: the coins that come out are no longer the same outputs that went in, so a naive output-to-output trace breaks at the service boundary.

This is a real obstacle, but it is also a fragile one, for two reasons that this case demonstrates. First, the *deposit edge* and the *withdrawal edge* are both still on-chain and permanent: you can see funds enter the service and see funds leave it, even if you cannot cross the internal pool by output-matching alone. Second — and this is the decisive one here — these services get **seized**. AlphaBay was taken down by law enforcement in mid-2017, and the seizure handed investigators the market's internal server records. Those records can directly connect a specific deposit to a specific withdrawal *inside* the pool, collapsing the one boundary the launderer was relying on. A service that commingles your coins is only as private as its database is secret, and that database is a single point of failure the launderer does not control.

The same logic applies to **chain-hops** — converting Bitcoin into another asset on another chain through a swap service or a bridge, then later converting back. Each conversion is a seam where a naive tracer can lose the trail, and a sophisticated one picks it back up by watching the seam: the deposit to the swap on chain A and the withdrawal from the swap on chain B are correlated in time and amount, and the services that perform the conversion are themselves labelable entities that can cooperate or be subpoenaed. The deep mechanics of these seams are in [cross-chain tracing: bridges and the USDT rails](/blog/trading/onchain/cross-chain-tracing-bridges-and-the-usdt-rails); the takeaway here is that a chain-hop, like a darknet hop, is a *delay at a boundary*, and boundaries are exactly where investigators have leverage.

There is a deeper structural reason these service hops are weaker than they look, and it is worth stating because it generalizes far beyond this case. A launderer who routes funds through a darknet market, a mixer, or a bridge is *outsourcing their privacy to a third party they do not control*. The privacy of that hop now depends entirely on the third party's database staying secret and the third party staying out of law enforcement's reach. But these services are high-value targets precisely because they sit on so much illicit flow, so they get seized, shut down, and subpoenaed at a steady rate. When AlphaBay went down, every launderer who had trusted it inherited a retroactive exposure: deposits and withdrawals they thought were safely commingled were now sitting in a database in the government's hands. The launderer made a decision in, say, 2017 that became a liability whenever the service fell — and on a permanent ledger, that liability never expires. You cannot un-deposit into a service that later gets seized.

This is the asymmetry that makes service hops a trap. The launderer needs *every* service they ever used to stay secure forever. The investigator needs only *one* of those services to break — one seized database, one cooperating operator, one subpoena answered — to collapse a boundary and reconnect the trail. Stacking more service hops does not improve those odds for the launderer; it worsens them, because each additional service is one more third party whose secrecy must hold indefinitely. More obfuscation is more counterparties, and more counterparties is more ways to be exposed.

### Mixers: noise, not erasure

The funds also passed through **mixers** — services and protocols designed to break the link between an input and an output by pooling many users' coins and paying out fresh ones. On Bitcoin this includes CoinJoin-style coordination; on Ethereum the canonical example is Tornado Cash, which over its life processed on the order of **\$7B** in deposits, of which analytics firms attributed roughly **30%** to illicit sources, and which the US Treasury sanctioned in August 2022. (The mixer mechanics and the sanctions story are covered in the series' [mixers, CoinJoin, and obfuscation](/blog/trading/onchain/mixers-coinjoin-and-obfuscation) post and the crypto track's [Tornado Cash and sanctioning code](/blog/trading/crypto/tornado-cash-and-sanctioning-code).)

A mixer is the strongest single obfuscation tool in the kit, and it is important to be honest about that: a well-used mixer genuinely degrades a trace to a *probability distribution* rather than a clean line. But "degrades to a probability" is not "erases." Demixing techniques use the structure that leaks anyway — timing correlations, characteristic amounts, fee fingerprints, the fact that a withdrawal often gets consolidated with other known coins shortly after — to re-associate likely inputs with likely outputs. The result is rarely a courtroom-grade single link on its own. But it does not have to be. Combined with what happens *after* the mixer — a withdrawal that flows into a labeled exchange deposit, or that gets clustered with the rest of the operation by behavior — the probabilistic mixer link gets pinned down by the certainty on either side of it.

#### Worked example: why mixing is a delay, not a defense

Suppose a launderer puts **\$30,000** of stolen value into a mixer and pulls **\$30,000** out a day later. The mixer breaks the direct output link, so an analyst can no longer say "this exact output came from that exact input." But suppose only **40** other deposits of similar size happened in that window; the analyst's candidate set is small. If the withdrawn coins then land in an exchange deposit that, by KYC, belongs to an account the analyst has *already* tied to the operation through other un-mixed legs, the probabilistic mixer link is confirmed by the labeled certainty downstream.

Put a number on the economics of relying on mixing: if pushing the whole **\$3.6B** through a mixer in **\$30,000** chunks means `\$3,600,000,000 ÷ \$30,000 = 120,000` separate mix-and-withdraw cycles, the operation becomes enormous, slow, and self-revealing — each cycle a fresh on-chain event in a flagged operation. *The intuition: a mixer raises the cost of tracing one hop, but it does not lower the cost of tracing the hundred hops on either side of it, and a funded investigation pays the higher cost where it has to and uses certainty elsewhere to pin down the noise.*

### Fictitious accounts and chunked cash-outs

The final laundering layer was the **cash-out**: converting Bitcoin into spendable money by depositing it to exchanges and other services that let you off-ramp to fiat. To do this without one giant suspicious deposit, the operation used **fictitious accounts** — exchange accounts opened under fake or borrowed identities — and **chunked deposits** sized to stay below the amounts that trigger enhanced reporting and review. The idea is to look, from each individual exchange's vantage point, like dozens of unrelated small retail customers rather than one entity moving a fortune.

This is the layer where the whole defense came apart, and the reason is the central irony of the case. To avoid one big flag, the launderers created **many small deposits across many accounts** — and that fan-out is exactly the structure clustering is built to collapse. We turn to that next.

## How investigators followed it: clustering the cash-outs

Everything upstream — peeling, darknet hops, mixing — was *delay*. The cash-out is where the funds had to re-converge and touch the regulated world, and that is where the investigation got its grip. The decisive analytical move was **clustering the cash-out addresses** back into a single owner, and then using exchange **KYC cooperation** to attach real identities to that owner.

![Graph showing many chunked deposits across fictitious accounts clustering into one owner that exchange KYC then attaches to named suspects](/imgs/blogs/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end-4.png)

The figure shows the move. On the left, three illustrative chunked deposits, each around **\$30,000**, each into a different fictitious account — exactly the fan-out the launderer built to look like unrelated customers. In the middle, the analytical collapse: shared funding sources, correlated timing, reused infrastructure, and consistent behavior tie those deposits into **one cluster**. On the right, the bridge to identity: the exchange's KYC records show those accounts were opened with information traceable to the same real people, and the named suspects follow.

How does clustering defeat fan-out? Several heuristics reinforce each other. **Common funding**: the fictitious accounts' deposit addresses were ultimately funded, directly or through a few hops, from the same upstream cluster — the stolen-coin operation — and that shared origin links them. **Behavioral fingerprints**: deposits that always come in the same size, at correlated times, following the same peel-chain structure, look like one operator's signature, not dozens of independent retail users. **Reused infrastructure**: shared services, shared withdrawal patterns, and the way value re-consolidated downstream all stitch the accounts together. None of these is a single proof, but stacked together they collapse the fan-out the launderers worked so hard to create. The very act of chunking — splitting one entity into many lookalike pieces — produces a web of correlations that clustering is purpose-built to find.

Then comes **labeling**, the bridge from cluster to courtroom. A cluster of exchange deposit accounts is still pseudonymous until a labeled, regulated entity attaches names. Exchanges that follow KYC rules hold verified identity records for their account holders. When investigators present a regulated exchange with the on-chain evidence that a set of accounts received funds traceable to a major theft, the exchange can cooperate and produce the identity records behind those accounts. That cooperation is the moment the chain connects to the physical world — the point where "cluster of addresses" becomes "this specific person."

#### Worked example: how chunking creates the cluster that catches you

Say the operation needs to off-ramp **\$2,400,000** while keeping each deposit at **\$30,000** to dodge enhanced review. That forces `\$2,400,000 ÷ \$30,000 = 80` deposits. To spread suspicion, the operator opens, say, **20** fictitious accounts and routes **4** deposits through each. Now there are **80** deposit events and **20** accounts — but all **20** were funded from the same upstream peel chains, all **80** deposits share timing and size fingerprints, and a few KYC records overlap.

From the launderer's view, that is 20 "unrelated customers." From the analyst's view, it is one cluster with 80 confirming data points and 20 identity records to pull. *The intuition: every deposit you split off to look independent is one more correlated data point proving you are not — at scale, chunking does not dilute the signal, it amplifies it, because you cannot fake 80 independent histories that all trace back to the same stolen pile.* The off-ramp is the chokepoint, and the chunking meant to ease through it is what jammed it.

### The breaks that turned a cold trail hot

Clustering and labeling are the analytical engine, but engines need fuel — specific, real-world evidence that converts probable links into proven ones. Public reporting on this case describes several independent **breaks**, each attaching identity to an on-chain hop, that together closed the loop.

![Matrix of three investigative breaks: the AlphaBay seizure data, exchange cooperation, and cloud key recovery, with what each gave investigators](/imgs/blogs/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end-6.png)

The matrix lays out three documented breaks. The **AlphaBay seizure data** — server records captured when the darknet market was taken down in 2017 — let investigators connect specific deposits inside that market to the stolen funds, collapsing a commingling boundary that output-matching alone could not cross. **Exchange cooperation** — KYC records from regulated venues where the chunked cash-outs landed — put real identities behind the deposit cluster, reinforcing the lesson that the off-ramp, not the chain, is the chokepoint. And the decisive **cloud key recovery**: investigators lawfully accessed an online account belonging to a suspect that contained a file listing wallet addresses and their private keys. That file did two things at once: it confirmed control of the stolen funds, and it literally handed agents the keys needed to *move and seize* the coins.

Notice the pattern across all three breaks. None of them was a clever new way to read the blockchain; the blockchain analysis had already drawn the graph and built the clusters. Each break was a piece of **off-chain evidence that attached identity or control to an on-chain node** — a seized database, a KYC record, a key file in the cloud. This is the real relationship between chain analysis and an investigation: the chain gives you the complete, permanent map of *where the value went*; the off-chain breaks give you the names and keys at specific points on that map. Neither alone is enough. Together they are decisive. The chain narrowed the search to a specific cluster and specific accounts; the breaks named the people behind them.

## How to read it: a walkthrough an analyst would actually run

Everything above is the narrative. This section is the procedure — how an analyst sitting at a screen would reconstruct a flow like this one, step by step, with the tools the series has introduced. You can run this method on any flagged cluster; the Bitfinex trail is just the largest example of it. Treat each step as a question you ask the chain, and the answer narrows the search.

**Step 1 — Anchor on the source.** Start from the labeled thief cluster. In a tool like a block explorer or a graph-based analytics platform, you load the cluster's known addresses and pull their transaction history. The goal of this step is not to follow anything yet; it is to establish the *total bag* — how many coins entered the cluster, when, and how they consolidated. This is your denominator. If you do not know how much you are chasing, you cannot tell later whether you have accounted for it.

**Step 2 — Watch for the waking event.** A flagged dormant cluster is a watch, not a chase, until it moves. Set an alert on the cluster's addresses. When any output spends, the Coin-Days-Destroyed metric on those coins spikes, and that is your trigger to go live. In this case the waking events came in waves over years; each wave reopened the trail with a new set of outputs to follow.

**Step 3 — Follow the change outputs.** Once coins move, the work is mechanical: at each transaction, identify which output is the payment and which is the change returning to the same owner, and follow the change. On Bitcoin, the change is usually the odd-valued output going to a fresh address, while the round-number output is the payment. Walking the change outputs is how you stay on the same owner through a peel chain of hundreds of hops. Analytics software automates this, but the logic is exactly the change-output heuristic from the foundations.

**Step 4 — Recognize the structure.** As you walk, the *shape* of the flow tells you what you are looking at. A long line of small-peel/large-forward transactions is a peel chain. A deposit into a service's pooled wallet followed by an unrelated-looking withdrawal is a service hop (exchange, darknet market, or mixer). A conversion into another asset on another chain is a chain-hop. You label each segment by its shape, because each shape calls for a different next move.

**Step 5 — Cross the boundaries.** At each service hop, you cannot follow outputs through the pool directly, so you switch techniques. For a mixer, you attempt demixing on timing and amount and keep a *candidate set* rather than a single link. For an exchange or darknet service, you note the deposit and withdrawal edges and flag the service for a possible records request or seizure. For a bridge or swap, you correlate the deposit on the source chain with the withdrawal on the destination chain by time and amount. The boundary is not the end of the trace; it is a place where you change tools.

**Step 6 — Cluster the destinations.** As the small peeled and withdrawn amounts pile up at exchanges, you cluster the deposit addresses. Shared funding, correlated timing, consistent sizes, and reused infrastructure collapse what looks like many independent depositors into one operation. This step is the inverse of step 1: at the source you established the bag; at the destination you re-assemble it from the scattered pieces.

**Step 7 — Attach identity at the off-ramp.** The clustered destination addresses are still pseudonymous until a labeled, regulated entity provides names. You package the on-chain evidence — the trace from the labeled theft cluster to the clustered deposit accounts — and present it to the exchange. With KYC records, the exchange attaches verified identities. This is the step that converts an on-chain graph into a real-world case.

**Step 8 — Corroborate with off-chain breaks.** Finally, you fold in evidence that the chain cannot give you: seized service databases that collapse a commingling boundary, key files that prove control, communications that establish intent. Each break attaches identity or control to a specific node on the graph you already built. The chain provides the complete map; the breaks provide the names and keys at the points that matter.

Run those eight steps and you have, in miniature, the entire Bitfinex investigation. The reason the method is robust is that it never depends on defeating obfuscation in real time. It depends on the permanent record (steps 1–4), on the fact that obfuscation services are themselves labelable, seizable entities (step 5), on the off-ramp being a chokepoint (steps 6–7), and on off-chain breaks attaching names (step 8). Take away any single obfuscation layer's secrecy and the method still closes. That is why a six-year-old trail with peel chains, a darknet hop, mixing, and chain-hops still ended in the largest seizure in DOJ history.

## The seizure: turning a trace into recovery

On February 8, 2022, the DOJ announced the result: the seizure of approximately **94,000 BTC** — about 80% of the originally stolen 120,000 — then valued at roughly **\$3.6 billion**, and the arrest of Ilya Lichtenstein and Heather Morgan in New York. It was, and as of this writing remains, the largest financial seizure in the Department's history.

The mechanics of *seizing* on-chain assets are worth making explicit, because they are different from seizing a bank account. There is no central administrator to call who can freeze a Bitcoin balance — the network has no off switch. To seize native Bitcoin, the government must obtain the **private keys** that control the addresses holding the coins, and then move the coins to government-controlled addresses. This is precisely why the cloud key-recovery break was so decisive: the file of addresses and keys did not just *prove* the case, it *enabled the recovery*. With lawful access to those keys, agents could sign transactions sending the stolen coins to seizure wallets, putting them permanently beyond the suspects' reach.

This is a key distinction from the kind of *freezing* that is possible with some assets and not others. Centrally issued tokens — notably stablecoins like USDT and USDC — have issuers who can blacklist an address and freeze its balance, which is why fast-moving investigations into stablecoin theft sometimes end in a freeze rather than a seizure. Native Bitcoin has no such issuer, so recovery requires the keys. (The differences between freezing, seizing, and the role of chain-analytics firms are the subject of the series' freezing, recovery, and chain-analytics material; the short version is that *what kind of asset the stolen value sits in* determines which recovery tool is even available.) For native BTC, key recovery is the whole game — and here, the suspects' own record-keeping handed it over.

It is worth dwelling on how unusual a near-total recovery is, because it is the exception that proves the rule about chokepoints. In most large crypto thefts — the Lazarus Group hacks of Ronin (\$625M in 2022) and Bybit (\$1.46B in 2025), for instance — the attackers move with operational discipline, cash out aggressively and early through services that do not cooperate, and convert into assets that are harder to freeze. In those cases recovery is partial at best, because the funds reach genuinely uncooperative off-ramps before investigators can act. The Bitfinex case recovered roughly 80% of the stolen coins for two reasons specific to this trail: the funds sat dormant for years instead of being cashed out fast, which kept them on-chain and seizable; and the suspects retained the keys in a recoverable form rather than handing the coins to services that would have dispersed them. Dormancy, which the launderers presumably saw as safety, was actually what kept the coins within reach. Coins that move fast through uncooperative off-ramps are harder to recover than coins that sit still — because sitting still keeps them on the permanent record, in addresses whose keys can still be obtained.

There is one more mechanical subtlety worth naming. Seizing the coins required moving them, and moving them is itself an on-chain event that the whole world watched. When the government swept roughly 94,000 BTC into seizure wallets, on-chain analysts saw an enormous, conspicuous movement of long-dormant coins — the very Coin-Days-Destroyed spike pattern from the dormancy section, except this time the mover was the government. The seizure was, fittingly, as public and permanent as the theft. The same property of the ledger that doomed the launderers — that nothing can move quietly or be undone — also made the recovery verifiable to anyone who cared to check.

#### Worked example: pricing the largest seizure in DOJ history

Put the seizure in dollar terms across a few reference prices. At the February 2022 valuation, the **94,000 BTC** were worth about **\$3.6B**, implying a price near `\$3,600,000,000 ÷ 94,000 ≈ \$38,000` per coin. The portion *not* recovered — roughly `120,000 − 94,000 = 26,000` coins — was, at that same price, about `26,000 × \$38,000 ≈ \$988,000,000`, nearly **\$1B** still outstanding, much of it already spent or cashed out over the years. And measured against the original crime, the recovery dwarfed the loss it reversed: a **\$72M** theft produced a **\$3.6B** seizure, a recovery worth roughly `\$3,600,000,000 ÷ \$72,000,000 = 50×` the dollar value of the original theft.

*The intuition: because the asset appreciated ~50× while the trail stayed intact, the government recovered far more dollar-value than was ever stolen — proof that on a permanent ledger, the passage of time can make a case bigger and more recoverable, not colder.* The seizure was historic in dollars precisely because the coins were historic in appreciation.

## Common misconceptions

The Bitfinex case is so widely cited that it has spawned its own folklore. Five corrections worth internalizing.

**"They got caught because they made a mistake."** It is comforting to believe the suspects only fell because of sloppy operational security — the cloud key file especially looks like an avoidable blunder. But the *trace* did not depend on that mistake. Clustering, labeling, and following the cash-outs had already drawn the map; the key file accelerated the *seizure* and strengthened the *proof*, but the on-chain analysis was grinding forward regardless. The deeper truth is that there is no clean way to launder 120,000 conspicuous coins on a permanent ledger; the "mistake" framing understates how structurally hard the launderers' task always was.

**"Mixing made the funds untraceable."** Mixing degrades a single-hop trace to a probability and genuinely raises the cost of that hop. It does not erase the record, and it does nothing to the certainty on either side of it. In this case, the mixed and hopped funds still had to converge on cash-outs that clustered and labeled cleanly. A mixer is a delay tax, not an invisibility cloak — and a funded investigation pays the tax.

**"The coins were anonymous."** They were *pseudonymous*. Every coin lived at an address — a number, not a name — but behavior, funding, timing, and especially interactions with KYC'd exchanges attach names over time. The whole point of this series is that pseudonymity is not anonymity, and a six-year, fully-recorded trail is the most vivid possible proof.

**"Crypto is mostly used for crime, and this proves it."** This case is a high-profile *exception*, not the rule. By Chainalysis estimates, illicit activity has been on the order of **0.1% to 0.6%** of all on-chain transaction volume in recent years — in 2024 around **0.14%**. The vast majority of on-chain flow is legitimate. The reason cases like Bitfinex are so visible is the same reason they get solved: the ledger is public, so the crime is loud and the trace is possible.

**"A bigger or faster mixer-and-bridge stack would have worked."** More obfuscation layers mean more hops, more conspicuous activity, and more boundaries — and every boundary (a darknet market, a swap service, an exchange) is a labelable entity that can be seized or subpoenaed. Stacking techniques multiplies the surface a launderer has to keep secret, while the investigator only needs *one* of those boundaries to break. Against a permanent ledger and a funded investigation, more obfuscation is more attack surface, not more safety.

## The playbook: what to do with it

This is a forensic case study, so the playbook is the analyst's and defender's checklist — how to read a flow like this when you encounter one, and what conclusions to draw. The signal, the read, the action, and the false-positive, for each stage.

- **A flagged cluster that has been dormant suddenly moves.** *Read:* a Coin-Days-Destroyed spike on tagged stolen funds is the starting gun — the launderer has finally had to touch the coins. *Action:* alert and begin live hop-by-hop tracing from the waking outputs; do not wait. *False-positive:* old coins moving can be an exchange re-organizing cold storage or a long-term holder, not always a launderer — confirm the cluster's prior labeling before treating the move as illicit.

- **A long sequence of transactions, each shaving a small amount off a large balance.** *Read:* a peel chain — follow the large change output at each hop to stay on the same owner. *Action:* let clustering software walk the change outputs and enumerate the small peeled cash-out legs; those are your candidate off-ramps. *False-positive:* legitimate wallets also produce change outputs constantly; what marks a peel chain is the deliberate, repeated small-peel/large-forward structure originating from a flagged cluster.

- **Many similar-sized deposits across many accounts at one or more exchanges.** *Read:* chunked cash-outs through fictitious accounts, sized to dodge thresholds — and a fan-out that clustering can collapse. *Action:* cluster the deposit addresses by common funding, timing, and behavior; then pursue exchange KYC cooperation to attach identities. *False-positive:* genuinely unrelated retail customers also make small deposits — the cluster is only real when shared funding *and* behavioral fingerprints *and* an upstream link to the flagged cluster all line up.

- **Funds enter a mixer, darknet service, or bridge and reappear later.** *Read:* an obfuscation boundary, not an endpoint — the deposit and withdrawal edges are still on-chain. *Action:* preserve both edges, attempt demixing on timing and amounts, and watch for a seizure of the service's records that collapses the boundary; pin the probabilistic link with labeled certainty downstream. *False-positive:* demixing yields probabilities, not proofs — never treat a single statistical mixer link as conclusive on its own; require corroboration on either side.

- **The trace reaches a regulated, KYC'd off-ramp.** *Read:* the chokepoint — this is where pseudonymity ends and identity begins. *Action:* this is the point to pursue cooperation, a subpoena, or a freeze (if the asset is a freezable stablecoin) or a seizure (if you can obtain keys for native coins). *False-positive:* an exchange deposit address is not proof the depositor *is* the thief — it is proof the *funds* arrived there; the identity behind the account, via KYC, is the link that matters.

The meta-lesson, true of this case and of tracing in general: **the off-ramp is the chokepoint, the ledger is permanent, and obfuscation buys delay, not erasure.** Coins on a public chain are only valuable when they re-enter the regulated world to become spendable, and that re-entry is exactly where they are most exposed. A funded, patient investigation does not need to defeat every obfuscation layer in real time; it needs to stay on the permanent record, wait at the chokepoints, and let off-chain breaks attach the names. The Bitfinex trail took six years, and at the end the same coins that were stolen as a **\$72M** problem were recovered as a **\$3.6B** headline — the clearest demonstration in crypto's history that permanence beats patience.
