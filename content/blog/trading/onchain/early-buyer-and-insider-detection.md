> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Before a listing, a pump, or an announcement, some wallets are already positioned; on a public ledger you can find them by tracing who funded them, when they bought, and how they link back to the team — and use that to protect yourself.
>
> - **The signal**: a token's earliest buyers, the source that funded their gas and first buy, and the timing of their entry relative to a public catalyst. Insiders and team-linked wallets leave a funding fingerprint a crowd does not.
> - **How to read it**: funding-source tracing (every wallet's first inbound transfer points somewhere), first-buyer and sniper detection at launch, pre-listing accumulation, and the deployer's funding graph — on Etherscan, Arkham, Nansen, DeBank, Dune, Solscan, and Bubblemaps.
> - **What you DO with it**: this is a *defense* tool. Heavy insider concentration means the deck is stacked and you would be exit liquidity, so you **avoid**. Genuinely independent informed money is a reason to **investigate**. It is never a license to trade on someone else's non-public information.
> - **The rule of thumb**: if a token's float is quietly held by wallets that all trace to one funder or to the team, the price you see is not the price you can exit at.

In early 2024 a wave of tokens debuted on major exchanges with the same uncomfortable signature. Days *before* the listing was public, a small set of fresh wallets — created the same week, holding nothing but the soon-to-be-listed token — had been buying steadily on a thin decentralized market. Then the listing dropped, the price gapped up, and those wallets sold into the first hour of retail demand. None of it was hidden. Every buy, every funding transfer, every sale sat on a public ledger that anyone with a block explorer could read. The only thing separating the people who got dumped on from the people doing the dumping was that one group knew how to read the chain and the other did not.

That is the entire premise of this post. On a public blockchain, *positioning is visible*. A wallet that buys before news cannot un-buy it; the transaction is permanent and timestamped. So the question "who knew first?" — which in traditional markets requires a subpoena — is, on-chain, a tracing exercise you can run yourself in an afternoon. This is a powerful and a dangerous idea, so let us be precise about the framing before we touch a single tool.

This is a **defensive** skill. We are learning to *detect* insiders so we are not their exit liquidity — not to *become* one. Trading on material non-public information is illegal in most jurisdictions and unethical everywhere; this post will never tell you how to obtain or act on a tip. What it will tell you is how to recognize, from the public record, when a token is rigged so heavily that buying it means handing money to people who already won. Detecting the stacked deck is how you avoid the table.

## Foundations: what "insider" means on-chain, and the four primitives

Before the tooling, four ideas have to be solid. Everything later is a combination of these.

**What an "insider" is, on a blockchain.** In equities, an insider is a defined legal category — an officer, director, or large shareholder with a fiduciary duty. On-chain, the word is looser and more behavioral. An on-chain insider is **a wallet acting on information or access the public does not have**: the team that will mint the token, a friend tipped off about a pending exchange listing, a market maker contracted to support a launch, or a bot operator who saw the liquidity-add transaction in the mempool a half-second before everyone else. These are very different actors with very different ethics — the team and the tipped friend may be committing a crime; the public bot is just fast. Detection lumps them together at first (they all "knew or saw first"), and the analyst's job is to *separate* them. The whole back half of this post is about that separation, because misreading a fast-but-legitimate market maker as a criminal insider is its own expensive mistake.

**Funding-source analysis: every wallet was funded by something.** A blockchain address starts life empty. To do anything — buy a token, even pay the gas fee for a transaction — it needs to receive value from somewhere. That *first inbound transfer* is the wallet's origin story, and it is permanently recorded. Trace it and you learn who paid to bring this wallet to life. Maybe it was a centralized exchange withdrawal (a partial dead-end — exchanges pool users). Maybe it was another wallet, which itself was funded by another, and so on back up a chain. The key insight: **a crowd of independent buyers has many independent funding sources; a coordinated group shares one.** When twelve wallets that all bought the same obscure token were each funded by the same address in the same hour, that is not twelve people who independently discovered a gem. That is one actor wearing twelve masks.

**The first-buyer set of a token.** When a token launches, there is a precise, ordered list of who bought it first — block by block, transaction by transaction. The blockchain *is* that list. The first ten buyers of a token are a tiny, scrutinizable set, and at launch the very earliest of them are almost never ordinary humans. Liquidity is added in one block; the next block already contains buys. No person sees a launch and reacts in twelve seconds. Those block-0 and block-1 buyers are bots, insiders who were told the exact launch time, or the team itself seeding "organic" demand. Knowing the first-buyer set, and what each of them did next, tells you who really owns a new token versus who is being sold a story.

**Fresh wallets.** A "fresh" wallet is newly created, has a short history, and often does exactly one thing — hold or trade one token. Fresh wallets are not inherently suspicious; everyone's wallet was new once. But a *cluster* of fresh wallets, created in a narrow window, all funded by one source, all buying one token, is a textbook coordination signature. The freshness strips away the camouflage of history: a wallet that has been trading for three years has plausible deniability for any single buy; a wallet born on Tuesday that bought an unlisted token on Wednesday and sold into its Friday listing has none.

**The timing fingerprint.** Informed buying clusters *before* a catalyst — that is the entire point of being informed. The fingerprint is the gap between the buy and the news: a position opened on a flat, low-volume chart three days before an announcement, then closed into the announcement's pump. Random luck does not cluster this tightly; a hundred lucky buyers are spread across weeks and price levels, while a tipped buyer is concentrated in the days before. Timing is the most legally loaded signal — "bought right before the news" is the literal definition of insider trading in equities — and on-chain it is *measurable to the second*.

With those four primitives — funding source, first-buyer set, fresh wallets, timing — we can build every detection technique in this post. The rest is learning to read them with real tools and, crucially, to second-guess them, because each one has an innocent explanation that the careful analyst must rule out before crying insider.

A fifth idea ties them together and is worth stating on its own: **pseudonymity is not anonymity.** A wallet address is not a name, but it is also not a mask that holds up under scrutiny. The moment a wallet receives its first funds, transacts with a counterparty, reuses a gas-funding source, or follows a recognizable timing rhythm, it leaks information that the rest of the chain can correlate. Over enough transactions, an address accretes a behavioral signature as distinctive as handwriting. The insider's problem is that they have to *do something* on-chain to profit — buy before the news, sell into it — and every one of those actions is permanent and public. They can create a fresh wallet to break the link to their identity, but the fresh wallet still has to be *funded*, and funding is itself a link. There is no move an insider can make that does not leave a trace; there is only how many hops of obfuscation they bother to add, and how patient the analyst is in unwinding them. This is the structural reason the whole detection game is even possible: the profit motive forces the insider onto the public record.

It is worth grounding why the chain you are on changes the work. On **Ethereum and EVM L2s** (Arbitrum, Base, Optimism, BSC, Polygon), the account model means each wallet has a single running balance and a readable transaction list, so funding-source tracing is a clean walk up a chain of `from` addresses, and the rich tooling (Etherscan, Arkham, Nansen, DeBank, Dune, Bubblemaps) is built here first. On **Solana**, where most modern memecoin launches happen, blocks are sub-second and a launch can be sniped within a slot or two; the account model is different in detail but funding-source logic still applies, and Solscan plus the Pump.fun-era dashboards expose the first-buyer set. On **Bitcoin**, the UTXO model (coins are discrete unspent outputs, not a running balance) makes "funding source" a question about which prior outputs fed a transaction — relevant for tracing but rarely the venue for token-insider games, which need programmable tokens. The techniques transpose across all of them; only the explorer and the granularity change. When this post says "open the wallet's oldest transaction," read that as Etherscan on EVM, Solscan on Solana, and a UTXO-graph tool on Bitcoin.

One more foundational distinction prevents a category error throughout: a **wallet** (an EOA, controlled by a private key) versus a **smart contract** (code at an address, like the token itself, a liquidity pool, or a multisig). Insiders act through EOAs — those are the wallets you trace and cluster. Contracts are the *venues* (the pool they buy in, the token they buy, the multisig that funds them). When you trace funding, you are following EOA-to-EOA transfers and EOA-to-contract interactions; do not mistake the router contract or the LP contract that every buyer touches for a shared *funder*. Everyone who buys on Uniswap interacts with the Uniswap router — that is not coordination, it is just how the venue works. The shared *funder* that matters is a private EOA upstream of the buys, not the public infrastructure they all necessarily pass through.

## Funding-source tracing: many buyers, one funder

Start with the single most powerful technique, because it underlies all the others. **Funding-source tracing** asks one question of a wallet: *where did its money first come from?* Then it asks that question of a whole set of wallets at once and looks for a shared answer.

Here is the mechanism. On an account-based chain like Ethereum, every externally-owned account (EOA — a wallet controlled by a private key, as opposed to a smart contract) has a transaction history you can read on Etherscan. Scroll to the very bottom — the oldest transactions — and you find the wallet's first inbound transfer: the moment someone sent it its first ETH or tokens. That sender is the funding source. On Etherscan you can literally click "First" on the transaction list, or use the address's "Funded By" line, which many explorers surface directly. Arkham and Nansen automate this across many wallets and draw the funding tree for you; Bubblemaps visualizes the resulting clusters as connected bubbles.

Now apply it to a group. Take the top twenty buyers of a suspicious token. Pull each one's first inbound transfer. If they scatter — twenty different sources, mixed exchanges, no overlap — you are probably looking at a real crowd. But if you keep landing on the *same* address, or the same batch of withdrawals from one exchange account, the buyers are not independent. They are spokes on one wheel.

![Funding-source tracing graph showing twelve fresh buyers all funded by one source address then buying one pool](/imgs/blogs/early-buyer-and-insider-detection-2.png)

The figure above shows the canonical shape: one funder fans out value to many fresh wallets, each of which then buys into the same liquidity pool inside a tight window. Reverse the arrows in your head and you see why it is so damning — the buys *converge* on one pool from wallets that *diverge* from one funder. That hourglass, narrow at both the funding source and the target pool, is what coordination looks like on a ledger.

#### Worked example: twelve fresh wallets, one funder, \$60,000 of coordinated buying

Say you are eyeing a small token that is trending. You pull its top buyers and notice twelve wallets, all created within the same 48-hour window, each holding only this token. You trace each wallet's first inbound transfer. All twelve received roughly \$5,000 in stablecoins from one address, `0xF1a2…`, over a two-hour span. Twelve wallets times \$5,000 each is \$60,000 of buying that *looks* like twelve independent believers but is one actor's coordinated position. The funder funded the gas, the funder funded the buy, and the funder will decide when all twelve sell — probably at once, probably into you. The lesson: when one funding source explains a dozen "independent" buys, treat the token's order book as a single hand of cards held by one player who can fold it on you instantly.

There is a deeper structural reason this technique works as well as it does, and it is worth internalizing because it tells you *when* it will fail. A coordinated actor faces an operational constraint: they need many wallets, and each wallet needs gas and buying capital. Distributing that capital is itself a transaction graph, and the cheapest, fastest way to do it is to send from one source — the actor's own consolidated wallet or one exchange account — out to the workers. Building a *clean* obfuscation (funding each worker from a different source, through different intermediaries, at different times) is expensive in time, gas, and operational discipline, and most coordinators do not bother because they do not expect anyone to look. The few who do bother leave a different but still detectable trace: a "peel chain" or a fan-out through several intermediary hops, where one wallet funds three, each of those funds three more, and the branching tree still converges if you walk far enough up. Tornado Cash and other mixers exist precisely to *cut* this funding link by breaking the deterministic path between deposit and withdrawal — and the use of a mixer in a funding trail is itself a loud signal, because legitimate early buyers of a normal token rarely route their buying capital through a sanctioned mixer. When the funding trail goes cold at a mixer, you have not lost the thread; you have found a very large red flag.

The mechanics of the walk are mundane and that is the point — anyone can do it. On Etherscan, the address page's transaction list sorted oldest-first gives you the first inbound; the "Funded By" annotation, where present, names it directly. To go up a hop, click into that funder and repeat. Arkham's entity graph and Nansen's "money flow" view automate the multi-hop walk and overlay labels, so a funder that is actually a known exchange hot wallet or a labeled fund shows up tagged rather than as a bare hex string. The discipline is to walk *until the trail diverges or hits a labeled service*, not to stop at the first hop. A single hop of "funded from Binance" tells you almost nothing; three hops that all reconverge on one private wallet tells you almost everything. The analyst's edge is patience: coordinators count on you stopping at hop one.

#### Worked example: the cost of a one-hop versus a three-hop trace

Suppose you trace a token's eight largest early buyers and stop at the first hop. Six are "funded from Binance," two from "Coinbase" — looks like a normal crowd, and a lazy analyst clears it. Now walk up two more hops on the six Binance-funded wallets: all six withdrawals trace to deposits made by a *single* wallet, `0xC0de…`, which had earlier consolidated funds from a private-sale contract. The "crowd" was one entity routing through an exchange to launder the funding link. The dollar stakes make the patience worth it: if those six wallets hold a combined \$240,000 of a token you were about to buy \$20,000 of, the one-hop trace would have had you buying into a position that one person can dump on you the moment it suits them. Three extra hops of clicking — maybe twenty minutes — separated "diversified holder base" from "one hand holding the float." The lesson: the funding trace is only as good as the number of hops you are willing to walk, and the coordinator is betting you walk exactly one.

A subtlety that separates careful analysts from dashboard-readers: an exchange withdrawal is a *weak* funding link, not a strong one. If your twelve wallets were each funded by a withdrawal from Binance, that does *not* prove one person — Binance has hundreds of millions of users, and "funded from Binance" is the on-chain equivalent of "lives on Earth." The strong signal is a withdrawal *batch*: twelve withdrawals of an identical, oddly specific amount, within minutes of each other, to twelve fresh wallets that then act in unison. That pattern survives the "it's just an exchange" objection because the *coincidence* of timing and amount is the tell, not the exchange itself. Always ask whether your shared funder is a genuine private wallet (strong) or a pooled service like an exchange or a bridge (weak unless the batch timing is suspicious).

## First-buyer and sniper detection at launch

The second technique zooms into the most information-rich moment in any token's life: its first two blocks. When a project adds liquidity and opens trading, the chain records the *exact order* in which buys arrived. That order is a leaderboard of who was ready, and at the very top of it, ordinary humans simply do not appear.

Define the terms. A **sniper** is a bot that watches the mempool — the waiting room of pending, unconfirmed transactions — for a liquidity-add transaction, and the instant it sees one, fires its own buy to land in the same block or the next. Snipers are an arms race of latency and gas bidding; the best ones effectively buy in the same block liquidity goes live. A **block** is one batch of transactions added to the chain (roughly every twelve seconds on Ethereum, sub-second on Solana). "Block 0" loosely means the launch block; "block 1" the next. The point is that *block-1 buyers cannot be reacting to a human-visible signal* — there isn't one yet. They either run a sniper bot or were told the launch time in advance. Both mean the supply was being captured before any normal buyer could touch it.

![Token launch timeline showing block 0-1 snipers grabbing supply before humans then retail buying late](/imgs/blogs/early-buyer-and-insider-detection-3.png)

The timeline above traces the sequence. Liquidity goes live; block 1 is already a feeding frenzy of bots and tipped insiders; the next minutes are more bots; and only *minutes later* do real humans show up — to buy the very bag the snipers are preparing to sell. By the time a launch is visible on a screen, the float distribution is already decided, and it was decided against the latecomer.

#### Worked example: a sniper cluster grabbing 15% of supply in block 1

A new token launches with a one-billion total supply and a small liquidity pool. You read the first-block trades and find that a cluster of related wallets — fresh, similar funding, near-identical gas settings — bought 150 million tokens, fifteen percent of the entire supply, in block 1 alone, at the launch price. The pool was thin, so they paid roughly \$1.5M to take that 15% before block 2 existed. Three hours later the token has pumped on retail FOMO, and that 15% block is now nominally worth far more — but here is the trap for *you*: the snipers' \$1.5M is a position they intend to sell into the very rally that drew you in. When the earliest, fastest 15% of a token is held by one coordinated cluster, the "circulating supply" you are buying into has a built-in seller sitting on top of it. The honest read is not "this is mooning"; it is "I am the demand that lets the snipers exit."

Worth pausing on the legitimacy spectrum here, because "sniper" spans a wide ethical range and the distinctions change your read. At the clean end is a *public* sniper bot: anyone can rent or run one, it watches the public mempool, and it competes purely on latency and gas-bidding. That is closer to high-frequency trading than to insider dealing — unfair to slow humans, certainly, but not built on private information. In the middle is a sniper that bought because the operator was *told the exact launch time* by the team — now the speed is not the edge, the tip is, and that shades toward insider behavior. At the dark end is the team *itself* sniping its own launch through fresh wallets to manufacture the appearance of organic demand while quietly capturing supply to dump later — that is closer to fraud. From the outside, in block 1, these can look identical: a fast buy by a fresh wallet. You separate them with the *funding* and *what-next* signals. A public bot is usually funded from a long-lived bot-operator wallet that snipes *many* launches and holds briefly; a tipped insider or self-sniping team is funded from a source that links to *this* token's deployer or to a tight single-token cluster. The action differs too: a defensible public bot is still a reason for caution (it captured supply), but a team self-sniping its own launch is a reason to leave entirely.

The mempool is the hinge that makes all of this possible, so define it cleanly: the mempool is the public waiting room of transactions that have been broadcast but not yet included in a block. A liquidity-add transaction sits there, visible, for the seconds before a validator includes it — and that visibility window is what snipers race in. This is the same mempool that gives rise to MEV (maximal extractable value): the profit a block producer or a sophisticated searcher can extract by choosing the order of transactions. Sniping is a flavor of MEV — the searcher reorders the world so their buy lands first. The defensive consequence for you is blunt: by the time a launch is visible on a price chart, the mempool race is long over and the float is already distributed against you. There is no version of "I'll just be fast" that beats a bot with co-located infrastructure and a gas budget. Your edge is not speed; it is the *decision not to play the launch at all* once you read that the early supply is captured.

Solscan, Etherscan, and Dune all let you reconstruct the first-buyer set. On Solana, tools like the Pump.fun ecosystem dashboards and bubble-map visualizers make the block-0 cluster jump out. The defensive habit is simple: **before buying any fresh launch, read the first ten to twenty buyers.** If they are a tight cluster of fresh, similarly-funded wallets that already own a large share, the launch is captured and you are late by design. If the early buyers are genuinely diverse — different ages, different funding, different sizes — the launch is at least not obviously rigged, though that is necessary, not sufficient.

## Pre-listing accumulation: the classic insider tell

Now the technique that comes closest to the legal definition of insider trading, and therefore the one to handle most carefully. **Pre-listing accumulation** is buying a token in the days before a major centralized-exchange listing is publicly announced. A listing on Binance, Coinbase, or another top venue is a massive, predictable demand shock — it exposes the token to millions of new buyers and is famous for gapping prices up. Anyone who *knows* a listing is coming, before the public does, has a near-risk-free trade: accumulate quietly, wait for the announcement, sell into the pump.

The fingerprint is unmistakable once you know it. On a flat, low-volume chart with no public catalyst, a wallet starts buying with conviction — steady, patient, not chasing anything, because there is nothing yet to chase. Then days later the listing is announced and the price gaps up, and the same wallet sells into the move. The buy *precedes the public information that justified it*. That is the on-chain shadow of a tip.

![Before and after comparison of a wallet accumulating a token quietly then a Binance listing multiplying the position](/imgs/blogs/early-buyer-and-insider-detection-4.png)

The before/after figure makes the asymmetry concrete. The "before" panel is boring on purpose — flat price, quiet volume, one wallet buying into the silence. The "after" panel is the payoff — the listing lands, the price gaps, the position multiplies, and the wallet exits. The boredom of the "before" is exactly what proves the buyer was not reacting to public excitement: there was none. They were reacting to something only they could see.

#### Worked example: an \$80,000 buy three days before a Binance listing, a 4× exit

A wallet you are watching buys \$80,000 of a mid-cap token over two days. The chart is flat, daily volume is thin, and there is no news, no influencer push, nothing public to explain the conviction. Three days later, Binance announces the token will list. The price gaps up and rallies; the wallet's \$80,000 position is now worth roughly \$320,000, a clean 4× — and the wallet sells most of it into the listing-day volume. The buy preceded the only event that could have justified it by three days, on no public signal. You cannot *prove* a tip from one wallet — but you can prove the *pattern*, and when you also find that the wallet's funding traces back toward the team or an exchange-adjacent address (next section), the innocent explanations evaporate. The defensive takeaway: if you discover this *after* the announcement, you are looking at a wallet that is about to sell — chasing the listing pump means buying its exit.

This pattern is not hypothetical, and the industry has reckoned with it publicly. The most cited episode is the 2022 admission and lawsuit around an exchange employee who, with associates, allegedly bought tokens ahead of the listings he knew were coming — the first widely-publicized crypto insider-trading case, built substantially on the on-chain trail of wallets that accumulated specific assets in the days before listing announcements and sold into the post-announcement pops. The legal theory was the same as in equities: trading on material non-public information. What made it tractable was the ledger. Investigators did not need to reconstruct phone calls to *start* — the wallets had already drawn the picture, accumulating right before non-public events with a regularity that luck cannot produce. Exchanges responded by tightening employee-trading policies and, in several cases, by running their own on-chain surveillance to catch the pattern before regulators do. The takeaway for a retail reader is not the courtroom outcome; it is that the *same trail the FBI followed is visible to you*, in advance, as a reason to avoid a token whose listing pump is already somebody's planned exit.

There is also a structural reason pre-listing accumulation is so detectable: a listing is a *coordinated* event. The exchange's listing team, the project's team, the market makers, and whoever each of them tells form a small circle that knows days in advance. The more people in that circle, the more likely one of them trades — and on-chain, even one defector's buying shows up as the anomalous pre-event accumulation on an otherwise dead chart. You do not need to catch the whole circle; you need to notice that *somebody* in it could not resist, and that their position is now sitting on top of the order book waiting for the pump you are tempted to chase.

The honest caveat, stated plainly because it matters: **not every pre-listing buyer is an insider.** Market makers are routinely contracted by projects and exchanges to provide liquidity around a listing, and they *legitimately* pre-position to do their job. A wallet accumulating before a listing might be a hired MM building inventory, not a criminal with a tip. The way you tell them apart is the funding and behavior: a contracted MM is usually a known, labeled, professional entity (Wintermute, GSR, and the like are tagged on Nansen and Arkham), trades on many tokens, and operates continuously. A one-token fresh wallet that bought once before the news and sold once after is not running a market-making book. The signal is the *combination* — fresh, single-token, funded from a suspicious source, perfectly timed — not the pre-positioning alone.

#### Worked example: a five-wallet accumulation syndicate, \$250,000 in, \$1M out

Stretch the single-wallet case into the more common syndicate. Five fresh wallets, each created within the same week, each funded with roughly \$50,000 in stablecoins from a common upstream address, begin buying a sleepy mid-cap over four flat days — \$250,000 of total accumulation on a chart with no news and daily volume so thin that their own buying barely moves it. On day five Binance announces the listing. The token gaps and runs; the syndicate's combined \$250,000 entry is now worth about \$1,000,000 at the post-announcement high, and the five wallets distribute into listing-day volume in a tight cluster of sells, walking away with roughly \$750,000 of profit that came directly out of the pockets of the retail buyers chasing the gap. The arithmetic that should stop you cold is the buy-side liquidity: those five wallets needed someone to sell \$1M worth of token into, and on a book that thin, *you and the rest of the FOMO crowd are that someone*. Five wallets, one funder, four quiet days, and a perfectly timed exit is not five lucky traders — it is one informed hand that pre-positioned \$250,000 against a non-public event and handed you the bill.

## Team and deployer-linked wallets: the deployer's funding graph

The fourth technique attacks the problem from the supply side. Forget the buyers for a moment and ask: *who controls the float the team claims is circulating?* The answer starts at the **contract deployer** — the address that created the token's smart contract. The deployer is the on-chain root of the entire project, and like every other wallet, it was funded by something and it pays out to somewhere. Walk its funding graph one or two hops and you frequently find the team's real wallet structure.

The pattern to look for is **one-hop links to a team multisig**. A multisig is a wallet that requires multiple signatures to move funds — projects use them to hold treasuries and "locked" supply. The deployer's gas was probably funded by the team's main wallet or multisig. That same multisig, or wallets one hop from it, often quietly hold large chunks of what the project's documentation calls "circulating supply." These are the friends-and-family wallets: addresses that received tokens from the team early, at no cost or a private-sale price, and that are not disclosed as team holdings.

![Team-linked wallet graph showing the deployer funded by a multisig that also funds wallets holding hidden float](/imgs/blogs/early-buyer-and-insider-detection-5.png)

The graph above shows the structure. The multisig funds the deployer's gas (the link that ties the team to the token's creation) and also funds wallets A, B, and C — one hop out — which between them hold several million dollars of supply that is, on paper, "circulating" and therefore counted in the market cap. It is not circulating in any meaningful sense; it is team-controlled float wearing different addresses. The market cap built on that float is a fiction, and you are the one being asked to value it at full price.

#### Worked example: team-linked wallets holding \$4M of "circulating" float

A token reports a \$20M circulating market cap and a healthy-looking holder count. You trace the deployer, find its gas was funded by a multisig, and then map every wallet that multisig funded one hop out. Three of them — fresh-ish, low-activity, never disclosed as team — hold a combined \$4M of the token at current prices. That is twenty percent of the entire reported market cap sitting in wallets the team controls but did not disclose. The "free float" you can actually trade against is not \$20M; it is \$16M minus whatever exchanges and LPs hold, and the \$4M is an overhang that can hit the market whenever the team chooses. The defensive read: real liquidity is far thinner than the market cap implies, the price is more manipulable than it looks, and a coordinated team sale would make you the exit. When team-linked wallets quietly hold a fifth of the float, you are not an investor in a \$20M project — you are exit liquidity for a controlled supply.

The reason this matters so much more than it first appears is the difference between **market cap** and **fully-diluted valuation (FDV)**, and how hidden float corrupts both. Market cap is price times *circulating* supply; FDV is price times *total* supply (including locked and unissued tokens). Projects love to quote a modest market cap while sitting on a vast unissued or team-held supply, so the "cap" you see understates the real overhang. When team-linked wallets quietly hold a chunk of what is *counted as circulating*, the deception is worse than a high FDV — it is a *misreported float*. The price discovery happening on the visible order book is occurring against a supply number that is simply false. You are valuing the token as if the float were diffuse and the cap honest, when in truth a single coordinated party can flip from holder to seller across a fifth of the "circulating" supply at a moment's notice. Thin real float plus concentrated control is the precondition for every manufactured pump and every catastrophic dump.

Vesting and disclosure are the line between governance and a trap. Many legitimate projects *do* allocate large shares to the team and investors — that is normal — but they put those allocations in **vesting contracts** (smart contracts that release tokens on a published schedule) and they *disclose* the allocation in their tokenomics. Disclosed-and-locked is fine; you can price the future supply. The trap is *hidden-and-liquid*: team-controlled tokens that are not in a vesting contract, not disclosed as team holdings, and free to sell today, parked in wallets one hop from the multisig to look like organic holders. The on-chain tell that separates the two is exactly whether the float-holding wallets are vesting contracts (with a readable unlock schedule) or plain EOAs (free to dump). When the "circulating" float is sitting in fresh EOAs linked to the deployer rather than in a disclosed vesting contract, the project has chosen camouflage over governance, and that choice is the signal.

#### Worked example: \$16M of "free float" that is really \$4M, priced as if it were honest

Return to the \$20M-cap token. Subtract the \$4M held by team-linked EOAs and you would think the tradeable float is \$16M — but now also subtract what is locked in disclosed vesting (say \$3M), what sits in the liquidity pool itself (\$2M), and what is held by long-term holders who will not sell near these prices (another \$5M). The float that is *actually* available to absorb a sale at today's price might be \$6M. So a team that decides to liquidate its \$4M of hidden EOA holdings is dumping an amount equal to roughly two-thirds of the real available float into the book in a window of its choosing. There is no price near \$20M-cap levels that survives that; the token would gap down brutally as the \$4M chases a \$6M float. You were never buying a \$20M project with deep liquidity — you were buying a thin book with a \$4M seller hiding behind it. Quantifying the real float in dollars, not trusting the reported cap, is what turns "the holder list looks fine" into "I would be exit liquidity for a hidden seller."

The tooling: Etherscan shows the deployer on any token's contract page ("Contract Creator"). Arkham and Nansen will draw the deployer's funding and payout graph and often label the multisig. Bubblemaps is purpose-built for this — it renders holders as bubbles with lines between wallets that have transacted, so a team-linked cluster shows up as a tight knot of connected bubbles separate from the diffuse retail crowd. The visual is so legible that "the Bubblemap looks like a spider's web" has become shorthand in due-diligence circles for "the supply is concentrated and connected."

## Timing analysis: the fingerprint of informed buying

Tie the techniques together with the dimension that makes them legally and practically meaningful: *when*. Funding source tells you *who*; first-buyer and deployer analysis tell you *how much* and *from where*; timing tells you whether the buying was *informed*. And informed buying has a shape.

The shape is concentration before a catalyst. Lucky or skilled buyers who genuinely found a token early are spread out — they accumulate over weeks, at many price levels, as their own research convinces them. Tipped buyers are concentrated — they buy in a tight window right before the event, because the tip has a date. So when you overlay the buy timestamps of a token's top early wallets against its catalyst (a listing, an announcement, a major partnership reveal), informed buying shows up as a *spike of accumulation in the days immediately prior*, then a *spike of selling on the event itself*. The cleaner that two-spike pattern, the harder it is to explain as coincidence.

![Grid of six timing-fingerprint signals that together indicate informed buying rather than luck](/imgs/blogs/early-buyer-and-insider-detection-6.png)

No single cell in the grid above is proof. Fresh wallets exist innocently; one funder might be a legitimate maker; large buys happen; pre-catalyst timing can be luck. The fingerprint is the *stack* — when fresh wallets, a shared funder, pre-catalyst timing, patient sizing, conviction on an illiquid token, and a clean sell-on-the-news all point the same direction at once, you are no longer looking at coincidence. You are looking at a signature. This is exactly how forensic on-chain analysts and exchanges' own surveillance teams flag suspected insider activity: not one smoking gun, but a convergence of weak signals that are individually deniable and jointly overwhelming.

#### Worked example: separating informed timing from luck with money on the line

Two wallets both bought a token that later 3×'d. Wallet A bought \$10,000 spread across six weeks at prices from \$0.04 to \$0.08, kept buying after the news, and is funded from a long-lived address with years of diverse activity — that is conviction, and probably real research; its average entry near \$0.06 grew to roughly \$0.18, a \$30,000 position from \$10,000. Wallet B bought \$10,000 in a single day at \$0.05, three days before a listing, from a fresh wallet funded by the same source as five others, and sold the entire \$30,000 position into the listing-day candle. Same 3× on paper, completely different stories. Wallet A is someone you might want to *follow*; Wallet B is someone you want to *avoid being downstream of*. The dollars are identical; the timing fingerprint is everything. When two buyers show the same return, the one whose entry clusters right before the catalyst and exits right into it is the one trading on something you do not have.

## How to read it: a walkthrough on three patterns

Theory is cheap. Here is the concrete pass an analyst makes, using public tools, on the three patterns that matter most. Use illustrative placeholder addresses throughout — never assume a wallet is a *specific* real person without the multi-signal confirmation described above, and never publish an accusation you cannot defend.

### Pattern 1 — tracing the funding source of a token's top early buyers

1. **Get the early-buyer set.** On Etherscan, open the token's contract page and the "DEX Trades" or "Token Transfers" tab, sorted oldest-first; or run a Dune query that returns the first N buyers of the pool by block. On Solana, use Solscan's token holders and earliest transfers. You want the first ten to twenty *buying* wallets, not the LP or the router.
2. **Pull each wallet's first inbound transfer.** For each buyer, open its address, go to the oldest transaction, and read the sender. That sender is the funding source. Record it.
3. **Look for convergence.** Lay the funding sources side by side. Do they scatter (many distinct, mostly exchanges) or converge (one private address, or one suspiciously tight exchange-withdrawal batch)? Convergence on a private wallet is the strong signal.
4. **Classify the funder.** Is `0xF1a2…` a labeled entity (exchange, bridge, known MM — weak link), or an unlabeled private wallet (strong link)? Arkham and Nansen labels help here; an unlabeled funder feeding many fresh single-token wallets is the high-confidence coordination tell.
5. **Check what the cluster does next.** Coordinated clusters tend to *sell* together too. If the same wallets that bought together later sent tokens to exchanges within the same window, you have the full round trip — coordinated accumulation and coordinated distribution.

### Pattern 2 — checking if pre-listing accumulators link to the team

1. **Identify the accumulation window.** Find wallets that bought meaningfully in the days before the catalyst, on a chart that was otherwise quiet. The "quiet before" is the part that makes timing meaningful.
2. **Trace each accumulator's funding back toward the project.** Walk the funding graph: who funded the accumulator, who funded *them*, and so on. You are looking for a path that lands on the deployer, the team multisig, or a wallet one hop from either.
3. **Cross-check against the deployer graph.** Independently map the deployer's funding and payouts (Pattern 3 below). If an accumulator and a deployer-linked wallet share a funder or transact with each other, the "lucky early buyer" is very likely team-adjacent.
4. **Rule out the market maker.** Before concluding "insider," check whether the accumulator is a labeled professional MM, trades many tokens, and operates continuously. A hired MM pre-positioning is legitimate; a fresh single-token wallet is not an MM.
5. **Hold the conclusion loosely.** Even a clean trace is *correlation*, not courtroom proof. The defensible statement is "this token shows team-linked accumulation before its listing, so the float is concentrated and the listing pump has built-in sellers" — a reason to avoid, not a public accusation.

### Pattern 3 — spotting a coordinated fresh-wallet cluster

1. **Open the holder map on Bubblemaps** (or build the holder graph on Dune). Connected bubbles — wallets that have transacted with each other — cluster visually; a coordinated group is a tight, interlinked knot distinct from the diffuse retail spread.
2. **Check wallet ages and histories.** Fresh wallets, created in a narrow window, each holding essentially one token, are the camouflage-free coordination signature.
3. **Confirm the shared funder.** Trace the cluster's funding (Pattern 1). One funder behind a fresh cluster upgrades "suspicious" to "coordinated."
4. **Measure the captured share.** Sum the cluster's holdings as a percentage of supply and as dollars. A cluster holding 15–40% of supply *is* the float — the rest of the order book is noise around their decisions.
5. **Decide.** Heavy, funded, fresh, captured = avoid (you are exit liquidity). Diffuse, aged, independently funded = at least not obviously rigged, worth deeper investigation.

This three-pattern pass is the "user manual." None of it requires private data or special access; it requires a block explorer, one or two of the labeling tools, patience, and the discipline to demand a *stack* of signals before believing the worst.

## Common misconceptions

**"Early buyers are just smart or lucky, so this is all noise."** Some are. Survivorship bias is real and savage: you only notice the early wallets of tokens that *worked*, so the ones you see look prescient even when they were guessing. The correction is the funding-and-timing stack. A genuinely lucky early buyer has diverse funding, an aged wallet, accumulation spread over time, and no link to the team. A wallet that is fresh, single-token, funded with eleven siblings, bought three days before a non-public listing, and sold into it is not "lucky" — luck does not cluster that precisely against private events. The signal is in the *convergence*, and convergence is what luck cannot fake.

**"On-chain proves who the insider is."** It proves *patterns*, not identities, and not intent. An address is pseudonymous; tying it to a named human requires off-chain information (an exchange KYC leak, a doxxing, a public claim) that you usually do not have and should not fabricate. The chain can show you that a wallet accumulated before news and links to the team's funding graph — strong grounds to *avoid the token* — but "Wallet `0xA11ce…` is the CTO's cousin" is a claim you cannot support from on-chain data alone. Detect patterns; do not publish accusations.

**"Market makers pre-positioning before a listing is insider trading."** No. Market makers are frequently *contracted* by the project or exchange to provide liquidity, and pre-positioning inventory is their legitimate job, disclosed in the arrangement. They are usually labeled, professional, multi-token, continuous operators. Mistaking a hired MM's pre-listing inventory for a criminal tip is a false positive that will make you dismiss perfectly normal liquidity provision — and, worse, make you over-trust your detection when it does fire on something genuinely fresh and single-token. Always carve out the MM hypothesis before concluding insider.

**"If I can detect insider buying, I should front-run it and buy too."** This is the dangerous misread, and it is wrong on two counts. First, by the time *you* detect pre-listing accumulation, the listing is often already announced — you are not front-running insiders, you are buying their exit. Second, even when you spot it early, *acting on someone else's non-public information is itself insider trading.* The signal's value is defensive: it tells you the deck is stacked so you stay out. It is not a tip you get to launder through your own keyboard.

**"A clean-looking holder list means no insiders."** A holder list can be made to look diffuse on purpose — split one entity's stake across fifty fresh wallets and a naive holder count reads as "decentralized." The defense against the cosmetic spread is the *connection* graph (Bubblemaps) and the *funding* graph: fifty wallets that all trace to one funder are one holder, no matter how the holder count displays. Count entities, not addresses.

**"A funding trail that ends at a centralized exchange means the trace failed."** It usually means the opposite — you found something, you just cannot follow it further without the exchange's internal records. When a fresh wallet's first inbound transfer is a withdrawal from Binance, the on-chain trail does go dark there because exchanges pool deposits and withdrawals across all users. But the analytically useful fact survives: if twelve wallets each received an oddly specific \$5,000 withdrawal within a fifteen-minute window from the same exchange, the *batch pattern* still screams coordination even though the individual identities are sealed behind the exchange. The trace did not fail; it handed you a strong probabilistic signal and a clear pointer for where a subpoena would go next. Treat "trail ends at a CEX with suspicious batch timing" as a yellow-to-red flag, not a dead end.

**"If the team allocation is huge, the token is automatically a scam."** A large team or investor allocation is normal and, by itself, says nothing — many legitimate projects allocate 20–40% to the team and backers. The variable that matters is not the *size* of the allocation but its *form*: is it sitting in a disclosed vesting contract with a published, multi-year unlock schedule that you can read on-chain, or is it parked in plain EOAs that can sell today? A 30% team allocation locked in a vesting contract releasing over four years is a known, priceable overhang — you can model exactly how much new supply hits the market each month. The same 30% sitting in fresh wallets one hop from the deployer is a hidden, instantly-liquid threat that can flip from "holder" to "seller" in a single block. Judge the lock and the disclosure, not the headline percentage.

## The playbook: what to do with it

Insider detection is a filter on your buy decisions and a sharpener on your investigation. Here is the if-then.

**Signal: a fresh-wallet cluster with one funder holds a large share.**
*Read:* coordinated buying — insiders, a launch syndicate, or a sybil. *Action:* avoid, or size so small it is a pure lottery ticket you can lose entirely. *Invalidation / false positive:* a labeled market maker legitimately seeding many maker wallets — check for professional labels and multi-token, continuous activity before crying foul.

**Signal: pre-listing accumulation that traces toward the team or deployer.**
*Read:* the price is being driven by privileged information; the pump has built-in sellers. *Action:* do not chase the listing — you are late, and the accumulators will sell into your buy. *Invalidation:* a contracted MM pre-positioning for a listing it was hired to support; or accumulation that is genuinely diffuse and independently funded.

**Signal: block-0/block-1 snipers hold most of the early supply.**
*Read:* the float was captured before any human could react; you would be buying the snipers' exit. *Action:* skip the fresh launch; if you must touch it, wait until the snipers visibly distribute and the float redistributes. *Invalidation:* fast *public* bots that any user could run — captured-by-bots is still a reason to be careful, but it is not the same as a privileged insider, and it sometimes redistributes.

**Signal: team-linked wallets quietly hold a large slice of "circulating" float.**
*Read:* the real free float is far smaller than the market cap implies; the price is thin and manipulable, and a team sale would crush it. *Action:* treat the market cap as fictional, demand a much larger margin of safety, or pass. *Invalidation:* disclosed, time-locked team allocations that the project is transparent about — locked-and-disclosed is governance; hidden-and-spread is a trap.

**Signal: many early buyers with genuinely independent funding and diffuse timing.**
*Read:* possibly real informed demand — separate people who found it first. *Action:* this is the one case to *investigate further* rather than avoid; cross-reference with smart-money tracking before acting on your own thesis. *Invalidation:* survivorship bias — confirm the buyers were early *and* independent *and* not team-linked before reading it as a positive.

It is about recognizing, from the public record alone, that you have been invited to a table where the deck was dealt before you sat down. The single most profitable thing on-chain analysis ever does for a retail investor is let you stand up and walk away from that table. The hand you do not play is the one you cannot lose.
