> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Tracing stolen funds is a **repeatable seven-step workflow**, not a magic trick: identify the attacker address from the exploit transaction, map the drain, follow the money hop by hop through swaps, handle mixers and bridges, cluster and label the destination wallets, watch for the off-ramp, and document the case for an exchange or law enforcement.
>
> - **What it is:** a defined investigative process that takes you from a single flagged transaction to a packet of evidence someone with power — an exchange compliance team, a court — can act on.
> - **How to read it / the tools:** Tenderly or Etherscan to decode the exploit tx; Arkham, MetaSleuth, or Breadcrumbs to draw the flow graph and set alerts; Chainalysis Reactor or TRM for institutions that need attribution-grade evidence and direct exchange channels.
> - **What you DO with it:** **anyone can trace; only an exchange or a court can freeze.** Your job as an analyst is to follow the money to a KYC off-ramp and hand over a clean, reproducible case file fast enough to catch the funds before they are withdrawn.
> - **The one rule to remember:** the recoverable break is the **off-ramp** — the moment stolen value touches an exchange that knows its customer. Everything else in the workflow exists to get you there with proof in hand.

On 23 March 2022, attackers tied to North Korea's Lazarus Group compromised the validator keys behind the **Ronin bridge** and drained roughly **\$625 million** of ETH and USDC in two transactions. The funds landed in a single fresh address. Then they sat — quietly, for days — before the laundering began: a slow march through swaps, through the Tornado Cash mixer, and toward exchange deposit addresses scattered across the world. The entire time, every hop was public. Investigators at Chainalysis, Elliptic, and the FBI did not need a warrant to *watch* the money move. They needed one only to *stop* it. Months later, the US Treasury attributed the theft to Lazarus, sanctioned the deposit addresses, and a coordinated effort eventually clawed back a fraction of the haul from exchange accounts the funds had touched.

That gap — between *watching* the money and *stopping* it — is the entire subject of this post. On a public ledger the getaway is filmed in 4K from every angle, forever, but a film of a robbery does not put the money back. Someone has to turn those raw transactions into a map, follow the map to the one place the thief has to interact with the regulated world, and hand a named human the evidence to act before the window closes. That someone is the on-chain investigator, and the work they do is not improvised. It is a **workflow** — the same seven steps, in the same order, on every case, from a \$70,000 wallet drain to a \$1.46 billion exchange hack.

This is the "how to use it" guide for forensics. By the end you will be able to take a single exploit transaction and run the full investigation: decode it to find the attacker, measure what was stolen, follow the value through swaps and across chains, group the destination wallets and put names on the services they touch, arm alerts so the off-ramp pages you the instant it fires, and assemble a case file an exchange can act on. We use **illustrative placeholder wallets** (`0xBAD1…`, "Wallet A", "the CEX") for invented hops, and pull **real magnitudes only from documented hacks** — never a real-looking address dressed up as a specific one.

## Foundations: the forensic goal, the starting point, and who can actually freeze

Before any step, get the goal straight, because it shapes every decision you make on the trail. The forensic goal is three verbs: **attribute, follow, and freeze (or recover).** Attribute means tie the stolen funds to a real-world actor or at least a named entity. Follow means trace the value from where it left the victim to where it tries to leave the crypto economy. Freeze or recover means get a party with power — an exchange, a court — to actually stop or return the money. As a public analyst you can do the first two completely on your own. The third you can only *enable*; you cannot do it yourself. Internalizing that division of labor is the difference between productive forensics and frustrated hand-waving.

A few terms, defined from zero, that the workflow leans on constantly:

- An **address** (or wallet) is a pseudonymous account identifier — a string like `0xBAD1…` on Ethereum or a `T…` string on Tron. It is **not a name**: no passport is attached to it on the chain. But every transfer it ever makes is permanently linked to it, and that behavior slowly **de-anonymizes** it. Pseudonymous, not anonymous — that distinction is why tracing works at all.
- An **EOA** (externally owned account) is a normal user-controlled wallet, the kind a person or a script signs from with a private key. A **contract** is code that lives at an address and runs when called. An exploit is usually a contract being made to do something its authors did not intend; the loot ends up in an EOA the attacker controls.
- A **transaction (tx)** is one signed action recorded on the chain, identified forever by a **transaction hash** like `0x9f3c…`. The **exploit transaction** is the specific tx (or small set of txs) in which the theft happened. It is your **starting point** — the single most important input to the whole investigation.
- A **hop** is one transfer from one address to the next. "Three hops out" means the value has moved through three transfers since you started. Tracing is, very literally, counting hops while keeping the total value accounted for.
- **KYC** ("know your customer") is the identity check a regulated exchange runs before letting someone deposit or withdraw. An exchange that did KYC on its customers is the one place the pseudonymous trail can be tied to a legal name — which is exactly why the off-ramp is where recovery lives.

One more foundational idea you will hear used loosely and should pin down now: **case documentation.** A "case" in forensics is not a hunch or a screenshot; it is a structured, reproducible record of an investigation. Good case documentation has a property borrowed from physical-evidence handling called **chain of custody**: anyone who picks up your file should be able to retrace exactly how you got from the starting transaction to your conclusion, verify each step against the public ledger themselves, and arrive at the same answer. On-chain forensics has an enormous advantage here over traditional financial investigation — the underlying evidence is *public and immutable*, so "reproducible" is not a courtesy, it is the default. If you cite every hop by its transaction hash, a compliance analyst, a defense lawyer, or a skeptical journalist can independently confirm your trace by reading the same chain you did. That is why a case file built on hashes is *evidence* and a case file built on screenshots is merely an *assertion*: one can be re-derived from first principles by anyone, the other asks the reader to trust your annotations. Building documentation this way from the first hop — not reconstructing it at the end — is what separates a trace that gets acted on from one that gets ignored.

The single property that makes the whole workflow possible is **value conservation.** On a ledger, money does not evaporate between hops. If \$30M leaves the attacker's wallet, then \$30M (minus a tiny fee) arrives somewhere — at one address, or split across forty, or swapped into a different token, or locked in a bridge to reappear on another chain. But it *arrives*. The first habit of a forensic analyst is to refuse to believe money vanished. When a trail seems to die, you have not hit a dead end; you have **missed a hop** — almost always a swap that changed the asset or a bridge that changed the chain. This is the same backbone idea built up in [how to trace a transaction flow](/blog/trading/onchain/how-to-trace-a-transaction-flow); here we wrap a full incident-response process around it.

### Tracing is for everyone; freezing needs a gatekeeper

Here is the hard line that beginners trip over. **Anyone with a browser can trace.** The ledger is public; the tools are free or freemium; a curious teenager can follow \$625M across a hundred hops from their bedroom. But **tracing changes nothing on its own.** You cannot reach into the chain and grab the coins back — they are controlled by the thief's private key, and the chain does not care who *deserves* them. The only places stolen funds can be stopped are the **chokepoints where crypto meets the regulated world**: a centralized exchange that can freeze a deposit, or a stablecoin issuer (Tether, Circle) that can blacklist an address so its USDT or USDC becomes un-spendable. Both act only on a credible request — a compliance referral, a subpoena, a court order.

So the public analyst's product is not "the money, recovered." It is **a case file good enough that a gatekeeper acts on it before the window closes.** That reframes the whole job. You are not playing detective for sport; you are building admissible-grade evidence and racing the clock to a chokepoint. The line between tracing and freezing maps onto the line between the **public sleuth** (zachxbt-style independent investigators, who trace and publish) and the **institutional analyst** (Chainalysis, TRM, Elliptic, and the exchanges' own compliance teams, who trace *and* can pull the lever). The freezing and recovery mechanics — who can blacklist, what a subpoena gets, how chain-analytics firms package evidence — are covered in depth in [freezing, recovery, and chain analytics](/blog/trading/onchain/freezing-recovery-and-chain-analytics); this post gets you to the door with the evidence in hand.

#### Worked example: why "I traced it" is not "I recovered it"

Say a DeFi lending protocol is drained for **\$30M** in a flash-loan exploit. You, a hobbyist, decode the tx and follow the funds in an afternoon: \$30M into a fresh wallet, swapped to ETH, split across forty addresses. You now *know* exactly where every dollar is. What can you do? Directly: nothing. The \$30M is controlled by a key you do not have. But of that \$30M, suppose \$2M eventually lands at the deposit address of a KYC exchange. If — and only if — you get a clean case file to that exchange's compliance team *before* the attacker withdraws, they can freeze that \$2M and tie it to a verified identity. Trace the whole \$30M, recover (at most) the \$2M that touched a gatekeeper. The intuition: tracing is necessary but never sufficient — recovery is bounded by how much of the stolen value passes through a chokepoint while you still have a live referral.

## The starting point: the exploit transaction is the seed of everything

Every investigation begins with one fact you are handed: **something was stolen in this transaction.** Maybe the protocol's monitoring fired, maybe a user reported a drained wallet, maybe a security researcher saw an anomaly in the mempool. (How that first alarm gets raised in real time — the watchtowers and heuristics that catch a drain as it happens — is the subject of the sibling post on [detecting an exploit in progress](/blog/trading/onchain/detecting-an-exploit-in-progress).) Whatever the trigger, you end up with a transaction hash, and that hash is the **seed** of the entire flow graph you are about to grow.

Two modes shape how the rest goes. In **real-time tracing**, the theft just happened and the funds are still moving; speed is everything, because every hop that goes by before you have alerts armed is a hop closer to a cash-out you will miss. In **cold-case tracing**, the theft is weeks or months old; the funds may be parked, the attacker may have grown careless, and your edge is patience and the fact that the ledger never forgets — a wallet that sat dormant for a year and then suddenly deposits to an exchange is a gift. Most real investigations start hot and go cold: you sprint to set alerts in the first hours, then settle in to watch a dormant cluster for the deposit that eventually comes.

The biggest cases — the ones that make headlines and the ones the curated leaderboard below tracks — all start exactly this way, with one flagged transaction and a number attached to it.

![Horizontal bar chart of the biggest on-chain thefts by USD stolen, from Bybit at 1.46 billion down to Mixin at 200 million](/imgs/blogs/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step-8.png)

The chart above is where careers' worth of fund-tracing cases begin. **Bybit at \$1.46 billion** (February 2025, Lazarus) is the largest theft in the history of money; **Ronin at \$625 million** and **Poly Network at \$611 million** are not far behind. Some of these resolved well — Poly Network's grey-hat attacker returned the entire \$611M, and Euler's \$197M came back too — and some, like the Lazarus jobs, were mostly laundered before anyone could act. The difference, again and again, comes down to how fast a clean trace reached a gatekeeper. Now let us walk the workflow that produces that trace.

## Step 1 — IDENTIFY: from the exploit transaction to the attacker address

The first move is to turn the exploit tx into the **attacker address** — the wallet the stolen value flowed into. On a block explorer like Etherscan you can read the basics (sender, receiver, value, token transfers), but an exploit transaction is rarely simple. A flash-loan attack can call a dozen contracts in one tx; a bridge exploit can move funds through internal transfers an explorer's default view hides. The right tool here is a **transaction decoder / simulator** like **Tenderly**, which gives you the full **call trace** — every internal call, every token transfer, the exact revert path — so you can see precisely which contract was drained and where the loot ended up.

![Graph from the exploit transaction through a decoder to the victim contract, the method, and the attacker address](/imgs/blogs/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step-2.png)

Reading the decoded trace, you answer three questions. **What was drained?** The victim contract — a lending pool, an AMM, a bridge. **How?** The method — a flash-loan manipulation, a compromised key, a math overflow. You do not need to fully reverse-engineer the bug to trace the money, but knowing the method tells you whether the loot is one clean lump (a key compromise) or scattered across a complex call path (a multi-step DeFi exploit). **Where did the value go?** The destination EOA — the attacker address. That address is your **new root node**. From here the flow graph grows forward.

A subtlety that trips up beginners at this step: the loot's first destination is often a **contract the attacker deployed**, not the attacker's personal wallet. A sophisticated exploit frequently routes funds through an "attack contract" — a throwaway piece of code that executes the drain and forwards the proceeds. That contract is a real node in your graph, but it is not the *actor*; you follow the value one more hop to the EOA that deployed and controls it, because an EOA is what eventually signs the swaps and deposits you will trace later, and an EOA is what ties back to a fundable, attributable human. So "identify the attacker" really means: find the contract that received the drain, then find the externally owned account behind it. Tenderly's trace shows you both — the contract that the value hit, and the `from` address that deployed it — in the same view. Conflating the attack contract with the attacker is how investigators waste an hour chasing a piece of code that never moves again.

It is worth one backward glance too. The attacker had to pay gas to deploy contracts and bootstrap the wallet, and that gas often came from somewhere revealing — a previous hack's proceeds, a mixer, or (carelessly) a KYC exchange. Tracing the attacker's *funding* backward, as much as following the loot forward, is how cold cases crack. The backward-and-forward discipline is the same one detailed in [how to trace a transaction flow](/blog/trading/onchain/how-to-trace-a-transaction-flow); apply it from the attacker address as your fixed middle point.

#### Worked example: identifying the attacker from a \$30M drain

A lending protocol's monitoring flags one transaction. You drop the hash into Tenderly and read the call trace. The trace shows a flash loan of \$200M borrowed and repaid in the same tx (so it nets to zero and is not the theft), a price-oracle read manipulated mid-transaction, and — the part that matters — a withdrawal of **\$30M** of USDC out of the pool to an address with **zero prior history**, call it `0xBAD1…`. That zero-history EOA receiving \$30M it never deposited is your attacker address. You have spent ten minutes and you now have the root of the case: one address, \$30M, a precise timestamp, a labeled victim, and a known method. The intuition: the exploit tx is noisy (flash loans, internal calls, reverts), but the *theft* is the one net transfer of real value to a fresh wallet — find that and you have your seed.

## Step 2 — MAP the initial drain: how much, in what, to where

Before you chase anything, take inventory. **Mapping the drain** means writing down, precisely: the total stolen, broken out by asset, and the wallet(s) it sits in right now. This sounds trivial; skipping it is how investigators lose track of value three hops later. A \$30M drain might be \$18M USDC, \$8M worth of ETH, and \$4M of a long-tail token. Each asset behaves differently downstream — stablecoins can be frozen by the issuer, ETH must be swapped before it can hop a stablecoin rail, and the long-tail token might be impossible to sell without crashing its own price (which is itself a trace signal).

Mapping also sets your **accounting baseline**. From now on, at every hop, you reconcile: does the value leaving match the value that arrived, minus fees and slippage? If \$30M went in and only \$26M comes out the other side of a swap, you are missing \$4M — either to slippage on an illiquid token, or to a hop you have not found. The baseline is what lets you *know* when you have lost a piece of the trail, instead of guessing.

#### Worked example: mapping a \$30M drain into a freeze list

You inventory the `0xBAD1…` wallet right after the drain: **\$18M USDC, \$8M in ETH, \$4M in a governance token.** Two moves drop out immediately. First, the \$18M USDC is issuer-freezable — you flag Circle's compliance contact *now*, because a USDC freeze does not require following the funds anywhere; it only requires the issuer to act on the address while the tokens are still USDC. Second, the \$8M ETH and \$4M token are not freezable as-is; the attacker must convert or move them, which means *those* are the legs you trace. So mapping splits a \$30M problem into "\$18M I escalate to an issuer immediately" and "\$12M I follow." The intuition: knowing the asset mix turns one big number into a prioritized action list before you trace a single hop.

## Step 3 — FOLLOW forward, hop by hop, through the swaps

Now you trace. Starting at the attacker address, you follow the **outputs** — the transfers leaving it — hop by hop, building the flow graph: nodes are addresses, edges are transfers, and every edge carries an amount and a timestamp. The core skill is **weighting by value**: at each hop, follow the largest meaningful flow, not every dust transfer. An attacker who wants to lose you in the noise will fan \$30M into one fat \$28M trail and a hundred tiny decoy transfers; you follow the \$28M and note the decoys without chasing them.

The reason value-weighting matters so much is combinatorial. If an attacker fans \$30M into four wallets and each of those fans into four more, you are two hops out and already staring at sixteen trails; one more level and it is sixty-four. Chase all of them equally and you drown — which is exactly what the fan-out is designed to do to you. But the value does not split evenly in the cases that matter; the attacker has to keep most of the loot concentrated to actually move it, so a real flow graph almost always has a few **fat edges** carrying the bulk of the value and a cloud of thin decoy edges carrying dust. You follow the fat edges and log the decoys without chasing them. A useful discipline: at each hop, sort the outputs by value and ask "which of these, if I followed it, would account for most of the \$30M?" — then follow that one first. The dust can wait, and usually never needs visiting at all.

Concretely, in a flow-graph tool the loop is: click the attacker node, expand its outgoing transfers, identify the largest meaningful one, follow it to the next node, and repeat — the tool draws the tree as you go and lets you collapse the branches you have ruled out. On a bare explorer you do the same by hand: open the address, read the "Erc-20 Token Txns" and internal-transfer tabs, find the big outflow, click through to the receiving address, and keep a running ledger of amounts and timestamps in your notes (this *is* the start of your case file). Either way the rhythm is identical: expand, weight, follow, reconcile.

The hop that breaks naive tracers is the **swap**. The attacker takes \$30M of USDC into a DEX like Uniswap and comes out with ETH. To a tool that only follows "the same token," the USDC trail just *ended*. It did not. Value was conserved — \$30M of USDC became ~\$30M of ETH (minus fees and slippage). You reconnect across the swap by matching **value and timing**: a ~\$30M ETH outflow from the same wallet, seconds after a ~\$30M USDC inflow to the DEX, is the same money wearing a new coat. Good flow-graph tools (Arkham, MetaSleuth, Breadcrumbs) decode DEX swaps for you and draw the continuation automatically; when you trace by hand on an explorer, you do the matching yourself.

![Pipeline showing 30 million in USDC swapped to 9,000 ETH, then 10 million bridged to a Tron rail while 20 million stays back](/imgs/blogs/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step-3.png)

#### Worked example: following \$30M through a swap, then a \$10M bridge

The attacker moves the \$30M USDC into Uniswap and out comes **9,000 ETH** (at ~\$3,333/ETH, that is the same \$30M, less a fee you can see). Your value baseline holds: \$30M in, ~\$30M out, asset changed. Next, the attacker sends **\$10M of that ETH** into a cross-chain bridge — locking it on Ethereum and minting \$10M of USDT on a Tron rail — while leaving \$20M behind on Ethereum for now. You record the split: one edge of \$10M to the bridge, one node still holding \$20M. The intuition: a swap changes the *asset* and a bridge changes the *chain*, but neither destroys value — match \$30M-in to \$30M-out and \$10M-locked to \$10M-minted, and the trail never actually broke. (The deeper mechanics of swap-tracing live in [how to trace a transaction flow](/blog/trading/onchain/how-to-trace-a-transaction-flow).)

## Step 4 — handle the MIXERS and the BRIDGES

Two structures are designed (or just happen) to break the trail: mixers and bridges. They need different handling, and confusing them is a common rookie error.

A **mixer** (Tornado Cash on Ethereum, CoinJoin-style coordination on Bitcoin) pools many users' deposits and lets each withdraw to a fresh address, so the on-chain link between "who deposited" and "who withdrew" is deliberately severed. You cannot follow a single coin straight through a well-used mixer the way you follow a normal transfer. What you *can* do is **bracket**: note the exact amount and timestamp going in, then look at withdrawals of a matching amount in a matching time window coming out. Tornado Cash uses fixed denominations (e.g., 100 ETH per note), which both helps the mixer (every 100 ETH note looks identical) and helps you (the attacker who deposits 100 ETH and withdraws 100 ETH a day later, with the timing and downstream behavior lining up, is a strong lead). Bracketing produces a **probabilistic** link, not a proof — you say "this withdrawal is very likely the same actor," and you treat it as a lead to confirm with other signals, never as certainty. The mixer mechanics and their detection limits are unpacked in [mixers, CoinJoin, and obfuscation](/blog/trading/onchain/mixers-coinjoin-and-obfuscation), and the sanctions context of Tornado Cash specifically in [Tornado Cash and sanctioning code](/blog/trading/crypto/tornado-cash-and-sanctioning-code).

A **bridge** is different: it does *not* try to break the link, it just moves value to another chain. Funds are locked on chain A and an equivalent amount is minted on chain B. The trail is fully intact — you just have to **jump chains** to follow it. The trick is to read the bridge's deposit event on chain A (amount, destination chain, often the destination address) and then pick up the matching mint on chain B. The favorite destination for laundering is the **Tron USDT rail**, because USDT on Tron is cheap, fast, deeply liquid, and the dominant retail and illicit settlement layer — the cross-chain seam is exactly where many traces have to leap, as covered in [cross-chain tracing across bridges and the USDT rails](/blog/trading/onchain/cross-chain-tracing-bridges-and-the-usdt-rails).

#### Worked example: bracketing a mixer by amount and timing

The attacker routes part of the loot through a mixer in fixed **100 ETH** notes — say **30 deposits of 100 ETH (~\$10M total)** over two hours. The mixer severs the direct link, but you bracket: you list every 100 ETH *withdrawal* in the following 48 hours and look for a cluster of ~30 of them landing in fresh wallets that then behave identically (same gas funder, same next-hop pattern). You find 28 such withdrawals totaling **~\$9.3M** funneling into one consolidation wallet. You cannot *prove* each note is the attacker's, but 28 matching notes converging on one wallet is a lead worth a high confidence score. The intuition: a mixer hides individual coins, not statistical patterns — match the in-and-out by denomination, count, and timing, and the crowd of identical notes betrays the one actor moving in bulk.

### Why bridges are easier than mixers (and where each one bites)

It is worth being precise about why these two structures need opposite mindsets. A bridge is a *liquidity* problem, not a *secrecy* problem. It announces, in a public event log, "I locked X on chain A; mint X on chain B for this recipient." The trail is fully intact; you just have to be willing to switch explorers — Etherscan for the lock, Tronscan or BscScan for the mint. The friction is operational (different chains, different tools, fees that nibble the amount you match on), not cryptographic. A diligent tracer loses nothing real to a bridge except time.

A mixer is the reverse: cryptographically designed to break the deposit-to-withdrawal link, so you genuinely cannot follow a specific coin straight through. What you exploit instead is that mixers are bad at hiding *flow at scale*. A retail user mixing 100 ETH once disappears into the crowd. An attacker mixing \$10M in a two-hour burst of identical-denomination notes, then re-consolidating the withdrawals into one wallet, leaves a statistical fingerprint the crowd does not. The mixer protects the individual transaction; it does not protect the *campaign*. This is why bracketing works against large, time-clustered, bulk laundering far better than against a single small deposit — and why a well-resourced attacker who mixes slowly, in small amounts, across months, is genuinely hard to follow. The honest framing, the one this series insists on: mixers raise the cost and the uncertainty of tracing; against patient, low-volume use they can win, and the analyst's job is to attach an honest confidence level rather than to pretend a probabilistic bracket is a proof.

#### Worked example: a bridge hop you almost match wrong

The attacker bridges \$10M of ETH from Ethereum to the Tron USDT rail. You read the Ethereum-side lock event: **\$10.00M** of ETH locked. You jump to Tronscan expecting \$10.00M of USDT to mint — but the matching mint is **\$9.94M**. Did \$60k vanish? No: the bridge charged a fee and the ETH-to-USDT conversion slipped slightly, eating ~0.6%. If you had insisted on an exact \$10.00M match you might have rejected the real continuation and declared a dead end. Reconciling to the *minted* amount (\$9.94M), within a tolerance for fees and slippage, you confirm the same money crossed the chain. The intuition: across a bridge or a swap, match value within a fee-and-slippage band, not to the penny — the small leakage is the cost of moving, not a hop you missed.

## Step 5 — CLUSTER the destination wallets and LABEL the known services

After a few hops the attacker has usually fanned the funds into many wallets to look like many actors. **Clustering** is the move that collapses the disguise: you group addresses that are really controlled by the same entity. The strongest single clue is a **shared funding source** — forty "independent" wallets that were all first funded (and all topped up with gas) from one address are not forty actors; they are one actor wearing forty hats. Other behavioral signals stack on top: identical timing, the same next-hop pattern, the same approval fingerprints, round-number amounts. On Bitcoin the gold-standard heuristic is **common-input-ownership** (two coins spent as inputs to one transaction share an owner); on account-chain EVM, where there is no co-spend, you stack the weaker behavioral signals until they harden into a high-confidence cluster. The full heuristic toolkit is in [address clustering and heuristics](/blog/trading/onchain/address-clustering-and-heuristics).

**Labeling** is the complementary move: you put names on the addresses your trail touches. A node is not just `0x…`; it is "Binance hot wallet," "Uniswap V3 router," "Tornado Cash 100-ETH pool," "a KYC exchange deposit address." Labels come from public datasets (Arkham's entity tags, community submissions on explorers, exchange-published addresses) and from your own prior cases. Labels are what turn a graph of anonymous hex into a *story*: "the cluster sent \$2M to a Binance deposit address" is actionable; "`0xc0ffee…` sent \$2M to `0xdecaf…`" is not. The art and the pitfalls of attribution — false labels, recycled deposit addresses, a router mistaken for an exchange — are the subject of [labeling and attribution](/blog/trading/onchain/labeling-and-attribution).

![Graph showing many fresh peel wallets funded from one attacker hub collapsing into one cluster, then resolving to a labeled exchange and a labeled mixer](/imgs/blogs/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step-4.png)

#### Worked example: clustering 40 peel wallets holding ~\$250k each

The \$10M that reached the Tron rail gets peeled into **40 fresh wallets, each holding about \$250k** (40 × \$250k = \$10M). To a naive view that is 40 small, unrelated holders below most alert thresholds. But you notice all 40 were funded for gas from one address within a 20-minute window, all 40 use the same round \$250k amount, and all 40 sit dormant in the same pattern. You collapse them into **one cluster (Owner X) holding \$10M**, and treat the cluster — not the 40 wallets — as your unit of analysis. The intuition: splitting \$10M into 40 lookalike wallets fools a per-wallet filter but not a per-entity one — the shared funding source re-merges what the attacker tried to fragment, and \$250k × 40 is the same \$10M you were already chasing.

## Step 6 — WATCH for the off-ramp: arm the alerts

Here is the strategic heart of the workflow. The attacker's loot is, for the moment, un-spendable in the real world — it is crypto sitting in a cluster you are watching. To turn it into money they can use, the attacker must eventually push it through an **off-ramp**: most often a centralized exchange that converts crypto to fiat. That off-ramp is the **recoverable break** — the one moment the funds touch a gatekeeper who knows (or can be subpoenaed for) the customer's real identity, and who can freeze the deposit.

So you do not sit and refresh an explorer by hand for weeks. You **arm alerts** on the entire cluster — every wallet, every entity — to page you the instant any of them sends to a known exchange deposit address. Arkham, MetaSleuth, and the institutional tools all support address- and entity-level alerts. The cluster might sit dormant for months (cold case), and then at 3 a.m. one wallet deposits \$2M to a KYC exchange. Without an alert, you find out two days later, after the funds were withdrawn — too late. With an alert, you are paged in seconds and you start the freeze referral inside the window. The whole rationale and the math of *which* signals are worth alerting on is built out in [anomaly detection: building the alerts](/blog/trading/onchain/anomaly-detection-building-the-alerts).

![Before and after comparison: manual daily refresh misses an overnight exchange deposit, while armed alerts page the investigator the moment 2 million reaches a KYC exchange](/imgs/blogs/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step-5.png)

#### Worked example: the alert that fires on a \$2M exchange deposit

You armed alerts on all 40 cluster wallets the day you identified them. For six weeks: nothing. Then at 03:14 one wallet sends **\$2M of USDT** to an address your label set marks as a **KYC exchange deposit address**. The alert pages you immediately. You have, realistically, a window measured in hours — maybe a day — before the attacker swaps and withdraws. You fire the case file you have been keeping current to that exchange's compliance team and to the relevant law-enforcement contact. They freeze the \$2M and pull the KYC identity behind the deposit. Of the original \$30M, the recoverable break was this \$2M — because it was the slice that touched a gatekeeper while your referral was live. The intuition: you cannot force the attacker to an off-ramp, but you can guarantee that *when* they go, you are awake — alerts convert a months-long stakeout into a one-second page at the exact moment recovery becomes possible.

## Step 7 — DOCUMENT the case and hand it off

A trace in your head, or a tangle of browser tabs, is worth nothing to an exchange. The final step is to turn the investigation into a **case file**: a reproducible packet that a compliance analyst — who may not be a blockchain expert — can read, verify, and act on under time pressure. This is the deliverable that does the actual work, so build it as you go, not at the end.

![Grid of the six components of a case file: flow graph, amounts and timeline, transaction hashes, the off-ramp, attribution, and a plain narrative](/imgs/blogs/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step-6.png)

A handoff-ready case file has six parts. **The flow graph**: an annotated picture from the exploit tx to the off-ramp deposit, with nodes and edges labeled. **Amounts and a timeline**: \$30M drained, \$10M bridged, \$2M to the CEX, every step timestamped. **Transaction hashes**: every hop cited by its hash, so a third party can reproduce the whole trace independently — this is what makes it evidence rather than assertion. **The off-ramp**: the exact exchange deposit address and the specific deposit transaction, which is what the exchange freezes. **Attribution**: the labeled entities, the cluster evidence, and an honest confidence level with its basis (e.g., "high — shared funding source across 40 wallets"; "medium — mixer bracket, probabilistic"). **A plain narrative**: what happened, in order, in language a non-technical compliance reader follows.

Then you **hand it off**. Public sleuths publish (a thread, a report) and tag the exchanges, applying pressure and crowd-sourcing more eyes. Institutional analysts route formal reports through direct exchange and law-enforcement channels — Chainalysis, TRM, and Elliptic exist substantially to package exactly this kind of evidence at a standard courts and exchanges accept, and to maintain the relationships that get a freeze actioned in hours. A worked, end-to-end version of this whole assembly — a single case carried from exploit tx to documented off-ramp — is the companion post [case study: tracing a real flow end to end](/blog/trading/onchain/case-study-tracing-a-real-flow-end-to-end).

#### Worked example: a case file that recovers \$2M of a \$30M theft

Your final packet reads: exploit tx `0x9f3c…` drained **\$30M** from the pool at 14:02 UTC; \$30M USDC swapped to 9,000 ETH at 14:09; \$10M bridged to Tron at 14:31; the bridged \$10M peeled into 40 wallets of ~\$250k (cluster Owner X, high confidence, shared funding source); on day 43, wallet 17 deposited **\$2M USDT** to exchange deposit address `T…abc` in tx `…def` at 03:14. The exchange freezes the \$2M and matches the deposit to a verified account. The other \$28M is still traceable but parked or already through chokepoints you reached too late. The intuition: the file's value is not that it is complete — most of the \$30M is unrecovered — but that it is *reproducible and timely*, so the one \$2M slice that touched a gatekeeper while you were watching could actually be stopped.

## The handoff: what actually gets a freeze actioned

The case file is necessary; it is not automatically sufficient. Whether an exchange freezes \$2M in time depends on a few things that have nothing to do with the quality of your trace and everything to do with the plumbing of the handoff. Knowing them changes how you build the file and who you send it to.

First, **the right channel matters more than the right argument.** A cold email to a generic support address will not get a deposit frozen in hours. Exchanges have compliance and law-enforcement liaison teams with defined intake processes; institutional analysts and the chain-analytics firms maintain standing relationships with those teams precisely so a referral lands on the correct desk fast. A public sleuth's substitute for that relationship is *pressure*: a well-documented public thread tagging the exchange creates reputational stakes that a private email does not, which is part of why ZachXBT-style investigators publish rather than just file quietly.

Second, **a freeze is usually a temporary administrative hold, not a court order.** An exchange can freeze a suspicious deposit on its own initiative under its terms of service for a window — but to *keep* the funds frozen, to compel disclosure of the KYC identity, and ultimately to return the money to the victim, you generally need law enforcement and a court in the loop. So the realistic recovery path is: your trace triggers an exchange hold (fast, hours), which buys time for a formal complaint and a court process (slow, months) that makes the hold stick and unlocks the identity. Your job is to make the fast step possible; the slow step belongs to lawyers and police.

Third, **jurisdiction is the silent variable.** The same trace that gets a deposit frozen at a US-regulated exchange in hours may get no response at all from an offshore venue with weak compliance — which is exactly why launderers prefer those venues, and why "the off-ramp is the recoverable break" comes with an asterisk: it is the break *only if the off-ramp is a gatekeeper that will act*. Part of mature attribution is knowing which exchanges respond and which do not, and routing the most time-sensitive referrals to the chokepoints where action is realistic.

#### Worked example: the same \$2M, two different exchanges

Suppose the attacker's \$2M deposit lands at a major, well-regulated exchange. Your case file reaches its compliance team through an institutional channel within the hour; they place an administrative hold on the \$2M, and a subsequent court order makes it stick and surfaces the KYC identity. Recovery: realistic. Now suppose instead the \$2M lands at an offshore venue that ignores referrals. Same trace, same quality file, same speed — and the funds are swapped and withdrawn within the day, recovering \$0. The difference was not your forensics; it was whether the chokepoint was a real gatekeeper. The intuition: the recoverable break is only as good as the willingness of the entity on the other side of it to act — trace toward gatekeepers that respond, and treat a deposit to an unresponsive venue as a near-loss to document, not a recovery to expect.

## Real-time versus cold-case tracing (and the role of public sleuths)

The same seven steps run at two very different tempos. **Real-time tracing** happens in the hours after a hack: the funds are in motion, and your priority order is identify → map → arm alerts, fast, even before you have followed every hop, because the goal is to have the off-ramp wired before the attacker reaches it. You accept a messy, incomplete graph if it means alerts are live. **Cold-case tracing** happens weeks or months later: the funds may be dormant, and now patience is your edge — the ledger never forgets, so a year-old cluster that suddenly deposits to an exchange is just as catchable as a fresh one, and attackers often get sloppy with time (reusing an old wallet, touching a KYC exchange they used before the hack). Most cases are both: a hot sprint to set up the watch, then a cold vigil for the deposit.

A distinctive feature of crypto forensics is the **public sleuth**. Because the data is open, independent investigators — the most famous being the pseudonymous **ZachXBT** — run this exact workflow in public, publishing detailed traces that have exposed scams, frozen funds, and forced exchange action through sheer transparency and pressure. Their reports are a masterclass in the documentation step: each claim is pinned to a transaction hash, each cluster is justified by a named heuristic, and the confidence is stated rather than implied — which is exactly why exchanges and law enforcement take them seriously despite coming from an anonymous account. The lesson for any analyst is that *the rigor of the documentation, not the authority of the author, is what makes a trace actionable*.

Some hacks are also chased via **bounties** and negotiated returns. Several of the biggest "recoveries" on the leaderboard were not seizures at all but **deals**: protocols offered the attacker a percentage to keep — a white-hat bounty — in exchange for returning the rest. Poly Network's entire **\$611M** came back this way, and Euler's **\$197M** was fully returned after negotiation; in both, the public, real-time trace was the leverage that made returning the funds the attacker's least-bad option (the money was watched, the off-ramps were covered, and laundering \$600M of fully-traced funds is genuinely hard). Separately, protocols and chain-analytics firms offer **rewards to analysts** whose traces lead to recovery, which is part of why so much high-quality forensic work happens in the open.

The open ledger turns forensics into a partly crowd-sourced public good — which is both a strength (more eyes, faster, with reputational pressure that a private referral lacks) and a caution. An unverified public accusation can be wrong, and a wrong public accusation against a real person is its own harm; confidence levels and reproducible hashes matter precisely *because* anyone can publish. The same transparency that lets a teenager trace \$625M also lets a careless analyst defame the wrong wallet — discipline, not access, is the scarce resource. The broader tooling ecosystem — who builds what, free versus institutional — is mapped in [the on-chain tooling landscape](/blog/trading/onchain/the-onchain-tooling-landscape).

## The tools, mapped to each step

You do not need every tool; you need the right one at each step. The matrix below maps the workflow to a free/freemium retail stack and the institutional grade.

![Matrix mapping each workflow step to what it needs, a retail or free tool, and an institutional tool](/imgs/blogs/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step-7.png)

Walking the rows: to **decode the exploit tx** (step 1) use **Tenderly** or Etherscan; institutions use the same call-trace view inside their own decoders. To **draw the flow graph** (step 3) use **Arkham**, **MetaSleuth** (by MistTrack), or **Breadcrumbs**; the institutional equivalents are **Chainalysis Reactor**, **TRM**, and **Elliptic Investigator**. For **mixers and bridges** (step 4), retail does manual amount-and-timing matching while institutional tools ship demixing heuristics and built-in cross-chain tracing. For **clustering and labeling** (step 5), Arkham's entity tags and community explorer labels cover most retail needs; institutions run proprietary cluster and attribution databases. For **alerts** (step 6), Arkham's address and entity alerts are the workhorse; institutions wire real-time feeds into compliance systems. For **documentation and handoff** (step 7), retail exports the graph and files a public report or ticket, while institutions produce a formal case report and use direct exchange and FBI channels.

Two practical notes. First, the free stack is genuinely powerful — most public ZachXBT-style investigations run on Arkham, MetaSleuth, Breadcrumbs, Etherscan, and Tenderly, all reachable from a browser. The institutional tier mostly buys you *speed, scale, attribution depth, and the relationships* that get a freeze actioned, not a fundamentally different method. Second, no tool is a truth oracle: a label can be stale, a cluster can over-merge, a demixer outputs probabilities. Treat every tool's output as a lead to verify, not a verdict — the same "verify, don't trust the dashboard's green number" discipline that runs through this whole series.

## Common misconceptions

**"If I can trace it, I can get it back."** No — and this is the single most important correction in this post. Tracing is free and open to anyone; recovery requires a gatekeeper (an exchange or an issuer) to act, and they act only on a credible referral, only on funds that touch them, and only inside a time window. You can perfectly trace \$30M and recover \$0 if none of it ever reaches a chokepoint while your referral is live, or recover \$2M of \$30M if only that slice does. The trace is necessary; it is never sufficient.

**"A mixer means the trail is dead."** Usually not. A mixer severs the *direct* link, but it does not destroy value or hide patterns. Bracketing by amount and timing turns a mixer into a probabilistic fork, not a wall — 28 matching 100-ETH notes converging on one wallet is a strong lead. Mixers raise the cost and the uncertainty of tracing; they rarely end it outright, especially for attackers moving large, distinctive amounts in bursts.

**"Funds vanished — I lost them."** Value is conserved. If \$30M went in and you cannot find \$30M coming out, you have missed a hop — almost always a swap that changed the asset or a bridge that changed the chain. The fix is to match value and timing across the gap, not to declare a dead end. The most common rookie failure is following "the same token" and stopping when it swaps.

**"Splitting into many wallets hides the money."** It hides it from a *per-wallet* view, not a *per-entity* one. Forty wallets funded from one source, behaving identically, collapse back into one cluster — and \$250k × 40 is the same \$10M. Fan-out raises the bookkeeping cost of tracing; clustering re-merges it. The attacker's split is a delay tactic, not an escape.

**"The crypto economy is mostly crime, so this is hopeless."** The opposite. Illicit volume is a small share of all on-chain activity (on the order of **0.1–0.6%** by Chainalysis estimates), and because the ledger is public and permanent, traced stolen funds are often *more* recoverable than in traditional finance, where the trail goes dark behind bank secrecy. The transparency that makes crypto theft fast also makes it filmable — and the workflow in this post is how that film becomes a recovery.

## The playbook: what to do with it

Run the seven steps in order, and let each signal map to a read, an action, and a false-positive to rule out.

- **Signal — you have a flagged exploit tx.** Read: this is your seed. Action: decode it in Tenderly, identify the attacker EOA, and immediately note real-time versus cold-case tempo. Invalidation: make sure the "theft" transfer is the net value move, not a flash loan that nets to zero — the noisy parts of the tx are not the loot.
- **Signal — you know the attacker address and the asset mix.** Read: some assets are issuer-freezable now, others must be traced. Action: escalate any stablecoin (USDC/USDT) to the issuer immediately; trace the ETH and long-tail legs. Invalidation: do not assume the whole balance is the theft — reconcile against what actually left the victim.
- **Signal — the trail hits a swap.** Read: the asset changed, value did not. Action: match value and timing across the DEX to reconnect; keep your accounting baseline. Invalidation: a swap can split value across two output legs — check both, and watch slippage on illiquid tokens so a "missing" \$4M is not just a hop you skipped.
- **Signal — the trail hits a mixer.** Read: the direct link is severed, the pattern is not. Action: bracket by denomination, count, and timing; assign a confidence level, never a proof. Invalidation: a clean amount/timing match is a lead — corroborate with funding source and downstream behavior before you assert it.
- **Signal — the trail hits a bridge.** Read: the chain changed, the trail is intact. Action: read the deposit event, jump to the destination chain (often the Tron USDT rail), pick the trail back up. Invalidation: bridge fees and slippage change the amount you match on — reconcile to the minted amount, not the deposited one.
- **Signal — funds fan out into many fresh wallets.** Read: a per-entity, not per-wallet, problem. Action: cluster on shared funding source and behavior; treat the cluster as one unit. Invalidation: a shared counterparty might be an exchange's hot wallet, not the actor — confirm the funding-source link before merging.
- **Signal — the cluster touches a KYC exchange.** Read: the recoverable break has arrived. Action: fire the case file to the exchange's compliance team and law enforcement *now*, within the window. Invalidation: confirm the destination is a real exchange deposit address (with a verified label), not a DEX router or another intermediary that looks like one.
- **Always — keep the case file current.** Read: the file is the only thing that does work outside your head. Action: maintain the flow graph, dated amounts, every tx hash, the off-ramp, attribution with honest confidence, and a plain narrative — built as you go, so it is ready the second an alert fires. Invalidation: a screenshot is not a case file; if a third party cannot reproduce your trace from the hashes, it will not survive scrutiny.

A few habits separate analysts who recover funds from analysts who merely admire the trail. They build the case file from hop one, not the night before the handoff, so it is ready the instant an alert fires. They reconcile value at every hop, so a swap or a bridge is a continuation to match, never a dead end to mourn. They weight by value and refuse to be drowned by fan-out decoys. They arm alerts on the *cluster*, not a single wallet, and they route the referral to a gatekeeper that actually responds. And they state confidence honestly — "high, shared funding source" or "medium, mixer bracket" — because a trace that overclaims is a trace that gets thrown out. None of this is genius; all of it is discipline, applied in the same order every time.

The deepest lesson of the whole workflow: **the chain gives you perfect visibility and zero authority.** You can see everything and stop nothing — until the funds touch a gatekeeper. So the entire process is engineered around getting to that one chokepoint, the off-ramp, with reproducible evidence, faster than the attacker can withdraw. Master the seven steps and you are not playing detective for the thrill; you are running the disciplined, repeatable process that turns a public ledger's perfect memory into the occasional, hard-won recovery — one frozen \$2M deposit at a time.
