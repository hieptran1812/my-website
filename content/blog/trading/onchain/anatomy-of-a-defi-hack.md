> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Almost every DeFi exploit, no matter how exotic it sounds, fits one of seven recurring attack vectors that all end the same way: funds leaving a contract or wallet to an attacker-controlled address.
>
> - The seven vectors are **reentrancy**, **oracle / price manipulation**, **flash-loan-powered attacks**, **bridge exploits**, **access-control / key compromise**, **logic / math bugs**, and **signature / approval exploits**. Learn the shapes and you can classify a new hack in minutes.
> - Each one has a documented case with real numbers — Curve (\$70M reentrancy), Mango (\$117M oracle), Ronin (\$625M key compromise), Wormhole (\$326M signature bug), Nomad (\$190M init bug), Euler (\$197M donation bug), Cetus (\$220M overflow), and the largest ever, Bybit (\$1.46B).
> - The on-chain footprint is almost always the same: a **fresh attacker contract**, a **huge anomalous outflow in one transaction**, and an **address funded from a mixer or no-KYC venue**. Those three fingerprints survive any cover story.
> - The one number to remember: roughly **\$2–4B is stolen from crypto platforms every year**, and a single state actor — North Korea's Lazarus group — accounts for well over a third of it.

In the early hours of 21 February 2025, a routine transaction left one of Bybit's Ethereum cold wallets. On the surface it looked like an ordinary multisig transfer being approved by the exchange's signers. Underneath, the signing interface had been tampered with: the transaction the signers thought they were approving was not the transaction that actually executed. When the dust settled, roughly **\$1.46 billion** in ETH and staked-ETH derivatives had moved to an address controlled by the attackers — the single largest theft in the history of crypto, later attributed by investigators to North Korea's Lazarus group. Within days the funds were being shredded across hundreds of intermediary wallets and pushed through swaps and bridges in a race against the analysts trying to follow them.

That heist did not look like a "smart-contract bug" at all. No clever recursion, no manipulated price feed — just a compromised signing process and a few approvals that should never have been given. And yet, eighteen months earlier, a *completely* different kind of attack drained roughly **\$70 million** from several Curve Finance pools, this time exploiting a flaw not even in Curve's own code but in a specific version of the Vyper compiler that mis-compiled a reentrancy lock. Two incidents, two utterly different mechanisms, a 20× difference in size — and yet, as we will see, both leave a recognizable signature on-chain, and both belong to a taxonomy small enough to memorize.

That is the promise of this post. DeFi feels like an endless parade of novel disasters, but it is not. The headlines change; the *vectors* barely do. Once you can name the seven recurring attack patterns, recognize their mechanism at a high level, and spot the footprint each one leaves on the public ledger, you stop being surprised by hacks and start being able to read them — as an investor deciding whether a protocol is safe to touch, or as an analyst reconstructing what just happened.

![Seven DeFi attack vectors converging on a single drain of funds to an attacker address](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-defi-hack-1.png)

This is a security-education piece, written from the defender's and investor's chair. We will explain each mechanism only to the depth you need to *recognize* and *defend against* it, and we will ground every vector in a documented, dated post-mortem with real numbers — not a working how-to. The goal is fluency in the shape of an attack, not a recipe for one.

## Foundations: what a smart-contract exploit actually is

Before the taxonomy, we need a shared vocabulary. If you have read the earlier posts in this series — [what is on-chain analysis](/blog/trading/onchain/what-is-onchain-analysis) and [addresses, wallets, and contracts](/blog/trading/onchain/addresses-wallets-and-contracts) — some of this will be familiar, but let us build it cleanly from zero because the precise definitions are where the attacks live.

**What is a smart contract?** A smart contract is a program that lives at an address on a blockchain. You do not "run" it on your own computer; it sits on the chain, and anyone can send it a transaction that calls one of its functions. When a function runs, it can move tokens, update internal records, and call other contracts. The crucial property is that the code is **public** (anyone can read it), **immutable** by default (you usually cannot patch it after deployment), and it **holds funds** (a lending protocol's contract literally custodies the deposits of every user). On Ethereum and the EVM chains, there are two kinds of accounts: an **externally owned account (EOA)**, controlled by a private key — a human's wallet — and a **contract account**, controlled by its own code. Hold that distinction; the difference between a "protocol hack" and a "key compromise" turns on it entirely.

**What is an exploit, then?** An exploit is simply *the code doing something the developers did not intend* — but which the code, read literally, actually permits. The contract is not "broken" in the sense of crashing; it does exactly what its bytecode says. The bug is the gap between what the developers *meant* the rules to be and what they *literally wrote*. A bank vault with a lock that opens to any key shaped like a "7" is not malfunctioning when a "7"-shaped key opens it; it is doing precisely what it was built to do. The flaw is in the specification, and the attacker is just a very careful reader.

**Protocol hack versus key compromise — the most important fork.** There are two fundamentally different ways money leaves a contract.

- A **protocol hack** abuses a flaw in the *logic of the public code*. The attacker sends transactions that the contract permits but the developers never anticipated — a reentrancy loop, a manipulated price, a math overflow. Nobody's secret was stolen; the rules themselves were exploitable.
- A **key compromise** does not abuse the contract's logic at all. The attacker obtains the *private key* (or enough of the keys, for a multisig) that legitimately controls the funds, and then signs a perfectly valid transaction. The code behaves exactly as designed; the problem is that the wrong person is now holding the keys. This is closer to a stolen password than a software bug, and it usually happens **off-chain** — a phished signer, malware on a developer's laptop, a tampered signing interface.

This fork matters because the defenses are completely different. You fix a protocol hack by auditing and re-architecting code; you fix a key compromise by hardening operational security, spreading keys across hardware and people, and watching for anomalous signer behavior. Many of the biggest losses — Bybit, Ronin, DMM Bitcoin, WazirX — are key compromises, not contract bugs, which is why "the code was audited" is cold comfort.

**What is a flash loan?** A flash loan is the single most important amplifier in the DeFi attacker's toolkit, and it is genuinely strange the first time you meet it. A flash loan is an **uncollateralized loan that must be borrowed and repaid within the same transaction**. Because a blockchain transaction is atomic — either every step inside it succeeds or the whole thing reverts as if it never happened — a protocol can safely lend you \$50 million with zero collateral, on the condition that by the end of the transaction the \$50 million (plus a small fee) is back. If it is not back, the transaction reverts and the loan never happened. So there is no risk to the lender. For an attacker, this is rocket fuel: it means your *own* capital no longer limits how big an attack you can run. A nobody with \$500 of gas money can borrow \$50 million for a few hundred milliseconds, use it to bend a market, and repay it — all in one block. Flash loans are not themselves an attack; they are a force multiplier strapped onto the other vectors.

**Why DeFi is uniquely exposed.** Four properties combine to make decentralized finance an unusually rich target:

1. **It holds funds directly.** A protocol's contracts custody real value — sometimes billions. The honeypot is on-chain, addressable, and quantified for everyone to see.
2. **The code is public.** Anyone, including the attacker, can read the exact logic, fork it, and probe it for flaws at leisure before striking. There is no security-through-obscurity.
3. **It is immutable.** Once deployed, most contracts cannot be quietly patched. A bug found by an attacker is live until the protocol can pause or migrate — if it even has that ability.
4. **It is composable.** Protocols call each other freely — a "money Lego" where a lending market reads a price from an exchange that reads a balance from a token. That composability is the whole point of DeFi, but it means a flaw in one Lego brick can be reached and amplified through three others, and a flash loan can chain the whole thing into one atomic strike.

For the deeper mechanics of how these protocols are built — automated market makers, lending pools, collateral — see the dedicated explainer on [DeFi protocols: Uniswap, Aave, MakerDAO](/blog/trading/crypto/defi-protocols-uniswap-aave-makerdao). Here we assume only that you know a protocol is a public program that holds money.

**The attacker contract.** One last building block. Sophisticated on-chain attacks are almost never run from a plain wallet clicking buttons. The attacker first **deploys their own smart contract** — a small program whose only job is to execute the exploit. Why? Because the attack usually needs to do many things in a precise sequence inside a single transaction: take a flash loan, call the victim, handle a callback, swap tokens, repay the loan. A contract can orchestrate all of that atomically; a human clicking a wallet cannot. So the very first on-chain artifact of a serious hack is typically a **freshly deployed contract**, created minutes before the strike, funded with a little gas from a suspicious source. Remember that — it is the single most reliable footprint we will return to at the end.

## The common shape of every hack

Before we walk the seven vectors one at a time, it helps to see the arc they all share. Strip away the specific bug and almost every documented exploit runs the same five-step lifecycle.

![Five-step exploit lifecycle from finding the flaw to laundering the proceeds](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-defi-hack-2.png)

1. **Find the flaw.** The attacker reads the public code, watches a fork, or notices a missing check. Because everything is open-source, this is patient, low-risk reconnaissance.
2. **Fund the attacker contract.** They deploy a fresh contract and seed it with a little gas, often sourced from a mixer like Tornado Cash or a no-KYC exchange so the funding wallet cannot be traced back to them. (We cover the mechanics of that obfuscation, defensively, in [mixers, CoinJoin, and obfuscation](/blog/trading/onchain/mixers-coinjoin-and-obfuscation).)
3. **Execute.** The strike itself — frequently a *single transaction*. Flash-loan in, trigger the bug, take the profit out.
4. **Drain.** Funds leave the protocol to the attacker, often in minutes or even one block.
5. **Launder.** Swap the stolen assets to ETH or a stablecoin, bridge them to another chain, run them through a mixer, and eventually try to cash out at a regulated edge.

That last step is its own discipline; the full hack → swap → bridge → mix → cash-out route is mapped in [how stolen funds are laundered](/blog/trading/onchain/how-stolen-funds-are-laundered), and tracing it after the fact is covered in [tracing stolen funds step by step](/blog/trading/onchain/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step). For now, just internalize the arc: **flaw → fund → execute → drain → launder.** Every vector below plugs into steps 1 and 3; the rest of the shape is constant.

Now, the seven vectors.

## Vector 1: Reentrancy

**The mechanism.** Reentrancy is the original DeFi bug — the one that took down "The DAO" in 2016 and forced the Ethereum hard fork that created Ethereum Classic. The DAO was a \$150-million crowdfunded investment fund, and a reentrancy bug let an attacker drain roughly \$60 million of it; the community's decision to roll the chain back to undo the theft split Ethereum in two and remains its most consequential governance moment. That a single ordering mistake could fracture a multibillion-dollar network is worth sitting with: reentrancy is not an obscure edge case, it is the bug that defined the security culture of the entire ecosystem.

It is, at bottom, an **ordering mistake**. A contract that lets you withdraw your deposited ETH should do three things: check that your balance is enough, send you the ETH, and update your balance to reflect the withdrawal. The fatal version does them in the wrong order: it **sends the ETH first, then updates the balance.**

Here is why that is catastrophic. On Ethereum, when a contract sends ETH to an address, if that address is itself a contract, the receiving contract's code runs automatically — a "receive hook" fires. A malicious receiver can use that hook to immediately call `withdraw()` *again*, before the first call ever got to the line that updates the balance. The balance still reads the old, un-decremented value, so the check passes, and the contract sends the ETH a second time. The attacker's hook fires again, calls `withdraw()` a third time, and so on — a loop that drains the entire pool in a single transaction, all because the contract trusted that the recipient would politely wait its turn.

![Reentrancy pipeline showing the pool sending funds before updating the balance, letting the attacker re-enter and loop](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-defi-hack-3.png)

The fix is almost insultingly simple and has a name: **checks-effects-interactions.** Do all your checks, then apply all your *effects* (update the balance to zero) **before** any *interactions* (sending the ETH out). Or wrap the function in a **reentrancy guard** — a lock that refuses any re-entrant call while the function is mid-execution. Every serious Solidity developer knows this pattern. So how does reentrancy still happen?

**The documented case: Curve Finance, July 2023, ~\$70M.** Curve's own developers knew the pattern perfectly well and used reentrancy guards. The catastrophe came from below: several specific versions of the **Vyper compiler** (Vyper is an alternative smart-contract language) contained a bug that *mis-compiled the reentrancy lock* — the guard the developers wrote was silently neutered by the tooling that turned it into bytecode. Pools written in those exact Vyper versions were suddenly reentrancy-vulnerable despite the source code looking correct. Attackers (a mix of malicious actors and white-hat MEV bots racing to "rescue" funds) drained roughly \$70 million across several pools. Much of it was eventually returned, but the lesson stuck: your security is only as good as the entire stack beneath your source code, compiler included.

**The footprint.** A reentrancy attack is loud on-chain. The exploit transaction shows the **same function called many times within one transaction** — the internal call trace looks like a staircase of repeated `withdraw()` invocations, each pulling value out, with the contract's balance ratcheting down in lockstep. On a tool like Etherscan, the "Internal Txns" tab of the exploit transaction reveals dozens or hundreds of self-similar transfers that no legitimate user flow would ever produce.

#### Worked example: reentrancy draining a pool one loop at a time

Say a vulnerable pool holds **\$2,000,000** of ETH and the attacker deposits **\$10,000** legitimately, so the contract records `balance[attacker] = $10,000`. The attacker calls `withdraw()`. The pool sends \$10,000 out — but before it updates the balance, the attacker's receive hook re-enters `withdraw()`. The balance still reads \$10,000, so the check passes and another \$10,000 goes out. Each loop extracts another \$10,000 while the recorded balance never moves. After **200 loops**, the attacker has pulled \$10,000 × 200 = **\$2,000,000** — the entire pool — for an initial outlay of just \$10,000 plus gas, all inside one transaction. The intuition: the bug let the attacker spend the same \$10,000 of "balance" two hundred times because the contract never wrote down that it had already paid.

## Vector 2: Oracle and price manipulation

**The mechanism.** DeFi protocols constantly need to know prices — how much is this collateral worth, what is the exchange rate between these two tokens? They get that number from an **oracle**: a source of price data the contract trusts. The vulnerability appears when a protocol uses a *manipulable* source as its oracle — most dangerously, the **spot price of an on-chain liquidity pool it can itself trade against.** If a lending market values your collateral using the instantaneous price in a pool, and you can move that pool's price by trading into it, then you can *make your collateral appear more valuable than it is* and borrow against the inflated figure.

The attack is therefore: push the oracle price far away from the true market price by trading hard into a thin pool, then exploit some other function — borrow, liquidate, redeem — that trusts that distorted price, and walk away with the difference. The defense is to use **robust oracles**: a time-weighted average price (TWAP) that cannot be moved in a single block, or an external decentralized oracle network like Chainlink that aggregates many off-chain sources. (For how those oracle networks are built, see [Chainlink and blockchain oracles](/blog/trading/crypto/chainlink-and-blockchain-oracles); for the market-microstructure version of this attack — spoofing depth to move a price — see the sibling post on [fake depth, spoofing, and oracle attacks](/blog/trading/onchain/fake-depth-spoofing-and-oracle-attacks).)

**The documented case: Mango Markets, October 2022, ~\$117M.** Mango was a Solana-based lending and trading protocol that valued positions using the spot price of its own perpetual-futures market — a market thin enough to move. The attacker, Avraham Eisenberg, took two accounts, bought a large long position in the MNGO perpetual on one and sold it on the other, then **aggressively bought MNGO across multiple venues to ramp its price** from cents to multiples of its starting value. With his long position now showing an enormous unrealized "profit" against the manipulated price, the protocol let him borrow against that paper gain — and he borrowed out essentially all of Mango's other assets, roughly \$117 million. Eisenberg publicly called it a "highly profitable trading strategy"; a US court disagreed, and he was convicted of fraud and market manipulation. The forensic point is clean: the protocol's accounting was correct given the price it was fed; the price was the lie.

**The footprint.** Oracle manipulation shows up as an **abnormal, sharp price excursion in a specific pool** immediately followed by a large borrow, liquidation, or redemption that benefits one address — and then the price snapping back. On a chart the manipulated asset spikes and reverts within a block or two; on the transaction trace you see the same actor both *moving* the price and *consuming* it. The signature is the tight coupling between a price distortion and an exploitative action by the same party.

#### Worked example: a \$50M flash loan to bend a pool for \$8M of profit

This is where Vectors 2 and 3 fuse. An attacker spots a lending protocol that prices a token using a single on-chain pool holding only **\$5,000,000** of liquidity. They take a **\$50,000,000 flash loan** and dump it into the pool, pushing the token's quoted price up roughly **3×**. Their own pre-positioned collateral, denominated in that token, now appears to be worth far more than it is. The protocol lets them borrow against the inflated value, and they extract, say, **\$13,000,000** of other assets. They repay the \$50,000,000 flash loan plus a ~0.09% fee of about \$45,000, unwind the pool position, and net roughly **\$8,000,000** — all in one atomic transaction, having started with essentially no capital of their own. The intuition: the flash loan rented them whale-sized buying power for a few hundred milliseconds, just long enough to lie to the oracle and cash the lie before anyone could react.

## Vector 3: Flash-loan-powered attacks

**The mechanism.** We have now met the flash loan twice, which is the point: it is rarely the *whole* attack and almost always the *amplifier*. A flash-loan attack takes one of the other vectors — an oracle manipulation, a logic bug, a governance exploit — and supplies the enormous, momentary capital needed to make it pay. The reason this matters as its own category is that it changes who can attack you. Without flash loans, an exploit that requires moving a deep pool is only available to a genuine whale, which dramatically shrinks the attacker pool and means slippage often eats the edge. With flash loans, **any anonymous actor with gas money can rent whale-scale capital for one transaction**, and the economics of the attack flip from "uneconomic" to "highly profitable."

![Before and after comparison showing a flash loan turning a small attacker into a market-moving whale](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-defi-hack-4.png)

The before/after above is the whole story. On the left, an attacker with \$20,000 of their own capital cannot move a \$50M pool — one small trade barely budges the price, slippage eats the edge, and the bug sits unused. On the right, the flash loan rents \$50M for a single transaction, large enough to bend the pool, trigger the bug against the distorted price, repay the loan plus fee, and keep the profit — all in one block, with no collateral ever posted.

**The defense.** You do not defend against flash loans by banning them — that is impossible and would break legitimate uses like arbitrage and collateral swaps. You defend by removing the *thing the flash loan amplifies*: never price off a single-block spot source (use a TWAP or external oracle), never let a single transaction's borrowed balance count toward governance votes or collateral valuation, and assume any input that *can* be momentarily controlled by the caller *will* be. The mental shift is to design as if every actor can become a whale for one block — because, thanks to flash loans, every actor can.

**The documented cases.** Flash loans sit underneath a large share of pure-DeFi exploits. The Euler Finance hack (\$197M, covered below as a logic bug) used a flash loan to set up the donation; many smaller oracle-manipulation drains follow the exact \$50M-borrow / bend-the-pool pattern of the worked example above. The recurring tell is a transaction that *opens* with a giant borrow from Aave, dYdX, or Balancer and *closes* by repaying it in the same transaction, with the protocol's funds extracted in between.

**The footprint.** A flash-loan attack has an unmistakable bookend structure in its transaction trace: a **massive borrow as one of the first internal calls and a matching repayment as one of the last**, with the victim protocol's balance falling sharply in between. Because the borrowed sum dwarfs the attacker's own holdings, the transaction shows tens of millions flowing through a contract whose deploying wallet was empty minutes earlier — capital that materialized and vanished within a single block.

## Vector 4: Bridge exploits

**The mechanism.** A **bridge** connects two blockchains that otherwise cannot talk to each other. Because you cannot literally move a coin from Ethereum to another chain, a bridge typically *locks* your asset on the source chain and *mints* a representative token on the destination chain; to come back, you burn the representative and unlock the original. The bridge therefore holds the **entire pool of locked collateral** for everything that has ever crossed it — which makes bridges among the largest honeypots in all of crypto. And the security question reduces to one brutal sentence: **who is allowed to authorize a mint or an unlock?** If an attacker can convince the bridge to release funds it should not — by stealing the keys that authorize releases, by forging the signatures the bridge checks, or by exploiting a bug that makes the bridge accept a fake message — they can drain the lot.

![Matrix comparing the Ronin, Wormhole, and Nomad bridge hacks by flaw, mechanism, loss, and defense lesson](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-defi-hack-5.png)

The matrix above shows the three largest bridge hacks, each of which broke a *different* link in that authorization chain:

- **Ronin Bridge, March 2022, \$625M (a key compromise).** Ronin's bridge was secured by nine validators, of which five signatures were needed to authorize a withdrawal. The Lazarus group compromised **five of the nine validator keys** (four via a spear-phishing campaign against Sky Mavis staff, plus one more whose access had been temporarily granted and never revoked), giving them the majority needed to sign a perfectly valid-looking withdrawal. They drained \$625M in two transactions — and it went **unnoticed for six days**, surfacing only when a user could not withdraw. This is a key compromise wearing a bridge costume; the code worked exactly as designed.
- **Wormhole, February 2022, \$326M (a signature-verification bug).** Wormhole's Solana-side contract was supposed to verify that a batch of "guardian" signatures had genuinely approved a mint. A flaw let the attacker **forge a message that passed the verification without legitimate guardian approval**, then use it to mint roughly 120,000 wrapped ETH on Solana out of thin air — tokens not backed by any real deposit. Jump Crypto, which backed Wormhole, refilled the \$326M hole to keep the bridge solvent.
- **Nomad Bridge, August 2022, \$190M (an initialization bug, then a free-for-all).** A routine upgrade initialized a trusted root to **zero**, which had the effect of marking *every* message as already-proven valid. The result was unique in hacking history: once the first attacker showed the trick, **anyone could copy a previous withdrawal transaction, swap in their own address, and replay it** to withdraw funds. Hundreds of opportunistic "copycats" piled in, draining the bridge in a chaotic crowd-looting that makes Nomad the textbook case of how an immutable, public, composable system turns a single bug into a stampede.

**The defense.** Bridges are hardened by raising the signing quorum, spreading keys across independent hardware and people, monitoring the validator set for unexpected changes, rigorously auditing the *verification* path (the code that decides a message is legitimate), and guarding the upgrade/initialization path as tightly as the funds themselves. The cross-chain seam is also where laundering becomes detectable — a deposit-here/withdraw-there pattern — which we treat from the tracing side in [cross-chain tracing: bridges and the USDT rails](/blog/trading/onchain/cross-chain-tracing-bridges-and-the-usdt-rails).

**The footprint.** Bridge exploits show up as **large, anomalous mint or unlock events with no corresponding deposit on the other side** — value appearing on the destination chain that was never locked on the source, or unlocks that no legitimate user initiated. With Nomad, the footprint was a flood of near-identical withdrawal transactions from dozens of fresh addresses, all replaying the same shape — a swarm rather than a single strike.

#### Worked example: the \$625M Ronin bridge via five compromised keys

Ronin's bridge required **5 of 9** validator signatures to release funds. Lazarus obtained five private keys: four through a spear-phishing campaign that delivered malware to Sky Mavis employees, and a fifth from an access grant that had been issued to a third party and never revoked. With **5 of 9** in hand, the attackers signed two withdrawals totaling **173,600 ETH and 25,500,000 USDC** — worth approximately **\$625,000,000** at the time. No contract bug was exploited; the bridge's code verified five valid signatures and dutifully released the funds, exactly as written. The theft sat undetected for **six days** because nothing on-chain looked malformed — only unusual. The intuition: a multisig is only as strong as the *independence* of its keys, and when one group can phish a majority, "5 of 9" provides no security at all.

## Vector 5: Access control and key compromise

**The mechanism.** This is the broadest and, by dollar volume, the most damaging category — and it is barely a "smart-contract" issue at all. **Access control** means: which addresses are allowed to call which privileged functions? Every serious protocol has powerful functions — pause the contract, upgrade the code, mint new tokens, drain the treasury to a "recovery" address. These are gated to an owner, an admin, or a multisig. Two things go wrong:

1. **Missing or broken access control.** A privileged function is left callable by *anyone* — the developers forgot to add the `onlyOwner` check, or an initialization function that sets the owner can be called a second time by an attacker to seize control. The contract then happily lets a stranger upgrade it or mint tokens.
2. **Key compromise.** The keys that *legitimately* hold the privileged role are stolen — through phishing, malware, a malicious insider, a compromised cloud server, or a tampered signing interface. The attacker then signs entirely valid transactions. As with Ronin, the code is innocent; the keys are in the wrong hands.

This is the category that makes "the code was audited" meaningless, because the failure is operational, not in the source. Most of the largest losses in crypto history live here.

It helps to see *why* this category dominates the dollar leaderboard while reentrancy and oracle bugs grab the technical headlines. A protocol bug requires the attacker to find a genuinely novel flaw in code that has often been audited by multiple firms — hard, uncertain, and increasingly rare as tooling matures. A key compromise requires the attacker to fool one human, or compromise one laptop, or find one stale permission that was never revoked — and the payoff is the *entire* treasury, not just the slice a clever loop can extract before the protocol pauses. For a professional adversary optimizing return on effort, the math is obvious: the weakest link in a well-audited protocol is almost never the Solidity. It is the people who hold the keys, the infrastructure those keys run on, and the processes by which a transaction gets signed. That is precisely why the largest single thefts — Bybit, Ronin, DMM, WazirX — cluster here rather than in the reentrancy column, and why a state-sponsored group with the patience to run a months-long phishing campaign treats operational compromise as its primary trade.

**The documented cases.** The **Bybit \$1.46B** theft of February 2025 — the largest ever — was a cold-wallet signing compromise: the signers were shown one transaction in their interface while a different one, transferring control of the wallet's assets to the attacker, was actually executed. The keys themselves were not "stolen" in the classic sense; the *signing process* was subverted so that legitimate signers approved a malicious action. The **Ronin \$625M** (above) was a straight validator-key compromise. The **DMM Bitcoin \$305M** (2024) and **WazirX \$230M** (2024) were also infrastructure and multisig signing compromises, both attributed to Lazarus. Notice the pattern: state-sponsored attackers overwhelmingly favor *operational* compromise over contract bugs, because phishing a human is more reliable than finding a novel reentrancy.

**The footprint.** A key compromise produces transactions that are **individually valid but behaviorally anomalous** — a privileged function called by the right key but at the wrong time, moving funds to a never-before-seen address, often a single enormous transfer rather than a clever multi-step exploit. There is no flash loan, no reentrancy staircase, no price spike. There is simply the treasury moving, correctly signed, to a fresh address with no history — which is exactly why the *funding source and destination* of that address become the investigation's center of gravity.

#### Worked example: the \$1.46B Bybit key compromise versus the \$70M Curve reentrancy

Put the two extremes side by side. The **Curve reentrancy** was a *protocol hack*: a flaw in the compiled code let attacker contracts re-enter and drain roughly **\$70,000,000** across pools. The defense is technical — fix the compiler, re-audit, add guards — and a chunk was returned because much of it was white-hat MEV activity. The **Bybit compromise** was an *operational hack*: no contract flaw at all, just a subverted signing flow that let attackers move roughly **\$1,460,000,000** — more than **20×** Curve's loss — out of a cold wallet with valid signatures. The defense is organizational: harden signing infrastructure, verify what you sign, segment keys. The intuition: the two largest categories of loss require *opposite* defenses, which is why a protocol can be flawlessly audited and still lose more than any code bug ever cost — because the attacker went after the people and the process, not the program.

## Vector 6: Logic and math bugs

**The mechanism.** This is the catch-all for exploits that abuse the protocol's *intended* logic in unintended ways — not reentrancy, not a bad oracle, just a subtle error in how the contract reasons about its own accounting. Two flavors dominate:

- **Math / arithmetic bugs.** A calculation overflows (a number wraps around past its maximum back to zero), underflows, or rounds in a way the attacker can exploit at scale. In older Solidity, an unguarded subtraction could turn a tiny balance into an astronomically large one; in AMMs, a flawed square-root or liquidity computation can let an attacker mint or withdraw far more than they deposited.
- **Logic / state bugs.** The contract's bookkeeping can be pushed into a state the developers never imagined — a "donation" that corrupts an internal ratio, a function that can be called in an order that breaks an invariant, a check that compares the wrong two values.

**The documented cases.** **Euler Finance, March 2023, \$197M** is the canonical *donation bug*. Euler had a function that let a user "donate" to a reserve, and another that handled liquidation of unhealthy positions. By using a flash loan to set up a position and then **donating** in a way that made their own account look unhealthy, the attacker triggered a self-liquidation that — because of how the donation had corrupted the internal accounting — paid *them* a windfall instead of penalizing them. The flaw was in the *interaction* between two individually reasonable functions. Remarkably, after weeks of public on-chain negotiation, the attacker returned essentially all \$197M.

**Cetus, May 2025, \$220M** is the canonical *math overflow*. Cetus, an AMM on the Sui chain, had a flaw in the library that computed liquidity. By exploiting an **overflow in the math**, the attacker tricked the pool into accepting a tiny token deposit as if it represented an enormous amount of liquidity, then withdrew real assets against that phantom position — draining roughly \$220M, a large part of which was subsequently frozen by the Sui validators. The lesson of both cases: the bug is not a stolen key or a manipulated outsider price; it is the protocol's own arithmetic or state machine being walked into a corner it was never tested in.

**The footprint.** Logic and math bugs are the subtlest to spot in advance and often the clearest in hindsight. The footprint is a transaction where **a small input produces a wildly disproportionate output** — a deposit of a few tokens followed by a withdrawal of millions, or an internal balance that jumps to an absurd value. On the trace, the *conservation* you expect (money in roughly equals money out) is violated in one specific function call, and that violated invariant is the fingerprint.

## Vector 7: Signature and approval exploits

**The mechanism.** The final vector usually targets *users* rather than protocols, and it is the one most likely to hit an ordinary reader directly. To use most DeFi, you must first grant a contract an **approval** — permission to move a specific token from your wallet on your behalf. (We covered this in detail in [tokens, on-chain transfers, and approvals](/blog/trading/onchain/tokens-onchain-transfers-and-approvals).) Approvals are necessary and routine, but they are also a standing liability: if you approve a malicious or compromised contract — or grant an *unlimited* approval to one that later turns hostile — it can move your tokens whenever it likes, with no further confirmation from you.

The modern twist is the **off-chain signature**, especially `permit` and `permit2`. These let you *sign a message* (which costs no gas and produces no on-chain transaction you would notice) that grants an approval, which an attacker then submits on-chain themselves. A phishing site presents a transaction that looks like "connect wallet" or "claim airdrop," but the message you sign is actually an approval — or worse, a `setApprovalForAll` on your NFTs or a transfer authorization — handing the attacker a key to your wallet. You see nothing leave; then, minutes or weeks later, your tokens are gone.

**The defense.** This is the vector with the most reader-level leverage. Treat **every signature request as a transaction** — read what you are actually signing, not what the website *says* you are signing. Grant **finite, per-use approvals** rather than unlimited ones. **Periodically revoke** stale approvals (tools like Revoke.cash and Etherscan's token-approval checker list every approval your wallet has ever granted). And be deeply suspicious of any site that asks you to sign a message to "verify," "claim," or "connect" — legitimate connection does not require signing a transfer authorization.

**The footprint.** Approval-drain attacks show up as an `Approval` event to a suspicious spender followed, sometimes much later, by a `TransferFrom` that empties the wallet — and crucially, the draining transaction's gas is paid by the *attacker*, not the victim, because the victim never knowingly initiated it. At scale, a single malicious spender contract will show **many victims' tokens flowing out to one collector address**, a fan-in pattern that is itself the signature of a phishing campaign rather than a one-off.

## The on-chain footprint: how an exploit reveals itself

We have now seen all seven vectors. Here is the payoff that ties the series' thesis — *the ledger is public; learn to read it* — to this taxonomy. However different the seven mechanisms are, they collapse to a small set of footprints, because they all have to do the same physical thing: **move funds out of a contract or wallet to an address the attacker controls.** That movement is public and permanent. You cannot hack quietly on a transparent ledger.

![Detection matrix mapping three exploit footprints to where to see them, why they signal an exploit, and the false positive](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-defi-hack-8.png)

Three footprints do most of the work:

1. **A fresh attacker contract.** As we established in the Foundations, a serious exploit is orchestrated by a contract the attacker deployed — frequently *minutes* before the strike. Real users do not deploy a brand-new contract just to interact with a protocol. On Etherscan, the contract-creation tab and the contract's age are the first things to check. (False positive: MEV arbitrage bots also deploy fresh throwaway contracts, so freshness alone is suggestive, not damning.)
2. **An anomalous huge outflow in one transaction.** Exploits move money fast — often millions in a single transaction, dwarfing the protocol's normal flow, and producing a step-function drop in its total value locked. Etherscan's token-transfer view and flow-alert tools like Arkham surface this immediately. (False positive: a large legitimate migration or treasury rebalance can look similar, so size must be paired with the *who*.)
3. **A suspicious funding source.** The attacker's wallet and the gas that funded the attacker contract almost always trace back to a **mixer (like Tornado Cash) or a no-KYC exchange**, because legitimate deployers fund from labeled, KYC'd, or doxxed wallets. Labeling tools like Arkham and Nansen, and the attribution discipline covered in [labeling and attribution](/blog/trading/onchain/labeling-and-attribution), make this funding trail visible. (False positive: privacy-conscious but entirely honest users also use mixers, so a mixer source raises a flag, it does not close a case.)

The detective's move is **conjunction**: any one footprint is noisy, but a *fresh contract, funded from a mixer, that produces a multimillion-dollar single-transaction outflow from a protocol* is, taken together, almost diagnostic of an exploit. Spotting that conjunction in real time is the subject of the sibling post [detecting an exploit in progress](/blog/trading/onchain/detecting-an-exploit-in-progress).

## How to read it: a walkthrough of an exploit transaction after the fact

Let us make this concrete with a high-level, defensive walkthrough of how an analyst reconstructs a hack from the public record — using a documented exploit, not a recipe for one. The tools are the standard kit from [the on-chain tooling landscape](/blog/trading/onchain/the-onchain-tooling-landscape): Etherscan for the EVM, plus a labeling tool like Arkham.

**Step 1 — Anchor on the exploit transaction.** Post-mortems and security firms publish the hash of the main exploit transaction within hours. Paste it into Etherscan. The top of the page tells you the basics: which contract was called, how much value moved, how much gas was burned, and the timestamp. A genuine exploit transaction usually burns an unusually large amount of gas (it is doing a lot of work) and moves an amount of value wildly out of proportion to a normal interaction.

**Step 2 — Read the internal transaction trace.** This is where the mechanism reveals itself. The "Internal Txns" and the decoded trace show the *sequence of calls inside the transaction*. A flash-loan attack opens with a giant borrow and closes with the matching repayment. A reentrancy shows the same function called over and over in a staircase. An oracle manipulation shows a large swap that moves a price, then a borrow or liquidation that consumes it. You are reading the attack's choreography, step by step, exactly as it executed.

**Step 3 — Identify the attacker contract and trace its birth.** The contract that initiated the calls is the attacker contract. Click through to it: when was it deployed, and by which wallet? A contract deployed minutes before the strike, by a wallet with almost no history, is the tell. Now trace that wallet *backwards* — where did its gas come from? This is where you typically arrive at a mixer withdrawal or a no-KYC exchange, the funding fingerprint from footprint #3.

**Step 4 — Follow the value out.** Trace the stolen funds *forwards* from the attacker contract. You will watch the drain land in a collector wallet, then begin the laundering arc: swapped to ETH, split across many fresh addresses, bridged to another chain, funneled toward a mixer. This forward trace is the on-ramp to the full tracing discipline in [tracing stolen funds step by step](/blog/trading/onchain/tracing-stolen-funds-step-by-step). For the canonical worked end-to-end example, the [case study of the Ronin bridge hack](/blog/trading/onchain/case-study-the-ronin-bridge-hack) walks the entire path from the compromised keys to the laundering.

**Step 5 — Classify it.** With the trace in hand, you can now place the hack in the taxonomy. Repeated self-calls and a drained pool? Reentrancy. A bookending flash loan and a manipulated price? Oracle manipulation, flash-loan-amplified. A bridge minting tokens with no matching deposit? Bridge exploit. A perfectly valid privileged transfer to a fresh address with no clever steps? Key compromise. The taxonomy is not academic — it is the lens that turns a wall of hex into a one-sentence diagnosis.

What you should *not* do, and what this walkthrough deliberately does not provide, is the forward direction — how to *find* an unpatched flaw and weaponize it. Reconstructing a documented, public exploit to understand and defend against it is forensics; building a working exploit against a live target is a crime. We stay firmly on the forensic side of that line.

## The data: who is losing what, and to whom

Two charts ground the taxonomy in scale. First, the leaderboard of the biggest individual hacks.

Two things jump out. First, the **sheer concentration**: Bybit's \$1.46B alone is more than double the next-largest, and the top three (Bybit, Ronin, Poly Network) account for the bulk of the chart. Second, the **attribution**: a striking share of the largest hacks — Bybit, Ronin, DMM, WazirX — are attributed to a single state actor, North Korea's Lazarus group (shown in red), versus grey-hat, individual, or unknown attackers (amber). The mega-hacks are not random script-kiddie events; they are increasingly the work of a professionalized, state-sponsored operation that treats crypto theft as a funding program.

#### Worked example: how a single state actor compares to a year of all other theft

Stack the numbers. In **2022**, total crypto stolen was about **\$3.8B**, of which Lazarus/DPRK accounted for roughly **\$1.7B** — nearly **45%** of *everything* stolen that year, from a single group. By **2024**, total theft was about **\$2.2B** with DPRK at roughly **\$1.34B** — over **60%**. Cumulatively, DPRK has stolen well over **\$5,000,000,000** in crypto across recent years. Put differently: the \$1.46B Bybit hack alone exceeds the *entire* global crypto-theft total of 2023 (\$1.7B) by relative scale and nearly matches it outright. The intuition: when you study DeFi hacks, you are not studying a diffuse crime wave — you are, more often than not, studying the operational playbook of one extraordinarily well-resourced adversary.

The second chart puts the annual totals and the DPRK share side by side over time.

![Grouped bar chart of crypto stolen per year with the North Korea-attributed share overlaid](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-defi-hack-7.png)

The trend is sobering: total theft does not fall in a straight line (it spiked in 2021–2022 with the DeFi and bridge boom, dipped in 2023, and rose again in 2024), but the **DPRK share grows relentlessly** — from a sliver in 2020 to the dominant component by 2024. This is the macro context for everything in this post: the vectors are recurring, the tooling to read them is mature, and the most dangerous practitioners are getting better, not worse.

#### Worked example: sizing a protocol's exposure before you deposit

Turn the taxonomy into a personal risk check. Suppose you are considering depositing **\$25,000** into a new yield protocol advertising a **40% APY**. Run the seven vectors as a checklist. Does it price collateral off a single on-chain pool (oracle risk)? Is it a bridge holding pooled collateral (the highest-value honeypot)? Who holds the admin keys, and is it a 2-of-3 multisig run by anonymous founders (access-control risk)? Has the code been audited, *and* the compiler and dependencies (the Curve lesson)? If the protocol holds, say, **\$5,000,000** in total value locked and three of those seven flags are red, your \$25,000 is sitting in a honeypot with three open doors. A 40% APY is roughly **\$10,000** of expected annual yield on your \$25,000 — and a single successful exploit takes **100%** of the principal in one transaction. The intuition: the taxonomy is not just forensics after the fact; it is a pre-mortem you can run before you ever click "deposit," and the expected-value math rarely favors the honeypot.

## Common misconceptions

**"If the contract was audited, it's safe."** An audit reduces risk; it does not eliminate it, and it does nothing about the largest category of loss. Audits examine the *source code* — but Curve was drained through a *compiler* bug beneath audited source, and Bybit, Ronin, DMM, and WazirX (well over \$2.6B combined) were *operational* key compromises that no code audit could have caught. Audits are necessary and worth demanding; treating them as a safety guarantee is a category error.

**"Hacks are too technical to follow as a normal person."** The *mechanisms* can be deep, but the *footprints* are not. You do not need to understand the exact Vyper mis-compilation to see, on Etherscan, that a fresh contract funded from a mixer drained \$70M from a pool in one transaction. The taxonomy in this post is deliberately memorizable precisely so that a non-engineer can classify a hack from its shape and its trail.

**"Crypto is mostly crime, so all this is just criminals robbing criminals."** The opposite is true at the aggregate level. Illicit transactions are a *tiny* share of all on-chain volume — on the order of **0.14% to 0.62%** depending on the year, per Chainalysis — meaning the overwhelming majority of activity is legitimate. The hacks are enormous in absolute dollars and genuinely matter, but they are a sliver of the flow. The reason they get so much attention is that they are *visible* and *quantified* in a way TradFi fraud rarely is — which is the whole edge of a transparent ledger.

**"Flash loans should be banned — they cause the hacks."** Flash loans are an amplifier, not a cause. The underlying flaw — a manipulable oracle, a logic bug — is the actual vulnerability; the flash loan just makes exploiting it economical for anyone. Banning flash loans would break legitimate arbitrage and collateral-swapping while leaving the real bug in place for any whale to exploit. The correct fix is to design protocols that are safe even when an attacker can momentarily command unlimited capital.

**"Returned funds mean no harm done."** Several large hacks (Poly Network, Euler, much of Curve) ended with funds returned, often after public negotiation. That is genuinely better than the alternative, but it is not "no harm": protocols were frozen, users were locked out, confidence cratered, and the return depended entirely on the attacker's choice — which, for state-sponsored groups like Lazarus, never comes. Recovery is a happy accident of *some* exploits, not a feature of the system.

**"Each new hack is a brand-new kind of attack."** This is the misconception this entire post exists to dispel. The *details* are always novel — a specific Vyper version, a particular donation function, one bridge's quorum — but the *vector* is almost never new. Every hack in the leaderboard fits one of the seven categories above, and most fit the first six; Vector 7 (approvals) drains users rather than protocols. When a new exploit hits the news, the productive question is not "what unprecedented thing just happened" but "which of the seven is this, and what's the specific twist this time?" Treating each incident as sui generis is how you stay perpetually surprised; treating it as a known shape with a new detail is how you learn to read the chain. The mega-hacks of the last few years did not introduce an eighth vector — they introduced a more capable, better-funded *attacker* running the same seven plays.

## The playbook: what to do with the taxonomy

Whether you are an investor sizing risk or an analyst reconstructing an incident, the taxonomy converts directly into action. Here is the if-then checklist.

**If you are an investor deciding whether to deposit:**
- **Signal:** the protocol prices collateral off a single on-chain pool's spot price. → **Read:** oracle-manipulation exposure. → **Action:** require a TWAP or external oracle (Chainlink) before depositing; if it spot-prices, treat any advertised APY as compensation for tail risk, not free money. → **False positive:** a deep, well-distributed pool with a TWAP is far harder to manipulate — depth and time-weighting are the mitigants.
- **Signal:** the protocol is a bridge or holds large pooled cross-chain collateral. → **Read:** highest-value-honeypot, key-compromise and verification-bug exposure. → **Action:** check the signing quorum and key distribution; prefer bridges with high quorums, independent validators, and a track record. → **False positive:** a battle-tested bridge with a high, well-distributed quorum is meaningfully safer than a new one with a 2-of-3.
- **Signal:** anonymous team, upgradeable contracts, admin controlled by a low-threshold multisig. → **Read:** access-control / key-compromise risk — the largest loss category. → **Action:** size your position as if the admin key could be compromised tomorrow, because that is the modal mega-hack. → **False positive:** a timelock on upgrades and a widely-distributed, doxxed multisig genuinely reduce this.

**If you are a user protecting your own wallet:**
- **Signal:** a site asks you to sign a message to "claim," "verify," or "connect." → **Read:** possible approval / permit phishing (Vector 7). → **Action:** read the actual signature payload; never sign a transfer or `setApprovalForAll` you did not intend. → **False positive:** legitimate logins use signed messages too — but they never request token-transfer authorization.
- **Signal:** you have years of accumulated, unlimited token approvals. → **Read:** standing liability if any approved contract turns hostile. → **Action:** periodically revoke stale and unlimited approvals via Revoke.cash or Etherscan. → **False positive:** active approvals to protocols you currently use are fine — revoke the dormant ones.

**If you are an analyst reconstructing a hack:**
- **Signal:** a protocol's TVL drops in a step function. → **Read:** likely exploit in progress or just completed. → **Action:** find the exploit transaction, read its internal trace, and classify by the choreography (flash-loan bookends → amplified attack; repeated self-calls → reentrancy; price spike + borrow → oracle; valid privileged transfer to a fresh address → key compromise). → **False positive:** a large legitimate migration or a depeg-driven exit can mimic the outflow — confirm with the *who* and the *contract age*.
- **Signal:** a fresh contract funded from a mixer produces a multimillion-dollar single-transaction outflow. → **Read:** the diagnostic conjunction of all three footprints. → **Action:** anchor on the attacker contract, trace funding backwards and stolen value forwards, and hand the trail to the laundering and recovery workflow. → **False positive:** an MEV bot can satisfy the fresh-contract-plus-mixer-gas part — the multimillion-dollar *theft* from a protocol is what closes the case.

The one rule that survives all of it: **on a public ledger, an attacker can hide their identity but not their actions.** Every vector in this post ends with funds moving in plain sight. The taxonomy tells you what to look for; the chain shows you it happened.
