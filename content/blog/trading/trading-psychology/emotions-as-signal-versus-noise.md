> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A gut feeling is not automatically wisdom and not automatically tilt; the skill that separates good traders from blown-up ones is *discriminating* an informative feeling from a misleading one, before the click.
>
> - Emotion can be real information. Antonio Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis and the Iowa Gambling Task show the body flags a bad bet (a rising skin-conductance response by about trial 10) long before the conscious mind can explain why, around card 80. Cutting emotion out entirely makes decisions *worse*, not more rational.
> - But a gut is only trustworthy under specific conditions. Kahneman and Klein's "conditions for intuitive expertise" say intuition earns trust only in a **high-validity** environment learned through **fast, clear feedback** — and most market predictions are low-validity, which is exactly why raw gut fails at forecasting while it can excel at execution.
> - The practical rule: a feeling leans **signal** when it arises inside a rep-heavy domain, about execution or risk you have seen thousands of times, in a calm state — and leans **noise** when it shows up right after a loss (tilt), in a novel situation, or at high arousal (fear or FOMO).
> - The one habit to remember: **before acting on any feeling, run the 3-gate triage — Is this my domain? Am I calm? Would I tell a peer to do exactly this? — and if any gate fails, stand down and log it.**

Every trader has had both experiences. One afternoon a position you are already in starts to feel *wrong* — nothing in the news, nothing on the chart you can point to, just a quiet certainty that you should be flat. You cut it. An hour later it gaps against where you were, and you avoided a nasty loss. Your gut saved you.

Another afternoon you take a loss, your face gets hot, and a different certainty arrives: *this next trade is the one, I can feel it.* You size up. You lose again, bigger. Your gut destroyed you.

Same organ, opposite outcome. The lazy advice — "trust your gut" from one guru, "never trade on emotion" from another — is useless precisely because both feelings *felt identical from the inside*. The real skill is not turning emotion off. It is telling the two apart. That is what this post is about.

![The discrimination funnel: one urge, two sources, two reads](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-1.webp)

The diagram above is the mental model for everything that follows. A single strong urge to act can flow from two completely different sources: **trained pattern-memory** — thousands of hours of reps compressed into a body signal — or **acute stress** — fear, fear-of-missing-out, or the itch for revenge after a loss. From the inside they can feel the same. A deliberate triage is what routes one to "informative signal, act on it" and the other to "tilt, stand down". Learn to run that triage and you keep the value of intuition without paying for its failures.

## Foundations: the building blocks of a gut feeling

Before we can sort good feelings from bad, we need to define the pieces cleanly. No finance or neuroscience background is assumed here; a professional can skim.

**An emotion** is a fast, whole-body response to a situation — a change in your heart rate, breathing, muscle tension, and attention that happens *before* you consciously reason about anything. It evolved as a rapid decision shortcut: your ancestors who felt fear and ran survived longer than those who stopped to calculate the probability of the rustling bush being a predator.

**A gut feeling** (or *intuition*) is emotion applied to a decision. It is the sensation of "knowing" something without being able to walk through the logic. The key word is *without the logic* — the reasoning is not absent, it is just not available to your conscious narrator.

**A somatic marker** is the mechanism behind that. Coined by neuroscientist Antonio Damasio, a *somatic marker* is a bodily signal (a "gut" sensation, literally *soma* = body) that your brain has attached to the remembered outcome of a past situation. When a similar situation reappears, the marker re-fires and biases your choice — steering you toward or away — faster than deliberate thought can run.

![How a gut feeling gets made (Damasio's somatic marker)](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-2.webp)

The pipeline above is how the machine works. You live through thousands of trades and see how each turned out. Your brain tags each experience with an emotional marker — this kind of setup felt good and paid; that kind felt queasy and hurt. Those tags get stored. Later, a *similar* setup appears, the matching marker fires as a body sensation (a tightness, an ease, a pull), and a decision arrives before your conscious reasoning has finished loading. That is a gut feeling. It is not mysticism. **It is compressed experience.**

Now the two words at the center of this post:

**Signal** is the part of a feeling that carries real information about the decision — a genuine pattern your experience has detected. **Noise** is the part that carries no information about *this* decision, generated instead by your internal state: how recently you lost, how afraid or greedy you are, how tired you are. A signal is *about the market*. Noise is *about you*.

Two more terms we will lean on:

- **Arousal** is your level of physiological activation — calm and relaxed at the low end, alert and focused in the middle, panicked or euphoric at the high end. It is the volume knob on your nervous system.
- **Tilt** is a poker term that migrated to trading: a state of emotional agitation, usually after a loss or a run of them, in which you abandon your process and make worse decisions than you know how to make. Tilt is noise turned all the way up.
- **Validity** (of an environment) is how reliably the cues you can observe actually predict the outcomes you care about. A world where the same cue leads to the same result is *high-validity*; a world where the link is loose, noisy, or keeps changing is *low-validity*. Hold onto this one — it is the hinge the whole argument turns on.

#### Worked example: the simplest possible case

Suppose you have placed the exact same trade — buy the open of a specific stock when it gaps up more than 2% on earnings, sell at the close — 1,000 times over three years. You have a spreadsheet. It wins 58% of the time, and your average win is \$120 while your average loss is \$100.

One morning the setup appears, but something feels off and you hesitate. Is that hesitation signal or noise? Here it is probably **signal-rich**: you are inside a domain where you have 1,000 labeled repetitions and fast feedback (you know by the close whether each worked). Your body may have detected a wrinkle — unusually thin pre-market volume, a market-wide risk-off tone — that your conscious mind has not named yet. The feeling is worth pausing on.

Now suppose instead the feeling is "I should double my size today because I'm *due* for a big one." That is **noise**: nothing in 1,000 reps says yesterday's result changes today's edge. The intuition that you are "due" is a story your emotions wrote, not a pattern your data holds.

The intuition to carry forward: *a feeling that points at the setup can be information; a feeling that points at you — your streak, your mood, your need — is almost always noise.*

## 1. The science: your body really does keep score

The strongest evidence that emotion is not the opposite of good judgment comes from watching what happens when you remove it. Damasio studied patients who, through injury or surgery, had lost function in a small region behind the forehead — the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex**, the part of the brain that ties emotional markers to decisions. These patients kept their IQ, their memory, their language. What they lost was the ability to *feel* their way through choices. And their lives fell apart: they made catastrophic personal and financial decisions, deliberating endlessly over trivial choices, unable to learn from bad outcomes. Pure reason without emotion did not produce a Vulcan super-decider. It produced a person who could not choose.

The most famous of these patients, described in Damasio's *Descartes' Error*, was a man he calls **Elliot** — a successful professional who had a benign brain tumor removed, taking a piece of the ventromedial cortex with it. Elliot's intelligence tested as high as ever. But he could no longer run his life: he would spend an entire afternoon paralyzed over how to sort documents, get lost in trivialities, and — most relevantly for us — he made a string of ruinous financial decisions, sinking his savings into obviously bad ventures and going bankrupt. In one telling session, Damasio described a gruesome accident scene to him; Elliot could discuss it with perfect intellectual detachment and then remarked that he knew the topic *should* stir feeling in him, but it no longer did. He had the facts and lost the feelings, and with the feelings went the judgment. Elliot is the human proof that a trader who genuinely felt nothing would not be a disciplined machine — he would be someone who cannot tell a good bet from a bad one at all.

To pin this down in the lab, Antonio Bechara and the Damasios built the **Iowa Gambling Task** (Bechara, Damasio, Damasio & Anderson, 1994). You sit in front of four decks of cards — A, B, C, D — and flip cards for play money. Each flip pays you something and sometimes charges you a penalty. The decks are rigged.

![The body knows first: anticipatory SCR leads conscious knowledge](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-3.webp)

Here is the setup and what they found. Decks A and B are the "bad" decks: each card pays a fat \$100, but the hidden penalties are brutal, so that over any 10 cards you *net about −\$250*. Decks C and D are the "good" decks: each card pays a modest \$50, the penalties are small, and over any 10 cards you *net about +\$250*. The trap is that the bad decks feel better card to card — bigger wins — while quietly bleeding you. Sound familiar? It is the exact shape of a negative-expectancy trading strategy that throws off exciting winners.

The famous result, published as Bechara, Damasio, Tranel & Damasio (1997), "Deciding advantageously before knowing the advantageous strategy," is the blue curve above. Players were wired to a **skin-conductance** sensor — a measure of emotional arousal, the same technology behind a lie detector; when you have an emotional reaction, your skin sweats microscopically and conducts electricity better. Healthy players began generating an **anticipatory skin-conductance response** — a spike of stress *as their hand hovered over the bad decks* — by around **trial 10**. Their bodies flinched at the losing decks before they had any idea which decks were losing.

Conscious knowledge lagged far behind (the gray dashed curve). Players did not enter the "hunch" stage — vaguely sensing some decks were dangerous — until around card 50, and only roughly **70% ever reached the "conceptual" stage** where they could actually explain the rigging, at around **card 80**. The remaining ~30% never figured out the logic at all — *yet still played the good decks*, steered by a body signal they could not articulate. The title of the paper is the whole finding: they were **deciding advantageously before knowing the advantageous strategy.**

The control condition seals it. The ventromedial-prefrontal patients — the ones who could not attach emotional markers — never developed the anticipatory response, and kept choosing the bad decks even in the minority of cases where they *could* verbally explain that A and B were the losing decks. They knew the right answer and their hand went to the wrong deck anyway. Knowledge without a working gut did not fix their behavior.

#### Worked example: the Iowa Gambling Task in dollars

Let us make the decks concrete, because the arithmetic is the whole point. Say you flip 10 cards from a bad deck (A or B). You collect 10 × \$100 = \$1,000 in card payouts. But the hidden penalties over those 10 cards total about \$1,250. Your net is \$1,000 − \$1,250 = **−\$250**.

Now flip 10 cards from a good deck (C or D). You collect 10 × \$50 = \$500 in payouts. The hidden penalties total only about \$250. Your net is \$500 − \$250 = **+\$250**.

The swing between a bad-deck decade and a good-deck decade is \$250 − (−\$250) = **\$500 per 10 cards** — enormous. A player who lets the anticipatory flinch pull their hand away from A and B, even without understanding why, captures that \$500 edge. A player who overrides the flinch because "the \$100 payouts feel great" hands it back.

The intuition to carry forward: *in the lab, the gut is not the irrational part — it is the part that priced the losing decks correctly before the conscious mind could, and the traders who feel the flinch and honor it are the ones the data rewards.*

So far this is a case *for* trusting the gut. Now comes the hard turn.

## 2. When a gut is actually trustworthy — the Kahneman-Klein conditions

If the body is such a good early-warning system, why do so many traders lose money following their instincts? Because the Iowa Gambling Task quietly satisfied two conditions that markets usually do not.

This was the subject of a remarkable paper by two researchers who started as intellectual opponents. Gary Klein studied firefighters, nurses, and chess masters and found their intuition was often astonishingly good. Daniel Kahneman spent a career cataloguing the ways human intuition is systematically, predictably wrong. In 2009 they wrote a joint paper — Kahneman & Klein, "Conditions for Intuitive Expertise: A Failure to Disagree" (*American Psychologist*) — in which they hammered out exactly when a professional's gut is worth trusting. Their answer has two parts.

![When is a gut trustworthy? The two Kahneman-Klein conditions](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-4.webp)

Intuition earns trust only when **both** boxes are checked:

1. **A high-validity environment.** There must be stable, repeating regularities linking the cues you can perceive to the outcomes you care about. A chess position genuinely predicts good moves. A patient's vital signs genuinely predict a crash. If the cue-outcome link is real and reasonably stable, there is something for skilled intuition to learn.
2. **Prolonged practice with rapid, clear feedback.** You must have had the chance to learn those regularities through many repetitions where the result comes back fast and unambiguous. The chess player sees the game end. The nurse sees the patient recover or not. Feedback that is delayed, noisy, or never arrives cannot train an intuition, no matter how many "reps" you log.

Now overlay a market. What is the *validity* of the environment for, say, "will this stock be higher in six months?" It is low. The link between what you can observe today and the price in six months is buried under noise — luck, macro shocks, flows, other people's psychology, sheer randomness. And the feedback is slow and ambiguous: you find out in six months, by which time a hundred other things have changed, so you cannot cleanly learn *what* your gut got right or wrong. Both conditions fail. This is why the gut of even a brilliant, experienced investor is close to worthless as a six-month price forecast — the environment simply does not contain a stable signal for intuition to have learned.

But move to a different question — "is this order about to get run over; is this fill bad; is the book thinning out under me right now?" — and the picture flips. For an experienced scalper or market-maker, execution is a **high-validity, fast-feedback** environment. The cue (order-flow, the shape of the book, the speed of the tape) is tightly and quickly linked to the outcome (you got filled well or badly, the move continued or reversed within seconds). Thousands of reps with feedback in seconds. *That* gut is trained on something real.

The lesson is not "trust your gut" or "distrust your gut." It is: **trust your gut exactly as far as the environment it was trained in is valid, and no further.** The same trader's instinct can be world-class about the next 30 seconds of the tape and pure superstition about the next 30 days of the economy.

#### Worked example: two forecasts, one trader

Suppose a veteran trader has 15 years and, conservatively, 200,000 execution decisions behind her — every one with feedback inside a minute. Say her intuition on "should I lean on this bid or pull my offer right now" is right 60% of the time when a coin flip would be 50%. On a scalp risking \$200 to make \$200, that 10-percentage-point edge is worth, per trade, roughly 0.60 × \$200 − 0.40 × \$200 = **+\$40 of expected value**. Over 40 discretionary execution calls in a day that is +\$1,600 of edge she can harvest by trusting a gut trained in a valid environment.

Now the same trader gets a strong feeling that "the market feels toppy, I should be short for the next quarter." How many clean, fast-feedback reps does she have on *quarter-ahead macro calls*? Maybe 60 in her whole career, each with feedback so slow and noisy she could never tell skill from luck. Her true edge there is plausibly 50% — no edge — even though the feeling is just as vivid. If she sizes that hunch like her execution edge and it is actually a coin flip, her expected value is 0.50 × \$X − 0.50 × \$X = **\$0 before costs, and negative after fees and slippage.**

The intuition to carry forward: *the vividness of a feeling tells you nothing about its validity; the number of fast-feedback reps behind it tells you almost everything.*

## 3. The discrimination framework: sorting signal from noise

We now have the raw material to build a practical filter. A feeling is more likely to be **signal** when it comes from a valid, well-trained source and a clear head, and more likely to be **noise** when it comes from an untrained source or a hijacked nervous system. Here is the checklist, laid out as the two columns you are really choosing between.

![The discrimination checklist: when a feeling leans noise vs signal](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-5.webp)

Read the right column first — the conditions under which a feeling **leans signal, and deserves to be trusted:**

- **It arises inside a domain where you have thousands of reps.** Not "I've been trading five years" in general, but "I have taken *this specific setup* hundreds or thousands of times."
- **It is about execution or risk you have seen constantly** — the shape of a fill, a stop that should move, a position that is quietly misbehaving — not a grand directional prophecy.
- **It arises in a calm, rested state** — the same feeling from the same trained source is far more likely to be real information when your nervous system is not flooded.
- **It survives the "tell a peer" test** — you can describe the feeling to a respected colleague and they would nod, not wince.

Now the left column — when a feeling **leans noise, and should be discarded or at least distrusted:**

- **Right after a loss.** A feeling that arrives in the minutes after you took a hit is contaminated by the loss. This is the tilt zone, and we will spend a whole section on it.
- **In a novel or unfamiliar situation.** A setup you have never seen, a regime that just changed, an instrument you do not normally trade — your gut has no valid reps here, so whatever it "says" is a guess dressed as knowing.
- **At high arousal — acute fear or FOMO.** When you are scared or grasping, your body is generating strong sensations *about your state*, not about the market, and they will masquerade as market read.
- **Outside your rep-heavy domain.** The macro call, the single-stock story you read once, the asset class you dabble in — vivid feelings here are borrowed confidence, not earned intuition.

The framework is not "emotion good" or "emotion bad." It is a router. The *same* felt intensity gets sent to "trust" or "discard" based on where it came from and what state you are in when it shows up.

#### Worked example: two traders, one identical feeling

Suppose two traders each feel a powerful, identical pull to *add* to a losing position — to average down. The feeling has the same intensity in both bodies.

Trader A is calm, rested, and running her core mean-reversion setup — one she has traded 3,000 times, where adding at a pre-planned second level is *part of the documented edge*. Her spreadsheet says setups that reach the second level and get the planned add have a positive expectancy: risking \$500 to make \$900, winning 55% of the time. Her expected value on the add is 0.55 × \$900 − 0.45 × \$500 = \$495 − \$225 = **+\$270**. Her feeling is signal — it is the trained recognition of a setup she has an edge on.

Trader B is down \$4,000 on the day, heart pounding, in a name he does not usually trade, adding for the first time with no plan. He has no reps on this, no defined risk, and he is post-loss and high-arousal — three noise flags at once. His true expectancy on the add is unknown and probably negative once you include the fact that he is now sizing off emotion. If it is a coin flip risking \$4,000 to make \$4,000, his expected value is **\$0 before costs**; in reality the tilt makes it worse. His feeling is noise — it is the loss talking.

The intuition to carry forward: *identical feelings can have opposite expected values, and the difference is not in the feeling but in the reps, the state, and the plan behind it.*

## 4. Arousal and tilt: why the same instinct flips

Why does a loss turn a good gut into a bad one? The mechanism is arousal, and it has been studied for over a century. In 1908, psychologists Robert Yerkes and John Dodson published what is now called the **Yerkes-Dodson law**: performance rises with arousal up to an optimal point, then *falls* as arousal keeps climbing. Plot it and you get an inverted U.

![Decision quality is an inverted U: past a point, arousal is tilt](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-6.webp)

Two features of that curve matter enormously for traders. First, there is a **peak** — some arousal is good. A completely flat, bored, under-aroused trader misses things and makes lazy errors; a little alertness sharpens perception. Second, and less known, Yerkes and Dodson found that **the harder the task, the lower the optimal level of arousal.** For a simple, well-drilled physical task, high arousal can help. For a *complex* cognitive task — weighing probabilities, holding a plan, resisting an impulse, i.e. exactly what trading is — the peak sits at a *low* level of arousal, and performance collapses quickly as arousal rises past it.

This is the physiology of tilt. When you take a loss, your arousal spikes — heart rate up, stress hormones up, attention narrowing. You slide rightward off the peak and down the steep side of the curve. And here is the cruel part: the *feeling of certainty does not fade as your decision quality drops.* On the far right of the curve you can feel more sure than ever ("I *know* this next one comes back") while actually deciding worse than ever. High arousal manufactures conviction and destroys judgment at the same time. That is why a revenge trade feels like conviction — it is conviction, chemically real, aimed at a decision your degraded brain can no longer make well.

Physiologically, this connects straight back to the somatic markers from Section 1. A calm nervous system lets the *subtle, trained* markers — the quiet flinch that flagged the bad deck — come through. A flooded nervous system drowns them under *loud, generic* stress signals that have nothing to do with the specific trade. It is a signal-to-noise problem in the most literal sense: the arousal is the noise, and it is loud enough to bury the very intuition you are trying to hear.

#### Worked example: the arousal tax on one tilt session

Suppose your honest, calm baseline is a trader who nets, on average, +\$300 a day across your normal 20 trades — a \$15 average edge per trade that reflects your real skill.

Now you take an early \$4,000 loss and tilt. Your decision quality drops down the right side of the Yerkes-Dodson curve. Concretely: you stop waiting for your setup, you widen no stops, you size up. Say your per-trade edge goes from +\$15 to −\$150 (you are now paying spread and making bad entries), and you take 25 trades instead of 20 because you are churning. That is 25 × (−\$150) = **−\$3,750** on top of the original \$4,000 loss, for a **−\$7,750 day** — when your skill, deployed calmly, was worth +\$300.

The gap between +\$300 and −\$7,750 is roughly **\$8,000**, and almost none of it was the original loss. It was the *arousal tax* — the cost of continuing to trade while sitting on the wrong part of the curve.

The intuition to carry forward: *the first loss is rarely what blows up an account; the tilt-driven trades taken in the high-arousal state after it are, and they are almost pure noise being acted on with maximum conviction.*

### Getting back to the peak: the arousal off-ramp

If tilt is the wrong side of the curve, the practical question is how to get back to the peak — because willpower alone does not move you there. Arousal is a physiological state, so it responds to physiological levers, not to self-scolding. Three that actually work:

- **Interrupt the loop with your breath.** A slow exhale that is longer than your inhale — say four seconds in, six to eight seconds out, for six or eight cycles — activates the parasympathetic "brake" on your nervous system and measurably drops heart rate and arousal within a minute or two. This is not wellness fluff; it is the fastest physical lever you have on the state that is degrading your decisions.
- **Change your physical position and remove the screen.** Stand up, walk away, get water, look at something 20 feet away. Arousal is partly maintained by the stimulus in front of you — the blinking P&L, the moving tape. Break the visual loop and the state starts to decay on its own.
- **Impose a hard time-out with a pre-committed rule.** The most reliable tool is a rule you set *while calm* that fires *while activated*: a daily max-loss limit that flattens you and closes the platform, or a "one loss over X, walk for 15 minutes" rule. Because you cannot trust the flooded brain to decide to stop, you make the decision in advance and let the rule execute it.

Notice what these have in common: none of them asks you to *make a good decision while tilted*. They all get you off the wrong part of the curve first, then let you decide. That ordering — regulate the state, then judge the feeling — is the whole game. A feeling re-examined after your arousal has come down is a feeling you can actually run through the triage; a feeling acted on at peak arousal is one the triage never got to see.

## What it looks like at the screen

All of this becomes useful only if you can catch it *in real time*, in your own body, at the moment it matters — not in a calm post-mortem that night. So here is the field guide to the physical and behavioral tells, the ones you can actually notice mid-session.

**When a feeling is more likely signal, it tends to feel like this:**

- **Quiet, not loud.** A real trained read is often understated — a mild "hm, this isn't behaving," a small hesitation, a soft pull to flatten. It rarely screams.
- **No urgency.** There is no clock in it. You could act now or in 30 seconds and it would not matter; the feeling is about the market's state, which is not begging you to hurry.
- **It points outward.** Your attention is on the tape, the book, the position — on *the trade*. You could name, if pressed, roughly what you are reacting to ("the bids keep getting pulled").
- **It survives a breath.** You take one slow exhale and the feeling is still there, unchanged. Real information does not evaporate when you calm down.
- **Your body is steady.** Normal breathing, relaxed shoulders, no heat in the face. The signal is coming through a calm channel.

**When a feeling is more likely noise, the tells flip:**

- **It's loud and urgent.** "I have to get in NOW." "This HAS to work." Urgency is the single most reliable tell of noise. The market almost never requires you to act in the next two seconds; your nervous system does.
- **It points inward.** The feeling is about *you* — your P&L, your streak, your need to be right, your fear of missing out. Notice the pronoun: signal is about *it*, noise is about *me*.
- **Your body is activated.** Hot face, tight chest, shallow fast breathing, a clenched jaw, leaning toward the screen, the mouse hand already moving. These are arousal — you are sliding down the wrong side of the curve.
- **It arrived right after an outcome.** A big win (euphoria) or a loss (tilt) just happened. Any strong feeling in the next few minutes is contaminated by that outcome, not by the next trade.
- **It dissolves under scrutiny.** You try to say out loud what you are reacting to and it comes out as "I just feel like it's going up" — a state, not a read.

The single most useful physical check is your **breath and your face**. If you notice your breathing has gone shallow and there is heat in your face, that is your body telling you the arousal is high — and per Yerkes-Dodson, any conviction generated up there is suspect by default. Traders who install one habit — *when I feel certain, I check whether I'm calm* — catch most of their worst decisions before they make them. The certainty is not the signal. The certainty *plus* a calm body is closer to the signal.

#### Worked example: reading your own body mid-trade

Suppose you are short a stock, risking \$800 on the trade, and two different feelings arrive on two different days. Day one: you notice a quiet unease — the stock keeps absorbing sellers and not dropping the way this setup normally does. Your breathing is normal, no heat in your face, no urgency; you could act now or in a minute. That combination — a read pointing *outward* at the tape, arriving through a *calm* body — is the signal profile. You cover for a small \$150 loss, and the stock rips higher an hour later; honoring the calm read saved you the difference between −\$150 and the full −\$800 stop, roughly **\$650**.

Day two: same position, but you just took a loss on the previous trade, your chest is tight, and the feeling is "I need to add here and make it back." That read points *inward* at your P&L, arrives through an *activated* body, and carries urgency. Same instrument, opposite profile — and the correct move is to run the triage, which this feeling fails on all three gates. The tell was never the strength of the feeling; it was where it pointed and what your body was doing when it showed up.

The intuition to carry forward: *you can often classify a feeling in real time by two cheap checks — is my attention on the trade or on myself, and is my body calm or activated — and those two reads predict signal-vs-noise better than the loudness of the feeling ever will.*

## The gut-check triage: a drill you can run in ten seconds

Awareness is not enough; under pressure you need a mechanical routine that runs faster than the impulse. This is the drill. Before acting on *any* strong feeling, run three questions in order. Any single "no" routes you to stand down — do not trade, flatten if needed, and log it.

![The gut-check triage: three questions before acting on any feeling](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-7.webp)

**Gate 1 — Is this my domain?** Have I taken *this specific* setup, in this instrument, hundreds or thousands of times, with fast feedback? Not "am I an experienced trader" — *have I earned reps on this exact thing?* If it is a novel setup, a new instrument, a macro prophecy, or something I read about once — no. The gut has no valid training here. Stand down.

**Gate 2 — Am I calm?** Right now, is my body steady — normal breath, no heat in the face, no urgency — and am I *not* in the minutes after a loss or a big win? Do a five-second body scan. If I am activated or post-outcome, whatever I am feeling is contaminated by arousal, not clean read. Stand down (or at minimum, wait until the arousal passes and see if the feeling survives).

**Gate 3 — Would I tell a peer to do exactly this?** Imagine describing the feeling and the intended action to a trader I respect: "I want to add 3x here because I feel like it comes back." Would they nod, or wince? If I would be embarrassed to say it out loud, it is noise wearing conviction's clothes. Stand down.

Only a feeling that clears **all three** gates — my domain, calm state, peer-defensible — earns the trade. Everything else gets flattened, waited out, and written down.

That last part matters as much as the gates. **Logging is what turns the triage into a skill instead of a slogan.** Every time you act on a gut feeling — or override one — write down: what you felt, which gates it passed, what state you were in, and how it turned out. Over 50 or 100 entries you get something priceless: a *calibration* of your own intuition. You learn that your "flatten this, it's misbehaving" feeling in your core setup is right 70% of the time and worth honoring, while your "this feels like a top" feeling is right 48% of the time and worth ignoring. This is the same discipline of scoring your own predictions that turns vague confidence into a measured edge — see [calibration: keeping score on your own forecasts](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts) for the full method. Without the log, you will remember your gut's wins and forget its losses, and conclude — wrongly — that you should always trust it.

#### Worked example: the triage saving a size-up

Suppose you are up \$2,000 on the day, feeling great, and a strong urge hits to put on a \$50,000 position in a hot name you saw trending on social media — 5x your normal size, because "the momentum is obvious."

Run the gates. Gate 1, my domain? No — you do not trade this name, and "obvious momentum from social media" is not a setup you have 1,000 reps on. That is already a "no," and you stand down. But notice the other flags too: Gate 2, am I calm? No — you are euphoric from being up \$2,000, which is high-arousal on the *greed* side, the same degraded part of the curve as tilt. Gate 3, would I tell a peer "I'm putting on 5x size in a meme name because momentum"? You would be embarrassed to say it.

Three "no"s. You pass. Say the name then drops 8% that afternoon; on \$50,000 that would have been a **−\$4,000** hit — turning a +\$2,000 day into a −\$2,000 one. The ten-second triage saved you \$4,000 by catching a feeling that was pure noise: no reps, high arousal, peer-indefensible.

The intuition to carry forward: *the triage does not ask whether the feeling is strong — noise is often the strongest feeling in the room — it asks whether the feeling came from a valid source and a calm state, which is the only thing that predicts whether it is worth money.*

## Common misconceptions

**"Good traders are unemotional."** No. Good traders are *well-regulated*, which is different. The evidence from Damasio's patients is that removing emotion from decisions makes them catastrophically worse, not coldly optimal. The skill is not amputation of feeling; it is discrimination between feelings. A trader who felt nothing would, like the ventromedial patients, keep flipping the bad decks while able to recite that they are bad.

**"Intuition is just superstition; only the model matters."** Also no. In high-validity, fast-feedback niches — order execution, reading a familiar tape, sensing a stop is about to run — trained intuition is real and measurable, and Kahneman, of all people, co-signed that. The mistake is not *having* intuition; it is applying execution-grade intuition to forecasting-grade questions where the environment has no valid signal to have trained it.

**"If I feel it strongly, it must be real."** This one costs the most money. Strength of feeling is generated largely by *arousal*, not by *validity*. The strongest, most certain feeling you have all week is disproportionately likely to arrive at high arousal — right after a loss or in a FOMO grab — which is exactly when your decision quality is lowest. Vividness is not evidence.

**"Revenge trading is a discipline problem — I just need more willpower."** Partly, but framing it as willpower misses the mechanism. After a loss your arousal is physiologically elevated and, per Yerkes-Dodson, your capacity for complex judgment is genuinely reduced. You are not just "being weak"; you are trying to make a hard decision on the wrong part of the curve. The fix is not to white-knuckle it — it is to *not decide there at all*: step away, let arousal fall, and re-approach calm. Willpower spent in a flooded state is willpower spent badly.

**"Cutting a winner or a loser on a gut feeling is always emotional and therefore wrong."** Not if the gut is trained and the state is calm. A veteran flattening a position because it is quietly not behaving the way that setup normally does — in her core domain, calmly — is acting on signal, and demanding she wait for a "logical" reason may just mean waiting until the loss is undeniable and larger. The question is never "was it emotional," it is "was it *informed* emotion from a valid source."

**"Once I've learned this, I'll know in the moment which is which."** The humbling truth is that signal and noise feel the same from the inside *by design* — that is why you need an external, mechanical triage and a written log rather than trusting your in-the-moment read of your own read. The whole point of the three gates is that you cannot reliably tell signal from noise by *feel*; you tell them apart by *source and state*, which the gates make you check explicitly.

## How it shows up in real markets

The tension between trusting and distrusting the gut is not academic — you can watch it play out in the careers of some of the most famous traders alive.

### 1. Paul Tudor Jones and the 1987 crash — trained gut, calm state

In the run-up to October 1987, hedge-fund manager Paul Tudor Jones became convinced a crash was coming. Crucially, his conviction was not a raw hunch — it sat on top of deep, rep-heavy pattern work: his team had studied the 1929 analog closely and mapped the market's structure against it, and Jones had years of reps reading market behavior. When the crash came on Black Monday, he was positioned short and his Tudor fund reportedly returned on the order of **+60% in October 1987 alone** and triple digits for the year (widely reported figures, popularized by the 1987 documentary *Trader* and subsequent profiles; treat the exact percentages as approximate and as-of that period). This is intuition working *as designed*: a feeling grounded in thousands of hours of valid, domain-specific study, acted on from a prepared and relatively calm state — with defined risk, not a panicked all-in. The gut was the compression of the analysis, not a substitute for it.

### 2. Stanley Druckenmiller and the 2000 tech top — elite gut, wrong domain, high arousal

Stanley Druckenmiller is one of the greatest macro traders in history, with one of the best long-run records ever recorded. Yet in early 2000, near the peak of the dot-com bubble, he bought a large amount of technology stocks — by his own later accounts, on the order of **\$6 billion** — chasing a rally he had earlier been *right* to be skeptical of, because watching younger traders make fortunes was unbearable. The timing was disastrous: the Nasdaq rolled over, and he has said the episode cost him roughly **\$3 billion** and contributed to his stepping back from Quantum (recounted by Druckenmiller in interviews, including his 2015 Lost Tree Club talk; figures are his own approximate recollections). His own framing is the lesson: he described it as an emotional, undisciplined trade driven by envy and FOMO — a feeling from a high-arousal state, chasing in a stretched market. World-class intuition, applied at maximum arousal in a grab-driven state, produced one of the most expensive trades of his life. Vividness of conviction did not save it; the state it came from doomed it.

### 3. Jesse Livermore — the original tale of gut turned to tilt

Jesse Livermore, the legendary speculator of the early 20th century, made and lost several enormous fortunes. At his peak he reportedly made a fortune of around **\$100 million** shorting the 1929 crash (a widely cited figure for that era, roughly equivalent to well over a billion in today's money; treat as an approximate historical estimate). Yet he died broke in 1940. His own writing — and the semi-biographical *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* — returns again and again to the same enemy: not a lack of skill, but the emotional overtrading, the trading on tips and impulse, the inability to sit still, that repeatedly undid his best work. Livermore is the archetype of a genuinely gifted intuition destroyed by the failure to distinguish trained read from emotional impulse. His famous line — that the money was made "in the sitting," in the waiting, not in the trading — is the same point this post makes: most strong urges to act are noise, and the edge is in not acting on them.

### 4. The rise of systematic trading — an entire industry built on distrusting the gut

The most quantitative funds took the low-validity problem to its logical conclusion: if human intuition cannot reliably forecast in a noisy environment, remove it from the forecast entirely. Systematic and high-frequency shops replaced discretionary gut calls with measured, backtested, statistically-edged rules — precisely because they concluded that in the low-validity domain of prediction, a disciplined model beats a human hunch. This is not a claim that intuition is worthless; it is a recognition of *where* it is worthless. These firms still rely on human intuition heavily — in research, in risk, in knowing when a model has broken — which are higher-validity, faster-feedback domains. They simply refused to let a gut feeling place the trade.

### 5. The everyday revenge trade — the case study you will live

You will not need a documentary to see the last one; you will live it. The pattern in the figure below is the one that quietly drains retail and professional accounts alike.

![Same intensity, opposite meaning: revenge trade vs a trained cut](/imgs/blogs/emotions-as-signal-versus-noise-8.webp)

On the left is the revenge trade, dressed up as conviction. The *trigger* is a fresh loss — say −\$4,000. The *state* is heart-racing, must-win-it-back arousal. The *size* is 3x normal with no defined risk. The *inner tell* is urgency: "this HAS to work." Every one of those is a noise flag, and together they are the anatomy of a blow-up.

On the right is the trained cut, which can feel exactly as intense but is signal. The *trigger* is that a setup you know cold has quietly stopped behaving the way it normally does. The *state* is calm and rested, inside your A-setup. The *size* is normal with pre-defined risk. The *inner tell* has no urgency: "this is off, flatten." Same felt intensity, opposite meaning — and the only reliable way to tell them apart in the moment is the source-and-state triage, not the strength of the feeling. This connects directly to the fuller anatomy of trading's core emotions in [fear, greed, hope, and regret — the four emotions](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/fear-greed-hope-and-regret-the-four-emotions), and to the underlying wiring in [the neuroscience of risk and reward](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/the-neuroscience-of-risk-and-reward).

## When this matters to you

This is not only a trading problem, and it is worth being honest that the skill is educational, not a formula for guaranteed gains — no framework makes a low-validity forecast reliable. But the discrimination itself touches every decision you make under uncertainty, with or without a P&L attached.

Where it touches your money most directly: the strongest feelings you will ever have about your portfolio — the urge to sell everything in a crash, the urge to pile into whatever just tripled — arrive at maximum arousal, in exactly the low-validity domain (predicting the market's next big move) where the gut is least trustworthy. The triage is built for precisely these moments. When you feel most certain about a big market call, that certainty is disproportionately likely to be arousal, not information, and the correct default is to slow down, not speed up.

Where it helps you *keep* the value of intuition: the goal is not to become the emotionless robot that Damasio's research shows is a worse decider, not a better one. It is to protect and honor the genuine, hard-won intuition you have built in your true domain — the quiet read that saves you from a bad fill or a misbehaving position — while ruthlessly filtering out the loud, arousal-driven impulses that only pretend to be knowledge.

The next time a powerful feeling grabs you mid-trade, do not ask "is this feeling strong?" Noise is usually the strongest feeling in the room. Ask the three questions instead — is this my domain, am I calm, would I tell a peer to do exactly this — and let the answer, not the intensity, decide whether your gut has earned the click.
