You already know the feeling, even if you have never traded a share in your life. A price is going up. Someone you know has made money — real money, easy money, more than they make at work. The story is intoxicating: a new technology, a new asset, a new era. You do the math on what your savings would be worth if you had bought a year ago, and it stings. Then you do the math on what they will be worth a year from now if the line keeps going, and you reach for your wallet.

That feeling has a four-hundred-year history, and it is astonishingly consistent. The instrument changes — a tulip bulb in 1637, a share of a trading monopoly in 1720, a radio stock in 1929, a dot-com in 2000, a house in 2006, a coin in 2021 — but the human being holding it does not. The same emotions arrive in the same order, produce the same behavior, and end the same way. This is not a metaphor or a vibe. It is a documented structure with named phases, and once you can see the structure you can locate yourself inside it, usually while there is still time to act.

This article is the anatomy lesson. We are going to take the bubble apart phase by phase, name the emotion that drives each one, ground every claim in a real historical episode with real numbers, and end with a practical drill for participating in a boom without being the person left holding the bag when the music stops. The diagram below is the whole map on one page — the five-phase arc every mania walks and the emotion that carries the crowd through each step. The rest of the post is a guided tour of it.

![The five-phase anatomy of a bubble: displacement, boom, euphoria, distress, and panic or revulsion, each driven by a different dominant emotion](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-1.webp)

Read it left to right. A real shock kicks things off. Optimism turns into belief and credit expands. Belief turns into greed and the "new era" story takes over. Then the first cracks appear, anxiety replaces greed, and the exit — which felt infinitely wide on the way up — turns out to be a single door. Everything that follows is an unpacking of that one picture.

## Foundations: what a bubble actually is

Before we walk the phases, we need shared vocabulary. You do not need any finance background — every term is defined here on first use, and if you already know them, skim.

### Price versus value

The single most important distinction in this entire article is the gap between **price** and **value**.

**Value** is what an asset is worth based on the cash it will actually produce for its owner over time: the rent a house collects, the dividends a share pays, the interest a bond throws off. You can estimate it. It is boring, it moves slowly, and it is anchored to the real economy.

**Price** is simply the last number someone was willing to pay. It is set at the margin — by the most recent, most eager buyer and seller — and it can wander far from value for a long time, in both directions.

A **bubble** is a sustained, self-reinforcing detachment of price from value, in which the price rises largely *because* it has been rising, rather than because the underlying asset is producing more. The defining feature is that the reason to buy quietly changes. Early on, people buy because the asset looks cheap relative to what it produces. Late on, people buy because the price is going up and they expect it to keep going up. When that second reason becomes the *main* reason, you are in a bubble, whatever the asset.

> A bubble is a market that has stopped pricing the thing and started pricing the crowd's opinion of the thing.

### The greater fool

The engine of that late-stage buying has a name: the **greater-fool theory**. It says you can rationally pay a silly price for something — more than it could ever be worth on its cash flows — as long as you believe a *greater fool* will come along and pay you even more. You are not buying the asset. You are buying a ticket in a chain, betting that the chain has at least one more link after you.

The problem is arithmetic, not morality. Every chain has a last link. Someone is the final buyer, at the highest price, with no one behind them. When the supply of greater fools runs out, the price does not gently drift back to value — it *gaps* down to whatever a genuine value buyer will pay, which can be a long way below.

### Leverage

**Leverage** is using borrowed money to increase the size of a position. If you have \$5,000 and borrow another \$5,000 to buy \$10,000 of an asset, you have 2x leverage. Leverage magnifies both gains and losses on your own money, and — crucially — it can force you to sell at the worst possible moment, because a lender who sees your collateral shrinking will demand more cash (a **margin call**) or liquidate your position for you. We will do the exact math later; for now, hold onto the idea that leverage is the accelerant that turns a painful decline into a fatal one.

### Drawdown and the cruel arithmetic of recovery

A **drawdown** is the percentage fall from a peak to a trough — the depth of the hole. It is the number that matters most in a bubble's aftermath, because of a piece of arithmetic almost everyone gets wrong: the gain you need to recover from a loss is *bigger* than the loss, and it grows non-linearly as the loss deepens.

#### Worked example: the arithmetic of the round trip

Start with \$100.

- It falls 50\%, to \$50. To get back to \$100 you need to make \$50 on a \$50 base — a gain of **+100\%**. A 50\% loss needs a 100\% gain to undo.
- It falls 80\%, to \$20. To get back to \$100 you need \$80 on a \$20 base — a gain of **+400\%**.
- It falls 89\% (the size of the 1929 crash), to \$11. You now need \$89 on \$11 — roughly **+809\%**.
- It falls 90\%, to \$10. You need \$90 on \$10 — a gain of **+900\%**.

Notice how the required gain explodes as the loss deepens. Going from a 50\% to an 80\% loss did not just add 30 points of pain; it quadrupled the recovery you need, from +100\% to +400\%. This is why the depth of a crash matters so much more than it feels like it should, and why the buyers who entered late — near the peak, into the deepest part of the eventual drawdown — so rarely make it back. **Losses and the gains that reverse them are not symmetric; the hole gets steeper the deeper you fall, so avoiding the deep end is worth more than any brilliant recovery.**

With price, value, the greater fool, leverage, and drawdown defined, we can walk the arc.

## 1. The five phases, and the emotion behind each one

The phase model at the heart of this article comes from the economist **Charles Kindleberger**, whose 1978 classic *Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises* distilled four centuries of financial disasters into one repeating template. Kindleberger built on the work of **Hyman Minsky**, whose *financial instability hypothesis* supplied the mechanism: stability itself breeds the risk-taking that eventually produces instability. Later editions, updated by Robert Aliber and Robert McCauley, keep finding the same shape in every new crisis. The phases are displacement, boom, euphoria, distress, and panic/revulsion.

The reason the model is so durable is that each phase is governed by a specific, predictable emotion — and the disciplined response in each phase runs directly *against* that emotion. The table below is the operating manual: what the crowd feels, the visible sign of it, what is happening with credit, and what a disciplined participant does instead.

![A five-by-four matrix mapping each bubble phase to its dominant emotion, its visible tell, the state of credit and leverage, and the disciplined move that runs against the crowd](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-2.webp)

Let us walk each phase.

**Displacement.** Something genuinely new arrives — a technology, a policy change, cheap credit, the end of a war. This is the part people forget: *bubbles start with something real*. The internet really did change commerce. Blockchains really are a new kind of database. The displacement is a legitimate reason for prices to rise. The emotion is curiosity, and the correct response is to study it and, if you act, to size small, because the range of outcomes is still enormous.

**Boom.** Prices start to trend up, and money notices. Credit expands — banks lend, brokers extend margin, new instruments appear to let more people in. The emotion is optimism curdling into belief. Early gains validate the story, which draws in more buyers, which produces more gains: the feedback loop switches on. A disciplined participant can ride this, but with a plan to exit — a trailing stop, a rule that sells if the trend breaks.

**Euphoria.** This is the dangerous phase, the one the whole article circles. Belief becomes greed. The "new era" narrative takes over completely, and valuation — the tether to value — is cut. Leverage peaks: people borrow to buy because it seems irrational not to. The fear of missing out ([FOMO](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/fomo-and-the-fear-of-being-left-behind)) becomes the dominant force in the market. The correct move is the hardest one emotionally: take profits, reduce, act *against* the feeling of invincibility. Almost nobody does.

**Distress.** The first cracks. Insiders and early buyers — "smart money" — begin to sell, quietly, into the strength that the late crowd is still buying. Prices stall, then wobble. The emotion shifts from greed to anxiety. Margin calls begin at the leveraged edges. Nothing has crashed yet, but the character of the market has changed: rallies get sold instead of bought. The disciplined move is to cut size fast, before you have to.

**Panic and revulsion.** The exit that looked infinitely wide turns out to be one door, and everyone reaches it at once. Bids vanish. Prices gap down. Leverage forces selling, which lowers prices, which forces more selling — the feedback loop that drove the boom now runs in reverse. Fear becomes despair. In the final phase, *revulsion*, the asset that everyone wanted becomes the asset nobody will touch at any price, often overshooting below fair value. The only job now is to preserve capital and survive.

There is a deeper reason the feedback loop is so powerful, which the investor George Soros named **reflexivity**: in a bubble, prices do not merely *reflect* the fundamentals, they *change* them. A rising stock price lets a company raise cheap capital and make real acquisitions, which improves its actual business, which seems to justify the higher price. Rising house prices make homeowners feel wealthy and spend more, boosting the economy that supports house prices. For a while, the bubble makes itself true — the story and the reality reinforce each other — which is exactly why "the fundamentals support the price" is never a reliable all-clear. In the late phases, the same reflexivity runs in reverse: falling prices destroy the collateral, the confidence, and the cheap capital that were holding the fundamentals up, so the decline feeds itself just as the rise did.

The single most useful thing about this model is that the emotions are legible from the *outside*. You do not need inside information to know which phase you are in. You need to notice whether the crowd is curious, optimistic, greedy, anxious, or terrified — and whether you are.

## 2. Why "this time is different" is the tell

If you learn to recognize only one signal, make it this one. In every mania, at the euphoric peak, a specific idea takes hold: **the old rules of valuation no longer apply, because something has fundamentally changed.** New technology. A new economy. A new kind of money. The claim is always that the historical relationship between price and value has been repealed.

The economists Carmen Reinhart and Kenneth Rogoff studied eight centuries of financial crises across sixty-six countries and titled the resulting book *This Time Is Different: Eight Centuries of Financial Folly* — precisely because that phrase, or its equivalent, appears at the top of every bubble, and it is wrong every time. They called "this time is different" the four most expensive words in the English language.

Here is why it is such a reliable tell. The "new era" story is not a lie — it usually contains a real truth, which is exactly what makes it so persuasive. The mistake is not believing the technology is real. The mistake is concluding that *because the technology is new, the laws of value and liquidity have been suspended.* They have not. Every euphoric claim has a matching old rule it pretends to have repealed, and the old rule always comes back.

![A before-and-after figure pairing each "this time is different" claim with the durable market rule that reasserts itself: cash flows still set value, leverage cuts both ways, liquidity vanishes when needed, and everyone exits through one door](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-6.webp)

Read the pairs. "New tech changes everything" — but cash flows still set value in the end. "Old valuations do not apply" — but leverage still cuts both ways. "Prices only go up" — but liquidity still vanishes exactly when you need it. "I will sell before the top" — but everyone plans to, and everyone exits through the same one door. The euphoric story on the left is seductive; the rule on the right is boring; the rule wins.

To see how concrete this is, let us map the whole arc onto one modern case, in real dollars you can look up.

#### Worked example: the phases of the 2020-2022 crypto cycle

These are real, dated figures, not hypotheticals — Bitcoin's price is public.

- **Displacement.** In May 2020, Bitcoin's supply-issuance rate halved (a scheduled "halving"), with the price around \$9,000. A real, novel change in the asset's mechanics — the displacement.
- **Boom.** Through late 2020, price broke its previous all-time high near \$20,000. Optimism, then belief. Institutions began to talk about it as an asset class; credit and new products (futures, lending, leverage) expanded around it.
- **Euphoria.** Bitcoin ran to roughly \$64,000 by April 2021, cooled to about \$30,000 mid-year, then made its final push to roughly **\$69,000 on 2021-11-10**. "This time is different" was everywhere: digital gold, the future of money, HODL forever. Leverage across the ecosystem hit extremes.
- **Distress and panic.** Through 2022, the cracks widened, leveraged funds and lenders failed, and forced selling cascaded. By November 2022, amid the collapse of the FTX exchange, Bitcoin traded around **\$15,500** — a drawdown of roughly **-77\%** from the peak.

Same five phases, same emotions, priced in dollars anyone can verify. **The 2021 crypto cycle was not a new phenomenon that broke the rules; it was the tulip trade with a blockchain, running the identical script Kindleberger described in 1978.**

## 3. Leverage: the quiet migration from safe to fragile

Euphoria is the psychological peak, but leverage is the physical mechanism that turns a correction into a collapse. And the most dangerous thing about leverage in a boom is that it grows without anyone deciding to make the system fragile. Minsky described this migration with a simple, powerful taxonomy of how borrowers finance their positions.

![A tree diagram of Minsky's three financing regimes: hedge finance where income covers principal and interest, speculative finance where income covers only interest, and Ponzi finance where income covers neither and solvency depends on rising prices](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-3.webp)

- **Hedge finance.** Your income from the asset covers both the interest *and* the principal on the money you borrowed to buy it. A landlord whose rent comfortably pays the whole mortgage is hedge-financed. This is robust: a price shock hurts, but you survive, because the asset pays for itself.
- **Speculative finance.** Your income covers the interest but *not* the principal. You can service the debt, but you must roll it over — refinance it — when it comes due. A borrower who can pay the interest but must keep renewing the loan is speculative-financed. You survive as long as you can keep refinancing.
- **Ponzi finance.** Your income covers *neither* interest nor principal. The only way you stay solvent is if the asset's price keeps rising, so you can sell part of it or borrow more against its new, higher value to make your payments. This is the buyer who is underwater on cash flow from day one and is betting entirely on price appreciation.

Minsky's insight is that a long boom *pushes the whole system down this ladder*. When prices have risen for years, hedge financing feels too conservative — you are leaving money on the table — so people take on more debt and slide into speculative finance. When that keeps working, they slide again into Ponzi finance, because in a market that only goes up, betting on appreciation is not just tempting, it is the winning move. Right up until it is not. The system becomes maximally fragile at the exact moment of maximum confidence.

#### Worked example: how leverage converts a survivable loss into a fatal one

Suppose you have \$5,000. You borrow another \$5,000 and buy \$10,000 of an asset — 2x leverage.

- The asset falls **25\%**. It is now worth \$7,500. You still owe the \$5,000 you borrowed, so your equity is \$7,500 - \$5,000 = \$2,500. A 25\% drop in the asset cut *your* money in half — a -50\% hit to your equity.
- The asset falls **50\%**. It is worth \$5,000. You owe \$5,000. Your equity is \$0. You are wiped out — and in practice your broker issued a margin call well before this, forcing you to sell into the decline, locking in the loss near the bottom.
- Compare the unleveraged buyer. They put in the full \$10,000 of their own money. A 50\% drop leaves them with \$5,000. Painful — but they are alive, they own the asset outright, and they can wait for a recovery.

The leveraged buyer and the unleveraged buyer held the *same asset* through the *same 50\% decline*. One is at zero; the other has half their money and full survival. **Leverage does not just magnify your loss — it takes away your ability to wait, forcing you to sell at the worst moment, which is how a temporary drawdown becomes a permanent one.**

## 4. The greater-fool engine

Underneath the euphoria and the leverage sits the coldest mechanism of all: the reason the price holds at absurd levels is that each buyer expects a later, more eager buyer to take it off their hands at a profit. Nobody in the euphoric phase is pricing the asset's cash flows. They are pricing the next person's willingness to pay.

![A pipeline showing the greater-fool chain: a value buyer at 100 dollars, a momentum buyer at 300, a FOMO buyer at 700, a leveraged latecomer at 1,000, and finally no greater fool, at which point the bid vanishes and the price gaps down](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-4.webp)

Follow the chain. A value buyer pays \$100 — roughly what the asset is worth. A momentum buyer pays \$300, not because it is worth \$300 but because the trend is up and trends tend to continue. A FOMO buyer pays \$700, driven by the fear of being left behind as friends get rich. A leveraged latecomer borrows to pay \$1,000, because in a market that only goes up, the rational move seemed to be maximum exposure. Each link is individually reasonable *given the belief that another buyer waits behind them*.

Then the chain ends. There is always a last buyer. When no greater fool arrives, the \$1,000 price has nothing holding it up — no cash flow justifies it, and the only reason anyone paid it was the expectation of the next buyer who did not come. The bid does not step down politely to \$900. It gaps to whatever a value buyer will pay, which might be \$150. The people in the last few links are not down a little; they are catastrophically underwater, and the most leveraged among them are wiped out entirely.

#### Worked example: the greater fool running out

Suppose a market is rising 40\% a month, and it can only do that because fresh money keeps arriving to buy from the people already in.

- Month 1: the asset is \$1,000. To lift it 40\% to \$1,400, new buyers must commit enough fresh cash to absorb the coins the current holders want to sell at the higher price.
- Month 2: now at \$1,400, to rise another 40\% to about \$1,960, you need *even more* fresh money than last month, because the base is bigger. The dollar inflows required to sustain a fixed percentage gain grow every month.
- Month 3: the pool of people who have not yet bought — and who can be persuaded to — starts to thin. Inflows slow. The price cannot rise 40\%; it rises 5\%, then stalls. The holders who were counting on the next wave to sell into now find no bids above them.
- The unwind: the marginal buyer is gone, and the last price (say \$1,960) has no support. Value buyers will step in only far below — perhaps \$300. The last-in holders take an 85\% loss almost overnight.

Nothing about the asset changed between the stall and the crash. What changed was the *flow of new fools*. **In a greater-fool market you are not paid by the asset, you are paid by the next buyer — and the whole structure depends on a supply of buyers that is, by definition, finite.**

This is also why the top is invisible while you are standing on it. The greater-fool chain does not send a warning when it is about to end; the last strong buyer looks exactly like all the strong buyers before them, right up until they turn out to have been the last. There is no bell at the top, and there cannot be one, because the top is simply the moment the marginal buyer failed to appear — a *non-event*, an absence, not a signal. That is the deep reason "I will sell before the top" fails as a plan: the top is only knowable in hindsight, so a plan that depends on identifying it in real time is a plan to be late. The only workable defense is structural, not predictive — to be getting smaller as the chain lengthens, so that whenever the last link snaps, you are not on it.

## 5. The crash, and why the depth rhymes across four centuries

When the greater fools run out and the leverage unwinds, the descent has its own grim regularity. The feedback loop that powered the boom reverses: falling prices trigger margin calls, forced selling drives prices lower, lower prices trigger more margin calls. Liquidity — the ability to sell without crashing the price — evaporates precisely when everyone wants it, because liquidity in a boom was always an illusion created by the presence of eager buyers. Remove the buyers and it is gone.

What is remarkable is not that bubbles crash, but how *similar* the crashes are in magnitude despite spanning different assets, centuries, and technologies. A deflating mania tends to retrace not just its excess but a huge share of the whole move.

#### Worked example: the parabola, then the 80\% give-back

Suppose you and a friend both buy the same asset, but at different phases.

- You buy early, at \$100. The asset goes parabolic — a near-vertical rise — to \$1,000, a 10x gain.
- Your friend, swept up in the euphoria, buys at the top, at \$1,000.
- The bubble bursts and the price falls 80\%, to \$200.
- Your position: you paid \$100 and it is worth \$200. You are still **+100\%**. The crash was uncomfortable but you are fine.
- Your friend's position: they paid \$1,000 and it is worth \$200. They are down **-80\%** and need a **+400\%** gain just to break even (the round-trip arithmetic from the Foundations section). If they used any leverage, they were wiped out long before the price reached \$200.

Same asset, same 80\% crash, opposite outcomes — decided entirely by which phase you entered in. **The crash does not care what you believe about the asset; it only cares what price you paid, which is to say which phase you bought in.**

Now hold that -80\% number against the historical record, because the real crashes cluster right around it. The figure below walks six of history's most famous bubbles through the same script — the displacement that started them, the euphoric peak, the "this time is different" story, and the peak-to-trough drawdown that ended them.

![A matrix of six bubbles across four centuries, each showing its displacement, its euphoria peak, its new-era story, and its peak-to-trough drawdown, illustrating that different assets and eras produced the same script and similar crash magnitudes](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-5.webp)

South Sea -80\%. The Crash of 1929 -89\%. The dot-com Nasdaq -78\%. The 2008 S&P 500 -57\%. Bitcoin -84\% in 2018 and -77\% in 2022. The asterisk on tulips is important, and we will get to it — the popular magnitudes there are mostly myth. But for the well-documented cases, the pattern is unmistakable: bubbles do not correct 10\% or 20\%. They give back most of the move, most of the time, in a range that clusters between roughly -55\% and -90\%. The next section walks each one.

## What it looks like at the screen

The phases and the numbers are the skeleton. Here is the flesh — what living through a bubble actually feels like, minute to minute, at the screen, because recognizing the *feeling* is how you locate the phase in real time.

**In the boom,** your account is up and it feels earned. You check it a little more than you used to, and each check is pleasant. A position you sized conservatively is now your best performer, and you feel a small regret that you did not buy more. The regret is the first symptom.

**In the euphoria,** the screen is a slot machine that keeps paying. Green candles. New highs that print faster than you expected. A number in the corner of your screen — your net worth — that climbs while you sleep, so you start checking it at night. People who never talked about markets are suddenly talking about this one. Your barber, your cousin, an influencer with a rented sports car and a screenshot of gains. New buyers arrive who cannot explain what they bought. You feel, distinctly, like a genius, and you feel that anyone *not* in is a fool. You size up. You maybe borrow to size up more. The plan you wrote — the one with the trailing stop and the profit-taking rule — feels absurdly timid against a market this obvious. So you quietly abandon it. That abandonment, made while feeling invincible, is the trade that costs you everything.

**At the top,** the strangest thing happens: nothing. An eerie calm. The furious buying slows. Prices stop making new highs but do not fall — they churn sideways, and the churn feels like consolidation, "healthy," a pause before the next leg. It is not. It is the sound of the last fools being spent. Smart money is selling into your optimism, and the tape looks fine because your buying is absorbing their selling. The absence of drama at the top is itself the tell.

**In the distress,** the first real down day arrives, sharp enough to notice. You are not worried — you have seen dips, and every previous dip was a gift. So you buy it. And it bounces, which confirms you were right, which is the cruelest part, because the bounce is what convinces you to hold and to add. Then the second down day is bigger, and it does not bounce as far. Your best performer is now merely a good one. The number you checked at night has stopped being fun to check.

**In the panic,** the checking becomes compulsive and awful. Every refresh is lower. The bounces get weaker and the drops get faster. You tell yourself you will sell on the next rally, and the rally is smaller than the last, and you hold out for the one after, which never comes. If you are leveraged, you get a message from your broker that is not a request. Eventually you feel not fear but numbness — the [capitulation](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/fear-at-the-screen-paralysis-and-panic-selling) that marks the bottom, where you sell not because you decided to but because you cannot stand to watch anymore. That sale, made in despair, is very often within days of the low.

If any of these paragraphs made you wince with recognition, that is the point. The tells are emotional before they are technical. You will feel the phase before any indicator confirms it.

## Common misconceptions

**"Bubbles are obvious, and I would see it and get out in time."** In hindsight, every bubble looks obvious; in real time, none do, because the euphoric story is genuinely persuasive and the people around you are getting rich, which is the most convincing argument there is. Everyone plans to sell before the top. The top is, by definition, the moment when the largest number of people are still holding and still believing. If getting out were easy, there would be no crash.

**"Only naive retail investors get caught."** Isaac Newton — literally one of the smartest humans who ever lived — was ruined by the South Sea Bubble. Sophisticated hedge funds blew up in 2008. Professional crypto lenders and funds imploded in 2022. Intelligence is not the protection; a *process* that overrides euphoria is. Smart people are, if anything, better at constructing the elaborate rationalization for why this time is different.

**"Tulip mania ruined the Dutch economy — people threw themselves into canals."** This is the most famous bubble story and it is mostly false. The historian Anne Goldgar spent years in the Dutch archives and found very few documented cases of real financial ruin, no wave of suicides, and no broad economic collapse. The trade was concentrated among wealthy merchants and skilled artisans who could absorb the loss, and the lurid stories were largely invented afterward by moralizing pamphleteers. The mania was real; the catastrophe was myth. We will treat this carefully below, because it matters that even our origin story about bubbles is exaggerated.

**"A bubble means the asset is worthless."** No. The displacement is usually real. The internet did transform the economy; the dot-com bust did not disprove that. Many bubble assets are genuinely valuable — the bubble is a statement about *price relative to value at that moment*, not about the technology. Some of the best long-term investments in history were, at some point, bubbles that crashed 80\% before the durable winners emerged.

**"If it crashed 80\%, the bottom must be in — it can't fall much more."** An 80\% drawdown can still be followed by another 50\% drop (which would take a \$20 asset to \$10, a further -50\%). "It already fell a lot" is not a floor. In the revulsion phase, prices routinely overshoot *below* fair value, because the same crowd emotion that ignored value on the way up ignores it on the way down.

**"You can time the exit by watching the fundamentals."** Fundamentals tell you an asset is overvalued, but they cannot tell you *when* it will stop rising — an overvalued market can double again before it breaks. The phase model works better than valuation for timing, because it tracks the thing that actually turns the market: the crowd's emotion and the supply of new buyers, not the spreadsheet.

**"The crowd in a bubble is just stupid, and I am smarter than that."** This is the most dangerous misconception of all, because it is the one that removes your defenses. The people in every bubble were not fools — they were responding rationally to their local incentives. A fund manager who refused to buy tech stocks in 1999 underperformed and lost clients; staying out was, professionally, the risky move. A 2005 homebuyer watching prices rise 15\% a year was "losing money" by renting. The crowd is not stupid; it is *trapped* by a structure that punishes caution in the short run and rewards it only after the crash. Believing you are immune because you are clever is precisely the overconfidence that puts you in the last few links of the chain. Newton was clever. Cleverness is not the antidote; a rule you follow when clever arguments say to break it is.

## How it shows up in real markets

Six episodes, four centuries, one script. Real instruments, real dates, real numbers — and, where the record is contested, an honest flag.

### 1. Dutch Tulip Mania, 1636-1637 (handle with care)

The founding legend of finance is that in the winter of 1636-37, the Dutch Republic went collectively insane over tulip bulbs, with single rare bulbs trading for the price of a canal house before the market collapsed in February 1637 and ruined the nation. It is a great story. It is also, in its dramatic form, largely a myth — and it is worth telling accurately precisely because it is the case people cite most and understand least.

Here is what is defensible. A speculative trade in tulip bulbs — especially rare, virus-"broken" varieties with flamed petals like the famous *Semper Augustus* — did run up sharply into early 1637 and then collapse, roughly around 2-5 February 1637. The most expensive bulb contracts historian Anne Goldgar documented in the archives reached about **5,000 guilders**, which was indeed the price of a comfortable Amsterdam house at the time and perhaps fifteen-plus times a skilled craftsman's annual income of a few hundred guilders. So the *peak prices for the rarest bulbs* were genuinely extraordinary.

Here is what is myth. In her book *Tulipmania: Money, Honor, and Knowledge in the Dutch Golden Age* (2007), Goldgar showed there was no mass bankruptcy, no epidemic of suicides, and no measurable damage to the broader Dutch economy. The trade was concentrated among a network of wealthy merchants and skilled artisans, not the whole populace, and most of the eye-watering contracts were never settled — when the price collapsed, buyers simply walked away from paper obligations, and Dutch courts largely declined to enforce them. The tale of ruined masses and bodies in the canals was manufactured after the fact by Calvinist moralists warning against the sin of speculation, and repeated uncritically for centuries.

The lesson is *doubly* useful. First, the psychological structure was real even here: a displacement (exotic new flowers, a hot new market), a euphoric run, a "new luxury asset" story, and a sudden collapse when buyers stopped showing up. Second, and just as important: **be skeptical of the magnitudes in every bubble story, including the ones told to warn you about bubbles.** If the most cited mania in history has been exaggerated for four hundred years, treat every "a bulb cost more than a house, and then everyone died" narrative — in any asset — as a claim to verify, not a fact to repeat.

### 2. The South Sea Bubble, 1720

The South Sea Company was granted a monopoly on British trade to South America and, more importantly, a scheme to convert government debt into company shares. The "new era" story was fabulous riches from the seas. Shares rose from about **£128 in January 1720** to roughly **£1,000 by the summer**, as a frenzy of speculation — and a wave of copycat "bubble companies," one reportedly for "an undertaking of great advantage, but nobody to know what it is" — swept London. By December 1720 the shares had collapsed back to around **£124**, a drawdown of roughly **80\%** from the peak.

Among the ruined was Isaac Newton. He had bought early, sold with a tidy profit, then watched the price keep soaring, could not stand missing out, and bought back in near the top with far more money — losing a reported **£20,000**, a fortune at the time. His remark, whether or not he actually said it, is the perfect epitaph for the euphoric phase: "I can calculate the motions of the heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people." The greatest quantitative mind of the age was undone not by bad math but by FOMO.

### 3. The Crash of 1929

The 1920s displacement was real and enormous: mass electrification, automobiles, radio, consumer credit. The stock market boomed on it, and by the euphoria of 1929 the market was heavily leveraged through "buying on margin," where investors put down as little as 10\% and borrowed the rest. The economist Irving Fisher captured the "this time is different" spirit days before the crash, declaring stocks had reached "a permanently high plateau."

The Dow Jones Industrial Average peaked at **381.17 on 3 September 1929**. Then came Black Thursday (24 October), Black Monday (28 October, down nearly 13\% in a day), and Black Tuesday (29 October, down nearly 12\%), as the margin loans that magnified the gains now forced cascading liquidation. The market did not bottom until **41.22 on 8 July 1932** — a drawdown of about **89\%**, the deepest in the modern record. It did not regain its 1929 peak until 1954, roughly a quarter century later. The leverage that made the boom possible is what made the crash total.

### 4. The Dot-com Bubble, 2000

The displacement — the commercial internet — was one of the most genuinely transformative technologies in history, which is exactly why the euphoria was so intense and so hard to resist. The "new era" story explicitly discarded valuation: profits were said not to matter for internet companies; what mattered was "eyeballs," growth, and getting big fast. Companies with no earnings and barely any revenue reached enormous valuations on the strength of a ".com" in the name.

The Nasdaq Composite index, heavy with technology stocks, peaked at **5,048.62 on 10 March 2000**. Over the next two and a half years it fell roughly **78\%**, bottoming near **1,114 in October 2002**, wiping out trillions in value and erasing essentially all of the bubble's gains. And here is the crucial nuance that connects to the misconceptions above: the internet thesis was *correct*. The companies that would dominate the next two decades were being born. The bubble was not wrong about the technology; it was wrong about the *price*, and about which specific companies would survive. Being right about the future is not the same as being right about the price today.

### 5. The Housing Bubble and the 2008 Crisis

The 2000s displacement was cheap credit and financial engineering: low interest rates, a global appetite for safe-seeming assets, and the securitization machine that bundled mortgages into products that could be sold worldwide. The "this time is different" story was seductive precisely because it sounded conservative: **national U.S. home prices had never fallen significantly, so mortgages were safe, so bonds built from them were safe.** It was Ponzi finance at national scale — loans made to borrowers who could only repay if home prices kept rising, so they could refinance.

U.S. national home prices, measured by the Case-Shiller index, peaked in 2006 and then fell on the order of **27\%** to their 2012 trough — the deepest national decline since the Great Depression, and the "never happens" event the whole structure had assumed away. As mortgage-backed securities imploded and the leverage unwound (Lehman Brothers failed in September 2008), the S&P 500 fell from **1,565 in October 2007** to **677 in March 2009**, a drawdown of about **57\%** — the worst since the Second World War. The lesson of 2008 is Minsky's exact lesson: the system was most fragile at the moment it felt safest, because years of stability had pushed everyone down the ladder into fragile financing.

### 6. Crypto, 2017 and 2021

Cryptocurrency has, helpfully for our purposes, run the full bubble cycle *twice* in living memory, which is about as clean a natural experiment as markets ever offer. The displacement is real: blockchains are a genuine innovation, and a new asset class was being built in public.

In the **2017** cycle, Bitcoin ran to a peak of roughly **\$19,783 in December 2017**, propelled by an initial-coin-offering mania, then fell to around **\$3,200 by December 2018** — a drawdown of about **84\%**. Then it did it again: in the **2021** cycle, Bitcoin reached roughly **\$69,000 on 10 November 2021**, amid "digital gold" and "future of money" euphoria and extreme ecosystem leverage, before collapsing to around **\$15,500 by November 2022** as leveraged funds, lenders, and the FTX exchange failed — a drawdown of about **77\%**. Two cycles, two "this time is different" stories, two crashes of the classic 75-90\% magnitude. The asset may well have a real long-term future — that is not the point. The point is that even a genuinely revolutionary technology prices through the identical human script, and the last buyers in each cycle took the same catastrophic losses as the last buyers in every cycle before them.

## The phase-locator drill

You cannot control the crowd, but you can locate yourself inside it and size your risk accordingly. That is the entire practical payoff of the phase model. Here is the drill.

### Step one: score the phase

Before you add risk to any hot market, run the scorecard below. Each item is a yes-or-no question. Count the yeses.

![A three-by-three scorecard of yes-or-no phase signals — euphoria, leverage, new-era talk, a broad crowd, stretched valuation, and your own behavior — mapped to an action based on how many are true](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-7.webp)

1. **Euphoria?** Is the price chart parabolic, and are people around you bragging about easy gains?
2. **Leverage?** Is the move powered by margin, options, or people borrowing to buy?
3. **New-era talk?** Is "this time is different" — old valuations do not apply — the dominant narrative?
4. **Broad crowd?** Are influencers and first-time buyers pouring in, people who cannot explain what they bought?
5. **Valuation?** Has the price detached from any cash-flow-based estimate of value?
6. **Your own behavior?** Are *you* sizing up beyond your plan and checking the price hourly?

Scoring: **0-2 yes** and you are early or in a normal trend — you can participate, with a stop. **3-4 yes** and you are in a late boom — reduce and de-risk. **5-6 yes** and you are in euphoria — the job is to protect capital, not to chase. Note that question six is about *you*: your own behavior is often the most honest indicator of the phase, because your euphoria is the crowd's euphoria felt from the inside.

### Step two: size to the phase, not to your conviction

The rule that lets you participate in a boom without being the last buyer is simple to state and hard to follow: **your position size should shrink as the phase gets later, so that when the top arrives, it finds you already small.**

![A decision graph for participating without being the last buyer: locate the phase, and in an early phase enter small with a trailing stop and risk only house money, while in a late phase scale out into strength and stop making new buys, so you survive to trade the next cycle](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-bubble-from-tulips-to-crypto-8.webp)

The logic runs opposite to instinct. Instinct says: the more a trade is working, the more conviction I have, so the bigger I should bet. The phase model says: the more a trade has worked, the later the phase, the higher the price relative to value, and the *smaller* I should bet. You want your largest exposure early, when the risk-reward is best and few believe, and your smallest exposure at the euphoric peak, when everyone believes and the reward is nearly spent.

Concretely, a phase-aware protocol looks like this:

- **Early / boom (0-2 yes):** enter, but with a predefined trailing stop, and — a discipline worth its own emphasis — risk only "house money," the profits from the move itself, rather than adding fresh capital at higher and higher prices. This is the single best defense against becoming the last buyer, because it caps how much *new* money you can lose to the top.
- **Late boom (3-4 yes):** stop adding. Begin scaling out — selling into strength, into the euphoria that is still buying — so that your position shrinks as the price rises. You will sell too early. Selling too early is the price of not selling too late, and it is a bargain.
- **Euphoria (5-6 yes):** make no new buys. Your job is capital preservation. Take profits, cut leverage to zero, and accept that you will leave money on the table if the mania runs further. Leaving money on the table is survivable; being the leveraged last buyer is not.

To make it concrete, take a simple phase-scaled position. You start a boom with \$10,000 committed. As the scorecard climbs from 2 yeses to 3 or 4, you sell a third, taking \$4,000 or so off the table and pocketing gains. As it reaches 5 or 6 yeses — full euphoria — you sell down to a small residual, maybe \$2,000, funded entirely by profits, with your original capital already home and safe. If the mania keeps running, you make less than the person who stayed fully in; you will feel that, and it will sting. But when the -80\% crash arrives, it hits a \$2,000 residual of house money instead of a \$10,000 stake plus leverage, and you keep the rest. You traded some upside for the one thing the last buyer never has: survival.

The whole protocol has one purpose: to make sure that when the greater fools run out — and they always do — you are not the last one holding. **You do not need to call the top. You only need to be getting smaller as everyone else gets bigger, so that the crash, whenever it comes, finds you with capital left to trade the next cycle.**

## When this matters to you

You will meet a bubble. Maybe not in a market you trade — maybe in your city's housing, your industry's hot startup, a coin your coworker will not stop mentioning, an AI stock, whatever the "this time is different" story of the moment turns out to be. When you do, you will feel every emotion in this article, in order, and they will feel like insight rather than symptoms. That is the trap: the euphoria does not announce itself as euphoria. It announces itself as *being right*.

The defense is not to avoid every boom — that costs you the real opportunities the displacement creates. The defense is to keep the phase model in view, to treat "this time is different" as a signal to reduce rather than a reason to press, to refuse leverage in the late innings, and to size down as the crowd sizes up. You can participate. You just cannot afford to be the last buyer, and the last buyer is always the one who felt the most certain.

None of this is investment advice; it is a description of a recurring human mechanism and how to keep it from taking your capital. No indicator here predicts a top, and no rule guarantees you keep your gains. What the phase model offers is not prophecy but *orientation* — knowing roughly where you are standing in a process that has repeated for four centuries, so that your decisions are made against the crowd's emotion instead of with it. Markets will keep producing bubbles for as long as humans price them, because the emotions that build bubbles are not bugs to be fixed but features of the mind. The most you can do — and it is a great deal — is learn to recognize the arc while you are still on the early part of it.

If you want to go deeper on the specific emotions that power the arc, the companion pieces to this one are the most useful next steps: [herding, social proof, and FOMO](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/herding-social-proof-and-fomo) on why crowds form, [the narrative fallacy](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/the-narrative-fallacy-when-a-good-story-beats-the-data) on why the "new era" story beats the data, and [greed, euphoria, and the anatomy of a blowup](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup) on the individual-account version of the same collapse.
