Master the amplifiers or they master you.

Here is a number that should stop you cold. On a single Friday — October 10, 2025 — more than **\$19 billion** of leveraged crypto bets were force-closed in twenty-four hours, and roughly **1.6 million** individual traders were wiped out, according to the derivatives-data firm [Coinglass](https://www.coindesk.com/research/market-spotlight-the-19-billion-liquidation-that-shook-crypto). It was the largest single-day liquidation in the history of the asset class, about nine times bigger than any previous record. The trigger was not a hack or a fraud. It was a sentence: a threat of new tariffs, posted online, that sent a jittery, over-leveraged market into a cascade. Millions of people lost real money in an afternoon because of a tweet and the leverage they were carrying when it landed.

If you have read the rest of this series, none of the *mechanisms* behind that day are new to you. Fear, greed, herding, loss aversion, the fear of missing out, falling in love with a position — those are human constants, and they run in every market, from Treasury bonds to farmland. What makes crypto different is not a new bias. It is that crypto takes every bias you already have and hands each one a power tool. The market that never closes takes your ordinary need for sleep and turns it into chronic impairment. The leverage that goes to 100x takes your ordinary greed and turns a 1\% wobble into a total loss. The social feed takes your ordinary social instincts and weaponizes them. And the culture takes your ordinary attachment to being right and fuses it to your identity. Same diseases; industrial-strength doses.

![The 24/7 amplification machine: an always-on crypto market feeds four amplifiers — no rest, extreme leverage, social feeds, and identity — that each turn an ordinary bias into a portfolio-ending failure, with retail as the exit](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-1.webp)

The diagram above is the mental model for this whole article, and everything below is a tour of it. On the left is the one structural fact that starts the chain: a market that is *always on*. Out of that flow the four amplifiers — no rest, extreme leverage, engineered social feeds, and identity fusion — and each one converts a specific, familiar mistake into a fatal one: an over-sized entry made while exhausted, a forced liquidation on a tiny move, buying the top into a shill, holding a coin all the way to zero because you can no longer tell the difference between selling the coin and betraying yourself. And they all drain into the same place on the right: *you* become the exit liquidity. Your loss is not lost to the universe. It is somebody else's realized gain. This piece takes the machine apart amplifier by amplifier, grounds each one in worked dollar examples and real, dated episodes, and ends with a boring, specific defense protocol. Let's start by defining the parts.

## Foundations: how the 24/7 machine works

You do not need any prior finance or crypto knowledge to follow this. But you do need a handful of plain ideas defined from zero, because the psychology later only bites once you can see the building blocks it runs on. A practitioner can skim this section; a newcomer should not skip it.

**A cryptocurrency, and why it trades all the time.** A *cryptocurrency* is a digital token whose ownership is tracked on a shared public ledger (a *blockchain*) instead of by a bank. Bitcoin and Ethereum are the two largest. The detail that matters for psychology is mundane: because these ledgers run on computers around the world with no central exchange that "opens" and "closes," the market for them runs **24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year**. There is no closing bell. There is no weekend. The price is doing something right now, while you sleep, and it will still be doing something at 3am your time.

**The stock market, for contrast, is built to rest you.** A stock exchange like the New York Stock Exchange is open roughly six and a half hours a day and is shut every night and every weekend. It also has *circuit breakers*: rules that automatically halt all trading if the market falls too fast. In US equities, a 7\% single-day drop in the S&P 500 triggers a 15-minute halt (Level 1), a 13\% drop triggers another (Level 2), and a 20\% drop closes the market for the rest of the day (Level 3). These are not conveniences; they are deliberate speed bumps designed to give panicking humans a forced pause. Crypto has none of them.

**Leverage, margin, and liquidation.** To trade *with leverage* is to trade with borrowed money. If you put up \$1,000 of your own cash (your *margin*) and the exchange lets you control a \$100,000 position, you are using **100x leverage** — your position is 100 times your own money. Leverage multiplies both your gains and your losses on the price move. The dangerous part is *liquidation*: the moment your losses eat through your margin, the exchange automatically closes your position by selling it, so it never has to cover your debt. You do not get a margin call and a friendly phone request for more cash the way an old-school broker might give you; the machine just sells you out, instantly. The higher your leverage, the smaller the price move it takes to reach that point.

**Volatility.** *Volatility* is just how much and how fast a price moves. Crypto is far more volatile than stocks or bonds — daily swings of 5\% to 10\% are routine, and a −20\% day is not rare. High volatility is neutral on its own, but combined with leverage it is lethal, because the same move that is a shrug at 2x is a wipeout at 50x.

**Supply, market cap, and "exit liquidity."** A token's *market capitalization* (market cap) is its price times the number of coins in existence. It sounds like "how much money is in it," but it is not — market cap is a *notional* headline number. If a token has one billion coins and the last trade was at \$5, the market cap reads \$5 billion, even if only a few thousand dollars actually changed hands to set that \$5 price. This gap is the engine of manipulation. *Exit liquidity* is the plain, brutal name for the traders who buy from an insider who is selling. If a founder holds most of the supply, they cannot cash out into an empty market — there has to be a crowd buying on the way up so they have someone to sell *to*. That crowd is the exit liquidity. Remember that phrase; half this article is about not being it.

**FOMO, FUD, and shilling — the feed's vocabulary.** *FOMO* is the fear of missing out — the panic that everyone else is getting rich without you. *FUD* stands for "fear, uncertainty, and doubt" — the deliberate spreading of scary narratives to make you sell. *Shilling* is talking up an asset you already own so other people buy it and lift your price. These are not fringe behaviors in crypto; they are the water the whole culture swims in, and much of the "conviction" you read online is one of them wearing a costume.

With those parts named, the four amplifiers each get their own section. We take them in the order the machine runs them.

## 1. No close, no rest: the market that never lets you sleep

Every other bias in this series assumes a rested brain making a bad decision. Crypto quietly removes the "rested" part. A market that never closes does not just offer you the *option* to trade at any hour — it applies a subtle, constant pressure to *check*, because something is always happening and the thing that is happening might be the move that matters. The result, for a serious crypto trader, is a life of interrupted sleep, phone-on-the-nightstand vigilance, and decisions made in states of fatigue that would be disqualifying in almost any other high-stakes job.

This is not a soft, hand-wavy "get more sleep" point. Sleep deprivation has a measurable, quantified cost to exactly the faculties trading depends on: judgment, impulse control, and risk assessment. The landmark study is Drew Dawson and Kathryn Reid's 1997 paper in *Nature*, ["Fatigue, alcohol and performance impairment"](https://www.nature.com/articles/40775). They kept people awake and measured their cognitive and motor performance against the same tests used to measure alcohol impairment. The finding has held up for nearly three decades: staying awake long enough degrades your performance the way *drinking* does.

![Every hour awake taxes your judgment like alcohol: by 17 hours your decision-making matches a 0.05% blood-alcohol level, by 22 hours 0.08% (the US legal driving limit), and by 24 hours 0.10%](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-2.webp)

The chart above shows the equivalence the study established. After **17 hours** awake, your performance matches a blood-alcohol concentration of about **0.05\%**. After **22 hours**, it matches **0.08\%** — the legal driving limit in every US state. After **24 hours**, it matches **0.10\%**, which is past the legal limit everywhere. Sit with what that means for a crypto trader specifically. If you woke at 7am and the market does something violent at 11pm, you are making a leveraged decision at hour 16, roughly a glass-of-wine's worth of impairment. If a 3am liquidation alert drags you out of bed, you may be deciding at a level the law would not let you drive a car at. And the market does not care. It has no closing bell to protect you from yourself.

#### Worked example: the 3am decision tax

Suppose you hold a leveraged long position and go to bed at 11pm, sixteen hours after waking. At 3am your phone buzzes: the price has dropped and your liquidation is getting close. You are now twenty hours awake, functioning at roughly a 0.07–0.08\% blood-alcohol equivalent. Half-asleep, you make a decision that feels reasonable in the moment: instead of accepting the loss, you *add margin* to push your liquidation price further away — you send another \$2,000 to "defend" the position. In the morning, rested, you look at the same chart and see what you could not see at 3am: the trend was clearly broken, the right move was to close and sleep, and you have now put \$2,000 of *fresh* money behind a losing bet purely to avoid feeling the loss at 3am. The price keeps falling; you lose the original margin and the \$2,000 you added.

The loss was not really caused by the price. It was caused by *when* you were forced to decide. A rested version of you, at 3pm, would very likely have taken the small loss and moved on. **A market that never closes guarantees that some of your decisions get made by your worst, most exhausted self — and it will happily let that self trade.**

### The structural asymmetry: it can hunt you while you sleep

The deepest problem is not that you *can* trade at 3am. It is that the market can *move against you* at 3am whether you are awake or not, and in crypto there is nothing to stop the move. A stock you own cannot gap down at 3am on a Sunday, because the market is closed and there is no trading — the danger is paused while you sleep. A leveraged crypto position can be liquidated at 3am on a Sunday, in a thin, illiquid market, with no circuit breaker to slow the fall, and you will wake up to the wreckage.

![The stock market rests you; crypto never does: a legacy exchange gives your emotions a nightly close, a weekend, and hard circuit breakers, and crypto removes all three](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-3.webp)

The comparison above is the whole structural argument on one page. The stock market's hours, weekends, and circuit breakers are not just rules — they are *externally imposed emotional regulation*. They force the rest and the pause that a stressed human cannot reliably impose on themselves. Crypto strips all three away and hands the entire job of regulation to your willpower, at every hour, forever. That is a losing trade for almost everyone, because willpower is a depletable resource and the market has infinite patience. The people who survive crypto's clock do not win a willpower contest; they *rebuild the missing speed bumps by hand* — scheduled screen-off hours, no-trade windows, alerts turned off overnight. We will get to exactly how in the drill section. For now, hold the core idea: **the first thing crypto takes from you is the rest that every other market gives you for free.**

## 2. Leverage: how a 1\% move liquidates you

The second amplifier is the one that turns crypto's ordinary volatility into a weapon aimed at you personally: leverage. Stocks in a normal US brokerage account can be leveraged perhaps 2x. Regulated futures might reach into the tens. Crypto derivatives venues have historically offered **up to 125x**. Binance, the largest exchange, raised its maximum leverage on Bitcoin contracts to 125x in 2019, and only in July 2021 did it and FTX [cut the cap for most users to 20x](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2021/07/26/binance-says-its-cutting-leverage-limit-to-20x-a-day-after-ftx-announces-the-same) under regulatory pressure. Plenty of offshore venues still dangle 100x and up. To a beginner, high leverage looks like a bigger opportunity. It is really a smaller distance to zero.

Here is the single most important arithmetic in this article, and it is simple enough to keep in your head. If you use leverage `L`, the adverse price move that wipes out your entire margin is roughly **100\% ÷ L** (before fees and the exchange's maintenance-margin buffer, which make it slightly worse). At 2x, you have 50\% of room before you are gone. At 10x, 10\%. At 100x, **1\%**.

![The higher the leverage, the smaller the move that wipes you out: distance to liquidation is roughly 100 percent divided by leverage, so at 100x a 1 percent move erases your whole margin](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-4.webp)

The bars above are that formula made visible. Read them from left to right and watch the room to survive collapse. At 2x, a coin has to fall by half before you are liquidated — you can ride out an ordinary bad week. At 100x, the coin only has to twitch **1\%** against you and your entire stake is gone. In a market that routinely moves 5\% in an hour, a 100x position is not an investment; it is a coin flip that resolves in minutes, and the house edge (funding fees, the spread, and the maintenance buffer) is against you. The cruel psychology is that leverage *feels* like ambition and conviction — "I'm so sure, I'll size up" — when mathematically it is the opposite: it is a bet that the price will not wobble even slightly in the near term, which in crypto is almost the least defensible bet you can make.

#### Worked example: the 100x liquidation

Suppose you have \$1,000 and you are certain Bitcoin is going up. At 100x leverage, your \$1,000 of margin controls a \$100,000 position. Bitcoin rises 1\% — your \$100,000 position gains \$1,000, and you have doubled your money. Intoxicating. So you do it again, and this time Bitcoin *falls* 1\%. Your \$100,000 position loses \$1,000 — which is your entire margin. You are liquidated. Position closed, account at roughly zero.

Now compare the same conviction expressed at 2x. Your \$1,000 controls a \$2,000 position. Bitcoin falls 1\%: you lose \$20, or 2\% of your margin. It would take a **50\%** crash to wipe you out at 2x — a move that gives you days of warning and many chances to exit. The *view* was identical in both cases; only the leverage differed, and the leverage is what decided whether a routine 1\% dip was a rounding error or the end of your account. **Leverage does not amplify your returns so much as it shrinks the distance between you and zero — and in a market this volatile, that distance is the only thing keeping you alive.**

There is a quieter cost that makes leverage even worse than the wipeout math suggests: *funding*. On perpetual futures — the most common leveraged crypto instrument, a contract with no expiry date — longs and shorts pay each other a periodic *funding fee* to keep the contract's price tethered to the spot price. In a market where most people are bullish and leveraged long, that fee is usually paid *by* the longs. Hold a leveraged long through a flat, boring stretch and you slowly bleed funding every few hours, so even when the price does nothing, your position is quietly losing money. Leverage does not just shorten the distance to zero; it also charges you rent for standing there. The combination — a tiny margin of error and a constant fee — is why leveraged positions held "just for a while" so often end underwater even when the trader's directional call was roughly right.

### When everyone is leveraged at once: the cascade

Individual liquidation is a personal tragedy. The systemic version is a *liquidation cascade*, and it is why crypto crashes are so much faster and deeper than the underlying news seems to justify. Because so many participants are leveraged, a small initial dip does not just hurt the first wave of traders — it *forces them to sell*, and their forced selling pushes the price down further, which forces the next wave to sell, and so on.

![How a liquidation cascade feeds itself: a small dip force-sells the most leveraged longs, whose selling pushes price lower and liquidates the next tier, in a self-feeding spiral down to the bottom](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-5.webp)

The chain above is the anatomy of a crash like October 10, 2025. A modest 1–2\% dip — a whale selling, a piece of news — is enough to touch the liquidation price of the most aggressively leveraged longs. The exchange force-sells their collateral *at market, immediately*, because it will not carry their losses. That forced market-selling pushes the price down further. Lower prices reach the next tier of leverage, and it liquidates too. Meanwhile the market makers who would normally buy the dip see the cascade coming and pull their bids, so liquidity vanishes exactly when it is needed and the price gaps down through empty space. Round and round until the leverage is flushed out of the system.

#### Worked example: how one liquidation triggers the next

Suppose the order book near the current price is thin, and there is a cluster of 50x long positions that all get liquidated if the price falls 2\%. A whale sells enough to nudge the price down that 2\%. Those 50x longs are now force-sold — say \$200 million of them — and that \$200 million of *forced market-sell orders* eats through the thin book and drops the price another 3\%. That further 3\% drop reaches a cluster of 25x longs, force-selling *them*, adding another wave of market sells, and the price falls again. Each tier's forced selling is the fuel for liquidating the next tier. No new information arrived; the price fell 10\% in minutes purely because leverage begets forced selling begets lower prices begets more forced selling. On the record day, this loop consumed **\$19.13 billion** and about 1.6 million accounts before it exhausted itself. **In a leveraged market, the crowd's stop-losses become each other's triggers, so the crash manufactures its own fuel — and the more certain everyone was, the bigger the fire.**

## 3. The feed is the weapon: social-media manipulation

The third amplifier does not live in the market at all. It lives on your phone. Crypto is the most *online* asset class ever created — it was born on forums, it trades on sentiment, and its prices are moved, minute to minute, by what is being posted. That makes the social feed not a place where you *learn about* the market but a place where the market is actively *done to you*. Shilling, paid promotion, coordinated FOMO campaigns, engineered FUD, and outright pump-and-dumps are not occasional abuses of crypto social media; for a large share of tokens, they are the entire business model. The token exists so that someone can sell it to you.

The cleanest way to see the whole scheme is to follow the money through a pump-and-dump from the insider's side.

![A pump-and-dump timeline: insiders accumulate cheap supply, promote hard, retail FOMO buys the top, insiders sell into that buying, and retail holds a collapsed bag — the cash flows straight from retail to insiders](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-6.webp)

The timeline above is the entire game, stage by stage. **Stage 1:** insiders accumulate a huge fraction of the supply at a cost basis near zero — they created the token, so their coins are essentially free. **Stage 2:** the promotion begins — a paid influencer, a coordinated group, sometimes a genuinely famous person posts about it, and the price and volume spike. **Stage 3:** retail FOMO takes over. Ordinary buyers, terrified of missing the next 100x, pile in, and the market cap balloons. **Stage 4:** this is the actual purpose of the whole exercise — the insiders *sell their nearly-free coins into the retail buying*. The green in that stage is the insider's realized gain; it is real money, and it comes out of the buyers' pockets. **Stage 5:** with the insiders gone and no real demand underneath, the price collapses, and retail is left holding a bag worth a fraction of what they paid. The money did not evaporate. It moved — from the many who bought the top to the few who sold it to them.

#### Worked example: you, the exit liquidity

This is not a hypothetical; it is the arithmetic of the LIBRA token in February 2025, which we will detail in the case studies, rendered as your personal experience. Suppose insiders hold 82\% of a token's supply at essentially \$0 cost. A famous person promotes it, and within hours the market cap runs to \$4.56 billion. You are watching this happen live, the chart is vertical, the feed is euphoric, and the fear of missing the easiest money of your life becomes unbearable. You buy \$5,000 worth near the top. Behind the scenes, the insiders are doing the other side of your trade: they sell into the wall of buying — over \$107 million pulled out — and step away. With their supply dumped and no real buyers left, the price falls 95\% within hours. Your \$5,000 is now worth about \$250.

Where did your \$4,750 go? Not to "the market." It went, quite literally, into the accounts of the people who sold to you. **In a pump-and-dump, the "we're all gonna make it" energy in the feed is not a community — it is the sound of your money being escorted to someone else's wallet, and the more organic the enthusiasm feels, the more likely it was manufactured to make you buy.** The feed is not reporting the opportunity to you. The feed *is* the trap.

### FUD: the same weapon pointed the other way

Shilling drives you to *buy* at the top. Its mirror image, coordinated FUD, drives you to *sell* at the bottom — and it is just as engineered. When a large buyer wants to accumulate a coin cheaply, the last thing they want is a calm, confident crowd holding tight; they want frightened sellers dumping into their bids. So the feed fills with dread on cue: exaggerated hack rumors, screenshots of "smart money exiting," confident predictions of a further collapse, all timed to make you hit the sell button at exactly the moment a patient accumulator is buying. The tell is identical to shilling — anonymous, emotional, urgent certainty — only the direction is reversed. In a market that trades on sentiment, whoever controls the sentiment controls the price, and the crowd's panic is as monetizable as its greed. The practical rule is symmetric: just as you should not buy because strangers are euphoric, you should not sell because strangers are terrified. Both emotions in the feed are more likely to be someone's position talking than your signal.

### Why the feed beats your defenses

You might think you are too smart to be shilled. The problem is that the feed does not attack your reasoning; it attacks the older, faster machinery underneath it. Social proof — the instinct to copy what the crowd is doing — is not a flaw you can think your way out of; it is a survival heuristic that is usually right and is trivially easy to fake at scale. A thousand accounts posting the same bullish take feels like a thousand independent judgments even when it is one paid campaign wearing a thousand masks. This is why crypto FOMO is a different animal from the ordinary [fear of being left behind](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/fomo-and-the-fear-of-being-left-behind): it is not just your own psychology turning against you, it is your psychology being *deliberately engineered against you* by people who profit from your buying. The related dynamics of [herding, social proof, and FOMO](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/herding-social-proof-and-fomo) are the same everywhere, but nowhere else are they so cheaply and precisely weaponized. The only reliable defense is structural, not intellectual: reduce the feed's access to you, and treat anonymous conviction as a sell order in disguise.

## 4. Identity: when the coin becomes who you are

The fourth amplifier is the quietest and the most destructive, because it disables the one safety mechanism the other three cannot touch: your ability to change your mind. Crypto culture, more than any market before it, invites you to make your holdings part of who you *are*. "Bitcoin maximalist." "Ethereum is my chain." "Diamond hands." "We're all in this together." These are not descriptions of a portfolio; they are declarations of identity and tribe. And once a position is fused to your identity, selling it stops being a financial decision and becomes a kind of self-betrayal — an admission not just that you were wrong about a trade, but that you were wrong about *who you are*.

This is the [endowment effect](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/position-sizing-as-emotional-regulation) — our tendency to overvalue what we own simply because we own it — on steroids. In an ordinary market, the endowment effect makes you hold a losing stock a little too long. In crypto, where the ownership is bound up with community, ideology, and public commitment, it makes you hold all the way down, defending the position to strangers online, because letting go would mean leaving the tribe. The math of that decision is merciless.

![Holding on conviction turns a bad loss into a permanent one: a rules-based exit at minus twenty percent keeps most of the capital, while identity holds to minus eighty percent, where breakeven needs a plus four hundred percent recovery](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-7.webp)

The chart above shows the two paths down the same crash. The dashed line is your \$100 entry; the dotted line is a rules-based stop at \$80. Follow the price as it falls. A trader with a pre-committed exit sells at \$80, taking a −20\% loss, and keeps \$8,000 of a \$10,000 stake. A trader whose identity is fused to the coin cannot sell at \$80 — selling would mean they were wrong about the thing they told everyone they believed — so they hold, and hold, and hold, all the way to \$20, a −80\% loss, with only \$2,000 left. And here is the part the identity-holder never does the math on: **recovering from −80\% requires a +400\% gain.** Not +80\%. To turn \$2,000 back into \$10,000, the coin must *quintuple*. The deeper the drawdown, the more absurd the recovery math, and identity is the thing that keeps you in the position precisely as the recovery math becomes impossible.

#### Worked example: holding a coin down 80\%

Suppose you buy 100 coins at \$100 each — \$10,000 at risk. You have posted about this coin. Your online handle references it. Your friends know you as the person who is early on it. The coin falls to \$80. A disciplined trader would be out here, down \$2,000, annoyed but intact. But selling at \$80 would mean telling your tribe you sold, admitting you were wrong, ceasing to be the person you have publicly become — so you hold, and you rationalize: "weak hands are selling, this is when conviction pays." The coin falls to \$60, then \$40. Each level, selling feels like a bigger betrayal, because you have now suffered *for* the position and quitting would make the suffering pointless (that is sunk-cost thinking layered on top of identity). It reaches \$20. Your \$10,000 is \$2,000. To break even you now need the coin to go from \$20 back to \$100 — a +400\% move — while you have already been wrong about its direction for the entire fall.

The financial loss is severe, but the mechanism is the point: **you did not hold because you analyzed the coin and judged it undervalued at \$20; you held because selling it had become indistinguishable from admitting something about yourself, and identity will happily bankrupt you to protect a story.** The defense, which we will formalize shortly, is deceptively simple to say and hard to live: it must be an *asset*, never an *identity*. An asset you can sell. An identity you can only defend.

> An asset is something you own. An identity is something that owns you. The moment a coin crosses from the first to the second, you have stopped being an investor and become a hostage — and hostages do not manage risk.

## What it looks like at the screen

Biases feel invisible until they cost you; the warfare, by contrast, is physical and specific, and you can learn to recognize it in your own body and behavior before the money goes. Here are the tells — what the 24/7 psychological war actually looks like at the screen, in the small hours, in the tightening of your own attention.

Your phone is on the nightstand, and it is the last thing you look at before sleep and the first thing you reach for on waking — not to check messages, but to check the price. You have the exchange app's price alerts on, and they go off at night, and you have trained yourself not to fully sleep through them. You find yourself doing the arithmetic of your liquidation price in idle moments — in the shower, in a meeting, at dinner — computing how far the coin can fall before you are gone. When a green candle prints, you feel a small chemical lift, a hit of something, and you refresh to get it again; when a red one prints, your stomach drops and you refresh to make it stop. You are refreshing a chart the way a person pulls a slot-machine lever.

The feed has colonized your attention. You have muted the people who doubt your coin and followed the ones who pump it, and you tell yourself this is "curating signal" when it is building an echo chamber. You feel a flash of anger at a stranger's bearish post — actual anger, at a person you will never meet, about a token — and that anger is the tell that the coin has moved from your portfolio into your identity. You check the price during moments that used to belong to other people: mid-conversation, at your kid's event, at 3am when you surface from sleep and cannot resist. You have started to narrate your life in the coin's terms — a good day is a green day — and the reverse, a red day bleeding into a bad mood, a bad week of the market becoming a bad week of *you*.

And the physical signs of the impairment are all there if you look: the thin, broken sleep; the jaw tension; the way your decisions get faster and worse as the night wears on; the "just one more check" that becomes an hour. None of these are moral failings. They are the predictable output of a machine engineered — by the market's structure, by the leverage, by the feed's designers — to capture exactly this much of you. The first act of defense is simply to *notice* them, out loud, and to name what is happening: this is the warfare, and right now it is winning.

## Common misconceptions

**"High leverage is for confident traders."** Backwards. High leverage is a bet that the price will not move against you *even slightly* in the near term, which in a market that swings 5\% an hour is close to the least confident thing you can believe. The professionals who trade crypto seriously use little leverage precisely because they respect the volatility. Maximum leverage is a beginner's tell, not a pro's.

**"If I just have conviction and hold, I'll be fine — look at the people who held Bitcoin."** This is survivorship bias wearing a motivational T-shirt. You see the handful who held the one asset that recovered; you do not see the vastly larger number who "held with conviction" through a token that went to zero and stayed there. Conviction is not a strategy; it is the *absence* of an exit plan, dressed up as a virtue. The coins that recovered and the coins that died looked identical on the way down.

**"The 24/7 market is an advantage — I can trade whenever I want."** The always-on market is a liability disguised as a feature. The freedom to trade at any hour is really the obligation to *defend* at any hour, against a market that can move while you sleep, with your judgment degrading the later it gets. Freedom you cannot switch off is not freedom; it is a leash.

**"I can tell when I'm being shilled."** Almost no one can, reliably, because shilling does not target your reasoning — it targets social proof, an instinct that runs beneath reasoning and is trivially faked at scale. A thousand coordinated accounts feel like a thousand independent opinions. Assuming you are immune is exactly the confidence the manipulation is designed to exploit.

**"Stablecoins and 'blue-chip' coins are safe, so I can size them big."** The word "stable" is a marketing term, not a guarantee. In May 2022, an algorithmic stablecoin called UST — explicitly designed to hold \$1 — lost its peg and helped erase roughly \$40 billion in days. "Blue-chip" and "stable" describe the story a token tells about itself, not a floor under its price.

**"It's down 80\%, so it's cheap now."** A token down 80\% can fall another 80\% from there; "cheap" is a comparison to a past price the market has already rejected. And even if it stabilizes, remember the recovery math — from −80\% you need +400\% just to break even. Price is not value, and a lower price on a broken asset is not a discount.

**"I'll just take profits on the way up and play with house money."** In a 24/7 market with engineered feeds, the plan to "take profits later" almost never survives contact with a vertical chart and a euphoric timeline — the same FOMO that got you in makes selling feel like quitting early, so you ride the whole gain back down. A profit you have not actually realized is not house money; it is a number on a screen that the market can repossess in an hour. The only house money is money that has left the exchange and landed in your bank.

## How it shows up in real markets

The machine is not a theory. Here are five named, dated episodes where these exact amplifiers did their work, with the figures sourced. The round numbers in the earlier worked examples were teaching tools; every figure below is a real event you can look up.

### 1. October 10, 2025: the \$19 billion cascade

On Friday, October 10, 2025, a threat of steep new US tariffs on Chinese imports hit a crypto market carrying enormous leverage. The result was the largest single-day liquidation in the asset class's history. Coinglass recorded **\$19.13 billion** of leveraged positions force-closed and roughly **1.6 million** traders wiped out in 24 hours, with long positions accounting for [85–90\% of the liquidations](https://www.coindesk.com/research/market-spotlight-the-19-billion-liquidation-that-shook-crypto). Bitcoin fell about 14\%, from roughly \$122,000 to about \$105,000 at the worst; some smaller tokens briefly lost 70–80\% of their value in the vacuum. This is the leverage-cascade section come to life: no fraud, no broken protocol, just a jittery over-leveraged crowd whose forced selling became its own fuel. The lesson is the one the cascade diagram teaches — in a leveraged market, a piece of news is only the spark; the leverage is the gasoline, and how much you are carrying when the spark lands decides whether you get singed or incinerated.

### 2. Terra/LUNA, May 2022: ~\$40 billion in days

Terra was an ecosystem built around UST, an *algorithmic stablecoin* engineered to hold a \$1 peg through a mint-and-burn link to a sister token, LUNA. It was widely held, paid a high yield, and had a passionate identity-fused community. On May 9, 2022, UST slipped from its peg; confidence cracked, a bank-run dynamic took hold, and the mechanism that was supposed to defend the peg instead hyperinflated LUNA's supply from a few hundred million coins into the trillions. LUNA collapsed from an April high around \$116 toward a fraction of a cent, and the ecosystem lost on the order of **\$40 billion** in a matter of days, per multiple accounts including a [Harvard Law analysis](https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2023/05/22/anatomy-of-a-run-the-terra-luna-crash/) of the run. Its founder was later criminally charged. Terra is the misconception "stable coins are safe" written in \$40 billion of losses, and a case study in how identity and yield can keep a crowd holding a thing all the way to zero.

### 3. FTX, November 2022: an \$8 billion hole

FTX was one of the largest, most trusted crypto exchanges in the world, run by a founder the industry lionized. On November 2, 2022, a [CoinDesk report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bankruptcy_of_FTX) revealed that FTX's affiliated trading firm, Alameda Research, held a huge, illiquid position in FTX's own exchange token, FTT. That single article triggered a bank run: customers rushed to withdraw, and the withdrawals exposed a roughly **\$8 billion** shortfall between what customers had deposited and what FTX actually held. On November 11, 2022, FTX filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy, its founder resigned, and John J. Ray III — the restructuring specialist who had handled Enron — took over. FTX is the counterparty-risk lesson every crypto trader eventually learns: the exchange holding your coins is itself a leveraged, opaque, human institution, and "not your keys, not your coins" is not a slogan but a survival rule.

### 4. LIBRA, February 2025: the \$4.56 billion promotion

On February 14, 2025, Argentina's president, Javier Milei, promoted a token called \$LIBRA to millions of followers. It ran to a market cap of about **\$4.56 billion** within hours, then collapsed roughly **95\%** almost as fast. On-chain analysis showed that a small cluster held about **82\%** of the supply and that insiders pulled over **\$107 million** in liquidity during the spike, with retail investors absorbing an estimated **\$251 million** in losses, per a [DL News timeline](https://www.dlnews.com/articles/markets/how-did-milei-libra-memecoin-crash/) of the scandal. LIBRA is the pump-and-dump timeline in its purest, most public form — famous promoter, concentrated insider supply, a wall of FOMO buying, insiders selling into it, and a crowd of exit liquidity left holding the collapse. That the promoter was a head of state changed the scale and the scandal, not the mechanics.

### 5. Squid Game token, November 2021: the smart contract that would not let you sell

Riding the popularity of a hit TV show, a token called SQUID launched in late 2021, spiked on hype, and reached a peak around **\$2,861**. Then, on November 1, 2021, it crashed **99.99\%** to essentially zero in minutes. The mechanism was brutal and instructive: the developers had written the smart contract to *block ordinary holders from selling* while they themselves drained the liquidity — a "rug pull." They vanished with an estimated **\$3.38 million**, funneled through a mixing service, per contemporaneous reporting including [The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2021/11/02/squid-game-crypto-rug-pull/). SQUID is the extreme end of "the feed is the weapon": the entire product was a trap, the enthusiasm was manufactured, and the code itself was engineered so that only the insiders could exit. It is a permanent reminder that in crypto, "you can buy it" and "you can sell it" are two different questions.

### 6. Bitcoin's bear markets: even the survivor bruises

The coins that go to zero are the obvious lesson; the subtler one lives in the asset that *survived*. Bitcoin is the great crypto success story, and it has still put its holders through repeated, brutal drawdowns that broke the "just hold with conviction" crowd every cycle. It fell roughly **77\%** from its November 2021 high near **\$69,000** to about **\$15,500** by November 2022 (as reported in Bitcoin price history), and earlier cycles were even worse — on the order of −84\% in 2018 and deeper still in 2015 and 2011. Each time, the price eventually recovered to new highs, which is exactly why the survivorship-bias misconception is so seductive. But look at what "hold with conviction" actually asked of you in real time: to sit through an 80\%-plus paper loss, for a year or more, while the feed screamed that it was going to zero, with no guarantee — *at the time* — that this cycle would be one of the ones that came back. Most people cannot do that, and the ones who tell you they did are, disproportionately, the ones it worked out for. The lesson is not "don't hold Bitcoin." It is that conviction is not a substitute for [position sizing](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/position-sizing-as-emotional-regulation). A stake small enough that an 80\% drawdown does not threaten your life is a stake you can actually hold through the bruising; a stake large enough to hurt is one you will sell at the bottom, conviction and all.

## The drill: a crypto-specific defense protocol

Every amplifier in this article has a specific counter, and none of them require you to be smarter or more disciplined in the moment than the machine is. They work by *removing the moment* — by making the good decision in advance, when you are rested and calm, so the exhausted 3am version of you has less room to act. This is the same logic as [pre-commitment and rules](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/position-sizing-as-emotional-regulation) everywhere in trading, tuned for crypto's specific weapons.

![The crypto defense protocol: seven rules, each aimed at one amplifier — size far smaller, use little or no leverage, schedule screen-off hours, mute the tribe, treat it as an asset, assume anon conviction is someone's exit, and pre-commit written rules](/imgs/blogs/crypto-and-the-24-7-psychological-warfare-8.webp)

The protocol above pairs each rule with the amplifier it neutralizes. Here is each one, with the reasoning.

**1. Size far smaller than you think — roughly a quarter of your normal position.** Crypto's volatility means the *same dollar position* carries several times the risk of an equivalent stock position. If a −20\% day is routine, then a position sized for a stock's typical move is grotesquely oversized for a coin. Cut it to a fraction, so a brutal night is a survivable annoyance, not a catastrophe. This directly caps how much the extreme volatility can cost you.

**2. Use little or no leverage — ever.** This one rule removes the entire liquidation section from your life. With no leverage, no ordinary price wobble can force you to sell; you can only lose money the slow, recoverable way, by the price falling, not the fast, permanent way, by getting liquidated. The 1\% wipeout stops being a thing that can happen to you.

**3. Schedule screen-off and no-trade hours, and enforce them mechanically.** Rebuild the nightly close and the weekend that crypto took away. Turn off overnight price alerts. Designate hours when you do not look and do not trade, and use app timers or a separate device to make it real rather than aspirational. You are buying back the rested judgment the 24/7 market steals — and the Dawson & Reid math is your reason: decisions made past a certain hour are made drunk, so do not make them.

**4. Mute the tribal and hype feeds.** You cannot out-argue a manufactured social-proof campaign in real time, so do not fight it — reduce its access to you. Mute the shill accounts and the doom accounts alike. Get your information from sources that are not also trying to move a price you hold. This cuts the FOMO and FUD injection at the source.

**5. Treat it as an asset, not an identity.** Do not tie your handle, your public persona, or your sense of self to a coin. The test is simple: if selling this position would feel like a personal defeat rather than a portfolio adjustment, the endowment trap already has you, and you need to consciously separate the position from your identity *before* the drawdown, because you will not be able to during it.

**6. Assume any anonymous "conviction" is someone's exit.** When a stranger online is loudly, emotionally certain that a coin is going up, the base-rate-correct assumption is that they own it and want you to buy it so they can sell to you. Read every anonymous bullish take as a sell order in disguise. This is how you stop being the exit liquidity: by refusing to supply it.

**7. Pre-commit your rules in writing, when calm.** Decide in advance — in daylight, rested, before you have a position — exactly what you will do: your maximum size, your exit price, your no-leverage rule, your screen-off hours. Write it down. The written rule made by your best self is the only thing that reliably beats the decision your worst self wants to make at 3am. Every rule above is really this rule, applied to a specific weapon.

None of this is exciting. That is the point. The crypto machine runs on excitement — the green-candle dopamine, the FOMO, the tribe, the 100x fantasy — and the defense is to make your process deliberately, structurally *boring*, so that the exciting parts have nothing to grab. The traders who last are not the ones with the most conviction. They are the ones who took the machine's amplifiers away one by one and left it nothing to amplify.

## When this matters to you

If you never touch crypto, this article is still the cleanest available X-ray of your own psychology, because crypto is just every ordinary bias with the volume turned up until you can finally hear it. The FOMO that is subtle in stocks is deafening in a memecoin; the endowment effect that whispers in a mutual fund shouts in a maximalist tribe; the fatigue that quietly costs you in any market is measured and undeniable when the market runs at 3am. Seeing the biases at industrial strength makes them easier to spot at their normal, sneaky strength everywhere else.

If you *do* trade crypto, take the risk seriously and personally: every one of the mechanisms above can and does take people to zero, and it takes the confident ones first, because confidence is what sells them the leverage and the identity. Everything here is educational, not advice — the point is the machine and the mind inside it, not any coin or any trade. But the defense is not complicated, and it does not depend on being the smartest person in the market. Size small. Skip the leverage. Turn off the screen. Mute the tribe. Keep it an asset. Assume the loud stranger is selling to you. Write your rules down when you are calm, and let that rested self govern the exhausted one. The market will be open at 3am no matter what you do. The whole discipline is making sure you are asleep.
