> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Losing money is not what ends most trading accounts; *winning* is. Greed and euphoria remove the brakes, and every blowup then follows the same short, mechanical sequence — the same one, every time.
>
> - **The anatomy of a blowup is always the same six steps:** a small real edge, plus leverage, plus concentration, plus euphoria, meets one fat tail, and goes to zero. It is a sequence, not an accident.
> - **Winning makes you bet bigger, biochemically.** The "winner effect" means a hot streak raises testosterone and confidence, which quietly enlarge your position size right before the regime that paid you reverses.
> - **A bull market disguises beta and leverage as skill.** A +60% year in a market that rose 30% is mostly the market times your leverage, not you — and mistaking one for the other is how traders press their bet at exactly the wrong moment.
> - **The math is merciless and asymmetric.** A 50% loss needs a 100% gain to recover; a 90% loss needs 900%. And past a point, *more size lowers your long-run return even with a real edge* — oversizing turns a winning strategy into a losing one.
> - **The one drill that works:** treat the feeling of being invincible as a SELL/REDUCE signal, not a green light. Pre-set your profit-taking and de-risking rules while calm, and cut size after a big run with a high-water-mark humility rule.

You have felt the good version of the problem. A few trades go your way, the account is at a new high, and something loosens in your chest — a warm, expansive certainty that you have finally *figured it out*. The next setup looks obvious. Your usual size feels timid. You lean in, a little bigger than the plan says, because why wouldn't you? You're hot. You can feel it.

That feeling is the single most expensive emotion in all of trading, and almost nobody treats it as a warning. We are trained to fear fear — to worry about panic-selling the bottom, about freezing, about capitulation. But fear, for all its damage, tends to make you *smaller*: it liquidates, it flattens, it gets you out. Greed and euphoria do the opposite. They make you *bigger* — more leveraged, more concentrated, more committed — precisely as the odds are quietly turning against you. Fear costs you a trade. Euphoria costs you the account.

This article is about the mechanics of that. Not "control your greed," which is about as useful as "control your heartbeat," but the actual anatomy: the specific, repeatable sequence by which a competent trader with a real edge converts a winning streak into a smoking crater. The remarkable thing about blowups — from a retail options account to a $36 billion family office — is how *identical* they are. Different instruments, different sizes, different decades, same six steps. Learn the sequence and you can see it forming in your own P&L, usually while there is still time to step off it.

![The anatomy of a blowup: a small real edge plus leverage plus concentration plus euphoria meets one fat tail and goes to zero, in six repeating steps](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-1.webp)

The diagram above is the spine of the whole piece, and we will walk it stage by stage. It starts, counterintuitively, with something *good*: a **small real edge** — a strategy that genuinely makes money on average. That edge invites **leverage** to make the small edge feel worth the effort. Leverage rewards **concentration**, because diversification dilutes the bet. A run of wins produces **euphoria**, which abandons the rules that were holding the risk in check. And then — not if, but when — **one fat tail** arrives: a gap, a shock, a normal-but-large adverse move that the oversized, concentrated, leveraged book cannot survive. The result is **zero**. Every box in that chain is a decision that felt reasonable, even smart, at the time. That is what makes the sequence so durable: it is built from good ideas pushed one step too far.

## Foundations: how greed removes the brakes

Before we walk the spine, we need the machinery underneath it. You do not need any finance or neuroscience background — you need three ideas, each of which has a name, real research behind it, and a direct line to your P&L. A practitioner can skim this section; if you are newer to it, do not skip it, because everything after assumes these three building blocks are in your head. The three are: *why winning makes you reckless* (the biology), *why a rising market feels like talent* (the psychology), and *why the sequence mathematically ends at zero* (the arithmetic).

We are talking about **greed** and **euphoria**, so let us define them plainly. *Greed* is the pull to take more than your plan allows — more size, more leverage, more trades — because more feels available. *Euphoria* is the emotional state that greed rides in on: the post-win glow of confidence, invincibility, and urgency. Greed is the behavior; euphoria is the fuel. Both are the emotional opposite of fear, and they fail you in the opposite direction — not by making you flee a good trade, but by making you oversize a bad one.

### The winner effect: winning literally makes you bet bigger

Here is the fact that reframes everything, and it comes not from a trading guru but from biology. When two animals compete, testosterone rises in both. The winner emerges with testosterone elevated *further*; the loser's falls. That extra testosterone is not just a trophy — it sharpens reactions, raises persistence and fearlessness, and increases appetite for the next contest. So the winner is now *biochemically primed* to win again, which raises testosterone again. Biologists call this the **winner effect**: winning makes you better at, and hungrier for, winning.

John Coates, a former Wall Street derivatives trader who became a neuroscientist at Cambridge, argued that a trading bull market is a winner-effect machine, and he and Joe Herbert went and *measured* it. In their study ["Endogenous steroids and financial risk taking on a London trading floor"](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0704025105) (*PNAS*, 2008), they took saliva samples from 17 male traders on a real City of London floor over 8 business days, and found that a trader's *morning testosterone predicted his profit that day*. Higher testosterone, more risk-taking, more P&L in a market that happened to reward it. The winner effect is not a metaphor; it is a hormone with a dose-response curve, and your trading account is the dose. (For the full neurochemistry — dopamine, cortisol, the amygdala, and the whole endocrine tide — see the companion piece on [the neuroscience of risk and reward](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/the-neuroscience-of-risk-and-reward); here we care only about what it does to your *size*.)

![The winner-effect engine: a win raises testosterone and confidence, which enlarge position size, right before an ordinary loss at peak size round-trips the whole run](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-2.webp)

The diagram above is the engine that drives the euphoria stage of the blowup. Follow it left to right. A **win** raises testosterone. Elevated testosterone raises **confidence** — and, critically, it does not know the difference between skill and luck, so it reads a lucky streak as proof of genius. Confidence raises **size**: the creep from 1R to 3R that nobody consciously decides. Then the flow forks. On the good days, the **regime holds** — the trend continues, another win lands, and the loop tightens: more winning, more testosterone, more size. But eventually the other branch fires: the **regime turns**, an entirely ordinary loss arrives, and because it lands at *peak size on the biggest bet of the run*, it does not just sting — it round-trips the entire streak. The winner effect's cruelty is precisely this: it makes your largest position coincide with your greatest confidence, which is the exact opposite of what a rational risk model would do. Your worst-sized bet is placed at your most euphoric moment.

### Bull-market genius: mistaking beta and leverage for skill

The winner effect needs a *story* to justify the extra size, and the market kindly provides one: **"I'm good at this."** The problem is a confusion between three very different sources of return. **Beta** is the return you get just for being in the market — the tide that lifts everything when stocks go up. **Leverage** is borrowing to control more than your cash, which multiplies whatever return you're getting. **Alpha** is the return that comes from *your* skill, over and above the market. In a bull market, beta and leverage generate enormous returns that *feel* exactly like alpha — and there is no sensation that distinguishes them. Your account doesn't tell you which dollars came from being right and which came from being long in a rising market with borrowed money.

![In a bull market, beta times leverage feels exactly like skill: a +60% year decomposes into the market's +30% amplified by 2x leverage, with true alpha near zero](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-3.webp)

The diagram above tells the whole story of "bull-market genius." On the left is the felt experience: the account is up 60%, so the trader *must* be great, so pressing bigger is obviously correct. On the right is the arithmetic: the market rose 30% (that's beta, the tide), the trader was running 2x leverage (which turns +30% into +60% gross), and the actual skill contribution — the alpha — is somewhere near zero. Same number, +60%, two completely different explanations, and only the honest one tells you what happens when the tide goes out. This is not a hypothetical; it is the most common way a mediocre trader in a bull market becomes convinced they are a great one, right up until the market stops cooperating and the "skill" evaporates.

#### Worked example: the +60% year that was really the market

Suppose you start the year with a \$100,000 account and you run a simple strategy: you buy an index-tracking basket, and you use 2x leverage (you control \$200,000 of exposure using \$100,000 of your own cash plus \$100,000 borrowed). Say the market rises 30% over the year.

- **Your gross return.** Your \$200,000 of exposure gains 30%, which is +\$60,000. Ignoring borrowing costs, your \$100,000 of equity is now \$160,000 — a **+60% year**.
- **What it feels like.** You beat the market by 30 whole points. You feel like a genius. Every instinct says: next year, run 3x, concentrate into your best names, press the edge.
- **What actually happened.** Strip out the market's 30% (beta) and the 2x multiplier (leverage), and your *skill* added roughly nothing. You did not outperform; you were the index, amplified. A monkey with 2x leverage had the same year.
- **The wrinkle that ends it.** Run the same structure into a down year: the market falls 30%, your \$200,000 exposure loses \$60,000, and against your \$100,000 of equity that is a **−60% year**, leaving \$40,000. Same leverage, same "skill," and a normal down move has cut your account by 60%.

The one-sentence intuition: a rising market pays beta and leverage in dollars that feel exactly like skill, and the bill for confusing them comes due the first time the tide goes out.

### The mathematics of ruin: why the sequence ends at zero

The last building block is the coldest, and it is arithmetic, not psychology. There is a mathematics to blowups that operates regardless of how you feel, and it has three parts a beginner can hold in their head.

First, **returns multiply, they do not add.** If you lose 50% and then gain 50%, you are not back to even — you are at 0.5 × 1.5 = 0.75, down 25%. Losses and the gains needed to undo them are asymmetric, and the asymmetry gets vicious as losses grow. Second, **volatility drags on compounding.** Because returns multiply, a bumpy path to the same average return ends lower than a smooth one — a portfolio that goes +50% then −40% has an *average* return of +5% per period but has actually *lost* money. The bumpier (more leveraged, more concentrated) the ride, the more this "volatility drag" eats. Third, and most surprising, **there is an optimal bet size, and betting past it lowers your long-run return even with a positive edge.** This last one — formalized by the physicist John L. Kelly Jr. in his 1956 paper ["A New Interpretation of Information Rate"](https://www.princeton.edu/~wbialek/rome/refs/kelly_56.pdf) (*Bell System Technical Journal*) and later put to work in markets by Ed Thorp — is the mathematical heart of every blowup. We will build it up properly in Section 4. For now, hold the shape: *more size is not more return; it is more return up to a point, and then less, and then ruin.*

Put these three facts together and the spine's ending is not bad luck — it is a theorem. Leverage amplifies the volatility drag. Concentration makes the tail fatter. Euphoria pushes size past the optimal point. And the multiplicative math means one large enough loss cannot be recovered from. The sequence ends at zero because the arithmetic points there; euphoria just presses the accelerator.

> A blowup is not the moment the market surprised you. It is the moment the arithmetic you had been ignoring finally presented its bill.

## 1. Leverage: the amplifier that has no opinion

Leverage is the first accelerant, so start there. *Leverage* means controlling more exposure than your cash — through borrowing (margin), through derivatives (futures, options, swaps), or through any instrument that moves more than the money you put up. The seductive thing about leverage is that it makes a thin edge feel worth trading. A strategy that earns 2% a year is boring; the same strategy at 10x leverage earns 20%, and now you are interested. But leverage has no opinion about direction. It multiplies your losses with exactly the same efficiency it multiplies your gains, and it introduces a new failure mode that unleveraged trading does not have: the **margin call**, where a large enough loss forces you to sell at the worst possible moment, turning a paper loss into a permanent one.

The deepest danger of leverage is not the average outcome — it is what a single bad period does to a compounded return stream. Because losses multiply, one large drawdown does not subtract from your gains; it *scales* them down, and the scaling is permanent.

![One fat tail eats a whole year of gains: eleven months of +3% compound to +38%, then a single -40% month drags the full year to roughly -17%](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-5.webp)

The timeline above shows the arithmetic that leverage sets up. Read it as one year. For eleven straight months the strategy grinds out +3% — steady, confident, compounding. By the end of month eleven the account is up about 38%. This is the euphoria zone: eleven green months, the winner effect in full flow, size creeping up. Then month twelve delivers one fat tail: a −40% month, the kind that leverage and concentration make possible. And here the multiplicative math does its work. The +38% is not a cushion of 38 points to absorb the loss; it is a *multiplier*, 1.38, and the loss is another multiplier, 0.60. Together: 1.38 × 0.60 = 0.83. Eleven months of disciplined gains and one bad month leave you down about 17% on the year. The gains were additive-feeling; the loss was multiplicative-real.

#### Worked example: eleven good months, one bad month

Suppose you run \$100,000 and, thanks to a genuine edge amplified by leverage, you compound +3% a month for eleven months.

- **The climb.** Each month multiplies your account by 1.03. After eleven months: \$100,000 × 1.03^11 ≈ \$100,000 × 1.38 = **\$138,000**. You are up 38%, feeling brilliant, and — because of the winner effect — running more size than you started with.
- **The tail.** Month twelve, the concentrated leveraged book takes a −40% hit. \$138,000 × 0.60 = **\$82,800**. On the year you are down about 17%, despite being *right eleven times out of twelve*.
- **The comparison.** Had you run half the leverage, that same fat tail might have been −20% instead of −40%. \$100,000 × 1.015^11 × 0.80 ≈ \$117,700 × 0.80 ≈ **\$94,200** — still a losing year, but you kept most of your capital and lived to compound again.
- **The lesson in the numbers.** The eleven wins bought you +38%. The one loss took −40%. Because they multiply rather than add, being right 92% of the time was not enough; the *size* of the one loss, which leverage set, decided the year.

The one-sentence intuition: with leverage, a single fat-tail month does not dent your gains — it multiplies them down, so the size of your worst month, not the count of your good ones, sets your result.

**What this costs / when it breaks:** leverage's damage is invisible in the good times and total in the bad one. It flatters every up month, so it accumulates confidence and size, and then it presents the whole bill at once. The tell is a P&L curve that climbs a smooth staircase and then falls down an elevator shaft — the elevator is always leverage meeting a tail.

## 2. The asymmetry of the hole: why the climb out is steeper than the fall

Section 1 showed one loss scaling down a year. Now confront the general rule, because it governs everything about how blowups become *permanent* rather than temporary. When you lose money, the gain required to get back to even is always *larger* than the loss — and the relationship is not linear. It curves upward, and past the halfway mark it becomes almost vertical.

![The asymmetry of the hole: a 10% loss needs +11% to recover, a 50% loss needs +100%, and a 90% loss needs +900% to get back to even](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-4.webp)

The table above is worth memorizing, because it is the reason "I'll make it back" gets harder to keep promising as the hole deepens. Lose 10% and you need +11% to recover — annoying but easy. Lose 25%, you need +33%. But lose 50%, and you now need to *double* your remaining money, a +100% gain, just to break even. Lose 80%, and you need +400% — a five-bagger — from what's left. Lose 90%, and you need +900%, which for practical purposes means you are not coming back. The math is simply that recovering a loss of L requires a gain of L / (1 − L): the (1 − L) in the denominator shrinks toward zero as L grows, so the required gain explodes. This is why the goal of risk management is not to maximize gains but to *stay out of the deep part of this curve*, where recovery stops being a matter of skill and becomes a matter of needing a miracle.

#### Worked example: the depth of the hole

Suppose you start with \$100,000 and take a series of losses. Watch how the recovery requirement accelerates.

- **Down 10%.** You have \$90,000. To get back to \$100,000 you need \$10,000 on \$90,000 = **+11.1%**. One decent month.
- **Down 50%.** You have \$50,000. To get back you need \$50,000 on \$50,000 = **+100%**. You must double your money — which, at any honest rate of return, takes *years*.
- **Down 80%.** You have \$20,000. You need \$80,000 on \$20,000 = **+400%**. You now have to make five times your remaining capital, with a fifth of the capital to make it with.
- **Down 90%.** You have \$10,000. You need **+900%**. The blowup is mathematically, not just emotionally, near-terminal.

The one-sentence intuition: losses and recoveries are not symmetric, and past 50% the curve turns nearly vertical — which is why avoiding the deep drawdown matters infinitely more than catching the big gain.

This asymmetry is explored at length, with its own recovery math and risk-of-ruin tables, in the companion piece [the asymmetry of losses: why a 50% loss needs a 100% gain](/blog/trading/risk-management/the-asymmetry-of-losses-why-a-50-percent-loss-needs-a-100-percent-gain). For our purposes the point is simpler: euphoria pushes you toward exactly the leverage and concentration that make the deep, unrecoverable losses possible. It sends you into the steep part of the curve while telling you the curve doesn't apply to you.

## 3. Concentration: the exit that isn't there

The third stage of the spine is **concentration** — putting a large fraction of the book into one position, one theme, or one directional bet. Concentration is the natural partner of leverage and euphoria, because when you *know* you're right, diversification feels like dilution. Why hedge a bet you're sure of? Why spread capital across mediocre ideas when your best idea is *this good*? Every argument for concentration is an argument that feels stronger the more euphoric you are.

Concentration does two dangerous things. First, it removes diversification's cushion: with one big theme, there is no uncorrelated position to soften a shock, so the whole book moves together. Second, and more subtly, a large enough position *destroys its own exit*. If you own a small position, you can always sell it — there's a buyer. If you own a position that is large relative to the market's daily volume, there is no quiet way out: your own selling pushes the price down, so trying to exit *causes* the loss you're trying to escape. The bigger and more concentrated the position, the more the exit door shrinks, until at the extreme there is no door at all — only a stampede in which you are the largest body.

This is the difference between a *drawdown* and a *blowup*. A diversified, unleveraged portfolio has drawdowns: it falls, it hurts, it recovers. A concentrated, leveraged position has blowups: it falls, the leverage forces selling, the concentration means there's no bid, and the forced selling accelerates the fall in a spiral. The concentrated trader does not get to choose when to exit; the margin clerk chooses, and the market chooses the price, and both choose the worst possible moment. We will see this exact mechanism turn a \$36 billion fortune into a criminal trial later, in the Archegos case.

| | Diversified & unleveraged | Concentrated & leveraged |
|---|---|---|
| A bad shock produces | a drawdown | a blowup |
| Who decides when you exit | you do | the margin clerk does |
| The price you exit at | roughly the market price | far below it — your selling moves it |
| Recovery | likely, given time | often impossible (see the asymmetry curve) |
| What it feels like beforehand | boring, "under-invested" | brilliant, "high conviction" |

**What this costs / when it breaks:** concentration costs you nothing — until it costs you everything at once. It is quiet on the way up, and it is the reason the way down has no brakes. The tell that you have drifted into dangerous concentration is emotional as much as numerical: when a single position is large enough that you check *it* rather than your *account*, and when the case for not hedging is "because I'm sure," you are in the exit-less zone.

## 4. The oversizing cliff: when more size lowers your return

Now the deepest and least intuitive piece of the whole story, and the mathematical core of why greed is self-defeating even for a trader with a genuine edge. Common sense says: if a bet is profitable on average, betting more of it makes more money. Common sense is wrong, and the way it is wrong is the reason disciplined traders obsess over size.

The key distinction is between two kinds of "average." The **arithmetic mean** is what you get by averaging the returns — it describes a single bet in isolation. The **geometric mean** is what you actually compound — it describes what happens when you reinvest and let the bets multiply over time. Because returns multiply, the geometric mean is what determines whether your account grows or shrinks over a career, and the geometric mean is *lowered* by volatility. Bet too large, and you inject so much volatility that the geometric mean turns negative *even though the arithmetic mean is positive*. You have a profitable strategy and a shrinking account, at the same time, purely because of size.

![The oversizing cliff: long-run growth per trade rises to a peak at the optimal fraction, then falls, turning negative past twice it, where euphoria and greed do their sizing](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-6.webp)

The chart above is the single most important picture in risk management, and it is the exact opposite of what greed believes. On the horizontal axis is your bet size, measured as a multiple of the growth-optimal fraction. On the vertical axis is your long-run (geometric) growth per trade. Follow it from the left. At a small fraction (0.25x), growth is modestly positive — you're under-betting, leaving money on the table. It rises as you size up, peaking at the optimal fraction (1.0x): this is the point that maximizes long-run growth, the size a rational trader wants. But then it *turns down*. Past the optimal size, more betting *lowers* your compound growth. At twice the optimal fraction (2.0x), growth hits exactly zero — you have a positive-edge strategy that compounds to nothing. And past that, in the red zone, growth goes negative: you lose money over time *with a real edge*, purely from oversizing. That red zone, on the far right of the chart, is exactly where euphoria and greed put you. The winner effect does not gently nudge you toward the peak; it shoves you past it, off the cliff, into the region where your own edge works against you.

#### Worked example: the coin that pays +5% on average and ruins you

Suppose someone offers you a coin-flip bet with a genuine edge. Heads (50%) you gain 50% of what you stake; tails (50%) you lose 40% of what you stake. The average is clearly positive — but watch what betting your whole bankroll does.

- **The edge looks great.** Average return per flip = 0.5 × (+50%) + 0.5 × (−40%) = +25% − 20% = **+5% per flip**. On a spreadsheet, this is a money machine.
- **Bet the whole bankroll, and compound.** A win then a loss leaves you with 1.50 × 0.60 = **0.90** — down 10% over the two flips, no matter the order. Every win-loss pair *shrinks* your account by 10%, despite the +5% average.
- **Over time, that's ruin.** Ten win-loss pairs: 0.90^10 ≈ 0.35. You've lost about 65% of your money running a bet with a +5% edge, because you sized it at 100%.
- **The fix is a fraction.** For this bet, the growth-optimal fraction (the Kelly bet) works out to staking about 25% of your bankroll per flip, not 100%. At that size the geometric growth is positive and you compound up. Bet more than roughly twice that — over 50% of bankroll — and, as the cliff chart shows, your growth turns negative.

The one-sentence intuition: a positive edge is necessary but not sufficient — size it past the optimal fraction and the same edge compounds you toward zero, which is why "I'm sure, so I'll bet big" is a mathematical error, not just an emotional one.

**What this costs / when it breaks:** oversizing is the trap that catches *good* traders specifically, because it requires a real edge to be present — you can't over-bet an edge you don't have. The better your strategy, the more tempting it is to size it past the cliff, and the winner effect guarantees you'll feel most justified in doing so right after the wins that prove the edge is real. The tell: your position size is set by your recent P&L and your conviction, not by a fixed fraction of your account.

## 5. Position-size creep: the giveback after the peak

The oversizing cliff explains *why* size past the optimum is fatal. This section is about *how* you get there without ever deciding to — the mechanism euphoria uses to walk you off the cliff one small step at a time. It is called **size creep**, and it is the most common way disciplined traders become undisciplined ones.

Nobody wakes up and decides to triple their risk. What happens is subtler. You start with a rule: risk 1R — say 1% of the account — per trade. You win a few. The wins feel like confirmation, and 1% starts to feel timid, almost cowardly, given how *right* you've been. So on the next trade you put on a little more. It works. So you put on a little more again. Within a hot streak, without any single decision you'd flag as reckless, your risk per trade has drifted from 1R to 2R to 3R. And then the streak ends — as streaks do — and the ordinary losing trade that would have cost you 1% now costs you 3%, at the exact moment your size is largest.

#### Worked example: the creep and the giveback

Start with a \$100,000 account and a disciplined rule: risk 1R = 1% = **\$1,000** per trade. Now run a winning streak and watch the size creep resize your book without permission.

- **The streak.** Six wins, each averaging +1.5R = +\$1,500. The account gains 6 × \$1,500 = **+\$9,000**, sitting at \$109,000. Confidence up, testosterone up, and 1% now feels like leaving money on the table.
- **The creep.** You don't consciously change the rule. But trade seven feels *obvious*, so you put on 2R. Trade eight, still hot, 2.5R. Trade nine, 3R. Your risk per trade has tripled and you never made a decision you'd call reckless.
- **The giveback.** Losses come in clusters, because regimes change. Say three of your next oversized trades lose at 3R: that's 3 × \$3,000 = **−\$9,000** — the entire six-win streak, erased in three ordinary losses. At the disciplined 1R, those same three losses cost \$3,000, leaving \$6,000 of the streak intact.
- **The round-trip.** Same win rate, same market, same edge. The only variable that changed was the size the euphoria talked you into — and it converted a good run into a full round-trip back to where you started, or worse.

The one-sentence intuition: size creep is euphoria's method — it never asks you to be reckless once, only to be a little bolder many times, until an ordinary loss at peak size gives back the whole run.

The defense against size creep is not willpower in the moment — that is precisely when the winner effect has your judgment. It is a *pre-committed* rule that ratchets size *down* after a run, which we build into the drill at the end. The core move is counterintuitive and worth stating as a principle: **your size should be smallest when your confidence is highest**, because that is exactly when the winner effect has distorted your judgment and the regime is most extended. This is also the mirror image of the [house-money effect](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/mental-accounting-and-the-house-money-effect), where "playing with the market's money" after a win licenses bets you'd never make with your "own" money — the same relabeling of profit that greed uses to justify the creep.

## Common misconceptions

**"Blowups happen to reckless gamblers, not to people with a real edge."** Backwards. A blowup *requires* a real edge — or at least a real winning streak — to generate the confidence and the capital that fund the oversized bet. Pure gamblers bleed out slowly and quit. Blowups are a disease of the competent: the edge is what gets you leveraged and concentrated enough for one tail to matter. The Archegos case below was run by a genuinely skilled investor, not a degenerate.

**"If I'm winning, I should press my advantage."** This is the oversizing cliff in one sentence, and it's wrong past the optimal fraction. Pressing a winning strategy is correct only up to the growth-optimal size; beyond it, more size *lowers* your long-run return and eventually turns it negative. "Press when hot" is exactly the instinct that walks you off the cliff. The correct move after a run of wins is usually to *reduce*, not press.

**"A big enough winning streak proves I've figured it out."** A winning streak is consistent with skill, but it is also exactly what a lucky run of beta and leverage looks like from the inside — there is no felt difference. In a bull market the base rate of "winning streak caused by the market" is enormous. The streak is evidence, but it is weak evidence, and the winner effect makes you treat weak evidence as proof. Overconfidence is the best-documented and most expensive bias in trading precisely because streaks feel like certainty; see [overconfidence and the illusion of control](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/overconfidence-and-the-illusion-of-control) for the full case.

**"I'll just get out before it turns."** Concentration destroys this plan. If your position is large and leveraged, you cannot quietly exit — your own selling moves the price, and margin calls force your hand at the worst moment. The plan to "get out in time" assumes a liquid exit that a big concentrated position does not have. You are planning an escape from a room whose door shrinks as you approach it.

**"Diversification is for people without conviction."** Concentration feels like conviction and diversification feels like doubt, but that framing confuses a feeling with a risk decision. Diversification is not a statement about how sure you are; it is insurance against being sure *and wrong*, which is the single most expensive way to be wrong. The most confident traders need it most, because their confidence is what removes their other brakes.

**"The giveback was just bad luck at the end."** The losing trade that ends the streak is usually ordinary — a normal loss the strategy always had. What made it catastrophic was not its badness but its *size*, and the size was set by euphoria during the streak. Calling it bad luck misplaces the cause: the luck was ordinary; the size was the choice.

## How it shows up in real markets

Neuroscience and arithmetic are one thing; the same sequence playing out with real money, real names, and real dates is another. Here are four episodes — a family office, a "genius" hedge fund, a retail product, and a single rogue trader — read through the spine. A note before we start: for the psychological readings I am describing a *behavioral pattern* that matches the winner-effect arc, not claiming to have measured anyone's hormones. What we can point to is the structure, and the structure is always the same.

### 1. Archegos and Bill Hwang: the parabola to zero

The cleanest modern anatomy of a blowup belongs to Archegos Capital Management, the family office of Sung Kook "Bill" Hwang. Every stage of the spine is visible, and the numbers are on the public record. Archegos grew from roughly **\$1.5 billion in capital with about \$10 billion of exposure in March 2020 to more than \$36 billion in capital with about \$160 billion of gross exposure at its peak in March 2021** ([SEC, "SEC Charges Archegos and its Founder with Massive Market Manipulation Scheme"](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2022-70); [Archegos Capital Management, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archegos_Capital_Management)). Read the spine into that: a real edge (Hwang was a genuinely talented investor, a "Tiger cub" from Julian Robertson's stable), enormous leverage (roughly four to five times capital — about \$160 billion of exposure on \$36 billion of capital — built through *total return swaps*, derivatives that let him control the exposure without owning the stock), and extreme concentration in a handful of names like ViacomCBS and a few Chinese tech stocks.

The concentration is what removed the exit. Because Hwang held his exposure through swaps spread across several prime brokers who could not see each other's books, no single lender knew the aggregate size — and because a family office faces less disclosure than a fund, the market couldn't see it either. When his most concentrated positions began falling in late March 2021, the margin calls came, and Archegos could not meet them. The forced unwind was violent precisely because the positions were too large relative to the market to exit quietly. In a single week the whole \$160 billion structure came apart, causing **more than \$10 billion in losses at its lending banks** — Credit Suisse alone reported about **\$5.5 billion**, and Nomura around \$2.85 billion ([Bank Losses From Archegos Collapse Exceed \$10B](https://www.investorlawyers.com/blog/archegos-capital-collapse-bank-losses/)). Hwang's personal fortune, reportedly north of \$30 billion on paper, was largely gone within days.

The coda is the criminal one: in **July 2024 Hwang was convicted on ten counts** including securities fraud and market manipulation, and in **November 2024 he was sentenced to 18 years in prison** ([U.S. Department of Justice, SDNY](https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdny/pr/founder-and-head-archegos-capital-management-bill-hwang-sentenced-18-years-prison); [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/11/20/archegos-bill-hwang-sentenced-to-18-years-in-prison-for-massive-us-fraud.html)). The sentencing judge said the losses were "larger than any" he had dealt with. The fraud is its own story — a later post in this series takes Archegos as a full case — but the *shape* is the shape of every blowup: a real edge, levered and concentrated to the point where one adverse move could not be survived, ridden all the way up and then all the way to zero. The parabola went up on skill-plus-leverage and came down on the arithmetic.

### 2. Long-Term Capital Management: the smartest men in the room

If Archegos shows the pattern at a family office, Long-Term Capital Management shows it is not a failure of intelligence. LTCM's partners included two Nobel laureates and a former Federal Reserve vice-chairman; its strategies were sophisticated arbitrage with a genuine, mathematically-grounded edge. And it followed the spine exactly. With only about **\$4.8 billion in equity, the fund borrowed more than \$125 billion** (balance-sheet leverage around 25 to 1) and layered on **derivatives with a notional value exceeding \$1 trillion** ([Long-Term Capital Management, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-Term_Capital_Management); [Federal Reserve History, "Near Failure of LTCM"](https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/ltcm-near-failure)). The edge was real but thin, so it was leveraged to enormous size, and the positions were concentrated in convergence trades that all depended on the same thing: that markets stay calm and spreads narrow.

Then came the fat tail. In August 1998 Russia defaulted on its debt, spreads blew out instead of converging, and every one of LTCM's correlated positions moved against it at once. The fund **lost about \$4.6 billion in a few months**, and because its positions were so large, unwinding them threatened the broader financial system. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York organized a **\$3.6 billion bailout by fourteen banks** in September 1998 to wind the fund down in an orderly way. The lesson is the one that stings the most: being the smartest, most credentialed people in the room provides no protection against the anatomy of a blowup. The math does not care about your IQ; it cares about your leverage and your concentration, and LTCM had ruinous amounts of both.

### 3. XIV and "Volmageddon": the retail parabola that went to zero in a day

The blowup spine is not only for billionaires — retail products deliver it too, and faster. XIV was an exchange-traded note issued by Credit Suisse (its ticker was "VIX" spelled backwards) that let ordinary investors bet *against* stock-market volatility. For years it was one of the best-performing products anyone had ever seen: as long as markets stayed calm, it ground steadily higher, and it attracted a devoted retail following who treated the steady climb as a free money machine. It was, in spine terms, a small edge (the volatility risk premium is real) that was *structurally leveraged* to short volatility, concentrated in a single trade, and beloved for a long streak of wins.

The fat tail arrived on **February 5, 2018**, a day now called "Volmageddon." Stock-market volatility spiked violently — the VIX roughly doubled in a single session — and because XIV was short exactly that, it **lost about 96% of its value in hours** ([Six Figure Investing, "What Caused the February 5th 2018 Volatility Spike"](https://www.sixfigureinvesting.com/2019/02/what-caused-the-february-5th-2018-volatility-spike-xiv-termination/)). Its own prospectus contained an "acceleration event" clause: a daily loss of 80% or more let Credit Suisse shut it down, which it did, terminating the product days later. Years of steady gains — the whole parabola — went to zero in an afternoon. Everyone who owned it had seen the same climb and drawn the same conclusion: that the streak was proof the trade was safe. The streak was the setup.

### 4. Nick Leeson and Barings: one trader, one tail, a 233-year-old bank

The final case compresses the whole spine into a single person. Nick Leeson was a 28-year-old trader running Barings Bank's Singapore futures operation in the mid-1990s. He had a run of success that earned him autonomy and trust — the winner effect at an institutional level — and he used it to take large, concentrated, leveraged positions on the Japanese stock market, hiding the growing risk in a secret error account. When the trades went against him, he did what the spine predicts: he doubled down, sizing up into the loss to try to win it back.

The fat tail was literal. On January 17, 1995, the Kobe earthquake sent Japanese markets sharply lower, and Leeson's enormous Nikkei positions moved violently against him. The losses eventually reached about **£827 million (roughly \$1.4 billion) — about twice Barings' entire available trading capital** ([Bankruptcy of Barings Bank, Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/bankruptcy-of-Barings-Bank); [Nick Leeson, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nick_Leeson)). Barings, one of the oldest merchant banks in Britain, founded in 1762, collapsed in February 1995 and was sold for a single pound. One trader, one concentrated leveraged bet, one tail, and a bank that had survived 233 years was gone. The scale differs from a retail account by orders of magnitude; the anatomy is identical.

## What euphoria looks like at the screen

Theory is useless if you cannot catch it happening in real time, and euphoria — unlike a lot of trading psychology — announces itself loudly. It has a *feel*, and the feel arrives before the bad decision. Learning to read the tells is the highest-leverage skill in this whole domain, because the tell shows up seconds to minutes before the click that costs you.

![What euphoria looks like at the screen: each tell maps to a specific cause, and every correct move is a version of reduce size and slow down](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-7.webp)

The table above is the real deliverable of this section — walk it row by row, because these are the exact sensations to watch for:

- **Feeling invincible, "I finally get it."** That is the **winner effect** talking — elevated testosterone reading a streak as skill. This is the most dangerous tell precisely because it feels the best. The move: treat the feeling itself as a SELL signal, and cut back to your baseline 1R.
- **Refreshing the P&L constantly, unable to look away.** That is the **dopamine chase** — you are trading for the feeling, not the plan. The move: close the position ticket, physically look away from the screen, and ask whether the next trade is in your written plan or just in your bloodstream.
- **Sizing up with no plan-based reason.** That is **size creep** — the rules quietly abandoned. The move: revert to your written R and freeze the size where the plan says it should be.
- **Refusing to trim, "there's so much more upside."** That is **greed** — the peak of a move feels like the safest place to be, when it is the riskiest. The move: let your pre-set trim fire and bank 25% to 50%, regardless of how much more upside you're sure is coming.
- **Chasing, adding far from your setup.** That is **FOMO plus euphoria** — you are entering with no edge left, only the fear of missing more. The move: no unplanned adds; stand down.

Notice the pattern in the right-hand column: every single fix is a version of **reduce size and slow down**. That is not a coincidence. Once greed is loud, your conscious mind has already lost the argument about *feelings* — but it still controls *size and speed*, and those two levers are where the entire game is won. You cannot decide to feel less euphoric. You can decide, in advance, to cut your size when you notice you do.

## The drill: the euphoria checklist

Everything above converges on one practical protocol. You cannot reliably talk yourself out of euphoria in the moment — that is exactly when the winner effect owns your judgment. So you pre-commit, while calm, to a set of rules that treat the *feeling of invincibility itself* as the trigger. The core reframe is the whole drill: **euphoria is not a green light; it is a SELL/REDUCE signal.**

![The euphoria checklist: the euphoric trader presses size and round-trips the run, while the checklist trader reads the feeling as a cue to trim and keeps most of the gains](/imgs/blogs/greed-euphoria-and-the-anatomy-of-a-blowup-8.webp)

The two paths above are the same hot streak, handled two ways. The euphoric trader feels invincible, presses size from 1R to 3R, holds the full position into the reversal, and round-trips the run to zero. The checklist trader reads the identical feeling as a cue, lets a pre-set trim fire, cuts size after the run, and gives back a little to keep most of the gains. Same market, same streak, opposite endings — and the only difference is a set of rules decided *before* the euphoria arrived. Here is the concrete protocol.

**The euphoria checklist (run it whenever you notice the good feeling):**

1. **Name the feeling as a signal.** The moment you catch yourself feeling invincible, unusually confident, or certain a trade "can't lose," say it out loud: *this feeling is a reduce signal.* Naming it engages the deliberate part of your brain before the size creep does.
2. **Pre-set your profit-taking.** Before entering any trade, define where you'll bank partial profit — a fixed target where you trim 25% to 50% of the position automatically, no discretion allowed. A trim rule set in advance fires even when euphoria is telling you to hold for more.
3. **Apply the high-water-mark humility rule.** After any run that takes your account to a new high — say, up more than 10% to 15% over a short stretch — cut your per-trade risk *down*, not up. Drop from 1R to 0.5R for the next several trades. You are deliberately smallest when you feel biggest.
4. **Cap total size and leverage in advance.** Set a hard ceiling on gross exposure and per-trade risk that you cannot override in the moment. The ceiling is decided by calm-you and enforced against euphoric-you.
5. **Check for the spine.** Before adding risk, ask: am I leveraged? Concentrated? On a streak? If two or more are true, the answer to "should I size up?" is no, by rule, regardless of how right the trade feels.

#### Worked example: the high-water-mark trim

Suppose you run \$100,000 and, after a strong run, your account is at \$120,000 — up 20% in a few weeks, and you feel unstoppable. Your normal size is 1R = 1% = **\$1,200** at the current balance. The high-water-mark rule says: after a run this big, cut to 0.5R = **\$600** per trade for the next several trades.

- **If the run continues (the cost).** You leave some money on the table. A +2R trade makes +\$1,200 at half size instead of +\$2,400 at full size — a real, felt **−\$1,200 opportunity cost**. This is the premium you pay for the insurance, and most of the time it is all you pay. It is supposed to sting.
- **If the run reverses (the payoff).** The regime turns and you take the ordinary cluster of losses that ends every streak — say three losers. At the euphoric 3R size the creep wanted, that's roughly 3 × \$3,600 = **−\$10,800**, handing back more than half your run. At the pre-committed 0.5R, the same three losses cost 3 × \$600 = **−\$1,800**. You kept about \$9,000 of the gains that euphoria would have given back.
- **The trade you're making.** A small, frequent, annoying opportunity cost in exchange for protection against the one round-trip that erases the whole run. The math only has to work on the reversals to be worth it — and the reversals are where accounts actually die.

The one-sentence intuition: the high-water-mark rule deliberately makes your size smallest exactly when your confidence is highest, trading a little upside for immunity to the giveback that ends most streaks.

> You cannot decide to feel less invincible. But you can decide, while calm, that feeling invincible is the moment you shrink the bet.

## When this matters to you

This matters most at the top of your own emotional range, which is exactly where it is hardest to hear. When you are frightened at the bottom of a drawdown, the whole world is telling you to be careful, and being careful is at least aligned with survival. But when you are euphoric at the top of a run, everything — your P&L, your recent memory, your own biochemistry, the market itself — is telling you to lean in, and the one voice saying "reduce" is the quietest and the most correct. The euphoria checklist exists to give that voice a set of pre-committed rules so it doesn't have to win the argument in real time, because in real time it will lose.

None of this is a promise that avoiding blowups will make you rich. It will not. Edge comes from research, process, and discipline; the anatomy of a blowup is just the thing that can take a real edge and convert it to zero faster than the edge can rebuild it. And a caveat worth stating plainly: this is educational — a way to understand the mechanics of your own decisions and the arithmetic of ruin — not financial advice, and nothing here tells you what to buy, sell, or how to size. The one durable takeaway is structural: the sequence that ends accounts is always the same, it is powered by winning rather than losing, and the only reliable defense is to treat the feeling of certainty as the signal to get smaller. For the emotional map that sits underneath all of this — how fear, greed, hope, and regret trade off against each other in real time — the sibling piece on [fear, greed, hope, and regret: the four emotions](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/fear-greed-hope-and-regret-the-four-emotions) is the natural next step.
