> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — After a blowup, the loss is not the danger. The danger is what the loss does to the person deciding what to do next, and there are two ways that person self-destructs: the revenge spiral and the fear spiral.
>
> - **Two failure modes, one grave.** The *revenge spiral* over-sizes and over-trades to get it back fast, digging the hole deeper. The *fear spiral* freezes, cuts size to zero, and sits flat through the recovery. Both end the same way.
> - **Recovery is convex.** A −10\% loss needs a +11\% gain, but a −50\% loss needs +100\% and a −90\% loss needs +900\% — a 10× account. The deeper the hole, the exponentially steeper the climb out.
> - **A drawdown is grief.** You move through shock, anger, bargaining, and depression before acceptance, and each stage has its own specific, predictable trading mistake.
> - **Rebuild small, on process.** Stop trading first, run a blameless post-mortem, restart at 25\% size, and let a *green process score* — not a good P&L — earn back each increment of risk.
> - **The one fact to keep:** almost every great trader blew up at least once and came back. What separated the comeback from the wipe-out was never talent. It was whether they rebuilt on a repaired process or on the same broken one.
> - This is educational, not individual financial advice; every dollar figure in a worked example is illustrative arithmetic.

There is a specific kind of loss that does not feel like the others. Not the ordinary red day, not the losing week you shrug off. This is the one that took a chunk of your account you cannot pretend away — a third, a half, sometimes more — in a stretch short enough that you can still feel it in your chest. The account statement is a fact. But the thing that will actually decide the rest of your trading life is not the number on the statement. It is the person who has to sit down tomorrow and decide what to do about it.

Because here is the cruel part. The blowup does not stop when the position is closed. It keeps working on you, quietly, for weeks. And it pushes you toward one of two reactions that feel like opposites but are really the same catastrophe wearing two masks. The first is the urge to *get it all back right now* — to double up, trade bigger, force it, chase. The second is the urge to *never feel that again* — to freeze, to cut your size to nothing, to sit in cash while the market you understand runs away without you. Both feel completely rational from the inside. Both, if you follow them, finish the job the blowup started.

Picture this fork as the map for the whole article. The same deep loss sits at the left. Three roads leave it. Two of them — the revenge spiral and the fear spiral — end your career. Only the middle road, a disciplined rebuild, puts you back in the game.

![The recovery fork: a blowup can be fed into a revenge spiral, a fear spiral, or a disciplined rebuild, and only the rebuild leads back into the game while the other two roads end the career](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-1.webp)

Notice what the diagram is really claiming. The blowup itself does not determine the outcome. The *reaction* does. The market handed the same −40\% to three versions of you, and one of them traded again next year while two of them did not. That is the load-bearing idea of this entire post: recovery is a skill, separate from the skill that made money in the first place, and almost nobody is taught it. This article is that lesson — built from the ground up, assuming no background in trading or psychology, and taken all the way to a protocol you can run the next time the market opens a hole under you.

Let me start with the two things that make a deep drawdown so uniquely dangerous: the math of climbing out, and the grief of living through it.

## Foundations: the recovery math and the two spirals

Before any protocol, you need three pieces of scaffolding, and I will build each from zero. The first is a piece of arithmetic that most people get wrong in their gut. The second is a map of the two ways traders self-destruct after a loss. The third is the emotional arc you will actually travel, borrowed from a very different field. None of these requires prior finance knowledge. If you already know the recovery math, skim; if you have never seen it, this is the single most important table in the post.

### The recovery math: why a hole is deeper than it looks

Start with a number almost everyone gets wrong. Suppose you lose 50\% of your account. What gain do you need to get back to where you started? The instinctive answer is 50\%. The correct answer is 100\%. You have to double what is left just to return to even.

The reason is simple once you see it. Percentages are taken from a *base*, and a loss shrinks the base that the recovery gain is measured against. Say you start with \$100,000 and lose 50\%. You now have \$50,000. To get back to \$100,000, you need to make \$50,000 — but \$50,000 on a base of \$50,000 is a 100\% gain, not 50\%. The loss was measured against the big number; the recovery has to be measured against the small one.

The general rule is worth committing to memory:

> The gain you need to break even equals your loss divided by one minus your loss. Small holes fill in almost symmetrically. Deep holes do not fill in at all.

Think of it as a curve that starts gentle and then goes nearly vertical. The intuition here is that the *asymmetry itself* accelerates: every extra unit of loss costs you disproportionately more gain to undo. Below is the whole table, and it is the reason a deep drawdown feels like dread rather than arithmetic.

![The recovery-math table: a 10 percent loss needs an 11 percent gain, a 50 percent loss needs 100 percent, a 75 percent loss needs 300 percent, and a 90 percent loss needs a 900 percent gain, colour-graded from survivable green to near-impossible red](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-2.webp)

Read the table top to bottom and watch the "loss-to-gain multiple" column, because that is the whole story. Down 10\%, and the gain you need is only 1.1× the loss — nearly symmetric, a bad week. Down 50\%, and the multiple is 2.0× — you must make back twice the percentage you lost. Down 90\%, and the multiple is 10.0×: a +900\% gain, meaning the surviving sliver of your account has to grow *tenfold* to get you home. That is not a bigger version of the same problem. It is a different problem, and no amount of ordinary skill grinds a tenfold gain out of a ruined account before the trader quits.

#### Worked example: the true cost of a 50\% drawdown

Suppose you run a \$100,000 account and a genuinely good strategy that averages 20\% a year in good years. One bad month takes you down 50\%, to \$50,000. How long, at your normal pace, to get back to \$100,000?

You need a 100\% gain to recover — you must double the \$50,000. At 20\% a year, doubling takes roughly 3.8 years of compounding (1.20 raised to the fourth power is about 2.07). So a single month of −50\% costs you *nearly four years* of your normal returns just to stand still. And that is the optimistic version, where your process is intact and you keep compounding at your best rate. It ignores the far more likely reality: that the −50\% also damaged the trader, who now compounds worse, not better.

The intuition to carry out of this: a deep loss does not cost you the loss — it costs you the years of good trading you now have to spend undoing it.

This convexity is exactly why professional risk managers obsess over one number above all others: *maximum drawdown*, the deepest peak-to-trough fall an account has suffered. They cap it — often at a hard 20\% or 25\% for a fund, with a mandatory de-risking at half that — not because a 30\% loss is unbearable in itself, but because of where the curve is heading. Every extra percentage point of drawdown you allow buys a disproportionately larger recovery burden and edges you toward the region of *risk of ruin*, the zone where the required gain is so large that no realistic edge produces it before the trader runs out of capital, time, or will. The whole discipline of position sizing exists to keep you on the gentle left side of the recovery curve, where a bad run is a bad week, and off the right side, where a bad run is the end. A blowup, by definition, has shoved you toward the right side. The recovery is the work of getting back to the left before the convexity does its worst.

### The two failure modes: revenge and fear

The recovery math explains why a deep drawdown *feels* so heavy. The next piece explains how that weight makes you destroy yourself. After a blowup, the pressure of that convex hole pushes almost every trader toward one of two reactions. They look like opposites. They are twins.

The **revenge spiral** is the over-aggression response. The hole is huge, the gain needed is enormous, and the ordinary pace of your strategy suddenly feels unbearably slow. So you speed up: bigger size, more trades, wider stops, chasing setups you would normally pass. The logic is "the only way out of a big hole is a big win." It feels like fighting back. It is actually the fastest way to turn a survivable loss into a fatal one.

The **fear spiral** is the paralysis response. The loss hurt so much that your entire nervous system reorganizes around not feeling it again. So you shrink: size cut to nothing, valid signals skipped, hours spent staring at charts you never act on. The logic is "I cannot afford another loss, so I will not risk anything." It feels like prudence. It is actually a slow-motion version of the same wipe-out, because a trader who is flat through the recovery never recovers — the edge decays, the capital idles, and eventually the account is closed out of sheer attrition and despair.

Here are the two side by side: what each feels like, what it makes you do, and why each one specifically kills the recovery.

![A comparison matrix of the two failure modes: the revenge spiral feels like adrenaline and urgency and makes you oversize and chase, while the fear spiral feels like dread and shame and makes you freeze and sit flat, and both destroy the recovery](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-4.webp)

The reason to hold both in view at once is that most trading advice only warns about revenge. "Don't revenge-trade" is on every checklist. But the fear spiral is just as common and, in some ways, more insidious, because it disguises itself as discipline. The gun-shy trader who has "stopped taking risk until things feel better" is not being careful. They are avoiding, and avoidance in a drawdown is its own slow blowup. The recovery protocol later in this post is built to defend against *both* poles at once, which is why it is more than "just calm down and cut your size."

### The stages of a drawdown: a drawdown is grief

The third piece of scaffolding comes from outside finance entirely. In 1969, the psychiatrist Elisabeth Kübler-Ross published *On Death and Dying*, and in it described five stages people move through when facing a terminal diagnosis: denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance. The [five stages of grief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_stages_of_grief) were never meant as a rigid ladder — real grief loops and skips — but as a rough map of the emotional territory, it has proven remarkably durable, and it maps almost perfectly onto what a trader feels while an account bleeds.

The mapping matters because each stage produces a *specific* trading mistake. If you can name the stage you are in, you can predict the mistake you are about to make. Consider the case of a trader watching a good account roll over into a deep drawdown; here is the arc they travel and the error waiting at each step.

![A timeline of the five stages of a drawdown mapped onto grief: shock and denial refuse to cut, anger produces revenge trades, bargaining promises to stop after getting back to even, depression brings paralysis, and acceptance finally allows a blameless rebuild](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-3.webp)

Walk the stages. In **shock and denial**, the loss does not feel real — "it is just noise, it will come back" — and the mistake is refusing to cut, holding the loser because closing it makes the loss official. In **anger**, the story becomes "the market is rigged against me," and the mistake is the revenge trade, an attempt to punish the market that only punishes you. In **bargaining**, you strike private deals — "if I just get back to even, I promise I will stop" — which keeps you in exactly the oversized, desperate posture that caused the damage. In **depression**, the adrenaline drains and leaves paralysis: gun-shy, unable to pull the trigger, deep in the fear spiral. Only in **acceptance** does the useful work begin — a blameless review, a rebuild from a small base, a return to trading the plan.

The reason to know this arc in advance is that naming a feeling defuses it. A trader who can say "this is the anger stage, and anger wants me to revenge-trade" has already put a wedge between the feeling and the finger on the button. That wedge is where recovery lives. It is worth reading more on the physiology underneath this in the companion piece on [stress, drawdown, and the psychology of a losing streak](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/stress-drawdown-and-the-psychology-of-a-losing-streak), which covers how chronic stress chemically degrades the exact part of the brain you need to climb out.

With those three pieces — the convex math, the two spirals, the grief arc — we can go deep on each mechanism and then build the protocol.

## 1. The revenge spiral: over-aggression as self-destruction

The revenge spiral deserves its own section because it is the failure mode that turns a bad month into a career-ending crater, and it does so through a mechanism that feels, from the inside, like courage.

Start with the emotional physics. A deep loss creates a felt sense of *urgency* that is wildly out of proportion to the actual situation. Your rational mind knows a good strategy recovers over months. But the older, faster part of your brain — the part that handles threat — treats the drawdown like a predator and screams for immediate action. Sitting still feels intolerable. And the market offers an endless supply of ways to *do something*: size up, trade more often, take the marginal setup. Every one of them feels like progress and every one of them raises your risk exactly when your judgment is worst. The deeper mechanics of that hijack — how cortisol shifts control from the deliberate brain to the reactive one — are covered in [tilt and revenge trading](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/tilt-and-revenge-trading); here I want to focus on the arithmetic of why it is fatal.

The revenge trader's core belief is "a big hole needs a big win." This is where the convex recovery math turns lethal. Because the gain needed is so large, ordinary-sized trades feel pointless, so the revenge trader reaches for size. But size cuts both ways, and a losing trade on doubled or tripled size does not just fail to fill the hole — it deepens it, and the deeper hole demands an even bigger win, which justifies even bigger size. That is the spiral: each loss makes the next bet bigger, and bigger bets make the next loss worse.

#### Worked example: how one revenge trade turns −50\% into −75\%

Suppose you are down 50\%. Your \$100,000 account is now \$50,000. Your normal risk per trade is 2\% of the account — about \$1,000 at full size. But 2\% feels hopeless against a hole this deep, so you decide to "make it back in one trade" and risk half of what is left, \$25,000, on a single high-conviction bet. That is 25× your normal risk.

It loses. You are now at \$25,000 — down 75\% of the original peak. Look up 75\% on the recovery table: you now need a +300\% gain to get back to even, a fourfold on the surviving quarter of your account. In trying to climb out of a hole that needed +100\%, you dug one that needs +300\%. One revenge trade turned a bad year into a near-impossible one.

The intuition to keep: oversizing to escape a drawdown does not shorten the climb — it lengthens it, because the loss you are risking is subtracted from an already-shrunken base.

### What it looks like at the screen

Theory is clean; the spiral is not. It is worth knowing the physical tells, because you will feel them before you can name them. In the revenge spiral, your heart rate is up and you notice it. You are refreshing the P&L every few seconds, watching the red number as if staring will change it. Your cursor hovers over the size field and you find yourself typing a bigger number than usual, then telling yourself a story about why *this* setup justifies it. You have stopped waiting for your actual signal; you are hunting for any reason to be in a trade, because being flat feels like losing. You are talking to yourself — "just one good trade," "I only need one" — and the phrase "get it back" is running on a loop. You feel hot, narrow, and fast. If you could see yourself, you would notice you are leaning toward the screen.

Those are not character flaws. They are the observable signature of a nervous system in threat mode, and the single most useful skill in recovery is learning to recognize them *as* tells — as data that says "your judgment is currently compromised, do not size up" — rather than as urges to obey. When you catch yourself leaning in and typing a bigger number, that is the moment the protocol exists for.

The revenge spiral has one more property worth naming: it destroys good traders specifically, not just bad ones. A bad trader with no edge blows up slowly through a thousand small mistakes. A good trader with real edge blows up fast, in a single spiral, because their confidence is what lets them reach for catastrophic size. Talent is no protection here. The historical record — which we will get to — is a graveyard of brilliant traders who revenge-traded a recoverable drawdown into a terminal one.

## 2. The fear spiral: paralysis and the missed recovery

If the revenge spiral is a fire, the fear spiral is a slow freeze, and it is under-warned-about precisely because it looks responsible.

After a loss that genuinely hurt, the nervous system does something protective: it tags the activity that caused the pain — trading — as dangerous, and it recruits every tool it has to keep you away from it. This is the same machinery that keeps you from touching a hot stove twice, and it is excellent at its job. The problem is that for a trader, *not trading* is not safety. It is a different way to lose.

The fear spiral shows up as a cluster of behaviors that each feel prudent in isolation. You cut your size — sensible after a loss, except you cut it to a level so small it cannot matter. You "wait for a really clean setup" — reasonable, except no setup is ever clean enough, and you pass on the exact signals your strategy is built to take. You spend hours watching the market without acting, telling yourself you are being patient when you are actually being paralyzed. You feel a small hit of relief every time you *don't* take a trade, and that relief is the trap: it reinforces the avoidance, so tomorrow it is a little harder to act than today.

The reason this kills the recovery is subtle. A trading edge is not a fixed asset that waits for you. It is a statistical property that only pays out if you take the trades. A trader who sits flat through the recovery is like an insurance company that stops writing policies after a bad year — the losses are booked, but so is the future income, and the business quietly dies. Meanwhile the deep drawdown still needs its convex gain, and now you are not even trying to earn it. The capital idles, the skill atrophies, and eventually the account is closed not with a bang but with a resignation.

#### Worked example: the cost of sitting out the rebound

Suppose two traders both end a brutal quarter down 40\%, each holding \$60,000 of a former \$100,000 account. Both have the same genuinely profitable strategy that, over the next year, would return 30\% if fully and normally traded.

Trader A rebuilds deliberately and is trading at normal size within a couple of months. Over the year they earn roughly their strategy's 30\% on the \$60,000 base, about \$18,000, ending near \$78,000 — most of the hole climbed back. Trader B is gun-shy: they trade at a tenth of normal size all year, "waiting to feel confident again." They capture roughly a tenth of the available return — about \$1,800 — and end near \$61,800, essentially flat, the drawdown fully intact. Same strategy, same starting hole, same market. The only difference was that one of them showed up and the other hid.

The intuition to keep: in a drawdown, being flat is not neutral — the rebound is where the recovery lives, and sitting it out is an active choice to stay broke.

### What the fear spiral looks like at the screen

The tells here are the mirror image of the revenge spiral. Your body is not hot and fast; it is heavy and slow. You open the platform and feel a small dread. The signal fires — the one you have taken a hundred times — and instead of clicking, you hesitate, then find a reason not to: "the market feels weird today," "I'll wait for confirmation." You replay the blowup trade in your head, uninvited, several times a day. You size a position, then cut it in half before sending, then cancel. You feel relief when you close the platform without having traded. If you could see yourself, you would notice you are sitting back, arms crossed, watching rather than doing.

Both spirals, notice, are your nervous system trying to protect you from the pain of loss — one by fighting, one by fleeing. Neither is a moral failing. Both are predictable, both are physical, and both can be defended against with structure rather than willpower, which is what the protocol provides. Position size, it turns out, is the single most powerful lever for regulating both, which is why the companion piece frames [position sizing as emotional regulation](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/position-sizing-as-emotional-regulation) rather than as mere risk math.

## 3. The graduated size-rebuild: restart small, scale on process

Now we turn from diagnosis to construction. The central mechanical move of any recovery is the *graduated size-rebuild*, and it directly disarms both spirals at once. Understanding why it works requires holding one principle: after a blowup, your size should be a function of your *proven process*, not of your P&L or your feelings.

Here is the core idea. You do not restart trading at full size — that hands the revenge spiral a loaded weapon. Nor do you restart at zero and wait to "feel ready" — that hands the fear spiral its excuse. Instead you restart at a small, deliberate base — a common choice is 25\% of your normal risk — and you climb back to full size in fixed increments, where each promotion is *earned* by objective evidence that your process is intact, not by a hot streak or a good mood.

You can think of it as a ladder with four rungs, each with an explicit gate you must clear before you climb.

![A four-stage size-rebuild pipeline: stage one is 25 percent size with 20 trades logged and every rule kept, stage two is 50 percent after two green process weeks, stage three is 75 percent with equity flat or up, and stage four is 100 percent with confidence rebuilt on repetitions](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-5.webp)

The gates are the whole point. Notice that not one of them is "you made money." The stage-1-to-2 gate is "you logged 20 trades and kept every rule." The stage-2-to-3 gate is "two clean process weeks with no rule breaks." Only stage 3 references equity at all, and even then it asks for "flat or up," not "a big win." This is deliberate. If you gate size increases on P&L, you promote yourself for luck and demote yourself for variance — you will size up right before a normal losing streak and size down right before a normal winning one. Gating on process breaks that trap: you earn size for *behaving correctly*, which is the only thing you actually control.

#### Worked example: a graduated rebuild on a \$60,000 account

Suppose your \$100,000 account is now \$60,000 after a −40\% blowup, and your normal risk is 2\% per trade. At full size that is \$1,200 of risk per trade. Instead, you rebuild:

- **Stage 1 — 25\% size.** Risk 0.5\% per trade, about \$300. You take 20 trades exactly by the book. The dollar stakes are small enough that a losing streak here cannot hurt you and cannot trigger the revenge urge — a string of five losses costs about \$1,500, under 3\% of the account. You clear the gate: 20 trades, rules kept.
- **Stage 2 — 50\% size.** Risk 1\% per trade, about \$600. Two weeks, no rule breaks. Because the stakes are still modest, your judgment stays clear, and clear judgment is what lets you keep the rules, which is what earns the next rung.
- **Stage 3 — 75\% size.** Risk 1.5\% per trade, about \$900. Equity is flat-to-up and the drawdown feels behind you. You are trading normally now, just slightly under weight.
- **Stage 4 — 100\% size.** Risk 2\% per trade, back to \$1,200. Full size, restored not because you had a good week but because you have stacked up weeks of correct behavior.

The whole climb might take six to ten weeks. That feels slow when a convex hole is staring at you. But compare it to the revenge alternative from Section 1, where oversizing turned −50\% into −75\% in a single afternoon.

The intuition to keep: a small base is not timidity — it is the only size at which your judgment survives long enough to rebuild the process that actually fills the hole.

## 4. Revenge-trading the recovery versus a disciplined rebuild

It helps to see the two paths race each other from the same starting line, because the difference is not subtle and it is not a matter of degree — it is the difference between climbing and cratering.

Consider two traders, both down 40\% to \$60,000, both with the same edge, starting the rebuild on the same day. One runs the graduated ladder from Section 3. The other decides the ladder is for cowards and goes for the fast recovery: full size immediately, then bigger when it does not work fast enough. Here is what their equity does, week by week.

![A grouped bar chart comparing two rebuild paths from the same 60 percent starting point: the disciplined bars climb steadily to 94 percent of the old peak over eight weeks, while the revenge bars spike to 72 percent then collapse through 40, 20, and 4 percent toward ruin](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-6.webp)

Watch the revenge path, in red. It actually *starts well* — week 2 it is at 72\%, ahead of the disciplined trader, because oversized bets that happen to win recover fast. This is the trap that makes the revenge spiral so seductive: it often works at first, which convinces the trader they were right to abandon discipline. Then variance does what variance does. A normal cluster of losses, amplified by oversized bets, craters the account: 40\%, then 20\%, then 4\% of the old peak. The early lead was not skill; it was a coin landing heads a few times before it landed tails on a bet far too large to survive.

The disciplined path, in green, is boring. It climbs 60, 70, 80, 90, 94 — never spectacular, never ahead of the revenge path in the early weeks, and it never craters. By week 8 it is nearly home while the revenge trader is functionally wiped out.

#### Worked example: the expected value of the two paths

Suppose your edge wins 50\% of trades at +1.5R and loses 50\% at −1R, where R is the risk per trade — a solid, realistic edge with positive expectancy of about +0.25R per trade. On the disciplined ladder, R is small (0.5\% of the account early on), so a run of bad luck costs a few percent and your expectancy grinds the account upward over dozens of trades. On the revenge path, R is enormous — say 25\% of the account — so the *same positive-expectancy edge* now has a fat left tail: two or three losses in a row, entirely normal for a 50\% win rate, subtract 50\% to 75\% of your account. The edge is identical. The sizing converts it from a slow climb into a coin flip for survival.

The intuition to keep: positive expectancy only compounds you upward if your size is small enough to survive the normal losing streaks — bet big enough and even a winning system becomes a game of Russian roulette.

This is why "revenge-trading the recovery" is not a smaller version of disciplined rebuilding. It is a categorically different bet: you are trading your genuine long-run edge for a short-run gamble that you will get lucky before you get unlucky. The house edge in that game belongs to variance, and variance is patient.

## 5. Rebuilding confidence on process, not P&L

There is a psychological engine underneath the graduated rebuild, and if you skip it the whole structure collapses. That engine is *how you keep score*. After a blowup, your confidence is shattered, and confidence is what you need to trade well — but you must rebuild it on the right foundation, because rebuilding it on P&L is how you re-trigger both spirals.

The problem with P&L as a scorecard is that in the short run it is almost pure noise. A run of ten trades tells you next to nothing about whether your edge is real — a good strategy loses ten in a row sometimes, and a broken one wins ten in a row sometimes. So if your confidence tracks your P&L, it is tracking randomness. A green day makes you feel "I'm back," which pushes you to size up too soon — the revenge door. A red day makes you feel "I'm broken," which pushes you toward despair or a revenge trade to fix the feeling — also the revenge door, or the fear door. Either way, you have bolted your emotional state to a random-number generator.

The fix is to keep score on *process metrics* — the things you actually control — instead of outcomes. Here are the two scorecards side by side.

![A before-and-after comparison of two scorecards: scoring by P and L swings the trader between overconfidence and despair on random outcomes, while scoring by process rewards following the plan, keeping rules, and correct sizing regardless of whether the trade won](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-8.webp)

The right-hand column is the whole discipline. Did you follow the plan? Then the rep was correct, win or lose — score it +1. Did you take the valid signal, size it right, and honor your stop? Then it was a good day, *even if it was red*. Did you keep every rule for five straight days? Then you have earned a size bump, regardless of the P&L. Confidence built this way compounds on repeatable behavior, which you can reproduce tomorrow, rather than on a lucky print, which you cannot.

#### Worked example: scoring a week by process instead of P&L

Suppose you take 12 trades in your first week back at 25\% size. Seven lose, five win, and the week finishes down \$400 — a red week on the P&L scorecard, the kind that historically would have wrecked your confidence and tempted a revenge trade.

Now score it by process. Of the 12 trades, you took only valid signals on 11 (one was a marginal setup you should have skipped — mark it a process error). You sized every trade correctly. You honored every stop. You logged all 12. That is 11 of 12 reps executed correctly: a 92\% process score, a strongly green week. On the process scorecard, this was a *good* week that happened to lose money — exactly the normal variance a positive-expectancy edge throws off. You keep your size, you fix the one marginal-entry error, and you show up Monday steady instead of shattered.

The intuition to keep: in a small sample, P&L measures luck and process measures skill — so rebuild your confidence on the one you control, and let the money follow the reps.

There is a deeper reason process-scoring works that is worth making explicit: it decouples your self-worth from your equity curve. After a blowup, the most corrosive belief is "I am a bad trader," because it is unfalsifiable by a losing day and it makes every red print feel like confirmation of your worthlessness. Process scoring gives you a second, more honest verdict to hold against it. You can have a losing week and still say, truthfully, "I traded well this week" — because trading well is defined as executing correct reps, which you did. That sentence is the antidote to the shame that feeds both spirals. The revenge trader is trying to prove they are not worthless by forcing a win; the fear trader has already concluded they are, and hides. A process scorecard makes the whole question moot: your job was never to be right about the next trade, only to execute the next rep correctly, and that job you can do on any day, in any drawdown, regardless of what the market hands you.

Keeping a scorecard like this requires a real logging habit, which is the subject of [the trading journal that actually changes behavior](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/the-trading-journal-that-actually-changes-behavior); a recovery is exactly when that habit earns its keep, because it is the only source of the process data your confidence is now supposed to run on.

## The recovery protocol

Everything so far has been diagnosis and principle. Here is the drill — a fixed sequence to run after any blowup, designed so that you do not have to make good decisions while your judgment is at its worst. The whole point of a protocol is that you decide *now*, calm, what you will do *then*, compromised. It has six steps and one honest off-ramp.

![The recovery protocol pipeline: stop trading, run a blameless post-mortem, rebuild small at 25 percent size, score the process not the P and L, cage the revenge urge with hard rules, and finally make an honest go or no-go decision about whether the edge is real](/imgs/blogs/coming-back-from-a-blowup-and-trading-through-a-drawdown-7.webp)

**Step 1 — Stop trading first.** Before anything else, flat the book and step away, for days to weeks depending on the depth of the loss. This is non-negotiable and it comes first because a compromised decision-maker cannot be trusted to make the very decisions the recovery requires. Stopping is not weakness or "giving up" — it is the equivalent of a surgeon refusing to operate with shaking hands. Many professional desks encode this as a hard rule: hit a daily or weekly loss limit and you are done, flattened and logged out, no discretion. The purpose of stopping is to let the stress chemistry clear so that the person who plans the recovery is not the person the blowup just broke.

**Step 2 — Run a blameless post-mortem.** Once you are calm, examine what happened with the tone of an investigator, not a prosecutor. The question is "what broke," never "who to blame" — and the answer is you, so the trick is to separate the decision from the identity. A good post-mortem asks: was this bad luck (a correctly sized, well-executed trade that lost) or bad process (oversizing, no stop, a trade outside the plan)? Those demand opposite responses. Bad luck means the process was fine and you simply met the left tail; change nothing. Bad process means you found the leak that must be fixed before a single dollar goes back to work. The discipline of doing this without spiraling into shame is covered in depth in [the pre-mortem and the blameless post-mortem](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/the-pre-mortem-and-the-blameless-post-mortem); the blameless framing is what makes the review usable instead of just painful.

**Step 3 — Rebuild size from a small base.** Restart at 25\% of normal risk, per Section 3. Small enough that a losing streak cannot hurt you and cannot trigger the revenge urge; large enough that you are genuinely trading, not hiding. This is the structural defense against both spirals at once.

**Step 4 — Score the process, not the P&L.** Run the process scorecard from Section 5. Green means rules kept, regardless of outcome. This is what your size increases are gated on and what your rebuilding confidence is allowed to feed on.

**Step 5 — Cage the revenge urge with hard rules.** Willpower fails under stress, so replace it with pre-committed constraints that do not require willpower to obey: a hard daily loss limit that flattens you automatically, a rule that size can only increase by one ladder rung at a time and only after a gate is cleared, a ban on adding to losers, a maximum number of trades per day. These are the guardrails that hold when your judgment does not. The general craft of pre-committing to rules you will want to break later is the subject of [rules, checklists, and pre-commitment](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/rules-checklists-and-pre-commitment).

**Step 6 — Make an honest go / no-go decision.** After enough clean reps to have real process data, ask the hardest question: is the edge actually real and repeatable, or was the blowup a symptom that this strategy — or this trader, right now — does not have one? This is the off-ramp, and it must be a genuine option, because the alternative to walking away is not always a comeback. Sometimes it is a second blowup. Honest criteria for walking away include: you cannot identify a real, testable edge; you have run the protocol cleanly and still cannot stay disciplined; the losses are threatening money you cannot afford to lose; or the toll on your health and life has stopped being worth it. Walking away with capital and sanity intact is not failure. It is the single most underrated skill in trading, and the traders who lack it are the ones who show up in the cautionary tales below.

> The protocol's real job is to make your recovery independent of your willpower, because willpower is exactly the resource a blowup depletes. Structure is what is left when discipline is gone.

## Common misconceptions

**"I need to make it back the same way I lost it."** This is the revenge spiral dressed as poetic justice. There is no rule that the account must be recovered through the same instrument, the same size, or the same aggression that lost it. In fact the opposite is true: the mindset that produced the loss is the last one you want running the recovery. You make it back slowly, small, and boringly — or you do not make it back at all.

**"Cutting my size means I'm scared / not a real trader."** Ego says full size is brave. Arithmetic says full size after a blowup is how good traders die. Cutting size in a drawdown is not fear; it is the single most protective move available, and every professional risk framework mandates it. The genuinely fearful move is the fear *spiral* — cutting size to *zero* and hiding — which is a different failure entirely from the deliberate, temporary size reduction the protocol calls for.

**"A big drawdown means my strategy is broken."** Usually it does not. A positive-expectancy strategy with a 50\% win rate throws off runs of seven to ten losses as a matter of pure arithmetic, not evidence of breakage. The blameless post-mortem exists precisely to tell the difference between a strategy that met its normal left tail (bad luck, change nothing) and one with a real leak (bad process, fix it). Assuming "broken" after every drawdown is how you abandon good systems at the worst possible time.

**"I should trade my way out fast, before I lose confidence."** The urgency is the illness, not the cure. Trading fast means trading big, and trading big after a blowup is the revenge spiral. Confidence is not rebuilt by a fast recovery; it is rebuilt by stacking correct process reps, which is inherently slow. The trader who rushes to "get confidence back" through P&L is chasing the exact thing that will re-break them.

**"The great traders never blew up — that's why they're great."** Almost the reverse. A striking number of the most celebrated traders in history blew up at least once, often early and sometimes more than once. What distinguished them was not an absence of blowups but the quality of their comeback — whether they rebuilt on a repaired process or repeated the mistake. Survivorship bias hides the blowups and shows you only the recoveries, which makes greatness look like it was never tested. It was.

**"Walking away for good is admitting failure."** Walking away with capital and health intact, after honestly concluding the edge is not there or the discipline will not hold, is a *win*, not a loss. The genuine failures are the traders who could not walk away and stayed until a second or third blowup took everything. The off-ramp in step 6 is there because a comeback is not guaranteed, and pretending it is has ended more careers than any single bad trade.

**"Stopping trading will make me lose my feel for the market."** This fear keeps people trading through the exact period when they should be flat, and it is almost entirely backwards. The "feel" you are protecting during a blowup is a stress-degraded, tilt-poisoned version of your skill — keeping it warm just keeps you making bad decisions fluently. Skill built on a sound process does not evaporate over a two-week pause; it comes back within a few sessions of screen time, and it comes back cleaner, because the stress chemistry has cleared. Athletes rest injuries precisely so they can return at full capacity; a trader who "keeps sharp" through a drawdown is training with a torn muscle and calling it dedication.

## How it shows up in real markets

The pattern is not theoretical. Here are four episodes where the recovery-versus-revenge fork played out in public, with real names and, where they can be sourced, real numbers.

### 1. Victor Niederhoffer: the recovery that repeated the mistake

Victor Niederhoffer is the cleanest case study of this entire post, because he blew up, came back spectacularly, and then blew up *again the same way* — proving both that recovery is possible and that recovery without a repaired process is just a delay.

Niederhoffer was a Harvard-trained statistician and champion squash player whose fund reportedly [returned about 35\% a year for roughly 15 years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Niederhoffer). Then, in 1997, two bets converged. He had bought heavily into Thai bank stocks, betting on a government rescue that never came; as the baht collapsed, that position reportedly lost around \$50 million, close to 40\% of his capital. Simultaneously he was short a large book of out-of-the-money S&P 500 put options — a strategy that collects small premiums until it does not. On October 27, 1997, in a session driven by the Asian crisis, [the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 554 points, about 7.2\%](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_27,_1997,_mini-crash), its worst point drop to that date. The puts he had sold exploded in value, the margin calls came, and his positions were liquidated. Niederhoffer Investments was wiped out — reportedly around \$100 million of client capital gone. The bitter irony: within weeks the market recovered and those puts expired worthless, so a trader who could have held would have been fine. Over-leverage removed the option to hold.

That is the blowup. Now the comeback: Niederhoffer mortgaged his house, sold his antique silver collection, and started trading his own account again in 1998. By 2002 he was managing outside money in the Matador Fund, and over the following years it reportedly compounded at roughly 50\% a year — a genuine, remarkable recovery. And then, in 2007, the credit crunch hit, and the Matador Fund, running the same kind of leveraged, short-volatility exposure, [fell more than 75\% and was closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Niederhoffer). He had recovered the money but never repaired the process, so the second blowup was the first one on a delay. The lesson is exactly step 2 and step 6 of the protocol: a comeback built on the same unfixed leak is not a comeback, it is an intermission.

### 2. Paul Tudor Jones: the blowup that made the trader

The counter-example is a trader whose early blowup became the foundation of everything that followed, because he responded with a repaired process instead of a repeated one.

Early in his career, around 1979, Paul Tudor Jones took a cotton trade that went badly wrong and, by his own account in Jack Schwager's *Market Wizards* interviews, [lost a large share of his equity — commonly cited as on the order of 60–70\% — on that single position](https://www.turtletrader.com/trader-jones/). He was so shaken he reportedly considered quitting the business entirely. Instead, he made the loss the pivot of his whole philosophy: he became, in his words, obsessed with defense over offense, with capital preservation as the first priority and cutting losses fast as the core habit. "The most important rule of trading is to play great offense" was not it; his rule was to play great *defense*. That single blowup, correctly metabolized, arguably created one of the most durable macro-trading careers of the era. The difference from Niederhoffer was not talent or luck — it was that Jones fixed the process the blowup exposed, and never let a single position threaten his survival again. The shared psychology of traders who convert their worst losses into their defining discipline is the theme of [the market wizards and the shared psychology of great traders](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/market-wizards-the-shared-psychology-of-great-traders).

### 3. Jesse Livermore: the comeback artist who could not walk away

Jesse Livermore is the archetype of the recovery skill taken to its extreme — and of what happens when the off-ramp is never taken. Livermore went bankrupt and rebuilt more than once across his career; he made and lost several fortunes, most famously reportedly making a fortune short-selling the 1929 crash. He was, in a sense, the greatest comeback trader who ever lived, proof that a ruined account can be rebuilt again and again by a person with genuine skill. And yet his story ends in tragedy: he blew up a final time, never recovered that last one, and died by suicide in 1940. The lesson is the dark mirror of step 6. Livermore had the recovery skill in abundance — what he lacked was the ability to *stop*, to walk away once with the money intact rather than always returning to the game that kept breaking him. His life is covered in full in [Jesse Livermore: genius, discipline, and self-destruction](/blog/trading/trading-psychology/jesse-livermore-genius-discipline-and-self-destruction); for our purposes he is the reminder that the ability to come back is necessary but not sufficient, and that the honest no-go is sometimes the only winning move.

### 4. The traders who blew up small before they ever succeeded

The final pattern is the quietest and the most encouraging: many traders who became consistently profitable blew up one or more *small* accounts on the way, treating each as tuition. In Schwager's *Market Wizards*, several of the interviewed traders describe early accounts they wiped out entirely before finding an approach that worked — Michael Marcus famously lost several small stakes before eventually turning a modest sum into a large fortune over decades at Commodities Corp, and Bruce Kovner describes an early over-leveraged soybean position that taught him risk management by nearly destroying him. The reason this matters is that it reframes an early blowup from a verdict into a lesson — *provided* the loss is small enough to be survivable tuition rather than a career-ender. This is the deepest argument for the graduated size-rebuild and for small size in general: it keeps your inevitable mistakes in the "expensive lesson" range instead of the "game over" range. A blowup you survive is education. A blowup you do not is the end of the story.

## When this matters to you

If you trade, you will have a drawdown that hurts. Not might — will. It is the one experience every trader who stays in the game long enough shares, and it is the one almost nobody prepares for, because preparing for it means admitting it will happen. So the time to internalize this protocol is now, while you are calm and solvent, not in the middle of the anger stage with your cursor hovering over a triple-sized order.

The single most important thing to carry away is the reframe at the very top: after a blowup, the loss is not your real problem. The person who has to decide what to do next is. Protect that person — by stopping first, by rebuilding small, by scoring process instead of P&L, by caging the revenge urge with rules that do not depend on willpower — and the capital has a chance to follow. Fail to protect that person, in either direction, and the two spirals will finish what the blowup started.

And hold onto the most hopeful fact in the whole post, because it is true: almost every great trader blew up at least once and came back. The blowup is survivable. What determines whether you are Paul Tudor Jones or Victor Niederhoffer's second act is not whether you get knocked down — everyone does — but whether you rebuild on a process you actually fixed. That, unlike the market, is entirely within your control.
