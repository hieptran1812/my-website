> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A cartel is a prisoner's dilemma wearing a suit. A few producers are collectively richest when they all restrict output to the monopoly level and split the spoils, but each one is privately richer by quietly overproducing — so the only one-shot equilibrium is everyone cheating and the price sliding toward cost. The whole drama of OPEC, of any oligopoly, and of the new worry about pricing algorithms is the fight between that collective temptation and the threat of future punishment.
>
> - The **Cournot model** is the cleanest lens: a few firms each pick a quantity, total output sets the price, and the equilibrium price falls smoothly from the monopoly level toward marginal cost as the number of firms rises. With our demand (`a=100, b=1, c=20`), one firm prices at \$60, two at \$46.67, ten at \$27.27, and a thousand at \$20.08 — the competition arrives gradually, not all at once.
> - Collusion at the cartel quota pays each of two firms \$800/period; cheating against a loyal partner pays the cheater \$900 and the sucker \$600; both cheating (the Nash outcome) pays \$711 each. Defecting dominates, so cartels are unstable by construction and members cheat.
> - A cartel only survives because the game **repeats**. With grim-trigger punishment, collusion holds only if every member's discount factor `δ ≥ (T−R)/(T−P)` — for our payoffs, `δ* ≈ 0.53`. If the future matters less than that (a member is broke, a regime is shaky, the game is ending), the cartel breaks.
> - **Bertrand** (firms competing on price, not quantity) collapses to marginal cost with just **two** firms — the opposite of Cournot. Which model fits tells you whether two competitors is a comfortable duopoly or a price war.
> - The one rule: a price held above cost is a truce, not a law of nature. Find the quota, find the cheater, and ask what would make the future stop mattering — that is the trade.

On the morning of 6 March 2020, OPEC and Russia walked out of a Vienna meeting without a deal. Saudi Arabia had wanted Russia to join a deeper production cut to defend the oil price against collapsing pandemic demand; Russia refused. Within hours, Saudi Arabia did the opposite of restraint — it announced it would *raise* output and slash its official selling prices, flooding a market that was already drowning. Brent crude fell roughly 30% in a single session, one of the worst one-day moves in the history of the contract. A month later the same two countries, plus the rest of OPEC+, signed the largest coordinated production cut ever agreed. Less than two years after that, several members were quietly pumping above their new quotas again, and the cartel had to publish "compensation cut" schedules to shame the cheaters back into line.

If you only watch the headlines, this looks like chaos — diplomacy, betrayal, reconciliation, betrayal again. It is not chaos. It is the single most-played game in economics, running in slow motion on the world's most important commodity. Every move in that sequence — the cartel, the walkout, the price war, the bigger cartel, the cheating — is a textbook position in a game that a French mathematician named Antoine Augustin Cournot wrote down in 1838, and that game theorists later recognized as a prisoner's dilemma you play over and over. Once you can see the game, the headlines stop being surprising. You can predict the cheating before it shows up in the tanker data, and you can read a price held above cost for exactly what it is: a fragile agreement that somebody is always tempted to break.

This post builds that lens from zero. We will define what an oligopoly even is, derive the Cournot model so you can see *why* more competitors means a lower price, turn the cartel into a payoff matrix and watch defection win, then ask the only question that matters for a trader — what holds a cartel together, and what makes it snap. Then we apply it: to OPEC, to the new antitrust panic about pricing algorithms, and to your own position when you are long a commodity whose price depends on whether a roomful of oil ministers can keep a promise.

![Cournot price falling from sixty dollars at one firm toward twenty dollars at a hundred firms on a log scale](/imgs/blogs/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms-1.png)

That curve is the whole story in one picture. The price does not jump from "monopoly" to "competition" the moment a second firm appears; it walks down a smooth ramp as the number of producers grows, never quite touching cost until there are effectively infinitely many of them. The amber region between the price line and the marginal-cost floor is the prize — the margin the producers extract over what it actually costs them to make the stuff. A cartel's entire job is to keep that amber region as fat as possible by pretending there are fewer firms than there really are. The rest of this post is about who tries to do that, why it almost works, and why it keeps falling apart.

## Foundations: oligopoly, Cournot, the cartel, and Bertrand from zero

Before any math, let us pin down the words, because the everyday meaning and the technical meaning diverge in ways that matter.

**Market structure** is just a count of how many sellers there are and how much each one's decision moves the price. At one extreme sits **monopoly**: one seller, who can set whatever price the demand curve allows because nobody undercuts them. At the other extreme sits **perfect competition**: so many tiny sellers that no single one matters, each takes the market price as given, and competition grinds the price down to the cost of producing one more unit (the **marginal cost**). In between, and far more common in the real world, sits **oligopoly**: a handful of sellers, each big enough that what it does visibly moves the price, and each therefore having to think about what the others will do. Oil, airlines, mobile carriers, memory chips, beer, ratings agencies, exchanges — almost every market you actually care about as a trader is an oligopoly.

Oligopoly is where game theory lives, because it is the only structure where the word "strategic" applies. A monopolist has no rival to outguess. A wheat farmer in perfect competition is a price-taker — nothing they do affects the world price, so there is no one to play against. But an oligopolist's profit depends directly on the choices of three or four named competitors, each of whom is reasoning about *them* in turn. That is a game in the precise sense: my best move depends on your move, which depends on mine.

There are two classic ways to model what the firms are choosing, and they give startlingly different answers — which is exactly why you have to know which one you are in.

**Cournot competition** assumes firms compete on **quantity**. Each firm independently decides how much to *produce* — how many barrels, how many chips, how many tons — and then the market price is whatever clears that total output through the demand curve. The mental model is everyone committing to a production plan in advance, building the capacity, opening the taps, and then discovering the price the market gives them for that combined flood. This is a good model for industries where capacity is lumpy and committed ahead of time: you build an oil field or a chip fab, you run it, and the price is determined after the fact by how much everyone collectively dumped onto the market. The key feature is that the *decision variable is output*, fixed before the price is known — which is realistic precisely for the heavy, capital-intensive commodity industries where cartels actually form.

**Bertrand competition** assumes firms compete on **price** instead. Each firm posts a price, and customers simply buy from whoever is cheapest. This is a good model for industries where you can flex supply instantly and customers will switch for a penny — undifferentiated commodities sold at a posted price, software, generic e-commerce. The punchline of Bertrand, which we will derive later, is brutal: with even two firms selling an identical product, the only equilibrium is to price at marginal cost and make zero profit, because each firm can always steal the whole market by undercutting the other by a cent, and that race ends at cost. Two Bertrand firms behave like perfect competition; two Cournot firms do not. Same number of competitors, opposite outcome. The model you pick is not a technicality — it is the difference between a fat margin and a price war.

A **cartel** is what happens when the oligopolists stop pretending to compete and instead **agree to act like a single monopolist**. They restrict their combined output to the level a monopolist would choose, which props the price up to the monopoly level, and then they divide the monopoly profit among themselves — usually by assigning each member a **quota**, a cap on how much it is allowed to produce. OPEC is the most famous example: a formal organization whose entire purpose is to set production quotas so the members collectively hold oil above its competitive price. A cartel is, in the language we will build, an attempt to collude your way back from the right side of that opening chart to the left side — to make a market with a dozen big producers behave as if it had one.

And here is the tension that makes the whole subject interesting, the one we will spend the rest of the post unpacking. The cartel is collectively the best outcome for its members — they make the most total money when they all restrict output. But each individual member is *privately* better off cheating: pumping a little more than its quota while everyone else holds the line, capturing extra sales at the high price the others are propping up. Everyone faces that same private temptation, so everyone is tempted to cheat, so the cartel is inherently unstable. That structure — collectively best to cooperate, individually best to defect — is the **prisoner's dilemma**, the most important idea in all of game theory, and a cartel is just a prisoner's dilemma played by oil ministers instead of prisoners. If you want the dilemma in its purest market form, the sibling post on [the prisoner's dilemma in markets](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-prisoners-dilemma-in-markets-why-everyone-sells-at-once) walks the same logic through a liquidity crash; here we walk it through a supply curve.

With those four words defined — oligopoly, Cournot, Bertrand, cartel — we can now build the model that makes them quantitative.

## The Cournot model: why more firms means a lower price

The Cournot model needs only three ingredients, and we will use the exact numbers from `data_gametheory.cournot` throughout so every figure and every worked example agrees to the penny.

First, **demand**. We assume an inverse demand curve `P = a − b·Q`, where `Q` is the *total* quantity that all firms produce together, `P` is the price the market pays for that total, and `a` and `b` are constants. With our numbers, `a = 100` and `b = 1`, so `P = 100 − Q`. The interpretation: if nobody produces anything (`Q = 0`), the price would be \$100 — that is the most a desperate buyer would pay for the very first unit. Every additional unit dumped onto the market drops the price by \$1. This downward slope is the entire reason a producer has any power: by holding back, it keeps the price up.

Second, **cost**. Every firm can produce as much as it likes at a constant **marginal cost** `c = 20` per unit. So it costs \$20 to make one more barrel, and a firm earns a profit of `(P − 20)` on every unit it sells. If the price ever falls to \$20, profit per unit is zero and there is no point producing.

Third, the **decision**. Each of the `n` firms simultaneously and independently chooses its own output `q`. They do not coordinate; each picks the quantity that maximizes *its own* profit, taking the others' output as given. The genius of Cournot — a century before John Nash formalized it — was to ask: is there a set of output choices where no firm, looking at what everyone else chose, would want to change its own? That self-consistent point is the **Nash equilibrium** of the quantity game, and the sibling post on [Nash equilibrium and the price as a truce](/blog/trading/game-theory/nash-equilibrium-best-response-and-the-price-as-a-truce) builds that solution concept from scratch if you want the general theory.

The math is short enough to walk through. Each symmetric firm produces `q* = (a − c) / (b·(n+1))`. With our numbers that is `q* = 80 / (n+1)`. Total output is `Q = n·q*`, the price is `P = (a + n·c)/(n+1)`, and each firm's profit is `(P − c)·q* = (a − c)² / (b·(n+1)²)`. You do not have to take the derivation on faith — let us watch the formula produce the entire spectrum of market structures by just dialing `n`.

#### Worked example: the monopoly-to-competition sweep in numbers

Set `n = 1` — a single firm, which is a monopoly and also exactly what a perfect cartel of any size achieves. The formula gives `q* = 80 / 2 = 40`, so total output `Q = 40`, price `P = 100 − 40 = \$60`, and profit `(60 − 20)·40 = \$1,600`. The lone producer holds output way back to 40 units and enjoys a fat \$40 margin on each.

Now let a second firm enter, `n = 2`, a **duopoly**. Each produces `q* = 80 / 3 = 26.67`, so total output jumps to `Q = 53.33`, and the price falls to `P = 100 − 53.33 = \$46.67`. Each firm earns `(46.67 − 20)·26.67 = \$711`. Notice what happened: total output *rose* (from 40 to 53.33) and the price *fell* (from \$60 to \$46.67) the instant a competitor appeared, even though the two firms never spoke and never agreed to compete. Each simply, selfishly, produced more than half the monopoly quantity because expanding was individually profitable — and their combined expansion dragged the price down.

Push to `n = 10` and the price is `P = (100 + 10·20)/11 = 300/11 = \$27.27`, with each of the ten firms earning a meager `\$52.89`. At `n = 1000` the price is `\$20.08` — eight cents above the \$20 marginal cost — and each firm's profit is rounding-error change. The intuition that this number nails: competition is a dial, not a switch. The price grinds down toward cost continuously as producers pile in, and the bulk of the damage to producer profits happens early — going from one firm to two already gives up more than half the per-firm prize.

That is the cover figure made arithmetic. The reason the curve bends the way it does is the `(n+1)` in the denominator: each new firm adds its output and shrinks everyone's slice, but with diminishing bite, so the approach to cost is asymptotic. The lesson for anyone trading a commodity is direct. When you hear that a new producer is entering — US shale in the 2010s, a new lithium mine, a fresh chip fab — do not ask only "how much will *they* add?" Ask what the new `n` does to the equilibrium price for *everyone*, because the incumbents will rationally expand too, and the price impact is larger than the newcomer's barrels alone.

## Profit: the prize shrinks as the cartel splits

The price chart tells you what buyers pay. The flip side is what producers earn, and it falls even faster, because profit depends on the *square* of `(n+1)`.

![Industry and per-firm profit bars falling sharply as the number of firms rises from one to fifty](/imgs/blogs/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms-2.png)

The blue bars are profit *per firm* and the amber bars are total profit summed across the *whole industry*. At `n = 1` they are equal — one firm is the whole industry — at \$1,600. The per-firm bar collapses immediately: a single competitor cuts it from \$1,600 to \$711, three firms make it \$400 each, ten firms make it \$53 each. But look at the amber industry bars too: total industry profit also bleeds away, from \$1,600 (monopoly) to \$1,422 (duopoly) to \$1,200 (three firms) to \$889 (five firms). This is the deep reason cartels exist. It is not merely that each firm wants a bigger slice; it is that the *entire pie shrinks* when firms compete, because competition forces total output up and the price down, destroying margin that nobody recovers. The firms are collectively burning money by competing.

#### Worked example: how much a cartel is worth

Put a dollar figure on the prize. If our `n = 5` industry — five firms each making \$178, for \$889 of total industry profit — could somehow agree to act as one and restrict total output to the monopoly level of 40 units, the industry would earn \$1,600. The cartel would *create* `1600 − 889 = \$711` of profit out of thin air, simply by everyone agreeing to produce less. Split five ways, each firm's profit would leap from \$178 to \$320 — nearly double — if they could split the monopoly quantity evenly (40 units / 5 firms = 8 units each, earning `(60−20)·8 = \$320`).

That \$711 of conjured profit is the cartel's reason to exist, and it is enormous relative to what competition leaves on the table. It is also exactly the money that antitrust law exists to protect for consumers, and exactly the money OPEC tries to capture for its members. The intuition this makes concrete: a cartel is not splitting a fixed pie more favorably; it is *growing* the pie by suppressing competition, which is why the incentive to form one is so powerful and so persistent — and why the temptation to cheat, which we turn to next, is equally powerful.

## Collusion as a prisoner's dilemma: why cartels cheat

Here is the cruel twist. The cartel agreement is collectively wonderful — each of two firms gets \$800 (half of the \$1,600 monopoly profit) instead of the \$711 they would get competing. But the agreement is not **self-enforcing**, because each firm, staring at its quota, can do the arithmetic on cheating.

Suppose two firms agree to split the monopoly output: each produces 20 units (half of 40), the price holds at \$60, and each pockets \$800. Now firm A asks: what if I quietly pump *more* than my quota of 20 while B loyally holds at 20? A's best response to B producing 20 is to produce `q_A = (a − c − b·q_B)/(2b) = (100 − 20 − 20)/2 = 30` units. Total output becomes `30 + 20 = 50`, the price falls to `100 − 50 = \$50`, and now A earns `(50 − 20)·30 = \$900` while loyal B earns only `(50 − 20)·20 = \$600`. By cheating, A jumps from \$800 to \$900; loyal B drops from \$800 to \$600. A is \$100 richer for breaking the promise, and B is \$200 poorer for keeping it.

Of course B can see this coming and faces the identical temptation. So we lay out all four combinations as a payoff matrix.

![Two by two payoff matrix of the cartel prisoner's dilemma with restrict and cheat strategies](/imgs/blogs/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms-3.png)

Read the matrix the way you read any game. Each firm chooses a row or column: **restrict** (honor the cartel quota of 20) or **cheat** (overproduce to its best response). The green cell, top-left, is both firms restricting — \$800 each, the collectively best outcome. The amber cell, bottom-right, is both cheating — \$711 each, the competitive Nash duopoly. The two red cells are the asymmetric outcomes: one firm cheats (\$900) while the loyal one is the sucker (\$600).

#### Worked example: why defection is a dominant strategy

The dominance argument is the heart of it, and it is worth walking slowly because it is *why* cartels are doomed. Ask: what should firm A do if it believes B will **restrict**? A compares its top-left payoff (restrict, \$800) against its bottom-left payoff (cheat, \$900). Cheating wins by \$100. Now ask: what should A do if it believes B will **cheat**? A compares top-right (restrict, \$600 — the sucker) against bottom-right (cheat, \$711). Cheating wins again, by \$111. So *no matter what B does*, A is better off cheating. Cheating is a **dominant strategy** — it beats restricting in every column. By perfect symmetry, the same is true for B. When both players have a dominant strategy, the equilibrium is for both to play it: both cheat, the \$711/\$711 amber cell, which is exactly the Cournot duopoly outcome we computed earlier.

Feed the matrix to `data_gametheory.nash_2x2` and it confirms the single pure Nash equilibrium is (cheat, cheat) — and that the (restrict, restrict) cartel is *not* an equilibrium, because each firm would defect from it. The tragedy is plain in the numbers: both firms end at \$711 when they could each have had \$800. They burned \$89 each — \$178 of collective profit — by being individually rational. That is the prisoner's dilemma in one line: the pursuit of the extra \$100 from cheating leads both to an outcome \$89 worse than the cooperation they abandoned. A cartel is this matrix, and the matrix says: cheat. This is why every cartel in history has been plagued by quota-busting, and why "we have a production agreement" and "the agreement is being honored" are two completely different statements.

If the game were played exactly once, that would be the end of the story — cartels could never form, and OPEC could not exist. But OPEC has existed since 1960. The thing that saves a cartel is that the game is not played once. It is played every month, forever, and that changes everything.

## The repeated game: how a cartel survives at all

The escape from the prisoner's dilemma is **repetition**. OPEC ministers do not meet once; they meet again and again, indefinitely. And when a game repeats, a firm that cheats today can be *punished* tomorrow. The threat of future punishment can make today's cooperation rational even though it is irrational in the one-shot game. This is the single most important idea for understanding why any cartel — or any cooperative arrangement in markets — ever holds.

The cleanest version is the **grim-trigger** strategy. Every firm announces, in effect: "I will honor my quota as long as everyone else does. But the first time I catch anyone cheating, I will overproduce forever, and so will everyone — we all revert to the cutthroat \$711 Cournot competition permanently." Now a firm contemplating cheating faces a different calculation. Cheating gives it a one-time bonus today (\$900 instead of \$800, a \$100 gain), but it triggers an eternity of \$711 instead of \$800 (an \$89 loss every period thereafter). Whether cheating pays depends on how much the firm values the future relative to today.

That trade-off is captured by the **discount factor** `δ` (delta), a number between 0 and 1 measuring how much a dollar next period is worth today. `δ = 0` means the firm cares only about today (the future is worthless); `δ` near 1 means the firm weighs the future almost as heavily as the present. A patient, long-horizon firm has a high `δ`; a firm that might not survive the year, or whose managers are paid on this quarter, has a low `δ`.

The condition for the cartel to hold is clean. Cooperation is sustainable under grim-trigger exactly when

`δ ≥ (T − R) / (T − P)`

where `T` is the temptation payoff from cheating, `R` is the reward from mutual cooperation, and `P` is the punishment payoff from mutual defection. (`S`, the sucker's payoff, does not enter the threshold because once you cheat you never get suckered.) The function `data_gametheory.repeated_pd_delta_threshold` computes exactly this. Plug in our cartel's payoffs — `T = 900` (cheat), `R = 800` (collude), `P = 711` (both cheat) — and the threshold is `δ* = (900 − 800)/(900 − 711) = 100/189 ≈ 0.53`.

![Delta threshold curve rising as the temptation to cheat grows, with cartel holds and cartel breaks regions](/imgs/blogs/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms-6.png)

The figure shows what happens as you vary the temptation `T`. The blue curve is the minimum `δ` needed to keep the cartel together; above the curve (green) the cartel holds, below it (red) the cartel breaks. The red dot marks our cartel at `T = 900`, sitting at `δ* ≈ 0.53`. The curve rises as the temptation grows: the juicier the one-shot grab from cheating, the more patient every member must be for the agreement to survive.

#### Worked example: is our cartel sustainable?

Suppose each firm in our cartel discounts the future at `δ = 0.6` — roughly what you would get with a 5% per-period interest rate and a small chance the game ends each period. Is the cartel stable? Compare the two streams. **Always cooperate:** the firm earns \$800 every period forever, worth `800/(1 − δ) = 800/0.4 = \$2,000` in present value. **Cheat once, then suffer:** the firm earns \$900 today, then \$711 forever after, worth `900 + δ·711/(1 − δ) = 900 + 0.6·711/0.4 = 900 + 1066.5 = \$1,966.50`. Cooperating (\$2,000) beats cheating (\$1,966.50), so a firm with `δ = 0.6` honors the quota. The cartel holds, because `0.6 > 0.53`.

Now drop the discount factor to `δ = 0.4` — an impatient firm, maybe one in financial distress that needs cash *now*. Cooperate: `800/0.6 = \$1,333`. Cheat: `900 + 0.4·711/0.6 = 900 + 474 = \$1,374`. Now cheating wins. The cartel breaks for this member, because `0.4 < 0.53`. The intuition this number delivers is the single most useful thing a commodity trader can carry: **a cartel's stability is not about goodwill — it is about whether the future matters enough to its weakest member.** The moment one important producer's `δ` drops below the threshold — because it is desperate for revenue, because its regime is unstable, because it thinks the game is ending (oil demand peaking, a substitute arriving) — that member cheats, and the grim trigger drags everyone back to competition. You do not read a cartel by reading the communiqué. You read it by asking which member is most desperate, and how close the game is to its end.

This is why three conditions make cartels more durable, and their absence makes them fragile. **A long horizon** (high `δ`): cartels of patient, stable producers last; cartels of desperate ones do not. **Good monitoring**: grim-trigger only works if cheating is *detected* — you cannot punish what you cannot see, which is why OPEC obsesses over tanker-tracking and satellite data on storage tanks, and why cartels in industries where output is hard to observe collapse fastest. **Few players**: with two firms, one cheater is obvious and the punishment is sharp; with twelve, cheating is easy to hide and the cooperative `δ` threshold rises, which is why OPEC's expansion to OPEC+ made discipline harder, not easier.

It is worth seeing *why* more members raises the threshold, because it is the same `(n+1)` logic from the price curve wearing a different costume. When the cartel has more members, two things move against cooperation at once. First, each member's slice of the cooperative pie shrinks — the \$1,600 monopoly profit split twelve ways is \$133 each, so the *reward* `R` for cooperating is small. Second, the temptation to cheat barely shrinks at all, because a single member overproducing against eleven loyal partners still captures a large one-shot grab while the others absorb the price hit. A small `R` and a large `T` push the ratio `(T−R)/(T−P)` toward 1, meaning every member must be extraordinarily patient for the cartel to hold. This is the structural reason large cartels are flimsier than small ones, and it is exactly why the most durable cartel in history — De Beers in diamonds — worked by having essentially *one* dominant player control supply rather than a committee of equals trying to police each other. A cartel run by a dominant firm that does most of the cutting and credibly punishes defectors is far more stable than a cartel of equals, because the dominant firm internalizes the whole cooperative payoff and has both the incentive and the means to enforce.

There is a subtler point hiding in the grim-trigger story that matters for reading the real world: the punishment has to be *credible*. Grim-trigger says "I will revert to competition forever if you cheat" — but reverting to competition hurts the punisher too, since everyone earns \$711 instead of \$800 in the punishment phase. A threat that hurts you to carry out is only believable if you would actually carry it out. This is why cartels invest so heavily in *demonstrating* willingness to punish even at a cost to themselves. When Saudi Arabia crashed the oil price in 2014 and again in 2020, it was lighting its own money on fire to make the threat credible — proving to every other member that defection would be met with a price war it was willing to suffer through. A cartel whose enforcer is unwilling to bleed has no credible punishment, and a punishment that is not credible does not deter, so the cartel unravels. Reading whether a cartel will hold is, in large part, reading whether its enforcer is still willing to absorb pain to keep the others in line.

## Cournot's best-response curves: seeing the equilibrium

Step back to the competitive (non-cartel) game for a moment, because there is a beautiful geometric way to see *why* the duopoly settles at 26.67 units each, and it sharpens your sense of what an equilibrium even is.

Each firm has a **best-response function** (also called a reaction function): for any quantity the rival produces, it tells you the quantity that maximizes your own profit. For firm A, `q_A = (a − c − b·q_B)/(2b) = (80 − q_B)/2`. It slopes down: the more B produces, the less A wants to produce, because B's flood has already pushed the price down, so A's profit-maximizing response is to pull back. If B produces nothing, A's best response is `q_A = 40` — the full monopoly quantity, because A would *be* the monopolist. If B produces the full 80 (the competitive total), A's best response is 0 — the price is already at cost, so there is no room.

![Two downward sloping best response curves crossing at the Cournot equilibrium of twenty six point six seven each](/imgs/blogs/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms-4.png)

The blue line is A's best response to every possible `q_B`; the lavender line is B's best response to every possible `q_A`. The equilibrium is where they cross — the red dot at (26.67, 26.67). That crossing is the *only* point where both firms are simultaneously playing a best response to the other. Anywhere else, at least one firm would want to move: if A produced 30 expecting B to stay at 20, B would see A's flood and react down, A would react up, and the chase would spiral until it landed on the dot. The green square marks the cartel split (each produces 20) — and notice it sits *inside* both curves, off the equilibrium. At the cartel point, each firm is producing *less* than its best response, which is exactly why each is tempted to push out toward its reaction curve. The cartel asks every member to sit at a point it privately wants to leave. The Nash equilibrium is the point nobody wants to leave — which is why it is stable and the cartel is not.

The diagram also explains a subtle real-world fact: when OPEC cuts and the price rises, *non-OPEC* producers (US shale, Brazil, Norway) happily expand. They are sitting on their best-response curve, reacting to OPEC's restraint exactly as the lavender line predicts — producing more because someone else is producing less and holding the price up. OPEC restricts; the fringe free-rides. That is the reaction function in the tanker data, and it is why OPEC's market share has eroded for decades even as it has tried to defend price.

## Bertrand: when two firms is already a price war

Now the model that flips everything. Cournot says two firms still earn fat profits (\$711 each, a price of \$46.67, well above the \$20 cost). **Bertrand** says the opposite: two firms competing on *price* drive the price all the way to marginal cost, earning zero, behaving exactly like perfect competition. Same two firms, wildly different outcome. Understanding which model applies is the difference between expecting a comfortable duopoly and expecting a bloodbath.

The Bertrand logic is a brutal little argument. Suppose two firms sell an identical product and customers buy entirely from whoever posts the lower price. Could the price sit at \$50, with the two firms splitting the market? No — either firm can post \$49.99, capture the *entire* market instead of half, and earn far more. But then the other firm posts \$49.98 to steal it back. This undercutting cascade has only one resting point: marginal cost, \$20. At \$20, neither firm can profitably undercut (going below \$20 means selling at a loss), and neither can raise its price (the other would keep the whole market). So both price at \$20, split the market, and earn zero profit — with just two firms. The price ladder makes the contrast vivid.

![Horizontal bar price ladder from monopoly at sixty dollars down to perfect competition at twenty dollars](/imgs/blogs/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms-5.png)

Same demand, same cost, four market structures, four prices. Monopoly/cartel at the top, \$60. Cournot duopoly, \$46.67. Cournot with ten firms, \$27.27. And at the bottom, *both* Bertrand duopoly *and* perfect competition collapse to the \$20 marginal-cost floor. The startling entry is that Bertrand reaches the bottom of the ladder with only two firms — the same two firms that, under Cournot, would be sitting near the top at \$46.67.

#### Worked example: which model is the airline in?

Why do two firms sometimes earn fat margins (Cournot) and sometimes bleed to zero (Bertrand)? The answer is what they can flex and how easily customers switch. Bertrand's death spiral needs three things: an identical product (customers don't care which firm), instantly flexible supply (a firm can serve the whole market the moment it wins the price), and customers who switch for a penny. Break any one and you climb back up the ladder toward Cournot. **Capacity constraints** break it most cleanly: if each firm can only serve half the market, undercutting to steal "the whole market" is meaningless — you can't supply it — so prices stay up. This is why airlines on a route with two carriers and limited slots behave Cournot-ish (planes are committed capacity, fares stay above cost), while two cloud-storage providers selling identical gigabytes with elastic capacity behave Bertrand-ish (price wars to near-cost). The practical read: ask whether the firms compete by *committing capacity in advance* (Cournot, margins survive) or by *posting flexible prices on an identical good* (Bertrand, margins die). Oil is committed capacity — Cournot — which is exactly why a cartel can hold price above cost at all. If oil were Bertrand, OPEC would be pointless.

## Tacit and algorithmic collusion: the cartel without a meeting

Everything so far assumed an *explicit* cartel — firms in a room agreeing on quotas. That is illegal in most of the world; price-fixing and output agreements are the heart of antitrust law. But the game does not require a meeting. It only requires that firms reach the cooperative outcome and sustain it with the threat of punishment. When firms achieve supra-competitive prices *without an explicit agreement* — each simply choosing, period after period, to keep prices high because it understands that cutting would trigger a price war nobody wants — that is **tacit collusion**.

Tacit collusion is the same repeated-game equilibrium reached without communication. Three gas stations at an intersection that all hover at the same high price, each quietly matching the others and none willing to start a price war, are tacitly colluding even if their managers never speak. It is collusion as a *focal point*: everyone understands the cooperative price, everyone understands that defecting invites retaliation, so everyone holds. Antitrust law struggles with tacit collusion precisely because there is no smoking gun — no meeting, no memo, no agreement to subpoena. There is only a price that sits suspiciously high and a pattern of mutual forbearance.

Now make it modern and uncomfortable. Increasingly, firms set prices not by hand but with **pricing algorithms** — software that watches competitors' prices and demand in real time and adjusts continuously. Airlines, hotels, ride-hailing, e-commerce marketplaces, even some gasoline retailers run dynamic pricing engines. And here is the live antitrust concern: pricing algorithms, especially ones using reinforcement learning, can *learn to collude* without anyone programming them to. They are explicitly trained to maximize profit in a repeated interaction against rivals who are doing the same. A reinforcement-learning agent that discovers, through millions of simulated rounds, that "if I cut price the competitor's algorithm retaliates and we both lose, but if I hold price high we both win" has *learned the grim-trigger equilibrium* — it has learned to collude — without a single line of code saying "fix prices." Academic experiments (notably work by Calvano, Calzolari, Denicolò, and Pastorello around 2019-2020) showed exactly this: independent Q-learning pricing agents, with no communication and no instruction to collude, reliably converged on supra-competitive prices and even learned punishment strategies to enforce them.

This is the prisoner's dilemma's repeated solution emerging from machines, and it terrifies regulators for two reasons. First, there is no agreement to find — the algorithms never "agreed" to anything; they each independently optimized. Second, algorithms remove the human frictions that used to make tacit collusion unstable: they monitor competitors continuously (perfect detection), they react in milliseconds (instant punishment, raising every rival's effective `δ`), and they never get greedy or make the emotional mistakes that cause human cartels to break. In the language of our `δ` threshold: algorithms push the discount factor toward 1 and make monitoring near-perfect, which makes the cooperative equilibrium far easier to sustain. The very features that make a cartel durable — patience, perfect monitoring, instant punishment — are exactly what a well-designed pricing algorithm provides by construction. The ethics framing here matters: the point is not how to *build* a colluding algorithm but how to *recognize* one — as a regulator, a competitor, or a consumer — by its fingerprint of prices that move together, hold above cost, and snap back into line after any deviation. That fingerprint is the grim-trigger equilibrium showing through the price data.

#### Worked example: how an algorithm lowers the cheating temptation

Make the algorithmic point quantitative, because it is the crux of the antitrust worry. Recall our cartel: cooperating pays \$800/period, cheating pays a one-shot \$900 then \$711 forever after the punishment, and the threshold for cooperation to hold was `δ* ≈ 0.53`. Now ask what changes when a *human* cartel is replaced by *algorithms*. A human cartel detects cheating slowly — it might take a quarter to confirm from lagging production data that a rival busted quota, so a cheater enjoys several periods of the \$900 grab before punishment kicks in. Suppose detection takes three periods: the cheater banks roughly `900 + 900·δ + 900·δ²` before the \$711 punishment begins, which fattens the temptation enormously and pushes the required `δ` well above 0.53 — often above any realistic discount factor, so the human cartel breaks. An algorithm detects and retaliates in *one* period (effectively instantly), so the cheater gets the \$900 exactly once before being slammed back to \$711 — the temptation stays at its minimum and the threshold stays at 0.53, which almost any firm clears. The intuition this delivers: the algorithm does not need to be programmed to collude; it simply makes detection and punishment so fast that the cooperative equilibrium that a slow human cartel could never sustain becomes trivially stable. Speed of retaliation, not any agreement, is what converts an unstable human cartel into a durable algorithmic one — which is precisely why regulators are looking at the *software*, not for a smoke-filled room.

## Common misconceptions

**"More competitors immediately means competitive prices."** No — the Cournot curve is a gentle ramp, not a cliff. Going from one firm to two only drops the price from \$60 to \$46.67, still \$26.67 above cost. You need roughly ten firms (\$27.27) to get even halfway down the ramp from monopoly to cost, and the price never literally reaches cost with any finite number of producers. A "competitive" market with four big players is still extracting a large margin. When you read that an industry has "real competition" because it has three or four firms, check the math — a Cournot quadropoly still prices at \$36, sixteen dollars above cost. The number of firms that delivers genuinely competitive pricing is much larger than intuition suggests.

**"If the cartel agreement is signed, the price will hold."** The agreement is the easy part; enforcement is everything. The signed quota is the green cell in the matrix — collectively best, individually unstable. Every signatory has a dominant strategy to cheat, and the only thing stopping them is the credible threat of future punishment, which requires monitoring and a long horizon. A cartel announcement should *raise* your suspicion that cheating is about to begin, not lower it. The relevant question after any OPEC cut is never "did they agree?" but "who is most tempted to bust quota, and can the others detect and punish it?"

**"A cartel cheater is being irrational or greedy."** The cheater is being *perfectly rational* — cheating is a dominant strategy in the one-shot game and a tempting deviation whenever the future stops mattering enough. Calling a quota-buster greedy misreads the game. They are responding correctly to incentives the cartel itself created by asking them to sit below their best-response curve. The cartel's design *manufactures* the temptation; the cheater merely accepts it. If you want to predict cheating, do not look for villains — look for the member whose `δ` is collapsing.

**"Two competitors guarantees a healthy market."** Sometimes two firms is a comfortable duopoly (Cournot, \$46.67, fat margins) and sometimes two firms is a price war to the floor (Bertrand, \$20, zero margin). "Duopoly" tells you the count, not the outcome. You have to know whether they compete on committed quantity or on flexible price, and whether capacity constraints blunt the undercutting. Two airlines on a slot-constrained route and two cloud providers selling identical storage are both "duopolies" with opposite economics.

**"Tacit and algorithmic collusion are not real collusion because nobody agreed."** Legally, the absence of an agreement matters; economically, the *outcome* is identical — supra-competitive prices sustained by mutual punishment. A consumer paying the monopoly price does not care whether the firms met in a hotel room or whether their algorithms independently learned the same equilibrium. The repeated prisoner's dilemma has many roads to the cooperative cell, and "we never spoke" is one of them. Treat a suspiciously stable high price as a cartel-shaped object regardless of whether you can find a meeting.

**"A cartel can set whatever price it wants."** No — even a perfect cartel is bounded by the demand curve. Our monopoly cartel does not charge \$100; it charges \$60, because at higher prices it would lose so many sales that total profit falls. The cartel chooses the output that maximizes profit *along* the demand curve, and that output already concedes a lot of the theoretical maximum. This is why even an undisputed monopolist leaves money on the table relative to "charge the most anyone would pay for the first unit" — the demand curve slopes down, so every price hike costs volume. A cartel is a profit-maximizer constrained by demand, not a price dictator; the ceiling on its greed is set by how fast buyers walk away, which is exactly the `b` in `P = a − b·Q`.

## How it shows up in real markets

**OPEC is the Cournot/cartel model running live, and its entire history is the `δ` threshold in motion.** OPEC formed in 1960 and has spent six decades trying to hold oil above its competitive price by assigning production quotas — exactly the cartel of our matrix, scaled to a dozen sovereign nations. And exactly as the theory predicts, it has been chronically plagued by cheating, because each member faces the dominant-strategy temptation to pump above quota while the others hold the line. The cheating is not a bug in OPEC; it *is* OPEC, the prisoner's dilemma showing through.

![OPEC plus members producing above their agreed quotas while Saudi Arabia holds at quota](/imgs/blogs/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms-7.png)

The figure shows the pattern from the 2024 overproduction episode. Several members — Iraq, Kazakhstan, Russia among the most cited — produced visibly above their agreed ceilings, the red bars exceeding the blue quotas. The cheating got bad enough that in 2024 the OPEC+ secretariat had to publish formal "compensation cut" schedules, demanding the over-producers make *extra* cuts in later months to offset their earlier busting — a bureaucratic version of the grim trigger, the cartel trying to manufacture punishment for detected defection. Meanwhile Saudi Arabia, by far the largest and most patient producer (the high-`δ` member with the deepest reserves and longest horizon), has repeatedly carried the cuts itself, holding at or below quota while others cheated — playing the role of the loyal firm that earns the sucker's \$600 to keep the price up for everyone, because as the marginal producer it has the most to lose from a price collapse.

The dramatic exception proves the rule. In March 2020, Saudi Arabia stopped playing the loyal firm and pulled the grim trigger: when Russia refused to deepen cuts, the Saudis flooded the market, crashing Brent ~30% in a day. That was punishment — a deliberate price war to demonstrate to Russia (and to the cheating fringe) that the cartel would inflict pain on defectors. It worked: a month later, with US shale also screaming and storage filling, all parties signed the record ~9.7 million barrel/day cut of April 2020. The sequence — cartel, defection, brutal punishment, restored cartel — is the repeated prisoner's dilemma's grim-trigger dynamic acted out on the world's most important commodity, with the price doing exactly what the model says it should at each step.

For a trader, this is not abstract. Oil is the inflation engine and the swing factor in cross-asset positioning, and OPEC's quota discipline is the variable that moves it; the [energy, oil and gas post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine) traces those macro linkages in depth. When you are long oil, you are implicitly betting that OPEC's cartel holds — that its members' `δ` stays above the threshold. When a major member becomes desperate for revenue (sanctions, a budget crisis, a regime under threat), its `δ` drops, cheating becomes rational, and the cartel risks snapping back toward the competitive price. The 2014-2016 crash was exactly this: Saudi Arabia, facing the rise of US shale, *chose* to let the cartel break and flood the market to crush the high-cost competitors rather than keep cutting and ceding share — a calculated decision that the game had changed enough that defending price was no longer worth it. Reading OPEC is reading the discount factors of a dozen oil ministers.

**Beyond oil, the same model prices a dozen markets you trade.** Memory chips (DRAM) are a Cournot oligopoly of three giants — Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron — whose coordinated capacity discipline has repeatedly turned the cycle, and who paid over a billion dollars settling price-fixing charges in the 2000s, the cartel matrix made literal in a courtroom. Potash, diamonds (De Beers ran the most successful cartel in history for a century by controlling supply and credibly punishing defectors), shipping container lines, and ratings agencies all show oligopoly pricing well above the Bertrand floor. And the algorithmic-pricing frontier is now a live antitrust battlefield: the US Department of Justice has pursued cases (notably against RealPage, a rent-pricing software firm, beginning 2024) on the theory that landlords using a *shared* pricing algorithm fed with competitors' data were tacitly colluding through the software — the algorithmic cartel of our final section, now in court.

## The playbook: how to trade a cartel

Pull it together into a way of reading any market where a few producers set the price. The spine of this series is that a trade is a strategic interaction — you are not betting against nature, you are positioned on the outcome of a game that specific players are playing — and nowhere is that truer than commodities under a cartel.

**Identify the structure first.** Count the producers and ask whether they compete on committed quantity (Cournot — margins survive, a cartel is possible) or flexible price on an identical good (Bertrand — margins die, a cartel is pointless). For most commodities the answer is Cournot, because capacity is lumpy and committed, which is precisely why cartels exist in oil, potash, and chips but not in, say, generic cloud storage. If you are looking at a "duopoly" and assuming fat margins, confirm it is Cournot-shaped before you size the trade.

**Find the quota and the cheater.** A price held above cost is a cartel truce, and every truce has a member tempted to break it. Your edge is identifying *who* — the producer whose discount factor is collapsing. Look for the member that is financially desperate (needs revenue now, low `δ`), the one losing market share to a non-cartel fringe (free-riders sitting on their best-response curves), or the one for whom the game looks like it is ending (a substitute arriving, demand peaking, reserves depleting). When a major member's `δ` drops below the threshold, cheating becomes rational, monitoring catches it, the grim trigger fires, and the price gaps toward the competitive level. That gap is the trade.

**Position and invalidation.** Being long a commodity under a cartel is implicitly a bet that the cartel holds — a short-volatility position on cooperation. It earns the propped-up price while the truce lasts and suffers a sharp loss when it breaks, because the break is sudden (grim trigger is discontinuous) and large (price falls toward cost). Size accordingly: this is a position with a fat tail to the downside, so it wants smaller sizing and a hard stop, not a "buy and forget." Your invalidation is concrete and observable: watch the monitoring data (tanker tracking, satellite storage, production figures) for the first evidence of quota-busting, and watch the weakest member's fiscal situation for a collapsing `δ`. The cartel's own published "compensation cut" demands are a tell that cheating is already underway — the cartel admitting in public that the matrix is winning.

**Read the punishment, not just the agreement.** When a cartel announces a cut, the market's first reaction prices the *agreement*; the durable move depends on *enforcement*. Fade announcements that lack a credible punishment mechanism (loose cartels with many members and poor monitoring rarely hold); respect cuts led by a dominant, patient producer that has demonstrated it will inflict pain on defectors (a high-`δ` enforcer like Saudi Arabia raises everyone's effective discount factor). And when the dominant producer itself defects — chooses a price war over continued restraint, as in 2014 and March 2020 — that is the regime change: the cartel is choosing competition, the price is going to the competitive level, and you do not want to be long into it.

**Watch the algorithms.** The newest version of this game has no oil ministers at all. When you trade or invest in industries running dynamic pricing — airlines, hotels, marketplaces, increasingly rents and retail — ask whether the pricing engines have learned the cooperative equilibrium. The fingerprint is prices that move together, hold above cost, and snap back into line after any deviation, with no agreement to point to. That pattern is supra-competitive margin that will persist until a regulator or a disruptive entrant breaks it — and the regulatory risk to such a margin is now real, as the RealPage and algorithmic-collusion cases show. A margin sustained by a machine-learned grim trigger is durable until the law arrives, and then it is not.

The one rule to carry out of all of this: a price above cost is never a fact of nature; it is a truce in a game that somebody is always tempted to break. Find the game, find the quota, find the member whose future is about to stop mattering — and you are no longer guessing where oil goes. You are reading the other side.
