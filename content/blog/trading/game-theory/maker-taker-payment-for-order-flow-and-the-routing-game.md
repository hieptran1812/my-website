> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When you tap Buy with zero commission, you are not the customer; you are the product. The fees, rebates, and payments that move underneath your order form a game whose players — the exchange, your broker, and the wholesaler — are all optimizing *their* take, and the equilibrium they reach is rarely the one that is best for you.
>
> - **Maker-taker** fees pay a rebate to whoever posts a resting quote (the *maker*) and charge whoever crosses the spread (the *taker*). That ~30-mil (\$0.0030/share) rebate turns posting into a competitive game: makers crowd the front of the queue, and your broker may route to whichever venue pays *it* the most, not whichever fills *you* best.
> - **Payment for order flow (PFOF)** exists because retail orders are *benign* — uninformed, non-toxic, the opposite of an adverse-selection problem. A wholesaler like Citadel Securities pays your broker ~\$0.0020/share for that flow because it can fill it for a near-guaranteed slice of the spread.
> - **The routing game** is the conflict at the center: your broker has a legal duty of **best execution** to you, but its revenue comes from rebates and PFOF that pull its router toward different venues. Best-ex sets a floor, not the best price.
> - **The one rule:** on every fill, ask *who paid whom, and what did they keep* — the spread you don't pay is being split three ways, and you only get the slice the broker chooses to pass through.

A retail trader opens an app, types in 100 shares of a \$50 stock, sees "\$0.00 commission," and taps Buy. The fill comes back in a few milliseconds at a price that looks like — maybe even a hair better than — the quote on the screen. It feels free. It feels like the trader, the app, and the market are all on the same side.

They are not. In the few milliseconds between the tap and the fill, the order was a *product* that got sold. Your broker handed it to a wholesale market maker, who paid the broker for the privilege of filling it, gave you a fraction of a cent of price improvement to stay inside the rules, and kept the rest of the spread as profit. The exchange you *didn't* trade on was meanwhile paying rebates to other firms to post quotes, and charging fees to anyone who crossed the spread to hit them. Every leg of that — the rebate, the payment, the price improvement — is a player in a game making a move, and the prices you see are the equilibrium those moves settle into.

This post builds the economics of *who pays whom* in modern markets from zero. We will define maker-taker fees, payment for order flow, and the duty of best execution; we will draw the flows of money explicitly; and we will model the conflict of interest as a game — because that is exactly what it is. The spine of this whole series applies here with full force: **a trade is a strategic interaction, not a bet against nature.** Every fraction of a cent you save is a fraction of a cent a specific party chose not to keep, and your edge as a participant is knowing which game you are in and who is reasoning one level deeper than you.

## Foundations: rebates, payment, and the duty you are owed

Before any game, define the board and the players. Five terms carry this whole post, so we build each from nothing.

**A maker and a taker.** Every trade has two sides, but in a modern electronic market the two sides arrive *differently*. One side got there *first* and is sitting in the order book as a resting limit order — a standing offer to buy at \$49.98 or sell at \$50.02, waiting. That side is the **maker**: it *makes* liquidity, it puts a price on the screen that others can trade against. The other side arrives later, sees the resting quote, and crosses the spread to trade against it *immediately* with a marketable order. That side is the **taker**: it *takes* liquidity off the book. The same trader can be a maker on one order and a taker on the next; the labels describe the order, not the person.

**The spread.** The gap between the best resting bid (\$49.98) and the best resting ask (\$50.02) is the **bid-ask spread** — here \$0.04, or four cents. It is the price of immediacy. If you want to buy *right now*, you pay the ask; if you are willing to wait, you post a bid and hope someone sells to you. The spread is the maker's compensation for two things: the service of standing ready to trade, and the risk that whoever takes their quote knows something they don't. (That second risk — adverse selection — is the engine of the whole PFOF story, and we cross-link the dedicated treatment below.)

**Maker-taker fees.** Here is where the exchange inserts itself as a player. To attract makers — because a market with deep, tight quotes is more valuable and draws more order flow — most US equity exchanges *pay you a rebate to be a maker* and *charge you a fee to be a taker*. A typical schedule pays the maker about **30 mils** and charges the taker about **30 mils**, where one *mil* is \$0.0001 per share, so 30 mils is \$0.0030 per share. Post a resting order that gets filled, and the exchange pays you \$0.0030 for every share. Cross the spread to take liquidity, and the exchange charges you \$0.0030 per share. The exchange keeps the small difference (and other fees) as its cut.

![Maker-taker fee schedule showing the rebate paid to makers and the fee charged to takers in mils per share](/imgs/blogs/maker-taker-payment-for-order-flow-and-the-routing-game-2.png)

The chart shows the four cells of the fee game. A standard maker-taker venue (Nasdaq, NYSE Arca) pays the maker +30 mils and charges the taker −30 mils. An **inverted** venue — also called **taker-maker** (Nasdaq BX, Cboe EDGA) — *flips the sign*: it pays the *taker* a rebate and charges the *maker* a fee. Why would a venue do that? To attract aggressive flow. If you are a taker and one venue charges you 30 mils while another pays you a rebate, you route your taking order to the inverted venue, all else equal. The existence of both kinds of venue is the first sign that fees are not a neutral plumbing detail — they are a competitive weapon, and every participant's router is tuned to them.

**Payment for order flow (PFOF).** Now the second player. A retail broker — Robinhood, Webull, much of Schwab and E\*TRADE's marketable retail flow — does not usually send your order to a public exchange at all. It sends it to a **wholesaler**, an off-exchange market maker like **Citadel Securities** or **Virtu**, who agrees to fill the order. And critically, the wholesaler *pays the broker* for the right to do so. That payment is **payment for order flow**: a per-share (or per-dollar-of-spread) fee the wholesaler hands the broker in exchange for the steady stream of retail orders. A representative figure is on the order of **\$0.0020 per share** for equities, though it varies a lot by symbol, spread, and contract; in options it is much larger per contract. PFOF is how "commission-free" trading is paid for. The customer pays no commission; the wholesaler pays the broker instead.

**Best execution.** The fifth term is the legal counterweight. A broker that handles your order owes you a **duty of best execution** — a regulatory obligation to seek the most favorable terms *reasonably available* for your order. In the US this lives in FINRA Rule 5310 and the SEC's oversight; in Europe, in MiFID II. "Best execution" does not mean *the* single best price on every trade — it means a *regular and rigorous* process to get favorable execution across price, speed, and likelihood of fill. That word *reasonably* is where all the slack lives, and the routing game is played inside it.

Put those together and the structure is clear. The exchange wants flow and uses rebates to get it. The wholesaler wants benign retail flow and pays for it. The broker sits in the middle, owes you best-ex, and earns its money from rebates and PFOF. You are the order. The game is: where does the broker send you, and who keeps what.

#### Worked example: the four cents on a 100-share trade

Stock quoted \$49.98 bid / \$50.02 ask, so the spread is \$0.04 and the **midpoint** is \$50.00. You send a marketable order to buy 100 shares. The three ways your broker could handle it, and the dollars on each:

- **Route 1 — cross the lit exchange as a taker.** You buy at the \$50.02 ask. Versus the \$50.00 mid, you paid the half-spread: \$0.02/share × 100 = **\$2.00**. On top, the broker is charged the taker fee of \$0.0030/share × 100 = \$0.30 by the exchange. You got the displayed price and no improvement.
- **Route 2 — post a resting bid and become a maker.** You join the bid at \$49.98 and *wait*. If you get filled, you bought \$0.02 below the mid — a \$2.00 *saving* versus the ask — and the exchange pays the rebate of \$0.30. But you bear *timing risk*: the price can run away while you wait, and you might never get filled.
- **Route 3 — send to a wholesaler with PFOF.** The wholesaler fills you at, say, \$50.005 — a hair *better* than the ask. That \$0.015/share of **price improvement** versus the ask is \$1.50 on the order... wait, let's be precise: improvement is measured against the quote you'd otherwise pay. We'll do the careful split in a moment. The wholesaler also pays your broker the PFOF.

The intuition: the four-cent spread is a pie. Crossing the lit book, you eat the whole half-spread as a cost. Posting, you *earn* the half-spread but risk not getting filled. Going to a wholesaler, the half-spread gets *split* — a slice to you as improvement, a slice to the broker as PFOF, and the rest kept by the wholesaler. Who decides the slicing? Not you.

## Maker-taker: the rebate turns posting into a queue game

Start with the exchange's game in isolation, because it is the cleaner of the two and it sets up everything that follows.

The rebate sounds like free money. Post a quote, get filled, collect \$0.0030/share. A firm trading a billion shares a month at 30 mils collects \$3 million a month in rebates *on top of* whatever it earns on the spread. So of course firms compete to be the maker. But here is the catch that turns it into a game: at any given price level, the exchange fills resting orders in **time priority** — first in, first out. The order that posted *earliest* at \$49.98 gets filled *first* when a seller arrives. So to collect the rebate reliably, you don't just want to post — you want to be at the *front of the queue*.

This is the **queue-for-the-rebate** game. Every maker wants the front of the line, because the front of the line is where the fills (and the rebates) actually land. But everyone reasoning that way leads to a crowd: makers post earlier, cancel and re-post to keep their place, jump to a new price the instant the inside moves, and generally fight for queue position. The rebate, which looked like a gift, gets *competed away* into the cost of winning the queue race. This is the central lesson of every rebate scheme: a subsidy offered to everyone is captured by whoever competes hardest for it, and in equilibrium the marginal participant earns close to zero net of the competition.

![Queue-for-the-rebate game where posting dominates and both makers crowd the inside, competing the rebate thin](/imgs/blogs/maker-taker-payment-for-order-flow-and-the-routing-game-6.png)

The chart models this as a two-maker game, solved with the series' `nash_2x2` helper. Two market makers each choose to **POST** (join the queue at the inside, fighting for the rebate) or **TAKE** (cross and pay the fee). Posting yields a higher payoff than taking against any choice the opponent makes — so "both POST" is the Nash equilibrium. And that is precisely the problem: the equilibrium is *everyone crowding the queue*, which is exactly what we observe in real rebate-bearing names, where the inside of the book is a churning wall of cancel-replace orders all racing for position.

#### Worked example: when is taking the rebate venue worth the queue risk?

You are a small systematic maker. You can post at the maker venue and *hope* for a fill that pays you the spread plus a 30-mil rebate, or you can cross at a wholesaler / lit book and pay up. Suppose at the inside, your probability of actually getting filled before the price moves is only **p = 40%** because the queue ahead of you is long. If filled, you earn the half-spread of \$0.02/share *plus* the rebate \$0.0030/share = **\$0.0230/share**. If *not* filled (60% of the time), the price has moved a tick against you and you must chase, costing you roughly \$0.01/share.

Expected value of posting per share: $EV = 0.40 \times (+\\$0.0230) + 0.60 \times (-\\$0.0100) = +\\$0.0092 - \\$0.0060 = +\\$0.0032$.

So posting is worth +\$0.0032/share — positive, but thin. Now suppose the queue gets more crowded and your fill probability drops to **p = 25%**: $EV = 0.25 \times 0.0230 + 0.75 \times (-0.0100) = 0.00575 - 0.0075 = -\\$0.0018$ — now *negative*. The intuition: the rebate is only worth chasing while your fill probability stays high enough; once the queue crowds past a threshold, the timing risk swamps the rebate, and the "free" 30 mils becomes a money-loser. The crowd self-limits — but only after the rebate is competed to nearly nothing.

### Inverted venues and rebate arbitrage

The existence of *both* maker-taker and inverted (taker-maker) venues opens a sliver of arbitrage that sophisticated firms harvest. Consider a firm that wants to be flat — buy and sell the same shares — but wants to do it *collecting* rebates on both legs. On a maker-taker venue it earns the rebate by posting (making); on an inverted venue it earns the rebate by *taking*. By carefully choosing which venue to post on and which to hit, a firm can structure trades so that it collects a rebate on the side it would otherwise pay a fee on. This is **rebate arbitrage**, and while the per-share amounts are tiny, at scale and at high turnover they are a real revenue line for the firms built to capture them.

The deeper point for *you* as a trader is not that you should run rebate arbitrage — you almost certainly can't compete with the firms colocated at the exchange. The point is that the fee schedule is *not symmetric* and *not neutral*: it actively shapes where liquidity sits and how aggressively it is quoted. When you see a deceptively tight, deep quote in a high-rebate name, part of what you are looking at is the rebate *manufacturing* liquidity that might evaporate the instant a real seller arrives, because much of that depth is queue-jockeying for the rebate rather than genuine willingness to trade.

#### Worked example: the rebate inside a "free" maker fill

You post to sell 500 shares at \$50.02 on a 30-mil maker venue and get filled. Two pieces of money arrive. First, the spread capture: if the true mid was \$50.00, you sold \$0.02 above mid, earning \$0.02 × 500 = **\$10.00** of half-spread. Second, the rebate: \$0.0030 × 500 = **\$1.50**. Total: **\$11.50** on the fill.

Now compare to crossing as a taker to sell the same 500 shares at the \$49.98 bid: you'd give up the half-spread (−\$10.00 vs mid) *and* pay the taker fee of \$0.0030 × 500 = \$1.50, a swing of **\$23.00** between making and taking on this single fill. The intuition: the difference between being the maker and the taker on the *same* trade is two half-spreads plus two fees — which is exactly why everyone wants to be the maker, and exactly why the queue is crowded. The rebate is the smaller of the two pieces, but it is the piece the exchange controls to tilt the game.

## Payment for order flow: why benign flow is worth paying for

Now the harder, more contested player. PFOF only makes sense once you understand *why retail flow is special* — and that requires the central concept of this whole series: **adverse selection.**

When a market maker posts a quote, the scary scenario is that whoever takes it *knows something*. If a hedge fund with a model that says the stock is about to jump buys all the maker's offered shares, the maker just sold low to someone who will be proven right in minutes — the maker is **adversely selected**, picked off by the informed side. To survive, makers must widen their spread to cover the average loss to informed traders. This is the Glosten-Milgrom logic, treated in depth in the sibling post on the spread as an adverse-selection game. Toxic flow — informed, sharp, anti-correlated with the maker's profit — is *expensive* to trade against.

Retail flow is the opposite. When a person taps Buy on a brokerage app because they got a bonus, or read about a company, or are rebalancing their 401(k), that order carries **no short-term price information**. The retail buyer is not predicting the next tick; they are expressing a preference or a need. Statistically, the price after a retail fill is just as likely to go up as down — the flow is **benign**, **uninformed**, **non-toxic**. And benign flow is *enormously valuable* to a market maker, because you can fill it inside the spread and almost always *not* get picked off. The wholesaler captures most of the spread as near-pure profit, with little adverse-selection cost to net against it.

![Why anonymous exchange flow carries adverse selection risk while segregated retail flow is benign and safe to internalize](/imgs/blogs/maker-taker-payment-for-order-flow-and-the-routing-game-3.png)

The diagram contrasts the two flows. On an anonymous exchange, the counterparty hitting your quote *could* be an informed sharp; the price often moves against you afterward; you carry adverse-selection cost and must widen; your expected edge per fill is thin or negative. With **segregated** retail flow — flow you *know* came from retail brokers, kept separate from the anonymous pool — the counterparty is rebalancing, not predicting; the price rarely moves against you; adverse selection is near zero; and your expected edge per fill is reliably positive. That gap is the whole reason PFOF exists. The wholesaler is willing to *pay* the broker to get the benign flow, because filling benign flow is a profitable business and filling anonymous flow is a dangerous one.

This is also why wholesalers want the flow **segregated** — separated from the toxic anonymous pool. The value of retail flow is precisely that it is *not* mixed with the informed flow. A wholesaler who internalizes a pure stream of retail orders can quote tightly and confidently. The moment that stream gets contaminated with informed flow, its value collapses, which is why the entire industry is built around identifying and ring-fencing genuinely retail orders.

#### Worked example: the wholesaler's expected profit on benign vs toxic flow

A wholesaler fills 1,000 shares at the \$50.00 mid on a stock with a \$0.04 spread. Suppose the *true* fair value is the mid, \$50.00, and the wholesaler captures, on average, \$0.01/share of effective spread per fill — call it the gross edge.

- **Benign retail flow:** the price after the fill is a coin flip — equally likely up or down — so the adverse-selection cost is ≈ \$0.00/share. Expected profit = \$0.01 × 1,000 = **+\$10.00**. The wholesaler keeps essentially all of it.
- **Toxic informed flow:** suppose 30% of the takers are informed and, when they trade, the price moves \$0.05 against the wholesaler on average. Expected adverse-selection cost = 0.30 × \$0.05 = \$0.015/share. Now expected profit = (\$0.01 − \$0.015) × 1,000 = **−\$5.00** — a *loss*.

The intuition: the *same* gross spread is wildly profitable on benign flow and a loser on toxic flow, and the entire difference is adverse selection. A wholesaler can comfortably pay the broker \$0.0020/share PFOF out of that \$10.00 benign profit and still keep \$8.00; it could never pay anything on the toxic flow. PFOF is the wholesaler *sharing* the benign-flow surplus with the broker who delivers it.

### The flows of money, explicitly

Let us nail the cash flows so there is no hand-waving. Take the 100-share buy at a stock quoted \$49.98 / \$50.02, mid \$50.00, spread \$0.04. The wholesaler internalizes it. Here is who pays whom, using representative numbers:

- **You pay** the execution price. Suppose the wholesaler fills you at **\$50.005** — that is \$0.015 *better* than the \$50.02 displayed ask. So your **price improvement** is \$0.015/share × 100 = **\$1.50**... let's be careful with which benchmark — see the figure below; improvement is conventionally measured against the National Best Bid and Offer (NBBO) quote you would otherwise have paid.
- **The wholesaler pays your broker** the PFOF: ~\$0.0020/share × 100 = **\$0.20**.
- **The wholesaler keeps** the rest of the captured spread as profit: the gap between what it filled you at and where it can lay off or value the position, minus the improvement it gave you and the PFOF it paid.

Every one of those is a real transfer, and they all come out of the same spread you *didn't* pay by crossing the lit book. The pie is the half-spread; the slicing is the game.

![Decomposition of a three-cent spread on a 100-share order into price improvement to the customer, PFOF to the broker, and net spread kept by the wholesaler](/imgs/blogs/maker-taker-payment-for-order-flow-and-the-routing-game-4.png)

The chart decomposes a stylized 3-cent spread on a 100-share order. Half the quoted spread — what crossing the lit book would have cost you — is \$1.50 (the red dashed line). Of that, the customer gets **\$0.15** of price improvement, the broker gets **\$0.20** of PFOF, and the wholesaler keeps the remaining **\$1.15** of net spread. Read it and one thing jumps out: the customer's slice is the *smallest* of the three. You got something — \$0.15 you wouldn't have gotten crossing the lit book — but the broker and the wholesaler split the larger pieces. "Price improvement" is real, and it is also the minimum the wholesaler must give up to keep the flow and stay onside with best-ex.

#### Worked example: is "price improvement" actually a good deal?

Robinhood and Citadel both, correctly, point out that retail orders routinely get filled *better* than the displayed quote. Is that the whole story? Take our 100-share order. You got \$0.15 of improvement versus the \$50.02 ask. Good — that is \$0.15 in your pocket.

But ask the counterfactual: what is the *most* you could have captured? If you had posted a limit order at the mid (\$50.00) and gotten filled, you'd have saved the full half-spread of \$2.00 versus the ask — but you'd have borne the risk of not filling. The wholesaler captured \$1.15 of that \$2.00 of potential. So the honest framing is: price improvement is genuinely better than the naive alternative of crossing the lit book and paying the full ask (you save \$0.15), but it is *far* less than the wholesaler keeps (\$1.15), and the *reason* you can't easily capture more is that you handed away the choice of where and how to execute. The intuition: improvement proves the wholesaler isn't robbing you blind, but it does *not* prove you got the best possible price — those are different claims, and the gap between them is the wholesaler's business model.

## The routing game: best execution versus the broker's take

Now we assemble the players into the game that actually matters: the **routing decision.** Your order arrives at the broker. The broker's smart-order-router (SOR) chooses where to send it: a lit exchange, a maker-rebate venue, an inverted venue, or a wholesaler. The broker owes *you* best execution. The broker earns *its* money from rebates and PFOF. When those two pull in the same direction, no problem. When they pull apart — and they often do — you have a **conflict of interest**, and the equilibrium of that conflict decides your fill.

Model it as a game. The broker is the player choosing the route. The payoffs split between two parties whose interests are *not* aligned: the **customer** (you), who wants the best price — maximum price improvement, minimum cost — and the **broker**, who wants maximum revenue from the route — rebate captured or PFOF collected. The best-execution rule is a *constraint* on the broker's choice, not the broker's objective.

![Routing conflict matrix comparing customer payoff and broker payoff across routing to a lit exchange, a maker rebate venue, and a PFOF wholesaler](/imgs/blogs/maker-taker-payment-for-order-flow-and-the-routing-game-5.png)

The matrix lays out three routing choices against the two payoffs. Route to the **lit exchange as a taker**: you pay the spread with no improvement (customer: −\$0.30 relative to mid on our scale) and the broker *pays* a taker fee (broker: −\$0.30) — bad for both, so nobody chooses it for marketable retail flow. Route to a **maker-rebate venue** by posting: you risk a worse price waiting in queue (customer: −\$0.10) but the broker *captures the rebate* (broker: +\$0.30) — good for the broker, mediocre for you. Route to a **PFOF wholesaler**: you get improvement (customer: +\$0.15) and the broker *collects PFOF* (broker: +\$0.20) — good for both, which is why it is the equilibrium for retail marketable flow. The wholesaler cell wins because it is the rare one where the broker's revenue and your improvement *both* go up. But notice: in the maker-rebate cell, the broker's incentive (+\$0.30) is *highest* even though your payoff is *worst* of the positive options. That cell is where the conflict bites — and where a broker that is optimizing its own revenue, with best-ex only as a loose floor, might route you anyway.

### Best execution is a floor, not the best price

Here is the subtle, crucial point that the whole regulatory tension turns on. Best execution does *not* require the broker to get you *the* best price available across all venues on every order. It requires a *reasonable, regular, and rigorous* process. As long as the broker can show it has a process, reviews execution quality periodically, and gets you fills that are *at or better than* the NBBO, it can satisfy best-ex *while still* routing to whichever venue pays it the most among the acceptable choices.

That is the loophole the routing game is played in. The NBBO is a *floor*: a wholesaler must fill you at least as well as the best displayed quote. But the floor leaves room. If the true achievable price was \$50.005 of improvement but the wholesaler gives you only \$0.001 of improvement (still technically "better than NBBO"), it has met the floor while keeping more for itself — and the broker, paid by that same wholesaler, has little incentive to push for more. Best-ex stops the broker from *robbing* you; it does not force the broker to *fight* for you against its own revenue.

#### Worked example: the broker's revenue-maximizing route vs your best price

Your broker can send your 100-share order to one of two acceptable venues, both of which beat the NBBO so both satisfy best-ex:

- **Venue A (wholesaler X):** gives you \$0.012/share improvement = \$1.20 to you, pays the broker \$0.0025/share PFOF = \$0.25 to the broker.
- **Venue B (wholesaler Y):** gives you \$0.018/share improvement = \$1.80 to you, pays the broker \$0.0010/share PFOF = \$0.10 to the broker.

Which does a revenue-maximizing broker choose? Venue A: it pays the broker \$0.25 versus \$0.10, an extra **\$0.15** to the broker. But Venue B would have given *you* \$1.80 versus \$1.20, an extra **\$0.60** to you. So the broker's choice costs you \$0.60 to gain itself \$0.15. Both fills "beat the NBBO," so both are defensible under a loose reading of best-ex. The intuition: when the broker's payment and your improvement are *negatively correlated across venues* — the venue that pays the broker more gives you less — best-ex as a mere floor lets the broker pick the venue that is worse for you. This is the exact mechanism the SEC has scrutinized, and the exact reason the routing decision is a genuine conflict and not a technicality.

### How the router actually decides

Put the router's logic on the table as a flow. An order arrives. The router first asks: is it **marketable** (crosses the spread now) or **resting** (rests as a quote)? Marketable retail flow tends to go to a wholesaler — that is where the PFOF and the benign-flow economics live. Resting flow can be posted on a maker-rebate venue to earn the rebate. Larger or sweep orders may cross lit exchanges. After the fill, a best-ex check (in principle) asks whether this was a reasonable price, and the broker files its routing disclosures.

![Order-routing decision flow where a smart order router classifies the order then chooses among a wholesaler, a rebate venue, or a lit exchange](/imgs/blogs/maker-taker-payment-for-order-flow-and-the-routing-game-7.png)

The figure traces it. The fork that matters is the second node: *marketable* flow routes to the wholesaler (PFOF + improvement, the benign-flow path); *resting* flow posts on a rebate venue (queue for the rebate, the maker path); large or sweeping orders cross the lit book (pay the taker fee for the best displayed price). The best-ex check sits *after* the route, as a review — which tells you something about its force. It is a constraint applied at the level of *process and periodic review*, not a per-order optimizer that re-routes you to the genuinely best venue every time. The router's *default* tilt is set by what pays the broker.

The two disclosure regimes are worth naming because they are the trader's window into this. **SEC Rule 606** requires brokers to disclose, quarterly, where they route customer orders and what PFOF they received — this is how the public knows Robinhood's PFOF revenue at all. **SEC Rule 605** requires market centers to publish execution-quality statistics (effective spread, price improvement, speed). These reports are the only public scoreboard for the routing game, and they are exactly where a careful trader looks to see who is actually getting good fills.

### The speed dimension: why the queue race is also a latency race

There is a layer underneath the queue game that makes it harsher than the simple model suggests: **speed**. Time priority means the order that posts *earliest* gets filled first — but "earliest" in a modern market is measured in microseconds. When the inside price moves, every maker wants to cancel its now-stale quote and re-post at the new price *before* the others, to claim the front of the new queue. The firm with the lowest latency — colocated in the exchange's data center, with the fastest network cards and the most optimized code — wins that race repeatedly, which means it collects a disproportionate share of the rebates and the good fills.

This is why the queue game is not really a game among equals. It is a game where one class of player (the latency-arbitrage firms) has a structural head start, and another class (you, with a retail app and a few milliseconds of round-trip) is always at the back. When you post a resting limit order as a retail trader hoping to "be the maker and collect the rebate," you are joining a queue whose front is permanently occupied by firms you cannot out-run. Your order fills *last* at any given price — which means it fills mostly when the price is *about to move against you* (because the informed, fast flow has already taken the good fills and left you holding the stale quote). This is adverse selection wearing a different hat: the slow maker is selected against by the fast taker.

The strategic takeaway connects directly to the order-types post cross-linked below: a retail limit order's value is in setting a *price ceiling or floor*, not in *winning the queue for a rebate*. Use limit orders to control your worst-case price, never with the expectation of harvesting the maker rebate — that rebate is already spoken for by firms operating thousands of times faster than you.

#### Worked example: why being last in the queue is a losing bet

You post a resting bid to buy 100 shares at \$49.98, joining a queue. Suppose there are 5,000 shares ahead of you, and the typical seller arrives in clips of about 200 shares. You will only get filled after 25 such sellers have arrived — and here is the trap: a long string of sellers arriving is *exactly* the situation where the price is drifting *down*. By the time the queue clears to you, the fair value has often dropped from \$50.00 to \$49.95. You buy at \$49.98, which *looked* like a \$0.02 discount to the old mid but is now \$0.03 *above* the new fair value of \$49.95 — an immediate \$0.03 × 100 = **\$3.00** mark-to-market loss, against the \$0.30 rebate you collected. Net: **−\$2.70**.

The intuition: being filled at the back of the queue is correlated with being filled at the *wrong* time, because the queue only clears to you when there is a wave of flow pushing the price your way. The rebate is real but small; the adverse selection from filling last is larger. Fast firms avoid this by being at the *front*, where they get filled before the price moves; you cannot, so the rebate is a trap, not a gift.

### Options PFOF: the same game, much larger stakes

Everything above used equities for concreteness, but the economically *largest* PFOF flows are in **options**, and the game there is even more lopsided in the wholesaler's favor. Options spreads are wide — a contract might be quoted \$1.00 bid / \$1.10 ask, a \$0.10 spread, which on a 100-share-equivalent contract is \$10.00 of spread per contract. Retail options flow is also exceptionally benign: a retail trader buying a weekly call lottery ticket carries no short-term edge against a professional options market maker. So the wholesaler's surplus per contract is large, and the PFOF it pays the broker is correspondingly large — often quoted at \$0.30 to \$0.60+ *per contract*, versus the fractions of a cent per share in equities.

This is why, at the peak of the retail boom, options PFOF dwarfed equity PFOF in brokers' revenue mix even though options were a smaller fraction of *trades*. A single options contract can pay the broker more PFOF than a thousand-share equity order. And the conflict is sharper precisely because the spreads are wider: with a \$0.10 options spread, the difference between a good fill and a mediocre one is large in dollar terms, and the wholesaler has far more room to give you a token improvement while keeping most of the spread.

![Decomposition of a three-cent spread on a 100-share order into price improvement to the customer, PFOF to the broker, and net spread kept by the wholesaler](/imgs/blogs/maker-taker-payment-for-order-flow-and-the-routing-game-4.png)

The same spread-decomposition breakdown applies — only the absolute numbers scale up. In equities the pie is the half-cent-to-a-few-cents spread; in options it is dimes per contract. The *shape* of the split is identical: you get a slice of improvement, the broker gets the PFOF, and the wholesaler keeps the residual. But because the options pie is so much bigger, the *dollars* of conflict are bigger, which is exactly why the SEC's scrutiny and the academic execution-quality studies focus heavily on options. If you trade options through a PFOF broker, the routing game is not a rounding error — it is a material cost on every contract.

#### Worked example: the options contract that pays the broker more than your improvement

You buy one call quoted \$1.00 / \$1.10, mid \$1.05. The wholesaler fills you at \$1.095 — \$0.005 better than the \$1.10 ask, so your price improvement is \$0.005 × 100 = **\$0.50** on the contract. The wholesaler pays your broker PFOF of, say, \$0.45 per contract. And the wholesaler captures the rest of the half-spread: the mid is \$1.05, it filled you at \$1.095, so it sold \$0.045 above mid = \$0.045 × 100 = **\$4.50** of gross spread, out of which it gave you \$0.50 and the broker \$0.45, keeping about **\$3.55**.

Compare the slices: you got \$0.50, the broker got \$0.45 — *almost as much as you* — and the wholesaler kept \$3.55, **seven times your improvement**. The intuition: in options the pie is so large that "price improvement" can be a genuinely real \$0.50 in your pocket while *still* being a small fraction of what the wholesaler keeps. The marketing ("you got price improvement!") is true and beside the point; the routing conflict is largest exactly where the spreads are widest.

## Common misconceptions

**"Commission-free means free."** No — it means *you* pay no commission. The cost moved, it did not vanish. The broker is paid by the wholesaler (PFOF) or earns rebates; the wholesaler is paid out of the spread you don't see; and the slice you keep (price improvement) is the smallest piece of the pie, as the spread-decomposition chart showed. Free at the cash register is not free in the spread. A useful reframe: if you are not paying for the product, in this market you *are* the product — your order flow is what is being sold.

**"Price improvement proves I got the best price."** No — it proves you got a price *better than the displayed NBBO*, which is a *floor*, not the *best achievable*. As the venue-choice example showed, two venues can both beat the NBBO while one gives you three times the improvement of the other. Improvement disproves "the wholesaler robbed me"; it does not prove "I got the best execution available." Those are different claims, and the marketing conflates them on purpose.

**"PFOF is just a kickback and should obviously be banned."** It is more complicated. PFOF exists because retail flow is *genuinely* cheap to fill (benign, low adverse selection), and *some* of that surplus does get passed back to you as improvement and as zero commissions. The honest critique is not "PFOF is theft" but "PFOF creates a conflict in *routing* — the broker is paid by the venue, so its router is tilted toward its own revenue rather than your best price." Several jurisdictions reason differently: the UK and Canada effectively ban PFOF; the EU is phasing it out; the US, after a long review, chose disclosure and execution-quality rules over an outright ban. Reasonable regulators disagree because the trade-off — cheaper-headline trading versus a cleaner routing incentive — is real.

**"The rebate is free money for the maker."** No — it is competed away. As the queue game showed, a subsidy offered to all makers gets captured by whoever wins the time-priority race, and in equilibrium the marginal maker earns close to zero net of the cost of fighting for the queue. The rebate is real revenue for the firms *built* to capture it (colocated, fast, high-turnover) and a mirage for everyone else. If you post a resting order as a retail trader thinking you'll "collect the rebate," you are usually at the back of a queue you cannot win.

**"Best execution guarantees the best price."** No — it guarantees a *reasonable process*, reviewed *regularly and rigorously*, that gets you fills at or better than the NBBO. The word *reasonably* carries the load. Best-ex is the floor that stops abuse; it is not a per-order optimizer that hunts down the single best price on every trade. The gap between "met best-ex" and "best price achievable" is exactly the room the routing game lives in.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Robinhood and the rise of PFOF (2013–2021).** Robinhood built its entire commission-free model on PFOF. By 2020–2021, the bulk of its revenue came from "transaction-based revenues" — overwhelmingly PFOF paid by wholesalers, with Citadel Securities its largest counterparty. In Q1 2021, at the peak of the retail trading boom, Robinhood reported on the order of hundreds of millions of dollars in PFOF in a single quarter, with options flow especially lucrative (PFOF per options contract dwarfs the per-share equity figure). The lesson the public learned: "free" trading was funded by selling order flow, and the volume of that flow — and thus the payments — was enormous. (The strategic-interaction framing is the same one in the series post on who is on the other side of your trade — the wholesaler is *always* on the other side of retail flow, by design.)

**The December 2020 SEC settlement.** In December 2020, the SEC charged Robinhood with misleading customers about its largest revenue source and with failing best execution — specifically, that its PFOF arrangements led to *inferior* execution prices that, the SEC alleged, cost customers tens of millions of dollars in worse prices even after accounting for the savings from zero commissions. Robinhood paid a **\$65 million** civil penalty to settle (without admitting or denying the findings). The case is the canonical real-world instance of the routing conflict biting: the regulator's theory was exactly the venue-choice example above — that the broker's PFOF incentive led it to routes that were worse for customers than what was reasonably available, while still nominally "beating the NBBO." It is the single best evidence that this game is not hypothetical.

**Citadel Securities as the wholesaler.** Citadel Securities is the largest US equity wholesaler, internalizing a very large share — frequently cited around a quarter or more — of all US retail equity volume, plus a dominant share of retail options. Its business *is* the benign-flow economics: pay brokers for segregated retail flow, fill it inside the spread at scale, and capture the residual. It is consistently profitable precisely because, as the benign-vs-toxic worked example showed, filling uninformed flow at scale is a high-Sharpe business when you are fast and large enough to lay off inventory. Understanding Citadel Securities' position is understanding the wholesaler node of the cover diagram: it sits at the receiving end of nearly every retail "Buy" tapped in America.

**The SEC's 2022–2023 market-structure proposals.** Under Chair Gary Gensler, the SEC proposed the most significant equity market-structure changes in nearly two decades, aimed squarely at this game. The proposals included **order-by-order competition** for retail marketable orders (forcing retail orders into auctions where venues bid to give the best price, rather than being routed to a single wholesaler), tighter **best-execution** rules (Reg Best Ex), and changes to **tick sizes and access fees** (capping the maker-taker fee/rebate). The industry pushed back hard — wholesalers argued retail execution quality is already excellent and auctions could *worsen* it; exchanges defended rebates. The fight is, in effect, an argument about *which equilibrium of the routing game* serves retail best: the current internalize-with-improvement model, or a forced-competition model. As of this writing the rules remain contested and partly scaled back, which is itself a data point: the incumbents like the current equilibrium, and they are powerful players.

**The meme-stock episode (January 2021).** When GameStop and others spiked, the surge of retail flow ran straight through this plumbing — Robinhood to wholesalers, with the resulting volume and volatility stressing the clearing and capital side (the famous deposit calls that led Robinhood to restrict buying). For our purposes the relevant lesson is volume: the episode made visible *just how much* retail order flow exists and how valuable it is, which is why PFOF revenue spiked and why the whole arrangement drew political and regulatory attention immediately afterward. The flow-is-the-product framing went from market-structure trivia to a congressional hearing in a matter of weeks.

**The off-exchange share keeps climbing.** A structural fact underneath all of this: the fraction of US equity volume that trades *off* the lit exchanges — internalized by wholesalers or crossed in dark pools — has risen for years and now regularly runs around *half* of total volume. Every share internalized by a wholesaler is a share that does *not* post a quote on the lit book, which means the publicly displayed quotes are built on a *thinner* slice of the real flow than they used to be. Critics argue this hollows out price discovery: if the most benign flow is siphoned off to be filled privately, the lit book is left with a more adverse-selected, harder-to-quote remainder, and spreads on the lit book may be *wider* than they would be if all flow competed in the open. Defenders counter that retail gets demonstrably better fills off-exchange. Both can be partly true — and the disagreement is, once again, an argument about which equilibrium of the routing game is best, this time at the level of the whole market's price-discovery process rather than a single fill.

**The academic execution-quality debate.** A genuine empirical literature studies whether retail orders get *good* execution under PFOF, using the Rule 605/606 data and proprietary samples. The honest summary is that retail price improvement is *real and measurable* — most marketable retail orders do fill at or inside the NBBO — *and* that the improvement is often *less* than the maximum reasonably achievable, with meaningful variation across brokers and wholesalers. In other words, both sides of the public argument cite true facts: PFOF defenders point to the real improvement, critics point to the gap between that improvement and the best-achievable price. The data does not settle the policy question because the policy question is normative — how much of the benign-flow surplus *should* flow back to the retail customer versus to the broker and wholesaler? That is a distribution question, and distribution questions are decided by regulators and competition, not by a regression.

## The playbook / How to play it

You are not going to out-route Citadel Securities. But understanding this game changes how you trade, where your costs really are, and what to ignore.

**Who is on the other side.** For your marketable retail orders, the other side is almost always a **wholesaler** internalizing your benign flow for a profit. You are not trading against "the market" — you are selling immediacy to a firm whose entire edge is that your order carries no information. That is *fine* for a long-term investor; it is a real, measurable cost for a high-frequency retail trader. Know which you are.

**The game you are in.** You are the *product* in a routing game between your broker (paid by rebates and PFOF) and venues bidding for your flow. Best-ex is the floor that protects you from outright bad fills; it does not fight for your best price. The broker's router is tuned to the broker's revenue, with your improvement as a constraint, not the objective.

**Your edge — where the costs actually are.**
- **For long-term investors:** the routing game is a *rounding error*. On a buy-and-hold position, a fraction of a cent of suboptimal improvement is dwarfed by everything else. Don't obsess over PFOF; the bigger costs are taxes, fees on funds, and behavioral mistakes. The honest takeaway is that for most investors, zero-commission-via-PFOF is a *good* deal — you save the old \$5–\$10 commission and lose pennies of improvement.
- **For active and high-frequency retail traders:** the costs are *real and cumulative*. If you trade hundreds of times a month, the half-cent of improvement you *don't* get, compounded over thousands of fills, is a meaningful drag. Here it pays to care which broker routes where.

**How to read the scoreboard.** The public data is your weapon. **Rule 606** reports tell you where your broker routes and how much PFOF it takes; **Rule 605** statistics tell you the *execution quality* (effective spread, price improvement, speed) at each market center. A serious active trader compares brokers on 605/606 data, not on app aesthetics. The broker with the most aggressive PFOF is not necessarily the one giving you the best fills — and the reports are the only way to tell.

The metric that cuts through the noise is the **effective-over-quoted-spread (E/Q) ratio** combined with **price improvement per share**. The effective spread measures what you *actually* paid relative to the mid at the moment your order arrived; the quoted spread is what was displayed. An E/Q ratio well below 1.0 means you consistently filled *inside* the quoted spread — good. A broker can advertise "price improvement on X% of orders" while still posting a mediocre E/Q ratio, because *some* improvement on *most* orders is a low bar. When you compare two brokers, line up their 605 statistics for the symbols you actually trade — large-cap, small-cap, options — and look at price improvement *in dollars per share* and the E/Q ratio, not the marketing percentage. The broker whose wholesaler relationships extract less of the spread for themselves shows up as higher dollar improvement and a lower E/Q ratio, and that difference, compounded over your turnover, is real money.

#### Worked example: comparing two brokers on a year of trading

You trade 200 round-trips a year, 200 shares each, so 40,000 shares bought and 40,000 sold = 80,000 shares of marketable flow. Broker A's 605 data shows average price improvement of \$0.0008/share on the names you trade; Broker B's shows \$0.0021/share. The difference is \$0.0013/share. Over 80,000 shares that is \$0.0013 × 80,000 = **\$104/year** that Broker B puts in your pocket that Broker A does not — for the *identical* trades. If you trade options, where improvement is dimes per contract, the same comparison can be hundreds of dollars a year.

The intuition: the per-fill difference is invisible — fractions of a cent — but it is *systematic*, applied to every share, so it compounds into a number you can actually feel over a year. Neither broker is "cheating" you; both beat the NBBO; both satisfy best-ex. The gap is simply how much of the benign-flow surplus each one's routing arrangement passes back to you, and the only way to see it is the 605 data. That is the whole reason the disclosure regime exists.

**How to reduce your exposure to the conflict.**
- **Use limit orders, not market orders**, when you can. A marketable limit order with a price cap removes the worst-case fills and gives the wholesaler less room. You control the floor.
- **Trade liquid, tight-spread names** where the spread pie is small and there is little for anyone to slice. The routing game barely matters on a penny-wide mega-cap; it matters a lot on a wide-spread small-cap.
- **Avoid trading in the first and last minutes** and around news, when spreads widen and the slice the wholesaler keeps grows.
- **For size, consider a broker or venue that offers direct market access** or routes to lit venues — you give up the PFOF-funded freebies but gain control of the route. This is a trade-off, not a free win.

**The invalidation / when this stops being true.** If the SEC's order-by-order-competition rules are adopted, the equilibrium changes: retail orders get auctioned, venues bid for them, and more of the spread flows back to you. If PFOF is banned (as in the UK), the model shifts to commissions or spread-based pricing — different costs, possibly higher headline fees but a cleaner routing incentive. Watch the regulatory state; the *current* equilibrium is a policy choice, not a law of nature.

**The sizing / exit.** There is no position to size here — the "trade" is choosing your broker and your order types. Size your *attention* to your turnover: a buy-and-hold investor should spend roughly zero minutes worrying about PFOF, while a trader doing thousands of round-trips a year should treat 605/606 execution quality as a first-class cost line, comparable to spreads and financing. The one rule, again: **on every fill, ask who paid whom and what they kept.** Once you can answer that, you are no longer the sucker who thinks the trade was free.
