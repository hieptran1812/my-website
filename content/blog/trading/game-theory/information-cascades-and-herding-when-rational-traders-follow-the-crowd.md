> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Herding in markets is usually not a crowd of fools panicking; it is a crowd of individually rational people each correctly deciding to ignore their own information and copy the people ahead of them. The moment two predecessors agree, your private signal is outvoted by the public record, so you copy too — and everyone after you inherits a consensus that contains almost none of their information.
>
> - **An information cascade** forms when it becomes rational to follow the observed actions of others *regardless* of your own private signal. In the classic urn model, this happens the instant the first two people guess the same way.
> - Because only the first two signals ever enter the public belief, a cascade **aggregates almost no information** no matter how large the crowd grows — a thousand "agreeing" traders can be standing on the evidence of exactly two.
> - That thinness makes cascades **fragile**: a single credible public signal can outweigh the whole frozen crowd and flip it, which is why consensus trades reverse so violently.
> - This is **rational herding**, distinct from panic: each person is doing the math correctly. The error is structural, not emotional — which is exactly why "everyone here is rational" is no protection.
> - **The one number to remember:** with a private signal that is right two-thirds of the time, a cascade that forms still locks onto the *wrong* answer about **20%** of the time — and that error is invisible from inside the crowd.

In the weeks before a small biotech reports a trial result, you watch the order flow. The first sell-side analyst to publish goes negative — a downgrade, a price target cut. The second analyst, who was quietly bullish, looks at the first note, looks at the stock already drifting down, and decides not to be the lone optimist on a name that just got hit; they go neutral. By the time the third and fourth analysts publish, the consensus is set, and not one of them wants to be the outlier defending a thesis the tape has already rejected. The "consensus" that ends up in everyone's model looks like the considered judgment of a dozen smart, independent people. It is nothing of the kind. It is the judgment of the first analyst, ratified by people who suppressed their own views because the crowd had already spoken.

This is one of the most unsettling results in all of game theory, and it has nothing to do with anyone being dumb, emotional, or panicked. It is a story about *rational* people — people doing the Bayesian arithmetic perfectly — who nonetheless converge on an answer that throws away almost everything they collectively know. The crowd is not failing to think; each member is thinking correctly and arriving, individually, at the conclusion that the smart move is to stop using their own information. That is the trap that no amount of individual intelligence escapes, because the flaw is in the *order* in which people act and what they can see when they act, not in their reasoning. The mechanism is called an **information cascade**, and once you see it, you stop trusting "everyone agrees" as evidence of anything. Agreement, it turns out, can be the cheapest signal in the market: it can be produced by a crowd of a thousand that is standing on the private knowledge of exactly two.

The diagram above is the mental model we are going to build from zero. Two people see their private information and act on it, so their guesses genuinely reveal what they saw. From the third person onward, the public record of those first two guesses outweighs any single new private signal — so everyone copies, and their own information is *dropped on the floor*, never entering the price. The crowd grows, the agreement looks more and more impressive, and the actual stock of information behind it stops growing the instant the cascade began.

## Foundations: the urn, herding, and Bayesian updating from zero

Before any markets, we need four ideas built carefully from nothing: what an **information cascade** is, what **herding** means, the **urn experiment** that produces a cascade in a controlled setting, and the **Bayesian updating** that makes the whole thing rational rather than foolish. Take these slowly — the entire post rests on them, and most popular accounts of "herd behavior" skip them and go straight to moralizing about sheep. We will not. A cascade is a *structure*, and the structure is the point.

### What "private signal" and "public action" mean

Two kinds of information float around any market. The first is your **private signal**: something only you know, or that you worked out yourself — your read of a balance sheet, your channel checks, a number you computed, a hunch from twenty years on a desk. A *signal* here just means a piece of evidence that points, imperfectly, toward the truth. "Imperfectly" is the key word: your signal is right more often than a coin flip, but not always. We will say your signal has **accuracy** $p$ — the probability it points the right way. Throughout this post we use $p = 2/3$, meaning your private read is correct two times out of three. That is a genuinely useful signal; an analyst who is right two-thirds of the time is very good.

The second kind is the **public action**: what other people actually *do* — the trades they place, the ratings they publish, the guesses they announce out loud. Crucially, you usually cannot see other people's private signals. You only see their *actions*. And an action is a compressed, lossy summary of a signal: when someone buys, you learn they think the price is going up, but you do not learn *how strongly* or *why*. This gap between the rich private signal and the thin public action is where cascades are born.

### What an information cascade is

An **information cascade** is a situation in which it becomes rational for a person to ignore their own private signal entirely and instead copy the actions they have observed — *regardless of what their own signal says*. The word "regardless" is doing all the work. Plenty of times you partly weight the crowd and partly weight yourself. A cascade is the extreme: the public actions are so loud that no private signal you could possibly have would change your decision, so you stop using your own information at all.

The consequence is brutal and easy to miss: **once you are in a cascade, your action conveys no new information to anyone watching you.** You are copying, so your guess tells observers only that you saw the same public record they did — it tells them nothing about your private signal, because your private signal had no effect on what you did. Information stops flowing into the public pool. The crowd keeps growing, but it stops learning.

### What herding is, and the kind we mean

**Herding** is the broad name for people clustering on the same action. There are several flavors, and conflating them is the single most common mistake people make about markets:

- **Spurious herding:** everyone independently reacts to the same public news (a rate cut, an earnings beat) and ends up doing the same thing. This is not herding in the interesting sense — they would have done it anyway; they are not copying each other.
- **Reputational or career herding:** you copy the crowd to protect your job, because being wrong-with-everyone is forgivable and being wrong-alone gets you fired. (We will return to this; it is real and large.)
- **Rational information cascade:** you copy the crowd because, given what their actions reveal, copying is *genuinely the choice that maximizes your probability of being right.*

This post is mostly about that last one — **rational herding** — because it is the most counterintuitive and the most dangerous. The herders are not stupid and not scared. They are correct, given what they can see. And that is precisely why "but these are sophisticated, rational people" gives you no comfort at all. The failure is in the *information structure*, not in anyone's intelligence or nerve. Panic is a separate phenomenon: a panic is people acting against their own analysis out of fear; a cascade is people acting against their own *signal* out of correct reasoning. The two can stack, but they are not the same, and the cure for one is not the cure for the other.

### The urn experiment: a cascade you can build in a lab

The cleanest way to see a cascade is the experiment Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer, and Welch built in their 1992 paper — the founding work of cascade theory. Here is the setup, which has been run with real people for real money many times since.

There are two urns, identical on the outside. Urn A holds mostly red balls (say two red, one blue). Urn B holds mostly blue (two blue, one red). A coin flip — that nobody sees — picks which urn is "the true urn" for the round, 50/50. The players' job, one at a time, is to guess which urn was chosen.

Each player, in sequence, does exactly two things. First, they step behind a screen and draw **one ball** from the true urn, look at its color, and put it back. That draw is their **private signal**: a red ball makes urn A (the mostly-red urn) more likely; a blue ball makes urn B more likely. Because the majority is two-out-of-three, a single draw matches the true urn with probability $p = 2/3$ — exactly our signal accuracy. Second, the player announces a guess out loud — "A" or "B" — which everyone hears and which is recorded on a board. Then the next player goes. Critically: **every player sees all the previous guesses, but no one ever sees anyone else's draw.** Public actions are visible; private signals are not. That is the whole asymmetry, and it is enough.

One detail of the setup is worth pausing on, because it is what makes the experiment *generalize to markets*. The players observe **actions** (announced guesses), not **signals** (the colors drawn). If everyone could see each other's draws, there would be no cascade at all — every signal would be public, the belief would aggregate all of them, and the crowd would converge on the right urn with near-certainty. The cascade exists *only* because actions are visible while the reasoning behind them is hidden. In a real market you see trades, ratings, and prices — actions — but you almost never see the private research, the channel checks, or the conviction behind them. That gap between the rich signal and the thin action is not a quirk of the lab; it is the permanent condition of every market, which is why cascades are not a curiosity but a structural feature of how prices form.

We are about to watch a cascade form in this lab, step by step, with exact numbers. But first we need the engine that makes each player's choice rational: Bayesian updating.

### Bayesian updating, from absolute zero

**Bayes' rule** is just the arithmetic of changing your mind when new evidence arrives. You start with a belief, you see evidence, you end with a sharper belief. The cleanest way to run it is in **odds**, because evidence then just *multiplies*.

Start with the **prior odds** — your belief before any private evidence. Here the urn is 50/50, so the prior odds that the true urn is A versus B are $1:1$. Now you draw a ball. A red ball is twice as likely to come from urn A (which is mostly red) as from urn B, so it carries a **likelihood ratio** of $2:1$ in favor of A. (Two-thirds chance of red if A, one-third if B; the ratio is $2$.) You multiply:

$$\text{posterior odds} = \text{prior odds} \times \text{likelihood ratio} = 1 \times 2 = 2:1 \text{ for A}.$$

Odds of $2:1$ mean a probability of $2/(2+1) = 2/3$. So after one red draw you are two-thirds sure it is urn A — exactly the signal accuracy, which is no coincidence. Every red draw multiplies your odds by 2 toward A; every blue draw multiplies by 2 toward B (equivalently, divides by 2). The beautiful part: **independent signals just multiply**, so the public belief after several signals is the product of all their likelihood ratios. Hold onto that — it is what makes the cascade arithmetic a one-liner. And it is what we lose when the cascade starts, because copied actions are not new signals to multiply in.

#### Worked example: the first two draws

Let us run the numbers for the first two players, assuming both happen to draw red. Player 1 draws red. Prior odds $1:1$, likelihood ratio $2:1$, posterior odds $2:1$ for urn A, which is probability $2/3 \approx 0.67$. With more than even odds on A, Player 1 rationally guesses A and announces it.

Player 2 now knows two things: Player 1 guessed A (which, since Player 1 was just acting on their own draw, *reveals* that Player 1 saw red), and their own draw — also red. Two red signals. Starting odds $1:1$, times $2$ for the first red, times $2$ for the second red, gives $4:1$ for urn A. That is probability $4/5 = 0.80$. Player 2 is now 80% sure and guesses A.

So after two players, with two red draws, the public board reads "A, A" and the rational belief is **80% urn A** — and so far, beautifully, *every private signal has entered the public belief*, because each player acted on their own draw, so each guess revealed a real piece of evidence. The trouble starts with Player 3, where the structure quietly breaks.

## How a cascade locks in: the arithmetic of Player 3

Player 3 walks up to a board reading "A, A." They draw a ball — and here is the crucial case — they draw **blue**, a signal pointing toward urn B, *against* the crowd. What should they do?

Run the odds. The two prior guesses revealed two red signals: that is $2 \times 2 = 4:1$ in favor of A. Player 3's own blue draw is one signal toward B, a likelihood ratio of $1:2$ — it divides the odds by 2. So:

$$\text{Player 3's odds for A} = 4 \times \tfrac{1}{2} = 2:1 \text{ for A},$$

which is probability $2/3 \approx 0.67$. **Even though Player 3's own draw said B, their posterior still favors A** — because two public reds outweigh one private blue. The two same-direction predecessors literally cancel and overpower a single opposing signal. So Player 3, doing the math correctly, guesses A. They follow the crowd against their own information.

That is the moment the cascade begins. And notice the devastating second-order effect: because Player 3 would have guessed A *whether their draw was red or blue* (red would have made A even more likely; blue still leaves A ahead), **their guess of A reveals nothing about which ball they drew.** Player 4, watching, learns only that Player 3 saw two A's on the board — which Player 4 already knew. Player 3's private signal has vanished from the public record. It was drawn, it was looked at, and it was thrown away.

Player 4 now faces the same board ("A, A, A") plus their own single draw. Two real reds plus a meaningless third A still gives at best $4:1$ for A against their lone draw — the math is identical to Player 3's. They copy too, revealing nothing. And so does Player 5, and Player 500. **The cascade is locked**: from Player 3 onward, no one's private signal enters the public belief, which is frozen forever at the $4:1$ / 80% that the first two draws produced.

![Posterior the crowd is right vs number of same-direction predecessors, crossing 0.50 at k equals 2](/imgs/blogs/information-cascades-and-herding-when-rational-traders-follow-the-crowd-2.png)

The chart makes the trigger precise. It plots *your* posterior probability that the crowd is right, when you are holding a single signal pointing the *opposite* way, as a function of how many same-direction predecessors $k$ you observe. Below $k = 2$ — that is, with zero or one predecessor — your own signal still wins: at $k = 1$ the one public signal and your one private signal exactly cancel ($0.50$), so you go with your own draw. At $k = 2$ your posterior that the crowd is right jumps to **0.67**, above the follow-regardless threshold of $0.50$, so you ignore your own signal and copy. That is the cascade trigger, and it is permanent: every later $k$ is even higher (0.80, 0.89, 0.94...), so once you are in, nothing short of new *public* information gets you out.

#### Worked example: the dollar version on a trading desk

Make it concrete with money. You run a book and you are deciding whether to buy a stock. Your own research — your private signal — says "up," and you trust it two-thirds of the time. Encode the bet as: if you are on the right side, you make \$2; if wrong, you lose \$1. On your signal alone, your expected value is $\tfrac{2}{3}(\$2) + \tfrac{1}{3}(-\$1) = \$1.33 - \$0.33 = +\$1.00$. A clear buy.

Now you notice that the two most recent, most respected funds to take a position both went *short* — they sold. Treat each of those as a revealed "down" signal of the same quality as yours. Your odds that "down" is right are now $2 \times 2 = 4$ from their two signals, divided by $2$ for your own "up" signal, $= 2:1$ that *down* is correct — probability $2/3$ that the stock falls. Recompute your buy: you now believe you are on the *wrong* side with probability $2/3$. Expected value of buying becomes $\tfrac{1}{3}(\$2) + \tfrac{2}{3}(-\$1) = \$0.67 - \$0.67 = \$0.00$ — and any tie-break (fees, the fact that they may know more) tips it negative. **The rational move flips from buy to don't-buy purely because two credible players already sold** — and if you instead short to match them, your own bullish research never enters the market price. Two visible trades outweighed your entire thesis, and that is the cascade arriving at your own desk.

## Why the cascade can be wrong — and how often

Here is the part that should keep you up at night. The cascade locks onto the answer implied by the **first two signals** — and the first two signals can be wrong. With $p = 2/3$, each individual draw is wrong one-third of the time. The cascade forms when the first two people *agree*, which happens when both draw the same color. They both draw the *correct* color with probability $p \times p = (2/3)^2 = 4/9$, producing a cascade onto the right answer. But they both draw the *wrong* color with probability $(1-p) \times (1-p) = (1/3)^2 = 1/9$, producing a cascade onto the **wrong** answer — and everybody after them dutifully copies it.

The remaining case, where the first two draws split (one red, one blue, probability $2 \times \tfrac{2}{3} \times \tfrac{1}{3} = 4/9$), leaves the board tied, so Player 3 falls back on their own draw and the problem resets symmetrically. Eventually some adjacent pair agrees and a cascade forms. Conditioning on a cascade forming at all, the probability it is the *wrong* one is:

$$P(\text{wrong cascade}) = \frac{(1-p)^2}{p^2 + (1-p)^2} = \frac{1/9}{4/9 + 1/9} = \frac{1}{5} = 20\%.$$

**One time in five, an arbitrarily large crowd of perfectly rational people locks onto the wrong answer** — with a signal that is individually right two-thirds of the time. The crowd's size buys you nothing, because the size is just copies of the first two draws. A million-person consensus built this way is exactly as reliable as flipping the first two coins: 80% right, 20% wrong, forever.

![Probability a cascade locks onto the wrong answer vs private-signal accuracy](/imgs/blogs/information-cascades-and-herding-when-rational-traders-follow-the-crowd-3.png)

The chart traces that wrong-cascade probability across signal accuracies. At our $p = 2/3$ it is the 20% we just computed. With a *weaker* signal — say analysts who are right only 55% of the time, which is realistic for many forecasts — the wrong-cascade probability climbs to about **40%**: nearly a coin flip that the entire consensus is backwards. Only with very sharp individual signals ($p$ near 0.9) does the wrong-cascade probability fall into the low single digits. The lesson for markets is uncomfortable: **the worse each participant's individual edge, the more likely the consensus they form is flat wrong** — and weak individual edges are the norm, not the exception, in forecasting.

#### Worked example: how often a 60%-right desk herds into a wrong consensus

Suppose you are surveying a sector where each analyst is right 60% of the time ($p = 0.60$). Wrong-cascade probability is $\frac{(0.40)^2}{(0.60)^2 + (0.40)^2} = \frac{0.16}{0.36 + 0.16} = \frac{0.16}{0.52} \approx 0.308$, about **31%**. So roughly one sector consensus in three, formed this way, is pointing the wrong direction — even though each analyst individually beats a coin. If you had simply *polled all twenty analysts' private views independently* and taken the majority, you would almost never be wrong (twenty 60%-signals make a majority vote right well over 99% of the time). The entire gap between "31% wrong" and "well under 1% wrong" is the cost of letting them observe each other before speaking. The intuition to carry: sequence and visibility, not stupidity, are what poison the consensus.

## How little information a cascade actually contains

We have been saying the cascade "freezes" the public belief. Let us measure exactly how much information it contains, and watch it flatten as the crowd grows — because this flatness is the whole reason cascades are fragile and reverse violently.

Think of information as **certainty**: how far the public belief has moved from the 50/50 starting point toward a confident 0 or 100%. A clean way to score it is one minus the *entropy* of the belief — a number that is 0 when you are maximally unsure (50/50) and 1 when you are certain. Genuine aggregation — where every person's private signal actually enters the pool, multiplying the odds — drives certainty toward 1 fast: each independent signal adds the same chunk of log-odds, so after a handful of people you are nearly sure, and after a few dozen you are effectively certain. That is the real "wisdom of crowds," and it is genuinely powerful.

A cascade does no such thing. It bakes in exactly the first two signals — $4:1$ odds, 80% certainty — and then *stops*, because every later action is a copy that adds zero log-odds. The certainty of a cascade is a flat line at the level two signals buy you, no matter whether the crowd is 5 people or 5,000.

![Information in a cascade flattening versus genuine aggregation as the crowd grows](/imgs/blogs/information-cascades-and-herding-when-rational-traders-follow-the-crowd-4.png)

The chart lays the two against each other. Genuine aggregation (green) climbs steeply and saturates near total certainty within about ten independent participants. The cascade (red dashed) locks at the information content of two signals — about 0.28 on this certainty scale — and stays there as $N$ grows to 30 and beyond. The widening green-shaded gap between the two curves is the information the cascade is *throwing away*: every person past the second one had a real private signal that, under genuine aggregation, would have sharpened the answer — and instead it was dropped. **A consensus of a thousand traders, formed by cascade, carries the informational weight of two.** That is not a metaphor; it is the literal log-odds arithmetic.

#### Worked example: two signals dressed as a thousand

Put numbers on the disguise. A cascade's belief sits at $4:1$ odds — the product of exactly two likelihood ratios of 2. If those same thousand traders had each contributed an independent signal pointing the right way, the odds would be $2^{1000}:1$ — a number with three hundred zeros, i.e., certainty so complete it is meaningless to write down. The cascade delivers $4:1$. The ratio of "what it looks like" (a thousand-strong consensus) to "what it is" (two draws) is the entire trap. When you see a crowded, unanimous trade, the right question is never "how many people are in it?" but "**how many independent signals are actually in it?**" — and in a cascade the answer is two. The intuition: count signals, not heads.

## Signal strength: why some crowds cascade fast and others never do

So far every player has had the same signal accuracy, $p = 2/3$, and we found the cascade triggers brutally early — at the third person. That is because a single signal of moderate strength is easily outvoted by two. But the threshold is not magic; it depends entirely on **how strong the private signal is relative to the public record**. Pushing on this tells you *which* markets cascade and which resist, and it is the most actionable generalization in the whole theory.

The rule is just the odds arithmetic, run in reverse. You ignore your own signal and copy the crowd precisely when the public log-odds against your signal exceed your own signal's log-odds. With same-strength signals, that happens the moment the crowd is ahead by two — hence the third-person trigger. But suppose your private signal is *much stronger* than each public action — you have done deep, original work and your read is right 90% of the time, while the actions you observe are noisy, low-conviction trades each worth only a 55%-accurate signal. Now it takes *many* public actions to outweigh your one strong signal, and you keep acting on your own information far longer. Cascades start late, or not at all, and the market aggregates much more before it freezes.

Flip it: when every participant has a *weak* signal — each barely better than a coin — even one or two visible actions dominate any individual's private view, and the cascade triggers almost immediately. The whole crowd locks onto the first mover's accidental signal. This is the precise sense in which **markets of weak, similar signals cascade fastest and are wrong most often** — and it lines up with the wrong-cascade chart, where low $p$ produced both faster locking and a near-coin-flip chance of error. The defensive reading: cascades are most dangerous exactly where each participant's edge is thinnest, which is most of the time for most forecasts.

#### Worked example: a strong signal that refuses to herd

You are a specialist who has spent a month on a name; your private signal is right 90% of the time — a likelihood ratio of $9:1$ ($0.9$ versus $0.1$). The visible flow is retail momentum trades, each worth a weak $55\%$-accurate signal — a likelihood ratio of about $1.22:1$ ($0.55$ versus $0.45$). Your one signal says "down," $9:1$. How many "up" momentum trades must you see before you flip and copy? You need their combined odds to beat $9$: $1.22^n > 9$, so $n > \ln 9 / \ln 1.22 \approx 2.20 / 0.20 \approx 11$. **You hold out against roughly eleven visible buyers before the public record outweighs your strong private read** — versus the *two* it took when everyone's signal was equal. The intuition: a genuinely strong, original signal is cascade-resistant, which is exactly why the players who do real work are the ones who keep information flowing into the price.

## The early-mover's accidental power

The cascade arithmetic hands enormous, undeserved power to the first one or two movers. Whoever acts first injects the only signals that ever make it into the public belief; everyone after merely ratifies. This is why **order matters more than merit** in a herded market — and it is a fact bad actors exploit and good analysts must defend against.

Two consequences follow. First, an early mover with a *wrong* signal can lock a large, confident crowd onto a wrong answer purely by going first — the $20\%$ wrong-cascade rate is, mechanically, the chance the first two movers were both wrong. The crowd's later members are not foolish for following; they are correctly inferring from a public record that happens to encode a bad early draw. Second — and this is the detection-and-defense point — anyone who can *manufacture* an early public signal can try to start a cascade deliberately. A coordinated cluster of early buyers, a planted "the deal is hot" headline, a wash-traded ramp on a thin token: each is an attempt to fabricate the first two signals so the genuine crowd cascades on top. Knowing the mechanism, you read an unexplained early ramp not as evidence of value but as a *possible engineered first signal* — and you refuse to be the third person who ratifies it. The model does not teach you to manipulate; it teaches you to spot the manufactured early signal and decline to copy it.

#### Worked example: the cost of going second on a fabricated signal

Take a thin micro-cap where two coordinated accounts buy first, creating a visible "A, A" of apparent demand. A genuine trader arrives third with a weak private signal worth $55\%$ ($1.22:1$). The fabricated public record is two same-direction actions; if the genuine trader treats them as honest $2/3$-signals, their odds that "A is right" are $2 \times 2 = 4:1$ — $80\%$ — against their lone $1.22:1$ private draw, so they buy regardless and the cascade is launched on two fake signals. The defense is to *discount the credibility of the public actions themselves*: if you suspect the first two are coordinated rather than independent, their true likelihood ratio is near $1:1$ — no information — and your own weak signal governs. The intuition: a cascade is only as trustworthy as the *independence* of its first movers, so when you cannot verify independence, treat the early consensus as worth nothing and act on your own draw.

## Why cascades are fragile — and shatter on one signal

Everything we have built explains the most violent feature of herded markets: the **reversal**. A consensus that took weeks to build can evaporate in an afternoon, and people reach for psychological stories — panic, fear, sentiment. The cascade model gives a cleaner reason. **Because the cascade contains so little information, it takes very little new information to break it.**

Recall the locked belief sits at $4:1$ — two signals' worth. Now suppose a single *public* signal arrives that is credible enough to be worth two or three private draws: a regulator's ruling, a CEO's resignation, a data print, a famous short-seller's documented report. A public signal that everyone sees and trusts does not get dropped on the floor the way private draws did inside the cascade — it enters everyone's belief at once. If it is worth, say, three signals against the crowd, it multiplies the odds by $\tfrac{1}{2^3} = \tfrac{1}{8}$, taking the public belief from $4:1$ for A down to $\tfrac{4}{8} = 0.5:1$ — i.e., now favoring B. The belief crosses back below 50%, the next person rationally guesses B, *and that guess once again reveals a signal*, so information starts flowing again. The herd doesn't just pause; it can flip and start a fresh cascade the other way.

![Before and after showing one credible public signal resetting a locked cascade](/imgs/blogs/information-cascades-and-herding-when-rational-traders-follow-the-crowd-6.png)

The figure contrasts the two states. On the left, the locked cascade: a public belief of 0.80 that *looks* like the verdict of hundreds but rests on two signals, with no new information entering. On the right, a single credible public signal lands — worth two-plus private draws — the belief snaps below 0.50, the herd re-evaluates, and a new cascade can form in the opposite direction. The fragility is not a flaw in the model; it is the *signature* of a cascade. **Robust consensus is hard to move because it is deep; cascade consensus is easy to shatter because it is shallow.** When you see a "consensus" reverse on a single piece of news that seems too small to justify the move, you are usually watching a cascade snap — the news was small relative to a deep belief, but enormous relative to the two signals actually holding the cascade up.

#### Worked example: the contrarian public signal that pays

Suppose a stock is in a locked-up bullish cascade: public belief 80% it is a "buy," trading rich. You privately compute that the cascade is the wrong-20% kind — the first two movers got it backwards. On your private signal alone, betting against an 80%-confident crowd is terrifying. But you also know two things the crowd's confidence cannot see: that the 80% rests on only two signals, and that one credible public catalyst is due (an earnings report, an FDA date). If that catalyst is worth three signals and comes in against the crowd, it takes the belief from $4:1$ to $0.5:1$ — a full reversal — and the unwinding cascade adds momentum as copiers flip. Your edge is not that you are smarter than 80% of the market; it is that you have correctly priced *how thin that 80% is* and *when a signal big enough to break it arrives*. Sizing follows: small while the cascade holds (it can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent), large into the catalyst that can break it. The intuition: trade the fragility, not your lonely contrarian signal.

## Rational herding versus genuine aggregation

It is worth pinning down the difference between the two, because from the outside they look identical — both produce a crowd doing the same thing — and telling them apart is the whole skill.

![Matrix comparing rational herding cascade against genuine information aggregation](/imgs/blogs/information-cascades-and-herding-when-rational-traders-follow-the-crowd-7.png)

The matrix lays out five distinctions. **What enters the price:** a cascade prices in only the first two signals; genuine aggregation prices in every participant's private signal. **The effect of more people:** in a cascade, more people add zero information (certainty frozen at 0.80); in aggregation, each new person sharpens the answer toward certainty. **Stability:** a cascade is fragile — thin information, flips fast; aggregation is robust — deep information, hard to move. **What breaks it:** a cascade needs only one credible public signal; aggregation needs large genuine new evidence. **Who is on the other side:** in a cascade, the crowd is copiers with no extra edge over you; in genuine aggregation, the crowd is independent informed traders who each know something you do not. That last row is the practical punchline of the whole series. *If the crowd is copiers, fading them is fine and sometimes rich. If the crowd is independent informed traders, fading them is how you go broke.* The cascade lens is, at bottom, a tool for telling which crowd you are facing.

#### Worked example: the diagnostic question on a crowded trade

You are looking at a trade that "everyone" is in — long a megacap, say, with unanimous sell-side buys. Run the diagnostic. Ask: *did these buyers form their views independently, or sequentially while watching each other?* If you learn that the second analyst upgraded the day after the first, the third cited "growing Street consensus," and the price-targets cluster suspiciously tightly, you are looking at a cascade — two signals wearing a costume. The information content is roughly $4:1$ odds, 80%, *not* the 99.9% the unanimity implies. Now ask: *is there a credible catalyst that could be worth two-plus signals against it?* If yes, the expected value of a contrarian position is far better than the 80%-against-you headline suggests, because you are really betting against a 20%-wrong, easily-broken belief. If the buyers genuinely researched independently, leave it alone — that 80% might really be 99.9%. The single number that decides it is *how many independent signals back the consensus*. The takeaway: unanimity is cheap; independence is expensive.

## The urn sequence, all at once

It helps to see the whole sequence laid out as a timeline, because the asymmetry between the first two players and everyone after is the entire story.

![Timeline of the urn experiment from person one through the locked cascade](/imgs/blogs/information-cascades-and-herding-when-rational-traders-follow-the-crowd-5.png)

Reading left to right: Person 1 draws A and guesses A (posterior 0.67) — a real signal enters. Person 2 draws A and guesses A (posterior 0.80) — a second real signal enters. Person 3 draws B — *their own signal says the opposite* — but two A's beat one B, so they guess A; the first signal that gets thrown away. Person 4's draw is now useless because the public record dominates; they copy. Person 5 and everyone after are in a locked cascade, all copying A, no new information entering. The visual makes the break obvious: the first two cards carry rising posteriors (information accumulating); from the third card on, the posterior is frozen and the draws are discarded. Two people did the market's thinking; everyone else did its echoing.

## Common misconceptions

**"Herding means the crowd is irrational or panicking."** This is the misconception that does the most damage, because it makes you underestimate how sturdy and self-justifying a cascade is. Every person in the urn cascade is *individually optimal* — they maximize their probability of being right given what they can see. The error is collective and structural, not individual and emotional. A room full of geniuses produces the same cascade as a room full of average people, because the failure is in the visibility of actions-but-not-signals, which intelligence does not fix. "But these people are sophisticated" is therefore no defense at all — it may be the opposite, since sophisticated players are *better* at correctly inferring that they should copy.

**"A bigger consensus is stronger evidence."** Intuitively, a thousand people agreeing should be far more convincing than two. Under genuine aggregation, yes. Under a cascade, no: the thousand are copies of the first two, so the consensus carries two signals' worth of information whether it is a thousand strong or ten. Size of crowd and amount of information *decouple* once a cascade starts. The figure on information content makes this exact: the certainty line is flat in $N$. Count independent signals, not heads.

**"If it's a cascade, I can just fade the crowd and get rich."** Dangerous half-truth. A cascade is *80% right* in our example — fading it loses four times out of five. The edge is not "fade all crowds"; it is "fade *fragile* crowds *when a signal big enough to break them is arriving*." Without the catalyst, the cascade can persist far longer than your capital, and you will be right eventually but bankrupt first. The contrarian's job is timing the break, not merely identifying the herd.

**"Prices aggregate everyone's information, so the market price is wise."** Sometimes — when traders act on independent signals, prices are remarkable aggregators. But the same market microstructure that lets you observe others' trades is exactly what *enables* cascades. The more transparent the order flow and the more people watch each other, the easier it is for the price to lock onto two early signals and stop learning. Transparency, paradoxically, can *reduce* the information in the price by triggering cascades earlier. A market that aggregates is wise; a market that cascades is merely loud.

**"Once a cascade forms, it's stuck forever."** No — that is its saving grace and its danger. Because it contains so little information, a single credible public signal flips it. Cascades are simultaneously *sticky* (private signals can't break them — only public ones can) and *fragile* (one good public signal does). This is why herded markets trend smoothly and then reverse violently rather than drifting back gently: the belief is shallow, so it doesn't erode, it snaps.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Sell-side analyst consensus and the "whisper" cluster.** The most direct real-world cascade is the way equity-research opinions cluster. The first analyst to publish a view on a name creates a public action; later analysts, who can see both the prior ratings and the stock's reaction, face real career risk in being the lone dissenter. Studies of analyst behavior have long documented that later forecasts cluster toward earlier ones and toward the consensus — Hong, Kubik, and Solomon (2000) and a large herding literature find that analysts, especially less-established ones, herd toward the existing consensus rather than toward their own information. The result is a "consensus EPS" that looks like the pooled wisdom of twenty analysts but often encodes the views of the first few movers, ratified by the rest. When earnings then "surprise" the consensus, part of what surprised was simply the information the herding analysts had but suppressed.

**The dot-com and meme-stock dynamics (2000, 2021).** Cascades are a clean lens on bubbles' final, parabolic legs — though they interact with the greater-fool and reflexivity mechanisms covered elsewhere in this series. In the late-1990s internet bubble, the public action "everyone is buying internet stocks and getting rich" became a louder signal than any individual investor's private doubt about a profitless company's valuation; rational copying poured in. In the January 2021 GameStop episode, the visible action — a stock ripping higher, screenshots of gains, a crowd piling in — functioned as a public signal that swamped private skepticism about a struggling retailer's fundamentals. In both cases the reversal was abrupt (the Nasdaq fell roughly 78% from its March 2000 peak to October 2002; GameStop fell from near \$483 to about \$53.50 within a week in early February 2021), consistent with a shallow, fragile belief snapping rather than a deep one eroding.

**IPO dynamics and the "hot deal" cascade.** Welch's 1992 work on IPOs (a companion to the founding cascade paper) models exactly this: early investors' decisions to buy are visible to later investors, so a deal that gets early support can cascade into being oversubscribed, while a deal that stumbles early can cascade into failure — sometimes nearly independent of the underlying quality. Bankers know this and deliberately manage the order book and allocations to *engineer* a positive early signal. This is the detection-and-defense framing: knowing the cascade mechanism, you read "the deal is hot, it's multiple-times oversubscribed" not as strong evidence of value but as a possibly-engineered public signal sitting on a thin base — and you size accordingly.

**Momentum and flow herding.** Systematic momentum strategies and flow-driven trades mechanically copy the recent action of the crowd — they buy what has been bought. This is herding by construction, and it works precisely because cascades make recent price action a persistent (if shallow) signal that others will keep following. The same model explains momentum's well-documented crash risk: momentum trends are cascade-like — they persist while no breaking signal arrives, then reverse violently when one does (momentum strategies suffered severe crashes in 2009, for example, as the prior trend snapped). Momentum is, in a real sense, the strategy of *renting* cascades and trying to step off before the snap.

**The "everyone agrees, so it's safe" crowded trade.** Perhaps the most expensive real-market cascade is the institutional crowded trade — the position that becomes consensus across hedge funds because each fund can see (through prime-broker data, 13F filings, and conversations) what the others are doing. The visible positioning becomes a public signal that pulls in more funds, the trade gets crowded, and the information backing it is far thinner than the crowd implies. When a catalyst breaks it, the exit is a stampede, because everyone is on the same side. This is the bridge to the [crowded-trades exit game](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game): a cascade is *how* the trade gets crowded; the exit game is *what happens* when the shallow consensus breaks and everyone reaches for the same door.

**Bank runs and deposit flight (2023).** When depositors can observe other depositors withdrawing — through social media, news, or a visible line at the branch — the public action "people are pulling money out" becomes a signal that swamps any individual depositor's private belief that the bank is probably fine. In March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank lost roughly \$42 billion in deposits in a single day as the withdrawal action cascaded through a tightly connected network of venture-backed depositors who could all see each other moving. The run was rational at the individual level — if you think others are pulling out, pulling out first is correct — which is the cascade and the coordination game stacking together.

**Crypto and the manufactured early signal.** Token markets are a near-perfect laboratory for cascades because on-chain activity is *publicly visible by design*: anyone can watch the order flow, the wallet inflows, the "smart money" addresses. That transparency, which is supposed to make crypto markets more honest, is exactly what makes them cascade-prone — early visible buying becomes a loud public signal that pulls in copiers. It also makes the early signal cheap to fabricate. Wash trading (buying and selling to yourself to fake volume) and coordinated pre-launch buys are attempts to manufacture the first two signals so the genuine crowd cascades on top, and the typical pump-and-dump arc — a thin token ramps on engineered volume, retail copies the visible action, then the early movers sell into the crowd and the price collapses — is the cascade mechanism weaponized. The defense, as the model prescribes, is to discount the *independence* of the early flow: if the first buyers may be coordinated, their public actions carry no real information, and a sharp on-chain ramp is a reason for suspicion, not confidence.

**Ratings, structured credit, and the 2008 cascade of models (2004-2008).** A subtler institutional cascade ran through the rating of mortgage-backed securities before the financial crisis. The first agencies and modelers to rate a class of structured product highly created a public action that later participants — investors, other modelers, regulators relying on the ratings — observed and largely ratified rather than independently re-deriving. A handful of early modeling choices about housing-correlation assumptions became a de facto consensus that thousands of downstream decisions copied, so the apparent agreement of the entire system rested on a very thin base of independent analysis. When the breaking public signal arrived — rising delinquencies that no model had priced — the consensus did not erode gently; it snapped, and the cascade reversed into a rush to mark down and sell, contributing to the speed of the 2008 collapse. The lesson is the cascade lesson in institutional dress: a consensus that looks like the independent judgment of the whole system can be the copied judgment of a few early movers, and its thinness is invisible until something breaks it.

## The playbook / How to play it

**Who is on the other side.** In a cascade, the crowd on the other side of your trade is mostly *copiers* — people whose action conveys no private information because they suppressed their own signal to follow the herd. That is good news and bad news. Good: their consensus is informationally thin, so it can be wrong and can break cheaply. Bad: it is *80%-right* thin, not 50%-right thin, so blindly fading it is a losing strategy. The skill is diagnosing *which* crowd you face — copiers in a cascade, or independent informed traders genuinely pooling private knowledge. Against copiers, you can fade and trade the fragility. Against independents, the consensus encodes real edge and fading is how you get run over.

**The game you are in.** You are in a sequential game of incomplete information where actions are visible but signals are not. Your edge does not come from having a better private signal than the crowd — inside a cascade, private signals are worthless, including yours. Your edge comes from reasoning one level deeper: recognizing that the consensus is a cascade, estimating *how few signals* actually back it, and identifying *what public signal* — and *when* — could be large enough to break it. You are not betting your view against the crowd's view; you are betting the *depth* of your read of the crowd's structure against their illusion of depth.

**Protect your own information from the cascade.** There is a defensive corollary that applies even when you are not trading against the herd: a cascade does not just trap the crowd, it can trap *you*. The same arithmetic that makes copying rational for the third urn-player makes it tempting for you to abandon your own research the moment you see two credible players on the other side. Sometimes that is correct — they may genuinely know more. But notice the trap: if you suppress your signal and copy, you have just added another copier to the cascade, made the consensus look one person deeper than it is, and contributed nothing. The discipline is to act on your own signal *when it is strong and independent* — the eleven-buyers calculation shows a genuinely strong read should hold out for a long time — and to copy only when you have honestly concluded the public actions outweigh it. A market stays informative only because some players refuse to herd; being one of them, when your edge warrants it, is both how you make money and how you keep the price honest. The failure mode to avoid is herding on *weak* conviction, which costs you your independence for nothing.

**Your edge and the diagnostic.** Concretely: (1) **Trace the sequence.** Did the consensus form independently or by people watching each other? Tight clustering, "growing consensus" language, and rapid sequential agreement are cascade tells. (2) **Count independent signals, not heads.** A unanimous crowd of fifty that formed sequentially carries roughly two signals — price it as $4:1$ / 80%, not as near-certainty. (3) **Price the fragility.** A cascade's belief sits on a thin base; estimate how many signals' worth of public news would flip it, and whether such news is due. (4) **Stack the catalysts.** The best contrarian setups are not "I disagree with the crowd" but "the crowd is a shallow cascade *and* a signal big enough to break it is scheduled."

**The invalidation.** This whole playbook is wrong, and dangerous, when the crowd is *not* a cascade. If the consensus formed from genuinely independent research — each participant pooling a different private signal — then the unanimity really does encode deep information, the belief is robust, and fading it is a fast way to lose. Your position is invalidated the moment you discover the crowd's signals were independent rather than copied: deep consensus is right far more than 80% of the time, and it does not snap on small news. So before you fade anything, *prove it is a cascade*. Absence of that proof is the invalidation.

**Sizing and the exit.** Because a cascade can persist longer than you can stay solvent — it is sticky to private signals and only public signals break it — size your contrarian position *small while the cascade holds* and add *into the catalyst* that can break it. Never size a fade as if your contrarian conviction were the edge; the edge is the fragility and the timing, both of which are uncertain. The exit on a winning fade is the reversal cascade itself: once the belief snaps below the threshold and copiers start flipping, the move feeds on itself (the unwinding crowd is now your tailwind), and you take profit into the stampede rather than trying to call the exact bottom of the old cascade. On a losing fade, the invalidation is mechanical: if the public belief *strengthens* rather than breaks on the catalyst you were waiting for, the consensus was deep, not shallow — you were wrong about the structure, and you cut.
