> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In Vietnam, who is on the other side of your trade is decided less by your forecast than by three structural games: the foreign-ownership "room" game, the settlement-and-margin game, and the manipulation game. Read the games, not the chart.
>
> - Most stocks cap foreign ownership at 49% (30% for banks). When a name hits its limit, foreigners can only buy from each other, often at a premium of 5–15% or more, so the published foreign room and net foreign flow become a coordination signal everyone watches.
> - T+2 settlement and the margin cycle decide who *can* act and when; local retail is roughly 80–90% of matched volume, so when foreigners and institutions sell, local individuals are the buyer of last resort. That is your counterparty.
> - The FLC Group / Trinh Van Quyet pump-and-dump shows the manipulation game as injecting a false signal into other people's inference. It was illegal, it was detected — the regulator cancelled the secret January 2022 sale of about 74.8 million FLC shares — and it ended in arrest and a 2024 conviction.
> - The one rule to remember: in an emerging market, watch the flows and the room, respect the structural rules, learn the manipulation footprints, and assume local retail is on the other side until proven otherwise.

In January 2022, a single attempted trade in Ho Chi Minh City told you almost everything about how the Vietnamese stock market actually works. The chairman of one of the country's best-known listed groups tried to quietly sell about 74.8 million shares of his own company without filing the disclosure that the law required. The price had been climbing. Retail buyers, watching that climb, were lining up to take the other side. And then the regulator did something that does not happen in a deep, liquid market with anonymous order flow: it looked at the trade, decided it was illegitimate, and *cancelled it*. The seller was arrested two months later and convicted in 2024.

That episode is not a quirk. It is a window into the three games that govern who wins and who loses on the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE) and across the VN-Index. There is a game about foreigners, who are capped in how much of most companies they can own. There is a game about plumbing — when trades settle, when margin gets called, who is even allowed to act on a given day. And there is a game about deception — how a manipulator injects a false signal into the market, and how a regulator catches it. Get those three games right and a lot of confusing Vietnamese-market behavior stops being confusing.

This post is the Vietnam case study for our running series on game theory in markets. The spine of the whole series is one idea: a trade is a strategic interaction, not a bet against nature. Every dollar you make is a dollar a specific counterparty loses or pays, so your edge is not a forecast — it is knowing who is on the other side and what game you are both playing. Vietnam makes that lesson unusually vivid, because the *rules of the board* are so visible: a hard ownership cap, a fixed settlement clock, a price-limit band, and a retail base so dominant that you can name the other side with confidence. The map below is how the three games fit together.

A note before we start: this is educational, not advice. We will explain mechanisms, history, and the strategic logic. We will not tell you to buy or sell anything. And the manipulation section takes a strict detection-and-enforcement view — the point is to recognize the footprints and understand how the scheme was caught and punished, never to provide a recipe for running one.

## Foundations: the VN-Index, HOSE, the room, the clock, and the players

Let us build the board from zero, because the rest of the post depends on a handful of terms that are specific to Vietnam.

**The VN-Index and HOSE.** The VN-Index is the headline stock-market index of Vietnam — a *market-capitalization-weighted* index, meaning each company counts in proportion to its total market value (shares outstanding times price). It is published by the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange, or HOSE, the larger of the country's two main exchanges. (The other is HNX in Hanoi, plus UPCoM, a board for less-regulated companies. We will focus on HOSE.) When you read "the VN-Index rose 1%," that is the value-weighted basket of the biggest HOSE-listed companies. As of recent years the VN-Index has hovered in the low- to mid-thousands of points, and total HOSE market capitalization is on the order of a few hundred billion US dollars — large enough to matter, small enough that a few big foreign flows can move it.

**Market capitalization, in plain terms.** A *market cap* is just price times share count. A company with 100 million shares at 50,000 VND each (about \$2.00 per share at roughly 25,000 VND to the dollar) has a market cap of 5 trillion VND, or about \$200 million. We will keep anchoring Vietnamese-dong figures in dollars like this throughout, because the dong numbers are large and unfamiliar; a "70,000 VND share" is about \$2.80, and a "trillion VND" is roughly \$40 million at recent exchange rates.

**The foreign-ownership limit — the "room."** Here is the first thing that makes Vietnam different from the US or Europe. Foreign investors, collectively, are *capped* in how much of a Vietnamese listed company they are allowed to own. For most stocks the *foreign ownership limit* (FOL) is 49% of the shares; for banks it is tighter, commonly 30%. The unused portion of that cap is called the *foreign room*, or just "the room." If a company's foreign limit is 49% and foreigners currently own 40%, there is 9% of room left. When foreigners own the full 49%, the room is *zero* — the stock is "full" or "room-locked." This number is published and watched daily.

**T+2 settlement and the price limit.** When you trade a HOSE stock, the trade does not finalize instantly. Vietnam runs on *T+2 settlement*: cash and shares actually change hands two business days after the trade date (T). For years it was effectively even slower in practice for retail, which mattered enormously for how fast you could redeploy capital. HOSE also imposes a *daily price limit* — a stock can move at most about ±7% from its prior reference price in a single session (the band differs slightly on the other boards; UPCoM is wider). A stock pinned at the top of its band ("trần", the ceiling) with buyers stacked behind it, or pinned at the bottom ("sàn", the floor) with sellers stacked, is a recurring feature of Vietnamese tape, and it is a direct consequence of the price-limit rule.

**The players.** Four kinds of participants sit on this board:

- **Local retail** — individual Vietnamese investors trading from home or a phone. They are the dominant force, routinely 80–90% of matched trading value. Many trade on *margin* (borrowed money from their broker) and chase momentum.
- **Local institutions** — domestic funds, insurers, proprietary desks at securities firms. A modest slice of volume.
- **Foreign investors** — global funds, regional funds, and the big passive index trackers (ETFs that follow FTSE or MSCI emerging/frontier benchmarks). Capped by the room.
- **The regulator and the exchange** — the State Securities Commission (SSC) and HOSE itself, which set the rules, run surveillance, and (as we will see) can cancel trades.

**Two ways to trade on HOSE.** One more mechanical fact matters for the room game later. There are two channels for getting a trade done. The first is the *order book* (continuous matching), where anonymous buy and sell orders meet at a price — this is the on-screen price you see quoted. The second is the *put-through* or *block* channel, where two parties negotiate a price and quantity privately and report the trade to the exchange. Put-through deals can happen at prices away from the on-screen price, which is exactly why they are the venue where foreigners trade scarce, room-locked shares with each other at a premium. Hold that distinction; it is the hinge of the first game.

**The solution concept.** Throughout, we will reason in terms of *best response* and *equilibrium*. A best response is the move that maximizes your payoff given what everyone else is doing. An *equilibrium* is a situation where everyone is simultaneously best-responding, so no one wants to change. The three games below are each defined by their players, the moves available to them, the payoffs, and the equilibrium that results — and, crucially, by where real players deviate from the textbook equilibrium and how that makes someone the sucker.

A useful everyday analogy for the whole board is a private members' club with a hard cap on the number of memberships. The club (the stock) has a fixed number of seats. Locals can move freely in and out of the general seating, but only 49 out of every 100 seats can be held by foreigners at once. When those 49 foreign seats are full, a new foreign member can only get in if an existing foreign member leaves — and the departing member can charge for the privilege. The doorman (the exchange) records who comes and goes and posts the count publicly. And every so often a fraudster tries to talk a crowd into paying inflated prices for seats by faking a stampede at the door. That club, with its cap, its doorman's ledger, and its occasional con artist, is the Vietnamese market in miniature.

## Game 1: the foreign-ownership-limit ("room") game

### Why the cap exists and what it does to price

The foreign cap is a policy choice: Vietnam wants foreign capital and the credibility that comes with global index inclusion, but it also wants to keep strategic control of key sectors (banks above all) in domestic hands. The 49% / 30% limits are the compromise. The unintended consequence is a beautiful little game.

Think about a popular blue chip that foreigners love and have already bought up to the 49% cap. The room is zero. Now a new foreign fund wants in. It *cannot* buy from a local investor on the open order book, because that would push foreign ownership above 49%, which is illegal. The only legal seller to a foreigner is *another foreigner* who is leaving. So foreign demand and foreign supply for that stock have been walled off into a private sub-market of just foreign participants — and in that walled garden, scarcity sets the price.

![Foreign room open versus full and the FOL premium](/imgs/blogs/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games-2.png)

When the room is open, the foreign price equals the local on-screen price — a foreigner just buys on HOSE like anyone else. When the room is full, an incoming foreigner must find a departing foreigner and pay whatever it takes to pry the shares loose, frequently through a negotiated *put-through* (block) deal off the main order book. The gap between what foreigners pay and the local screen price is the *foreign ownership limit premium*, or **FOL premium**. For sought-after, room-locked names it has historically run anywhere from a few percent to 15% or more, and occasionally far higher for the most coveted blue chips.

#### Worked example: the FOL premium as the price of a scarce seat

Suppose a room-locked blue chip trades at 100,000 VND on the local screen — about \$4.00 per share at roughly 25,000 VND to the dollar. Foreigners own the full 49%; the room is zero. A global fund wants to add a position and finds a departing foreign holder willing to sell, but only at a 10% premium through a put-through deal.

The fund pays 110,000 VND per share, about \$4.40. On 1 million shares that is 110 billion VND, roughly \$4.4 million — versus the 100 billion VND (about \$4.0 million) a local buyer would pay on screen. The extra 10 billion VND, around \$400,000, is the FOL premium. It is not a fee to a broker or the exchange; it is a pure transfer to the departing foreigner, who captured the scarcity value of one of only 49 "foreign seats" out of every 100 shares.

Now flip it. If foreign sentiment sours and foreigners start fighting to *exit* a room-locked name, the premium can compress to zero or even invert (a foreign *discount*) as departing foreigners undercut each other to find the few incoming foreign buyers. The premium is a thermometer for how badly foreigners want in versus out.

The intuition: a hard ownership cap turns "buy the stock" into "buy a scarce, transferable license to hold the stock," and like any license in fixed supply, its price floats on demand.

There is a subtler strategic layer here that is worth making explicit, because it is where the game gets interesting. When the room is full, the *local* price and the *foreign* price are two different numbers for the same share — a thing that should not exist in a textbook market and only exists because of the cap. The local price is set by the on-screen order book among domestic participants who are still free to trade. The foreign price is set in the thin put-through market among foreigners. The two are linked but not equal, and the size of the gap between them — the FOL premium — is itself a tradeable, watchable quantity. Specialized brokers run desks that match incoming and outgoing foreign demand precisely to capture or arbitrage this gap. The existence of two prices for one asset is the cleanest possible demonstration that the cap is a binding constraint that fragments the market into two pools of buyers who cannot freely trade with each other.

This also explains a recurring puzzle for newcomers: why a Vietnamese blue chip can sit at the top of foreign appetite for *years* without the room ever reopening. As long as foreigners who want in outnumber foreigners who want out, the room stays at zero, the premium persists, and the on-screen price the domestic crowd trades can drift independently of the foreign demand that is dammed up behind the cap. The wall does not go away; the pressure just builds against it.

### The room and net flow as a coordination signal

Because the room is published and the daily *net foreign flow* (foreign buys minus foreign sells) is reported on every terminal and in the financial press, these numbers become a *coordination signal* — a public statistic that everyone updates on simultaneously. This is exactly the "who's on the other side" problem the series keeps returning to. If you see foreigners persistently buying a name and its room shrinking toward zero, you are watching a slow squeeze form: the closer the room gets to zero, the harder it is for the next foreigner to buy on screen, and the more likely the price runs as that last sliver of room gets competed for. Conversely, persistent net foreign *selling* tells you that a large, often price-insensitive group is leaving — and someone, usually local retail, is absorbing it.

![Net foreign flow on HOSE by year as a watched signal](/imgs/blogs/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games-3.png)

The chart shows approximate full-year net foreign flow on HOSE, in trillion VND, rounded from broker year-reviews and the press. The headline is the run of red bars: foreigners were heavy net sellers in several recent years, with 2024 an especially large net outflow (on the order of tens of trillions of VND, i.e. a few billion dollars). When you see numbers like that, the strategic question is not "are foreigners smart or dumb?" It is "if foreigners sold roughly that much, who bought it?" The answer, structurally, is local retail. That tells you who has been on the other side of the foreign exit — and it tells you that the marginal price-setter in those years was a domestic individual, not a global allocator.

It is worth being precise about *why* net foreign flow is treated as a signal at all, because the reasoning is a small game in itself. A signal is informative only if it correlates with something you care about and is hard to fake. Foreign flow scores on the first count — foreigners as a group include large, research-driven funds whose buying sometimes leads fundamentals — but it is contaminated on the second, because a big chunk of foreign flow is *passive*: index trackers mechanically buying or selling to match a benchmark, currency hedging adjustments, or whole-fund redemptions that force selling regardless of any view on the specific stock. So the sophisticated reading is not "foreigners bought, therefore good." It is to *decompose* the flow: is this discretionary foreign buying that might contain information, or is it calendar-driven passive flow that contains none? The trader who can tell the difference is reasoning one level deeper than the one who treats the headline net-flow number as a single signal. That decomposition is the whole edge in the foreign-flow game.

#### Worked example: reading the net-flow signal against the room

Say a mid-cap industrial trades at 60,000 VND, about \$2.40, with a 49% foreign cap and 6% room remaining (foreigners own 43%). Over a month, foreigners net-buy steadily and the room falls from 6% to 1%. The float available to the *next* foreign buyer has shrunk by five-sixths.

If the stock has, say, 200 million shares, 6% room is 12 million shares foreigners may still buy on screen; 1% room is just 2 million. A foreign fund that wanted to build a 3-million-share position can no longer do it on the open book at all — it must either stop, or move to the premium put-through market. The on-screen buying pressure that drove the room down also drove the price up, and the closing of the room now caps how much *more* on-screen foreign buying can happen, which often marks the local top of the foreign-driven leg.

A trader who watches only price sees "stock went up." A trader who watches the room sees "the fuel is nearly gone." The room is the second-order variable that tells you whether the foreign bid can continue.

The intuition: in a capped market, the *quantity of remaining room* is as informative as the price, because it tells you how much of the dominant marginal buyer is left.

### The index-effect game: FTSE, MSCI, and ETF rebalances

There is a second, calendar-driven layer to the foreign game. Global index providers — FTSE Russell and MSCI — maintain emerging-market and frontier-market indices, and passive funds (ETFs and index trackers) hold whatever those indices say to hold. When an index provider adds, drops, or reweights a Vietnamese stock at a scheduled review, every passive fund tracking that index must trade the same names in the same direction at roughly the same time. That is the *index effect*: a wave of price-insensitive, calendar-locked flow that active traders can anticipate.

Vietnam sits in FTSE's *frontier* index and has been on FTSE's watchlist for promotion to *secondary emerging* status — a long-running saga, because the very room-and-settlement frictions we are describing are part of what FTSE and MSCI cite as obstacles to reclassification (along with the historical pre-funding requirement, where buyers had to have cash in the account before trading). A confirmed upgrade would, in one stroke, pull a much larger pool of emerging-market passive money toward Vietnamese blue chips — which is exactly why the room on the most likely beneficiaries is watched like a hawk. We treat the index-effect mechanics in depth in the companion piece [Foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam); here the point is narrower: an index rebalance is a *publicly pre-announced* flow, so it is a coordination signal with a date attached, and the game is about who positions ahead of the passive herd — and who gets caught holding when the one-day rebalance flow is done.

#### Worked example: front-running a known rebalance flow

Suppose an index review is expected to add a Vietnamese bank to a frontier benchmark, and analysts estimate passive trackers will need to buy 30 million shares on the effective date. The bank trades at 25,000 VND, about \$1.00, with average daily volume of 5 million shares. The required passive buy is six times a normal day's volume, concentrated into essentially one print.

An active trader who buys ahead of the rebalance is betting the 30-million-share demand will lift the price into the event. If the trader buys 1 million shares at 25,000 VND (25 billion VND, about \$1.0 million) and the rebalance pushes the price 8% higher to 27,000 VND, that is a 2 billion VND gain, roughly \$80,000, before costs. But the catch — the equilibrium catch — is that *everyone* can read the same review, so the front-running itself moves the price *before* the event, and by the effective date the passive buyers may be paying the elevated price to the very front-runners who are now selling. If too many traders crowd the same anticipated flow, the edge is competed away and the late front-runner is the one who overpays. That is the crowded-trade exit problem, applied to a calendar event.

The intuition: a pre-announced flow is free information, which means its edge gets arbitraged toward zero — the money is made by being early and *out* before the herd's one-day flow is spent, not by holding into it.

The room constraint and the index effect interact in a way that is unique to capped markets. A passive index fund that *must* buy a Vietnamese name to track a benchmark faces the same wall everyone else does: if the name is room-locked, the passive fund cannot buy it on screen either. This creates genuine tracking problems for index providers and is one reason the room is part of the reclassification conversation. From a game-theory standpoint, it means the index-effect flow on a room-locked name may be forced into the premium put-through market, widening the FOL premium right around the rebalance — so a trader watching both the review calendar *and* the room can sometimes see two of the foreign games line up. The cleanest setups are names that are *near* but not at their cap going into an anticipated index inclusion: there is still on-screen room for the passive buyers to fill, so the flow lands on the visible price rather than disappearing into a private premium.

## Game 2: the settlement and structural game

### The clock decides who can act

The second game is about plumbing, and plumbing is destiny in a retail-dominated market. Under T+2 settlement, when you sell a stock you do not have usable cash until two business days later, and when you buy, the shares are not fully yours to re-sell on the same terms instantly. For years, Vietnamese retail effectively could not sell a stock until it had settled into the account, which meant a buyer was *locked in* for a couple of days regardless of what the price did. That single fact changes the game: it removes a move (immediate exit) from one player's strategy set for a window of time, and any player whose legal moves are restricted is at a structural disadvantage.

Layer the *margin cycle* on top. A huge share of local retail trades on margin — borrowed money from the broker, secured by the stock itself. When prices fall, the collateral falls, and brokers issue *margin calls*: put up more cash or we sell your shares. Because so many retail accounts are leveraged against the same popular names, a down move can trigger correlated forced selling — a *margin cascade* — where each forced sale pushes the price down and triggers the next call. We unpack this loop in [Liquidity and the margin cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam); for the game-theory point, the margin cycle means a large bloc of the market can be *forced to sell at the worst possible time*, and the player who is forced is, again, the one whose moves are constrained by the rules.

#### Worked example: the T+2 and margin trap on a leveraged retail account

You buy 10,000 shares of a momentum name at 30,000 VND — 300 million VND, about \$12,000 — using 50% margin, so you put up 150 million VND (about \$6,000) of your own cash and borrow the other 150 million VND from your broker. The trade is on day T.

On day T+1, bad news hits and the stock drops the daily limit of 7%, to about 27,900 VND. Your position is now worth 279 million VND, about \$11,160. You have lost 21 million VND (around \$840) of equity, and your own equity has fallen from 150 to 129 million VND. If your broker's maintenance margin requires equity of at least, say, 40% of position value, you need equity of at least 0.40 × 279 = 111.6 million VND — you are still above it, barely. But here is the structural trap: under the old settlement and margin rules you may not be able to *sell to cut the loss freely* the same day, and if the stock limits down again on T+2, your equity erodes toward the threshold and a margin call forces a sale at the bottom. You bought a stock and, for two days, did not fully control your own exit.

The intuition: leverage plus a settlement delay plus a price-limit band means a falling stock can take your decision away from you — the rules, not your conviction, decide when you sell.

### The price-limit band as a strategic object

The ±7% daily band on HOSE deserves its own treatment, because it changes the *game* and not just the speed. A price limit does two things. First, it caps how far the price can travel in a session, which means information that would move a stock 20% in a free market gets *rationed out* over several days of limit moves. Second — and this is the strategic part — when a stock is pinned at its ceiling or floor, the visible order queue becomes a coordination signal of its own. A stock locked at the ceiling ("trần") with a huge stack of unfilled buy orders behind it is telling everyone "there is far more demand than the band will let through today," which itself attracts more buyers tomorrow. A stock locked at the floor ("sàn") with a stack of unfilled sell orders is the mirror image: a queue of people desperate to get out who could not, which terrifies the next holder into joining the sell queue.

So the band, intended to dampen volatility, can paradoxically *coordinate* it across days. The reader who understands this does not look only at today's close; they look at *how* the stock closed — did it close locked-limit with a queue, or did it close mid-band on balanced flow? A locked-limit close with a one-sided queue is a far stronger directional signal than the 7% move alone, because it reveals suppressed demand or supply that the band did not let clear. This is a piece of microstructure that simply does not exist in an unlimited market like the US, and it is part of why reading the Vietnamese tape is a distinct skill.

### Reform is slowly changing the board

The structural rules are not frozen. Vietnam has been steadily reforming exactly the features that frustrate foreign participation: the historical *pre-funding requirement* — under which a foreign buyer had to have 100% of the cash in the account before placing a buy order, a serious friction for global funds used to settling later — has been a central target, with moves to relax it for institutional investors. Settlement and the broader trading system have been modernized over time. Each reform is itself a move in the meta-game, because easing these frictions is widely seen as a prerequisite for the FTSE upgrade that would pull a large new pool of passive money into the market. When you read that a settlement or pre-funding rule has changed, the second-order question is always "which players does this newly *enable*, and which names will they buy first?" The structure is a constraint on the strategy set, and changing the structure changes who can play.

### Local retail dominance: naming the other side

Now the central question of the whole series, answered cleanly for Vietnam: who is on the other side of your trade? The answer is mostly *local retail*, because local individuals are the overwhelming majority of matched volume.

![Local retail share of HOSE volume versus institutions and foreigners](/imgs/blogs/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games-4.png)

The chart shows the approximate participant mix on HOSE: local individuals routinely supply on the order of 80–90% of matched trading value, with local institutions and foreigners splitting the small remainder. The exact split moves around, and the number is an order-of-magnitude figure from exchange and broker reports, not a precise constant. But the order of magnitude is the whole point. In a US large-cap, your counterparty is usually a market maker or an algorithm with no view — you are trading against *liquidity*, not against an opinion. In Vietnam, when you buy, the person selling to you is very likely another individual, often leveraged, often momentum-driven, often reading the same handful of forums and broker reports you are. You are trading against *opinions* held by people structurally similar to you.

That has two consequences. First, the market can be *reflexive and herd-prone*: when everyone is the same kind of player reading the same signals, moves feed on themselves, both up and down. Second, it tells you where to look for an edge — not in out-forecasting a superhuman quant, but in being one level less emotional and one level better-informed than a leveraged retail crowd, and in recognizing when that crowd is being *played*. Which brings us to the third game.

There is a deeper point hiding in the 80–90% number, and it connects directly to the series' spine. In a market dominated by anonymous institutional liquidity, the "who is on the other side" question is genuinely hard to answer — you might be trading against a passive rebalance, a hedger with no view, or an informed insider, and you cannot easily tell which. In Vietnam, the prior is strong and stable: the other side is almost certainly a domestic individual, and a large fraction of them are leveraged and trading on momentum and sentiment rather than on a model of intrinsic value. That changes the *type* of opponent you model. Against a no-view liquidity provider, your job is to minimize impact and capture spread. Against a leveraged, emotional crowd, your job is to anticipate where the crowd will be *forced* to act — by a margin call, by a limit-down queue, by a piece of news hitting a thinly-traded name — and to be positioned on the other side of that forced move. The structural game and the manipulation game are both, at bottom, about predicting forced or fooled behavior in a counterparty whose identity you already know.

## Game 3: the manipulation game (detection and enforcement framing)

A reminder before we proceed: everything in this section is framed so that a reader can *recognize and avoid* manipulation and understand *how regulators catch it*. Market manipulation is illegal in Vietnam, as it is everywhere with a functioning securities regulator, and the central case here ended in criminal conviction. This is not, and must not be read as, a how-to. The value is in spotting the footprints and understanding why the scheme is detectable and was punished.

### The pump-and-dump as injecting a false signal

In the series we treat spoofing and layering as *signaling games* — a manipulator's orders are a message, deliberately crafted to be misread by other players who are trying to infer true value from order flow. We develop that framing in [Spoofing, layering, and manipulation as signaling games](/blog/trading/game-theory/spoofing-layering-and-manipulation-as-signaling-games). A pump-and-dump is the same idea stretched over days or weeks instead of milliseconds.

Here is the structure, stated abstractly. A manipulator, often an insider with a large holding and influence over the float, buys and trades a thinly held stock among accounts they control to *manufacture* a rising price and rising volume. To an outside observer using the normal inference — "rising price plus rising volume on a stock usually means informed buyers know something good" — this looks like a genuine bullish signal. Retail chases it. Once enough outside buyers have piled in, the manipulator *dumps* the accumulated stock onto them at the inflated price and disappears, leaving the chasers holding shares whose price collapses back toward reality. The crime is the deliberate injection of a false signal into other people's inference process.

We can put numbers on the strategic core with a simple 2×2 game, computed from the series' Nash solver, framed for detection.

![Manipulation false-signal payoff matrix with and without enforcement](/imgs/blogs/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games-5.png)

Read the matrix. The row player is the manipulator, choosing between *ramp-and-dump* (inject a false signal) and *trade honestly*. The column player is retail, choosing between *chase the ramp* and *stay skeptical*. The payoffs come from the series' `nash_2x2` model. In the danger cell — manipulator ramps, retail chases — the manipulator earns +9 and retail loses −7: that is the trap, and it is the cell the manipulator is trying to steer the game into. If retail stays skeptical, the manipulator who ramped wastes capital pushing a price no one chases (−3), and the honest manipulator and skeptical retail land in fair price discovery (+1, +2).

#### Worked example: the payoffs that make detection the whole game

Without any enforcement, look at the manipulator's incentives. If retail chases, ramping pays +9 versus +1 for honesty; the manipulator wants to ramp. The only thing stopping a ramp is retail *staying skeptical*, which makes ramping a −3 loser. Using the mixed-strategy logic from the series model, retail only has to be skeptical *often enough* — about two-thirds of the time, given these numbers — to make the manipulator indifferent between ramping and not. Skepticism is the retail defense.

Now add enforcement. Suppose disclosure rules, surveillance, and the credible threat that a regulator will cancel an illegitimate trade and prosecute the actor impose an expected penalty of, say, 14 units on the ramp move. The manipulator's ramp payoffs become 9 − 14 = −5 (if retail chases) and −3 − 14 = −17 (if retail stays skeptical). Both are now worse than the +1 the honest row pays in either column. Ramping is *dominated* — there is no longer any column in which it pays. The equilibrium collapses to honest trading and fair price discovery.

The intuition: detection and enforcement do not just punish a manipulator after the fact; they change the *payoff matrix* so that injecting a false signal stops being a winning move in the first place. That is the entire purpose of disclosure rules and surveillance.

### The FLC Group / Trinh Van Quyet case

The most consequential Vietnamese illustration is the FLC Group case and its chairman, Trinh Van Quyet. (We romanize Vietnamese names to plain ASCII throughout.) FLC was a high-profile conglomerate spanning real estate and the airline Bamboo Airways, and a cluster of related tickers traded on the Vietnamese market — FLC itself, plus names like ROS (FLC Faros), ART, HAI, and AMD. Over 2021 and into early 2022, these FLC-group tickers saw heavy ramping: prices and volumes were pushed up in ways that, the authorities later established, involved coordinated trading across many accounts controlled by or linked to the principals, manufacturing the appearance of genuine demand.

#### Worked example: why a thin float makes a ramp cheap to fake and easy to detect

Manipulation is most feasible on a stock with a small *free float* — the portion of shares actually available to trade, excluding large locked-up insider stakes. Suppose a stock has 500 million shares outstanding, but insiders hold 80%, leaving a free float of just 100 million shares. Now suppose normal daily turnover is only 1% of that float — 1 million shares a day, worth perhaps 30 billion VND (about \$1.2 million) at a 30,000 VND price.

To push a stock with that little real trading, a manipulator does not need to buy hundreds of millions of dollars of stock. By trading a few million shares a day *among controlled accounts*, they can dominate the day's volume and drag the price up the band, manufacturing both a rising price and rising "volume" that is really the same shares cycling between related accounts. That is what makes a thin-float name a target: a small amount of capital moves the price a lot.

But the same thinness is what makes it *detectable*. When a handful of accounts account for an outsized share of a stock's volume, and when several related tickers all show the same pattern at the same time, surveillance has a clean statistical signature to flag — abnormal concentration, wash-like trading (buying and selling among linked accounts), and correlated moves across names with no common news. The very feature that makes a ramp cheap to run is the feature that leaves fingerprints.

The intuition: a thin float is a double-edged sword for a manipulator — it makes the price cheap to move *and* makes the manipulation statistically loud, which is precisely why investigators can reconstruct it from the trading record.

The defining moment came on January 10, 2022. Trinh Van Quyet sold a very large block — about 74.8 million FLC shares — *without* the prior disclosure that Vietnamese rules require for a major insider sale. Large insider transactions must be pre-registered with HOSE and the SSC before they are executed, precisely so the market can see who is selling and how much. By selling secretly, he was trying to dump into the inflated price he and associates had helped create, before the market could react to the news that the chairman himself was bailing. In Vietnamese press the maneuver is described with the phrase that translates roughly as "stealth selling" — selling without the mandatory announcement.

![Timeline of the FLC stealth-sell detection and enforcement](/imgs/blogs/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games-6.png)

### How detection actually worked

This is the part worth dwelling on, because it shows the enforcement game in motion. Three mechanisms caught it:

First, **disclosure rules created a tripwire.** Because a large insider sale is supposed to be pre-registered, an *unannounced* sale of 74.8 million shares was, by definition, a rule violation that surveillance could flag. The rule does not just inform the market; it makes a secret dump *detectable as anomalous* the moment it happens. A trade that should have a filing attached, and doesn't, lights up.

Second, **surveillance and the exchange acted in real time.** HOSE and the SSC identified the unregistered transaction and the SSC *cancelled the trade* — an extraordinary step only possible in a market structure where the exchange and regulator have that authority and use it. The proceeds were unwound; the dump did not stand. In a fully anonymous, instantly-final settlement system this would be far harder. Here, the very structural frictions that frustrate foreign index inclusion also gave the regulator a chokepoint to intervene.

Third, **the pattern across related tickers told the story.** The coordinated ramping across FLC-group names — multiple related stocks moving in lockstep on coordinated account activity rather than independent news — is a classic manipulation footprint that an investigation can reconstruct from trading records. The cancelled trade was the smoking gun; the broader pattern was the case.

### The enforcement outcome

The chain ran to its end. Trinh Van Quyet was arrested on March 29, 2022, on charges including stock-market manipulation and, in the broader case, appropriating investors' money through the inflated value of the FLC-group shares (notably the ROS listing). In 2024 he was convicted and sentenced — reported at a total of 21 years across the manipulation and fraud charges — and ordered to pay very large damages to the many investors harmed. The number of affected retail investors ran into the tens of thousands. The lesson for our game: the manipulator landed in the −5 / −17 region of the enforcement matrix. The scheme that looked like +9 became a multi-decade prison sentence and a ruinous damages order. Enforcement made the false-signal strategy a catastrophic losing move, exactly as the payoff analysis predicts it must if a market is to function.

It is worth being explicit about what enforcement is *for*, in game terms, because it is easy to read a conviction as mere punishment after the harm is done. The deeper function is *deterrence through credible commitment*. The reason a clean market can sustain honest price discovery as its equilibrium is that every potential manipulator knows the regulator will, with meaningful probability, detect a scheme and impose a penalty large enough to flip the payoff of cheating negative. The FLC outcome — a high-profile chairman of a well-known group, arrested and given a 21-year sentence — is valuable precisely because it is *visible*: it updates every other potential manipulator's estimate of the detection probability and the penalty severity upward, which raises the expected cost of the ramp move for everyone, not just the one who was caught. A single loud, credible prosecution shifts the whole population's payoff matrix toward honesty. That is why disclosure rules, real-time surveillance, and the willingness to cancel a crooked trade and prosecute its author are not bureaucratic overhead — they are the machinery that makes "trade honestly" the equilibrium rather than the exception.

## Common misconceptions

**"Foreigners are the smart money, so I should just follow the net foreign flow."** Foreign flow is a *signal*, not an oracle. Much of it is passive index money that trades on a calendar, not on a view — when an index review forces buying, that flow contains no information about whether the stock is cheap. And foreigners can be wrong, early, or forced sellers redeeming their own funds. Treat net foreign flow as one input about *who is on the other side and why*, especially around index reviews and room limits, not as a buy/sell button. The years of heavy foreign net selling absorbed by local retail did not all mark tops.

**"If a stock hits its foreign room, the price must keep going up."** A full room caps further *on-screen* foreign buying, which often removes the marginal buyer that was driving the move. It can just as easily mark a local top as a continued squeeze — and the FOL premium can collapse or invert if foreign sentiment turns and departing foreigners undercut each other. Room-locked is a statement about scarcity of *seats*, not a guarantee of direction.

**"T+2 and the price limit protect me by stopping crashes."** The price limit slows a single day's move, but it does not remove selling pressure — it *queues* it. A stock locked at the floor ("sàn") with sellers stacked behind it can grind down the limit for several consecutive days, and you may be unable to exit the whole way down. Combined with margin calls, the structural rules can *trap* a leveraged holder rather than protect them. The band changes the speed, not the destination.

**"Local retail is dumb money, so I'll easily beat them."** Local retail is your counterparty, but "retail" is not one person — it is a large, adaptive, fast-moving crowd that often herds in the same direction you want to. When 80–90% of volume is people like you, the crowd can be *right for a while* and can stay irrational longer than your margin can stay solvent. The edge is not contempt; it is being one level more disciplined and better at spotting when the crowd is being manipulated, not assuming you are automatically smarter.

**"A pump-and-dump is easy money if you get in early and out before the top."** This is the bagholder's fantasy, and it is also a confession of intent to join a crime. Beyond the ethics and the criminal liability — the FLC principals went to prison — the coordination math is brutal: you cannot know when the manipulator dumps, and the manipulator's entire edge is that *they* dump on people who think exactly this. By construction, most chasers are too late. The only winning move against a ramp is skepticism, which is why detection and disclosure are designed to make skepticism rational.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The FLC stealth-sell, January 2022.** The anchor case, covered above: about 74.8 million FLC shares sold without the required disclosure, the SSC's cancellation of the trade, the March 2022 arrest, and the 2024 conviction with a reported 21-year total sentence and large damages. The mechanism — manufactured price-and-volume signal, secret dump, disclosure tripwire, real-time cancellation — is the template for how an emerging-market regulator catches a pump-and-dump, and it is why the disclosure rule for insider sales is load-bearing.

**The room-locked blue chips.** For years, a cluster of the most foreign-favored Vietnamese names traded at persistent FOL premiums because they were chronically room-locked — consumer, retail, and select industrial leaders that global frontier funds wanted and could only buy from each other. The premium was a standing feature of the tape, and watching it widen or compress was a read on foreign appetite. Banks, capped at 30%, hit their lower limit even sooner, which is part of why bank-sector foreign room is among the most closely tracked numbers in the market.

**The FTSE reclassification saga.** Vietnam's long campaign for promotion from FTSE frontier to secondary emerging status is itself a multi-year coordination game. Each review cycle, the market positions on the odds of an upgrade, because a confirmed reclassification would pull a much larger pool of passive emerging-market money toward the likely-included blue chips. The reforms cited as prerequisites — easing the pre-funding requirement, improving settlement, and addressing the room constraints themselves — are precisely the structural features in this post. The anticipation trade around each review is the index-effect game with the highest stakes in the market.

**Margin-cascade selloffs.** Sharp VN-Index drawdowns have repeatedly been amplified by the margin cycle: a fall triggers margin calls on heavily leveraged retail, forced selling pushes prices into the floor band, which triggers more calls. Episodes of consecutive limit-down days in popular speculative names are the visible signature of this loop. Brokers tightening or loosening available margin ("margin room" at the brokerage level) becomes its own watched variable, because it gates how much leverage the retail majority can deploy. The structural game and the manipulation game intersect here: thinly-held, ramped names are exactly the ones where a margin cascade is most violent when the ramp ends.

**Foreign net-selling years absorbed by retail.** In several recent years foreigners were large net sellers on HOSE — 2024 notably so, on the order of tens of trillions of VND, a few billion dollars — while the index held up or rose. The only way both can be true is that local retail bought what foreigners sold. This is the clearest real-world demonstration of "who is on the other side": for a sustained stretch, the marginal Vietnamese stock price was set by domestic individuals stepping in front of a foreign exit. Whether that was brave or foolish depended on the name, but the *identity* of the counterparty was never in doubt.

**The post-2022 enforcement and reform wave.** The FLC case did not happen in isolation; it came amid a broader push by Vietnamese authorities to clean up the capital markets, including high-profile actions in the corporate bond market and a general tightening of disclosure and surveillance. For a market chasing index reclassification, this matters strategically: each credible enforcement action and each structural reform raises the perceived integrity of the market, which is itself a prerequisite for attracting the larger pool of long-term foreign capital that an upgrade would unlock. The games are linked. A cleaner manipulation game (Game 3) and a friendlier structural game (Game 2) are exactly what foreign allocators in Game 1 are waiting on before committing more capital. An investor reading the market through these three games watches not just prices but the *rules*, because a change in the rules changes who will show up to play — and changing who shows up is what re-rates an entire market.

## The playbook: the emerging-market defender's lens

This is where every post in the series lands — not on a recap, but on how you actually play the game. For an emerging market like Vietnam, the playbook is a defender's checklist, because in a retail-dominated, capped, manipulation-prone market your first job is to not be the sucker.

![Emerging-market defender's checklist grid](/imgs/blogs/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games-7.png)

**1. Watch the foreign flows and the room.** Track daily net foreign flow and the *remaining room* on names you care about, and keep the FTSE/MSCI review calendar handy. The room tells you how much of the dominant foreign marginal buyer is left; net flow tells you which direction the large, sometimes price-insensitive bloc is leaning. A shrinking room on a name foreigners love is a different setup from a room-locked name foreigners are fleeing.

**2. Price the FOL premium as a real cost.** If a name is room-locked, understand that foreign entry means paying a premium — historically up to 15% or more — to a departing foreigner via a put-through deal. That premium is your cost of admission and your risk on exit; it is not a number to discover after you trade.

**3. Respect the structure.** Internalize T+2 settlement, the margin cycle, and the ±7% HOSE price band before you size a position. The rules can take your exit decision away from you for days, and leverage turns that into a trap. Size so that a couple of limit-down days plus a margin call cannot force you out at the bottom. The plumbing is not a footnote; it is a constraint on your strategy set.

**4. Know your counterparty.** Assume local retail — leveraged, momentum-driven, herd-prone — is on the other side of your trade until proven otherwise. That is not contempt; it is information. It tells you the market is reflexive, that moves can overshoot in both directions, and that your edge is discipline and one level of extra inference, not raw forecasting horsepower against a non-existent superhuman quant.

**5. Read the manipulation footprints.** Learn to recognize the shape of a ramp without ever participating in one: a thinly-held stock climbing hard on no real news, a controlling insider with an outsized stake, a cluster of *related* tickers moving in lockstep, volume that appears from nowhere. These are the patterns surveillance flags and the patterns you should treat as a reason for *more* skepticism, not less.

**6. Verify the signal before you act on it.** The deepest lesson of the false-signal game is that a rising price plus rising volume is *not* automatically informed buying — it can be a manufactured signal. Check the disclosures and filings. Ask whether the flow is real or injected. Skepticism is the dominant defense, and it is exactly the behavior that disclosure rules and enforcement are built to make rational. The FLC case is the proof: the regulator's tools — the disclosure tripwire, real-time surveillance, the power to cancel an illegitimate trade, and the credible threat of prosecution — exist precisely to change the manipulator's payoffs from +9 to a prison sentence, so that honest price discovery is the equilibrium. Your job as a defender is to behave as though those tools are watching, and to never be the chaser on the wrong side of the dump.

The through-line across all three games is the series' core claim: your edge is not a forecast, it is knowing who is on the other side and what game you are both in. In Vietnam, the board is unusually legible — a hard cap, a fixed clock, a dominant retail crowd, and a regulator that will cancel a crooked trade. Read those rules and you can usually name your counterparty before you ever click buy.
