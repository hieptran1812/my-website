> **TL;DR** — The 2010 Flash Crash was not a glitch and not a single villain; it was a game reaching its equilibrium. Liquidity is a strategic choice, not a constant, and when a price-insensitive sell program made staying in the market a losing bet for every high-frequency market maker at once, their individually rational best response was to step away — so they all stepped away together and the price fell through air.
>
> - The market makers who normally quote both sides of the book provide liquidity **only while it is safe to**. Facing a one-sided toxic flood, withdrawing strictly *dominates* staying for each of them, so the unique Nash equilibrium is "everyone pulls quotes at once."
> - The trigger was a roughly **\$4.1 billion** E-mini S&P 500 futures sell program run by a single mutual-fund complex (Waddell & Reed) through a volume-participation algorithm with **no price limit** — a predictable, price-insensitive opponent that execution-game theory says you must never be.
> - HFTs first absorbed the futures, then passed them among themselves in a **"hot potato"**: volume screamed while real depth collapsed. The gap then spread from futures into the SPY ETF and into roughly 300 individual stocks, some of which printed at **\$0.01** or **\$100,000** stub quotes.

At 2:45 p.m. Eastern on Thursday, May 6, 2010, the Dow Jones Industrial Average was already having a bad day — down a few hundred points on fear that Greece's debt crisis was spreading through Europe. Then, in the space of about five minutes, it lost another six hundred points. By 2:47 p.m. the index was down nearly a thousand points from the previous close, the largest intraday point drop in its history to that moment, a roughly nine-percent fall that erased close to a trillion dollars of market value. Some individual stocks did something even stranger. Shares of Accenture, a large consulting firm worth around \$40 each, traded for a single penny. Sotheby's, the auction house, printed at \$100,000 a share. And then — this is the part that still unsettles people — most of it came back. Within about twenty minutes the Dow had clawed back the bulk of the extra plunge, and by the close it was down about 348 points, a bad but ordinary day.

For weeks afterward the explanation was a guess: a "fat finger," some trader who typed billion where they meant million. That story was wrong. There was no fat finger, no rogue button. What happened, according to the joint report the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) published on September 30, 2010, was that a perfectly ordinary large sell order met a market whose liquidity — the ease of trading without moving the price — turned out to be a *choice* that everyone providing it decided, simultaneously and rationally, to stop making. The book did not break. The book did exactly what game theory says it should: every player took their best response, and the joint best response was a cliff.

This post tells the Flash Crash as a game. The chart below is the mental model for the whole thing: an orderly market, a sell flood hitting a thinning book, market makers pulling their quotes, and the price falling through the empty space they left behind before liquidity returned and snapped it back.

![Stylized share price path on May 6 2010 showing an orderly book then a gap down to a one cent stub quote then a fast recovery](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-2010-flash-crash-and-the-hft-game-1.png)

Here is the shape of the day itself, in the one index everyone watched. The DJIA was already sliding on Europe fears, then fell off a cliff into the ~2:47 p.m. trough at about −998 points (roughly 9%), then took most of that extra drop back within about twenty minutes and closed down a comparatively mundane 348 points. The whole catastrophe and recovery is the narrow spike near the bottom right.

We will build every piece of this from zero — what a high-frequency market maker actually does, what "liquidity" really is, why depth on the screen is a promise rather than a guarantee — then walk the timeline minute by minute as a strategic interaction, compute the payoff matrix that makes "withdraw" the dominant move, and end with how a trader, a long-term investor, and a market designer should each think about a market that can do this. The Sarao spoofing controversy — the trader the U.S. government later charged in connection with the day — gets its own honest footnote near the end: contributing factor or convenient scapegoat, framed so you can tell the difference.

## Foundations: market making, fragmentation, and liquidity as a choice

Before the timeline makes sense, we need four building blocks, each defined plainly for a reader with no finance background. None of them are hard; the whole story is just these four ideas colliding.

### What a market maker actually does

A **market maker (MM)** is a firm whose business is to stand ready to buy and sell a security continuously. It posts two prices at once: a **bid**, the price at which it will buy from you, and an **ask** (also called the offer), the slightly higher price at which it will sell to you. The gap between them is the **bid-ask spread**, and it is the market maker's gross margin. If a market maker buys 100 shares from a seller at a \$40.00 bid and a moment later sells 100 shares to a buyer at a \$40.02 ask, it has earned the two-cent spread — \$2.00 on the round trip — for the service of always being there to trade with. That service is **liquidity**: the ability of someone who wants to trade *now* to find a counterparty *now* without having to move the price much to attract one.

The crucial thing to understand is *why* a market maker is willing to do this. It is not charity. The market maker is betting that the flow of orders it trades against is, on average, balanced and uninformed — a retiree selling here, a 401(k) buying there, a fund rebalancing — random noise that nets out, so the spread it collects on round trips more than covers the risk of holding a position briefly. We covered the full machinery of how this works — inventory risk, the spread as compensation for adverse selection — in [The market maker's game: inventory, the spread, and fear of the informed](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-market-makers-game-inventory-the-spread-and-fear-of-the-informed). The one-line version you need here: a market maker makes money from *two-sided, uninformed* flow and loses money from *one-sided, informed* flow. When everyone wants to sell and no one wants to buy, the market maker is the buyer of last resort, accumulating an inventory that is falling in value with every fill. That is the nightmare. Hold that thought.

### What high-frequency market making added

By 2010, most market making in U.S. equities and futures was done not by humans on a floor but by **high-frequency trading (HFT)** firms — automated systems that submit, modify, and cancel orders in millionths of a second, holding positions for milliseconds to minutes. Names like Citadel Securities, Getco (now part of Virtu), Jump Trading, and Tradebot. These firms are extraordinarily good at the core market-making job: their spreads are tighter than any human's, and on a normal day they make markets far cheaper and deeper than the old specialist system ever did. That is the genuine, measurable benefit HFT brought, and it is why spreads for ordinary investors are a fraction of what they were in the 1990s.

But HFT market makers added one feature the old floor specialists, for all their flaws, did not have to the same degree: **they can disappear instantly.** A human specialist on the New York Stock Exchange floor in 1987 had an affirmative obligation to make a market and a physical body that could not vanish. An HFT firm's quotes are software. When the firm's risk model decides the flow has turned toxic, it cancels every resting order across every exchange in microseconds and stops sending new ones. The same speed that makes HFT liquidity so cheap on a calm day makes it so fragile on a violent one. The depth you see is real right up until the microsecond it isn't. This is the heart of the whole post: **HFT market makers provide liquidity only while it is safe to.** Their entire business depends on the right to leave, and they exercise it at exactly the moment you need them most.

The speed race that made these firms so fast — and the prisoner's-dilemma structure of spending billions on microwave towers to shave microseconds — is its own story, told in [The HFT latency arms race: a prisoner's dilemma at light speed](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-hft-latency-arms-race-a-prisoners-dilemma-at-light-speed). What matters for the Flash Crash is the consequence: the firms that dominate liquidity provision are fast, automated, unobligated, and all running broadly similar risk logic.

### What "fragmented electronic markets" means

In 2010 there was no longer one place where a stock traded. **Fragmentation** means that a single stock's orders are spread across many competing venues — about a dozen public exchanges plus dozens of private "dark pools" — all connected by the speed-of-light race to route orders to wherever the best price sits. The official price, the **National Best Bid and Offer (NBBO)**, is stitched together in real time from all of them. Fragmentation is mostly good — competition among venues drives down fees and spreads — but it has a brittle side. When liquidity gets thin on one venue, orders cascade to the next, and the next, and a localized shortage can become a system-wide one in milliseconds. There is no single circuit on which a human can hit a pause button, because there is no single place.

### Liquidity is a choice, not a constant

Put the three pieces together and you arrive at the idea this whole post turns on. When you look at an order book and see thousands of shares bid and offered a penny apart, it is natural to read that depth as a *property of the market* — like the depth of a swimming pool, just there, a fact. It is not. Every one of those resting orders exists because some specific firm, running some specific risk model, decided a microsecond ago that posting it was a good bet. **Liquidity is the aggregate output of thousands of individual best-response decisions, and every one of those decisions can flip.** The book is not a pool of water; it is a crowd of people who will all leave the room the instant they each, independently, decide it is no longer safe to stay.

That reframing — depth as a strategic equilibrium rather than a physical constant — is the lens for everything that follows. The Flash Crash is what it looks like when the crowd's joint best response flips from "stay" to "leave" all at once.

## The trigger: a price-insensitive whale walks into a nervous market

The match that lit the fire was, on paper, completely mundane: a large investor wanted to sell.

According to the SEC-CFTC report, a single mutual-fund complex — later identified as Waddell & Reed Financial — decided on the afternoon of May 6 to hedge an existing equity position by selling **75,000 E-mini S&P 500 futures contracts**, a notional value of roughly **\$4.1 billion**. The **E-mini** is a futures contract that tracks the S&P 500 index; it is the single most liquid equity-index instrument in the world and the venue where the price of "the whole U.S. stock market" is effectively discovered. Selling \$4.1 billion of it is large but not unheard of — comparable-size programs had been run before without incident.

The problem was *how* they sold it. Rather than work the order patiently with a human trader watching the screen, or with an algorithm that throttles itself when the market is moving against it, the fund used a **volume-participation algorithm** configured to sell at a rate equal to **9% of the trailing one-minute trading volume**, with **no price limit and no time limit**. Let us unpack why each of those three words — *no price limit* — is the entire problem.

A **volume-participation algorithm** (a "percentage-of-volume" or POV algo) tries to sell in proportion to how much everyone else is trading, the logic being that trading more when the market is active hides your footprint in the crowd. That is a reasonable idea in a stable market. But it contains a vicious feedback loop. When prices start falling, panic *increases* trading volume. A 9%-of-volume algo, seeing volume rise, *sells faster*. Selling faster pushes the price down more. The lower price scares more participants into trading, which raises volume again, which makes the algo sell faster again. The throttle was wired to the gas pedal.

And it had **no price limit**, meaning it would keep selling no matter how far the price fell. A human seeing the E-mini drop 3% in two minutes would stop and ask what was going on. This algorithm could not ask. It was, in the language of [Execution as a game: VWAP, TWAP, and hiding from predators](/blog/trading/game-theory/execution-as-a-game-vwap-twap-and-hiding-from-predators), the worst possible thing to be in a market full of fast, adaptive opponents: **predictable and price-insensitive.** Every other participant could infer, within seconds, that a large mechanical seller was in the market and would keep selling regardless of price. That is not a counterparty; that is a free option. And in a strategic interaction, the moment your opponents know you will keep doing the same thing no matter what they do, they will arrange themselves to profit from it.

#### Worked example: why "no price limit" is a different order than it looks

Imagine you must sell 75,000 contracts and the E-mini is at 1,160 index points. Each point is worth \$50 per contract, so one contract is about \$58,000 and 75,000 contracts is about \$4.35 billion of notional (the report's \$4.1B reflects the slightly lower average price as it sold). Suppose normal one-minute volume is 5,000 contracts. At a 9% participation rate, your algo sells about 450 contracts a minute. At that pace, dumping 75,000 contracts takes over two and a half *hours* — fine, if volume stays at 5,000.

Now the feedback loop bites. As your selling pushes the price down, total volume jumps to 25,000 contracts a minute as everyone reacts. Your algo, pegged to 9% of volume, now sells **2,250 contracts a minute** — five times faster — precisely when the market is least able to absorb it. The same nominal order has silently become five times more aggressive at the worst possible moment. The report found the entire 75,000-contract program executed in about **20 minutes**, not hours. A human with a price limit of, say, "don't sell below 1,140" would have paused after the first 1% drop and saved themselves — and the market — the cascade. The price limit you leave off is the brake you remove. The intuition: an order with no price limit is not really a sell order, it is a promise to keep feeding a falling market, and a promise like that is something faster players can trade against.

## The cascade: the hot potato

Now we can run the game forward.

In the first stage, the HFT market makers did exactly what they are supposed to do: they bought. As the Waddell & Reed algo hit the E-mini with sells, the HFTs on the other side absorbed the contracts, accumulating a long position. This is the normal, valuable function — they were providing liquidity, taking the other side, betting the selling was temporary noise. For a minute or two, the system worked as designed.

But the HFT market makers had no desire to *hold* a large, growing long position in a falling market. Their business model is to be flat — to buy and immediately re-sell, capturing the spread, ending each second with little net inventory. So as their inventory built up, they did what their risk models told them to do: they sold the contracts back into the market to get flat. And who bought them? Other HFTs, who also did not want to hold them, and who turned around and sold them again. The same contracts bounced from one high-frequency firm to another, each holding them for a sliver of a second before passing them on. The SEC-CFTC report described this vividly: in the roughly **14 seconds** between 2:45:13 and 2:45:27 p.m., HFTs traded **over 27,000 E-mini contracts**, accounting for about **49% of total volume**, while *net* buying only about **200 contracts.** They were trading enormous size with each other and absorbing almost none of the actual selling pressure.

This is the **"hot potato"** effect, and it is the single most counterintuitive thing about the Flash Crash. Volume — the number of contracts changing hands — exploded. If you were watching a volume ticker, the market looked *incredibly* liquid, more active than ever. But **real liquidity — the willingness to *absorb* the one-directional selling and hold it — had collapsed.** The contracts were a hot potato being tossed around a shrinking circle of players, none of whom would hold them. The chart below shows the divergence that defines the whole episode: transacted volume spiking while the resting depth that represents genuine willingness to buy quietly drains away.

![Grouped bars showing E-mini transacted volume spiking while resting buy-side depth collapses across the phases of the crash](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-2010-flash-crash-and-the-hft-game-4.png)

The lesson hiding in that chart is one every trader should tattoo somewhere visible: **volume is not liquidity.** Volume measures how much trading happened. Liquidity measures whether *you* can trade a meaningful size right now without moving the price. On May 6, the two numbers pointed in opposite directions, and anyone reading the volume ticker as a liquidity gauge was reading a broken instrument.

#### Worked example: counting the hot potato

Let us make the 27,000-contracts-but-only-200-net figure concrete, because it is the numerical heart of the cascade. Suppose in a 14-second window the HFTs as a group buy 13,600 contracts and sell 13,400 contracts. Total volume from their activity: 13,600 + 13,400 = 27,000 contracts traded. Net position change: 13,600 − 13,400 = **200 contracts** absorbed. So for every 27,000 contracts of activity, only 200 represented genuine absorption of the seller's flow — a net-to-gross ratio of about **0.7%.** Put another way, 99.3% of the frantic activity was contracts circulating among makers who refused to hold them.

Now compare what the seller needed. The Waddell & Reed algo was trying to offload thousands of contracts. If the HFTs are net-absorbing 200 contracts per 14 seconds, that is roughly 850 contracts a minute of genuine absorption — while the algo, accelerating on rising volume, was firing far faster than that. The arithmetic does not balance, and when supply of sells permanently exceeds the demand willing to *hold* them, the only variable left to move is price. It falls until either a price-insensitive buyer appears or the sellers run out. The intuition: a market with huge volume and tiny net absorption is a market that *looks* deep and *is* hollow, and the gap between those two is exactly how far the price has to fall.

### From futures to ETFs to stocks

The crash did not stay in the futures pit. It spread, and the way it spread is a clean illustration of how tightly modern markets are wired together by arbitrage.

When the E-mini futures gapped down, they became *cheap* relative to the actual basket of stocks they track. That gap is money to an **index arbitrageur** — a trader who profits by buying the cheap side and selling the expensive side of two equivalent things. With futures suddenly cheap, arbitrageurs bought futures and *sold* the equivalent exposure in the cash market, most directly through the **SPDR S&P 500 ETF (ticker SPY)** — an exchange-traded fund (ETF) that holds the S&P 500 stocks and trades like a single stock. So the selling pressure jumped a rail, from futures into the most liquid ETF.

Then it jumped again. An ETF has its own arbitrage mechanism: **authorized participants (APs)** keep the ETF's price glued to the value of its underlying stocks (its net asset value, or NAV) by creating or redeeming ETF shares. When SPY fell below the value of the stocks it held, APs sold the individual underlying stocks to capture the difference. So the selling pressure jumped a second rail, from the ETF into roughly **300 individual stocks and ETFs.** The pipeline below traces that contagion path, from one price-insensitive sell program to thousands of single-name stub quotes.

![Pipeline showing the crash spreading from the E-mini sell program through index arbitrage to the SPY ETF and through AP arbitrage to single stocks](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-2010-flash-crash-and-the-hft-game-5.png)

The arbitrage links that normally keep all these instruments in lockstep — and that are, on a calm day, a wonderful source of price efficiency — became the transmission cables of the crash. Each link was, individually, a rational trade. Buy the cheap futures, sell the expensive ETF. Sell the rich ETF, buy the cheap stocks. No one in that chain was doing anything irrational or manipulative. They were each taking their local best response. And the sum of all those local best responses was a shock propagating from a single futures contract into thousands of stocks in under five minutes.

## The withdrawal: when staying becomes the losing game

Now we reach the equilibrium — the moment the game tips. Up to this point the HFT market makers had been buying, then hot-potatoing. But as the selling refused to stop and their inventory of falling assets refused to clear, each firm's risk model arrived at the same conclusion: **the flow has turned toxic, and continuing to make a market in it is a losing game.**

A market maker can survive being wrong occasionally. What it cannot survive is being the standing buyer in a flood of informed, one-directional selling — because every share it buys is falling in value, and there is no offsetting buy flow to let it get flat. In game-theory language, the flow had become **adverse selection** of the worst kind: everyone trading against the market maker knew (or behaved as if they knew) that the right move was to sell, so every fill the market maker got made it poorer. The rational response to flow you are certain is toxic is not to widen your spread a little. It is to *leave.* Cancel the bids. Stop quoting. Wait until the storm passes and you can tell uninformed flow from informed flow again.

And here is the trap that makes the Flash Crash a *game* rather than a *panic*: **every market maker reasons this way at the same time, and each one's incentive to leave is made stronger by the others leaving.** If you are the last market maker still posting bids while everyone else has pulled, you do not get a medal — you get to absorb the *entire* remaining flood by yourself. So the safest move, once you suspect the others are about to leave, is to leave first. The exits do not happen in sequence; they happen in a rush, because being the last one out is catastrophic.

We can write this as a payoff matrix and find its equilibrium. Consider two representative market makers, A and B, in the middle of a toxic one-sided sell flood. Each chooses to **keep quoting** (continue providing liquidity) or **withdraw** (pull quotes and stand aside). The matrix below uses stylized payoffs in cents-per-share of profit, computed so that the game has the structure the report describes; the equilibrium is solved with the series' `nash_2x2` helper.

![Two by two payoff matrix for the market maker quote or withdraw decision showing withdraw as the dominant strategy and both withdraw as the Nash equilibrium](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-2010-flash-crash-and-the-hft-game-3.png)

#### Worked example: solving the quote-or-withdraw game

Read the matrix one cell at a time. Payoffs are written as (A's payoff, B's payoff) in cents per share.

- **Both keep quoting** — they split the toxic flow between them. Each still eats adverse selection, but the load is shared: **(−4, −4).**
- **A keeps, B withdraws** — A is the last one standing and absorbs the entire flood alone, a catastrophic inventory: **(−12, 0).** B, having stepped aside, loses nothing.
- **A withdraws, B keeps** — the mirror image: **(0, −12).**
- **Both withdraw** — neither holds any toxic inventory, so each is flat: **(0, 0).** But notice what just happened to the *market*: with both makers gone, there are no real bids left. The price falls through air. The cost did not vanish — it landed on the seller and on anyone whose order hit the empty book.

Now find each player's best response. Look at A. If B keeps quoting, A compares keeping (−4) to withdrawing (0); withdrawing is better. If B withdraws, A compares keeping (−12) to withdrawing (0); withdrawing is better. **Withdrawing is better for A no matter what B does** — it is a *dominant strategy.* By symmetry, the same is true for B. So both withdraw. Feeding this matrix to `nash_2x2` returns a single pure-strategy Nash equilibrium at *(withdraw, withdraw)* and no mixed equilibrium — the outcome is not a coin flip, it is a certainty.

Here is the unsettling part, and the whole moral of the episode. The Nash equilibrium *(0, 0)* is not bad for the market makers — they each end up flat, unharmed. It is bad for *the market.* The outcome the seller desperately needed — both makers staying and absorbing the flow at *(−4, −4)* — is the one outcome the game's incentives will never produce, because each maker can do better for itself by leaving. The intuition: when every individual's best response is to step away, the market does not gently thin out — it *gaps,* because liquidity is not a thing that degrades smoothly, it is a coordination outcome that collapses all at once.

This is a different beast from the [HFT latency arms race prisoner's dilemma](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-hft-latency-arms-race-a-prisoners-dilemma-at-light-speed), and the difference is worth seeing. In the arms race, the bad equilibrium hurts the *players themselves* (they all overspend on speed). Here, the equilibrium is fine for the players and devastating for a *third party* (the seller and the public). The makers did nothing wrong by their own lights; they protected their capital exactly as their fiduciary duty to their own firms demands. The catastrophe is structural — it lives in the rules of the game, not in the character of the players.

## The breakdown: stub quotes and a penny

When the real market makers pulled their bids, the order books did not become empty in a way the trading system could recognize as "halt everything." They became empty except for **stub quotes** — placeholder orders that exchange rules required market makers to post so that there was technically *always* a quote, but priced so far from reality that they would never be hit in normal conditions. A stub bid might be **\$0.01.** A stub ask might be **\$100,000.** They exist as a formality, a "I am technically here" marker, never meant to actually trade.

On May 6, with the real quotes gone, the stub quotes were all that was left. And a **market order** — an instruction to "sell at the best available price, whatever it is" — does exactly what it says. With no real bid in the book, a market sell order to dump shares found the only bid available: the one-cent stub. So Accenture, a roughly \$40 stock, printed trades at **\$0.01.** Shares of other large companies printed at similarly absurd prices. On the other side, market *buy* orders with no real ask available climbed all the way up to \$100,000 stub asks — which is how Sotheby's, a stock worth around \$34, printed at **\$100,000.** The chart below contrasts a healthy two-sided book with the stub-quote wreckage that replaced it.

![Before and after comparison of a tight two sided order book versus a book with only penny stub quotes left](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-2010-flash-crash-and-the-hft-game-6.png)

This is the visible scar of liquidity-as-a-choice. A stub-quote print is not a market saying "this stock is worth a penny." It is a market saying "no one is choosing to make a real price right now, so your order fell into the void and hit a placeholder." The price did not *discover* anything; it simply ran out of real quotes and printed against the formal floor.

The aftermath was a mess that took days to clean up. The exchanges and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) agreed to **cancel ("bust") roughly 20,000 trades** that had executed at prices more than 60% away from their 2:40 p.m. levels — the trades that were so obviously the product of a broken book that letting them stand would have been indefensible. But that cutoff was itself arbitrary and contentious: a trade that filled 59% away from fair value stood, and the investor ate the loss; a trade 61% away was reversed, and the investor was made whole. Two investors who did the identical thing — placed a market sell into the void — got opposite outcomes based on a line drawn after the fact. Which is its own quiet lesson about the fragility of "the price" when the makers walk.

#### Worked example: the cost of a stop-loss in a vacuum

Here is how this reached ordinary people, not just algorithms. Suppose you owned 200 shares of a \$40 stock and, being a careful investor, you had placed a **stop-loss order** — an instruction that automatically sells if the price falls to a trigger, say \$36, to protect you from a big decline. A stop-loss, when triggered, typically converts into a *market* order: sell at whatever price is available.

On a normal day this is prudent. On May 6, when your stock briefly traded down through \$36 as the cascade hit, your stop triggered, became a market sell order, and went looking for a bid. But the real bids were gone. The only bid in the book was the **\$0.01** stub. Your 200 shares sold for **\$2.00 total** — a position worth \$8,000 a minute earlier, liquidated for the price of a gumball. Two minutes later the stock was back near \$40, but you were already out, your shares gone to whatever opportunistic buyer's resting low-ball order or stub got filled. If your trade happened to fall outside the 60% bust threshold, you simply ate it. The protective tool — the stop-loss — became the weapon, because it forced a market order into a market that had momentarily ceased to exist. The intuition: a stop-loss is a market order you have pre-committed to send at the worst possible moment, and in a liquidity vacuum a market order is a blank check.

## The game-theory lesson: liquidity is an equilibrium, and equilibria can collapse

Step back from the minute-by-minute and look at the structure, because the structure is the lesson.

A liquid market is a **coordination equilibrium.** Many market makers each post tight, deep quotes *because* the others do — a market where everyone provides liquidity is a market where adverse selection is diluted across many participants, so providing liquidity is profitable, so everyone keeps doing it. It is self-reinforcing in the good direction. This is the same mathematical structure as a **bank run**, the classic coordination game where depositors leave their money in the bank as long as they believe other depositors will too, and rush to withdraw the instant they fear others will — covered as a game in the bank-run and coordination literature this series draws on. There are two equilibria: a good one where everyone stays, and a bad one where everyone runs. Both are self-consistent. Which one you land in depends on *beliefs about what everyone else will do.*

The Flash Crash was a liquidity run. The price-insensitive selling shifted every market maker's belief from "the others will keep quoting, so I will too" to "the others are about to pull, so I must pull first." Once that belief flipped, the bad equilibrium was as stable and self-consistent as the good one had been. **Liquidity did not erode; it switched states.** That is why the move was a gap and not a glide. Coordination equilibria do not degrade gracefully — they tip.

#### Worked example: the coordination payoff that makes both states stable

Let us put numbers on why both the deep-market and the dried-up-market states are genuine equilibria. Model a single market maker deciding whether to post a tight bid. The payoff to posting depends on the *fraction* `f` of other makers who also post.

- Say posting earns you a spread of **+3 cents** per round trip when liquidity is abundant (most others post, `f` high), because flow is diluted and mostly uninformed.
- But posting *costs* you **−9 cents** when you are nearly alone (`f` low), because you absorb concentrated toxic flow.
- Model your expected payoff from posting as roughly `12·f − 9` cents: at `f = 1` (everyone posts) you make `12 − 9 = +3`; at `f = 0` (you are alone) you lose `−9`; the break-even is at `f = 0.75`.

Now the logic of two equilibria. If you believe `f` is high — almost everyone is posting — your best response is to post, which *makes* `f` high. That belief is self-fulfilling: the deep market is an equilibrium. If you believe `f` is low — almost everyone has pulled — your best response is to *not* post (you would lose 9 cents), which *makes* `f` low. That belief is also self-fulfilling: the dried-up market is equally an equilibrium. The break-even at `f = 0.75` is the knife's edge between them. The price-insensitive flood on May 6 was a shove that pushed collective belief from above the knife's edge to below it. The intuition: with two stable equilibria, the market's depth is not determined by fundamentals alone — it is determined by what every maker believes every other maker is about to do, and a big enough shock can flip that belief in seconds.

This is also why "I'll just get out before the crash completes" is so much harder than it sounds, a point we return to in the misconceptions below. You are not racing the price; you are racing *every other participant's decision to pull*, and they are all faster than you.

## How regulators broke the equilibrium: circuit breakers and LULD

If the problem is that every market maker's best response is to leave at once, the fix is not to lecture them about civic duty — it is to change the rules so the leave-all-at-once cascade cannot complete. You do that with a **forced pause.** If you halt trading the instant price moves too far too fast, you interrupt the feedback loop before it reaches stub quotes, and you buy time for the makers to look at a stable book, reassess, and come back. A pause turns a self-reinforcing run into a moment for re-coordination.

That is exactly the direction U.S. regulators took. The timeline below traces the fixes, each one a mechanism for breaking the everyone-step-away equilibrium.

![Timeline of regulatory fixes after the flash crash from single stock circuit breakers in 2010 to limit up limit down in 2013 and updated market wide breakers](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-2010-flash-crash-and-the-hft-game-7.png)

The first patch, rolled out in **June 2010**, was **single-stock circuit breakers**: if an individual stock's price moved more than **10% within a five-minute window**, trading in that stock paused for five minutes. Crude, but it stopped a single name from snowballing all the way to a stub quote.

In **2013** that was replaced by the more elegant **Limit-Up Limit-Down (LULD)** mechanism. Instead of halting trading outright, LULD defines a moving price band — roughly **±5% to ±10%** around the recent average price, depending on the stock — and forbids trades and quotes *outside* the band. If the price wants to move beyond the band, it enters a brief (usually 15-second) "limit state," and if it cannot pull back inside, trading pauses. The genius of LULD is that it makes a stub-quote print *structurally impossible*: a one-cent trade in a \$40 stock is wildly outside any band, so the system simply will not let it happen. It does not rely on busting trades after the fact; it prevents the absurd print in the first place.

Alongside the single-stock tools, the **market-wide circuit breakers** — which halt the *entire* market — were recalibrated in 2013 to trigger on S&P 500 declines of **7%, 13%, and 20%** from the prior close (a 7% or 13% drop pauses the whole market for 15 minutes; a 20% drop closes it for the day). These are the big red switches for a true market-wide cascade, as opposed to a single-name break.

#### Worked example: what a circuit breaker is worth in game terms

Why does a pause change the equilibrium rather than just delay the inevitable? Recall the quote-or-withdraw matrix, where withdrawing dominated because *being the last one quoting* costs you −12 cents. The entire reason to flee is the fear of absorbing the flood alone while everyone else escapes.

Now insert a circuit breaker. The moment the price moves 10%, trading *stops for everyone simultaneously.* During the halt, no one can absorb flow, so no one can be the sucker left holding it — the −12 payoff is taken off the table. When trading resumes, every maker faces a *fresh* book at a known reference price, and the question is no longer "will the others pull before me in the next microsecond" but "given a clean restart, is it now safe to post?" The pause converts a real-time race-to-the-exit into a discrete, coordinated re-entry. Suppose the halt lets the price-insensitive seller's order get worked off in the meantime (or lets a human override the algo, which is exactly what a price limit would have done). Then the toxic flow is gone, posting earns +3 again, and the good equilibrium re-forms. The intuition: a circuit breaker is worth precisely the −12 catastrophe it removes — by guaranteeing no one can be the last one quoting, it removes everyone's reason to flee first.

The fixes are not free. A halt can also trap you — if you wanted to sell during a genuine, fundamental crash, a pause keeps you from getting out, and LULD bands can themselves cause cascading mini-halts on a fast day. They are a tradeoff, not a cure. But they directly target the structural flaw the Flash Crash exposed: they make "everyone leaves at once" something the market mechanically cannot finish.

## Common misconceptions

**"It was a fat finger — someone typed billion instead of million."** This was the first story and it is false. The SEC-CFTC report found no erroneous order, no typo, no rogue trade. The triggering sell program was a deliberately chosen \$4.1 billion hedge executed by a legitimate fund. The catastrophe came not from the *size* of the order — which was large but ordinary — but from its *execution*: a price-insensitive, accelerating algorithm meeting a market whose liquidity providers all rationally fled. There was no villain to fire. There was a game with a bad equilibrium.

**"HFT firms caused the crash by pulling out — they should be forced to keep quoting."** This conflates the *trigger* with the *amplifier* and misreads the incentives. The HFTs did not start the selling; a mutual fund's algo did. And the HFTs that withdrew were behaving exactly as any rational, capital-protecting firm must when flow turns demonstrably toxic — staying would have been negligence toward their own investors. The deeper point: an "obligation to quote" without an obligation to quote *good prices* just produces stub quotes, which is what already existed and which made things worse. You cannot legislate away a dominant strategy by command; you have to change the payoffs (which is what circuit breakers do). The honest verdict from the report is that HFTs were not the cause but did amplify the move once it started — a distinction worth holding onto.

**"I would have just sold before the bottom — or bought the penny prints."** Two fantasies, both defeated by the same fact: you were not the only one with that plan, and you were slower than everyone who mattered. Selling "before the bottom" required you to act in the ~five-minute window when prices were in free fall and your market sell order would itself print into the vacuum — quite possibly at the bottom you were trying to avoid. And the people who *did* buy the penny prints mostly had their trades busted: about 20,000 trades more than 60% from fair value were cancelled, so the dream of buying Accenture at a penny and keeping it was, for most, reversed within days. The coordination math is unforgiving: in a liquidity run, your timing edge is competing against firms measuring their reactions in microseconds.

**"The recovery proves it was harmless — nothing really happened."** The fast recovery shows the *prices* were nonsense, not that the *event* was harmless. Real investors with stop-loss orders were sold out at the bottom and did not get to ride the recovery. Confidence in market structure took a lasting hit. And the recovery itself was partly luck: had the cascade triggered just before the close with no time to reverse, or had the busted-trade cleanup gone differently, the damage would have been permanent. "It bounced back" is what survivorship bias sounds like when a coordination equilibrium happens to flip back to the good state in time.

**"Stub quotes mean the market thought the stock was worth a penny."** A stub quote is the opposite of a valuation — it is the *absence* of one. It is a formal placeholder that exists precisely so the book is never technically empty, priced where it should never trade. A penny print is the market reporting "no one chose to make a real price here, and your order hit the floor," not "consensus value: \$0.01." Reading a stub print as information is reading static as a signal.

## How it shows up in real markets: the flash event becomes a genre

The 2010 Flash Crash was the first famous one, but the structural flaw it exposed — liquidity as a fragile coordination equilibrium that can flip in seconds — recurs across every electronic market. A few of the most instructive later episodes:

**The August 24, 2015 ETF "flash crash."** On a turbulent Monday open, dozens of large, normally liquid ETFs briefly traded far below the value of the stocks they held — some down 30% to 40% intraday — even though the underlying stocks were down far less. The mechanism was the Flash Crash in miniature: at the open, many underlying stocks had not yet started trading (their own circuit breakers and opening auctions had not resolved), so ETF market makers could not reliably price or hedge the ETFs, so they *widened or pulled* their quotes. LULD halts, designed to help, fired hundreds of times and in some cases worsened the dislocation by freezing the hedging instruments. The lesson repeated: when makers cannot price risk, they leave, and the price gaps to wherever the stub-adjacent orders sit.

**The October 15, 2014 Treasury "flash rally."** This one hit the most important market in the world — U.S. Treasuries — and demolished the comfortable belief that government bonds are too deep and important to flash. In a roughly 12-minute window, the 10-year Treasury yield plunged and round-tripped with no clear news, a move that "should" happen once in many billions of years under a normal-distribution model. The official interagency report again found no single cause but heavy involvement of automated, self-trading liquidity that thinned dramatically just as volume spiked — the hot potato, in bonds. The point: the fragility is not about "risky" assets; it is about *market structure.* Any fast, fragmented, automated market can do this.

**The May 2010 cascade's own ETF damage.** Worth noting that on the original day, ETFs were disproportionately hit — roughly two-thirds of the busted trades were in ETFs. ETFs sit downstream of two arbitrage links (futures-to-ETF and ETF-to-stocks), so they absorbed the cascade from both directions. This is why ETF liquidity, which looks bottomless on a calm day, is one of the first things to evaporate in a stress event: the ETF is only as liquid as the arbitrage that connects it to its underlying, and that arbitrage is the first thing to break.

**Single-name flash crashes are routine.** Far below the headlines, individual stocks flash-crash regularly — a large market sell hits a thin book at an odd hour, LULD fires, the price snaps back. These rarely make the news because the circuit breakers now catch them, which is the system working as designed. Every one of them is the same game: a price-insensitive order meets makers who would rather leave than absorb, and the pause is what keeps the gap from completing.

**The crypto parallel.** Decentralized and 24/7 crypto markets, which have no circuit breakers, show the un-patched version of this game constantly: a large market order hits a thin order book or a low-liquidity automated market maker, the price gaps violently, cascading liquidations of leveraged positions add more one-directional flow, and the "liquidity" that looked deep at calm prices proves to be a few makers who vanish under stress. The mechanics of how on-chain liquidity is provided and withdrawn — and how it gets sandwiched and front-run — are covered in this series' on-chain and MEV material; the game-theory skeleton is identical to May 6, 2010, just without the pause button.

## A footnote on Sarao: contributing factor or scapegoat?

No telling of the Flash Crash is complete without Navinder Singh Sarao, the London-based trader who, in 2015, was arrested and later pleaded guilty to charges connected to the day. Sarao engaged in **spoofing** — placing large sell orders he intended to cancel, to create a false impression of selling pressure and push prices down, then profiting from the moves. (Spoofing as a deception-and-signaling game, and how to *detect* rather than perform it, is the subject of [Spoofing, layering, and manipulation as signaling games](/blog/trading/game-theory/spoofing-layering-and-manipulation-as-signaling-games).) The U.S. Department of Justice alleged his spoofing in the E-mini contributed to the conditions on May 6.

The honest assessment, which the careful sources support, is that Sarao is best understood as a *contributing irritant that got disproportionately blamed*, not the cause. His spoofing was real and illegal, and it did inject extra fake selling pressure into an already-stressed E-mini book. But the SEC-CFTC report — written years before his arrest — had already located the mechanism in the Waddell & Reed algo and the liquidity withdrawal, with no need for Sarao to make the story work. Spoofing happens constantly; it did not single-handedly knock a trillion dollars off the market in five minutes. Treating Sarao as *the* Flash Crash villain is satisfying — a person to point at instead of a structural flaw — but it is the same impulse as the "fat finger" myth: the search for a single bad actor in what was actually a bad equilibrium. The detection-and-defense framing matters here: spoofing is worth policing because it degrades the quality of the signals everyone trades on, not because any one spoofer can reliably trigger a systemic crash.

## The playbook: how to play a market that can vanish

The Flash Crash is a case study, not a strategy, but it carries hard, usable lessons about *how to position yourself* in markets where liquidity is a choice that others can revoke. Here is the playbook, organized the way this series always organizes it — who is on the other side, what game you are in, your edge, your invalidation, and your sizing.

**Who is on the other side of your "liquidity."** When you see a deep, tight book and feel safe putting in a large market order, your counterparty is a fleet of automated market makers whose entire business depends on the right to disappear the instant your flow looks toxic to them. They are not obligated to be there. The depth you are relying on is the sum of their best-response decisions, every one of which can flip in microseconds. You are not trading against "the market"; you are trading against thousands of risk models that will all reach the same conclusion at the same time if the conditions are right.

**The game you are in.** It is a coordination game whose good equilibrium (everyone provides liquidity) you benefit from for free on 99.9% of days, and whose bad equilibrium (everyone withdraws) you pay for catastrophically on the rare day it flips. Your job is not to predict the flip — you can't, reliably — but to make sure a flip cannot ruin you.

**Your edge — which is mostly defense.** You will not out-react the HFTs in a flash event; do not try. Your edge is structural humility:

- **Use limit orders, not market orders, for anything you care about.** A *limit order* — "buy at \$40.00 or better, sell at \$36.00 or better, but no worse" — refuses to print into a vacuum. The stop-losses that got filled at a penny on May 6 were market orders in disguise. A limit sell at \$36 would simply have gone unfilled in the vacuum and lived to fight at \$40 two minutes later. The single most protective habit you can build is to never hand the book a blank check.
- **Treat depth on the screen as a promise, not a guarantee.** Size your orders to the liquidity you would expect under *stress*, not the liquidity you see on a calm screen. If a name only has real depth because three HFTs are quoting it, assume that depth is zero in a panic.
- **Be the opposite of the Waddell & Reed algo.** Never be predictable and price-insensitive. If you must move size, vary your pace, use price limits, and do not let an algorithm keep feeding a market that is moving against you. The execution-as-a-game post has the full treatment, but the one rule is: an order with no price limit is a gift to faster opponents.
- **Respect the queue and the make-take game.** Where the depth comes from — who is ahead of you in line, who is paying or being paid to provide it — is covered in [The order book as a battlefield: queue priority and the make-take game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-order-book-as-a-battlefield-queue-priority-and-the-make-take-game). The relevant takeaway for stress: rebate-seeking liquidity is the first to vanish, because it was never there to take risk in the first place.

**Your invalidation — the signs the equilibrium is flipping.** The warning that liquidity is about to switch states is *not* falling prices; it is falling *depth* while volume rises. If you can see it, a collapsing order book with a spiking volume ticker is the hot potato forming. Most retail platforms hide depth, which is precisely why retail orders are the ones that get filled at a penny — you cannot see the vacuum you are about to fall into.

**Your sizing and exit.** The discipline is to size every position so that a Flash-Crash-style 10% gap that reverses in twenty minutes is survivable without forced action. That means: no stop-losses that become market orders in thin names; no leverage that triggers a margin liquidation on a transient spike; no position so large that you would *need* to hit a market order into a stressed book. The investors hurt on May 6 were overwhelmingly the ones who had pre-committed to act automatically at the worst moment — stops that fired, margins that called. The ones who were unharmed were the ones who could simply do nothing for twenty minutes and let the equilibrium flip back. In a market that can momentarily cease to exist, **the ability to do nothing is a position, and it is often the winning one.**

The deepest lesson of May 6, 2010 is not about HFT, or algorithms, or any villain. It is that the liquidity we treat as a permanent feature of modern markets is actually a fragile agreement among self-interested players to all keep showing up — an agreement that holds beautifully right up until the moment each player independently decides it is safer to leave, at which point they all leave together and the price falls through the space where the market used to be. Build your trading so that you are never the one standing in that space when it opens.
