> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The market does not move when everyone *knows* something; it moves when everyone knows that everyone knows, because only then is it safe for each trader to act on it. That second thing is **common knowledge**, and the gap between it and mere private knowing is who is on the other side of half the violent moves you have ever seen.
>
> - **First-order knowledge** is "I know X". **Common knowledge** is "I know X, and I know you know X, and I know you know I know X, forever." A public signal is what closes that infinite chain in one stroke.
> - A fact that *every* trader privately knows is **mutual knowledge**, not common knowledge. It can sit unpriced for weeks because no one trusts the others to act on it first.
> - A Fed statement or an earnings print moves price even when "everyone already knew the number" because the announcement converts mutual into common knowledge and *lets everyone reposition at the same instant*.
> - "Priced in" is not a fact about information existing — it is a fact about coordination having already happened. If the coordination has not happened, the information is not priced in, no matter how widely it is known.
> - **The one rule:** watch for the moment private information becomes public. That conversion — not the information itself — is when the positioning unwinds, and it is the most predictable violent move in markets.

On 13 March 2023, every serious bank-watcher on Wall Street already knew that Silicon Valley Bank was sitting on enormous unrealized losses in its bond portfolio. The numbers were in the 10-K. Anyone who wanted to read them could read them. And for months, nothing happened — the stock drifted, deposits sat, the world turned. Then, over roughly forty-eight hours, the bank died. What changed was not the *information*. The losses had been there the whole time. What changed was that a specific set of venture-capital firms, talking to each other on group chats, simultaneously realized that *everyone else was realizing the same thing* — and at that moment, sitting still stopped being safe. The run was not caused by new facts. It was caused by old facts becoming **common knowledge**.

This is one of the deepest and least-taught ideas in all of market microstructure, and it is the reason for a phenomenon every trader has cursed: the news comes out, the number is *exactly* what the consensus expected, and the price still rips fifty handles. "But everyone already knew," you say. Right — everyone knew. But until the print hit the tape, no one was *sure that everyone else knew*, and no one was sure that everyone else was *sure*. The public release did not deliver information. It delivered **certainty about other people's certainty**, and that is the thing that lets a crowd move together.

This post builds the idea from absolute zero — what it means to know something, what it means to know that someone else knows it, and why that recursion does not bottom out — using the classic puzzles that game theorists invented to pin it down: the blue-eyed islanders, the two generals trying to coordinate an attack, the dirty-face problem. Then we take it to the only place that matters here: the tape. Why a Fed statement moves a "fully priced-in" market. Why bubbles and bank runs are common-knowledge events. And how to position around the single most predictable thing in trading — the moment a private fact goes public.

![The ladder of belief from I know to common knowledge](/imgs/blogs/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model. Knowledge is not a switch that is on or off; it is a *ladder*. The bottom rung is "I know the fact." The next rung is "I know you know it." Above that, "I know you know I know it." Most of the time, traders are scattered across the lower rungs, each privately confident but unsure of the others — and that is a state in which no one dares move. Common knowledge is the top rung, where the chain of "I-know-that-you-know" runs all the way up with no end. The thing that gets a whole market from the lower rungs to the top, in one instant, is a **public signal** that everyone sees and everyone sees everyone see. Hold that picture; the rest of the post is just filling in every rung.

## Foundations: what it means to know, to know that you know, and to all know forever

Let us define everything from scratch, because the whole edifice rests on three terms that sound like the same thing and are not.

**First-order knowledge** is the plain kind. *I know X.* The 10-K says SVB has \$15 billion of unrealized losses; I read it; I know it. That is it — one person, one fact. In game theory we call this **private information** when others do not have it, and the entire field of asymmetric-information trading (who knows what the other side does not) is built on it. But first-order knowledge alone tells you nothing about what *other* people know.

**Second-order knowledge** is one level up. *I know that you know X.* I read the 10-K, and I also know that you, a competent analyst, surely read it too. Now I know something about your head, not just about the bank. This matters enormously for action, because what you do depends on what *you* know, so to predict you I need to model your knowledge.

**Third-order, fourth-order, and onward.** *I know that you know that I know X.* *I know that you know that I know that you know X.* Each rung adds one layer of "I-know-that-you-know." In everyday life we rarely climb past the second or third rung explicitly, but the rungs are real, and — crucially — they do not have to all be true at once. I can know X (rung 1) while being genuinely unsure whether you know X (rung 2 fails). That mismatch is the whole game.

**Mutual knowledge** is when *everyone* knows X — every single person has rung-1 knowledge — but the higher rungs may be missing. Every trader privately knows the inflation print will probably be hot. But no trader is *sure* the others know, or sure the others will act, or sure the others are sure the others will act. Mutual knowledge is a pile of private certainties that have not been stitched together.

**Common knowledge** is the top of the ladder: everyone knows X, *and* everyone knows that everyone knows X, *and* everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone knows X, and so on without end. The chain never terminates. This is a much stronger condition than mutual knowledge, and the gap between them is the single most important distinction in this entire post.

Here is the cleanest way to feel the difference. Suppose ten traders are in a room, each handed a sealed envelope containing the same true fact. Now every one of them *knows* the fact — that is mutual knowledge, perfect and complete. But not one of them knows that the *others* received the same envelope. So if acting on the fact only pays off when everyone acts together, no one moves. Now the moderator stands up and reads the fact aloud. Nothing new was learned about the *fact* — they all already knew it. But everything changed about the *coordination*, because now each trader knows the others heard it, and knows the others know he heard it, and so on up the ladder. *That* announcement is what unlocks the simultaneous move.

### Why the recursion does not bottom out

A reader new to this always asks: surely after three or four levels the difference stops mattering? In casual life, yes. In any situation that requires *coordinated* action — where my best move depends on whether you will move, which depends on whether you think I will move — the recursion is load-bearing all the way up. The reason is that coordination is a fixed point: I attack only if you attack, you attack only if I attack, and to be confident at the top I need confidence at every level below. Knock out any rung and the whole tower of "it is safe to move" collapses. We will prove this with the two-generals problem in a moment, where you can watch each missing rung kill the coordination concretely.

In essence, the question is never "does the market know?" — it almost always does. The question is "has the market's knowledge climbed the ladder to common knowledge, so that everyone is willing to act on it at once?" The diagram above lays out the rungs; the rest of the post shows you how to spot which rung the market is standing on, and how to trade the jump to the top.

### The formal definition, in plain English

Game theorists pin common knowledge down precisely, and the precise version is worth a paragraph because it tells you exactly what to look for in a market. A fact X is **common knowledge among a group** when an infinite list of statements is all true at once: everyone knows X; everyone knows that everyone knows X; everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone knows X; and so on without end. Mutual knowledge is only the *first* line of that list. Common knowledge is the *whole* list. The reason the whole list matters is the fixed-point structure we just met: to be willing to act on X in a coordination problem, I need line two (I need to know you will act, which means you know X). But to be *confident* in line two I need line three (I need to know that you know that I know, so that you are confident I will act). The need cascades all the way down the list, and a single broken line breaks the chain of willingness above it.

What makes a single event establish the *entire* infinite list at once? **Publicness** — a signal that is observed by everyone, in a setting where everyone observes that everyone observes it. When the oracle speaks in the square, every islander hears it (line one), every islander sees every other islander hearing it (line two), every islander sees that every other islander sees that (line three), and because the observation is mutual and visible, the recursion is satisfied at every level simultaneously. There is no last unconfirmed rung, because no one had to *send* anything to anyone — the signal arrived at everyone at the same instant by being public. This is the property a private channel can never have, and it is the entire reason markets are obsessed with official, scheduled, public releases rather than the leaks and whispers that often carry the same raw information earlier.

So when you watch a market, the question to hold in your head is not "is this known?" but "is there an unbroken public chain that makes everyone confident everyone else will act?" If the chain has a missing rung — if the fact is traveling by whisper and group chat rather than by broadcast — you are looking at mutual knowledge wearing a costume, and the coordinated move has not happened yet.

## The blue-eyed islanders: an announcement of nothing new that changes everything

The cleanest puzzle ever built to make common knowledge tangible is the blue-eyed islanders (sometimes told as the muddy children). It is worth walking through with numbers, because it contains the entire SVB story in miniature.

A hundred logical islanders live under a strange taboo: if you ever deduce the color of your own eyes, you must leave on the next morning's ferry. No one talks about eye color, there are no mirrors, and no one ever tells anyone anything. Of the hundred, three have blue eyes and ninety-seven have brown. Everyone can see everyone else's eyes — so every islander already *knows*, with certainty, that there are blue-eyed people on the island. This is perfect mutual knowledge of the fact "there is at least one blue-eyed person." It has been true and known for years. Nobody has ever left.

One day a visiting oracle, trusted by all, stands in the square and says aloud: "I can see that at least one of you has blue eyes." She has told them *nothing they did not already know* — every islander could see at least two blue-eyed people before she spoke. And yet, on the morning of the third day, all three blue-eyed islanders board the ferry together. A statement of zero new information triggered an exodus. How?

![Blue eyes induction over three days](/imgs/blogs/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know-2.png)

The figure traces the induction. The trick is that the oracle did not add a *fact*; she added *common knowledge of the fact*. Walk it up from the simplest case.

Suppose there were only **one** blue-eyed islander. He sees ninety-nine brown-eyed people and zero blue. Before the oracle, he had no idea his own eyes were blue. The instant she says "at least one of you has blue eyes," he looks around, sees nobody else with blue eyes, and deduces it must be him. He leaves on the first morning. One blue-eyed person leaves on day one.

Now suppose there are **two**, call them A and B. A sees exactly one blue-eyed person (B) and reasons: "If I have brown eyes, then B is the only blue-eyed one, and B will leave on the first morning." Morning one comes — and B does *not* leave (because B is running the identical reasoning about A). So on the morning of day two, A knows his earlier hypothesis was wrong: B did not leave, which means B also saw a blue-eyed person, which can only be A. So A deduces his own eyes are blue. Both A and B leave on the second morning. Two blue-eyed people leave on day two.

#### Worked example: counting the nights to the ferry

Let us nail the number for our actual case of three blue-eyed islanders, because the count is the whole point. Each blue-eyed islander sees exactly two others with blue eyes. Islander number one reasons: "I see two blue-eyed people. If my own eyes are brown, those two are in the two-person case, and by the logic above they will both leave on the morning of day two." Day two arrives — and they do not leave (each of the three is making the same inference and waiting). So on day three, each blue-eyed islander knows the two-person hypothesis failed, which can only be true if there are *three* blue-eyed people, which can only be true if his own eyes are blue. All three leave on the morning of day three. With \$3\$ blue-eyed islanders the induction resolves on exactly night \$3\$; with \$n\$ blue-eyed people it resolves on night \$n\$, and not one night sooner. The intuition: each missing departure is itself a public signal that climbs the ladder one rung per day.

The deep point — the one that maps straight onto markets — is *what the oracle actually changed*. Before she spoke, "there is at least one blue-eyed person" was mutual knowledge but not common knowledge. With three blue-eyed islanders, here is what was missing: each islander knew it; each knew the others knew it (everyone can see at least two blue-eyed people); but it was *not* true that everyone knew that everyone knew that everyone knew it. There was a rung high up the ladder that was false, and the induction needs every rung. The oracle's announcement, heard by all and seen to be heard by all, made the fact common knowledge — it filled in every rung at once — and only then could the chain of deductions run.

This is SVB. The unrealized losses were mutual knowledge for months. What the rapid-fire group chats and the public "get your money out" posts did was make it *common knowledge* that everyone knew, and everyone knew everyone knew — and the run, like the islanders' exodus, became unstoppable on a fact that had been sitting in plain sight the whole time.

## The two generals: why coordination over a private channel is impossible

The blue-eyes puzzle shows a *public* signal creating common knowledge. The two-generals problem shows the opposite, and it is the more important lesson for markets: **you cannot build common knowledge out of private messages, no matter how many you send.**

Two generals, A and B, command armies on opposite hills, with the enemy in the valley between them. If they attack together, they win. If only one attacks, that one is annihilated. They can communicate only by sending a messenger through the valley — and the messenger might be captured, so any single message might not arrive. The question: can they agree on a dawn attack and *both be confident* it is safe to charge?

General A sends a messenger: "Attack at dawn." But A cannot charge yet, because he does not know the message arrived — the messenger might have been caught. So B, on receiving it, sends an acknowledgment: "Got it, I will attack." Now B cannot charge either, because *B* does not know whether the acknowledgment arrived; if it did not, A will not attack, and B would charge alone into annihilation. So A sends an acknowledgment of the acknowledgment. But now A is in the same bind B was just in. And so on, forever.

![Two generals message acknowledgment tree](/imgs/blogs/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know-3.png)

The tree above shows the trap. No matter how long the chain of acknowledgments grows, the general who sent the *last* message is never sure it arrived, and therefore is never sure the other will attack. There is always a top rung of the ladder that is unconfirmed. With a private, lossy channel, common knowledge is provably unreachable — you can climb arbitrarily high, but you can never reach the top. The right branch of the figure is the only escape: a single signal both generals observe *simultaneously* — a flare both can see, a public order broadcast to both at once — establishes common knowledge in one stroke, because each sees it and each sees the other see it.

#### Worked example: pricing the cost of an unconfirmed rung

Put dollars on it so the abstraction bites. Suppose two trading desks at the same firm want to put on a coordinated block — each will lose \$1 million if it trades alone, and each makes \$3 million if they trade together, but the desk heads can only confirm by a private message that has a 10% chance of being lost. Desk A messages "I am in." If it arrives (a 90% chance), B is willing. But B's confirmation back to A also has a 10% miss rate. Chain two private confirmations and the probability that *both* the message and the acknowledgment got through is $0.9 \times 0.9 = 0.81$. So in roughly 19% of cases someone is unsure and rationally holds back — and the expected value of the "coordinated" trade is dragged down by the cases where one desk charges alone and eats the \$1 million loss. The fix is not more private messages (each adds another 10% failure rung); it is a single shared trigger both desks watch — a public price level, a screen both see. The intuition: every additional private acknowledgment adds a rung that can fail, so reliable coordination comes from a public signal, never from a longer chat thread.

Notice the asymmetry with the blue-eyes case. The oracle's announcement worked precisely because it was *public* — heard by all, in front of all. A private messenger, even a reliable one, can never do the same job, because the receiver can never be sure the sender knows it arrived. This is why traders trust the *official* print over the leak, the *broadcast* over the whisper. A whisper is a private channel; it can make you privately confident, but it cannot coordinate the crowd.

## The email game: arbitrarily high knowledge is still not common knowledge

The two-generals story has a precise, slightly unsettling formalization that the game theorist Ariel Rubinstein built in 1989, called the **electronic-mail game** — and it is worth its own section because it makes the gap between "very high-order knowledge" and "common knowledge" mathematically sharp, in a way that maps directly onto why markets refuse to move on near-certainty.

Two players want to coordinate an attack that pays off only if the world is in a particular state. One player's computer detects the state and automatically emails the other. Because email can be lost, each computer is set up to send an automatic confirmation of every message it receives — and a confirmation of the confirmation, and so on, each message having a small chance of being lost. After the dust settles, the two computers might have exchanged a hundred messages back and forth. Player A knows the state. Player A knows that B knows. A knows that B knows that A knows. The chain of "I-know-that-you-know" runs to a depth of *dozens* of levels — astronomically higher than anything humans track in practice.

And yet Rubinstein proved the players still cannot rationally coordinate the attack. The reason is the same top-rung problem as the two generals, applied with mathematical force: whoever sent the *last* message that got through cannot be sure it arrived, so there is always one unconfirmed rung at the very top — and that single missing rung, by a backward-induction argument identical to the blue-eyes one, unravels the whole tower of confidence. The shocking part is the discontinuity: a hundred levels of confirmed mutual knowledge produces *exactly* the same failure to coordinate as zero levels. Common knowledge is not "lots of levels of mutual knowledge." It is a different thing entirely — the *completed* infinite chain — and you cannot approach it by stacking finite confirmations.

#### Worked example: a hundred confirmations and still no trade

Put it in trading terms. Two desks set up an automated handshake before a coordinated trade: every message gets an auto-acknowledgment, and the channel drops each message with probability 1%. After the handshake, suppose 100 messages got through cleanly — the chance of that long a clean run is about $0.99^{100} \approx 0.37$, so it does happen. Now each desk's confidence in the other is enormous: the probability that the other desk also saw at least 99 of those messages is over 99%. By any practical standard they are certain. But the desk that sent message number 100 still does not *know* it arrived — there is a 1% chance it was the message that got dropped — so that desk cannot be sure the other will trade, which means it should not trade alone, which (working the induction backward) means message 99 was not safe to rely on either, and so on all the way down. The rational outcome with this protocol is that *neither desk ever trades*, despite a hundred confirmed levels of mutual knowledge. The intuition: confidence that climbs toward certainty is still categorically not the certainty-about-certainty that a single public signal delivers for free.

This is the dirty-face puzzle's lesson too, in a different costume: in the dirty-face problem, each child can see the others' dirty faces (mutual knowledge that someone is dirty) but no child acts until a parent publicly announces "at least one of you is dirty" — the public statement adds no new fact, but it is the only thing that makes the existing fact common knowledge and starts the induction. The email game just shows that you cannot substitute a long private exchange for that one public word. For a trader, the takeaway is blunt: do not mistake a market that is *almost sure* of something for a market that has reached common knowledge of it. The near-sure market is the email game at message ninety-nine — still dammed up, still waiting, still tradeable. The public signal is the only thing that finishes it.

## Mutual versus common knowledge: the distinction that moves prices

We now have the two halves of the engine. Mutual knowledge is a pile of private certainties (the islanders before the oracle; the desks before the public trigger). Common knowledge is the stitched-together version where everyone is confident all the way up the ladder (the islanders after the oracle; the generals after the flare). The market lives almost entirely in the gap between them, and a public announcement is the bridge.

![Mutual knowledge versus common knowledge before and after a public announcement](/imgs/blogs/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know-4.png)

The before-and-after lays out the conversion. On the left, every trader privately knows the number — perfect mutual knowledge — but no one is sure the others will act, so no one moves first; the fact is *not* yet reflected in the price. On the right, the public release lets each trader safely assume the others will trade it too, and the positioning that everyone was holding back unwinds at the same instant. The announcement did not change the world. It changed everyone's confidence about everyone else, and that confidence is what licenses the move.

Why does this *force* a price move rather than just allow one? Because a market is a coordination problem dressed up as a guessing game. The price you should pay for an asset depends on what *other people* will pay for it next, which depends on what they think *you* will pay, and so on. As long as a fact is only mutual knowledge, each trader, unsure the others will act, hedges by waiting — and the waiting is self-fulfilling, so the fact stays unpriced. The public signal breaks the standoff: it is now safe to move because everyone is moving, and the asset jumps to the level everyone privately thought it should be at all along. The jump *looks* like a reaction to news, but the "news" was already known. The real event was the climb to common knowledge.

This is also why a leaked number and an official number behave so differently even when they are identical. A leak is a private channel — it makes some traders privately confident, but it does not make it common knowledge that everyone saw it, so the market chops and second-guesses. The official release, seen by all at the same instant, is the oracle in the square: it converts the same number into common knowledge and triggers the coordinated move. Traders who do not understand this will keep being baffled that "old news" moves markets.

## A coordination game and the power of a public focal signal

To make the coordination structure exact, let us look at the simplest game that has *two* good outcomes and no way for the players to pick between them on their own — and then add a public signal and watch it pick.

![Coordination game payoff matrix with two equilibria](/imgs/blogs/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know-5.png)

Two traders each choose Attack or Hold. The payoff matrix — its values verified as equilibria by the `nash_2x2` solver in the series toolkit — works like this. If both Attack, each gets a payoff of \$3 (they coordinated on the aggressive, profitable move and split the spoils). If both Hold, each gets a safe \$1. But if one Attacks while the other Holds, the lone attacker is exposed and gets \$0 while the holder, sitting safe, gets \$2. This is a **stag hunt**: the cooperative, high-payoff outcome requires both to commit, and committing alone is the worst thing you can do.

#### Worked example: two equilibria, and why you do not dare pick the good one alone

Check the equilibria by hand. Start at both-Attack, the payoff pair \$3 and \$3. Can you do better by switching to Hold while the other still Attacks? You would move from \$3 to \$2 — worse, so you stay. Neither side wants to deviate, so both-Attack is a Nash equilibrium. Now check both-Hold, the pair \$1 and \$1. Can you do better by switching to Attack while the other Holds? You would move from \$1 to \$0 — much worse, because you would be the lone exposed attacker. So neither side deviates, and both-Hold is *also* a Nash equilibrium. The game has two stable resting points: a good one paying \$3 each and a safe one paying \$1 each. The solver confirms both pure equilibria exist (and a knife-edge mixed one at a 50/50 coin flip that pays only \$1.50 each in expectation). Nothing inside the game tells you which equilibrium the players will land on. The intuition: the better outcome is available to both, yet neither dares reach for it alone, so the crowd can get stuck in the worse equilibrium forever.

This is where the **focal point** — Thomas Schelling's idea — comes in. If you and a stranger had to meet in New York City on a given day with no way to communicate, where would you go? Most people say Grand Central at noon, not because it is written anywhere, but because it is *obvious that it is obvious* — a place that stands out as the natural answer, so each of you expects the other to expect it. A Schelling point is a solution that is *salient* enough to become common knowledge of intent without any message being sent. In the coordination game, a public focal signal — a number on a screen that everyone watches, a round price level, a central-bank announcement — tells each trader "the others will go for the \$3 outcome now," and that shared expectation selects the good equilibrium. The signal does not change a single payoff in the matrix. It changes what each player believes the other will do, which is the only thing the matrix leaves undetermined.

Markets run on focal signals constantly. A big round number like \$100 on a stock, or 4,000 on the S&P, becomes a magnet not because the math says so but because everyone knows everyone is watching it, so it becomes a common-knowledge coordination point. The opening auction price, the official fixing, the VWAP benchmark — these are focal signals that let scattered traders coordinate without a word.

## Priced in is a statement about coordination, not information

Now we can dismantle one of the most abused phrases in trading. "It's priced in" is said as though it means "the information exists in public." It does not. **Priced in means the coordination has already happened** — the market has climbed to common knowledge of the fact *and* repositioned around it. If the climb has not happened, the fact is not priced in, regardless of how many people privately know it.

![Priced in requires common knowledge flow](/imgs/blogs/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know-6.png)

The flow above shows the two paths a new fact can take. Down the private path, the fact becomes mutual knowledge — every desk privately holds it — but the price barely moves, because no one will front-run a repricing they are unsure the others will join; the fact stays unpriced, waiting. Down the public path, the official release makes the fact common knowledge, everyone knows everyone will now trade it, and the coordinated repricing fires. *Both paths start from the same fact.* The difference between "sitting unpriced" and "priced in" is entirely whether the climb to common knowledge has occurred. A fact can be universally known and completely unpriced at the same time — that is not a paradox, it is the normal state of a market between the leak and the print.

#### Worked example: the same fact, two different prices

Suppose a chip company's quarter is obviously going to be strong — channel checks, supplier data, everyone with a Bloomberg can see it. The stock trades at \$100. The "information" is fully out there: every analyst privately expects revenue around \$5 billion, comfortably above the \$4.5 billion official consensus the company itself guided to. Is the beat priced in? Watch what happens. Before the print, the stock sits near \$100, drifting; the smart desks privately expect a beat but will not chase it, because if they buy and the beat fails to become common knowledge — if the print somehow disappoints or the guidance is soft — they are the lone attacker eating the loss. The print lands: revenue \$5.1 billion, guidance raised. Now it is common knowledge, every desk knows every other desk is buying, and the stock gaps to \$112 in seconds — a \$12 move on a number the whole street "already knew." The \$12 was not the value of the information (that was known). It was the value of the *coordination* finally happening. The intuition: the gap measures how much repositioning was dammed up behind the missing common knowledge, not how surprising the fact was.

This reframes "buy the rumor, sell the news" precisely. The rumor phase is when the fact is mutual knowledge and a few brave traders position on it privately. The news phase is when it becomes common knowledge — and the coordinated crowd piles in, which is exactly when the early traders, who already have their position on, sell into the wave. The "sell the news" is not irrationality; it is the early movers monetizing the moment the rest of the market is finally *allowed* to agree with them. The reaction-mechanics of this — how the surprise versus the expectation maps to the size of the move — are worked out in detail in [the surprise framework for news](/blog/trading/event-trading/why-news-moves-markets-the-surprise-framework); here we are isolating the *coordination* layer underneath it.

## Why the Fed moves a fully-priced market

The central-bank case is the purest example, and it confuses people endlessly. The Fed hikes by 25 basis points. The market had a 98% probability of exactly that hike priced into futures the day before. The decision lands precisely as expected — and bonds, stocks, and the dollar still move sharply on the release. "But it was priced in!" Yes — the *number* was priced in. The *common knowledge* was not.

Three things happen at a Fed announcement, and only the first is "the number." The second is the **statement language** — the exact wording about future policy, which everyone reads at the same instant and which becomes common knowledge of the committee's intent. The third is the **press conference**, where the chair's tone and emphasis are seen by all and known to be seen by all. The decision itself can be perfectly anticipated while the wording and tone resolve a coordination problem: every desk has been holding a view about the path of rates, unsure how aggressively the others will lean, and the statement is the public signal that lets them all reposition together. This is the same machinery as the blue-eyes oracle — an announcement that adds little raw information but converts scattered private views into common knowledge, unleashing the coordinated move.

This is also why **forward guidance** is such a powerful tool, and why central banks guard their credibility so fiercely. Forward guidance works *only* as a public, common-knowledge signal: when the Fed says "we expect to hold rates here through next year," it is not delivering new data, it is creating a focal point that everyone can coordinate around, which moves the entire yield curve without a single trade by the Fed itself. The mechanics of how that toolkit operates — rates, balance-sheet policy, and the signaling channel — are laid out in [the central-bank toolkit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance); the point here is *why* a mere statement has teeth: because it manufactures common knowledge, and common knowledge is what coordinates a market.

#### Worked example: the priced-in hike that still moves the curve

Put numbers on the "priced-in still moves" puzzle. Say the 2-year Treasury yield sits at 4.50% the morning of a meeting, with the 25 bp hike fully discounted. The hike lands as expected; the 2-year does almost nothing on the decision itself — that piece *was* priced in. But the statement drops the phrase "ongoing increases" and the chair calls the policy stance "sufficiently restrictive." That wording is the public signal: every desk that privately suspected the Fed was near done now knows that *everyone* knows it, so it is safe to position for a pause. The 2-year drops 15 bps to 4.35% within minutes — a real move on a meeting where the rate decision surprised no one. On a \$10 million notional 2-year position with roughly 2 years of duration, a 1 bp yield move is about \$2,000 of P&L, so a 15 bp drop is on the order of a \$30,000 swing, all driven by the language, not the number. The intuition: the decision was mutual knowledge already; the move came from the wording converting the *path* into common knowledge.

## Bank runs and bubbles as common-knowledge events

The most violent market events are coordination collapses, and they are best understood as sudden jumps in common knowledge. We will reference these forward rather than re-derive them — the bank-run mechanics and the bubble dynamics get full treatment elsewhere in this series — but the common-knowledge lens makes the trigger legible in a way nothing else does.

A **bank run** is a coordination game with two equilibria, exactly like the stag hunt above. If everyone leaves their money in, the bank is fine and each depositor earns interest (the good equilibrium). If everyone pulls their money, the bank is forced to dump assets at fire-sale prices and fails, and the depositors who pulled first get more than those who waited (the bad equilibrium). Each depositor's best move depends entirely on what they think the others will do. The series toolkit's stylized run model puts the good equilibrium at a payoff of \$1.50 (your deposit plus interest) and the bad one at \$0.50 (a partial recovery after the fire sale), with a critical threshold around half the depositors. As long as it is only *mutual* knowledge that the bank has problems, the good equilibrium can hold — everyone privately worried, but no one moving. The instant it becomes *common* knowledge — a viral post, a public statement, a queue visible outside the branch that everyone can see everyone seeing — the bad equilibrium snaps into place and the run is on. SVB is the textbook case: the run started the moment the losses and the worry became common knowledge in the tight-knit VC community, not when the facts first existed.

A **bubble** is the same machinery run in reverse and in slow motion. Many sophisticated holders privately suspect an asset is overvalued, but each stays long because each believes the others will stay long a while longer — the greater-fool coordination. The bubble persists as long as "it is overvalued" remains mutual knowledge that no one is sure the others will act on. It pops when overvaluation becomes common knowledge — some public, salient signal that makes everyone realize everyone else is about to sell — and the coordinated exit is the crash. The reason bubbles end so abruptly, rather than deflating gently, is that the jump from mutual to common knowledge is itself abrupt: the focal signal flips the whole crowd from one equilibrium to the other in an instant. (Tracks C and F of this series build the bank-run and bubble games out in full; here the job is only to plant the flag that both are common-knowledge events.)

## Common misconceptions

**"If everyone knows it, it is priced in."** This is the central error, and we have now dismantled it twice. Everyone knowing a fact is mutual knowledge; pricing requires common knowledge *plus* the coordinated repositioning it enables. A fact can be universally known and completely unpriced — that is the normal state of the market between a leak and the official print, and it is exactly the window in which old news produces violent moves.

**"The Fed surprised no one, so the move was irrational."** The rate decision surprised no one; the statement language and the conference resolved a coordination problem that the decision did not touch. The move was a perfectly rational response to the conversion of scattered private views about the rate *path* into common knowledge. Calling it irrational is a sign you are tracking the information and ignoring the coordination.

**"I will get out before the music stops."** This is the bubble-and-run fantasy, and the coordination math is brutal about it. The bad equilibrium triggers the instant overvaluation becomes common knowledge, and that instant is, by construction, when *everyone* tries to leave at once. You cannot reliably be early to a common-knowledge event, because the event *is* the moment everyone acts simultaneously. Liquidity vanishes precisely when you need it, because every other "smart" holder is running the identical plan. The honest version of "get out early" is "get out while it is still only mutual knowledge" — which means selling into strength, before the crowd is allowed to agree with you.

**"More communication always helps coordination."** The two-generals result says otherwise. Private messages, however numerous, cannot manufacture common knowledge — each one adds a rung that can fail, and the sender of the last message is never sure it arrived. Reliable coordination comes from a single *public* signal everyone observes at once, not from a longer chain of confirmations. A group chat can make a clique privately confident, but it cannot move a market the way a public print does.

**"Common knowledge is just a fancy word for consensus."** Consensus is about *agreement on the answer* — everyone reaching the same view. Common knowledge is about *certainty about each other's certainty* — everyone knowing everyone knows, up the ladder. You can have full consensus that is only mutual knowledge (everyone privately agrees, no one is sure the others will act) and it will not move the price. The move needs the higher rungs, not just the shared answer.

**"A reliable leak is as good as the official print."** This is the email-game error in disguise. A leak — even a perfectly accurate one, even one that reaches every desk — travels by private channels, so it leaves an unconfirmed top rung: I saw the leak, and I think you saw it, but I am not certain you are certain that I saw it and will act. The official print, observed by all at the same instant, has no such missing rung. That is precisely why markets frequently *pre-position* on a credible leak and then move *again*, often harder, on the official confirmation: the leak handles the information, the print handles the common knowledge, and the second move is the coordination the leak could not complete. If you treat a leak as fully equivalent to a print, you will be repeatedly surprised by the "why did it move again on old news" second leg.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Silicon Valley Bank, March 2023.** The unrealized losses on SVB's held-to-maturity bond portfolio — on the order of \$15 billion against roughly \$16 billion of equity — were disclosed in public filings well before the collapse. They were mutual knowledge among bank analysts for months. The run materialized over 9–10 March when a few prominent venture-capital firms publicly and simultaneously advised portfolio companies to pull deposits; the advice, broadcast across tightly-connected group chats, converted the long-known solvency worry into *common* knowledge within the depositor base. Roughly \$42 billion of deposit-withdrawal requests hit in a single day. The facts had not changed; the knowledge had climbed the ladder, and the coordination game flipped from the good equilibrium to the bad one almost instantly.

**The "fully priced" Fed hikes of 2022–2023.** Through the 2022 hiking cycle, fed-funds futures repeatedly assigned over 90% probability to the exact size of the next hike, and the decision routinely landed as expected. Yet Treasury yields and equities moved sharply on the releases anyway — the December 2022 and February 2023 meetings each produced multi-handle equity moves and double-digit-basis-point yield swings on "no surprise" decisions. The mover each time was the statement language and the chair's tone, which resolved the market's coordination problem about the *path* of rates by making the committee's leaning common knowledge. The number was priced in; the path's common knowledge was created in the room.

**GameStop, January 2021.** A coordination event built almost entirely out of a public focal signal. The thesis that GameStop was heavily shorted was mutual knowledge — the short interest figures were published. What turned it into a squeeze was a *public* rallying point on a forum everyone could see everyone watching: the shared, salient target made "everyone is buying and holding" common knowledge, which selected the high-payoff coordinated equilibrium against the short sellers. The squeeze was a stag hunt won by a focal signal. (The mechanics of who was on the other side of that trade are taken up elsewhere in this series.)

**Currency-peg breaks.** A central bank defending a peg is the defender in a coordination game against speculators. As long as it is only mutual knowledge that the peg is overvalued, each speculator hesitates to attack alone — a lone attacker against a determined central bank loses. The peg breaks when overvaluation and the central bank's dwindling reserves become common knowledge, and the speculators coordinate their attack. The 1992 sterling break and various emerging-market peg collapses follow this script: the reserves were depleting in public view (mutual knowledge) long before the abrupt, coordinated assault that broke the peg (common knowledge).

**Earnings reactions on "in-line" prints.** It is routine for a company to report exactly the revenue and earnings the consensus expected and still gap several percent on the release. The whisper number — the figure traders privately believed — was mutual knowledge, but the official print is what makes the result common knowledge and licenses the coordinated repositioning. The gap is the dammed-up positioning releasing, not a reaction to a surprise that, by the headline numbers, did not occur. This is the single most common live demonstration of the mutual-versus-common-knowledge gap, and it happens dozens of times every earnings season.

**Index inclusions and the rebalance trade.** When a company is added to a major index, the news that it *will* be added is often public weeks ahead, and every index fund knows it must buy on the effective date. So the information is mutual knowledge early — yet the largest price impact clusters around the official effective date, when the buying becomes common-knowledge-coordinated and everyone front-running it must close. The whole "buy on announcement, sell on inclusion" pattern that quantitative desks trade is a clean coordination structure: the announcement is the focal signal that tells everyone everyone else will buy by the deadline, and the effective date is when the dammed-up flow finally clears. The lesson repeats: the scheduled, public, common-knowledge moment is where the move concentrates, not the moment the raw fact first became known.

**Sovereign defaults and credit events.** A government's inability to pay is frequently visible in the data — reserves draining, debt-to-GDP climbing, bond yields creeping — long before any default. That deterioration is mutual knowledge among credit analysts for months or years. The violent repricing comes at the public, common-knowledge trigger: a missed coupon, a ratings downgrade announced to all at once, an IMF statement, or an official declaration of default. Greek government bonds through 2010–2012 are the textbook sequence — the solvency problem was widely understood and only gradually priced, and the sharp legs down clustered on the public events (downgrades, summit announcements, the formal restructuring) that converted the shared private worry into common knowledge and forced the coordinated exit.

## The playbook: how to play the climb to common knowledge

The whole point of this series is *so how do you play it?* Common knowledge is not a curiosity — it is a tradeable structure, and the edge comes from being early to the *conversion*, not the information.

**Who is on the other side.** When old news moves a market, your counterparty is the crowd that was holding back — every desk that privately knew the fact but would not act until it was safe to act together. They are not stupid; they were rationally waiting for common knowledge. Your edge is recognizing the conversion moment *as* a coordination event rather than as new information, so you are not the one chasing the gap after it has already fired.

**The model of coordinated success versus depth of private belief** is the quantitative backbone of the playbook, and it explains why the public conversion is so valuable.

![Coordinated success probability versus required depth of private belief](/imgs/blogs/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know-7.png)

The chart computes a two-generals model: if a coordinated action needs $k$ confirmed levels of "I-know-that-you-know" to be safely acted on, and each level of private confirmation survives a noisy channel with probability $r$, then the chance the whole chain is in place is $r^k$ — and it *falls* steeply as the required depth rises. At $r = 0.85$ the probability of getting six clean rungs is only about $0.85^6 \approx 0.38$, or 38%. The flat purple line at the top is the public signal: common knowledge arrives at every depth at once, so the success probability is 100% regardless. The gap between the falling private curves and the flat public line *is the value of converting mutual into common knowledge* — it is why a single public print is worth more than any number of private confirmations, and why the conversion moment is where the action concentrates.

**The game you are in.** Whenever a known fact is sitting unpriced, you are in a coordination game with two equilibria — the "stays unpriced" resting point and the "repriced" one — waiting for a focal signal to select between them. Your job is to identify (a) the fact that is currently mutual but not common knowledge, and (b) the scheduled or likely public signal that will convert it. The scheduled conversions — the earnings date, the Fed meeting, the CPI release, the index rebalance — are the most tradeable, because the *timing* of the common-knowledge jump is itself common knowledge.

**Your edge and how to size it.** The edge is to position *while the fact is still only mutual knowledge* and the price has not yet moved — accepting the risk that the conversion fails to happen, in exchange for being ahead of the coordinated crowd. Size it against exactly that risk: the chance that the public signal does not land the way you expect, or that the fact never becomes common knowledge at all (the leak that fizzles, the print that comes in line and lets the crowd shrug). Because you are betting on a coordination conversion, your downside is being the lone attacker — long into an event that fails to coordinate the crowd your way — so size small enough to survive the conversions that go against you.

**The invalidation.** Your thesis is invalidated the moment the public signal arrives and the coordinated move *does not* happen — that tells you the fact was already common knowledge and fully priced, or that the crowd's private view was different from yours. If the print lands and the market does not reposition the way your "dammed-up positioning" thesis predicted, you were wrong about which rung the market was on. Cut it; do not wait for a coordination that the market just told you will not come.

**The exit.** The conversion event *is* the exit for the early position. When the fact becomes common knowledge and the coordinated crowd piles in, that wave of buying (or selling) is the liquidity you sell into — the "sell the news" leg. The discipline is to take the gap, not to hold for more, because once the conversion has happened, the easy coordination edge is gone and you are back to forecasting, which is a different and harder game.

The single habit that pays off forever: stop asking "does the market know this?" and start asking "has this climbed to common knowledge yet, and what will convert it when it does?" The information is almost always already out there. The money is in the moment it becomes common — the moment everyone learns that everyone else knows, and the positioning that was dammed up behind that uncertainty unwinds all at once.
