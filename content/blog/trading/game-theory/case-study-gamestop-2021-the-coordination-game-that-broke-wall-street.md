> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The GameStop episode was not one event; it was four games happening at the same time, and the famous price chart is just the scoreboard of who got *forced* to act. Once you label the players and the game each was in, the whole story stops looking like magic and starts looking like a chain of forced moves.
>
> - GameStop (GME) was the most-shorted large stock in the market: short interest peaked around **140% of the public float**, meaning more shares had been sold short than actually existed to buy back. That is a crowded short with no exit.
> - A forum, r/wallstreetbets, turned thousands of strangers into a *coordination game* by making everyone's buy-and-hold visible to everyone else — that shared visibility, called **common knowledge**, is what let dispersed retail behave like one large coordinated buyer.
> - Mass buying of call options forced the dealers who sold those calls to buy GME shares to stay hedged — a **gamma squeeze** that added fuel having nothing to do with the company.
> - The price ran from roughly **\$4 split-adjusted (about \$17 as traded) in early January to an intraday \$483 on January 28, 2021**; a major short, Melvin Capital, lost about **53% that month** and took a **\$2.75 billion** cash injection. The one rule: the coordination held until a player changed the rules, and most latecomers were left holding the bag.

On the morning of January 28, 2021, the most popular feature on Robinhood's stock-trading app stopped working. You could still sell GameStop. You could not buy it. The "buy" button for GME and a short list of other names was simply switched off, replaced by a notice that the broker was restricting these securities to "position close only." Within hours, a stock that had touched **\$483** intraday was trading near **\$112**, and by the next week it was back under **\$60**. A United States congressional hearing followed within three weeks. Lawsuits followed for years.

To most people watching, it looked like chaos — a casino, a mob, a glitch, a conspiracy, depending on whom you asked. To a game theorist, it looked like something much more precise: a chain of *forced moves*, where each player did the only thing the rules of their particular game allowed, and where the single most important question was always the same one this whole series is built around — **who is on the other side, and what game are they in?** The diagram below is the mental model for the entire episode: four players, four different games, one shared scoreboard.

![Grid of four GameStop players, the crowded shorts, the coordinated longs, the options dealers, and the broker, each labeled with the game they were playing](/imgs/blogs/case-study-gamestop-2021-the-coordination-game-that-broke-wall-street-1.png)

Notice what that picture is *not*. It is not "retail versus Wall Street," even though that was the headline. The dealers were not on anyone's side; they were hedging. The broker was not protecting hedge funds; it was protecting its own balance sheet against a clearinghouse demand. And the "retail" side was not one mind — it was a coordination game that could shatter at any moment. The price of GameStop was simply the running total of how much *forced buying* was being squeezed out of two of these players, the shorts and the dealers, by the choices of the other two, the longs and the broker. Everything that happened in late January 2021 is downstream of that structure. Let us build it from zero.

## Foundations: the pre-conditions, the players, and the games in play

Before any squeeze can happen, the board has to be set a particular way. Three things had to be true about GameStop, and a fourth thing had to be true about the people watching it. We will define each from scratch, because the whole episode is unintelligible without them.

### What it means to be "short" a stock

When you buy a stock, the worst that can happen is it goes to zero and you lose what you paid. *Short selling* is the mirror image, and its risk profile is the opposite. To short a stock, you **borrow** shares from someone who owns them (usually through your broker, who borrows them from a big custodian), you **sell** those borrowed shares immediately at today's price, and you promise to **buy them back later** and return them. If the price falls, you buy back cheaply, return the shares, and pocket the difference. If the price *rises*, you must buy back at the higher price — and there is no ceiling on how high a price can go.

This asymmetry is the engine of everything that follows. A long position can lose 100%. A short position can lose 200%, 500%, 1000% — the loss is unbounded, because the price you may be forced to pay to buy back is unbounded. And critically, a short seller does not get to choose *whether* to buy back. If the position moves against them far enough, the rules of margin force them to. We will return to that word — *force* — again and again.

#### Worked example: the unbounded loss of a short

Suppose you short 100 shares of a stock at \$20. You receive \$2,000 today (100 × \$20), which sits in your account as collateral, not profit — you owe 100 shares back. Your broker requires you to keep, say, 50% of the position's value as margin. As long as the stock stays near \$20, you are comfortable.

Now the stock goes to \$120. To buy back 100 shares now costs \$12,000. You sold for \$2,000, so you are down \$10,000 — a loss of 500% on a position you opened for \$2,000 of value. Worse, your broker's margin math now demands far more collateral than you have. You get a *margin call*: post more cash by the deadline or we close the position for you. If you cannot, the broker buys those 100 shares back at \$120 (or whatever the market is at the moment of forced execution), and your loss is locked in. You did not choose to buy at \$120. The rules chose for you.

The intuition: a short seller is a buyer-in-waiting whose hand can be forced at any price, which makes a heavily shorted stock a pile of fuel waiting for a match.

### Float, short interest, and "more shares short than exist"

Two more terms. The **float** is the number of shares actually available to trade in the public market — total shares minus the big locked-up blocks held by insiders and long-term holders who never sell. **Short interest** is the total number of shares that have been sold short. Expressing short interest as a percentage of float tells you how crowded the short trade is.

Here is the part that breaks intuition. Short interest can exceed 100% of the float. How? Because a borrowed-and-sold share can be bought by someone who then *lends it out again* to a second short seller, who sells it to a third buyer, who lends it again. The same physical share gets re-shorted down a chain. By January 2021, GameStop's short interest was widely reported at roughly **140% of its float** — more shares had been sold short than existed in the tradable float at all. The chart below puts that in context against other famous crowded shorts.

![Bar chart of short interest as a percent of public float for GameStop, Volkswagen, KOSS, and Tesla with a 100 percent reference line](/imgs/blogs/case-study-gamestop-2021-the-coordination-game-that-broke-wall-street-3.png)

When short interest is above 100% of float, the shorts collectively face a logical trap: to close their positions, they all need to buy shares — but there are not enough shares in the float for all of them to buy at once without driving the price violently higher. This is the structural pre-condition of a squeeze. It is what game theorists call a *commitment trap*: a group has collectively promised to do something (buy back) that they cannot all do simultaneously without hurting themselves. The crowded-short setup is exactly the [exit game](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game) — the moment everyone wants to leave through a door too small for them all.

### Margin, the cost of carry, and what "forced" really means

The word *forced* is doing so much work in this story that it deserves a precise definition. A short seller is not forced to cover the moment they have a paper loss — paper losses on a short are normal and survivable. They are forced when their *margin* runs out. Margin is the collateral a trader must keep posted against a position; for a short, it rises with the position's value, because the obligation to buy back grows as the price rises. When the equity in the account falls below a *maintenance margin* threshold, the broker issues a margin call, and if it is not met, the broker liquidates — for a short, "liquidate" means *buy the shares back*, at whatever the market is at that instant.

There is a second, quieter pressure: the *cost of carry*. A short seller pays a borrow fee to whoever lent them the shares, quoted as an annualized rate. For an ordinary stock that fee might be a fraction of a percent. For a stock as crowded and hard-to-borrow as GameStop became, the borrow fee can spike into the tens of percent annualized, because lenders can charge what the market will bear when shares are scarce. So even a short seller whose margin is intact is *bleeding* a daily fee that grows precisely when the squeeze is on. The crowded short is squeezed from two directions at once: rising price eats the margin, and a rising borrow fee taxes every day they hold on.

#### Worked example: the borrow fee that taxes a stubborn short

Suppose you are short \$10 million of a stock and the borrow fee is a benign 1% annualized. That costs you 0.01 × \$10,000,000 ÷ 365 ≈ **\$274 a day** to carry — negligible. Now the stock becomes a squeeze target and the borrow fee jumps to 30% annualized as shares get scarce. On the *same* \$10 million notional, the carry is now 0.30 × \$10,000,000 ÷ 365 ≈ **\$8,219 a day**. And remember the notional itself is climbing as the price rises, so the dollar fee climbs faster still. A short seller who was "right but early" can be bled out by carry alone before the thesis ever has a chance to pay off. The intuition: in a crowded short, time is not on your side — every day you hold against a squeeze, the borrow fee and the margin math both work harder to force you out.

This is why the shorts in the GameStop story could not simply "wait it out." Waiting cost them margin (eroded by the rising price) and carry (a borrow fee that exploded as the stock became impossible to borrow). The forced-buyer label is not a metaphor; it is the mechanical end-state of two costs converging on a trader who has run out of room.

### The four players and their four games

The grid in the cover figure named them; let us define each player precisely, because mislabeling a player is the single most common way people misread this episode.

- **The crowded shorts.** Hedge funds and other professionals, the most prominent being Melvin Capital, who had collectively sold short more shares than existed in the float. Their game is the [exit game](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game): they all need to buy back, the door is small, and rising prices can force them through it whether they want to or not. They are the players who can become *forced buyers*.
- **The coordinated longs.** Hundreds of thousands of individual retail traders, organized loosely on the r/wallstreetbets forum. Individually, each one is a rounding error. Collectively, if they buy and *hold*, they shrink the already-tiny tradable float and corner the shorts. Their game is a **coordination game** — specifically a *stag hunt*, which we will model below. The squeeze only fires if enough of them hold.
- **The options dealers.** The market makers who *sold* the call options that retail bought by the millions. They are not betting on GameStop at all; they are required, by their own risk management, to hedge. When the calls they sold gain value, they must buy the underlying stock to stay neutral. Their game is delta hedging, and it makes them a *second* set of forced buyers — the [gamma squeeze](/blog/trading/options-volatility/dealer-gamma-charm-and-vanna-how-options-flows-move-the-spot).
- **The broker and the clearinghouse.** Robinhood (and other zero-commission brokers) and behind them the DTCC, the central clearinghouse that guarantees trades while they settle. Their game is solvency: they must front collateral to the clearinghouse for trades that have not yet settled, and when volatility explodes, that collateral demand can balloon beyond what the broker has on hand. Their available move — the one that ended the squeeze — was to *change the rules of the game* by restricting buying.

Each of these is a game we have already studied in this series in the abstract. GameStop is where they all fired at once. The rest of this post walks the timeline as a sequence of forced moves, game by game.

## The setup: a crowded short with no exit

By late 2020, GameStop was a textbook short. The thesis was reasonable on its face: a brick-and-mortar video-game retailer in shrinking malls, in a world of digital downloads, with declining revenues. Professional short sellers piled in. The trade became *crowded* — and a crowded trade is dangerous not because the thesis is wrong but because of the [exit-game](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game) structure: when everyone is on the same side, everyone needs the *same* counterparty to get out, and that counterparty is scarce.

Two things made GameStop's short uniquely fragile. First, the short interest above 100% of float meant the shorts literally could not all cover from the existing float without bidding the price up against themselves. Second, the *days-to-cover* ratio — total short interest divided by average daily trading volume, an estimate of how many days of normal buying it would take the shorts to close — was high. If it would take, say, five trading days of *all* the volume just for the shorts to exit, then any sudden surge of buying from someone else slams the door shut.

#### Worked example: why days-to-cover is the fuse length

Imagine a stock with 50 million shares short and average daily volume of 10 million shares. Days-to-cover is 50 ÷ 10 = 5. That number is the length of the fuse. It says: even if shorts were the *only* buyers, it would take five full days of total market volume for them to close out. Now suppose a wave of new buyers shows up and absorbs 8 million of the 10 million daily shares. The shorts can now only buy 2 million shares a day to cover, so their effective days-to-cover jumps to 50 ÷ 2 = 25 days. Every day they wait, the price climbs, their margin worsens, and the math gets worse. A short seller in this position is doing arithmetic that ends with a margin call. The intuition: high short interest is the gunpowder, but high days-to-cover is what makes a small spark of outside buying detonate it.

That was GameStop's board at the end of 2020: more shares short than existed, a high days-to-cover fuse, and a set of professional shorts who — like all crowded trades — were each individually fine but collectively trapped. All that was missing was a coordinated outside buyer big enough to light the fuse. That buyer did not exist as a single entity. It had to be *manufactured* out of thousands of strangers.

## The coordination: a forum that became a common-knowledge machine

This is the part of the GameStop story that is genuinely novel, and it is pure game theory. Retail investors had always been dispersed and uncoordinated — that is precisely why they were considered "dumb money," easy for professionals to trade against. A short squeeze requires a coordinated long who buys and *holds* against the shorts. No individual retail trader is large enough to matter. So how did a crowd of small traders behave like one big one?

The answer is that r/wallstreetbets functioned as a **common-knowledge machine**. Recall from our post on [common knowledge](/blog/trading/game-theory/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know) that there is a profound difference between *everyone privately believing* something and *everyone knowing that everyone knows it*. Coordination requires the second, stronger condition. A single person who privately wants to squeeze the shorts does nothing. A person who can see thousands of others publicly posting their buys, their hold-the-line conviction, and screenshots of their positions can reason: "they will hold because I can see them holding, and they can see me, so we will all hold together." The forum did not just spread a belief; it made the belief *common knowledge*, and common knowledge is what turns a crowd into a coordinated player.

This converted a mass of dispersed retail into the kind of coordinated long that a [short squeeze](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-short-squeeze-game-shorts-longs-brokers-and-gamma) needs. But coordination is fragile, and the right model for *how* fragile is the stag hunt.

### Modeling the longs as a stag hunt

A stag hunt is the classic two-equilibrium coordination game. Two hunters can together bring down a stag (a big shared prize) only if both commit; if either one defects to chase a rabbit (a small, safe, solo prize), the stag escapes and the committed hunter goes hungry. The GameStop longs faced exactly this structure: hold together and the squeeze fires (the stag); sell early while others hold and you grab a modest gain (the rabbit); hold while others sell and you are the one left holding an overpriced share (you go hungry). The payoff matrix below uses numbers from the `nash_2x2` solver to make the equilibria concrete.

![Two by two coordination payoff matrix for a long who can hold or sell against the rest of the crowd, showing two Nash equilibria](/imgs/blogs/case-study-gamestop-2021-the-coordination-game-that-broke-wall-street-4.png)

#### Worked example: the two equilibria of holding the line

Read the matrix as a game between *you* and *the rest of the crowd*, where each side chooses HOLD or SELL. Using illustrative payoffs (in units of, say, thousands of dollars per 100 shares):

- Both HOLD: **(+20, +20)**. The squeeze fires, the price rockets, every holder cashes in big. This is the good equilibrium.
- You HOLD, crowd SELLS: **(−8, +6)**. The squeeze fizzles. You are the bagholder, stuck with a share that has lost its bid; they escaped first.
- You SELL, crowd HOLDS: **(+6, −8)**. You escape with a modest gain and miss the top; they are now the ones at risk.
- Both SELL: **(+1, +1)**. No squeeze, everyone exits near where they got in. The safe equilibrium.

Run this through the equilibrium solver and you get two pure equilibria — **(HOLD, HOLD)** and **(SELL, SELL)** — plus a mixed equilibrium where each side holds with probability about **0.39**. Two pure equilibria is the signature of a coordination game: which one you land in depends entirely on *what each player believes the others will do*, not on any fundamental fact about GameStop. The forum's job was to push everyone's beliefs toward the (HOLD, HOLD) corner and keep them there. The mixed-equilibrium probability of 0.39 is the knife-edge: below it, the rational move flips to selling. The intuition: a squeeze is a self-fulfilling belief, which is exactly why it is so violent when it works and so sudden when it breaks — beliefs can flip in an afternoon.

This is the deep reason "diamond hands" and "hold the line" were not just memes; they were *coordination devices*, public commitments designed to keep beliefs parked in the good-equilibrium corner. Every screenshot of someone refusing to sell was a signal nudging the crowd's shared expectation toward HOLD. The squeeze was, quite literally, a belief held in common — and it would last exactly as long as that belief did.

### Why the forum's architecture mattered

It is worth being precise about *how* a website manufactured common knowledge, because the architecture is the whole trick, and it is what made this episode different from every prior retail mania. Three features of the forum did the work, and each maps to a piece of game theory.

First, **public, timestamped, hard-to-fake commitment.** A trader did not just say "I'm holding." They posted a screenshot of an account showing a six-figure unrealized loss and the caption "I'm not selling." A *costly, visible* signal is far more credible than cheap talk — it is the same logic by which a company that pays a dividend signals confidence it cannot easily fake. Thousands of these posts told every reader not merely that others held, but that others were willing to *suffer* to hold, which is a much stronger reason to believe the coordination will survive.

Second, **a shared focal point.** Coordination games are solved by *focal points* — obvious, salient choices that everyone expects everyone to converge on. The forum supplied them relentlessly: a single ticker (GME), a single instruction ("hold"), round-number price targets, and daily threads that re-established the shared script. A focal point lets dispersed strangers coordinate without communicating directly; each one reasons "the obvious move is to hold GME, so the others will hold GME too." Without that salient anchor, the same number of buyers would have scattered across dozens of names and coordinated on nothing.

Third, **visible aggregate state.** Upvotes, comment counts, and the sheer volume of identical posts let any individual *observe how many others were in*. That observability is the difference between a private belief and common knowledge. When you can see ten thousand people publicly committing, you can update not just your belief about GameStop but your belief about *everyone else's belief* — which is exactly the higher-order reasoning a coordination game runs on. A bank run works the same way in reverse: people run because they see others running. The forum was a bank run pointed at the shorts instead of at a bank.

The fragility is baked into the same architecture. Because the equilibrium was sustained by *observing* commitment, anything that made commitment unobservable or impossible — a buy button switched off, a flood of "I sold" posts, a broker outage — could collapse the shared belief faster than any change in GameStop's business ever could. The machine that built the coordination was also the machine that could break it.

## The gamma squeeze: forcing a second buyer who never picked a side

So far we have two players: the trapped shorts and the coordinated longs. The longs' direct buying alone would have moved GameStop. But there was a second, amplifying loop that pushed it from a sharp rally into a vertical spike, and it came from a player with no opinion on GameStop at all — the options dealer. This is the [gamma squeeze](/blog/trading/options-volatility/dealer-gamma-charm-and-vanna-how-options-flows-move-the-spot), and it is worth slowing down for, because it is the most misunderstood part of the episode.

A *call option* gives its buyer the right to buy 100 shares at a fixed *strike* price before a fixed expiry. When retail traders bought call options on GameStop by the millions — especially cheap, short-dated, out-of-the-money calls — someone had to *sell* those calls. That someone was a market-making dealer. The dealer who sells a call is now *short* that call: if GameStop rises, the dealer owes the buyer the payoff. To avoid betting against GameStop (which is not the dealer's business), the dealer **hedges** by buying some shares of the underlying — enough to offset the option's sensitivity to the share price, a quantity called *delta*.

Here is the loop. As GameStop's price rises, those out-of-the-money calls move closer to being in-the-money, and their delta increases — the rate of that increase is called *gamma*. A higher delta means the dealer must hold *more* shares to stay hedged, so the dealer buys more stock. That buying pushes the price up further, which raises delta again, which forces more buying. The pipeline below traces one full turn of this self-reinforcing loop.

![Gamma squeeze pipeline showing retail call buying forcing dealers to hedge by buying stock which lifts the price into the next strike](/imgs/blogs/case-study-gamestop-2021-the-coordination-game-that-broke-wall-street-5.png)

The dealer is the second *forced buyer* in the story, and like the short seller, the dealer is not choosing to buy because they believe in GameStop. They are buying because their risk model requires it. This is the crucial point: a gamma squeeze injects buying pressure that is completely divorced from any view on the company. It is pure mechanics, and it stacks directly on top of the longs' coordinated buying and the shorts' forced covering.

#### Worked example: how much stock one call forces a dealer to buy

Suppose a retail trader buys one GameStop call (covering 100 shares) with a delta of 0.30 when the stock is at \$60. To hedge, the dealer who sold it buys 0.30 × 100 = **30 shares**, costing 30 × \$60 = **\$1,800**.

Now the stock jumps to \$120. The call is now much closer to the money, so its delta rises to, say, 0.70. The dealer must now hold 0.70 × 100 = 70 shares — so they buy an *additional* 40 shares, this time at \$120, spending another 40 × \$120 = **\$4,800**. A single small option contract just forced \$6,600 of net stock buying, almost all of it *after* the price had already risen, which is the worst possible time to be a buyer.

Now multiply by *millions* of contracts. In late January 2021, GameStop's options volume exploded, and a large share of it was short-dated calls. The intuition: in a gamma squeeze, the higher the price goes, the more the dealers are *forced* to buy, so the rally feeds on its own success — until the calls expire or the buying stops.

### Why short-dated, out-of-the-money calls are the perfect accelerant

Not all call buying creates a gamma squeeze of equal force. The reason GameStop's options flow was so explosive is that retail concentrated on *cheap, short-dated, out-of-the-money* calls, and those are precisely the contracts with the most gamma per dollar spent. Gamma — the rate at which an option's delta changes as the stock moves — is highest for options that are near the money and close to expiry. A call that is far out of the money and months from expiry barely moves the dealer's hedge as the stock ticks up; a call that is just out of the money and expiring this Friday flips from a delta near zero to a delta near one over a small price range, forcing the dealer to scramble to buy shares fast.

So a dollar spent on a weekly, slightly-out-of-the-money call buys far more *forced dealer buying* than the same dollar spent on the stock directly. This is the leverage that turned a sharp rally into a vertical one. It also explains the brutal time-dependence: short-dated options expire in days, so the forced hedging they create is intense but brief. When a big block of weekly calls expires, the dealer no longer needs to hold the hedge shares — and may *sell* them — which removes a buyer (and can add a seller) exactly when the coordination is most fragile.

#### Worked example: gamma per dollar, weekly versus monthly

Suppose two traders each have \$1,000 to spend on GameStop calls with the stock at \$60. Trader A buys a near-the-money call expiring in three days, priced at \$2.00 (so they buy 5 contracts, controlling 500 shares). Trader B buys a further-out, three-month call priced at \$5.00 (so they buy 2 contracts, controlling 200 shares). Suppose the stock jumps \$10 to \$70. Trader A's near-dated calls might see their delta leap from 0.45 to 0.90 — a delta change of 0.45 across 500 shares, forcing the dealer to buy roughly 0.45 × 500 = **225 extra shares**. Trader B's longer-dated calls see delta crawl from 0.40 to 0.50 — a change of 0.10 across 200 shares, forcing only 0.10 × 200 = **20 extra shares**. The same \$1,000, but Trader A's choice forced more than ten times the dealer buying. The intuition: in a gamma squeeze, *which* options the crowd buys matters as much as how many — short-dated near-the-money calls are the contracts that turn a dollar of premium into the most forced buying.

## The squeeze: from \$4 to \$483 and the forced covering

Now stack the three loops. The coordinated longs buy and hold, shrinking the tradable float. The dealers, forced by their hedges, buy more as the price rises. The shorts, watching their losses mount and their margin evaporate, start to *cover* — to buy back shares and close their positions — which is itself buying that drives the price still higher. Three streams of buying, two of them forced, all hitting a float that had been deliberately starved by the holding longs. The result is the most famous parabola in modern market history.

![GameStop January 2021 daily price path rising from about seventeen dollars to an intraday high near four hundred eighty-three dollars and then collapsing](/imgs/blogs/case-study-gamestop-2021-the-coordination-game-that-broke-wall-street-2.png)

In numbers, GameStop began January 2021 around **\$17 as traded** — roughly **\$4 on a split-adjusted basis** after the company's later 4-for-1 split. It crossed \$30 by mid-January, \$65 on January 22, and then went vertical: it closed near **\$148 on January 26**, near **\$348 on January 27**, and on **January 28 it touched \$483 intraday** before the rules changed. That is roughly a 100-fold move from the early-January split-adjusted low to the intraday peak, compressed into a few weeks.

On the other side of that move were the shorts, and their losses were staggering. The most visible casualty was Melvin Capital, a large hedge fund that had been heavily short GameStop. As the squeeze ran, Melvin's losses became existential: it reportedly lost about **53% of its value in January 2021** alone, and on January 25 it took a combined **\$2.75 billion** cash injection — **\$2 billion** from Citadel and **\$750 million** from Point72 — to stay afloat. Across the whole short side, mark-to-market losses on GameStop ran into the billions of dollars in January, with data firms estimating roughly **\$12.5 billion** of losses on GME shorts and far more across the broader basket of heavily shorted names that retail traders targeted next.

#### Worked example: the dollar pain of being short into the squeeze

Suppose a fund is short 1 million shares of GameStop at an average price of \$15 — a \$15 million short position, opened in the belief the stock would fall. Then the squeeze hits and the stock trades at \$300. To close, the fund must buy 1 million shares at \$300, for \$300 million. It originally sold them for \$15 million. The loss is \$300 million − \$15 million = **\$285 million** on a position that, at entry, looked like a sensible \$15 million bet against a dying retailer.

And the fund may not get to wait for a better price. With a loss like that, margin requirements explode; the fund either posts hundreds of millions in fresh collateral or its broker force-covers the position into the rising market, locking in the worst price. This is why a short squeeze is so violent: the losers are *forced buyers* whose buying is the very thing driving the price they are being forced to pay. The intuition: in a squeeze, the shorts' attempts to limit their losses are exactly what create everyone else's gains.

By January 27, the squeeze had its own momentum: forced shorts, forced dealers, and committed longs were all buying, and the float was effectively cornered. In a frictionless market, this might have continued until the shorts were fully covered. But the market is not frictionless. There was a fourth player whose available move had nothing to do with believing or disbelieving in GameStop, and who was about to be forced into action by *their own* game — the broker.

## The broker halt: a player forced to change the rules of the game

On the morning of January 28, 2021, Robinhood and several other retail brokers restricted trading in GameStop and a handful of other squeeze targets to **"position close only" (PCO)** — customers could sell their existing shares but could not buy more. The stock, which had been near \$483 intraday, collapsed toward \$112 that day. To the millions of retail traders who suddenly could not add to their positions, this looked like the system rigging the game to save the hedge funds. The reality, while less conspiratorial, is in some ways more interesting from a game-theory perspective: a player was *forced* to change the rules because their own game — solvency — left them no choice.

Here is the mechanism. When you buy a stock, the trade does not settle instantly. In the U.S. at the time, settlement took *two business days* (the "T+2" cycle). During those two days, the broker is on the hook to the central clearinghouse, the DTCC, which guarantees that both sides of every trade will be honored even if one side fails. To cover that risk, the DTCC requires each broker to post **collateral** — and that collateral requirement scales with how much, and how *volatile*, the broker's unsettled trades are.

GameStop in late January was both enormous in volume and extraordinarily volatile, and Robinhood's customers were overwhelmingly *buying* it. That combination sent Robinhood's collateral requirement vertical. By Robinhood's own later accounts, the DTCC's deposit demand on the morning of January 28 spiked to around **\$3 billion** — a sum far larger than the cash the broker had on hand. Robinhood negotiated the demand down, drew on bank credit lines, and ultimately raised roughly **\$3.4 billion** in fresh capital over the following days to keep operating. But in the immediate moment, with the clock running, it had two choices: post collateral it did not have, or reduce the unsettled buying that was generating the requirement. It chose the second. Restricting buying to position-close-only was not (on this account) a favor to hedge funds; it was a player avoiding insolvency. The before-and-after below shows how that single move changed the game.

![Before and after diagram showing the broker with buying open then restricting buying to close only after a clearinghouse collateral demand](/imgs/blogs/case-study-gamestop-2021-the-coordination-game-that-broke-wall-street-6.png)

Regardless of the *motive*, the *effect* on the game was decisive, and it is the cleanest illustration in the whole episode of what game theorists call a "game-changing move" — an action that alters the available strategies of the other players. Recall the coordination matrix: the squeeze lived in the (HOLD, HOLD) equilibrium, sustained by the common-knowledge belief that everyone would keep buying and holding. Restricting buying did two things to that equilibrium at once. First, it mechanically removed the longs' ability to add fuel — no new buying could enter through the largest retail channel. Second, and more subtly, it *shattered the common knowledge*. Once it was clear that buying could be switched off, every long had to wonder whether everyone else would keep holding a stock they could no longer defend. The belief that held the (HOLD, HOLD) equilibrium together cracked, and a coordination game whose belief cracks does not glide down — it gaps.

#### Worked example: the collateral math that forced the halt

A clearinghouse deposit is, in part, a function of the size and volatility of a broker's unsettled net buying. Suppose, very roughly, the deposit is set as some percentage of the dollar value of unsettled buys, and that percentage rises sharply with volatility. If a broker has \$5 billion of unsettled GameStop buys and the normal deposit rate is 3%, the deposit is \$150 million — manageable. Now volatility quintuples and the rate jumps to, say, 30% on the most volatile names (and a special "excess capital premium" charge is added on top). The deposit on the same \$5 billion of buys is now \$1.5 billion — and as more buying piles in, it climbs toward the ~\$3 billion that Robinhood reported facing. A broker with a few hundred million in cash simply cannot meet that overnight. The only lever it controls in the moment is the *quantity of new unsettled buying*, so it throttles buying. The intuition: the buy button was switched off not by a villain but by a balance-sheet constraint that the squeeze itself had triggered — the volatility the longs created became the collateral demand that forced the halt.

The controversy over the halt was — and remains — entirely legitimate. Even if the immediate cause was a collateral demand, the episode exposed how the *structure* of the clearing system can hand one player a rule-changing move at the exact moment another player is winning. That is a fairness question worth asking. But as a *positive* description of what happened, the game-theory reading is clean: a player constrained by their own game made the only move their game allowed, and that move ended the coordination.

## Who won and who lost

When the dust settled, the question this series always asks — who was on the other side? — has a precise and uncomfortable answer. The squeeze transferred enormous sums, and the transfers did not run simply from "Wall Street" to "retail." They ran from the people who were *late and forced* to the people who were *early and free*. The bar chart below sorts the outcomes.

![Horizontal bar chart of January 2021 outcomes showing Melvin down 53 percent, a top buyer down 89 percent, shorts losing their full positions, and early longs up sharply](/imgs/blogs/case-study-gamestop-2021-the-coordination-game-that-broke-wall-street-7.png)

- **The shorts lost, badly.** Melvin Capital's roughly **53%** January loss and **\$2.75 billion** rescue is the headline, and across the short side the mark-to-market damage ran into the billions. These were the forced buyers; the squeeze was designed to break them, and it did.
- **The early longs won, hugely.** The traders who bought GameStop near its split-adjusted single digits and sold somewhere on the way up captured the largest percentage gains in the whole episode. Keith Gill, the trader known online as "Roaring Kitty," posted a position that swung into the tens of millions of dollars at the peak. They were on the *winning* side of both the shorts' forced covering and the dealers' forced hedging.
- **The latecomers lost.** And this is the part the triumphant narrative leaves out. Anyone who bought near the **\$483** intraday top — drawn in by the headlines and the "hold the line" energy at the worst possible moment — was buying from an early long who was *selling to them*. A buyer at the top who held into the February 4 close near **\$53.50** was down roughly **89%**. They were the [greater fool](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-greater-fool-and-rational-bubbles-the-musical-chairs-game) — the buyer who pays a price that only makes sense if an even later buyer shows up, and for them, no later buyer did.

This is the brutal symmetry of a coordination game that depends on belief. The same mechanism that made early holders rich made late holders the exit liquidity for those early holders. "Retail won" is true only if you mean *some* retail, *early* retail. The retail traders who bought the top and held in solidarity were not on the other side of the hedge funds; they were on the other side of the *early retail traders who were selling to them*. The coordination held until it didn't, and when it broke, the latecomers were precisely the bagholders the matrix warned about — the (HOLD, crowd-SELLS) cell, where you take the −8.

#### Worked example: the wealth transfer is a chain of counterparties

Trace a single share through the episode to see where the money actually went. Suppose an early long buys one share at \$15. The price runs, and on January 27 they sell it to a momentum buyer at \$300 — the early long banks \$285. The momentum buyer sells it on January 28 to a euphoric latecomer at \$450, banking \$150. The latecomer holds in solidarity, and by February 4 the share is worth \$53.50 — a loss of \$396.50 for them. Meanwhile, somewhere upstream, a short seller who originally sold a borrowed share at \$15 had to buy one back at, say, \$320 to cover, locking in a \$305 loss. Add it up: the short lost \$305, the latecomer lost \$396.50, and those losses funded the early long's \$285 and the momentum buyer's \$150 (the rest is paid by other shorts and other latecomers along the chain). Every dollar of gain is a specific dollar of someone else's loss, and the losers are *forced* (the short) and *late* (the latecomer) while the winners are *early* and *free*. The intuition: a squeeze is not "retail beats Wall Street," it is a chain of counterparties in which the early and unforced collect from the late and forced — and your only real question is which end of that chain you are on.

## Common misconceptions

**"It was retail versus Wall Street, and retail won."** Partly true, mostly a simplification. The shorts (Wall Street) genuinely lost billions, and *early* retail genuinely won. But the dealers were neutral hedgers on no one's side, the broker acted on its own solvency, and the *late* retail buyers lost — and they lost to *early retail sellers*, not to hedge funds. The clean "us versus them" framing obscures the four-player structure and, worse, encourages exactly the late-buying behavior that turned solidarity into someone else's exit liquidity.

**"Robinhood halted buying to protect the hedge funds."** This is the most persistent claim, and the evidence assembled by regulators and in Robinhood's own disclosures points instead to a clearinghouse collateral demand of roughly **\$3 billion** that the broker could not meet. That does not make the outcome *fair* — the clearing structure handed one player a rule-changing move at a decisive moment — but the immediate cause was a balance-sheet constraint, not a conspiracy to rescue Melvin. Confusing the *effect* (the squeeze ended) with a malicious *motive* is a category error that game theory helps you avoid: ask what game the player was forced to play before assigning intent.

**"A short squeeze means the company is great."** No. Not one dollar of the GameStop move required the company's prospects to improve. The buying came from coordinated longs (belief-driven), forced shorts (margin-driven), and forced dealers (hedge-driven). A squeeze is a statement about *positioning and mechanics*, not about fundamentals. The clearest proof is that the price collapsed roughly 90% within days while the company's actual business was unchanged.

**"If you'd just held, you'd have won."** This is survivorship talking. The coordination matrix shows two equilibria, and the move that pays depends entirely on what *everyone else* does. "Hold" pays only in the (HOLD, HOLD) corner; in the (HOLD, crowd-SELLS) corner it is the worst cell on the board. After the halt, the common knowledge that anchored the good equilibrium was gone, and "just hold" became the recipe for an 89% loss. There was no strategy that was correct independent of the crowd, which is the defining feature of a coordination game.

**"The gamma squeeze was the cause."** The gamma squeeze was an *amplifier*, not the root cause. The root cause was the crowded short above 100% of float — the loaded gun. The coordinated longs lit it, and the gamma loop poured accelerant on it, but without the structural short-interest trap there would have been nothing to squeeze. Treating the gamma loop as the prime mover leads people to hunt for "the next gamma squeeze" while ignoring the short-interest pre-condition that actually matters.

## How it shows up in real markets

GameStop was extreme, but every element of it recurs. Spotting the pattern is the practical payoff of the whole exercise.

**Volkswagen, October 2008 — the squeeze that briefly made VW the world's most valuable company.** Porsche had quietly accumulated control of around 74% of Volkswagen, and the state of Lower Saxony held about 20%, leaving a tiny free float. Short sellers, who had bet VW would fall in the financial crisis, suddenly faced a float far too small for them all to cover. The stock spiked roughly five-fold in two days, briefly touching over €1,000 and making VW the most valuable listed company on Earth on paper, before collapsing. Same structure as GameStop: a cornered float, trapped shorts, forced covering — minus the retail coordination, which Porsche supplied instead by hoarding the shares.

**KOSS and the "meme basket," January 2021.** GameStop was not alone. The same retail coordination spilled into other heavily shorted small caps — KOSS, AMC, BlackBerry, Bed Bath & Beyond — many of which spiked hundreds of percent in the same week on the same playbook: high short interest, a small float, and a forum turning dispersed buyers into a coordinated long. Most round-tripped just as fast. The pattern, not the ticker, is the lesson.

**The "most-shorted" factor as a tradable signal.** After 2021, the structure became something professionals actively watch and, in some cases, front-run. Baskets of the most heavily shorted stocks are now tracked precisely because crowded shorts above a high percentage of float carry asymmetric squeeze risk. The very fact that this is now a known, watched pattern changes the game again — which is the [reflexive](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game) nature of all such edges: once everyone knows where the gunpowder is, the dynamics shift.

**Settlement reform — T+2 to T+1.** The collateral squeeze on brokers was a direct input to a structural change: in May 2024, U.S. equity settlement moved from T+2 to **T+1**, halving the window during which brokers carry clearinghouse collateral on unsettled trades. The explicit goal was to reduce exactly the kind of collateral spike that forced Robinhood's hand. The episode literally changed the plumbing of the market — a rare case of a single squeeze rewriting the rules of the game for everyone.

**AMC and the second wave, mid-2021.** A few months after GameStop, the same coordinated-long machine turned to AMC Entertainment, another heavily shorted name beloved on the forums. AMC ran from low single digits to roughly \$72 intraday in June 2021. The structure was identical — crowded short, retail coordination, heavy call buying — but the *fundamentals* response differed: AMC's management, unlike GameStop's at the time, aggressively issued new shares into the rally, selling hundreds of millions of dollars of stock to the very buyers driving the squeeze. That is a quietly important game-theory move: a company facing a squeeze in its own stock can *be* the seller the coordinated longs are buying from, converting the squeeze into a capital raise. The longs cornering the float can find that the float is no longer fixed when the issuer chooses to expand it.

**The dog that did not bark: why most crowded shorts never squeeze.** It is easy to look at GameStop and conclude that any heavily shorted stock is a coiled spring. Most are not. Plenty of names carry short interest above 50% of float for months and simply drift lower as the thesis plays out, because the missing ingredient is the *coordinated long*. Sears, J.C. Penney, and a long list of declining retailers were heavily shorted for years and mostly just declined. The short interest is the gunpowder, but gunpowder without a spark is just a pile of risk that quietly pays the shorts. This is the single most important corrective to the "find the next GameStop" instinct: the rare ingredient is not the crowded short, which is common, but the common-knowledge machine that turns dispersed buyers into one coordinated player, which is rare and fragile.

**Every "diamond hands" rally since.** Whenever a heavily shorted name catches a coordinated retail bid, the same four-player grid applies. The questions are always the same: How crowded is the short (percent of float, days to cover)? Is there a common-knowledge machine making the longs coordinate? Are dealers being forced to hedge a wall of call buying? And what rule-changing move can a broker or exchange make if it gets out of hand? If you can answer those four, you understand the rally better than the headlines do.

## The playbook: how to read a squeeze before you are in it

This series is built to answer one question — who is on the other side? — and GameStop is the purest case study for turning that question into a checklist. None of what follows is advice to trade these situations; squeezes are among the most dangerous setups in markets, and the latecomers' 89% loss is the base rate, not the exception. The point is to *read* the game well enough to know which player you would be.

**Identify the four players before you do anything.** Who is short, and how trapped are they (short interest as a percent of float, days to cover)? Who is the coordinated long, and is there a common-knowledge machine sustaining their belief? Are dealers being forced to hedge a wall of calls? And — the one almost everyone forgets — what rule-changing move can a broker, exchange, or clearinghouse make? If you cannot name all four, you are not reading the game; you are watching a chart.

**Know which equilibrium you are betting on, and what breaks it.** A squeeze lives in the (HOLD, HOLD) corner of a coordination game, and that corner is held together by *belief*, not by fundamentals. Your real risk is not that the company's business changes; it is that the *belief* changes. Anything that shatters common knowledge — a broker halt, a wave of profit-taking by early longs, a sudden expiry of the calls fueling the gamma loop — can flip the equilibrium in an afternoon. The invalidation of a squeeze trade is "the coordination is breaking," and it usually shows up in price *before* it shows up in any news.

**Respect that the forced buyers are finite.** The entire reason a squeeze is violent and brief is that forced buying has a hard ceiling. The shorts can only be squeezed until they are covered; the dealers can only be forced to buy until the calls expire or roll off. Once the forced buyers are out of the market, the only remaining buyers are the discretionary longs — and if they start selling to each other, the bid that held the price up simply vanishes. A squeeze does not "top out" gently; it runs out of forced buyers and gaps.

**Assume you might be the greater fool.** The hardest discipline. In a coordination game that depends on belief, the late buyer is buying from the early buyer, and *someone* has to be last. If your reason for buying is "it is going up and everyone is holding," you are describing the (HOLD, crowd-SELLS) risk, not an edge. The early longs who quietly sold into the strength were the ones on the right side of the late retail buyers' solidarity. Being on the right side of the squeeze means being early and free, not late and committed.

**Size for the gap, not the trend.** If you are ever near one of these, the position-sizing math is dominated by the downside gap, not the upside slope. A stock that ran 100-fold can retrace 90% in days, and a forced-off buy button means you may not be able to defend or even exit at the price you expect. The honest sizing assumption is that the worst-case exit is a gap to a fraction of the peak with the buy side switched off — which, for most people, argues for treating these as something to *study*, not something to *hold*.

The deepest lesson of GameStop is not "retail can beat Wall Street." It is that markets are made of players in different games, each forced into moves by constraints the others cannot see, and that the price is just the running scoreboard of those forced moves. The shorts were forced by margin. The dealers were forced by their hedges. The broker was forced by its collateral. And the longs were "forced" only by a belief they held in common — which is the most fragile constraint of all, because beliefs are the one thing in this whole machine that can change their mind overnight.
