> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A bank run is not a bet against the bank's assets; it is a *coordination game* with two self-fulfilling equilibria, and which one you land in depends only on what you believe everyone else will do.
>
> - A bank borrows short (deposits you can pull today) and lends long (loans and bonds that mature in years), so it physically cannot pay everyone at once — this *maturity mismatch* is the root of every run.
> - That mismatch creates two equilibria (Diamond-Dybvig 1983): the **good** one where everyone waits and earns interest, and the **bad** one where everyone runs and the bank fire-sells and fails. The fundamentals can be *identical* in both.
> - Running is rational once you believe enough others will run. In the model, the tipping point is about half the depositors; below it waiting pays \$1.50 per \$1, above it waiting pays \$0.40 and running pays more.
> - Deposit insurance and a central-bank lender of last resort do not "calm nerves" — they *delete the bad equilibrium* by making waiting always safe, so no one has a reason to run first.
> - **The one rule:** in a coordination game the dangerous variable is not the balance sheet, it is common belief — and in 2023, SVB proved a digital-speed run can drain \$42 billion in a day once that belief goes common knowledge.

On the afternoon of Thursday, March 9, 2023, depositors at Silicon Valley Bank tried to withdraw about \$42 billion — roughly a quarter of the bank's entire deposit base — in a single day. They did it without standing in a single line. They did it from their phones, from group chats, from Slack channels, from a Twitter feed that had spent twenty-four hours convincing everyone that everyone else was about to leave. By the next morning, queued withdrawal requests had ballooned toward \$100 billion more, and before lunch on Friday the regulators had seized the bank. The Federal Reserve's vice chair for supervision later testified that customers had tried to pull out nearly the bank's *entire* deposit base over those two days.

Here is the strange part, and the part this whole post is about. The bank that everyone was fleeing on Friday morning held mostly the same assets it held on Wednesday afternoon, when it looked perfectly healthy. No new loans had gone bad overnight. No fraud had been discovered. The bond portfolio that was sitting on a paper loss had been sitting on roughly that same loss for months. What changed was not the assets. What changed was *what each depositor believed the other depositors were about to do.* And once enough of them believed everyone else was running, running became the only sane choice — which made the belief true.

That is the signature of a *coordination game*: a strategic situation where the best thing for you to do is whatever everyone else does, so the outcome is decided not by the underlying facts but by a shared, self-fulfilling expectation. A bank run is the cleanest, deadliest coordination game in all of finance, and once you can read it as one, you understand not just SVB but every panic from 1907 to 2008 to the next one. The chart below is the mental model for the entire post: one bank, one set of assets, two completely different fates, and the only thing that selects between them is belief.

![Two columns showing the good all-wait equilibrium and the bad all-run equilibrium of a bank run](/imgs/blogs/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb-1.png)

Read the two columns side by side. On the left, everyone believes everyone will wait, so everyone waits, the bank holds its loans to maturity and stays solvent, and you collect your interest — a payoff of \$1.50 on every \$1 you left in. On the right, everyone believes everyone will run, so everyone runs, the bank is forced to dump its illiquid assets at fire-sale prices and collapses, and you scramble to get out with \$0.50 on the dollar. The bank is *the same bank* in both columns. The difference is entirely in the first box: what you believe about everyone else. That is what we are going to take apart, build back up from zero, and turn into a way of reading real panics.

## Foundations: what a bank is, and why it is built to be fragile

Let us start with absolutely no assumptions and build the bank, the mismatch, and the game from scratch. By the end of this section you will understand exactly why a perfectly solvent bank can be killed by a rumor.

### What a bank actually does

Strip away the marble lobbies and the apps and a bank is a very simple machine with two sides. On one side it takes in money from people who want it back whenever they ask — that money is a *deposit*, and a deposit is technically a loan *you* make to the bank, repayable *on demand*. On the other side, the bank takes that pooled money and lends it out: to homebuyers as 30-year mortgages, to businesses as multi-year loans, or it buys bonds — IOUs from governments and companies that pay it back over five, ten, or thirty years. The gap between the low interest the bank pays you and the higher interest it earns on those loans is, roughly, how a bank makes money.

Notice the asymmetry, because it is the entire ballgame. The money the bank *owes* (your deposits) can be demanded back **today**. The money the bank *is owed* (its loans and bonds) comes back over **years**. A bank deliberately runs a mismatch between the timing of what it owes and the timing of what it will be paid. The industry term is *maturity transformation* — the bank "transforms" short-term savings into long-term loans, and society mostly wants it to, because that is how a sleepy savings account becomes someone's mortgage. But the same trick that funds the economy is also a structural fault line.

![Two columns contrasting short-term liabilities due now with long-term assets that mature in years](/imgs/blogs/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb-4.png)

The diagram above is the whole vulnerability in one picture. On the left are the liabilities — what the bank owes — and every one of them is due *now*: deposits redeemable today, promised back at *par* (their full face value, a dollar for a dollar), and in the modern world, movable out of the bank in *seconds* by tapping a phone. On the right are the assets — what the bank owns — and every one of them matures in *years*: mortgages running 5 to 30 years, held-to-maturity bonds running a decade, all of them worth less than face value if the bank is forced to sell them early in a hurry. A *held-to-maturity bond*, or HTM bond, is one the bank intends to keep until it matures and pays back its full face value; the bank is allowed to carry it on the books at that face value precisely *because* it plans to hold it — but if it is forced to *sell* before maturity, it gets only today's market price, which can be far lower.

This is the structural fact that makes a run possible: **the bank cannot pay everyone at once, even when it is solvent.** *Solvent* means the bank's assets, valued properly and held to maturity, are worth more than what it owes — the bank is not bankrupt; it has enough, eventually. But *liquid* means having the cash *right now*. A bank can be solvent and illiquid at the same time, and in a run, illiquidity is what kills you. If every depositor demands cash on the same Tuesday, the bank has to turn long bonds into immediate cash, and the only way to do that fast is to sell them to someone else at whatever price that someone will pay today — a *fire sale* — which means selling a \$100 asset for \$80 or \$70. Do enough of that and a solvent bank really does run out of money. The run *manufactures* the insolvency it was afraid of.

### What "a game" means here, in one minute

The word *game* in game theory does not mean something frivolous. A *game* is any situation where your best move depends on what other people do, so you have to reason about *their* reasoning. The pieces are always the same. The *players* are the decision-makers — here, the depositors, each deciding whether to leave their money in or pull it out. Each player has a set of *strategies*, the moves available — here just two: **WAIT** (leave your money in) or **RUN** (pull it out now). Each combination of everyone's moves produces a *payoff* for each player — here, how many cents on the dollar you walk away with. And the *equilibrium* is a combination of strategies where no single player, looking at what everyone else is doing, wishes they had chosen differently. An equilibrium is a *stable* situation: nobody wants to unilaterally deviate.

The specific equilibrium concept we will use is the *Nash equilibrium*, named for the mathematician John Nash. A Nash equilibrium is a set of strategies — one per player — such that each player's choice is the *best response* to everyone else's choice. "Best response" just means: given what everyone else is doing, this is the move that pays me the most. If you are in a Nash equilibrium and I freeze everyone else's behavior, you cannot do better by changing yours alone. We covered this engine in [Nash Equilibrium, Best Response, and the Price as a Truce](/blog/trading/game-theory/nash-equilibrium-best-response-and-the-price-as-a-truce); here we only need the punchline, which is that *some games have more than one Nash equilibrium*, and a bank run is the most consequential example.

### Why some games have two answers

Most people meet game theory through the *prisoner's dilemma*, where the game has exactly one equilibrium and it is the bad one — both players betray each other even though both would be better off cooperating. A bank run is a different animal. It is a *coordination game*, and the defining feature of a coordination game is that you want to *do the same thing as everyone else*. If everyone waits, you want to wait. If everyone runs, you want to run. There is no single "right" move independent of the crowd; the right move is *match the crowd.* And that is exactly the property that produces *multiple equilibria*: both "everyone waits" and "everyone runs" are self-consistent, because in each, no individual gains by being the odd one out.

The everyday version is which side of the road to drive on. If everyone drives on the right, you must drive on the right; if everyone drives on the left, you must drive on the left. *Both* are stable equilibria — neither is "wrong," and a country can sit in either one forever. There is nothing in the asphalt that prefers left or right; the only thing that matters is that everyone agrees. A bank run is the malignant cousin of this game: one of its two equilibria (everyone waits) is wonderful and the other (everyone runs) is catastrophic, and there is nothing in the *balance sheet* that decides which one a real bank falls into. The thing that decides is collective belief — and collective belief can flip in an afternoon.

This is the core idea of the model that won Douglas Diamond and Philip Dybvig (with Ben Bernanke) the 2022 Nobel Prize in economics, published in 1983 and now simply called *Diamond-Dybvig*. Their insight was to show, with a clean mathematical model, that bank runs are not necessarily the result of a bank being secretly rotten. A perfectly healthy bank has *two* equilibria baked into its very structure, and a run is just the economy sliding from the good one to the bad one — sometimes for a good reason, sometimes for no reason at all. Let us now make the payoffs concrete with numbers.

## The payoffs: why running is rational once others run

To see the game clearly we need actual numbers for the payoffs, so let us use the stylized Diamond-Dybvig setup from the series' model code (`data_gametheory.bank_run`). Imagine you have \$1 in the bank. You have two choices and the payoff to each depends on the *fraction of other depositors who run*, which we will call $f$.

If the bank survives (few enough others run), then:

- If you **WAIT**, you earn interest and get back \$1.50 — the bank had time to collect on its profitable long-term loans, so patient depositors are rewarded.
- If you **RUN**, you just get your \$1 back early. You forfeit the interest but you are safe.

If the bank fails (too many others run), then:

- If you **WAIT** (you were slow, you trusted the system), you are a *late* depositor: the bank has already paid out the runners and fire-sold its assets, and you split the scraps. You get back only about \$0.40.
- If you **RUN** early, you grab cash before the bank empties: you get roughly \$1 if you are near the front, declining toward \$0.50 as more people pile in and the pro-rata share shrinks.

The single number that flips the whole game is the *critical fraction*, $f_{crit}$ — the share of depositors who must run before the bank tips from "survives" to "fails." In our model $f_{crit} = 0.5$: the bank can handle up to half its depositors pulling out, but past that it breaks. (Real banks fail at far lower thresholds, because they hold only a sliver of deposits as cash; we will fix the realism shortly.) Here is the payoff to each choice, plotted against how many others run.

![Line chart of the wait payoff and run payoff against the fraction of others who run with a tipping point at fifty percent](/imgs/blogs/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb-2.png)

Trace the two lines. On the left side of the chart — when few others are running ($f$ below 50%) — the green WAIT line sits at \$1.50, comfortably above the red RUN line at \$1.00. Waiting is strictly better; you would be silly to run and give up the interest. But the instant the fraction of runners crosses the tipping point at 50%, the green WAIT payoff *collapses* from \$1.50 to \$0.40, because now the bank fails and you are a late, scrap-collecting depositor. On the right side, the red RUN line ($\$1.00$ falling toward $\$0.50$) is *above* the green WAIT line ($\$0.40$). Now running is strictly better. The chart proves the central, unsettling claim: **your optimal move flips depending only on a number you do not control — how many others run.** Below the tipping point, wait. Above it, run. And everyone is staring at the same chart, trying to guess which side of the line everyone else will be on.

#### Worked example: the two equilibria, in dollars

Let us nail down why "everyone waits" and "everyone runs" are *both* stable, using the payoffs above. Start in the good world: suppose every other depositor is going to wait, so $f = 0$. What should you do? Look at the chart at $f=0$: WAIT pays \$1.50, RUN pays \$1.00. You wait. And critically, *every other depositor faces the exact same arithmetic and also chooses to wait* — which is what made $f=0$ true in the first place. Nobody wants to deviate. "Everyone waits" is a Nash equilibrium, and your payoff is \$1.50.

Now start in the bad world: suppose every other depositor is going to run, so $f = 1$. What should you do? Look at the chart at $f=1$: WAIT pays \$0.40, RUN pays \$0.50. You run — getting \$0.50 beats getting \$0.40. And again, *every other depositor faces the same arithmetic and also runs*, which is what made $f=1$ true. Nobody wants to deviate from running either. "Everyone runs" is *also* a Nash equilibrium, and your payoff is \$0.50.

The intuition: the same bank, with the same loans and the same bonds, supports two completely different self-consistent outcomes — \$1.50 if belief is good, \$0.50 if belief is bad — and the model cannot tell you which one will happen, because both are equilibria.

That last point is worth sitting with, because it is what makes runs so frightening and so hard to prevent. A model with a *unique* equilibrium is reassuring: tell me the fundamentals and I will tell you the outcome. A coordination game with *two* equilibria is the opposite. The fundamentals are consistent with paradise *and* with catastrophe, and the selection between them is made by something the model does not contain — a sunspot, a rumor, a tweet, a CEO's clumsy email. Economists literally call these triggers *sunspots*: events with no fundamental relevance that nonetheless move the economy from one equilibrium to another, purely by coordinating everyone's beliefs.

## The run as a 2×2 game: matching the crowd

Diamond-Dybvig is a many-player model, but the strategic heart of it shrinks to a 2×2 game between *you* and *everyone else as one bloc.* Collapsing "all the other depositors" into a single representative player loses nothing essential — what matters to you is just whether *that bloc* waits or runs — and it lets us draw the canonical payoff matrix that makes the coordination structure unmistakable.

![Two by two payoff matrix for the bank run coordination game showing both wait and both run as equilibria](/imgs/blogs/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb-3.png)

Read the matrix the way you read any game's payoff table: you pick a row (WAIT or RUN), the crowd picks a column (WAIT or RUN), and the cell where they meet gives both payoffs — yours first, theirs second. The two cells on the *diagonal* — top-left (both wait, \$1.50 / \$1.50) and bottom-right (both run, \$0.50 / \$0.50) — are the equilibria, and you can verify it by the no-regret test. In the top-left cell, if the crowd is waiting, switching to RUN would drop you from \$1.50 to \$1.00; you do not want to deviate, so it is stable. In the bottom-right cell, if the crowd is running, switching to WAIT would drop you from \$0.50 to \$0.40; again you do not want to deviate, so it is stable too. The *off-diagonal* cells (one of you runs, the other waits) are unstable: whoever is out of step wishes they had matched the crowd.

When you feed these payoffs into the series' Nash solver (`data_gametheory.nash_2x2`), it confirms exactly this: two pure-strategy equilibria — both-WAIT and both-RUN — plus one knife-edge *mixed* equilibrium where each side runs with probability about 1/6. Let us work through that mixed case, because it tells you what the "panic threshold" really is.

#### Worked example: the belief threshold that tips you into running

A *mixed strategy* means randomizing — running with some probability $p$ rather than always doing one thing. There is a special probability at which you become *indifferent* between waiting and running, and that probability is the real danger line, because below it you wait and above it you run. Indifference means the expected payoff of WAIT equals the expected payoff of RUN. Let $p$ be the probability the crowd runs.

Expected payoff of WAIT $= (1-p)\times\$1.50 + p\times\$0.40$. (If the crowd waits, you get \$1.50; if it runs, you are a late waiter and get \$0.40.)

Expected payoff of RUN $= (1-p)\times\$1.00 + p\times\$0.50$. (If the crowd waits, running gets your \$1 back; if it runs, you grab roughly \$0.50.)

Set them equal: $\$1.50 - 1.10p = \$1.00 - 0.50p$. Solving, $\$0.50 = 0.60p$, so $p = 0.50/0.60 \approx 0.83$. So you are indifferent when you believe the crowd runs with probability about 0.83 — equivalently, when you think there is only about a 1/6 chance the crowd *waits*, which is exactly the mixed equilibrium the solver reports. Below that danger line you wait; above it you run.

The intuition: you do not need to see a single other person withdraw to start running. You only need to *believe* the probability of a run has crossed your personal threshold — and so does everyone else, which is why panics ignite from pure belief.

This is the engine of every panic. Because the threshold is about *belief*, anything that suddenly raises everyone's estimate of "the chance others will run" — a scary headline, a falling stock price, a regulator's terse statement, a screenshot in a group chat — can shove the whole population across the line at once. No fundamental needs to change. The belief *is* the fundamental, in a coordination game.

#### Worked example: why "I'll just be early" is a trap for everyone

Suppose a bank has \$100 of deposits across 100 depositors (\$1 each) but holds only \$30 in immediate cash, with the other \$70 tied up in loans it can sell at a fire-sale price of just 50 cents on the dollar this week. The first 30 depositors to the door get paid in full from the cash: \$1 each. Depositor number 31 finds the cash drawer empty; the bank must now fire-sell loans. To raise \$1 of cash it must dump \$2 of face-value loans (at 50 cents each), so the \$70 of loans yields only \$35 of cash — enough to pay 35 more depositors. So 65 of 100 depositors get paid in full and 35 get *nothing* this week (they wait for the slow liquidation and recover maybe 40 cents later).

Now reason as a depositor: if you think a run is coming, being in the first 65 means \$1.00 and being in the last 35 means \$0.40. The expected reward for *speed* is enormous and the penalty for *trust* is brutal. So everyone races to be early — which guarantees the run. The "I'll get out before the music stops" plan is not a clever edge; it is the *precise mechanism* that stops the music.

The intuition: when being early is richly rewarded and being late is punished, "be early" is everyone's best response, so the orderly equilibrium cannot hold — the race to the exit *is* the run.

This is also why the cliché *"I'll get out before everyone else"* is so dangerous. In a coordination game, the payoff to being early is exactly what *causes* the stampede. We dissect that exact fallacy for crowded positions in [Crowded Trades and the Exit Game](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game) — a bank run is the same exit game, except the asset you are trying to exit is your own deposit, the door is the teller window, and there is no liquidity provider on the other side willing to buy at a fair price.

## The cure: how deposit insurance deletes the bad equilibrium

Here is the most beautiful and counterintuitive result in the whole theory, and it is the part most people get wrong. The fix for bank runs is not to make depositors braver, or to lecture them about the bank's solvency, or to slow them down with withdrawal limits. The fix is to *change the payoffs* so that the bad equilibrium *stops existing.* If waiting is always at least as good as running — no matter what everyone else does — then running is never a best response, the bottom-right cell of the matrix is no longer stable, and the only equilibrium left is the good one.

This is exactly what *deposit insurance* does. Deposit insurance is a government promise that even if your bank fails, you get your money back up to a limit (in the U.S., the FDIC insures \$250,000 per depositor per bank). And a *lender of last resort* — the central bank — does the same thing from the asset side: it stands ready to lend the bank cash today against its good long-term assets, at or near their full value, so the bank never has to fire-sell. Either tool snaps the WAIT payoff flat: you get your money whether or not the crowd runs.

![Two panels comparing the bank run payoffs without insurance and with insurance where the run payoff never beats waiting](/imgs/blogs/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb-5.png)

Compare the two panels. On the left — *no insurance* — you see the same crossing lines as before: above the 50% tipping point, the red RUN line beats the green WAIT line, so the bad equilibrium is alive and a run can happen. On the right — *with insurance* — the green WAIT line is now *flat at \$1.50 across the entire chart*. It never collapses, because an insured depositor is paid in full no matter how many others run. The red RUN line never gets above it. There is no region where running pays more than waiting. **The bad equilibrium has been deleted from the game.** And here is the magic: because the insurance removes the *incentive* to run, the run never happens, so the insurance fund almost never has to pay out. The promise is mostly self-enforcing. The mere existence of the backstop is what makes the backstop rarely needed.

#### Worked example: the backstop you never have to use

Picture two identical banks, each with \$1 billion in deposits and a solid but illiquid loan book. Bank A is uninsured; Bank B is fully insured. A rumor hits both on the same Monday. At Bank A, each depositor reasons: "If others run and I wait, I get 40 cents; if I run, I get a dollar. Better run." The run happens, Bank A fire-sells, and the deposit base that flees costs society the fire-sale loss — say \$200 million of value destroyed turning good loans into rushed cash. At Bank B, each depositor reasons: "If others run and I wait, the FDIC pays me 100 cents anyway. Why would I bother running?" Nobody runs. Bank B holds its loans to maturity, loses *nothing*, and the insurance fund pays out *zero*. The insurance cost society \$0 in this episode and saved \$200 million — by changing a belief, not a balance sheet.

The intuition: a credible backstop is cheapest precisely when it works, because it earns its keep by *preventing* the event it insures against, not by paying claims after the fact.

This is why the 2008 and 2023 interventions looked, to an untrained eye, like the government "bailing out" banks for free. From the coordination-game view they were doing something subtler and cheaper: re-engineering the payoff matrix so that the panic equilibrium evaporated. When U.S. regulators announced on the weekend after SVB's failure that *all* depositors — even the uninsured ones above \$250,000 — would be made whole, they were not being generous. They were flattening the WAIT line at every other regional bank in the country, because depositors at First Republic and Pacific Western were doing exactly the same arithmetic, and the cheapest way to stop *those* runs was to delete the bad equilibrium everywhere at once.

There is, of course, a cost, and any honest treatment has to name it. If waiting is *always* safe, depositors stop caring whether their bank is well-run — they have no reason to monitor it, and bankers have a reason to take bigger risks knowing the downside is socialized. This is *moral hazard*: insurance against a bad outcome makes people behave more recklessly because they no longer bear the consequence. The whole apparatus of bank regulation — capital requirements, stress tests, supervision — exists to offset the moral hazard that the run-stopping insurance creates. You remove one game (the run) and you open another (the regulator-versus-risk-taking game). There is no free lunch; there is only a better-chosen game.

## The digital-speed run: when belief goes common knowledge instantly

For ninety years after the 1930s, the Diamond-Dybvig model had a built-in shock absorber that nobody put in the equations: *friction.* A classic run required physically showing up at a branch, standing in a line, and watching the line grow — a process that took days, gave regulators a weekend to act, and gave the panic time to either build or fizzle. The 2023 episode revealed that we had quietly removed that shock absorber. SVB was the first major *digital-speed run*, and understanding why it was so fast is understanding the difference between *information* and *common knowledge.*

### Information versus common knowledge

There is a deep distinction at the heart of every coordination game, and it is the hinge on which a run turns. *Private information* is something you know. *Common knowledge* is something everyone knows, *and* everyone knows that everyone knows, *and* everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone knows, all the way down. The difference is not pedantic — in a coordination game it is the difference between calm and stampede. You can privately worry that the bank is shaky and still wait, because you do not know whether *others* are worried. But the moment it becomes *common knowledge* that everyone is worried, the calculation changes: now you know others will run, so you must run, and you know they are reasoning the same way about you. Common knowledge is the trigger that fires the bad equilibrium.

We unpack this machinery in depth in [Common Knowledge and "I Know That You Know That I Know"](/blog/trading/game-theory/common-knowledge-and-i-know-that-you-know-that-i-know); the one thing to carry here is that *the run does not start when people get bad news — it starts when the bad news becomes common knowledge.* A whispered worry is private. A trending hashtag is common knowledge. A screenshot of a venture capitalist telling all his portfolio companies to "get your money out NOW," forwarded to ten thousand founders in an hour, is the purest common-knowledge generator ever built — because every recipient knows that every other recipient just got the same message and is, right now, deciding to run.

![Pipeline showing a worry posted on social media becoming common knowledge and accelerating into a faster bank run](/imgs/blogs/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb-6.png)

The diagram traces the feedback loop that made SVB different. A worry posts on social media. Thousands see it at the same instant — that simultaneity is what manufactures common knowledge. Each of them taps an app and wires money out in *seconds*, with no line, no friction, no cooling-off period. The outflow spikes, the spike itself becomes news, the news reaches *more* depositors, *they* run, and the faster outflow accelerates the next round. The loop closes on itself: each turn of the wheel makes the next turn faster. A 1930s run was a slow burn measured in days; SVB's run was a flash fire measured in hours, because mobile banking removed the friction of *acting* and social media removed the friction of *coordinating belief.*

#### Worked example: how fast is a digital run, really?

Put numbers on it. SVB held about \$175 billion in deposits. On Thursday, March 9, depositors attempted to withdraw about \$42 billion — call it 24% of the entire deposit base — *in one business day.* In our coordination model the bank tips at $f_{crit}$; a real bank like SVB, holding only a thin slice of deposits as ready cash, tips at a far lower fraction. If the survivable fraction is, say, 20% in a day, then \$42 billion of attempted withdrawals against a 20% line means the run blew through the tipping point before the bank could even react. For comparison, in the 1984 run on Continental Illinois — then the largest U.S. bank failure — the outflow that doomed it played out over about *ten days*. SVB compressed a comparable proportional run into roughly *one*. Same game, same math, ten times the speed.

The intuition: digital banking did not change the equilibria — both still exist — but it slashed the time to jump from the good one to the bad one from days to hours, leaving no window for a calming intervention.

This is the genuinely new risk of the 2020s, and it is structural, not a one-off. The Diamond-Dybvig equilibria are timeless, but the *transition speed* between them is a function of technology, and technology has made the transition nearly instantaneous. Regulators built their entire run-fighting playbook — the FDIC seizure done over a weekend, the lender-of-last-resort facility opened on Monday — around the assumption that a run takes days. A run that takes hours can finish before the weekend even arrives. That is the policy problem SVB exposed, and nobody has fully solved it yet.

## Why SVB specifically: the modern twists that loaded the gun

A coordination game needs two things to go badly: the *structure* that makes a bad equilibrium possible (the maturity mismatch, always present) and a *trigger* that flips belief. SVB had the structure of every bank, but it also had three modern twists that made it unusually flammable — a concentrated depositor base, a mountain of uninsured deposits, and a specific kind of bond loss that the system had underestimated. Let us go through the balance sheet that the runners were staring at.

![Bar chart of SVB deposits uninsured deposits HTM bond book HTM unrealized loss and one day withdrawals](/imgs/blogs/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb-7.png)

Read the bars left to right. SVB held about \$175.5 billion in total deposits, of which roughly \$151.6 billion — about 86% — were *uninsured*, meaning above the \$250,000 FDIC limit. It held a \$91.3 billion *held-to-maturity* bond portfolio carried at face value, sitting on an unrealized loss of roughly \$15.1 billion because interest rates had risen and the old low-rate bonds had fallen in market value. And the last bar is the run itself: the \$42 billion that depositors tried to yank on a single Thursday. Each bar maps to one of the twists, so let us take them in turn.

### Twist one: the maturity mismatch was unusually severe

SVB had taken in a flood of deposits during the 2020–2021 tech boom and parked an enormous share of it in long-dated bonds — exactly the maturity mismatch from our Foundations section, but stretched to an extreme. When the Federal Reserve raised interest rates sharply in 2022, the market value of those long bonds dropped, the way the price of any fixed-rate bond falls when new bonds pay more. On paper, SVB's *held-to-maturity* book carried a \$15.1 billion unrealized loss — "unrealized" because the bank had not sold the bonds and, if it held them to maturity, would still get its money back. The mismatch did not make SVB insolvent on a hold-to-maturity basis. But it meant that if SVB were *forced to sell* those bonds to raise cash — the exact thing a run forces — the loss would crystallize and the bank's thin equity cushion would be wiped out. The fire-sale risk from our model was not hypothetical; it was \$15 billion, itemized.

#### Worked example: the loss that turned paper into fire

SVB's equity cushion — the buffer between what it owned and what it owed — was on the order of \$16 billion. Its unrealized bond loss was about \$15 billion. Hold the bonds to maturity and that \$15 billion stays on paper, the bonds repay at par, and the cushion is intact. But sell even a chunk of them to fund withdrawals and the loss becomes *real*: SVB actually did sell \$21 billion of its available-for-sale bonds and realized about a \$1.8 billion loss, then announced it needed to raise \$2.25 billion of new capital to plug the hole. That announcement, on Wednesday March 8, was the spark. Depositors did the arithmetic — \$15 billion of latent losses against a \$16 billion cushion, and a bank now scrambling to raise capital — and concluded the bank might be one big sale away from insolvency. Which, in a run, became a self-fulfilling forecast.

The intuition: an unrealized loss is harmless while you can hold, and lethal the instant a run forces you to sell — the run converts a paper loss into a real one, and the fear of that conversion is what causes the run.

### Twist two: almost everyone was uninsured

Recall the cure: deposit insurance flattens the WAIT payoff so nobody runs. But insurance only flattens it *up to the limit.* SVB's depositors were overwhelmingly tech startups and venture funds with payrolls, runway, and operating cash measured in the millions or tens of millions — sums that dwarf the \$250,000 FDIC cap. About 86% of SVB's deposits were uninsured. For an uninsured depositor, the bad equilibrium is fully alive: if the bank fails and you waited, you are a late, scrap-collecting creditor who might recover 80 cents on the dollar in a year — and if your company needs that cash to make Friday's payroll, an 80% recovery in a year is a death sentence today. So SVB's depositors faced exactly the un-flattened payoff chart, and they responded exactly as the model predicts: they ran.

#### Worked example: why an uninsured depositor runs and an insured one shrugs

Take two depositors. Insured Irene has \$200,000 at the bank, all under the FDIC cap. If the bank fails and she waited, the FDIC pays her \$200,000 — full stop. Her WAIT payoff is flat; she has no reason to run, so she does not. Uninsured Uma has \$20 million — her startup's entire payroll account. If the bank fails and she waited, she is insured on \$250,000 and an unsecured creditor on the other \$19.75 million, recovering maybe 80 cents on the dollar *eventually.* Call it \$16.05 million, possibly months from now, when payroll is due next week. If she runs early and gets out at par, she keeps all \$20 million *today.* For Uma, running is worth nearly \$4 million and a solvent payroll. She runs the instant she suspects others will. A bank funded almost entirely by Umas is a bank with no shock absorber at all.

The intuition: deposit insurance only deletes the bad equilibrium for the money it covers — pile a bank high with uninsured deposits and you have rebuilt the un-insured game on top of an insured system, run and all.

### Twist three: a concentrated, herd-like depositor base

The final twist was *who* the depositors were and how connected they were. A normal bank's depositors are a diffuse crowd — millions of households who do not know each other, read different news, and would never run in unison. SVB's depositors were a tight, homogeneous club: Silicon Valley startups, funded by an overlapping set of a few dozen venture-capital firms, who all knew each other, sat in the same group chats, and *listened to the same handful of influential VCs.* That concentration is jet fuel for a coordination game, because it makes coordination trivial. When a few prominent venture capitalists told their portfolio companies to pull their money, that single message reached a huge fraction of the depositor base *simultaneously* and made the worry *common knowledge* in minutes. A diffuse crowd has to discover a panic gradually; a tight network manufactures one with a single forwarded email. SVB's depositor base was, in effect, pre-coordinated — which is the worst possible property for a bank that depends on its depositors *failing* to coordinate.

## Common misconceptions

**"A bank run means the bank was insolvent — the runners were right."** Sometimes, but not necessarily, and that is the whole Diamond-Dybvig point. A coordination game has two equilibria *for the same fundamentals.* A run can hit a perfectly solvent bank purely because belief slid to the bad equilibrium, and the run then *manufactures* the insolvency it feared through forced fire sales. The runners can be both individually rational *and* collectively wrong about the bank's underlying health. "The bank failed, so it must have been bad" confuses the run's *cause* with its *effect.*

**"If I just stay calm and rational, I'll be fine."** This is the most expensive misconception, because in a coordination game *being rational means running once you believe others will run.* Staying put while everyone else withdraws does not make you wise; it makes you the late depositor who recovers 40 cents. The rational move is not independent of the crowd — it *is* matching the crowd. Your calm is only correct if everyone else is also calm, and you cannot guarantee that. The model punishes the lone holdout, not rewards them.

**"I'll get my money out before everyone else does."** Everyone thinks this, which is precisely why it fails. The expected reward for being early is exactly the incentive that triggers the stampede; the belief "I can beat the crowd" is *held by the crowd.* In a digital run, the window between "things look fine" and "the bank is gone" can be a few hours, far too short to time. We trace this same fallacy through trading positions in [Crowded Trades and the Exit Game](/blog/trading/game-theory/crowded-trades-and-the-exit-game): the door is small, everyone heads for it at once, and the plan to leave first is what makes the door small.

**"Deposit insurance is just a taxpayer bailout fund."** It is the opposite of a fund that pays out — it is a *belief technology* that mostly never pays out. By flattening the WAIT payoff so that running is never a best response, insurance deletes the bad equilibrium, the run does not happen, and the fund stays full. Its value is in *preventing* the event, not reimbursing it. A backstop that works is a backstop that is rarely tapped; the 2023 guarantee of uninsured deposits stopped a cascade across dozens of regional banks at a fraction of the cost of letting them all fail.

**"A bank just needs enough capital to be safe."** Capital protects against *insolvency* — assets falling below liabilities. A run is an *illiquidity* event — not enough cash *right now*, even if assets exceed liabilities. A bank can have ample capital on a hold-to-maturity basis and still die in a run, because the run forces it to convert long assets to cash at fire-sale prices, which *destroys* the capital that was adequate a moment earlier. Capital and liquidity are different defenses against different attacks, and a run attacks liquidity. This is exactly why the *lender of last resort* — which supplies liquidity directly — is the run-specific cure, not more capital.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Silicon Valley Bank, March 8–10, 2023.** The textbook case, and the reason for this post. SVB announced a \$1.8 billion realized loss on bond sales and a need to raise \$2.25 billion of capital on Wednesday. By Thursday, prompted by venture capitalists broadcasting "get out" to a pre-coordinated depositor base, customers attempted to withdraw about \$42 billion — a quarter of deposits — in one day. The Fed's vice chair later testified that customers queued to pull *nearly the entire \$175 billion deposit base* across Thursday and the planned Friday. Regulators seized the bank Friday midday, the fastest meaningful run in modern U.S. history. Over the weekend, regulators guaranteed *all* deposits, insured and uninsured, explicitly to flatten the WAIT payoff at every other regional bank and stop the cascade. We cover the full episode, including the parallel collapse of Credit Suisse the same week, in [SVB, Credit Suisse, and the 2023 Bank Runs](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs).

**Credit Suisse, March 2023.** Days after SVB, a 167-year-old global systemically important bank suffered its own modern run — not from retail depositors in a line, but from institutional clients, wealth-management money, and counterparties pulling funds and credit lines as confidence evaporated. Credit Suisse had been bleeding deposits for months after a 2022 scare, and the SVB panic was the sunspot that tipped belief over the edge. Swiss authorities forced an emergency takeover by UBS over a weekend, with central-bank liquidity backstops attached — the lender-of-last-resort and the equilibrium-deletion playbook applied to a global bank in real time. Same coordination game, different depositors, same cure.

**The 2008 financial crisis: runs without retail lines.** Most of 2008's runs happened in the *shadow banking* system, where the "depositors" were institutions and the "deposits" were short-term funding called *repo* (repurchase agreements) and *commercial paper.* Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers funded long-dated, illiquid mortgage assets with overnight borrowing — the same maturity mismatch — and when lenders refused to roll over that funding, it was a run in every sense: a coordination collapse where each lender pulled because it believed the others would. There was no line at a teller window, but the game was identical, and it ended the same way, with fire sales and failure. The lesson is that a "bank run" is any run on short-term funding of long-term assets, whoever the runners are.

**The 1930s and the birth of deposit insurance.** Between 1930 and 1933, thousands of American banks failed in waves of runs, many of them solvent banks killed purely by panic. The policy response was the creation of the FDIC in 1933 — federal deposit insurance — and it worked exactly as the coordination-game theory predicts: bank runs by insured retail depositors essentially *vanished* from the U.S. for decades, not because banks got safer but because the bad equilibrium had been deleted for the insured. The 1980s savings-and-loan crisis and the 2008 and 2023 episodes all happened at the *edges* of insurance — uninsured deposits, shadow funding, institutions above the cap — exactly where the bad equilibrium still lives.

**Money market funds, September 2008.** When the Reserve Primary Fund "broke the buck" (its shares fell below the \$1.00 that money funds promise) after Lehman's failure, investors ran on money market funds — supposedly the safest cash-like instrument — pulling hundreds of billions in days. It was a textbook coordination collapse: the funds held assets that were fine to maturity but had to be sold into a frozen market to meet redemptions. The U.S. Treasury stopped it the same way you stop any run: it temporarily *guaranteed* money fund balances, flattening the WAIT payoff and deleting the bad equilibrium overnight.

**Stablecoins, the bank run with no backstop.** A *stablecoin* is a crypto token that promises to be worth exactly \$1, redeemable on demand — which is a deposit by another name, and therefore a coordination game by another name. The "asset side" varies: a *fiat-backed* coin holds cash and short bonds, while an *algorithmic* one held a sister token and a promise. Terra's UST in May 2022 is the cleanest catastrophe. UST claimed a \$1 peg defended by a mechanism that minted and burned its sister token LUNA. When a few large holders sold UST and the price slipped below \$1, every other holder did the coordination arithmetic — "if others redeem and I wait, the peg breaks and I get scraps; if I redeem now I might still get a dollar" — and ran. The redemption mechanism printed LUNA to absorb the selling, LUNA's price collapsed toward zero under the flood of new supply, which made redemption worthless, which accelerated the run. About \$40 billion of value evaporated in days. It was Diamond-Dybvig with the friction removed *and* the backstop removed: no FDIC, no lender of last resort, no weekend seizure — just the pure bad equilibrium running to completion at the speed of a blockchain. Even fiat-backed coins are not immune: USDC briefly traded to \$0.88 in March 2023 precisely because some of its cash reserves were *at Silicon Valley Bank*, and holders ran on the stablecoin on the news, until the SVB deposit guarantee flattened that payoff too. The lesson is the one this whole post has been building: a redeem-on-demand promise backed by slower or riskier assets is a coordination game whether you call it a bank, a money fund, or a token — and without a credible backstop, the bad equilibrium is always one sunspot away.

## The playbook: how to read and play a coordination game

You will probably never run a bank, but the coordination game is everywhere a crowd's belief is self-fulfilling — in your deposits, in crowded trades, in stablecoins, in any asset whose value depends on everyone agreeing it has value. Here is how to think and act like someone who understands the game rather than someone who gets caught in it.

**Who is on the other side.** In a run there is no clever counterparty taking the opposite view of value — the "other side" of your decision is *every other depositor*, and your payoff is determined by their collective belief, not by any fact about the bank. This is the rare game where the other players are not adversaries with private information but a herd you are trying to *match.* Your edge is not knowing the bank's true asset value (you usually can't); it is correctly forecasting *whether belief is about to tip*, and getting to the door a half-step ahead of the tipping — while recognizing that everyone is trying to do the same, which is what makes timing it nearly impossible.

**What game you are in.** Diagnose the structure before the trigger. Ask: is this asset or institution funded *short* and invested *long*? Is there a maturity or liquidity mismatch where the holders can demand cash faster than the underlying can be turned into cash at fair value? If yes, the bad equilibrium *exists* — it is dormant, waiting for a sunspot. This is true of a bank, a stablecoin that promises \$1 redemption but holds slower assets, a leveraged fund offering daily liquidity on illiquid positions, and a crowded trade where everyone plans to exit through the same narrow door. The structure tells you the bad equilibrium is *possible*; it does not tell you *when* it fires.

**Your edge.** The real, durable edge is structural avoidance, not heroic timing. Prefer institutions and instruments where the bad equilibrium has been *deleted* — insured deposits under the cap, central-bank-backstopped systems, vehicles whose redemption terms match their asset liquidity (gates, lockups, or genuinely liquid holdings). Spread balances across banks to stay under insurance limits. Treat any promise of *instant* liquidity on *illiquid* assets as a coordination accident waiting to happen, and price the run risk you cannot see. For the rare cases where you must hold un-backstopped exposure, your edge is watching the *common-knowledge* indicators — concentrated holders, a tight social network among them, a falling price that itself becomes the news — because those are what move belief, and belief is the fundamental.

**The invalidation.** Your read is wrong, and you should stand down from "this is about to run," when the bad equilibrium has been credibly removed: a blanket guarantee is announced, a lender of last resort opens a facility, or the holder base is diffuse, insured, and uncoordinated enough that belief cannot go common knowledge quickly. Conversely, your "this is safe" read is invalidated the moment a credible common-knowledge event lands — an authoritative "get out" message to a connected holder base, a forced asset sale that crystallizes a loss, a price move large enough to make the front page. When belief can tip and nothing flattens the WAIT payoff, the dormant equilibrium is live.

**Sizing and exit.** This is risk management, not a trade to size up. The asymmetry is brutal: in the good equilibrium you earn a thin spread (the interest), and in the bad one you can lose a large fraction of principal, fast, with little warning. So the position to take is *insurance-shaped*: keep exposure to any un-backstopped, mismatch-funded institution small relative to what you cannot afford to lose, stay under guarantee limits where they exist, and never rely on being able to exit first — because the entire population is relying on the same thing, and that shared reliance is what slams the door. The honest exit plan is to *not need one*, by choosing the backstopped game in the first place. None of this is individualized financial advice; it is the mechanism, so you can recognize the game you are standing in.

The single sentence to carry out of this post: a bank run is a coordination game with two self-fulfilling equilibria, the dangerous variable is common belief rather than the balance sheet, and the only reliable cure is to change the payoffs so that waiting is always safe — which is why the boring institution with the government backstop is, in the one moment that matters, the safe one.
