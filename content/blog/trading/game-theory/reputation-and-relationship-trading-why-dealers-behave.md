> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In dealer and over-the-counter markets you trade with the same counterparties again and again, so reputation becomes collateral: a dealer who burns you forfeits the entire discounted stream of future business, and that threat enforces honest fills without a single line of contract.
>
> - A *one-shot* trust game has only one equilibrium, and it is the bad one: both sides cheat. *Repeated* dealing flips it, because cooperation can be sustained as long as the future is worth at least as much as a one-time defection.
> - The exact line is a discount factor: with a temptation payoff of \$5k, a cooperation payoff of \$3k, and a punishment payoff of \$1k, honesty holds whenever you value next quarter at least δ\* = 0.50 as much as this one. Above that, behaving is worth \$30k and cheating is worth \$5k once.
> - Relationship counterparties get tighter quotes, real capital commitment, and the first call on a hot IPO; anonymous one-shot flow gets priced as if it might be toxic, because the dealer has no reputation to lose to you and you have none to lose to them.
> - The dangerous moment is the **last period** — a dying firm, a relationship that is ending, a trader on his way out the door. When the future value vanishes, so does the reason to behave, and trust unravels backward. Watch for the endgame, not the average day.

In September 2008, as Lehman Brothers was failing, a strange thing happened on trading desks across Wall Street. Counterparties who had dealt with Lehman for decades — who had given Lehman tight prices on swaps, lent it securities at friendly rates, and routed it block orders without a second thought — suddenly stopped picking up the phone. The quotes Lehman could get went from competitive to punitive overnight. Not because the traders on the other side were cruel, but because they had done a cold calculation: the future stream of business with Lehman was about to end, which meant the relationship was about to stop being worth protecting, which meant *nobody had any reason to behave well anymore*. The relationship that had been an asset for thirty years evaporated in seventy-two hours.

That is the whole subject of this post in a single episode. Most of the time, a bond dealer who gives you a bad fill, an FX bank that fades a quote it just showed you, or a prime broker that front-runs your order would be doing something *stupid*, not just dishonest — because the money they would steal in one trade is tiny next to the money they would lose when you stop trading with them. The good behavior you see in dealer markets is not mostly virtue and not mostly regulation. It is the cold logic of a repeated game: reputation is collateral, and the threat of losing the relationship is what enforces the contract that nobody ever signs.

This is the natural sequel to the [repeated-games and tit-for-tat](/blog/trading/game-theory/who-is-on-the-other-side-of-your-trade) idea — that cooperation can emerge among self-interested players who expect to meet again. Here we take that one idea and build a whole layer of markets on top of it: why your broker treats you differently from a stranger, why an "axe" from a salesperson is worth listening to, why soft-dollar arrangements and relationship tiers exist, and why the single most dangerous counterparty in the world is one with nothing left to lose.

![Reputation as collateral, cheat once and lose the future stream of business](/imgs/blogs/reputation-and-relationship-trading-why-dealers-behave-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for the entire post. On the left, a dealer who keeps behaving collects a stream of roughly \$3k of relationship value every quarter; discounted back to today, that stream is worth about \$30k. On the right, the dealer cheats once — grabs a \$5k bad fill — and the relationship dies, so the stream after that is zero. Cheating buys \$2k extra today and destroys \$30k of tomorrow. The reason dealers behave is not on the left or the right of that picture; it is the comparison between them. Hold that comparison in your head and almost everything else in dealer markets falls out of it.

## Foundations: reputation, the relationship game, and the last-period problem

Before we can reason about any of this, we need four ideas built from zero: what a *dealer market* actually is, what a *repeated game* is, what *reputation* means precisely (not as a vibe but as a quantity), and why a *known ending* poisons the whole thing. Take them one at a time.

### What a dealer (OTC) market is

Start with the thing most people picture when they think "the market": a stock exchange, where buyers and sellers post orders into a central book and a computer matches them anonymously. You never know who is on the other side, and you do not care, because the exchange guarantees the trade. That is an *order-driven, anonymous, all-to-all* market. It is one model, and it is not the dominant one by dollar volume.

The bigger world by notional is the *dealer* market, also called *over-the-counter* (OTC) — "over the counter" because, historically, you walked up to a dealer's counter and asked for a price rather than posting an order into a public book. Government and corporate bonds, interest-rate swaps, most foreign exchange, credit default swaps, repo (short-term secured lending), and large block trades in equities all live here. In a dealer market there is a *dealer* (also called a *market-maker* — a firm that stands ready to buy and sell, quoting a *bid*, the price it will pay, and an *ask* or *offer*, the price it will sell at) and a *client* (a pension fund, an asset manager, a hedge fund, a corporate treasurer). The client calls the dealer — by phone, by chat, or through an electronic *request-for-quote* (RFQ) system — and asks for a price on a specific size. The dealer quotes; the client trades or doesn't.

The crucial difference from the anonymous exchange: in a dealer market, *the two sides know exactly who they are dealing with, and they expect to deal again.* Goldman Sachs knows it is quoting PIMCO. PIMCO knows it is asking Goldman. They have a name, a history, a sales coverage relationship, and a strong expectation that next week there will be another trade. That repetition is the entire engine of what follows.

### What a repeated game is

A *game*, in the technical sense we use throughout this series, is any situation where your best move depends on what someone else does. The players have *strategies* (the moves available), *payoffs* (what each combination of moves is worth to each player), and *information* (who knows what). The *Nash equilibrium* — named for John Nash — is a combination of strategies where no player can do better by unilaterally changing, given what everyone else is doing. It is the "no regrets" outcome: a resting point where nobody wants to deviate.

A *one-shot* game is played once and never again. A *repeated game* is the same game played over and over by the same players, who remember what happened. This memory is everything. In a one-shot game you can be a jerk and walk away. In a repeated game your move today changes how the other player treats you tomorrow, and that future consequence becomes part of today's payoff. The key quantity is the *discount factor*, written δ (delta), a number between 0 and 1 that says how much you value the next round relative to this one. δ = 0 means you only care about right now (the future is worthless to you). δ close to 1 means the future matters almost as much as the present. We will see that δ is the hinge on which honest behavior swings.

### What reputation actually is

Here is the precise definition, and it is more useful than the everyday one. *Reputation* is the present value of the future stream of business that good behavior keeps alive. It is not a feeling other people have about you; it is an *asset on your balance sheet that does not appear on your balance sheet* — the discounted cash flow of all the trades a counterparty will route to you *as long as you keep behaving*, and that they will route elsewhere the instant you don't.

Three properties follow immediately, and we will return to each:

- Reputation is **costly to build**. You earn it by repeatedly giving good fills, honoring quotes, and not exploiting information — choosing the smaller, honest payoff over the larger, dishonest one, trade after trade. Each clean trade is a small investment.
- Reputation is **easy to destroy**. One detected cheat — one faded quote at a critical moment, one front-run order, one "your fill was 30 ticks worse than the screen" — can wipe out years of building, because it tells every counterparty that the implicit contract is void.
- Reputation is **collateral**. *Collateral* is something of value you post to guarantee you will perform; if you default, the other side seizes it. Your reputation is collateral you post on every trade — and if you cheat, the counterparty "seizes" it by withdrawing all future business. The genius is that you post it without a contract, a custodian, or a court.

This is why we say reputation behaves like a *signal* in the [signaling and screening](/blog/trading/game-theory/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades) sense: it is a costly-to-fake, easy-to-verify indicator of type. A dealer who has behaved for fifteen years is *revealing* that it values the relationship more than any single trade — precisely because behaving that long was expensive.

Note that reputation runs in *both* directions, and this symmetry matters for everything that follows. The dealer has a reputation with you (will it honor its quotes, give you size, not abuse what it learns about your flow?), and you have a reputation with the dealer (is your flow benign or toxic, do you settle cleanly, do you try to pick off stale prices?). Both reputations are collateral; both discipline behavior; both can be destroyed by a single detected cheat. A great deal of the confusion people have about dealer markets comes from imagining the relationship is one-sided — that the big bank holds all the cards and the client just takes what it's given. In fact the client is scoring the dealer as relentlessly as the dealer scores the client, and a dealer that develops a reputation for fading quotes or front-running loses its best clients exactly as fast as a toxic client loses its best dealers.

### The last-period problem

Now the dark side, and the reason the Lehman story opened the post. Suppose everyone knows the relationship will end at a *specific, known* time — the fund is winding down, the firm is being liquidated, the trader is leaving Friday. Work backwards from that ending.

In the *very last* trade, there is no future to protect. The whole apparatus that made you behave — "if I cheat, I lose tomorrow's business" — is gone, because there is no tomorrow. So in the last period, the rational move is to cheat. But here is the poison: if both players *know* the other will cheat in the last period, then the second-to-last period has effectively become the last one (the future after it is already lost to cheating), so they cheat then too. And the third-to-last. The cheating unravels backward through the whole relationship like a zipper. This is *backward induction*, and it is why a finitely repeated game with a known end can collapse to the bad equilibrium even though an infinitely repeated one cooperates beautifully.

The practical lesson is sharp: **the most dangerous counterparty is one that has reached, or can see, its last period.** A failing bank, a fund in redemption death-spiral, a desk being shut down, a salesperson on garden leave — these are entities whose future stream of business has gone to zero, which means their reputation collateral is worthless, which means nothing is holding their behavior in place. We will quantify all of this. First, let's see the better treatment that the relationship buys while the game is still being played.

## Why relationship counterparties get the better deal

Walk into the same bond dealer as two different clients. You are a \$50 billion asset manager that trades with this desk twenty times a week, every week, and has for a decade. The other client is an anonymous name routing a one-off order through an electronic platform, never seen before, possibly never seen again. Ask for the same price on the same bond. You will get a meaningfully better deal. This is not favoritism in the petty sense; it is the rational output of the repeated game, and it shows up across every dimension of execution quality.

![Relationship versus one-shot counterparty fill quality across four dimensions](/imgs/blogs/reputation-and-relationship-trading-why-dealers-behave-2.png)

The chart compares a stylized \$10M block trade for a relationship counterparty (green) versus an anonymous one-shot (red), across four dimensions. Lower is better for quote width, price impact, and fill speed; higher is better for size shown. Read it left to right. The relationship name gets a *quote width* of about 4 basis points — a *basis point* (bp) is one hundredth of a percent, 0.01% — versus 12 bps for the stranger. It gets \$25M of *size shown* (the dealer is willing to commit its own capital to fill the whole block at once) versus a thin \$3M for the anonymous flow. It suffers a *price impact* of 6 bps — how much the act of trading moves the price against you — versus 22 bps. And it gets filled in a minute versus eight. Every number favors the relationship. Why?

The deep reason ties back to [adverse selection and the winner's curse](/blog/trading/game-theory/adverse-selection-and-the-winners-curse-why-a-fast-fill-is-bad-news): *anonymous flow is treated as potentially toxic.* When a stranger asks a dealer to take the other side of a trade, the dealer must worry that the stranger knows something it doesn't — that this is *informed* flow, and the dealer is about to be the sucker who buys right before the price drops. The dealer protects itself by widening the spread, the way an insurance company charges more to a driver it can't assess. With a relationship counterparty, the dealer has *history*: it has seen this client's order flow for years and knows whether it is mostly *uninformed* (a pension fund rebalancing, a corporate hedging payroll) or *informed* (a hedge fund with an edge). If the flow has been benign, the dealer can quote tight, because it has built a *prior* — a probability estimate — that this particular trade is not a trap. The relationship is, among other things, a long track record that lets the dealer set adverse selection aside.

There is a second reason, and it is the repeated-game one. The dealer behaves well toward the relationship client *because the relationship is worth protecting.* The salesperson who gives you a tight quote and commits capital is making a small sacrifice today (a thinner margin, real balance-sheet risk) to keep a stream of future business alive. The same desk, facing an anonymous one-shot, has no future to protect — it will never see this counterparty again — so it has no reason to sacrifice anything, and it quotes to maximize the single trade. This is exactly the difference between a one-shot and a repeated game, played out in basis points.

#### Worked example: pricing the relationship into the spread

Let's put numbers on it. You are a bond dealer quoting a \$10M trade. Suppose you estimate that 30% of *anonymous* flow is informed (it knows something you don't) and that when you trade against informed flow you lose, on average, 30 bps — that's \$30,000 on \$10M (because 30 bps of \$10M is 0.0030 × \$10,000,000 = \$30,000). To break even against the toxic share, you must charge the rest of the flow enough to cover it: the expected adverse-selection cost per trade is 0.30 × \$30,000 = \$9,000, which on \$10M is 9 bps. So you quote roughly 9 bps wide on the anonymous flow *just to cover adverse selection*, before any profit margin.

Now the relationship client. You have ten years of data showing this client's flow is only 5% informed, not 30%. Your expected adverse-selection cost is now 0.05 × \$30,000 = \$1,500, which on \$10M is 1.5 bps. You can quote 1.5 bps wide instead of 9 and still break even on toxicity. The relationship client's tighter quote is not charity — it is *correctly priced lower risk*, and the thing that lets you price it is the relationship history. The intuition: a relationship is a long enough sample to tell signal from noise, and a dealer charges the stranger for the noise.

## The trust game: one shot versus repeated

We can now make the whole thing precise with a payoff matrix. Model a single trade between a dealer and a client as a two-by-two game. Each side chooses one of two strategies: *Behave* (give an honest fill, honor the quote) or *Cheat* (a bad fill, a faded quote, a front-run). I'll use the column player's strategies as *Trust* (route the trade and accept the fill) versus *Cheat* (try to game the dealer too), but the heart of it is the dealer's Behave-versus-Cheat choice.

![The dealer client trust game payoff matrix with the Nash equilibrium](/imgs/blogs/reputation-and-relationship-trading-why-dealers-behave-3.png)

The matrix shows the payoffs (in \$ thousands of value per trade) for each combination, dealer first, client second. When both **Behave/Trust**, both get +3: a fair fill, a happy client, repeat business — the cooperative outcome, shaded green. When the dealer **Cheats** a trusting client (bottom-left), the dealer grabs +5 by skimming the fill and the client gets 0 — that's the *temptation*. When the dealer behaves but the client cheats (top-right, e.g. the client lies about its intentions and dumps a huge informed order), the dealer gets 0 and the client gets +5. And when **both Cheat** (bottom-right), both get a measly +1: a defensive, mutually suspicious, wide-spread trade — the punishment outcome, shaded amber and labeled **Nash**.

Why is mutual cheating the Nash equilibrium of the one-shot game? Check each player's best response. If the client trusts, the dealer's best move is to cheat (5 beats 3). If the client cheats, the dealer's best move is *still* to cheat (1 beats 0). So cheating is a *dominant strategy* for the dealer — better no matter what the client does. By symmetry, cheating is dominant for the client too. Both cheat, both get +1, and neither can do better by switching. That is the tragedy of the one-shot trust game: it is a *prisoner's dilemma*. The cooperative +3/+3 corner is better for both, but it is not stable, because each player is individually tempted to defect from it.

#### Worked example: confirming the one-shot equilibrium

Run the numbers through the model to be sure. With the dealer's payoff matrix A = [[3, 0], [5, 1]] (rows = Behave/Cheat, columns = client Trust/Cheat) and the client's symmetric matrix B = [[3, 5], [0, 1]], a standard 2×2 Nash solver returns a single pure equilibrium: both Cheat. Let me show the arithmetic the solver does. For the dealer in the **Trust** column: Behave pays 3, Cheat pays 5, so Cheat wins by 2. In the **Cheat** column: Behave pays 0, Cheat pays 1, so Cheat wins by 1. Cheat is better in *every* column, so it is dominant. The same check on the client's columns gives the same answer. The only cell where neither player wants to switch is (Cheat, Cheat) at +1/+1. The cooperative \$3k/\$3k outcome — worth \$6k of total value versus the \$2k the cheating equilibrium creates — is left on the table. The intuition: in a single trade with a stranger, distrust is not paranoia; it is the equilibrium.

If that were the end of the story, dealer markets could not function. Every quote would be a trap, every fill would be skimmed, and spreads would be punitively wide to compensate. The reason real dealer markets are *not* like that is that they are not one-shot. They are repeated.

### How repetition rescues cooperation

Now play the same trust game over and over, the same dealer and the same client, quarter after quarter, with a discount factor δ. Introduce a strategy called *grim trigger* (or its gentler cousin, *tit-for-tat*): "I will Behave as long as you Behave, but the first time you Cheat me, I stop trading with you forever." The threat of that permanent cutoff is what changes the math.

Consider the dealer's choice. If it Behaves forever, it collects the cooperation payoff R = 3 every quarter. The present value of that infinite stream, discounted at δ, is a geometric series:

$$V_{\text{behave}} = R + \delta R + \delta^2 R + \dots = \frac{R}{1 - \delta}$$

If instead it Cheats *this* quarter, it grabs the temptation payoff T = 5 once — but then the client triggers, the relationship collapses to mutual cheating, and the dealer collects only the punishment payoff P = 1 forever after:

$$V_{\text{cheat}} = T + \delta P + \delta^2 P + \dots = T + \frac{\delta P}{1 - \delta}$$

Cooperation is sustainable — the dealer prefers to keep behaving — exactly when $V_{\text{behave}} \geq V_{\text{cheat}}$. Solving that inequality gives a clean threshold on the discount factor:

$$\delta \geq \delta^\* = \frac{T - R}{T - P}$$

This δ\* is *the* number of the whole subject. Below it, the future is discounted too steeply for the threat to bite, and even repeated players cheat. Above it, the future is worth enough that honesty is the equilibrium, with no contract required.

![Discounted value of behaving versus cheating once, crossing at the threshold](/imgs/blogs/reputation-and-relationship-trading-why-dealers-behave-4.png)

The chart plots both present values as the discount factor δ runs from 0 to nearly 1, using T = 5, R = 3, P = 1. The green line is $V_{\text{behave}} = 3/(1-\delta)$; the red line is $V_{\text{cheat}} = 5 + \delta/(1-\delta)$. They cross at the dashed amber line, δ\* = 0.50. To the *left* of that line — when you barely value the future — cheating wins (red is above green): grab the \$5k now, the future is too cheap to protect. To the *right* — when the future matters — behaving wins (green above red), and the gap widens fast: at δ = 0.9 the honest relationship is worth \$30k against the cheater's \$14k. The shaded green region is the zone where reputation does its job. The whole logic of dealer good-conduct lives in that crossing.

#### Worked example: computing the discount threshold and the relationship's value

Plug in the trust-game numbers. The threshold is δ\* = (T − R) / (T − P) = (5 − 3) / (5 − 1) = 2 / 4 = 0.50. So cooperation holds whenever the dealer values next quarter at least half as much as this one — a very easy bar to clear for a desk that expects to trade with this client for years.

Now value the relationship at a realistic δ = 0.9 (a dealer who heavily values future business). Behaving forever is worth V_behave = 3 / (1 − 0.9) = 3 / 0.1 = \$30k. Cheating once is worth V_cheat = 5 + (0.9 × 1) / 0.1 = 5 + 9 = \$14k. The dealer gives up \$30k − \$14k = \$16k of value to grab a one-time \$2k edge over honest play (5 versus 3). No rational desk does that. Notice what raises δ\*: a bigger temptation T (a juicier one-time skim) or a smaller gap between T and P (cheating barely punished) pushes the threshold up and makes cheating more likely. The intuition: cooperation survives when the future is valuable and the punishment for cheating is severe — which is exactly the condition a healthy, ongoing relationship creates.

This is the engine. A dealer behaves not because it is honest by nature but because the discounted value of your future business, \$30k, dwarfs the one-time skim, \$2k. *That gap is the collateral.* You never posted it, you never signed for it, but it is sitting there guaranteeing your fills on every trade.

## Reputation as a slow-build, fast-destroy asset

The continuation-value math assumes a binary world: behave or get cut off forever. Reality is grainier. Reputation is a *stock* that accumulates and depletes, and its defining feature is brutal asymmetry: it takes years of clean trades to build and one detected cheat to vaporize.

![Reputation builds slowly over years and collapses in a single quarter](/imgs/blogs/reputation-and-relationship-trading-why-dealers-behave-5.png)

The chart traces a dealer's reputation score — an index from 0 to 100 of how much business counterparties will route to it — over ten years of quarters. The green curve is the slow, concave climb of honest dealing: every clean trade adds a little, but with diminishing returns, asymptoting toward a ceiling. It takes the full seven years (28 quarters) to climb into the high 80s. Then, at the dashed line, *one cheat is detected* — a faded quote at a critical moment, a front-run that the client's transaction-cost analysis catches. The red curve falls off a cliff: the score collapses from the high 80s to near zero within a quarter or two, because the cheat tells every counterparty the implicit contract is void. And the painful part is the tail: the rebuild, when it even happens, is far slower than the original build and never quite recovers the old level inside the window. Reputation is a long climb and a short fall.

This asymmetry is *why* reputation works as collateral. If a tarnished reputation could be rebuilt cheaply, the threat would be toothless — cheat, take the gain, buy your reputation back. Because rebuilding is so expensive and so uncertain, the threat of destruction is credible, and credibility is what makes the collateral binding. It also explains a behavior that puzzles outsiders: why a dealer will eat a loss to honor a quote it technically didn't have to. The salesperson who says "we showed it, we'll stand behind it" even when the market gapped against them is not being soft. They are protecting a reputation stock worth vastly more than the loss on this one trade. They are choosing a point on the green curve over the cliff.

#### Worked example: is it worth eating a \$40k loss to protect reputation?

Concrete decision. A dealer quoted a client a price on a swap, the market moved hard in the thirty seconds before the client hit the quote, and honoring it now means eating a \$40,000 mark-to-market loss. The dealer is technically within its rights to *re-quote* (fade the original price). Should it eat the loss instead?

Compute the relationship's value. This client routes about \$200k of spread revenue to the desk per year, and the desk expects the relationship to last, conservatively, ten more years. Even ignoring discounting, that is \$2M of future revenue. Discount it at δ = 0.9 the way we did above and the present value is still on the order of \$200k / (1 − 0.9) ≈ \$2M. Fading the quote risks a meaningful chunk of that — say it cuts the relationship's value by even 20%, that's \$400k of damage to avoid a \$40k loss. Eating the \$40k is a ten-to-one trade in the desk's favor. So it honors the quote, swallows the loss, and the client tells three other funds that this desk stands behind its prices. The intuition: a small certain loss that protects a large reputation stock is one of the best trades a dealer ever makes, even though it shows up as red ink today.

## The mechanics: scoring, tiers, axes, and soft dollars

The repeated-game logic is not abstract on a real desk. It is institutionalized into concrete machinery that prices the relationship and routes the benefits. Four pieces of that machinery are worth understanding, because as a client you are scored by all of them whether you know it or not.

### Client scoring and relationship tiers

Banks formally *score* their clients. The score blends how much commission or spread revenue the client pays, how *toxic* the flow is (does trading with this client systematically lose the dealer money, the adverse-selection question again), how much ancillary business the relationship brings (financing, research, corporate access), and how reliable the client is to deal with. Clients get sorted into *tiers* — platinum, gold, silver, or some internal equivalent. A top-tier client gets the desk's best capital commitment, the tightest quotes, the first phone call when something interesting is happening, and the largest allocation in a hot new issue. A bottom-tier or unscored client gets the screen price and nothing more.

This is the repeated game made into a spreadsheet. The tier *is* your reputation, quantified and acted upon. And it is a two-way signal: the dealer is also building *its* reputation with you, because a desk that consistently gives its platinum clients great fills earns the right to be those clients' first call — which is itself worth enormous money, as we'll see with IPOs.

### Axes: when the dealer wants to trade

An *axe* (from "axe to grind") is a position a dealer particularly wants to put on or take off — say it is long a bond it would love to sell, or short one it needs to cover. When a salesperson tells a trusted client "we're axed to sell this name," they are revealing private information about the desk's inventory in exchange for the client's order flow. This only works inside a relationship. The salesperson is trusting the client not to use the axe against the desk (e.g., by inferring the desk is desperate and squeezing it), and the client is trusting the axe is real and not a trick to offload a bad position. An axe shared with a trusted counterparty is a small act of cooperation in the repeated game — information given freely because the relationship makes it safe to give. An anonymous counterparty never gets an axe, because there is no trust to make the disclosure safe.

For the client, an axe is genuinely valuable: it means the dealer will pay up (or sell cheap) to do the trade it wants, so the client gets a better-than-market price on exactly that side. The relationship literally pays you in basis points when your need lines up with the dealer's axe.

### Soft dollars

*Soft dollars* are an arrangement where a client pays a slightly higher commission than the bare-bones execution would cost, and the excess is credited toward research, data, or other services the broker provides. It is, in effect, a bundled way of paying for a relationship: the client overpays a little on execution in exchange for research and access, and the broker provides those services because the bundled revenue makes the relationship worth more. Soft dollars have been controversial and heavily regulated — Europe's MiFID II rules in 2018 forced the *unbundling* of research from execution precisely because regulators worried soft dollars obscured who was paying for what and dulled the incentive to get the best execution price. The point for us is structural: soft dollars are a mechanism for *pricing and funding the relationship itself*, separate from any single trade. They are the relationship game with an invoice attached.

### The first call on an IPO

The highest-value relationship perk in equities is *allocation* in a hot initial public offering (IPO) or other new issue. When a bank underwrites a sought-after IPO that is going to "pop" on the first day, it decides who gets shares at the offer price — and those allocations are worth real, immediate money if the stock jumps. Banks allocate disproportionately to their best relationship clients. This is the repeated game's reward at its most naked: the bank is paying its loyal clients, in IPO pops, for years of order flow and a reputation as a reliable, non-toxic counterparty. The 1990s "spinning" scandals — where banks allocated hot IPOs to executives to win their companies' investment-banking business — were this mechanism abused, and the rules tightened afterward. But the legitimate core remains: your relationship buys you the first call, and the first call is where the easy money in new issues lives.

#### Worked example: what the first call on an IPO is worth

Quantify it. A bank is allocating a hot IPO priced at \$20 per share that everyone expects to pop 30% on day one. A platinum-tier relationship client gets an allocation of 100,000 shares; a bottom-tier client gets 5,000 or none. The platinum client's allocation is worth 100,000 × \$20 × 30% = \$600,000 of first-day gain. The bottom-tier client's 5,000 shares are worth 5,000 × \$20 × 30% = \$30,000. The difference, \$570,000, is what the relationship is worth *on this single deal*. Over a career of deals, the first-call advantage compounds into the millions. The intuition: in primary markets, the scarce resource is the allocation, and the relationship is the currency you buy it with — which is exactly why funds work so hard to be top-tier, and why being seen as toxic, one-shot flow is so costly.

## The last-period problem in the wild

Everything above describes the game *while it is being played*. The most important — and most dangerous — moment is when the game is about to end. We met the logic in the Foundations section; now let's see it bite.

![The last-period unraveling as a relationship nears a known end](/imgs/blogs/reputation-and-relationship-trading-why-dealers-behave-6.png)

The timeline reads left to right toward the end of a relationship. **Far from the end**, the future stream is worth \$30k, so behaving is easy: cheating would cost the whole stream. As the firm gets into trouble, the future shrinks to \$9k — still positive, so the dealer keeps behaving, barely. When a **wind-down is announced**, the remaining future is worth only \$3k, while the one-time cheat gain is \$2k and climbing in relative terms — the temptation is now real. In the **last quarter**, the future is worth \$0, the entire restraint mechanism is gone, and the rational move is to cheat. And then the kicker, the rightmost box: **clients foresee all of this**, so by backward induction they stop trusting *early* — trust dies before the relationship formally does. A counterparty that can see its own ending is a counterparty whose word is already worthless.

This is why the failing-firm endgame is so treacherous. As the future value collapses toward zero, the discount-factor condition δ ≥ δ\* fails not because δ changed but because the *stream being discounted* vanished — V_behave = R/(1−δ) only holds if there is an R to collect next quarter. When R itself goes to zero, behaving is worth nothing and the temptation T is all that's left. The dying firm has nothing to protect, so it protects nothing.

#### Worked example: when does behaving stop being worth it?

Make the endgame quantitative. The dealer's per-quarter cooperation payoff is R = 3 and the one-time cheat temptation is T = 5, so cheating beats behaving by T − R = \$2k *today*. Behaving is only worth it if the future stream it preserves exceeds that \$2k edge. With the relationship having N quarters left and the future approximated (undiscounted, for simplicity) as N × R = 3N, behaving protects 3N of future value. Behaving beats cheating while 3N > T − R = 2, i.e. for essentially any N ≥ 1 quarter — *as long as the future is genuinely there.*

Now suppose the firm is being liquidated and everyone knows there are exactly two quarters left, after which the desk closes. In the *final* quarter, N_remaining = 0: no future, so the dealer cheats (grabs 5 instead of 3). Knowing that, in the *second-to-last* quarter the client already expects the dealer to cheat next time regardless, so the "future" the dealer is protecting by behaving now is itself a cheating quarter worth only P = 1, not R = 3 — the incentive to behave has already rotted. The dealer cheats then too. Two quarters from the end, with a *known* end, cooperation has already unraveled. The intuition: it is not the firm's last *day* that turns dangerous — it is the moment everyone can *count the days*, because a countable end unravels backward.

The clean historical case is *Drexel Burnham Lambert*, the junk-bond powerhouse of the 1980s. As Drexel slid toward bankruptcy in early 1990 under the weight of the Milken-era scandals and a collapsing high-yield market, counterparties pulled away exactly as the model predicts. A firm whose future was visibly ending could no longer trade on its name; its relationship capital, once enormous, went to zero ahead of the formal failure because everyone could see the last period coming. The same script ran with [Bear Stearns and Lehman in 2008](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-market-makers-game-inventory-the-spread-and-fear-of-the-informed): once the market concluded the firm might not survive, the relationship value collapsed, funding dried up, and the run accelerated the very ending everyone was pricing. The last-period problem is not a curiosity; it is the mechanism by which troubled financial firms die suddenly rather than gradually.

## Relationship liquidity versus anonymous liquidity

We now have both halves of the trade-off. Relationship dealing gives you deep liquidity and good treatment, but the dealer learns your flow and watches you. Anonymous, all-to-all dealing gives you cheap, impersonal execution, but it is shallow and priced as if you might be toxic. Neither is strictly better; they serve different needs, and a sophisticated trader uses both.

![Relationship liquidity versus anonymous all to all liquidity trade-off grid](/imgs/blogs/reputation-and-relationship-trading-why-dealers-behave-7.png)

The grid lays the trade-off out across four attributes. On **depth**, relationship liquidity is deep — the dealer will commit its own capital to fill a big block at once — while anonymous all-to-all venues offer thin, small clips you have to work over time. On **cost and spread**, the relationship gets tighter quotes for trusted names, while anonymous flow gets a cheap headline price that is quietly *toxic-priced* (wide enough to cover the dealer's adverse-selection fear). On **information leakage**, the relationship has a real downside — the dealer learns your flow, your positions, your patterns, and can use that knowledge — while anonymity gives you no identity and no memory, so nothing leaks. And on **best use**, relationship liquidity suits big blocks and the first call on a new issue, while anonymous venues suit small, frequent, price-sensitive orders where you don't want anyone to know your name.

The information-leakage row is the subtle one and the reason anonymous markets exist at all. When you trade through a relationship dealer, you are handing it a stream of data about what you are doing. A clever dealer can *infer* your strategy, anticipate your next order, and — within the bounds of the relationship — position around you. That is the deep-but-informed cost: the price of depth is that the deep counterparty knows you. Anonymous all-to-all venues (electronic platforms, dark pools, central limit order books) sell you the opposite trade: nobody knows who you are, so nobody can target you, but precisely because nobody knows you, nobody will commit capital to you and the price is set for the average — possibly toxic — participant. This is the same tension the [market-maker's game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-market-makers-game-inventory-the-spread-and-fear-of-the-informed) lives inside, viewed from the client's chair.

The practical upshot, which we will turn into a playbook: route your *uninformed, large, urgent* trades through relationships, where depth and trust pay off, and your *informed, small, patient* trades through anonymous venues, where hiding your identity protects your edge. Using the wrong channel for a trade is one of the most common and expensive execution mistakes there is.

## Common misconceptions

**"Dealers behave because of regulation and ethics."** Mostly no. Regulation sets a floor and punishes egregious fraud, and most people on a desk are honest. But the *day-to-day* good conduct — the tight quote, the honored price, the capital commitment — is enforced by economics, not by rules or virtue. The proof is the last-period problem: the *same* people, at the *same* firm, behave very differently when the future disappears. If ethics and regulation were doing the work, behavior wouldn't collapse the moment the relationship ends. It collapses because the collateral — the future stream — is what was holding it up.

**"A better reputation just means people like you more."** No — reputation is a *quantity*, the discounted value of future business, and it functions as collateral with a price. The mistake is treating it as a soft, social thing rather than a hard, financial one. A desk can tell you almost to the dollar what a given relationship is worth and what it would cost to damage it. When a salesperson eats a \$40k loss to honor a quote, they are not being nice; they are protecting an asset worth fifty times that.

**"If I'm a small client, the relationship game doesn't apply to me."** It applies, just at a different tier. You are scored whether you know it or not, and your flow is classified as benign or toxic from the first trade. A small, consistent, non-toxic client can earn genuinely good treatment over time; a small client who repeatedly tries to pick off stale quotes gets flagged as toxic and quoted wide forever. The repeated game runs for everyone — you just start lower on the curve.

**"Anonymous trading is always cheaper, so I should always use it."** No. Anonymous venues *look* cheaper on the headline spread, but they are toxic-priced (wide enough to cover the dealer's fear of you) and shallow (they won't show you size). For a large, urgent, uninformed block, an anonymous venue can be *far* more expensive once you account for the price impact of working the order in small clips, and for the missed depth a relationship dealer would have committed. Cheap-looking and actually-cheap are different things; the channel that minimizes your *total* cost depends on the trade.

**"I'll cheat the dealer when I get the chance and find a new one if it goes wrong."** This is the one-shot-game fallacy applied to a repeated game. The dealer community is small and talks; a client that picks off one desk gets flagged across the street faster than you'd think, because dealers share toxicity assessments through their pricing even when they don't share data directly. Your reputation as a counterparty is itself collateral, and burning one dealer can quietly widen your quotes everywhere. The repeated game has two players, and the dealer is scoring *you* too.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Lehman Brothers, September 2008.** The opening example is the canonical last-period case. As survival became doubtful, counterparties withdrew the relationship terms — tight prices, securities lending, willingness to face Lehman in derivatives — that had been extended for decades. The withdrawal was rational repeated-game behavior: a firm whose future is ending has no reputation collateral left, so its word stops being worth honoring, and the relationship value collapses ahead of the formal default. The collapse of relationship terms accelerated the funding run, which is precisely how the last-period problem turns a solvency scare into a sudden death.

**Drexel Burnham Lambert, 1990.** Drexel built an extraordinary relationship franchise in high-yield bonds through the 1980s — it was *the* call for junk-bond financing. When the Milken scandal and the high-yield market's collapse pushed it toward bankruptcy, that franchise evaporated as counterparties priced in the ending. A firm that had owned its market could not trade on its name once the market concluded the name had no future. Drexel is the textbook illustration that relationship capital is an asset that can go to zero faster than any balance-sheet asset, because it depends entirely on a future that everyone can suddenly agree won't arrive.

**The 2013 FX "fixing" scandal.** Several major banks were fined billions after traders in chat rooms (with names like "The Cartel") were found to have shared client order information and coordinated around the daily currency *fix*. This is the dark mirror of relationship trading: the dealers used the *information their relationships gave them* about client flow to trade ahead of and around those very clients. It shows both sides of the leakage problem — relationships give dealers information, and the temptation to abuse that information is real, which is exactly why clients also value anonymous channels and why the abuse, once detected, destroyed the franchises and careers involved. The detected cheat collapsed reputations the way the model predicts.

**IPO allocation and the "spinning" cases.** In the late 1990s tech boom, banks allocated shares of hot IPOs to favored clients and corporate executives, who reaped large first-day gains. The legitimate version — rewarding loyal, non-toxic relationship clients with the first call — shades into abuse when allocations become bribes to win banking mandates. Regulators (the SEC, FINRA) cracked down in the 2000s. The episode shows how valuable the "first call" perk really is: valuable enough that people committed crimes to direct it, which tells you the relationship game's rewards in primary markets are not small.

**MiFID II research unbundling, 2018.** Europe's MiFID II regulation forced asset managers to pay for research separately from execution, ending the soft-dollar bundling that had funded research-for-flow relationships for decades. The reform's premise was exactly our model: bundled soft dollars obscured the price of the relationship and could dull the duty to get the client best execution. Unbundling made the relationship's cost explicit — and, notably, research budgets shrank sharply afterward, revealing how much of that "research" had been priced into the relationship rather than valued on its own. The episode is a natural experiment in what happens when you force the relationship to show its invoice.

**The corporate-bond electronification wave.** Over the 2010s, corporate-bond trading shifted partly from relationship phone-and-chat dealing toward electronic all-to-all platforms (MarketAxess, Tradeweb) where buy-side firms can trade with each other and with dealers, sometimes anonymously. The shift is the relationship-versus-anonymous trade-off playing out at market scale: all-to-all venues gave the buy-side cheaper, more competitive prices on *small, liquid* trades, while *large, illiquid* blocks stayed in the relationship channel where a dealer would still commit capital. Years on, both channels coexist for exactly the reason the trade-off grid shows — neither dominates; each owns the trades it fits.

**Prime brokerage and the Archegos blow-up, 2021.** A *prime broker* is a bank that lends a hedge fund cash and securities, clears its trades, and finances its leverage — the deepest relationship a fund has, and one built on years of mutual trust and information sharing. When Archegos Capital, a family office running enormous concentrated leveraged positions through total-return swaps, began to fail in March 2021, its prime brokers faced the trust game's last-period version directly. The banks that moved *first* to unwind the positions — Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley among them — protected themselves; the ones that hesitated, trying to preserve an orderly process and the relationship, took the worst losses (Credit Suisse lost roughly \$5.5 billion, Nomura around \$2.9 billion). This is the repeated game collapsing in real time: once a counterparty's survival is in doubt, the relationship's future value goes to zero, the polite cooperative unwind becomes a race to seize collateral, and the player who acts on the last-period logic fastest loses the least. The episode also shows the leakage cost — the prime brokers knew the size of Archegos's positions, but each knew only its *own* slice, so none could see the whole picture until it was too late.

## The playbook: how to play the relationship game

You sit on one side of every dealer trade. Here is how to use the game theory rather than be used by it.

**Know which game you're in.** Before you trade, ask: is this a relationship channel or an anonymous one? A named RFQ to your coverage dealer is a *repeated game* — your behavior today prices your next hundred trades, and so does theirs. An order into a dark pool or all-to-all venue is closer to a *one-shot* — nobody is building a relationship, so don't expect relationship treatment, and don't leak more than you must. Misreading which game you're in is how clients either burn relationships they should protect or expect favors from venues that owe them nothing.

**Build your reputation as deliberately as a dealer builds theirs.** Your reputation as a *counterparty* is an asset. Pay your tickets, settle cleanly, don't systematically pick off stale quotes, and let your benign flow build a track record. A client known for clean, non-toxic flow earns tighter quotes, real capital commitment, and the first call — worth far more over time than the few basis points you'd grab by gaming a dealer once. You are climbing the green curve; treat every trade as an investment in it.

**Route trades to the right channel.** The single highest-value execution decision. Large, urgent, *uninformed* trades (a rebalance, a hedge, an index change) belong in the relationship channel, where depth and trust get you filled at size without working the order for hours. Small, patient, *informed* trades (where you have an edge and your identity would tip your hand) belong in anonymous venues, where hiding protects the edge. Using a relationship dealer for your most informed flow burns the relationship (you become toxic); using anonymous venues for a huge urgent block costs you depth you needed. To make the cost concrete: a \$50M block worked through anonymous venues in small clips might suffer 25 bps of price impact as the market detects the persistent buyer — that's \$125,000. The same block handed to a relationship dealer who commits capital at a 6 bp premium costs \$30,000. The relationship saves \$95,000 on one trade, and the only thing that bought it was years of being a clean, non-toxic counterparty. Picking the wrong channel is rarely a rounding error; it is frequently the largest line in your execution cost.

**Price the relationship into your decisions — both directions.** When a dealer eats a loss to honor a quote, recognize it as them spending reputation on you, and value it: that desk just earned your next order. When *you* are tempted to squeeze a dealer that's clearly axed and desperate, remember you are spending *your* reputation, and the dealer is scoring you. The relationship is a two-way ledger; keep it roughly balanced and it pays both sides for years.

**Watch for the last period — it's your biggest risk and occasionally your edge.** The model's sharpest practical warning: a counterparty whose future is ending will behave worse, possibly much worse, precisely when you most need it to behave. Before a big trade, ask whether the counterparty (or its desk, or its parent) is in trouble — facing redemptions, being wound down, rumored to be failing. If the future stream that disciplines its behavior is gone, the discipline is gone, and you should reduce exposure, demand collateral or central clearing, or step away. The *invalidation* of "the relationship will protect me" is "the relationship is ending" — and that is the one case where the protection silently flips to zero.

**Size and exit with counterparty risk in the math.** Concretely: cap how much faces any single relationship counterparty, especially in instruments that settle later (swaps, forwards, repo) where you carry the counterparty for the life of the trade. When you see last-period warning signs, your exit isn't a price level — it's a *counterparty* level: move the relationship's open risk down, novate or clear what you can, and stop adding. The whole point of understanding reputation-as-collateral is to know when the collateral is about to be worthless, and to be the one who acted on it first — the way Lehman's counterparties did, and the way the ones who didn't wished they had.
