> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — If information asymmetry can quietly rot a market, the way out is for the side that knows to take an action that is only worth doing when the news is good — a move too expensive for a faker to copy — and for the side that does not know to offer a menu that makes the informed sort themselves.
>
> - **Signaling** (Michael Spence): the *informed* party burns something real — cash, lock-up time, personal money — to prove what it knows. The signal is credible precisely because a bad type could not afford to send it. A started dividend, a buyback that actually runs, an insider buying with after-tax savings: all are costly bullish signals.
> - **Screening** (Rothschild-Stiglitz): the *uninformed* party designs a menu — an insurance deductible, a loan covenant, a fee tier — so that each hidden type reveals itself by which option it picks. You do not ask the driver if they are risky; you offer a deductible the risky one will not take.
> - **The separating condition** is one inequality: a signal sorts the types only when its cost is *below* the good type's benefit and *above* the bad type's. Too cheap and everyone fakes it (a pooling mess); too dear and even the good type quits.
> - **The one rule to keep**: actions cost; words are free. When you read a company, a counterparty, or a fill, weight what they *paid* to do, not what they *said* — and ask whether a bad type could have afforded the same move.

In August 1982, Apple Computer did something that, on its surface, looked like a mistake. It had been public for less than two years, it was burning to fund growth, and it was sitting on a fast-moving business that could swallow every dollar of cash it generated. And yet, plenty of companies in exactly that position started paying a dividend — handing cash back to shareholders every quarter — the moment they felt secure. Decades later, the question that haunts every finance student is the mirror image: why did Apple, by then the most profitable company on Earth, refuse to pay a dividend for so long, and then in 2012 finally start one? The answer to both questions is the same, and it has almost nothing to do with the cash itself. It is about what the *decision* tells everyone watching.

Here is the puzzle this post solves. In the previous post in this track, [the lemons problem](/blog/trading/game-theory/asymmetric-information-the-lemons-problem-in-markets), we watched a market rot: when one side knows the quality and the other can only pay the average, the good sellers withdraw, the average drops, and the market can collapse to junk. That post ended on an open question it deliberately did not answer. If information asymmetry is what breaks markets, how do the informed *credibly reveal* what they know — so the good type does not have to disappear? You cannot just say "trust me, my company is great" or "trust me, this car is a peach." Talk is free, so a liar would say exactly the same thing. The whole problem is that words carry no information when both types can speak them.

The solution, worth two Nobel Prizes, is that there are exactly two ways out, and they are mirror images. The figure below is the mental model for the entire post: a good firm pays a costly signal and comes out ahead, while a weak firm that tries to copy it ends up worse off and quits — so the action itself, not any promise, separates the two.

![A good firm pays a costly signal and nets sixty dollars while a weak mimic loses sixty and quits](/imgs/blogs/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades-1.png)

The first way out is **signaling**, and it belongs to the side that *knows*. You take a costly action that is only worth taking if your private information is good — and you let the market draw the obvious conclusion. The second way out is **screening**, and it belongs to the side that *does not know*. You design a menu of choices so cunning that each hidden type, acting purely in its own interest, picks the option that gives itself away. One mechanism is the informed proving themselves; the other is the uninformed laying a trap that only the informed can spring honestly. By the end, you will read a dividend, a buyback, an insider's open-market purchase, an IPO lock-up, and an insurance deductible as the same machinery running in different costumes — and you will know which of them you can actually trust, and why.

## Foundations: signals, screens, and the cost that makes them credible

Before any of the finance, we need five plain-English ideas, built from zero. Everything in the post is an application of these, so we go slowly and define each the first time it appears.

### Information asymmetry, one more time

**Information asymmetry** is just the fact that the two sides of a deal rarely know the same things. The firm's managers know whether next year's earnings will hold; the public guesses. The executive knows whether the launch is going well; the shareholder reads a press release. The driver knows whether they text behind the wheel; the insurer does not. A *symmetric* deal is one where both sides hold the same facts and trade only because they want different things. An *asymmetric* deal is one where at least one side acts *because* it knows something the other does not. The lemons post showed what asymmetry destroys. This post shows what fixes it.

There is a subtle split inside "asymmetry" worth naming, because the two mechanisms map onto it. **Hidden type** (also called *adverse selection*) is when one side's quality is fixed but secret — the firm is already either strong or weak, the driver is already either careful or reckless, and the only question is how the uninformed side finds out. **Hidden action** (also called *moral hazard*) is when one side can *change its behavior* unobserved after the deal — the insured drives worse once covered. Signaling and screening are both answers to *hidden type*: they are about revealing a quality that already exists, not policing behavior. We will stay on hidden type throughout.

### A signal: an action that speaks because it costs

A **signal** is an action taken by the *informed* side whose whole purpose is to reveal private information to the *uninformed* side. The defining property — and this is the entire idea — is that a signal must be **costly**, and costly in a particular way: *more costly for the type you do not want to be mistaken for than for the type you are.* A signal that is equally cheap for everyone reveals nothing, because everyone sends it. A signal that is equally expensive for everyone reveals nothing either, because nobody can. The signal only works when the cost falls unevenly across types — cheap for the good type, ruinous for the bad.

The original example, from Michael Spence's 1973 paper that won him a share of the 2001 Nobel Prize, is *education as a job-market signal*. Suppose employers cannot tell a productive worker from an unproductive one before hiring. A worker cannot just claim to be productive — everyone claims that. But if getting a degree is *easier* for a genuinely capable person (less effort per grade) and *harder* for a less capable one, then a degree becomes a credible signal of ability, even if the coursework taught nothing useful for the job. The employer rationally pays more to the degree-holder. The striking, slightly cynical punchline of Spence's model is that the signal can work *purely* because it is differentially costly — the schooling need not raise the worker's actual productivity by a single dollar. What matters is that the bad type could not afford the same diploma.

Hold onto that, because every finance signal in this post has the identical shape. A dividend, a buyback, an insider's purchase: each is an action that a strong firm can afford and a weak one cannot fake without ruining itself.

### A screen: a menu the uninformed designs to make types sort themselves

A **screen** runs the mechanism backward. Now the *uninformed* side acts first. It cannot see the hidden type, so instead of guessing, it offers a *menu* of options engineered so that each type, choosing what is best for itself, picks a different option — and by picking, reveals which type it is. The insurer does not interrogate the driver; it offers two policies, one with a low premium and a big deductible, one with a high premium and no deductible, and lets the careful driver and the reckless driver walk to opposite ends of the counter of their own free will.

This is Michael Rothschild and Joseph Stiglitz's 1976 idea (Stiglitz shared that same 2001 Nobel). The art is in designing a menu where the option attractive to the good type is *unattractive to the bad type*, so they cannot both want the same thing. If you get it wrong — if the cheap-premium policy is best for everyone — both types pick it, you learn nothing, and you are back in the lemons soup. The screen succeeds only when the menu's geometry forces a split.

### Two equilibria: pooling versus separating

This is a game-theory series, so we name the two endings precisely. When the types cannot be told apart and all end up at the same price or choosing the same option, the market sits in a **pooling equilibrium** — everyone is "pooled" together at the average. When the types end up at *different* prices or options, with each type's behavior distinct, the market sits in a **separating equilibrium**. The whole drama of signaling and screening is the move from a pooling equilibrium (which the lemons post showed is fragile and tends to rot) to a separating equilibrium (where the good type is correctly recognized and priced).

![A tree splitting into a pooling branch where both types share the average price and a separating branch where the good type is priced high and the bad type low](/imgs/blogs/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades-3.png)

The tree above lays out the fork. On the pooling branch, no credible signal exists, both types are priced at the average — say \$50 when the good type is worth \$100 and the bad worth \$0 — and the good type is quietly subsidizing the bad. On the separating branch, a signal exists that only the good type can afford, so the good type sends it and is priced high (\$100), the bad type stays silent and is priced low (\$0), and the average is no longer a lie that hurts the good firm. The signal is the bridge from the left branch to the right.

### The separating condition: the one inequality that makes it all work

Now the load-bearing piece. A signal separates the types — produces that clean right-hand branch — only when one inequality holds. Let the **benefit** of being recognized as the good type (the price premium you capture if the market believes you) be $B$, the same for whoever earns it. Let the cost of sending the signal be $c_G$ for the good type and $c_B$ for the bad type, with $c_B > c_G$ because the signal is built to hurt the bad type more. A clean **separating equilibrium** exists when:

$$c_G \le B < c_B$$

In words: the signal must be **cheap enough that the good type still profits** from sending it ($B \ge c_G$, so the good type's net $B - c_G \ge 0$), and **expensive enough that the bad type would lose by faking it** ($c_B > B$, so the bad type's net $B - c_B < 0$). Inside that band, the good type signals and the bad type does not, and the market correctly reads the signal. Below the band the signal is so cheap the bad type happily mimics (pooling returns); above the band it is so dear that even the good type stays home. Every real-world signal you will ever evaluate is a question about whether this single inequality holds.

A *basis point*, since we will use it later, is one hundredth of a percent (0.01%). A *dividend* is a cash payment a company makes to its shareholders, usually quarterly. A *buyback* (share repurchase) is a company using its cash to buy its own shares on the open market. Those three terms carry the second half of the post.

## The signal: why a costly action proves what a promise cannot

Let us turn the separating condition into a chart and a number, because "the cost must fall between the two types' benefits" is the kind of sentence that sounds obvious and is actually doing enormous work.

![Two net-payoff lines against signal size showing a separating band where the good type profits and the bad type loses](/imgs/blogs/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades-2.png)

The chart plots each type's **net payoff from signaling** — benefit minus cost — against how big and costly the signal is. Both types capture the same benefit if believed: here \$100, the price premium of being seen as the good firm. But the *cost* of the signal rises differently. For the good type the cost is the face value of the action (the green line falls one-for-one as the signal gets bigger). For the bad type the cost rises faster and starts from a penalty (the red line drops steeply), because a weak firm cannot back the action out of real earnings — it must borrow, divert cash it needs, or strain itself to fake the move. The shaded band is where the good type's net is positive *and* the bad type's net is negative: the separating zone. Anywhere in that band, the good type signals and the bad type refuses, and the action sorts them.

#### Worked example: a started dividend as a costly signal

Make it fully concrete with the numbers from the cover figure. A firm is worth \$100 more to the market if it is believed to be the strong type — that is the benefit $B = \$100$ of separating yourself. The firm is considering committing to a quarterly dividend.

For a genuinely strong firm, the dividend is *cheap*. It has real, durable earnings that more than cover the payout, so the true cost of committing to it — the constraint it places on the firm, the small drag of paying out cash it could have reinvested — is modest. Call it $c_G = \$40$. The strong firm's net from signaling is $B - c_G = \$100 - \$40 = +\$60$. Worth doing.

For a weak firm, the same dividend is *ruinous*. It does not have the earnings to back a standing quarterly promise, so to start one it must borrow the cash or pay out money it desperately needs for operations — and then keep doing it, quarter after quarter, or suffer the brutal punishment of a *cut* (we will price that punishment shortly). The true cost to the weak firm is far higher; call it $c_B = \$160$. The weak firm's net from mimicking is $B - c_B = \$100 - \$160 = -\$60$. It will not do it.

Check the separating condition: $c_G = 40 \le B = 100 < c_B = 160$. It holds. So the strong firm starts the dividend, the weak firm does not, and the market — seeing the dividend — correctly infers strength and pays the premium. The dividend revealed the firm's private information *without the firm saying a word about its earnings*, because only a firm that actually had the earnings could afford the gesture. **That is the entire trick: a signal is a promise that bankrupts you to break, so it is the only kind worth believing.**

### Why words fail and money talks

It is worth pausing on *why* the costless version of this fails completely, because it is the deepest point in the post. Suppose instead of starting a dividend, the CEO simply issues a press release: "We are extremely confident in our future earnings." What does the market learn? Nothing. A weak firm's CEO can type exactly the same sentence at exactly the same zero cost. When an action is equally available to both types, both types take it, and an observer who sees it cannot update their belief at all. Game theorists call this **cheap talk**: communication that costs nothing and therefore, in a setting where the types' interests conflict, conveys nothing. The reason guidance, "investor confidence," and bullish CEO interviews move markets so much less than people expect is that they are cheap talk — a bad type can fake every one of them for free.

A signal escapes the cheap-talk trap by being expensive *in a way that correlates with the truth*. The strong firm pays \$40 of real cost; the weak firm would pay \$160. Because the cost is differential, the *willingness to pay it* is itself the information. This is the inverse of the lemons problem's central sentence — there, the willingness to *trade* was bad news; here, the willingness to *burn money* is good news. The market does not believe the dividend because dividends are magic. It believes the dividend because it ran the separating condition in its head and concluded that no weak firm could have afforded to send it.

#### Worked example: why a "confidence" press release moves nothing

Put a number on the cheap-talk failure so the contrast with the dividend is sharp. Suppose, instead of a dividend, both firms can issue a press release saying "management is highly confident in future earnings." The cost of a press release is, generously, a few thousand dollars of legal review — call it \$0 against the \$100 benefit at stake. Now run the separating condition with $c_G = c_B \approx \$0$. The strong firm's net from the release is $\$100 - \$0 = +\$100$; the weak firm's net is also $\$100 - \$0 = +\$100$. *Both* types issue the release, because it is free and the upside is identical. So when the market sees "management is highly confident," it cannot tell which type sent it — the signal arrives equally from both — and a rational observer's belief stays exactly where it was: 50/50. The press release moved the belief by zero percentage points, and it moved it by zero *for a precise reason*: the cost did not fall unevenly across the types, so the separating condition $c_G \le B < c_B$ fails at the right-hand inequality ($c_B$ is not above $B$, it is equal to $c_G$). The lesson, in one line: a claim that is equally cheap for a liar to make carries exactly as much information as silence.

## The signaling game as a 2x2: the only equilibrium is "good signals, bad stays silent"

We can write the whole thing as a game and solve it formally, which makes the separating outcome airtight rather than hand-waved. Two players: a strong (good) firm choosing whether to **signal** or **stay silent**, and a weak (bad) firm choosing whether to **mimic** the signal or **stay silent**. The market prices each firm based on what it observes, and that pricing feeds back into each firm's payoff.

![Two by two payoff matrix of the signaling game with the good firm signaling and the bad firm staying silent as the only Nash equilibrium](/imgs/blogs/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades-4.png)

Each cell shows the good firm's net payoff first, then the bad firm's. The logic of the payoffs: if both signal, the market cannot separate them and prices both at a muddled middle, so the good firm only nets \$10 (it paid \$40 to signal but got pooled), and the bad firm nets a disastrous −\$110 (it paid \$160 and still got pooled, not lifted). If the good firm signals and the bad stays silent, the market separates them cleanly: the good firm captures the full premium and nets \$60, the bad firm sits at the low price and nets \$0 (it paid nothing). If the good firm stays silent while the bad mimics, the only firm sending the signal is the bad one, so the market reads the signal *as* a bad-type tell and prices it low — the bad firm nets −\$140 and the good firm, pooled at a low average, nets \$20. And if both stay silent, nobody signals, everyone is priced at the \$50 average, and each nets \$50.

#### Worked example: solving the game for its Nash equilibrium

A **Nash equilibrium** is a pair of choices where neither player can do better by switching alone — nobody has a profitable deviation. Let us find it by checking each player's best response, exactly as we would by hand, the computation confirmed by the series' `data_gametheory.nash_2x2` helper.

Start with the bad (weak) firm. If the good firm is signaling, the bad firm compares mimicking (−\$110) against staying silent (\$0). Silence wins by \$110. If the good firm is staying silent, the bad firm compares mimicking (−\$140) against silence (\$50). Silence wins by \$190. So **the bad firm stays silent no matter what** — silence is a *dominant strategy* for the weak type. The signal is simply too expensive for it; there is no scenario where faking pays.

Now the good (strong) firm, knowing the bad firm will stay silent. It compares signaling (\$60) against staying silent (\$50). Signaling wins by \$10. So **the good firm signals.** The unique Nash equilibrium is (good firm signals, bad firm stays silent) — the green cell — which is exactly the clean separating outcome. The model does not just *suggest* the types separate; with these payoffs it *forces* it, because the bad type can never afford to mimic and the good type always profits from revealing itself. The whole separating equilibrium falls out of one dominated strategy.

The number to carry: in a well-built signaling game, separation is not fragile cooperation that might collapse — it is the equilibrium, robust to both players reasoning it through, because faking the signal is a dominated move for the type that would want to.

#### Worked example: what happens at the edges of the separating band

The separating band — the shaded zone in the net-payoff chart — has two edges, and walking off either one breaks the equilibrium in an instructive way. Keep the benefit at $B = \$100$ and the good type's cost at $c_G = \$40$, and vary the bad type's cost $c_B$.

First, make the signal *too cheap to be type-differentiated*. Suppose a faintly bigger dividend were somehow only $c_B = \$80$ for the weak firm to fake — still above the good type's \$40 but now below the \$100 benefit. The weak firm's net from mimicking is $\$100 - \$80 = +\$20$: *positive*. So the weak firm now profits by faking, both types pay the dividend, the market sees the same signal from everyone, and it pools them back at the \$50 average. The separating condition $c_B > B$ fails ($80 < 100$), and we are right back in the lemons soup the signal was supposed to fix. A signal that the bad type can afford is no signal at all.

Now make the signal *too expensive even for the good type*. Suppose, in a stretched balance sheet, the dividend's true cost to the strong firm climbed to $c_G = \$120$ — above the \$100 benefit. The good firm's net from signaling is $\$100 - \$120 = -\$20$: *negative*. So even the strong firm refuses to send it. The separating condition $c_G \le B$ fails ($120 > 100$), nobody signals, and the types pool by *silence* rather than by mimicry — but they still pool. The band $20 < c_B$ and $c_G \le 100$ exists for exactly one reason: a usable signal must thread the needle between cheap enough that the good type wants it and dear enough that the bad type can't bear it. The intuition: a signal is a knife edge — sharpen it too little and everyone holds it, sharpen it too much and no one will.

## Finance signals, ranked by how hard they are to fake

Now we can grade the real signals. Every corporate action that markets treat as "bullish" or "bearish" is, underneath, a costly signal — and they are *not* equally credible, because they are not equally expensive to fake. The ranking is simply: the more real cash a signal burns, and the more it would hurt a *weak* firm specifically to send it, the more the market should (and does) trust it.

![A grid ranking finance signals from insider buying and dividends as most credible down to guidance and buyback announcements as cheap talk](/imgs/blogs/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades-5.png)

Walk the grid from most to least credible.

**Insider buying with personal cash** sits at the top, and it deserves its own track because it is the purest costly signal in all of finance. When a CEO or director buys shares of their own company on the open market with their own after-tax money, they are doing something a bluffer would never do: betting their personal savings, undiversified, on a stock they cannot legally sell for a window of time. The cost of faking this signal is not abstract — it is the executive's own net worth at risk. Academic studies of insider transactions consistently find that insider *purchases* predict positive future returns far more reliably than insider *sales* (sales happen for a hundred innocent reasons — taxes, a house, diversification — but a purchase has essentially one reason). This is also where the post connects to the order-flow side of the series: an insider buying is exactly the *informed flow* that the [adverse selection and fast-fill](/blog/trading/game-theory/adverse-selection-and-the-winners-curse-why-a-fast-fill-is-bad-news) post taught you to fear being on the wrong side of. The detection logic and the signaling logic are the same coin: an insider's purchase is informed, and informed is expensive to fake.

**Starting or raising a dividend** is next, and it is credible for a reason specific to its structure: it is a *standing promise*. A dividend is not a one-time gesture; it is a commitment to pay cash every quarter, and the market punishes a *cut* so severely (often a 10-20% single-day stock drop) that no weak firm dares start one it cannot sustain. The dividend's credibility comes from the asymmetric punishment for breaking it — which is exactly why the long Apple holdout makes sense: a firm reinvesting every dollar at high returns *shouldn't* signal with a dividend, and starting one in 2012 was Apple's way of telling the market "we now generate more cash than we can productively reinvest," a genuinely informative (and slightly bittersweet) admission.

**A buyback actually executed** is credible but a notch below a dividend, for a precise reason: it is discretionary. Real cash is spent retiring shares, which is a true cost, but a buyback can be quietly paused without the punishment a dividend cut carries — so its commitment value is weaker. Crucially, the grid's bottom-right cell separates the *announcement* of a buyback from its *execution*: announcing a \$10 billion repurchase authorization costs nothing and binds the firm to nothing, so it is cheap talk, while actually spending the \$10 billion is the costly signal. Firms have long exploited this gap — announcing buybacks they only partially complete — which is why sophisticated readers track buyback *execution* in the cash-flow statement, not the press-release headline.

**Taking on bonding debt** is a moderate signal. Debt forces fixed coupon payments the firm must meet, which *bonds* it to perform — Michael Jensen's classic argument that debt disciplines managers by leaving no slack. A firm that loads up on debt is signaling confidence it can service it. But the signal is muddier than a dividend, because a *desperate* firm also borrows, for the wrong reasons, so debt alone does not separate cleanly.

**IPO underpricing plus lock-ups** is the signaling bundle around going public. When insiders price an IPO below where it trades on day one — the average first-day "pop" in U.S. IPOs has run roughly 15-20% over decades — they leave real money on the table, a cost that compensates buyers for the lemons risk and signals confidence the stock can rise. The **lock-up** (insiders barred from selling, typically for ~180 days) is a complementary signal: insiders putting their own exit on hold tells buyers they are not dumping. Both are costly, but they are one-time signals tied to the sale, weaker than an ongoing dividend.

**Guidance and buyback *announcements*** sit at the bottom: cheap talk. Words and authorizations cost nothing to issue, so a bad type can fake them as easily as a good one, and the market rightly discounts them. The discipline is to weight what a firm *paid* to do over what it *said* it would do.

#### Worked example: pricing the punishment that makes a dividend credible

A dividend's credibility rests entirely on the cost of cutting it, so let us price that cost. Suppose a firm trades at \$50 and pays a \$2.00 annual dividend — a 4% yield, the kind income investors buy specifically for the dividend. The shareholder base is full of pension funds and retirees who hold the stock *because* of that reliable payout. Now the firm cuts the dividend to \$1.00.

The mechanical loss of income is small: \$1.00 per share per year. But the *signal* is catastrophic, because the only firms that cut a dividend are firms in real trouble — a healthy firm has no reason to. So the cut tells the market "our earnings can no longer support the payout," and the income investors who held for the yield now flee, all at once. Historically, dividend cuts trigger immediate stock drops on the order of 10-20%; a 15% drop on a \$50 stock is \$7.50 per share of market value vaporized — more than three years of the entire \$2.00 dividend — in a single day, on the *announcement*, before the firm has paid out a cent less. That \$7.50 hit is the punishment $c_B$ that a weak firm faces for starting a dividend it cannot sustain. The dividend is credible going in *because* the exit is so expensive: a firm only starts a payout it is confident it can defend, since the market makes quitting cost roughly four times the annual dividend itself.

## Screening: when the uninformed lays the trap

Signaling is the informed side proving itself. Now flip the table: what does the *uninformed* side do when it cannot wait for a signal and must act first? It **screens** — it designs a menu so that each hidden type sorts itself by which option it chooses. The cleanest example is insurance, and it is worth building from zero because the geometry is exactly the same separating condition, just operated by the other player.

An insurer faces two kinds of drivers it cannot tell apart: **low-risk** drivers who rarely crash and **high-risk** drivers who crash often. If the insurer offers a single, average-priced policy, the lemons logic from the previous post bites: the low-risk drivers find the average price too high (they rarely claim) and drop out, leaving the insurer with only the high-risk pool, which makes the average price too low, which drives out the next-safest tier — the classic insurance "death spiral." A single price *pools* the types and the pool rots. The insurer's escape is to stop offering one policy and offer a *menu*.

![Two insurance plans where the low-risk driver picks the high-deductible plan and the high-risk driver picks the full-cover plan](/imgs/blogs/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades-6.png)

The menu in the chart has two plans built so the types split. **Plan A** is full coverage with no deductible at a high premium of \$1,500. **Plan B** is a cheap \$300 premium but with a large \$4,000 deductible — the amount you pay out of your own pocket on any claim before insurance kicks in. The deductible is the trap: it is a penalty you only pay *if you crash*, so it costs a frequent-crasher a fortune and a rare-crasher almost nothing. That differential is what makes the menu sort.

#### Worked example: the deductible that makes drivers sort themselves

Compute each driver's expected annual cost under each plan. Expected cost = premium + (probability of a claim) × (deductible you'd pay on a claim). The low-risk driver claims with probability 5%; the high-risk driver claims with probability 40%.

Low-risk driver. Under Plan A: pays the \$1,500 premium, no deductible exposure, so \$1,500. Under Plan B: pays the \$300 premium plus an expected deductible of 5% × \$4,000 = \$200, totaling \$500. Plan B costs \$500 versus Plan A's \$1,500, so **the low-risk driver picks Plan B** — the cheap premium with the big deductible, which barely hurts because they almost never claim.

High-risk driver. Under Plan A: \$1,500, same as anyone (no deductible). Under Plan B: \$300 premium plus an expected deductible of 40% × \$4,000 = \$1,600, totaling \$1,900. Now Plan A's \$1,500 beats Plan B's \$1,900, so **the high-risk driver picks Plan A** — the expensive full-cover policy, because their frequent crashes would make the deductible bite hard.

The two types walked to opposite ends of the menu *of their own free will*, and the insurer learned each driver's hidden risk purely from which box they ticked — no interrogation, no lie-detector, just a menu whose geometry forced a split. The insurer can then price each plan against the type it knows will choose it: cheap for the self-revealed safe drivers, full-margin for the self-revealed risky ones. **A screen is a question the uninformed asks not in words but in incentives, designed so that only the truth is in each type's self-interest to answer.**

### Screens are everywhere once you see them

The deductible is the archetype, but the same trap is laid all over finance and commerce. **Loan covenants** screen borrowers: a confident, low-risk borrower happily accepts a covenant that lets the lender call the loan if leverage rises (they never intend to breach it), while a risky borrower balks — so accepting tight covenants signals quality, and the menu of covenant-light-but-expensive versus covenant-heavy-but-cheap loans sorts borrowers. **Warranties** screen sellers: a manufacturer confident in its product offers a long warranty cheaply (it rarely pays out), while a maker of junk cannot afford the same warranty (it would pay out constantly) — so the warranty length is a screen the *buyer* reads and, from the seller's side, a signal. **Fee tiers and account minimums** screen clients; **frequent-flyer status hurdles** screen travelers; **the choice between a salary and commission** screens job candidates by their private confidence in their own ability. In every case the uninformed side offers options engineered so the hidden type reveals itself through choice.

## How a credible signal updates the market's belief

We have one piece left: what the signal *does* to the market's belief, mechanically. The answer is that belief does not drift — it *jumps* — because the action is the information, and the market updates via Bayes' rule the instant it sees the action.

![A bar chart of the market's belief jumping from fifty percent to ninety-one percent on a credible signal and collapsing to six percent on a dividend cut](/imgs/blogs/signaling-and-screening-dividends-buybacks-and-insider-trades-7.png)

Start with a market that has no idea: prior belief P(firm is the good type) = 50%. Then a signal arrives, and the market updates using **Bayes' rule** — the formula for revising a probability when new evidence lands, which weights how much more likely the evidence is under one hypothesis than the other.

#### Worked example: the Bayesian jump on a dividend and the collapse on a cut

Take the positive signal first: the firm starts a dividend, or an insider buys. Suppose every genuinely strong firm sends this signal — P(signal | good) = 1.0 — but only one weak firm in ten manages to fake it (borrowing to do so) — P(signal | bad) = 0.10. Bayes' rule gives the updated belief:

$$P(\text{good} \mid \text{signal}) = \frac{P(\text{good}) \cdot P(\text{signal} \mid \text{good})}{P(\text{good}) \cdot P(\text{signal} \mid \text{good}) + P(\text{bad}) \cdot P(\text{signal} \mid \text{bad})}$$

Plugging in: numerator = 0.5 × 1.0 = 0.5; denominator = 0.5 × 1.0 + 0.5 × 0.10 = 0.5 + 0.05 = 0.55. So P(good | signal) = 0.5 / 0.55 ≈ **0.91**. The market's belief leapt from 50% to 91% the moment the dividend was announced, because the signal is sent overwhelmingly by good firms.

Now the negative signal: a dividend *cut*. Almost no strong firm cuts — P(cut | good) = 0.05 — while most firms in real trouble eventually do — P(cut | bad) = 0.80. Bayes: numerator = 0.5 × 0.05 = 0.025; denominator = 0.5 × 0.05 + 0.5 × 0.80 = 0.025 + 0.40 = 0.425. So P(good | cut) = 0.025 / 0.425 ≈ **0.06**. The market's belief *collapses* from 50% to 6% on the cut. This is why a dividend cut is one of the most brutal one-day repricings in all of equities: it is not the lost \$1 of income, it is the belief jumping from "probably fine" to "almost certainly in trouble" in a single Bayesian step. The intuition to keep: a signal does not nudge the market's belief, it *re-anchors* it, because a credible action is sent by one type far more than the other — so the price gaps rather than drifts.

## Common misconceptions

**"A dividend is just the company giving me my own money back, so it can't be informative."** Mechanically, a dividend does reduce the share price by roughly the dividend amount on the ex-date — it is partly your own equity handed back. But the *decision to commit* to an ongoing dividend is the signal, separate from the cash transfer. A firm that starts a sustainable quarterly dividend is making a costly, punishable promise that only a firm with durable earnings can credibly keep. The information is in the commitment, not the cash flow. That is why starting or cutting a dividend moves the stock far more than the dividend's own size would justify.

**"Buyback announcements are bullish, so I should buy when one is announced."** An *announcement* of a buyback authorization is close to cheap talk — it costs nothing and obligates the firm to nothing; firms routinely announce repurchases they only partly complete. The costly signal is the buyback *executed*, the real cash spent retiring shares, which shows up in the cash-flow statement quarters later, not the headline. Treating the press release as the signal is exactly the cheap-talk trap. Read the execution, not the announcement.

**"If insiders are buying, the stock must go up."** Insider purchases are a genuinely credible signal — executives betting personal money is expensive to fake — and they do predict positive returns on average. But "on average" and "over months" is not "this week." The signal tells you the *informed* side is bullish; it does not tell you the timing, and a single insider can be wrong. It is information about the odds, not a guarantee, and it is already partly priced once it is public. The edge is in the base rate, not the certainty.

**"Education signaling means college is worthless."** Spence's model shows a signal *can* work purely through differential cost, without the schooling adding any productivity. That is a statement about what is *logically possible*, not a claim that real education teaches nothing. The honest reading is that a degree's value is a *mix* of genuine human-capital gains and pure signaling, and the signaling share is larger than comfortable — which is why credential inflation happens. The point for markets is the structural one: any costly, type-differentiated action carries information, whether or not it also does something useful.

**"More debt is always a bullish signal that management is confident."** Debt does carry a signal — fixed coupons bond the firm to perform, and a confident management uses leverage it knows it can service. But debt is one of the muddiest signals on the ranking, because the separating condition is weak: a *desperate* firm also borrows, often more aggressively, precisely because it is out of other options. The willingness to take on debt does not fall cleanly across types the way a dividend's punishment-for-cutting does. So debt separates confident-and-able firms from confident-and-unable ones only loosely, and reading every leverage-up as bullish ignores that the bad type borrows too. Pair the debt signal with what the cash is *for* before you trust it.

**"Screening and signaling are basically the same thing."** They are mirror images, not synonyms, and which one you are looking at tells you *who moved first and who holds the information*. In signaling, the **informed** party acts first to reveal itself (the firm pays a dividend). In screening, the **uninformed** party acts first by offering a menu that makes the informed sort themselves (the insurer offers a deductible). Confusing them leads you to look for the trap on the wrong side of the table. Ask: who knows the hidden quality, and who is taking the first action? That answers which mechanism you are in.

## How it shows up in real markets

Concrete, dated episodes where the signaling and screening machinery ran in the open.

### Apple's dividend, 2012: an informative surrender

For years, Apple's refusal to pay a dividend was itself a signal — a firm reinvesting every dollar at high returns *should not* hand cash back, and Apple's holdout told the market it still saw enormous growth ahead. In March 2012, sitting on roughly \$100 billion of cash, Apple announced it would start a dividend and a buyback. The signal was bittersweet and genuinely informative: it told the market that Apple now generated more cash than it could productively reinvest — a tacit admission that the hyper-growth phase was maturing. The stock initially rose on the capital return, but the deeper signal was about the change in Apple's own assessment of its reinvestment runway. A dividend is not always pure good news; here it was the costly, credible admission that a company had become too profitable to keep plowing it all back, exactly the private information a press release alone could never have conveyed.

### The 1987 share-buyback wave as a crash signal

In the days around the October 1987 crash, with stock prices in free fall, hundreds of U.S. corporations announced share buybacks. The signal worked precisely because it was costly and timely: a company spending real cash to buy its own shares *during a panic* is telling the market "we think our stock is worth more than this price, and we are putting our balance sheet behind that view." Buybacks announced into a crash are among the most credible bullish signals a firm can send, because the action is expensive and the timing rules out the cheap-talk interpretation — you do not commit cash to repurchases in a falling market unless you mean it. The wave helped stabilize sentiment in a way that any number of reassuring statements could not have.

### Insider buying at the March 2009 bottom

In the depths of the 2008-2009 financial crisis, as the S&P 500 bottomed in March 2009, the ratio of insider *buying* to *selling* spiked to levels rarely seen — executives across the market were purchasing their own shares with personal cash at prices they judged absurdly low. Aggregate insider-buying data is one of the few signals that flashed clearly near that generational bottom, and for the reason this post explains: an insider buying in a collapse is making an expensive, undiversified, personally-risky bet that a bluffer would never make. Those who read the signal — not the financial-media panic, but the cluster of insiders quietly buying — caught one of the great entry points in market history. The cost of the signal is what made it trustworthy when everything else was noise.

### IPO underpricing as a persistent signaling cost

The IPO market is a standing, quantified demonstration of costly signaling. Decade after decade, U.S. IPOs are systematically underpriced, with first-day "pops" averaging roughly 15-20% — money the issuer leaves on the table. Hot markets push it far higher: during the 1999-2000 dot-com peak, average first-day returns ran well above 50%, and some issues doubled on day one. The underpricing is a cost the *good* companies accept to separate themselves from the lemons, paying buyers to bear the asymmetric-information risk of a first-time issue. Combined with insider lock-ups (the ~180-day selling ban), the IPO bundle is signaling theory operating at the scale of trillions in new capital — and the persistence of the underpricing, even as the mechanics of IPOs have changed, is evidence that the underlying information problem never goes away.

### Insurance deductibles and the death-spiral fix

Health and auto insurers run screening menus continuously. The clearest natural experiment is what happens when a regulator *forbids* screening — for instance, mandating that everyone be offered the same policy at the same price regardless of risk. Without the deductible-and-tier menu that lets the safe and risky sort themselves, the low-risk customers (who are overpaying) drop out, the pool worsens, the price rises, and the next tier drops out — the adverse-selection death spiral the previous post described. This is exactly why the design of the U.S. Affordable Care Act fought so hard over the *individual mandate*: forcing everyone into the pool was an attempt to prevent the screening-by-self-selection from emptying it of the healthy. The deductible menu is not insurer greed; it is the mechanism that keeps a pooled market from rotting into a lemons market.

### Berkshire's buyback rule: execution over announcement

Warren Buffett spent decades refusing to repurchase Berkshire Hathaway shares unless he judged the stock to be trading meaningfully below its intrinsic value, and he said so in writing. The policy is a textbook lesson in the announcement-versus-execution distinction. Berkshire did not pre-commit to a fixed-dollar buyback program the way many firms announce a "\$10 billion authorization" they may never complete; instead, the repurchases happened opportunistically and showed up as *actual cash spent* in the financials — over \$25 billion in 2020 and 2021 each as the stock lagged. Because the buying was tied to Buffett's own value judgment and executed with real cash rather than promised in a headline, the market reads Berkshire's repurchases as a high-credibility signal that insiders judge the shares cheap. It is the cleanest public demonstration that the costly, executed buyback sits a tier above the free, announced one — the signal is the dollars that left the balance sheet, not the press release that never did.

### Private equity's GP commitment and the lock-up

In private markets, where asset quality is deeply hidden and cannot be priced on a screen, the signals get heavy and explicit. When a private-equity or venture fund raises capital, the general partners are typically required to invest a meaningful slug of their own money — the **GP commitment**, often 1-5% of the fund. This is a costly signal in the purest Spence sense: a manager who secretly believed the fund was a lemon would not lock their own capital in it for a decade. Investors *demand* the commitment precisely because they cannot otherwise verify the manager's confidence, and the multi-year lock-up that traps investor capital screens out investors who would run at the first wobble. Private markets, having the worst information asymmetry, carry the most explicit and expensive signaling apparatus — which is the theory's prediction exactly.

## The playbook: how to play it

This is a trading series, so the payoff is turning all of the above into how you read a company, a counterparty, and a fill. The reframing is the whole point.

**Weight actions, not words — and ask if a bad type could have copied them.** The single discipline this post buys you is a filter: when you assess any bullish or bearish claim, ask what the party *paid* to make it, and whether a weak type could have afforded the same move for free. A CEO's confident interview, a buyback *authorization*, upbeat guidance — cheap talk, a bad type can fake every one, discount them toward zero. A started dividend, a buyback actually executing in the cash-flow statement, an insider buying with personal money, a GP putting capital in their own fund — costly signals a bad type could not afford, weight them heavily. The question is never "what did they say?" but "what would it have cost a liar to do exactly this, and could a liar have paid it?"

**Read insider buying as informed flow, and respect the asymmetry.** When you see cluster insider *buying* — several executives purchasing on the open market with their own cash — you are looking at the informed side revealing itself, the same informed flow the [adverse selection](/blog/trading/game-theory/adverse-selection-and-the-winners-curse-why-a-fast-fill-is-bad-news) post taught you to fear being against. Here it works in your favor if you align with it: insiders buying is a credible, costly, type-differentiated signal that the people who know most are bullish. Insider *selling* carries far less information (it has a hundred innocent reasons), so do not treat a sale as the mirror of a purchase. And remember it is a base-rate edge, not a timing call: it shifts the odds over months, it does not promise next week.

**When you are the uninformed side, design a screen instead of guessing.** If you are structuring a deal — lending, investing, hiring, buying a business — and you cannot verify the other side's hidden quality, do not interrogate; build a menu. Offer the covenant the confident borrower will accept and the shaky one will refuse; offer the earnout the honest seller will take and the inflator will not; offer the equity-heavy package the able candidate will choose and the mediocre one will decline. Let the counterparty's choice reveal what they will not tell you. The menu's job is to make truth the self-interested answer.

**Know which side of the menu you are on.** When *you* are offered a screening menu — a deductible, a fee tier, a lock-up, a vesting schedule — recognize that the menu was built to sort you, and that the option you find most attractive is itself a confession about your type. That is fine when your type is genuinely the good one (take the high-deductible plan if you really are low-risk; it is cheaper for a reason). The danger is choosing the option that flatters you while quietly revealing a weakness you did not mean to disclose. Read the menu as the trap it is, and pick the box that matches your true situation, not your self-image.

**Size and invalidate around the credibility of the signal, not its direction.** A signal's tradeable value scales with how expensive it was to fake, so size your conviction to the signal's cost. A dividend *initiation* by a firm with the cash flow to back it is a high-credibility, low-fakeability signal — lean into it. A buyback *announcement* with no execution behind it is cheap talk — do not size to it. Your invalidation is informational: if the costly signal you traded on turns out to have been affordable to a weak type after all (the dividend was funded by debt, the insider buy was a token gesture, the GP commitment was waived), the separating condition never held, the signal was never credible, and the position should come off regardless of price.

One honest caveat: none of this is individualized financial advice, and no signal guarantees an outcome. It is a lens. The lens says that in any market interaction, the relevant question is not "what is being claimed?" but "what did it cost to make this claim, and could a bad type have paid that cost?" Asymmetric information is not only the disease the lemons post diagnosed — it is also, through signaling and screening, the thing that lets honest information move through a market at all. The spread, the dividend, the deductible, the insider's purchase: every one of them is the market's machinery for separating what is true from what is merely said.
