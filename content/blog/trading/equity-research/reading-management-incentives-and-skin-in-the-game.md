> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Behind every capital-allocation decision and every accounting choice is a management team responding to its incentives; learn to read those incentives in the proxy statement and you can predict behavior the financials will only confirm later.
>
> - **"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."** Owners hire managers to run the business, but managers are separate people with their own goals — the **agency problem**. The pay package is the wiring that either aligns the manager with you or sets them against you.
> - **The proxy statement (DEF 14A) is where it's all written down.** The 10-K tells you what the business did; the proxy tells you *why* — how executives are paid, what targets trigger the bonus, how much stock insiders own, and who sits on the board.
> - **The metric the bonus pays on predicts the behavior.** Pay tied to **revenue** buys empire-building; pay tied to **EPS** buys debt-funded buybacks and accretive deals; pay tied to **adjusted EBITDA** invites the gaming of "one-time" costs; pay tied to **ROIC and per-share value** aligns the manager with the owner.
> - **Skin in the game is the single most reliable signal.** A founder with \$500M — 80% of their net worth — in the same shares you own behaves differently from a hired CEO paid in fresh options with a golden parachute. A *cluster* of open-market insider buying is one of the few honest bullish signals in markets.
> - **Stock-based compensation is a real, hidden cost.** A share count creeping up 3% a year quietly transfers value from owners to employees — a flat business is worth steadily less per old share even though nothing on the income statement looks wrong.
> - **Governance is the qualitative discipline that protects the quantitative thesis.** Build a five-question scorecard — pay-for-performance, the pay metric, skin in the game, dilution, and the board — and a great DCF on a badly-governed company is a trap.

A company is not a spreadsheet. It is a group of people, and at the very top sits a small number of them — the chief executive, the chief financial officer, a handful of senior managers, and the board that is supposed to supervise them — who make the decisions that determine whether your investment compounds for a decade or quietly bleeds out. They decide what to build and what to abandon, what to buy and what to sell, whether to borrow, whether to pay a dividend or buy back stock, and — most insidiously, because it is the hardest to see — how aggressively to interpret the gray areas of accounting. Every number you study in a financial statement is the *downstream consequence* of choices these people made. And those people, like all people, do what they are rewarded for doing.

Charlie Munger compressed the entire subject into one sentence: *"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."* It is the most useful single idea in all of equity research, and it is the one beginners ignore the longest, because it does not appear in any ratio. You can build a flawless discounted cash flow model, nail the margins, get the cost of capital exactly right — and still lose money, because you never asked the prior question: *is the person running this business paid to make me richer, or paid to make themselves richer at my expense?* Those two are not the same, and the gap between them is where fortunes are made and destroyed.

![Two side by side versions of the same company, one where the hired manager is paid for size and reported earnings so behavior diverges from the owner, and one where the manager is a large owner paid for returns on capital so behavior aligns with the owner](/imgs/blogs/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model that anchors this whole post. On both sides sits the same business — call it Northwind Industries, the maker of industrial pumps we have followed through this series — owned by you, the shareholder, and run by a manager you will probably never meet. The only thing that differs is the *wiring* between the owner's goal and the manager's reward. On the left, the manager is paid for size and reported earnings per share, so the manager rationally pursues acquisitions, buybacks, and growth that earns less than it costs — the business gets bigger while your slice of it shrinks. On the right, the manager is a large owner paid for returns on capital and value per share, so the manager does exactly what you would do: invest only above the hurdle, buy back stock only when it is cheap, walk away from dumb deals. Same business, same assets, same products. The difference is the incentive, and the incentive is everything.

This post builds the skill from absolute zero. We start with *why* incentives drive behavior — the agency problem between owners and managers that is the root of the entire subject. Then we go to *where* you read it: the proxy statement, section by section. Then the *substance* — how executives are actually paid, which metrics the pay is tied to and what each metric makes them do, and the dozen ways those metrics get gamed. Then the most important single concept, **skin in the game**: insider ownership, how much of a manager's net worth rides on the stock, and how to read insider buying and selling. Then the quieter dangers — stock-comp dilution, option repricing, related-party deals, and entrenched boards. We close by turning all of it into a practical **governance scorecard** of red flags and green flags, and look at how this shows up in real, recognizable companies. By the end you will read a proxy statement the way a detective reads a motive.

## Foundations: the agency problem and the vocabulary of incentives

Before we can read a single proxy statement, we need to understand *why* there is a conflict to read about at all, and we need an exact vocabulary. The words "incentive," "compensation," "equity," "options," and "ownership" get thrown around loosely; here they have precise meanings, and pinning them down now prevents the confusion that sinks most beginners.

**The agency problem is the conflict between owners and managers.** In a tiny business, the owner *is* the manager — the founder of a corner bakery both owns the bakery and runs it, so there is no conflict; every dollar they save or earn is their own. But in a large public company, the owners are thousands of dispersed shareholders, and the people who actually run the business are hired professionals — the executives. The shareholders are called the **principals** (they own the thing and want it to do well), and the managers are called the **agents** (they act on the principals' behalf). The trouble is that agents have their own interests, which do not automatically match the principals'. A manager might want a bigger company to run (more prestige, more pay), a corporate jet, a quiet life, or a fat bonus this year — none of which is the same as *maximizing the long-run value of each share you own*. Economists call this the **principal-agent problem**, and the entire field of corporate governance exists to address it.

**Incentives are the rewards and penalties that shape behavior.** An incentive is anything that makes a particular action more or less attractive to the person taking it. A bonus that pays out when revenue hits a target is an incentive to grow revenue — by any means, including means that destroy value. The central insight, and it is almost a law of nature, is that *people respond to the incentives they actually face, not to the goals they are told to pursue*. A board can tell a CEO "create long-term shareholder value" all day long; if the CEO's bonus is wired to this year's reported earnings, the CEO will manage to this year's reported earnings. The incentive wins.

**Compensation is the total package a manager is paid**, and it has three broad components that we will return to constantly:

- **Base salary** — a fixed cash amount paid regardless of performance, like any employee's salary. For a large-cap CEO this is often a small fraction of total pay (perhaps \$1M–\$2M), there to cover living expenses, not to motivate.
- **Cash bonus** (also called the annual incentive) — a variable cash payment, usually paid once a year, that depends on hitting specified targets (a revenue number, an EPS number, an "adjusted EBITDA" number). This is short-term: it rewards what happened *this year*.
- **Equity awards** (long-term incentives) — grants of company stock or options that vest over several years. This is meant to be the part that aligns the manager with shareholders, because its value depends on the share price. It is also where most of the money is, and where most of the games are played.

**Equity is ownership of the business itself.** A share of stock is a slice of ownership — a claim on the company's future cash flows and assets (we built this up in the opening post on [stock as a claim on a business](/blog/trading/equity-research/stock-as-claim-on-a-business-investor-mindset)). When a manager owns shares, they own the same thing you own, and their wealth rises and falls with yours. There are several flavors of equity *award*, and the differences matter enormously:

- **Restricted stock units (RSUs)** are grants of actual shares that vest over time (say, 25% a year for four years). An RSU has value as long as the stock is worth anything at all — even if the stock falls 40%, the RSU is still worth 60% of what it was. RSUs reward *retention* and broad price level, but they do not require the manager to *create* value; just showing up and not blowing the company up is enough to collect.
- **Stock options** give the manager the *right to buy* shares at a fixed price (the "strike" or "exercise" price) for a number of years. If the strike is \$50 and the stock rises to \$80, the option is worth \$30 a share; if the stock stays at \$50 or falls, the option is worth nothing. Options are a *one-way bet*: huge upside, zero personal downside. They reward swinging for the fences — and, as we will see, sometimes for gaming the timing and the strike.
- **Performance shares (PSUs)** are shares that vest only if specified performance targets are hit over a multi-year period — for example, "you get these shares only if our return on invested capital averages above 12% over three years." Done right, with the right metric, performance shares are the best-aligned form of equity. Done wrong, with a soft metric or a resettable target, they are a sophisticated way to pay for nothing.

**Skin in the game is how much of the manager's own wealth rides on the stock.** This is the single most important concept in the post, and it is distinct from compensation. Compensation is what the company *gives* the manager; skin in the game is how much the manager *stands to lose* if things go badly. A founder who owns \$500M of stock that they bought or earned over decades, representing 80% of their personal net worth, has enormous skin in the game — a bad decision costs *them* enormously. A hired CEO who was granted some options last year, holds little stock outright, and has a golden parachute that pays out regardless, has almost none. Same title, completely different behavior.

**The proxy statement (DEF 14A) is the document where all of this is disclosed.** Public companies are required to file an annual proxy statement before their shareholder meeting. "DEF 14A" is the SEC's code for the definitive version. The 10-K (the annual report, covered in our post on [reading the 10-K](/blog/trading/equity-research/reading-the-10k-footnotes-and-mda)) tells you what the business did. The proxy tells you how the people running it are paid, measured, and invested — and it is, astonishingly, the part of the filing pile that most retail investors never open.

With those terms — agency problem, principals and agents, incentives, the three pay components, the flavors of equity, skin in the game, and the proxy statement — we can finally do the work: open the proxy and read the motive.

## Where to read it: a guided tour of the proxy statement

The proxy statement is not hard to find. Go to the SEC's EDGAR database (or any filings site), search the company, and look for the filing type "DEF 14A." It will be a long document, much of it boilerplate about the annual meeting and voting procedures, but buried inside is everything you need. Here is the tour, in the order it appears, with what to look for at each stop.

![A vertical six step guided tour through the proxy statement from the filing itself, to the compensation discussion and analysis, to the pay metrics, to the summary compensation table dollars, to the beneficial ownership table, to the board and committees](/imgs/blogs/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game-2.png)

**1. The DEF 14A itself.** Confirm you are reading the *definitive* proxy (DEF 14A), not a preliminary version (PRE 14A) or a supplement. It is filed annually, usually a few weeks before the shareholder meeting. This is a separate document from the 10-K; many investors who diligently read the 10-K never realize the proxy exists.

**2. The Compensation Discussion & Analysis (the CD&A).** This is management's own narrative explaining *why* they pay the way they do — their stated "compensation philosophy." Read it, but read it skeptically: it is written by the company to justify the pay, and the language is almost always reassuring ("our program is designed to align management with shareholders and pay for performance"). The CD&A is the *claim*. The job of the rest of the proxy is to *check the claim* against the actual numbers. When the narrative says "pay for performance" but the tables show enormous payouts in a year the business shrank, the narrative is the spin and the tables are the truth.

**3. The metrics that set pay.** Somewhere in the CD&A, usually in a table, the company discloses which financial metrics the annual bonus and the long-term equity awards vest on, and the targets for each. This is the most predictive single thing in the document, because — by the law of incentives — *whatever the pay is tied to is what management will optimize*. Note every metric: revenue, EPS, adjusted EBITDA, total shareholder return (TSR), return on invested capital (ROIC), free cash flow, "strategic objectives," and so on. Note also the *weights* (is the bonus 70% revenue and 30% EPS?) and the *targets* (what number triggers a payout, and is that number a stretch or a gimme relative to last year?).

**4. The Summary Compensation Table.** This is the dollars. By SEC rule, the proxy must disclose, for the CEO and the next several most-highly-paid officers (the "named executive officers"), a table showing base salary, bonus, stock awards, option awards, and total compensation for each of the last three years. This is where you see *how much* and *in what form*. Pay particular attention to the ratio of variable (bonus + equity) to fixed (salary), and to the fraction that is genuinely long-term equity versus cash collected this year.

**5. The beneficial ownership table.** This is skin in the game. The proxy must disclose how many shares each director and named officer *beneficially owns* — actual shares held, plus options exercisable within 60 days — and the percentage of the company each represents. It also lists every outside holder above 5%. This table tells you whether the people running the company are real owners or hired hands, and it is the single most important table in the document.

**6. The board and committees.** Finally, the proxy describes the board of directors: who they are, whether they are "independent" (not employed by or financially entangled with the company), how long they have served, and which committees they sit on — especially the **compensation committee**, which sets executive pay, and the **audit committee**, which oversees the accounting. It also discloses **related-party transactions**: any business the company does with insiders or their relatives. A captured board and a web of related-party deals are among the loudest red flags in all of investing.

Read these six stops in order and you reconstruct the full incentive structure: the philosophy, the metrics, the dollars, the ownership, and the oversight. Now let us go deep on the part that drives behavior most directly — the metrics.

## The metric is the message: how pay targets drive behavior

Here is the most useful operating principle in this entire post: **the metric the bonus pays on is a near-perfect predictor of the behavior you will get.** Management is a small group of intelligent, ambitious people whose enormous compensation depends on hitting specific numbers. They will hit those numbers. The only question is *how* — and the "how" is determined by which number it is. The same operating reality routes to wildly different actions depending on which metric triggers the payout.

![A four row matrix mapping each compensation metric of revenue, earnings per share, adjusted EBITDA, and return on invested capital to what it literally rewards, the behavior it buys, and whether it aligns with owners](/imgs/blogs/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game-3.png)

Let us walk the metrics from worst-aligned to best.

**Revenue (or "size") pay buys empire-building.** If the bonus rises with revenue, the rational manager grows revenue — and the fastest way to grow revenue is to *buy other companies* and to *spend aggressively on growth*, whether or not that growth earns a decent return. A manager paid for size will do deals that make the company bigger and the per-share value smaller, because bigger is what pays them. This is **empire-building**, and it is one of the most common ways shareholder value is destroyed in plain sight. The company's revenue chart goes up and to the right; the owners get poorer. (We treat the mechanics of value-destroying acquisitions in depth in the companion post on [capital allocation, the CEO's most important job](/blog/trading/equity-research/capital-allocation-the-ceos-most-important-job).)

**EPS (earnings per share) pay buys financial engineering.** Earnings per share is net income divided by share count. A manager paid to hit an EPS target has two honest levers (grow earnings, which is hard) and several easy ones. The easiest is to *shrink the denominator*: buy back shares. Fewer shares means higher EPS *even if total earnings do not change at all*. Worse, the buyback can be funded with borrowed money, and if the after-tax cost of the debt is below the earnings yield of the stock, the buyback is "accretive" — it raises EPS — while doing nothing for, or even destroying, per-share *value*. The other easy lever is to do **EPS-accretive acquisitions**: buy a company whose earnings yield is higher than your financing cost, and EPS goes up mechanically, again regardless of whether the deal created value. EPS is a seductive metric because it sounds owner-aligned ("earnings, per share!") but it is trivially gameable.

#### Worked example: a debt-funded buyback that hits the EPS bonus without creating a cent of value

Northwind's CEO has a bonus that pays out if EPS reaches \$4.40 this year, up from \$4.00 last year — a 10% increase. The business itself is flat: net income is stuck at \$400M and there are 100 million shares, so EPS is exactly \$4.00 and the bonus is at risk. The CEO has no time to grow earnings 10% organically, so they reach for the share count.

The company borrows \$500M at a 5% interest rate and uses it to buy back stock at the current price of \$50 a share. \$500M buys 10 million shares (`\$500M ÷ \$50 = 10M`), shrinking the count from 100M to 90M. The new debt costs `\$500M × 5% = \$25M` of interest a year; at a 21% tax rate the after-tax cost is `\$25M × (1 − 0.21) = \$19.75M`, which reduces net income from \$400M to about \$380.25M. But that smaller pile of earnings is now divided across only 90 million shares:

`new EPS = \$380.25M ÷ 90M ≈ \$4.225`

That is not quite \$4.40, so suppose the buyback is a bit larger — \$700M of debt retiring 14 million shares. Net income falls to `\$400M − \$700M × 5% × 0.79 ≈ \$372.35M`, divided by 86 million shares = **\$4.33**, and with a little operating luck the CEO clears \$4.40 and collects the bonus. The business produced *less* total profit than last year. The company is now carrying \$700M of new debt and is riskier. The intrinsic value per share did not rise — arguably it fell, because the firm took on leverage to buy its own stock at a price that may not have been cheap. But EPS went up, the target was hit, and the CEO got paid.

*EPS can be manufactured by shrinking the share count with borrowed money, so an EPS-linked bonus rewards financial engineering that can leave owners no better off — or worse off — than before.*

**Adjusted EBITDA pay invites the gaming of "one-time" costs.** EBITDA is earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization — a profit measure that ignores real costs. "*Adjusted* EBITDA" goes further: it adds back, on top of those, whatever the company decides to call "non-recurring," "one-time," or "unusual" — restructuring charges, stock-based compensation, legal settlements, write-offs, "transformation costs." The trouble is that the company chooses what to add back, and a management team paid on adjusted EBITDA has every incentive to add back as much as possible and to keep finding "one-time" items every single year. We covered the accounting mechanics of this in [quality of earnings: accruals, one-offs, and red flags](/blog/trading/equity-research/quality-of-earnings-accruals-one-offs-red-flags); here the point is the *incentive*: when pay is tied to a number management defines, management will define the number generously.

**TSR (total shareholder return) pay is better but blunt.** Total shareholder return measures the actual return to a shareholder — stock price appreciation plus dividends — usually relative to a peer group or index. Because it is tied to the actual stock, TSR is harder to game than EPS or adjusted EBITDA, and "relative TSR" (beating peers) is genuinely useful because it filters out market-wide moves. Its weaknesses: it can reward luck (a rising market lifts everyone), it can be gamed at the margins by timing the measurement windows, and over short periods the stock price is noise rather than signal. TSR over a long, relative, multi-year window is a reasonable metric; TSR over one year is mostly randomness.

**ROIC and per-share value pay aligns the manager with the owner.** The best-aligned metrics are the ones that capture what an *owner* actually cares about: earning a return *above the cost of capital* on the money invested (return on invested capital, ROIC, built up in our post on [returns on capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa)), and growing the *intrinsic value per share* over time. A manager paid for ROIC will invest only in projects that clear the hurdle rate, will refuse to grow for growth's sake, and will return cash when there is nothing good to do with it — exactly the owner's playbook. A manager paid for per-share value growth (net of dilution) cannot win by getting bigger or by financial engineering; they can only win by making each share genuinely worth more. These metrics are harder to game because they are precisely the things that *cannot* be faked without actually creating value.

#### Worked example: an ROIC-and-per-share plan that makes the manager think like the owner

Northwind's board, advised by an activist investor, rewrites the CEO's incentive plan. The old plan paid on revenue growth and an EPS target. The new plan pays on two things: (a) average ROIC above the company's cost of capital over a rolling three-year period, and (b) growth in book value per share net of dividends — a rough proxy for per-share intrinsic value.

Now watch the behavior change. Last year, under the old plan, the CEO was evaluating a \$1,000M acquisition of a rival pump maker that would add \$900M of revenue but earn only a 5% return on the capital deployed — below Northwind's 9% cost of capital. Under the old (revenue) plan, the CEO would have done the deal: it grows revenue 30% and triggers the bonus. Under the new (ROIC) plan, the CEO *kills the deal*, because adding \$1,000M of capital earning 5% drags the company's blended ROIC *down*, below the cost of capital, and *reduces* the bonus. The same CEO, facing the same deal, now makes the value-creating choice — declining it — because the incentive rewards returns, not size.

Suppose instead the CEO finds a genuine \$300M expansion of the existing plant that will earn 18% — twice the cost of capital. The new plan rewards it richly: ROIC stays high, per-share value grows, the bonus pays. So the manager invests in the good project and declines the bad one. That is not because the CEO became a better person overnight; it is because the *metric changed*, and the metric is the message.

*When pay is tied to earning above the cost of capital and growing value per share, the manager's self-interest finally points the same direction as yours — they get rich exactly when, and only when, you do.*

The practical takeaway: when you open a proxy, find the metrics first. A plan built on revenue, raw EPS, and self-defined adjusted EBITDA is a plan that *predicts* empire-building and financial engineering, no matter how owner-friendly the CD&A narrative sounds. A plan built on ROIC, per-share value, and long-window relative TSR is a plan that predicts owner-aligned behavior. You are reading the future of the company's capital allocation, written in the language of pay.

## The gaming of metrics: resetting, sandbagging, and the moving goalposts

Even a well-chosen metric can be neutered by *how the targets are set and moved*. Three games recur so often that you should look for them in every proxy.

**Target resetting (the moving goalposts).** A bonus pays out if the company hits a target. But who sets the target, and what happens when it is missed? Far too often, when the business stumbles and the old target becomes unreachable, the compensation committee quietly *lowers the target* for the next year — so the manager gets paid for clearing a bar that was dropped to meet them. The most damning version is the year-over-year comparison: if you see a company whose performance declined but whose CEO's bonus was still near-maximum, somewhere a target got reset. Look for footnotes explaining "adjustments to the target" or "in light of macroeconomic conditions the committee exercised discretion."

**Sandbagging (the low bar).** The opposite-looking but equivalent game is setting the target low to begin with — "guiding" analysts and the board to a conservative number that is easy to beat, so the bonus pays out almost automatically. A target that the company has hit at maximum payout for five years running is not a stretch goal; it is a salary in disguise. Genuine pay-for-performance means the payout *varies* — some years below target, some above. A bonus that is always maxed out is a bonus that is not actually tied to performance.

**Metric switching.** The most revealing game of all is changing *which* metric the pay is tied to. When revenue growth stalls, a company quietly shifts the bonus to "adjusted EBITDA margin"; when that deteriorates, it shifts to "free cash flow conversion"; when *that* slips, to "strategic milestones." Each switch is defended in the CD&A as "better aligning pay with our evolving strategy," but the pattern — pay always tied to whatever metric currently looks good — reveals that the goal was never to measure performance honestly; it was to keep the checks flowing. Compare the metrics in this year's proxy against the proxy from three and five years ago. Stability of metric is a green flag; serial switching is a red one.

#### Worked example: the bonus that paid out in full the year the business shrank

Acme Software (a second recurring company we will use for the bad examples) had a rough year: revenue fell 8%, operating income fell 20%, and the stock dropped 35%. You would expect executive bonuses near zero. Instead, the proxy shows the CEO collected 95% of the maximum bonus. How?

Reading the CD&A and the metric table reveals the machinery. The annual bonus had been tied to a revenue-growth target of \$1,200M. Actual revenue came in at \$1,050M — a clear miss. But a footnote explains that "in light of unforeseen macroeconomic headwinds, the committee adjusted the revenue target to \$1,030M to reflect conditions management could not control." Against the *reset* target of \$1,030M, the \$1,050M result was a *beat*, and the bonus paid out. Separately, the long-term equity plan, which had been tied to relative TSR (which would have paid zero given the 35% stock decline), had been *amended the prior year* to vest on "adjusted EBITDA margin" instead — and because the company added back \$140M of "restructuring and transformation costs," adjusted EBITDA margin actually *rose*, vesting the equity in full.

So the CEO collected near-maximum cash bonus (target reset) and full equity vesting (metric switched to a self-adjusted number) in a year the owners lost 35% of their money. Nothing illegal happened. The proxy disclosed all of it. But an investor who read the proxy saw, in advance, a board that would pay the CEO regardless of results — which told them everything about how this company would allocate capital and treat its owners.

*When the target moves to meet the result and the metric switches to whatever looks good, "pay for performance" becomes "pay regardless of performance" — and the proxy spells it out for anyone who reads the footnotes.*

## Skin in the game: the owner-operator versus the hired hand

We now reach the most reliable signal in the entire field. Compensation tells you what management is *paid*; skin in the game tells you what management *stands to lose*. And it turns out that the second matters far more than the first. A manager with little of their own wealth at risk will, consciously or not, optimize for their pay package — the options, the bonus, the parachute. A manager with most of their net worth in the same shares you own will optimize for the share price, because the share price *is* their wealth. They feel every dollar created and every dollar destroyed, exactly as you do.

![Two columns contrasting a hired hand manager with a small granted stake, fresh options, and a golden parachute against an owner operator with a large bought stake, pay taken in stock, no meaningful parachute, and most of their net worth riding on the same shares the investor holds](/imgs/blogs/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game-4.png)

**The hired hand** is paid to play and protected if it fails. Their stock holding is small and mostly *granted* by the company rather than *bought* with their own cash. They are paid in fresh options each year — a one-way bet with upside and no personal downside. They have a golden parachute that pays out tens of millions if they are fired or the company is sold, regardless of how shareholders did. Their net worth at risk in the stock might be one or two years of salary — a bad year barely dents their lifestyle. Their rational incentive is to take the option lottery: swing for volatility, because if it works they get rich, and if it does not, they collect the parachute and move on.

**The owner-operator's** wealth *is* your wealth. Their stake is large and mostly *bought* — a founder or an owner who put real cash in and holds the same shares you hold. They are paid mostly *in* the stock: a modest salary, with their fortune coming from the shares appreciating, the same way yours does. There is no separate exit package worth caring about, because their fortune is the share price. Their net worth at risk is enormous — perhaps 80% of a \$500M fortune. Their rational incentive is to protect and compound: think in decades, avoid ruin, and spend a dollar only where it returns more than a dollar.

This is why the percentage of net worth a manager has in the stock matters more than the dollar amount. A CEO might own \$50M of stock — sounds like a lot — but if that is 5% of a \$1 billion net worth (the rest in diversified assets, real estate, and other ventures), it barely moves them. A founder who owns \$50M that is 90% of everything they have will treat each share like the family silver. *Concentration*, not the headline number, is the measure of skin in the game.

#### Worked example: the owner-operator with \$500M on the line versus the hired CEO with options and a parachute

Compare two companies, identical businesses, different captains.

**Founders Inc.** is run by its founder, who owns 25 million of the company's 100 million shares — 25% of the company — worth \$500M at the \$20 share price. That \$500M is roughly 80% of the founder's entire net worth; almost everything they have is in this one stock, bought and earned over twenty years, not granted last quarter. The founder takes a \$300,000 salary and no bonus. If the stock falls 30%, the founder personally loses \$150M. When this founder considers a risky \$200M acquisition, the question in their head is "do I want to bet \$50M of my own money on this?" — because their 25% stake means a quarter of any loss is theirs. They are cautious, long-term, and ruthless about returns, because the downside is *their* downside.

**Hired Corp.** is run by a professional CEO hired three years ago. They own \$8M of stock, mostly RSUs the company granted, which is about 4% of their \$200M net worth (the rest diversified). Each year they receive a fresh grant of options struck at the current price — a one-way bet. Their employment contract includes a golden parachute: if the company is acquired or they are terminated without cause, they collect three times their \$5M annual pay plus accelerated vesting of all equity — call it \$40M, paid regardless of how shareholders fared. When this CEO considers the same risky \$200M acquisition, the calculus is different: if it works, the stock rises and their options become hugely valuable; if it fails, the stock falls but their option downside is zero (options can't go below zero), their RSU loss is a rounding error on their net worth, and the parachute is intact. The asymmetry pushes them toward the swing.

Same decision, opposite incentives. The founder, with \$500M and 80% of their wealth on the line, behaves like the careful owner you want. The hired CEO, with a one-way option bet and a \$40M parachute, is rationally pulled toward volatility and size. Neither is a villain; each is responding to the structure they face. Your job as an investor is to read which structure you are buying into.

*The percentage of a manager's net worth that rides on the same shares you own — not their salary, not even their dollar stake — is the truest predictor of whether they will treat your capital like their own.*

## Reading insider transactions: buying is signal, selling is mostly noise

Beyond the static ownership table, insiders *trade* their stock, and those trades are disclosed (in the US, on SEC Form 4 within two business days of the transaction). Investors pore over insider trading for signal — and there is real signal there, but it is profoundly asymmetric: **insider buying is informative; insider selling usually is not.**

![A bar chart of monthly net insider dollar transactions showing scattered small red selling bars below the zero line as routine noise and a tall green cluster of open market buying above the line flagged as the rare informative signal](/imgs/blogs/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game-5.png)

The asymmetry comes from the reasons people trade. An insider sells for a dozen reasons that have *nothing* to do with the company's prospects: they are diversifying (an executive is dangerously over-concentrated in their own employer's stock), they owe taxes on vested RSUs, they are buying a house, paying tuition, getting divorced, or simply rebalancing a fortune that is 70% one stock. A single insider sale, or even a steady drip of sales, tells you almost nothing — it is the financial equivalent of an employee cashing part of their paycheck. There is exactly *one* reason an insider sells that *would* be informative ("I think the stock is overvalued"), and it is drowned out by the dozen innocent reasons, so the signal-to-noise ratio of selling is terrible.

Buying is the opposite. An insider who already owns plenty of the stock, already has their salary and bonus and option grants, and then goes into the *open market and spends their own after-tax cash* to buy *more* shares, is doing something with only one sensible explanation: they think the stock is going to rise. There is no diversification reason to buy more of what you already own too much of; no tax reason; no liquidity reason. Open-market buying is a costly, voluntary signal, and costly signals are credible. The signal is strongest when it is a **cluster** — several different insiders (the CEO, the CFO, a couple of directors) all buying around the same time, especially after a price decline. A cluster of independent insiders putting their own money in is one of the few genuinely bullish signals in markets, because it is hard to fake and expensive to send.

#### Worked example: a buying cluster that front-ran a recovery

Northwind's stock falls from \$50 to \$30 after a disappointing quarter — a 40% drop on fears that a key product line is dying. Over the next six weeks, the proxy and Form 4 filings show a flurry of open-market purchases: the CEO buys \$6M of stock at around \$31, the CFO buys \$8M at \$30, two independent directors buy \$5M and \$3M, and the head of the troubled division buys \$2M. That is \$24M of personal, after-tax cash, from five different insiders, all within six weeks, all in the open market — not option exercises, not grants, but purchases.

Contrast that with the prior two years of insider activity at Northwind, which the same filings show was *all selling*: a few million dollars a quarter as executives diversified and paid taxes on vesting RSUs — routine, scattered, uninformative. The new pattern is categorically different. Five insiders do not coordinate to spend \$24M of their own money on a "dying" company unless they collectively believe the market has overreacted and the division will recover. They have information and conviction the market lacks, and they are backing it with cash.

An investor who noticed the cluster — and verified it was *open-market buying*, not option exercises dressed up to look like buying — had a strong, if not certain, signal that insiders saw the \$30 price as a gift. Whether they were ultimately right is a separate question; the point is that the cluster was a high-quality piece of evidence that the routine selling of the prior years never was.

*Routine insider selling is noise because there are a dozen innocent reasons to sell, but a cluster of open-market insider buying is signal because there is only one reason to spend your own cash on more of a stock you already own — you think it is going up.*

A caution: distinguish real *open-market purchases* from *option exercises*. When an executive exercises options and the filing shows an "acquisition" of shares, that is not the same as buying with cash in the open market — they are converting a grant, often selling most of it immediately. Read the transaction codes (Form 4 marks open-market buys with code "P" for purchase and option exercises with code "M"). Only the genuine, cash, open-market purchase carries the signal.

## The quiet thief: stock-based compensation and dilution

We now turn to the most underappreciated way management transfers value from owners to themselves: **stock-based compensation (SBC)** and the dilution it causes. This one is dangerous precisely because it does not look like anything is wrong. There is no scandal, no fraud, no obvious bad deal — just a share count that creeps upward year after year, quietly shrinking each existing owner's slice of the pie.

Here is the mechanism. When a company pays employees in stock and options rather than cash, it issues *new shares* to do so. Every new share issued divides the same business among more owners, so each existing share's claim on the company's value falls. This is **dilution**. Companies love to point out that SBC is "non-cash" — it does not reduce the cash on the balance sheet — and to add it back in their "adjusted" earnings. But "non-cash" does not mean "free." The cost is borne by shareholders, in the form of a shrinking ownership percentage, and it is just as real as a cash expense. In fact it is arguably worse, because it is invisible: it shows up not as a line on the income statement that anyone watches, but as a slow upward drift in the diluted share count that most investors never track.

![A bar chart over five years showing shares outstanding rising about three percent a year while the business equity value stays flat, with an annotation that value per share falls from fifty dollars to forty three dollars and thirteen cents, a loss of nearly fourteen percent purely from dilution](/imgs/blogs/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game-6.png)

The figure above makes the theft visible. The business is held perfectly flat — the same total equity value of \$5,000M every year. The only thing that changes is the share count, which grows about 3% a year as the company issues stock to employees. With the business flat, the value *per share* must fall by the dilution rate, and a few percent a year compounds into real money.

#### Worked example: 3% annual dilution quietly transfers 14% of value over five years

Northwind is worth \$5,000M in equity value and has 100 million shares, so each share is worth \$50.00. The business does not grow at all over the next five years — same value, year after year. But Northwind pays its employees generously in stock, issuing new shares equal to about 3% of the count each year (net of any buybacks). Watch the per-share value:

- **Year 0:** 100.0M shares, \$5,000M ÷ 100.0M = **\$50.00** per share.
- **Year 1:** 103.0M shares (`100 × 1.03`), \$5,000M ÷ 103.0M = **\$48.54**.
- **Year 2:** 106.1M shares, \$5,000M ÷ 106.1M = **\$47.13**.
- **Year 3:** 109.3M shares, \$5,000M ÷ 109.3M = **\$45.76**.
- **Year 4:** 112.6M shares, \$5,000M ÷ 112.6M = **\$44.43**.
- **Year 5:** 115.9M shares, \$5,000M ÷ 115.9M = **\$43.13**.

The business did *nothing* — no decline, no scandal, the same \$5,000M of value the whole time. Yet an owner's stake fell from \$50.00 to \$43.13 a share, a loss of **13.7%**, purely from dilution. That \$687M of value (`\$5,000M × 13.7%`) did not vanish; it was *transferred* — from the existing shareholders to the employees who received the new shares. The income statement never showed a dramatic cost; the cash balance never dropped. The value simply leaked out through the share count while everyone watched the wrong number.

Now layer on the common defense: "but the company buys back stock to offset the dilution." Often it does — and that is the second half of the trick. If Northwind spends \$150M a year of real cash buying back shares *just to keep the count flat*, then those buybacks are not returning capital to owners at all; they are *mopping up the dilution* from stock comp, funded with shareholders' own cash. The company reports "\$150M of buybacks" as if it were returning cash to you, when in truth it is paying to stand still — using your cash to buy back the shares it just gave to employees. A genuine buyback *shrinks* the count; a dilution-offsetting buyback merely prevents it from growing, at full cash cost. (We separate real buybacks from this sleight of hand in [dividends versus buybacks: returning cash](/blog/trading/equity-research/dividends-vs-buybacks-returning-cash).)

*Stock-based compensation is a real cost paid by owners through a rising share count, so a flat business worth the same every year is worth steadily less per old share — and buybacks that merely offset the dilution are paying full cash price just to keep you in place.*

The investor's defense is simple but rarely practiced: **track the diluted share count over five and ten years, not just the price.** A company whose share count grows 3–5% a year is handing a large slice of the business to employees annually, and you should demand that the business grow *faster* than the dilution just for your per-share value to stand still. A company whose share count is flat or falling — that pays modestly in stock and buys back shares genuinely, at cheap prices, beyond what is needed to offset comp — is one where the value created accrues to *you*. The share-count trend is one of the cleanest tells of whether management treats owners as partners or as a funding source.

## The other red flags: option repricing, related-party deals, and entrenched boards

A few more governance pathologies round out the picture. Each is disclosed in the proxy if you know to look.

**Option repricing.** When a company's stock falls, the options it granted executives at the old, higher strike price go "underwater" — worthless, because the strike is above the market price. A well-governed company lets those options stay underwater; the executives lose along with shareholders, which is the point. A badly-governed company *reprices* the options — lowers the strike to the new, lower market price, or cancels the old options and reissues new ones at the lower strike. This hands the executives a fresh upside bet *precisely because the stock fell*, which is the opposite of pay-for-performance: it rewards management for the decline by resetting their lottery ticket. Repricing must be disclosed in the proxy; treat it as a serious red flag about the board's posture toward management.

**Related-party transactions.** The proxy must disclose any material business the company does with its insiders or their relatives — leasing a building owned by the CEO, buying services from a company a director controls, employing the founder's family members at notable salaries, lending money to executives. Each such deal is a channel through which value can flow *out* of the company and *to* insiders, outside the scrutiny that ordinary compensation receives. A web of related-party transactions is one of the most reliable warning signs in all of investing, because it reveals a culture in which the company is treated as the insiders' personal property rather than the shareholders' common asset. Some related-party dealing is innocuous; a pattern of it, especially involving the CEO's family or private companies, is a flashing red light.

**Board independence and entrenchment.** The board is supposed to be the shareholders' representative, hiring and firing the CEO and setting their pay. But boards are often *captured* — packed with the CEO's friends, former colleagues, and people whose lucrative board seats depend on staying friendly with management. The proxy discloses each director's independence, tenure, and other relationships. Warning signs: long-tenured directors who have served fifteen-plus years (entrenched, unlikely to challenge management), directors with business ties to the company or the CEO (not truly independent), a combined chairman-and-CEO role (the CEO supervising themselves), and **anti-takeover provisions** like staggered boards and poison pills (which entrench management by making it hard for owners to remove them). A board that exists to ratify management's wishes is no check at all, and the incentives we have been reading are exactly the incentives a captured board will indulge.

**Pay for failure.** Pull it all together and the single loudest red flag is *huge pay in a year of poor performance*. When you see a CEO collect a near-maximum bonus and full equity vesting in a year the business shrank and the stock fell, you are looking at the end product of every game in this post — reset targets, switched metrics, soft bars, captured boards. It is the clearest possible statement that this company's pay is *not* tied to your outcomes, and it tells you, in advance and with great reliability, how the company will treat you as an owner.

## Building the governance and incentive scorecard

You do not need to be a forensic accountant to use all of this. You need a short, repeatable checklist that you run on every management team before you trust a single number in their financials. Five questions cover the ground, and the answers cluster — a team rarely fails one and aces the rest.

![A six row by three column scorecard listing five governance dimensions of pay versus performance, the pay metric, skin in the game, dilution and share count, and the board and candor, with the red flag pattern and the opposite green flag pattern for each](/imgs/blogs/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game-7.png)

**1. Pay versus performance — did pay track results?** Compare the CEO's total compensation against the business's results and the stock's return over the last three to five years. *Red flag:* huge pay in a year the business shrank, with bonuses still hitting because targets were reset. *Green flag:* modest pay, mostly in stock that is worthless unless the stock compounds over many years; pay that genuinely *fell* in a bad year.

**2. The pay metric — what is rewarded?** Read the metrics the bonus and equity vest on. *Red flag:* revenue or self-defined adjusted EBITDA, and a metric that was *switched* the moment the old one stopped paying out. *Green flag:* ROIC and per-share value, the same simple owner-aligned metrics year after year.

**3. Skin in the game — how much do they own?** Read the ownership table and the insider transactions. *Red flag:* a tiny stake, all granted; heavy, persistent insider selling into strength; a large golden parachute. *Green flag:* a large stake bought with their own cash; a cluster of open-market insider buying; a founder or owner-operator with most of their net worth in the shares.

**4. Dilution and the share count — who gets the value?** Track the diluted share count over five and ten years. *Red flag:* a count creeping up 3%+ a year from stock comp, with buybacks merely mopping up the dilution. *Green flag:* a flat or falling share count, with genuine buybacks done at low prices.

**5. The board and candor — who watches the CEO?** Read the board's independence, tenure, related-party deals, and the tone of the shareholder letters. *Red flag:* a captured board, related-party dealing with insiders, and letters that spin and dodge the bad year. *Green flag:* an independent board with no self-dealing, and candid letters that own mistakes in plain language.

A company that is green across all five is one where the people running it are wired to make you richer — where the quantitative thesis you built in your DCF rests on a qualitative foundation that will not betray it. A company that is red across several is one where the incentives are pointed at the managers' wallets and away from your returns, and where a beautiful valuation model is a trap, because the numbers it rests on will be bent, the cash will be allocated badly, and the value you compute will leak away through dilution and self-dealing before you ever see it. The scorecard does not replace the financial analysis; it tells you whether to *believe* the financial analysis.

## Common misconceptions

**"High pay is the problem."** Not really. A CEO who is paid \$50M but created \$5 billion of genuine value for shareholders is a bargain. The problem is never the *level* of pay; it is the *link* between pay and the value created for owners. A \$50M package that pays out regardless of results is a scandal; the same \$50M earned only when the stock compounds for a decade is a fair deal. Read the *structure* and the *metric*, not the headline number. Outrage about pay levels is a distraction from the question that matters: what is the pay tied to?

**"Insider selling means insiders are bearish."** Almost never, on its own. Insiders sell for diversification, taxes, houses, and divorces — a dozen reasons unrelated to the company's prospects. A senior executive whose entire net worth is one stock is being *prudent* to sell some of it. Treat routine selling as noise. It is *buying* — voluntary, open-market, with their own cash, especially in a cluster — that carries signal, and even then it is evidence, not proof.

**"Stock-based comp is free because it's non-cash."** This is the company's favorite line and it is false. SBC is a real cost, paid by existing owners through dilution — a shrinking ownership percentage that is every bit as real as a cash expense. The fact that the cash balance does not drop does not mean nobody paid; *you* paid, in the form of a smaller slice of the same pie. A company that adds SBC back to "adjusted" earnings is asking you to pretend a real cost is not a cost.

**"Founders are always better than professional managers."** A useful prior, not a law. Founder-operators with large stakes have wonderful incentive alignment — but some founders are empire-builders, autocrats who refuse to return cash, or visionaries with no discipline; and some hired professional managers are superb capital allocators with the right incentives bolted on. Skin in the game *tilts the odds* toward owner-aligned behavior; it does not guarantee it. Read each case on its merits — the scorecard, not the biography.

**"Good governance is just box-ticking — independent directors, separate chairman, the right committees."** Formal governance structures matter, but they are necessary, not sufficient. A company can have every governance box ticked and still pay for failure, switch metrics, and dilute owners; another can have an old-fashioned structure (a founder-chairman-CEO) and treat owners impeccably because the founder *is* a giant owner. Governance is about *substance* — whether the incentives actually align the people in charge with you — not about the formal architecture. Read the behavior, not just the boxes.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The owner-operators who compounded fortunes.** The most celebrated examples of skin in the game are the great owner-operators. Warren Buffett has run Berkshire Hathaway for decades while keeping essentially his entire fortune in its stock and taking a salary of \$100,000 — the textbook case of a manager whose wealth *is* the shareholders' wealth, with incentives that need no committee to align them. His annual letters are the gold standard of candor: he names his mistakes plainly and explains his capital allocation in owner's language. The lesson generalizes: founder-led and family-controlled companies, where insiders hold large bought stakes, have on average rewarded long-term shareholders well, precisely because the people in charge feel every dollar. (We treat the owner mindset and intrinsic value at length in the companion piece on [Warren Buffett, Berkshire, and value investing](/blog/trading/finance/warren-buffett-berkshire-value-investing).)

**The empire-builders who destroyed value getting bigger.** The opposite archetype recurs across decades: the conglomerate-builder who, paid and praised for size, assembles a sprawling empire through serial acquisitions that grow revenue and headcount while quietly earning below the cost of capital — until the whole structure is broken up at a loss and the "value creation" is revealed to have been value *destruction* dressed in a growing revenue chart. The tell was always in the incentives: pay tied to size, a captured board waving every deal through, and a share count or debt load climbing to fund the empire. The financials confirmed the destruction years later; the proxy predicted it.

**The accounting frauds, where bad incentives met no oversight.** The great accounting scandals were, at root, incentive failures. When executive pay and personal fortunes are tied tightly to a stock price or an earnings number, and the board and auditors fail to check the methods, the temptation to manufacture the number rather than earn it becomes overwhelming. Enron's executives were paid in ways that rewarded a rising stock and reported earnings while off-balance-sheet structures hid the truth (our case study on the [Enron 2001 accounting fraud](/blog/trading/finance/enron-2001-accounting-fraud) walks through the mechanics), and Wirecard's insiders sustained a fiction of cash that did not exist while the governance that should have caught it was absent or complicit (see [Wirecard, the German fintech fraud](/blog/trading/finance/wirecard-the-german-fintech-fraud)). In both, an investor who had read the *incentives* and the *governance* — who had asked "what is this management paid to do, and who is checking them?" — had warning long before the financials collapsed.

**The serial diluters hiding in growth stocks.** A subtler, more common pattern lives in fast-growing technology companies that pay employees lavishly in stock and report glowing "adjusted" earnings that add the stock comp back. For years, the rising share count is masked by a rising stock price, so owners do not notice that 4–5% of the company is being handed to employees annually. When growth slows and the price stops rising, the dilution is suddenly visible: per-share value has been quietly leaking out the whole time, and the "adjusted profitability" was always flattered by pretending a real cost was free. An investor who tracked the *diluted share count* and treated SBC as the expense it is saw the transfer happening in real time, while the headline metrics looked fine.

**The activist's playbook.** When activist investors target a company, the changes they demand most often are, at bottom, *incentive* changes: rewrite the compensation plan to pay for ROIC and per-share value instead of size, refresh a stale and captured board, force the return of cash that an empire-building management was hoarding, and tie the CEO's fortune more tightly to the stock. The activist is, in effect, re-wiring the agency relationship so the managers' self-interest finally points at the owners' returns — which is exactly the diagnosis this post teaches you to make from the outside, by reading the proxy.

## When this matters and further reading

Reading management is the qualitative discipline that protects the quantitative thesis. Every other post in this series teaches you to *measure* a business — its margins, its returns on capital, its cash flows, its value. This post teaches you to ask whether the people running that business are wired to make those numbers compound in *your* favor or to bend them toward their own. A perfect DCF on a company run by misaligned, self-dealing managers is a trap; a merely-good business run by aligned owner-operators is often a far safer place for your capital. When the incentives and the financials disagree, trust the incentives — they are the cause, and the financials are the effect.

The skill is concrete and learnable: open the proxy, find the metrics, read the ownership table, track the share count, judge the board, and run the five-question scorecard. Do it on every company you own or consider, and you will catch the empire-builders, the financial engineers, the serial diluters, and the pay-for-failure boards long before the financial statements confirm what their incentives already told you.
