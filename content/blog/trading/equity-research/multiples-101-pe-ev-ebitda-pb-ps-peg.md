> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A multiple is the market's shorthand for value: a price divided by some fundamental, so you can compare companies of any size on the same scale. They are fast, comparable, and universal — but each one bakes in different assumptions and breaks in a different situation, and knowing exactly what each says (and hides) is the line between *using* multiples and *being fooled* by them.
>
> - There are two families. **Equity multiples** put a price in the numerator (P/E, P/B, P/S, dividend yield) and answer "what do I pay for the shareholders' slice?" **Enterprise multiples** put enterprise value in the numerator (EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT, EV/Sales) and answer "what do I pay for the whole business, debt and equity together?"
> - The **consistency rule** is the one law you can never break: an equity numerator goes over an after-financing denominator, and an EV numerator over a pre-financing one. **P/EBITDA is wrong** — it puts the shareholders' price over the whole firm's cash, comparing apples to a basket of apples and oranges.
> - Each multiple has a home and a failure mode. P/E breaks when earnings go negative; P/B suits banks and breaks for asset-light firms; P/S is the lifeboat for unprofitable growth; EV/EBITDA is the banker's favorite but flatters capital-hungry businesses by ignoring the capex and depreciation that are very real.
> - A multiple is **not** an opinion about cheapness — it is a compressed discounted cash flow. A P/E is *justified* by growth, returns on capital, and risk, which is why a "high-quality compounder" deserves a high multiple and a 6x P/E can be a value trap while a 30x can be a bargain.
> - **PEG** tries to fix P/E for growth (a 15x P/E growing 15% has a PEG of 1.0), and it is a useful first-glance filter — but it is a crude rule of thumb that ignores risk, returns, and the *durability* of growth, so it is as easy to abuse as it is to use.

Walk into any conversation about stocks and within a minute someone will say a sentence like "it trades at twenty times earnings" or "the deal was done at nine times EBITDA." These are *multiples* — the everyday language of valuation, the way professionals and amateurs alike talk about whether a stock is cheap or dear. They are everywhere because they are wonderfully convenient: a single number that compresses an entire company's price into something you can compare against any other company, instantly, on the back of a napkin. You do not need a spreadsheet, a five-year forecast, or a discount rate. You just divide a price by a fundamental and you have a multiple.

That convenience is exactly why multiples are so dangerous in untrained hands. Each multiple is a tiny machine with assumptions welded inside it, and the assumptions are invisible. When you say a stock is "cheap at 8x earnings," you are making a claim that depends on what those earnings are, whether they will persist, how the company is financed, and a dozen other things the number 8 says nothing about. A multiple is shorthand, and like all shorthand it omits — and what it omits is precisely where people get hurt. The reader who memorizes "low P/E good, high P/E bad" will spend a career buying melting ice cubes and selling compounders.

![A multiple turns a raw share price into a comparable rate by dividing it by a fundamental like earnings per share](/imgs/blogs/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg-1.png)

The figure above shows the single idea every multiple is built on. A raw share price is *not comparable* across companies: Northwind trades at \$30 a share and a peer trades at \$300, and that tells you nothing about which is cheaper, because share counts are arbitrary — a company can split its stock and halve its price without changing anything real. But divide each price by a *fundamental*, the earnings per share, and the prices become comparable rates: Northwind's \$30 over \$2 of earnings is 15x, the peer's \$300 over \$10 of earnings is 30x. Now the comparison is meaningful — you are paying \$15 of price for every \$1 of Northwind's annual profit versus \$30 for the peer's. The multiple is a *unit price*: the price of one dollar of fundamental.

This post is a tour of the whole zoo. We will build the idea of a multiple from absolutely nothing, define every fundamental that can sit in the denominator, and walk through the major multiples one at a time — P/E, P/B, P/S, P/FCF, dividend yield, EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT, EV/Sales, EV/FCF, and PEG — saying for each exactly what it measures, what kind of company it suits, and how it lies. We will nail down the *consistency rule* that separates the equity family from the enterprise family and show why P/EBITDA is a category error. Then we get to the part that separates people who use multiples from people who are used by them: **a multiple is not a verdict, it is a compressed DCF**, and we will decode one explicitly. Everything is grounded in dollar arithmetic using **Northwind Industries**, the fictional company this series follows, and its peers. If you have read [the two pillars of valuation](/blog/trading/equity-research/two-pillars-of-valuation-intrinsic-vs-relative), this is the deep dive into the second pillar; if you have not, the one thing to carry in is that relative valuation prices your company by what the market pays for similar ones — and a multiple is the unit of that price.

## Foundations: the building blocks every multiple rests on

Before we can compare multiples we have to define, from zero, the handful of terms that appear inside them. A multiple is a fraction; its meaning lives entirely in what is in the numerator and what is in the denominator. Get those wrong and every conclusion downstream is wrong. So we go slowly.

### A multiple is a unit price: price per unit of fundamental

Start with the definition and never lose it. **A multiple is a ratio of a price to a fundamental — the number of dollars of price you pay for one unit of something the business produces or owns.** The "price" is either the price of a share (or the whole equity, the *market capitalization*) or the price of the whole business (*enterprise value*, which we define below). The "fundamental" is some real quantity: a dollar of earnings, a dollar of sales, a dollar of book value, a dollar of cash flow. Divide the first by the second and you get a multiple.

The reason this is useful is *comparability*. Absolute prices cannot be compared — a \$2,000 share and a \$5 share tell you nothing — because the price per share depends on how many shares exist, which is an accounting choice. But the *rate* you pay per dollar of earnings is comparable across any two companies in the world, big or small, because the arbitrary share count cancels out. A multiple normalizes away size and share count and leaves you with the thing that matters: how richly the market prices each dollar of fundamental. That is the whole trick, and it is why "it trades at 15x" is a sentence everyone in finance understands instantly.

### Market capitalization and enterprise value: two different "prices"

The numerator of a multiple is a price, but there are two prices a company has, and confusing them is the most common beginner mistake. The first is **market capitalization** (market cap): the price of all the *equity*, the shareholders' slice. It is simply the share price times the number of shares. If Northwind trades at \$30 and has 1 billion shares, its market cap is \$30 billion. Market cap is the value of the *equity claim* — what the owners' stake is worth — and it is what sits in the numerator of every *equity multiple*.

The second price is **enterprise value (EV)**: the price of the *entire business*, regardless of how it is financed. A business is funded by two kinds of capital — debt and equity — and both have a claim on its cash. Enterprise value is the value of the whole pie: market cap (the equity claim) *plus* net debt (the debt claim). The formula is `EV = market cap + total debt − cash`. We subtract cash because cash is a negative debt: if you bought the whole company you would get its cash and could use it to pay down some of the price. If Northwind has a \$30 billion market cap, \$8 billion of debt, and \$3 billion of cash, its enterprise value is \$30 + \$8 − \$3 = \$35 billion. EV is what it would actually cost to buy the entire business free and clear — to acquire the equity and assume (or repay) the debt — and it is what sits in the numerator of every *enterprise multiple*. The full mechanics of getting from EV down to a per-share equity value are the subject of [enterprise value to per-share, the bridge](/blog/trading/equity-research/enterprise-value-to-per-share-the-bridge); for multiples, the one thing to hold is: **market cap is the price of the equity; enterprise value is the price of the whole firm.**

### The fundamentals that go in the denominator

Now the denominators. Each multiple scales price to a different fundamental, and each fundamental measures the business at a different point in its income statement or balance sheet. Define them once, cleanly:

- **Earnings (net income)**: the bottom line — what is left for shareholders after *everything*, including interest to lenders and taxes to the government. This is the most "after-financing" number there is; it belongs only to equity. Per share, it is **EPS** (earnings per share).
- **EBIT (earnings before interest and taxes)**: operating profit. It is what the business earns from operations *before* it pays lenders or the tax collector. Because it is computed before interest, it belongs to *all* capital providers (debt and equity), not just shareholders.
- **EBITDA (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization)**: EBIT with depreciation and amortization (D&A) added back. D&A are non-cash accounting charges for the wearing-out of assets, so adding them back gives a rough proxy for operating *cash* generation. Like EBIT, it is a pre-financing, whole-firm number.
- **Sales (revenue)**: the top line — total money coming in the door before any costs. It belongs to the whole firm and is the hardest number to fake or manipulate, which is its virtue.
- **Book value (of equity)**: the accounting net worth of the shareholders' stake — total assets minus total liabilities, as recorded on the balance sheet. It is a backward-looking, accounting measure of what the owners "put in plus retained."
- **Free cash flow (FCF)**: the actual cash the business generates after the investment it needs to keep running. It comes in two flavors — **FCFF** (free cash flow to the *firm*, before financing, belonging to all capital) and **FCFE** (free cash flow to *equity*, after paying lenders, belonging only to shareholders). The distinction is the entire subject of [free cash flow, FCFF vs FCFE](/blog/trading/equity-research/free-cash-flow-fcff-vs-fcfe), and it matters enormously for which multiple a cash number may sit in.

Notice the pattern already forming: some fundamentals are computed *after* paying lenders (net income, FCFE, book equity) and belong to shareholders only; others are computed *before* paying lenders (EBIT, EBITDA, sales, FCFF) and belong to the whole firm. That distinction is the spine of the consistency rule we are about to meet.

### Trailing, forward, and normalized: which version of the number

One more foundation, because it trips up everyone. The fundamental in the denominator can be measured over different periods, and the choice changes the multiple materially. **Trailing** uses the last reported twelve months of actuals (often written "TTM" or "LTM") — a fact, but a backward-looking one. **Forward** uses an *estimate* of next year's number (consensus analyst forecasts) — what most professionals actually use, because a stock's price is about the future, not the past. A "forward P/E" of 18 and a "trailing P/E" of 22 for the same stock simply mean analysts expect earnings to grow: the same price divided by a bigger (forecast) earnings number gives a smaller multiple. **Normalized** uses an estimate of mid-cycle or sustainable earnings, stripping out a boom or a one-off — essential for cyclical companies whose reported earnings swing wildly. Whenever someone quotes a multiple, the first question is always *which E?* — trailing, forward, or normalized — because the same word "P/E" can name three quite different numbers.

## What each multiple really says: the equity family

We start with the **equity multiples** — the ones whose numerator is the price of the equity (share price or market cap). Each answers the question "what am I paying for the shareholders' slice, per dollar of some fundamental?" These are the multiples a retail investor meets first, because they are quoted per share on every brokerage screen.

### Price-to-earnings (P/E): the famous one, and its traps

The **price-to-earnings ratio** is the share price divided by earnings per share — or equivalently, market cap divided by net income. It is the most quoted number in all of investing, and it answers a beautifully simple question: *how many dollars do I pay for one dollar of this company's annual profit?* A P/E of 15 means \$15 of price for every \$1 of yearly earnings. Flipped over, 1 ÷ 15 ≈ 6.7%, the **earnings yield** — the profit the business earns relative to the price you pay, like an interest rate on your purchase.

#### Worked example: computing Northwind's P/E

Northwind Industries trades at **\$30 per share**. Last year it earned **\$2.0 billion** of net income on **1.0 billion shares**, so its earnings per share is \$2.0b ÷ 1.0b = **\$2.00**. Its trailing P/E is \$30 ÷ \$2.00 = **15.0x**. Its earnings yield is the reciprocal, 1 ÷ 15 = **6.7%** — meaning at this price, every dollar you invest is backed by about 6.7 cents of annual earnings. Now suppose analysts expect Northwind to earn \$2.40 next year. The *forward* P/E is \$30 ÷ \$2.40 = **12.5x** — lower than the trailing 15x, because the same price is spread over a bigger expected earnings number. *The P/E is just the price of one dollar of profit; a lower forward P/E than trailing simply encodes the market's expectation that profits will grow.*

The P/E's simplicity hides three traps. First, **the E can be negative or zero**. A company that lost money has no meaningful P/E — the ratio is negative, which is nonsense (a "negative price per dollar of profit" means nothing), or it is enormous and meaningless if earnings are barely positive. For unprofitable companies, P/E is simply unavailable, which is why early-stage and turnaround firms need a different multiple entirely. Second, **earnings are an accounting opinion, not cash** — they can be inflated by aggressive revenue recognition, depressed by one-off charges, or distorted by non-cash items, so a P/E computed on a manipulated or one-time E is built on sand. (This is the whole reason the series spends posts on [quality of earnings](/blog/trading/equity-research/quality-of-earnings-accruals-one-offs-red-flags) and [accruals vs cash](/blog/trading/equity-research/accruals-vs-cash-why-earnings-are-an-opinion).) Third, **P/E ignores the balance sheet**: two companies with identical earnings but wildly different debt loads will have the same P/E, even though one is far riskier — a flaw the enterprise multiples exist to fix.

### Price-to-book (P/B): the multiple for asset-heavy and financial firms

The **price-to-book ratio** is market cap divided by book value of equity — the market's price for the shareholders' stake versus its *accounting* net worth. A P/B of 1.0 means the market values the equity at exactly its book value; a P/B of 3.0 means the market pays three times the accounting net worth, betting the company will earn far more than its book assets suggest; a P/B below 1.0 means the market values the company at *less* than its stated net worth — either a bargain or a warning that the book value is overstated.

P/B has a specific home: **financial firms** — banks, insurers, and other businesses whose assets and liabilities are mostly financial instruments carried at (something close to) market value on the balance sheet. For a bank, "book value" is a fairly honest measure of net worth, because its assets are loans and securities, not hard-to-value factories. P/B is also the natural lens for **asset-heavy** businesses (shipping, real estate, commodity producers) where the balance sheet is the business. Where P/B *breaks* is the modern asset-light firm: a software company or a brand has its real value in intangibles — code, customer relationships, network effects — that accounting either omits entirely or records at a tiny historical cost. Such a firm can have a P/B of 20 or a *negative* book value (after years of buybacks) while being immensely valuable, so P/B is useless for it. The deep driver of P/B is the relationship between **return on equity (ROE)** and the cost of equity: a firm earning more on its book than investors demand deserves a P/B above 1; one earning less deserves a P/B below 1. (That linkage — P/B is justified by ROE — is the bridge to [returns on capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa).)

### Price-to-sales (P/S): the lifeboat for unprofitable companies

The **price-to-sales ratio** is market cap divided by revenue — what you pay per dollar of the top line. Sales is the one fundamental that *always exists and is hardest to manipulate*: a company can lose money, have negative book value, and generate no cash, but it still has revenue. That makes P/S the lifeboat for valuing **early-stage and unprofitable growth companies**, where every after-cost fundamental (earnings, cash flow) is negative or meaningless. When a fast-growing software company has no profits yet, the market prices it on sales — "it trades at 10 times revenue" — implicitly betting that today's revenue will become tomorrow's profit.

The danger of P/S is its great blind spot: **it ignores profitability entirely**. A dollar of sales at a company with 40% margins is worth far more than a dollar of sales at a company with 2% margins, but P/S treats them identically. So P/S is only meaningful when comparing companies with *similar margin structures* — comparing the P/S of a grocer (razor-thin margins) to a software firm (fat margins) is meaningless, because the same revenue converts to wildly different profit. P/S is best used *within* an industry, or with an explicit eye on margins. Used carelessly, it makes low-margin businesses look cheap (low P/S) when they are merely low-quality.

### Price-to-free-cash-flow and dividend yield: paying for the cash that actually reaches you

Two more equity multiples round out the family. **Price-to-free-cash-flow (P/FCF)** is market cap divided by free cash flow to equity — what you pay per dollar of cash the business can actually hand to shareholders. It is in many ways *better* than P/E, because it scales price to real distributable cash rather than to accounting earnings, which can diverge from cash for years. Its drawback is volatility: free cash flow swings with the timing of capital spending and working capital, so a single year's P/FCF can be noisy, and it is best smoothed over several years.

**Dividend yield** is the flip side — annual dividends per share divided by share price, the cash return you receive directly as a shareholder, expressed as a percentage. A 4% dividend yield means \$4 of dividends per \$100 invested. It is not usually called a "multiple," but it is one in disguise (price-to-dividends inverted), and it matters for income investors and mature, cash-returning companies. Its trap is that a *high* dividend yield is often a *warning*: when a price falls because the market fears a dividend cut, the yield mechanically spikes, so the highest yields frequently belong to the most troubled companies — the same value-trap logic we will see with low P/Es.

## What each multiple really says: the enterprise family

Now the **enterprise multiples** — the ones whose numerator is enterprise value, the price of the *whole firm*. These answer a different question: "what am I paying for the entire business — debt and equity together — per dollar of some pre-financing fundamental?" Bankers and acquirers live in this family, because when you buy a whole company you buy its debt too, so the relevant price is EV, not just the equity.

### EV/EBITDA: the banker's favorite, and its great flaw

The **EV/EBITDA** multiple is enterprise value divided by EBITDA — the price of the whole firm per dollar of operating cash proxy. It is the most-used multiple in investment banking and private equity, and for good reasons. First, it is **capital-structure-neutral**: because both the numerator (EV) and the denominator (EBITDA, computed before interest) are measured before financing, the ratio does not change just because a company borrows more or less. Two identical businesses with different debt loads have the same EV/EBITDA but very different P/Es, so EV/EBITDA lets you compare them on equal footing — invaluable in M&A, where the acquirer will choose its own financing. Second, EBITDA is computed before depreciation and amortization, so EV/EBITDA is **comparable across companies with different accounting** for the wearing-out of assets and across different tax regimes. It is the closest thing to a universal "what does the operating business cost" number.

#### Worked example: computing Northwind's EV/EBITDA

Recall Northwind's market cap is **\$30 billion** (\$30 × 1.0 billion shares), it has **\$8 billion** of debt and **\$3 billion** of cash, so its enterprise value is \$30 + \$8 − \$3 = **\$35 billion**. Last year Northwind's operating profit (EBIT) was \$3.0 billion, and depreciation and amortization were \$1.2 billion, so EBITDA = \$3.0b + \$1.2b = **\$4.2 billion**. Its EV/EBITDA is \$35b ÷ \$4.2b = **8.3x**. Note that the equity-only P/E was 15x, but the whole-firm EV/EBITDA is 8.3x — they are not comparable to each other (different numerators and denominators), and that is the point: EV/EBITDA measures the cost of the *operating business*, P/E measures the cost of the *equity slice* after debt and taxes. *EV/EBITDA prices the engine of the business before anyone argues about how it is financed or taxed.*

But EV/EBITDA has a famous and dangerous flaw, and Warren Buffett has spent years warning about it: **EBITDA pretends depreciation is not a real cost, and for many businesses it absolutely is.** Depreciation is the accounting recognition that machines, trucks, and factories wear out and must be replaced — and replacing them costs real cash (capital expenditure, capex). A business that must spend \$1 billion a year on new equipment just to stand still has a *real* \$1 billion cost that EBITDA cheerfully adds back, making the business look far more cash-generative than it is. So EV/EBITDA systematically *flatters capital-intensive businesses* — telecoms, manufacturers, cable companies — that have huge ongoing capex. For an asset-light software firm where capex is trivial, EBITDA is close to cash, and EV/EBITDA is honest. For a capital-hungry firm, EBITDA is a fantasy of cash that never reaches owners, which is why the harder-nosed multiple is **EV/EBIT** (which keeps depreciation as a cost) or **EV/FCF** (which subtracts actual capex). The rule of thumb: *the more capital a business eats, the more EV/EBITDA lies to you, and the more you should look at EV/EBIT or EV/FCF instead.*

### EV/EBIT, EV/Sales, and EV/FCF: the rest of the enterprise family

**EV/EBIT** is enterprise value divided by operating profit *after* depreciation — the same whole-firm logic as EV/EBITDA, but keeping the cost of asset wear in the denominator. It is the more conservative, more honest enterprise multiple, and it is the right one whenever capex is material. Many serious analysts prefer EV/EBIT precisely because it refuses to let capital-intensive businesses hide their reinvestment burden.

**EV/Sales** is enterprise value divided by revenue — the enterprise-family cousin of P/S, used for the same reason (unprofitable or early companies with no meaningful profit) but with the advantage of being capital-structure-neutral. When a loss-making company carries debt, EV/Sales is more honest than P/S because it counts the debt in the price.

**EV/FCF** (or EV to free-cash-flow-to-the-firm) is the purest enterprise multiple of all: enterprise value divided by the actual cash the whole business throws off after capex. It fixes EV/EBITDA's flaw by *subtracting* the capex EBITDA ignores, so it cannot be fooled by a capital-hungry business. Its only drawback is the noisiness of free cash flow year to year, the same issue as P/FCF. If you could quote only one enterprise multiple and wanted the truth, EV/FCF would be it; EV/EBITDA wins on popularity only because EBITDA is easier to compute and compare.

## The consistency rule: why P/EBITDA is a category error

We now arrive at the single law that organizes everything above, and the most common way intelligent people produce nonsense with multiples. **The numerator and denominator of a multiple must belong to the same claimholders.** An *equity* numerator (price, market cap — which is what is left for shareholders *after* lenders are paid) must sit over an *after-financing* denominator (net income, FCFE, book equity — also after lenders). An *enterprise* numerator (EV — the price of the whole firm, debt and equity) must sit over a *pre-financing* denominator (EBITDA, EBIT, sales, FCFF — also belonging to the whole firm). You can pair price with earnings (both equity) or EV with EBITDA (both whole-firm). You can **never** mix them.

![The consistency rule pairs an equity numerator with an after-financing denominator and an enterprise numerator with a pre-financing one](/imgs/blogs/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg-2.png)

The figure above lays out the two families and the forbidden mix. The top row is the equity family: the numerator owns the equity slice only, so it pairs with after-financing denominators (net income, book equity, EPS) to give P/E, P/B, P/S, P/FCFE. The middle row is the enterprise family: the numerator owns the whole firm, so it pairs with pre-financing denominators (EBITDA, EBIT, sales, FCFF) to give EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT, EV/Sales. The bottom row, in red, is the error: putting an equity price over a whole-firm cash number — **P/EBITDA** — which compares the shareholders' slice of the price against the cash that belongs to shareholders *and lenders together*. It is internally incoherent.

#### Worked example: why P/EBITDA produces a nonsense answer

Take two companies, identical in every operating respect — same revenue, same EBITDA of **\$4.2 billion**, same everything — except how they are financed. **Northwind** has \$8 billion of debt; call the other firm **Debtwind** with \$20 billion of debt. Both have an enterprise value of \$35 billion, so both trade at EV/EBITDA = \$35b ÷ \$4.2b = **8.3x** — correctly identical, because they are the same operating business. Now compute P/EBITDA. Northwind's market cap is \$30 billion (EV \$35b − net debt \$5b), so P/EBITDA = \$30b ÷ \$4.2b = **7.1x**. Debtwind's market cap is only \$35b − \$17b net debt = \$18 billion, so its P/EBITDA = \$18b ÷ \$4.2b = **4.3x**. P/EBITDA says Debtwind is far "cheaper" (4.3x vs 7.1x) — but the two businesses are *operationally identical*; the only difference is Debtwind carries more debt. The ratio has been corrupted by leverage it pretends to ignore: it divides an *after-debt* numerator by a *before-debt* denominator, so more debt mechanically shrinks the numerator while leaving the denominator untouched, manufacturing fake cheapness. *P/EBITDA breaks because it compares the shareholders' price against the whole firm's cash, so it confuses being heavily indebted with being cheap.*

This is not a pedantic technicality — it is the most consequential single rule in relative valuation, because the mistake is so easy to make and so confidently wrong. Whenever you see or build a multiple, run the check: *is the top belonging to the same people as the bottom?* If the numerator is a price (equity) and the denominator is computed before interest (whole firm), stop — you have mixed the families, and your "cheap" is an artifact of leverage.

## The multiple zoo at a glance: what each one suits

We have now met the whole menagerie. Before going deeper into *why* multiples are what they are, it helps to see them side by side — each scaling price to a different fundamental, each with a home where it shines and a situation where it breaks.

![Each multiple scales price to a different fundamental so each suits a different kind of company and breaks in a different situation](/imgs/blogs/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg-3.png)

The figure above is the field guide. **P/E** scales price to net earnings; it suits stable, profitable firms and breaks when earnings are negative or lumpy. **EV/EBITDA** scales the whole-firm price to operating cash; it suits capital-intensive, levered firms (where capital structure differences would distort P/E) and breaks because the heavy capex it ignores is real. **P/B** scales price to book equity; it suits banks, insurers, and asset-heavy firms whose book value is honest, and breaks for asset-light or intangible-rich firms whose real value is off the balance sheet. **P/S** scales price to revenue; it suits early, unprofitable growth firms with no meaningful profit yet, and breaks when the companies being compared have very different margins. **PEG** scales the P/E itself to growth; it lets you compare growers at a glance and breaks when the growth it divides by is fragile or unsustainable. The lesson the table teaches is that *there is no universal multiple* — you pick the one whose denominator is meaningful for the business in front of you, and you cross-check it against the others.

A professional never looks at one multiple in isolation. A bank is valued primarily on P/B but checked against P/E; an unprofitable software company is valued on EV/Sales but watched for the day it can be valued on EV/EBIT; an industrial is valued on EV/EBITDA but cross-examined with EV/FCF to expose the capex EBITDA hides. The multiples triangulate, and where they disagree is where the interesting questions live. A stock that looks cheap on P/E but expensive on EV/EBITDA is telling you something about its balance sheet; one that looks cheap on P/S but the earnings never come is telling you its margins are broken.

## What drives a multiple: a P/E is justified by growth, returns, and risk

Here is the conceptual leap that separates someone who *uses* multiples from someone who merely *quotes* them. A multiple is not a free-floating opinion about whether a stock is cheap. **A multiple is justified — it is set by the underlying economics of the business, and you can say exactly which economics.** A P/E is high *for a reason*, and the reasons are three: **growth, returns on capital, and risk.** A business that will grow its earnings fast, earns high returns on the capital it deploys, and faces low risk *deserves* a high multiple — and paying a high multiple for it can be a bargain. A business that will shrink, earns poor returns, and faces high risk deserves a low multiple — and even a low one can be too expensive.

![A justified P/E rises with faster growth and higher returns on capital and falls with higher risk](/imgs/blogs/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg-4.png)

The figure above traces the three drivers. **Growth**: faster earnings growth means more future cash to discount, which raises the justified multiple — but only if the growth is *durable*, because growth that evaporates was never worth paying for. **Returns on capital (ROIC)**: a business that earns high returns on the capital it reinvests can grow while consuming little cash, so more of each dollar of earnings becomes free cash for owners — and a firm whose ROIC exceeds its cost of capital (WACC) *creates value* as it grows, which justifies a premium multiple. **Risk**: lower risk means a lower discount rate, and since a multiple is mathematically `1 / (r − g)` scaled by payout, a smaller `r` shrinks the denominator and raises every multiple. These are not three separate stories — they are the three inputs of a discounted cash flow, which is the secret we unpack in the final section. For now, internalize the claim: *a high multiple is the market's statement that a business has some combination of high growth, high returns, and low risk; whether that statement is right is the analyst's whole job.*

This reframing dissolves the beginner's instinct that "low multiple = cheap, high multiple = expensive." The correct instinct is: *a multiple is cheap or expensive only relative to what the business's growth, returns, and risk justify.* A 30x P/E is cheap if the business deserves 40x; a 6x P/E is expensive if the business deserves 4x. The number alone tells you nothing about cheapness — it tells you what you are paying, and you must separately work out what you *should* pay.

### The same multiple can hide completely different quality

The cleanest way to feel this is to take two companies at the *identical* P/E and show they are worth completely different amounts. The multiple looks the same; the businesses behind it are not.

![Two companies at the same twenty times earnings deserve different prices once you reveal the return on capital behind each](/imgs/blogs/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg-5.png)

The figure above shows two firms, both trading at **20x earnings**. On the left, **Lowgrade Co** earns a return on invested capital (ROIC) of 8%, just equal to its cost of capital — so when it reinvests earnings to grow, it must pour in lots of capital and creates no value doing so, leaving little free cash for owners. On the right, **Highgrade Co** earns an ROIC of 25%, far above its cost of capital — so it can grow while reinvesting only a small fraction of earnings, and most of each dollar of earnings becomes free cash that owners actually receive. Same 20x P/E, but Highgrade is worth dramatically more, because the *quality* of its earnings — how much of each dollar reaches owners as free cash — is far higher. The multiple was silent about this; the ROIC reveals it.

#### Worked example: two firms at 20x, different ROIC

Both **Lowgrade** and **Highgrade** earn \$1.00 of EPS and trade at 20x, so both have a \$20 share price. Both want to grow earnings at 6% a year. The difference is ROIC. To grow earnings 6%, a company must reinvest a fraction of its earnings equal to growth ÷ ROIC (the *reinvestment rate*). Lowgrade's ROIC is 8%, so it must reinvest 6% ÷ 8% = **75%** of earnings to fund 6% growth, leaving only 25% — about **\$0.25** per share — as free cash for owners. Highgrade's ROIC is 25%, so it reinvests only 6% ÷ 25% = **24%** of earnings, leaving **76%** — about **\$0.76** per share — as free cash. For the same \$1.00 of earnings and the same 6% growth, Highgrade hands its owners three times the cash. If we value each as free cash growing at 6%, discounted at 9%, Lowgrade is worth \$0.25 ÷ (0.09 − 0.06) ≈ **\$8.30** and Highgrade is worth \$0.76 ÷ (0.09 − 0.06) ≈ **\$25.30**. At the same 20x P/E (\$20 price), Lowgrade is *overpriced* and Highgrade is a *bargain*. *Two identical-looking multiples can hide a threefold difference in real value, because the multiple says nothing about how much of each earnings dollar survives the reinvestment needed to grow.*

This is the single most important idea in the whole post, so let it land: **the multiple is the price; the quality of the business is what justifies the price; and a high-ROIC compounder genuinely deserves a higher multiple than a capital-hungry peer.** It is exactly why disciplined investors like those described in [Warren Buffett's owner mindset](/blog/trading/finance/warren-buffett-berkshire-value-investing) happily pay "high" multiples for great businesses and avoid "cheap" ones with poor returns. The multiple without the ROIC is half a sentence.

## Why a "cheap" multiple is often a trap and a "rich" one a bargain

The everyday error — buy low multiples, avoid high ones — has a name when it goes wrong: the **value trap**. A value trap is a stock that looks cheap on a multiple and stays cheap, or gets cheaper, because the business is deteriorating faster than the price. The multiple was low *for a reason*, and the reason was bad.

![A six times earnings multiple on a shrinking business can be dearer than a thirty times multiple on a durable compounder](/imgs/blogs/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg-6.png)

The figure above contrasts the two cases. On the left, the **cheap trap**: a business at 6x earnings whose earnings are *falling* 10% a year. The 6x looks like a screaming bargain, but the E it is computed on is melting — next year's earnings are lower, so the *price* you are really paying relative to future profit is much higher than 6x, and the business may be worth nothing if the decline does not stop. On the right, the **rich bargain**: a business at 30x earnings whose earnings are *growing* 20% a year on a 30% ROIC. The 30x looks expensive, but the E is compounding upward, so in a few years today's price will be a small multiple of the much larger future earnings — and because the high ROIC means the growth is cheap to fund, that growth translates into real free cash. The cheap business destroys value; the rich one creates it.

#### Worked example: the 6x value trap vs the 30x compounder

**Meltco** trades at **6x** trailing earnings of \$1.00 EPS, so \$6.00 a share. But its earnings are declining 10% a year: next year \$0.90, then \$0.81, then \$0.73. The price you paid (\$6.00) against next year's earnings is \$6.00 ÷ \$0.90 = 6.7x; against year-three earnings it is \$6.00 ÷ \$0.73 = 8.2x — the multiple *rises* as the business shrinks, the opposite of cheap. Worse, if the decline never stops, the stream of falling earnings is worth something like \$1.00 ÷ (0.09 − (−0.10)) = \$1.00 ÷ 0.19 ≈ **\$5.30** — *below* the \$6.00 price. The "cheap" 6x stock is actually overvalued. Now **Compoundco** at **30x** earnings of \$1.00, so \$30.00 a share, growing 20% a year at 30% ROIC. With ROIC of 30%, funding 20% growth costs only 20% ÷ 30% = 67% of earnings, leaving 33% — \$0.33 — as free cash, growing at 20%. Even valued conservatively (say the high growth fades after a decade to a steady state), the present value of that rising free-cash stream comfortably exceeds \$30. The "expensive" 30x is the better buy. *A multiple computed on a shrinking earnings base is a mirage of cheapness, and one computed on a compounding base can be a bargain even when the number looks rich — the trajectory of the denominator matters more than its current ratio to price.*

The practical lesson is to always ask *why* a multiple is low or high before acting on it. A low P/E driven by a temporary, fixable problem (a cyclical trough, a one-off charge) is a genuine opportunity. A low P/E driven by structural decline — a melting business, a dying industry, a broken moat — is a trap. The number is identical; the future is opposite. This is why screening for "low P/E stocks" mechanically is a losing strategy: it systematically over-weights value traps, because the cheapest-looking stocks are disproportionately the ones the market has correctly marked down. The full anatomy of these traps, including the accounting tricks that make a doomed business look cheap, runs through [quality of earnings](/blog/trading/equity-research/quality-of-earnings-accruals-one-offs-red-flags) and the fraud case studies in [Enron](/blog/trading/finance/enron-2001-accounting-fraud).

## A multiple is a compressed DCF: decoding the hidden assumptions

We have been circling the deepest truth about multiples, and now we state it plainly: **a multiple is a discounted cash flow with its assumptions hidden inside.** A P/E is not a separate, simpler kind of valuation that competes with the DCF — it *is* a DCF, compressed into a single number, with the growth rate and discount rate folded away out of sight. When you understand this, multiples stop being magic numbers and become readable: you can decode any multiple back into the growth-and-risk assumptions it secretly contains.

![A bare twenty-five times earnings multiple decodes into a discounted cash flow with payout growth and discount rate inside](/imgs/blogs/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg-7.png)

The figure above performs the decoding. On the left is what you *see*: a bare 25x P/E — a price of \$50 over \$2 of EPS, one opaque number whose assumptions are hidden, not absent. On the right is what it *encodes*. Start from the simplest valuation of a growing stream of dividends (the Gordon growth model): the value of a share equals next year's dividend divided by (discount rate minus growth rate), `P = D / (r − g)`. Divide both sides by earnings `E` and you get the justified P/E directly: `P/E = (D/E) / (r − g) = payout / (r − g)`, where *payout* is the fraction of earnings paid out, *r* is the discount rate (risk), and *g* is the growth rate. So a 25x P/E with a 75% payout decodes as 0.75 ÷ (r − g) = 25, which means r − g = 0.03 — for instance a 9% discount rate and 6% growth. **The multiple was a DCF all along.** The "25x" is shorthand for "I believe this business grows around 6% forever, I demand a 9% return, and it pays out three-quarters of earnings."

#### Worked example: decoding a P/E and a PEG

Northwind trades at a **15x** trailing P/E. What growth is the market pricing in? Using the justified-multiple formula with a 75% payout and a 9% discount rate: 15 = 0.75 ÷ (0.09 − g), so 0.09 − g = 0.75 ÷ 15 = 0.05, which gives **g = 4%**. The 15x P/E is the market saying "Northwind grows about 4% a year forever." Now a peer trades at **25x**. Same payout and discount rate: 25 = 0.75 ÷ (0.09 − g), so 0.09 − g = 0.03, giving **g = 6%**. The peer's richer 25x is justified *if and only if* it grows 6% rather than 4%. The multiple difference (15x vs 25x) is not arbitrary — it is a precise bet on a 2-percentage-point difference in perpetual growth. If you believe the peer will actually grow 8%, its justified multiple is 0.75 ÷ (0.09 − 0.08) = **75x**, so 25x is *cheap*; if you think it will only manage 4% like Northwind, its justified multiple is 15x and 25x is *expensive*. *Decoding a multiple turns "is 25x too much?" into the answerable question "do I believe this business grows 6% forever?" — which is the question you should have been asking all along.*

This decoding is the antidote to every multiples mistake in this post. The value trap? It is a multiple that *looks* low but, once you plug in the negative growth, decodes into a value below the price. The same-P/E-different-quality puzzle? It is two multiples with the same headline but different hidden ROIC and payout. The high-multiple compounder? It is a multiple whose hidden growth justifies it. The reason a high-ROIC business deserves a higher multiple drops straight out: high ROIC means high payout for a given growth (less reinvestment needed), and a higher payout in the numerator of `payout / (r − g)` lifts the justified multiple directly. Once you can decode a multiple into `payout / (r − g)`, you are no longer at the mercy of the number — you are reading the assumptions behind it, which is exactly what the DCF makes explicit and the multiple hides. (For the full machine the multiple compresses, see [building a DCF, part 1](/blog/trading/equity-research/building-a-dcf-part-1-forecasting) and [part 2 on the cost of capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/building-a-dcf-part-2-cost-of-capital-wacc-capm).)

## PEG: P/E adjusted for growth, and how it is abused

The most popular attempt to fix the P/E's blindness to growth is the **PEG ratio** — the price-to-earnings ratio divided by the earnings growth rate (in percentage points). The idea is intuitive: a high P/E is fine if the company grows fast, so divide the P/E by the growth to get a "growth-adjusted" multiple. A rule of thumb, popularized by Peter Lynch, says a PEG of **1.0** is fair, below 1.0 is cheap, and above 1.0 is expensive.

#### Worked example: PEG for a 15x P/E growing 15%

A company trades at a **15x** P/E and is growing earnings **15%** a year. Its PEG is 15 ÷ 15 = **1.0** — fairly valued by the rule of thumb. Now compare two companies: **FastCo** at a 30x P/E growing 30% has a PEG of 30 ÷ 30 = **1.0**, while **SlowCo** at a 12x P/E growing 6% has a PEG of 12 ÷ 6 = **2.0**. By PEG, the 30x FastCo is *cheaper* than the 12x SlowCo — the opposite of what the raw P/Es suggest — because FastCo's high multiple is more than paid for by its faster growth. *PEG's whole purpose is to stop you from rejecting a high-multiple stock that earns its multiple with growth, and from buying a low-multiple stock whose growth does not justify even that.*

PEG is a useful *first-glance filter*, and that is exactly the right way to hold it — as a quick screen, not a precise valuation. Its abuses are several and worth naming. First, **it ignores risk entirely**: two companies growing 20% can deserve very different multiples if one is a stable software franchise and the other a speculative biotech, but PEG treats them identically. Second, **it ignores returns on capital**: growth funded at high ROIC is far more valuable than growth funded at low ROIC (as we saw), but PEG counts both growth rates the same. Third, and most dangerously, **it assumes the growth is real and durable** — and growth is the single most over-forecast quantity in all of finance. A PEG of 0.5 that looks like a screaming bargain is only a bargain if the 40% growth it is built on actually materializes for years; growth that fades makes the "cheap" PEG a trap. Fourth, **the math is crude**: dividing a multiple by a growth rate has no rigorous theoretical basis (unlike `payout / (r − g)`, which does), so PEG is a heuristic that approximates the right answer in the middle of the range and misleads at the extremes — a PEG built on 50% growth or 2% growth is nearly meaningless. Use PEG to *flag* candidates worth a closer look, never to *conclude* a valuation.

## Common misconceptions

**"A low P/E means a stock is cheap."** No — a low P/E means you are paying few dollars per dollar of *current* earnings, which is only cheap if those earnings will persist or grow. A low P/E on a declining business is a value trap: the earnings melt, and what looked like 6x becomes 8x and then infinite. The multiple measures price per dollar of profit; it says nothing about whether the profit survives. Cheapness requires that the future justify the multiple, which the number alone cannot tell you.

**"A high multiple means a stock is expensive."** Equally false. A high multiple is the market's statement that the business has high growth, high returns, or low risk — and if that statement is true, the high multiple can be a bargain. The best-performing stocks of the last several decades spent almost their entire run looking "expensive" on trailing multiples, because their earnings kept compounding into the rich price. Refusing to ever pay above 15x P/E would have excluded most of the great compounders. Expensiveness, like cheapness, is relative to what the economics justify.

**"You can compare any two companies' P/Es."** Only if they have similar growth, returns, and risk. A 4%-growing utility and a 25%-growing software firm will always have different P/Es, and the difference is *correct*, not an arbitrage. Comparing raw multiples across businesses with different economics is the most common amateur mistake — the whole craft of relative valuation is *adjusting* for the differences, never slapping one company's multiple onto another. A multiple is comparable only within a genuinely comparable peer set.

**"EV/EBITDA is the truest measure of a business's value."** It is the most *popular*, not the truest. EBITDA adds back depreciation, which for capital-intensive businesses represents very real cash that must be spent to keep the business alive. EV/EBITDA systematically flatters capex-heavy companies and should always be cross-checked with EV/EBIT (which keeps depreciation) or EV/FCF (which subtracts actual capex). Buffett's quip — "does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?" — is aimed precisely at people who treat EBITDA as cash.

**"P/EBITDA is fine, it's just an equity version of EV/EBITDA."** It is a category error. EBITDA belongs to the whole firm (it is computed before interest), so it must be paired with an enterprise-value numerator. Pairing it with equity price means a more-indebted company looks artificially cheaper, because debt shrinks the equity numerator while leaving the whole-firm EBITDA untouched. The consistency rule — equity numerator over after-financing denominator, EV numerator over pre-financing denominator — is not optional, and P/EBITDA violates it.

**"PEG below 1 is always a buy."** PEG ignores risk, returns on capital, and the durability of growth, and its math is a crude heuristic that breaks at high and low growth rates. A PEG of 0.5 built on optimistic 40% growth forecasts that never materialize is a trap, not a bargain. PEG is a screen to *flag* candidates, not a verdict — the moment you act on it you must check whether the growth is real, how risky it is, and what returns fund it.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The same business, two multiples, two different stories — banks vs software.** Walk through a brokerage screen and you will see banks trading at single-digit P/Es and around 1–2x book value, while software companies trade at 30–50x earnings (when they have earnings at all) and book value is nearly irrelevant. This is not an inefficiency to arbitrage — it is the multiples correctly reflecting different economics. Banks are valued on **P/B** because their assets are financial and their book value is honest; their low P/Es reflect modest growth and the risk that loan losses can wipe out years of earnings. Software firms are valued on **revenue and forward earnings** because their value is intangible (book value is meaningless after years of buybacks and R&D expensing), and their high multiples reflect high growth, asset-light models with huge ROIC, and recurring revenue. The lesson: *the right multiple is chosen by the business's economics, and the same headline number means completely different things in different industries.*

**The EV/EBITDA flattery in telecom and cable.** Telecom and cable companies are perennially quoted at "cheap" EV/EBITDA multiples — 6x, 7x — that draw in investors hunting bargains. But these businesses spend enormous sums every year on network equipment, fiber, and spectrum just to stand still, and that capex is exactly what EBITDA adds back. Look instead at **EV/FCF** (after capex) and the "cheap" 7x EV/EBITDA can become a much richer 20x+ on free cash, because so much of the EBITDA never reaches owners — it is recycled straight back into the network. Investors who bought "cheap" capital-intensive businesses on EV/EBITDA and ignored the capex have learned this the hard way for decades. The multiple was flattering the business by hiding its reinvestment burden.

**The dot-com bubble and price-to-sales.** In 1999–2000, profitless internet companies had no earnings, negative cash flow, and trivial book value, so they could only be valued on **price-to-sales** — and they traded at 20x, 30x, even 50x revenue. Relative valuation, applied diligently, confirmed each new internet stock was "reasonably priced" *relative to its peers* — all of which were detached from any defensible link between revenue and eventual profit. P/S was the only available multiple, and it has the deepest blind spot (it ignores profitability entirely), so it was uniquely unsuited to flag the danger. The episode is the canonical lesson that a multiple can be computed precisely and still be precisely wrong when its denominator (revenue) has no reliable path to the thing that actually has value (cash).

**The value-trap graveyard: newspapers, malls, and legacy retail.** Through the 2010s, screens for "low P/E" and "low P/B" stocks were dominated by newspapers, shopping-mall operators, and brick-and-mortar retailers — businesses trading at 5–8x earnings and below book value that looked like deep bargains. Most were value traps: their earnings and book values were being structurally eroded by the internet, and the cheap multiples were the market correctly pricing a melting future. Investors who bought the low multiples mechanically, without asking *why* they were low, bought a decade of decline. The handful of genuine bargains in that group were the ones where the cheapness reflected a *temporary*, fixable problem rather than structural decline — and telling those apart required understanding the business, not the multiple. This is the practical face of "a cheap multiple is often a trap."

**The compounders that always looked expensive.** Conversely, the great consumer-staples and software compounders of the last few decades almost never traded below 20–25x earnings, and the disciplined "never pay more than 15x" investor missed nearly all of them. Their high multiples were *justified* — high ROIC, durable growth, low risk — and decoded back through `payout / (r − g)`, the rich multiples encoded perfectly reasonable assumptions about growth and quality. Paying 30x for a business compounding earnings at 15% on a 30% ROIC was, repeatedly, one of the best trades available, while paying 6x for a melting business was one of the worst. This is the real-market proof of the post's thesis: *the multiple is the price, the economics justify the price, and the number alone tells you nothing about which is the bargain.*

## When this matters and further reading

Multiples are the working language of valuation, and now you can speak it without being fooled by it. The discipline reduces to a handful of reflexes. When you see a multiple, first ask *which family* — is it equity (price over an after-financing number) or enterprise (EV over a pre-financing number) — and check the consistency rule before you trust it. Then ask *which version* of the denominator — trailing, forward, or normalized — because the same "P/E" names three different numbers. Then, crucially, refuse to read the number as a verdict on cheapness: decode it into the growth, returns, and risk it implies, and ask whether you believe those implied assumptions. A multiple is a compressed DCF, and your job is to decompress it and judge the assumptions inside.

The highest-leverage habits are these: never compare multiples across businesses with different economics without adjusting; always pair EV/EBITDA with EV/EBIT or EV/FCF to expose hidden capex; always ask *why* a multiple is low before buying it (temporary problem or structural decline?); and treat PEG as a screen, never a conclusion. Above all, internalize that a high-quality, high-ROIC, durably growing business genuinely deserves a high multiple — and that the cheapest-looking stocks are disproportionately the ones the market has correctly given up on.
