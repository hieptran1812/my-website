> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A high return on capital only makes a business a great investment if the business can *keep deploying* capital at that return; the length of that reinvestment runway, alongside the spread it earns, is what really sets intrinsic value.
>
> - **A wonderful business with nowhere to reinvest returns cash but does not compound.** Two firms can earn the identical 20% on capital and be worth wildly different amounts because one has fifteen years of runway and the other has three.
> - **TAM (total addressable market) is the most abused number in investing.** The honest question is never the giant top-down number but the *serviceable obtainable market* — the slice you can actually reach and win, profitably, against real competitors.
> - **Bottom-up beats top-down.** Units × realistic price × realistic penetration is an honest estimate; "we'll get 1% of a \$500 billion market" is a red flag, not a forecast.
> - **Penetration follows an S-curve and growth rates fade with size** (the law of large numbers) — a 30% grower adding \$30M on a small base must add \$3B on a large one to hold the rate, which it eventually cannot.
> - **The skill is sizing the real, reachable, profitable runway and being honest about its limits** — distinguishing a genuine long-runway compounder from a high-return cash cow, and a real adjacency from a fantasy one.

There is a particular kind of mistake that destroys more money in growth investing than any accounting fraud ever has, and it does not look like a mistake at all. It looks like optimism. An analyst finds a business earning a spectacular return on its capital — 25%, 30%, more — protected by a real competitive advantage, run by capable people. Everything in the financials says *great business*. And then, asked how big it can get, the analyst reaches for a number that was never meant to be reached: the total addressable market. "The global market for this is \$400 billion," the slide reads, "and the company has barely 1% of it, so the runway is enormous." The stock gets priced as if that \$400 billion were a finish line the company is walking toward, and years later, when revenue stalls out at a small fraction of it, everyone is baffled. The business was real. The return was real. The runway was a fiction.

This post is about the two halves of that mistake and how to avoid both. The first half is *the runway* — the idea that what makes a high-return business valuable is not the return alone but its ability to keep *deploying* capital at that return, year after year, for many years. A business that earns 30% on its capital but has nowhere left to invest is a cash machine, not a compounder; it should hand the cash back to owners. A business that earns a more modest return but can pour ever-larger sums into that return for two decades can compound into something enormous. The runway, not just the return, is the engine of long-run value.

The second half is *honest market sizing* — the discipline of figuring out how long and how wide that runway actually is, without fooling yourself. This is where TAM comes in, and where most analysis goes wrong. The total addressable market is a useful concept badly abused: inflated, computed top-down, and almost never the market a company can actually serve at a profit. The skill is to size the *real, reachable, profitable* opportunity — the part of the runway the company can genuinely traverse — and to be ruthlessly honest about where it ends.

![A chart comparing two rising value curves over years of reinvestment, a steep green compounder curve labeled long runway reaching about nine times capital and a flat blue cash-cow curve labeled short runway leveling off near one point seven times capital, both starting from the same point](/imgs/blogs/growth-runways-and-tam-without-fooling-yourself-1.png)

The figure above is the whole thesis in one picture. Two businesses start at the same place and earn the *identical* 20% return on every dollar they reinvest. The only difference is how long they can keep finding places to put that money. The compounder, with a long runway, keeps reinvesting at 20% for fifteen years and grows to roughly nine times its starting capital. The cash cow, with a short runway, reinvests for three years, runs out of attractive projects, and then sensibly returns its cash to owners — ending up worth a fraction as much in compounded terms. Same return on capital, profoundly different outcomes, and the difference is entirely the runway. Hold this image in mind for the rest of the post; everything else is the working-out of it.

## Foundations: the building blocks of a runway

Before we can talk about runways and markets, we need a handful of ideas defined precisely. Several of them have full posts of their own in this series, so here we will define them tightly enough to do the arithmetic and point to the deep treatments. None of this requires any finance background — we build each idea from zero.

**Invested capital** is the pool of money tied up in running the business — what owners have put in (equity) plus what lenders have lent (interest-bearing debt), minus any idle cash not actually deployed in operations. If you have poured \$1,000M into factories, inventory, receivables, and product development, that \$1,000M is your invested capital: the base on which you expect to earn a return.

**ROIC — return on invested capital** — is the percentage that pool earns each year. It is after-tax operating profit (NOPAT) divided by invested capital. A company earning \$200M of NOPAT on \$1,000M of capital has a 20% ROIC: twenty cents of profit per year for every dollar tied up in it. ROIC is the single best one-number summary of business *quality*, because it answers the question that actually matters — when this company invests a dollar, how many cents does it get back? The full mechanics live in the [returns-on-capital post](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa).

**WACC — the weighted average cost of capital** — is what that capital *costs*. Lenders charge interest; shareholders demand a return for the risk they bear. Blend the two, weighted by how much of each the company uses, and you get WACC, the minimum return the business must earn just to keep its capital providers whole. If WACC is 10%, then ten cents of every dollar of capital is owed each year before the business has created a cent of value for anyone.

**The spread** is ROIC minus WACC — the excess return, the value created per dollar of capital per year. A 20% ROIC against a 10% WACC is a +10-point spread: every dollar of capital throws off ten cents of pure economic profit, value created above and beyond what the money costs. The spread is the heart of the matter, and the [ROIC–WACC spread post](/blog/trading/equity-research/roic-wacc-spread-the-engine-of-intrinsic-value) is its dedicated deep dive. For our purposes, the spread is *the rate at which a reinvested dollar creates value*.

**Reinvestment** is the act of putting capital back into the business to grow it — building capacity, funding working capital, developing products, making acquisitions. A company that reinvests grows; a company that pays everything out as dividends does not. And here is the connection that ties this whole post together: **the value a business creates is the spread, earned on the capital it reinvests, for as long as it can keep doing both.** Three things, multiplied: how wide the spread, how much capital can be reinvested at it, and for how many years. The first has its own post. This post is about the second and third — the *runway*.

**The runway**, then, is our central term. The **reinvestment runway** is the amount of capital a company can deploy at an attractive return, multiplied by the number of years it can keep doing so before the opportunities run out or the spread fades. A long runway means the company can keep reinvesting large sums at a high return for a long time; a short runway means it will soon run out of attractive places to put money. A high-ROIC business with a long runway is the rarest and most valuable thing in investing. A high-ROIC business with a short runway is merely a good cash generator.

**TAM, SAM, and SOM** are the three nested ways people size the *market* a runway runs through — total addressable, serviceable addressable, serviceable obtainable. We will define each precisely in its own section, because the gaps between them are exactly where honest sizing happens. For now, hold the rough shape: TAM is everything, SOM is the realistic sliver you can actually win, and the runway is the distance between where the company is today and that realistic sliver.

With those pieces in hand, we can build the argument. We start with *why the runway matters at all*, because it is not obvious — and getting it wrong is the most expensive error in growth investing.

## Why the runway matters: a great return needs somewhere to go

Here is the counterintuitive core of the whole subject, and it trips up nearly everyone the first time. A high return on capital, by itself, does not make a business a great long-term investment. It makes it a *good* business. To be a *great investment*, the business must be able to keep *deploying* capital at that high return — and that is a completely separate question from how high the return is.

Think about why. Suppose a business earns a magnificent 30% on its capital, but it is a single restaurant on a single corner. It throws off cash beautifully, but there is nowhere to put that cash back at 30% — you cannot build a second restaurant on the same corner, and the next corner over earns an ordinary return. So the cash comes out as profit and sits there, or gets paid to owners, or worse, gets reinvested at a *lower* return into something the owner does not understand. The 30% return is real, but it applies only to the original small pile of capital. The business is a wonderful cash cow. It is not a compounder.

Now suppose a different business earns a more modest 18% on its capital, but it operates in a vast, growing market where it can keep opening new locations, entering new geographies, and adding new products — every one of them earning roughly 18%. Year after year, it takes the cash it generates and reinvests it at 18%, and the base on which it earns 18% keeps growing. After two decades, the small original business has become a large one, and the whole time, every dollar was earning 18% above a 10% cost. This second business compounds. It is worth far more than the first, despite the *lower* return, because its runway is long and the first business's runway is short.

This is the distinction the cover figure draws, and it is worth stating as a principle: **a high ROIC tells you how good each reinvested dollar is; the runway tells you how many dollars you can reinvest, for how long.** Value is the product of the two. A great return on a tiny, finite base is a small amount of value. A good return on a base that keeps growing for twenty years is a fortune. The runway is the multiplier, and it is the thing the market most often mis-estimates — usually by assuming a short-runway cash cow is a long-runway compounder, and paying compounder prices for cash-cow economics.

The mechanism by which a long runway creates value is *compounding*, and compounding is the most powerful and least intuitive force in finance. When a business reinvests its profits at a high return, next year's profits are larger, so it can reinvest more, so the year after's profits are larger still. The capital base grows geometrically, and the value compounds. But compounding only works if there is somewhere to reinvest. The moment the runway ends — the moment the company runs out of projects that earn above its cost of capital — compounding stops, and the business should return its cash rather than force it into low-return projects. (Forcing it is a classic value-destroyer, and it is the subject of the [capital-allocation post](/blog/trading/equity-research/capital-allocation-the-ceos-most-important-job): a great business can quietly become a mediocre one when its runway runs out and management refuses to admit it.)

#### Worked example: same return, short runway versus long runway

Let us anchor this immediately in dollars, using a fictional company we will return to throughout the post: **Northwind**, which comes in two flavors. Both start with \$1,000M of invested capital, both earn a 20% ROIC, and both face a 10% WACC — a +10-point spread. The only difference is the runway.

**Northwind Cash Cow** has a three-year runway. For three years it reinvests 60% of its NOPAT (growing 12% a year — recall the reinvestment rate is g ÷ ROIC = 12% ÷ 20% = 60%), then runs out of attractive projects and pays everything out. Its capital grows from \$1,000M to about \$1,400M over three years and then stops. From year four on, it is a static \$1,400M business earning 20%, paying out all \$280M of NOPAT as dividends. It is a fine business — but it has stopped compounding.

**Northwind Compounder** has a fifteen-year runway. It reinvests 60% of NOPAT at the same 20% ROIC, growing 12% a year, for fifteen years. Its capital compounds: \$1,000M → \$1,120M → \$1,254M → … and after fifteen years it has grown to roughly \$1,000M × 1.12¹⁵ ≈ \$5,470M — more than five times the starting capital, every dollar of it earning the same +10-point spread. The economic profit it throws off in year fifteen alone (10% × \$5,470M ≈ \$547M) is larger than the cash cow's *entire* capital base.

The two businesses had the identical return on capital and the identical spread. The compounder is worth several times the cash cow purely because it could keep reinvesting at 20% for fifteen years instead of three.

*A high return tells you each dollar is good; only a long runway lets you keep finding more dollars to make good — and value is the product of the two.*

## TAM, SAM, SOM: the three circles, and where the lying happens

To size a runway, you have to size the market it runs through, and the standard vocabulary for that is three nested circles: TAM, SAM, and SOM. They are simple to define and constantly abused, and learning to read the *gaps* between them is most of the skill.

![A vertical nested stack of four shrinking boxes, the largest labeled TAM total addressable market eighty billion dollars, then SAM serviceable addressable market eighteen billion, then SOM serviceable obtainable market three billion, then a small box for today's revenue of four hundred million, illustrating how each ring is a fraction of the one above](/imgs/blogs/growth-runways-and-tam-without-fooling-yourself-2.png)

**TAM — total addressable market** — is the total annual revenue available if *one* company served *every* customer who has the problem, everywhere, with no competition and no practical limits. It is the biggest, roundest, most quotable number, and it is almost never the right one to reason about, because no company ever gets it all. In the figure, Northwind's TAM is \$80B — every dollar spent on the problem its product solves, worldwide.

**SAM — serviceable addressable market** — narrows TAM to the slice a company's actual product, business model, and geography can *reach*. If Northwind sells to mid-sized manufacturers in North America and Europe, the SAM excludes the giant enterprises it cannot serve, the small shops that cannot afford it, and the regions it does not operate in. The figure shows a SAM of \$18B — less than a quarter of the TAM. The gap between TAM and SAM is the part of the "huge market" that the company's product and model simply do not address.

**SOM — serviceable obtainable market** — narrows SAM further to the share a company can *realistically win*, against real competitors, over a realistic time horizon. Even within its serviceable market, Northwind competes with three established rivals and a dozen smaller ones; it will not win the whole thing. The figure shows a SOM of \$3B — the realistic ceiling on Northwind's revenue, perhaps a 15–20% share of its serviceable market. The gap between SAM and SOM is the part competition will keep from you.

And then there is **today's revenue**: \$0.4B. The *runway* is the distance between today's \$0.4B and the realistic \$3B ceiling — a roughly 7× opportunity, not the 200× the TAM would suggest. That distinction is the entire point. A naive analyst sizes the runway as TAM ÷ today's revenue (\$80B ÷ \$0.4B = 200×) and concludes the company can grow 200-fold. An honest analyst sizes it as SOM ÷ today's revenue (\$3B ÷ \$0.4B ≈ 7×) and concludes the company can perhaps grow seven-fold before it bumps into the realistic ceiling. The two analysts are looking at the same company and the same slides; one of them has fooled himself with the biggest circle.

The reason the gaps matter so much is that *value lives in the SOM, not the TAM*. The runway you can actually traverse is bounded by the obtainable market, because that is the most revenue the company can ever realistically earn. Pricing a stock off the TAM is like pricing a marathon runner off the distance to the moon: technically a distance, utterly irrelevant to how far they will actually run.

#### Worked example: pricing the runway off the right circle

Suppose the market is valuing Northwind at \$12B. Northwind earns \$0.4B of revenue today at a 20% net margin (\$80M of earnings), and the question is whether \$12B is reasonable. The bullish slide says: "TAM is \$80B; at a 20% margin that's \$16B of potential earnings; the company is worth a tiny fraction of that, so it's cheap." This is the TAM trap in its purest form — it implicitly assumes Northwind captures the *entire* \$80B market, which no company in history has done.

Size it honestly off the SOM instead. The realistic obtainable market is \$3B of revenue. At a 20% net margin, that is \$600M of earnings *at full maturity* — years from now, after the company has traversed its entire runway and won its realistic share. Put a mature earnings multiple of, say, 18× on that \$600M and you get \$10.8B of value *at maturity*, which must then be discounted back to today for the years it takes to get there. Discounting \$10.8B back ten years at a 10% cost of capital gives roughly \$4.2B in today's terms. Against that honest figure, the market's \$12B price looks *expensive*, not cheap — it is paying nearly three times the present value of even the fully-traversed realistic runway.

The TAM framing said "cheap"; the SOM framing said "expensive." Both used the same company. The difference is which circle you sized the runway against.

*The total addressable market flatters every story; only the serviceable obtainable market tells you how far the company can actually run, and value is built on the SOM.*

## Top-down versus bottom-up: the honest way to build a TAM

There are two ways to arrive at a market-size number, and the difference between them is the difference between analysis and wishful thinking. They go by *top-down* and *bottom-up*, and the rule is simple: bottom-up is honest, top-down is a red flag.

![A before and after comparison with two columns, the left labeled top-down inflated showing a five hundred billion dollar global market times an assumed one percent equals five billion with no check on buyers price or rivals, the right labeled bottom-up honest showing forty thousand target firms times thirty thousand dollars per year times twenty-five percent reachable penetration equals three hundred million, sixteen times smaller](/imgs/blogs/growth-runways-and-tam-without-fooling-yourself-3.png)

**Top-down** starts with a giant industry number and claims a slice of it. "The global software market is \$500 billion; we only need to capture 1% to be a \$5 billion company." It sounds modest — *only* 1%! — and that modesty is precisely the trick. The 1% is plucked from the air; there is no mechanism behind it, no check on who actually buys, at what price, or against which competitors. The giant starting number does all the persuasive work, and the "1%" is a fig leaf over the absence of any real reasoning. Whenever you hear "we only need a small percentage of a huge market," your hand should move toward your wallet, because that sentence is the single most reliable marker of an unexamined market size in all of investing.

**Bottom-up** builds the number from the ground up, out of things you can actually count and defend: the number of potential customers who genuinely have the problem, the realistic price each will pay, and the realistic share you can win. In the figure, the bottom-up build for the same company is: ~40,000 target firms that actually have the problem and could buy, times \$30,000 per year in realistic contract value, times a 25% reachable penetration (10,000 firms won) = \$0.3B of serviceable revenue. That is *sixteen times smaller* than the top-down \$5B — and it is the honest number, because every input is something you can investigate, challenge, and revise.

The discipline of bottom-up is that it forces you to confront reality at every step. How many firms *really* have this problem badly enough to pay for a solution? (Not "every company in the world" — the ones with the specific pain, the budget, and the authority to buy.) What will they *really* pay, once a competitor is offering an alternative at a lower price? What share can you *really* win against incumbents with existing relationships? Each question shrinks the number toward honesty. Top-down asks none of them; it just multiplies a big number by a small fraction and calls it a forecast.

This connects directly to forecasting a [discounted cash flow](/blog/trading/equity-research/building-a-dcf-part-1-forecasting). A DCF's revenue forecast is, in the end, a bottom-up runway estimate: how many customers, at what price, growing at what penetration rate, toward what realistic ceiling. A forecast built bottom-up has *defensible joints* — you can stress each assumption and see what it does to the value. A forecast built top-down (just grow revenue 30% a year for ten years because "the market is huge") has no joints to stress; it is a single act of faith dressed up as arithmetic. The most dangerous DCFs in the world are the ones whose growth assumptions trace back, when you dig, to a top-down TAM.

#### Worked example: a bottom-up TAM versus a top-down one

Let us build both for a fictional software company, **Acme Workflow**, which sells project-management software to engineering teams, so the contrast is concrete.

**Top-down:** "The global enterprise-software market is \$500B and growing. We only need 1% to be a \$5B revenue company." The number on the slide is **\$5.0B**, and there is nothing behind it but the \$500B and the casually chosen 1%.

**Bottom-up:** Start with who actually buys. There are roughly 40,000 companies worldwide with engineering teams large enough to need (and pay for) dedicated workflow software — that is the realistic universe, not "every company." Each such customer would pay, realistically, about \$30,000 a year for the product (a defensible figure based on team sizes and competitor pricing). If Acme can realistically win 25% of that universe over its runway — 10,000 customers, a strong but not fantastical share against entrenched rivals — then:

$$\text{Bottom-up serviceable revenue} = 10{,}000 \text{ firms} \times \$30{,}000 = \$300\text{M}$$

The bottom-up number is **\$0.3B** — about one-sixteenth of the top-down \$5B. And critically, every input is now something you can argue about: maybe the universe is 60,000 firms, maybe the price is \$40,000, maybe the share is 30%. Flex all three optimistically and you might get to \$0.7B; flex them pessimistically and you might get to \$0.15B. *That range* — \$0.15B to \$0.7B — is the honest runway, and it sits an order of magnitude below the top-down fantasy.

*A bottom-up market built from countable customers, real prices, and realistic share is something you can argue about and improve; a top-down "1% of a huge number" is something you can only believe or disbelieve.*

## Penetration curves and the S-curve of adoption

Even a correctly-sized SOM is not reached in a straight line. Markets get penetrated along an **S-curve**: slow at first, then steep, then flattening as the market saturates. Assuming a straight line to full penetration — a constant growth rate marching steadily to the ceiling — is one of the most common and most expensive sizing errors, because it both overstates the early growth and ignores the inevitable slowdown at the end.

![An S-curve chart with penetration as a percent of the serviceable market on the vertical axis and time on the horizontal axis, the curve rising slowly in an early phase labeled slow and unproven, steepening through a growth phase labeled the real runway, then flattening into a saturation phase labeled ceiling reached runway gone](/imgs/blogs/growth-runways-and-tam-without-fooling-yourself-4.png)

The figure shows the three phases. In the **early phase**, adoption is slow: the product is unproven, customers are cautious, the company is still figuring out its go-to-market. Growth in absolute terms is small even if the percentage growth looks high (because the base is tiny). In the **growth phase**, adoption accelerates — the product has proven itself, word spreads, the sales engine works, and penetration climbs steeply. *This is the real runway*, the period when the company adds the most value, and it is where compounding does its work. Then comes the **saturation phase**: most of the reachable customers have been won, each new customer is harder to find than the last, and the curve flattens toward its ceiling. The runway is, by definition, used up at the ceiling.

The S-curve matters for sizing because *where on the curve a company sits changes everything about its forward growth*. A company in the early phase has its whole growth phase ahead of it — the runway is long. A company well into the growth phase has explosive growth now but a flattening ahead. A company in the saturation phase has a short runway no matter how high its current growth *rate* looks, because the rate is about to collapse as it bumps into the ceiling. Mistaking a late-growth-phase company for an early one — extrapolating the steep middle of the S-curve as if it continues forever — is precisely how analysts overpay for businesses about to decelerate.

The ceiling itself deserves emphasis. The S-curve does not flatten at 100% of the TAM, or even 100% of the SAM. It flattens at the *realistic maximum penetration* — the SOM expressed as a share of the serviceable market. Some markets saturate at 60% penetration (a dominant player), many at 20–30% (a fragmented market with several competitors), some at single digits (a product only a minority ever adopts). Picking the ceiling honestly is half the battle; assuming the curve climbs to 100% is the lazy default that inflates every runway.

#### Worked example: the S-curve and the cost of assuming a straight line

Return to Acme Workflow, whose honest SOM we sized at \$0.3B (10,000 customers at \$30,000). Suppose Acme has 2,000 customers today (\$60M of revenue) and is growing customer count 40% a year. The naive straight-line forecast says: "40% growth, so in three years we'll have 2,000 × 1.4³ ≈ 5,500 customers, and in six years ≈ 15,000."

But 15,000 customers *exceeds* the entire 10,000-customer SOM — the straight line marches the company straight through its own ceiling, which is impossible. The S-curve tells the truthful story instead. Acme is currently in its growth phase, adding customers fast, but as it climbs from 2,000 toward 10,000 the growth *rate* must fall, because each year there are fewer un-won customers left to win:

- **Years 1–2 (growth phase):** customer count grows ~40%, then ~35%, reaching perhaps 3,700 customers (\$111M).
- **Years 3–4 (mid S-curve):** growth slows to ~25%, then ~18%, reaching perhaps 5,500 (\$165M).
- **Years 5–7 (approaching saturation):** growth fades to ~12%, ~8%, ~5%, reaching perhaps 8,000 (\$240M) and crawling toward the 10,000 ceiling.

The straight-line model put Acme at 15,000 customers in six years; the S-curve puts it at perhaps 8,000, with growth visibly decelerating toward the \$0.3B ceiling. An analyst who used the straight line overstated Acme's six-year revenue by nearly double and, worse, completely missed the deceleration that the market will eventually price in — the moment when "40% grower" becomes "12% grower" and the multiple compresses hard.

*Penetration is an S-curve, so a company's growth rate is highest before it has proven anything and lowest just when its absolute revenue is largest — extrapolating the steep middle is how you forecast straight through a ceiling that does not move.*

## Market growth versus share gains: two engines, two durabilities

A company's revenue can grow for exactly two reasons, and they are not equally durable, so an honest sizing keeps them separate. Revenue growth comes either from the **overall market growing** (the whole pie getting bigger, lifting everyone) or from the company **taking share** (getting a bigger slice of the pie at competitors' expense). Real growth is usually a blend, and the blend tells you how sustainable it is.

**Market growth** is, in a sense, the easy kind: if the market grows 8% a year and you hold your share, you grow 8% with no special effort. But it has a ceiling that is not yours to control — the market grows as fast as it grows, and when it matures, your market-driven growth matures with it. Market growth is durable while the market is young and fades as the market ages. It is also shared with every competitor, so it confers no advantage; everyone in a fast-growing market grows, which is exactly why a rising tide can make mediocre businesses look like good ones for a while.

**Share gains** are the harder, more revealing kind. Taking share means you are growing *faster than the market* by winning customers away from rivals — which only a genuinely better product, lower price, or stronger distribution can sustainably do. Share gains are evidence of a real competitive advantage (the kind the [economic-moats post](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage) is about), but they have their own ceiling: you cannot take more than 100% of the market, and in practice share gains slow sharply as you approach a dominant position, both because there is less left to take and because rivals fight hardest for their remaining base.

The reason to separate them is durability. A company growing 25% a year in a market growing 20% is mostly riding the market — it is taking a little share, but most of its growth will vanish the moment the market matures. A company growing 25% a year in a market growing 5% is taking *enormous* share — it is winning, and its growth is far more impressive and (if the advantage is real) more durable, because it does not depend on the market staying young. Two companies with the identical 25% revenue growth can have completely different futures depending on which engine is driving it.

#### Worked example: decomposing 25% growth into market and share

Two fictional companies, **Northwind Pumps** and **Acme Workflow**, both grew revenue 25% last year. Identical headline growth — but decompose it.

**Northwind Pumps** operates in the industrial-pump market, which grew 22% last year (a cyclical boom in factory construction). Northwind's share of that market actually *fell* slightly, from 12.0% to 11.8%. So nearly all of Northwind's 25% growth — about 22 points of it — was the market lifting everyone, and the remaining ~3 points were... actually *negative* share, masked by the market boom. When the construction cycle turns and the market growth drops to 3%, Northwind's growth will collapse to low single digits or worse. Its 25% was almost entirely borrowed from the market.

**Acme Workflow** operates in workflow software, a market that grew only 6% last year. Acme grew 25%. So Acme's share rose sharply — roughly 18 points of its growth came from *taking share* from rivals, and only ~6 points from the market. Acme is winning. Its growth does not depend on the market staying hot; it depends on Acme's product staying better, which (if the advantage is real) it can sustain. When the market growth stays at 6%, Acme can still grow 20%+ for years by continuing to take share, until it approaches a dominant position.

The headline 25% was identical. Northwind's was a market loan due to be repaid at the next downturn; Acme's was earned share that reflects a real edge. An analyst who did not decompose the two would price them the same and be badly wrong about both.

*Identical revenue growth can be a fragile loan from a booming market or a durable gain of share from rivals — the two have opposite futures, so an honest sizing always asks which engine is running.*

## The reinvestment runway: capital × spread × years

We can now make the runway precise. The value a long runway creates is, in essence, the product of three numbers: how much **capital** the company can deploy at a high return, the **spread** that capital earns over its cost, and the number of **years** the company can keep doing both before the opportunities run out or the spread fades. Change any one of the three and the whole value moves.

![A vertical tree breaking the reinvestment runway into three branches, how much capital five billion dollars deployable, at what spread twenty percent ROIC minus ten percent WACC equals ten points, and for how many years about ten years, each branching into a detail node, with the spread branch reaching five hundred million dollars of economic profit per year and the years branch reaching roughly three billion dollars of value created](/imgs/blogs/growth-runways-and-tam-without-fooling-yourself-5.png)

The figure decomposes it. The first branch, **how much capital?**, is bounded by the runway we have spent the whole post sizing: the company can only deploy capital profitably up to the point where its serviceable obtainable market is filled. It is also bounded mechanically by the reinvestment rate (g ÷ ROIC), which sets how much of its own profit the company must plough back to grow. The second branch, **at what spread?**, is the ROIC–WACC gap — the rate at which each reinvested dollar creates value. The third branch, **for how many years?**, is the durability of both the opportunity (the runway's length) and the spread (the moat's strength). Multiply capital deployed × spread × years (with discounting) and you get the value the runway creates.

The figure puts concrete numbers on it: a company that can deploy \$5B of capital at a 10-point spread (20% ROIC, 10% WACC) earns \$500M of economic profit per year, and sustained for roughly ten years and discounted, that runway is worth on the order of \$3B of value created above and beyond the capital itself. Notice that all three inputs are *honest, sizeable* numbers — the \$5B comes from the SOM analysis, the 10-point spread from a real ROIC and WACC, the ten years from a judgment about durability. None of them is a top-down fantasy. That is the whole discipline: build the runway value from defensible inputs, not from the size of the biggest circle.

The most important and most overlooked of the three is the *capital* branch — specifically, the question of whether the company can actually *deploy* the capital. A wide spread and a long durability are worthless if there is nowhere to put the money. This is the cash-cow trap from the opening: a business can have a magnificent spread and a strong moat and *still* be a mediocre compounder if its market is small and it has nowhere to reinvest. When that happens, the right move is to stop reinvesting and return the cash — a discipline explored in the [dividends-vs-buybacks post](/blog/trading/equity-research/dividends-vs-buybacks-returning-cash). Forcing capital into a runway that has ended, chasing growth that earns below the cost of capital, is one of the most reliable ways management destroys value while looking busy.

#### Worked example: what a reinvestment runway is worth

Make the figure's runway concrete with Northwind. Northwind can deploy a total of \$5B of additional capital over its runway (this is the gap between today's capital and what filling its SOM would require), at a 20% ROIC against a 10% WACC — a +10-point spread — and judgment says the spread and the opportunity both hold for about ten years before competition and saturation close them.

The economic profit the runway throws off, once the full \$5B is deployed, is:

$$\text{Economic profit per year} = \text{spread} \times \text{capital} = 10\% \times \$5{,}000\text{M} = \$500\text{M}$$

That \$500M a year, earned for roughly ten years and then fading, discounted back at the 10% cost of capital, is worth — very roughly, for intuition — on the order of \$3B of value *created above and beyond the \$5B of capital itself*. (The capital, earning exactly its cost, is worth its \$5B; the *spread* on top is the \$3B premium.) So the runway is worth about \$3B of value creation.

Now watch what happens when you change one input. Cut the durability from ten years to three — the same \$500M a year but for a third as long — and the runway's value collapses to roughly \$1B, because most of the value was in the later years. Cut the deployable capital from \$5B to \$1B (a much smaller market) and the annual economic profit falls to \$100M and the runway's value to roughly \$0.6B, no matter how wide the spread. Each of the three inputs is a lever, and a great-looking spread cannot save a runway that is too short or too small.

*The value of a runway is capital times spread times years, discounted — so a wonderful spread on a tiny base or for a few years is worth little, and the size and length of the runway matter as much as its quality.*

## The law of large numbers: why growth rates fade

There is an iron law that bends every long runway downward eventually, and ignoring it is the most common way growth forecasts go wrong. It is the **law of large numbers** as it applies to business: *a company's growth rate must fade as its revenue base grows*, because holding a given percentage growth requires adding an ever-larger absolute amount of revenue, and at some point the absolute amount becomes impossible.

![A chart with annual growth rate as a percent on the vertical axis and revenue base in billions of dollars on the horizontal axis, a curve descending from a high growth rate at a small base toward low single digits at a large base, with markers showing a small base needs thirty million dollars of new revenue for thirty percent growth, a one billion base needs three hundred million, and a ten billion base needs three billion, ending in a red zone where the rate fades to GDP-like single digits](/imgs/blogs/growth-runways-and-tam-without-fooling-yourself-6.png)

The figure shows the fade. The same 30% growth rate requires wildly different absolute dollars depending on the base. On a \$0.1B base, 30% growth means adding \$30M of new revenue in a year — quite achievable for a small, fast-moving company. On a \$1B base, the same 30% means adding \$300M — much harder. On a \$10B base, 30% means adding \$3B of new revenue *every single year* — and \$3B a year is an enormous absolute amount, more than the entire revenue of most companies. At some size, the absolute dollars required to maintain the percentage simply exceed what any market can provide, and the growth rate *must* come down, fading toward the growth rate of the broader economy (GDP-like single digits) for the very largest firms. This is not a failure of management; it is arithmetic.

The law of large numbers is why no company grows 30% forever, and why the most dangerous assumption in any growth valuation is a high growth rate extrapolated for too many years. It is also why a small company's high growth rate is genuinely more sustainable than a large company's identical rate: the small company is adding small absolute amounts that the market can easily provide, while the large company is fighting against the sheer size of the numbers. When you see a \$50B-revenue company guiding to 25% growth, the law of large numbers says: that requires \$12.5B of new revenue next year, which is a colossal absolute amount, and it will fade — the only question is how fast.

This connects directly to the S-curve and to the runway. The fade in growth rate is the mathematical shadow of the runway running out: as the company climbs the S-curve and fills its SOM, both the *percentage* growth fades (law of large numbers) and the *opportunity* shrinks (saturation). A reverse DCF (see the [reverse-DCF post](/blog/trading/equity-research/reverse-dcf-and-sensitivity-analysis)) makes this brutally concrete — it extracts the growth rate and duration the current price implies, and you can then ask whether the law of large numbers will allow it. Surprisingly often, the price implies a large company sustaining a high growth rate for a decade, which would require it to add more absolute revenue than its entire market contains. That is the law of large numbers quietly telling you the price is wrong.

#### Worked example: a 30% grower meeting the law of large numbers

Take a fictional fast-grower, **Acme Workflow** in its prime, growing revenue 30% a year. Start it at \$0.1B and run it forward, watching the absolute dollars.

- **Year 0:** \$0.1B revenue. To grow 30%, it must add \$30M next year. Easy.
- **Year 5:** revenue has compounded at 30% to about \$0.37B. To grow 30%, it must add \$111M. Harder, but doable.
- **Year 9:** revenue is about \$1.06B. To grow 30%, it must add \$318M of new revenue in a single year — roughly the company's *entire* revenue from four years earlier. Now it is genuinely difficult.
- **Year 13:** if it somehow held 30%, revenue would be about \$3.0B, requiring \$900M of new revenue a year.
- **Year 17:** revenue would be \$8.6B, requiring \$2.6B of new revenue a year — almost certainly more than its serviceable market can provide.

In reality the growth rate fades long before year 17. Perhaps Acme holds 30% for five years, then decelerates to 20%, then 12%, then settles into high single digits as it fills its SOM — exactly the S-curve. An analyst who modeled a flat 30% for fifteen years would project Acme to about \$5.1B of revenue; the realistic fading path lands it closer to \$2.0B. The difference — more than 2.5× — is entirely the law of large numbers, and it is the single most common reason aggressive growth valuations turn out to be too high.

*A growth rate is a percentage, but growth is paid for in absolute dollars, and as the base swells the dollars required to hold the rate eventually exceed what any market can supply — so every high growth rate is on a clock.*

## TAM expansion: real adjacencies versus fantasy ones

So far we have treated the market as fixed, but the best companies *expand* their addressable market over time — entering new geographies, launching new products, moving into adjacent categories. A real TAM expansion can extend a runway that would otherwise have ended, turning a one-trick business into a serial compounder. But TAM expansion is also where the most seductive fantasies live, because "we'll expand into adjacent markets" is an infinitely flexible justification for any growth assumption. The skill is telling a *real* adjacency from a fantasy one.

A **real** adjacency is one where the company's existing competitive advantage *transfers*. If a company has a powerful brand, a loyal customer base, a distribution network, a data asset, or a technology platform, and the new market lets it *reuse* that advantage, then the expansion is real: the company arrives in the new market already strong, with a genuine edge over the incumbents there. The classic example is a company that sells one product to a set of customers, then sells those same customers a second product — the customer relationship is the transferable asset, and the second product rides on it. The adjacency is real because the advantage came along for the ride.

A **fantasy** adjacency is one where the company's advantage *does not transfer* — where it is entering a new market as a stranger, competing against entrenched players who have their own moats, with none of its existing edge applying. "We're a great payments company, so we'll expand into lending" sounds like an adjacency, but lending is a different business with different risks, different competitors, and different required skills; the payments brand may help a little, but the payments *advantage* mostly does not transfer. Many "TAM expansion" stories are really a strong company wandering into markets where it is just another competitor — burning the cash its core business generates on adventures that earn below the cost of capital. The fantasy adjacency is how a great business *destroys* the value its core business creates.

The test, then, is always the same question: *does the company's existing advantage transfer to the new market, or is it starting from scratch?* If the moat comes along, the adjacency extends the runway and is worth paying for. If the moat stays behind, the "expansion" is a new venture with its own (probably worse) economics, and it should be valued — skeptically — on its own merits, not bolted onto the core as if it were free runway. Reading management's track record on this is part of [reading management incentives](/blog/trading/equity-research/reading-management-incentives-and-skin-in-the-game): some teams expand only where their edge transfers and compound brilliantly; others chase every shiny adjacency and bleed.

#### Worked example: a real adjacency and a fantasy one

Northwind, our industrial-pump maker, is considering two expansions. Both are pitched as "TAM expansion." Evaluate each by whether the advantage transfers.

**Real adjacency — pump maintenance and parts.** Northwind already sells pumps to 5,000 industrial customers, and it has a trusted brand, a service network, and deep relationships with those customers' plant engineers. It now offers *maintenance contracts and replacement parts* to those same 5,000 customers. The advantage transfers completely: same customers, same relationships, same brand, same service network. The new market adds, say, \$1.5B to the serviceable runway, and Northwind arrives with a real edge — it knows the installed base better than anyone. At a 20% ROIC (the maintenance business is even higher-return than the pumps, being capital-light), this adjacency is *real runway*, worth adding to the valuation. The cross-sell rides on the existing moat.

**Fantasy adjacency — industrial software.** Northwind also proposes to build a software platform for managing entire factory operations — a \$10B market, the slide says. But Northwind has no software competency, no relationships with the IT buyers who purchase such platforms, and faces entrenched enterprise-software incumbents with their own switching-cost moats. Its pump brand means almost nothing to a CIO choosing a factory-operations platform. The advantage does not transfer; Northwind would be a stranger in a tough market, likely earning *below* its cost of capital while incumbents defend. This "expansion" is a fantasy adjacency — a \$10B TAM that is, for Northwind, mostly unreachable and unprofitable. Bolting it onto the valuation as free runway would be exactly the TAM trap that opened this post.

The two expansions look similar on a slide ("entering a large adjacent market"). One extends the runway; the other burns the cash the runway generates. The only reliable difference is whether the moat comes along.

*A TAM expansion is real only when the company's existing advantage transfers to the new market; when the advantage stays behind, the "expansion" is a new venture with a stranger's economics, and treating it as free runway is how great businesses fund their own value destruction.*

## Reconciling the runway with the DCF: growth duration and the fade

Everything in this post feeds, in the end, into a valuation — most directly a [discounted cash flow](/blog/trading/equity-research/building-a-dcf-part-1-forecasting). The runway is not an abstraction floating beside the DCF; it *is* the DCF's growth assumption, made honest. Understanding how the two connect is what keeps a model from quietly baking in a fantasy.

A DCF forecasts cash flows year by year and discounts them. The growth rate in those forecasts, and crucially *how many years the high growth lasts*, is the runway expressed as a model input. A long runway means high growth for many years (a long, high-growth explicit forecast); a short runway means high growth fades fast toward a terminal rate. The single most consequential thing in most growth DCFs is not the peak growth rate but the **growth duration** — how many years before the fade sets in — because, exactly as in the [ROIC–WACC spread](/blog/trading/equity-research/roic-wacc-spread-the-engine-of-intrinsic-value), the value compounds with the *number of years* the advantage and the runway last.

The honest way to set the growth path is to let it fade — to model the S-curve and the law of large numbers directly into the forecast. Start at the current high growth rate, then step it down year by year toward the long-run rate of the economy as the company fills its SOM and the base swells. This produces a *concave* revenue path that bends toward maturity, not the straight exponential line that a constant growth rate produces. The fade is not pessimism; it is the empirical reality that high growth rates regress toward the mean, that markets saturate, and that the law of large numbers is undefeated. A model that does *not* fade the growth — that holds 25% for ten years and then drops to a 3% terminal rate in a single step — is the most common modeling error in growth investing, because it implicitly assumes a runway with no S-curve and no law of large numbers, which is to say no reality.

The [terminal value](/blog/trading/equity-research/terminal-value-the-part-that-dominates) is where the runway's end gets priced, and it is the most dangerous part of the model precisely because it assumes the company has reached a stable, mature state at the end of the runway. If the explicit forecast did not fade the growth honestly — if it marched the company to the terminal year still growing fast — then the terminal value is computed off an inflated base, and the whole valuation is too high. The runway and the terminal value are two ends of the same rope: the explicit forecast traverses the runway, and the terminal value captures the cash-cow steady state *after* the runway has ended. Get the runway's length wrong and both ends break.

#### Worked example: fading the growth versus a constant rate

Value Acme Workflow two ways, starting from \$60M of revenue, to see what the fade does. Use a 10% discount rate and value the next ten years of revenue growth (ignoring margins and terminal value for the intuition).

**Constant-rate model (no fade):** hold 30% revenue growth for all ten years. Revenue reaches \$60M × 1.30¹⁰ ≈ \$827M in year ten. The model implies Acme blows through its \$300M SOM by year six and keeps going — an impossibility — but the spreadsheet does not know that, and it produces a large, confident, *wrong* number.

**Faded model (honest):** step the growth down as Acme climbs its S-curve and meets the law of large numbers: 30%, 28%, 25%, 21%, 17%, 13%, 10%, 8%, 6%, 5%. Revenue reaches about \$60M compounding through that fading path to roughly \$285M in year ten — landing right at the \$300M SOM ceiling, exactly as the runway analysis says it should. The faded model produces a year-ten revenue about *one-third* of the constant-rate model's, because it respects the runway, the S-curve, and the law of large numbers.

The constant-rate model said \$827M; the faded model said \$285M. The faded number is honest because it traces back to a real SOM, a real S-curve, and the iron law of large numbers. The constant-rate number traces back to nothing but the convenience of typing one growth rate down a row. The difference between them is the difference between a runway sized honestly and a runway imagined.

*A DCF's growth path is just the runway in spreadsheet form, so an honest model fades the growth down the S-curve toward the SOM ceiling rather than extrapolating the steep middle — the fade is where the runway's real length gets respected.*

## Honest sizing: the compounder versus the cash cow

We can now draw the master distinction the whole post has been building toward: the difference between a **long-runway compounder** and a **short-runway cash cow**, and why the runway, more than the return, decides which compounds more wealth.

A **cash cow** is a high-return business with a short runway. It earns a wonderful ROIC on its existing capital, but its market is largely filled or small, so it has few attractive places to reinvest. The right thing for a cash cow to do is *return its cash* — pay dividends, buy back stock — because forcing the cash into low-return projects would destroy value. A well-run cash cow is a fine investment at the right price; it just does not *compound*. Its value is roughly its current earnings power capitalized at a fair multiple, with little growth premium, because there is no long runway to grow into.

A **compounder** is a business — sometimes with a *lower* current ROIC than the cash cow — that has a long runway: a large, growing serviceable market it can keep reinvesting into at an attractive return for many years. The compounder retains its cash and ploughs it back, and because it does so at a spread, every reinvested dollar creates value, and the base on which it earns the spread keeps growing. The compounder's value is dominated by the *growth premium* — the present value of all that future reinvestment at a spread — which is exactly why compounders command high multiples and why the market pays up so dearly for a *durable, long runway*.

The counterintuitive punchline, which the cover figure drew, is that a long-runway compounder with a *modest* spread can compound more wealth than a short-runway cash cow with a *wide* spread. Because compounding is geometric and runway-length enters as an exponent, a modest spread reinvested for twenty years can dwarf a wide spread reinvested for three. This is why the great long-term fortunes in equity markets have so often come not from the highest-return businesses but from the longest-runway ones — businesses that found a way to keep reinvesting at a good (not necessarily spectacular) return for decades. The discipline of honest sizing is, in the end, the discipline of telling these two animals apart: not "how high is the return?" but "how long and how wide is the runway, and can the company actually reinvest down it?"

#### Worked example: the cash cow versus the compounder, head to head

Two businesses, each starting with \$1,000M of capital and a 10% WACC. Which compounds more wealth over twenty years?

**Wide-spread cash cow:** a 25% ROIC — a +15-point spread, magnificent — but a short runway. It can reinvest at 25% for only three years (growing fast briefly), then its small market is filled and it returns all its cash. Its capital grows from \$1,000M to about \$1,250M over three years (reinvesting to grow ~8% a year) and then stays flat, throwing off ~\$310M of NOPAT a year forever. The total value created above capital is the three years of compounding plus the perpetual cash stream — a fine business, but the value-creation premium over its capital is modest because it stopped reinvesting at a spread after year three.

**Modest-spread compounder:** an 18% ROIC — a +8-point spread, good but not spectacular — and a *long* runway. It reinvests 55% of NOPAT at 18% (growing ~10% a year) for twenty years. Its capital compounds from \$1,000M at 10% a year to \$1,000M × 1.10²⁰ ≈ \$6,730M — nearly *seven times* the starting capital — and every one of those dollars earned the +8-point spread the whole way. The economic profit in year twenty alone is 8% × \$6,730M ≈ \$538M, on a base that the reinvestment itself built.

The cash cow had a *far wider spread* (15 points versus 8) but the compounder created far more total value, because it reinvested at its spread for twenty years instead of three. The runway, entering as an exponent, overwhelmed the higher per-dollar return. This is the single most important lesson in growth investing, and it is why the length of the runway is, with the spread, the true determinant of intrinsic value.

*A long runway at a good return beats a short runway at a great one, because compounding makes the number of years an exponent and the spread merely a base — which is why honest investors hunt for durable runways even more than for high current returns.*

## Common misconceptions

**"A bigger TAM means a bigger opportunity."** Almost never the way the bigness is used. The TAM is the largest, least relevant circle; the opportunity lives in the SOM — the slice the company can actually win, profitably, against real competitors. A company can sit inside a \$500B TAM and have a \$2B realistic runway; the \$500B tells you nothing about how far the company will actually run. Whenever a thesis leans on the size of the TAM, ask for the bottom-up SOM, and watch the opportunity shrink to its honest size.

**"We only need 1% of a huge market."** This is the single most reliable red flag in all of investing. The "1%" is plucked from the air; the giant number does all the persuading; and there is no mechanism — no count of real customers, no realistic price, no defensible share — behind it. Honest sizing is always bottom-up (units × price × penetration), built from things you can investigate and challenge. The "small percentage of a huge market" sentence is the absence of analysis wearing the costume of modesty.

**"A high return on capital makes a great investment."** A high return makes a good *business*; a great *investment* also needs a long runway. A 30% ROIC on a tiny, filled market is a cash cow that should return its cash, not a compounder. The return tells you how good each reinvested dollar is; the runway tells you how many dollars you can reinvest, for how long. Value is the product of the two, and the runway is the part the market most often gets wrong — usually by paying compounder prices for cash-cow economics.

**"This grower will keep growing at this rate."** No company grows fast forever; the law of large numbers is undefeated. Holding a high percentage growth requires adding ever-larger absolute dollars, and at some size the dollars exceed what any market can provide, so the rate must fade. Penetration follows an S-curve, not a straight line, so the steep middle decelerates as the company fills its market. Any forecast that extrapolates a high growth rate for a decade is implicitly betting against arithmetic.

**"TAM expansion gives the company endless runway."** Only when the company's existing advantage *transfers* to the new market. A real adjacency — same customers, transferable brand or platform or distribution — extends the runway. A fantasy adjacency — a stranger entering a tough new market where its moat does not apply — is a new venture with a stranger's (usually worse) economics, and bolting it onto the valuation as free runway is how great businesses fund their own value destruction. The test is always: does the moat come along, or does it stay behind?

**"If the market is growing, the company's growth is safe."** Market-driven growth is borrowed from the market and comes due when the market matures; it is shared with every competitor and confers no advantage. Durable growth comes from *taking share* — growing faster than the market by winning customers from rivals, which only a real edge can sustain. Two companies with identical revenue growth can have opposite futures depending on whether the growth is a market loan or an earned share gain; always decompose the two.

## How it shows up in real markets

The runway-versus-return distinction and the TAM trap are not academic; they are the difference between the great compounding stories and the cautionary tales of the last few decades. A handful of patterns recur, and once you see them through this lens they stop being mysterious. (The figures below are illustrative of the patterns, not precise current data for any one company.)

**The long-runway compounders the market adores.** The businesses that have created the most shareholder wealth over decades — dominant payments networks, branded consumer-staples giants, entrenched enterprise-software platforms — share a profile that is not merely "high return on capital" but "high return *with a long runway*." They earned good-to-great ROICs *and* found ways to keep reinvesting at those returns for many years: opening new geographies, cross-selling to the same customers, expanding into real adjacencies where their advantage transferred. The high multiples these businesses command are (usually) the market correctly pricing a wide spread reinvested over a long, durable runway — exactly the compounder profile. Warren Buffett's entire approach, laid out in the [Berkshire study](/blog/trading/finance/warren-buffett-berkshire-value-investing), is essentially the search for businesses that can reinvest at a high return for a long time behind a moat — the runway and the spread, in plain English, decades before "TAM" became a pitch-deck word. Notably, the businesses that compounded most were often *not* the highest-return ones but the ones with the longest runways and the discipline to reinvest only where the return was real.

![A two-by-three grid of the six ways a TAM analysis fools you, counting markets you cannot serve, double-counting overlapping markets, assuming straight-line penetration, ignoring competition for the same pie, fantasy pricing, and assuming the runway never fades, each cell describing the specific error](/imgs/blogs/growth-runways-and-tam-without-fooling-yourself-7.png)

The grid above catalogs the six recurring ways a TAM analysis fools you, and every inflated market size you will ever encounter is one of these moves. **Counting markets you cannot serve** profitably (geographies, segments, or price tiers your product and model never reach). **Double-counting**, where the same dollar of spend appears in two "adjacent" TAMs that are then added as if they did not overlap. **Straight-line penetration**, assuming a linear march to full adoption instead of an S-curve that flattens at a realistic ceiling. **Ignoring competition for the same pie**, treating the whole TAM as yours when it is shared with rivals and your SOM is a slice of it. **Fantasy pricing**, multiplying units by a price the market will not actually pay once a real competitor appears. And **assuming the runway never fades**, treating the spread and the growth as permanent and ignoring saturation and the law of large numbers. When you read a market-size claim, run it against these six — it will be committing at least one.

**The TAM-trap blow-ups.** The other recurring story is the company priced off an enormous TAM that it never came close to capturing. The pattern is always the same: a real business with a real product gets a valuation built on the assumption that it will capture a large share of a giant top-down market, the stock trades at a price that only makes sense if the SOM equals the TAM, and then growth decelerates as the company bumps into its actual serviceable ceiling — far below the TAM — and the multiple collapses. The disappointment is rarely that the business was fake; it is that the *runway* was imagined. The company did fine; it just did fine into a \$3B market that had been priced as a \$300B one. This is the most common way growth investors lose money: not on frauds, but on honest businesses sized dishonestly.

**The cash cow mistaken for a compounder.** A subtler version is the high-return business that the market priced as a compounder when it was really a cash cow with a short runway. It earned a magnificent ROIC, so the market assumed it could reinvest at that ROIC indefinitely and paid a high growth multiple — but its market was small or already filled, so it could not actually deploy more capital at the high return. Management, under pressure to "grow," then forced the cash into low-return adjacencies (the fantasy-adjacency trap), destroying value, while the stock de-rated from a compounder multiple to a cash-cow multiple. The error was sizing the runway off the return rather than off the reachable market. Reading whether management *recognizes* a short runway and returns cash, versus forcing growth, is one of the clearest tells of [capital-allocation skill](/blog/trading/equity-research/capital-allocation-the-ceos-most-important-job).

**The market as a runway-estimating machine.** The cleanest discipline, finally, is to treat the stock price as the market's implied runway and reverse-engineer it. A [reverse DCF](/blog/trading/equity-research/reverse-dcf-and-sensitivity-analysis) extracts the growth rate and duration the current price requires, and you can then test that implied runway against the SOM, the S-curve, and the law of large numbers. Surprisingly often the price implies a runway that the bottom-up market size cannot support — a company that would have to capture more than its entire serviceable market, or sustain a growth rate that the law of large numbers forbids at its scale. When the implied runway exceeds the honest one, the stock is priced for a fantasy; when the implied runway is *shorter* than the honest one (the market mistaking a compounder for a cash cow), there may be a genuine opportunity. Your edge, if you have one, is an honest runway against the market's imagined one.

## When this matters, and further reading

Sizing the runway honestly is the skill that separates durable growth investing from the expensive fantasies that masquerade as it. Every time you are tempted to celebrate a high return on capital, ask the second question — *can the company keep deploying capital at that return, and for how long?* — because a high return with no runway is a cash cow, not a compounder, and the runway is what compounds. Every time a thesis leans on a giant TAM, demand the bottom-up SOM, because the opportunity lives in the smallest circle, not the largest. And every time a forecast extrapolates a high growth rate, hold it up against the S-curve and the law of large numbers, because no growth rate survives a swelling base forever.

The deeper truth underneath all of it is that intrinsic value is the spread times the capital reinvested times the years the runway lasts — and of those three, the runway is the one most often imagined rather than measured. The discipline of this post is to *measure* it: to size the real, reachable, profitable market from the bottom up, to fade the growth down the S-curve toward an honest ceiling, to distinguish a real adjacency from a fantasy one, and to be clear-eyed about the difference between a long-runway compounder and a short-runway cash cow. Do that, and you will avoid the most expensive mistake in growth investing — paying compounder prices for a runway that was never there.
