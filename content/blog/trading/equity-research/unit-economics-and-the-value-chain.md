> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The financial statements show you the whole company added together; unit economics zoom in on a *single repeatable unit* — one customer, one subscription, one store, one ride — to reveal whether the business model actually works before scale either multiplies the profit or multiplies the loss.
>
> - **A unit's economics are its revenue, its variable cost, and the profit that survives.** Price minus variable cost is the **contribution margin** — the cash each unit throws off to cover everything fixed and then become profit. If a unit's contribution margin is negative, no amount of growth saves you.
> - **For businesses that pay to acquire customers, two numbers rule everything: CAC and LTV.** Customer acquisition cost is what you spend up front to win a customer; lifetime value is the total contribution that customer delivers before they leave. The ratio **LTV/CAC above roughly 3×**, with a **payback under ~18 months**, is the rough line between a machine that compounds and one that bleeds.
> - **Growth hides both heaven and hell.** A company with *great* unit economics can look unprofitable on the P&L because it is front-loading acquisition spend on customers who will pay back later — the **J-curve**. A company with *terrible* unit economics can look fine for years because new revenue papers over the losses on old units. The income statement alone cannot tell these two apart; the unit can.
> - **Cohort analysis is the lie detector.** Group customers by when they arrived and watch each group over time. Do older cohorts spend *more* (net revenue retention above 100%) or quietly leak away? The cohort curve reveals retention and expansion that the blended numbers smear into mush.
> - **Where you sit in the value chain decides whether good unit economics are durable.** Profit does not spread evenly across an industry — it pools at the stages with pricing power (design, IP, brand) and gets competed away in the commoditized middle (assembly, distribution). The "smiling curve" is why a chip *designer* earns 60% margins while the *assembler* of the same device earns 3%.

Open the financial statements of a fast-growing company and you will often see something that looks alarming: revenue up 60% year over year, and a fat operating *loss* at the bottom. A naive reader concludes the business is broken. A second reader, who has heard that "growth companies lose money on purpose," concludes it is fine. Both are guessing, because the income statement they are staring at sums *every* customer and *every* year of spending into one number, and that number cannot answer the only question that matters: **does the business make money on each thing it sells, or not?** A company can grow revenue at a blistering pace while every incremental customer it adds destroys value — and a company can post losses for a decade while building one of the most profitable franchises in history. The whole-company P&L cannot distinguish these. The economics of *one unit* can.

Unit economics are the discipline of zooming in. Instead of asking "is the company profitable?", you ask "is *one* of the things this company repeatedly sells profitable, and by how much, and when?" You pick the natural repeatable unit — a subscriber for a software firm, a store for a retailer, a ride for a ride-hailing app, a customer for a bank, a transaction for a payments network — and you build a tiny income statement for that single unit. What does it earn? What does it cost to serve? What did it cost to *win*? How long does it stay? When that one unit makes sense, scale is a money-printing machine: every copy you add carries the same favorable economics. When it doesn't, scale is a faster way to lose money, and the bigger losses are *evidence the model is working as designed*, not evidence it will turn around.

![A side by side figure showing the whole company P and L on the left summed into an operating loss, and a single isolated customer on the right with price, variable cost, contribution and a profitable verdict](/imgs/blogs/unit-economics-and-the-value-chain-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model for the entire post, and it is worth fixing before we define anything. On the left is the whole-company P&L — every customer, every year of spending, summed into a single operating loss of \$24 million. It looks like a failing business. On the right is *one customer* pulled out of that aggregate: they pay \$1,200 a year, cost \$240 to serve, throw off \$960 of contribution, stay four years, and were won for a \$1,500 acquisition cost they repay in under two years. That single unit is *wildly* profitable. The company loses money in aggregate only because it is spending acquisition cost *today* on customers who will pay it back over the *next four years* — the loss is an artifact of growth timing, not of broken economics. The skill this post teaches is reading the right-hand picture out of the left-hand one, and then judging — using the **value chain** — whether those good unit economics are the kind that last.

We will build two recurring fictional companies so the numbers compound instead of arriving disconnected. **Acme Cloud** is a software-as-a-service business that pays sales and marketing money to acquire subscribers — our vehicle for CAC, LTV, churn, cohorts, and the J-curve. **Northwind Industries** is the manufacturer we have followed through the rest of this series — our vehicle for contribution margin, the value chain, and profit pools. By the end you will be able to take a real company, build the economics of one of its units, judge whether growth is creating or destroying value, and locate the business in its industry's chain to decide whether its margins will survive contact with competition. This post is the strategic companion to [profitability and margins](/blog/trading/equity-research/profitability-margins-gross-operating-net), which dissected the *reported* margins of the whole firm; here we go beneath them to the economics of the single unit that produces them, and we close the loop to [returns on capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa) and [economic moats](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage).

## Foundations: first principles of one unit

Before we take unit economics apart, we need a precise vocabulary. Each term below appears constantly in real analysis — especially of consumer, subscription, and platform businesses — and getting them exact now prevents the confusion that wrecks most beginners.

**A unit.** The single repeatable thing a business sells, chosen so that the company is essentially "a large number of these stacked together." For Acme Cloud the unit is *one subscriber*. For a retailer it is *one store* (or sometimes one customer). For a ride-hailing app it is *one ride* or *one rider*. For a bank it is *one account or one loan*. The art is picking the unit at which the economics are stable and additive: if you understand one and you know how many there are, you understand most of the business. The wrong unit (too broad, too narrow) makes the analysis meaningless.

**Revenue per unit.** What one unit brings in over the relevant period — Acme's subscriber pays \$1,200 a year; Northwind's pump sells for \$1,000. Simple, but be careful about the period: a subscription's revenue recurs, a product sale happens once. That difference is the whole reason subscription businesses get analyzed with LTV and product businesses often don't.

**Variable cost per unit.** The cost that genuinely rises with each additional unit served — the hosting and payment-processing and support cost of one more Acme subscriber, the materials and per-unit labour of one more Northwind pump. Its defining property: serve zero units and this cost is zero; serve a million and it is a million times larger. Costs that *don't* move with the unit count (the engineering team that built the software, the headquarters) are **fixed costs** and live outside the unit.

**Contribution margin.** Revenue per unit minus variable cost per unit. This is the single most important number in unit economics. It is the cash each unit *contributes* toward covering the company's fixed costs and, after those are covered, toward profit. Acme's subscriber pays \$1,200 and costs \$240 to serve, so the contribution margin is \$960 — or 80% as a percentage of revenue. Contribution margin is a close cousin of gross margin but not identical: gross margin subtracts *all* accounting cost-of-revenue including some fixed overhead, while contribution margin subtracts only the *truly variable* cost. (We drew this distinction carefully in [the margins post](/blog/trading/equity-research/profitability-margins-gross-operating-net); here we use contribution margin because it is what the *unit* actually throws off.) **A negative contribution margin is a death sentence**: it means each unit you sell loses money before you have spent a dollar on overhead or acquisition, and growth strictly accelerates the loss.

**Customer acquisition cost (CAC).** For any business that *pays* to win customers — through sales reps, advertising, promotions, free trials, referral bonuses — CAC is the total of that spend divided by the number of customers it produced. If Acme spends \$3 million on sales and marketing in a quarter and acquires 2,000 new subscribers, CAC is \$1,500. CAC is paid **once, up front**, before the customer has paid you much of anything — which is exactly why it creates the J-curve. The honest CAC includes *all* the cost of acquisition (salespeople's salaries, ad spend, the cost of free trials that convert), not just the media bill.

**Lifetime value (LTV, sometimes LTC or CLV).** The total contribution margin a customer delivers over their entire relationship with the company, before acquisition cost. If Acme's subscriber contributes \$960 a year and stays an average of four years, the LTV is \$960 × 4 = \$3,840. (Purists discount future years back to present value and subtract ongoing service cost, which we will do; the simple version is the intuition.) LTV is the *prize*; CAC is the *price you paid for it*.

**Churn and retention.** **Churn** is the fraction of customers who leave in a period; **retention** is the fraction who stay (retention = 1 − churn). If 25% of Acme's subscribers cancel each year, annual churn is 25% and retention is 75%. Churn is the hidden engine of LTV, because the average customer lifetime is roughly **1 ÷ churn rate**: at 25% annual churn the average customer stays 1 ÷ 0.25 = 4 years; cut churn to 15% and the average life jumps to 6.7 years — and LTV jumps with it. Small changes in churn produce large changes in value, which is why retention obsesses subscription businesses.

**The LTV/CAC ratio.** Lifetime value divided by acquisition cost — the return on each acquisition dollar. Acme's \$3,840 LTV against its \$1,500 CAC is an LTV/CAC of 2.56× (we will improve it in a moment). The rough rule of thumb in software and consumer subscriptions: **below 1× you are destroying value on every customer** (you pay more to win them than they will ever return); around **3× is healthy**; far above 3× often means you are *under-investing* in growth and could profitably spend more on acquisition. The ratio is a sanity check, not a law — but a business consistently below 1× is, by construction, a machine for converting investor cash into losses.

**CAC payback period.** How long it takes the customer's contribution margin to repay the acquisition cost. Acme's subscriber contributes \$960 a year against a \$1,500 CAC, so payback is \$1,500 ÷ \$960 ≈ 1.56 years, or about **19 months**. Payback matters *alongside* LTV/CAC because it measures *cash risk and speed*: a 3× LTV/CAC with a 6-year payback ties up cash for years and bets heavily on retention you can't yet observe, while the same 3× with a 12-month payback recycles cash fast and de-risks the whole model. Investors love a payback under ~12 months and get nervous past ~24.

**Cohort.** A group of customers defined by *when they arrived* — the "January 2024 cohort" is everyone who signed up that month. Cohorts are the unit of honest measurement, because they let you watch a *fixed* group of customers age without new arrivals diluting the picture. **Net revenue retention (NRR)** is a cohort's revenue this year divided by its revenue a year ago: above 100% means the surviving customers are spending *more* (upgrades, expansion) faster than churn removes others — the holy grail of subscription economics.

**The J-curve.** The shape that a company's cumulative cash flow traces when it has good unit economics but front-loads acquisition spending: cash goes *negative* first (you pay CAC for many customers before any of them pay back), bottoms out, then climbs back above zero and keeps rising as the old cohorts mature into profit. The depth of the J is the cash you must finance; the slope of the recovery is the quality of the unit economics.

One foundational warning before we begin, the mirror of the warning in every post in this series: **a single quarter's P&L cannot reveal unit economics.** Profit or loss at the company level is the *sum* of mature units (paying back) and freshly acquired units (still underwater), and the mix of those two changes every quarter as growth speeds up or slows down. You cannot back out the economics of one unit from the aggregate without doing the cohort work. That is precisely why this analysis exists, and why it so often disagrees with the headline number.

## Choosing the right unit

Before you can build the economics of a unit, you have to *choose* the unit — and this choice quietly determines whether the analysis is illuminating or worthless. The right unit has three properties: it is **repeatable** (the business is essentially many copies of it), it is **stable** (its economics don't change wildly from one copy to the next), and it is **additive** (the company's total economics are roughly the sum of its units). Get the unit right and a single tiny income statement, multiplied by a count, explains the whole firm. Get it wrong and you learn nothing.

Different business models have different natural units. For a **subscription** business the unit is one *subscriber* (or one *account*), because revenue recurs and the analysis is dominated by retention and expansion over a customer's life — exactly the LTV/CAC/cohort machinery. For a **transactional** business — a payments network, a marketplace, an exchange — the unit is often one *transaction* or one *order*, because the customer relationship is loose and what matters is the contribution per transaction times the number of transactions. For a **physical-footprint** retailer or restaurant the unit is one *store*: its build cost (the equivalent of CAC), its annual store-level contribution, and the payback period on the build are the whole game, and "same-store" economics are watched obsessively. For a **lending** business the unit is one *loan* or one *account*, where the "contribution" is net interest income minus credit losses and servicing. The discipline is identical across all of them — revenue per unit, variable cost per unit, cost to originate, and how long the unit lasts — only the names change.

#### Worked example: store-level economics for a retailer

Northwind opens a new branch to sell its pumps direct. Building and fitting the store costs **\$800,000** (this is Northwind's "CAC" for a store — the up-front cost to create the unit). Once open, the store generates **\$2,000,000** of revenue a year at a **20% store-level contribution margin** — \$400,000 a year of contribution after the variable cost of goods and the store's direct operating costs, *before* corporate overhead. The **payback** on the store build is \$800,000 ÷ \$400,000 = **2 years**, and over a 10-year lease the store delivers roughly \$4,000,000 of cumulative contribution against an \$800,000 build — a unit "LTV/CAC" of about **5×**. If those numbers hold, Northwind should open as many stores as it can find good sites for: each one is a 5× unit that pays back in two years. If a new store cohort instead paid back in *six* years because the best sites are gone and new locations underperform, the unit has degraded and further expansion *destroys* value — the same cohort-decay logic as a SaaS business, wearing a hard hat.

*Every business, however physical, has a unit and a payback; a retailer opening stores faces exactly the CAC-LTV-payback arithmetic of a software company acquiring subscribers, and "should we keep expanding?" is the same question as "should we keep acquiring?"*

## Building the economics of one unit

Start with the simplest possible version and add realism in layers. The economics of one unit are a tiny income statement: what it earns, what it costs to serve, and — for businesses that pay to acquire — what it cost to win and how long it lasts.

![A vertical waterfall starting from price per year, subtracting variable cost to reach contribution margin, then showing CAC and the payback period, ending in profit in later years](/imgs/blogs/unit-economics-and-the-value-chain-2.png)

The figure walks Acme Cloud's subscriber down the waterfall. At the top, the subscriber pays **\$1,200 a year**. Subtract the **\$240** of variable cost to serve them — the cloud hosting their data sits on, the payment-processing fee, the marginal support load — and you reach a **contribution margin of \$960 a year**, which is 80% of revenue. That \$960 is what one subscriber throws off *every year they stay*, available first to cover Acme's fixed costs (the engineers, the office) and then to become profit. But Acme paid **\$1,500 of CAC** to win this subscriber, all up front. So in year one the subscriber is a *net loss*: \$960 in, \$1,500 already spent, the unit is \$540 underwater. Only partway through year two does the cumulative \$960-a-year contribution climb past the \$1,500 CAC — the **payback** of about 19 months. From then on, every \$960 is pure profit on this unit.

This is the entire logic of a subscription business in one picture, and it explains the central paradox: **a single subscriber is unprofitable for the first ~19 months and richly profitable forever after.** If Acme is growing fast, *most* of its subscribers at any moment are in their first 19 months — still underwater — so the *blended* P&L shows a loss, even though every subscriber will become profitable. Slow the growth down and stop acquiring, and the same company would suddenly show a fat profit as the existing base matures past payback. The loss is a *growth-timing artifact*. The unit is sound. Reading the unit, not the aggregate, is what tells you so.

#### Worked example: the SaaS unit — LTV, LTV/CAC, and payback

Let's compute Acme's unit economics precisely. The subscriber pays **\$1,200 per year** with an **80% gross/contribution margin**, so contribution is \$1,200 × 0.80 = **\$960 per year**. CAC is **\$1,500**. We need the customer lifetime, which comes from churn: assume **25% annual churn**, so average life ≈ 1 ÷ 0.25 = **4 years**. Then:

- **LTV** (simple, undiscounted) = \$960 × 4 years = **\$3,840**.
- **LTV/CAC** = \$3,840 ÷ \$1,500 = **2.56×** — below the 3× rule of thumb, so *acceptable but not great*.
- **CAC payback** = \$1,500 ÷ \$960 = **1.56 years ≈ 19 months** — comfortably inside the 24-month danger line.

Now improve retention, the highest-leverage variable. Cut churn from 25% to **15%**: average life jumps to 1 ÷ 0.15 = **6.67 years**, LTV rises to \$960 × 6.67 = **\$6,400**, and LTV/CAC leaps to \$6,400 ÷ \$1,500 = **4.27×** — now genuinely excellent, with *the same product, the same price, the same CAC*. (If we discount future contribution at a 10% cost of capital, the LTV shrinks somewhat — roughly \$3,200 at 25% churn — because dollars five years out are worth less today, which is exactly the [time value of money](/blog/trading/equity-research/time-value-of-money-discounting-for-investors) at work; the *ranking* of the two scenarios is unchanged.)

*The fastest way to transform a mediocre subscription business into a great one is almost never to raise prices or cut CAC — it is to reduce churn, because lifetime, and therefore LTV, is roughly the reciprocal of the churn rate.*

![An XY chart with years on the horizontal axis and cumulative contribution dollars on the vertical axis, showing a rising green line crossing a dashed CAC line at the payback point and entering a profit zone above it](/imgs/blogs/unit-economics-and-the-value-chain-3.png)

The chart makes payback and LTV visual. Time runs along the bottom; the green line is the *cumulative* contribution one subscriber has delivered. It starts at zero and climbs \$960 each year. The dashed horizontal line is the \$1,500 CAC — the hole you dug to acquire them. Below that line the customer is **underwater**: you have spent more to win them than they have returned. The line crosses CAC at about **1.6 years** — that is payback. Everything above the dashed line, all the way up to the \$4,800-ish lifetime total, is **profit on this unit**. The two numbers investors quote — LTV/CAC ≈ 3× and payback < 18 months — are just two readings off this one rising line: *how high does it ultimately go* (LTV vs CAC) and *how fast does it cross the line* (payback). A business with a steep line that crosses fast and climbs high is a compounding machine; a business whose line is shallow, crosses late, or never reaches the CAC line is a value destroyer wearing a growth costume.

## When growth lies: negative unit economics behind a great top line

Here is the trap that catches investors, founders, and analysts alike. Because the P&L sums mature and immature units, **rapid revenue growth can completely conceal the fact that each unit loses money.** The mechanism is simple and brutal: if every new customer is unprofitable over their whole life, then adding customers adds losses — but it also adds *revenue*, and revenue growth is what gets celebrated. The faster such a company grows, the worse its economics and the better its headline. Growth is not evidence of a good business; it is evidence of *spending*, and spending can buy either great units or terrible ones.

#### Worked example: 50% revenue growth where each unit loses money

Consider a competitor to Acme — call it **Mayfly Software** — that also pays to acquire subscribers and is growing revenue **50% a year**, the kind of number that wins funding rounds and press. But its unit is broken. Mayfly discounts heavily to win deals, so its subscriber pays \$1,200 but only carries a **50% contribution margin** = **\$600 a year** of contribution. Its product is needy, so it spends another \$400 a year per customer on success and support that it can't avoid — call true ongoing contribution **\$600 − \$400 = \$200 a year**. And its sales process is expensive: CAC is **\$2,000**. Customers churn at 30% (life ≈ 3.3 years). Then LTV ≈ \$200 × 3.3 = **\$660**, against a \$2,000 CAC — an **LTV/CAC of 0.33×**. Even on the rosier assumption that the \$400 service cost falls away and contribution is the full \$600, LTV ≈ \$600 × 3.3 = \$2,000 against \$2,000 CAC — **LTV/CAC ≈ 1.0×**, break-even at best after years of risk.

Mayfly *loses money on every customer it acquires*, yet its revenue is growing 50% because it keeps acquiring. Each new \$1,200/year subscriber adds \$1,200 to the top line and a *negative* number to lifetime value. The income statement shows a growing, exciting company; the unit shows a machine that converts every dollar of investor capital into less than a dollar of customer value. **More growth makes Mayfly worse, not better** — the losses scale with the wins.

*When a company with poor unit economics grows faster, do not relax — get more worried, because you are now watching it destroy value at a higher rate, and the only thing the growth proves is that it can spend.*

The tell that separates Mayfly from Acme is never in the revenue line. It is in three places: the **contribution margin** (is it high and stable, or thin and propped up by discounts?), the **CAC trend** (is each new customer getting *more* expensive to acquire — a sign the cheap demand is exhausted?), and the **cohort behaviour** (do older customers stay and expand, or churn out before payback?). A company can fake the top line for years. It cannot fake a cohort table for long.

![A side by side comparison of two companies both growing revenue 50 percent per year, one with negative unit economics leading to bigger losses and one with healthy unit economics leading to compounding profit](/imgs/blogs/unit-economics-and-the-value-chain-7.png)

The figure puts the two companies side by side with *identical* headline growth. Both grow revenue 50% a year; both would headline the same way. Company A (left) carries a thin \$600 contribution against a \$2,000-plus all-in CAC, an LTV/CAC below 1×, and so *faster growth means bigger losses*. Company B (right) carries an \$960 contribution, a \$1,500 CAC paid back in 19 months, an LTV/CAC above 3×, and so *faster growth means compounding profit*. The revenue chart for these two businesses is the same line. The unit economics are opposite. This is the single most valuable thing unit-economics analysis does: it tells the two apart while the income statement insists they are twins.

## The J-curve and the path to profitability

If a company has *good* unit economics but is growing fast, it will *look* unprofitable — and that apparent loss is not a problem, it is the signature of front-loaded acquisition. The shape this traces over time is the **J-curve**, and learning to read it is how you tell "investing aggressively in a great machine" apart from "bleeding out."

![An XY chart with time on the horizontal axis and cumulative cash flow on the vertical axis, showing a curve that dips below zero into a cash burn region, bottoms out, then climbs back above the zero line into profit](/imgs/blogs/unit-economics-and-the-value-chain-4.png)

The figure traces cumulative cash flow over time for a company acquiring customers with Acme-quality units. Early on, cash dives *below* zero: the company is paying \$1,500 of CAC for each of thousands of new subscribers, and those subscribers have only paid back a fraction of it. The more aggressively it acquires, the deeper the dive — this is the cash you must raise and finance. The curve bottoms at the **deepest cash hole**, then turns: as the early cohorts cross their 19-month payback and start contributing pure \$960-a-year profit, they begin to *fund* the acquisition of new cohorts. Eventually the old cohorts throw off more cash than the new ones consume, the curve crosses back through zero at **cumulative breakeven**, and then it climbs — the early burn revealed, in retrospect, as an *investment* that is now paying out.

The crucial diagnostic: **the J-curve only works if the unit economics are good.** If LTV/CAC is above ~3× and payback is short, the curve *will* eventually turn up — the only question is how much cash you must finance to get there, which is set by the depth of the J (how fast you acquire relative to how fast units pay back). If the unit economics are *bad*, there is no curve — just a line heading down forever, because the units never pay back enough to fund the next cohort. **A J-curve is a promise that good units make and bad units cannot.** The way you tell, before the curve has turned, which kind you are looking at is — again — the unit economics and the cohorts, never the aggregate loss.

#### Worked example: financing the J — how deep is the hole?

Suppose Acme acquires **10,000 new subscribers a year**, each costing **\$1,500** of CAC (so \$15 million of acquisition spend annually) and contributing **\$960 a year** thereafter, churning at 25%. In year one, Acme spends \$15M on CAC and collects only the first year's contribution from that one cohort: 10,000 × \$960 = \$9.6M (before churn). Net cash from the new cohort in year one ≈ \$9.6M − \$15M = **−\$5.4M**. In year two, the year-one cohort (now ~7,500 still subscribed after 25% churn) contributes another ~\$7.2M while Acme spends \$15M acquiring a *new* 10,000 — and so on. Each year's *new* cohort is cash-negative, but the *stack* of surviving older cohorts grows and eventually their combined contribution exceeds the \$15M acquisition bill. With these numbers the company is cumulatively cash-negative for roughly **three to four years**, reaches a trough of order **\$20–30 million** of cumulative burn, then climbs out — *provided it keeps the churn and CAC where they are*.

*The depth of the J-curve is a financing question, not a viability question: good units guarantee the curve turns, and the only thing the burn tells you is how much capital you must raise to reach the turn.*

This is why mature, slowing companies with good units suddenly gush cash, and why the market sometimes *punishes* a profitable quarter from a growth company: profitability can mean the company *stopped investing* in acquiring great units — it harvested the J instead of digging it deeper. The reverse — a widening loss — can mean it found *more* great units to acquire and is digging the J on purpose. You cannot tell which from the loss; you tell from the unit economics and whether LTV/CAC is holding as they scale.

## The mechanics that get faked: CAC, contribution, and lifetime

Three numbers carry the entire analysis, and each is routinely understated — sometimes innocently, sometimes not. Knowing exactly how they are bent is most of the skill.

**CAC is almost always understated.** The honest CAC is *fully loaded*: it includes the salaries and commissions of the sales team, the cost of the free trials and freemium users who never convert, the discounts and promotions used to close deals, and the marketing overhead — not just the media spend. A company can quote a flattering "CAC" by dividing only its paid-advertising bill by *all* new customers, including the ones who arrived organically by word of mouth. The right denominator is *paid* acquisitions and the right numerator is *all* acquisition cost. Watch especially the **blended versus paid** distinction: blended CAC mixes free organic customers into the count and looks great, but the *marginal* CAC — what it costs to acquire the *next* customer once the organic well is dry — is what determines whether the company can keep growing profitably. Marginal CAC almost always rises as a company scales, because it exhausts its cheapest demand first and must reach further (and pay more) for each additional customer.

#### Worked example: blended CAC hides a rising marginal cost

Acme acquired **2,000 customers** last quarter: **800 organically** (zero acquisition cost — they came by referral) and **1,200 through paid channels** on which Acme spent **\$2,400,000**. The *blended* CAC the company likes to quote is \$2,400,000 ÷ 2,000 = **\$1,200** — comfortably below the \$1,500 in our base case, and reassuring. But the *paid* CAC, the one that governs growth, is \$2,400,000 ÷ 1,200 = **\$2,000**. And if Acme wants to *double* paid acquisition next quarter, the cheap demand is gone and the *marginal* CAC on those extra customers might be **\$2,800**. So the LTV/CAC the company reports (against \$1,200) is 3.2×, looks healthy; the LTV/CAC on its *growth* (against \$2,000–2,800) is 1.3–1.9×, looks marginal. The blended number flatters; the marginal number governs.

*Always ask which CAC a company is quoting — a falling blended CAC can coexist with a rising marginal CAC, and it is the marginal cost of the next customer, not the average cost of all of them, that decides whether more growth creates or destroys value.*

**Contribution margin is often propped up by temporary discounts and unscaled service.** A young company may show a low contribution margin because it over-serves early customers (white-glove onboarding that won't scale) — which *understates* the mature unit — or a flatteringly high one because it hasn't yet built the support organization real scale demands — which *overstates* it. The question is what the contribution margin looks like *at steady state*, for a customer the company serves the way it will serve millions.

**Lifetime is a forecast wearing the costume of a fact.** LTV multiplies contribution by an assumed lifetime, and that lifetime is *the reciprocal of a churn rate you may have only two years of data to estimate*. A two-year-old company claiming a five-year average customer life is extrapolating three years it has never observed. Worse, churn is rarely constant: early churn (customers who never fit) is high, then it settles, so a naive 1 ÷ churn from blended early data can be wildly off. This is precisely why cohorts matter — they let you *measure* lifetime instead of assuming it.

## Cohort analysis: the lie detector

Everything above hinges on two numbers you cannot directly observe from a single income statement: the customer's *lifetime* and whether older customers spend *more* or *less* over time. **Cohort analysis** is how you observe them. You group customers by when they arrived, then track each group — each cohort — separately as it ages. Because a cohort is a fixed set of customers, new arrivals can't dilute or flatter the picture: you are watching the *same* people get older, churn, and either expand or shrink.

![An XY chart with years since signup on the horizontal axis and percent of the cohort original revenue on the vertical axis, showing a healthy green curve rising above the 100 percent line and a red leaky curve decaying well below it](/imgs/blogs/unit-economics-and-the-value-chain-5.png)

The figure shows the two outcomes that matter, both measured as **net revenue retention** — a cohort's revenue this year as a percentage of its revenue in year zero. The green curve is a *healthy* cohort: even though some customers churn, the survivors *expand* — they upgrade tiers, add seats, buy more — fast enough that the cohort's total revenue *grows past 100%* of where it started, reaching about **130% by year three**. This is the magic of best-in-class subscription businesses: a cohort you acquired once keeps getting *more* valuable every year with no new acquisition cost, so your existing base alone grows revenue. The red curve is a *leaky* cohort: churn dominates, no expansion offsets it, and the cohort decays to **50% of its starting revenue by year four**. Same revenue at signup; opposite destinies. The blended company-level revenue number can look identical for both in the early years — only the cohort curve shows which one is quietly hollowing out.

#### Worked example: year-3 cohorts at 130% net revenue retention

Take Acme's **year-1 cohort**: 1,000 customers who together pay **\$1,000,000** in their first year (\$1,000 each, say). Suppose Acme runs **130% net revenue retention**: each year, the surviving-plus-expanding cohort is worth 1.3× the prior year. Then:

- **End of year 1:** \$1,000,000 (base).
- **End of year 2:** \$1,000,000 × 1.30 = **\$1,300,000**.
- **End of year 3:** \$1,300,000 × 1.30 = **\$1,690,000**.

This cohort is worth **69% more** in year three than the day it was acquired — *despite some customers having churned* — because the customers who stayed bought far more. Acme paid CAC *once*, in year one, and the cohort's value has compounded ever since at zero incremental acquisition cost. Now compare a leaky competitor at **80% NRR**: the same \$1,000,000 cohort shrinks to \$800,000, then \$640,000 — worth **36% less** by year three. The competitor must run *harder and harder* on acquisition just to stand still, because its existing base is melting; Acme grows even if it stops acquiring entirely.

*Net revenue retention above 100% is the closest thing in business to a perpetual motion machine: a cohort you paid to acquire once keeps growing in value on its own, which is why investors will pay enormous multiples for businesses that reliably clear it.*

Cohort analysis also answers the question the simple LTV calculation fudged: **do older cohorts get more profitable?** If year-3 cohorts show better retention and higher spend than year-1 cohorts did at the same age, the business is *improving* — the product is stickier, the customers better-targeted — and the LTV you should use is rising. If newer cohorts are *worse* than older ones were at the same age (a common late-stage symptom: the company has exhausted its best customers and is now acquiring marginal ones), then the blended LTV is *flattering* and the unit economics are quietly deteriorating even as revenue grows. The cohort table catches this years before the blended numbers do. This is the same forensic instinct as reading [accruals and quality of earnings](/blog/trading/equity-research/quality-of-earnings-accruals-one-offs-red-flags): the aggregate can be true and misleading at once; you disaggregate to find the truth.

## The value chain: where in the business does the margin live?

Good unit economics tell you the business *currently* makes money on each unit. They do not tell you whether it will *keep* doing so. For that you need to know **where the company sits in its value chain** — because profit is not distributed evenly across an industry, and a unit's margin is durable only if the company occupies a stage that can *defend* its share of the profit.

The **value chain**, in Michael Porter's framing, is the sequence of activities that turn raw inputs into a product a customer pays for: design and R&D, procurement of components, manufacturing or assembly, distribution and logistics, marketing and brand, and after-sale service. Each activity adds *value* — the product is worth more after it than before. But here is the non-obvious and decisive point: **the stage that adds the most value is frequently not the stage that captures the most profit.** Value *added* and value *captured* are different things. An assembler can add enormous value — turning a pile of components into a working device — and capture almost none of the profit, because anyone can assemble and competition drives the price of assembly down to its cost. The company that *captures* the profit is the one with pricing power at its stage, whoever that is.

#### Worked example: the value chain of a \$100 retail product

Trace a product that sells at retail for **\$100** and see where the profit pools. Suppose the bill of materials — the physical components — costs **\$40**, and the component makers earn an operating margin of, say, 15% on their portion, so roughly **\$6** of profit lives in components. **Assembly** — bolting the components into the finished product — adds maybe **\$5** of cost and earns a razor-thin 4% margin: about **\$0.20** of profit; assembly is a commodity. **Distribution and logistics** take the \$45-ish factory-gate product to shelves, adding cost and earning a modest margin — call it **\$3** of profit. The **brand owner** — the company whose name is on the box, which designed the product and owns the customer relationship — buys the finished good for perhaps \$55, sells it into retail at a wholesale price that supports the \$100 shelf tag, and captures the largest profit pool: on the order of **\$20–30**. The **retailer** earns its own slice — a few dollars — for the last step to the consumer.

Add it up: of the \$100 the consumer pays, the *physical making* of the product (components + assembly) captured only about **\$6** of profit, while *design, brand, and the customer relationship* captured **\$20–30**. The company that touched the product least — never bent metal, never ran a soldering line — earned the most, because it owned the two things competition cannot easily replicate: the intellectual property and the brand. The assembler, who did the most visible work, earned almost nothing.

*Follow the profit, not the activity: the stage that does the most physical work is usually the most commoditized and the least profitable, while the profit pools at the stages that own irreplaceable IP, design, or the customer relationship.*

![An XY chart shaped like a smile with value chain stages along the horizontal axis from IP and design through components, assembly, and distribution to brand and retail, and profit margin on the vertical axis, high at both ends and collapsing in the middle](/imgs/blogs/unit-economics-and-the-value-chain-6.png)

The figure is the famous **smiling curve**, first described for the electronics industry. Plot the stages of the value chain left to right and the profit margin captured at each stage on the vertical axis, and the line smiles: **high at the left end** (design, IP, R&D — the chip *designer*, the architecture licensor), **collapsing in the middle** (component fabrication and final assembly — the commoditized, capital-intensive, competed-to-the-bone work), and **high again at the right end** (brand, marketing, the customer relationship, retail). The middle is a valley because the work there is *substitutable*: there are many contract manufacturers, and the brand owner can play them against each other until assembly margins approach the cost of capital. The ends are peaks because the work there is *protected*: an irreplaceable chip architecture or a beloved brand has pricing power no rival can easily erode. Reading a company's *position on this curve* tells you whether its good unit economics are *durable* — sitting on a peak (owning IP or brand) means the margin can persist; sitting in the valley (assembling other people's designs) means the margin is one tough negotiation away from disappearing.

#### Worked example: the assembler versus the IP owner in the same chain

Make the smiling curve concrete with two real-feeling companies in the *same* product's value chain. **Apex Assembly** is a contract manufacturer: it buys components, assembles finished devices, and ships them to the brand owner. On each device it handles perhaps **\$50** of value-add (the assembly labour and overhead), and because contract assembly is fiercely competitive and undifferentiated, it earns a **3% operating margin** on that work — about **\$1.50** of operating profit per device, on a business that requires huge factories, armies of workers, and constant capital reinvestment. Its [return on capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa) is chronically thin and its bargaining power is nil — the brand owner can switch to a rival assembler at the next contract.

**Cortex Designs** sits at the *left* peak: it designs the device's core processor and licenses the architecture, owning patents and a design ecosystem competitors cannot replicate. It touches no metal and runs no factory. On the **\$30** of value its IP contributes to each device, it earns a **60% operating margin** — about **\$18** of operating profit per device — because nobody else can supply that design. Same device, same value chain. Apex did the visible, capital-hungry work and earned \$1.50 at 3%; Cortex did the invisible, capital-light work and earned \$18 at 60%. The difference is *not* effort or even value added — it is **pricing power**, which comes from owning a stage of the chain that competition cannot enter. This is exactly the [economic moat](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage) made physical: a moat *is* control of a profitable stage of the value chain.

*Two companies in the same value chain can earn 3% and 60% margins on the same end product; the gap is entirely explained by which stage owns something competitors cannot copy.*

## Vertical integration versus specialization

Once you see the value chain as a map of profit pools, a strategic question follows: should a company **own more stages** of the chain (vertical integration) or **specialize** in the one stage where it has an edge? The answer depends on where the profit pools sit and where the company has durable advantage. A firm that owns a peak stage and *also* integrates into the commoditized valley is often *diluting* its returns — taking capital that earns 60% in design and plowing it into assembly that earns 3%. A firm that specializes in a peak and *outsources* the valley keeps its capital concentrated where it earns the most, which is one reason many of the most profitable companies in the world are "asset-light" designers and brand owners that manufacture nothing.

But integration can also *create* and *defend* profit pools. Owning a stage can lock out competitors (control the scarce input), capture a margin a supplier was extracting, or guarantee quality the brand depends on. The judgement is always the same: integrate into a stage only if you can earn an *attractive return on the capital* it consumes and it *strengthens* your position in your profitable stage — never merely because the stage is "part of your industry." The value chain is not a thing to own; it is a map of where the money is, and the strategic art is occupying the profitable parts while letting competition grind down the cost of the rest *on your behalf*.

This connects directly to [capital allocation](/blog/trading/equity-research/capital-allocation-the-ceos-most-important-job): the decision to integrate or specialize is a capital-allocation decision, and the discipline of judging it by return on incremental capital is identical. A management team that integrates into low-return stages to "control the value chain" is often destroying value with a strategic-sounding excuse — the kind of empire-building the [returns-on-capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa) lens exposes.

## Common misconceptions

**"Revenue growth proves the business works."** The most expensive misconception in investing. Revenue growth proves the company is *spending* and *selling* — it says nothing about whether each unit sold makes money. Mayfly grew 50% while destroying value on every customer; the growth was the *symptom* of the problem, not evidence against it. Always ask what the *unit* economics are beneath the growth, and whether faster growth makes the company better or worse.

**"The company is unprofitable, so the business model is broken."** Equally wrong in the other direction. A company with great unit economics that is growing fast *should* be unprofitable on a blended P&L, because it is front-loading CAC on units that will pay back later — the J-curve. The aggregate loss is a growth-timing artifact. Read the unit and the cohorts; if LTV/CAC is healthy and payback is short, the loss is an investment, not a wound.

**"LTV/CAC above 3× means the business is great."** It is *necessary* but not *sufficient*, and it is only as good as its inputs. LTV depends on a churn assumption you often can't yet verify and a contribution margin that may be propped up by temporary discounts; CAC may be understated by omitting salaries or rising fast as cheap demand exhausts. A 3× ratio built on a heroic 5-year-retention assumption for a two-year-old company is a guess dressed as a fact. Demand to see the *cohort curves* behind the ratio, and check whether newer cohorts are as good as older ones.

**"High gross margin means good unit economics."** Gross margin is the *contribution* side of the unit, but it ignores the *acquisition* side. A business can have an 85% gross margin and *terrible* unit economics if its CAC is so high, or its churn so fast, that customers never repay the cost of winning them. Contribution margin and CAC are two different things, and a unit only works when the contribution *over the customer's life* exceeds the CAC. Many failed startups had gorgeous gross margins and broken LTV/CAC.

**"The company that adds the most value earns the most profit."** The smiling curve's whole lesson is that this is false. Value *added* and value *captured* diverge constantly. The assembler adds enormous physical value and captures almost none; the designer adds invisible value and captures most. Profit pools at stages with pricing power, not at stages with the most activity or the most capital. Follow the margin, not the effort.

**"Owning more of the value chain is always stronger."** Vertical integration into a low-return stage *dilutes* returns and ties up capital where competition has already crushed the margin. Integration is valuable only when it earns an attractive return on the capital it consumes *and* defends your profitable stage. Owning the whole chain often means earning a *blended* margin between your great stage and the commodity stages — worse than specializing in the great one and outsourcing the rest.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The smartphone value chain — the smiling curve made flesh.** The economics of a flagship smartphone are the textbook case. The chip *designers* and architecture licensors earn enormous gross and operating margins because their IP is irreplaceable; the contract *assemblers* that physically build the phones — handling tens of billions in revenue — earn famously thin single-digit operating margins because assembly is a substitutable, fiercely competitive service. The *brand owner* who designs the device, owns the operating system and the customer relationship, and never runs a factory captures the largest profit pool of all. The device that the assembler builds with its own hands earns the assembler a few percent; the brand whose logo is on the back earns dramatically more. (Exact figures vary by company and year and are competitively sensitive; the *shape* — peaks at design/brand, valley at assembly — is the durable, well-documented pattern.) Same product, profit pooling at the protected ends.

**SaaS and the cohort revolution.** The entire valuation regime for software companies rests on unit economics and cohorts. Investors learned to look past the blended loss of a fast-growing SaaS company to its LTV/CAC, payback, and especially its **net revenue retention** — and to pay extraordinary revenue multiples for businesses that reliably clear 120–130% NRR, because such a business *grows even if it stops acquiring*. The discipline cuts both ways: when a high-flying SaaS company's NRR slips below 100% or its CAC payback stretches past two years, the multiple collapses, because the cohort curve has revealed that the growth was being *bought* rather than *compounded*. The same cohort lens repeatedly exposed consumer and delivery businesses whose dazzling growth concealed negative unit economics — units that lost money on every order, papered over by ever-larger order counts, exactly the Mayfly pattern.

**Branded goods versus their manufacturers.** Across consumer industries — apparel, footwear, packaged food, beauty — the brand owner and the contract manufacturer occupy opposite ends of the smiling curve. The factory that physically makes a premium sneaker or a designer handbag earns a modest manufacturing margin; the brand that designs it, markets it, and owns the customer's desire for it earns a large one and sells the same physical object for a multiple of its production cost. The durable lesson for an investor: a *brand* with pricing power is sitting on a profit-pool peak and can defend its unit economics; a *manufacturer* of others' brands is sitting in the valley and is one lost contract away from a margin collapse. This is why, when two companies in the same chain trade at similar revenue multiples, the one that owns the IP or the brand is usually the safer compounder — its good unit economics are *defended*, while the assembler's are *rented*.

**Quality-of-earnings frauds and fake units.** The forensic cases in this series — the fabricated revenue of [Enron](/blog/trading/finance/enron-2001-accounting-fraud) and the imaginary cash of [Wirecard](/blog/trading/finance/wirecard-the-german-fintech-fraud) — are unit-economics failures at the limit: a "unit" whose revenue or cash does not actually exist. The discipline of building the economics of one real unit, and tracing the cohort that produced it, is a defense against exactly that kind of deception, because a fabricated business cannot produce a coherent cohort table — the made-up customers don't age, churn, or expand the way real ones do. The investor who insists on seeing the unit, and the cohort, is harder to fool than the one who reads only the growing top line.

## When this matters and further reading

Unit economics matter most precisely when the headline P&L is *uninformative* — which is exactly when most investors are reading it hardest. Any company that is growing fast, paying to acquire customers, or operating below scale will have an aggregate profit number that says little about whether its model works. For those companies, the unit is the truth and the P&L is the noise. For mature, slow-growing companies the two converge — but even then, the value chain tells you whether the margin you see is *durable* or about to be competed away. The questions to carry into any business: *What is the unit? Does it make money — contribution positive, LTV above CAC, payback short? Is growth making the units better or worse? Where in the value chain does this company sit, and is its stage defended?*
