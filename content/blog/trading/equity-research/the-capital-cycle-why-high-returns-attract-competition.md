> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Capital flows toward high returns and away from low ones, and that flow is self-defeating: the very act of chasing high returns builds the excess capacity that destroys them, while years of starved investment quietly create the scarcity that revives them.
>
> - **The capital cycle is a self-correcting loop.** A high return on capital invites a flood of investment — new entrants, capex, capacity — which lifts supply, drives down prices and returns, then triggers a retreat of capital that tightens supply and lets returns recover. Round and round.
> - **Most analysts over-focus on demand and under-weight supply.** Demand is easy to forecast and everyone already does it, so a consensus demand view is priced in. Supply — driven by where capital is flowing across the whole industry — is the real swing factor and the place where an edge survives.
> - **The tells are visible if you look.** A capex boom, a wave of IPOs and share issuance, optimistic capacity plans and a crowd of new entrants mark a top; collapsing capex, bankruptcies, consolidation and capitulation mark a bottom. The signals invert across the cycle.
> - **It is deeply contrarian.** You lean into industries capital is *fleeing* and away from industries capital is *flooding into* — the opposite of what feels comfortable, because the comfortable industry is the one building the glut.
> - **Moats interrupt the cycle.** A genuine competitive advantage keeps outside capital from entering, which is why a few rare businesses sustain high returns for decades while commodity industries snap back to the cost of capital — and why the counter-cyclical capital allocator who builds at the bottom wins the next cycle.

There is a pattern in capital-intensive industries so reliable that you can almost set your watch by it, and yet it fools nearly every participant nearly every time. It goes like this. An industry — let us say it makes a basic chemical, or ships dry bulk cargo, or fabricates memory chips, or refines copper — earns, for a stretch, a wonderful return on the capital invested in it. Prices are high, plants run flat out, margins are fat, and the businesses inside the industry throw off cash. And then, with the inevitability of a tide, that very success destroys itself. The high returns attract capital the way blood in the water attracts sharks: new entrants appear, incumbents announce expansion plans, banks line up to lend, equity investors fund IPOs and rights issues, and within a few years a wave of brand-new capacity arrives — far more than the market can absorb. Supply overshoots demand, prices crack, margins collapse, and the industry that was the toast of the market becomes a graveyard of write-offs and bankruptcies. Then, slowly, the reverse happens: capital flees, capacity is shuttered, the weak die, the survivors consolidate, and the scarcity that builds up over years of underinvestment eventually lifts prices and returns again — setting the stage for the next boom, and the next flood, and the next bust.

This is the **capital cycle**, and it is the central idea of one of the most quietly influential investment frameworks of the last few decades: the approach articulated by the partners of Marathon Asset Management in their letters and collected in the book *Capital Returns*. The framework's great insight is almost embarrassingly simple, and yet it cuts against the grain of how nearly all of equity research is actually practiced. It says: *stop staring so hard at demand, and start watching supply and capital allocation.* The thing that determines whether an industry earns good returns or terrible ones over the next several years is rarely a surprise in demand — demand is the part everyone forecasts, the part that is already in the price. It is what is happening to *supply*: whether capital is flooding into the industry to build new capacity, or fleeing it and letting capacity wither. High returns contain, encoded within them, the seeds of low returns, because high returns summon the capital that builds the glut. And low returns, miserable as they are to live through, contain the seeds of high returns, because they drive away the capital and let scarcity do its work.

If you have read the rest of this series, you will recognize that the capital cycle is, at bottom, a dynamic theory of [the ROIC–WACC spread](/blog/trading/equity-research/roic-wacc-spread-the-engine-of-intrinsic-value). That post established that value is created when a business earns more on its capital than that capital costs, and it noted in passing that competition tends to drag high returns back down toward the cost of capital over time — the phenomenon of ROIC mean-reversion. The capital cycle is the *mechanism* of that mean-reversion. It is the answer to "*how, exactly, does competition crush a high return?*" The answer is: through capital. High returns are a signal, broadcast to every would-be competitor and capital allocator in the world, that says "deploy money here." They do, the supply they build crushes the return, and the spread reverts. The moat, which we will return to, is simply anything that jams this signal — that keeps the outside capital out and lets a lucky business hold its spread far longer than the cycle would otherwise allow.

![A vertical six-phase loop showing high returns drawing capital in, supply overshooting, returns collapsing, capital fleeing, supply tightening, and returns recovering to repeat the cycle](/imgs/blogs/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition-1.png)

The figure above is the whole framework in one picture, and it is worth fixing in your mind before we go further. Read it top to bottom as a loop. High returns at the top draw capital in; the capital builds capacity faster than demand grows; the resulting glut collapses returns; the collapse drives capital out; the absence of capital tightens supply; and the tightened supply revives returns — which sends us back to the top to do it all again. The crucial, counterintuitive thing the figure encodes is that *each phase causes the next*. The boom is not interrupted by the bust as if by bad luck; the boom *produces* the bust, by attracting the capital that builds the excess capacity. And the bust is not ended by good luck; the bust *produces* the recovery, by driving away the capital and starving supply. Once you see the cycle as self-correcting rather than as a sequence of accidents, you stop asking "what will demand do?" and start asking the far more useful question: "*where is capital flowing, and what is that going to do to supply two or three years out?*"

This post builds the capital cycle from the ground up, the way the whole series builds everything from the ground up. We will define the moving parts, walk the loop phase by phase with the numbers attached, learn to read the tells that mark tops and bottoms, see why the framework is so deeply contrarian, connect it back to moats and valuation, and ground all of it in the real capital-intensive sectors — chemicals, shipping, memory, mining, energy, autos — where the cycle plays out in plain sight, decade after decade, fooling fresh waves of investors who cannot quite believe that *this time* the good returns will summon the competition that ends them. They always do.

## Foundations: capacity, supply, returns, and the difference between a price and a margin

Before we can trace the cycle, we need a handful of ideas defined precisely. Most of them have fuller treatments elsewhere in the series; here we define them tightly enough to do the arithmetic, and point you onward where the depth lives.

**Return on invested capital (ROIC)** is the percentage that the pool of money tied up in a business earns each year — operating profit after tax, divided by the capital (debt plus equity, net of idle cash) deployed in operations. A business with \$1,000M of invested capital earning \$200M of after-tax operating profit has a 20% ROIC. ROIC is the single best one-number summary of how good a business is at turning capital into profit, and the full mechanics live in the [returns-on-capital post](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa). For the capital cycle, the thing to hold in mind is that ROIC is *not* a stable property of a company — in a cyclical industry it swings violently, from spectacular to dreadful and back, as the cycle turns.

**WACC — the weighted average cost of capital** — is what that capital costs: the blended return that lenders and shareholders demand for supplying the money. If a company's WACC is 9%, it must earn at least 9% on its capital just to keep its financiers whole. The derivation is in the [cost-of-capital post](/blog/trading/equity-research/cost-of-capital-and-the-hurdle-rate). The key relationship, again, is the **spread**: ROIC minus WACC. A positive spread creates value; a zero spread treads water; a negative spread destroys value even while the income statement may show a profit.

**Capacity** is the maximum output an industry's physical assets can produce — the tonnes of chemical its plants can make per year, the cargo its ships can carry, the wafers its fabs can fabricate. Capacity is built with **capital expenditure (capex)**: money spent on plant, equipment, ships, mines, and fabs. Capacity is sticky in two directions that matter enormously. It takes years to build — a new chemical plant or semiconductor fab can take three to five years from decision to first output — so the supply you commit to today arrives in a market you cannot fully see. And it is hard to remove — a built plant has low marginal cost, so even a money-losing operator keeps running it rather than scrapping it, which means gluts clear slowly. This combination of *lagged arrival* and *sticky exit* is the engine of the cycle's overshoot.

**Supply and demand set the price; price and capacity set the margin.** This is the crux, so go slowly. In a commodity industry the *price* is set at the margin by the balance of supply and demand — when capacity is tight relative to demand, price rises; when capacity is in glut, price falls toward the marginal producer's cash cost. The *margin* (and therefore the ROIC) is then that price minus the cost of producing, spread over the capital invested. The vital point is that **strong demand does not guarantee good returns.** An industry can have wonderfully growing demand and still earn miserable returns, if supply has grown even faster. The thing that determines returns is not the level of demand but the *balance* between demand and supply — and supply is the side that swings, because it is built by capital allocation decisions that are themselves driven by the level of returns. This is precisely the relationship that demand-obsessed analysis misses, and it is why the capital cycle insists on watching the supply side.

**Mean reversion** is the tendency of an unusually high or low value to drift back toward its long-run average. Applied to returns on capital, it is one of the most robust empirical regularities in all of finance: across large samples of companies, those with the highest ROIC tend to see it decline over the following years, and those with the lowest ROIC tend to see it improve. The capital cycle explains *why*: high returns attract the capital that competes them down, and low returns repel the capital, allowing scarcity to lift them back up. Mean reversion is not a mystical force; it is the capital cycle, observed in aggregate.

**A moat** is a durable structural feature that lets a business sustain a high ROIC against the competition that mean reversion would otherwise impose — a cost advantage, a network effect, switching costs, an intangible like a brand or a patent, or efficient scale in a niche. The full treatment is in the [economic-moats post](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage). For our purposes, a moat is *anything that keeps outside capital from entering the industry or the niche.* A moat is, quite literally, a defense against the capital cycle — and the businesses that escape the cycle's gravity are exactly the ones with the deepest moats.

#### Worked example: same demand growth, opposite returns

Let me anchor the supply-versus-demand point in numbers immediately, with the company we will return to throughout this post: **Cresswell Chemicals**, a fictional maker of a basic petrochemical we will call "polyresin." Suppose polyresin demand grows at a healthy, steady 5% a year — a number an analyst would call "good." There is nothing wrong with demand.

In Scenario A, the industry's collective capacity also grows at about 5% a year. Supply and demand stay in balance, the plants run at a comfortable 90% utilization, prices hold, and Cresswell earns a 15% ROIC against its 9% WACC — a healthy +6-point spread.

In Scenario B, demand grows at the *same* 5% — but, lured by the good returns of recent years, the industry has committed to capacity additions that grow supply at 12% a year. Within three years, capacity has run far ahead of the 5% demand growth. Utilization falls from 90% toward 70%. With plants half-empty and everyone desperate to cover fixed costs, prices crack — and Cresswell's ROIC collapses from 15% to, say, 4%, well below its 9% WACC. Same demand. Same 5% growth. Opposite outcome. The only thing that differed was what capital did to supply.

*Demand growth tells you almost nothing about future returns; the balance between demand and supply — and supply is what capital builds — tells you almost everything.*

## Why analysts watch the wrong variable

Here is a question worth sitting with: if the supply side is where returns are actually decided, why does the overwhelming majority of equity research focus on demand?

The answer is not that analysts are foolish. It is that demand is *tractable and crowded*, and supply is *intractable and lonely* — and human beings, including very smart ones, gravitate toward the variable that is easy to model and that everyone else is modeling. Demand forecasting feels like real analysis: you build a model linking the industry's product to GDP growth, to end-market drivers, to demographic trends, to the adoption curve of some new application. There is data; there are broker estimates to anchor on; there is a consensus to be slightly more or slightly less optimistic than. It is comfortable. And it is almost entirely useless as a source of edge, precisely because everyone is doing it. A consensus demand forecast, by the time you have built it, is already in the price.

![A before and after comparison contrasting demand analysis that the crowd does with supply analysis that the capital cycle does, showing demand is easy crowded and priced in while supply is hard lonely and the real swing factor](/imgs/blogs/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition-2.png)

The figure contrasts the two postures. On the left is what the crowd analyzes: demand. It is easy to model, everyone uses the same broker numbers, and the resulting view is already in the price — and, fatally, it misses the glut, because even a correct, bullish demand forecast can sit alongside an industry earning awful returns if supply has grown faster. On the right is what the capital-cycle investor analyzes: supply, driven by where capital is flowing. It is hard — it requires aggregating capex and capacity plans across an entire industry, often piecing together fragments from many companies' disclosures and trade-press announcements. Few bother. And precisely because few bother, it is where the swing factor lives and where an edge can survive. The contrarian conclusion writes itself: buy where capacity is shrinking, avoid where it is growing.

There is a deeper reason supply is the better variable to watch, beyond the fact that it is less crowded. **Supply is more forecastable than demand, on the horizon that matters.** This sounds backwards until you see it. Demand depends on the whole sweep of the macroeconomy, on consumer behavior, on technological substitution — genuinely unpredictable things. But supply, on a two-to-three-year horizon, is *already largely determined*, because capacity takes years to build and the projects that will come online in 2028 have, for the most part, already been announced, financed, and broken ground by 2026. You can, with patient industry work, build a fairly reliable map of how much new capacity is in the pipeline and when it will arrive. That map — the supply pipeline — is one of the few things in markets that is both important and knowable in advance. The capital-cycle investor's edge is, to a large degree, simply the willingness to do the unglamorous work of building that supply map when the rest of the market is busy fine-tuning a demand forecast that is already in the price.

#### Worked example: the capex boom that doubles capacity

Return to Cresswell and the polyresin industry, now at the top of a cycle. Polyresin has been earning the industry a glittering 20% ROIC for three years running — well above the industry's 9% cost of capital, a +11-point spread that makes every player look like a genius and every banker eager to lend.

Predictably, the industry responds. Existing producers announce expansions; two well-funded new entrants announce greenfield plants; private capital backs a third. Across the industry, capacity that stood at, say, 10 million tonnes a year is set to rise to roughly 20 million tonnes over the following three years as all this plant comes online — a doubling.

Demand, meanwhile, grows at its steady 5% a year. Over three years that compounds to roughly +16%, lifting demand from (say) 9 million tonnes consumed to about 10.4 million. So we arrive at the moment the new capacity lands: capacity of 20 million tonnes chasing demand of 10.4 million. Utilization, which had been a tight 90%, collapses toward 52%. Plants sit half-idle. Every producer, facing high fixed costs and low marginal costs, cuts price to fill its plant and cover overhead. Polyresin's price falls by a third. And Cresswell's ROIC, which had been 20%, crashes to 4% — below the 9% cost of capital. The +11-point spread has become a −5-point spread. The industry is now *destroying* value, three years after looking like the best business in the market.

*The capacity that crushed the return was not built in response to a demand surprise; it was built in response to the high return itself — the boom contained the bust.*

## Walking the loop: the six phases of the capital cycle

Let us now walk the cycle deliberately, phase by phase, attaching the economics to each. The six phases map onto the loop in Figure 1.

**Phase 1 — High returns.** An industry earns a ROIC well above its cost of capital. This can begin for many reasons: a demand surge the supply side has not yet caught up with, a period of underinvestment that left capacity tight, a technological or regulatory shift that favors incumbents, a commodity-price spike. Whatever the trigger, the result is fat margins and high returns, and — crucially — *visible* high returns. The whole world can see that this industry is minting money.

**Phase 2 — Capital floods in.** Those high returns are a beacon. They attract capital in every form. Incumbents, flush with cash and confidence, announce expansions and approve every project on the docket. New entrants appear, funded by venture capital, private equity, or the public markets. Investment banks, eager for fees, underwrite a wave of IPOs, secondary share offerings, and rights issues to fund the building. Lenders, seeing fat coverage ratios, extend credit cheaply. Optimistic capacity plans proliferate, each justified by extrapolating recent demand growth and recent high prices indefinitely into the future. Capex across the industry surges — often to multiples of its historical level.

**Phase 3 — Supply overshoots.** Here the cycle's two structural features — lagged arrival and the herd instinct of capital — combine to guarantee an overshoot. Because every player observes the same high returns and responds at roughly the same time, and because capacity takes years to build, the industry collectively commits to far more capacity than the market needs, and that capacity all arrives in a bunch a few years later, into a market that has grown only modestly. No single producer intends to create a glut; each is responding rationally to attractive returns. But the *sum* of rational individual decisions is a collective catastrophe — the textbook tragedy of the commons, playing out in steel furnaces and container ships and chip fabs.

**Phase 4 — Returns collapse.** The new capacity lands. Utilization falls, prices crack toward the marginal producer's cash cost, margins evaporate, and ROIC plunges — often well below the cost of capital. The industry that was the toast of the market becomes its pariah. Earnings disappoint, guidance is cut, write-downs are taken, and the share prices of the entire sector are punished. The investors and managers who extrapolated the boom are left holding expensive, half-empty plant.

**Phase 5 — Capital flees.** Now the cycle reverses. Faced with terrible returns, the industry slashes capex — often by 50%, 60%, or more — and cancels or mothballs projects. The weakest, most-leveraged players go bankrupt, and their capacity is either shut or bought cheaply by stronger hands. Consolidation accelerates: a fragmented industry of twenty players becomes an oligopoly of three or four. Equity investors who funded the boom swear off the sector; new capital becomes impossible to raise except at fire-sale terms. Crucially, *almost no new capacity is being commissioned* — the pipeline of future supply runs dry.

**Phase 6 — Supply tightens, returns recover.** This is the phase that requires the most patience and that the market most reliably overlooks. With no new capacity arriving and demand continuing its steady grind upward, the glut slowly works off. Years of underinvestment leave the industry's capacity flat or shrinking while demand keeps growing. Utilization climbs back toward full; the surviving, consolidated players regain pricing power; prices and margins recover; and ROIC rises back above the cost of capital. The industry is, once again, minting money — which broadcasts the high-return beacon, attracts the capital, and starts the whole loop over again.

![Two opposing waves over a full cycle, one showing return on invested capital starting high near twenty percent collapsing below the nine percent cost of capital and recovering, the other showing industry capacity rising as the glut builds then falling as it is cut, with the two curves mirror images](/imgs/blogs/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition-3.png)

The chart traces the two variables that matter against each other across a full cycle. The solid curve is ROIC: it starts high (around 20%), and as the industry's capacity ramps up — the dashed curve climbing through the middle years — the return collapses, falling below the 9% cost-of-capital hurdle (the dashed horizontal line) into the value-destroying zone. Only when capacity is finally cut, late in the cycle, does ROIC turn and recover. The two curves are near mirror images, and the *lead-lag* is the whole story: ROIC peaks *before* capacity does, because the high return is what triggers the capacity building, and the capacity building is what then destroys the return. An investor watching only ROIC sees a company at the peak and thinks "great business"; an investor watching the capacity curve sees the supply wave already cresting and knows the return is about to follow it down.

#### Worked example: tracing Cresswell through the full cycle

Let me put numbers on all six phases with Cresswell, so the loop is concrete. We will track invested capital, ROIC, the spread, and the value being created or destroyed each year.

- **Phase 1 (years 1–3), high returns.** Cresswell has \$1,000M of invested capital, earns a 20% ROIC, faces a 9% WACC. NOPAT is \$200M; the capital "rent" is \$90M; economic profit is \$110M a year. A genuinely wonderful business — for now.
- **Phase 2–3 (years 3–5), capital floods in, supply overshoots.** Seeing the +11-point spread, Cresswell and its rivals invest heavily. Cresswell itself adds \$500M of new plant, lifting its invested capital to \$1,500M; the whole industry roughly doubles capacity. Cresswell expected the new capacity to earn 20% too — another \$100M of NOPAT on the \$500M.
- **Phase 4 (years 5–6), returns collapse.** The industry-wide glut crushes prices. Far from earning 20% on the new \$500M, Cresswell now earns just 4% on its entire \$1,500M base. NOPAT falls to \$60M. The capital rent on \$1,500M at 9% is \$135M. Economic profit is *negative* \$75M a year. Cresswell is destroying \$75M of value annually — and the \$500M expansion, financed at the top, is the reason its capital base is now so large that the rent alone swamps the meager profit.
- **Phase 5 (years 6–8), capital flees.** Cresswell slashes capex to near zero, mothballs its least efficient line, and two over-leveraged rivals go bankrupt; their capacity is shut. Industry capacity stops growing and begins to shrink. Cresswell's ROIC is still poor — 6%, say — but the trend has turned.
- **Phase 6 (years 8–10), supply tightens, returns recover.** With no new capacity and demand having grown ~25% over the lean years, the glut clears. Utilization climbs, prices recover, and Cresswell's ROIC rises back to 16% on its \$1,500M base — NOPAT of \$240M, a rent of \$135M, economic profit of \$105M a year. The cycle has turned full circle, and the high returns are once again sending the beacon.

*The cruelest detail is that the \$500M Cresswell invested at the top — at 20% expected returns — is exactly what made the trough so painful, because a bigger capital base means a bigger rent to cover when the price cracks; capital deployed at the peak compounds the misery of the bust.*

## Reading the tells: what a top looks like, what a bottom looks like

The practical payoff of the framework is that the cycle leaves *tells* — observable, public signals that mark where in the loop an industry sits. They are visible in capex figures, in capital-raising activity, in competitive structure, and in sentiment and valuation. And — this is the useful part — the tells at a top are the mirror image of the tells at a bottom. Learn to read them and you can place an industry on the loop without a crystal ball.

![A matrix with rows for the cycle top the cycle bottom and what it means, and columns for capex and capacity capital raising competitive structure and sentiment, showing how every supply signal inverts between top and bottom](/imgs/blogs/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition-4.png)

The matrix lays the tells out side by side. At a **top** (returns peak, avoid): capex is booming and capacity plans are up sharply; IPOs, rights issues and debt issuance flood the sector; new entrants pile in and the industry fragments; sentiment is euphoric and valuations are high — on *peak* earnings, which makes them doubly dangerous. At a **bottom** (returns trough, buy): capex has been slashed, with capacity closures and write-offs; there are no IPOs, and equity is raised only to survive, at a discount; bankruptcies and M&A consolidate the industry to a handful of disciplined players; and sentiment is one of capitulation, with cheap valuations — on *trough* earnings. The bottom row spells out what each tell means for returns ahead: capex builds the future supply that will hurt; capital-raising shows the crowd funding the glut at the worst possible moment; consolidation puts pricing power back in fewer, more disciplined hands; and the valuation pays for a future that is either too rosy or too bleak — to be faded.

A few of these tells deserve extra attention, because they are the ones that most reliably separate a true top from a mere good year.

**Aggregate industry capex relative to depreciation, or relative to its own history, is the single most powerful supply signal.** When an industry is spending two or three times its depreciation on capex, it is growing capacity fast; when it is spending less than depreciation, capacity is actually shrinking. The capital-cycle investor watches this ratio across the whole sector — not just one company — because the glut is built by the *sum* of everyone's investment. A company that is being disciplined while its rivals build like mad is still going to suffer the industry glut; supply is an industry-level variable.

**Capital-raising is the crowd's vote, and the crowd votes at the worst time.** A flood of IPOs and equity issuance in a sector is a near-perfect contrarian indicator, because companies and their bankers raise equity when it is easy — when valuations are high and the story is hot, i.e., at the top. The same companies that cannot give equity away at the bottom were selling it eagerly at the top. When you see a sector's capital markets running hot — new listings, big secondaries, SPAC-style vehicles, easy high-yield debt — the supply that money funds is being built into a market that will not need it.

**Optimistic capacity plans, stated openly, are a gift.** Management teams, in their pursuit of growth and their tendency to extrapolate, will often *tell you* about the glut they are building, in earnings calls and investor days, by announcing aggressive expansion targets. When every player in an industry is guiding to double-digit capacity growth into the same end-market, the arithmetic of the coming overshoot is laid out for anyone willing to add it up. The information is public; the work of aggregating it is what most people skip.

#### Worked example: reading aggregate capex as a top signal

Suppose you are studying the polyresin industry and you do the unglamorous aggregation. You find that across the eight largest producers, total capex has risen from roughly \$2 billion a year (about equal to the industry's \$2 billion of annual depreciation — i.e., merely maintaining capacity) to \$6 billion a year, triple depreciation. You also count, from press releases and investor decks, announced greenfield projects and expansions totaling about 9 million tonnes of new annual capacity, against a current installed base of 10 million tonnes and demand of 9 million growing at 5%.

You don't need a demand model to see the problem. Capacity is set to rise ~90% over the next three years; demand will rise ~16%. Capex at 3× depreciation confirms the building is real and well underway, not just talk. The capital-raising is hot — two of the eight just did large secondary offerings to fund their expansions. Every tell points the same way: this is a top. The industry's 20% ROIC and its excited analysts are describing the *present*, but the supply pipeline is describing a future of 50%-utilization plants and collapsed prices. You would avoid the sector here — or, if you were inclined to act on the signal, fade it — *despite* the wonderful current returns, precisely *because* of them.

*The numbers that scream "great industry" today — high ROIC, surging capex, easy financing — are, read through the capital cycle, the exact numbers that forecast terrible returns tomorrow.*

## Why the framework is contrarian, and why that is hard

The capital cycle leads to conclusions that feel deeply wrong, which is exactly why it works: comfortable conclusions get arbitraged away, and uncomfortable ones survive. Its core instruction is to *lean into the industries capital is fleeing and away from the industries capital is flooding into* — to buy the left-for-dead sector that no analyst wants to cover and avoid the glamorous, well-funded growth sector that everyone is excited about.

![A before and after comparison of an industry capital is flooding into which should be avoided versus an industry capital is fleeing which is the setup, contrasting the story the capital signal what it builds and the resulting setup for each](/imgs/blogs/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition-5.png)

The figure makes the contrarian instruction explicit. On the left, the industry capital is *flooding into*: the story is a hot growth industry everyone wants to own; the capital signal is record capex, IPOs and rising share counts; what it builds is future excess supply that will crush returns; and the setup — expensive *and* capacity growing — is a trap waiting to spring. On the right, the industry capital is *fleeing*: the story is one of an unloved, left-for-dead sector no analyst wants; the capital signal is collapsing capex, plant closures, and buybacks; what it builds is future scarcity that will revive returns; and the setup — cheap *and* capacity shrinking — is the buy. The whole edge is the willingness to take the right-hand side when every instinct, every incentive, and every colleague is pulling you toward the left.

This is psychologically and professionally brutal, and it is worth being honest about why. Buying the fleeing-capital industry means buying businesses with *currently terrible* and *still-falling* earnings, in a sector everyone agrees is uninvestable, where the news flow is uniformly bad and will likely stay bad for a while before it gets better. You will look wrong for months or years before the cycle turns. Avoiding the flooding-capital industry means *not owning* the sector that is going up the most right now, the one your clients are asking why you don't own, the one that makes you look like a dinosaur for missing. The capital cycle does not promise comfort; it promises that the discomfort is the source of the return. As the saying in the framework goes, the time to be interested in an industry is when capital is leaving it and capacity is being destroyed — when, in other words, it feels most foolish to be there. This is the same temperamental edge that runs through all great long-horizon investing; it is no accident that it rhymes with [Buffett's owner-mindset, buy-fear approach to value](/blog/trading/finance/warren-buffett-berkshire-value-investing).

There is an important refinement here, because "buy what is hated" without discipline is just a recipe for buying value traps. The capital cycle is not "buy any cheap, hated sector." It is "buy the cheap, hated sector *where the supply side is healing*" — where capex has actually collapsed, capacity is actually shrinking, weak players have actually died, and the survivors have actually consolidated. A hated sector where capital is *still* flooding in (think of certain perennially-hyped technology niches that stay cheap on a forward basis because every dollar of profit is competed away by the next funded entrant) is not a capital-cycle buy; it is a value trap with a low multiple. The signal is not the low price alone — it is the *combination* of a low price with a supply side that is mending. We will return to that combination when we connect the cycle to valuation.

#### Worked example: the contrarian buy at the trough

Here is the inverse of the capex-boom example, and it is where the money is actually made. Polyresin has now been through its bust. After three years of 4–6% ROIC, the industry has capitulated. Aggregate capex has fallen 65% — from \$6 billion a year at the top to about \$2 billion, now *below* the \$2.5 billion of depreciation, which means industry capacity is actually *shrinking* as worn-out plant is retired and not replaced. Two of the eight major producers have gone bankrupt; their capacity has been shut or absorbed, and the industry has consolidated from eight players to five. No new greenfield projects have been announced in two years. Sentiment is despair; the sell-side has downgraded the sector to "uninvestable"; Cresswell trades at a low fraction of the replacement cost of its plant.

Run the tells: capex collapsed and below depreciation (capacity shrinking — good), no capital raising except distressed (the crowd has fled — good), consolidation to five disciplined survivors (pricing power returning — good), capitulation valuation on trough earnings (cheap — good). Every signal that screamed "top" three years ago now screams "bottom." Meanwhile demand has kept grinding up at 5% a year — roughly +16% over the lean three years — against a capacity base that is flat-to-shrinking. The glut is being worked off. You don't know the exact month the price will turn, but you know the supply side is healing and the valuation is paying you to wait. *This* is the capital-cycle buy — and it is available only because it feels terrible to make.

*You buy at the trough not by predicting the recovery's timing but by confirming the supply side is mending and the price already assumes it never will.*

## Moats: the businesses that escape the cycle

If the capital cycle reliably drags high returns back to the cost of capital, how do a handful of businesses sustain a high ROIC for decades? The answer is the [moat](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage) — and seeing the moat *as a defense against the capital cycle* sharpens what a moat actually is and why it is so valuable.

Recall the mechanism. High returns destroy themselves by attracting outside capital that builds competing supply. So *anything that prevents the outside capital from entering* will let a business keep its high returns. That is exactly what a moat does. A genuine cost advantage means a new entrant, even with all the capital in the world, cannot produce profitably at the prices the incumbent can sustain — so the capital does not enter. A network effect means a new entrant's product is worth less to customers no matter how much capital it raises — so the capital does not enter, or enters and dies. Switching costs, regulatory barriers, scarce inputs, brand, patents — every species of moat works, at bottom, by raising the return that new capital would actually earn *below the threshold that makes entry worthwhile*. The capital looks at the industry, does the arithmetic on what it could actually earn against the incumbent's advantage, and stays away. The beacon of high returns is broadcasting, but the would-be competitors cannot answer it profitably.

This reframes the relationship between the capital cycle and quality investing, and reconciles two ideas that can seem to conflict. The capital cycle says "buy cyclical, capital-intensive, no-moat commodity industries at the bottom and sell them at the top," which sounds like the opposite of "buy wonderful moat businesses and hold them forever." But they are two faces of one coin. In a *no-moat* commodity industry, returns are governed by the capital cycle, so you play the cycle — buy when capital has fled, sell when it has flooded in. In a *deep-moat* industry, the capital cycle is *jammed* — the moat keeps the capital out, the high returns persist, and the right move is to own the business through the cycle and let it compound. The skill is in telling the two apart: is this high return a temporary cyclical peak that capital is about to compete away, or a durable moat-protected return that capital cannot touch? Misjudge that, and you either sell a great compounder too early (mistaking durable returns for a cyclical peak) or hold a commodity producer too long (mistaking a cyclical peak for a moat). The capital-flow tells help: if you see capital pouring into the industry and capacity ramping, the high returns are cyclical and doomed; if high returns persist *while* capital conspicuously fails to enter — few new entrants, no capacity additions despite the fat margins — you are looking at a real moat.

#### Worked example: the moat that jams the cycle

Set two businesses side by side, each earning a 25% ROIC today. **Cresswell Chemicals** makes commodity polyresin; **Halverson Labs** makes a patented specialty additive with no substitute, protected by patents and deep formulation know-how. Both look identical on a screen: 25% ROIC, far above their 9% WACC.

Now apply the capital cycle. Cresswell's 25% return is visible to every chemicals producer on earth, and polyresin plants are buildable by anyone with capital. So the capital floods in, capacity doubles, and within three years Cresswell's ROIC has reverted toward — and below — its 9% cost of capital. The 25% was a cyclical peak, and the capital cycle did exactly what it does.

Halverson's 25% return is just as visible, and the beacon is broadcasting just as loudly — but when a competitor with capital does the arithmetic, it finds that it cannot replicate Halverson's product (patents), cannot match its cost (know-how and scale in a niche), and would earn a *negative* spread trying to enter. So the capital stays away. Halverson keeps its 25% ROIC for a decade or more, compounding. The beacon shines, but the moat jams the signal, and the capital cycle never gets going.

*The same 25% ROIC means opposite things depending on one question — can outside capital answer the beacon? — and that question is the definition of a moat.*

## Combining the cycle with valuation: cheap plus fleeing capital

The capital cycle is a framework for *timing the supply side*, but timing alone is not investing — you also need to buy at a price that pays you. The two combine into the framework's most powerful setup: **cheap valuation plus a supply side that is healing.** Neither alone is enough.

Cheap alone is a trap. A commodity producer can trade at a low multiple of earnings and still be a terrible investment if those earnings are at a cyclical *peak* and capital is flooding in — because the "cheap" multiple is on earnings that are about to collapse. This is the single most common error in cyclical investing, and it has a name: buying a cyclical when it looks cheapest, which is precisely at the top, on peak earnings and a low trailing multiple. A steel mill or a chemical producer at the top of its cycle can trade at, say, 6× earnings — looking far cheaper than the market's 18× — and be a catastrophe, because the earnings halve and the "6×" was really 12× on normalized earnings, on a business about to destroy value. *Low multiples on peak earnings are the most dangerous numbers in cyclical investing.*

A healing supply side alone is not enough either, because even if the cycle is about to turn, you can overpay for the recovery. If a cyclical has already run up to a high multiple in *anticipation* of the recovery, much of the upside is priced in.

The setup that works is the *intersection*: a low valuation — ideally on normalized or trough earnings, or relative to the replacement cost of the assets — *combined with* the capital-cycle tells pointing toward a healing supply side (collapsed capex, shrinking capacity, dead competitors, no new entrants, capitulation sentiment). At that intersection, you are buying assets below their replacement cost, in an industry where the supply that would compete those assets down is being destroyed rather than built, at a price that assumes the misery is permanent. The valuation discipline and the cycle discipline reinforce each other: the cheap price gives you a margin of safety if your read on the cycle is early or wrong, and the healing supply side gives you the catalyst that the cheap price alone would lack. The detailed mechanics of valuing the cyclical itself — normalizing earnings across the cycle, using mid-cycle margins, anchoring to replacement cost — are exactly what the [valuing-the-hard-cases post](/blog/trading/equity-research/valuing-the-hard-cases-banks-insurers-reits-cyclicals) develops, and the capital cycle is the demand-and-supply context that tells you *where in the cycle* the earnings you are normalizing actually sit.

The capital cycle also connects, more deeply than it first appears, to [industry structure and the five forces](/blog/trading/equity-research/industry-structure-five-forces-for-investors). The threat of new entrants and the intensity of rivalry — two of Porter's forces — are precisely the forces the capital cycle dynamizes: they are not fixed properties of an industry but variables that *swing* with where the industry sits on the loop. At a top, the threat of entry is realized — entrants pour in — and rivalry intensifies as everyone fights to fill new plant; at a bottom, entry stops and rivalry eases as the survivors consolidate. Reading the five forces *through the cycle* tells you not just how attractive an industry's structure is on average, but which way that structure is currently moving.

## Common misconceptions

**"Strong demand means good returns."** This is the deepest and most common error, and the whole framework exists to correct it. Returns are set by the *balance* of supply and demand, and supply is the swinging variable. An industry with booming demand can earn miserable returns if capital has built even more supply — as the polyresin example showed, the same 5% demand growth produced a 15% ROIC or a 4% ROIC depending entirely on what capacity did. Conversely, an industry with flat or even declining demand can earn excellent returns if supply has shrunk faster (think of certain declining-but-consolidated industries where the last few rational players harvest a shrinking pie at high margins). Demand is the variable you should worry about *least*, because it is the one everyone else is already worrying about.

**"This high-return industry is a great business."** Sometimes — if it has a moat. But a high return in a no-moat, capital-intensive commodity industry is not a sign of a great business; it is a sign of a *cyclical peak* and a flashing warning that capital is about to flood in and compete it away. The mistake is to extrapolate the peak return as if it were durable. The diagnostic question is always: *can outside capital answer this beacon?* If yes, the high return is temporary; if no, you have found a moat.

**"Cheap cyclicals are good value."** Cheap cyclicals are *frequently* value traps, because they look cheapest exactly at the top, on peak earnings. A 6× P/E on a steel mill at the cycle's peak is far more expensive than it looks. The capital cycle tells you to do the opposite of what the trailing multiple suggests: be wary of cyclicals when they look cheap on high earnings with capital flooding in, and interested when they look *expensive or loss-making* on depressed earnings with capital fleeing. Cyclicals are cheap when they should be sold and dear when they should be bought, on trailing multiples — which is why you must normalize.

**"Management is being prudent by investing in growth at the top."** Investing heavily when returns are high *feels* prudent and is rewarded by the market in the moment, but it is usually the single most value-destructive thing a cyclical management can do — because it builds capacity at peak cost, into the glut, financing a larger capital base whose rent then swamps the trough's meager profit. The prudent capital allocator does the opposite: builds at the trough when assets are cheap and rivals are dead, and *returns* cash (buybacks, dividends) at the peak rather than plowing it into capacity. Most managements, driven by extrapolation and a growth bias, do exactly the wrong thing — which is why the rare counter-cyclical allocator is so valuable, a theme that runs straight into the [capital-allocation post](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage).

**"The cycle is unpredictable, so there is no edge."** The *timing* of the turns is genuinely hard to predict — you will rarely call the exact bottom or top. But the *supply pipeline* is far more knowable than the demand future, because capacity takes years to build and the projects are announced in advance. The edge is not in market timing; it is in doing the patient, unglamorous work of mapping where capital is flowing and what that will do to supply two or three years out, and then having the temperament to act against the crowd and the patience to wait for the cycle to do its work.

## How it shows up in real markets

The capital cycle is not a theory in search of evidence; it is a description of what has actually happened, over and over, in the most capital-intensive corners of the market. A few well-known examples make it vivid. (Figures here are illustrative magnitudes meant to convey the shape of each cycle, not precise historical quotes.)

**Shipping (dry bulk and containers).** This is the textbook capital cycle, because ships take a couple of years to build, last for decades, and are ordered en masse by owners extrapolating high freight rates. In the mid-2000s, soaring demand (largely from Chinese commodity imports) sent dry-bulk freight rates to extraordinary highs; ship owners, flush with profits and cheap credit, ordered new vessels at a furious pace. The orderbook ballooned to a large fraction of the existing fleet. Those ships were delivered into the post-2008 demand slump — supply arriving exactly when demand fell — and freight rates collapsed, staying depressed for years as the glut of long-lived ships could not be removed. Owners who had ordered at the top went bankrupt; the few who had stayed disciplined, or who bought distressed vessels near the bottom, did well as the glut slowly worked off and rates eventually recovered. The whole arc — high rates, an ordering frenzy, a delivery glut, a multi-year bust, consolidation, recovery — is the capital cycle in its purest form.

**Memory semiconductors (DRAM and NAND).** Memory is a commodity (a bit is a bit), fabs cost billions and take years to build, and the makers have historically all expanded at once when prices and margins are high. The result has been a long history of brutal boom-bust cycles: a shortage sends prices and margins soaring, the makers race to add fab capacity, the new capacity arrives together and creates a glut, prices crash (sometimes below cash cost), the weak makers exit or get absorbed, and the cycle eventually turns again on the consolidated, more disciplined survivors. Over decades the memory industry consolidated from dozens of players to a small handful — a textbook capital-cycle consolidation that has made the cycles somewhat less violent than they once were, as fewer, more disciplined hands now control supply.

![A real cycle traced as a DRAM price curve booming gluting and recovering, with an industry capex curve that chases its own tail, showing the boom pulling in fab investment whose output creates the next glut and the recovery only coming after capex is cut](/imgs/blogs/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition-6.png)

The chart traces the memory cycle in the two variables that drive it. The solid curve is the DRAM price: a spike as a shortage bites, then a steep collapse as new fab supply lands, then a recovery once capex has been cut. The dashed curve is industry capex, and notice how it *lags* the price — capex ramps up *after* the price has spiked (the makers chasing the boom), and the resulting capacity arrives a year or two later to create exactly the glut that crashes the price. The boom tag, the glut tag, and the recovery tag mark the phases. The figure's lesson is the cycle's central one: the investment that destroys the price was triggered by the price, and the recovery comes only after the investment is cut — the supply side, not demand, drives the whole thing.

**Commodity chemicals, mining, oil and gas, and steel** all run the same loop, with different clock speeds set by how long their capacity takes to build. Mining is slow — a new copper mine can take a decade from discovery to production — so its cycles are long and its supply responses sluggish, which means shortages and gluts can both persist for years. Shale oil, by contrast, has a fast cycle, because shale wells can be drilled and brought online in months rather than years, so capital floods in and out quickly and the cycle is more compressed. Commodity chemicals sit in between. In every case the framework is the same: watch industry capex and capacity, not just end-demand, and lean against the flow of capital.

**The link to commodity-trading houses** is worth drawing, because firms like [Glencore, Vitol and Trafigura](/blog/trading/finance/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura) live and breathe the capital cycle from the inside. Their business is, in large part, intermediating the physical mismatches between supply and demand that the capital cycle creates — moving, storing, and financing commodities through gluts and shortages. They are among the most sophisticated readers of the supply side in the world, precisely because their profits depend on understanding where capacity is being built and where it is being shut, often well before the equity market catches on. Watching how the great trading houses position — building storage and inventory when a glut is depressing prices, releasing it into a shortage — is itself a window into where the capital cycle sits.

**The counter-cyclical operator** is the hero of the framework, and the real markets reward it richly when it appears. The disciplined producer who *builds capacity at the trough* — when plant and acquisitions are cheap, when rivals are dead or retrenching, when no one else is investing — and who *returns cash rather than building at the peak*, captures the next up-cycle with low-cost capacity while the herd is stuck with expensive plant built at the top. This is rare, because it is so hard temperamentally and so contrary to the growth-extrapolating instincts of most management teams and the short-term incentives most operate under (the link to [management incentives and skin in the game](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage) is direct: a manager paid on growth or short-term earnings will build at the top; an owner-operator thinking in decades will build at the bottom).

![A chart showing the industry price cycle as a solid curve and a counter-cyclical operators smart capex as a dashed curve running opposite to it, heavy at the trough when assets are cheap and light at the peak when rivals are building, with tags for the trap build here and harvest here](/imgs/blogs/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition-7.png)

The final chart shows what disciplined counter-cyclical allocation looks like against the cycle. The solid curve is the industry price (and roughly, the return), running through its trough and peak. The dashed curve is a smart operator's capex, and it runs *opposite* to the price: heavy at the trough — building capacity when assets are cheap and rivals have fled — and light at the peak, when everyone else is building and assets are expensive. The "build here" tag sits at the price trough; the "harvest here" tag sits at the peak, where the operator reaps high prices from capacity it added cheaply; and "the trap" tag marks where the herd builds — at the top, into the coming glut. This is the inverse of what feels natural, and the inverse of what most companies do, which is exactly why it works: the operator who builds when it is cheap and frightening, and harvests when it is dear and exciting, accumulates a cost and timing advantage that compounds across the cycle.

#### Worked example: the counter-cyclical allocator versus the herd

Make the counter-cyclical advantage concrete with two polyresin producers across one cycle. **Cresswell** follows the herd; **Northwind Materials** is a disciplined counter-cyclical allocator. Both start with \$1,000M of invested capital and a 20% ROIC at the top.

- **At the top (years 1–3),** Cresswell, seeing 20% returns, invests \$500M in new capacity at peak construction cost, lifting its capital to \$1,500M. Northwind, seeing the same 20% but reading the capacity pipeline, *declines to build* — instead it returns \$300M to shareholders via buybacks and keeps its capital at \$700M (its base shrinks as buybacks reduce equity).
- **At the bottom (years 5–8),** the glut hits. Cresswell, with its bloated \$1,500M base earning just 4%, is destroying value (NOPAT \$60M against a \$135M rent) and has no spare cash to invest. Northwind, having stockpiled cash and avoided the peak build, now *buys* a bankrupt rival's modern plant for \$200M — a fraction of its \$500M replacement cost — lifting its capital to \$900M, all of it acquired cheaply.
- **At the recovery (years 8–10),** prices and returns recover to a 16% ROIC. Cresswell earns 16% on its \$1,500M (NOPAT \$240M, economic profit \$105M) — recovering, but saddled with capacity bought at peak cost. Northwind earns 16% on its \$900M of *cheaply acquired* capacity — and because that capacity was bought at a fraction of replacement cost, its *economic* return on the actual dollars spent is far higher, perhaps 30%+; NOPAT of \$144M against a rent of just \$81M, economic profit of \$63M on a far smaller, far cheaper base. Per dollar invested, Northwind has crushed Cresswell across the full cycle.

*The counter-cyclical allocator does not beat the herd by predicting the cycle perfectly; it beats the herd by doing the opposite of the comfortable thing at each phase — returning cash at the top, deploying it at the bottom — and letting the cycle's own swings do the work.*

## When this matters, and further reading

The capital cycle is most powerful in **capital-intensive, commodity-like industries** — chemicals, shipping, mining, energy, memory, steel, autos, airlines, paper — where capacity is expensive and slow to build, products are undifferentiated, and returns therefore swing on the supply-demand balance. It is least relevant for **moat-protected businesses** where the cycle is jammed — the high-return software franchise, the branded consumer staple, the network-effect platform — where the right frame is durable compounding, not cyclical timing. The first job, when you meet a high-return business, is to decide which world you are in: is this return cyclical (about to be competed away by incoming capital) or durable (protected by a moat that keeps the capital out)? Everything follows from that judgment.

The practical discipline distills to a short checklist. *Watch supply, not just demand.* Map the industry's capex and capacity pipeline — across the whole industry, not one company — and ask what it will do to the supply-demand balance two or three years out. *Read the capital-flow tells.* A capex boom, an issuance wave, a crowd of new entrants and euphoric capacity plans mark a top; collapsed capex below depreciation, bankruptcies, consolidation and capitulation mark a bottom. *Lean against the flow.* Be wary of the industry capital is flooding into; get interested in the one capital is fleeing — but only where the supply side is genuinely healing, not merely cheap. *Combine the cycle with valuation.* The setup that pays is cheap-on-normalized-earnings *plus* a mending supply side. And *prize the counter-cyclical allocator* — the management that builds at the trough and returns cash at the peak — because over a full cycle it compounds an advantage the herd cannot match.

Master the capital cycle and you will never again look at a high return the same way — you will see it for what it is: a beacon, calling the very capital that will, unless a moat stands in the way, compete it into the ground.
