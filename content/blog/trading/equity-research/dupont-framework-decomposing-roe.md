> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A single return-on-equity number hides where the return came from; the DuPont framework splits ROE into its drivers so you can tell a genuinely profitable business from one that simply borrowed a lot.
>
> - **ROE = net margin × asset turnover × equity multiplier** — profitability × efficiency × leverage. Two firms with the *same* 20% ROE can be built from completely different combinations of these three, and the combination is what tells you the quality.
> - **Net margin and asset turnover are signs of business quality; the equity multiplier is a sign of risk.** A high ROE driven by margin and turnover is earned; a high ROE driven by the equity multiplier is *borrowed*, and it comes bundled with the risk of the debt that produced it.
> - **The 5-step extended DuPont** splits net margin further into tax burden × interest burden × operating margin, so a one-time tax cut or a refinancing can never masquerade as a real improvement in the operating business.
> - **DuPont is at its most powerful year-over-year and against peers.** A flat ROE can hide a collapsing business when a shrinking equity base (from a buyback) quietly lifts leverage to offset deteriorating operations — only the decomposition reveals it.
> - **ROIC is the leverage-neutral cousin.** Where ROE rewards borrowing, ROIC strips financing out entirely, so the two read together separate genuine business quality from the financing bet layered on top.

Two companies report the identical headline: a 20% return on equity. By the single most-quoted measure of how well a company earns for its owners, they are twins. An investor skimming a screen sees two businesses each turning every dollar of shareholders' equity into twenty cents of annual profit, and moves on, treating them as equivalent. That investor has just made one of the most common and most expensive mistakes in all of equity analysis, because those two 20% ROEs can be produced by completely different machines — one a genuinely excellent business, the other a mediocre business wearing a costume of debt.

The number that conceals this is not lying, exactly; it is just *compressed*. Return on equity packs three distinct facts about a business into one figure, and the act of compression throws away precisely the information you need to judge quality. Is the company earning its return because it makes a fat profit on every sale? Because it spins its assets quickly and wrings a lot of revenue out of a small base? Or because it has borrowed heavily, so a thin operating return gets magnified onto a small sliver of equity? These are three radically different stories — *profitability*, *efficiency*, and *leverage* — and a single ROE figure can be assembled from any blend of them. Reading ROE without decomposing it is like reading a final exam score without knowing which questions the student got right.

![A tree splitting a single twenty percent return on equity into three branches for net margin, asset turnover, and equity multiplier, each labeled profitability, efficiency, and leverage](/imgs/blogs/dupont-framework-decomposing-roe-1.png)

The figure above is the entire idea of this post in one picture, and it is worth fixing in your mind before we define anything precisely. A company's return on equity is the *product* of three independent levers. The first, **net margin**, measures profitability — how many cents of profit survive on each dollar of sales. The second, **asset turnover**, measures efficiency — how many dollars of sales the company generates from each dollar of assets. The third, **the equity multiplier**, measures leverage — how many dollars of assets the company controls for each dollar of its owners' equity. Multiply the three together and the revenue and asset terms cancel, leaving exactly net income over equity, which is ROE. This identity, born inside the DuPont chemical company in the 1920s as a way to manage its sprawling divisions, is the most useful single decomposition in financial analysis. The rest of this post is about what each lever means, which ones signal *quality* and which signal *risk*, and how to use the split to see things a raw ROE can never show you.

This builds directly on [returns on capital — ROIC, ROE, ROA](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa), which introduced ROE and warned that it can be flattered by leverage. DuPont is the tool that makes that warning quantitative: it isolates *exactly how much* of an ROE is operating quality and how much is borrowed. We will keep building **Northwind Industries**, the fictional manufacturer from those posts, and introduce a levered twin called **Riverstone** to make the comparison concrete. By the end you will be able to take any company's ROE, split it three (or five) ways, compare two firms with identical ROEs and tell which is the better business, and watch a year-over-year decomposition reveal a deterioration that the headline number was busy hiding.

## Foundations: the three levers, defined from zero

Before we decompose anything, we need each piece defined precisely, because the whole power of DuPont comes from the levers being *independent* — each measures something different about the business, and confusing them defeats the purpose. Every term below appears constantly in real analysis; getting them exact now is what makes the decomposition click.

**Return on equity (ROE).** The starting point. ROE is net income — the bottom-line profit that belongs to shareholders — divided by shareholders' equity, the owners' stake in the business. It answers: *for every dollar the owners have tied up in this company, how many cents of profit did it earn this year?* If Northwind earns \$80 million of net income on \$400 million of equity, its ROE is \$80M ÷ \$400M = 20%. ROE is the headline because it speaks directly to the owner: it is *their* return on *their* capital. But as we will see, it bundles together the business and the financing, and the bundling is the problem.

**Net margin (profitability).** Net margin is net income divided by revenue — the fraction of each sales dollar that survives all the way to the bottom line after every cost, including interest and tax. If Northwind earns \$80 million of net income on \$1,200 million of sales, its net margin is \$80M ÷ \$1,200M = 6.7%. Net margin is the **profitability** lever: it captures pricing power, cost discipline, and the basic economics of a sale. A high net margin means the business keeps a lot of each dollar it sells; a low one means it operates on thin slices. We covered the full ladder of margins in [profitability and margins — gross, operating, net](/blog/trading/equity-research/profitability-margins-gross-operating-net); here, net margin is the single rung DuPont uses.

**Asset turnover (efficiency).** Asset turnover is revenue divided by total assets — how many dollars of sales the company generates from each dollar of assets it controls. If Northwind produces \$1,200 million of revenue on \$1,200 million of assets, its asset turnover is \$1,200M ÷ \$1,200M = 1.0x. This is the **efficiency** lever: it measures how hard the company's asset base is working. A supermarket spins its assets several times a year (high turnover); a power utility, with billions tied up in plants, spins them a fraction of a time (low turnover). Asset turnover answers a question margins ignore entirely: *how much capital did you have to tie up to generate those sales?*

**The equity multiplier (leverage).** The equity multiplier is total assets divided by shareholders' equity — how many dollars of assets the company controls for each dollar of its owners' equity. If Northwind has \$1,200 million of assets standing on \$400 million of equity, its equity multiplier is \$1,200M ÷ \$400M = 3.0x, meaning every dollar of equity supports three dollars of assets. Where does the other two dollars come from? From liabilities — debt and other obligations. So the equity multiplier is the **leverage** lever: a multiplier of 1.0x means a debt-free company (assets = equity); a multiplier of 3.0x means two-thirds of the asset base is funded by creditors. The higher the multiplier, the more the company is leaning on borrowed money, and the more both its returns *and* its risks are amplified.

Now the magic, which is nothing more than arithmetic. Write the three out as fractions and multiply them:

$$\text{ROE} = \underbrace{\frac{\text{Net income}}{\text{Revenue}}}_{\text{net margin}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Revenue}}{\text{Assets}}}_{\text{asset turnover}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Assets}}{\text{Equity}}}_{\text{equity multiplier}}$$

Revenue cancels against revenue, assets cancel against assets, and you are left with net income over equity — which is exactly ROE. The identity is *always* true, for every company, in every year, by construction. That is what makes it a safe lens: you are not approximating anything, you are simply rewriting one ratio as the product of three. The insight is not in the algebra but in what the three pieces *mean* — because each one is moved by a different part of the business, decomposing ROE tells you *which* part is producing the return.

**Profitability and efficiency are signs of the business; leverage is a sign of the financing.** Hold onto this distinction, because it is the spine of the entire post. Net margin and asset turnover both describe the *operating business itself* — how good its products are, how disciplined its costs, how productively it uses its assets. Multiply just those two together and you get **return on assets (ROA)** — net income over assets — the return the business earns before any financing is layered on. The equity multiplier is different in kind: it describes how the business is *funded*, not how it operates. It can lift ROE without the business changing at all. That is the crack in ROE that DuPont pries open.

## The 3-step decomposition: profitability × efficiency × leverage

Let us put real numbers through the three-lever identity for Northwind and see what it reveals. Here is Northwind's picture for the year:

```
INCOME STATEMENT (this year)
  Revenue                          $1,200M
  Net income                          $80M    -> net margin = 6.7%

BALANCE SHEET (year-end)
  Total assets                     $1,200M    -> asset turnover = 1.0x
  Shareholders' equity               $400M    -> equity multiplier = 3.0x
```

Run the three levers: net margin 6.7% × asset turnover 1.0x × equity multiplier 3.0x = **20%**, which matches the direct calculation of \$80M net income ÷ \$400M equity = 20%. The decomposition has not changed the answer — it has *explained* it. Northwind's 20% ROE comes from a modest 6.7% profit on sales, an average 1.0x asset turnover, and a 3.0x equity multiplier that is doing a meaningful share of the lifting. Already you know more than the headline told you: a third of Northwind's asset base is funded by owners and two-thirds by creditors, and that leverage is contributing materially to the 20%.

The deeper point is that the *same* 20% could have been assembled from radically different levers. A debt-free company (equity multiplier 1.0x) would need net margin × turnover = 20% on its own — say a 13.3% margin at 1.5x turnover — to reach the same ROE, and that company would be a far better and safer business, because all of its return is earned by the operations rather than borrowed. The whole art of DuPont is reading the *mix*. The headline says how much; the decomposition says how, and *how* is what determines whether the 20% is durable and safe or fragile and borrowed.

A useful way to internalise the levers is to notice that they answer three different "per dollar" questions. Net margin: *per dollar of sales, how much profit?* Asset turnover: *per dollar of assets, how much sales?* Equity multiplier: *per dollar of equity, how many dollars of assets?* Chain those three "per dollar" ratios together and the units telescope — profit per sale, times sales per asset, times asset per equity, equals profit per equity — which is ROE. Each link is a distinct managerial competence (or, for the multiplier, a financing choice), and DuPont lets you grade each one separately.

It also helps to see where each lever lives in the financial statements, because that tells you what kind of evidence moves it. Net margin is an *income-statement* ratio: it is moved by everything between revenue and net income — pricing, the cost of goods, overhead, interest, tax. Asset turnover *spans both statements*: revenue from the income statement over assets from the balance sheet, so it links how much the company sells to how much capital it has parked on the balance sheet to sell it. The equity multiplier is a *pure balance-sheet* ratio: assets over equity, with no reference to the income statement at all, which is exactly why it can change (through a buyback, a dividend, or new borrowing) without a single thing happening to the operating business. Knowing which statement each lever draws from is half the work of diagnosing a move: an operating change shows up in the income-statement and cross-statement levers, while a pure financing change shows up only in the balance-sheet lever.

#### Worked example: Northwind's 20% ROE, decomposed three ways

Take Northwind's reported figures: \$80M net income, \$1,200M revenue, \$1,200M total assets, \$400M equity. **Net margin** = \$80M ÷ \$1,200M = **6.7%**. **Asset turnover** = \$1,200M ÷ \$1,200M = **1.0x**. **Equity multiplier** = \$1,200M ÷ \$400M = **3.0x**. Multiply: 0.0667 × 1.0 × 3.0 = **0.20 = 20% ROE**, exactly matching \$80M ÷ \$400M. Now ask what each lever contributed. Without leverage (multiplier set to 1.0x), the same operations would produce an ROE of 6.7% × 1.0 = 6.7% — this is Northwind's **ROA**, the return the *business* earns on its assets. The jump from 6.7% to 20% — a tripling — is entirely the work of the 3.0x equity multiplier. So roughly two-thirds of Northwind's headline ROE is borrowed, not earned on the operations.

*The decomposition leaves the ROE unchanged but reveals that most of it comes from leverage, which a reader of the headline number alone would never have guessed.*

## Two firms, one ROE, opposite engines

Now the demonstration that makes the whole framework worth learning. We will build two companies that report the *identical* 20% ROE and show, by decomposition, that they are nothing alike. Meet **Riverstone**, a competitor to Northwind. Both post a 20% return on equity. An investor comparing them on ROE alone would call it a tie. DuPont calls it a rout.

![A before and after comparison showing Northwind reaching twenty percent ROE on margin and turnover versus Riverstone reaching the same twenty percent mostly through a high equity multiplier](/imgs/blogs/dupont-framework-decomposing-roe-3.png)

To make the contrast sharp, let us hold their asset turnover identical at 1.5x and vary the other two levers, so the difference is purely *profitability versus leverage*. **Northwind** earns a healthy 10% net margin, spins its assets 1.5x, and uses a modest 1.33x equity multiplier (very little debt). Its ROE is 10% × 1.5 × 1.33 = **20%**, and almost all of that return is *earned*: a high margin on each sale, assets working efficiently, and only a sliver of leverage. **Riverstone** earns a thin 4% net margin — a quarter of Northwind's profitability — spins its assets at the same 1.5x, but stacks on a 3.33x equity multiplier, meaning roughly 70% of its assets are funded by debt. Its ROE is 4% × 1.5 × 3.33 = **20%**, the *same headline* — but built almost entirely on borrowed money compensating for a feeble operating profit.

These two businesses could not be more different, and every difference matters to an owner. Northwind makes real money on what it sells and barely needs to borrow; if a recession cuts everyone's sales, it has a fat margin to absorb the blow and little debt to service. Riverstone makes almost nothing on each sale and survives only by leveraging that thin profit onto a small equity base; if a recession cuts its sales, its already-thin margin can vanish entirely, and it still owes interest on a mountain of debt that does not shrink when revenue does. Riverstone's 20% is a high-wire act; Northwind's 20% is a walk on solid ground. **The ROE is the same; the quality and the risk are worlds apart** — and only the decomposition shows it.

This is the single most important lesson of DuPont: *a high ROE is a question, not an answer*. When you see 20%, the next move is always to decompose it. If the 20% comes from margin and turnover, you have found a candidate for a genuinely good business. If it comes from the equity multiplier, you have found a company that has borrowed its way to a flattering number, and you should immediately ask whether the operating business underneath could survive without the leverage — and what happens to that 20% if the debt ever has to come down.

#### Worked example: Northwind versus Riverstone at an identical 20% ROE

**Northwind:** net margin 10%, asset turnover 1.5x, equity multiplier 1.33x. ROE = 0.10 × 1.5 × 1.33 = **0.1995 ≈ 20%**. Its ROA (margin × turnover) = 0.10 × 1.5 = **15%** — the business earns a strong 15% on its assets *before* any leverage, so the jump to 20% comes from just 0.33 turns of a modest multiplier. **Riverstone:** net margin 4%, asset turnover 1.5x, equity multiplier 3.33x. ROE = 0.04 × 1.5 × 3.33 = **0.1998 ≈ 20%**. Its ROA = 0.04 × 1.5 = **6%** — the business earns only 6% on its assets, and the leap from 6% to 20% is manufactured entirely by piling on debt until the multiplier hits 3.33x. Same 20% ROE; one built on a 15% ROA, the other on a 6% ROA tripled by leverage. If you could buy only one of these businesses, you would pay far more for Northwind, because its return is real and its balance sheet is safe.

*Identical ROEs can sit on completely different ROAs, and the gap between ROE and ROA is exactly the leverage the equity multiplier is supplying — which is the first thing to check whenever an ROE looks good.*

## The 5-step extended DuPont: splitting profitability further

The three-step decomposition is powerful, but it leaves net margin as a single lump, and net margin itself is the product of several distinct forces. A company's bottom-line margin reflects how profitable its operations are, how much it pays in interest on its debt, and how much it pays in tax — and these are very different things. A firm can boost its net margin by genuinely improving operations, by refinancing debt at a lower rate, or by simply enjoying a lower tax rate, and an investor should care enormously about *which*. The **5-step extended DuPont** pulls net margin apart into three pieces so you can tell them apart.

![A vertical tree splitting return on equity into five levers tax burden, interest burden, operating margin, asset turnover, and equity multiplier](/imgs/blogs/dupont-framework-decomposing-roe-2.png)

The extended formula splits net margin (net income ÷ revenue) into three ratios that, multiplied together, reconstruct it:

$$\text{ROE} = \underbrace{\frac{\text{Net income}}{\text{Pre-tax income}}}_{\text{tax burden}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Pre-tax income}}{\text{EBIT}}}_{\text{interest burden}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Revenue}}}_{\text{operating margin}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Revenue}}{\text{Assets}}}_{\text{asset turnover}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\text{Assets}}{\text{Equity}}}_{\text{equity multiplier}}$$

Each new piece isolates one influence on the bottom line. **Tax burden** = net income ÷ pre-tax income is the fraction of pre-tax profit that survives tax; a value of 0.75 means a 25% effective tax rate (the company keeps 75 cents of every pre-tax dollar). **Interest burden** = pre-tax income ÷ EBIT is the fraction of operating profit that survives after interest; a value of 0.90 means interest expense eats 10% of operating profit before tax. **Operating margin** = EBIT ÷ revenue is the profitability of the core operations *before* financing and tax — the cleanest single measure of how good the business is at turning sales into operating profit. The last two levers — asset turnover and the equity multiplier — are unchanged from the three-step version.

Why bother with five pieces instead of three? Because **the extended decomposition separates the operating business (operating margin × asset turnover) from the financing and tax effects (interest burden, tax burden, equity multiplier) with surgical precision.** Operating margin and asset turnover are the *business*. Interest burden and the equity multiplier both reflect the *debt* (one its cost, one its quantity). Tax burden reflects the *tax regime*. When ROE moves, the five-step split tells you which of these moved — and that is the difference between an analyst who knows that earnings improved and one who knows *why*. A rise in ROE driven by operating margin is a real improvement in the business; a rise driven by a falling tax burden is a windfall that may reverse; a rise driven by the equity multiplier is just more leverage. They look identical in the headline and could not be more different in what they tell you about the future.

#### Worked example: a tax-rate drop that flattered ROE

Northwind reports a jump in ROE from 18% to 20% and management calls it operating progress. The five-step decomposition tells the truth. **Last year:** tax burden 0.70 (a 30% tax rate), interest burden 0.90, operating margin 14.8%, asset turnover 1.0x, equity multiplier 2.0x. ROE = 0.70 × 0.90 × 0.148 × 1.0 × 2.0 = **0.1865 ≈ 18.6%**. **This year:** the only thing that changed is the tax rate, which fell from 30% to 25%, lifting the tax burden from 0.70 to 0.75. Every operating lever is identical: interest burden 0.90, operating margin 14.8%, turnover 1.0x, multiplier 2.0x. ROE = 0.75 × 0.90 × 0.148 × 1.0 × 2.0 = **0.1998 ≈ 20%**. The ROE rose two full points, but operating margin — the actual business — did not budge. The entire improvement came from a lower tax rate, a one-time gift from the government that says nothing about how well Northwind makes and sells pumps, and that will not repeat next year.

*A rising ROE feels like good news, but the five-step split can reveal that a tax cut or a refinancing — not the operating business — did all the work, which is exactly the kind of low-quality improvement a careful analyst discounts.*

## Year-over-year DuPont: when a flat ROE hides a sick business

DuPont is useful on a single year, but it becomes indispensable when you run it across *two* years and watch the levers move. The reason is subtle and important: because ROE is a product of three (or five) terms, **a deterioration in one lever can be perfectly masked by an improvement in another, leaving the headline ROE unchanged while the business quietly rots.** The decomposition is the only way to see the offsetting moves. Nowhere is this trap more common than with share buybacks, which shrink equity and mechanically lift the equity multiplier — propping up ROE even as the operating business weakens.

![A matrix comparing Northwind's DuPont levers across two years showing asset turnover collapsing from one point zero to zero point eight while the equity multiplier rises from two to two point five, holding ROE flat at twenty percent](/imgs/blogs/dupont-framework-decomposing-roe-5.png)

Watch what happens to Northwind across two years. In **Year 1**, its levers are net margin 10%, asset turnover 1.0x, equity multiplier 2.0x, for an ROE of 10% × 1.0 × 2.0 = **20%**. In **Year 2**, the headline ROE is *still 20%* — and a casual observer concludes nothing changed. But decompose it. Net margin held at 10%. Asset turnover, however, *collapsed* from 1.0x to 0.8x: sales fell while the asset base stayed put, a clear sign the business is generating less revenue from the same capital — softening demand, building inventory, idling factories. That should have dragged ROE down to 10% × 0.8 × 2.0 = 16%. So why is the headline still 20%? Because over the same year, Northwind spent cash buying back its own stock, shrinking shareholders' equity and lifting the equity multiplier from 2.0x to 2.5x. The leverage increase (10% × 0.8 × 2.5 = 20%) exactly offset the turnover collapse. The ROE looks identical; the business is visibly deteriorating; and the gap was filled by financial engineering, not operations.

This is the year-over-year trap in its purest form, and it is everywhere in real markets. A management team facing a weakening business has a powerful, perfectly legal tool to keep ROE flat: borrow money or spend cash to repurchase shares, shrinking the equity denominator and inflating the multiplier. The reported ROE holds steady, the headline reassures investors, and the rot in asset turnover (or margin) stays hidden — *unless someone decomposes the ROE and notices that the operating levers fell while the leverage lever rose to cover them*. Running DuPont across several years, lever by lever, is how you catch this. A stable ROE built on rising leverage and falling turnover is a warning, not a comfort.

#### Worked example: a buyback masking a turnover collapse

**Year 1:** net margin 10%, asset turnover 1.0x, equity multiplier 2.0x → ROE = 0.10 × 1.0 × 2.0 = **20%**. Concretely, say \$1,000M revenue, \$100M net income, \$1,000M assets, \$500M equity. **Year 2:** revenue falls to \$800M while assets stay at \$1,000M, so asset turnover drops to \$800M ÷ \$1,000M = **0.8x**. Net income, at the same 10% margin, falls to \$80M. On the *old* \$500M equity, that would be an ROE of \$80M ÷ \$500M = **16%** — a clear two-point-plus decline. But during Year 2 Northwind bought back stock, cutting equity from \$500M to \$400M, which lifts the equity multiplier to \$1,000M ÷ \$400M = **2.5x**. Now ROE = \$80M ÷ \$400M = **20%** — flat versus Year 1. Decomposed: 10% × 0.8 × 2.5 = 20%. The turnover collapse from 1.0x to 0.8x (a 20% drop in operating efficiency) was masked perfectly by the multiplier rising from 2.0x to 2.5x. The headline says "no change"; the levers say "the business shrank and the buyback hid it."

*A flat ROE across two years can conceal a real deterioration when a buyback shrinks equity and lifts leverage to offset falling operations, which is why DuPont must be read lever by lever, not just at the headline.*

## The leverage lever: how borrowing inflates ROE without improving the business

We have seen the equity multiplier do heavy lifting in every example. Now let us isolate it completely and watch what leverage does to ROE while the underlying business is held perfectly constant — because this is the mechanism behind the most common way ROE deceives.

![A bar chart showing return on equity climbing from twelve to twenty percent as the equity multiplier rises, while a flat dashed line shows return on invested capital staying at twelve percent](/imgs/blogs/dupont-framework-decomposing-roe-4.png)

Imagine a business whose *operations* earn a steady **12% return on invested capital** — a real, leverage-neutral measure of the business's quality that we will define properly in a moment. Hold the operations fixed and vary only the financing. With **no debt** (equity multiplier 1.0x), the owners get exactly the business's return: ROE = 12%. Now borrow. Each dollar borrowed at, say, a 6% after-tax cost is invested in operations earning 12%, and the 6-point spread accrues entirely to the equity holders, because the lenders only ever collect their fixed 6%. At an equity multiplier of **1.5x**, ROE climbs to about 15%; at **2.0x**, to 18%; at **2.33x**, to 20%. The bars rise steadily — and yet the dashed line, the 12% return on the actual business, *never moves*. **The entire increase in ROE is borrowed.** It is the spread between what the business earns and what the debt costs, multiplied by how much debt is piled on, and it is paid for in risk, not skill.

This is the crucial intuition the equity multiplier makes visible: **a high equity multiplier inflates ROE without improving the business one iota.** The operations are identical at every bar; only the financing changes; and ROE marches from 12% to 20% purely on leverage. That extra eight points of ROE is not a sign the company got better at making or selling anything — it is a sign the company borrowed more. And leverage cuts both ways. The same multiplier that magnifies a 12% return into 20% in good times will magnify a *loss* into a catastrophe in bad times, and the interest must be paid in every year, boom or bust. A company that cannot make its debt payments goes bankrupt no matter how fine its products are. So the leverage component of ROE is a higher *return* bought with higher *risk* — and the return ratio, read alone, hides the risk entirely. When you decompose an ROE and find the equity multiplier doing most of the work, you have not found a great business; you have found a leveraged one, and the next question is whether the balance sheet can survive a bad year.

#### Worked example: leverage lifting ROE from 12% to 20% while ROIC stays flat

Take a business with a constant 12% after-tax operating return on its capital and an after-tax cost of debt of 6%, and fund it at four leverage levels. The levered-ROE relationship is ROE ≈ ROIC + (ROIC − cost of debt) × (Debt ÷ Equity), where Debt ÷ Equity = equity multiplier − 1. **Equity multiplier 1.0x** (no debt, D/E = 0): ROE = 12% + (12% − 6%) × 0 = **12%**. **Multiplier 1.5x** (D/E = 0.5): ROE = 12% + 6% × 0.5 = **15%**. **Multiplier 2.0x** (D/E = 1.0): ROE = 12% + 6% × 1.0 = **18%**. **Multiplier 2.33x** (D/E = 1.33): ROE = 12% + 6% × 1.33 = **20%**. The ROIC sat at 12% the entire time — the business never changed — yet ROE rose from 12% to 20% purely by borrowing more. The eight extra points of ROE are the 6-point spread captured on a growing pile of debt, and every one of them carries the risk of that debt.

*Leverage manufactures ROE out of the spread between the business's return and the cost of its debt, so a rising ROE on a flat ROIC is borrowed return, not earned quality — and it is paid for in risk.*

## ROIC: the leverage-neutral cousin DuPont points you toward

The previous figure quietly introduced the antidote to ROE's leverage problem: a return measure that *ignores* financing entirely, so leverage cannot inflate it. That measure is **return on invested capital (ROIC)**, and understanding how it relates to the DuPont decomposition closes the loop on the whole framework.

Recall that DuPont splits ROE into operating levers (net margin, or operating margin × the two burdens, times asset turnover) and a financing lever (the equity multiplier). ROIC is precisely *what you get when you keep the operating levers and throw the financing lever away*. Formally, ROIC is after-tax operating profit (NOPAT — operating profit taxed as if the firm had no debt) divided by invested capital (debt plus equity, the total capital actually put to work). Both its numerator and its denominator are deliberately blind to the debt-versus-equity split: the numerator is profit *before* interest, and the denominator counts *all* capital regardless of source. As a result, ROIC measures the *business* — how well the operations turn invested capital into operating profit — with the financing decision stripped out. The full construction lives in [returns on capital — ROIC, ROE, ROA](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa); here the point is simply how it relates to the DuPont levers.

The relationship is clean and worth memorising: **ROE ≈ ROIC + leverage effect.** ROE equals the business's leverage-neutral return (ROIC) plus a bonus (or penalty) from leverage — the spread between ROIC and the after-tax cost of debt, times the debt-to-equity ratio. When ROIC exceeds the cost of debt, the leverage term is positive and ROE rises above ROIC (the eight points we just watched). When ROIC falls *below* the cost of debt, the term goes negative and leverage *destroys* ROE — borrowing to fund a business that earns less than its interest rate torches the owners' return. So the two metrics, read together, separate quality from financing perfectly: **ROIC tells you how good the business is; the gap between ROE and ROIC tells you how much leverage management has bolted on, and whether it is helping or hurting.** This is why sophisticated investors rarely look at ROE without also looking at ROIC. ROE alone confuses a great business with a heavily borrowed mediocre one; the pair tells them apart.

#### Worked example: ROE of 20% on an ROIC of just 12%

Riverstone reports a 20% ROE and management touts it as evidence of a high-quality business. Compute its ROIC and the story collapses. Suppose Riverstone's operations generate \$72M of NOPAT (after-tax operating profit) on \$600M of invested capital, so its **ROIC = \$72M ÷ \$600M = 12%**. Its after-tax cost of debt is 6%, and it runs a debt-to-equity ratio of about 1.33 (an equity multiplier near 2.33x). The leverage effect adds (12% − 6%) × 1.33 = **8 points**, lifting ROE to roughly 12% + 8% = **20%**. So the gleaming 20% ROE sits on a merely-average 12% ROIC; eight of those twenty points are borrowed. Compare a debt-free rival with the *same* 12% ROIC: its ROE would be exactly 12%, because with no leverage ROE equals ROIC. Riverstone's 20% is not a better business than the rival's 12% — it is the *same-quality* business with more debt strapped on. The ROIC sees through the leverage that the ROE was busy hiding.

*ROE rewards leverage and ROIC ignores it, so a wide gap between a high ROE and a modest ROIC is the unmistakable signature of a business whose headline return is borrowed rather than earned.*

## The margin-turnover trade-off: many routes to the same return

So far we have treated the leverage lever as the main source of mischief. But there is a second, more benign, source of variety in ROE worth understanding: **net margin and asset turnover trade off against each other, so very different business models can earn the same operating return (ROA) through opposite combinations of the two.** A high-margin, slow-turnover business and a low-margin, fast-turnover business can be equally good — they have simply chosen different points on the same curve.

![A downward-sloping curve with net margin on the vertical axis and asset turnover on the horizontal axis, showing a luxury brand at high margin low turnover and a volume retailer at low margin high turnover sitting on the same iso-return line](/imgs/blogs/dupont-framework-decomposing-roe-6.png)

The figure plots net margin against asset turnover, and the curve is a *constant-ROA* line: every point on it has margin × turnover equal to the same value — here 12%. A **luxury brand** sits at the top-left: a fat 20% net margin, but a slow 0.6x asset turnover, because its goods are expensive, sell slowly, and tie up capital in inventory and stores. A **volume retailer** sits at the bottom-right: a razor-thin 2% net margin, but a blistering 6.0x asset turnover, because it sells cheap goods in enormous quantities and spins its inventory many times a year. Northwind sits in the middle, at 12% margin and 1.0x turnover. **All three earn the same 12% ROA** — 20% × 0.6, 12% × 1.0, and 2% × 6.0 all equal 12% — through completely different business models. The brand makes its money on the *size* of each sale; the retailer on the *number* of sales; and neither is inherently better than the other.

This trade-off is why you must always compare a company's margins and turnover *against the right peer group*. A 2% net margin would be a catastrophe for a luxury brand and a triumph for a supermarket; a 0.6x asset turnover would be alarming for a retailer and entirely normal for a maker of heavy machinery. Reading either lever in isolation, or against the wrong benchmark, produces nonsense. DuPont's gift here is that it forces you to look at margin and turnover *together*: a business with a falling margin might be perfectly healthy if its turnover is rising to compensate (it is shifting toward a higher-volume model), and a business with a rising margin might be in trouble if its turnover is collapsing (it is selling less even as each sale gets more profitable). The position *on the curve* is a strategic choice; the question for an investor is whether the company is good at the model it has chosen — and whether it is staying on its curve or sliding toward the origin, where ROA shrinks for everyone.

#### Worked example: a retailer and a luxury brand at the same ROA

**Volume retailer:** net margin 2%, asset turnover 6.0x. ROA = margin × turnover = 0.02 × 6.0 = **12%**. It keeps two cents on each dollar of sales but turns its asset base six times a year, so the thin margin compounds into a healthy return on assets. **Luxury brand:** net margin 20%, asset turnover 0.6x. ROA = 0.20 × 0.6 = **12%**. It keeps twenty cents on each dollar of sales but turns its assets only six-tenths of a time a year, so the fat margin, applied to a slow base, lands at the *same* 12% ROA. **Northwind (middle):** 12% margin × 1.0x turnover = **12%**. Three businesses, three completely different operating shapes — scarcity-and-price versus volume-and-velocity versus the balanced middle — all landing on an identical 12% return on assets. None is better than the others on ROA alone; the decomposition just tells you *which game each one is playing*.

*Margin and turnover trade off along a constant-return curve, so a low margin is not bad and a high one is not good in the abstract — what matters is whether the combination earns a strong return for that kind of business.*

## Comparing peers and reading what changed

We now have the full toolkit, so let us use it the way professionals do: to compare a set of peers lever by lever, and to diagnose precisely what changed. DuPont turns the vague question "why is this company's ROE higher than its rival's?" into a specific, answerable one — *which lever is different, and is the difference quality or risk?*

![A matrix decomposing the return on equity of a volume retailer and a luxury brand into net margin, asset turnover, equity multiplier, and the resulting ROE, showing the same eighteen percent from mirror image drivers](/imgs/blogs/dupont-framework-decomposing-roe-7.png)

The matrix above lays out a full DuPont side-by-side for our retailer and luxury brand, now including leverage so the comparison runs all the way to ROE. Hold the equity multiplier equal at 1.5x for both (so financing is not the variable) and the two firms post an identical **18% ROE** — the retailer from 2% margin × 6.0x turnover × 1.5x leverage, the brand from 20% margin × 0.6x turnover × 1.5x leverage. Reading the matrix row by row is exactly how an analyst compares peers: the *net margin* row instantly flags that the brand has twenty-five times the profitability per sale; the *asset turnover* row flags that the retailer spins its assets ten times as fast; the *equity multiplier* row confirms the financing is held equal here, so it is not the source of the difference; and the *ROE* row shows them tied. The decomposition has converted a tie at the headline into a rich, specific story about *how* each firm earns its return — which is the whole point.

The same lever-by-lever reading is how you diagnose change over time, and how you spot the quality of a peer's advantage. If a competitor's ROE is consistently higher than the company you are studying, decompose both and find the lever responsible. A rival winning on *operating margin* has pricing power or a cost advantage — a sign of a possible moat, and the most durable kind of edge. A rival winning on *asset turnover* runs a leaner, more capital-efficient operation — also real, also durable. But a rival winning purely on the *equity multiplier* is just more leveraged — a higher return bought with higher risk, not a better business, and one that will look far worse the moment credit conditions tighten. The lever that produces a superior ROE tells you whether the superiority is something to admire and pay for, or something to fear. That single diagnostic — *which lever, and is it quality or risk?* — is what separates a DuPont-literate analyst from someone reading ROEs off a screen.

#### Worked example: same 18% ROE, diagnosed lever by lever

**Retailer:** net margin 2%, asset turnover 6.0x, equity multiplier 1.5x. ROE = 0.02 × 6.0 × 1.5 = **0.18 = 18%**. **Brand:** net margin 20%, asset turnover 0.6x, equity multiplier 1.5x. ROE = 0.20 × 0.6 × 1.5 = **0.18 = 18%**. Tied at the headline. Now diagnose. The leverage lever is identical (1.5x), so financing is *not* why they differ — a crucial finding, because it means neither is borrowing its way to the number. The difference is entirely operating: the brand wins profitability (20% vs 2%) by a factor of ten, the retailer wins efficiency (6.0x vs 0.6x) by the same factor, and the two advantages cancel to leave equal ROEs. Both are good businesses earning a real 12% ROA before leverage; both add the same modest leverage; both land at 18%. The decomposition tells you they are *equally good but completely different*, and that the right peer for each is another firm running the *same* model — not each other.

*Comparing peers lever by lever turns an uninformative tie at the ROE line into a precise account of who earns their return on profitability, who on efficiency, and who is merely borrowing — which is the only comparison that tells you about quality.*

## Common misconceptions

**"A high ROE means a high-quality business."** Not on its own. ROE is the product of profitability, efficiency, and leverage, and a high value can come from any of them — including a high equity multiplier that simply reflects a lot of debt. A 20% ROE built on a 6% ROA and heavy leverage is a leveraged mediocre business; a 20% ROE built on a 15% ROA and little debt is a genuinely good one. Always decompose before you admire. The first check is the gap between ROE and ROA (or ROIC): if it is wide, leverage is doing the work, and the quality is not in the headline.

**"The three DuPont levers are roughly interchangeable, so it doesn't matter which one is high."** It matters enormously. Net margin and asset turnover are signs of *business quality* — pricing power, cost discipline, capital efficiency — and they tend to be durable. The equity multiplier is a sign of *financing risk* — it lifts ROE in good years and amplifies losses in bad ones, and it can be reversed (or forced down by creditors) overnight. A high ROE driven by the operating levers is something to pay up for; a high ROE driven by the leverage lever is something to be wary of. The levers are not interchangeable; they carry opposite information about risk.

**"A rising ROE always means the business is improving."** Often it just means more leverage, a lower tax rate, or a share buyback. The five-step DuPont exists precisely to separate operating improvement (rising operating margin or asset turnover) from financial and tax effects (rising equity multiplier, falling tax burden, falling interest burden). A two-point rise in ROE driven by a tax cut or a buyback is a far lower-quality improvement than the same rise driven by operating margin — and only the decomposition tells them apart. A rising ROE is a prompt to ask *which lever rose*, not a conclusion.

**"A low net margin means a bad business."** Only relative to the wrong peer. Margin and asset turnover trade off: a supermarket's 2% net margin is excellent when paired with a 6x asset turnover, landing at the same ROA as a luxury brand's 20% margin on a 0.6x turnover. Judging a margin without the matching turnover, or against a company running a different model, produces nonsense. The right comparison is always within a business model, and DuPont forces you to read margin and turnover together rather than fixating on one.

**"A flat ROE year over year means nothing changed."** A flat ROE can hide a business in decline. Because ROE is a product of levers, a falling operating lever (say asset turnover collapsing as sales weaken) can be perfectly offset by a rising leverage lever (the equity multiplier climbing as a buyback shrinks equity), leaving the headline unchanged while the operations deteriorate. Only a year-over-year decomposition reveals the offsetting moves. A stable ROE built on rising leverage and falling turnover is a red flag wearing a green costume.

**"DuPont works for every company."** It breaks for the same firms ROE-based analysis always struggles with — most acutely **financial companies**. For a bank or insurer, debt is not financing layered on top of operations; it is the raw material of the business itself (deposits and borrowings are what a bank lends out). The clean separation between operating and financing levers that DuPont depends on collapses, the equity multiplier runs to enormous values (banks routinely operate at 10x or higher), and the standard decomposition misleads. Banks use their own specialised versions. And any company with **negative equity** — where buybacks or accumulated losses have pushed shareholders' equity below zero — produces a nonsensical equity multiplier and an undefined or negative ROE that DuPont cannot sensibly decompose at all.

## How it shows up in real markets

The DuPont framework is not an academic exercise; it is how professional analysts dissect returns every day, and the patterns it reveals recur across the market in recognisable shapes.

**The leverage-flattered ROE in financials and buyout targets.** The most consistent real-world use of DuPont is catching ROEs that are high because of leverage rather than quality. Before the 2008 crisis, many banks and leveraged financial firms posted gorgeous returns on equity — 20%, 25% and higher — that drew investors in. Decomposed, much of that ROE was the equity multiplier doing the work: borrowing thirty or more dollars for every dollar of equity to magnify thin operating spreads into fat equity returns. When the spreads turned and the leverage worked in reverse, the same firms that had posted spectacular ROEs were wiped out, because the high return had always been a high-leverage bet in disguise. The lesson the market relearns periodically is exactly what DuPont teaches: an ROE that lives in the equity multiplier is not quality, it is risk, and the decomposition shows it long before the leverage unwinds.

**The high-margin moat versus the high-turnover grinder.** Two of the great archetypes of business quality sit at opposite ends of the margin-turnover trade-off, and DuPont is how you tell which kind you are looking at. The branded-goods giants — the franchise consumer products with pricing power — earn their high ROAs through fat *operating margins*: customers pay up, and the margin is the visible footprint of the brand's moat. The dominant low-cost retailers and distributors earn comparable ROAs through blistering *asset turnover*: thin margins spun many times a year by relentless operational efficiency. Both are admirable; both are durable; but they are durable for different reasons, and a DuPont decomposition tells you which competence — pricing power or operational efficiency — you are betting on, and therefore what could erode it. A brand's edge dies if customers stop paying the premium; a discounter's edge dies if a rival out-operates it on cost.

**The buyback that holds ROE up while the business softens.** A pattern worth watching for in any mature company returning cash to shareholders: a steady or rising ROE held aloft, year after year, by an equity multiplier climbing as buybacks shrink the equity base — even as asset turnover or operating margin quietly slips. The buyback is often perfectly sensible capital allocation; the danger is that it *also* flatters the headline ROE and can mask an operating deterioration that the decomposition would otherwise flag. When you see a company whose ROE has been stable for years while its operating levers have weakened and its leverage has risen to compensate, you are looking at a business whose reported return is being propped up by financial engineering. That is not always a problem — but it is always a thing to know, and only DuPont makes it visible.

**The empire-builder whose returns the decomposition unmasks.** Companies that grow rapidly through debt-funded acquisitions often report rising or stable ROEs that the equity multiplier is manufacturing. A serial acquirer loading on debt to buy revenue can keep its ROE elevated through the leverage lever even as the underlying businesses earn mediocre operating returns — the same dynamic that, in the extreme and fraudulent cases, played out at firms like [Enron](/blog/trading/finance/enron-2001-accounting-fraud), where reported returns drew investors in while the operating reality was being obscured. DuPont is one of the first lenses that exposes this: decompose the ROE, find the equity multiplier doing the lifting, check the operating margin and asset turnover underneath, and the story of a business borrowing its way to a flattering number becomes plain. The investors who avoided the great leverage-driven blowups were, almost always, the ones who refused to take a high ROE at face value and asked *which lever*.

## When this matters and further reading

The DuPont framework is the tool that converts a single, compressed ROE into the three (or five) facts it was hiding: how profitable the business is, how efficiently it uses its assets, and how much it has borrowed. Once you can decompose any ROE on sight, you stop treating the headline as an answer and start treating it as a question — *which lever is producing this return, and is that lever quality or risk?* That single habit is the difference between an investor who can be fooled by a leveraged 20% and one who cannot.

You will reach for DuPont constantly: to tell two same-ROE companies apart (Northwind versus Riverstone), to separate a real operating improvement from a tax cut or a buyback (the five-step split), to catch a deteriorating business hiding behind a flat ROE (the year-over-year decomposition), and to diagnose exactly why one peer out-earns another (the lever-by-lever comparison). It is also the bridge between the parts of equity analysis you already know. The net-margin lever runs straight into [profitability and margins — gross, operating, net](/blog/trading/equity-research/profitability-margins-gross-operating-net); the whole framework is the fine-grained companion to [returns on capital — ROIC, ROE, ROA](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa), which is where the leverage-neutral ROIC the decomposition keeps pointing toward is built from the ground up. The leverage lever, in turn, opens onto the question of how much debt is healthy and how much is lethal — the subject of [leverage and coverage — debt that compounds versus debt that kills](/blog/trading/equity-research/leverage-and-coverage-debt-that-compounds-vs-kills) — and the asset-turnover lever ties to how efficiently a company runs its operations day to day, the territory of [working capital and the cash conversion cycle](/blog/trading/equity-research/working-capital-and-the-cash-conversion-cycle).

Read enough companies through this lens and a reflex forms: you never again see a 20% ROE and nod. You decompose it. You find out whether the twenty points are earned on margin and turnover or borrowed through the equity multiplier; whether they rose this year because the business improved or because a tax rate fell; whether they have held flat because nothing changed or because a buyback is papering over a turnover collapse. The number is the same; the truth is in the decomposition. That is what it means to take ROE apart and find the real driver — and it is one of the most reliably profitable habits an equity investor can build.
