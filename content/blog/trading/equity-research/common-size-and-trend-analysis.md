> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A single year's financial statements are a snapshot; the signal a professional actually reads lives in the *motion* — how the numbers change across time and how they stack against peers — and two simple techniques expose that motion from a wall of raw figures.
>
> - **Common-size analysis** restates every income-statement line as a percent of revenue and every balance-sheet line as a percent of total assets. It strips out size, so a \$1.4 billion firm and a \$9 billion rival become directly comparable, and a column of dollars becomes a readable map of where each sales dollar goes.
> - **Trend analysis** (also called horizontal analysis) reads the *direction*: index every line to 100 in a base year and watch which lines outrun revenue. When SG&A climbs to 159 while revenue is only at 136, overhead is creeping faster than sales, and margins are quietly compressing — something no single year reveals.
> - **CAGR** — the compound annual growth rate — collapses a lumpy multi-year path into the one smooth rate that links the first year to the last. It is the honest way to describe growth, and it is trivial to compute once you know the formula.
> - **The real skill is combining them.** Common-size tells you the *level* and structure; trend tells you the *direction*; benchmarking tells you whether either is good. Layer the ratios from earlier posts on top and a five-year "spread" becomes a coherent read of whether a business is getting better or worse.
> - **The hardest judgment is cyclical wobble versus structural decline.** Revenue can rise while returns rot. Margins can dip for one bad year or erode for five. Distinguishing the two — and normalizing for acquisitions and one-offs so you compare like with like — is where statement reading becomes investing.

Open a company's annual report and you are handed a snapshot: one year's income statement, one year's balance sheet, frozen at a single instant. You can read every line, compute every ratio, and still know almost nothing — because a snapshot cannot tell you whether you are looking at a business on the way up or a business on the way down. A company earning a 6.4% operating margin might be a steady performer that has earned 6.4% for a decade, or it might be a former 12% business in the third year of a quiet collapse. The number is identical. The investment is the opposite. The difference is invisible in the snapshot and obvious the moment you put five years side by side.

This is the central truth of statement analysis, and it is the reason professionals almost never look at a single year in isolation. The real information is not in the *level* of any number but in its *motion* — how it has moved over time, and how it sits relative to the companies it competes with. A margin that is falling tells a different story than the same margin rising. A capital base that is ballooning faster than sales tells a different story than one growing in step. The numbers come alive only when you give them a second axis: time, and peers.

![A column of raw dollar figures on the left restated as percentages of revenue on the right, turning an uninterpretable list into a readable cost structure](/imgs/blogs/common-size-and-trend-analysis-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model for the first half of this post. On the left is a column of raw dollars — Northwind's Year 5 income statement, an uninterpretable wall of figures. On the right is the *same data*, restated as a percent of revenue: COGS is 63% of sales, operating costs eat 30.6%, and only 6.4 cents of every sales dollar survives as profit. Nothing changed but the units, and yet the right column is *readable* — it is a map of where the money goes — while the left column is just a list. That transformation, called common-sizing, is half the toolkit. The other half is trend analysis: doing the same comparison across years instead of down a single statement. Together they convert raw financials into a story.

Throughout this post we will keep building **Northwind Industries** — the same fictional manufacturer with \$1,000 million of revenue we have followed through the [income statement walked line by line](/blog/trading/equity-research/income-statement-line-by-line-revenue-to-net-income), the [margin ladder](/blog/trading/equity-research/profitability-margins-gross-operating-net), and the [returns on capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa). But this time we will follow Northwind across *five* years, watching its revenue climb a healthy 8% a year while its margins and returns quietly rot underneath — exactly the kind of divergence that this post's techniques are built to catch. By the end you will be able to take any company's five-to-ten years of financials, "spread" them the way an analyst does, and read out loud where the business is heading.

## Foundations: the building blocks of statement comparison

Before we spread anything, we need precise definitions. Each term below appears constantly in real analysis, and getting them exact now prevents the confusion that derails beginners.

**Common-size analysis (vertical analysis).** Restating every line of a financial statement as a percentage of one base figure *within that same statement and year*. For the income statement, the base is revenue (the top line is 100%); for the balance sheet, the base is total assets (the total is 100%). It is called *vertical* because you read down a single year's statement, expressing each line as a fraction of the same denominator. Its purpose is to reveal *structure* — the proportions of a business — and to make companies of different sizes comparable.

**Trend analysis (horizontal analysis).** Comparing the *same line across multiple years* to see its direction and rate of change. It is called *horizontal* because you read across the years, row by row. Its purpose is to reveal *motion* — whether a line is rising, falling, or flat, and how fast. The two techniques are perpendicular: vertical reads down one year, horizontal reads across many years, and you use both at once.

**Base year and indexing.** To make a trend legible, you pick a **base year**, set every line in it to 100 (or to its actual value as the "index 100" reference), and express every later year as that line's value relative to the base. If revenue was \$1,000M in the base year and \$1,360M four years later, the index is 1,360 ÷ 1,000 × 100 = **136**. Indexing lets you plot revenue, COGS, and SG&A on the same chart even though they are wildly different dollar amounts, because they all start at 100 — so you can see at a glance which line is *outrunning* the others.

**Year-over-year (YoY) growth.** The percent change in a line from one year to the next: (this year − last year) ÷ last year. It is the most common growth measure and the noisiest, because any single year can be distorted by a one-off. A 1.7% YoY revenue figure sandwiched between two double-digit years is usually noise, not a trend.

**Compound annual growth rate (CAGR).** The single constant annual rate that would carry a value from its starting point to its ending point over a given number of years, as if it had grown smoothly. It is the right way to summarize multi-year growth because it averages out the lumpiness. The formula is:

$$\text{CAGR} = \left(\frac{\text{Ending value}}{\text{Beginning value}}\right)^{\frac{1}{n}} - 1$$

where *n* is the number of years *between* the endpoints (not the count of data points). We will compute it by hand below.

**Benchmarking.** Comparing a company's common-size lines or ratios against an external reference — a direct competitor, the median of a peer group, or an industry-wide figure. A number is meaningless until benchmarked: a 37% gross margin is neither good nor bad until you know the peer earns 22% or 52%. The two most useful benchmarks are a company's *own past* (trend) and its *peers' present* (cross-sectional).

**Peer group and industry median.** A **peer group** is the set of companies most comparable to the one you are analyzing — same industry, similar business model, roughly comparable scale or positioning. The **industry median** is the middle value of a metric across that group; analysts prefer the median to the mean because a single giant or a single outlier distorts an average but barely moves a median.

**Segment.** A distinct line of business or geography that a company reports separately (for example, a "Products" segment and a "Services" segment). Segment trend analysis applies common-size and trend techniques *within* each segment, which often reveals that a flat consolidated number is hiding a booming segment and a dying one cancelling out.

**Cyclical versus structural.** A **cyclical** change is a temporary swing driven by the business cycle or a one-off event — a recession year, a supply shock, a strike — that reverses when conditions normalize. A **structural** change is a permanent shift in the economics of the business — a new competitor, a commoditizing product, a broken cost model — that does *not* reverse. Telling them apart is the single hardest and most valuable judgment in trend analysis, and we devote a full section to it.

**Normalizing.** Adjusting reported figures to remove distortions so that periods are comparable — stripping out one-time gains and charges, restating for an acquisition that suddenly inflated revenue, or backing out a divested business. You normalize *before* you trend, because comparing a year that includes a big acquisition to one that doesn't is comparing two different companies.

One foundational warning before we begin: **a single number is never read alone, and never in raw dollars.** The two great sins of beginner analysis are quoting a dollar figure with no sense of scale ("they made \$87 million" — is that good?) and quoting one year with no sense of direction ("their margin is 6.4%" — up or down from what?). Common-sizing cures the first; trend analysis cures the second. Hold that discipline through everything that follows.

## Common-size analysis: turning dollars into proportions

Start with the simplest, most powerful move in all of statement reading. Take an income statement and divide every single line by revenue. That's it. Revenue itself becomes 100%, and every cost and profit below it becomes a fraction of that 100%. You have just built a **common-size income statement**, and you have transformed an uninterpretable column of dollars into a readable description of the business's economics.

Why is this so powerful? Because the dollar amounts on an income statement are dominated by *size*, which is usually the least interesting thing about a company. A \$50 million regional manufacturer and a \$50 billion global one have income statements that look nothing alike in dollars — every line differs by a factor of a thousand. But once you common-size them, the *size* cancels out and only the *structure* remains. If both keep 40 cents of gross profit per sales dollar and spend 28 cents running the company, their common-size statements are nearly identical — because they are, fundamentally, the same kind of business at different scale. Common-sizing is the lens that makes that visible.

![A vertical stack where each band's height equals its share of revenue, from a tall red COGS band down to a thin green operating-profit sliver](/imgs/blogs/common-size-and-trend-analysis-2.png)

The figure makes the idea physical. Imagine one dollar of revenue as a tall bar. Common-sizing slices that bar into bands, each band's height equal to its share of the dollar. For Northwind in Year 5, the biggest band by far is COGS at 63 cents — almost two-thirds of every sales dollar goes straight to making the product. Then overhead — SG&A, R&D, and depreciation — eats another 30.6 cents. What's left, the thin green sliver at the bottom, is the 6.4 cents of operating profit. The entire economics of the business is in those proportions, and you can read them in a glance once they're expressed as shares of the whole.

#### Worked example: building Northwind's common-size income statement

Let's build it from the raw numbers. In Year 5, Northwind reported: revenue \$1,360M, COGS \$857M, SG&A \$286M, R&D \$76M, D&A \$54M, and operating income \$87M. To common-size, divide each by the \$1,360M revenue. **COGS** = \$857M ÷ \$1,360M = **63.0%**. **Gross profit** = (\$1,360M − \$857M) ÷ \$1,360M = \$503M ÷ \$1,360M = **37.0%**. **SG&A** = \$286M ÷ \$1,360M = **21.0%**. **R&D** = \$76M ÷ \$1,360M = **5.6%**. **D&A** = \$54M ÷ \$1,360M = **4.0%**. **Operating income** = \$87M ÷ \$1,360M = **6.4%**. Now the statement reads as a sentence: of every sales dollar, 63 cents makes the product, 21 cents runs the sales-and-admin machine, 9.6 cents goes to R&D and depreciation, and 6.4 cents is operating profit. You could not have said that from the dollar column without a calculator and a lot of squinting.

*Common-sizing the income statement turns a list of dollar amounts into a single readable sentence about where each sales dollar goes.*

### The common-size balance sheet

The same trick works on the balance sheet, with one change: the base is **total assets**, not revenue. Every asset, liability, and equity line becomes a percent of the total the company controls. This reveals the *shape* of the balance sheet — how asset-heavy the business is, how much of it is financed by debt versus equity, how much is tied up in inventory versus cash.

Suppose Northwind's Year 5 balance sheet shows total assets of \$1,500M, composed of \$90M cash (6.0%), \$240M receivables (16.0%), \$300M inventory (20.0%), and \$870M of net property, plant and equipment (58.0%). On the other side, \$300M of payables (20.0%), \$600M of debt (40.0%), and \$600M of equity (40.0%). Common-sized, you can now say: "Northwind is a capital-intensive manufacturer — 58% of its assets are fixed plant — and it is financed half by debt, half by equity." That single read tells you the business needs heavy reinvestment and carries meaningful financial risk, and you got it from proportions, not dollars.

The common-size balance sheet is where you spot **capital intensity** and how it is changing. A retailer might be 10% fixed assets; a steel mill 70%. A software firm might be 5% inventory; a manufacturer 25%. These proportions are the fingerprint of the business model, and watching them drift over time is how you catch a company quietly becoming more asset-heavy — which, as we'll see, is often the hidden cause of falling returns.

#### Worked example: a common-size balance sheet exposes working-capital creep

Here is a subtle signal that only common-sizing reveals. In Year 1, Northwind's inventory was \$200M on \$1,200M of total assets — **16.7%**. By Year 5, inventory is \$300M on \$1,500M of assets — **20.0%**. In dollars, inventory "just grew with the business." But as a *share of assets*, it climbed from 16.7% to 20.0%, meaning Northwind is now tying up proportionally more of its capital in unsold goods. Worse, relative to revenue: inventory was 20.0% of sales in Year 1 (\$200M ÷ \$1,000M) and is 22.1% in Year 5 (\$300M ÷ \$1,360M). The company is carrying more inventory per dollar of sales — a classic sign of slowing demand, weakening operations, or goods piling up that may need to be written down. None of this is visible in the raw dollar figure, which only ever said "inventory went up."

*Common-sizing the balance sheet turns "the numbers all grew" into "this line grew faster than the business," which is where the warning signs hide.*

### The common-size cash flow statement

The third statement gets common-sized too, and it is the one most analysts neglect — which is a mistake, because the cash flow statement is where the truth of earnings quality lives. There is no single universal base for the cash flow statement; the most useful conventions are to express the investing and financing lines as a percent of **cash flow from operations (CFO)**, and to express CFO itself as a percent of revenue or of net income. Each of these ratios, trended over time, exposes a behavior that the income statement and balance sheet can hide.

The two most revealing common-size cash-flow metrics are **capital intensity** and **free-cash-flow conversion**. Capital intensity here means capital expenditure (capex) as a percent of CFO or of revenue — how much of the cash the business generates it must immediately plow back just to keep running and growing. A business whose capex is a small and stable share of CFO is throwing off free cash; a business whose capex is creeping up toward 100% of CFO is a treadmill that consumes everything it earns. **FCF conversion** — free cash flow (CFO minus capex) divided by net income — tells you what fraction of reported accounting profit actually shows up as spendable cash. A healthy business converts 80–100%+ of net income into free cash; a business where conversion is falling year after year is reporting profits it cannot turn into cash, which is the classic signature of earnings that are [an accounting opinion rather than a fact](/blog/trading/equity-research/accruals-vs-cash-why-earnings-are-an-opinion).

#### Worked example: trending Northwind's capex intensity and FCF conversion

Watch the cash story deteriorate alongside the income story. In Year 1, Northwind generated \$150M of CFO and spent \$60M of capex, so capex was 40% of CFO and free cash flow was \$90M — against net income of \$79M, that's a 114% FCF conversion (free cash exceeded reported profit, the mark of a high-quality earner). By Year 5, CFO had risen only modestly to \$165M while capex had ballooned to \$140M (the capital-hungry growth we saw crush ROIC), so capex was now 85% of CFO and free cash flow had collapsed to just \$25M. Against Year 5 net income of roughly \$50M, FCF conversion fell to about 50%. So in four years Northwind went from converting more than all of its profit into free cash to converting only half — and the capex treadmill (40% to 85% of CFO) is the mechanism. The income statement showed "growing revenue"; the common-size cash flow statement showed "the business now eats almost everything it earns just to stand still." That divergence — earnings holding up while cash conversion falls — is precisely the trend a short-seller hunts for.

*Common-sizing the cash flow statement catches the capex treadmill and falling cash conversion that a rising-revenue income statement is built to disguise.*

## Trend analysis: reading the direction with indexing

Common-sizing gives you the structure of one year. Trend analysis gives you the *direction* across many. The simplest way to see a trend is to **index** every line to a base year — set the base-year value to 100, and express each later year as a ratio to it. This does something a table of dollars cannot: it puts lines of completely different magnitudes on the same scale, so you can see which one is pulling away from the others.

The reason indexing matters is that the human eye is terrible at comparing growth rates across different magnitudes. Revenue went from \$1,000M to \$1,360M; SG&A went from \$180M to \$286M. Which grew faster? You can't tell at a glance from those dollars — the revenue increase is bigger in absolute terms (\$360M vs \$106M), which fools you into thinking revenue grew faster. But index them both to 100 and the truth jumps out: revenue is at 136, SG&A is at 159. SG&A grew *far* faster. That single fact — overhead outrunning sales — is the diagnosis of a margin problem, and it was hiding in plain sight in the dollar table.

![Three indexed lines climbing from 100 in the base year, with SG&A pulling sharply above revenue while COGS tracks just above it](/imgs/blogs/common-size-and-trend-analysis-3.png)

The figure shows exactly this. All three lines — revenue, COGS, and SG&A — start at 100 in Year 1, by construction. Then they fan out. Revenue (the dashed line) climbs steadily to 136. COGS rises a bit faster to 143, meaning the cost of making each dollar of product is creeping up — gross margin is slipping. But SG&A is the alarm: it rockets to 159, far above revenue's 136. The gap between the SG&A line and the revenue line *is* the margin compression. Every year that overhead grows faster than sales, the operating margin falls, and the indexed chart makes that mechanical relationship visible as a widening gap.

#### Worked example: indexing exposes SG&A creeping up faster than sales

Let's index Northwind's three key lines to Year 1 = 100 and read the story. The raw figures across five years: revenue \$1,000M → \$1,090M → \$1,180M → \$1,270M → \$1,360M; COGS \$600M → \$659M → \$720M → \$787M → \$857M; SG&A \$180M → \$203M → \$228M → \$256M → \$286M. Now index each to its Year 1 value. **Revenue index:** 100, 109, 118, 127, **136**. **COGS index:** 100, 110, 120, 131, **143**. **SG&A index:** 100, 113, 127, 142, **159**. Lay them side by side and the diagnosis writes itself. Revenue grew 36% over four years. COGS grew 43% — faster than revenue, so gross margin fell (from 40.0% to 37.0%). SG&A grew 59% — far faster than revenue, so SG&A as a percent of sales rose from 18.0% to 21.0%. Both costs outran sales, and the two leaks compounded: that is exactly why operating margin collapsed from 12.0% to 6.4% even as the business "grew." The indexing turned a benign-looking growth story into a clear picture of erosion.

*Indexing to a base year converts "everything went up" into "these lines went up faster than sales," which is the precise mechanism of margin compression.*

### Year-over-year versus multi-year trends

There are two ways to read a trend, and confusing them is a common error. **Year-over-year (YoY)** looks at the change from one period to the next — it is sensitive and timely, but noisy. **Multi-year** trends look at the trajectory across the whole span — they are slower to react but filter out single-year noise. A professional reads both: YoY to catch the latest inflection, multi-year to confirm it is a trend and not a blip.

The danger of YoY alone is that any single year can be distorted. A company might post a great YoY number because the *prior* year was terrible (an "easy comp"), or a terrible YoY number because the prior year was unusually strong. Northwind's Year 3 revenue grew only a few percent — read alone, that looks like stalling. But in the context of the multi-year trend (a steady ~8% climb), Year 3 was just a soft patch, not a stall. Conversely, a single great year inside a multi-year decline is a dead-cat bounce, not a recovery. The trend is the context that tells you which.

## CAGR: the honest summary of growth

When someone says a company "grew at 8% a year," they almost never mean it grew exactly 8% every year — real growth is lumpy. What they mean is the **compound annual growth rate**: the single smooth rate that, applied every year, would have carried the starting value to the ending value over the same span. CAGR is the honest one-number summary of multi-year growth, and it is the right way to compare growth across companies and periods of different lengths.

![A smooth blue CAGR line rising steadily while green dots for actual revenue bounce above and below it, both meeting at the same endpoints](/imgs/blogs/common-size-and-trend-analysis-4.png)

The figure shows what CAGR really is. The green dots are Northwind's *actual* revenue each year — lumpy, with a big 15% jump one year and a near-flat 1.7% year right after. The smooth blue line is the CAGR: the constant 8.0% path that starts at the same \$1,000M and ends at the same \$1,360M. CAGR ignores the bumps in between and reports only the average compounding that connects the endpoints. It is a deliberate simplification — it throws away the path — but that's exactly its value: it lets you say "8% a year" and compare it cleanly to another company's "5% a year" without drowning in the year-to-year noise.

#### Worked example: computing Northwind's 5-year revenue CAGR

Northwind's revenue was \$1,000M in Year 1 and \$1,360M in Year 5. The two endpoints are *four years apart* (Year 1 to Year 5 spans 4 intervals — this is the most common CAGR mistake, so count the gaps, not the data points: 5 data points, 4 years of growth). Apply the formula: CAGR = (1,360 ÷ 1,000)^(1/4) − 1 = (1.36)^(0.25) − 1. The fourth root of 1.36 is about 1.0799, so CAGR ≈ **7.99%, or roughly 8.0% a year**. Sanity-check it forward: \$1,000M × 1.08⁴ = \$1,000M × 1.3605 ≈ \$1,360M. It checks. Notice how much cleaner "8.0% CAGR" is than reciting "+9%, +8.3%, +7.6%, +7.1%" — and how it lets you instantly compare Northwind to a peer growing at, say, a 5% CAGR. One number, honestly computed, that you can actually reason with.

*CAGR is the single smooth rate hidden inside a lumpy growth path — the honest way to say "how fast did this grow" in one number.*

A warning about CAGR's blind spot, which sophisticated readers exploit: **CAGR depends entirely on the two endpoints and ignores everything in between.** If you pick a depressed base year (the bottom of a recession) as your starting point, you can make almost any company look like a rocket. Sell-side decks love to quote CAGR "from 2009" — the trough of the financial crisis — precisely because the depressed base inflates the rate. Always ask what the endpoints are, and prefer measuring CAGR between two *comparable* points in the cycle (peak-to-peak or mid-cycle-to-mid-cycle), not trough-to-peak. A CAGR is only as honest as the years you anchor it to.

## Benchmarking against peers and the industry

Common-sizing and trending tell you a company's structure and direction, but they cannot tell you whether either is *good*. For that you need a benchmark, and the two best ones are the company's own past (which trend analysis already gives you) and its competitors' present. Benchmarking against peers is where common-sizing earns its keep, because once every company is expressed as a percent of its own revenue, companies of wildly different sizes line up in the same table and you can read their cost structures side by side.

![A grid comparing a mid-size firm, a giant low-cost rival, a small premium maker, and the industry median across common-size income-statement lines](/imgs/blogs/common-size-and-trend-analysis-5.png)

The figure does exactly this. It places Northwind (\$1.4B revenue) next to two competitors of completely different scale — Eastgate, a \$9 billion low-cost volume player, and Vantage, a \$0.6 billion premium niche maker — plus the industry median. In raw dollars these three could never sit in the same table; one is fifteen times the size of another. But common-sized, they reveal three distinct business models. Eastgate runs a brutal 78% COGS (22% gross margin) but a lean 9% SG&A — the scale-and-thin-margin model. Vantage runs a luxurious 48% COGS (52% gross margin) and a heavy 30% SG&A — the premium, high-touch model. Northwind sits in the middle, and crucially, *slipping toward the median*. The comparison instantly locates Northwind's competitive position and shows that its 6.4% operating margin is now *below* the industry median of 10%.

#### Worked example: common-sizing two peers of different size

Eastgate has \$9,000M of revenue and \$1,980M of gross profit; Vantage has \$600M of revenue and \$312M of gross profit. In dollars, Eastgate's gross profit is over six times Vantage's — so Eastgate is the better business, right? Common-size them and the truth inverts. **Eastgate's gross margin** = \$1,980M ÷ \$9,000M = **22.0%**. **Vantage's gross margin** = \$312M ÷ \$600M = **52.0%**. Vantage keeps more than twice as many cents of gross profit per sales dollar. They are opposite business models: Eastgate wins on volume and scale (its tiny 9% SG&A turns that thin gross margin into an 11% operating margin), while Vantage wins on pricing power (its rich gross margin survives even a heavy 30% SG&A to land at a 6% operating margin). Neither is "better" in the abstract — but you could only see the structural difference *after* common-sizing erased the fifteen-to-one size gap.

*Common-sizing lets a \$600M firm and a \$9B firm sit in the same table, so their cost structures — not their scale — decide which is the better business.*

A practical note on building peer benchmarks: choose the peer group carefully, because a bad comparison is worse than none. The peers must share the same business model, not just the same broad industry — comparing a premium maker to a commodity producer "because they both make pumps" will mislead you. Prefer the **median** over the mean, because one giant or one distressed outlier distorts an average. And benchmark the *trend*, not just the level: a company at the industry-median margin but falling toward it from above is a very different investment than one rising toward it from below. The most damning benchmark is "below median *and* declining" — Northwind's exact situation by Year 5.

## Segment trend analysis: the story inside the story

Consolidated financials blend everything a company does into one set of numbers, which can hide as much as it reveals. A flat overall revenue line might be a thriving segment and a dying one cancelling out. A stable consolidated margin might be a high-margin business shrinking while a low-margin one grows. **Segment analysis** applies common-size and trend techniques *within* each reported segment, and it routinely uncovers stories the consolidated statement buries.

Suppose Northwind reports two segments: legacy Pumps and a newer Services business. Consolidated revenue grew a steady 8% CAGR — looks healthy. But split it: Pumps revenue went \$800M → \$816M over four years (a 0.5% CAGR — essentially dead), while Services went \$200M → \$544M (a 28% CAGR — exploding). The consolidated 8% is a weighted average of a dying core and a booming new business. That changes everything about how you value the company: the durable, high-margin Pumps franchise is stagnant, and the growth is all coming from a younger, possibly lower-margin Services business. The consolidated trend told you "8% grower"; the segment trend told you "stagnant core plus a startup bolted on" — a completely different investment thesis.

#### Worked example: a consolidated trend hiding two opposite segment trends

Let's make the margin mix concrete. In Year 1, Pumps earned a 14% operating margin (\$112M on \$800M) and Services earned 4% (\$8M on \$200M); blended, that's \$120M on \$1,000M = 12.0%, matching Northwind's reported figure. By Year 5, Pumps' margin has slipped to 10% (\$82M on \$816M — competition is eroding the core) and Services, still subscale, earns just 1% (\$5M on \$544M). Blended: \$87M on \$1,360M = 6.4%. So the consolidated operating-margin collapse from 12.0% to 6.4% has *two* distinct causes that the consolidated number fuses into one: the profitable core is eroding *and* the growth is coming disproportionately from a lower-margin segment (mix shift). Only segment analysis separates "the core is deteriorating" from "we're growing into a worse business" — and they call for very different responses from management and very different valuations from you.

*A consolidated trend is a weighted average that can hide two opposite stories; segment analysis is how you pull them apart.*

## Spotting the inflection: where the trend turns

The whole point of trend analysis is to catch the *inflection* — the moment a business changes direction — early, while it's still subtle, before it shows up in headline numbers that everyone can see. The classic inflections that common-size and trend analysis are built to detect are three: **margin compression** (costs rising faster than revenue), **rising capital intensity** (the asset base growing faster than sales), and **working-capital creep** (receivables and inventory ballooning relative to revenue). Each is a quiet leak that a single year hides and a multi-year trend exposes.

The most dangerous inflection of all is the one where the headline number *improves* while the underlying economics deteriorate. Revenue going up is the most reassuring number in all of finance — it feels like unambiguous good news. But revenue can rise while the *returns* on the capital invested to produce that revenue fall. A company can grow its way into value destruction, pouring more and more capital into a business that earns less and less on each dollar. This is the inflection that fools the most people, because the comforting top-line growth masks the rotting return underneath.

![Rising blue revenue bars across five years beneath a falling red ROIC line that slides through the 8 percent cost-of-capital hurdle](/imgs/blogs/common-size-and-trend-analysis-6.png)

The figure is the picture of that trap, and it is the most important one in this post. The blue bars are Northwind's revenue, climbing dutifully every year from \$1,000M to \$1,360M. By that measure the company is a success. But the red line is **ROIC** — return on invested capital, the [master ratio from the returns post](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa) — and it is falling: from 11.9% in Year 1, down through the dashed 8% cost-of-capital hurdle, to just 6.0% by Year 5. Once ROIC drops below the cost of capital, *growth destroys value* — every additional dollar Northwind invests to grow earns less than that dollar costs. The revenue chart says "winner"; the ROIC chart says "this business is getting bigger and worse at the same time." Trend analysis is what lets you see the second chart when everyone else is celebrating the first.

#### Worked example: a trend that looks like growth hiding a deteriorating return

Let's prove the divergence with numbers. Northwind's NOPAT (operating profit after tax, at a 21% rate) and invested capital across five years: in Year 1, EBIT was \$120M, so NOPAT = \$120M × 0.79 = \$94.8M, on invested capital of \$800M → ROIC = \$94.8M ÷ \$800M = **11.9%**. By Year 5, EBIT had fallen to \$87M (margins compressed), so NOPAT = \$87M × 0.79 = \$68.7M — *less profit than four years earlier* — while invested capital had ballooned to \$1,150M (the company poured money into the asset base to chase growth). ROIC = \$68.7M ÷ \$1,150M = **5.97%, about 6.0%**. So profit fell while capital rose, and ROIC was crushed from both ends. With a cost of capital around 8%, Northwind crossed from creating value (11.9% > 8%) to destroying it (6.0% < 8%) — all while revenue rose 36%. An investor reading only the top line would have seen a healthy grower; an investor trending ROIC would have seen a value-destruction machine.

*Revenue is the most reassuring number and the easiest to grow; pairing its trend with the return-on-capital trend is what stops growth from fooling you.*

## Cyclical wobble versus structural decline

Here is the hardest judgment in all of trend analysis, the one that separates a mechanical spreadsheet-filler from a real analyst: when a trend turns down, is it a **cyclical wobble** — a temporary dip that will reverse when conditions normalize — or a **structural decline** — a permanent shift in the economics that will not? Get this wrong in either direction and you lose money: mistake a structural decline for a cyclical dip and you "buy the dip" into a value trap; mistake a cyclical dip for structural decline and you sell a great business at the bottom.

The two look identical in a single year — both are "the number went down." They diverge in their *signature* across multiple years and in their *cause*. A cyclical dip is usually **broad** (the whole industry dips together, because it's a macro or end-market shock), **mean-reverting** (margins fall and then recover toward their historical band), and **explainable by external conditions** (a recession, a commodity spike, a temporary oversupply). A structural decline is usually **company-specific or industry-wide-but-permanent** (a new technology, a new low-cost competitor, a regulatory change), **non-reverting** (margins step down to a new, lower plateau and stay there), and **traceable to a changed competitive dynamic** rather than the weather.

The diagnostic questions are concrete. *Is the decline broad or narrow?* If the whole peer group's margins fell together and Northwind's fell in line, that points cyclical; if Northwind's fell while peers held, that points structural — Northwind is losing specifically. *Does it revert?* A margin that dipped in a recession year and climbed back is cyclical; one that has fallen for five straight years to a new low is structural. *What is the mechanism?* "Steel prices spiked, then normalized" is cyclical; "a Chinese competitor entered at 30% lower prices and isn't leaving" is structural. *Is gross margin or just net margin moving?* A net-margin dip from a one-off tax or interest event is transient; a sustained *gross*-margin decline means the core product economics are eroding — the most structural signal of all.

#### Worked example: distinguishing Northwind's wobble from its decline

Northwind's operating margin across five years: 12.0%, 11.1%, 9.9%, 8.3%, 6.4%. Now compare two readings. **Reading A (cyclical):** "Year 3 was a soft patch; it'll bounce back." But this isn't one dip and a recovery — it's a *monotonic five-year slide*, every year lower than the last, with no reversion. That signature is structural, not cyclical. **Reading B (structural), confirmed by mechanism:** the slide is driven by gross margin falling 40.0% → 37.0% (core product economics eroding — a Chinese competitor is undercutting) *and* SG&A rising 18.0% → 21.0% of sales (overhead the company can't grow into). Both are persistent, mechanism-driven, and company-specific — peers held their margins over the same span. The verdict is structural decline, and the "buy the dip" investor who treated it as cyclical was buying into a value trap. The five-year *shape* (steady slide, not V-dip) plus the *mechanism* (gross-margin erosion vs peers) is what makes the call.

*A cyclical dip is a V that reverts; a structural decline is a staircase that doesn't — and the give-away is whether peers fell with the company and whether gross margin, the core economics, is the line that's eroding.*

To make the two signatures unmistakable, put them side by side as raw five-year margin sequences. A **cyclical** business — say a commodity chemical maker — might post operating margins of 14%, 15%, **6%**, 13%, 16%. The middle year is a violent dip (a recession crushed end-market demand and a glut hit prices), but the line snaps back to and above its prior band the moment conditions normalize. The shape is a sharp V, the dip coincides with an external shock that every peer felt, and the margin returns to its long-run mean. The right way to value such a business is on its *mid-cycle* margin (around 13–14% here), not on the trough year (6%) or the peak year (16%) — both of which are temporary. A **structural** decline looks completely different: 14%, 13%, 11%, 9%, 7% — a smooth, monotonic staircase down, every year lower than the last, with no reversion and no single shock to blame. Here there is no mid-cycle to revert to, because the economics themselves have permanently changed; valuing this business on its five-year-average margin would dangerously overstate it, because the average is dragged up by years that will never return. The cyclical sequence has a *spike* of badness; the structural sequence has a *slope*. When you see a V, ask "what shock, and did peers feel it too?" When you see a slope, ask "what changed in the competitive landscape, and is gross margin sliding?" — the slope plus eroding gross margin plus peers holding steady is the structural verdict.

There is a third, trickier pattern worth naming: a **structural decline masked by the cycle**. A business in genuine structural decline can still bounce in a cyclical upturn, producing a sawtooth that fools people — 14%, 9%, 12%, 7%, 9% — where each cyclical recovery peaks lower than the last. The trend is down, but the cyclical bounces give bulls a story every other year. The tell is in the *peaks*: in a healthy cyclical business the peaks are roughly level across cycles, while in a structurally declining one each successive peak is lower than the prior peak. Connect the cyclical highs with a line, and if that line slopes down, you have structural decline wearing a cyclical costume. This is why professionals insist on spreading enough years to span at least one full cycle: you cannot ask the cyclical-versus-structural question, let alone answer it, inside a two- or three-year window.

## Normalizing for acquisitions and one-offs

Before you trend anything, you must make the years *comparable*, and the two biggest threats to comparability are acquisitions and one-time items. Trending across a year where the company bought another business, or a year that included a giant one-off gain or charge, compares two different things and produces a nonsense trend. **Normalizing** is the discipline of adjusting the reported figures so that you compare like with like.

**Acquisitions** are the worst offender for trend analysis because they create *inorganic* growth that masquerades as organic. If Northwind's revenue jumps 20% in a year because it bought a competitor mid-year, that 20% is not the underlying business accelerating — it's a bolt-on. The fix is to look at **organic growth** (growth excluding acquisitions and divestitures), which companies often disclose, or to estimate it by backing out the acquired revenue. A revenue trend that looks like acceleration but is entirely acquisitions is a company *buying* growth, not generating it — and buying growth with capital is exactly what crushes ROIC, as we saw. Always ask whether a growth trend is organic or bought.

**One-time items** — a litigation settlement, a restructuring charge, a gain on selling a building, an asset write-down, a tax windfall — distort a single year and break the trend on either side of it. If Year 4's net income was depressed by a \$50M restructuring charge, the YoY decline into Year 4 and the YoY "recovery" out of it are both artifacts of that one charge, not real operating swings. The fix is to normalize: strip the one-off out to see the *underlying* trend. This is the same instinct behind reading the [quality of earnings and the footnotes](/blog/trading/equity-research/reading-the-10k-footnotes-and-mda) — the reported number is a starting point, not the truth.

#### Worked example: normalizing a one-off charge before trending margins

Northwind's reported operating income in Year 4 was \$105M, but that included a \$25M one-time restructuring charge (closing a plant). Reported, the operating-margin trend reads 12.0%, 11.1%, 9.9%, **8.3%**, 6.4% — and the Year 4 figure of 8.3% looks like a sharp acceleration of the decline. But normalize Year 4 by adding back the one-off \$25M: underlying operating income was \$130M, a normalized margin of \$130M ÷ \$1,270M = **10.2%**. The normalized trend reads 12.0%, 11.1%, 9.9%, **10.2%**, 6.4% — and now Year 4 is *not* a step down; it's roughly flat with Year 3. The real cliff is Year 5. Without normalizing, you'd have mis-dated the inflection by a year and possibly misread a one-off as the start of the collapse. Normalizing tells you the decline genuinely accelerated only in Year 5 — a sharper, later, and more alarming signal than the reported numbers suggested.

*Normalize before you trend: a one-off charge can fake an inflection a year early or hide one a year late, and the trend is only honest once the years are comparable.*

## The analyst's spread: putting it all together

Everything in this post converges on a single artifact that every professional analyst builds: the **spread** — a spreadsheet with five to ten years of a company's financials laid out side by side, common-sized, indexed, and accompanied by the key ratios, so the entire trajectory is legible on one screen. Spreading financials is the foundational, unglamorous craft of equity research, and it is where common-size, trend, CAGR, benchmarking, and the ratios from earlier posts all come together into a coherent read.

![A five-year scorecard grid of gross margin, operating margin, SG&A percent, ROIC, and leverage, color-shifting from green in early years to red in later ones](/imgs/blogs/common-size-and-trend-analysis-7.png)

The figure is a compact spread — the distilled version an analyst would build for Northwind. Each row is a metric (gross margin, operating margin, SG&A as a percent of revenue, ROIC, and leverage); each column is a year. The colors tell the story before you even read the numbers: the grid shifts from green in the early years to red in the late ones, because *every metric is deteriorating in the same direction*. Margins fall, returns fall, overhead rises, and leverage climbs from a comfortable 1.0× to an alarming 3.4× net debt to EBITDA. That uniform color gradient — every row reddening left to right — is the visual signature of a structurally weakening business, and it is the kind of pattern that a spread surfaces instantly and a single year's statements completely hide.

The discipline of the spread is what ties this whole series together. You take the income statement and common-size it (margins). You take the balance sheet and common-size it (capital structure, working capital). You compute the [returns on capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa) and trend them (ROIC vs WACC). You index revenue and costs to expose which lines outrun sales. You benchmark every line against peers and the industry median. You normalize for acquisitions and one-offs so the years are comparable. And then you read the *whole motion at once*: not "the margin is 6.4%" but "the margin has fallen by half over five years, faster than peers, driven by gross-margin erosion against a low-cost entrant and overhead the company couldn't grow into, while ROIC dropped below the cost of capital and leverage tripled." That sentence — built entirely from common-size and trend analysis — is what a snapshot can never give you, and it is the bridge to actually valuing the business in [building a DCF](/blog/trading/equity-research/building-a-dcf-part-1-forecasting), where these very trends become the assumptions you forecast forward.

#### Worked example: reading Northwind's full five-year spread

Let's read the spread as an analyst would, in one pass. **Revenue:** \$1,000M → \$1,360M, an 8.0% CAGR — superficially healthy. **Gross margin:** 40.0% → 37.0% — core product economics eroding, the most worrying single line. **Operating margin:** 12.0% → 6.4% — halved, because gross-margin erosion and SG&A creep (18.0% → 21.0% of sales) compounded. **ROIC:** 11.9% → 6.0% — crossed below the ~8% cost of capital, so the business now destroys value as it grows. **Leverage:** net debt/EBITDA 1.0× → 3.4× — the company funded its capital-hungry, low-return growth with debt, adding financial risk on top of operating deterioration. Put together, the spread reads: *a company growing revenue by buying capacity and taking share at falling prices, earning less on more capital each year, financed increasingly by debt — top-line growth masking a structural decline in returns.* That is a sell, or at best an avoid, and you reached it entirely through common-size and trend analysis. A reader who saw only Year 5's snapshot, or only the revenue line, would have reached the opposite conclusion.

*The spread is where every technique in this post converges into one screen, and a uniform color gradient across it — every metric reddening together — is the unmistakable signature of a business in structural decline.*

#### Worked example: spreading the income statement and balance sheet together

The deepest read comes from spreading *both* statements common-sized across all five years and narrating how the three diseases — margin compression, rising capital intensity, and working-capital creep — reinforce each other. Start with the common-size income statement, year by year. **Gross margin:** 40.0%, 39.5%, 39.0%, 38.0%, 37.0% — a slow, relentless three-point erosion in the core product economics. **SG&A as a percent of revenue:** 18.0%, 18.6%, 19.3%, 20.2%, 21.0% — overhead the company kept adding faster than it added sales. **Operating margin** (the sum of those two leaks plus a creeping R&D and D&A line): 12.0%, 11.1%, 9.9%, 8.3%, 6.4% — halved. That is the income-statement story: pricing power eroding from below while cost discipline erodes from above, the two squeezing the profit sliver to nothing.

Now lay the common-size balance sheet beneath it. **Net property, plant and equipment as a percent of total assets:** 50.0%, 52.0%, 54.0%, 56.0%, 58.0% — the business is becoming steadily more capital-intensive, sinking more of every dollar of assets into fixed plant to chase the revenue growth. **Inventory as a percent of revenue:** 20.0%, 20.5%, 21.0%, 21.6%, 22.1% — working-capital creep, more cash tied up in unsold goods each year. **Receivables as a percent of revenue:** 18.0%, 19.0%, 20.5%, 22.0%, 24.0% — the company is collecting more slowly, perhaps extending generous terms to *hold* the revenue it's reporting (a yellow flag for revenue quality). Read the two statements together and the single story snaps into focus: Northwind is **defending a structurally declining business by spending more capital and stretching its working capital to prop up a top line that is only growing because price and terms are being given away.** Margin compression (income statement) explains *why* returns are falling; rising capital intensity and working-capital creep (balance sheet) explain *why the falling returns are spread over an ever-larger capital base*, which is exactly why ROIC collapsed from 11.9% to 6.0%. Neither statement alone tells that story; spread together and common-sized, they tell it inescapably.

*Spreading both statements common-sized across years shows the diseases reinforcing each other: the income statement explains why returns fell, and the balance sheet explains why they fell across a bigger and bigger pile of capital.*

## Common misconceptions

**"Bigger dollar numbers mean a better business."** The single most common beginner error, and the one common-sizing exists to cure. Eastgate's \$1,980M of gross profit dwarfs Vantage's \$312M, yet Vantage is the higher-quality business by margin (52% vs 22%). Raw dollars measure size, not quality. Always common-size before you judge.

**"Revenue growth is unambiguously good."** Revenue is the most reassuring number and the easiest to game — you can grow it by cutting prices, buying competitors, or pouring capital into low-return capacity. Northwind grew revenue 36% while destroying value. Growth is only good if it earns a return above the cost of capital. Pair every revenue trend with a return-on-capital trend.

**"CAGR tells you how the company actually grew."** CAGR is a deliberate smoothing that *throws away the path* — it depends only on the two endpoints. A company with a 8% CAGR might have grown smoothly or lurched from boom to bust. Worse, a cleverly chosen base year (a recession trough) inflates the CAGR artificially. Always check the endpoints and look at the year-by-year path alongside the CAGR.

**"A margin or ratio is good or bad on its own."** No number means anything until it is benchmarked against the company's own history and its peers. A 37% gross margin is strong for a commodity maker and weak for a luxury brand. "Below median and declining" is the damning combination; "above median and rising" is the great one. Level without trend, or trend without benchmark, is half a read.

**"One bad year means the business is broken."** A single down year is just as likely to be a cyclical wobble or a one-off charge as a structural decline. The fix is to look at the multi-year *shape* (a V that reverts vs a staircase that doesn't), check whether peers fell too, and normalize for one-offs. Mistaking a cyclical dip for structural decline makes you sell great businesses at the bottom.

**"Consolidated numbers tell the whole story."** A flat or growing consolidated line can hide a dying core propped up by a booming new segment, or a high-margin business shrinking while a low-margin one grows. Segment trend analysis routinely reverses the conclusion you'd draw from the consolidated statement. Always split the segments when they're disclosed.

## How it shows up in real markets

The techniques in this post are not academic — they are exactly how real declines were caught (or missed) in famous cases, and how the best investors read businesses.

**The "growth masking declining returns" trap is the most common destroyer of capital in public markets.** Companies that grow revenue through serial acquisitions while their organic returns on capital quietly fall are a recurring pattern — the financial engineering inflates the top line while ROIC erodes underneath. Investors who trend ROIC and distinguish organic from acquired growth catch it; investors who watch only the revenue line and the headline EPS get caught. This is precisely the divergence Northwind illustrates, and it has played out in real "roll-up" companies across many industries, where a string of acquisitions produces years of revenue growth followed by a sudden collapse when the returns finally show through. The discipline of separating organic from inorganic growth, and pairing the revenue trend with the ROIC trend, is what would have flagged it years early.

**Forensic cases hinge on trend and common-size anomalies.** In frauds like [Enron](/blog/trading/finance/enron-2001-accounting-fraud) and [Wirecard](/blog/trading/finance/wirecard-the-german-fintech-fraud), one of the earliest tells was a line that didn't move the way it should relative to the business — receivables or "cash" growing far faster than revenue, or margins implausibly stable while peers' swung. Common-sizing and trending the balance sheet is how short-sellers spot the line that's growing faster than it can legitimately grow. A receivables balance climbing from 16% of revenue to 30% over a few years, while the business model hasn't changed, is the kind of trend anomaly that precedes a discovery that the revenue (or the cash) wasn't real. The technique is mundane; the discovery is not.

**Great investors are, at bottom, trend-and-quality readers.** The owner-mindset, moat-focused approach associated with [Buffett and Berkshire](/blog/trading/finance/warren-buffett-berkshire-value-investing) is fundamentally about finding businesses with high *and durable* returns on capital — which you can only assess by trending ROIC over five-to-ten years and benchmarking it against peers. A high ROIC that holds steady through a decade is the fingerprint of a moat; a high ROIC that erodes is a moat being competed away. The whole framework rests on reading the multi-year trend in returns, not the single-year level — exactly the muscle this post builds.

**Cyclical industries punish the trend-reading mistake in both directions.** In deeply cyclical sectors — commodities, semiconductors, shipping, autos — a single year's margins are nearly meaningless because they swing violently with the cycle. Analysts who mistake a cyclical-peak margin for a structural one over-pay at the top; analysts who mistake a cyclical-trough margin for structural decline sell at the bottom. The correct read normalizes across the whole cycle (mid-cycle margins, peak-to-peak CAGR) and distinguishes the cycle from any genuine structural shift layered on top. This is the practical reason professionals spread *many* years: one cycle is the minimum window in which the cyclical-versus-structural question can even be asked.

**Retailers are the textbook case of SG&A creep hiding in plain sight.** Brick-and-mortar retail has produced a long line of companies whose gross margins held up reasonably well — they were still buying and selling goods at a normal markup — while their operating margins quietly bled out because *occupancy and overhead costs crept up as a percent of sales for years*. When same-store sales stop growing but the rent, the store payroll, the corporate overhead, and the legacy fixed costs keep rising, SG&A as a percent of revenue ratchets up a point or two every year. A casual observer watching gross margin (stable) and revenue (roughly flat) sees nothing wrong; an analyst common-sizing and trending the full income statement sees SG&A climbing from, say, 28% to 34% of sales over five years and operating margin halving as a direct consequence. Department stores and mall-based chains have lived this exact pattern: the deterioration was visible in the common-size trend years before it became a headline about store closures and restructuring. The number that mattered was never the dollars of SG&A — which "only grew with inflation" — but SG&A as a *share of a no-longer-growing revenue base*.

**Hardware and component makers show gross-margin erosion as a product commoditizes.** A technology product that starts out differentiated and premium often earns a lush gross margin — 50%, 60% — in its early years. But as competitors catch up and the product becomes a commodity, that gross margin erodes a few points every year as pricing power evaporates, even while revenue may still be growing on volume. PC makers, disk-drive manufacturers, memory and display producers, and countless component suppliers have traced this arc: gross margin sliding from the high 30s into the low 20s or teens over a decade as the product turned from a differentiated good into an interchangeable one. The revenue line can look fine — even rising — throughout, which is why so many investors held on too long. The signal was always in the *gross-margin trend*: a sustained, multi-year decline in gross margin is the single most reliable early warning of a product losing its differentiation, because gross margin sits closest to the core economics of "can we charge more than it costs to make this?" When that answer is permanently shrinking, no amount of cost-cutting below the gross line will save the business — and the common-size trend names the disease while there is still time to act on it.

## When this matters and further reading

Common-size and trend analysis is the capstone of learning to *read* statements, because it is what turns the static skills — knowing what each line means, computing each ratio — into a dynamic read of where a business is heading. Every time you pick up a company's financials, the first thing to do is not to admire a single year but to spread five-to-ten of them, common-size them, index the key lines, compute the growth rates, benchmark against peers, normalize the distortions, and read the whole motion at once. The output is a sentence about the trajectory of margins, returns, capital intensity, and leverage — the story the numbers are telling about the future, not the past.

This post completes the analytical toolkit built across the series. It rests directly on the [income statement walked line by line](/blog/trading/equity-research/income-statement-line-by-line-revenue-to-net-income) (the lines you common-size), the [profitability and margins](/blog/trading/equity-research/profitability-margins-gross-operating-net) post (the margins you trend), and the [returns on capital](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa) post (the ROIC whose trend separates real growth from value destruction). And it sets up the next move: once you can read where the trends are heading, you can forecast them forward — which is exactly what [building a DCF](/blog/trading/equity-research/building-a-dcf-part-1-forecasting) does, turning the historical trends you've spread into the explicit assumptions that drive an intrinsic value. The snapshot tells you what a company is; the trend tells you what it is becoming; and what it is becoming is what you are actually buying.
