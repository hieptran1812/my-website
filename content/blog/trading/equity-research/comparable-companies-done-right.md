> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A comparable-companies analysis is only as good as the peer set it rests on, and most peer sets are chosen lazily, computed inconsistently, and applied without thought. Done right, comps are a powerful cross-check on value; done wrong, they quietly launder the market's mistakes into your "analysis."
>
> - **A good comparable shares the business itself, not the sector label** — the same business model, unit economics, growth, returns on capital, risk, and capital intensity. Two companies in "the same industry" can deserve completely different multiples, and two companies in different industries can be near-perfect comps.
> - **The multiples must be computed identically across every peer** — same calendar period, same diluted share count, the same adjustments for one-offs and stock-based compensation, a consistently built enterprise value. One peer measured on a different basis poisons the whole set.
> - **Multiples differ for reasons, and the reasons are knowable**: growth, return on capital (ROIC and margins), risk, and leverage. The professional move is to regress the multiple on its drivers — find the *justified* multiple your company deserves — rather than slapping the peer median on it.
> - **You then adjust** for the things the raw multiple can't see: control versus minority, liquidity, country and accounting differences (IFRS vs GAAP, leases, stock comp). Trim outliers, use the **median not the mean**, and never trust a peer group that is expensive together.
> - **Comps are a cross-check, not a verdict.** Triangulate the comps value against an intrinsic DCF and present the whole thing as a **football field** — a range, with the overlap as your defensible estimate — never a single number dressed up as precision.

Open almost any equity research report, any pitch deck, any back-of-the-envelope valuation, and you will find the same gesture: a table of "comparable companies," a column of multiples, a peer median highlighted in bold, and then — applied to the company in question — that median multiple, producing a value. It looks rigorous. It has numbers in it. It cites real prices that real investors are paying right now. And most of the time it is close to worthless, because the peer set was chosen by typing the company's name into a screen and grabbing whatever shared its sector code, the multiples were lifted off a data terminal without checking how each was computed, and the median was applied with no thought about *why* the company in front of us might deserve more or less than its peers.

This is the great paradox of relative valuation. It is the most widely used valuation method on earth — the overwhelming majority of real-world valuations are done with multiples, not discounted cash flows — and it is also the most widely abused. The mechanics are so easy that people skip the thinking. You can build a comps table in twenty minutes, and the twenty-minute version is the one that ends up in most reports. But comparable-companies analysis done *right* is genuinely hard, and the difficulty is exactly where the value lives. The skill is not in computing a multiple; a spreadsheet does that. The skill is in choosing companies that are genuinely comparable, computing the multiples so consistently that the comparison is fair, understanding *why* the numbers differ across the set, and adjusting for those differences instead of pretending they don't exist.

![A side by side comparison of a sloppy comps set chosen by sector label against a disciplined comps set chosen by the real dimensions of comparability](/imgs/blogs/comparable-companies-done-right-1.png)

The figure above is the whole argument of this post in one picture. On the left is the lazy comps set: same sector, and nothing else checked. On the right is the disciplined one: the companies are matched on the dimensions that actually determine what a business is worth — its business model, its economics, its growth, its returns on capital, its risk, and its capital intensity. The label "same sector" is a starting filter, not an answer. A regional bank and a global investment bank are both "financials"; a high-growth subscription software company and a shrinking legacy IT-services firm are both "technology." Treating them as comps because a database put them in the same bucket is how you end up applying a 30x multiple to a business that deserves 12x, and calling the resulting number "value."

This post is about doing it right. We are going to build a comparable-companies analysis from absolutely nothing, using a make-believe company called **Northwind Industries** that we follow through this whole series. We will assemble a peer set, compute each multiple identically across every peer, spot a "peer" that isn't really comparable and throw it out, apply the median honestly, and then — the part almost everyone skips — adjust the multiple for the real differences between Northwind and its peers, using the logic of *why* multiples differ in the first place. Then we will reconcile the comps value with a discounted cash flow and present the whole thing as a football field. By the end you will never again look at a comps table the same way: you will see, instantly, whether it was built by someone thinking or someone copying.

If you are arriving cold, the one idea this rests on is that there are [only two pillars of valuation](/blog/trading/equity-research/two-pillars-of-valuation-intrinsic-vs-relative): intrinsic value, computed from the cash a business will produce, and relative value, read off what similar businesses sell for. Comparable companies are the workhorse of the second pillar, and that earlier post showed the single secret that makes all of this work — **a multiple is just a discounted cash flow with its assumptions compressed and hidden.** Hold that idea close, because it is the key that unlocks why multiples differ and how to adjust them.

## Foundations: the building blocks of a comp, from zero

Before we can build a peer set we have to be precise about a handful of terms. Relative valuation has its own vocabulary, and getting it exact now prevents the sloppiness that ruins most comps tables.

### A multiple: price scaled to a fundamental

A **multiple** is a ratio of a company's value to some measure of its size or performance. The most famous is the **price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio** — the share price divided by earnings per share. If a stock trades at \$30 and earned \$2 per share last year, its P/E is 15, and people say "it trades at fifteen times earnings." The multiple answers a simple question: how many dollars of price are you paying for one dollar of the fundamental?

There are two great families of multiples, and the difference between them is the source of half the errors in amateur comps. **Equity multiples** put a measure of the *equity's* value (the share price, or the whole equity market capitalization) over a fundamental that belongs to the *equity holders* — earnings, book value, equity cash flow. P/E, price-to-book (P/B), and price-to-earnings-growth (PEG) are equity multiples. **Enterprise-value multiples** put the value of the *whole business* — debt and equity together — over a fundamental that belongs to the whole business, before the split between lenders and owners. EV/EBITDA and EV/Sales are enterprise multiples. The complete taxonomy of these, and what each one lies about, is the subject of [multiples 101](/blog/trading/equity-research/multiples-101-pe-ev-ebitda-pb-ps-peg); here we only need to keep the two families from getting crossed, because mixing them is the single most common mechanical error in a comps table.

### Enterprise value: the price of the whole business

**Enterprise value (EV)** is the value of the entire business — what it would cost to buy the whole thing, lock, stock, and barrel, free of its existing financing. You compute it from the market value of the equity and then adjust for the debt and cash:

$$\text{EV} = \text{Market capitalization} + \text{Total debt} - \text{Cash and equivalents}$$

The logic is the logic of an acquirer. If you buy all of a company's stock (its market cap), you also inherit its debt, which you will have to pay off, so you add the debt to the price. But you also get its cash, which you can use to pay down some of that debt, so you subtract the cash. The result is the true economic cost of owning the business's operations. Enterprise value is the number you pair with operating fundamentals like EBITDA and sales, because those fundamentals belong to the whole business — they are earned *before* any interest is paid to lenders, so they must be compared to the value of the whole business, not just the equity slice. Getting EV right, consistently, across every peer, is most of the discipline in an enterprise-multiple comps set.

### EBITDA, earnings, and the fundamentals on the bottom

The denominators of the multiples are the fundamentals, and each tells a different story. **Earnings (net income)** is the bottom line — profit after every cost, including interest and taxes — and it belongs to the equity holders, which is why it pairs with the equity price in P/E. **EBITDA** — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization — strips out the financing decision (interest), the tax regime (taxes), and the big non-cash accounting charges (depreciation and amortization) to get a rough proxy for the operating cash the business throws off before any of those. Because it is measured before interest, it belongs to the whole business, which is why it pairs with enterprise value in EV/EBITDA. **Sales (revenue)** is the top line, used when earnings are negative or distorted. **Book value** is the accounting net worth of the equity, used for asset-heavy and financial firms. Each fundamental is the right one for a different situation, and we will see when each fits.

### Comparable, peer, and the meaning of "the same business"

Finally, the word at the heart of it all. A **comparable company** — a "comp" or "peer" — is a company similar enough to the one you are valuing that the market's verdict on it tells you something about yours. The word that matters is *similar*, and the whole craft is in defining it precisely. Two companies are genuinely comparable when they share the things that determine value: the **business model** (how they make money), the **unit economics** (margins, the profit on each sale), the **growth** prospects, the **return on capital** they earn, the **risk** of their cash flows, and the **capital intensity** (how much they must reinvest to grow). Sharing a sector label is neither necessary nor sufficient. The deepest single idea in this post is that *companies deserve the same multiple to the extent they share the same hidden DCF*, and two businesses share a hidden DCF when they share those value drivers — not when a database files them under the same code.

## What makes a company genuinely comparable

Start with the central question, because everything downstream depends on getting it right: **what makes two companies comparable?** The lazy answer — "they're in the same industry" — is the source of most bad comps. The real answer is that two companies are comparable when an investor would price them off the same fundamental logic, which means they must match on the small set of things that actually drive what a business is worth.

Recall the hidden-DCF idea from the [two pillars post](/blog/trading/equity-research/two-pillars-of-valuation-intrinsic-vs-relative): every multiple secretly encodes a discounted cash flow. For a stable business the fair P/E is roughly `payout ÷ (r − g)` — the payout ratio over the discount rate minus the growth rate. That tiny formula tells you exactly which dimensions matter, because they are the only things that move the formula. Growth (`g`) is in there. Risk is in there, through the discount rate (`r`). Return on capital is in there, hidden inside the payout ratio — a business that earns high returns on the capital it reinvests can grow faster while paying out more. So two companies deserve the same multiple when they share growth, risk, and returns on capital. That is the whole theory of comparability, and everything practical descends from it.

Let me lay out the dimensions concretely, because in practice you check them one by one:

**Business model.** How does the company actually make money? A subscription software business with recurring revenue and 90% gross margins is a fundamentally different animal from a hardware business that sells a box once at 30% margins, even if both are "technology." Recurring revenue deserves a higher multiple than one-time revenue because it is more predictable and longer-lived — a smaller `r`, a more reliable `g`. Match the model first.

**Unit economics and margins.** How profitable is each dollar of sales? A business that converts 25 cents of every revenue dollar into operating profit is worth more per dollar of sales than one that converts 8 cents — and that difference shows up directly in EV/Sales and indirectly in every other multiple. Two retailers with very different margins are not clean comps for a sales multiple.

**Growth.** How fast is the business growing, and how durably? Growth is the single biggest driver of multiples, because in `payout ÷ (r − g)` a higher `g` shrinks the denominator and explodes the multiple. A company growing 20% a year genuinely deserves a higher P/E than one growing 3%, and refusing to account for that gap is the most common comps mistake there is.

**Return on capital.** How much profit does the business earn on the money invested to run it? This is the deepest measure of business quality, and it is the engine behind why two companies with identical growth can still deserve different multiples. A business that earns a 30% return on invested capital can fund its growth cheaply and pay out more; a business earning 8% must plow everything back just to stand still. The full machinery is in the [returns on capital post](/blog/trading/equity-research/returns-on-capital-roic-roe-roa), and it is no accident that we will use ROIC as a key driver when we regress multiples on their causes.

**Risk.** How certain are the cash flows? A company with a cyclical, commodity-exposed, customer-concentrated, or heavily regulated business has riskier cash flows — a higher discount rate `r` — and deserves a lower multiple. A stable consumer-staples business with diversified, repeatable demand deserves a higher one. Two companies with the same growth but very different risk are not the same comp.

**Capital intensity.** How much must the business reinvest to grow? A business that must build a new factory for every dollar of new revenue produces less *free* cash per dollar of earnings than an asset-light business that grows on almost no incremental capital. Capital intensity lowers the payout the business can sustain at a given growth rate, which lowers the justified multiple.

#### Worked example: two "tech" companies that are not comps

Suppose you are valuing **Northwind Software**, a subscription-based business-software company. Your data terminal, asked for "technology" peers, hands you two candidates. The first, **Evergreen Apps**, is another subscription-software business: 88% gross margins, 18% revenue growth, recurring revenue, a 28% return on invested capital, light capital needs. The second, **Granite Systems**, is a legacy IT-hardware-and-services firm: 34% gross margins, 1% revenue growth, mostly one-time project revenue, a 9% return on invested capital, and it must carry heavy inventory and equipment.

On the screen both are "technology," and a lazy comps set would average their multiples and apply the average to Northwind. But look at the drivers. Evergreen matches Northwind on every dimension — model, margins, growth, returns, capital intensity. Granite matches on *none* of them. Granite trades at, say, 7x EV/EBITDA; Evergreen trades at 22x. The "average" of 14.5x is a number that describes no real business and certainly not Northwind. Evergreen is a genuine comp whose 22x is a meaningful data point; Granite is not a comp at all, and including it would drag Northwind's implied value down by nearly half for no reason other than a shared sector code. *Comparability lives in the value drivers — model, margins, growth, returns, risk, capital intensity — and a shared industry label is a hint to start checking them, never a substitute for checking them.*

## Building the peer set: the funnel from universe to clean comps

Knowing *what* makes a comp does not yet tell you *how* to assemble a set of them. In practice you build a peer set as a funnel: you start with a broad universe and narrow it through a sequence of filters, each one tightening the match, until you are left with a handful of genuinely comparable companies. Get the funnel right and the rest of the analysis almost builds itself; get it wrong and no amount of careful arithmetic downstream can save you.

![A vertical funnel narrowing from the broad sector universe down through business model size geography and lifecycle filters to a clean set of five genuine peers](/imgs/blogs/comparable-companies-done-right-2.png)

The figure above shows the funnel for Northwind. You start at the top with the **broad universe** — every company a data screen returns for the sector, maybe forty names. Then you apply filters in order of how fundamental they are. First the **business model**: keep only companies that make money the same way, which immediately cuts the hardware firms, the services firms, and the conglomerates that happen to have a software division. Then **size**: a company ten times larger or smaller than Northwind faces different economics, different growth ceilings, and often a different valuation regime, so you keep peers within a reasonable size band. Then **geography**: a company whose revenue, costs, and risks live in a different country or currency carries country risk, tax, and accounting differences that muddy the comparison, so unless you are deliberately building a global set with adjustments, you keep peers in comparable markets. Then **lifecycle stage**: a hyper-growth startup burning cash and a mature cash cow in the same business are not comps, because they sit at completely different points on the growth-and-margin curve. What drops out the bottom of the funnel is a clean set — five or so genuine peers — that you can actually trust.

A few principles make the funnel work:

**Prefer pure-plays to conglomerates.** A *pure-play* company does essentially one thing, so its multiple reflects that one business cleanly. A *conglomerate* does many things, so its blended multiple is a weighted average of several different businesses and reflects none of them precisely. If you are valuing Northwind's software business, a pure-play software peer is worth ten muddy conglomerate "peers" whose software division is buried inside an industrial holding company. When you must use a conglomerate, you cannot use its overall multiple — you would have to value its software segment separately (a sum-of-the-parts move), which is rarely worth the trouble when pure-plays exist.

**Match size, but don't fetishize it.** Size matters because it changes economics — bigger companies often have scale advantages, more stable cash flows, better access to capital, and so trade at different multiples — but you can stretch the band if the business is genuinely identical. A peer half Northwind's size in the exact same business is a better comp than one the exact same size in a different business. Use size as a tiebreaker among model-matched peers, not as a primary filter that overrides the model.

**Be honest about geography.** Country matters more than people think. The same business in a high-risk emerging market deserves a lower multiple than in a stable developed one, because of country risk, currency risk, and often weaker investor protections. Different countries also use different accounting standards (IFRS versus US GAAP), which can make the *same* business look different on paper. You can include foreign peers, but only if you adjust for the country and accounting gaps — and you must flag that you have done so.

**Match the lifecycle stage.** A company's multiple depends enormously on where it sits in its life. The same business, as a young hyper-growth firm, might trade at 15x sales; as a mature, slow-growth incumbent, at 3x sales. Mixing the two produces a peer median that describes neither. Match the stage, or explicitly adjust for the gap using the growth logic we develop below.

A practical rule for the size of the set: **aim for five to ten genuine comps, and prefer five clean ones to fifteen muddy ones.** A larger set is not more reliable if half of it isn't really comparable — it just buries the signal under noise and makes the median meaningless. The quality of each peer matters far more than the count.

#### Worked example: Northwind's funnel from forty names to five

You screen for software companies and get **forty** names. First filter, business model: you keep only subscription-software businesses with recurring revenue, which cuts the list to **fourteen** — gone are the IT-services firms, the hardware makers, and a games company that happened to be classified as software. Second filter, size: Northwind has about \$1.2 billion in revenue, so you keep peers between roughly \$400 million and \$4 billion, which trims to **nine**. Third filter, geography: you keep companies that report in US GAAP and earn most of their revenue in comparable developed markets, dropping two European peers you would have to adjust, leaving **seven**. Fourth filter, lifecycle: you drop one pre-profitability hyper-growth peer trading at 25x sales (a different animal entirely) and one shrinking peer in run-off, leaving **five** clean comps: Evergreen Apps, Beacon Systems, Cardinal Cloud, Delta Workflows, and Foundry Analytics.

Five peers, each genuinely matched on model, size, market, and stage. *The funnel is the part of comps that separates real analysis from copy-paste: by the time five names survive every filter, the comparison they offer is meaningful, whereas the original forty would have produced a median that described no business at all.* And notice — you have not computed a single multiple yet. That is correct. Choosing the set comes first, because the most carefully computed multiple on the wrong peer is still noise.

## The mechanics: computing every multiple identically

Now the part that looks easy and isn't. You have your five comps; you need each one's multiples. The cardinal rule, the one violated constantly, is this: **every multiple must be computed identically across every peer.** Same calendar period, same definition of the numerator and denominator, same adjustments, same share count basis. The whole point of a comps table is to compare like with like, and the instant one peer is measured on a different basis, the comparison is corrupted and the median is a lie.

![A clean comparable companies table for Northwind and five peers with market cap enterprise value EBITDA earnings and the EV to EBITDA and price to earnings multiples computed on the same basis](/imgs/blogs/comparable-companies-done-right-3.png)

The figure above is what a clean comps table looks like. Each row is a peer; each column is a building block (market cap, debt, cash, enterprise value, EBITDA, net income, shares) and then the multiples (EV/EBITDA, P/E). The discipline is in the *consistency*: every market cap uses the current share price times the same fully-diluted share count; every enterprise value adds the same definition of debt and subtracts the same definition of cash; every EBITDA uses the same forward (or trailing) period and the same adjustments. When the columns are built consistently, the multiples on the right are comparable, and the median at the bottom means something. Let us walk through the specific traps.

**Same calendar period.** Compare every peer on the same time basis — all trailing-twelve-months (TTM, the last four reported quarters), or all next-twelve-months forward estimates (NTM), but never a mix. A peer's forward multiple looks cheaper than its trailing multiple simply because the forward earnings are higher; mixing the two makes one peer look artificially cheap or dear. Professionals usually prefer **forward** multiples (NTM) because valuation is about the future, but whichever you pick, pick it for all of them.

**Diluted shares, consistently.** Market cap is price times shares — but *which* shares? Use **fully diluted shares**, which include the shares that would be created if all the in-the-money options, warrants, and convertible securities were exercised. A company with a lot of employee options has more shares lurking than its basic count shows, and ignoring them understates its market cap and overstates its per-share metrics. Use the same diluted basis for every peer.

**A consistently built enterprise value.** EV is where most inconsistency creeps in. Use the same definition of debt (do you include operating leases? pension deficits? preferred stock?) and the same definition of cash (all cash, or only "excess" cash beyond operating needs?) for every peer. The most common modern trap is **operating leases**: since recent accounting changes, leases sit on the balance sheet as debt-like liabilities, and whether you include them in EV must be consistent — and must match how you computed EBITDA (if EBITDA is before lease expense, the lease liability belongs in EV).

**The same adjustments to the fundamentals.** Reported EBITDA and earnings are full of one-time items — restructuring charges, litigation settlements, gains on asset sales, impairments — that are not part of the ongoing business. To compare peers fairly you must **normalize** each one's fundamental by stripping out the same kinds of one-offs across the board. If you adjust one peer's EBITDA for a restructuring charge, you must check every peer for the same kind of charge. Inconsistent normalization is how one peer ends up looking cheap purely because nobody cleaned up its messy quarter.

**Stock-based compensation.** This deserves its own line because it is a modern minefield, especially in software. **Stock-based compensation (SBC)** is a real cost — the company is paying employees in shares, diluting owners — but it is a *non-cash* charge, so "adjusted EBITDA" that adds it back inflates the number. Some companies report EBITDA before SBC, some after. You must treat it identically for every peer: either all of them add SBC back (and then you remember the dilution shows up in the share count) or none of them do. A peer that adds back a huge SBC charge will look far more profitable than one that doesn't, purely as an accounting choice.

#### Worked example: building Northwind's comps table consistently

Take Northwind and its five clean peers. For each, you compute the building blocks on the same basis: current price × fully-diluted shares for market cap; plus total debt (including capitalized operating leases) minus all cash for enterprise value; forward-twelve-months EBITDA and net income, each normalized for one-time items, with SBC treated the same way (you choose to leave it as an expense — not added back — for every peer, the more conservative and consistent choice).

Here is what the consistent table yields for the five peers:

| Peer | EV (\$bn) | NTM EBITDA (\$bn) | EV/EBITDA | NTM EPS (\$) | Price (\$) | P/E |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Evergreen Apps | 4.40 | 0.20 | 22.0x | 1.20 | 30.0 | 25.0x |
| Beacon Systems | 3.60 | 0.18 | 20.0x | 1.05 | 21.0 | 20.0x |
| Cardinal Cloud | 5.04 | 0.24 | 21.0x | 1.30 | 28.6 | 22.0x |
| Delta Workflows | 2.88 | 0.16 | 18.0x | 0.90 | 16.2 | 18.0x |
| Foundry Analytics | 3.20 | 0.20 | 16.0x | 0.85 | 17.0 | 20.0x |

Because every number was built the same way, the multiples on the right are genuinely comparable. The EV/EBITDA multiples cluster between 16x and 22x; the P/E multiples between 18x and 25x. That clustering is the *reward* for consistency — when peers are well-chosen and consistently measured, their multiples bunch up, and the bunching is the signal that you have a real peer group. *The single most important habit in comps is monotonous consistency: same period, same shares, same EV definition, same adjustments — and the payoff is that the multiples cluster tightly enough to mean something.*

## Trim the outliers, use the median not the mean

You have a column of multiples. Before you reduce them to a single number to apply to your company, two small disciplines protect you from being misled: trim the outliers, and summarize with the **median**, not the mean.

![A before and after comparison showing how one outlier peer drags the mean far above the median while trimming the outlier brings the mean back in line](/imgs/blogs/comparable-companies-done-right-6.png)

The figure above shows why this matters. On the left, the raw set includes one peer trading at a wild 40x while the others sit between 16x and 22x. The **mean** of that set is dragged up to nearly 24x by the single outlier — even though four of the five peers trade well below it. The **median** — the middle value when you line them up — is barely moved, sitting calmly at 21x, because it doesn't care how extreme the outlier is, only that it is above the middle. On the right, after you investigate and trim the 40x peer (it turned out to have a temporary collapse in earnings that inflated its P/E, or it isn't really comparable), the mean falls back into line with the median. The lesson is twofold: **the median is robust to outliers and the mean is not**, and **outliers should be investigated, not blindly averaged in.**

Why the median? Because multiples are not symmetric. A multiple can't go below zero but can spike arbitrarily high — a company with tiny earnings has an enormous P/E, a company with a temporary loss has a *negative* P/E that is meaningless. These spikes are common, and they pull the mean around violently while leaving the median stable. In a set of five to ten peers, one weird quarter at one peer can swing the mean by several turns of multiple; the median shrugs it off. Professionals quote the median as the central estimate and often show the mean alongside only as a cross-check — if the two diverge a lot, that divergence is itself a flag that an outlier is lurking.

Why trim outliers rather than just rely on the median to ignore them? Because an outlier is *information*. A peer trading at 40x when everyone else is at 20x is telling you something: either it is genuinely different (faster growth, a takeover rumor, a temporary earnings dip) and not a clean comp, or its number is computed wrong. Either way you should investigate it, and usually the answer is to exclude it with a stated reason rather than let it sit in the set distorting your sense of the range. Trimming is not data-fudging if you do it transparently and for a reason — "excluded because a one-time charge depressed its EBITDA, inflating EV/EBITDA to 40x" is honest analysis. Silently dropping the peers that don't suit your thesis is not.

#### Worked example: the outlier that isn't really a peer

Return to Northwind's five-peer set, but suppose a sixth name, **Phoenix Data**, survived your funnel and shows a P/E of **40x** against the others' 18x–25x. Before you average it in, you investigate. You find that Phoenix took a large one-time litigation charge last year that crushed its net income, so its trailing P/E is mechanically inflated — the denominator collapsed temporarily. On a *normalized* basis, stripping out the one-off charge, Phoenix's P/E is actually about 23x, right in line with the group. So there are two honest moves: either re-include Phoenix at its normalized 23x (consistent with how you normalized everyone), or exclude it and note why.

Watch what the choice does to the summary. With the raw 40x included, the **mean** P/E of six peers is about 24.7x and the **median** is 22.5x — a 2.2-turn gap that should make you suspicious. After normalizing Phoenix to 23x, the mean falls to 21.5x and the median to 22x — they converge, the sign of a clean set. Applying 22x to Northwind's \$0.95 forward EPS gives \$20.90; applying the contaminated 24.7x mean would have given \$23.47 — a 12% overvaluation born entirely of one peer's messy quarter and the wrong summary statistic. *Use the median because it ignores the spikes, but investigate every spike anyway, because an outlier is either a peer you must fix or a peer you must remove — never a number you quietly average in.*

## Spotting the peer that isn't really comparable

The most consequential decision in a comps analysis is which companies are *not* in it. A peer that doesn't belong can shift your value estimate by 20% or more, and the failure mode is insidious because the offending company really does share the sector label — it looks like it belongs. Developing the instinct for "this one isn't really a comp" is the heart of the craft.

The tell is almost always a **driver mismatch**: the candidate peer differs from your company on one of the value drivers — growth, returns on capital, risk, capital intensity, business model — by enough that the market correctly prices it differently, and including it would import that difference into your estimate as if it applied to your company. The discipline is to look past the multiple at the *drivers behind it*, and ask whether those drivers match.

A useful habit: for every candidate peer, before you accept its multiple, decode *why* the market gives it that multiple, and check that the reason applies to your company too. If a peer trades at 25x because it is growing 30% a year and your company grows 8%, that 25x is a fact about a fast grower, not about your company, and importing it is an error. If a peer trades at 12x because it is in structural decline and your company is healthy, that 12x is a fact about a dying business, and importing it would unfairly punish you. The multiple is downstream of the drivers; match the drivers, not the multiple.

#### Worked example: removing the peer that isn't a peer

Suppose your Northwind set of five looks clean, but a colleague insists on adding **Summit Legacy**, an old-line enterprise-software company that screens as a perfect match: same size, same sector, same country, profitable. Its EV/EBITDA is **11x** — well below the group's 16x–22x — and adding it would pull the median down and lower Northwind's implied value by roughly 15%. Should it go in?

Decode the 11x. You find Summit's revenue is *shrinking* 4% a year as customers migrate off its aging product, its net revenue retention is below 100%, and it earns a 10% return on invested capital versus the group's 25%+. The market is pricing Summit as a melting ice cube — a business in slow structural decline — which is exactly why it trades at 11x. Northwind, by contrast, is *growing* 14% with 28% returns on capital. Summit's 11x is a true and correct multiple — for a declining business. It is simply not a fact about a healthy grower like Northwind. Including it would be like valuing a new car using the price of a similar-model car with a blown engine: same make and model, completely different worth. You exclude Summit, with the stated reason "structural decline, negative net retention, sub-group ROIC — not comparable on growth or quality," and your median holds at 21x. *The peers you exclude shape your answer as much as the ones you keep; a company can match on every surface attribute and still belong out of the set because the one thing it does not share — its growth or its quality — is the very thing the multiple is pricing.*

## Why multiples differ: the regression to the justified multiple

Here is the intellectual core of doing comps right, the idea that separates a thoughtful analyst from a median-slapper. Even within a *clean* peer set, the multiples are not identical — they range from 16x to 22x EV/EBITDA, from 18x to 25x P/E. Those differences are **not noise**. They exist for reasons, and the reasons are the value drivers: peers with higher growth, higher returns on capital, lower risk, and lower leverage trade at higher multiples. If you understand the relationship between the multiples and their drivers, you can compute the multiple your company *deserves* given its own drivers — the **justified multiple** — rather than blindly applying the peer median.

![A scatter plot of each peer price to earnings multiple against a composite of its growth and return on capital with an upward sloping fitted line showing the justified multiple for Northwind](/imgs/blogs/comparable-companies-done-right-4.png)

The figure above is the picture of this idea. On the horizontal axis is a driver — here a composite of each peer's growth and return on capital (you could use just growth, or just ROIC, or a blend). On the vertical axis is each peer's P/E multiple. Each peer is a dot. And the dots line up: the peers with higher growth-and-returns trade at higher multiples, the ones with lower drivers trade lower. You fit a line through them — a **regression** — and that line *is* the relationship between the driver and the multiple, encoded by the market itself. Then you find your company's position on the horizontal axis (its own growth and returns), read up to the line, and the height of the line at that point is your **justified multiple** — the multiple the market's own logic says your company deserves, given its drivers. If Northwind's growth-and-returns put it above the average peer, the line says it deserves an *above-median* multiple; if below, a discount.

This is the single most powerful upgrade you can make to a comps analysis, and it is worth understanding why it works. The peer median throws away information — it treats every peer as equally relevant and ignores the fact that they have different drivers. The regression *uses* that information: it learns, from the spread of the peers themselves, how much an extra point of growth or returns is worth in multiple terms, and then applies that learned relationship to your specific company. It turns the comps set from a single blunt number (the median) into a *function* that prices your company's particular profile. You no longer ask "what's the median multiple?" — you ask "given how the market prices growth and returns *in this exact peer group*, what multiple does my company's growth and returns earn?"

The driver you regress on depends on the multiple. For **P/E**, the classic drivers are growth and risk — which is exactly why the **PEG ratio** (P/E divided by the growth rate) exists: it is a crude one-variable version of this regression, normalizing the P/E by growth so you can compare a fast grower's high P/E to a slow grower's low one on a level field. For **EV/EBITDA**, growth and returns on capital and risk matter. For **P/B** (price-to-book), the single cleanest driver is **return on equity** — a bank earning 18% on its book equity deserves a far higher price-to-book than one earning 8%, and a price-to-book-versus-ROE regression is one of the most reliable relationships in all of finance. The general move is always the same: identify the drivers the multiple should depend on, regress the peers' multiples on those drivers, and read your company's justified multiple off the fitted line.

#### Worked example: regressing Northwind's justified P/E

Take Northwind's five peers and their forward P/E ratios, alongside each peer's expected earnings growth:

| Peer | Growth | P/E |
|---|---|---|
| Delta Workflows | 9% | 18.0x |
| Beacon Systems | 11% | 20.0x |
| Foundry Analytics | 10% | 20.0x |
| Cardinal Cloud | 16% | 22.0x |
| Evergreen Apps | 20% | 25.0x |

Plot P/E against growth and the points line up beautifully on a rising line. Fit a simple regression and you get roughly **P/E ≈ 11.5 + 0.68 × (growth in %)**. In words: a base multiple of about 11.5x, plus about 0.68 of a turn for every percentage point of growth. Check it against a peer: Evergreen at 20% growth → 11.5 + 0.68 × 20 = 25.1x, right on its actual 25x. The line captures the group's logic.

Now Northwind. Its expected growth is **14%**. Plug into the fitted line: 11.5 + 0.68 × 14 = **21.0x**. That is Northwind's *justified* P/E — what the market's own pricing of growth, learned from this exact peer group, says a 14%-grower like Northwind deserves. Notice it is *not* the peer median (which was 20x) and *not* the peer mean — it is tailored to Northwind's specific growth. Apply 21.0x to Northwind's \$0.95 forward EPS and you get **\$19.95 per share**, versus \$19.00 from blindly applying the 20x median. The regression earned Northwind a deserved premium for growing faster than the median peer. *The differences between peers' multiples are a dataset, not noise — regress the multiple on its drivers and the market will tell you, in its own pricing, exactly what your company's particular growth and quality are worth.*

## Adjusting a peer multiple for a driver gap

The regression is the rigorous tool, but you will not always have enough clean peers to fit a stable line — with only three or four comps a regression is fragile. So you also need the simpler, intuition-driven version of the same idea: take a peer (or the peer median) and **adjust** the multiple up or down for the specific ways your company differs from that peer on the drivers. This is the regression's logic applied by hand, one driver at a time, and it is what analysts do most of the time in practice.

![A before and after figure showing a raw peer median multiple on the left adjusted upward on the right for the company's higher growth and higher return on capital](/imgs/blogs/comparable-companies-done-right-5.png)

The figure above shows the adjustment. On the left is the raw input: the peer median EV/EBITDA of 20x, which you would apply if you did nothing. On the right is the adjusted multiple, built by walking through each driver gap. Northwind grows faster than the median peer — that earns a premium. Northwind earns higher returns on capital — another premium. Northwind carries slightly more risk in one product line — a small discount. You size each adjustment using the logic of the hidden DCF, and you arrive at an adjusted multiple that reflects Northwind specifically rather than the average peer. The "before" multiple launders the median onto your company; the "after" multiple prices your company's actual profile.

The cleanest way to size an adjustment is to go back to the hidden-DCF bridge. Recall that for a stable business, the fair multiple is `payout ÷ (r − g)`. If your company grows faster than the peer (`g` higher), the denominator `(r − g)` shrinks and the justified multiple rises. If your company is riskier (`r` higher), the denominator grows and the multiple falls. You can compute the implied gap precisely — though you must do so with care, because when `(r − g)` is small, the multiple is hyper-sensitive to the inputs, and a mechanical application can produce absurd premiums. The professional move is to use the bridge to size the *direction and rough magnitude* of the adjustment, then temper it with judgment, never to apply a raw formula and accept whatever extreme number falls out.

#### Worked example: adjusting the median for a growth-and-returns gap

Northwind's peer median EV/EBITDA is **20x**, but the median peer grows about **10%** a year while Northwind grows **14%**, and Northwind earns a **28%** return on invested capital versus the median peer's **22%**. Both gaps argue for a premium. How big?

Use the hidden-DCF bridge to size the growth piece. Suppose the peer group's typical discount rate is **9%**. The median peer's 20x EV/EBITDA, decoded, is consistent with about 10% growth (the algebra: a higher implied multiple corresponds to a smaller `r − g`). Re-run the bridge with Northwind's 14% growth instead of 10%: holding `r` at 9%, moving `g` from 10% to 14% shrinks `(r − g)` and, by the bridge, lifts the justified multiple by roughly a quarter — pushing the 20x toward **25x** on growth alone. But — and this is the discipline — that raw bridge number is hyper-sensitive (you are dividing by a small `r − g`), so you temper it: you do not pay the full implied premium, you settle on perhaps **23x** to reflect that 14% growth is real but not guaranteed to last. Then the returns-on-capital gap (28% vs 22%) argues for a touch more — Northwind converts its growth into free cash more efficiently — nudging you to about **24x**. Against that, a small risk discount for one volatile product line pulls you back to a final adjusted **23x**.

Apply the adjusted 23x to Northwind's \$0.21 billion forward EBITDA: 23 × \$0.21bn = **\$4.83 billion** enterprise value, versus the \$4.2 billion you would have gotten from the raw 20x median — a 15% difference, every dollar of it justified by Northwind's superior growth and returns. *Adjusting a multiple is just decoding both companies' hidden DCFs and pricing the difference one driver at a time — but the same small `r − g` denominator that makes the adjustment necessary also makes it treacherous, so you size the direction with the bridge and temper the magnitude with judgment.*

## The harder adjustments: control, liquidity, country, and accounting

Beyond the driver gaps — growth, returns, risk — there is a second class of adjustments that the raw multiple cannot see at all, because they live outside the operating fundamentals. Ignoring them is how a technically-correct comps table still produces a wrong answer. There are four big ones.

**Control versus minority.** Public-market multiples reflect *minority* stakes — the price of a single share, which buys you no control over the company. But when one company *acquires* another outright, it pays a **control premium** above the market price, because owning the whole thing lets the acquirer change management, capture synergies, and direct the cash. So trading comps (built from minority share prices) give a *minority* value, while precedent-transaction comps (built from actual acquisition prices) give a *control* value — typically 20% to 40% higher. You must match the multiple to the question: if you are valuing a minority stake, use trading comps; if you are valuing the company as an acquisition target, use precedent transactions and expect the higher number. Mixing them — applying a control-premium transaction multiple to value a single share you intend to hold passively — overstates the value. The full mechanics of the control premium are the subject of the forthcoming [precedent transactions post](/blog/trading/equity-research/precedent-transactions-and-the-control-premium); here the rule is simply: know whether your multiple embeds control, and use the right one for your purpose.

**Liquidity.** A share you can sell instantly on a public exchange is worth more than an otherwise-identical share in a private company you cannot easily sell. The discount for the difference is the **illiquidity discount** (or "discount for lack of marketability"), typically 20% to 30%. So if you build a peer set out of large, liquid public companies and apply their multiples to value a small, illiquid private business, you will *overstate* the private company's value unless you apply an illiquidity discount. Conversely, peers that are themselves thinly traded may carry an illiquidity discount baked into their prices that doesn't apply to your more-liquid company.

**Country.** As noted in the funnel, the same business in a riskier country deserves a lower multiple, because of country risk, currency risk, and weaker investor protections. If you must use a foreign peer, adjust for the country-risk gap — usually by adding a country-risk premium to the implied discount rate (raising `r`, lowering the justified multiple) for the riskier market. Comparing a developed-market peer at 20x to an emerging-market company without this adjustment will overvalue the emerging-market company.

**Accounting differences.** This is the most technical and the most overlooked. Two companies running the identical business can report very different fundamentals because they use different accounting choices or standards, and a multiple built on incomparable fundamentals is meaningless. The big three:

- **IFRS versus US GAAP.** The two major accounting standards treat several things differently — development costs (capitalized under IFRS, often expensed under GAAP), inventory, certain financial instruments — so a US-GAAP company and an IFRS company in the same business can show different EBITDA and earnings for purely standards-driven reasons. To compare them you must normalize to a common basis.
- **Leases.** Whether a lease is treated as an operating expense (reducing EBITDA) or as a capitalized asset-and-liability (keeping it out of EBITDA but adding to debt and EV) changes both the multiple's numerator and denominator. Recent standards moved most leases onto the balance sheet, but the transition created comparability gaps, and you must treat leases identically across peers — in EBITDA *and* in EV.
- **Stock-based compensation.** As covered in the mechanics section, whether SBC is expensed or added back swings reported profitability substantially, and it must be handled identically across the set.

The unifying principle of all four adjustments is the same as the whole post: **the multiple is a comparison, and a comparison is only valid between like things.** Control, liquidity, country, and accounting are four ways two companies can be *unlike* even when their operating businesses match — and each one, left unadjusted, silently corrupts the comparison.

## The danger of circular comps: everyone expensive together

Now the deepest limitation of relative valuation, the one no amount of careful technique can fix, because it is built into the method itself. **Comparable-companies analysis can only tell you whether your company is cheap or expensive relative to its peers. It is completely silent on whether the peers themselves are sanely priced.** If the entire peer group is in a bubble, a flawless comps analysis will confidently tell you your company is "fairly valued" — at a bubble price. The method does not check the market's homework; it copies it.

This is the circularity at the heart of comps. You price Northwind off its peers. But its peers are priced off *their* peers — which include Northwind-like companies. Everyone in the group is priced relative to everyone else, and the whole group can float up or down together, untethered from any underlying cash flows, with every individual comparison remaining internally consistent the entire way. In a sector mania, each company looks "reasonably valued relative to peers" even as the whole sector trades at multiples that no plausible future cash flow could ever justify. Relative valuation, applied with perfect diligence, produces confidently insane numbers in a bubble, because it inherits the collective mistake as its baseline.

The history of markets is a graveyard of this failure. In 1999, internet companies were "valued" off other internet companies, and because the entire group traded at preposterous multiples of sales, every new entrant got a preposterous multiple too — each one perfectly justified by comparison to its equally-overvalued peers. The comps were internally consistent and externally delusional. The only defense is to step *outside* the relative framework entirely and ask the intrinsic question: regardless of what the peers trade at, what cash will this business actually produce, and what is that cash worth? That is why comps must never stand alone.

#### Worked example: the comps that confirmed a bubble

Suppose in a frothy market Northwind's five peers all trade at **35x EV/EBITDA** — far above the 20x of our base case — because the whole software sector is in a mania. A diligent comps analysis would compute the median (35x), perhaps adjust Northwind up to 38x for its superior growth, apply it to \$0.21 billion of EBITDA, and conclude Northwind is worth about **\$8.0 billion** — and that it is "fairly valued relative to peers." Every step is technically correct. Every peer comparison is internally consistent.

Now run the intrinsic check. A 38x EV/EBITDA multiple, decoded into its hidden DCF, implies the market expects Northwind to grow its cash at a rate, for a duration, that would require it to become several times the size of its entire current market within a decade — a bet the business almost certainly cannot deliver. The DCF, built on defensible cash-flow assumptions, says Northwind is worth perhaps **\$4.5 billion**. The comps and the DCF disagree by nearly a factor of two, and that disagreement is the single most valuable signal in the analysis: it says the *peer group itself* is mispriced, and Northwind's comps value is a bubble price wearing the costume of analysis. *Relative valuation can confirm that a company is reasonably priced relative to a peer group that is itself completely detached from reality — which is exactly why a comps value that has not been cross-checked against an intrinsic DCF is not yet an analysis, only a mirror.*

## Triangulating comps with a DCF and presenting the football field

So we arrive at the punchline, the same one that ends every honest valuation: **comps are a cross-check, not a verdict.** You run the comparable-companies analysis to get a market-anchored, fast, real-price read on value — and then you run an intrinsic discounted cash flow as an independent check, precisely because each method is blind exactly where the other sees. The DCF is anchored to fundamentals but adrift on assumptions; the comps are anchored to real prices but adrift on whether those prices are sane. When the two agree, you have a robust estimate. When they disagree, the disagreement is information that sends you back to understand why — and that understanding is worth more than either number alone.

The professional way to present all of this is the **valuation football field**: a chart with one horizontal bar per method, each bar showing a *range* rather than a point, laid side by side so you can see where the methods overlap. The overlap is your defensible estimate, because for the truth to fall outside it, multiple independent methods would all have to be wrong in the same direction.

![A valuation football field for Northwind with horizontal range bars from the comparable companies analysis the precedent transactions and the discounted cash flow showing where the methods overlap](/imgs/blogs/comparable-companies-done-right-7.png)

The figure above is Northwind's football field, and it ties the whole post together. The **comparable companies** bar — built from the consistent multiples, the median, and the driver adjustments we developed — gives a range of about \$4.4 to \$5.3 billion enterprise value (the low end from the raw median, the high end from the growth-and-returns-adjusted multiple). The **precedent transactions** bar sits higher, \$5.2 to \$6.4 billion, because acquisition multiples embed a control premium a minority shareholder will not capture. The **DCF** bar, run across a sensible span of growth and discount-rate assumptions, gives \$4.0 to \$5.5 billion. Where do they overlap? Right around **\$4.6 to \$5.3 billion** — the consensus band. That overlap is far more trustworthy than any single method's point estimate. And the discipline of the football field is that it forces you to *show the range*: a single bold number hides the uncertainty that is the most honest thing about any valuation.

#### Worked example: reconciling Northwind's comps and DCF in a football field

Pull together everything we built. The **comps**: the consistent table gave a peer median of 20x EV/EBITDA, the driver regression and hand-adjustment lifted Northwind's justified multiple to about 23x for its 14% growth and 28% returns, and the range across the raw median to the adjusted multiple spans roughly 21x–25x. Applied to \$0.21 billion forward EBITDA, that is **\$4.4–\$5.3 billion** enterprise value. The **precedent transactions** — actual acquisitions of similar software businesses — happened at 25x–30x including a control premium, giving **\$5.2–\$6.4 billion**, which you flag as a control value not a minority one. The **DCF**, built independently on Northwind's cash-flow forecast, gives **\$4.0–\$5.5 billion** across a reasonable assumption span.

Now triangulate. The comps and the DCF overlap heavily in the \$4.6–\$5.3 billion zone — two independent methods, one anchored to peer prices and one to forecast cash, agreeing. The precedent transactions sit above, and correctly so, because they price control; for valuing Northwind as a going concern from a minority shareholder's seat, you weight the comps and the DCF most heavily and treat the transaction range as the "what an acquirer might pay" upper bound. Blending with judgment lands you on an estimate of about **\$5.0 billion** enterprise value, squarely inside the consensus band. *No single method produced \$5.0 billion — it emerged from the agreement of a market-anchored comps analysis and a fundamentals-anchored DCF, which is exactly why you trust it more than either alone, and exactly why comps are a cross-check to be triangulated, never a verdict to be slapped down.*

## Common misconceptions

**"Comps are easy — you just grab the peer multiples and apply the median."** This is the belief this entire post exists to demolish. The mechanical act of applying a median is easy; doing it *correctly* is hard, and the difficulty is exactly where the value is. The hard parts — choosing genuinely comparable companies, computing every multiple identically, understanding why the multiples differ, adjusting for the differences, and cross-checking against a DCF — are invisible in the final table, which is why the lazy version and the rigorous version look superficially identical and produce wildly different numbers. The polished comps table in a report tells you nothing about whether the thinking behind it was done.

**"Companies in the same industry are comparable."** A sector label is a starting filter, not a verdict on comparability. Two companies can share an industry code and differ on every driver that matters — growth, returns, risk, capital intensity, business model — and therefore deserve completely different multiples. And two companies in different industries can be near-perfect comps if they share those drivers. Comparability lives in the value drivers, and the sector label is at best a hint about where to start looking for them.

**"Use the average multiple of the peer group."** Use the **median**, not the mean. Multiples are asymmetric — bounded below at zero, unbounded above — so a single outlier (a peer with a temporary earnings collapse, a takeover rumor, a one-time charge) drags the mean around violently while the median stays stable. And don't just rely on the median to ignore outliers: investigate every outlier, because it is either a peer you must normalize and re-include or a peer you must remove with a stated reason. An outlier is information, never a number to silently average in.

**"A peer's multiple is the multiple my company deserves."** Only if your company matches that peer on every driver. The peer's multiple is a fact about *that peer's* growth, returns, and risk; importing it onto a company with a different profile imports a difference that doesn't apply. The whole point of the regression and the hand-adjustment is to translate the peer's multiple into the one *your* company deserves given *its* drivers. Applying a fast grower's multiple to a slow grower, or a high-quality business's multiple to a low-quality one, is the most common error in all of comps.

**"If my company looks cheap versus its peers, it's cheap."** It is cheap *relative to its peers* — a crucial qualifier. If the entire peer group is overvalued, the "cheapest" member can still be expensive in absolute terms. Relative valuation answers "is this the best-priced horse in this race?" not "should I be at this racetrack at all?" For the second question you need an intrinsic anchor, which is exactly why comps must be triangulated with a DCF and never trusted alone.

**"Once I have the comps value, I'm done."** A comps value is one input, not an answer. It is blind to whether the peer group itself is sanely priced, so it must be cross-checked against an independent DCF and presented as a range in a football field, not a single number. The output of a proper valuation is not "it's worth \$5.0 billion"; it is "the comps and the DCF overlap around \$4.6–\$5.3 billion, so I think it's worth about \$5.0 billion, and here is the range and the disagreement that defines my uncertainty."

## How it shows up in real markets

**The dot-com bubble and circular comps.** The clearest historical demonstration of the circularity trap is the internet bubble of 1999–2000. Because most internet companies had no earnings, they were valued on price-to-sales and even cruder multiples, and the comps were drawn entirely from *other* internet companies — which were themselves valued the same way. The whole group floated up together, each name perfectly "justified" by comparison to its equally-inflated peers, while the entire sector traded at multiples that no plausible future cash flow could ever support. Diligent analysts produced internally-consistent, externally-delusional valuations, because relative valuation faithfully inherited the market's collective mistake. The investors who avoided the wreckage were the ones who stepped outside the comps and asked the intrinsic question — what cash will this actually produce? — and got the unfashionable answer "almost none."

**Bank valuations and the price-to-book versus ROE regression.** One of the most reliable real-world uses of the regression-to-justified-multiple idea is in bank stocks. Banks are valued primarily on **price-to-book**, and the single cleanest driver of a bank's P/B is its **return on equity**: a bank earning 18% on its book equity deserves a far higher price-to-book than one earning 8%, and across a set of banks the relationship between P/B and ROE is famously tight and linear. Analysts routinely plot the peer banks' P/B against their ROE, fit a line, and read off the justified P/B for the bank they are valuing given its own ROE — exactly the regression we built. It is the textbook example of using the spread of peer multiples as a *dataset* about how the market prices a driver, rather than throwing that information away by taking a median.

**The control premium in M&A.** The gap between trading comps and precedent transactions shows up in every public-company acquisition. When an acquirer bids for a public company, the offer almost always sits well above the pre-bid market price — the control premium, frequently 20% to 40% — because owning the whole company is worth more than owning a minority share of it. Analysts valuing a company as a potential takeover target use precedent-transaction multiples and expect the higher, control-inclusive number; analysts valuing the same company for a minority investor use trading comps and the lower number. Confusing the two — quoting an acquisition multiple to justify buying a single share you intend to hold passively — is a recurring and expensive error, and it is exactly the control-versus-minority adjustment the post warns about.

**Conglomerates and the sum-of-the-parts problem.** Large conglomerates routinely trade *below* the sum of what their individual divisions would be worth as standalone businesses, because no single company-wide multiple can fairly price a bundle of very different businesses. This "conglomerate discount" is the negative image of the comps lesson: just as you cannot use a conglomerate as a clean comp (its blended multiple reflects no single business), you cannot value a conglomerate with one multiple either — you must value each segment against its own pure-play peers and add them up. It is a standing reminder that the *unit* of comparability is the business model, not the legal entity, and that a single multiple on a mixed business is meaningless.

**Forensic accounting and garbage-in-garbage-out.** Every multiple rests on the fundamentals the company reports, and when those numbers are fabricated, the comps are worthless — a clean multiple on fictional earnings is still a fiction. The collapses of [Enron](/blog/trading/finance/enron-2001-accounting-fraud) and [Wirecard](/blog/trading/finance/wirecard-the-german-fintech-fraud) are the canonical reminders: analysts argued about whether the stock was cheap on this or that multiple while the underlying earnings did not exist. The lesson sits underneath all of comps — verify the inputs before you trust any output, and pay special attention to the consistency of adjustments, because aggressive "adjusted EBITDA" that adds back everything inconvenient is precisely where companies hide the truth that would make their multiples look expensive.

## When this matters and where to go next

Comparable-companies analysis is the most-used valuation method on earth, and now you can tell the rigorous version from the lazy one at a glance. Whenever someone shows you a comps table — in a research report, a pitch deck, a tweet that "the stock is cheap at 12x versus peers at 18x" — you have the questions that cut through it: *Are these genuinely comparable on the drivers, or just on the sector label? Were the multiples computed identically — same period, same shares, same EV, same adjustments? Is that a median or a mean, and were the outliers investigated? Why do the multiples differ, and does the company deserve a premium or a discount given its own growth and returns? Is this a control multiple or a minority one? And has anyone cross-checked it against a DCF, or is the whole peer group expensive together?* That checklist will make you a sharper reader of every valuation you encounter for the rest of your investing life.

The deeper point is that comps done right are a genuine cross-check on value — a fast, market-anchored second opinion that catches a DCF's heroic assumptions — while comps done wrong are a machine for laundering the market's mistakes into the appearance of analysis. The difference is entirely in the thinking the table conceals: the choice of peers, the consistency of the math, the understanding of why the numbers differ, and the humility to present a range and triangulate against an independent method.
