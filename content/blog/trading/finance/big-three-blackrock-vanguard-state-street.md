> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — By collecting the savings of ordinary people into cheap index funds, three firms (BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street) have become the largest shareholders of almost every major public company, which hands them enormous, soft, much-debated voting power even though the money is not theirs.
>
> - An index fund just buys a slice of every company in a market, charges almost nothing, and so quietly attracted trillions of dollars over fifty years.
> - The crucial split: the savers **own** the shares and keep the gains; the manager **votes** the shares on their behalf. Ownership and control come apart.
> - Combined, the Big Three manage roughly \$20 trillion or more (approximate, as of around 2024) and are a top-three shareholder in the overwhelming majority of S&P 500 companies.
> - Their power is real but constrained: they are custodians of other people's retirement money, mostly vote with management, and are now handing some votes back to underlying savers.
> - The one number to remember: a fee of about **0.03%** on a few trillion dollars is a multi-billion-dollar business, which is exactly why this model grew so large.

Here is a question almost no one stops to ask. When you put money into a retirement account and tick the box marked "S&P 500 index fund," who shows up at Apple's annual meeting and votes on who sits on Apple's board? It is not you. You will never get a ballot in the mail, and you would not know what to do with it if you did. Instead, the ballot goes to whichever firm runs your fund, and three firms run an astonishing share of those funds. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: millions of small savers feed a handful of giant index-fund managers, and that pooled money makes those managers the single largest reported shareholder of company after company after company.

![Tree showing savers feeding the Big Three who become top holders of major companies](/imgs/blogs/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street-1.png)

This is one of the strangest facts about modern finance, and it is hiding in plain sight. The money belongs to teachers, nurses, plumbers, and software engineers saving for retirement. But the *votes* attached to that money have been concentrated into three companies you may never have chosen and may barely have heard of. Critics call it a quiet takeover of Corporate America. Defenders call it the cheapest, fairest deal small investors have ever gotten. Both are describing the same machine. This post is about how that machine works, brick by brick, with the dollars made explicit, so that by the end you can argue either side honestly.

We will build everything from zero. If you do not know what a share is, what a fund is, or what "passive investing" means, you are exactly the reader this is written for. We will define every term the first time it appears, ground each idea in a worked example with real numbers, and only then climb up to the big, contested questions about power and concentration.

## Foundations: shares, funds, fees, and the gap between owning and voting

Let us start with the smallest piece and build up.

A **share** (also called a *stock*) is a slice of ownership in a company. If a company has carved itself into one billion equal pieces and you hold one of those pieces, you own one-billionth of the company. That ownership entitles you to two things: a proportional claim on any profits the company chooses to hand out (called a **dividend**, a cash payment per share), and a proportional vote on certain decisions, most importantly the election of the company's board of directors. The **board of directors** is the small group, usually around ten people, that hires and fires the chief executive and oversees the company on behalf of all the owners. So a share is not just a lottery ticket on the price going up. It is a small piece of legal control.

A **public company** is one whose shares trade on a stock exchange, so anyone can buy a piece. The total market value of all its shares is its **market capitalization**, or market cap: share price times number of shares. Apple, Microsoft, and ExxonMobil are public companies; the corner bakery is not.

Now, most people do not buy individual shares one at a time. Picking the right companies is hard, and putting all your money in one or two of them is risky. So instead people use a **fund**: a pool of money from many investors that is used to buy a basket of many shares (or bonds, or other assets). When you put \$1,000 into a fund, you are not buying Apple shares directly. You are buying a tiny slice of the whole basket the fund holds, and the fund holds the actual shares. This matters enormously later, so hold onto it: **the fund holds the shares; you hold a slice of the fund.**

There are different kinds of funds, and the distinction is the heart of this story.

An **active fund** employs a manager (and a team of analysts) who tries to pick winning stocks and avoid losers, aiming to *beat the market*. Beating the market means earning a higher return than the overall market did, and that extra return is called **alpha**. For this effort, the fund charges a fee. The fee is quoted as an annual percentage of the money you have in the fund, called the **expense ratio**. An expense ratio of 1.0% means that every year the fund quietly takes 1% of your balance. On \$10,000 that is \$100 a year, deducted whether the fund made money or lost it.

An **index fund** does something almost insultingly simple. Instead of picking stocks, it just buys *all* of them in a chosen list, or "index," in the same proportions as the index. The most famous index is the **S&P 500**, a list of roughly 500 of the largest US public companies, weighted by market cap. An S&P 500 index fund holds all 500, big positions in the big companies and small positions in the small ones, and does nothing clever. Because there is no expensive manager trying to outsmart anyone, an index fund can charge a tiny fee, often 0.03% to 0.04% a year. On \$10,000 that is \$3 to \$4 a year.

This split, picking-and-charging versus copying-and-barely-charging, is the difference between **active investing** and **passive investing**. Active investing tries to do better than the market. Passive investing accepts the market's return and just tries to do it as cheaply as possible. The diagram below puts the two side by side, and the punchline is in the bottom row: over decades, the fee gap dwarfs almost everything else.

![Before and after comparison of a high-fee active fund versus a low-fee passive index fund](/imgs/blogs/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street-2.png)

A close cousin of the index mutual fund is the **ETF**, short for *exchange-traded fund*. An ETF is also a basket of shares, often tracking an index, but it trades on a stock exchange all day like a single stock, so you can buy or sell it any minute the market is open. A traditional mutual fund, by contrast, only prices and trades once a day after the market closes. For our purposes the ETF and the index mutual fund do the same economic job, hold the whole basket cheaply, and the ETF's special creation-and-redemption plumbing is something we will dissect later because it is genuinely clever.

Two more terms and we are ready. **AUM** stands for *assets under management*, the total dollar value of all the money a firm manages on behalf of its clients. When you read that BlackRock has roughly \$10 trillion in AUM, that does not mean BlackRock owns \$10 trillion. It means clients have entrusted \$10 trillion to BlackRock to manage, and BlackRock earns its fee on that pile. AUM is the single most important number in this business, because the firm's revenue is basically its fee rate times its AUM.

And finally, the term that the entire controversy turns on. There is a difference between **owning** shares and **voting** them. The saver who put money in the fund is the **beneficial owner**, the person who is actually entitled to the gains and dividends. But the fund manager is the **registered holder** who exercises the rights that come with the shares, above all the vote. The vote is cast by **proxy**, meaning the manager votes on the owner's behalf without the owner showing up. The annual flurry of these votes across thousands of companies is called **proxy season**. The work of deciding how to vote, and of meeting with companies to push for changes, is called **stewardship**. And the legal duty the manager owes is a **fiduciary** duty, a duty to act in the best interest of the savers rather than its own. Owning, voting, stewardship, fiduciary: keep these four straight and the rest of the post is easy.

So here is the foundational picture. You own the shares and keep the gains. The manager holds the shares' legal rights and casts the votes. Three firms run so many of these funds that they cast a huge share of the votes across nearly all of Corporate America. That is the whole story in one breath. Now let us slow it down.

## The rise of passive: how Jack Bogle's boring idea ate the world

To understand why three firms ended up so large, you have to understand a single, almost philosophical bet made in the 1970s.

In 1976, a man named John Bogle, known to everyone as Jack, launched something at his new company Vanguard that Wall Street openly ridiculed. It was the first index fund available to ordinary people: a fund that would simply buy the whole S&P 500 and never try to beat it. Competitors called it "Bogle's Folly" and "un-American," on the theory that aiming for *average* was a betrayal of the entrepreneurial spirit. The fund raised a disappointing \$11 million at launch, far below its target.

Bogle's argument was arithmetic, not ideology. The total return of all investors in a market, before fees, must equal the market's return, because together they *are* the market. So for every investor who beats the market by a dollar, another must lag it by a dollar; it is a zero-sum game before costs. After you subtract fees, the average actively managed dollar must therefore *underperform* the market by roughly the size of its fees. Index funds, by charging almost nothing, keep more of the market's return in the saver's pocket. It is not that index funds are brilliant. It is that they refuse to be expensive.

The other half of Bogle's insight was that small fees compound into enormous gaps over a lifetime. A 1% fee does not feel like much in year one. But it is charged every single year, on a balance that is itself growing, so it quietly eats a slice of your compounding for thirty or forty years. Let us make that concrete.

#### Worked example: the thirty-year fee drag on \$10,000

Suppose you invest \$10,000 once and leave it for 30 years. Imagine two worlds that are identical except for the fee. In both, the underlying market returns 7% a year before fees.

In the low-fee index fund, the expense ratio is 0.03%, so your money compounds at about 6.97% a year. After 30 years:

```
10,000 x (1.0697)^30  =  about 75,400 dollars
```

In the high-fee active fund, the expense ratio is 1.0%, so your money compounds at about 6.0% a year (and we are generously assuming the manager exactly matches the market before fees). After 30 years:

```
10,000 x (1.06)^30  =  about 57,400 dollars
```

The gap is roughly \$18,000, on a starting stake of just \$10,000. The fee did not cost you 1% a year. Over 30 years it quietly cost you about a quarter of your final wealth. And remember, this assumed the active manager *matched* the market before fees; most do not. The intuition: a fee is not a one-time charge, it is a tax on every future year of compounding, which is why a fraction of a percent turns into a fortune over a lifetime.

![Timeline of passive investing from the first index fund in 1976 to surpassing active in 2019](/imgs/blogs/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street-5.png)

That arithmetic, repeated across millions of savers, is the engine. As the timeline above shows, the idea spread slowly at first and then all at once. State Street launched the first US ETF in the United States in 1993, a fund nicknamed SPY that tracked the S&P 500 and still trades today. BlackRock, which had started as a bond-focused firm, bought the iShares ETF business in 2009 and became the world's largest ETF provider almost overnight. Fidelity launched the first true zero-fee index funds in 2018, charging literally nothing as a way to win customers. And in 2019, a milestone that would have stunned Bogle in 1976 arrived: for the first time, more money sat in passive US-stock funds than in active ones. The boring idea had won.

The money did not spread evenly across hundreds of firms. Passive investing has a powerful "winner takes most" dynamic: because the product is nearly identical no matter who sells it, savers gravitate to whoever is biggest, cheapest, and most trusted, and being biggest lets you be cheapest, which makes you bigger still. Three firms got there first and got there hardest. That is how we end up with a Big Three rather than a Big Thirty.

## How big is "big"? Putting numbers on the giants

It is hard to overstate the scale, so let us anchor it with figures, all of which should be read as approximate and roughly as of 2024, because these numbers move with the market every single day.

BlackRock manages on the order of \$10 trillion. Vanguard is close behind at roughly \$9 trillion. State Street Global Advisors, the asset-management arm of State Street Corporation, runs roughly \$4 trillion. Add them up and you get somewhere north of \$20 trillion under the control of three firms. For a sense of scale, the entire annual economic output of the United States is around \$28 trillion. These three firms steward a pool of savings comparable in size to the whole American economy.

But the AUM number, huge as it is, is not the part that makes governance scholars nervous. The part that does is *concentration of ownership*. Because index funds buy every company in an index, and because these three firms run such a large fraction of all index money, they end up as one of the very largest shareholders in nearly every large company at once. Studies of the S&P 500 have repeatedly found that the Big Three are collectively a top-three shareholder in the large majority of those 500 companies, and that their combined stake in a typical large US company runs in the high teens to low twenties as a percentage. The matrix below lays out how the three firms differ from one another, because they are not interchangeable.

![Matrix comparing BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street on size, ownership, flagship, and voting](/imgs/blogs/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street-4.png)

Notice the differences. BlackRock is itself a public company, owned by its own shareholders, with the iShares ETF brand and the Aladdin risk system (more on that later) as its crown jewels. Vanguard is structurally unusual: it is owned by its own funds, which are in turn owned by the savers in them, a mutual structure with no outside shareholders demanding profit, which is part of why Vanguard has pushed fees toward zero so relentlessly. State Street, the smallest of the three, is the old-money custody-and-trading bank whose SPDR family includes the original SPY ETF.

Let us make the ownership concentration vivid with a worked number.

#### Worked example: how a 7% stake in everything becomes a controlling bloc

Suppose, for round numbers, that BlackRock holds about 7% of a given large company, Vanguard about 8%, and State Street about 3%. None of those is a controlling stake on its own; you generally need far more than that to dictate to a board. But add them:

```
7% + 8% + 3%  =  18% of the company, held by three firms
```

Now consider how votes actually win at a shareholder meeting. Most public-company votes are decided by a simple majority of the shares that actually show up and vote. In a typical large company, a meaningful chunk of shares are held by small retail investors who never vote at all, so the *votes actually cast* might represent only 60% to 80% of all shares. If only 70% of shares vote, then our 18% bloc is not 18% of the decision, it is:

```
18% / 70%  =  about 26% of the votes that decide the outcome
```

A single coordinated 26% of the cast votes is, in practice, often enough to swing a close board election or a contested proposal, especially when the rest of the voters are scattered. The intuition: you do not need a majority to be decisive, you only need a large enough bloc that the other side cannot easily out-vote you once the no-shows are stripped out, and three firms voting together clear that bar at a huge number of companies.

It helps to see the same scale from the saver's end, because it makes vivid how thin each individual claim is even as the aggregate becomes a bloc. Your slice of any one company is microscopic; the power lives only in the pooling.

#### Worked example: an index-fund saver's share of one company's dividend

Suppose you have \$50,000 in a total-market index fund, and that fund holds about 6% of its assets in a single huge company, call it BigCorp. Your indirect stake in BigCorp is:

```
50,000 x 6%  =  3,000 dollars of BigCorp shares
```

Now suppose BigCorp pays an annual dividend equal to about 2% of its share value. Your share of that dividend, flowing through the fund and back to you, is:

```
3,000 x 2%  =  60 dollars a year
```

Sixty dollars. That is your entire economic stake in one of the largest companies on earth: a \$60 annual dividend and a vote you do not personally cast. Multiply your situation by the tens of millions of savers in the same fund, though, and those \$60 slices add up to a 6%-plus position in BigCorp, large enough that the fund manager is one of its biggest shareholders. The intuition: the Big Three's power is built entirely out of tiny, individually-negligible stakes like your \$60, which is exactly why it is so strange that it concentrates into a controlling-sized bloc, and exactly why no individual saver feels they have handed any power to anyone.

There is a second-order point hiding in those numbers worth drawing out. Because the manager is one entity holding the pooled stake, the *transaction cost of voting* collapses. If those tens of millions of savers each had to vote their own \$60 slice, almost none would bother, and the company's owners would be a scattered, voiceless crowd. By pooling, the index fund turns a crowd that would never show up into a single large, organized voter that shows up at every meeting. That is genuinely valuable in principle: dispersed owners have always been the weak point of public companies, easily ignored by entrenched managers. The index funds reassembled that dispersed ownership into something with a voice. The unresolved question is simply whether that voice should belong to three stewardship teams or be handed back, slice by slice, to the savers it represents.

## The common-ownership concern: when the same three firms own all the competitors

Here is where the academic alarm bells start ringing, and it is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing.

**Common ownership** is the situation where the same set of investors are large shareholders in *competing* firms in the same industry. Because index funds buy the whole market, the Big Three simultaneously own big stakes in, say, all the major US airlines, or all the big banks, or all the large drugmakers. A traditional investor who loved airlines might pick one and root for it to crush the others. An index fund owns them all, so in a sense it is indifferent to which airline wins; it just wants the airline *industry* to be profitable.

The worry, raised in influential academic papers in the 2010s, is that this could soften competition. The argument goes: if the same owners hold all the airlines, those owners have no reason to want a brutal price war that benefits customers but shrinks industry profits. Even without any explicit coordination, executives who know their largest owners hold all their rivals might compete a little less ferociously. Researchers pointed to airline ticket prices and bank fees as possible evidence. This is the **common-ownership hypothesis**, and it turned into a live debate among antitrust economists about whether ownership concentration should itself be treated as an anticompetitive problem.

It is genuinely contested. Critics of the hypothesis point out that index funds are mostly *passive*, that they do not sit in pricing meetings, that the statistical evidence on airline prices has been challenged on methodology, and that the funds publicly insist they want each company to maximize its own value. But the structural fact underneath the debate is undeniable: never before in financial history have a handful of investors simultaneously been among the largest owners of nearly every competitor in nearly every industry. Whether or not it dampens competition, it is a new kind of ownership map, and regulators have been studying it precisely because it is unprecedented.

It is worth being precise about *why* this is hard to regulate with the tools we have. Antitrust law was built to stop one company from buying or merging with its rival, or from colluding on prices. It assumes a clear actor with a clear motive to suppress competition. Common ownership fits none of those boxes. No one bought a rival; an index fund mechanically holds all of them because that is what tracking an index means. There is no smoke-filled room; the alleged effect, if it exists at all, would emerge from incentives rather than instructions. And the "owner" is not really one decision-maker but millions of savers whose money happens to be pooled. So even economists who take the concern seriously struggle to name a remedy that would not also destroy the cheap, diversified index fund that ordinary people rely on. Proposed fixes, like capping how much of an industry a single fund family can hold, or stripping index funds of their votes in concentrated industries, each carry side effects that could leave savers worse off. The honest state of the field is that the problem is easier to describe than to solve.

There is also a quieter, more mundane channel of influence that gets less attention than airline prices: the sheer gravitational pull of being everyone's largest shareholder on how managers *behave*. A chief executive who knows that the same three firms are the top owners of the company, and will be next year, and the year after, regardless of performance, faces a different incentive landscape than one watched by impatient active investors who will dump the stock at the first stumble. Permanent owners are patient owners. That patience can be a virtue, letting companies invest for the long term without quarterly panic, or a vice, letting weak managers coast because their largest owners will not sell. Which one it is depends on how actively those permanent owners use their voice, which brings us straight to the machinery of voting.

The corporate-governance version of the same worry is about voting power rather than competition. If three firms vote roughly a fifth of the shares at thousands of companies, they have an outsized influence over who sits on boards and which shareholder proposals pass. That is power. The fact that the money is not theirs does not make the power disappear; it just makes the question of *how they should use it* genuinely hard.

## The voting machine: stewardship teams and proxy season

So how do these votes actually get cast? Not by algorithms blindly, and not by the people whose money it is. They are cast by small, specialized teams inside each firm.

Each of the Big Three runs a **stewardship** team, sometimes called investment stewardship or corporate governance. These teams are surprisingly small relative to the empire they vote: on the order of a few dozen to perhaps seventy people deciding votes across many thousands of companies. They publish voting guidelines, meet with the management of large holdings during the year (this engagement is the quieter and arguably more influential half of stewardship), and then during the spring **proxy season** they push the button on tens of thousands of individual ballot items: director elections, executive pay approvals, auditor ratifications, and shareholder proposals on everything from climate disclosure to board composition.

The single most important fact about how they vote is anticlimactic: most of the time, they vote *with* management. They support the board's slate of directors, approve the pay packages, and oppose most outside shareholder proposals. There are good reasons for this default. A passive fund cannot sell a stock it disapproves of (it must hold the whole index), so its main lever is the vote and the private conversation, not the exit. And a fund that fought management at every company on every issue would need an enormous, expensive apparatus, which would push up fees, which would defeat the entire low-cost purpose. So the houses lean toward management and reserve their firepower for a smaller set of clear-cut concerns. The graph below traces the path your dollar takes to become one of these votes, and the crucial fork: the gains flow back to you, but the vote does not stay with you.

![Graph showing a 401k dollar flowing into a fund where the manager casts the proxy vote](/imgs/blogs/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street-3.png)

That "vote with management" default has come under fire from both directions. Some critics say the funds are too cozy with management and rubber-stamp bloated pay. Others, especially after the funds began supporting some climate and diversity proposals around 2020 and 2021, accused them of pushing a political agenda with other people's money. The funds were getting hit from the left for doing too little and from the right for doing too much, which is often the sign of an institution stuck in the genuinely uncomfortable middle.

Their response to this crossfire has been one of the most important recent developments in the whole field: **pass-through voting**, also called voting choice. Rather than deciding every vote centrally, the firms have started offering the underlying investors a menu of voting policies, or in some cases the ability to direct the vote themselves. If you hold one of these funds, you might be able to tell BlackRock to follow a particular set of governance guidelines, or to side with a third-party advisor's recommendations, rather than letting the stewardship team decide for you. It is an attempt to defuse the concentration concern by giving the vote back, at least in part, to the people whose money it actually is.

#### Worked example: a proxy vote swinging a single board seat

Imagine a company with a contested board election: a small activist investor has nominated its own candidate against one of the company's incumbent directors. The company has 1 billion shares. On election day, 700 million shares are actually voted (the other 300 million belong to people who ignored the ballot). To win the seat, a candidate needs a simple majority of the votes cast, so 350 million plus one.

Now suppose the Big Three together hold 18%, which is 180 million shares, and they decide to back the activist's candidate. The activist itself holds, say, 30 million shares. Their combined bloc is:

```
180 million + 30 million  =  210 million votes for the challenger
```

That is 210 million out of 700 million cast, or 30%. The activist needs another 140 million from the remaining 490 million voting shares, a bit under 29% of the rest, to cross the 350 million line. Without the Big Three, the activist had only 30 million and needed to round up 320 million from others, an almost hopeless task. With the Big Three, the math flips from hopeless to very winnable. The intuition: the Big Three rarely *propose* a change, but because their bloc is so large, their decision to support or oppose a challenger often determines whether that challenger has any realistic path at all. They are the swing vote at thousands of companies at once.

## Aladdin: the other kind of power that is not about ownership

There is a second source of BlackRock's influence that has nothing to do with which shares it holds, and it is easy to miss because it is invisible to the public. It is a software platform called **Aladdin**, which stands for Asset, Liability, Debt and Derivative Investment Network.

Aladdin is a risk-management and portfolio system. In plain terms, it is the operating system that many large investors use to see what they own, model how their portfolios would behave if interest rates spiked or a market crashed, and run the day-to-day machinery of trading and accounting. BlackRock built it for itself and then began licensing it to other institutions: insurers, pension funds, other asset managers, even some central banks reportedly run on it. The amount of money managed *on* Aladdin, by BlackRock and its clients combined, is estimated in the tens of trillions of dollars, far larger than BlackRock's own AUM.

Why does this matter for our story about ownership and power? Because it is a different and subtler kind of dominance: **infrastructure power**. Owning a fifth of a company's shares gives you a vote. Running the risk system that a large slice of the world's institutions rely on gives you something else: a window into how the financial system is positioned, and a position as critical plumbing. If many institutions all see risk through the same lens, they may react to a shock in the same way at the same time, which can amplify a sell-off. None of that requires owning a single share. It is the same lesson as common ownership in a different key: concentration can create system-level effects that no single firm intended and that traditional antitrust, focused on prices, was not built to see.

There is also a more prosaic worry that comes with being indispensable plumbing: a single point of failure. If a large share of the world's portfolios are managed, monitored, and risk-checked on one platform, then an outage, a bug, or a flawed model assumption in that platform does not stay contained to one firm. It propagates. Regulators who worry about **systemic risk**, the risk that the failure of one critical node takes down much of the system, have noted that infrastructure concentration is its own category of fragility, separate from ownership stakes and separate from leverage. BlackRock and its defenders argue that Aladdin is a tool, not a decision-maker, that each client makes its own choices, and that better risk visibility makes the system safer rather than more fragile. That is a fair point. But "everyone uses the same map" is a double-edged thing: it is efficient until the moment the map is wrong, at which point everyone is wrong together. The deeper takeaway is that when we ask "who controls Corporate America," the honest answer includes not just who holds the shares but who runs the machinery the share-holders themselves depend on.

## The fee model: why 0.03% on trillions is a wonderful business

If index funds charge almost nothing, how is this one of the most profitable corners of finance? The answer is the magic and the menace of scale. A tiny percentage of an enormous number is still an enormous number.

#### Worked example: the expense-ratio economics of a giant fund

Take a flagship S&P 500 index fund with a 0.03% expense ratio and \$3 trillion in assets. The annual revenue is simply the fee rate times the assets:

```
0.0003 x 3,000,000,000,000  =  900,000,000 dollars
```

That is \$900 million a year in revenue from a single fund, for the genuinely low-effort task of holding 500 stocks in their index weights. The fund does not need star stock-pickers or a big research budget; it needs computers, custody, and compliance. Once the fund exists, each additional dollar that flows in costs almost nothing extra to manage, so margins are wide and the business throws off cash. The intuition: in index investing, the product is cheap to *build* and the customer base is gigantic, so a microscopic fee on a colossal pile becomes one of finance's best businesses, which is exactly why three firms fought so hard to get so big.

The fee is not even the only revenue stream. There are two quieter ones worth knowing.

The first is **securities lending**. A fund that holds, say, billions of dollars of Apple shares can lend some of those shares to other market participants (often short-sellers who want to borrow them) in exchange for a fee and collateral. The fund earns extra income on assets it was going to hold anyway, and that income partly subsidizes the fee, which is one reason some funds can advertise a near-zero or even zero expense ratio without losing money. It carries its own modest risks (the borrower could fail, the collateral could fall short), but managed carefully it is found money.

The second is **index licensing**, which is its own small ecosystem. The S&P 500 is not free to use; the index is a product owned by an index provider (S&P Dow Jones Indices) that licenses the right to track it for a fee. So part of what you pay flows to the company that maintains the *list*. This has made index providers themselves quietly powerful, because deciding which companies go into a major index can move billions of dollars of passive money, a power we will return to in the real-markets section.

The matrix earlier in this post hinted at how the three firms monetize differently. BlackRock leans on ETFs and on Aladdin licensing. Vanguard, owned by its own funds, runs closer to at-cost and uses low fees as a weapon. State Street leans on its custody and trading franchise alongside its SPDR ETFs. Same broad business, three different engines.

## The counterargument: they are custodians, not kings

It would be easy to read everything above and conclude that three firms secretly run the economy. The honest counterargument deserves equal time, because it is strong.

First, the money is not theirs. Every dollar of that \$20-trillion-plus belongs to the savers in the funds. The firms are bound by a **fiduciary** duty to act in those savers' financial interest, and they are watched by regulators, the press, and competitors who would love to peel away assets by accusing them of self-dealing or political games. A firm that abused its voting power to enrich itself, or to pursue a manager's personal politics at the expense of returns, would be breaking the law and inviting an exodus.

Second, they cannot just "do whatever they want" with the companies. A 20% stake is large, but it is not control. They almost never have a majority. They cannot fire a CEO by fiat or set a company's prices. Their power is the soft power of the vote and the meeting, exercised mostly in support of management, and constrained by the fact that an index fund must keep holding the stock no matter what. The threat "we will sell" that gives an active investor leverage is one a pure index fund cannot credibly make.

Third, the structure of voting choice and pass-through voting is actively dispersing some of that power back to savers. The trajectory of the last few years has been *away* from centralized control, not toward it, precisely because the firms understand how uncomfortable the concentration looks.

Fourth, and most fundamentally: the alternative to the Big Three is not some idyllic world of empowered individual shareholders. It is a world where ordinary savers either pay much higher fees to active managers who mostly underperform, or do not invest at all. The Big Three's real achievement is that they made owning a diversified slice of the entire economy almost free for a schoolteacher. The concentration of voting power is the price of that achievement, not a separate scheme. Whether the price is too high is the real debate, and reasonable people land on both sides.

The vote happens near the top, far from where your money entered, which is exactly why it can feel as if it has nothing to do with you, even though it is, ultimately, your money.

## How an ETF actually works: the creation and redemption machine

Before we get to misconceptions, it is worth opening the hood on one piece of plumbing, because it explains how an ETF can hold trillions of dollars of real stock while trading like a single ticker, and because it is genuinely elegant.

The problem an ETF must solve is this: it trades on an exchange all day at whatever price buyers and sellers agree to, but it also holds a basket of real stocks with their own constantly changing total value. If the ETF's price drifted far from the value of its holdings, it would be broken. The mechanism that keeps them glued together involves a special class of large traders called **authorized participants**, or APs (usually big banks or trading firms).

Here is the dance. When demand for the ETF is high and its price starts to float above the value of its holdings, an AP can assemble the actual basket of underlying stocks (say, the 500 S&P companies in the right proportions) and hand that basket to the ETF issuer. In return, the issuer hands the AP a big block of brand-new ETF shares, called a **creation unit**. The AP then sells those new shares on the exchange, which increases supply and nudges the price back down toward fair value, pocketing the small difference as profit. When demand falls and the ETF trades below its holdings, the process runs in reverse: an AP buys up cheap ETF shares, hands them back to the issuer, and receives the underlying stocks (**redemption**), which it sells. The pipeline below traces the creation half of that loop.

![Pipeline of an authorized participant swapping a stock basket for new ETF shares](/imgs/blogs/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street-7.png)

#### Worked example: an AP keeping an ETF's price in line

Suppose an S&P 500 ETF's holdings are worth exactly \$500.00 per share, but heavy buying pushes the ETF's market price to \$500.50. An AP sees the gap. It buys \$500 million worth of the underlying 500 stocks in the open market, delivers that basket to the issuer, and receives 1 million new ETF shares (a creation unit). It immediately sells those 1 million shares on the exchange at \$500.50:

```
sell:  1,000,000 x 500.50  =  500,500,000 dollars
cost:  1,000,000 x 500.00  =  500,000,000 dollars
profit:                       500,000 dollars
```

By selling new shares into the hungry market, the AP increased supply and pushed the ETF's price back toward \$500.00, closing the gap. The intuition: this risk-free-looking arbitrage is what keeps an ETF's price chained to the real value of its stocks, so the elegance is that the issuer never has to manage that link directly. The APs do it for profit, automatically, all day long. This in-kind swap of stocks for shares is also why ETFs are often tax-efficient, since the fund rarely has to sell stocks for cash and trigger taxable gains.

## Common misconceptions

A topic this loaded attracts myths from every direction. Here are the most common, and why each is wrong.

**Misconception 1: "The Big Three own the companies and pocket the profits."** They do not own the companies in any economic sense, and they do not pocket the profits. The shares are held on behalf of millions of savers, who are the true owners and who receive the dividends and gains. The firms earn only their small management fee on the assets. When Apple pays a dividend, that cash flows through the fund to the savers, not into BlackRock's own bank account. Confusing "largest registered holder" with "owner who keeps the money" is the single most common error in this entire subject.

**Misconception 2: "Index funds are safe or risk-free."** An index fund is *diversified*, which removes the risk of any single company blowing up your savings. It does nothing to remove **market risk**, the risk that the whole market falls. If the S&P 500 drops 40% in a crash, your S&P 500 index fund drops about 40% too. Index funds spread your bet across the whole market; they do not protect you from the market itself. "Diversified" and "safe" are different words for a reason.

**Misconception 3: "Passive investing has no downside, so everyone should be 100% passive."** Passive investing has real costs that are just less visible. Because index funds buy companies in proportion to their market cap, they automatically pour the most money into whatever has already gone up the most, which can inflate the biggest stocks. They also outsource the job of *price discovery* (figuring out what companies are actually worth) to the shrinking set of active investors; if literally everyone were passive, no one would be left to set sensible prices, and the market would misprice things badly. Passive investing works partly *because* active investors still do the pricing work. It is a great default for most savers, not a free lunch with no system-level cost.

**Misconception 4: "The Big Three secretly coordinate to run the economy."** There is no evidence of secret coordination, and the structure makes it unlikely. The three are fierce competitors who undercut each other on fees, vote independently (sometimes on opposite sides of the same proposal), and are watched closely. The genuine concern is *structural*, that three large owners of everything can have system-level effects even without coordinating, not that they hold meetings to plot. Replacing a real structural question with a cartoon conspiracy actually makes the real issue harder to discuss.

**Misconception 5: "Because they vote my shares, my money supports whatever politics they vote for."** This is partly why pass-through voting exists. Historically the stewardship team decided, but the trend is toward letting savers choose a voting policy or direct votes themselves. And in any case the funds mostly vote with management on routine matters; the politically charged votes are a small minority of the tens of thousands cast. The vote is not a hidden donation to a cause; it is mostly mundane corporate housekeeping.

**Misconception 6: "Owning the index means I own a tiny piece of every company, so I get a meaningful say."** You own a real economic slice, but your individual voice in governance is essentially zero, because the vote is exercised by the manager at the fund level, not by you. That is the entire point of the owning-versus-voting split. Your dividends are real; your direct vote, absent a pass-through program, is not something you hold.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstract power becomes concrete in specific episodes. Here are several, with the mechanism from this post visible in each.

**The 2010s active-to-passive megatrend.** Across the 2010s, money drained out of actively managed US stock funds and poured into index funds and ETFs, year after year, often hundreds of billions of dollars annually. The trigger was simple disillusionment: study after study showed that the large majority of active managers failed to beat their benchmark over ten years, especially after fees. Savers and their advisors did the arithmetic from Bogle's old argument and voted with their wallets. The mechanism was exactly the fee-drag compounding we worked through: a 1% edge handed to the saver every year, multiplied across a decade and a bull market, was impossible to ignore. The lesson is that this concentration was not engineered by the Big Three forcing anything on anyone; it was pulled into being by millions of individual rational decisions to stop overpaying. That makes it both more legitimate and harder to reverse than a top-down scheme would be.

**ExxonMobil and Engine No. 1, 2021.** This is the case that made the Big Three's voting power undeniable. A tiny activist hedge fund called Engine No. 1, holding roughly 0.02% of ExxonMobil, launched a campaign to put its own climate-focused candidates on Exxon's board, arguing the company was mismanaging the energy transition. On paper this was a flea attacking an elephant. But Engine No. 1 did not need to own much; it needed to persuade the giant index holders. When the votes were counted in May 2021, Engine No. 1 won at least three board seats, a stunning upset, and it won precisely because large shareholders, reportedly including the Big Three, backed its candidates. The mechanism is the swing-vote arithmetic from our worked example: a fund holding almost nothing achieved a board coup by convincing the holders of a fifth of the company. The lesson is that the Big Three's votes are decisive even when they neither propose nor lead a fight; whoever wins their support wins.

**The 2020 to 2021 ESG proxy battles, then the retreat.** Around 2020 and 2021, the big managers, BlackRock most loudly, signaled they would weigh climate and social risk more heavily and support more shareholder proposals on those themes. This drew furious political backlash, especially in the United States, where some state officials pulled public pension money from BlackRock and accused it of using other people's savings to push politics. By 2022 to 2024 the firms had visibly dialed back their support for such proposals and leaned harder into "we are just here for long-term financial value" language, while rolling out pass-through voting. The mechanism on display is the fiduciary-and-backlash constraint from the counterargument section: a manager of other people's money cannot get far ahead of its clients' wishes without losing assets, so its stewardship posture is pulled back toward the center by the threat of withdrawals. The lesson is that the power, however large, is leashed to client sentiment.

**The fee wars and the zero-fee fund, 2018 onward.** In August 2018, Fidelity launched index mutual funds with an expense ratio of exactly 0.00%, charging nothing, betting that free funds would pull in customers it could monetize elsewhere. Vanguard and BlackRock had already been driving fees toward the floor for years. The mechanism is the scale economics from our \$900-million example: when the product is nearly identical across providers, the only durable edge is being the cheapest, and being the cheapest requires being the biggest, which entrenches the incumbents. The lesson cuts two ways: savers got an almost unbelievably good deal, and the same dynamic concentrated the industry into a few hands, which is the root of everything contested in this post.

**The "universal owner" theory in practice.** A line of thinking holds that because the Big Three own a slice of essentially the entire economy across decades, they are **universal owners**: investors so diversified that they are effectively betting on the long-term health of the whole system rather than any one company. The theory suggests such an owner should rationally care about economy-wide problems, like pollution or systemic risk, because a harm one company inflicts on the wider economy hurts the owner's other holdings. This idea was used to justify some of the funds' engagement on broad risks. The mechanism is common ownership turned on its head: the same fact that worries antitrust economists (owning all the competitors) is reframed as a reason to act as a steward of the whole. The lesson is that the very structure of universal ownership is genuinely double-edged, plausibly anticompetitive and plausibly a force for long-term stewardship at the same time, which is why the debate never fully resolves.

**Index-inclusion effects: the day a stock joins the S&P 500.** Because trillions of passive dollars must hold exactly the companies in an index, the decision to *add* a company to a major index forces a wave of mechanical buying. When Tesla was added to the S&P 500 in December 2020, index funds had to buy tens of billions of dollars of its shares to match the index, and the stock rose sharply into the inclusion. The mechanism is index licensing and passive scale combined: the index provider's list became a button that moved enormous sums, and the funds tracking it had no choice but to buy. The lesson is that the rise of passive investing handed a strange new power not only to the Big Three but to the obscure committees that decide what goes into an index, because their list now commands the flows of a fifth of the market.

**The 2008 crisis and BlackRock as the government's analyst.** During the 2008 financial crisis, the US government and the Federal Reserve repeatedly hired BlackRock to value and manage troubled assets, because BlackRock's Aladdin-powered analytics were among the few tools capable of pricing the toxic securities at the heart of the meltdown. The mechanism is the infrastructure power from the Aladdin section: BlackRock's influence flowed not from owning shares but from being the institution everyone, including the government, turned to in order to understand risk. The lesson is that concentration in finance is not only about ownership stakes; it is also about who runs the systems everyone else depends on, a kind of power that ownership-focused rules miss entirely.

## When this matters to you, and further reading

For most people most of the time, the practical takeaway is reassuring and simple. If you hold a broad, low-cost index fund in a retirement account, you are very likely getting one of the best deals in the history of personal finance: ownership of a slice of the entire economy for a few hundredths of a percent a year. The fee-drag arithmetic is overwhelmingly on your side, and the diversification protects you from any single company failing. That is the part worth internalizing first, before any of the controversy.

The places it starts to matter to you personally are these. First, understand that "index fund" still means you eat the *whole market's* risk, so a crash will take your balance down with it; diversification is not a hedge against the market itself. Second, if you care about how the votes attached to your money are cast, check whether your fund offers a voting-choice or pass-through program, because increasingly you can have a say rather than leaving it entirely to a stewardship team. Third, if you ever find yourself reading a headline that says the Big Three "own" or "control" a company, remember the owning-versus-voting split: they hold the votes on behalf of savers like you, they mostly vote with management, and the money and gains are not theirs.

And the place it matters to everyone, whether or not you invest, is the unresolved structural question. We have run an enormous, decades-long experiment in which the savings of hundreds of millions of people have been funneled, cheaply and sensibly, into three firms that now sit atop the ownership map of Corporate America. That experiment has made investing radically fairer for ordinary people and has simultaneously created a concentration of voting and infrastructure power with no real precedent. Both things are true. The interesting question is not whether the Big Three are heroes or villains, because they are neither; it is how a democracy and a market should handle ownership that has quietly become this concentrated, while preserving the genuinely good deal that created it.
