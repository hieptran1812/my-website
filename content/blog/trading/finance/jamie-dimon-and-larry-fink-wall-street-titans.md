> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Jamie Dimon's JPMorgan and Larry Fink's BlackRock are the two faces of modern financial power: one owns risk on its own balance sheet, the other manages other people's money and owns a slice of almost everything.
>
> - A **bank** like JPMorgan takes deposits and makes loans, so the risk lives on *its* books; it is the largest US bank with roughly \$4 trillion in assets.
> - An **asset manager** like BlackRock invests money *for clients*, so the assets are not on its books at all; it oversees roughly \$10 trillion of other people's money.
> - Dimon's edge is the **fortress balance sheet** — so much capital and cash that JPMorgan grows by *buying* failed rivals in every crisis (Bear Stearns, WaMu, First Republic).
> - Fink's edge is **scale plus plumbing** — cheap index funds that quietly own large stakes in nearly every public company, and Aladdin, the risk system much of the industry runs on.
> - The one fact to remember: power in finance comes in two forms, **the balance sheet and the index**, and these two men are the living embodiment of each.

In May 2023, a bank with about \$230 billion in assets — First Republic — was failing. Over a single panicked weekend, the US government seized it and quietly auctioned it off. The winning bidder was JPMorgan, already the largest bank in the country, which absorbed the carcass and booked a multi-billion-dollar accounting gain almost immediately. Three years earlier, in March 2020, when financial markets froze at the start of the pandemic, the US Federal Reserve needed to buy hundreds of billions of dollars of bonds in a hurry — and it hired a *private company*, BlackRock, to do the buying on its behalf. Two firms. One grew bigger by swallowing a failure; the other was trusted to run part of the central bank's rescue. The diagram above is the mental model: these are the two forms modern financial power takes.

![Matrix comparing Dimon and Fink across what they do, scale, and source of power](/imgs/blogs/jamie-dimon-and-larry-fink-wall-street-titans-1.png)

This post is about the two men behind those firms — Jamie Dimon, the chief executive of JPMorgan, and Larry Fink, the co-founder and chief executive of BlackRock — and, more importantly, about the *two different machines* they run. By the end you should be able to explain, to a friend who knows nothing about finance, why a bank and an asset manager are completely different animals even though both are described as "Wall Street giants," why crises make the strong ones stronger, and why a handful of people now sit close enough to the controls of the global economy that governments call *them* when something breaks. No prior finance knowledge is assumed. We will define every term as we go, and we will ground every idea in concrete dollar arithmetic.

## Foundations: a bank, an asset manager, and what "power" even means here

Before we can talk about Dimon and Fink, we have to be ruthlessly clear about a distinction that trips up almost everyone: **a bank and an asset manager are not the same kind of company, even though both move money around.** Confusing them is like confusing a casino with a coat-check. Let us build the difference from zero.

**A bank** (more precisely, a *commercial bank*) is in the business of taking **deposits** and making **loans**. A deposit is money you hand the bank for safekeeping; legally, the moment you deposit it, that money becomes the bank's — it owes you a *promise* to give it back, but it is free to lend most of it out to someone else. The bank's profit, in its simplest form, is the gap between the interest it charges borrowers and the (lower) interest it pays depositors. That gap is called the **net interest margin**. Because the bank lends out money it owes to depositors, the loans it makes show up as **assets** on the bank's own books, and the deposits it owes show up as **liabilities**. The whole point is that the risk lives on the bank's own balance sheet. If the loans go bad, *the bank* eats the loss.

A **universal bank** is a bank that has bolted many other businesses onto that deposit-and-loan core: trading stocks and bonds, advising companies on mergers, underwriting new share issues, managing the wealth of rich families. JPMorgan is the archetype. We will draw its full structure shortly.

**An asset manager**, by contrast, takes no deposits and makes no loans. It is hired to **invest other people's money**. When you put \$10,000 into a retirement fund, that money does not become the asset manager's; it stays *yours*, held in a separate account, and the manager simply decides what to buy with it and charges you a small fee for the service. The assets the manager oversees are called **assets under management**, almost always abbreviated **AUM**. The crucial point: AUM is *not* on the manager's balance sheet. If the investments fall in value, the *client* loses money, not (directly) the manager. BlackRock is the largest asset manager in the world.

That single distinction — *assets on your own books versus assets you manage for others* — is the whole game. Hold onto it.

A few more building blocks, each glossed on first use:

- **Capital** (a bank's *equity capital*): the money the bank's owners — its shareholders — have permanently tied up in it. It is the cushion that absorbs losses before depositors are touched. More capital means a safer, harder-to-topple bank.
- **A capital ratio**: capital expressed as a percentage of the bank's risk-weighted assets. Regulators set minimums. A bank with a higher capital ratio than required is said to be **well-capitalized**.
- **Systemically important**: so large and interconnected that if it failed, it would drag down the rest of the financial system. Regulators formally label the biggest banks *Global Systemically Important Banks* (G-SIBs) and force them to hold extra capital. Both JPMorgan and BlackRock are watched as systemically important, though in different ways.
- **A stress test**: a regulator-run simulation that asks, "If unemployment spiked and markets crashed, would this bank still have enough capital to keep operating?" Banks that pass can pay dividends; banks that fail must raise capital.
- **The fortress balance sheet**: Dimon's own phrase for running JPMorgan with so much capital, cash, and conservative funding that it can not only survive a crisis but *act* during one. We will return to this repeatedly.
- **AUM, again**: the pile of client money a manager invests. BlackRock's roughly \$10 trillion (as of 2024-2025, approximate and constantly moving) is the largest in the world.

One more idea deserves a paragraph of its own, because it is the secret engine of the whole bank: **deposits are the cheapest money in finance, and a bank that gathers the most of them wins.** When you leave \$5,000 in a checking account earning near zero interest, you are effectively lending the bank \$5,000 at almost no cost. The bank then lends or invests that money at, say, 5%. Multiply by tens of millions of accounts and you have hundreds of billions of dollars of funding that costs the bank almost nothing. This is why the consumer-banking arm — which looks like the boring, low-margin part of JPMorgan — is actually its crown jewel: it is the spigot of cheap money that funds everything else. A rival bank without that deposit base has to borrow in the open market at much higher rates, and so can never quite match JPMorgan's economics. The fight for deposits is the fight for the cheapest raw material in the business.

It also explains why a bank's funding *stability* matters as much as its profit. Deposits that flee at the first sign of trouble — large, uninsured corporate balances, for instance — are dangerous funding, because the bank may have to dump assets at fire-sale prices to repay them in a hurry. Deposits that stay put — millions of small, insured household accounts — are golden, because they fund the bank reliably through good times and bad. The 2023 regional-bank failures were, at bottom, a lesson in this difference: the banks that died were funded by skittish, concentrated, uninsured money, while JPMorgan's vast base of sticky retail deposits barely flinched. We will see this play out concretely later.

With those terms in hand, here is the comparison that organizes the entire post. Dimon's power flows from what JPMorgan *owns and owes* — its balance sheet. Fink's power flows from what BlackRock *manages and watches* — the index funds and the risk system. The first is concentrated and visible; the second is diffuse and quiet. Both are enormous. And both, as we will see, grew largest precisely because each man understood, earlier and more obsessively than his rivals, the unglamorous mechanic at the heart of his business: for Dimon, the sanctity of capital and the stickiness of deposits; for Fink, the brutal arithmetic of fees at scale and the value of measuring risk before taking it.

## Jamie Dimon and JPMorgan: power as a balance sheet

### Who Dimon is, and why a bank CEO matters

Jamie Dimon has run JPMorgan since 2005, which makes him one of the longest-serving and most influential bank chief executives in the world. He learned the trade under Sandy Weill, the dealmaker who assembled the financial supermarket that became Citigroup; Dimon was famously fired by Weill in 1998, ran the smaller Bank One, and then engineered its 2004 merger into JPMorgan Chase, taking the top job a year later. The biography matters less than the doctrine, so we will keep it brief and spend our time on the *machine*.

What does a bank chief executive actually control? Not the day-to-day lending decisions — those are made by thousands of employees following risk models. A bank CEO controls four big levers: **how much capital to hold** (the safety cushion), **how much risk to take** (which loans and trades to allow), **what to buy or sell** (acquisitions, exits), and **the firm's posture toward regulators and the public**. Dimon's distinctive choice on the first two levers — hold more capital and take less hidden risk than rivals — is the fortress doctrine, and it is the reason JPMorgan keeps emerging from crises *stronger*.

### The shape of the machine: four businesses, one balance sheet

JPMorgan is the largest bank in the United States, with roughly \$4 trillion in assets (as of 2024-2025, approximate). To see where its power comes from, you have to see that it is not one business but four, all funded from the same giant pool of cheap deposits.

![Tree of JPMorgan's four business lines under one balance sheet](/imgs/blogs/jamie-dimon-and-larry-fink-wall-street-titans-2.png)

The four lines, stated plainly:

1. **Consumer and community banking** — the part you would recognize: checking accounts, savings, credit cards, auto loans, mortgages. This is where the cheap deposits come from. Tens of millions of ordinary customers each leave a few thousand dollars in an account paying almost no interest, and in aggregate that becomes the cheapest funding in finance.
2. **The corporate and investment bank** — advising companies on mergers, underwriting bond and stock issues, and trading currencies, bonds, and equities for clients. This is the high-octane, high-fee Wall Street business. (For a full tour of how this side makes money, see [inside an investment bank](/blog/trading/finance/inside-an-investment-bank-how-they-make-money).)
3. **Commercial banking** — lending to mid-sized businesses and real-estate developers; less glamorous, very profitable.
4. **Asset and wealth management** — JPMorgan also runs its *own* asset-management arm, with roughly \$5 trillion or more of AUM, managing money for institutions and wealthy families. (Yes — JPMorgan competes with BlackRock here too, which tells you how blurry the lines have become.)

The magic of the universal-bank model is that all four feed off the same deposit base and the same risk-management plumbing. The consumer bank gathers cheap money; the other three deploy it at higher returns. That is why a single firm can earn over \$50 billion of profit in a strong year.

### The fortress doctrine, with numbers

The phrase "fortress balance sheet" sounds like marketing, but it has a precise meaning: hold capital *well above* the regulatory minimum, keep a mountain of liquid cash and government bonds, and avoid funding the bank with money that can flee overnight. The key insight is that excess capital is not idle — it is *optionality*. When rivals are forced to sell assets at fire-sale prices in a panic, the bank with the fortress is the only buyer with the firepower to act, and it buys cheap. A bank run conservatively in calm times looks like it is leaving money on the table; the payoff arrives all at once, in the crisis, when everyone else is a forced seller and it is the only buyer.

#### Worked example: JPMorgan's balance sheet and its return on equity

Let us build a stripped-down version of JPMorgan's books to see how a bank actually earns. Suppose the bank has:

- **Assets:** \$4,000 billion (loans, bonds, cash, trading positions).
- **Liabilities:** \$3,700 billion (mostly deposits it owes back to customers).
- **Equity capital:** \$4,000 billion minus \$3,700 billion = \$300 billion — the owners' cushion.

Now suppose in a good year the bank earns a **net profit** of \$50 billion. The headline number investors care about is **return on equity (ROE)** — profit divided by the equity capital that produced it:

```
ROE = net profit / equity capital
    = $50 billion / $300 billion
    = 0.1667  =  16.7%
```

A 16.7% ROE means the owners earned almost 17 cents of profit for every dollar of capital they left in the bank that year. For a bank of this size, a sustained ROE in the mid-to-high teens is excellent; many large banks struggle to clear 10%. Notice the *leverage* baked in: \$300 billion of equity supports \$4,000 billion of assets, a ratio of more than 13 to 1. That leverage is exactly why a bank's capital cushion is sacred — a loss of just 7.5% on its assets (\$300 billion) would wipe the owners out entirely. **The intuition: a bank is a highly leveraged machine, and the size of its capital cushion is the difference between a fortress and a house of cards.**

#### Worked example: where the profit actually comes from (net interest margin)

The ROE above is the *result*; let us trace the *source*. The core of any bank's earnings is the **net interest margin** — the spread between what it earns on assets and what it pays for funding. Suppose JPMorgan has \$3,200 billion of interest-earning assets (loans and bonds) yielding an average of 5.5%, funded largely by \$3,000 billion of deposits and borrowings costing an average of 2.0%. The interest math:

```
Interest income  = $3,200 billion × 5.5% = $176 billion
Interest expense = $3,000 billion × 2.0% = $60 billion
Net interest income = $176 - $60 = $116 billion
```

Expressed as a margin on earning assets:

```
Net interest margin = net interest income / earning assets
                    = $116 billion / $3,200 billion
                    ≈ 3.6%
```

A 3.6% margin sounds thin, but on \$3,200 billion of assets it throws off \$116 billion of gross interest profit a year before costs and loan losses. And here is the deposit advantage made numeric: if a rival had to fund the same assets at 3.5% instead of 2.0% — because it lacks JPMorgan's sticky, cheap retail deposits — its interest expense would be \$105 billion instead of \$60 billion, slicing \$45 billion straight off the bottom line. **The intuition: a bank does not get rich on a fat margin; it gets rich on a thin margin applied to a colossal balance sheet funded more cheaply than anyone else can manage.**

Even a fortress is not invincible. In 2012, a JPMorgan trading desk in London took outsized derivative positions that went badly wrong — the so-called **London Whale** episode — and the bank lost more than \$6 billion. The loss was survivable precisely *because* of the capital cushion, but it punctured the myth of flawless risk management and drew heavy regulatory scrutiny and fines. It is a useful reminder that the fortress doctrine is about *surviving* mistakes with the cushion intact, not about never making them.

### The crisis acquirer

Here is the part that makes Dimon singular. In each of the last two great banking panics, JPMorgan did not merely survive — it *expanded* by buying the failures. This is the fortress doctrine paying off.

![Timeline of the two titans' rise and the crisis acquisitions that fed them](/imgs/blogs/jamie-dimon-and-larry-fink-wall-street-titans-4.png)

In **2008**, as the financial crisis tore through Wall Street, JPMorgan bought the collapsing investment bank **Bear Stearns** (in a rescue brokered by the Federal Reserve, initially at a stunning \$2 per share, later raised to \$10) and then, months later, acquired the failed savings bank **Washington Mutual** (WaMu) — the largest bank failure in US history — out of receivership for about \$1.9 billion, instantly gaining a huge retail-deposit network. In **2023**, when a cluster of regional banks ran into trouble, JPMorgan bought **First Republic** after the government seized it. Each time, the pattern was the same: a rival's fear became JPMorgan's bargain.

#### Worked example: buying a failed bank's assets at a discount

Why is buying a *failed* bank a good deal rather than a curse? Because in a forced sale, the buyer pays less than the assets are worth, and the regulator often shares the downside. Let us make it concrete with simple numbers in the spirit of the First Republic deal.

![Pipeline showing the steps of a crisis bank acquisition](/imgs/blogs/jamie-dimon-and-larry-fink-wall-street-titans-7.png)

Suppose the failed bank has:

- **Loans and securities (assets):** \$200 billion, but in a panic everyone assumes they are impaired, so the *market* would only pay, say, \$170 billion for them today.
- **Deposits and other liabilities the buyer must honor:** \$190 billion.

The regulator (the FDIC) runs a closed auction. JPMorgan, the strongest bidder, offers to take on the \$190 billion of liabilities and pay the FDIC a modest sum, in exchange for the \$200 billion of assets — *and* the FDIC agrees to share losses on the worst loans. JPMorgan's own analysis says the assets are really worth more than the panicked market thinks, because the bank can fund them cheaply and hold them to maturity. Suppose JPMorgan marks the assets it received at a conservative \$185 billion and the liabilities it assumed at \$190 billion, and the structure leaves it with a net acquired position it values at, say, \$2 billion *more* than it paid. That difference is booked immediately as a one-time **bargain-purchase gain**:

```
Bargain-purchase gain = fair value of net assets acquired - price paid
                      ≈ $2 billion+
```

That is roughly what JPMorgan reported on the First Republic deal — a multi-billion-dollar accounting gain in the very quarter it absorbed a failed bank, plus a stream of future profit from the loans and the new customers. **The intuition: in a panic the fortress bank is the only buyer with cash, so it names a price that books a profit on day one — fear is its discount.** This is precisely why critics worry that crises make the biggest banks even bigger; we will return to that.

### The shareholder letter as quasi-policy

One more lever Dimon controls is *the microphone*. Every spring he publishes an annual letter to JPMorgan shareholders that runs tens of thousands of words and ranges far beyond the bank — into the economy, geopolitics, regulation, and government. Because he runs the largest US bank, markets and policymakers read it as a quasi-official statement on the state of the financial world. When Dimon warns about interest rates, recession risk, or a regulatory rule, the warning itself can move the debate. A bank CEO whose words are treated as policy input is exercising a kind of power that does not show up on any balance sheet — but it flows *from* the balance sheet's size.

## Larry Fink and BlackRock: power as the index and the plumbing

### Who Fink is, and why an asset manager matters

Larry Fink co-founded BlackRock in 1988. The origin story is instructive: Fink had earlier, at another firm, lost a fortune because he misjudged interest-rate risk on mortgage bonds, and he resolved to build a firm obsessed with *measuring* risk before taking it. That obsession became a software system — we will meet it shortly — and the firm grew on top of it. Today BlackRock is the largest asset manager on earth, overseeing roughly \$10 trillion of client money (as of 2024-2025, approximate). To grasp how a company that owns *almost nothing on its own books* can be so powerful, you have to understand three things: the scale of passive investing, the Aladdin risk system, and the role of a *steward*.

### The empire: index funds plus a risk system

![Stack of BlackRock's AUM layers with Aladdin spanning the industry](/imgs/blogs/jamie-dimon-and-larry-fink-wall-street-titans-3.png)

Most of BlackRock's \$10 trillion is in **passive index funds** — funds that do not try to pick winners but simply buy *every* stock in an index like the S&P 500, in proportion to size, and hold them. (The mechanics and consequences of this are the whole subject of [the Big Three index giants](/blog/trading/finance/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street).) Roughly speaking, the firm's index and institutional base accounts for around \$4 trillion, its **iShares** exchange-traded-fund brand for around \$3 trillion, and its active funds plus alternatives for around \$3 trillion more (all figures approximate and constantly moving). iShares — bought from Barclays in 2009 in a deal that vaulted BlackRock to the top — alone holds trillions. The reason this matters for *power*, not just profit, is mechanical: if you run a fund that must hold every company in the index, you end up as one of the three or four largest *shareholders* of nearly every large public company in the world. You did not choose to own Apple, Exxon, and a defense contractor for any view about them — the index made you own them. But own them you do, and with ownership comes votes.

How does an ETF — an *exchange-traded fund* — actually hold trillions while charging almost nothing? Through a clever plumbing trick called **creation and redemption**. An ETF like an iShares S&P 500 fund trades on the stock exchange just like a single share, but behind it sits a basket of the 500 underlying stocks. Large trading firms called **authorized participants** are allowed to hand BlackRock a basket of the actual 500 stocks and receive newly *created* ETF shares in return, or to hand back ETF shares and *redeem* them for the underlying stocks. This in-kind swap keeps the ETF's market price glued to the value of its basket and lets the fund grow or shrink without BlackRock itself constantly buying and selling — which is a big part of why the fund can run so cheaply. The structure is mostly mechanical, which is exactly why a tiny team can administer trillions of dollars: the index defines what to hold, and the authorized participants do the heavy lifting of keeping price and value aligned.

The other pillar is **Aladdin** — short for *Asset, Liability, Debt and Derivative Investment Network*. It is the risk-and-portfolio software BlackRock built for itself and then *rented out* to the rest of the industry. Pension funds, insurers, other asset managers, and even some governments run their portfolios on Aladdin. Estimates put the total value of assets analyzed through Aladdin at well over \$20 trillion — far more than BlackRock's own AUM, because so much of it belongs to clients of the software. This is the quiet kind of power: not owning the money, but being the system through which much of finance *sees its own risk*. The historical irony is sharp — Fink built Aladdin *because* he had once been burned by risk he could not see, and that scar tissue became the most valuable product in the firm.

#### Worked example: a 0.03% fee on \$10 trillion

How does a firm that takes no deposits and makes no loans get rich? Tiny fees on an enormous base. Index funds are cheap — a big stock-index fund might charge an **expense ratio** of just 0.03% per year, meaning 3 cents annually per \$100 invested. That sounds like nothing. Now multiply by the base.

Suppose BlackRock charges a *blended* average fee of 0.15% across its whole \$10 trillion (some funds are nearly free; active funds and alternatives charge much more, so the average lands higher than the cheapest index fund):

```
Annual base fee revenue = AUM × blended fee rate
                        = $10,000 billion × 0.0015
                        = $15 billion per year
```

Now consider just the cheapest slice — say \$3 trillion sitting in funds at 0.03%:

```
Fee on the cheap slice = $3,000 billion × 0.0003
                       = $0.9 billion  ≈  $900 million per year
```

Almost a billion dollars a year from a fee so small the client barely notices it — because the base is three *trillion* dollars. **The intuition: an asset manager's profit is a rounding error per client multiplied by an oceanic base, which is why scale, not fee level, is the entire competitive battle.** It is also why the business is so concentrated: only a few firms can afford to charge near-zero fees and still earn billions, so the giants get gigantic and everyone else is squeezed out.

#### Worked example: what that "tiny" fee costs you over a lifetime

The same fee that earns BlackRock billions is, from your seat as a saver, a slow leak — and the difference between a cheap index fund and an expensive active fund compounds into real money. Suppose you invest \$100,000 and it grows at 7% a year before fees for 30 years.

With a cheap index fund charging **0.03%**, your net return is about 6.97% a year:

```
Ending value ≈ $100,000 × (1.0697)^30 ≈ $755,000
```

With an actively managed fund charging **0.85%**, your net return is about 6.15% a year:

```
Ending value ≈ $100,000 × (1.0615)^30 ≈ $599,000
```

The fee gap of about 0.82% a year — pennies per \$100 — quietly costs you roughly **\$156,000** over three decades, more than the entire amount you originally invested. **The intuition: the same low-fee scale that makes BlackRock rich is, for the index-fund saver, the best deal in finance — and it is the high fees of the old active world that paid for the wealth being competed away.** That two-sided coin is why the rise of cheap index funds was both a triumph for ordinary savers and the engine that built a \$10 trillion colossus.

#### Worked example: Aladdin watching \$20 trillion of risk

Aladdin's economics are different and, in a way, even more enviable. Suppose BlackRock charges institutional clients to run their portfolios on Aladdin, and the assets monitored through the system total around \$20 trillion. The software-licensing revenue is a few billion dollars a year — small next to the fund-fee business — but consider what it *buys* in influence rather than dollars.

If \$20 trillion of the world's investment assets are risk-managed on one company's system, then that company's models, its definitions of risk, and its assumptions about how markets move are quietly baked into the decisions of pensions, insurers, and other managers across the globe. When Aladdin says an asset is risky, capital tends to move away from it. No single decision; a *gravitational field*.

```
Assets analyzed via Aladdin ≈ $20 trillion+
BlackRock's own AUM        ≈ $10 trillion
→ Aladdin's reach is roughly 2× the firm's own money
```

**The intuition: BlackRock's deepest power is not the \$10 trillion it manages but the much larger pile of money that sees the world through its software.** That is plumbing, and whoever owns the plumbing shapes the flow.

### Stewardship and the ESG fights

Because index funds own so much of every company, BlackRock holds an enormous number of **shareholder votes** — the votes that, for instance, elect company boards or approve executive pay. The team that decides how to cast them is called the **stewardship** team. For several years Fink used his annual letter to corporate CEOs to push companies on climate risk and other **ESG** factors — *Environmental, Social, and Governance* considerations, the idea that long-term investors should care about a company's carbon emissions, labor practices, and board quality, not just next quarter's earnings.

To grasp the scale, consider what stewardship actually involves: across the tens of thousands of public companies its funds hold worldwide, BlackRock casts hundreds of thousands of individual votes at annual shareholder meetings every year — on board elections, executive pay, mergers, and shareholder proposals. No human reads every proxy statement; the team works from published voting guidelines, much like Aladdin's risk models, so that the firm's *philosophy* scales across an ocean of votes. That mechanical scaling is what makes the power both vast and, paradoxically, impersonal — and it is why a single shift in BlackRock's published guidelines can move corporate behavior across an entire economy at once.

This made BlackRock a lightning rod. Critics on the political right accused it of using *other people's* retirement money to push a political agenda; some US states pulled money from BlackRock in protest. Critics on the left accused it of greenwashing — talking about climate while still holding huge stakes in oil and gas through its index funds (which, by design, it *cannot* sell without abandoning the index). Caught in the middle, BlackRock has since dialed back the public ESG rhetoric, even retiring the term in some communications, and built out a program — **voting choice** — that lets large clients direct how the shares *they* own are voted, rather than leaving every ballot to BlackRock's central team. That deliberate dispersal of voting power is itself a tell: it is a giant trying to *give back* some of the influence that scale forced upon it, precisely because that influence had become a liability. The episode is a perfect illustration of the asset manager's bind: when you own a slice of everything, *every* political fight becomes your fight, and you cannot win them all.

## Where their power converges: regulation, policy, and the ripple effect

So far we have two separate machines. Where do they meet? In their gravitational pull on **policy**. Both men sit in the small room where private finance and public power overlap.

![Graph showing how one firm's decision ripples into national policy](/imgs/blogs/jamie-dimon-and-larry-fink-wall-street-titans-6.png)

The mechanism is the ripple shown above. A decision that, for a small firm, would be a private business choice becomes, for a firm of this size, a *public fact* that everyone else must price in. When Dimon's annual letter warns of recession, markets and even regulators take note. When BlackRock's stewardship team signals how it will vote on climate proposals, thousands of company boards adjust their behavior in advance to keep their largest shareholder content. And because both firms are systemically important, regulators consult them — formally and informally — when writing the very rules that will govern them. During the 2008 crisis the US Treasury leaned on the strongest private banks to stabilize the system; during the 2020 crisis the Fed hired BlackRock to execute bond purchases. The line between regulator and regulated blurs precisely *because* these firms are large enough to be part of the solution.

It is worth being precise about *why* the consultation happens, because it is not (mostly) corruption — it is information and capacity. When a regulator wants to know how a proposed rule will ripple through the bond market, the people with the best real-time view are the firms that *are* the bond market: a JPMorgan trading desk, a BlackRock portfolio team. When a central bank needs to buy hundreds of billions of dollars of bonds in days, the only organizations with the systems and staff to do it at that speed are the giant private managers. So the regulator turns to the regulated not because it is captured but because the expertise and the machinery live there. The danger is subtler than bribery: it is that a firm large enough to be *indispensable* to solving a crisis is also large enough to shape the rules in ways that protect its own position, and that the same handful of people end up sitting on both sides of the table over a career — a pattern often called the *revolving door* between Wall Street and Washington.

There is also a sharp asymmetry between the two kinds of power that is easy to miss. Dimon's power is **visible and accountable**: JPMorgan is named in every headline, its capital ratio is published quarterly, it is stress-tested in public, and Dimon himself is hauled before legislative committees. Fink's power is **diffuse and quiet**: most savers have no idea BlackRock is, on their behalf, among the largest shareholders of the companies they work for, and the votes it casts are buried in disclosures almost no one reads. A bank that is too big to fail at least *announces itself*; an asset manager that owns a slice of everything exercises its influence in a thousand small, invisible ballots. Which form of concentrated power is more worrying is a genuinely open question — and the fact that it is open is exactly why both men are permanent fixtures of the policy debate.

The flip side, traced at the right of the diagram, is that the costs of these arrangements often land on ordinary savers — through deposit-insurance assessments, through the implicit subsidy a too-big-to-fail bank enjoys, through the fees embedded in the funds in your retirement account. Power at this scale is never free; it is paid for somewhere downstream.

## The critiques: too big to fail, ESG backlash, concentration, and conflicts

A neutral account has to take the critiques seriously, because they are the reason these two firms are perpetually in the political crosshairs.

**Too big to fail.** A bank that the government cannot allow to collapse — because its failure would crater the economy — enjoys a hidden subsidy: lenders will fund it more cheaply, knowing the state stands behind it. That lets it grow even larger, which makes it even more unfailable. Critics argue JPMorgan's crisis acquisitions (Bear, WaMu, First Republic) entrenched exactly this dynamic: each rescue made the biggest bank bigger and the system *more* concentrated, not less. Dimon's reply is that a stronger, better-capitalized bank is a feature, not a bug — the fortress is what let JPMorgan be the buyer rather than the failure. Both things are true, which is what makes the debate hard.

#### Worked example: putting a dollar figure on the too-big-to-fail subsidy

The subsidy sounds abstract until you price it. Suppose lenders charge a giant bank that everyone assumes the government will rescue just **0.25%** less to borrow than they would charge an otherwise-identical small bank with no such backstop. On a bank funding, say, \$2,000 billion of its balance sheet in the wholesale markets, that quarter-point edge is:

```
Annual funding subsidy = funding base × rate advantage
                       = $2,000 billion × 0.0025
                       = $5 billion per year
```

\$5 billion a year of cheaper funding, handed over not by any deliberate policy but by the market's belief that the state will not let the bank fail. Economists argue endlessly about the true size of this number, but even a fraction of it is an enormous, recurring advantage that smaller banks simply cannot get. **The intuition: being too big to fail is not just a risk the public bears — it is a real, ongoing cash subsidy the biggest banks collect, which is precisely why letting them grow by absorbing failures is so controversial.**

**Concentration of ownership.** When three asset managers — BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street, the "Big Three" — collectively are among the largest shareholders of almost every big public company, some economists worry about **common ownership**: if the same few owners hold large stakes in *competing* companies (say, all the major airlines), do those companies compete a little less hard, to the benefit of their shared owners? The evidence is genuinely contested, but the *structure* is real and unprecedented. (The argument is laid out in detail in [the Big Three post](/blog/trading/finance/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street).)

**ESG backlash, from both sides.** As described above, BlackRock's stewardship pushed it into a political fight it could not win, drawing fire from the right for overreach and from the left for hypocrisy. The deeper point is structural: a passive owner cannot vote *with its feet* by selling, so its only lever is its *voice* — and any use of that voice is, inevitably, political.

**Conflicts of interest.** When BlackRock is hired by the Fed to buy bonds *while also* managing its own funds that hold similar bonds, who is being served? BlackRock erects internal walls and the government imposes conditions, but the optics — a private firm executing public policy in markets it also trades — are uncomfortable, and critics argue the walls are thinner than claimed. The same charge of conflicts dogs universal banks, where the side advising a company on a merger sits under the same roof as the side trading that company's stock.

None of these critiques means the firms are villains; they mean that *scale itself* creates risks that no amount of good intentions fully neutralizes. That is the price of sitting at the center.

## The Wall Street these two helped build

It is worth stepping back to see how different Wall Street looks because of consolidation — much of it set in motion by the 2008 crisis and JPMorgan's role in it.

![Before-and-after of Wall Street's structure across the 2008 crisis](/imgs/blogs/jamie-dimon-and-larry-fink-wall-street-titans-5.png)

Before 2008, the landscape had many independent investment banks (Bear Stearns, Lehman Brothers, Merrill Lynch) and a long tail of regional and savings banks (Washington Mutual among them). The 2008 panic was a culling: Lehman failed outright (the story is told in [the Lehman Brothers post](/blog/trading/finance/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis)); Bear and Merrill were absorbed in rescues; WaMu was seized. What emerged was a smaller field of much larger universal banks, of which JPMorgan became the largest and strongest. The 2023 regional-bank scare (covered in [the SVB and Credit Suisse post](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs)) repeated the pattern at a smaller scale, with First Republic folding into JPMorgan. Meanwhile, on the asset-management side, the parallel consolidation ran toward passive scale, and BlackRock — turbocharged by buying iShares in the depths of 2009 — emerged as the giant. The two trends are mirror images: the balance-sheet world consolidated into a few fortress banks; the savings world consolidated into a few index managers. Dimon and Fink run the largest survivor of each.

The arithmetic of consolidation is worth pausing on, because it explains why these patterns are so durable. Each merger does not just add assets; it adds the *cheaper funding* and *lower per-dollar costs* that come with size, which lets the survivor outbid everyone else for the next deal — a flywheel. When JPMorgan absorbed WaMu's deposit network, it did not merely get bigger; it got *cheaper to run* per dollar of assets, which raised its ROE, which raised its share price, which gave it stronger currency to make the *next* acquisition. The same flywheel turns in asset management: every trillion of new AUM lets BlackRock spread its fixed technology and compliance costs over a larger base, which lets it cut fees further, which attracts the next trillion. In both worlds, scale is not a one-time prize but a self-reinforcing advantage, and that is the deep reason finance keeps concentrating rather than fragmenting. Left unchecked, the flywheel runs toward a handful of giants — which is exactly the outcome regulators now wrestle with, and exactly why the size of these two firms is a permanent policy question rather than a settled fact.

#### Worked example: a capital ratio in a stress test

To make the "fortress" idea fully concrete, let us run a miniature stress test on a hypothetical bank — the kind of arithmetic regulators do at scale.

A bank has **\$300 billion of equity capital** and **\$2,000 billion of risk-weighted assets** (assets adjusted so that a risky loan counts for more than a safe government bond). Its capital ratio today is:

```
Capital ratio = equity capital / risk-weighted assets
             = $300 billion / $2,000 billion
             = 0.15  =  15%
```

The regulator's stress scenario assumes a severe recession that inflicts **\$120 billion of losses** on the bank's loans and trading positions. Equity absorbs those losses first:

```
Post-stress capital = $300 billion - $120 billion = $180 billion
Post-stress ratio   = $180 billion / $2,000 billion = 9%
```

Suppose the regulatory minimum the bank must clear even *after* the stress is 7%. The bank ends the scenario at 9% — comfortably above the floor — so it **passes** and is allowed to keep paying dividends and buying back shares. A weaker bank that started at, say, 9% capital would have fallen to 3% after the same losses, **failed** the test, and been ordered to raise capital or halt payouts. **The intuition: the capital cushion is what lets a bank take a haymaker and stay standing — and the fortress bank deliberately carries a bigger cushion than it needs, so it can throw punches while rivals are on the canvas.** That extra cushion is exactly the firepower behind the crisis acquisitions.

## Common misconceptions

**"JPMorgan and BlackRock are basically the same kind of company — both are huge Wall Street firms."** No. JPMorgan is a *bank*: it takes deposits and lends, so the assets and risks sit on its own balance sheet. BlackRock is an *asset manager*: it invests other people's money for a fee, so almost nothing it oversees is on its books. JPMorgan's risk is *its* risk; BlackRock's clients' risk is the *clients'* risk. This is the most important distinction in the whole post.

**"BlackRock owns \$10 trillion of stocks and bonds."** It does not *own* them — it *manages* them on behalf of clients (pensions, individuals, governments). If those assets lose value, the clients lose, not BlackRock (BlackRock loses only the fee income on a smaller base). The \$10 trillion is **assets under management**, not BlackRock's wealth. BlackRock's own balance sheet is a tiny fraction of that.

**"A bank failing is always a disaster, so JPMorgan buying failed banks is a bailout of JPMorgan."** The opposite, usually. When JPMorgan buys a failed bank in an FDIC auction, it is *taking on* a problem the government wants solved, often at a price that makes the deal profitable. The controversy is not that JPMorgan is being bailed out — it is that letting the strongest bank absorb the failures makes the banking system *more concentrated*, which raises the too-big-to-fail risk for everyone.

**"ESG means BlackRock refuses to invest in oil companies."** No. BlackRock's largest funds are *index* funds that, by design, must hold every company in the index — including oil and gas firms — because their job is to track the index, not to express a view. BlackRock's ESG activity was mostly about how it *voted* its shares and what *optional* funds it offered, not about purging energy from its core index products. This is precisely why it was attacked from both sides.

**"Larry Fink personally decides how BlackRock votes its shares in your company."** Not directly. A dedicated stewardship team votes according to published guidelines, and BlackRock increasingly lets clients direct their *own* votes through a program called voting choice. Fink sets tone via his letters, but the firm has deliberately moved toward dispersing the vote rather than concentrating that power in one office.

**"Aladdin is just BlackRock's internal software."** It started that way, but BlackRock rents Aladdin to much of the rest of the financial industry. The total assets analyzed through it — pensions, insurers, rival managers — vastly exceed BlackRock's own AUM. That is why Aladdin, not the funds, is arguably the firm's most systemically significant asset.

**"JPMorgan made huge profits from the 2008 crisis, so the crisis was good for it."** This needs care. JPMorgan came through 2008 in far better shape than its rivals and was able to make opportunistic acquisitions — but it also took real losses, paid out tens of billions of dollars in legal settlements over mortgage-related conduct in the years that followed, and operated under heavy government pressure during the rescues. The right summary is not that JPMorgan *profited from* the crisis but that it *survived* the crisis intact enough to buy the wreckage — survival first, opportunity second. The fortress is a defensive doctrine that happens to create offensive options.

**"BlackRock and JPMorgan are rivals fighting over the same business."** Mostly they are not. A bank that lends and an asset manager that invests client money sit in different parts of the system and largely need each other — JPMorgan's funds may run on Aladdin; BlackRock's ETFs trade through banks like JPMorgan; the two firms hold each other's securities. They overlap only at the edges (JPMorgan also runs an asset-management arm, and BlackRock has pushed into private credit that competes with bank lending). For the most part they are complements, not competitors — two different organs of the same financial body.

## How it shows up in real markets

Here are the named episodes where these dynamics became visible. Each one is a clean illustration of a mechanism we have built up.

1. **2008 — JPMorgan rescues Bear Stearns.** With the Fed brokering the deal and backstopping the riskiest assets, JPMorgan bought the collapsing investment bank at a price (initially \$2, later \$10 per share) far below where it had traded weeks earlier. The fortress balance sheet made JPMorgan the only credible buyer in the room. This is the crisis-acquirer mechanism in its purest form.

2. **2008 — JPMorgan acquires Washington Mutual.** When WaMu — the largest US bank ever to fail — was seized by regulators, JPMorgan bought its banking operations out of receivership for about \$1.9 billion, instantly acquiring a coast-to-coast retail-deposit network. A failure for WaMu's owners; a discounted expansion for JPMorgan.

3. **2009 — BlackRock buys iShares (Barclays Global Investors).** In the wreckage after the crisis, a wounded Barclays sold its index-fund business to BlackRock for roughly \$13.5 billion. The deal made BlackRock the dominant player in exchange-traded funds and set up its march toward \$10 trillion. Like JPMorgan's bank purchases, it was a crisis bargain — bought when the seller was desperate.

4. **2020 — the Fed hires BlackRock.** When pandemic panic froze the bond market, the Federal Reserve launched programs to buy corporate bonds and bond ETFs — and hired BlackRock to manage the purchases on its behalf. A private asset manager executing the central bank's market intervention is the starkest example of how the regulated and the regulator have fused at the top.

5. **2023 — JPMorgan acquires First Republic.** A regional-bank panic (triggered by the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank) saw roughly \$100 billion of deposits flee First Republic. After the FDIC seized it and ran an auction, JPMorgan won, took on the assets and most liabilities, and booked a multi-billion-dollar bargain-purchase gain. The 2008 playbook, run again fifteen years later.

6. **The early-2020s ESG fight and reversal.** Fink's climate-focused CEO letters made BlackRock the central character in a culture war over corporate purpose. Several US states pulled or threatened to pull pension money; activists on the other side accused it of doing too little. BlackRock subsequently softened its public ESG language, even shelving the acronym in places, and leaned into client *choice* over its votes — a live demonstration of the asset manager's no-win position when it owns a slice of everything.

7. **Aladdin's quiet ubiquity.** No single dramatic day, but a structural fact: through the 2010s and 2020s, Aladdin's reach across pensions, insurers, and rival managers grew until well over \$20 trillion of assets were analyzed through it. When markets convulse, a large share of the world's portfolio managers are looking at the same risk numbers from the same system — a concentration of *perception* that has no historical precedent.

8. **The 2000 Chase-JPMorgan merger that made the modern firm.** The bank Dimon runs is itself the product of consolidation: the 2000 union of Chase Manhattan and J.P. Morgan & Co. fused a giant consumer bank with a blue-blooded investment bank, and the 2004 Bank One deal then brought in Dimon himself. Each merger added a different organ — retail deposits, dealmaking, card lending — until the four-business universal bank we mapped earlier existed under one balance sheet. The firm's very name is a fossil record of the consolidation that built it.

9. **The post-2008 mortgage settlements.** In the years after the crisis, JPMorgan paid out tens of billions of dollars — including a roughly \$13 billion settlement with US authorities in 2013 — over mortgage practices, much of it inherited from Bear Stearns and WaMu, the very firms it had bought. It is the unglamorous footnote to the crisis-acquirer story: buying a failed bank means buying its legal problems too, and even a bargain purchase can carry a long, expensive tail.

## When this matters to you, and further reading

You may never bank at JPMorgan or knowingly buy a BlackRock fund — and yet both of these machines almost certainly touch your money. If you have a retirement account in the US, the odds are very high that some of it sits in a low-cost index fund run by BlackRock (or one of its two great rivals), which means BlackRock is, on your behalf, a part-owner and a voter in hundreds of companies you have never thought about. If you keep cash in a US bank account, you benefit from a system whose stability rests, in part, on a few fortress banks being strong enough to absorb the failures of weaker ones — and you also bear, somewhere in the fees and the risks, the cost of that concentration. The two forms of power are not textbook curiosities; they are the architecture of where your savings actually live.

The practical takeaways are modest but real. First, when you read that a "Wall Street giant" did something, ask the foundational question: *is this a bank or an asset manager?* The answer tells you immediately whose risk is on the line and what lever the firm is actually pulling. Second, understand that in finance, crises do not democratize — they concentrate; the strong buy the weak, and the field gets smaller. Third, recognize that the most consequential financial power is often the quietest: not the bank that lends you money, but the index fund that owns the companies you work for and buy from, and the software that tells the whole industry what to fear.

It is tempting to read all this as a story about two extraordinary men, and they are extraordinary — Dimon for his discipline about capital through two crises, Fink for seeing, decades early, that measuring risk and selling cheap index exposure at vast scale would beat picking stocks. But the deeper lesson is structural and would hold even if neither man had ever been born. The two shapes of financial power — the leveraged balance sheet that owns its own risk, and the fee-charging manager that owns a slice of everyone else's — are features of how modern capitalism allocates money, and *something* would grow to fill each shape. Dimon and Fink are the people who happened to run the machines best and longest. When they eventually step down, the machines, and the two forms of power they embody, will remain. That is the real reason to understand them: not as biographies, but as the two answers to a single question — *what does it look like when financial power gets as large as our system allows?*
