> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The US dollar is the currency the world saves, trades, and borrows in, a status cemented when oil was priced in dollars, and that dominance hands America an "exorbitant privilege" while quietly straining the system that sustains it.
>
> - A reserve currency is the money other countries hold, price their trade in, and flee to in a crisis. The dollar does all of that better than any rival.
> - The system was built in stages: gold-backed dollars under Bretton Woods (1944), the end of gold convertibility (1971), and oil priced in dollars (1973-74), which kept demand for dollars high even after gold was gone.
> - The dollar is roughly 58% of the world's official reserves and about half of all trade invoicing, and most cross-border debt is dollar-denominated. No rival is even half its size.
> - The benefits to America are real: it borrows more cheaply, runs persistent deficits the rest of the world finances, and can weaponize access to dollar payment rails through sanctions.
> - The costs are real too: the "Triffin dilemma" means supplying the world's reserve asset forces America to run trade deficits forever, and weaponizing the dollar gives rivals a reason to look for exits.
> - De-dollarization is talked about constantly and happens very slowly, because no other money offers the dollar's combination of deep markets, free convertibility, trusted courts, and crisis liquidity.

Here is a fact that sounds made up but is not: when a refinery in India buys a cargo of crude oil from a producer in the Middle East, the price is quoted in US dollars, the invoice is settled in US dollars, and neither party is American. Strip out the United States entirely and the deal still runs on America's money. Multiply that by every barrel of oil traded on Earth, then add most of the world's commodities, a huge share of its trade in everything else, and the bulk of its cross-border lending, and you arrive at the strangest feature of the modern financial system: the planet keeps its accounts, and stores its savings, in the currency of one country. The diagram above is the mental model: a reserve currency is money that does four jobs at once, and the dollar is the only money that does all four at global scale.

![Tree of the four jobs a reserve currency does, all centered on the US dollar](/imgs/blogs/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance-1.png)

This post explains how that happened and why it persists. We will build everything from zero: what a reserve currency even is, how the dollar inherited the role from gold, what the "petrodollar" actually means (it is more interesting and less conspiratorial than the internet suggests), how oil exporters' surpluses get recycled back into America, what privileges this hands the United States, what it costs America in return, and why the steady talk of "de-dollarization" keeps running into the same brick wall. By the end you should be able to trace a single barrel of oil into a US Treasury bond and back, and reason clearly about whether the dollar's reign is ending. This is educational, not financial advice; nothing here is a recommendation to buy, sell, or hold any currency or asset.

## Foundations: what a reserve currency is, and the words this rests on

Start with the thing everyone gets vaguely and almost no one gets precisely: what is a **reserve currency**?

A reserve currency is the money that governments and central banks around the world choose to *hold* as their savings, that exporters and importers choose to *price and settle* their trade in, and that investors *flee to* when they are frightened. It is not declared by treaty or assigned by a committee. It emerges because millions of separate actors, each acting in their own interest, keep choosing the same currency for the same reasons. Once enough of them choose it, choosing it becomes the obviously correct decision for everyone else too. That self-reinforcing quality is the single most important thing to understand about dollar dominance, and we will return to it repeatedly.

Before we go further, here are the terms this whole post leans on. I will define each precisely once, here, and use them freely after.

- **Currency** is a particular country's money. The dollar, the euro, the yen, the yuan (China's currency, also called the renminbi), the British pound. Each is issued by a central bank.
- A **central bank** is the institution that issues a country's currency and manages its money supply. The Federal Reserve ("the Fed") issues dollars; the European Central Bank issues euros. A central bank can never run out of its own currency because it creates it.
- **Foreign-exchange reserves** (often "FX reserves" or just "reserves") are the foreign money a country's central bank keeps on hand. China, for example, holds trillions of dollars' worth of reserves, most of it in dollars. Reserves are a country's rainy-day fund and its tool for managing its own exchange rate.
- The **exchange rate** is the price of one currency in another, for example how many Indian rupees it takes to buy one dollar. It can be **fixed** (the government pins it at a set level) or **floating** (the market sets it, and it moves constantly).
- A **Treasury** (or "Treasury bond," "T-bill," "Treasuries" in the plural) is an IOU issued by the US government. You lend the government money, it pays you interest and returns your principal. Treasuries are widely treated as the safest, most liquid financial asset on Earth, which matters enormously for everything below.
- **Liquidity** is how easily you can turn something into spendable money without moving its price. Cash is perfectly liquid. A house is illiquid. The dollar and the Treasury market are the most liquid things in finance, and that is much of their power.
- A **trade deficit** is what a country has when it imports more than it exports, so it sends more money abroad for goods than it takes in. A **trade surplus** is the reverse. Hold this; the deficit is central to the whole story.

A reserve currency does four jobs, the four branches in the figure above. It is a **unit of account**: the world quotes prices in it, most famously the price of oil. It is a **medium of exchange**: parties settle trade in it even when neither is from the issuing country. It is a **store of value**: central banks park their savings in it, overwhelmingly by buying Treasuries. And it is a **safe haven**: when a crisis hits, capital sprints toward it, which we will see vividly in 2008 and 2020. The dollar is the only money that does all four at once, for the whole planet. The euro does some of them regionally; gold does the store-of-value job but none of the others well; the yuan is blocked from most of them by China's own rules. That is the asymmetry the rest of the post unpacks.

There is one more foundational idea, and it is the one most people miss. The dollar's strength is not really backed by gold (not since 1971) or by oil. It is backed by the **full faith and credit of the United States**, by the depth and openness of American financial markets, and by trust in American courts and contracts. When you hold a dollar, you are holding a claim on the most liquid, most legally reliable financial system in the world. That is an abstract kind of backing, and the next section shows how the dollar got there.

It is worth dwelling on why all four jobs reinforce one another, because the interlock is the secret of the whole arrangement. If oil is priced in dollars (unit of account), then every importer must hold dollars (medium of exchange), so central banks keep dollars on hand to make sure their economies can always pay for energy and imports (store of value), and when a crisis erupts the dollars they already hold are the obvious thing to clutch (safe haven). Each job makes the next one cheaper and more sensible. A rival currency cannot win one job at a time; it would have to win several at once, against an incumbent that gets stronger every time anyone uses it. Economists call this a **network effect**, the property that a thing becomes more useful the more people already use it, the same dynamic that makes a single dominant language, or a single dominant social network, so hard to displace. The dollar is, in a precise sense, the world's financial common language, and you cannot get half the planet to switch languages on a Tuesday.

A final piece of vocabulary you will need: the **plumbing**. When a bank in Brazil pays a bank in Japan in dollars, the dollars do not physically travel; they move as messages and ledger entries through a small set of systems. **SWIFT** is the messaging network banks use to instruct each other to move money. **CHIPS** and **Fedwire** are the US systems where large dollar payments actually settle. Almost every cross-border dollar payment touches American or American-supervised infrastructure at some point, which is why the United States can police who is allowed to use the dollar. That plumbing is invisible until it is switched off, and switching it off is what "sanctions" ultimately means.

## How the world used to work: gold, and the system before the dollar

For most of recorded history, "international money" meant gold (and silver). Gold has the rare virtue of being scarce, durable, and universally wanted, so a merchant in Venice and a merchant in Canton could both trust a gold coin without trusting each other. Under a **gold standard**, a country promises that its paper money can be exchanged for a fixed amount of gold on demand. The British pound dominated nineteenth-century world trade precisely because Britain credibly stood ready to swap pounds for gold, and because London was the deepest financial market on Earth.

The gold standard has one enormous virtue and one fatal flaw. The virtue is discipline: a government cannot print more money than it has gold to back, so inflation is held in check almost mechanically. The flaw is that the same discipline becomes a straitjacket in a crisis. When an economy needs more money, for example to fight a depression or finance a war, the gold supply cannot keep up, and the government must either break its promise or strangle its own economy. The interwar period (the 1920s and 1930s) was a long, painful demonstration of the gold standard cracking under exactly this pressure, and it is one reason the Great Depression was so deep. By 1944, with the Second World War ending, the world's governments wanted something better: the discipline of gold without quite so rigid a straitjacket, and a single anchor everyone could trust.

The figure below contrasts that older gold-anchored world with the one we live in now. Keep it in mind as we walk through the two systems, because the *transition* between them is where the dollar's dominance was forged.

## Bretton Woods: the dollar inherits the throne (1944)

In July 1944, delegates from 44 Allied nations gathered at a hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to design the postwar financial order. The United States arrived holding most of the cards, literally: by the end of the war America held a majority of the world's monetary gold and was the only major economy not flattened by the fighting. The system they built, named after the town, made the dollar the hub of the world.

The design was elegant. The **dollar** was pegged to **gold** at a fixed price of \$35 per ounce, and the United States promised that foreign governments could redeem dollars for gold at that rate. Every *other* currency was then pegged to the **dollar** at a fixed exchange rate. So the dollar sat in the middle: gold backed the dollar, and the dollar backed everyone else. This is the left side of the figure above. In effect the world ran on a gold standard *operated through the dollar*. Two new institutions were created to police and support the system, the International Monetary Fund (to lend to countries with balance-of-payments trouble) and the World Bank (to fund reconstruction and development).

For a quarter-century it worked. World trade boomed, exchange rates were stable, and the dollar became the natural currency for international transactions because it was as good as gold and far more convenient to use. Central banks began holding dollars instead of gold in their reserves, since a dollar earned interest and gold sat inert in a vault. The dollar's reserve role was born here, under the umbrella of gold.

But the seeds of the system's collapse were planted at the same moment, and a Belgian-American economist named Robert Triffin spotted them early.

#### Worked example: the Triffin dilemma in numbers

Robert Triffin pointed out, in the early 1960s, a contradiction at the heart of Bretton Woods that now bears his name, the **Triffin dilemma**. Let us make it concrete with round numbers.

The world economy is growing, and as it grows it needs more dollars to conduct trade and to hold as reserves. Suppose the rest of the world wants to accumulate an extra \$10 billion of dollar reserves this year. Where do those dollars come from? They can only come from the United States *spending more abroad than it earns*, that is, from America running a balance-of-payments deficit of \$10 billion. The act of supplying the world's reserve currency *requires* the issuing country to send more money out than it takes in.

Now run that forward. Year after year, to feed a growing world's appetite for dollars, the US must run persistent deficits, which steadily increases the stock of dollars held abroad. By the late 1960s, foreign-held dollars far exceeded the US gold stock backing them at \$35 an ounce. So foreigners faced a creeping doubt: if everyone tried to redeem their dollars for gold at once, America could not possibly deliver, because the dollars outstanding (say \$40 billion held abroad) dwarfed the gold available to redeem them (worth perhaps \$10 billion at the official price). The dilemma is a trap: supplying enough dollars to run the world *guarantees* that, eventually, there are too many dollars relative to the gold promised behind them.

The one-sentence intuition: a single country cannot simultaneously supply the world's money and keep that money fully backed by a fixed pile of gold; growth forces the two goals apart.

## The Nixon shock: gold convertibility ends (1971)

By the late 1960s the Triffin trap was springing. France, under President de Gaulle, began aggressively converting its dollars to American gold, literally shipping gold out of the United States, on the logic that it would rather hold the metal than the increasingly suspect paper. Other countries eyed the exits. America's gold reserves were draining fast, and the math was undeniable: there were far more dollars in the world than there was gold to redeem them.

On August 15, 1971, President Richard Nixon went on television and, in what came to be called the **Nixon shock**, announced that the United States would "temporarily" suspend the convertibility of dollars into gold. The suspension was never reversed. The gold window slammed shut for good. With one announcement, the dollar stopped being a redeemable claim on metal and became what economists call **fiat money**: money that is valuable because a government declares it legal tender and because people accept it, not because it is backed by any commodity.

This is the right side of the before-after figure above. After 1971, exchange rates gradually shifted from fixed to mostly **floating** (set by markets), the dollar's value was no longer pinned to anything physical, and, crucially, the dollar's role as the world's reserve currency *did not collapse*. That is the surprising part. Conventional logic said that a currency unmoored from gold should lose its special status. Instead the dollar kept it. The question is why, and the answer has a lot to do with oil.

## The petrodollar: what it actually means

Here we need to be careful, because the "petrodollar" is one of the most mythologized concepts in finance. The internet is full of claims that a secret deal in the 1970s "forced" the world to use dollars for oil, and that this single arrangement is the sole pillar holding up the dollar. The reality is more grounded and, honestly, more interesting.

The plain meaning of **petrodollar** is simply this: dollars earned by oil-exporting countries from selling oil that is priced and paid for in dollars. There is no exotic instrument involved; a petrodollar is just an ordinary dollar that happens to have arrived in a Saudi or Kuwaiti or Nigerian bank account in exchange for crude oil. The term was coined in the 1970s when, after the oil price quadrupled, exporters were suddenly swimming in dollars and the world had to figure out where all that money would go.

Why is oil priced in dollars at all? Partly inertia and convenience. After the war the dollar was already the dominant trade currency, and oil is a global commodity that benefits enormously from a *single* pricing standard, just as it helps if everyone measures it in the same barrels. If every cargo were priced in a different currency, every buyer and seller would face constant exchange-rate guesswork on the world's most important commodity. A single unit of account removes that friction. And there were diplomatic arrangements too: in the mid-1970s the United States and Saudi Arabia reached understandings under which the Saudis would continue selling oil for dollars and invest their surplus dollars in US assets, in exchange for American security guarantees and access to American markets and weapons. These deals were real, but they ratified a pattern that was already the path of least resistance rather than inventing dollar dominance from scratch.

There is also a reason the *exporters themselves* preferred dollars, which the conspiracy framing ignores. An oil producer earning a windfall wants to store that windfall in something safe, liquid, and useful, and in the 1970s the only market deep enough to absorb tens of billions of dollars of surplus *was* the US market. A Gulf state could not park \$50 billion in, say, the German or Japanese bond markets without distorting them; only US Treasuries were vast enough to swallow that much money without choking. So pricing oil in dollars and then investing the proceeds in dollars was not a favor to America so much as the only practical option for an exporter that needed somewhere enormous and reliable to put its money. The dollar's pre-existing market depth, the very thing the Bretton Woods era had built, made it the natural home for oil wealth, which in turn deepened the dollar's markets further. The reinforcing loop again.

It also helps to understand who **OPEC** is, since the organization sits at the center of this story. OPEC, the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries, is a cartel of major oil-producing nations, founded in 1960, that coordinates production to influence the world oil price. When OPEC restricts supply, prices rise; when it opens the taps, they fall. In the 1970s OPEC discovered it could move the oil price dramatically, and because the price was set in dollars, every swing in OPEC policy translated directly into a swing in the flow of dollars to exporters, which is precisely what created the recycling problem we turn to next.

The pipeline below shows the mechanism that matters most, and it is mechanical, not conspiratorial. Because oil is invoiced in dollars, *every* importer must first obtain dollars before it can buy oil. A country that does not earn dollars must go to the foreign-exchange market, sell its own currency, and buy dollars, just to fill up. Global energy demand thereby manufactures standing, structural demand for dollars.

![Pipeline showing how an oil purchase forces a buyer to acquire dollars first](/imgs/blogs/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance-4.png)

#### Worked example: trade priced in dollars versus local currency

Let us see exactly why dollar pricing creates dollar demand, by comparing two worlds.

In the first world, oil is priced in dollars (the real world). India wants to import 1 million barrels of crude at \$80 per barrel, a bill of \$80 million. India's central bank does not magically have \$80 million lying around; rupees are its currency. So an Indian refiner sells rupees on the FX market and buys \$80 million, then pays the seller. To keep oil flowing, India must keep a stock of dollars on hand and keep buying more. Across all oil-importing nations, this adds up to a permanent, enormous demand for dollars that has nothing to do with anyone wanting to invest in America; they simply need dollars to keep the lights on.

In the second world, imagine oil were instead priced in each buyer's home currency. India would pay 6.6 billion rupees (at, say, 83 rupees per dollar) directly, no dollar purchase required. The Saudi seller would receive a pile of rupees it cannot easily use, since it does not buy much from India, and would then have to sell those rupees for something useful. Now the *exchange-rate risk and the inconvenience* sit with the seller, and on the world's largest commodity the seller has the market power. Sellers therefore much prefer to be paid in a single, universally useful currency, and the dollar is the obvious choice.

Run the arithmetic across the whole oil market. Global oil consumption is roughly 100 million barrels per day; at \$80 per barrel that is about \$8 billion of oil changing hands *every day*, around \$2.9 trillion a year, nearly all of it requiring dollars to settle. The one-sentence intuition: pricing the world's most-traded commodity in dollars turns the daily, unavoidable act of buying energy into a daily, unavoidable demand for dollars.

## Petrodollar recycling: how the surpluses come back

So oil exporters end up with mountains of dollars. A country like Saudi Arabia earns far more from oil than it can possibly spend at home; the surplus piles up. What does it do with all those dollars? It cannot just leave them idle, earning nothing. So it invests them, and the overwhelmingly favored destination is the United States, above all US Treasuries. This round-trip, dollars flowing out to buy oil and flowing back to buy American assets, is called **petrodollar recycling**, and it is the engine that connects oil to America's borrowing.

The graph below traces the loop. Exporters bank their surplus dollars, then channel them into Treasuries (safe, liquid, interest-bearing) and other US assets like stocks and real estate, often through **sovereign wealth funds** (state-owned investment funds that manage a country's surplus wealth). That flow lends money to the US government and corporations, which lets America finance its deficits at lower interest rates than it otherwise could.

![Graph of petrodollar recycling, with exporter surpluses flowing into Treasuries and back to cheaper US borrowing](/imgs/blogs/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance-5.png)

#### Worked example: recycling a \$100 billion oil surplus into Treasuries

Make the loop concrete. Suppose in a given year an oil exporter, call it a stand-in Gulf state, runs a \$100 billion surplus: it earns \$100 billion more from oil than it spends on imports and domestic needs. That \$100 billion sits in dollar bank accounts. Leaving it as cash earns essentially nothing and slowly loses value to inflation, so the country's central bank or sovereign wealth fund puts it to work.

It buys, say, \$70 billion of US Treasuries yielding about 3% a year, \$20 billion of US and global equities, and keeps \$10 billion in cash and short-term instruments for flexibility. The \$70 billion of Treasuries earns roughly \$2.1 billion a year in interest (70 billion x 0.03), a steady, safe return paid by the US government. From America's side, that \$70 billion is \$70 billion the US Treasury was able to borrow from a willing foreign buyer, money that finances the federal deficit without the government having to offer higher interest rates to attract domestic lenders.

Now scale it. Across all oil exporters, plus the even larger surpluses of manufacturing exporters like China and Japan (who recycle their *trade* surpluses the same way), foreign official holdings of US Treasuries run into the trillions of dollars, roughly \$7-8 trillion held by foreign governments and central banks as of the mid-2020s (approximate, as of then). That is trillions of dollars of demand for US debt that exists largely *because* the dollar is the reserve currency. The one-sentence intuition: the world's surplus savings, earned in dollars, flow back to America and quietly finance its government, which is the deepest mechanical reason American borrowing stays cheap.

## The exorbitant privilege: what dominance buys America

In the 1960s a French finance minister, Valery Giscard d'Estaing, complained that the dollar's reserve status gave the United States an "**exorbitant privilege**," and the phrase stuck because it is exactly right. Issuing the world's reserve currency is like being the only bank in town that everyone keeps their savings in: you get to lend out other people's money, charge less for the privilege, and never quite run out of customers.

The privilege has several concrete pieces. First and most important, **America borrows more cheaply** than it otherwise could, because the constant global demand for dollars and Treasuries means there is always an eager buyer for US debt, which pushes interest rates down. Second, the US can run **persistent trade deficits** without the usual punishment. Normally a country that imports far more than it exports must eventually face a currency collapse as it runs out of foreign money to pay for imports; America largely escapes this because it pays for imports in its *own* currency, which it can always supply, and the world is happy to hold the dollars it sends out. Third, the dollar's centrality gives the US enormous **sanctions power**, because so much of world finance passes through dollar payment systems that Washington controls, a lever we will examine shortly. Fourth, in a crisis, capital floods *into* dollars, so American borrowing costs often *fall* exactly when a panic would raise everyone else's.

#### Worked example: pricing the exorbitant privilege on America's debt

How much is "borrowing more cheaply" actually worth? Let us estimate it. The US federal government's debt held by the public is on the order of \$28-30 trillion (approximate, as of the mid-2020s). Suppose, conservatively, that the dollar's reserve status lowers the interest rate the US pays by just 1 percentage point (100 basis points) compared to a world where it had to borrow like an ordinary large economy. Economists' estimates of the "convenience yield" on safe dollar assets cluster in roughly this range, sometimes higher.

Take \$30 trillion of debt and a 1-percentage-point discount: the annual saving is \$30 trillion x 0.01 = \$300 billion per year. That is \$300 billion the US government does *not* have to raise in taxes or cut from spending, every single year, purely because the world wants to hold its debt. For scale, \$300 billion is larger than the entire annual budget of most countries on Earth, and comparable to a big chunk of the US defense budget. Even if you think the true discount is half a point rather than a full point, you are still talking about \$150 billion a year.

The one-sentence intuition: the exorbitant privilege is not a metaphor, it is worth hundreds of billions of dollars a year to the United States in interest it never has to pay.

#### Worked example: what a \$1 trillion reserve hoard earns and risks

Look at the same coin from the other side, the country *holding* the reserves. Suppose a large exporter holds \$1 trillion of foreign-exchange reserves, mostly in US Treasuries (this is roughly the order of magnitude for several big economies; China holds the most, over \$3 trillion). At a 3% Treasury yield, that hoard earns \$30 billion a year in interest (1 trillion x 0.03), a tidy return on what is essentially the nation's rainy-day fund.

But notice what the holder gives up and risks. By parking \$1 trillion in low-yielding US debt rather than investing it at home in roads, factories, or schools, the country forgoes potentially higher domestic returns; this is the real cost of "self-insurance." And it takes on two risks. The first is currency risk: if the dollar falls 10% against its home currency, the reserve hoard loses \$100 billion of value measured at home, wiping out three years of interest in one move. The second, which used to feel theoretical and no longer does, is **political risk**: the assets are ultimately claims on the US financial system, and if relations sour, that system's gatekeepers can freeze them. We will see exactly this happen to Russia in 2022.

The one-sentence intuition: holding the world's reserve asset is convenient and pays interest, but it quietly subsidizes the issuer and leaves your savings exposed to its currency swings and its courts.

## The size of the dominance: the numbers that matter

It is worth pausing on just how lopsided the dollar's position is, because abstract talk of "dominance" understates it. The stack below shows the world's official foreign-exchange reserves broken down by currency. The dollar is roughly 58% of the total (approximate, as of the mid-2020s); the euro, a distant second, is around 20%; everything else, the yen, the pound, the Canadian and Australian dollars, the Swiss franc, splits the rest; and the Chinese yuan, despite China being the world's second-largest economy and largest trading nation, is only around 2%.

![Stack of global FX reserves by currency share, dollar near 58 percent and yuan near 2 percent](/imgs/blogs/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance-6.png)

Reserves are only one measure. By others the dollar looks even more entrenched. Roughly half of all international trade is invoiced in dollars, far more than America's roughly 10-12% share of world imports, meaning huge volumes of trade between *non-US* countries are still priced in dollars. Around 90% of all foreign-exchange trades have the dollar on one side. The majority of international bank loans and debt securities are denominated in dollars, which means companies and governments worldwide *owe* in dollars and must earn or buy dollars to repay. And the dollar is on one side of the overwhelming majority of cross-border payments routed through the global messaging system that banks use.

Add these up and you see why "the world runs on the dollar" is not hyperbole. The dollar is the unit of account for the most important commodity, the medium of exchange for half of trade, the store of value for most reserves, and the safe haven in every panic, the four jobs from the very first figure, all held by one currency at once.

#### Worked example: the savings every dollar trade route quietly hands America

Tie the reserve and trade numbers together. Global FX reserves total roughly \$12 trillion (approximate, as of the mid-2020s). If the dollar is 58% of that, foreign authorities hold about \$7 trillion in dollar reserves (12 trillion x 0.58 = 6.96 trillion). A large share of that \$7 trillion sits in US Treasuries and other US government-backed debt, which is \$7 trillion of demand for American IOUs that exists *because* these are reserve assets, not because anyone made an ordinary investment decision.

That demand is what drives the borrowing-cost discount we priced earlier. It also means foreign central banks are, collectively, one of the largest lenders to the US government, larger than many domestic institutions. When people ask "what would happen if the world stopped using the dollar," this is the number under the question: trillions of dollars of cheap, reliable financing for the United States that would have to be replaced from somewhere. The one-sentence intuition: dollar dominance is not just prestige, it is a multi-trillion-dollar standing line of credit to America from the rest of the world.

## The costs: why dominance is not a free lunch

If you have read this far thinking the United States holds all the cards, the picture needs balancing, because the exorbitant privilege comes with an exorbitant burden, and serious economists argue the costs are larger than commonly admitted.

The first cost is the flip side of the Triffin dilemma we met earlier. To supply the world with dollars and dollar assets, the US must run **persistent trade deficits**, sending more money abroad than it takes in, year after year, decade after decade. The world's hunger for dollar reserves can only be fed by America buying more from abroad than it sells. Many economists argue this contributes to the long hollowing-out of American manufacturing: a permanently strong, in-demand dollar makes US exports more expensive and imports cheaper, tilting the economy away from making things and toward consuming and financing them. The reserve currency, on this view, is a kind of curse dressed as a blessing, the trade deficit is not a sign of weakness so much as the *price of admission* to issuing the world's money.

The second cost is fragility and dependence. Because so much of the world borrows in dollars, a sudden rise in the dollar's value or in US interest rates tightens financial conditions everywhere on Earth, even in countries with no direct link to America. When the Fed raises rates to fight US inflation, it can trigger debt crises in emerging markets that borrowed in dollars and now must repay in a currency that just got more expensive. America exports its monetary policy whether it intends to or not, which breeds resentment and gives other countries a motive to reduce their dollar exposure.

The third cost is the one that has grown sharpest recently: the more aggressively the US uses the dollar as a weapon, the more it teaches the rest of the world to seek alternatives. Every sanction that freezes a country's dollar assets is also an advertisement to every other country that its own dollar assets could be frozen next. We turn to that tension now, because it sits at the heart of the de-dollarization debate.

## De-dollarization: the debate, and why it is so hard

"De-dollarization" is the idea that the world is, or should be, moving away from dependence on the dollar. It is one of the most discussed topics in international finance, and the discussion generates far more heat than the data justifies, at least so far. Let us separate the genuine pressures from the wishful thinking.

The genuine pressures are real, and it is worth taking each contender seriously in turn rather than waving them all away.

The **Chinese yuan** is the most discussed challenger. China is the world's largest trading nation, so on the surface it seems a natural candidate to internationalize its currency. Beijing has promoted yuan invoicing for trade, built its own cross-border payment system (CIPS) as an alternative to the dollar plumbing, signed currency-swap lines with dozens of central banks, and struck deals to pay for some oil and commodities in yuan. But China faces a contradiction it has chosen not to resolve: a true global reserve currency must be **freely convertible**, meaning anyone, anywhere, can move it in and out without permission. China maintains **capital controls** precisely to keep tight control over its economy and exchange rate, and abandoning them would mean surrendering control it values above reserve-currency status. As long as that trade-off stands, the yuan can grow at the margins but cannot become the world's primary store of value.

**Gold** is the oldest alternative and, in a sense, the most credible store-of-value rival, which is why central banks (China and Russia prominent among them) have been buying it heavily, especially since 2022. Gold's great virtue is that it is *no one's liability*: no foreign government issues it, so no foreign government can freeze it, the exact property that made it attractive after Russia's reserves were seized. But gold cannot do the other three jobs. You cannot price global trade in it conveniently, you cannot settle a wire transfer in it, and the gold market, while large, cannot absorb the trillions that flow through reserves without violent price swings. Gold is a hedge *against* the dollar system, not a replacement *for* it.

**Digital currencies** are the newest wildcard. Central-bank digital currencies (state-issued digital money) and private **stablecoins** (privately issued tokens pegged to a currency, usually the dollar) both promise faster, cheaper cross-border payments that might route around the existing plumbing. The irony is sharp: the overwhelming majority of stablecoins are pegged to, and backed by, *dollars and Treasuries*, so the booming stablecoin market is currently *reinforcing* dollar demand rather than undermining it. A digital yuan or a digital settlement system could in principle reduce reliance on US-controlled rails, but it would still need a currency people *want* to hold, which loops back to the same convertibility and trust problems.

The **BRICS** bloc (originally Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, since expanded) ties these threads together rhetorically, regularly discussing trade in members' own currencies and floating, mostly as a slogan, the idea of a shared currency. The genuine progress, more bilateral trade settled in local currencies, is real but incremental.

Across all of this, the dollar's share of reserves *has* drifted down, from around 70% two decades ago to around 58% today (approximate, as of the mid-2020s). The drift is real diversification, not the arrival of a successor.

But here is why dethroning the dollar is so hard, and the matrix below is the clearest way to see it. A reserve currency has to pass several tests *at the same time*, and every rival fails at least one test that a reserve asset simply cannot fail.

![Matrix scoring the dollar, euro, yuan, and gold against four reserve-currency tests](/imgs/blogs/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance-7.png)

Walk down the matrix. A reserve currency needs a **deep, liquid market** to park trillions in, and only the US Treasury market, at roughly \$27 trillion, is deep enough; the euro is fragmented across many national bond markets, the yuan's bond market is comparatively shallow and restricted, and gold, while valuable, cannot absorb trillions without wild price swings. It needs to be **freely usable**, convertible without permission, and the yuan fails this outright because China maintains **capital controls** (legal restrictions on moving money across its borders) to manage its economy, which is fundamentally incompatible with being the world's open reserve currency. It needs **trusted, neutral rule of law**, courts and contracts that a foreign holder believes will be honored regardless of politics, where the US and Europe score well and China's state-controlled system does not. And it needs **crisis liquidity**, the ability to be deployed instantly when a panic hits, where the dollar's safe-haven status is unmatched and gold is slow and clumsy to mobilize.

The deepest reason, though, is the self-reinforcing dynamic from the foundations section. The dollar is dominant *because* it is dominant. Everyone uses it because everyone else uses it, so the cost of being the first to switch is enormous and the benefit is small. A country that prices its oil in yuan must find buyers willing to hold yuan, which are scarce precisely because China restricts the currency. **Network effects** like this, where a thing becomes more valuable the more people use it, create lock-in that is extraordinarily hard to break. Currencies have lost reserve status before, the pound did, over decades, but it usually takes a generational shift in economic and military power plus a credible, open rival ready to take over. No such rival exists today.

#### Worked example: why an oil seller is reluctant to take yuan

Make the switching problem concrete from the seller's chair. Suppose a Gulf exporter agrees to sell \$10 billion of oil to China priced in yuan, receiving roughly 72 billion yuan (at about 7.2 yuan per dollar). Now the exporter is holding 72 billion yuan and faces three uncomfortable questions. First, what can it *buy* with yuan? It imports far less from China than \$10 billion, so most of those yuan have no natural use. Second, where does it *invest* the surplus? China's bond market is restricted and shallow compared to the \$27 trillion Treasury market, and capital controls mean the exporter may not freely move the money back out. Third, what is the *currency risk*? If the yuan falls 5% against the dollar, the exporter's hoard loses about \$500 million of value measured in the dollars it actually needs to pay its own bills.

To escape these problems the exporter would have to sell most of the yuan back for dollars anyway, paying transaction costs and exchange-rate risk for the privilege of a round trip it did not want. Compare the dollar version: receive \$10 billion, park it in Treasuries earning interest, spend it anywhere on Earth, with no permission required. The one-sentence intuition: a seller will only abandon the dollar for a currency that is itself freely usable and backed by a deep market, which is exactly the test no challenger yet passes.

#### Worked example: the cost of a reserve freeze, and the dilemma it creates

The single event most cited as accelerating de-dollarization is the freezing of Russia's reserves in 2022, so let us put numbers on it. When Russia invaded Ukraine, the US and its allies froze roughly \$300 billion of Russia's foreign-exchange reserves, the portion held in dollars, euros, and other Western currencies and parked in Western financial institutions. In an instant, \$300 billion of what Russia thought was its safe, liquid rainy-day fund became inaccessible, money it owned on paper but could not touch.

For Russia the cost was direct: it lost the use of about half its total reserves at the worst possible moment. But the *systemic* cost lands on the dollar itself, and it is subtle. Every other large reserve-holder, China above all with over \$3 trillion, watched and drew a lesson: reserves held in dollars are safe *only as long as you stay on Washington's good side*. That realization is exactly why central-bank gold buying jumped and why de-dollarization talk intensified after 2022. Yet here is the dilemma the US faces, in one line of arithmetic: the sanctions power that comes from dollar dominance is most potent when used, but each use chips away at the trust that *makes* the dollar dominant. Freezing \$300 billion was a display of overwhelming financial power and, simultaneously, an advertisement for the dollar's competitors.

The one-sentence intuition: the dollar's greatest weapon and its greatest vulnerability are the same thing, because every freeze teaches the world that dollar reserves come with a political condition attached.

For more on how that payment-rail weapon actually works, see the companion piece on [SWIFT and the weaponization of payments](/blog/trading/finance/swift-and-the-weaponization-of-payments).

## Common misconceptions

The petrodollar and dollar dominance attract more myths than almost any topic in finance. Here are the ones worth dismantling.

**Misconception 1: "A secret 1970s deal forces the world to buy oil only in dollars, and that single deal is the only thing propping up the dollar."** This is the big one, and it is wrong in both halves. The diplomatic arrangements of the mid-1970s were real, but they ratified a pattern the dollar already had rather than creating dominance from nothing. And oil pricing is one pillar among several, alongside the depth of US markets, the safety of Treasuries, the rule of law, and network effects. Oil can be, and increasingly is, priced or settled in other currencies at the margins without the dollar collapsing, precisely because its strength rests on a much broader base. The petrodollar is a *consequence* and a *reinforcer* of dollar dominance, not its sole cause.

**Misconception 2: "The dollar is backed by oil (or by gold, or by the US military)."** The dollar has not been backed by gold since 1971, and it has never been backed by oil in any legal sense. It is fiat money, backed by the full faith and credit of the United States, the depth and openness of American financial markets, and trust in American institutions. Oil pricing creates *demand* for dollars, which is different from *backing* them. Military power matters indirectly, by underpinning trust in American stability, but no one redeems a dollar for a barrel of oil or a tank.

**Misconception 3: "If oil stopped being priced in dollars, the dollar would immediately collapse."** Demand for dollars from oil trade is large but is dwarfed by demand from reserves, cross-border lending, financial transactions, and safe-haven flows. Several oil deals already settle in other currencies. A gradual shift in oil invoicing would chip at dollar demand at the margin, not trigger a collapse, because the currency's dominance rests far more on the depth of its capital markets than on energy invoicing.

**Misconception 4: "BRICS is about to launch a currency that replaces the dollar."** This is mostly rhetoric. A shared currency among countries as economically and politically diverse as the BRICS members, with no common central bank, no fiscal union, and deep mutual distrust (India and China, for instance, are rivals), faces obstacles that took Europe decades to overcome for the euro, and the euro still has not displaced the dollar. Increased trade in members' *own* currencies is happening and is meaningful, but it is incremental, not a dollar-killer.

**Misconception 5: "America can print unlimited dollars with no consequences because it has the reserve currency."** The reserve role gives the US extra room to borrow and print before inflation bites, more room than any other country, but it is not infinite. Print far too many dollars and you get inflation at home and erode the very trust that underpins the reserve status. The 2021-2023 inflation surge was a reminder that the privilege has limits.

**Misconception 6: "The dollar's declining reserve share means its dominance is ending soon."** The share has indeed drifted from around 70% to around 58% over two decades, but much of that drift went into *smaller* currencies (the Australian and Canadian dollars, the Swiss franc, the Chinese yuan) used for diversification, not into a single rival capable of taking the throne. A slow diversification away from over-concentration is very different from a regime change. Dethroning requires a successor, and the matrix above shows none qualifies yet.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstract dominance becomes concrete in specific episodes. Here are the ones that illuminate the mechanics.

**The 1973-74 petrodollar deals.** In October 1973, in response to Western support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War, Arab members of OPEC imposed an oil embargo and engineered a price spike that roughly quadrupled crude prices, from around \$3 to around \$12 a barrel. Oil exporters were suddenly drowning in dollars. The recycling problem, where to put all that money, became urgent, and the US-Saudi understandings of 1974 channeled much of it into US Treasuries and American markets. This is the moment the modern petrodollar system crystallized: high oil prices created huge dollar surpluses, and those surpluses flowed back into America, financing its deficits and cementing the recycling loop diagrammed above.

**The 1985 Plaza Accord.** By the mid-1980s the dollar had become *too* strong, hammering American exporters and stoking protectionist anger in Congress. In September 1985, finance ministers from the US, Japan, West Germany, France, and Britain met at the Plaza Hotel in New York and agreed to coordinate intervention to push the dollar *down*. It worked, the dollar fell sharply over the following two years. The Plaza Accord is the clearest demonstration that dollar dominance is not always an unalloyed gift; sometimes the issuer wants its own currency *weaker* and must coordinate with rivals to engineer it, because a too-strong reserve currency hollows out the issuer's exporters, exactly the Triffin-flavored cost we discussed.

**The 2008 safe-haven surge.** The 2008 global financial crisis originated *in* the United States, in American mortgage securities, and yet, paradoxically, capital fled *toward* the dollar as the crisis worsened. Investors worldwide dumped riskier assets and bought US Treasuries, the one thing they trusted, driving the dollar up and Treasury yields down even as America was the epicenter of the panic. This is the safe-haven job from the first figure, and it revealed a remarkable feature of dominance: in a true crisis the dollar strengthens *regardless of where the crisis starts*, because there is nowhere else big and safe enough to hide. The Fed had to lend dollars to foreign central banks (via "swap lines") to relieve a global dollar shortage, which only underscored how dependent the world is on dollar plumbing.

**The 2020 pandemic dollar spike.** When COVID-19 hit in March 2020, the same pattern repeated even more violently. A global "dash for cash" sent the dollar soaring as institutions everywhere scrambled for the world's most liquid asset, and a dollar funding shortage threatened to seize up global finance. The Fed responded by massively expanding its dollar swap lines to other central banks, effectively acting as the world's lender of last resort *in dollars*. The episode showed both the dollar's unmatched safe-haven pull and the uncomfortable fact that the stability of the entire global system depends on the Fed's willingness to supply dollars in a crisis. For how the Fed actually pulls these levers, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).

**Russia's 2022 reserve freeze.** As covered in the worked example, the freezing of roughly \$300 billion of Russian reserves after the 2022 invasion of Ukraine was a watershed. It demonstrated the raw power of dollar (and broader Western-currency) dominance, the ability to render an adversary's savings inert, and simultaneously gave every non-aligned country a vivid reason to diversify. The surge in central-bank gold buying that followed, and the intensified de-dollarization rhetoric from China and others, trace directly to this event. It is the single clearest illustration that the dollar's strength and its strategic vulnerability are two faces of one coin.

**The dollar's share slowly drifting.** Less dramatic but more telling than any single event is the long, slow data trend: the dollar's share of global reserves has eased from around 70% around the year 2000 to around 58% by the mid-2020s. Crucially, the slack was taken up mostly by *diversification into smaller currencies and gold*, not by a single challenger. This slow drift is the real story of de-dollarization, not a collapse, not a coup, but a gradual loosening of an over-concentrated grip, which still leaves the dollar overwhelmingly dominant and without a credible successor.

## When this matters to you / further reading

You may never trade a barrel of oil or manage a central bank's reserves, but dollar dominance reaches into ordinary life in ways worth recognizing.

If you live *outside* the United States, the dollar's dominance is why your country's central bank watches the Fed so closely, why a strong dollar can make your imports (and your country's dollar-denominated debt) suddenly more expensive, and why a US interest-rate decision can move your local mortgage rate and stock market. Your savings and your government's finances are quietly tethered to a currency you may never spend.

If you live *inside* the United States, dominance is part of why your government can run such large deficits at relatively low interest rates, why sanctions are such a potent foreign-policy tool, and, on the cost side, part of the long story of why so much manufacturing left, since a permanently in-demand dollar tilts the economy toward consuming and financing rather than making.

For everyone, the practical takeaway is interpretive: when you read headlines about "the end of the dollar," "BRICS launching a new currency," or "the petrodollar collapsing," you now have the tools to weigh them. Ask the matrix's questions, does the proposed alternative offer a deep market, free convertibility, trusted law, and crisis liquidity? Almost always the answer is no on at least one count, which tells you to expect slow drift rather than sudden collapse. And watch the one variable that *could* genuinely accelerate change: not a clever new currency, but a steady erosion of trust, through overused sanctions, runaway US deficits, or political instability, in the institutions that ultimately back the dollar.
