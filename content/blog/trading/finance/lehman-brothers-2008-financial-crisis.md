> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 2008 crisis was a chain reaction in which subprime mortgages, repackaged into "safe" securities and funded with extreme short-term leverage, detonated through an interconnected financial system until the failure of Lehman Brothers froze global credit.
>
> - Banks lent to weak borrowers, bundled the loans into bonds rated AAA, and sold them worldwide while running roughly 30 to 1 leverage funded overnight in the repo market.
> - When subprime borrowers defaulted, the value of those bonds collapsed, the overnight funding vanished, and the firms holding them discovered their thin sliver of equity was already gone.
> - On September 15, 2008, Lehman Brothers filed the largest bankruptcy in US history, roughly \$600 billion in assets, and the panic that followed nearly stopped the world's payment system.
> - The bailouts that followed were enormous on paper, AIG alone around \$182 billion and the TARP program authorized for \$700 billion, but much was eventually repaid; the real bill was paid in roughly 8.7 million lost US jobs and trillions in lost wealth.
> - The durable lesson: leverage plus a maturity mismatch plus dense interconnection is a machine for turning a manageable loss into a system-wide panic, and it has reappeared, in new costumes, ever since.

On the morning of Monday, September 15, 2008, employees of a 158-year-old Wall Street institution walked out of its Manhattan headquarters carrying their belongings in cardboard boxes. Lehman Brothers, which had survived the Civil War, two world wars, the Great Depression, and every panic of the twentieth century, had filed for bankruptcy overnight. Its assets, roughly \$600 billion, made it the largest corporate failure in American history by a wide margin. Within days the entire global financial system, the invisible plumbing that lets a coffee shop accept a card and a company make payroll, came within a hairsbreadth of seizing solid. The diagram above is the mental model: a problem that started with mortgages in suburban America, traveled through securities sold all over the world, and ended as a panic in the most boring corner of finance, the market where banks borrow money overnight.

![Timeline of the 2006 to 2008 financial crisis cascade](/imgs/blogs/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis-1.png)

This is the story of how that happened. It is not a story about one greedy villain or one stupid mistake. It is a story about a *machine*, an arrangement of incentives and instruments and funding structures that each made local sense and together built a bomb. To understand it you need to understand a dozen pieces of financial plumbing, most of which are genuinely simple once someone explains them without jargon. We will build every one of those pieces from zero, ground the whole thing in real dollar arithmetic, walk through the collapse step by step, and then dissect exactly why a manageable mortgage problem turned into a near-death experience for the world economy. By the end you will understand not just what happened in 2008 but why the same shape of disaster keeps recurring, from a hedge fund in 1998 to a regional bank in 2023.

## Foundations: the building blocks of the crisis

Almost everyone who lived through 2008 came away with a vague sense that "the banks did something with mortgages and it blew up." That is true but useless. The crisis turned on about a dozen specific mechanisms, and if you understand each one as plain machinery, the whole catastrophe becomes legible. Let us install them one at a time.

A **mortgage** is the most familiar piece. It is a loan to buy a house, secured by the house itself. You borrow, say, \$300,000 from a bank, you make a monthly payment that covers interest plus a little principal, and if you stop paying, the bank can take the house. The house is **collateral**: the thing the lender seizes if you default. Because the loan is secured by a real asset, a mortgage was historically considered one of the safest loans a bank could make. Hold that thought, because the whole crisis is the story of that assumption breaking.

A **subprime** mortgage is a loan to a borrower the lender considers risky: someone with a low credit score, little or no down payment, or no documented income. "Prime" borrowers are the safe ones; "subprime" is everyone below that bar. A subprime loan charges a higher interest rate to compensate the lender for the higher chance of default. There is nothing inherently evil about lending to riskier borrowers at higher rates; that is just risk-based pricing. The danger arises when you pretend the risk is small, lend to enormous numbers of such borrowers at once, and then convince yourself the resulting pile is safe. That is exactly what happened.

Now the central trick, the one that made the crisis global: **securitization**. Suppose a bank has made a thousand mortgages, each paying it a monthly stream of cash. Instead of holding all thousand loans on its books and waiting twenty years to be repaid, the bank can bundle them into a single pool and sell *shares of the pool's cash flow* to investors as a bond. That bond is called a **mortgage-backed security**, or **MBS**. The homeowners' monthly payments flow into the pool; the pool pays the bondholders. The bank gets its money back immediately and can go make a thousand more loans. To investors, an MBS looks like a bond that pays interest, except the interest comes from thousands of homeowners instead of one company.

The legal machinery that holds the pool is a **special purpose vehicle**, or **SPV**: a separate shell company, often called a trust, created solely to own the loans and issue the bonds. The point of the SPV is to wall the loans off from the bank that created them, so that legally the bonds are backed by the mortgages alone, not by the bank's own health.

Here is where it gets clever, and dangerous. The pool's cash flow is not sold as one uniform bond. It is sliced into layers called **tranches** (from the French for "slice"), each with a different claim on the money and a different level of risk. Picture the cash from the homeowners pouring into a set of buckets stacked vertically. The top bucket, the **senior tranche**, fills first: it gets paid before anyone else, so it is the safest. The bottom bucket, the **equity tranche**, fills last and is the first to go empty if homeowners stop paying: it absorbs the first losses, so it is the riskiest. In between sit **mezzanine** tranches. The genius and the curse of this structure is that by carving a pool of mediocre loans into layers, you could create a large senior slice that looked extremely safe, because it would only lose money if an implausibly large fraction of the underlying loans defaulted.

A **collateralized debt obligation**, or **CDO**, is the same idea taken one level further. A CDO is a security built by pooling other securities, often the *mezzanine* tranches of many different MBS deals, and then re-slicing *that* pool into a fresh set of tranches with their own senior, mezzanine, and equity layers. You can build a CDO out of CDOs (a "CDO-squared"). Each repackaging step was sold as making the result safer through diversification. In reality, each step moved the final investor one more degree away from the actual mortgages and made the risk harder to see.

To grade all this, the system relied on **credit ratings**. Three firms, Standard and Poor's, Moody's, and Fitch, assign letter grades to bonds estimating how likely they are to be repaid. The top grade is **AAA** (or Aaa), meaning the agency judges default almost inconceivable; US Treasury bonds and the bluest of blue-chip companies carry it. As you descend, AA, A, BBB, and so on, the perceived risk rises. Below BBB- a bond is "junk," or sub-investment-grade. Crucially, many large investors, pension funds, insurers, foreign central banks, are *required* by their own rules to hold mostly AAA-rated bonds. So the ability to stamp a slice of subprime mortgages "AAA" was not a cosmetic nicety; it was a key that unlocked trillions of dollars of conservative money. We will see that the agencies handed out that stamp far too freely.

The **originate-to-distribute** model is the name for the whole assembly line. In the old model, a bank that made a mortgage *kept* it, so it cared deeply whether the borrower could actually pay, because it ate the loss if they could not. In the originate-to-distribute model, the originator makes the loan and immediately *sells* it down the chain to be securitized. The originator's profit comes from the *volume* of loans it writes and the fees it earns, not from whether they are repaid. This is a textbook **moral hazard**: when the person taking the risk is not the person who bears the loss, the risk gets taken carelessly. Suddenly the incentive at the front of the chain was to write as many loans as possible, to anyone, and pass the hot potato along.

Now the funding side, which is where the crisis turned lethal. A bank does not buy its inventory of bonds with its own cash; it borrows most of the money. The key borrowing tool is the **repurchase agreement**, or **repo**. A repo is a loan dressed up as a sale: a bank sells a security today and agrees to buy it back tomorrow at a slightly higher price, the difference being the interest. Functionally it is a one-day secured loan, where the security is the collateral. Wall Street financed mountains of long-term mortgage bonds with repo borrowing that had to be renewed, "rolled," every single morning. The lender protects itself with a **haircut**: it lends you less than the collateral is worth. A 2 percent haircut on \$100 of bonds means you can borrow \$98 against them; you must fund the other \$2 yourself. When lenders get scared, they raise the haircut, demanding far more of your own money, or refuse to lend at all.

**Leverage** is the amount you borrow relative to your own money. If you control \$100 of assets with \$3 of your own equity and \$97 of borrowed money, your leverage is about 33 to 1. Leverage magnifies returns in both directions: a small gain on the assets is a huge gain on your sliver of equity, and a small *loss* on the assets can wipe your equity out entirely. The major investment banks of the mid-2000s ran leverage around 30 to 1, meaning a loss of just a few percent on their assets could erase all their capital.

There is one more instrument, and it is the one that knit every firm to every other: the **credit default swap**, or **CDS**. A CDS is insurance on a bond. The buyer pays a periodic premium to the seller; in return, if the bond defaults, the seller pays out the loss. You can buy a CDS even if you do not own the bond, which means a CDS is also a pure bet that something will default. The insurance giant AIG sold colossal quantities of CDS protection on mortgage securities, collecting premiums in good times. The catch, which we will dissect, is that CDS contracts require the seller to post **collateral** as the insured bonds deteriorate or as the seller's own credit rating falls, and AIG had promised far more than it could ever post.

Two concepts complete the toolkit. **Mark-to-market** accounting requires a firm to value its assets at what they could be sold for *today*, not what it paid or hopes to receive. In a panic, when prices are crashing, marking to market forces firms to recognize losses immediately, even on bonds they intend to hold, which can trigger margin calls and forced sales. And a **fire sale** is what happens when many firms are forced to sell the same asset at once: prices crater far below any sane estimate of value, which forces *more* firms to mark down and sell, in a vicious circle.

Finally, **too-big-to-fail**: the idea that some institutions are so large and so interconnected that their failure would take down the whole system, so the government cannot allow them to fail and must rescue them. The crucial twist of 2008 is that the authorities did *not* rescue Lehman, and the world learned in real time exactly how interconnected it had become.

With those building blocks installed, the rest of the story is just watching them interact. Let us see how the machine was assembled.

## The setup: a world primed to blow up

The 2000s housing boom did not come from nowhere. After the dot-com bust and the shock of September 11, 2001, the Federal Reserve cut interest rates aggressively, holding its policy rate near 1 percent into 2004. Cheap money flooded the world looking for return. (If you want the deeper mechanics of how the central bank sets that rate and why it matters, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).) At the same time a global "savings glut," surpluses from exporting nations and oil producers, poured into US markets hunting for safe, dollar-denominated bonds that paid more than Treasuries. The single most sought-after product in the world was a bond that was both safe and paid a decent yield. Wall Street's securitization machine existed to manufacture exactly that, or at least the appearance of it.

So the assembly line ran at full speed. Mortgage originators, many of them lightly regulated specialty lenders, wrote loans as fast as they could find borrowers, knowing they would sell every loan within weeks. To keep the volume up as the pool of creditworthy buyers ran dry, lending standards collapsed. Loans appeared that would have been unthinkable a decade earlier: "no-doc" loans requiring no proof of income, "NINJA" loans (no income, no job, no assets), and **adjustable-rate mortgages** with low "teaser" rates for the first two or three years that then reset sharply higher. House prices had risen every year for a decade, so everyone, borrowers, lenders, and investors alike, assumed they would keep rising, which made the credit risk seem to vanish: if a borrower could not pay, they could just sell the house at a profit or refinance. The entire edifice rested on the assumption that national house prices would never fall significantly. They had not done so since the Great Depression.

![The securitization chain from borrower to investor](/imgs/blogs/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis-3.png)

The securitization chain above shows where the risk traveled. A borrower takes a mortgage from an originator, who immediately sells the loan to an investment bank, which pools thousands of such loans inside an SPV trust, which issues bonds sliced into tranches rated from AAA down to equity, which are sold to investors around the world. At each handoff the risk moved one step further from anyone who had actually met the borrower or seen the house. By the time a pension fund in Norway or a bank in Germany bought the AAA tranche, it was trusting a rating, not a judgment about whether suburban Floridians could pay their mortgages.

The investment banks in the middle of this chain did not just package and sell the bonds; they *held* huge inventories of them, especially the harder-to-sell mezzanine tranches and the CDOs built from them, and they financed those holdings with breathtaking leverage. By 2007 the five big US investment banks, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Merrill Lynch, Lehman Brothers, and Bear Stearns, were running leverage in the neighborhood of 30 to 1, and they funded a large share of it overnight in the repo market. (For how an investment bank's businesses fit together and why this inventory sat on its books, see [inside an investment bank](/blog/trading/finance/inside-an-investment-bank-how-they-make-money).) This is the combination that turns a bad year into a catastrophe: long-term, hard-to-value assets financed by borrowing that had to be renewed every morning.

And stitched across the whole system was AIG. Through a unit called AIG Financial Products, the insurer had written credit default swaps on roughly \$440 billion to \$500 billion of securities, much of it mortgage-related, collecting premiums and booking profits. Because AIG itself carried a pristine AAA rating, its CDS protection was treated as nearly riskless, which is why so many banks bought it: holding a risky bond plus AIG insurance let a bank treat the combination as safe and hold less capital against it. AIG had, in effect, sold earthquake insurance on every building in the city and assumed the earthquakes were uncorrelated. They were not.

#### Worked example: how 30 to 1 leverage is wiped out by a 3.3 percent fall

Let us make leverage concrete, because it is the beating heart of the whole disaster. Suppose an investment bank holds \$1,000 of mortgage-backed assets. It funded them with \$967 of borrowed money (mostly overnight repo) and just \$33 of its own equity. Its leverage ratio is assets divided by equity: \$1,000 / \$33, which is about 30 to 1. Put concretely, for every \$30 of bonds on its books, only \$1 belongs to the bank; the other \$29 is borrowed.

- Start: assets \$1,000, debt \$967, equity \$33.
- Now the assets fall in value by 3.3 percent. New asset value: \$1,000 times 0.967, which is about \$967.
- The debt does not shrink; the bank still owes \$967.
- New equity = assets minus debt = \$967 - \$967 = \$0.

A mere 3.3 percent decline in the value of the assets has wiped out 100 percent of the bank's equity. The firm is now insolvent: it owes exactly as much as it owns, and one more dollar of loss makes its liabilities exceed its assets. The intuition to carry forward: at 30 to 1 leverage, a rounding-error move in your assets is a death blow to your capital, and mortgage bonds fell far more than 3.3 percent.

## The blow-up, step by step

The machine started to seize in 2006, when US house prices stopped rising and began, for the first time in living memory, to fall nationally. The timing was lethal because millions of subprime adjustable-rate mortgages were written to reset to much higher payments in 2007 and 2008. Borrowers who had counted on refinancing or selling at a profit found they could do neither: the house was now worth less than the loan, and the new payment was unaffordable. Defaults climbed.

#### Worked example: a 2/28 ARM resets from \$1,200 to \$1,900 a month

Consider a typical "2/28" subprime adjustable-rate mortgage: a 30-year loan with a low fixed "teaser" rate for the first 2 years, then a floating rate for the remaining 28. Say the loan is \$200,000.

- Years 1 to 2: a teaser rate of about 7 percent. The monthly principal-and-interest payment is roughly \$1,330. To keep the example clean, call the borrower's payment about \$1,200 after a slightly lower introductory structure; either way it is "affordable" by design.
- Year 3, the reset: the rate jumps to, say, a margin plus the prevailing index, landing near 11 percent. On the remaining balance the monthly payment climbs to roughly \$1,900.
- The increase: about \$700 more per month, an extra \$8,400 a year, on a household that was already stretched.

In a rising market the borrower would have refinanced before the reset, rolling into a new teaser loan, or sold the house at a gain. With prices falling and the loan now larger than the home's value, both escape routes are shut. The borrower defaults. The single-sentence intuition: the resets were a synchronized time bomb, set to detonate millions of payments upward in the same eighteen months, precisely when the housing exit doors slammed shut.

As defaults rose through 2007, the cash flowing into the mortgage pools fell short, and the value of MBS and CDO tranches, even ones rated AAA, began to drop. In June 2007 two hedge funds run by Bear Stearns that were stuffed with subprime CDOs collapsed, an early tremor. In August 2007 the French bank BNP Paribas froze withdrawals from three funds, saying it could no longer value their mortgage holdings at all, which marked the moment the wider funding markets first took fright. Interbank lending rates spiked as banks grew afraid to lend even to each other.

The first major institution to fall was Bear Stearns, the smallest and most thinly capitalized of the five big investment banks and one of the most reliant on overnight repo funding. In March 2008, as rumors spread about its health, its repo lenders and trading counterparties pulled back almost overnight, a classic run, and its cash evaporated in days. To prevent a disorderly failure, the Federal Reserve engineered a rescue: JPMorgan Chase bought Bear Stearns, initially at the shocking price of \$2 per share (later raised to \$10), with the Fed agreeing to absorb up to \$30 billion of Bear's most toxic assets. The rescue calmed markets for a few months, but it also planted a fateful expectation: that the government would not let a major investment bank fail. Markets assumed Lehman, next in line, would be caught the same way.

Through the summer of 2008 the rot spread to the two giants at the center of the US mortgage market, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the government-sponsored enterprises that owned or guaranteed roughly half of all US mortgages, on the order of \$5 trillion. As mortgage losses mounted, their thin capital was overwhelmed. On September 7, 2008, the US Treasury placed both into **conservatorship**, effectively a government takeover, wiping out their shareholders and committing taxpayer support to honor their guarantees. The two largest mortgage financiers on earth had failed in all but name.

That set the stage for the week the world changed.

Lehman Brothers was deeply exposed to commercial real estate and subprime mortgages, ran leverage around 31 to 1, and, like Bear, relied heavily on overnight funding. The picture below shows what that meant: in 2006, roughly \$1,000 of assets sat on about \$33 of equity at 30 to 1 leverage, funded overnight in repo; during the 2008 run, a fall of just a few percent in those assets erased the equity entirely while the repo lenders refused to roll the loans. That is the trap Lehman could not escape.

![Investment bank balance sheet before and during the run](/imgs/blogs/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis-5.png)

Through 2008 Lehman scrambled to raise capital and find a buyer. Over the weekend of September 13 to 14, the Treasury and Fed convened the heads of Wall Street and tried to broker a private rescue. Two suitors emerged, Bank of America and the British bank Barclays, but Bank of America peeled off to buy Merrill Lynch instead (more on that below), and the Barclays deal was blocked when UK regulators refused to approve it without a shareholder vote that could not happen in time. Crucially, the government declined to provide the kind of backstop it had given Bear, partly out of a judgment that it lacked legal authority to lend to an insolvent firm without good collateral, and partly out of a belief, soon regretted, that markets had had six months to prepare. With no buyer and no rescue, Lehman Brothers filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in the early hours of Monday, September 15, 2008. Its roughly \$600 billion in assets made it the largest bankruptcy filing in US history.

The market did not greet Lehman's failure as a healthy purge of a reckless firm. It greeted it as proof that *no one* was safe and that the government's safety net had holes. Within 24 hours the panic detonated. The very next day, September 16, AIG, drowning in collateral calls on its CDS book, was rescued by the Federal Reserve with an initial \$85 billion credit line that ballooned, with later additions, to a commitment of around \$182 billion. The same day, the Reserve Primary Fund, a giant money-market fund that held \$785 million of now-worthless Lehman commercial paper, announced that its share price had fallen below \$1.

That phrase, **breaking the buck**, sounds technical but it caused a stampede. Money-market funds are supposed to be as safe as cash; they always trade at exactly \$1 per share. When the Reserve Primary Fund "broke the buck," trading at 97 cents, ordinary investors and corporate treasurers concluded that even cash-like funds could lose money, and they yanked their money out. Hundreds of billions of dollars fled money-market funds in days. Because those funds were among the biggest buyers of **commercial paper**, the short-term IOUs that ordinary companies use to fund payroll and inventory, the commercial-paper market and the repo market froze almost solid. Healthy industrial companies that had nothing to do with mortgages suddenly could not borrow to make payroll. The crisis had jumped from Wall Street to the real economy.

To stop the freefall, the US government moved at emergency speed. On October 3, 2008, Congress passed the **Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP)**, authorizing the Treasury to spend up to \$700 billion to stabilize the financial system. Rather than buying up toxic assets as originally pitched, the Treasury used much of it to inject capital directly into banks, taking ownership stakes, while the Fed and the FDIC rolled out an alphabet soup of emergency lending and guarantee programs. The point was no longer to be fair or to punish recklessness; it was to keep the payment system alive.

![Matrix of key firms by exposure and fate in the 2008 crisis](/imgs/blogs/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis-6.png)

The matrix above sorts the major players by their exposure and their fate, and it makes the crisis's cruelest feature plain: firms with nearly identical exposures met wildly different ends, depending almost entirely on timing and on who happened to be standing nearby with a checkbook. Bear Stearns, exposed to subprime and dependent on repo, was rescued in March and sold at \$2 a share. Lehman, with very similar exposure and around 31 to 1 leverage, was allowed to fail in September at roughly \$600 billion. AIG, on the hook for CDS on hundreds of billions of dollars of securities, was bailed out for around \$182 billion. Merrill Lynch, sitting on a mountain of CDO inventory, was bought by Bank of America in a deal struck the same weekend Lehman died. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, with some \$5 trillion of mortgage guarantees between them, were taken into conservatorship. Same disease, different sentences.

## The mechanism dissected: why a mortgage problem became a global panic

The chronology tells you *what* happened. To understand *why* a wave of mortgage defaults, large but not by itself catastrophic, nearly took down the world economy, you have to see how several mechanisms multiplied one another. Estimates of total US subprime mortgage losses run in the few hundreds of billions of dollars, painful but, in a roughly \$14 trillion economy, survivable on its own. The system turned that into trillions of dollars of damage. Here is how.

![Contagion graph showing subprime losses spreading to a credit freeze](/imgs/blogs/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis-2.png)

The contagion graph above traces the transmission. Subprime defaults hit the MBS and CDO tranches; the markdowns on those securities hit the banks holding them and triggered claims on AIG's CDS protection; the wounded banks faced rising repo haircuts while AIG faced collateral calls; both fed into a run on the repo and commercial-paper markets and on the money-market funds that fueled them; and the end state was a credit freeze. Notice that the original shock, falling house prices, is a single node on the far left, and the catastrophe, a frozen credit system, is a single node on the far right. Everything in between is the amplification machinery. Let us take it apart piece by piece.

### Leverage plus a maturity mismatch

The first and deepest flaw was the combination of extreme leverage with a **maturity mismatch**: holding long-term, illiquid assets (mortgage bonds that mature over decades and are hard to sell quickly) while financing them with the shortest possible borrowing (repo that must be renewed every single morning). This is structurally identical to the classic bank run, just moved out of the regulated banking system into the "shadow banking" world of investment banks and repo. A normal bank takes deposits (short-term money that can be withdrawn any day) and makes loans (long-term assets). It works only because depositors do not all withdraw at once. Deposit insurance exists precisely to stop them from panicking. But repo lenders had no insurance and no patience.

So when the value of the collateral became doubtful, the repo lenders did what any rational lender does: they demanded bigger haircuts or simply stopped rolling the loans. A firm financing \$1,000 of bonds with overnight repo does not have \$1,000 in the bank to repay; it was always planning to borrow the money again the next morning. When the morning comes and no one will lend, the firm must sell assets immediately to raise the cash, into a market where everyone else is being forced to sell the same thing. This is the run, and at 30 to 1 leverage it can kill a firm in days, exactly as it killed Bear Stearns and Lehman.

#### Worked example: a repo haircut jumping from 2 percent to 20 percent on \$1 billion

Suppose a firm funds \$1 billion of mortgage bonds in the repo market. In calm times the haircut is 2 percent, meaning lenders advance 98 percent of the collateral's value.

- Calm: haircut 2 percent. The firm borrows \$1,000 million times 0.98 = \$980 million against the bonds and funds the remaining \$20 million from its own equity.
- Panic: lenders, now frightened the bonds are worth less and harder to sell, raise the haircut to 20 percent. They will now advance only \$1,000 million times 0.80 = \$800 million.
- The funding hole: the firm can now borrow \$800 million where it used to borrow \$980 million. Overnight it must find \$180 million in cash, either by posting more of its own money or by selling \$180 million of bonds into a falling market.

A jump in the haircut from 2 to 20 percent did not change the bonds at all; it changed how much the firm could borrow against them, opening a \$180 million hole that had to be filled *today*. The intuition: a haircut spike is a margin call on the entire firm, and when it hits every firm at once, the only way to raise cash, selling, is the very thing that drives prices, and everyone else's collateral value, down further.

### The mark-to-market fire-sale doom loop

That last point is the second mechanism, and it is the engine that turned a sell-off into a death spiral. Because of mark-to-market accounting, when one firm was forced to dump mortgage bonds at a fire-sale price, *every other firm holding similar bonds* had to mark their own holdings down to that new, lower price, even if they had no intention of selling. Those markdowns ate into their capital and made their own repo lenders nervous, raising their haircuts, which forced *them* to sell, which pushed prices down again. Each turn of the loop made the next turn worse.

![The repo run and fire-sale doom loop traced forward](/imgs/blogs/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis-7.png)

The doom loop above traces one turn of the cycle: assets get marked down about 3 percent, repo haircuts jump from 2 to 20 percent, the firm is forced into a fire sale of collateral, prices fall further, that triggers more markdowns, and around it goes, until lenders simply refuse to participate at any price and credit freezes. This is a positive feedback loop, in the engineering sense: the output (lower prices) feeds back as a larger input (bigger losses and haircuts). Such loops do not settle down; they accelerate until something external stops them. In 2008 the only thing big enough to stop the loop was the government, which is why the rescues, however galling, were not optional once the spiral was running.

A subtle and vicious feature is that the doom loop made the AAA ratings actively dangerous. Because so many institutions held the "safe" senior tranches, and because their rules let them hold those tranches with very little capital, the senior layer was where the system had concentrated its largest, most leveraged, most complacent bets. When even the AAA tranches began to take losses, an outcome the models had deemed all but impossible, the holders were the least prepared and most leveraged against exactly that bond. Safety, falsely labeled, had become the system's largest single point of failure.

### The interconnection through CDS

The third mechanism was the dense web of credit default swaps, with AIG at its center. CDS contracts contained two features that combined explosively. First, as the insured bonds fell in value, the protection seller had to post **collateral** to the buyer, cash handed over to prove it could pay the eventual claim. Second, the contracts also demanded extra collateral if the *seller's own credit rating* was downgraded, on the logic that a weaker insurer is a riskier counterparty. AIG was hit by both at once: the mortgage bonds it had insured were falling, *and* its own rating was cut as the losses mounted. The result was a cascade of collateral calls, demands for tens of billions of dollars in cash, that AIG simply did not have.

#### Worked example: AIG's CDS collateral calls as ratings drop

Imagine AIG has sold CDS protection on \$100 billion of mortgage securities. The contracts say AIG must post collateral equal to the decline in those securities' value plus an extra amount triggered by AIG's own rating.

- Step 1, the bonds fall. The insured securities drop 10 percent in value. AIG must post collateral covering the loss the buyers now fear: roughly 10 percent of \$100 billion = \$10 billion in cash, handed to counterparties.
- Step 2, the bonds fall further. They drop another 10 percent (now down 20 percent overall). AIG owes roughly another \$10 billion, for \$20 billion posted in total.
- Step 3, the rating cut. Because AIG is now visibly wounded, the rating agencies downgrade it. The downgrade triggers contract clauses demanding *additional* collateral regardless of the bonds, say another \$10 billion or more.
- The total: AIG faces demands on the order of \$30 billion-plus in cash, growing by the day, against an insurance book it never expected to pay out on.

A firm that had booked steady premium income for years discovered, almost overnight, that it owed tens of billions in cash it did not have. The single-sentence intuition: AIG's CDS were not really insurance against a rare event; they were a hidden borrowing arrangement that demanded cash precisely when AIG was least able to raise it, the same liquidity trap that killed the banks, in a different costume.

The reason the government rescued AIG the day after letting Lehman fail is that AIG's collapse would have detonated every counterparty at once. Banks all over the world held risky bonds plus AIG insurance and counted the pair as safe. If AIG defaulted, that insurance vanished, every one of those banks would have had to recognize the full loss simultaneously, and several would likely have failed in a chain. AIG was the load-bearing wall the whole system had quietly leaned against.

### The ratings failure

Underlying all of this was a failure of the credit ratings that the entire structure depended on. The agencies gave AAA stamps to vast quantities of bonds built from subprime loans, using models that assumed house prices would not fall nationally and that defaults in different regions were largely independent. Both assumptions were wrong in the same direction at the same time: house prices fell everywhere at once, and defaults turned out to be highly **correlated**, because they shared a common cause (the national housing bust). The agencies were also paid by the very issuers whose bonds they rated, the "issuer-pays" model, a conflict of interest that encouraged generous grades. When the AAA tranches started defaulting, the foundational assumption that let conservative money pour into mortgage bonds, that AAA meant safe, was revealed as false, and the conservative money fled all at once.

#### Worked example: a subprime MBS tranche waterfall and who takes the first dollar of loss

Now let us walk the loss waterfall itself, the structure that made bad loans look safe. Suppose a pool of subprime mortgages totals \$1,000 million and is sliced into three tranches:

- Senior (AAA): \$800 million, paid first, loses last.
- Mezzanine (BBB): \$150 million, the middle buffer.
- Equity: \$50 million, paid last, loses first.

![Capital stack of a CDO loss waterfall](/imgs/blogs/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis-4.png)

The capital stack above shows the buckets. Now let the underlying loans default and lose money. Losses fill from the bottom up:

- First \$50 million of losses: entirely absorbed by the equity tranche, which is wiped out. The mezzanine and senior holders lose nothing. (Loss so far: 5 percent of the pool.)
- Next \$150 million of losses: now the mezzanine (BBB) tranche is consumed, down to zero. The senior AAA holders still lose nothing. (Cumulative loss: 20 percent of the pool.)
- Beyond 20 percent: only now does the senior AAA tranche start to take losses.

In calm reasoning, this looked bulletproof: for the AAA holder to lose a single dollar, more than 20 percent of a pool of mortgages had to be wiped out, which historical data said was nearly impossible. The trick is that the "historical data" came from an era when house prices never fell nationally. In 2008, losses on the worst subprime pools blew straight through 20 percent, and the AAA holders, the pension funds, the foreign banks, the money-market funds, took losses they had been told were inconceivable. The single-sentence intuition: a loss waterfall does not eliminate risk, it just concentrates the worst of it in the bottom tranches and gives the top a false halo, and that halo lured the most risk-averse money into the most fragile assets.

### Why letting Lehman fail spread panic

A natural question is why letting *one* firm fail, after rescuing Bear and before rescuing AIG, mattered so much. The answer is **expectations**, the same force that drives any bank run. After the Bear Stearns rescue in March, the market had concluded that the government would catch any large investment bank that fell. That belief was itself a kind of stability: counterparties kept dealing with weak firms because they assumed a backstop existed. When Lehman was allowed to fail, that belief shattered in an instant. Suddenly every firm asked the same terrifying question about every counterparty: if even Lehman can go, who is safe, and will I be repaid? The rational response to that question is to stop lending to everyone and hoard cash, which is precisely the behavior that freezes a credit market. Lehman's failure did not just destroy Lehman; it destroyed the *assumption of rescue* that had been quietly holding the rest of the system together. Whether that made letting Lehman fail a catastrophic error or a painful necessity is a genuine debate, which we will take up in the misconceptions.

## The aftermath: who paid, and what changed

The damage was staggering and fell most heavily on people who had never heard of a CDO. In the US, the crisis and the Great Recession that followed destroyed roughly 8.7 million jobs and pushed unemployment to about 10 percent in October 2009. US household wealth fell by an estimated \$11 trillion or more, much of it the home equity of ordinary families. Millions of homes were lost to foreclosure. Globally, output and trade collapsed in late 2008 and early 2009 at a pace not seen since the 1930s, and many economies took years to recover; some, especially in Europe, slid into a follow-on debt crisis.

The financial firms themselves were remade. Lehman was gone. Bear Stearns and Merrill Lynch were absorbed into JPMorgan and Bank of America. The two surviving independent investment banks, Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, converted into bank holding companies so they could access Federal Reserve support, ending the era of the standalone Wall Street investment bank. Fannie and Freddie remained wards of the state for years.

It is worth being precise about the bailout numbers, because they are widely misunderstood. The TARP program was *authorized* for up to \$700 billion, but far less was actually disbursed, and most of what was disbursed was *repaid with interest*. The capital injected into banks was largely returned, and the Treasury ultimately recorded a modest profit on the bank portion. The AIG rescue, around \$182 billion committed, was also substantially repaid as AIG sold assets and recovered, with the government again roughly breaking even or better on that piece. The auto-industry portion lost money; the housing-support programs cost money outright. The headline lesson stands: the *cash* cost of the bailouts to taxpayers was far smaller than the scary authorization numbers suggested, and the true, enormous cost of the crisis was borne not through the bailouts but through lost jobs, lost homes, lost output, and years of slow recovery.

The regulatory response was the **Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act** of 2010, a sweeping law aimed at the specific failures of 2008. Among its key provisions: higher capital and liquidity requirements for big banks, so a few percent loss can no longer wipe them out; a process for the orderly wind-down of a failing systemic firm, so authorities are not stuck choosing between a chaotic Lehman-style bankruptcy and an unconditional bailout; the routing of most standardized derivatives like CDS through central clearinghouses to reduce the opaque, bilateral interconnection that AIG exploited; the **Volcker Rule** limiting banks' proprietary speculative trading; reforms to make rating agencies more accountable; and the creation of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau to police the kind of predatory and deceptive lending that fed the subprime boom. Stress tests, regular simulations of how big banks would fare in a severe downturn, became a permanent fixture. Whether all this is enough is contested; many of the deepest incentives, including too-big-to-fail itself, were softened rather than solved.

## Common misconceptions

The crisis is so politically charged that it accreted a thick shell of myths. Here are the ones worth correcting, with the reasons.

**"It was just greedy bankers."** Greed was abundant, but greed is the constant in finance, not the variable; it is present in every boom that does *not* end in catastrophe too. Blaming greed explains nothing about why *this* boom blew up so violently. The honest answer is structural: a set of incentives and instruments, originate-to-distribute, which broke the link between the lender and the loss; AAA ratings, which laundered risk into something conservative money could hold; and 30 to 1 leverage funded overnight, which made the system fatally fragile, turned ordinary greed into a system-wide bomb. Replace every banker with a saint operating inside the same structure and you would still get a crisis, just with more apologies. The lesson is that you regulate *structures and incentives*, not character, because you cannot legislate character.

**"The Community Reinvestment Act and poor borrowers caused it."** This is a popular but evidence-poor claim. The Community Reinvestment Act, a 1977 law encouraging banks to lend in the communities where they take deposits, predated the crisis by three decades, and the bulk of the worst subprime lending came from independent mortgage companies *not* covered by it. Studies by the Federal Reserve and others found that loans originated under CRA obligations performed comparably or better than the wider subprime market. The boom was driven by the *profitability* of the originate-to-distribute machine and the investor appetite for AAA-stamped yield, not by a law nudging banks toward low-income neighborhoods. Blaming the borrowers also inverts the moral hazard: the originators who wrote loans they knew were unsound, planning to sell them within weeks, were the ones with the information and the incentive to behave badly.

**"Letting Lehman fail was clearly the right thing,"** or its mirror, **"letting Lehman fail was clearly the wrong thing."** Both certainties are wrong; this is the genuinely hard call of the whole episode. The case for letting Lehman fail: rescuing every firm creates a permanent expectation of rescue, which encourages exactly the recklessness that caused the crisis (the moral-hazard argument), and officials argued they lacked the legal authority to lend to a firm without adequate collateral. The case against: Lehman's failure shattered the assumption of rescue at the worst possible moment, triggering the very panic and freeze the authorities then spent trillions trying to reverse. The defensible conclusion is not that one side is obviously right, but that being forced to choose between a disorderly bankruptcy and a blank-check bailout was itself the failure. That is precisely why Dodd-Frank created an orderly-resolution process: to give future officials a third option that did not exist in September 2008.

**"The bailouts cost taxpayers everything."** As covered above, this confuses the *authorized* amounts with the *net* cost. TARP was authorized for \$700 billion, but far less was disbursed and most was repaid with interest; the bank-capital piece roughly broke even or turned a small profit, and the AIG rescue was largely recovered. The genuine, vast cost of the crisis was not the bailout outlays but the lost output, jobs, and household wealth, which dwarfed any bailout figure. Conflating the two both overstates the bailout's direct cost and, more dangerously, understates the crisis's true cost.

**"It was a black swan no one could have foreseen."** Some risks were genuinely mismodeled, but the broad shape was visible. A number of investors and economists warned, well before 2008, that house prices were a bubble, that subprime underwriting had collapsed, and that the leverage in the system was dangerous. The famous investors who shorted the mortgage market made their bets precisely because the danger was *legible* to anyone willing to read the loan documents. "No one could have known" is mostly the comfort of those who chose not to look, because the machine was lucrative while it ran.

**"Mark-to-market accounting caused the crisis."** Some argued that forcing firms to mark assets to fire-sale prices created the losses out of thin air, and that suspending the rule would have helped. But mark-to-market did not create the losses; the bad loans did. The accounting *revealed* the losses faster, which was painful, but hiding them, letting firms carry worthless bonds at made-up values, would have destroyed the one thing the system most needed in a panic: trust that a firm's reported numbers were real. Marking to market was the messenger, not the murderer.

## How it echoes in other markets

The specific instruments of 2008 were novel, but its *mechanism*, leverage plus a maturity or liquidity mismatch plus interconnection, igniting a self-feeding run, is one of the oldest patterns in finance. Once you can see the shape, you find it everywhere.

**Long-Term Capital Management, 1998.** A hedge fund founded by star traders and two Nobel laureates ran leverage estimated at around 25 to 1, and far higher counting derivatives, on convergence trades that assumed historical relationships would hold. When Russia defaulted and markets moved against it in correlated ways its models deemed nearly impossible, its capital evaporated, and because it was entangled with every major bank, the Fed organized a private rescue to prevent a cascade. The DNA is identical: extreme leverage, a bet that diversification had killed correlation, and dense interconnection that turned one fund's failure into a systemic threat. We tell that story in full in [LTCM 1998, when genius failed](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed); read it as the dress rehearsal the system ignored.

**The margin spiral of 1929.** The crash that began the Great Depression was, mechanically, a leverage-and-fire-sale doom loop in equities. Investors bought stocks "on margin," borrowing up to 90 percent of the price. When prices fell, brokers issued margin calls; investors who could not pay were sold out; the forced selling drove prices down further, triggering more margin calls, in exactly the self-feeding spiral that ran through repo in 2008. Different asset, different decade, same loop. The 1929 episode is why margin requirements were later regulated, just as 2008 is why repo and capital rules were tightened.

**Silicon Valley Bank, 2023.** SVB took in a flood of deposits and parked them in long-dated government bonds, safe from credit risk but exposed to interest-rate risk. When the Fed raised rates sharply, those bonds lost market value, and when uninsured depositors, a concentrated, jittery base of tech startups, realized the bank would have to sell those bonds at a loss to fund withdrawals, they ran, draining tens of billions in a single day, supercharged by mobile banking and social media. This is the 2008 maturity mismatch in its purest classroom form: long assets, short funding, a loss that became fatal only because the funding fled. No subprime, no CDOs, same fault line.

**Archegos, 2021.** A family office built enormous, concentrated equity positions using total-return swaps that hid its leverage from its own banks, each of which thought it was the only major lender. When the stocks fell, the banks issued margin calls simultaneously, and the resulting fire sale cost the lenders billions, with Credit Suisse losing around \$5 billion. The echo: hidden leverage, concentrated bets, and an interconnection (multiple prime brokers blind to one another) that turned one client's collapse into a multi-bank loss, exactly the opacity Dodd-Frank's derivatives reforms were meant to reduce. The leverage mechanics here rhyme with those in [how hedge funds work](/blog/trading/finance/how-hedge-funds-work-leverage-2-and-20).

**The Eurozone sovereign-debt crisis, 2010 to 2012.** The 2008 panic flowed straight into Europe, where it mutated. Banks across the continent held large amounts of the government bonds of weaker member states like Greece. As those governments' finances deteriorated, the bonds fell, wounding the banks, which forced the governments to support the banks, which worsened the governments' finances, the "doom loop" between sovereigns and their banks. It is the same positive-feedback structure as the repo fire sale, with national treasuries and bank balance sheets in the loop instead of mortgage bonds and haircuts.

**The 2007 quant quake.** In August 2007, a year before Lehman, many quantitative hedge funds running similar statistical strategies suffered sudden, severe, simultaneous losses. The likely trigger: one large fund, hit by losses elsewhere (very possibly subprime), was forced to liquidate its quant positions; because so many funds held *the same* positions, that selling moved prices against all of them at once, forcing more liquidations, a fire-sale loop running through crowded trades rather than through repo. It was an early, contained demonstration of exactly the contagion-through-shared-positions that would go global the following year.

**The savings-and-loan crisis, 1980s.** Hundreds of US savings institutions failed after taking in short-term deposits and lending long on fixed-rate mortgages; when rates spiked, they were paying more on deposits than they earned on loans, and deregulation let many gamble for resurrection on risky assets. The cleanup cost taxpayers on the order of \$120 billion to \$130 billion. The mismatch, short funding against long assets, is the same fault that ran through 2008 and SVB; the period just lacked the securitization and CDS that made 2008 global.

Across all of these, the constant is not the asset, subprime mortgages, Russian bonds, government bonds, tech stocks. The constant is the *structure*: borrow short to hold long, lever it up, tie everyone to everyone else, and assume diversification has banished correlated losses. When the correlated loss arrives, the run begins, and the run is what kills you.

## When this matters to you and further reading

You are unlikely to run a 30 to 1 repo book, but the mechanics of 2008 touch ordinary financial life more than you would think. The deposit insurance that protects your bank account up to a limit exists because of bank runs like the ones described here; understanding *why* a run happens tells you why staying under the insured limit, and not panicking, is the rational move when headlines scream. The reason money-market funds are now more tightly regulated and sometimes "gate" withdrawals is the Reserve Primary Fund breaking the buck. The reason your mortgage application now demands exhaustive documentation is the no-doc loans of the boom. And whenever you read that some new corner of finance, a crypto lender, a private-credit fund, a stablecoin, is "perfectly safe" because the risk has been cleverly diversified and tranched away, you should hear the echo of "AAA-rated subprime" and ask the two questions that always matter: how much leverage is in this, and what happens if the short-term funding stops?

The single most useful habit the crisis teaches is to look past the *label* on a risk to its *structure*. AAA was a label; 20 percent of a subprime pool defaulting was the structure. "Insurance from AIG" was a label; tens of billions in collateral calls AIG could not meet was the structure. Safe, liquid, diversified, hedged: these are words, and 2008 is the permanent reminder that words on a risk are not the same as the cash flows underneath it.

For the surrounding machinery, [inside an investment bank](/blog/trading/finance/inside-an-investment-bank-how-they-make-money) explains the businesses that held the toxic inventory and why; [how hedge funds work](/blog/trading/finance/how-hedge-funds-work-leverage-2-and-20) unpacks the leverage and fee structures that recur in Archegos and LTCM; [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) covers the policy lever that inflated the early-2000s credit boom and later detonated SVB; and [LTCM 1998, when genius failed](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed) is the closest cousin to this story, the same machine, a decade earlier, on a smaller stage. Read them together and the pattern becomes impossible to unsee, which is the entire point. The next crisis will wear a new costume. The machine underneath will be the same one that walked out of Lehman Brothers in cardboard boxes on a Monday morning in September.
