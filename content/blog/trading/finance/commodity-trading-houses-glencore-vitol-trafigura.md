Here is a question almost nobody asks at the gas pump. The fuel in your tank started as crude oil pumped from a field that might be in West Texas, the North Sea, or the Persian Gulf, and it ended up at a refinery thousands of miles away, then at the station on your corner. Somebody had to decide which barrels went where, charter the tanker, find a buyer before the ship even arrived, pay the producer up front, and absorb the risk that the price would lurch while the cargo was still floating in the middle of the ocean. That somebody is almost never a household name. It is a commodity trading house, and a small group of them sit in the middle of the flows that feed, fuel, and build the entire world. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: a producer on one side, a consumer on the other, and a trader in the middle who owns the cargo in transit and earns the difference between the two prices, minus the cost of getting it there.

![Pipeline showing a commodity flowing from producer to trader to ship to consumer with a margin at the end](/imgs/blogs/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura-1.png)

These firms are some of the largest companies you have never heard of. Vitol, a privately held oil trader headquartered in Switzerland, has in some years reported more revenue than Apple. Glencore is so big in metals and mining that a hiccup in its operations can move the price of copper. Cargill, a family-owned American firm founded in 1865, touches a meaningful slice of the world's grain trade and has never sold a share to the public. Trafigura, spun out of the old Marc Rich empire, moves enough crude, refined products, and metal that a liquidity scare at the firm would make finance ministries nervous. And yet, because most of them are private, you can search for hours and learn less about their internals than you could learn about a mid-cap retailer in an afternoon.

This post builds the whole thing from zero. If you do not know the difference between a physical commodity and a financial one, what a futures contract is, what "contango" means, or why anyone would want to store oil rather than sell it, you are exactly the reader this is written for. We will define every term the first time it appears, ground each idea in a worked example with real dollar figures, and only then climb up to the big, uncomfortable questions about secrecy, scandal, and the 2022 energy crisis that nearly broke some of these firms.

## Foundations: physical versus financial, spot versus futures, and what a trader actually does

Let us start with the smallest distinction and build up, because almost every misunderstanding about this industry comes from blurring it.

A **commodity** is a basic raw material that is more or less interchangeable from one producer to the next. A barrel of a given grade of crude oil is a barrel of that grade no matter who pumped it; a ton of a given purity of copper is a ton of that copper; a bushel of a given grade of wheat is a bushel of that wheat. That interchangeability is the technical meaning of **fungible**: one unit substitutes for another. Because commodities are fungible, they trade on price, not on brand, which is the opposite of how you buy a phone or a car.

There are two very different ways to "trade" a commodity, and keeping them apart is the single most important thing in this whole article. A **physical commodity** is the actual stuff: real barrels of oil sitting in a tank, real metal stacked in a warehouse, real grain in a silo. A **financial commodity** is a paper (now electronic) contract whose value is *derived* from the price of the physical stuff but which usually never results in anyone touching a barrel. The financial contract is a **derivative**, meaning its value derives from something else.

Commodity trading houses are, first and foremost, in the **physical** business. This is the thing most people get wrong. When you hear "commodity trader," your mental image is probably a frantic person betting on whether oil goes up or down. That is a speculator, and speculation is a side activity at best for these firms. What a commodity house actually does is **move and transform real cargoes**: it buys physical oil, metal, or grain in one place, arranges to transport, store, blend, or refine it, and delivers it to a buyer somewhere else. The profit comes from the *gap* between the price at the origin and the price at the destination, after costs. That gap is an **arbitrage**, a price difference between two markets that someone can capture by bridging them.

So the cleanest one-line definition is this: a commodity trader is a **logistics-plus-arbitrage** business, not a betting shop. It earns money by solving a real-world mismatch (the oil is here, the demand is there; the harvest is now, the consumption is spread across the year) rather than by guessing direction. Hold that thought, because everything else hangs on it.

Now we need the price vocabulary. The **spot price** is the price for a commodity delivered right now (or very soon). If you want a cargo of crude this week, you pay roughly the spot price. A **futures price** is the price agreed today for delivery at a fixed date in the future. A **futures contract** is a standardized agreement, traded on an exchange, to buy or sell a set quantity of a commodity at a set price on a set future date. Futures exist precisely so that buyers and sellers can lock in a price in advance and stop worrying about which way the spot price wanders.

The relationship between the spot price and the futures price has a name, and the two cases matter enormously to traders. When the futures price is *higher* than the spot price, the market is in **contango**: it costs more to buy for later delivery than for now. When the futures price is *lower* than the spot price, the market is in **backwardation**: buyers are paying a premium to get the commodity *now*, so future delivery is cheaper. Contango usually signals plenty of supply (nobody is in a hurry, so storing it for later is worth paying for); backwardation usually signals scarcity (everyone wants it immediately). We will see in a moment that contango is what makes a **storage trade** profitable, and that is one of the cleanest ways these firms make money.

There is one more pair of ideas, and they are the heart of why these firms can run on so little of their own money. The first is **hedging**: taking an offsetting position so that a price move you do not want to bet on cancels out. If you own a physical cargo of oil (you are **long** the oil, meaning you profit if its price rises and lose if it falls), you can **sell futures** against it (go **short** the futures, meaning you profit if the price falls). Now if the oil price drops while your cargo is at sea, the loss on the cargo is roughly matched by a gain on the futures, and vice versa. You have converted a price bet into a locked margin. The trader does not want to gamble on the oil price; it wants to be paid for the *logistics*, and hedging strips out the gamble.

The second is **trade finance**: the borrowed money that pays for the cargo. Because a single cargo of crude can be worth tens of millions of dollars, no trader could fund many cargoes from its own pocket. Instead a bank issues a **letter of credit**, a promise to pay the producer on the trader's behalf once shipping documents prove the cargo exists and has been loaded. The cargo itself is the collateral. When the buyer at the far end pays for the delivered goods, that money repays the bank. The trader's own cash is barely involved. This is why commodity houses run enormous **leverage** (using borrowed money to control assets far larger than your own capital) on razor-thin margins, and it is why a freeze in bank credit is the thing that can actually kill one of them. We will build all of this up with numbers shortly.

That is the entire foundation: physical versus financial, fungible commodities, spot versus futures, contango versus backwardation, long and short, hedging, leverage, and trade finance. Define those and the rest of the post is just consequences.

## What a commodity trader actually does, with the numbers

Let us make the core business concrete before we meet the firms. Everything a commodity house does is some flavor of moving a real cargo from a cheaper place or time to a dearer one and pocketing the gap, with the price risk hedged away. There are three classic flavors of that gap.

The first is **geographic arbitrage**: the same commodity is cheaper in one location than another, by more than the cost of moving it. Crude trapped behind a pipeline bottleneck in one region sells below the world price; ship it to a port that connects to global demand and it fetches more. The second is **time arbitrage**: the commodity is cheaper now than the market will pay for later delivery (contango), so you buy it now, store it, and deliver later into a contract you locked at the higher price. The third is **quality arbitrage**: you buy a cheaper, off-spec, or unblended product and *transform* it (blend two crudes to hit a refinery's specification, clean up a sulfurous fuel, mix grain lots to a target grade) so the blended product is worth more than the sum of the inputs.

In every case the trader is paid for solving a real mismatch, and in every case the price level itself is hedged out so that the move is about the *spread*, not the direction. Let us put numbers on the simplest version.

#### Worked example: a basic geographic arbitrage on a crude cargo

Suppose you are a crude trader. In one port, a producer is selling crude at \$80 a barrel because the local market is glutted and there is nowhere nearby to send it. Across the water, a refinery needs that exact grade and the going rate there is \$83 a barrel. You charter a tanker; the freight, insurance, port fees, and financing cost come to about \$1 a barrel.

Step one: you buy 1 million barrels at \$80, a cargo worth \$80 million. Step two: you ship it, paying \$1 a barrel, or \$1 million of cost. Step three: you sell it to the refinery at \$83, receiving \$83 million. Your gross spread is \$83 minus \$80, or \$3 a barrel. Subtract the \$1 of cost and you keep \$2 a barrel. On 1 million barrels that is \$2 million of profit on a single cargo.

Notice three things. First, you never bet on whether oil goes up or down; you captured a *location* spread of \$3 that existed at the moment you transacted. Second, the profit per barrel (\$2) is tiny next to the price per barrel (\$80) — that is the thin margin that defines this industry. Third, you tied up \$80 million to make \$2 million, which is why you will want to fund that \$80 million with a bank's money, not your own. The intuition: a commodity trader gets paid a small fee per unit for bridging a real-world gap, and makes it large only by doing it on enormous volume.

Now multiply that across thousands of cargoes a year, across oil, fuel, gas, metals, and grain, and you have the business model of a major trading house. The per-unit margin stays small; the volume is the whole point.

## The majors: who trades what, and the niche each one owns

The world of large commodity traders is surprisingly concentrated. A handful of firms dominate, and each one has a distinct lane. The diagram below lays them out by commodity class and by the particular edge each firm owns.

![Matrix of Vitol, Glencore, Trafigura, and Cargill against energy, metals, agriculture, and their niche](/imgs/blogs/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura-2.png)

**Vitol** is the giant of oil. Privately held and headquartered in Switzerland (with deep roots in Rotterdam, where it was founded in 1966), Vitol trades enormous volumes of crude and refined products and in some recent years has reported revenue well above \$200 billion (approximate, varies sharply with oil prices). It is a relatively pure trading firm: it owns some storage, refining, and infrastructure, but its core identity is moving energy, not digging it out of the ground. Because it is private and owned by its partners and employees, it discloses very little, and that opacity is the norm for the industry rather than the exception.

**Glencore** is the one that broke the secrecy mold, and it is structurally different from the others. Glencore traces its lineage to the trading empire built by Marc Rich (more on that controversial figure later), and it pairs a giant trading arm with its *own* mines. This **mine-to-market** integration is its niche: Glencore both produces metals and coal (copper, cobalt, zinc, nickel, coal, and more) and trades them, so it captures margin at the production stage and the trading stage. In 2011 Glencore did something unusual for the industry: it listed its shares on the public stock market through an initial public offering, an **IPO**, which is the first sale of a company's shares to the public. That made Glencore far more transparent than its private peers and gave the world its first real look inside a commodity house's finances. It also made Glencore the most scrutinized of the bunch, which matters when we get to the scandals.

**Trafigura** is the other oil-and-metals heavyweight, founded in 1993 by former associates of Marc Rich after his empire fragmented. It trades crude, refined products, liquefied natural gas, and metals, and it has grown extraordinarily fast, at times rivaling Vitol in oil volume. Like Vitol it is private, owned mostly by its senior employees, and famously tight-lipped. Trafigura's niche is aggressive, fast expansion across the energy and metals lanes, paired with a willingness to operate in difficult markets that more conservative firms avoid.

**Cargill** and **ADM** (Archer Daniels Midland) are the agricultural counterparts. Cargill, founded in 1865 and still family-owned, is one of the largest privately held companies in America and a central player in the global trade of grain, soybeans, vegetable oils, and other food commodities. Its niche is the **farm-gate-to-port** logistics chain: collecting crops from thousands of farms, storing and transporting them through elevators and barges and ships, and delivering them to processors and importers around the world. ADM plays a similar role with more emphasis on processing. Together with two other firms, Bunge and Louis Dreyfus, the agricultural majors are collectively nicknamed the **ABCD** group (ADM, Bunge, Cargill, Dreyfus), and they sit at the center of the world's food flows much as Vitol and Trafigura sit at the center of its energy flows.

Every traded commodity falls into one of three broad classes, and the firms above sort themselves across those classes. The tree below shows the taxonomy.

![Tree of commodity classes splitting into energy, metals, and agriculture with examples under each](/imgs/blogs/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura-7.png)

The first class is **energy**: crude oil, refined fuels like gasoline and diesel and jet fuel, natural gas (including liquefied natural gas, or LNG, which is gas chilled into a liquid so it can be shipped), and coal. The second is **metals**: copper, nickel, zinc, aluminum, cobalt, and precious metals like gold. The third is **agriculture** (often called *softs* for the soft commodities): grain, soybeans, sugar, coffee, cocoa, cotton. Each class has its own logistics (pipelines and tankers and storage tanks for energy; warehouses and the London Metal Exchange for metals; elevators and silos and barges for agriculture) and its own dominant traders. A firm's edge is usually deep specialization in the logistics of one or two classes, not breadth across all three.

That single fact explains most of the secrecy we will dwell on later.

## How they make money: arbitrage, storage, and the thin-margin machine

We have seen the simplest geographic arbitrage. Now let us widen it, because the time-arbitrage version — the **storage trade** — is one of the most elegant and most reliable ways these firms make money, and it is worth a careful worked example.

Recall contango: the futures price for later delivery is higher than the spot price for now. When a market is in steep contango, the future is paying you to hold the commodity. If you can buy the physical commodity cheaply today, store it cheaply, and simultaneously *sell* a futures contract at the higher future price, you have locked in a profit that does not depend on where the price goes — you only need the storage to cost less than the contango spread. This is the storage trade, sometimes called a **cash-and-carry**.

#### Worked example: a contango storage trade in crude oil

Suppose crude is trading at a spot price of \$60 a barrel, and the futures contract for delivery six months from now is trading at \$66 a barrel. The market is in steep contango: the six-month future is \$6 above spot. You can rent storage (a tank, or even a chartered tanker sitting at anchor) for \$3 a barrel over those six months, and your financing cost on the money tied up is another \$1 a barrel.

Here is the trade. Today, you buy 1 million barrels at the spot price of \$60, spending \$60 million. At the very same moment, you sell 1 million barrels of the six-month futures at \$66, which obligates you to deliver that oil in six months for \$66 a barrel. You put the oil into storage. Six months later, you take the oil out of storage and deliver it against your futures contract, receiving \$66 a barrel.

Now tally it. You bought at \$60 and you are delivering at \$66, a gross spread of \$6 a barrel. Your storage cost \$3 and your financing cost \$1, totaling \$4 of cost. You keep \$6 minus \$4, or \$2 a barrel. On 1 million barrels that is \$2 million, locked in on day one, regardless of whether oil rose to \$80 or crashed to \$30 in the meantime — because you already agreed to sell at \$66 via the futures. The intuition: in steep contango, storage turns a parking lot for oil into a money machine, and the only real risk is that storage or financing costs more than the contango spread you captured.

This is exactly the trade that lit up in 2020, and we will return to that episode in the real-markets section. For now, notice the pattern: again the trader is not betting on direction. It locked the spread on day one. The profit is a fee for performing a real economic service — moving the commodity through *time* from a moment of glut to a moment of need — just as geographic arbitrage moves it through *space*.

Quality arbitrage works the same way. A refinery needs crude with a particular sulfur content and density. A trader can buy a cheap heavy, sour crude and a cheap light, sweet crude, blend them in a tank to hit the exact specification the refinery wants, and sell the blend for more than the weighted cost of the inputs, because the blended barrel is now *usable* where neither input was. The margin is the value the blending created, minus the cost of doing it.

Across all three flavors, the economics share one brutal feature: the margin per unit is tiny relative to the value flowing through. The stack diagram below shows where the money goes.

![Stack showing revenue passing through, costs, a thin net margin kept, and thin equity funded by bank debt](/imgs/blogs/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura-5.png)

The top layer is **revenue**, but here is the trap in that word. When a trader buys oil for \$80 million and sells it for \$83 million, it books something close to the full \$83 million as revenue, even though the oil was never really "its" product — it was just passing through. So a firm can report \$200 billion of revenue while the value it actually *added* (the spreads it captured) is a small fraction of that. Beneath revenue sit the **costs**: freight, insurance, financing interest, storage, port fees, and the occasional loss on a deal that went wrong. What is left, the **net margin**, is often just 1 to 2 percent of revenue. The bottom layer is the punchline: because the firm keeps so little per dollar of revenue and ties up so much capital in cargoes, it funds those cargoes with a thin sliver of its own equity and a mountain of short-term bank debt. Let us make that concrete.

#### Worked example: the thin-margin economics of a \$200 billion trader

Imagine a major oil trader pushes \$200 billion of revenue through its books in a year. Suppose its net margin, after every cost, is 1.5 percent. Then its net profit is 1.5 percent of \$200 billion, which is \$3 billion.

Three billion dollars of profit sounds enormous, and it is. But look at the ratio. The firm handled \$200 billion of value to keep \$3 billion. For every \$100 of oil that flowed through, it kept \$1.50. If margins compressed to 1 percent — which happens in calm, oversupplied markets when location and time spreads narrow — the same \$200 billion of revenue would yield only \$2 billion of profit, a third less, from doing the same amount of work. And if a few large deals soured, or freight costs spiked, the margin could be thinner still.

The intuition: a commodity trader is a high-volume, low-margin machine, more like a global wholesaler than a hedge fund, and its profitability is exquisitely sensitive to small changes in the spreads it captures and the costs it pays. That sensitivity is exactly why the firms obsess over logistics efficiency and cheap financing, and why a market that suddenly offers wide spreads (a crisis, a dislocation, a war) can be wildly profitable for them even as it is painful for everyone else.

## The central role of bank trade finance

We have said it twice now: these firms run on borrowed money. It is time to look at *how* the borrowing works, because trade finance is the load-bearing wall of the whole industry, and understanding it is the key to understanding both the firms' resilience and their fragility.

Recall the problem. A single cargo of crude is worth tens of millions of dollars. A major trader might have hundreds of cargoes in motion at once. No trading firm has hundreds of billions of dollars of its own cash sitting around to pay for all of them. So instead the trader borrows against each cargo, and the cargo itself is the collateral. The instrument that makes this work is the **letter of credit**.

A letter of credit (often abbreviated **LC**) is a promise from a bank to pay a seller a specified amount once the seller presents documents proving that the goods were shipped as agreed. The producer selling the oil does not have to trust the trader's creditworthiness; it trusts the *bank's*. The bank, in turn, holds a claim on the cargo and will be repaid when the cargo is sold downstream. The whole arrangement is **self-liquidating**: the loan exists only for the life of one cargo, and the cargo's sale pays it back. The graph below traces the money.

![Graph showing a bank, a trader, a producer, a cargo, a buyer, and repayment of the loan plus margin](/imgs/blogs/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura-4.png)

Read the flow. The trader arranges a deal and asks its bank to issue a letter of credit (top of the diagram). The bank, on the strength of that LC, pays the producer when the cargo is loaded and shipping documents are presented — say \$50 million for the cargo. The trader meanwhile owns the cargo as it sails. When it is delivered, the buyer pays — say \$52 million. That incoming \$52 million repays the bank's \$50 million plus financing, and the roughly \$2 million spread is the trader's margin. Crucially, the trader's *own* cash was barely involved at any point. It supplied a thin layer of equity and its expertise; the bank supplied the bulk of the money, secured against a real, salable cargo. Let us put exact numbers on it.

#### Worked example: a letter of credit financing a \$50 million cargo

A trader strikes a deal to buy a cargo of crude for \$50 million and has already lined up a refinery buyer who will pay \$52 million on delivery. The trader does not want to (and could not easily) hand over \$50 million of its own cash.

Step one: the trader asks its bank to issue a letter of credit for \$50 million in favor of the producer. The bank charges a fee for this — say 0.5 percent annualized, and the cargo will be financed for two months, so the fee is roughly 0.5 percent times two-twelfths of \$50 million, which is about \$42,000. The bank also charges interest on the actual funds it advances. Suppose the financing rate is 6 percent annualized; over two months on \$50 million that interest is 6 percent times two-twelfths times \$50 million, which is \$500,000.

Step two: when the cargo loads, the bank pays the producer \$50 million against the shipping documents. The trader has now committed essentially none of its own cash, only the fee and the obligation to repay.

Step three: two months later the cargo is delivered, the buyer pays \$52 million, the bank takes back its \$50 million plus the \$500,000 of interest, and the trader is left with \$52 million minus \$50 million minus \$500,000 minus \$42,000, which is roughly \$1.46 million of profit. The trader earned \$1.46 million on a \$50 million cargo while putting up almost no cash of its own.

The intuition: trade finance lets a lightly-capitalized firm control cargoes worth many multiples of its equity, because each loan is short, self-liquidating, and secured by a real cargo the bank can seize and sell if anything goes wrong. This is the magic that makes the thin-margin model viable — and, as we will see, the dependency that makes it fragile when banks suddenly pull back.

A few important wrinkles. First, the security is only as good as the cargo and the documents. Fraud in commodity trade finance — fake warehouse receipts, the same cargo pledged to several banks at once, cargoes that do not exist — has periodically burned lenders badly, which is why banks pulled back hard after a string of frauds and failures around 2020. Second, the financing is overwhelmingly *short-term and revolving*: the firm continuously rolls over billions of dollars of credit lines as cargoes turn over. That is efficient in calm times but means the firm must refinance constantly, so a freeze in credit markets hits it almost immediately. Third, the relationship between the big trading houses and a small club of trade-finance banks is deep and mutually dependent; when those banks get nervous, the whole industry feels it at once.

## Hedging a physical position: the move that makes it all safe

We have repeatedly claimed that traders strip out price risk and keep only the logistics margin. The mechanism is hedging with futures, and it deserves its own worked example because it is the difference between a stable wholesaler and a casino.

The setup: you own a physical cargo, so you are long the commodity. If its price falls before you sell it, you lose. You do not want that bet; you only want the spread. So you sell futures against the cargo. If the price falls, your physical cargo loses value, but your short futures position gains an almost equal amount, and the two cancel. The before-and-after diagram shows the difference starkly.

![Before-after comparison of an unhedged cargo losing money versus a hedged cargo keeping its margin](/imgs/blogs/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura-3.png)

On the left is the unhedged cargo. You buy 1 million barrels at \$80, expecting to sell higher. While the oil is in transit, the price falls to \$75. Your cargo, bought for \$80 million, is now worth only \$75 million, an unrealized loss of \$5 million that swamps the \$2 million arbitrage you were chasing. The price moved against you, and because you did nothing to protect yourself, the whole trade is underwater. On the right is the hedged cargo, and the outcome is completely different.

#### Worked example: a futures hedge protecting a cargo when prices fall

You buy 1 million barrels of physical crude at \$80, the same as before. But this time, at the same moment, you sell 1 million barrels of crude futures at \$82 (futures for near-term delivery typically trade close to spot; we will use \$82 to keep the arithmetic clean). You are now long 1 million physical barrels and short 1 million futures barrels.

Now the price falls. By the time your cargo reaches the buyer, crude has dropped by \$5. Your physical cargo is worth \$5 a barrel less than you paid, an unrealized loss of \$5 million on the physical side. But your short futures position has *gained* \$5 a barrel, because you agreed to sell at \$82 and can now buy back the futures at \$77 to close the position — a \$5-a-barrel gain, or \$5 million. The two move in opposite directions and cancel: minus \$5 million on the cargo, plus \$5 million on the futures, net zero from the price move.

What is left is the spread you actually came for. You bought physical at \$80 and your hedge was placed at \$82, and once everything is sold and the futures are closed, you retain roughly the \$2-a-barrel margin between your physical purchase and the level you locked — about \$2 million on the cargo, give or take costs. The price swing, which would have cost you \$5 million unhedged, has been neutralized. The intuition: hedging does not make you money on the price move; it *removes* the price move entirely, leaving only the logistics spread you set out to earn — which is exactly what a logistics business wants.

There is a catch hiding in this happy story, and it is the catch that detonated in 2022. The physical loss and the futures gain do not settle at the same time. A futures exchange demands cash collateral — called **margin** — to back your futures position, and it demands *more* cash, called a **margin call**, whenever the position moves against you on paper, even if your offsetting physical cargo has not yet been sold. So a trader who is perfectly hedged in economic terms can still face a sudden, brutal demand for cash if futures prices spike, because the futures leg loses on paper *today* while the physical gain arrives only *later*. Hold that thought; it is the key to the energy-crisis story.

## The secrecy: why you have never seen inside these firms

Now we can address the word in the title: secretive. Why do firms that move the lifeblood of the global economy disclose almost nothing?

The simplest answer is **ownership structure**. Most of the big trading houses are **private partnerships** or closely held companies, owned by their senior employees and founders rather than by public shareholders. A public company that lists its shares on an exchange must publish detailed audited financial statements, disclose risks, and answer to outside investors and securities regulators. A private partnership has no such obligation. Vitol, Trafigura, Cargill, and others have for decades chosen to remain private precisely so they can avoid that scrutiny, retain control, and keep their trading strategies, counterparties, and margins out of competitors' view. Glencore is the great exception, having listed in 2011, which is why nearly everything the public knows in granular financial detail about a commodity house comes from Glencore's filings.

There are real business reasons for the secrecy beyond mere preference. In a low-margin business, your edge *is* information: which producer is desperate to sell, which buyer is short and will pay up, where a bottleneck is forming, what blend a refinery secretly needs. Broadcasting any of that to competitors destroys the edge. The partnership model also aligns incentives tightly — the people taking the risk own the firm — and lets the firms make fast, large bets without explaining themselves to a quarterly-earnings audience.

But the secrecy has a darker side, and it is the reason these firms periodically erupt into the news. When a firm discloses almost nothing, operates across dozens of jurisdictions with very different rules, deals with state-owned producers and governments of every stripe, and pays for cargoes through opaque webs of intermediaries, the opportunities for sanctions evasion, bribery of foreign officials, and other misconduct multiply, and the external checks are weak. For decades the industry's culture treated "facilitation payments" and creative dealings with pariah states as the cost of doing business in hard places. As global anti-corruption and sanctions enforcement tightened in the 2010s, that legacy caught up with several of the majors, in settlements that ran to billions of dollars.

It is worth being precise about what secrecy does and does not mean here. These firms are not criminal enterprises; they are mostly legitimate, sophisticated logistics businesses performing a genuine service that the world relies on. But the combination of enormous scale, minimal disclosure, and operations in the murkiest corners of the global economy means that *when* misconduct happens, it tends to be large, durable, and invisible until an enforcement action drags it into the open. The next section walks through the most consequential of those cases.

## The controversies: sanctions, bribery, and the legacy of Marc Rich

You cannot understand the culture of this industry without understanding **Marc Rich**, the trader from whose empire both Glencore and Trafigura descend. Rich pioneered the modern independent oil-trading model in the 1970s, trading aggressively with countries and counterparties that the established oil majors avoided. He was indicted in the United States in 1983 on charges including trading with Iran during the hostage crisis (in violation of sanctions) and tax evasion, fled to Switzerland, spent years as a fugitive, and was controversially pardoned by President Clinton in 2001. The firm he built, Marc Rich + Co, was eventually bought out by its management and renamed Glencore; other former associates founded Trafigura. The willingness to operate where others would not — and the legal jeopardy that came with it — is part of the industry's DNA.

That DNA produced a series of modern scandals. The most significant is the **Glencore bribery settlement of 2022**. After multi-year investigations by authorities in the United States, the United Kingdom, and Brazil, Glencore pleaded guilty to bribery and market-manipulation charges and agreed to pay penalties totaling roughly \$1.5 billion (approximate, across jurisdictions). Prosecutors described a scheme in which Glencore paid tens of millions of dollars in bribes to officials in several countries in Africa and Latin America to secure oil cargoes and favorable terms, and separately manipulated fuel-oil benchmark prices. It was one of the largest corporate corruption resolutions ever in the commodity sector, and it pulled back the curtain on practices that the industry's secrecy had long kept hidden.

**Trafigura** has faced its own serious episodes. The best known is the 2006 **Probo Koala** affair, in which toxic waste linked to a cargo the firm was processing was dumped around Abidjan in Ivory Coast, causing widespread illness and public outrage; Trafigura ultimately paid large settlements and compensation while contesting the science. More recently, Trafigura disclosed a large loss tied to an alleged fraud in its Mongolian oil operations and has been entangled in bribery investigations of its own. These cases share a pattern: a sprawling, opaque operation in a difficult jurisdiction, intermediaries whose conduct is hard to police, and a reckoning that arrives years later.

There is also the recurring theme of **sanctions**. Because commodity traders deal in exactly the goods that sanctions try to choke off — oil above all — they live perpetually at the boundary of what is permitted. Some have been accused of skirting sanctions by routing cargoes through intermediaries, mislabeling origins, or trading just inside the letter of evolving rules. Enforcement has tightened, and the firms now spend heavily on compliance, but the structural temptation remains: a sanctioned barrel sells at a steep discount, and the spread for anyone willing to move it is enormous. We will see the modern version of this in the wartime oil-rerouting episode below.

The honest summary is this. The service these firms provide is real and largely benign: the world needs its oil, metal, and grain moved, and somebody has to do it. But the same features that make them efficient — secrecy, scale, presence in the hardest markets, deep ties to governments and state producers — are exactly the features that, left unchecked, breed corruption and sanctions evasion. The settlements of recent years are the system's attempt to impose external discipline on businesses that historically had almost none.

## The 2022 energy crisis and the margin-call scare

Now we arrive at the episode that nearly broke parts of the industry and that ties together everything above: leverage, hedging, margin, and the dependence on bank credit. To see it clearly we need to walk through the timeline, and then the mechanics.

![Timeline of commodity-trading milestones from Vitol's founding through the 2022 crisis](/imgs/blogs/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura-6.png)

The timeline runs from Vitol's founding in 1966, through the rise of the Marc Rich empire in the 1970s that seeded Glencore, the spinout of Trafigura in 1993, Glencore's landmark IPO in 2011, and then two shocks clustered in 2022: the London Metal Exchange nickel blow-up and the Glencore bribery plea. The 2022 cluster is no coincidence. When Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, commodity prices exploded and then whipsawed violently. For an industry that hedges its physical positions with futures, violent price moves are not just a market story — they are a *cash* story, because of the margin mechanism we flagged earlier.

Here is the trap, restated. A trader that owns physical commodities and has hedged by selling futures is economically protected: a price rise that loses money on the short futures is offset by the rising value of the physical cargo it owns. But the futures exchange does not care about the physical cargo. When prices spike, the short futures position shows a paper loss *today*, and the exchange issues a margin call demanding cash *immediately*. The matching gain on the physical side is real but will only turn into cash *later*, when the cargo is sold. So a perfectly hedged firm can face an enormous, sudden demand for cash that it does not yet have, simply because the two legs of the hedge settle at different times. When prices moved as far and as fast as they did in 2022, those margin calls ran into the *tens of billions of dollars* across the industry.

#### Worked example: a margin call on a hedged position during a price spike

Suppose a trader owns 10 million barrels of physical crude, bought to be sold over the coming months, and has hedged the lot by selling 10 million barrels of futures at \$90. The firm is economically flat: if prices rise, the physical gains and the futures lose, in equal measure.

Now prices spike by \$30 a barrel in a matter of days, to \$120, because of a supply shock. On the short futures position, that is a paper loss of \$30 a barrel times 10 million barrels, which is \$300 million. The exchange demands that \$300 million in cash as a variation-margin call, and it demands it within a day or two. Meanwhile the physical crude the firm owns has *gained* \$30 a barrel, also \$300 million — but that gain is locked inside cargoes that will not be sold and turned into cash for weeks or months.

So the firm's net economic position is unchanged (it is still hedged), but it must find \$300 million of cash *right now* to meet the margin call, against a gain it cannot yet monetize. Multiply this across a firm's entire hedge book during a market-wide spike and the cash demand becomes existential. The intuition: hedging protects your profit but creates a brutal timing mismatch in your *cash*, and in a violent enough market that timing mismatch — not any bad bet — is what can sink a fully-hedged firm.

This is exactly what happened in 2022. The largest European energy traders, including firms central to keeping the lights on across the continent, faced collective margin calls so large that they warned governments they might not be able to fund them. Several pleaded for, and some received, state-backed credit lines and liquidity support — a remarkable moment in which private, secretive trading firms had to be backstopped by taxpayers to keep the energy system functioning. The firms were not insolvent; they were illiquid, caught in the timing trap. But illiquidity can kill just as surely as insolvency if the cash demand comes due before the offsetting gain arrives.

And the most spectacular single blow-up of 2022 was in metals, not energy: the **London Metal Exchange nickel squeeze**, which is worth its own treatment in the real-markets section because it shows the same mechanics in their most extreme form.

## Common misconceptions

Let us clear away the beliefs that most people hold about this industry, because each wrong belief hides a real mechanism.

**Misconception 1: Commodity traders are basically speculators betting on prices.** This is the deepest and most common error. The core business is physical: buying, moving, storing, blending, and delivering real cargoes, and capturing the spread between locations and times. The price level itself is usually *hedged out* with futures, precisely so the firm is *not* betting on direction. Speculation exists at the margins, but the bread and butter is logistics-plus-arbitrage. A trader who relied on guessing prices would not survive the thin margins; one who reliably captures spreads will.

**Misconception 2: Because they are huge, these firms must be hugely profitable.** Their *revenue* is huge, but revenue mostly reflects the value of the commodities passing through, not value the firm created. Net margins are often just 1 to 2 percent. A firm can run \$200 billion of revenue and keep only \$2 to \$4 billion. They are high-volume, low-margin wholesalers, and their profits swing sharply with the width of the spreads they can capture, which is why crisis years can be far more profitable than calm ones.

**Misconception 3: They use their own money to buy all that oil and grain.** Almost none of it. The cargoes are funded overwhelmingly by short-term bank credit, secured against the cargo itself through letters of credit and revolving trade-finance lines. The firm supplies a thin layer of equity and its expertise. This is why a freeze in bank credit, not a bad price bet, is the thing most likely to kill one of them.

**Misconception 4: Hedging makes a trader completely safe.** Hedging removes *price* risk on the economic value of a position, but it introduces a *liquidity* risk: the futures leg can demand cash via margin calls long before the offsetting physical gain turns into cash. A fully hedged firm can still be brought to its knees by margin calls in a violent market, which is exactly what threatened European energy traders in 2022.

**Misconception 5: These firms are shadowy because they are doing something illegal.** Mostly they are secretive because they are *private partnerships* with no disclosure obligation, and because in a low-margin business, information is the edge and broadcasting it destroys profit. The secrecy does create cover for misconduct, and real scandals (the Glencore bribery plea, the Trafigura cases) have followed. But the default explanation for the opacity is ordinary business structure and competitive secrecy, not criminality.

**Misconception 6: If these firms vanished, the market would route around them easily.** They perform genuinely hard, capital-intensive logistics — chartering ships, navigating documentation across dozens of jurisdictions, financing tens of billions of dollars of cargoes, blending to specification, managing storage. Producers and consumers *could* in principle do this themselves, but most do not want to and are not good at it. The traders exist because the coordination problem they solve is real and expensive, which is why even governments, in 2022, chose to backstop them rather than replace them.

## How it shows up in real markets

Enough theory. Here are concrete episodes where commodity trading houses sat at the center of events you may have heard about, each one a live demonstration of the mechanics above.

### The 2020 oil-price crash and the great storage trade

In the spring of 2020, the pandemic collapsed demand for oil almost overnight while production kept flowing. The world ran out of places to put the surplus, and the market lurched into the steepest contango in memory — at one infamous moment in April 2020, a US crude futures contract actually traded *below zero*, meaning sellers paid buyers to take oil off their hands because there was nowhere to store it. For commodity trading houses, this was a once-in-a-generation **storage trade**. Firms that could secure tank capacity, or charter the huge tankers known as VLCCs to use as floating storage, bought cheap spot crude, sold far more expensive futures against it, and parked the oil. As demand recovered over the following year and the contango unwound, those stored barrels were delivered at the high locked prices. The trade was almost exactly the worked example earlier in this post, executed on a massive scale, and it made the storage-capable traders some of their best profits in years — a vivid illustration that the firms make the most when the rest of the world is in distress.

### The 2022 LME nickel short squeeze

In March 2022, the price of nickel on the London Metal Exchange roughly tripled in a matter of days, briefly exceeding \$100,000 a ton, in one of the wildest moves any major commodity has ever made. At the center was a large producer that held a huge *short* position in nickel futures — a hedge against the metal it produced. As the Russia-Ukraine shock sent nickel soaring, that short position generated paper losses and margin calls on a scale that threatened the holder and, through it, the exchange's clearing system. The LME took the extraordinary step of *cancelling* hours of trades and suspending the nickel market, a decision that sparked lawsuits and a lasting crisis of confidence in the exchange. The episode is the purest illustration of the margin trap: a hedger, economically protected by the metal it produced, was nearly destroyed by the *cash* demands of its futures hedge during a violent spike, exactly as our worked example predicts.

### Glencore's 2022 bribery plea

Also in 2022, Glencore pleaded guilty in the United States, the United Kingdom, and Brazil to bribery and market-manipulation charges and agreed to penalties totaling roughly \$1.5 billion (approximate, across jurisdictions). US prosecutors detailed how the firm paid tens of millions of dollars in bribes to officials across several countries to win oil business and favorable terms, and separately manipulated fuel-oil price benchmarks. Because Glencore is publicly listed, the case produced an unusual level of documented detail about how a commodity house actually operated in hard markets, and it stands as the clearest evidence that the industry's secrecy had long sheltered serious misconduct. It also marked a turning point: the era in which such practices were quietly tolerated as the cost of doing business is closing, replaced by heavy compliance and the threat of billion-dollar settlements.

### Wartime oil rerouting after 2022

When Western nations sanctioned Russian oil following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, they did not stop that oil from flowing — they changed *where* it flowed. Russian crude that once went to Europe was rerouted, often at steep discounts, to buyers in Asia, and a complex grey market of intermediaries, reflagged tankers, and opaque trading entities grew up to move it. This is geographic arbitrage and sanctions risk fused into one: the discount on sanctioned barrels created an enormous spread for anyone willing and legally able to move them, and the flows physically reorganized around the new political map. The established majors mostly stepped back from Russian barrels under compliance and reputational pressure, but the episode showed how quickly the world's commodity flows re-route around political shocks, and how the spread on a dislocated barrel pulls cargoes toward whoever will carry them.

### The 2022 European energy-trader liquidity scare

As gas and power prices in Europe spiked and gyrated through 2022, the large energy traders and utilities that hedge their physical positions with futures faced margin calls running into the tens of billions of dollars collectively. Economically they were hedged and solvent; in *cash* terms they were squeezed, because the futures legs of their hedges demanded immediate collateral while the offsetting physical value was locked in supply they had not yet sold. Several firms and national champions warned governments that the liquidity strain threatened their ability to keep operating, and a number of European governments responded with state-backed credit lines and emergency liquidity facilities. It was a startling moment: private, secretive trading firms whose business the public barely understands had to be backstopped by taxpayers to keep Europe's energy system running through the winter. The scare is the definitive real-world proof of this post's central warning — that the deadly risk in this business is liquidity, not price, and that the firms' dependence on credit makes them a systemic concern when markets convulse.

## When this matters to you and further reading

Why should a curious non-specialist care about a handful of secretive trading firms? Because they sit on the supply chain of nearly everything you consume, and their plumbing decides, quietly, what you pay and whether the shelves and tanks stay full. When oil reroutes around a war, when a metal squeezes on an exchange, when European governments scramble to backstop energy traders through a winter, the prices you pay at the pump and the till move in response. Understanding that there is a layer of arbitrage-and-logistics firms between the producer and you — funded by banks, hedged with futures, and exposed to sudden cash demands — turns a lot of confusing headlines into a single coherent picture. It also reframes the recurring scandals: they are not random villainy but the predictable result of enormous scale meeting minimal disclosure in the hardest corners of the global economy.

The practical takeaways are modest but real. First, when you read that a trading house made record profits in a crisis year, you now know why: dislocations widen the spreads these firms capture, so chaos for the world can be a windfall for the intermediaries. Second, when you hear that a firm needed a government credit line despite being "hedged," you now know that hedging protects profit but not liquidity, and that the timing mismatch in margin is the real danger. Third, when a commodity house settles a bribery case for a billion dollars, you can place it: the secrecy that gives these firms their competitive edge is the same secrecy that, unpoliced, breeds the misconduct.

The deepest point to carry away is the one we opened with. A commodity trading house is not a casino and not a villain. It is a logistics-plus-arbitrage business that earns a sliver of margin for moving the world's raw materials from where they are cheap to where they are needed, funded almost entirely by the banking system, with the price risk hedged away and only a thin layer of its own capital at stake. That model is enormously useful, quietly powerful, and structurally fragile in exactly one way: it lives and dies on access to cash and credit. Master that single idea and the secretive giants stop being mysterious and start looking like what they are — the indispensable, under-scrutinized middlemen of the physical world.
