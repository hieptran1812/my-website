> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The IMF is an emergency lender to whole countries that have run out of foreign money, and it attaches policy "strings" to its loans; the World Bank is a long-horizon lender that finances roads, power grids, and schools. Both were born at the same 1944 conference, both are run by the same rich countries, and both have spent eighty years being praised as firefighters and condemned as enforcers.
>
> - When a country can no longer pay its foreign bills, almost no private lender will touch it. The IMF will, in exchange for the country agreeing to fix the policies the Fund blames for the crisis. That trade is called *conditionality*.
> - The two institutions split the work: the *Fund* handles short-run crises (months to a few years), the *Bank* handles long-run development (a decade to forty years). They are siblings, not the same thing.
> - Voting power follows money. A country's *quota* sets both how much it contributes and how many votes it gets, and the United States holds enough of the votes (~16.5%) to veto the biggest decisions single-handedly.
> - The signature controversy is *austerity*: critics like Joseph Stiglitz argue the Fund's standard demand to cut spending and raise rates in the middle of a slump made crises like Asia 1997 deeper, not shorter.
> - The number to remember: in August 2021 the IMF created and handed out about \$650 billion of new reserve assets called *Special Drawing Rights* — the largest such allocation in history — to help countries through the pandemic.

Here is a question almost nobody can answer cleanly: when an entire country goes broke — not a company, not a bank, but a *government* that can no longer pay what it owes abroad — who exactly does it borrow from? A person with bad credit has payday lenders. A failing company has bankruptcy court. But there is no bankruptcy court for nations, and when a country is in a full-blown crisis, the private banks that were happy to lend last year are sprinting for the exits. The answer, for most of the world since 1944, has been a single institution in Washington, D.C.: the International Monetary Fund. And right next door, occupying a completely different role that people constantly confuse with the Fund's, sits the World Bank.

This article is about those two institutions — what they actually are, why they exist, how they wield a kind of power no private lender ever could, and why, for all the good they have done, they are among the most resented organizations on earth in much of the developing world. The diagram above is the mental model: one 1944 conference, two siblings with two different jobs, a string of crises across eighty years, and a steadily growing list of rivals trying to build an alternative.

![Timeline from Bretton Woods 1944 through the 2021 SDR allocation](/imgs/blogs/imf-and-world-bank-1.png)

By the end, you will be able to read a news headline like "Argentina secures \$57 billion IMF program with fiscal conditions" and understand every word of it: what a balance-of-payments crisis is, why the Fund is the lender of last resort, what the "fiscal conditions" demand and why they are so bitterly fought over, how the money is structured, who controls the institution making the decision, and why a growing number of countries would rather borrow from China than walk through the Fund's door. None of this assumes you know any economics. We build it from zero.

## Foundations: the building blocks you need

Before we can dissect either institution, we need to define every moving part. None of this assumes a finance background. If you already know what a balance-of-payments crisis is, skim; if not, read carefully, because the entire story turns on these nine ideas.

**Foreign currency, and why a country can run out of it.** A country prints its own money. Vietnam prints the dong, Argentina prints the peso, Thailand prints the baht. A government can never literally run out of *its own* currency — it owns the printing press. But here is the catch: most of what a country buys from the rest of the world (oil, machinery, medicine, food) must be paid for in a currency the seller will accept, which in practice means US dollars, euros, or a handful of others. And much of what a country *borrowed* from foreigners must also be repaid in those currencies. A government cannot print dollars. So a country absolutely *can* run out of the foreign money it needs to pay its foreign bills, even while its own printing press runs fine. That is the trap the entire IMF exists to address.

**The balance of payments.** This is just a country's financial scorecard against the rest of the world over a period of time — every dollar that flows in (from exports, from tourists, from foreign investment, from loans) and every dollar that flows out (for imports, for debt repayment, for citizens sending money abroad). A *balance-of-payments crisis* — the phrase you will see again and again — happens when far more foreign money is leaving than arriving, the country's stockpile of foreign currency is draining toward zero, and it is about to be unable to pay for essential imports or service its foreign debt. It is the national version of being unable to make rent: the lights are about to go out.

**Foreign-exchange reserves.** These are the hard-currency war chest a central bank holds — mostly US dollars and dollar bonds — precisely so it can pay foreign bills and defend its currency. Reserves are the ammunition. The crucial fact about ammunition is that it is finite. When reserves approach zero, the crisis is no longer theoretical; it is happening.

**A currency / sovereign crisis.** A *currency crisis* is when a country's exchange rate collapses — its money suddenly buys far less abroad than it did a week ago. A *sovereign crisis* (or sovereign-debt crisis) is when a government can no longer pay the debt it owes, mostly to foreigners, and faces *default* — failing to make a scheduled payment. The two usually travel together: a collapsing currency makes foreign-currency debt explode in local terms, which pushes the government toward default, which scares more money out, which collapses the currency further. It is a doom loop.

**The IMF as lender of last resort to nations.** A *lender of last resort* is whoever will lend when no one else will. Inside a single country, the central bank plays this role for its banks — when a healthy bank suddenly cannot borrow, the central bank lends so a temporary cash crunch does not become a collapse. But who plays that role for a *country* that has run out of dollars? No private bank will, because lending to a country mid-crisis looks like throwing money into a fire. The International Monetary Fund was built to be exactly this: an emergency lender of hard currency to governments in a balance-of-payments crisis. It is a fire department for national finances, funded by its member governments.

**Conditionality.** IMF loans are not gifts and not no-strings bailouts. They come with *conditionality*: the borrowing country must agree to a list of policy changes the Fund judges necessary to fix the underlying problem and ensure the loan gets repaid. Typical conditions: cut the government's budget deficit, raise interest rates, devalue or float the currency, close insolvent banks, cut subsidies, sometimes privatize state companies or open markets. Conditionality is the heart of the controversy. Supporters call it the discipline that makes the money work. Critics call it a rich-world committee dictating the domestic policy of a desperate poor country.

**Austerity.** A subset of conditionality. *Austerity* means cutting government spending and/or raising taxes to shrink a budget deficit — often sharply, often fast. The Fund frequently demands it because a country in a balance-of-payments crisis is usually spending more than it earns, and closing that gap is part of the cure. The fierce objection is timing: austerity slams the brakes on an economy that is already crashing, deepening the recession before any recovery arrives. We will spend a whole section on this fight.

**Quotas and voting power.** The IMF is owned by its 190-odd member countries. Each member is assigned a *quota* — roughly its financial stake, set by the size of its economy. The quota does three things at once: it sets how much money the country must contribute to the Fund's resources, it caps how much the country can normally borrow, and — critically — it determines how many *votes* the country gets. Bigger economy, bigger quota, more votes. This is why the IMF is not one-country-one-vote; it is closer to one-dollar-one-vote, and that single design choice explains most of the criticism about *whose* interests the institution serves.

**Special Drawing Rights (SDRs).** The strangest and most misunderstood thing the IMF does. An *SDR* is a reserve asset the IMF can create out of thin air and distribute to member countries in proportion to their quotas. It is not a currency you can spend at a shop; treat it as an international "claim on hard currency" — a member holding SDRs can exchange them with other members for usable dollars or euros. When the IMF wants to inject liquidity into the whole world at once (as in 2021), it does an *SDR allocation*: it credits every member's account with new SDRs, instantly boosting their reserves without anyone having to lend or repay. We will work an example of exactly how an allocation is split.

**The World Bank and development lending.** The Fund's sibling, born at the same conference, does a completely different job. The World Bank lends for *development*: long-term loans (and grants) to finance specific projects — a dam, a national power grid, a highway, a vaccination campaign, a school system — and to support the broad reforms meant to reduce poverty over decades. Where the Fund is the emergency room (stabilize the patient, days to a few years), the Bank is the long course of physical therapy (rebuild capacity, ten to forty years). The Bank's controversial tool — *structural adjustment programs*, big reform-conditioned loans pushed especially in the 1980s and 1990s — sits at the border between the two institutions and drew much of the same fire as IMF conditionality.

Hold all nine in mind. The rest of the article is just these pieces interacting: a country running out of foreign money, with finite reserves and a collapsing currency, forced to a lender of last resort that attaches conditions, inside an institution whose votes are owned by the rich countries, while its sibling lends for the long haul and a new set of rivals builds an alternative.

## Two siblings, two jobs: never confuse the Fund and the Bank

The single most common error — made by journalists, students, and politicians alike — is treating "the IMF and the World Bank" as one blurry thing, or using the names interchangeably. They are distinct institutions with distinct mandates, distinct instruments, distinct time horizons, and even distinct funding models. Getting the split clear is the foundation for everything else.

![Matrix comparing the IMF and World Bank by mandate, borrower, time frame, and funding](/imgs/blogs/imf-and-world-bank-2.png)

The cleanest way to hold it is the matrix above. The **IMF** is about *monetary and financial stability*. Its job is to make sure the international payments system keeps working: that countries can pay their foreign bills, that exchange-rate chaos does not spread, that a crisis in one place does not infect the whole world. It lends to *governments* (specifically their central banks) when those governments are in a balance-of-payments crisis, and it lends for the *short term* — months to typically three to five years — because the goal is to get the patient stable and off the support, not to keep them on it forever.

The **World Bank** is about *development*. Its job is the long, slow work of building the physical and institutional capacity of poorer countries: infrastructure, health, education, agriculture, the things that raise living standards over a generation. It lends for *specific projects and programs* over a *long horizon* — a typical Bank project loan runs fifteen to thirty-five years — because building a power grid and seeing it pay off takes decades, not quarters.

They are even funded differently, which most people never realize. The IMF's lending capacity comes mostly from the *quotas* its members pay in — a pool of national currencies and reserve assets that members contribute and can draw against. The World Bank, by contrast, mostly *borrows from global bond markets*: it has a top-tier AAA credit rating (backed by the guarantees of its member governments), it issues bonds to investors at low rates, and it lends the proceeds onward to developing countries at rates those countries could never get on their own. The Bank is, in effect, a giant credit-transformation machine: it borrows the rich world's cheap money and passes it to the developing world.

| | IMF — "the Fund" | World Bank — "the Bank" |
|---|---|---|
| **Core mandate** | Monetary & financial stability | Development & poverty reduction |
| **Lends to** | Governments in balance-of-payments crisis | Governments & projects in developing countries |
| **Typical loan** | Stand-by / extended arrangement, drawn in tranches | Project & program loans, plus grants |
| **Time horizon** | Months to ~3-5 years | ~15-40 years |
| **Conditionality** | Macro policy (deficit, rates, currency) | Project & sector reforms (structural adjustment) |
| **Funded by** | Member quotas (~\$1 trillion firepower) | Bond markets (AAA-rated) + member capital |
| **The unit** | Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) | US dollars (mostly) |
| **Headed by** | Traditionally a European | Traditionally an American |

That last row is its own scandal, and we will return to it: by an informal convention dating to 1944, the IMF is always led by a European and the World Bank always by an American — a carve-up of the top jobs between the two power blocs that has survived every reform attempt for eighty years.

## Where they came from: Bretton Woods, 1944

To understand why these institutions are shaped the way they are — why the votes are weighted, why a European runs one and an American the other, why the dollar sits at the center — you have to go back to a hotel in the New Hampshire mountains in July 1944.

World War II was not over, but the Allies could see the end, and they were determined not to repeat the catastrophe that had followed World War I. In the 1920s and 1930s, the global economy had fractured into hostile blocs. Countries had engaged in *competitive devaluation* — each one cheapening its currency to make its exports cheaper than its neighbors', a beggar-thy-neighbor race that helped nobody. They had thrown up trade barriers. The result was the Great Depression, and many believed that economic chaos had helped fuel the rise of fascism. (For the mechanics of how a single country's monetary collapse can spiral, see the related deep-dive on [how the 1929 crash became the Great Depression](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system).)

So 730 delegates from 44 nations gathered at the Mount Washington Hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to design a postwar economic order deliberately built to prevent that. Two giants dominated the negotiations: John Maynard Keynes for Britain, and Harry Dexter White for the United States. The two had sharply different visions — Keynes wanted a powerful new institution with its own international currency that could lend freely; White wanted something smaller and tightly controlled, with the US dollar at the center. The United States held the money and the leverage, having emerged from the war as the dominant economy holding most of the world's gold. White's vision largely won.

Out of Bretton Woods came two institutions and one system:

1. **The International Monetary Fund (IMF)** — to oversee a system of fixed-but-adjustable exchange rates and to lend short-term to countries with balance-of-payments problems, so that no country would feel forced into the competitive devaluation and trade war that had wrecked the 1930s.
2. **The International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD)** — the original World Bank — to lend for the *reconstruction* of war-shattered Europe and then for the *development* of poorer countries.
3. **The Bretton Woods monetary system** — a regime in which the US dollar was fixed to gold (\$35 per ounce) and every other currency was fixed to the dollar. The dollar became the anchor of the world.

That third pillar is gone. In 1971, facing a drain on US gold reserves, President Nixon ended the dollar's convertibility to gold — the "Nixon shock" — and the system of fixed exchange rates collapsed into the floating rates we have today. But the two *institutions* survived the death of the system that created them, and they quietly redefined their roles. The IMF, no longer policing fixed exchange rates, became the crisis lender and macroeconomic surveillance body it is today. The World Bank, having finished rebuilding Europe (the Marshall Plan did most of that work), pivoted fully to developing-country development.

The deepest fact about both institutions is encoded in that origin story: they were designed by the victors, funded mostly by the United States, and governed by votes weighted to economic power. That was a reasonable engineering choice in 1944. Eight decades later, when the borrowers are overwhelmingly poor countries and the deciders are overwhelmingly rich ones, it is the central grievance.

## How the IMF actually works: quotas, SDRs, and tranches

Let us get concrete about the machinery. When you read that the IMF "approved a \$57 billion program for Argentina," what literally happens? Where does the money come from, what form does it take, and how is it released?

### Quotas: the stake that sets everything

Every member's quota is the master variable. It is denominated in SDRs and set by a formula weighted mostly toward the size of the country's economy (GDP), with smaller weights on its openness, economic variability, and reserves. The quota simultaneously determines:

- **Contribution:** how much the country pays into the Fund's resource pool (partly in reserve currencies, partly in its own).
- **Access:** how much it can normally borrow — typically expressed as a multiple of its quota.
- **Votes:** its share of the vote on the Executive Board. Each member gets a small fixed number of "basic votes" plus one vote per unit of quota, so in practice quota dominates the vote.

The Fund's total quota resources run on the order of \$1 trillion (SDR ~477 billion, plus large supplementary borrowing arrangements that can roughly double the firepower in a pinch). That pool is the ammunition for crisis lending.

#### Worked example: how a quota determines voting share

Let us make the one-dollar-one-vote logic concrete with round numbers. Suppose the IMF's total quota is \$500 billion (a simplified figure for arithmetic — the real number is larger). A large advanced economy, call it Country A, has a quota of \$83 billion. A developing economy, Country B, has a quota of \$5 billion.

Ignore the small fixed "basic votes" for a moment and let votes track quota directly:

- Country A's vote share is \$83 billion / \$500 billion = 16.6%.
- Country B's vote share is \$5 billion / \$500 billion = 1.0%.

Now the kicker. The IMF's most important decisions — quota increases, the SDR allocation, changes to the Articles of Agreement — require a *supermajority of 85% of the votes*. Notice what that means for Country A at 16.6%: to pass an 85% threshold, you need the support of votes summing to 85%, which is impossible if any single bloc holding *more than 15%* objects. Country A holds 16.6% — more than 15%. Therefore Country A can block any of those decisions *by itself*, simply by voting no. No other single country can.

The intuition: in a weighted-vote system, you do not need a majority to rule — you need a blocking share on the decisions that matter, and the 85% supermajority rule was set just high enough that the largest shareholder gets a unilateral veto.

That "Country A" is, of course, the United States, whose vote share has hovered around 16.5-17.5% for decades, kept deliberately above the 15% blocking line through successive quota reforms. No other member comes close to a unilateral veto. This is the single most important fact about who controls the IMF.

![Stacked layers of IMF voting power by region, US holding a lone veto](/imgs/blogs/imf-and-world-bank-5.png)

### SDRs: reserve money the IMF can create

The Special Drawing Right deserves its own treatment because it is genuinely peculiar and genuinely powerful. Created in 1969, an SDR is a reserve asset whose value is defined by a basket of major currencies (currently the US dollar, euro, Chinese renminbi, Japanese yen, and British pound). One SDR is worth roughly \$1.30 (as of early 2024; it floats with the basket — treat it as approximate).

Here is the magic and the danger of it: the IMF can *create* SDRs and allocate them to members without anyone lending or borrowing. In an *SDR allocation*, the Fund simply credits each member's reserve account with new SDRs in proportion to that member's quota. Overnight, every country's reserves go up. A country short of dollars can then swap its SDRs with a member that has spare dollars, getting usable hard currency in return. It is, in effect, the closest thing the world has to a global central bank creating global reserve money — though it is rare, requiring that 85% supermajority to approve.

#### Worked example: an SDR allocation across member quotas

In August 2021, the IMF executed the largest SDR allocation in history: about SDR 456 billion, worth roughly \$650 billion at the time, to help member countries cope with the COVID-19 pandemic. Because allocations follow quotas, the split was proportional. Let us trace it.

Suppose the total allocation is \$650 billion and three members have these quota shares (approximate, for illustration): the United States ~17.4%, China ~6.4%, and a small developing country ~0.1%.

- The United States receives 17.4% × \$650 billion = about \$113 billion in new SDRs.
- China receives 6.4% × \$650 billion = about \$42 billion.
- The small developing country receives 0.1% × \$650 billion = about \$650 million.

Now look at the perversity that critics immediately pointed out. The allocation was meant to help *poor* countries through the pandemic — but because the split follows quota, the rich countries that needed it least got the most. The United States, which can borrow all the dollars it wants in its own currency, received \$113 billion it had no use for; the poorest 50-odd countries combined received a small fraction of the total. This is why, after 2021, there was a major push for rich countries to *re-channel* their unneeded SDRs to poorer ones through trust funds. The allocation created the reserves; it did not automatically send them where the need was greatest.

The intuition: an SDR allocation is global reserve money created from nothing and split by economic size — which makes it a fast, debt-free injection of liquidity, but also one that, left alone, rains down hardest on the countries that need it least.

### Stand-by arrangements and the tranche structure

When a country actually borrows from the Fund in a crisis, the standard instrument is a *Stand-By Arrangement* (SBA) — or, for deeper structural problems, an *Extended Fund Facility* (EFF), which runs longer. The defining feature of both is that the money is not handed over in one lump. It is released in *tranches* — installments — and each tranche is unlocked only when the country has met the conditions attached to it.

![Pipeline of an IMF program from crisis through conditions to repayment](/imgs/blogs/imf-and-world-bank-3.png)

The life cycle, shown above, runs like this:

1. **The crisis hits.** Reserves are draining, the currency is under pressure, foreign payments are coming due. The government concludes it cannot self-rescue.
2. **The request and the letter of intent.** The country approaches the Fund. Negotiations produce a *letter of intent* and a *memorandum of economic and financial policies* — documents in which the government spells out the reforms it will undertake. (Officially the country "owns" the program; critics say the Fund's staff write the terms and the government signs.)
3. **Approval.** The Executive Board approves the arrangement: a total amount (say \$58 billion), a duration, and a schedule.
4. **The conditions.** The program specifies *performance criteria* — measurable targets like "reduce the fiscal deficit to 3% of GDP by year-end" or "hold net international reserves above a floor." Some are *prior actions* the country must complete before any money flows.
5. **Tranches release.** The first tranche is disbursed on approval. Each later tranche is released only after a *review* confirms the country hit its targets. Miss a target and the program can be suspended — the money stops.
6. **Repayment.** The country draws the foreign currency it needs, then repays over the following years with interest (the *rate of charge*, a modest market-linked rate, plus *surcharges* for large or prolonged borrowing).

The tranche structure is the enforcement mechanism. Conditionality would be toothless if the Fund handed over all the money at once; the leverage comes entirely from holding back the next installment until the country complies. That is also why programs so often *fail mid-stream*: a government agrees to painful conditions in the desperation of a crisis, the first tranche stabilizes things, and then — as the political cost of austerity bites — it misses a target, the program stalls, and the country and the Fund end up in a standoff. Argentina, as we will see, has done this so many times it is almost a national tradition.

## The doom loop: how a crisis forces a country to the Fund's door

Why does a country end up with no choice but to accept the Fund's conditions? Because by the time the IMF is the answer, every other door has closed. It is worth tracing the mechanism as a chain, because the inevitability is the point.

![Graph of capital flight draining reserves into an IMF program and adjustment](/imgs/blogs/imf-and-world-bank-4.png)

The chain above starts with *capital flight* — foreign (and often domestic) money rushing out of the country. Maybe a fixed exchange rate is looking unsustainable; maybe the government's debt looks unpayable; maybe a neighbor just collapsed and fear is contagious. Whatever the trigger, money heads for the exit, and it does two things at once. First, as everyone sells the local currency to buy dollars, the *currency falls* — which makes every imported good and every foreign-currency debt more expensive in local terms. Second, the central bank burns through its *reserves* trying to defend the currency and pay foreign bills, and reserves drain toward zero.

Both branches converge on the same wall: the country *cannot pay its foreign bills*. It cannot import the oil and medicine it needs; it cannot make the next payment on its foreign debt without defaulting. At that point, private lenders are not an option — no commercial bank lends fresh money to a country actively running out of reserves and facing default; that is how you lose your money. The country has, quite literally, nowhere else to go. The IMF becomes the lender of last resort *by elimination*.

And that is exactly the source of the Fund's leverage and the resentment toward it. The country is not negotiating from strength; it is negotiating from the edge of a cliff. Whatever conditions the Fund attaches — austerity, devaluation, bank closures, subsidy cuts — the alternative is a disorderly default and an even deeper collapse. So the conditions get accepted. Supporters say this is precisely when a country *should* be forced to fix the policies that caused the crisis. Critics say it is coercion dressed as advice, and that the conditions often reflect a rich-world economic ideology more than the borrowing country's actual needs. Both things can be true at once.

## Conditionality and austerity: the central fight

Nothing about the IMF is argued over more than conditionality, and within conditionality, nothing more than austerity. So let us put real numbers on what conditionality demands, and then lay out the case for and against as fairly as possible.

#### Worked example: conditionality in numbers

A country comes to the Fund with a fiscal mess. Say its economy (GDP) is \$200 billion, and its government is running a budget *deficit* — spending more than it collects in taxes — of 8% of GDP. In dollars, that is 8% × \$200 billion = \$16 billion of overspending each year, financed by borrowing.

The Fund's diagnosis: a deficit that large, in a country that has lost access to foreign borrowing, is unsustainable. A standard condition is to cut the deficit from 8% of GDP to 3% of GDP over the program. Let us trace what that requires.

- Target deficit: 3% of \$200 billion = \$6 billion.
- Current deficit: 8% of \$200 billion = \$16 billion.
- Required improvement (*fiscal consolidation*): \$16 billion − \$6 billion = \$10 billion.

So the government must find \$10 billion a year — five percent of the entire economy — through some combination of spending cuts and tax increases. To put that in human terms: \$10 billion might mean slashing fuel and food subsidies, freezing public-sector wages and pensions, cutting infrastructure budgets, and raising sales taxes. Each of those lands on real households, and many of them land hardest on the poor, who spend the largest share of their income on the subsidized basics.

Here is the second-order effect the critics emphasize. Cutting \$10 billion of government spending does not just shrink the deficit — it shrinks *demand* in the economy. The government was buying goods, paying salaries, funding projects; pull \$10 billion out and the businesses and workers who depended on that spending lose income too. So GDP itself falls, perhaps to \$185 billion. But now the same \$6 billion deficit is a *larger* share of the smaller economy: \$6 billion / \$185 billion = 3.2%, not 3.0%. The austerity partly undermines its own target by shrinking the denominator. This is the mechanism behind the charge that IMF austerity can be *self-defeating* in a downturn.

The intuition: a budget deficit is a ratio, and austerity attacks the numerator (spending) while accidentally also shrinking the denominator (GDP) — which is why cutting hard in a slump can fail to close the gap as fast as the arithmetic promises.

### The case for conditionality

The Fund's defenders make a serious argument, and it deserves to be stated at full strength. A country in a balance-of-payments crisis almost always *got there* through some combination of unsustainable policies: spending far beyond its means, an overvalued exchange rate it refused to adjust, a banking system stuffed with bad loans, or borrowing it could never repay. Simply handing such a government dollars with no conditions would, the argument goes, just fund more of the same — the country would burn through the loan and be back in crisis within a year, having added the IMF to its list of creditors it cannot repay. Conditionality forces the policy changes that actually fix the underlying problem, so the loan buys a real recovery rather than a brief reprieve. And because the Fund's money is *revolving* — repaid and re-lent to the next country in crisis — it has a duty to its other members to lend only where it will be repaid. Conditions are how it protects that money.

There is also a political-economy defense: conditionality gives reform-minded officials inside the borrowing government *cover*. A finance minister who wants to cut a ruinous subsidy but cannot survive the politics of it can say, "The IMF is making us do this." The Fund becomes a useful external scapegoat for reforms a country needed anyway.

### The case against — and Stiglitz's critique

The most influential critic is Joseph Stiglitz, the Nobel-laureate economist who served as chief economist of the *World Bank* (so an insider to the system) and who watched the Asian crisis up close. His critique, laid out in *Globalization and Its Discontents*, has several prongs:

**One-size-fits-all.** Stiglitz argues the Fund applied a standard template — austerity, high interest rates, open capital markets, privatization — regardless of whether it fit the country's actual problem. The template suited a country whose crisis came from *government* overspending and inflation. But the Asian crisis of 1997 was not that kind of crisis. The Asian governments mostly ran *balanced* budgets; the problem was private-sector debt and a sudden flight of foreign capital. Prescribing austerity and sky-high interest rates for that diagnosis, Stiglitz argued, was like treating a broken leg with chemotherapy: a real treatment for the wrong disease, which made the patient worse.

**Pro-cyclical austerity.** Demanding spending cuts and rate hikes in the middle of a collapse, the critics say, deepens the collapse. A normal central bank *cuts* rates and *increases* spending to fight a recession; the Fund did the reverse, draining demand from economies that were already cratering, turning sharp recessions into depressions.

**Capital-account liberalization.** Stiglitz reserved special anger for the Fund's push to make developing countries open their borders to free flows of short-term capital. That openness, he argued, is precisely what lets "hot money" rush in during good times and stampede out during a panic — manufacturing the very crises the Fund then arrives to "solve."

**Whose interests?** The deepest charge is that the Fund's conditions often served the interests of foreign creditors (who got repaid) and rich-country exporters (who got open markets) more than the borrowing country's citizens (who got the recession). When the votes are owned by the rich countries, the suspicion that the policies favor the rich countries is structurally hard to dispel.

The honest summary: conditionality is a genuine dilemma, not a simple villainy. Some conditions clearly fix real problems; some have clearly made things worse; the Fund itself has, over the years, admitted mistakes (it later conceded its fiscal projections during the Greek program were too optimistic about how much austerity the economy could absorb). The argument is not settled, and it should not be — it is a real trade-off between discipline and damage.

## The World Bank up close: projects, IDA, and structural adjustment

Now the sibling. The World Bank is not one institution but a *group* of five, and the two that matter most for our purposes are:

- **IBRD** (International Bank for Reconstruction and Development) — the original 1944 institution. It lends to *middle-income* developing countries at near-market rates, funding itself by issuing AAA-rated bonds.
- **IDA** (International Development Association, created 1960) — the part of the Bank for the *poorest* countries. IDA lends at near-zero interest with very long repayment periods (often 30-40 years), and increasingly gives outright grants. IDA is funded not by bond markets but by periodic *replenishments* — donations from rich member countries every three years.

![Tree of the Bretton Woods family and its rival institutions](/imgs/blogs/imf-and-world-bank-7.png)

The tree above shows the family. From the single 1944 root grow the IMF and the World Bank Group; within the Bank, IBRD serves the middle-income countries and IDA the poorest; and off to the side sit the newer rivals (AIIB, the BRICS bank) that we will come to.

#### Worked example: how the Bank's credit transformation saves a country money

Here is the World Bank's core economic trick, in numbers. Suppose a developing country, on its own, would have to pay 9% interest to borrow on international markets — lenders demand that much to compensate for the risk of a poor, volatile economy. It wants to build a \$1 billion power grid that will serve the country for thirty years.

- Borrowing \$1 billion at 9% costs \$90 million a year in interest alone.

Now the World Bank steps in. Because the Bank is backed by the guarantees of all its wealthy members, it carries a AAA rating and can borrow from global bond investors at, say, 4%. It lends the \$1 billion onward to the country at, say, 5% (its borrowing cost plus a small margin to cover its expenses).

- Borrowing \$1 billion through the Bank at 5% costs \$50 million a year in interest.
- The country saves \$90 million − \$50 million = \$40 million every year, for thirty years.

Over the life of the loan, that interest-rate transformation saves the country well over a billion dollars — more than the cost of the grid itself. And for the *poorest* countries borrowing through IDA at near-zero rates, the saving is even larger.

The intuition: the World Bank's superpower is its credit rating — it borrows the rich world's cheap money on the strength of rich-world guarantees and passes the low rate to countries that could never get it alone, which is a real and large transfer of value even before a single project is built.

### Structural adjustment: the Bank's controversial era

For its first decades the Bank financed discrete *projects* — this dam, that road. But in the 1980s, amid a wave of developing-country debt crises, the Bank (alongside the IMF) shifted toward *structural adjustment lending*: large loans conditioned not on building a specific thing, but on the country adopting a broad package of *market-oriented reforms*. The standard menu — later nicknamed the *Washington Consensus* — included fiscal austerity, privatizing state-owned enterprises, deregulating, liberalizing trade, and opening to foreign investment.

The theory was that poor countries stayed poor partly because of bloated, distorted, state-dominated economies, and that freeing markets would unlock growth. In some cases it helped. But *Structural Adjustment Programs* (SAPs) became, in much of Africa and Latin America, a byword for hardship: privatizations that sold national assets cheaply (sometimes to politically connected buyers), cuts to health and education spending, the removal of food and fuel subsidies that triggered riots, and growth that often failed to materialize as promised. A generation of development economists came to see the SAP era as, at best, oversold and, at worst, a decade of damage justified by ideology. The Bank itself has substantially moved away from the model, toward poverty-reduction strategies that it at least frames as country-owned. But the reputational scar remains, and it is a large part of why "World Bank" and "IMF" are dirty words to many in the developing world.

It is worth being precise about the criticism leveled at the Bank's *projects*, too. Critics point to large infrastructure projects — giant dams especially — that displaced hundreds of thousands of people, damaged ecosystems, and sometimes never delivered the promised economic returns, all financed with debt the country had to repay regardless. The Bank now runs elaborate environmental and social safeguards in response. Whether those safeguards are adequate, or whether they merely slow good projects while bad ones still slip through, is an ongoing argument.

## Who controls them: votes, the leadership convention, and the legitimacy problem

We have already seen the core mechanism — votes follow quotas, quotas follow economic size, the US holds a unilateral veto. Let us widen the lens to the full governance landscape and the legitimacy crisis it has produced.

At the IMF, the rough vote shares (approximate, as of the latest quota review) tell the story: the United States ~16.5%, then a cluster of advanced economies — Japan, Germany, France, the United Kingdom — followed by China at around 6% (its quota share lags far behind its actual economic weight), and then everyone else. The advanced economies as a bloc command well over half the votes. The countries that actually *borrow* from the Fund — the emerging and developing world — collectively hold a minority of the votes on the institution that dictates their crisis policies. That is the legitimacy problem in one sentence: the borrowers do not control the lender.

Then there is the **leadership convention**. By an unwritten gentlemen's agreement struck at the founding, the Managing Director of the IMF is always a European, and the President of the World Bank is always an American. This has held without exception for eighty years — every IMF chief from Camille Gutt (Belgium) to Christine Lagarde (France) to Kristalina Georgieva (Bulgaria) has been European; every World Bank President from Eugene Meyer onward has been American. There is no rule requiring this; it is pure custom, and it is increasingly indefensible. When emerging economies put forward highly qualified candidates for the IMF top job, they lose to the European consensus pick. The message it sends — that the top jobs belong to the West regardless of merit or the shifting balance of the world economy — corrodes the institutions' claim to be neutral global bodies rather than instruments of the rich world.

There have been **quota reforms** — notably in 2010, which (after years of US delay in ratifying it) modestly raised the shares of China and other emerging economies. But the reforms have been incremental and grudging, always preserving the US veto and the advanced-economy majority. China's quota share remains far below its share of world GDP. The persistent gap between economic reality and institutional voting power is the slow-burning fuel for the alternatives we turn to next.

## The rise of alternatives: China, the AIIB, and the BRICS bank

If you are a developing country that resents the conditions, the lectures, and the rich-world control of the Bretton Woods institutions, what do you do? Increasingly, you find another lender. The most consequential development in international finance over the past fifteen years is the emergence of a parallel system, led largely by China.

**Chinese bilateral lending** is now enormous. Through its policy banks (China Development Bank, Export-Import Bank of China) and the *Belt and Road Initiative*, China has lent hundreds of billions of dollars to developing countries — for ports, railways, power plants, roads. For many borrowers the appeal is precise: Chinese loans historically came with *no policy conditionality* — no demand to cut your deficit, float your currency, or privatize your state companies. China lent for infrastructure and (officially) did not tell you how to run your country. To a government that found IMF conditionality humiliating, that was a powerful pitch.

But it came with its own risks, and the phrase that attached to it is *debt-trap diplomacy* — the charge that China deliberately lent more than countries could repay so that, on default, it could seize strategic assets. The most-cited example is Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port, leased to a Chinese company for 99 years after Sri Lanka could not service the debt. (Scholars argue the "debt trap" framing is overstated — much of the lending was driven by borrower demand and commercial logic, not a master plan — but the pattern of opaque, hard-to-restructure Chinese debt is real and has badly complicated recent crises in Zambia and Sri Lanka.)

**The AIIB** (Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank), launched by China in 2016 with about \$100 billion in authorized capital, is a multilateral development bank explicitly modeled as an alternative to the World Bank, focused on Asian infrastructure. The United States lobbied its allies *not* to join; most joined anyway (the UK, Germany, and dozens of others), a vivid sign of waning American ability to dictate the architecture.

**The BRICS bank** — formally the New Development Bank (NDB), founded in 2015 by Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa with \$100 billion in authorized capital, plus a companion \$100 billion *Contingent Reserve Arrangement* meant to be a mini-IMF for the BRICS — is the most direct attempt to build a non-Western alternative to *both* Bretton Woods institutions at once. In practice the NDB has lent far less than the World Bank and remains modest, but its symbolism is large: it says the emerging powers no longer accept that Washington must be the only address for crisis money and development finance.

The strategic stakes are obvious. For eighty years, the dollar-centered Bretton Woods system gave the United States and its allies enormous leverage over the developing world — the power to attach conditions, to set the rules, to choose the leaders. (For the bigger picture of how that monetary power works, see [who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system).) The rise of alternatives does not yet rival that system in scale, but it erodes its monopoly. A country with somewhere else to borrow is a country the IMF can lecture less.

## Common misconceptions

**"The IMF and the World Bank are basically the same thing."** They are siblings born at the same conference, headquartered across the street from each other, and run by the same set of countries — which is why people conflate them. But their jobs are opposite in time horizon and purpose: the IMF is the *short-term crisis lender* fixing balance-of-payments emergencies, the World Bank is the *long-term development lender* building infrastructure and cutting poverty over decades. Confusing them is like confusing the emergency room with a thirty-year mortgage lender. They even fund themselves differently — the Fund from member quotas, the Bank mostly from bond markets.

**"The IMF gives countries money."** It *lends* money, and only foreign currency, and only against conditions, and only in tranches it can cut off. An IMF program is a loan that must be repaid with interest, not a grant. The borrowing country usually has to enact painful, politically costly reforms to keep the money flowing, and many programs collapse midway when the country misses its targets. "Bailout" makes it sound like free money; it is closer to a supervised emergency loan with a probation officer attached.

**"The IMF prints money / SDRs are world money you can spend."** The IMF cannot print dollars, and an SDR is not a currency you can spend at a shop. An SDR is a *claim* on the hard currencies of other members — useful only because a member holding SDRs can swap them with another member for actual dollars or euros. The IMF can *create* SDRs in an allocation, which does add to global reserves, but it requires an 85% supermajority and happens rarely (the big 2021 allocation was the first since 2009).

**"It's one country, one vote, like the UN General Assembly."** It is emphatically not. Voting power follows quota, which follows economic size — closer to one-dollar-one-vote. The United States holds enough votes to veto the most important decisions by itself, and the advanced economies as a bloc hold a majority, while the developing countries that actually borrow hold a minority. The governance is weighted to wealth by design, dating to 1944.

**"Austerity is just the IMF being cruel."** Austerity is a genuine policy dilemma, not gratuitous cruelty. A country deep in a balance-of-payments crisis is usually spending more foreign money than it earns, and *something* has to close that gap. The serious criticism is not that the Fund demands adjustment, but that it has historically demanded *too much, too fast, in the wrong kind of crisis* — slamming the brakes on economies already in free-fall, deepening recessions. The Fund itself has conceded it underestimated the damage in some programs (notably Greece). The fight is about dosage and diagnosis, not whether discipline is ever needed.

**"Borrowing from China is conditionality-free and therefore better."** Chinese lending historically skipped the *policy* conditions (cut your deficit, float your currency) that make IMF programs so resented — a real difference. But it brought its own problems: loans that were often opaque, collateralized against strategic assets, and notoriously hard to *restructure* when a country could not pay. In recent crises (Zambia, Sri Lanka), the difficulty of getting Chinese lenders to the table alongside Western creditors has *slowed* debt relief, not sped it. "No conditions" is not the same as "no strings."

## How it shows up in real markets

**The Asian Financial Crisis, 1997.** This is the case study that defined the modern critique of the IMF. When Thailand's baht peg broke in July 1997, the panic spread to Indonesia, South Korea, and Malaysia. The Fund assembled enormous rescues — roughly \$17 billion for Thailand, ~\$40 billion for Indonesia, ~\$58 billion for South Korea — but attached harsh conditions: sharp spending cuts, sky-high interest rates, and rapid bank closures. The interest-rate hikes, meant to defend the currencies, instead bankrupted otherwise-sound companies; the abrupt bank closures in Indonesia triggered a run on the whole banking system. Critics, led by Stiglitz, argued the Fund had misdiagnosed a private-sector capital-flight crisis as a public-sector overspending crisis and applied exactly the wrong medicine. Malaysia famously refused an IMF program, imposed capital controls instead — heresy at the time — and recovered comparably well, which became Exhibit A against the Fund's one-size-fits-all template. For the full mechanics of how the crisis spread, see the dedicated deep-dive on [the Asian financial crisis of 1997](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997).

**Argentina's serial crises, 2001 and 2018.** No country has a more tortured relationship with the IMF. In 2001, after years of a rigid dollar peg (the *currency board*) and ballooning debt, Argentina collapsed: it defaulted on roughly \$100 billion, the peso was devalued, banks froze deposits (the *corralito*), and the country churned through five presidents in two weeks. Many Argentines blamed the IMF, which had backed the doomed peg and then, critics say, pulled support at the worst moment. The scar made "the IMF" politically toxic in Argentina for a generation. Yet in 2018 a market-friendly government went *back* to the Fund and secured the largest program in IMF history — about \$57 billion — to defend the peso. It failed: the peso kept collapsing, the government lost the next election, capital controls returned, and Argentina was soon back negotiating. The episode showed both the Fund's enormous firepower and the limits of that firepower when the underlying politics will not hold.

#### Worked example: a country's debt and growth before vs after a program

Let us trace, with round numbers, what a "successful" adjustment looks like on paper and what it costs in the real economy. A country enters a program with GDP of \$100 billion, government debt of \$120 billion (debt-to-GDP = 120%), a fiscal deficit of 8% of GDP, and reserves near zero.

The program runs three years. Year one, austerity bites: the deficit is cut toward target, but demand collapses and GDP *shrinks 10%*, to \$90 billion. Now watch the debt ratio: even if the debt total barely changed at \$120 billion, the ratio *rises* to \$120 billion / \$90 billion = 133%. The country did everything the Fund asked and its debt burden got *worse* in year one, purely because the economy shrank. This is the brutal arithmetic of austerity in a downturn, and it is exactly what happened in Greece.

By year three, suppose the reforms work: the deficit is down to 3% of GDP, reserves are rebuilt, confidence returns, and GDP recovers to \$98 billion — still below where it started. The debt ratio settles around \$118 billion / \$98 billion = 120%, roughly where it began, after three years of pain. On the Fund's scorecard the program "succeeded": deficit fixed, reserves restored, default avoided. On the street, the country endured a deep recession and ended three years later no less indebted, with a generation marked by unemployment. Both readings are accurate, and the gap between them is the entire political controversy.

The intuition: a program can hit every macroeconomic target the Fund sets and still feel like a failure to the people living through it, because the targets measure the patient's vital signs, not the patient's pain.

![Before and after a structural adjustment program: budget and reserves fixed, GDP falls first](/imgs/blogs/imf-and-world-bank-6.png)

**Greece, 2010-2015.** The largest and most fraught Western program. After the 2008 financial crisis exposed that Greece had been understating its deficits for years, it was locked out of bond markets and turned to a "Troika" — the IMF, the European Commission, and the European Central Bank — for bailouts eventually totaling over €260 billion. The conditions were savage austerity: deep cuts to pensions and public wages, tax hikes, privatizations. The result was a depression worse than America's 1930s: Greek GDP fell roughly 25%, unemployment hit 27%, youth unemployment over 50%. The debt ratio, which the austerity was supposed to lower, *rose* — exactly the denominator effect from our worked example, on a national scale. The IMF later published a remarkable self-critique acknowledging it had underestimated the *fiscal multiplier* (how much austerity shrinks the economy) and that the first program had pushed too hard, too fast. Greece is the case where even the Fund admitted the medicine was overdosed.

**The 2021 SDR allocation, \$650 billion.** In August 2021, with the developing world reeling from COVID-19, the IMF executed the largest SDR allocation ever — about \$650 billion credited to members' reserve accounts overnight. It was a rare display of the Fund acting as a near-global central bank, injecting liquidity without debt. But, as our worked example showed, the proportional-to-quota split meant rich countries received the bulk while the poorest got slivers, sparking an ongoing push to re-channel unused SDRs from rich to poor through trust funds. It was simultaneously a triumph of multilateral cooperation and a vivid demonstration of how the institution's quota-weighted design shapes even its acts of generosity.

**Zambia and Sri Lanka, recent defaults.** The two cases that defined the 2020s sovereign-debt landscape and exposed the China problem. Zambia defaulted in late 2020 — the first African default of the pandemic era — and then spent *years* trying to restructure its debt, stuck because a large chunk was owed to Chinese lenders who would not engage with the Western creditors and the IMF on common terms. Sri Lanka defaulted in 2022 amid an economic and political collapse — fuel and medicine shortages, mass protests that toppled the president — with a debt load heavy in Chinese loans, including the infamous Hambantota Port. Both eventually reached IMF programs, but the delays were caused largely by the new, fragmented creditor landscape: with China now a major lender outside the old Western "Paris Club" framework, the cooperative debt-restructuring machinery that the IMF used to coordinate has repeatedly jammed. These cases are the clearest sign that the Bretton Woods-centered order is being challenged not just rhetorically but mechanically.

## When this matters to you, and where to go next

You might think two institutions that lend to *governments* have nothing to do with your life. They have more than you would guess. If you live in a developing or emerging economy, an IMF program can directly change the price of fuel and bread at your corner shop (when subsidies are cut), the size of a public pension, the interest rate on your mortgage, and whether your currency holds its value — austerity and devaluation are not distant concerns; they show up in your grocery bill. (Vietnam, for instance, manages its monetary policy with its own tools and credit ceilings precisely to keep control of these levers — see [Vietnam's monetary policy](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).) And if you live in a rich country, you are a *shareholder*: your government's contributions fund these institutions and cast their votes, which is why the leadership convention and the lending choices are, ultimately, made partly in your name.

More broadly, the IMF and World Bank are where the abstract idea that "money is power" becomes concrete at the level of nations. The same logic that lets a central bank create money for its own economy (see [how money is created](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier)) operates one level up: the institution that controls the world's emergency hard currency, and the votes that govern it, holds leverage over the domestic policy of any country that runs out of dollars. Understanding who that institution is, how its money is structured, and whose votes control it is understanding a real and underappreciated axis of global power.

Where to go next, if you want to keep pulling this thread: read the [Asian financial crisis of 1997](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997) for the crisis that most sharpened the critique of IMF conditionality; read [who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system) for the dollar-centered system these institutions sit inside; and watch the slow contest between the old Bretton Woods order and its new rivals — the AIIB, the BRICS bank, Chinese bilateral lending — because how that contest resolves will shape who gets to be the lender of last resort to nations for the next eighty years.

A closing reminder in the spirit of honesty: none of this is investment advice, and none of it is a verdict. The IMF and the World Bank have prevented financial fires from spreading, kept countries fed through crises, and financed real development that lifted real people out of poverty. They have also, by their own occasional admission, prescribed medicine that deepened the disease, and they remain governed by a balance of power frozen in 1944. Both things are true. The point of understanding them is not to cheer or to condemn, but to be able to read the next headline about a \$50 billion program and know exactly what is being traded, by whom, and at whose expense.
