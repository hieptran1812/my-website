> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Insurers turn the premiums they collect into a giant pool of investable money called **float**, which makes them some of the largest long-horizon investors alive; but when an insurer writes financial guarantees instead of ordinary policies, it can become a systemic time bomb, as AIG proved in 2008.
>
> - Insurance is **risk pooling**: many people pay a small, certain premium so the unlucky few get a large, uncertain payout. The insurer profits two ways, by paying out less than it takes in (**underwriting profit**) and by investing the money in between (the **float**).
> - **Float** is the gap between collecting a premium and paying a claim. It can be tens of billions of dollars an insurer gets to invest, and if underwriting is profitable the insurer is effectively being *paid* to borrow it. This is the engine behind Berkshire Hathaway.
> - **Reinsurance** is insurance for insurers: firms like Munich Re and Swiss Re take the catastrophic tail off primary insurers' books so a single hurricane does not bankrupt them.
> - **AIG** wrote over **\$500 billion** of credit default swaps, which are insurance on securities, with almost no reserves against them. When the housing market fell, the same loss hit every contract at once, its rating was cut, collateral calls it could not meet poured in, and the U.S. government rescued it for about **\$182 billion**.
> - The one fact to remember: insuring thousands of cars works because the losses are independent and small; insuring thousands of mortgage securities fails because the losses are correlated and arrive together.

What does an insurance company do with your premium the moment you pay it? You probably picture it sitting in a vault, waiting for the day you crash your car. It is not waiting anywhere. Within the quarter it has been lent to the U.S. Treasury, parked in corporate bonds, used to buy a slice of an office tower, or in Warren Buffett's case used to buy an entire railroad. The premium you paid for protection you may never claim has become working capital for one of the largest investors on earth. The diagram above is the mental model: money flows *in* as premiums long before it flows *out* as claims, and the insurer gets to invest everything in the middle. That middle pool has a name, **float**, and understanding it explains both why insurers are quietly among the most powerful institutions in finance and why one of them nearly took the whole system down.

![Pipeline from premiums in to a float pool to investments to reserves to claims out](/imgs/blogs/insurance-giants-and-systemic-risk-1.png)

This post builds the whole machine from zero. We start with what insurance even is, define every term as we meet it, and then climb to the depth a practitioner respects: how insurers actually make money, why reinsurance exists, how Lloyd's of London runs a 300-year-old market of syndicates, and finally the slow-motion detonation of AIG in September 2008, when a company famous for boring car and life policies blew an estimated \$182 billion hole in the public balance sheet because one small unit had quietly turned the firm into a securities-insurance machine with no reserves. By the end you will be able to look at any insurer and ask the four questions that tell you whether it is a sturdy long-horizon investor or a time bomb: what does it insure, are those losses independent, where is the float, and what happens to it if its credit rating falls.

## Foundations: what insurance actually is

Strip insurance down to its core and it is a bet that benefits both sides. You pay a small, **certain** amount of money now to avoid a large, **uncertain** loss later. The insurer takes the opposite side of that bet thousands or millions of times, and because the losses of all those people are mostly unrelated to one another, the insurer can predict the *total* payout far more accurately than any one customer can predict their own. That predictability is the entire trick. Let us define the vocabulary precisely, because the rest of the post leans on it.

A **premium** is the price you pay for coverage, usually periodically (monthly, annually). It is the insurer's revenue. A **claim** is what you ask the insurer to pay when the insured event happens, the car is totaled, the house burns, the policyholder dies. A **policy** is the contract specifying what is covered, for how much, and for how long. The maximum the insurer will pay is the **coverage limit**; the amount you pay out of pocket before coverage kicks in is the **deductible**. None of this is mysterious, but the precision matters because insurers live and die on the gap between premiums collected and claims paid.

The mechanism that makes the whole thing work is **risk pooling**. Suppose one in a hundred drivers totals a car worth \$30,000 in a given year. If you face that risk alone, your expected loss is \$300 a year (one percent of \$30,000), but the *actual* outcome is brutally lumpy: almost certainly you pay nothing, and with small probability you eat a \$30,000 hit you cannot afford. Now pool ten thousand drivers. The *number* who crash is now highly predictable, close to one hundred, so the total payout clusters tightly around \$3 million. The insurer charges each driver a premium a little above \$300, collects more than \$3 million, pays out about \$3 million, and keeps the difference. The pool converted ten thousand terrifying individual gambles into one boringly predictable aggregate. This only works when the individual risks are **independent**, meaning one person's crash does not make another's more likely. Hold that word *independent* in your mind; it is the hinge on which the entire AIG story turns.

The mathematical engine under risk pooling is the **law of large numbers**, and it is worth stating precisely because the entire industry rests on it. The law says that as you average more and more independent random outcomes, the *average* converges to the true expected value and its *swing around that average shrinks*. For an insurer this means the percentage of policyholders who file claims becomes more and more predictable as the pool grows, even though any single policyholder remains a coin flip. The uncertainty does not vanish, it *concentrates*: the insurer can be quite sure that close to one percent of its drivers will crash, while remaining totally unable to say which ones. That asymmetry, predictable in aggregate, unpredictable individually, is the precise edge the insurer sells. A useful rule of thumb from statistics is that the relative wobble of the claims rate falls roughly with the square root of the number of policies, so a pool a hundred times larger has about a tenth the proportional uncertainty. This is why insurers chase scale: a bigger independent pool is a *steadier* pool, and a steadier pool can be reserved against more thinly and priced more keenly. It is also why the law offers no protection at all when the risks are correlated, because correlated risks do not average out no matter how many you add, a point that becomes the hinge of the AIG story.

The discipline of deciding whom to insure and at what price is called **underwriting**. An underwriter estimates how likely a given policyholder is to file a claim and sets a premium that covers the expected payout plus the insurer's costs plus a margin. The expected payout is called the **pure premium** (the bare cost of the risk itself, before any loading); on top of it the underwriter adds a **loading** for expenses, the cost of capital, and profit, to arrive at the **gross premium** the customer actually pays. Good underwriting means the gross premiums collected genuinely exceed the claims plus expenses they will eventually have to pay. Bad underwriting, charging too little for the risk, is the original sin that sinks insurers, and it is insidious because its consequences arrive *years* after the mistake: a book underpriced today looks wildly profitable this year (premiums are in, claims have not yet come) and only reveals itself as a catastrophe when the delayed claims finally land. We will see exactly this delayed detonation at Lloyd's and at AIG.

Two more terms and the foundation is laid. **Reserves** are the money an insurer formally sets aside to pay claims it knows are coming but has not paid yet, either because the claim is still being processed or because the event (a death, a future accident on a current policy) has not happened yet but is statistically certain to. Reserves are an accounting liability: they represent money that belongs, in effect, to future claimants, parked on the insurer's balance sheet. And the star of the show, **float**, is the pile of money an insurer holds at any moment that it has collected as premiums but not yet paid out as claims. Reserves and float are two views of the same money: reserves are the *accounting label* for the obligation, float is the *cash* that backs it and that the insurer gets to invest while it waits. That investable pile is what turns an insurance company into an investment company wearing a policyholder's coat.

### Life versus property-and-casualty

Insurance splits into two great families, and the split is all about *time*. **Life and health insurers** insure events tied to human lives, dying, getting sick, living longer than expected and outliving your savings. **Property-and-casualty insurers** (P&C, sometimes called general insurance) insure things and liabilities: cars, homes, ships, factories, and the lawsuits that arise when those things hurt someone. The defining difference is how long the insurer holds the money.

A life policy can run for fifty years. You pay premiums for decades, and the claim, your death benefit, lands far in the future. The float on a life book is enormous and *long-duration*: the insurer holds the money for decades and can invest it in long-term assets like thirty-year bonds and real estate. A car policy, by contrast, runs a year, and if you crash, the claim is paid within months. P&C float is shorter and lumpier. This duration difference drives everything about how each family invests, how it is regulated, and how it can fail. Long-duration life float is exquisitely sensitive to interest rates; short-duration P&C float is exposed to sudden catastrophes. We will return to both failure modes.

### Reinsurance, in one sentence

Here is a term most people have never heard that runs a colossal hidden industry: **reinsurance** is insurance that insurers buy from other insurers. A primary insurer that has written a million Florida homeowner policies faces a nightmare: a single Category 5 hurricane could trigger every one of those claims *at the same time*, far more than its premiums and reserves can cover. So the primary insurer pays a premium to a **reinsurer**, a firm like Munich Re or Swiss Re, which agrees to absorb the losses above some threshold. The primary insurer keeps the small, predictable losses and offloads the catastrophic tail. Reinsurers are the insurers' insurers, and they are why one hurricane does not bankrupt your home insurer. We will give them a full section.

### The balance sheet, glossed

To make the float concrete we need to look at an insurer's balance sheet, which is just a snapshot of what it owns (**assets**) and what it owes (**liabilities**), with the leftover (**equity**, the owners' stake) balancing the two. The diagram below shows the shape that makes insurers unusual.

![Stacked balance sheet showing loss reserves and unearned premium dominating liabilities above a thin equity cushion](/imgs/blogs/insurance-giants-and-systemic-risk-3.png)

On the asset side, an insurer holds mostly investments: government bonds, corporate bonds, stocks, mortgages, real estate, the deployed float. On the liability side, the dominant items are **reserves**, and they come in two flavors. **Loss reserves** (also called claim reserves) are the insurer's estimate of claims it will have to pay on events that have already happened or are statistically locked in; these are typically the largest single liability, often around 60 percent of the balance sheet for a P&C insurer. The **unearned premium reserve** is premiums already collected for coverage periods that have not elapsed yet, money the insurer technically still owes back if the policy were cancelled. Beneath all of it sits a comparatively thin slice of **equity**, the insurer's own capital, perhaps fifteen percent of the total. That equity is the buffer: it takes the first losses if claims overshoot reserves. The whole structure is a small sliver of the owners' money perched on top of a mountain of policyholders' money. The insurer invests the whole mountain and keeps the returns; the owners' thin equity is what is at risk if it all goes wrong. This is **leverage**, in the sense that a little equity controls a large pool of assets, and it is why a modest mistake in pricing or investing can wipe out the owners.

### The crucial difference: insuring cars versus insuring securities

We now reach the single most important idea in this post, the one that separates ordinary insurance from the thing that detonated AIG. Insuring cars and homes works because the losses are **independent and small**: your neighbor's fender-bender tells you nothing about whether you will crash, and any one claim is tiny next to the whole pool. The law of large numbers does its quiet work and the aggregate is predictable.

Insuring *securities* is a different animal entirely. A **security** is a tradable financial asset, a stock, a bond, or a bundle of mortgages packaged into a bond. You can insure a security against default using an instrument called a **credit default swap (CDS)**, which is, functionally, an insurance policy on a bond: the buyer pays a periodic premium, and if the bond defaults, the CDS seller pays out the loss. It looks just like insurance. But the risks are not independent. The thing that makes one mortgage bond default, a nationwide fall in house prices, makes *every* mortgage bond default at the same time. There is no law of large numbers to save you when all your bets go bad together. Worse, unlike a car policy, a CDS requires the seller to post **collateral** (cash or safe assets pledged to the buyer) as the insured bond loses value, even before any actual default, and the amount of collateral demanded rises if the seller's own credit rating falls. We will unpack every link of that chain, because it is exactly the chain that hanged AIG.

![Before-after comparison of ordinary insurance pooling independent losses versus AIG writing correlated credit default swaps](/imgs/blogs/insurance-giants-and-systemic-risk-2.png)

The figure above is the whole thesis in two columns. On the left, ordinary insurance: many small policies, independent losses, reserves comfortably covering the expected claims. On the right, AIG's Financial Products unit: over \$500 billion of guarantees on securities, losses that are deeply correlated so they all arrive together, and crucially no reserves at all, only an obligation to post collateral on demand. Same word, *insurance*, two utterly different machines.

## How insurers actually make money: underwriting plus the float

Most people assume insurers make money by paying out less than they collect. That is half the story, and often not even the bigger half. An insurer has two profit engines, and a great insurer runs both.

The first engine is the **underwriting profit**: premiums collected minus claims paid minus operating expenses. The standard scorecard for this is the **combined ratio**, defined as claims plus expenses divided by premiums, expressed as a percentage. A combined ratio below 100 percent means the insurer made money on underwriting alone, before earning a cent on investments. Above 100 percent and it lost money on underwriting and is relying entirely on its investments to make the year work. The combined ratio is the first number a serious analyst looks at, because it tells you whether the insurer is a disciplined risk-taker or a company quietly buying market share by underpricing risk.

#### Worked example: the combined ratio above and below 100 percent

Suppose an insurer collects \$1,000,000 in premiums this year. It pays \$700,000 in claims and spends \$250,000 on expenses (agents, salaries, processing). Add the two outflows: \$700,000 plus \$250,000 is \$950,000. Divide by premiums: \$950,000 divided by \$1,000,000 is 0.95, or a combined ratio of 95 percent. The insurer made an **underwriting profit** of \$50,000, five cents on every premium dollar, before any investment income. That is a healthy, disciplined book.

Now run a bad year. Same \$1,000,000 in premiums, but claims spike to \$850,000 (a rough storm season) and expenses stay \$250,000. Total outflow is \$1,100,000. Divide by premiums: a combined ratio of 110 percent. The insurer lost \$100,000 on underwriting; it paid out ten cents more than every premium dollar it took in. The only way this year is not a disaster is if the insurer earned more than \$100,000 investing its float. The intuition: a combined ratio under 100 means underwriting alone is profitable and the float comes free; above 100 means the insurer is paying for the privilege of holding the float, and it had better invest it well.

The second engine is the **investment income on the float**, and this is where insurers quietly become investment giants. Recall float is the money held between collecting premiums and paying claims. An insurer always has a large float because there is always a stock of premiums collected on policies whose claims have not yet been paid. As long as the business keeps writing policies, the float is *durable*: as old claims are paid, new premiums arrive, and the pool stays large or grows. The insurer invests this pool and keeps the returns. If the underwriting breaks even (combined ratio exactly 100), the float is *free money to invest*. If underwriting is profitable (combined ratio under 100), the insurer is being *paid to hold* the float, which is the financial equivalent of a bank paying you to take out a loan.

#### Worked example: collect \$1,000 now, pay \$900 in five years, invest the float

This is the simplest possible model of the whole industry, so we will do it slowly. Today you, the insurer, sell a policy and collect a \$1,000 premium. Based on your underwriting, you expect to pay a \$900 claim in five years. So far it looks like a thin business: you will keep \$100, an underwriting profit, but only after waiting five years.

Now add the float. For those five years you hold the \$1,000 and invest it. Say you earn a steady 5 percent a year. Compounding \$1,000 at 5 percent for five years: year one gives \$1,050, year two \$1,102.50, year three \$1,157.63, year four \$1,215.51, year five \$1,276.28. After five years your \$1,000 has grown to about \$1,276. Now you pay the \$900 claim. You are left with \$1,276 minus \$900, which is \$376. Compare that to the \$100 underwriting profit alone: the float roughly *tripled* your profit, turning \$100 into \$376. And notice the \$276 of investment gain was earned on money that was never yours, it was the policyholders' premium, held in trust until the claim came due. The intuition: even a mediocre insurer with a slim underwriting margin can be a wonderful business, because the real profit is the return on a giant pile of other people's money it gets to invest for years.

### Warren Buffett and the Berkshire float machine

No one has explained or exploited the float better than Warren Buffett, and Berkshire Hathaway is, underneath the famous stock picking, an insurance company. Berkshire owns GEICO (car insurance), General Re and the Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance Group (reinsurance), and a stack of other insurers. The float these generate has grown from a few million dollars in the late 1960s to well over \$160 billion in recent years (as of the mid-2020s, approximate). Buffett has been explicit for decades that the float is the secret engine: it is, in his framing, money he can invest that costs him *less than nothing*, because Berkshire's insurance operations have, over the long run, run at an underwriting profit. He is borrowing more than \$160 billion at a *negative* interest rate, and investing the proceeds in stocks and whole companies.

#### Worked example: negative-cost borrowing on \$100 billion of float

Imagine an insurer holds \$100 billion of float and, thanks to disciplined underwriting, runs a combined ratio of 98 percent. That means for every \$100 of premium it writes, it pays out \$98 in claims and expenses and keeps \$2. The \$2 of underwriting profit is, in effect, what the insurer is *paid* to hold the float. So its cost of funds is negative: rather than paying interest to borrow, it is being handed money to hold the loan.

Now invest that \$100 billion at, say, 8 percent (a long-run equity-like return). Eight percent of \$100 billion is \$8 billion a year in investment income. Because the float cost nothing, indeed less than nothing, essentially all \$8 billion drops toward the bottom line. Compound \$100 billion at 8 percent for ten years and it grows to roughly \$216 billion, more than doubling, on borrowed money that the lender (the policyholders, collectively) effectively paid the insurer to take. The intuition: profitable underwriting turns float into the cheapest leverage in all of finance, negative-cost, long-term, and unrecallable as long as the book keeps renewing, which is precisely why Buffett built an empire on top of insurers rather than banks.

The catch, and Buffett says this himself, is that the magic only works if underwriting stays disciplined. Float obtained by *underpricing risk* (writing policies too cheap to win volume) is not free; it is expensive money whose true cost shows up later as claims that overwhelm the premiums. The negative-cost float is a *reward for good underwriting*, not a free feature of being an insurer. An insurer that chases growth by underpricing is borrowing at a high and hidden interest rate, payable in catastrophe.

## Reinsurance: the insurers' insurers

We met reinsurance in the foundations; now we give it the depth it deserves, because the reinsurance market is one of the most important and least understood parts of global finance. A reinsurer sells insurance to insurance companies. Its customers are not households; they are the primary insurers who want to cap their exposure to catastrophic, correlated losses. The two giants of the field are **Munich Re** and **Swiss Re**, German and Swiss firms respectively, each more than a century old, each holding tens of billions of dollars of capital specifically to absorb the world's worst days.

Reinsurance comes in two basic shapes. **Proportional** reinsurance: the reinsurer takes a fixed percentage of every policy's premiums and pays the same percentage of every claim, sharing the book pro rata. **Non-proportional** (or excess-of-loss) reinsurance: the reinsurer pays only the losses *above* a threshold, leaving the primary insurer to absorb everything below it. Excess-of-loss is the more important shape for understanding catastrophes, because it is structured exactly like a deductible, but for the whole company.

#### Worked example: a reinsurance layer, first \$10M to the insurer, next \$90M to the reinsurer

A primary insurer has written a book of coastal homeowner policies. It buys an excess-of-loss reinsurance treaty structured so that the insurer keeps the first \$10 million of total losses from any single event (this kept portion is its **retention**), and the reinsurer covers the next \$90 million (this is the **layer**, often written as "\$90 million in excess of \$10 million"). Above \$100 million the insurer is on its own again unless it bought a higher layer.

Run three storms through it. A small storm causes \$6 million of claims: it is entirely below the \$10 million retention, so the insurer pays all \$6 million and the reinsurer pays \$0. A medium storm causes \$40 million of claims: the insurer pays its first \$10 million, and the reinsurer pays the remaining \$30 million. A monster storm causes \$120 million of claims: the insurer pays its \$10 million retention, the reinsurer pays its full \$90 million layer, and the insurer is again exposed for the \$20 million above \$100 million. The intuition: reinsurance does not make catastrophes disappear; it slices the loss into layers and lets each party hold the slice it can survive, so that one bad hurricane bruises the primary insurer instead of killing it.

In exchange for taking the dangerous top layers, the reinsurer collects a premium and, like any insurer, invests its own float in the meantime. Reinsurers diversify the only way that helps against correlated catastrophes: *geographically and by peril*. A Japanese earthquake, a Florida hurricane, an Australian wildfire, and a European windstorm are independent of one another even though the homeowner claims within any one of them are not. By pooling perils across the entire planet, a global reinsurer turns a handful of correlated regional disasters back into a roughly independent global portfolio, recovering the law of large numbers at a scale no single-country insurer can. When even that is not enough, reinsurers offload risk to capital markets through **catastrophe bonds** (cat bonds), securities that pay investors a juicy yield but wipe out the principal if a named catastrophe occurs, effectively turning bond investors into the ultimate reinsurers of last resort.

## Lloyd's of London and the syndicate market

Lloyd's of London is not a company. It is a *marketplace*, more than three centuries old, where insurance is bought and sold, and it works unlike anything else in finance. It began in Edward Lloyd's coffee house in the 1680s, where ship-owners and merchants met people willing to insure voyages. Those backers would write their name *under* the description of the risk to signal how much of it they would cover, which is the literal origin of the word **underwriter**. The structure that grew out of the coffee house still runs today.

At Lloyd's, the risk-takers are organized into **syndicates**, which are pools of capital assembled to underwrite insurance for a year. The capital behind a syndicate comes from **members**, historically wealthy individuals called **Names** who pledged their entire personal fortunes, with *unlimited liability*, to back the risks, and today mostly from corporations and institutional capital. The syndicates are run by **managing agents** who employ the actual underwriters. Brokers bring risks to the underwriters in the famous trading floor, and a single large risk, an oil rig, a satellite launch, a celebrity's voice, is often split across many syndicates, each taking a slice. Lloyd's specializes in exactly the unusual, large, hard-to-price risks that ordinary insurers will not touch.

The unlimited-liability model made Lloyd's powerful and, eventually, nearly fatal to its Names. For centuries, being a Name was a genteel way for the British wealthy to earn underwriting profits on capital they also kept invested elsewhere; the liability was unlimited but the disasters were rare. Then came the asbestos and pollution catastrophe of the late 1980s and early 1990s, which we will detail in the case studies. For now the structural point: Lloyd's is a market of syndicates, not a single balance sheet, and its historic strength, deep capacity for weird risks backed by members' fortunes, was also the channel through which a wave of long-tail claims could ruin thousands of individuals who thought they had simply made a clever investment.

![Tree of insurance types branching into life and health, property and casualty, and reinsurance, with a financial-guarantee leaf](/imgs/blogs/insurance-giants-and-systemic-risk-7.png)

The tree above places all of this in one taxonomy. Insurance branches into life and health (term and whole life policies, annuities), property and casualty (which itself contains catastrophe cover), and reinsurance, the insurers' insurer. The branch to watch is the one hanging off property-and-casualty on the lower right: **financial guarantees**, including bond insurance and credit default swaps. That branch looks like the others, it is sold by insurers, priced by underwriters, booked as a policy, but as the next section shows, it does not obey the law of large numbers, and that is what made it a time bomb.

## The matrix: how the three families differ

Before the AIG story, one consolidating figure. The three insurer families, life, P&C, and reinsurance, differ along three axes that determine how each one invests and how each one can die.

![Matrix comparing life, property-casualty, and reinsurance by what they insure, float duration, and main risk](/imgs/blogs/insurance-giants-and-systemic-risk-4.png)

A **life insurer** insures death and longevity, holds its float for *decades*, and its dominant risks are *low interest rates* (which starve the long-dated investments backing long-dated promises) and *longevity* (policyholders living longer than priced, which inflates annuity payouts). A **P&C insurer** insures cars, homes, and liability, holds float for *months to a few years*, and its dominant risk is *catastrophe*, a single correlated event swamping the pool. A **reinsurer** insures other insurers' tail losses, holds *lumpy* multi-year float, and its dominant risk is *correlated mega-losses*, the simultaneous worldwide bad day. Read across the rows and you can predict each family's behavior: life insurers are the great buyers of long-term bonds and are tortured by low rates; P&C insurers hold safer short-term assets and are tortured by storms; reinsurers hold huge capital buffers and are tortured by the rare global cluster. Every insurer's strategy is downstream of which row it lives in.

## AIG 2008: the time bomb in the Financial Products unit

American International Group was, for most of its history, exactly the boring giant the matrix describes: a sprawling, AAA-rated, globe-spanning insurer selling life and P&C policies to hundreds of millions of customers. The core insurance company was sound. The catastrophe lived in a unit most of AIG's own customers had never heard of: **AIG Financial Products (AIG-FP)**, a small London-and-Connecticut-based derivatives shop bolted onto the giant insurer, staffed by a few hundred people, that turned the company's pristine AAA credit rating into a money machine, and then into a noose.

### What AIG-FP actually sold

AIG-FP sold credit default swaps, the insurance-on-securities instrument we defined earlier. Specifically, it sold **protection** on **collateralized debt obligations (CDOs)**, which are bonds built by bundling thousands of loans, heavily mortgages, into a single security and slicing it into tranches of varying safety. Banks and investors who held these CDOs wanted insurance against them defaulting, and AIG-FP, leaning on the parent company's AAA rating, was delighted to sell it. Why delighted? Because the CDOs were rated triple-A and seemed almost impossible to default. To AIG-FP it looked like the easiest money in the world: collect a small premium every year for insuring something that, the models said, would essentially never pay out. By 2007 AIG-FP had written more than **\$500 billion** of this protection, with the mortgage-linked exposure famously around \$80 billion of it, and against this mountain it held essentially *no reserves*, because the accounting and the models treated default as a non-event.

Here is the fatal difference, and it is the foundations section coming home to roost. When you insure a million cars, the losses are independent; the law of large numbers makes the total predictable and you reserve against it. When you insure half a trillion dollars of mortgage securities, the losses are *correlated by a single national factor*: house prices. The models assumed house prices could not fall nationwide all at once, because they never had in the post-war data. They did. And when they did, every CDO soured at the same time. There was no diversification, because there was only ever one bet, made 500 billion dollars large: that American home prices would not fall together. AIG-FP had not built an insurance pool; it had built a single, gigantic, undiversified, unreserved short position on the U.S. housing market and labeled it insurance.

### The collateral trap

A CDS is more dangerous than a normal insurance policy in a way that has nothing to do with default. Most CDS contracts require the protection seller to post **collateral** as the insured security falls in market value, and as the seller's own credit rating drops, *even if no default has yet occurred*. This is the mechanism that turned a paper loss into a cash crisis. As mortgage CDOs lost market value in 2007 and 2008, AIG-FP's counterparties (banks like Goldman Sachs and Société Générale that had bought the protection) demanded billions in collateral to cover the growing mark-to-market loss. AIG could dispute the marks, and it did, but the contracts were against it.

![Timeline of AIG's 2008 unraveling from housing losses to ratings cut to collateral calls to the Fed rescue](/imgs/blogs/insurance-giants-and-systemic-risk-5.png)

The timeline above traces the unraveling. Through 2007, house prices fell and the CDOs began to sour. Through the first half of 2008, AIG-FP's losses on the CDS book mounted and the collateral calls grew. Then came the hammer blow. On September 15, 2008, the day Lehman Brothers failed, the credit rating agencies downgraded AIG. The downgrade was the trigger written directly into the CDS contracts: a lower rating automatically required AIG to post *much more* collateral, on top of the collateral already demanded by the falling marks. Within a day the combined demand exceeded \$20 billion, and the total need over the following weeks was far larger. AIG, the company, did not have the cash. The boring, solvent insurance subsidiaries held assets, but those assets belonged to policyholders and were ring-fenced by regulators; the parent could not simply raid them to feed AIG-FP's collateral calls.

### The collateral-call spiral

The two pressures fed each other in a way worth drawing explicitly, because it is the anatomy of the failure.

![Directed graph showing falling CDO prices driving both mark-to-market losses and a ratings downgrade, both forcing collateral calls](/imgs/blogs/insurance-giants-and-systemic-risk-6.png)

The graph above shows two reinforcing pressures converging on the same fatal demand. Falling CDO prices fed straight into two places at once. First, they created **mark-to-market losses**: AIG had to revalue its CDS book lower, and the lower marks triggered collateral calls to top up the counterparties' security. Second, the same falling prices and mounting losses fed the **ratings downgrade**, and the downgrade triggered *its own* additional collateral calls written into the contracts. Both arrows pointed at the same node: collateral calls, north of \$20 billion, that AIG could not meet. From there the path was mechanical: the calls drained AIG's liquidity, and when liquidity ran dry, the only remaining node was the Federal Reserve.

#### Worked example: AIG's collateral calls as its rating drops

Let us put rough numbers on the spiral to feel its mechanics, using illustrative figures that mirror the real structure. Say AIG-FP has written \$80 billion of CDS protection on mortgage CDOs. While the CDOs trade at par (100 cents on the dollar), no collateral is owed. Now the CDOs fall to 90 cents: the mark-to-market loss is 10 percent of \$80 billion, which is \$8 billion, and AIG must post roughly that \$8 billion as collateral to its counterparties.

The CDOs keep falling to 80 cents: the loss is now 20 percent of \$80 billion, which is \$16 billion of collateral owed, an extra \$8 billion on top. Then the ratings downgrade hits. The contracts say a downgrade requires additional collateral regardless of the marks, say another \$5 billion across the book. Add it up: \$16 billion from the marks plus \$5 billion from the downgrade is \$21 billion of cash AIG must produce, immediately, against contracts on which not a single bond has yet actually defaulted. The intuition: a credit default swap can bankrupt its seller with cash calls long before any insured event occurs, because the contract demands collateral against *paper* losses and against the seller's *own* weakness, two things ordinary insurance never does, and AIG had set aside nothing for either.

### The \$182 billion rescue

On September 16, 2008, one day after Lehman was allowed to fail, the Federal Reserve concluded that AIG could not be. AIG was woven into the financial system in a way Lehman, for all its size, was not: it had written CDS protection to banks all over the world who were *relying* on that protection to claim their own assets were safe. If AIG defaulted on the CDS, every one of those banks would suddenly have to write down the assets AIG had been insuring, blowing holes in balance sheets across Europe and the United States simultaneously. AIG was, in the phrase that entered the language that month, **too interconnected to fail**.

The Fed extended an initial \$85 billion secured credit line, taking a roughly 80 percent ownership stake in AIG in return. Over the following months the support was restructured and expanded repeatedly, through additional Fed facilities and Treasury's TARP program, until the total committed reached approximately **\$182 billion** (the figure most commonly cited; the amount actually drawn was somewhat less, and the government ultimately recovered its money and recorded a small profit by 2012 as AIG repaid and the stake was sold). A chunk of that money flowed straight through AIG to its CDS counterparties, who were paid out at or near par, a detail that caused lasting public fury, since it amounted to a public bailout of the banks on the other side of AIG's bets. The boring insurer in the middle had become the conduit through which the taxpayer backstopped the entire CDS-on-mortgages market.

The lesson regulators drew is the lesson of this whole post. The insurance company was not the problem. Insuring cars and lives, pooling independent risks against honest reserves, is one of the sturdiest businesses ever invented. The problem was a unit that used the insurer's credibility to write *financial guarantees on correlated securities with no reserves and a collateral trigger*. It was insurance in name and a leveraged, undiversified, mark-to-market derivative position in substance. The float machine had been quietly rewired into a time bomb.

## Systemically important insurers and the regulation that followed

For most of modern history, insurers were regulated very differently from banks, and largely not as systemic threats at all. In the United States, insurance is regulated state by state (there is no single federal insurance regulator the way there is for banks), with state commissioners overseeing reserves, capital, and the ring-fencing of policyholder assets. The logic was sound for *ordinary* insurance: because an insurer's liabilities (claims) come due slowly and somewhat predictably, an insurer cannot suffer a classic bank run, depositors cannot demand all their money back tomorrow, so it does not need the same liquidity backstop a bank does. AIG broke that comfortable assumption, because a CDS *collateral call* is a bank-run-like demand for immediate cash bolted onto an entity nobody was watching for liquidity risk.

There is a second, less-famous drain that hit AIG at the same time and reinforces the point that the danger lived in the *non-insurance* activities. AIG's life-insurance subsidiaries ran a large **securities-lending** program: they lent out the high-quality bonds backing their policies to other institutions in exchange for cash collateral, and then, fatefully, reinvested that cash collateral in mortgage-backed securities to earn extra yield. When the crisis hit, the borrowers wanted their securities back and demanded their cash collateral returned, but AIG had sunk that collateral into mortgage assets that were now illiquid and falling in value. So even the regulated insurance entities sprang a liquidity leak, not because insuring lives went wrong, but because a yield-chasing financial program bolted onto them went wrong, the same disease as AIG-FP in a different organ. The clean lesson: it was never the policies, it was always the financial engineering wrapped around the policies.

After 2008, the response was to designate the most interconnected insurers as **systemically important financial institutions (SIFIs)**, subjecting them to tougher capital rules and federal oversight, and internationally to label a handful of **global systemically important insurers (G-SIIs)** with extra capital requirements. AIG itself was designated a SIFI. Over the following years, as AIG shrank and shed AIG-FP, and as the industry pushed back arguing that *traditional* insurance is genuinely not systemic, several of these designations were rescinded; AIG's was removed in 2017. The regulatory pendulum settled on a nuanced position that maps exactly onto our thesis: traditional insurance, pooling independent risks against reserves, is not systemic and does not need bank-style oversight; *non-traditional, non-insurance* activities, the financial guarantees and securities lending and derivatives that AIG-FP embodied, are where insurers can become systemic, and those activities now draw scrutiny regardless of which legal entity books them.

## Life insurers and the slow danger of low rates

The AIG story is the spectacular failure mode, the explosion. There is a quieter, slower danger that threatens the largest and most boring insurers of all, the life insurers, and it deserves its own section because it has been a defining force in markets for over a decade.

Recall a life insurer's float is *long-duration*: it sells policies and annuities promising payouts decades into the future, and it backs those promises by buying long-dated bonds whose interest income funds the eventual claims. The arithmetic of this business assumes a certain level of interest rates. A policy sold in 1995 might have been priced assuming the insurer could earn 7 percent on the bonds backing it; some older annuities and whole-life policies even carry **guaranteed minimum interest rates** the insurer contractually promised to pay the policyholder. When market interest rates collapsed after 2008 and stayed near zero for over a decade, life insurers faced a brutal squeeze: the new bonds they had to buy yielded 2 or 3 percent, or less, while the old promises still demanded 5, 6, or 7. The spread that funded the business inverted.

#### Worked example: a life insurer caught by falling rates

A life insurer sold a block of annuities in 1995 promising to pay policyholders a guaranteed 6 percent a year. To fund that promise it bought \$1 billion of long-term bonds yielding 7 percent at the time. The math worked beautifully: the bonds threw off \$70 million a year, the insurer owed policyholders \$60 million a year, and it pocketed the \$10 million spread, a 1 percent margin on \$1 billion, as profit for bearing the risk.

Now run the clock to 2012. Those original bonds have matured, and the insurer must reinvest the \$1 billion. But market yields on safe long-term bonds have collapsed to 3 percent. Reinvested at 3 percent, the \$1 billion now earns only \$30 million a year, while the guaranteed annuity promise still obligates the insurer to pay \$60 million a year. The spread has flipped from plus \$10 million to *minus* \$30 million: the insurer is now losing \$30 million a year on a block of business that was a moneymaker for fifteen years, and it cannot cancel the guarantee. The intuition: a life insurer's profit is the spread between what its investments earn and what it promised, and when rates fall below the level a policy was priced at, that spread can turn deeply negative on promises the insurer is contractually trapped inside for decades.

It is precisely this trap that pushed conservative insurers toward riskier behavior, a phenomenon called **reaching for yield**: buying lower-quality, higher-yielding bonds, less-liquid private credit, and complex structured products to manufacture the returns their old promises required. None of this is a sudden explosion like AIG. It is a slow accumulation of credit and liquidity risk inside the most trusted, longest-horizon institutions in finance, justified by an interest-rate environment that made the safe path unprofitable. When rates rose sharply in 2022 and 2023, the squeeze eased on new business but created new pain, the old long-dated bonds insurers held fell in market value, and any insurer forced to sell them took losses. Life insurers live permanently impaled on the horns of interest-rate risk: low rates starve them slowly, and rate spikes mark down the assets they already hold.

## Common misconceptions

**"Insurers make money by denying claims."** This is the most common and most wrong belief. An insurer that systematically denied valid claims would lose its license, its customers, and most of its lawsuits. The real profit engine is the *combined ratio* (paying out a little less than premiums plus expenses) and, far more importantly, the *investment return on the float*. As the worked examples showed, an insurer with a 98 percent combined ratio is paying out 98 cents of every premium dollar in claims and costs; it is not getting rich by stiffing claimants, it is getting rich by investing the float for years before the legitimate claims come due.

**"Insurance is just gambling."** It is the structural *opposite* of gambling. A gambler takes on risk to chase an uncertain gain; an insured person *sheds* risk to buy certainty, paying a known premium to remove an unknown catastrophe. And the insurer is not gambling either: by pooling thousands of independent risks it converts an unpredictable individual bet into a predictable aggregate, which is exactly the move a casino makes by being the house across many bets. The mathematics runs in the insurer's favor *because* of pooling, not in spite of randomness. The only time insurance truly becomes gambling is when the pooled risks are not independent, which is precisely the AIG failure.

**"The float is the insurer's profit, so a bigger float is always better."** No. The float is *borrowed money*, the policyholders' premiums held in trust against claims that will come due. Its value depends entirely on its *cost*, which is set by underwriting. Float obtained through profitable underwriting is negative-cost leverage and is wonderful; float obtained by underpricing risk is expensive money whose true cost arrives later as claims that swamp the premiums. A ballooning float at an insurer with a deteriorating combined ratio is not a triumph, it is a warning that the company is buying growth by writing risk too cheaply.

**"AIG failed because its insurance business went bad."** AIG's core insurance subsidiaries were solvent throughout the crisis; their assets were ring-fenced and protected policyholders. The failure came entirely from AIG Financial Products, a derivatives unit that wrote credit default swaps, an instrument that is insurance in name only. The lesson is not that insurance is dangerous; it is that *financial guarantees on correlated securities, written with no reserves and a collateral trigger*, are dangerous no matter what kind of company writes them.

**"A credit default swap is the same as buying insurance on a bond."** Economically similar, structurally very different in two lethal ways. First, you can buy a CDS on a bond you do not own (a **naked CDS**), which is like insuring your neighbor's house, turning a hedge into a speculative bet and multiplying the total exposure far beyond the underlying bonds. Second, a CDS requires the seller to post *collateral* against paper losses and against its own credit downgrade, before any default occurs, which is what turns a CDS seller's bad year into an immediate cash crisis. Ordinary bond insurance has neither feature.

**"Reinsurance means the risk has gone away."** Reinsurance *moves and slices* risk; it does not delete it. When a primary insurer cedes its catastrophe tail to a reinsurer, the risk now sits on the reinsurer's books, and the primary insurer has gained a new risk, **counterparty risk**, the chance the reinsurer cannot pay when the catastrophe hits. In a truly global disaster, or if reinsurers themselves have concentrated the same peril, the risk can come right back. Reinsurance makes the system more robust by diversifying across perils and geographies, but the total risk in the world is conserved; it has only been redistributed to whoever is best able to hold it, until they are not.

## How it shows up in real markets

**AIG, September 2008.** The defining episode, detailed above, and the reason this post exists. A few hundred people in AIG Financial Products wrote more than \$500 billion of credit default swaps against the parent's AAA rating, held no reserves against them, and when U.S. house prices fell together for the first time in the post-war era, the correlated losses plus a ratings downgrade triggered more than \$20 billion of collateral calls the company could not meet. The Federal Reserve and Treasury committed roughly \$182 billion to prevent a default that would have cascaded through every bank holding AIG's protection. It remains the textbook case of how non-insurance activity inside an insurer can become systemic, and it links directly to the broader meltdown chronicled in the [Lehman Brothers 2008 crisis](/blog/trading/finance/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis).

**Berkshire Hathaway's float, compounding for half a century.** The mirror image of AIG, the float used wisely. Warren Buffett built one of history's great fortunes by recognizing that a disciplined insurer's float is negative-cost, long-term leverage. Berkshire's insurance float grew from a few million dollars in the late 1960s to well over \$160 billion (approximate, mid-2020s), and Buffett invested it in stocks and whole businesses, compounding the returns on money the policyholders effectively paid him to hold. The contrast with AIG is the entire lesson: the same float that built Berkshire blew up AIG, and the difference was whether the underwriting was disciplined and the risks independent.

**Catastrophe reinsurance after the big hurricanes.** Reinsurance pricing moves in dramatic cycles driven by disasters. After Hurricane Andrew in 1992, after Katrina in 2005, and after the active hurricane seasons and wildfires of recent years, reinsurance prices (the cost of catastrophe cover) spiked sharply, a phase the industry calls a **hard market**, because a big loss simultaneously destroys reinsurers' capital *and* reveals that the risk was underpriced. Andrew alone bankrupted multiple insurers and helped birth the modern catastrophe-bond market and the Bermuda reinsurance hub. Each hard market draws fresh capital chasing the now-higher premiums, which eventually softens prices again, until the next catastrophe. The cycle is the visible heartbeat of how the world prices its worst days.

**Lloyd's of London and the asbestos catastrophe.** From the late 1980s into the 1990s, Lloyd's was nearly destroyed by a wave of claims for *long-tail* liabilities, chiefly asbestos-related disease and environmental pollution, on policies written decades earlier. The losses were enormous and, crucially, fell on the **Names**, the individual members who had pledged their personal fortunes with unlimited liability. Thousands of Names were financially ruined; some lost their homes and a number died by suicide. Lloyd's survived only by a sweeping 1996 reconstruction that herded the toxic old liabilities into a dedicated reinsurance vehicle called **Equitas** and shifted the market toward limited-liability corporate capital. It is the canonical lesson in *long-tail* risk: a policy can sit quiet for thirty years and then deliver a claim larger than every premium it ever earned, and unlimited liability means there is no floor on the loss.

**Life insurers reaching for yield in the low-rate decade.** After 2008, with interest rates pinned near zero for over a decade, life insurers across the developed world faced old policy promises they could no longer fund with safe bonds. Many responded by reaching for yield, shifting into lower-rated corporate credit, commercial mortgages, private credit, and complex structured assets to manufacture the returns their guarantees demanded. Regulators in the U.S., Europe, and Japan flagged the slow buildup of credit and liquidity risk inside these supposedly ultra-conservative institutions. It is the quiet systemic risk that does not make headlines, an entire industry nudged toward riskier assets not by greed but by an interest-rate environment that made prudence unprofitable, and it is exactly the kind of pressure that the [field guide to financial institutions](/blog/trading/finance/field-guide-to-financial-institutions) frames as liability-driven investors stretching to meet long-dated promises.

## When this matters to you and further reading

If you ever buy a policy, the float model tells you why a financially strong, disciplined insurer matters more than the cheapest quote: the company holding your premium for thirty years against a future claim has to still be solvent and well-invested when the claim comes due, and the cheapest premium often signals an insurer underpricing risk to win volume, the very behavior that builds expensive float and ends in failure. The combined ratio and the credit rating of an insurer are public; they tell you whether the company protecting you is run by adults.

If you invest, insurers are a distinct and often misunderstood asset. A great insurer is a leveraged, tax-efficient investment fund whose leverage costs less than nothing, which is why Buffett built an empire on them; a poorly underwritten insurer is a slow-motion loss generator whose ballooning float is a trap. The four questions from the introduction, what does it insure, are the losses independent, where is the float, and what happens to it if the rating drops, are the whole analytical toolkit. Apply them to any insurer and you will know quickly whether you are looking at Berkshire or at AIG-FP.

And if you simply want to understand the financial system, the deepest takeaway is that the *form* of a contract matters far less than its *economic substance*. AIG-FP called its product insurance, booked it as policies, and priced it with underwriters, but underneath it was an undiversified, unreserved, collateral-triggered short position on the entire U.S. housing market. The label said insurance; the substance said leveraged derivative. Learning to see through the label to the substance, to ask whether the underlying risks are genuinely independent and genuinely reserved, is the single most valuable habit in finance, and it is the habit that would have let anyone reading AIG's footnotes in 2007 see the time bomb ticking.
