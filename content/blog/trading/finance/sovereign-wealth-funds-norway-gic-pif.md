> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A sovereign wealth fund is a government-owned investment fund that takes a national surplus (oil money, trade reserves, savings) and invests it across global markets over a multi-decade horizon, making some governments among the largest and most patient owners of the world's companies and bonds.
>
> - Two engines fund these pools: selling a commodity (oil, gas, copper) or running persistent trade and currency surpluses; in both cases the money belongs to the nation, not a private manager.
> - The model exists to cure the *resource curse* — the trap where a windfall is spent fast, the currency overheats, and the country is left poorer once the resource runs out.
> - Norway's fund holds roughly \$1.6 trillion (approximate, as of around 2024), owns about 1.5% of every listed company on earth, and is allowed to spend only about 3% of itself per year.
> - Their core edge is patience: with no depositors to repay and no quarterly redemptions, they can hold assets for decades and buy when everyone else is forced to sell.
> - The catch is power and opacity: the same patience makes them political actors — buying influence, rescuing banks, funding sports leagues — and many disclose far less than Norway does.
> - The one number to remember: Norway's fund is worth roughly **\$300,000 per citizen**, which is what saving a windfall instead of spending it looks like after thirty years.

Here is a number that should stop you. A country with about 5.5 million people — fewer than live in metropolitan Atlanta — owns a pot of investments worth roughly \$1.6 trillion, which works out to around \$300,000 for every single resident, newborns included. That country is Norway. It did not earn that wealth by being clever traders. It earned it by pumping oil out of the North Sea, refusing to spend most of the money, and parking the surplus in a fund that quietly bought a slice of almost every large company on the planet. Today that one fund owns, on average, about one and a half percent of every listed share in the world.

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: a nation runs a surplus — from selling a resource or from years of trade gains — and instead of consuming it, it pours that surplus into a state-owned fund, which spreads the money across thousands of companies and bonds and then lives off a thin slice of the returns. Multiply that by the dozen biggest funds and you get governments that are, collectively, some of the largest landlords, shareholders, and lenders the world has ever seen.

![Tree of sovereign wealth funds split by funding source into commodity and reserve types](/imgs/blogs/sovereign-wealth-funds-norway-gic-pif-1.png)

This is one of the quietest concentrations of economic power in modern finance, and most people have never thought about it. When you read that an Abu Dhabi fund owns part of a London office tower, that a Saudi fund bankrolls a new golf league, or that a Singapore fund took a bath on a crypto exchange, you are watching the same machine at work: national savings, invested at planetary scale, by people who answer to a government rather than to depositors or shareholders. That machine can stabilize a fragile economy, fund a nation's pensions for centuries, and rescue banks in a panic. It can also project geopolitical influence, launder reputations, and operate with almost no transparency. Both descriptions are true at once.

We will build the whole thing from zero. If you do not know what a bond is, what "reserves" means, or why selling oil can secretly make a country poorer, you are exactly the reader this is written for. Every term gets defined the first time it appears, every big claim gets grounded in a worked example with real dollars, and only then do we climb to the hard questions about patient capital and national power.

## Foundations: surpluses, reserves, and the curse a fund is built to cure

Let us start with the smallest pieces and stack them up.

A **surplus** is simply money left over after you have paid for everything you needed. A household that earns \$60,000 and spends \$50,000 has a \$10,000 surplus. A government runs a surplus when its income (taxes, and for some countries the sale of state-owned resources) exceeds what it spends. Most governments run the opposite — a *deficit*, spending more than they take in and borrowing the difference. But a handful of countries, for a while, take in far more than they can sensibly spend. The question of what to do with that excess is where sovereign wealth funds are born.

There are two main ways a country ends up with such a surplus, and they define the two great families of funds.

The first is **commodity wealth**. A *commodity* is a raw material that is more or less interchangeable from one seller to the next — a barrel of oil, a ton of copper, a cubic foot of natural gas. When a country sits on a large deposit of one and sells it abroad, foreign buyers send in a torrent of cash. Norway has North Sea oil. Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, and the United Arab Emirates have oil and gas. Chile has copper. The key feature of commodity wealth is that it is *finite and volatile*: the resource will eventually run out, and its price swings wildly from year to year. A barrel of oil might fetch \$120 one year and \$30 the next.

The second is **reserve or savings wealth**. To understand this we need the term **foreign-exchange reserves**, usually shortened to *reserves*: the stockpile of foreign money (mostly US dollars) and safe foreign bonds that a country's central bank holds. A country builds up reserves when it consistently exports more than it imports — it keeps receiving foreign currency and salting it away. China, with decades of selling manufactured goods to the world, and Singapore, a trading hub that runs persistent surpluses, accumulated enormous reserves this way. Reserves are not finite like oil; they are the residue of a trading machine.

Now, a **fund** in this context is a single pooled pot of money invested across many different assets. The country puts its surplus into the fund, and professional managers invest it. That brings us to the central definition.

A **sovereign wealth fund** (often abbreviated *SWF*) is a state-owned investment fund that holds and invests a nation's surplus wealth — commodity revenue, foreign reserves, or fiscal savings — typically across a globally diversified portfolio and over a very long horizon. Three features matter. It is *owned by the state*, not by private investors or depositors. It invests *the nation's own surplus*, not borrowed money or money it owes back to anyone. And it has a *long horizon*, often measured in generations, because there is no depositor who can show up tomorrow demanding their cash.

That last point deserves a contrast. A **bank** takes deposits it must repay on demand, so it cannot lock money up for thirty years. A **pension fund** owes monthly checks to retirees, so it must keep enough liquid. A **hedge fund** answers to investors who can pull their money out, often within months. (For how those leverage-and-fee machines work, see [how hedge funds work](/blog/trading/finance/how-hedge-funds-work-leverage-2-and-20).) A sovereign wealth fund, uniquely, owes *no one* a near-term payment. That single difference — no one can force it to sell — is the source of nearly all its advantages and several of its dangers.

We need two more building blocks before the deep part.

The **mandate** is the written purpose a fund is given by its government. Some funds are *savings funds*, built to preserve wealth for future generations long after the oil is gone. Some are *stabilization funds*, built to smooth the national budget when commodity prices crash. Some are *development funds*, built to invest at home and reshape the domestic economy. A few try to do all three. The mandate determines everything downstream: how aggressively the fund invests, how much it is allowed to spend, and whether it buys boring index funds or trophy real estate.

And finally, the problem all of this is meant to solve: the **resource curse**. It sounds paradoxical — how can finding oil make you poorer? — so let us be precise. When a flood of foreign money pours into a country to buy its oil, several bad things tend to happen together. The local currency strengthens because everyone wants it, which makes every *other* export (farming, manufacturing) too expensive to sell abroad, so those industries wither. This specific channel has a name: **Dutch disease**, after what happened to the Netherlands' manufacturing after it found natural gas in the 1960s. Meanwhile the government, flush with easy cash, spends freely, lets corruption fester, and builds a budget that depends on the windfall continuing. Then the price crashes or the resource runs dry, and the country is left with an overvalued economy, hollowed-out industries, and a spending habit it can no longer afford. The resource curse is the empirical observation that, on average, resource-rich countries have grown *more slowly* than resource-poor ones. A sovereign wealth fund is the leading institutional cure: take the windfall *out* of the domestic economy, invest it abroad, and spend only a sustainable trickle.

So here is the foundational picture in one breath. A country with more money than it can wisely spend puts the excess into a state-owned fund. The fund invests it across the whole world for decades. The country lives off a thin slice of the returns, and in doing so dodges the curse that has impoverished other resource-rich nations. Now let us slow it down and look at how the money actually moves.

## How a barrel of oil becomes a permanent claim on the world

Trace a single dollar of oil money through the system and the genius of the design becomes obvious.

![Pipeline from oil revenue through the government budget into a fund and out as a small payout](/imgs/blogs/sovereign-wealth-funds-norway-gic-pif-4.png)

Step one: a foreign refiner buys Norwegian crude and pays for it in dollars. Step two: through taxes and royalties (a *royalty* is a fee the state charges for the right to extract a public resource), a large share of that payment lands in the government's hands. Step three — and this is the crucial discipline — instead of routing that money into this year's spending, the government deposits it into the fund. Step four: the fund's managers convert it into a diversified portfolio, buying small stakes in thousands of companies and governments' bonds across dozens of countries. Step five: years later, the fund hands a thin, sustainable slice of its returns back to the national budget.

The transformation here is profound. A barrel of oil is a *one-time, finite* asset — once you sell it, it is gone forever. By running it through the fund, the country converts that finite, vanishing resource into a *permanent, renewable* claim on the productive output of the entire world economy. The oil will run out. The portfolio, if managed well, never does. Norway frames this explicitly: the fund is meant to turn the nation's depleting petroleum wealth into a financial endowment that benefits Norwegians forever, not just while the wells flow.

This is what people mean by **patient capital** — money that can be invested for the long run because no one will demand it back on short notice. We will return to why patience is such a powerful edge. First, an example to make the saving discipline concrete.

#### Worked example: \$50-a-barrel oil flowing into the fund

Suppose a country produces 2 million barrels of oil a day and sells them at \$50 a barrel. That is \$100 million a day of oil revenue, or about \$36.5 billion a year (\$100 million × 365). Now suppose its production cost is \$20 a barrel, so the *surplus* — the profit beyond what extraction cost — is \$30 a barrel. On 2 million barrels a day, that is \$60 million a day of pure surplus, roughly \$21.9 billion a year.

A disciplined country routes that \$21.9 billion annual surplus into its fund rather than its budget. If the fund already holds \$500 billion and earns a 5% real return (real meaning *after inflation*), it generates another \$25 billion a year from investments alone. So in this single year the fund grows by about \$46.9 billion — \$21.9 billion of fresh oil money plus \$25 billion of investment returns — even before next year's oil sales. Within a decade, the *returns* on the accumulated pile start to rival or exceed the oil inflows themselves.

The intuition: the point of a fund is not the oil money per se, it is the moment the fund grows faster from investing than from drilling — that is when a country has truly escaped its dependence on the ground beneath it.

## The big funds: five giants, one idea, very different temperaments

The largest sovereign wealth funds all run the same basic play, but they differ enormously in size, in where their money comes from, in how aggressively they invest, and — most importantly for the governance debate — in how much they tell the public. The matrix below lays the five biggest side by side; we then walk through each.

![Matrix comparing five large sovereign wealth funds by assets, source, strategy, and transparency](/imgs/blogs/sovereign-wealth-funds-norway-gic-pif-2.png)

All AUM figures below are approximate and as of roughly 2024; they move constantly with markets and oil prices. **AUM** stands for *assets under management* — the total dollar value of everything the fund holds. It is the headline number for any fund, but as the matrix shows, it tells you less than the other three columns.

### Norway's GPFG: the transparent giant

Norway's fund has a formal name, the *Government Pension Fund Global*, almost always shortened to **GPFG** and known at home simply as "the oil fund." Despite the word "pension," it is not a pension fund in the usual sense; it is a national savings fund whose returns help backstop future government spending, including pensions. At roughly \$1.6 trillion it is the largest single sovereign wealth fund in the world.

Its defining trait is **transparency**. Norway publishes the fund's holdings — every company, down to the share count — once a year. It reports performance quarterly. It explains its votes. This openness is partly cultural and partly strategic: a fund that owns pieces of thousands of foreign companies is less threatening to host governments if everyone can see exactly what it owns and that it behaves as a passive, rule-bound investor rather than a political arm of the Norwegian state.

The GPFG is also overwhelmingly a **passive, index-style investor**. Rather than betting on a handful of winners, it buys a little of nearly everything, weighted by company size, to mirror the global market. This is the same low-cost philosophy that built the giant US index-fund managers; if that mechanism is new to you, the [Big Three](/blog/trading/finance/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street) post unpacks how passive investing quietly became dominant. The GPFG owns stakes in roughly 9,000 companies across more than 70 countries, which is how it ends up holding, on average, about 1.5% of every listed company on earth.

Two features make Norway the textbook model. The first is the **spending rule**: by law, the government may withdraw only the fund's expected long-run real return — set at about 3% a year — into the budget. This is the discipline that keeps the fund from being raided. The second is the **Council on Ethics**, an independent body that can recommend *excluding* companies the fund refuses to own on ethical grounds — makers of certain weapons, companies that violate human rights, severe polluters, and, in a landmark move, coal producers. We will return to both. Norway is the fund the others are measured against precisely because it solved the two hardest problems — spending discipline and legitimacy — out in the open.

### Singapore: two funds, two philosophies

Singapore is unusual in running *two* state investment vehicles with deliberately different jobs.

**GIC** (originally the Government of Singapore Investment Corporation, now just GIC) manages the country's foreign reserves — the savings-wealth model. Its mandate is to preserve and grow the international purchasing power of those reserves over the long run. It invests globally across every asset class — public stocks, bonds, real estate, private companies — and is famously secretive about its exact size, though it is estimated to manage on the order of \$770 billion or more. GIC behaves like a careful, globally diversified long-term endowment.

**Temasek** is a different animal. It is a state-owned investment company that holds stakes in operating businesses, originally the government's shareholdings in Singaporean companies like Singapore Airlines and DBS Bank, and increasingly direct stakes in companies around the world. Where GIC is a diversified portfolio manager, Temasek is more like a giant, government-owned private-equity and direct-investing firm that takes concentrated, active stakes and sometimes sits on boards. The two-model split is instructive: a nation can run both a cautious reserve-preserver and an aggressive direct-investor under one government, and Singapore deliberately keeps them separate so each can do its job well.

### The Gulf: oil money at scale

The Persian Gulf is the historic heartland of sovereign wealth funds, and it hosts several of the largest.

**ADIA**, the Abu Dhabi Investment Authority, is one of the oldest and most secretive of the giants, managing on the order of \$1 trillion of the emirate's oil wealth. For decades it disclosed almost nothing, investing quietly through external managers across global markets. Abu Dhabi also runs other vehicles (Mubadala, ADQ), making the emirate as a whole one of the most concentrated pools of sovereign capital anywhere.

**PIF**, Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund, is the most consequential newcomer. Historically a sleepy holding company, since 2015 it has been transformed into the financial engine of *Vision 2030* — Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's plan to wean the Saudi economy off oil. PIF, holding on the order of \$925 billion, is unusual among the giants because much of its mandate is *domestic and developmental*: it is funding NEOM (a planned \$500-billion futuristic city), buying stakes in electric-vehicle maker Lucid and ride-hailing firm Uber, taking over English football club Newcastle United, and bankrolling the LIV Golf league. PIF is the clearest example of a fund used not just to grow wealth but to reshape an economy and project soft power.

**QIA**, the Qatar Investment Authority, runs perhaps \$500 billion and is known for trophy assets — it owns Harrods, large stakes in Volkswagen and Barclays, and chunks of prime London and Paris real estate. **KIA**, the Kuwait Investment Authority, is the *oldest* sovereign wealth fund in the world, tracing to 1953, and runs perhaps \$800 billion through a long-horizon savings fund for future generations.

### China's CIC: investing the world's largest reserves

**CIC**, the China Investment Corporation, was created in 2007 to earn a higher return on a slice of China's gargantuan foreign-exchange reserves, which at the time were the largest on earth. Initially funded with around \$200 billion and now managing well over \$1 trillion, CIC is the flagship reserve-funded fund. Its early forays — a stake in private-equity firm Blackstone and in Morgan Stanley right before the 2008 crisis — lost money fast and taught it caution. CIC's challenge is that it is a Chinese state entity investing in Western strategic assets at a time of geopolitical tension, which means many of its would-be deals draw national-security scrutiny abroad.

#### Worked example: 1.5% of a \$100,000 company is your stake — and your vote

To feel what "owning 1.5% of every listed company" means, shrink the world to one small firm. Imagine a company worth \$100,000 in total, divided into 100,000 shares worth \$1 each. Norway's fund, holding 1.5%, owns 1,500 of those shares, worth \$1,500.

That \$1,500 is small change to a \$1.6 trillion fund. But here is the part people miss: each share usually carries one *vote* on company decisions, most importantly electing the board of directors (the small group that hires and fires the CEO). So Norway's stake is not just \$1,500 of money — it is 1,500 votes out of 100,000, a 1.5% voting bloc.

Now scale it back up. The fund holds roughly 1.5% of around 9,000 companies. In aggregate, that is millions of votes across nearly every major corporation on earth. No single 1.5% stake controls anything. But a consistent 1.5% voter that shows up at 9,000 annual meetings, every year, with published voting principles, becomes a force management cannot ignore.

The intuition: a small percentage of everything is still an enormous amount of influence, because influence comes from the *votes attached to ownership*, not just the dollars.

## How they actually invest: from index funds to trophy assets

Underneath the differences, every large fund faces the same problem — how to spread an enormous sum across the world's assets in a way that grows over decades without blowing up. The answer is a layered portfolio, and the layers tell you the fund's temperament.

![Stack of a fund's asset allocation from equities at the base up to private equity](/imgs/blogs/sovereign-wealth-funds-norway-gic-pif-5.png)

The foundation of most funds is **listed equities** — shares of publicly traded companies. A *share* is a slice of ownership in a company; *listed* means it trades on a public stock exchange so anyone can buy it. Equities are where the long-run growth comes from, so a long-horizon fund tilts heavily toward them. Norway's GPFG holds roughly 70% of its money in equities, far more than a pension fund could stomach, precisely because its horizon is so long that it can ride out crashes.

The next layer is **bonds** — loans to governments and companies that pay fixed interest. A *bond* is an IOU: you lend \$1,000, the borrower pays you interest each year and returns your \$1,000 at maturity. Bonds are the ballast: they fall less in a crisis and provide income, smoothing the ride. The GPFG holds roughly a quarter of its money in bonds.

Then come the **real assets** — chiefly **real estate** (office towers, shopping centers, logistics warehouses) and increasingly infrastructure (toll roads, wind farms, ports). These are illiquid, meaning you cannot sell them quickly, which is exactly why a patient fund likes them: it gets paid an *illiquidity premium*, an extra return for accepting that it cannot get its money out fast — and it does not need to.

At the top sits the most aggressive layer: **private equity** and **venture capital**. *Private equity* means owning companies that are *not* listed on a public exchange — buying them whole, improving them, and selling years later. *Venture capital* is investing in young, risky startups. These can earn the highest returns but lock money up for a decade or more and can fail outright. The funds with the most appetite here — PIF, Temasek, GIC — are reaching for trophy stakes and direct deals; the most conservative, like the GPFG historically, have kept these tiny.

The trend across the industry is unmistakable: funds are shifting *up the stack*, away from plain public stocks and bonds and toward private equity, venture, infrastructure, and direct trophy investments. The reason is partly the hunt for higher returns in a world of low bond yields, and partly the unique advantage we turn to next.

#### Worked example: PIF takes a \$3.5 billion stake in Uber

In 2016, Saudi Arabia's PIF invested \$3.5 billion into Uber — at the time one of the largest single checks ever written into a private startup. Let us see why a sovereign fund, specifically, could do that deal.

Uber was not listed on any stock exchange in 2016. To put in \$3.5 billion, an investor had to be willing to hold an illiquid private stake with *no way to sell quickly* — Uber would not go public until 2019. A pension fund needing to pay monthly checks, or a bank needing daily liquidity, would struggle to lock up that much for years. PIF could, because no one can demand its money back tomorrow.

The size also matters. \$3.5 billion is a rounding error against PIF's hundreds of billions, so even if Uber had failed entirely, the loss would have been survivable. And the deal bought more than a financial return: it placed a PIF official on Uber's board and signaled that Saudi capital was a serious player in Silicon Valley — a strategic, not purely financial, payoff.

The intuition: a sovereign fund can write checks no one else can, because it combines a near-bottomless balance sheet with a horizon long enough to wait out illiquidity, and it often values influence alongside returns.

## The patient-capital advantage: why "no one can make us sell" is a superpower

We keep returning to patience, so let us make it precise, because it is the single most important concept for understanding why these funds are so powerful.

Most investors have a *liability* — something they owe to someone else on a schedule. A bank owes depositors their money on demand. A pension fund owes retirees their checks every month. A mutual fund must give investors their cash within days if they redeem. An insurance company must pay claims as they arrive. These liabilities force selling at the worst possible times: in a crash, depositors withdraw, fund investors redeem, claims spike — and the investor must dump assets into a falling market to raise cash, locking in losses.

A sovereign wealth fund, by design, has almost none of this. Its only outflow is the slow, rule-bound trickle to the national budget — Norway's 3%, paid out of a \$1.6 trillion base. So when a crisis hits and everyone else is a *forced seller*, the sovereign fund is one of the few investors who can be a *buyer*. It can step into a panic, buy assets at fire-sale prices from desperate sellers, and wait years for the recovery. This is the mirror image of the forced-seller dynamic that destroys leveraged investors; the same crash that wiped out [LTCM](/blog/trading/finance/how-hedge-funds-work-leverage-2-and-20) is a buying opportunity for a fund that owes no one.

This patience compounds into three concrete edges. First, the **illiquidity premium**: the fund earns extra return on assets it cannot sell quickly, because it genuinely does not need to. Second, **buying low in crises**: it provides liquidity exactly when it is scarcest and most expensive, which is when returns are highest. Third, **time itself**: with a multi-decade horizon, the fund can ride the long upward drift of the global economy through every panic and recovery, while shorter-horizon investors get shaken out.

But — and this is the honest counterweight — patience is not magic. A patient investor who buys the *wrong* thing simply holds a bad investment for a very long time. Patience lets you wait out volatility; it does not let you wait out a permanent loss. CIC's pre-crisis Blackstone stake and Temasek's crypto bet (both below) show that a long horizon protects you from being *forced* to sell at the bottom, but it does nothing to protect you from buying something that goes to zero.

There is a fourth, quieter edge that only a patient owner enjoys: the freedom to *not* trade. Every time an investor buys or sells, it pays a cost — the broker's commission, the *bid-ask spread* (the gap between the price you can buy at and the price you can sell at), and the tax on any gain. A jittery investor who reshuffles its portfolio constantly bleeds a little on every trade. A sovereign fund that holds for decades trades rarely, so those frictions barely touch it. Combined with the index approach — owning the whole market rather than paying analysts to pick stocks — this is why Norway's all-in costs run to just a few hundredths of a percent a year, a fraction of what an active manager charges. Low cost is not glamorous, but compounded over thirty years it is worth more than almost any clever trade.

#### Worked example: why patient compounding beats a clever year

Compare two funds that both start with \$100 billion. Fund A is brilliant but restless: it earns 8% one year, but its constant trading and fees cost 2% a year, netting 6%. Fund B is boring and patient: it earns 7% and pays just 0.1% in costs, netting roughly 6.9%. The gap looks tiny — 6% versus 6.9%, less than a percentage point.

Now let time do its work. After 30 years, Fund A grows to \$100 billion × (1.06)^30 ≈ \$574 billion. Fund B grows to \$100 billion × (1.069)^30 ≈ \$740 billion. The patient, low-cost fund ends up with about \$166 billion *more* — roughly 29% larger — despite earning a lower headline return in our example, purely because it kept its costs near zero and let compounding run undisturbed.

The intuition: over a multi-decade horizon, the boring advantages — low fees, low turnover, never panic-selling — compound into a lead that a flashier, costlier strategy almost never catches.

## When the oil price crashes: the macro-stabilization role

For petro-states — countries whose budgets depend heavily on oil revenue — a sovereign fund plays a second role beyond growing wealth: it stabilizes the entire economy across the brutal swings of the commodity cycle. This is worth its own diagram, because it is where the fund earns its keep for an ordinary citizen.

![Graph showing how a fund cushions a petro-economy across the oil price cycle](/imgs/blogs/sovereign-wealth-funds-norway-gic-pif-6.png)

Follow the two paths. Both start at an oil *boom*, with prices high at, say, \$100 a barrel. A country *without* a fund tends to spend the windfall — it raises public salaries, launches projects, builds a budget that assumes \$100 oil continues. A country *with* a fund saves the windfall instead, sending the surplus into the fund and keeping its budget on a sustainable path.

Then the *bust* arrives — and it always does — with oil at, say, \$30. The country that spent it all now faces a budget crisis: revenue has collapsed, but the salaries and projects are committed, so it must slash spending or borrow heavily in a panic, often triggering a recession or a currency crisis. The country with the fund draws its steady payout, which is calculated off the fund's *whole* value rather than this year's oil price, so the budget barely notices the crash. The fund acts as a giant shock absorber, decoupling government spending from the wild swings of the oil price.

#### Worked example: a 3% spending rule on \$1.6 trillion

This is the most important arithmetic in the entire model, so we will do it carefully. Norway's rule says the government may transfer to the budget, on average, only the fund's *expected real return* — about 3% a year.

Take the fund at \$1.6 trillion. Three percent of \$1.6 trillion is \$48 billion (0.03 × 1,600 = 48). So Norway can spend roughly \$48 billion per year from the fund without shrinking it in real terms, because that is roughly what the fund is expected to earn after inflation. The principal stays intact; only the *fruit* is harvested.

Why 3% and not 10%? Because spending more than the fund earns would eat the principal. If Norway withdrew \$160 billion (10%) one year, and the fund earned only \$48 billion, it would have to liquidate \$112 billion of holdings — and a fund that shrinks each year eventually disappears. The 3% rule is precisely the rate that lets the fund pay out *forever*. It is the same logic an endowment uses: spend the income, never the seed corn.

Now feel the scale. That \$48 billion is roughly a fifth of Norway's entire annual government budget — schools, hospitals, roads — funded not by taxing Norwegians but by the returns on saved oil money. And it arrives whether oil is at \$100 or \$30, because it is computed off the fund, not the oil price.

The intuition: a spending rule turns a finite, volatile resource into a permanent, steady income — the difference between eating the goose and eating its eggs.

#### Worked example: Norway's fund is worth \$300,000 per citizen

Let us verify the headline number, because it captures the whole point. The GPFG holds roughly \$1.6 trillion. Norway has about 5.5 million people. Divide: \$1,600,000,000,000 ÷ 5,500,000 ≈ \$290,000 per person, which rounds to roughly \$300,000 per citizen, infants included.

Compare two countries that both struck oil. One spent the money as it came — higher salaries, subsidized fuel, grand projects — and has little to show for it now that prices have fallen. The other, Norway, saved it, and every citizen now notionally "owns" about \$300,000 of global stocks and bonds working on their behalf. Neither family in either country feels richer day to day; the Norwegian one cannot withdraw their \$300,000. But Norway's government can draw \$48 billion a year, forever, to fund services without taxing its people as heavily.

The intuition: the difference between a resource blessing and a resource curse is almost entirely a question of whether the windfall was *consumed* or *saved* — and \$300,000 per citizen is what thirty years of saving looks like.

## A history in milestones: from one Kuwaiti reserve to an \$11 trillion bloc

The idea did not arrive all at once. It spread, fund by fund, as more nations watched the alternative — spending the windfall and suffering the curse — and chose to save instead.

The story starts in **1953**, when Kuwait — then still a British protectorate — set up the Kuwait Investment Board in London to invest its early oil revenue, the first recognizable sovereign wealth fund. **1976** brought ADIA, as Abu Dhabi institutionalized its oil wealth. **1990** saw Norway pass the law creating its fund, though the first oil money was not actually deposited until 1996 — a reminder that the discipline of *not* spending takes years to bite.

The model went global in the 2000s. **2007** brought China's CIC, funded from the world's largest reserve pile, signaling that the reserve-funded model had arrived at scale. Then came **2008**: when the global financial crisis hit and Western banks teetered, Gulf and Asian sovereign funds were among the few investors with cash to inject, and they bought stakes in Citigroup, Merrill Lynch, Morgan Stanley, Barclays, and UBS. For a moment, the patient-capital advantage was on full display — and so was the discomfort of having Western banking rescued by foreign governments.

**2016** marked a new phase, as Saudi Arabia repurposed PIF as the engine of Vision 2030, signaling that funds would increasingly be tools of economic transformation and influence, not just quiet savings vehicles. By **2024**, sovereign wealth funds collectively held more than \$11 trillion — a sum that rivals the entire annual economic output of China, controlled by a few dozen governments and growing.

## The Santiago Principles: trying to make giants behave

As these funds grew, a fear grew with them in the capital-importing countries of the West: were these funds *investors*, or were they *foreign governments* buying strategic influence under the cover of profit-seeking? When a state-owned fund buys a stake in a defense contractor, a bank, or a critical port, the host country worries it is being not invested in but quietly controlled. This anxiety peaked around 2007–2008 as Gulf and Chinese funds bought into Western finance.

The response, in 2008, was the **Santiago Principles** — a voluntary set of 24 *Generally Accepted Principles and Practices* drawn up by the funds themselves under the International Monetary Fund's wing. The principles ask funds to disclose their governance, separate the fund's investment decisions from short-term political direction, invest on a commercial basis, and be transparent about their objectives. The bargain was explicit: funds would accept some openness and self-restraint, and in exchange Western countries would keep their markets open to sovereign capital rather than slamming the door.

The Santiago Principles matter, but their limits matter more. They are *voluntary* — there is no enforcement, no penalty for ignoring them. Compliance varies enormously: Norway and New Zealand are gold-standard transparent; several Gulf and Asian funds disclose little beyond what the principles minimally require. A useful yardstick here is the **Linaburg-Maduell Transparency Index**, a 10-point score for fund disclosure; Norway scores a perfect 10, while some funds sit near the bottom. The principles civilized the conversation without resolving it: they made the funds promise to behave like commercial investors, but left each fund to decide how much to reveal and how independent of its government it would truly be.

## Who do they answer to? Governance and the ethics question

This is the deepest question, and it has no clean answer. A sovereign wealth fund sits at an awkward intersection: it manages public money, so it should answer to citizens; but it is controlled by a government, which may have its own agenda; and it invests in foreign companies, where it wields shareholder power without being accountable to those host countries.

The best-governed funds erect walls between the politicians and the portfolio. Norway's structure is the model: the *Ministry of Finance* sets the high-level mandate (the allowed asset mix, the spending rule), but the day-to-day investing is delegated to *Norges Bank Investment Management*, an arm of the central bank, which operates at arm's length from elected officials. The fund's actions are debated in parliament and reported to the public. The Norwegian people are, in a real sense, the fund's owners and overseers.

The ethics dimension is where Norway went furthest. Its independent **Council on Ethics** reviews the fund's holdings against published criteria and can recommend that the fund *exclude* — refuse to own — companies on ethical grounds. Over the years the fund has excluded makers of nuclear weapons and certain other armaments, tobacco producers, companies tied to severe human-rights abuses or environmental destruction, and — in a globally significant decision starting around 2015 — many coal companies. Because the fund is so large, an exclusion is a real signal: being dropped from the world's biggest single equity owner carries reputational weight. This is a government using its investment power as a moral and political instrument, which is exactly the capability that makes other observers nervous.

The contrast with the most opaque funds is stark. When a fund discloses almost nothing — not its holdings, not its votes, not its decision-making — citizens cannot tell whether it is being managed for their benefit or raided for an elite's, and foreign companies cannot tell whether the stake is a passive investment or the opening move of a strategic play. Opacity is not neutral; it concentrates power and removes the check that disclosure provides.

The structural design choices that produce good governance are surprisingly concrete. Three guardrails recur in the well-run funds. The first is a *clear, legislated mandate* — the fund's purpose, allowed assets, and spending rule are written into law, not left to a minister's discretion, so the rules cannot be quietly bent in a tough budget year. The second is *operational independence* — the people choosing the actual investments sit at arm's length from the politicians who set the mandate, with their own board and a fixed term, so a single election cannot redirect the portfolio. The third is *mandatory disclosure* — regular public reporting of holdings, returns, and votes, which lets citizens and parliaments hold the fund accountable. Where all three are present, as in Norway, the fund stays a savings vehicle. Where they are absent, the same pool of money can drift toward serving whoever controls the government rather than the nation, which is precisely the risk that the resource curse describes in financial rather than political terms.

## The politics: leverage, opacity, and sportswashing

The same features that make sovereign funds economically powerful make them politically potent, and not always benignly. Three concerns recur.

The first is **geopolitical leverage**. When a government owns large stakes in another country's banks, ports, energy firms, and real estate, those holdings can become bargaining chips. A fund can, in principle, threaten to dump a country's bonds, withhold investment, or vote its shares to political ends. In practice the giant funds mostly behave as commercial investors — dumping assets would hurt the fund itself — but the *capability* shapes diplomacy, and host countries increasingly screen sovereign investments in sensitive sectors through national-security reviews.

The second is **opacity**. We have covered it, but the political edge is this: a fund that does not disclose its holdings can build strategic positions quietly and act without public scrutiny at home or abroad. Opacity converts an investment fund into a potential instrument of statecraft that is hard to monitor.

The third, and most discussed lately, is **sportswashing** — the use of high-profile sports investments to improve a country's international image and distract from its human-rights record. PIF's takeover of Newcastle United, its creation and funding of LIV Golf, and Gulf funds' broader push into football, Formula 1, and global sports are widely read this way: the financial return is secondary to the reputational and soft-power return. Whether you see this as savvy nation-branding or as image-laundering, it is a clear case of a fund being used for ends well beyond growing wealth — which is precisely what the Santiago Principles asked funds *not* to be seen doing.

## Common misconceptions

**"A sovereign wealth fund is just the country's bank account."** No. A bank account holds cash you can spend tomorrow; a sovereign wealth fund is a long-horizon investment portfolio of stocks, bonds, real estate, and private companies that you specifically are *not* supposed to spend down. The whole point is that the principal is locked away and only a thin slice of the returns is spent. Treating it like a checking account — raiding it when budgets are tight — is exactly the failure mode the spending rule exists to prevent.

**"These funds own and control the companies they invest in."** Mostly no. The GPFG owns about 1.5% of an average company — a meaningful voting bloc, but nowhere near control. Even the largest single stakes are usually minority positions. The funds are large *owners* in aggregate across thousands of firms, which gives them soft influence and a lot of votes, but only rarely outright control of any one company. The exceptions — Temasek's controlling stakes in some Singaporean firms, PIF's full takeover of Newcastle — are deliberate and visible, not the norm.

**"Sovereign wealth funds are risk-free because governments stand behind them."** No. The money is invested in markets, and markets fall. The GPFG lost over \$160 billion in the 2022 market downturn (and recovered later); CIC lost heavily on pre-crisis bank stakes; Temasek wrote off its FTX investment entirely. A government owner does not make the assets safe — it just means no depositor can be wiped out, because there are no depositors. The *fund* can and does lose enormous sums; it simply cannot suffer a run.

**"They earn their money by being brilliant investors."** Rarely. Norway's returns come overwhelmingly from being a cheap, diversified, patient owner of the whole market, not from clever stock-picking — its edge is *behavioral and structural* (it does not panic-sell, it pays tiny fees, it holds forever), not predictive genius. The funds that have tried to be clever, like CIC's early concentrated bets, often did worse than the boring index approach.

**"A big sovereign wealth fund means the country's people are rich."** Not necessarily in the way you would expect. Norwegians cannot withdraw their \$300,000 share; it funds public services rather than personal accounts. And a large fund can coexist with ordinary citizens who feel no richer — the wealth is collective and future-oriented, not a check in the mail. The fund's benefit is steadier public finances and a buffer for future generations, not present-day personal wealth.

**"All sovereign wealth funds are alike."** No — and conflating them is the biggest analytical error. A transparent, rule-bound, passive Norwegian savings fund and an opaque, activist, domestically-deployed development fund are radically different institutions that happen to share a label. The matrix earlier in this post exists precisely because the differences in transparency and strategy matter more than the similarity in size.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Gulf and Asian funds rescue Western banks (2007–2008).** As the subprime crisis cracked open, Western banks desperately needed fresh capital, and the investors with cash to spare were sovereign funds. ADIA put roughly \$7.5 billion into Citigroup in late 2007. Singapore's GIC injected billions into Citigroup and UBS; Temasek bought into Merrill Lynch; the Kuwait and Qatar funds backed Barclays. The patient-capital advantage was on vivid display — these were among the only buyers when everyone else was selling. But the timing was painful: many of these stakes were made *before* the worst of the crash, so the funds bought too early and sat on large paper losses for years. The episode showed both the power of patient capital to provide liquidity in a panic and the limit of patience: it spares you the forced sale, not the bad entry price.

**Norway divests from coal and weapons (2015 onward).** Acting on its Council on Ethics, the GPFG began excluding companies that derive large shares of their business from thermal coal, alongside its long-standing exclusions of nuclear-weapons makers, certain other arms producers, and firms tied to severe human-rights or environmental harm. Because the fund is the single largest equity owner on earth, exclusion is a real reputational penalty and a signal other investors watch. This is the clearest case of a government deploying investment power as ethical leverage — and a live example of the governance question: a Norwegian ethics body is, in effect, passing judgment on foreign companies' conduct through the lever of ownership.

**PIF and the sports-and-tech push (2016 onward).** Saudi Arabia's PIF took its \$3.5 billion Uber stake in 2016, became the anchor investor in electric-vehicle maker Lucid (eventually owning a majority and helping take it public via a SPAC), bought Newcastle United in 2021, and launched LIV Golf in 2022 to compete with the established PGA Tour — a venture widely read as soft power. Lucid's stock fell sharply after its public debut, costing PIF on paper, while the sports investments are explicitly about influence and image more than financial return. PIF is the textbook case of a fund used to transform an economy and project a nation's brand, with financial logic sometimes secondary.

**Temasek's FTX write-down (2022).** Singapore's Temasek invested about \$275 million in the crypto exchange FTX across 2021 and 2022. When FTX collapsed into fraud and bankruptcy in November 2022, Temasek wrote the entire stake down to zero and publicly acknowledged the misjudgment. The episode punctures the "governments are careful, sophisticated investors" assumption: a large, respected sovereign vehicle can still be taken in by a fraud, and a long horizon offers no protection against a total loss. Patience cannot wait out zero.

**GIC and the 2008 crisis stakes.** Singapore's GIC made two of the most-studied sovereign bets of the crisis: a roughly \$11 billion convertible-securities investment in UBS and around \$6.8 billion into Citigroup, both in late 2007 to early 2008. GIC later restructured and exited portions at a loss on the UBS position while the Citi stake recovered as it converted to equity and the bank rebounded. GIC's own retrospective framed it as a reminder that even a disciplined long-term investor can be early and oversized in a crisis, and that diversification across many such bets — not the success of any single one — is what protects the portfolio.

**CIC's pre-crisis bank stakes (2007).** China's brand-new CIC put about \$3 billion into Blackstone's IPO and roughly \$5 billion into Morgan Stanley in 2007, just before both cratered in the crisis. The losses were large and politically embarrassing at home, and they pushed CIC toward a more cautious, diversified approach thereafter. The lesson the fund drew is the one this whole post circles: a state fund's edge is patience and diversification, not bold concentrated calls — and learning that cost CIC billions up front.

**The 2022 drawdown at Norway's GPFG.** In 2022, as global stocks and bonds fell together, the GPFG posted a loss of over \$160 billion for the year — one of the largest single-year losses in its history. Norway did not panic, did not change its strategy, and did not stop its budget transfer, because the spending rule is computed off a long-run average rather than one bad year. The fund recovered strongly afterward. This is the patient-capital model working exactly as designed: a loss that would force a leveraged or short-horizon investor to sell at the bottom is, for a sovereign fund, just a year to ride through.

## Spend it now, or save it for generations

Step back, and every sovereign wealth fund is an answer to one stark choice a lucky nation faces. A windfall arrives — oil is discovered, or years of trade surpluses pile up. The country can spend it now, on higher wages, subsidies, and grand projects, enjoying a decade of plenty. Or it can save it in a fund, spend only a thin slice of the returns, and turn a one-time windfall into a permanent income.

![Before and after comparison of spending an oil windfall versus saving it in a fund](/imgs/blogs/sovereign-wealth-funds-norway-gic-pif-7.png)

The "spend it now" path feels great and ends badly: the currency overheats, other industries wither (Dutch disease), the budget grows dependent on the windfall, and when the resource runs out or its price crashes, the country is left poorer than before — the resource curse in full. The "save it" path feels austere and ends well: the surplus is invested abroad, the domestic economy is shielded from the overheating, and a steady payout funds public services long after the oil is gone. The figure above is the whole thesis of the institution in two columns. Norway, with \$300,000 per citizen working in global markets, chose the right column. Many resource-rich countries chose the left and have little to show for their windfall today.

## When this matters to you, and where to go next

You will almost certainly never deposit a krone into a sovereign wealth fund, so why does any of this touch your life? Three ways.

First, these funds are among the largest owners of the world's companies, including very possibly the ones in your own retirement account. When Norway's fund votes against a CEO's pay package, or excludes a coal company, or when a Gulf fund buys a stake in a firm you own, the patient national capital described here is shaping the same markets your savings sit in. Understanding who the big patient owners are helps you read the market you are already invested in.

Second, the *idea* is portable and increasingly copied. US states (Alaska's Permanent Fund pays residents an annual dividend from oil savings), and a growing list of countries are setting up sovereign or "future generations" funds. The debate over whether your own country should save a windfall — or a budget surplus, or even borrowed money — in such a fund is a live political question, and you now have the framework to judge it: what is the mandate, what is the spending rule, and how transparent is the governance?

Third, sovereign funds are reshaping geopolitics in ways that hit the news constantly — a Gulf fund buying a football club, a Saudi fund anchoring an AI deal, a Chinese fund blocked from buying a port. You can now see these stories for what they are: a few governments deploying enormous, patient, national capital, with real economic logic and real questions of power and accountability sitting side by side.

[Who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system) zooms out to the whole system of central banks, reserves, and capital flows that produce the surpluses these funds invest. This is educational, not investment advice. The aim is only to make one of the most powerful and least-understood institutions in global finance legible: how a nation turns oil and reserves into patient global power, what that buys, and what it costs.
