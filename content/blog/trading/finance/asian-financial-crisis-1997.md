> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A currency peg combined with a mountain of short-term, foreign-currency debt is a trap: it works beautifully until confidence breaks, and then it detonates.
>
> - In July 1997 Thailand gave up defending its pegged exchange rate, the baht collapsed, and within five months the panic had spread to Indonesia, South Korea, and Malaysia.
> - The mechanism: companies and banks had borrowed in dollars while earning in local currency, so when the local currency halved, their dollar debts doubled in local terms and balance sheets blew up.
> - The scale: the IMF assembled rescue packages of roughly \$17 billion for Thailand, ~\$40 billion for Indonesia, and ~\$58 billion for South Korea, the largest of its kind at the time.
> - Who paid: ordinary households through deep recessions and mass unemployment; Indonesia's President Suharto fell in 1998; Malaysia broke from the script and imposed capital controls instead of taking an IMF program.
> - The durable lesson: a healthy-looking, fast-growing economy can still be one broken peg away from disaster if it has borrowed short-term and in a foreign currency.

In the first half of 1997, Thailand looked like a poster child for the "Asian economic miracle." Growth had run near 8 percent a year for a decade. Skyscrapers were going up in Bangkok. Foreign money was pouring in. The Thai baht had been quietly fixed at roughly 25 to the US dollar for years, and that stability was treated as a fact of nature, like the tides.

On the night of July 2, 1997, that fact of nature ended. After months of secretly burning through its foreign-currency reserves to defend the peg against waves of selling, Thailand's central bank gave up and let the baht float. It did not drift. It fell off a cliff. Within months it had lost roughly half its value, and within those same months the same fear had jumped to Indonesia, then South Korea, then Malaysia and beyond. Currencies that had looked rock-solid were down 40, 50, 80 percent. Companies that had been profitable became insolvent overnight, without selling a single fewer widget, purely because of what happened to an exchange rate. Whole economies that had been the envy of the developing world contracted by double digits in a single year.

This article is about exactly how that happened, mechanically, step by step, with the numbers worked out. The diagram above is the mental model: a single trigger in one country (Thailand), traveling along a channel of fear and re-pricing, knocking over four economies that all happened to share the same hidden flaw.

![Timeline of the 1997 Asian crisis spreading from Thailand to Korea](/imgs/blogs/asian-financial-crisis-1997-1.png)

The flaw was not corruption, or laziness, or bad luck, though all of those got blamed. The flaw was a specific, identifiable financial structure: a fixed exchange rate sitting on top of large amounts of short-term debt owed in a foreign currency. That combination is a trap. It pays you to walk into it. And it springs the instant enough people lose confidence at the same time. By the end, you will be able to look at any emerging economy and ask the three questions that would have flagged the danger in 1997, and you will recognize the same trap in episodes before and since.

## Foundations: the building blocks you need

Before we can dissect the crisis, we need to define every moving part from zero. None of this assumes any finance background. If you already know what a currency peg is, skim; if you do not, read carefully, because the whole story turns on these eight ideas.

**An exchange rate** is just the price of one currency in terms of another. "25 baht per dollar" means it takes 25 Thai baht to buy one US dollar. If that number rises to 50 baht per dollar, the baht has gotten *weaker* (you need more of it to buy a dollar); if it falls to 20, the baht has gotten *stronger*. A currency getting weaker is called *depreciation* (if it happens in the market) or *devaluation* (if a government deliberately moves it). The two words describe the same direction of motion.

**A currency peg (a fixed exchange rate)** is a promise by a government or central bank to keep that price from moving. Thailand promised the world: "We will keep the baht at about 25 per dollar." A peg is attractive because it removes uncertainty. A foreign investor lending money to a Thai company does not have to worry that the baht will swing wildly; a Thai importer knows exactly what a shipment priced in dollars will cost in baht. Stability invites investment.

But a promise to fix a price is only as good as your ability to keep it. To defend a peg, a central bank acts as a buyer and seller of last resort for its own currency. If too many people are *selling* baht (and buying dollars), the price of the baht wants to fall. To stop it, the central bank must step in and *buy* baht, paying for it with dollars out of its own stockpile. That stockpile has a name.

**Foreign-exchange reserves** (or just "reserves") are the hard-currency war chest a central bank holds, mostly US dollars and dollar bonds, precisely so it can defend its currency. Reserves are the ammunition. The crucial fact about ammunition is that it is finite. A central bank can buy back its own currency only until the dollars run out. Once the reserves approach zero, the peg is undefendable, and everyone in the market knows it.

**Hot money (portfolio capital flows)** is the term for foreign money that can leave fast. Some foreign investment is "sticky," like a factory: if you build a plant in Thailand, you cannot pack it up and fly home next Tuesday. That is *foreign direct investment*. But a lot of the money flowing into 1990s Asia was not factories. It was short-term bank loans, money parked in local bonds, and portfolio investment in stocks. That money is *liquid*, meaning it can be converted and pulled out almost instantly. Money that can leave fast is called hot money, and it behaves like a crowd in a theater: calm until someone smells smoke, and then everyone heads for the same exit at once.

**A current-account deficit** sounds technical but is intuitive. A country's current account is, roughly, whether it is a net earner or a net spender against the rest of the world in a given year, combining trade (exports minus imports) and a few related flows. A *deficit* means the country is buying more from abroad than it sells abroad, so it must finance the gap by borrowing from foreigners or attracting their investment. A country running a persistent current-account deficit is, put simply, living on the kindness of foreign lenders. That is fine while the lenders are willing. It is a catastrophe the moment they are not, because the country has come to depend on a flow of money that can stop.

**Currency mismatch** is the single most important idea in this entire story, so slow down here. A currency mismatch happens when you *owe* money in one currency but *earn* money in a different one. Take a Thai property developer. It borrows \$10 million from a foreign bank because dollar loans carry a low interest rate. But its income, the rent and sale proceeds from Thai apartments, comes in baht. So its debt is in dollars and its revenue is in baht. As long as the exchange rate is fixed at 25, this is invisible and harmless. The moment the baht falls to 50, the company's revenue (in dollars) has been cut in half while its debt (in dollars) is unchanged, which is the same as saying its debt has *doubled* measured in the baht it actually earns. Nothing about the business changed. Only the exchange rate did. Currency mismatch is a hidden short position against your own currency, and a peg is what hides it.

**Short-term external debt** is debt owed to foreigners that comes due soon, typically within a year. "External" means owed to lenders abroad; "short-term" means it must be repaid or *rolled over* (renewed) shortly. Rolling over is normal: a bank borrows for 90 days, and when the 90 days are up it simply borrows again from the same or another lender to repay the first. This works perfectly until lenders collectively decide not to renew. Then the borrower must actually find the cash to repay, all at once, and there is not enough. Short-term external debt is therefore the maturity that can run, the financial equivalent of a deposit that can be withdrawn on demand.

**The IMF and conditionality.** The International Monetary Fund is an organization funded by member governments that acts as an emergency lender to countries in a financial crisis, a sort of fire department for national balance sheets. When a country runs out of reserves and cannot pay its foreign debts, it can ask the IMF for a loan of hard currency. But IMF loans come with strings, called *conditionality*: the borrowing country must agree to policy changes the IMF judges necessary to fix the underlying problem, such as cutting government spending, raising interest rates, closing insolvent banks, or opening markets. Conditionality is the source of much of the controversy in this story, because the conditions imposed in 1997 turned out, in the judgment of many economists, to have deepened the recessions rather than cured them.

**Contagion** is the spread of a financial crisis from one country to others that are not obviously connected by trade or direct lending. It is named after disease for a reason: it travels through a channel you cannot see. We will spend a whole section on *why* a crisis in Thailand should infect Korea, because the answer, "fear and re-pricing," is the heart of the matter.

**Capital controls** are government rules that restrict how money moves across the country's borders, for example by limiting how much currency can be taken out, taxing fast outflows, or forbidding short-term speculation. Most economists in the 1990s regarded capital controls as backward, the sign of an economy afraid of the free market. Malaysia's decision to impose them in 1998, against the advice of the day, is one of the most argued-about choices of the crisis, and we will return to it.

Hold all eight of these in mind, because the crisis is just these pieces interacting. A peg, defended with finite reserves, sitting on top of hot money and short-term foreign-currency debt, in economies running current-account deficits, until confidence breaks and contagion does the rest.

## The setup: the "Asian miracle" and the trap inside it

To understand why the crisis was such a shock, you have to feel how good things looked beforehand.

Through the late 1980s and into the 1990s, a cluster of Southeast and East Asian economies posted some of the fastest sustained growth the world had ever seen. Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, and South Korea (along with the already-richer "tiger" economies of Hong Kong, Singapore, Taiwan, and earlier Japan) grew so quickly that the World Bank wrote a celebrated 1993 report calling it the "East Asian Miracle." These were high-savings, high-investment, export-oriented economies. They built things, sold them to the world, and grew.

Foreign capital wanted in on this growth, and three features of the setup made the inflow enormous, and dangerous.

First, **the pegs invited carry**. Because currencies like the baht were fixed to the dollar, a foreign lender faced what looked like a free lunch. US dollar interest rates in the mid-1990s were modest, while interest rates inside the booming Asian economies were higher. If you borrowed dollars cheaply, converted them to baht, and lent the baht out at the higher local rate, you pocketed the difference, and the fixed exchange rate seemed to guarantee you could convert back to dollars at the same price whenever you liked. This is a *carry trade*: borrow where money is cheap, lend where it is dear, and collect the spread. The peg made the carry trade look almost riskless, which is exactly the kind of "almost" that ends careers.

Second, **local banks and firms borrowed short-term and in dollars**. From the borrower's side, the same logic applied. A Thai bank or a Korean conglomerate could borrow in dollars at a low rate rather than in local currency at a high rate, and as long as the peg held, why wouldn't it? So companies and banks across the region loaded up on dollar-denominated, often short-maturity loans from foreign banks. They were, without quite admitting it, betting the entire enterprise on the peg never breaking. That is the currency mismatch from the foundations section, multiplied across thousands of balance sheets and trillions of dollars (well, hundreds of billions) of debt.

Third, **the inflows fueled property and credit booms**. Money that floods into an economy has to go somewhere, and a lot of it went into real estate, stock markets, and overcapacity. Bangkok sprouted office towers faster than tenants could fill them. Banks lent freely against rising property values, which pushed property values higher, which justified more lending. Credit grew far faster than the underlying economies. By 1996, there were warning signs visible to anyone who cared to look: export growth was slowing, some of the property was plainly speculative, current-account deficits were wide (Thailand's was running around 8 percent of GDP), and short-term foreign debt was piling up faster than reserves were growing.

The carry trade is worth a worked example, because it shows both why the money flowed in and why it would flee.

#### Worked example: the carry trade that built the bubble

Suppose in 1995 a foreign investment fund wants to profit from Thailand's high interest rates and fixed peg. It does the following:

1. It borrows \$10,000,000 in US dollars at a 5 percent annual interest rate. Annual cost of the loan: \$500,000.
2. At the peg of 25 baht per dollar, it converts the \$10,000,000 into 250,000,000 baht (10 million times 25).
3. It lends that 250,000,000 baht inside Thailand, say into Thai bonds or bank deposits, at a 12 percent annual rate. Annual income: 30,000,000 baht.
4. Converting that income back at the peg, 30,000,000 baht divided by 25 equals \$1,200,000.
5. The profit is the income minus the borrowing cost: \$1,200,000 minus \$500,000, which is \$700,000 a year, on borrowed money, for doing essentially nothing but holding a position.

That 7-percentage-point spread (12 percent earned minus 5 percent paid) on \$10 million is \$700,000 a year, and the peg appears to remove all the currency risk. This is why hundreds of billions of dollars flooded in. But notice the hidden assumption baked into step 4: it works *only* if you can still convert at 25 baht per dollar. The instant the baht falls to 50, your 30,000,000 baht of income converts to just \$600,000, which is now less than your \$500,000 cost only on paper, but your *principal* of 250,000,000 baht is now worth only \$5,000,000 when you try to take it home. You have lost half your capital. **The same fixed peg that made the trade look riskless is the thing whose failure makes it ruinous, which is why everyone in the carry trade tries to exit through the same door at the same instant.**

This is the trap, fully assembled. It is not visible in the growth numbers. It is not visible in the skyline. It is only visible if you ask: what is the structure of the debt, and what happens to it if the peg breaks?

## The blow-up, step by step

By early 1997, the cracks were widening. Thai exports had stalled. A major Thai finance company, Finance One, was in trouble. Property prices were sagging, which threatened the banks that had lent against them. And speculators, most famously hedge funds, had noticed something: Thailand's current-account deficit was large, its short-term foreign debt exceeded its usable reserves, and its export engine was sputtering. The peg, in their judgment, was overvalued and underfunded. They began to bet against it.

**Speculating against a peg** works like this. A speculator borrows baht and immediately sells it for dollars at the pegged rate of 25. If the central bank is forced to devalue, so the rate moves to, say, 50, the speculator buys baht back at the new cheap rate to repay the loan, pocketing the difference. The speculator's downside is small (the cost of borrowing baht for a while if nothing happens); the upside is enormous (the entire devaluation). It is a one-way bet against a price that can only really move one direction, and when many speculators pile in, the selling pressure itself helps force the devaluation. It is partly self-fulfilling.

Through the spring of 1997, the Bank of Thailand fought back. It bought baht with its dollar reserves. It raised interest rates to make holding baht more attractive. And, crucially and secretly, it sold dollars *forward*, committing to deliver dollars in the future, which let it defend the peg without immediately depleting its visible reserves. This bought time but dug the hole deeper: it meant Thailand's *true* reserve position, net of those forward commitments, was far weaker than the published numbers suggested. The central bank was bluffing with a much worse hand than the market could see.

The bluff could not last. By late June, usable reserves were nearly gone. On **July 2, 1997**, Thailand stopped defending the peg and let the baht float. The official framing was a "managed float." The reality was a collapse. The baht fell quickly, and over the following months it lost roughly half its value against the dollar, sliding from about 25 toward 50 and beyond at the worst.

Now the contagion began, and the speed of it is the part that still astonishes. Within weeks, the same questions investors had asked about Thailand, "is the peg overvalued, is the short-term dollar debt larger than the reserves, is the export engine slowing?", were being asked about every economy in the region. And uncomfortably often, the answer was yes.

![Contagion map showing Thailand triggering Indonesia, Korea, and Malaysia](/imgs/blogs/asian-financial-crisis-1997-2.png)

The Philippines, Malaysia, and Indonesia came under pressure almost immediately. **Indonesia** at first widened its currency's trading band and then, in August 1997, let the rupiah float. The rupiah did not just slide; it cratered, eventually losing around 80 percent of its value at the depths of the crisis in 1998. Because Indonesian companies had enormous unhedged dollar debts, this was financial annihilation: the local-currency value of their debts roughly quintupled. Indonesia's crisis became as much political as economic. Riots broke out, and in May 1998 President Suharto, who had ruled for over three decades, was forced to resign.

**Malaysia's** ringgit fell sharply, ultimately losing roughly 40 percent against the dollar from its pre-crisis level, and its stock market collapsed. But Malaysia would make a famously different policy choice, which we come to in the aftermath.

The most shocking casualty was **South Korea**, because Korea was not supposed to be in this category at all. It was the world's eleventh-largest economy, an industrial powerhouse of globally known names. But beneath the surface, Korea had the same disease in an acute form. Its giant conglomerates, the *chaebol*, were heavily indebted, and Korean banks had borrowed enormous sums short-term in dollars to fund them. When foreign banks stopped rolling over those short-term loans in the autumn of 1997, Korea faced a national liquidity crisis. Its reserves were far smaller than its short-term obligations. The won, which had traded around 800 to the dollar, weakened past 1,000, then 1,500, touching roughly 1,700 at the worst around the turn of the year. In December 1997, Korea agreed to the largest IMF rescue package assembled to that point.

The rescues themselves were historic in scale: roughly **\$17 billion for Thailand**, roughly **\$40 billion for Indonesia**, and roughly **\$58 billion for South Korea** (headline totals that combined IMF money with contributions from the World Bank, the Asian Development Bank, and individual governments). And every package came with conditionality: austerity, high interest rates, bank closures, and structural reforms. The recessions that followed were brutal. Indonesia's economy shrank by around 13 percent in 1998. Thailand and Korea contracted sharply too. Unemployment soared. The "miracle" had become, in the space of half a year, a region-wide depression.

## The mechanism dissected: why a peg plus dollar debt is a trap

Now we get to the depth: *why* did this happen, mechanically? The narrative above is what happened. This section is the engine underneath it. There are four interlocking mechanisms, and understanding them is understanding the crisis.

### Mechanism 1: currency mismatch turns a price move into insolvency

The detonator was currency mismatch. We met it in the foundations; now watch it explode in slow motion.

![Pipeline showing peg breaking leading to dollar debt exploding and default](/imgs/blogs/asian-financial-crisis-1997-4.png)

A firm or a bank with a currency mismatch is, whether it understands this or not, *short its own currency*. Its assets and income are in baht; its debt is in dollars. If the baht falls, the dollar value of what it owes does not change, but the baht value does, and the baht value is what matters because baht is what the firm earns and what its local creditors and shareholders measure it in. When the baht halves, the baht-measured burden of the dollar debt doubles. This is not a paper loss that recovers; it is a permanent transfer of real wealth from the borrower to the lender, locked in the moment the currency moves.

#### Worked example: the Thai firm whose debt doubled

Take a single, representative Thai company. Before the crisis, with the peg at 25 baht per dollar:

- Assets (a building, inventory, cash): 30,000,000 baht.
- Debt: a \$1,000,000 dollar loan from a foreign bank. At 25 baht per dollar, that is 25,000,000 baht.
- Equity (assets minus debt): 30,000,000 minus 25,000,000 equals 5,000,000 baht. The firm is solvent, with a comfortable cushion.

Now the baht floats and falls to 50 baht per dollar, a 50 percent devaluation. The firm did not borrow another dollar. Its building did not burn down. Nothing operational changed. But:

- Assets (still the same physical things): about 30,000,000 baht.
- Debt: the same \$1,000,000, but now at 50 baht per dollar, that is 50,000,000 baht. The debt *doubled* in the currency the firm earns.
- Equity: 30,000,000 minus 50,000,000 equals *negative* 20,000,000 baht. The firm is now deeply insolvent.

The firm has been bankrupted by an exchange rate. **A 50 percent currency fall did not cut this firm's value by 50 percent; it pushed a solvent firm 20 million baht into the hole, because the loss lands entirely on the thin slice of equity while the debt sits unchanged in a currency that just got twice as expensive.** Multiply this across a whole economy of firms and banks that all borrowed in dollars, and you do not have a recession. You have a balance-sheet apocalypse, where the productive companies of the country are technically bankrupt all at once.

![Before-and-after corporate balance sheet at a 50 percent devaluation](/imgs/blogs/asian-financial-crisis-1997-3.png)

The cruel twist is that this mechanism is *self-reinforcing through the exchange rate itself*. When all these firms become insolvent, foreign lenders rush to pull their money out, which means selling local currency for dollars, which pushes the currency down further, which makes the dollar debts even bigger, which bankrupts even more firms. The devaluation feeds the very insolvency that drives the devaluation.

The same mechanism hits a country's *imports*, not just its corporate debts, and that is how the crisis reached ordinary households. Anything bought from abroad, oil, machinery, imported food, foreign components for local factories, is priced in foreign currency, so when the local currency collapses, the local-currency cost of every import jumps. South Korea is the sharpest case, because the won fell so far so fast.

#### Worked example: the won collapse and a \$10,000 import

Consider a South Korean firm (or family) that needs to buy something priced abroad at \$10,000. It could be a piece of imported machinery, a stockpile of foreign raw materials, or the foreign-currency cost of a child's overseas tuition. Watch what happens as the won slides.

1. Before the crisis, with the won at about 800 per dollar, the importer needs 800 times 10,000, which is 8,000,000 won, to buy the \$10,000.
2. As the contagion hit Korea in late 1997, the won weakened past 1,000, then 1,500. At 1,500 won per dollar, the same \$10,000 import now costs 1,500 times 10,000, which is 15,000,000 won.
3. At the worst, the won touched roughly 1,700 per dollar. Now the \$10,000 costs 1,700 times 10,000, which is 17,000,000 won.
4. The price in won has gone from 8,000,000 to 17,000,000, an increase of 9,000,000 won, or about 112 percent, on a purchase whose dollar price never moved.

The importer is paying more than double the won, for the identical \$10,000 of goods, purely because the won halved. **A currency collapse is, for everyone who buys anything from abroad, an instant and brutal inflation in imported goods, which is why a devaluation drives up the cost of living even as it bankrupts the firms with dollar debt: the same fall in the won that doubles a company's debt also doubles the won price of every imported input it needs to keep operating.** This is the channel through which an abstract "exchange-rate event" became, for tens of millions of households, a sudden, painful rise in the price of fuel, food, and everything imported.

### Mechanism 2: reserves are finite, so a peg can always be overrun

The second mechanism is why the peg could not simply be defended forever. The answer is arithmetic: reserves are a finite stockpile, and short-term foreign debt is a claim on that stockpile that can be presented all at once.

![Stack comparing short-term foreign debt against reserves](/imgs/blogs/asian-financial-crisis-1997-6.png)

The figure above frames the central bank as a bank, and its reserves as the cash in the vault. Short-term foreign debt is like deposits that can be withdrawn on demand: every dollar of short-term debt is a foreigner who, when his loan matures, can demand dollars and take them out of the country. If the total of those demands exceeds the cash in the vault, the bank cannot pay everyone, and a run becomes rational: get your money out before the vault empties. The single most predictive number going into the crisis was not the budget deficit or the inflation rate. It was the ratio of short-term external debt to reserves. Where short-term debt exceeded reserves (as in Korea, badly, and Thailand on a net basis), the economy was a tinderbox.

#### Worked example: reserve coverage and why the peg had to break

Consider a stylized central bank with the kind of numbers that flashed red in 1997:

- Usable foreign-exchange reserves: \$30,000,000,000 (30 billion).
- Short-term external debt due within twelve months: \$45,000,000,000 (45 billion).
- Coverage ratio: reserves divided by short-term debt, which is 30 divided by 45, equals about 0.67, or 67 percent.

A coverage ratio above 1.0 (more reserves than debt coming due) means the central bank could, in principle, honor every short-term claim even if every single foreign lender refused to roll over. A ratio below 1.0 means it mathematically cannot. At 0.67, this central bank can repay only two-thirds of the foreigners who might demand their money within a year. The other third would arrive at an empty vault.

Now add the run. Foreign lenders, seeing the coverage ratio, race to be in the first two-thirds. They refuse to roll over. The central bank pays out dollars to those it can, reserves drain toward zero, and once they are gone the peg is mechanically impossible to hold, so the currency floats and collapses. **A coverage ratio below one converts a question of confidence into a question of arithmetic: there is literally not enough hard currency to keep the promise, and every lender who understands that has a rational incentive to run first.** This is why economists obsess over the short-term-debt-to-reserves ratio for emerging markets to this day; it is the closest thing to a single-number crisis predictor that 1997 produced.

### Mechanism 3: the self-fulfilling run

The third mechanism explains the *speed*. Currency crises of this kind have multiple equilibria, which is a precise way of saying the outcome depends on what everyone expects everyone else to do.

![Graph of the capital-flight feedback loop reinforcing itself](/imgs/blogs/asian-financial-crisis-1997-7.png)

Here is the self-fulfilling logic. If no foreign lender expects the others to flee, then no one flees, the loans roll over normally, the peg holds, and the country is fine, its fundamentals were never *that* bad. But if each lender expects the others to flee, then each one rushes to get out first (so as not to be the one left at the empty vault), the rush itself drains reserves and breaks the peg, and the flight is *vindicated*. Both outcomes, calm and crisis, are consistent with the same underlying economy. What flips the country from one to the other is a coordinated shift in expectations, a loss of confidence, and that can happen for a reason as flimsy as "the country next door just collapsed."

This is the engine of *contagion*. Investors looking at Korea in late 1997 did not have new bad news about Korea specifically. What they had was a demonstration, in Thailand and Indonesia, that an economy with a peg and short-term dollar debt could collapse, and a sudden awareness that Korea fit the profile. The re-pricing of risk that the Thai collapse triggered made the Korean run rational, and the Korean run then made the Korean collapse real. Fear was the channel. The countries were connected not by trade or by direct lending so much as by *resemblance*: they all looked like the same trade, so when one blew up, the whole class got dumped.

There is also a second, mechanical contagion channel worth naming: forced selling. A fund that suffered losses on Thai baht may face redemptions (its own investors pulling money) or margin calls (lenders demanding more collateral). To raise cash, it sells what it can, including its Indonesian and Korean positions, regardless of whether those countries had any fresh bad news. So losses in one market mechanically generate selling in another, transmitting the panic even among investors who never lost confidence in the second country at all. Fear and forced selling work together, which is why contagion can move faster than any rational re-assessment of fundamentals could explain.

The self-fulfilling structure also creates a vicious bind for the central bank, and it is worth seeing why there was no easy way out. To defend a falling currency, the standard tool is to *raise interest rates*: higher rates reward anyone holding the local currency and make it expensive to borrow it to sell short, so they should slow the outflow. But in 1997 raising rates was a poison as much as a cure. The domestic firms and banks were already drowning in debt; jacking up interest rates pushed more of them toward bankruptcy, which made foreign lenders *more* worried about the economy's solvency, which made them *more* eager to flee. So the very medicine meant to stop the currency fall could accelerate the underlying insolvency that was driving it. There was no clean lever: defend the currency and crush the economy, or save the economy and lose the currency. This bind is exactly what made the crisis so intractable and the policy debates so bitter.

### Mechanism 4: the IMF, austerity, and the moral-hazard debate

The fourth piece is the response, and it is where reasonable people still disagree fiercely.

When a country runs out of reserves and its firms and banks cannot pay foreign debts, it has, crudely, two options: default on the foreign debt (which freezes the country out of global credit, potentially for years) or borrow emergency hard currency from the IMF and accept the conditions. Thailand, Indonesia, and Korea took IMF money. The conditions, especially early on, followed the IMF's standard playbook for a different kind of crisis (the kind caused by government overspending and loose money): cut the budget, raise interest rates to defend the currency and fight inflation, and close insolvent financial institutions.

The trouble is that the 1997 crisis was *not* primarily caused by government overspending. These governments mostly ran responsible budgets. The crisis was a private-sector, balance-sheet, capital-flight crisis. Applying an overspending-crisis remedy to a capital-flight crisis was, many economists (including some who later worked at or led the IMF) came to argue, counterproductive. Raising interest rates to defend a currency that was already in free-fall crushed the domestic firms that were already drowning in dollar debt. Forced austerity in the middle of a collapsing economy deepened the recession. Abruptly closing banks during a panic worsened the bank runs. The deepest part of the recessions, in this reading, was partly self-inflicted by the cure.

There is a counter-argument, and it deserves a fair hearing: the IMF brought hard currency that genuinely stopped the bleeding, the structural reforms (cleaning up crony lending, recapitalizing banks, improving transparency) addressed real weaknesses, and the fastest-recovering economy, Korea, was also the one that most thoroughly implemented the reforms. The IMF itself revised its approach as the crisis went on, easing fiscal targets when the depth of the downturn became clear.

#### Worked example: the conditions on a \$58 billion package

To make conditionality concrete, consider the structure of a Korea-style rescue. The headline figure was about \$58,000,000,000, but that number bundles three things:

1. The IMF's own loan: roughly \$21,000,000,000.
2. Loans from the World Bank and the Asian Development Bank: together roughly \$14,000,000,000.
3. A "second line of defense," pledges from major-economy governments to lend if needed: roughly \$23,000,000,000.

So the \$58 billion was a *coordinated* package, not a single check, and not all of it was even drawn. In exchange, Korea agreed to a list of conditions that ran to dozens of specific commitments. Representative ones: lift interest rates sharply in the short run to stabilize the won; tighten the budget; close or merge insolvent merchant banks; open the financial sector and equity markets more fully to foreign ownership; increase the independence of the central bank; and reform the relationship between the chaebol and the banks. **A rescue at this scale is never just money; it is money plus a contract that rewrites domestic economic policy, which is exactly why conditionality is so politically explosive: the price of the cash is a loss of sovereignty over your own economy at the worst possible moment.** Whether the specific 1997 conditions helped or hurt remains genuinely debated; that they were painful is not.

The *moral hazard* debate sits on top of all this. Moral hazard is the risk that rescuing someone from the consequences of a risky bet encourages more risky bets next time. Critics argued that bailing out the foreign banks that had recklessly lent short-term dollars to Asian borrowers (because the rescue money ultimately allowed those debts to be repaid) taught lenders that they would be made whole, encouraging the next round of reckless lending. Defenders argued that letting a region's financial system collapse to teach lenders a lesson was a cure worse than the disease. This is the permanent tension in every bailout, and it has no clean resolution.

## The aftermath: scars, lessons, and a global aftershock

The immediate aftermath was grim. The affected economies suffered sharp recessions in 1998: Indonesia's GDP fell roughly 13 percent, Thailand's and Korea's by high-single-digit to double-digit percentages, with mass unemployment, collapsing real wages, and a surge in poverty. The human cost was enormous and fell hardest on ordinary people who had never made a leveraged bet in their lives. In Indonesia the economic collapse merged with political crisis and brought down a 31-year regime.

But the recoveries, especially in Korea, were faster than the worst predictions, in part because the collapsed currencies made exports extremely competitive: once the won was cheap, Korean goods were cheap on world markets, and export-led recovery pulled the economy back up. By 1999 and 2000, growth had returned across much of the region.

The deeper, more lasting consequences were three.

**First, a region-wide decision to never be caught short of reserves again.** The single clearest legacy of 1997 is the enormous accumulation of foreign-exchange reserves across emerging Asia (and emerging markets generally) in the years that followed. Having learned the lesson of the coverage ratio the hard way, these countries spent the 2000s building war chests far larger than their short-term external debts. This was a deliberate insurance policy against ever again being at the mercy of the IMF or a sudden stop in capital. It also had global side effects, contributing to the large flows of Asian savings into US assets in the 2000s, a phenomenon later dubbed the "global savings glut."

**Second, a more skeptical, more nuanced view of free capital flows.** Before 1997, the prevailing wisdom (often associated with the IMF and the "Washington Consensus") was that emerging economies should open fully to foreign capital, including short-term flows, as fast as possible. After 1997, that confidence cracked. The recognition that hot money could flood in during good times and flee catastrophically in bad times made even mainstream economists more sympathetic to managing the *composition* and *speed* of capital flows, favoring sticky long-term investment over flighty short-term debt, and treating some capital controls as a legitimate tool rather than a sin. The IMF itself, over the following two decades, moved toward acknowledging that capital controls can be appropriate in some circumstances, a remarkable reversal.

**Third, and immediately, the crisis did not stay in Asia.** The fear that began in Bangkok rippled outward through the global financial system in 1998. Investors, burned by emerging-market risk, fled it everywhere. In August 1998, **Russia**, already weakened by low oil prices and its own fiscal problems, devalued the ruble and defaulted on its domestic debt. That Russian default triggered a global flight to safety so violent that it blew up **Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM)**, a huge, highly leveraged US hedge fund whose models had not anticipated that markets all over the world could move against it at once. LTCM's potential failure was judged so dangerous to the financial system that the Federal Reserve organized a rescue by its creditor banks in September 1998. So a chain that started with a property and currency problem in Thailand ran, within fourteen months, all the way to a near-systemic event in New York. That is contagion operating at global scale, and it is the clearest possible proof that these episodes are not isolated local accidents but expressions of a common mechanism.

Rather than take an IMF program, in September 1998 Malaysia imposed capital controls: it pegged the ringgit, restricted the outflow of short-term capital, and refused the IMF's conditionality. In fact Malaysia's recovery was broadly comparable to its neighbors', and over time a number of economists concluded that, in the specific circumstance of a panic-driven capital flight, temporary capital controls to stop the bleeding were a defensible, even effective, policy. Malaysia did not "win," but it did not lose either, and its experiment permanently changed the debate about whether capital controls are always wrong.

## Common misconceptions

Several tidy stories about 1997 are popular and wrong. Each is worth correcting, because the wrong version teaches the wrong lesson.

**Misconception 1: "These countries collapsed because they were corrupt and cronyist."** Crony capitalism (cozy lending between banks, big firms, and politically connected families) was real and made the damage worse, by funding bad projects and hiding bad loans. But cronyism is not what *caused* the crisis, and the proof is timing. The crony connections existed for years *during* the boom, when foreign capital was happily pouring in and nobody called these economies corrupt; they were called miracles. The crisis was triggered by a sudden reversal of capital flows acting on a currency mismatch, not by a sudden outbreak of corruption. Blaming cronyism alone is comforting (it implies "this could not happen to a well-governed economy") but it lets the real mechanism, peg plus short-term dollar debt, off the hook. A clean, well-run economy with the same financial structure would have been just as vulnerable.

**Misconception 2: "The IMF clearly helped."** The IMF brought desperately needed hard currency, and that mattered. But the claim that its intervention was straightforwardly helpful is contested by serious economists, including some who later led or advised the Fund. The early conditionality, austerity and high interest rates applied to a capital-flight crisis rather than an overspending crisis, plausibly deepened the recessions. The honest statement is that the IMF's money helped while its early conditions may have hurt, and the Fund itself revised its approach mid-crisis. "The IMF clearly helped" and "the IMF clearly hurt" are both too simple.

**Misconception 3: "If they had just floated their currencies earlier, they would have avoided the crisis."** This is half-true and dangerously incomplete. Floating earlier might have let the currencies adjust gradually instead of crashing, and a more flexible exchange rate does reduce the temptation to build up currency mismatches. But floating earlier would *not* by itself have removed the underlying time bomb, which was the unhedged dollar debt. A floating currency that then falls 50 percent *still* doubles dollar debts and *still* bankrupts firms with a currency mismatch. The peg made the trap deeper and the crash more sudden, but the detonator was the debt structure, not the exchange-rate regime alone. Float without also fixing the mismatch, and you have merely changed *when* the explosion happens, not whether.

**Misconception 4: "It couldn't happen to a healthy economy."** This is the most dangerous misconception, because it is exactly the one that lets the next crisis build. South Korea in 1997 *was* a healthy economy by almost every conventional measure: an industrial giant, a strong exporter, a responsible government budget, high savings. It was the eleventh-largest economy in the world. And it still needed the largest bailout in history at that point, because it had the specific vulnerability of large short-term foreign debt relative to reserves. Overall health does not immunize you against a *specific* financial fragility. A fit person can still die of one untreated artery.

**Misconception 5: "Speculators like hedge funds caused the crisis."** Speculators, including currency funds betting against the pegs, were certainly present and added to the selling pressure, and some leaders (notably Malaysia's prime minister) blamed them loudly. But speculators do not bring down a sound currency; they attack one that is *already* overvalued and underfunded. The Thai baht was vulnerable because of the current-account deficit and the short-term-debt-to-reserves problem; the speculators noticed the gap between the pegged price and the sustainable price and bet on the difference. Killing the messenger does not change the message. If there had been no gap, there would have been nothing to bet on.

**Misconception 6: "Capital controls are always backward and harmful."** The pre-1997 consensus held this firmly. Malaysia's 1998 experiment, and the analysis that followed it, showed that *temporary* controls to stop a panic-driven outflow can be a legitimate emergency tool, without the catastrophe that orthodoxy predicted. This does not mean controls are always good (permanent, badly designed controls do real harm by deterring useful long-term investment). It means the blanket claim "controls are always wrong" did not survive contact with the evidence.

## How it echoes in other markets

The 1997 crisis is one instance of a recurring pattern: a fixed or managed exchange rate, plus a vulnerability in the country's external financing, plus a shift in confidence, equals a currency collapse and often a debt crisis. Once you can see the pattern, you see it everywhere. Here are seven other episodes that share the mechanism, before and after 1997.

**The ERM crisis and Black Wednesday (1992).** Five years before Asia, the United Kingdom was forced out of Europe's Exchange Rate Mechanism, a system of semi-fixed exchange rates among European currencies. The pound was effectively pegged too high, the Bank of England burned through reserves and raised interest rates dramatically trying to defend it, and George Soros (among others) bet billions that the defense would fail. It did, on September 16, 1992, "Black Wednesday." This is the cleanest possible example of the "finite reserves cannot defend an overvalued peg against a determined market" mechanism that drove Thailand, and it is worth studying in detail in our companion piece on [Soros and the Bank of England in 1992](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday). The difference is that Britain had no large dollar mismatch, so it suffered a currency embarrassment, not a balance-sheet apocalypse.

**The Mexican "Tequila crisis" (1994-1995).** Just two years before Asia, Mexico ran almost exactly the play that Thailand would later run, and it should have been the warning. Mexico had a quasi-peg to the dollar, a wide current-account deficit, and a large amount of short-term dollar-linked government debt (the infamous *tesobonos*). When confidence broke in December 1994, the peso collapsed by roughly half, the dollar-linked debt detonated the government's finances, and the US Treasury and IMF assembled a roughly \$50 billion rescue. The mechanism, peg plus short-term dollar-linked debt plus a confidence break, is the Asian crisis in miniature, three years early. That the world did not learn from Mexico is part of why Asia happened.

**Russia (1998).** As described above, Russia is the direct aftershock of Asia, but it is also an independent instance of the pattern: a managed ruble, fiscal weakness, falling oil revenue, and short-term debt. When the Asian-crisis flight from emerging markets reached Russia, the government could no longer defend the ruble or roll its debt, and in August 1998 it devalued and defaulted. The same machinery, different country.

**Long-Term Capital Management (1998).** LTCM is the echo in a completely different instrument, leveraged arbitrage rather than a currency peg, but it shares the deepest part of the mechanism: a strategy that looks low-risk because of a stable relationship (here, the tendency of certain prices to converge) loaded up with enormous leverage, which becomes catastrophic the moment the stable relationship breaks under a global panic. The Russian default, itself an aftershock of Asia, was the trigger. LTCM is the proof that the "stable relationship plus leverage plus a confidence shock" pattern is not specific to currencies; it is a general shape that recurs across instruments.

**Argentina (2001-2002).** Argentina ran a hard peg, a "currency board" that fixed the peso one-to-one to the dollar and was supposed to be unbreakable. Layered on top were large dollar debts, both government and private, the deepest possible currency mismatch. When the economy weakened and capital fled, defending the peg required crushing deflation, and eventually it broke anyway. In 2001-2002 Argentina abandoned the peg, the peso collapsed, the dollar debts detonated, the government defaulted on roughly \$80-100 billion, and the banking system froze depositors out of their accounts (the *corralito*). Argentina is the Asian crisis with an even more rigid peg and an even larger mismatch, and it shows the trap is not unique to Asia or to any particular culture.

**The 2013 "taper tantrum."** A gentler but instructive echo. In 2013, when the US Federal Reserve merely *signaled* that it might slow ("taper") its bond-buying, capital began flowing out of emerging markets in anticipation of higher US rates. The currencies of the so-called "Fragile Five" (including India, Indonesia, Turkey, Brazil, and South Africa), economies with current-account deficits and reliance on foreign capital, fell sharply. No country collapsed, partly because of the post-1997 reserve war chests, but the *direction* of the mechanism was identical: a change in the willingness of global capital to fund deficit economies, transmitted instantly through exchange rates. It is the same wind, just at a lower speed, and it shows how the global cost of money set in Washington ripples outward, a theme we develop in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).

**Turkey and Argentina, the 2018 emerging-market stress.** More recently, Turkey (2018 and again in the early 2020s) and Argentina (again) showed the pattern is alive. Turkey combined a current-account deficit, heavy private-sector foreign-currency borrowing, and a leadership reluctant to raise interest rates; when confidence wavered, the lira fell hard and the foreign-currency debts of Turkish firms ballooned in lira terms, precisely the 1997 currency-mismatch mechanism. Argentina, having returned to the markets, found itself in renewed crisis and back at the IMF for one of the largest programs ever. The names change; the structure, deficit plus foreign-currency debt plus a confidence break, does not.

What unites all of these, from 1992 to today, is a single sentence: **a fixed or managed exchange rate cannot be held against a determined loss of confidence once the claims on the central bank's reserves exceed the reserves themselves, and where the country's borrowers owe in a foreign currency, the resulting devaluation turns a price move into mass insolvency.** Thailand in 1997 is simply the most vivid, most contagious, most studied instance of that sentence.

## When this matters to you, and further reading

You are probably not running a central bank. So why should this thirty-year-old crisis matter to you?

Because the underlying mechanism governs risk far beyond national currencies, and because the three diagnostic questions it teaches are useful for the rest of your financial life.

If you ever invest in or work for a company (or country, or even a household) ask: **Is there a mismatch between what it owes and what it earns?** A business that earns in one currency and borrows in another, a homeowner with income in one currency and a mortgage in another, a company with short-term debt funding long-term assets, all share the deep structure of the 1997 trap. The specific danger is always the same: a stable relationship you are relying on (an exchange rate, an interest rate, the willingness of a lender to keep lending) is treated as permanent, and the whole position only works if that relationship holds.

Second, ask: **What is the coverage ratio?** Whether it is a country's reserves versus its short-term debt, or your own liquid savings versus the bills that could come due suddenly, the question "if everyone who could ask for their money back asked at once, is there enough?" is one of the most powerful in finance. Below a coverage ratio of one, you are relying on the kindness of the crowd not to run, and crowds run.

Third, ask: **Is this money sticky or hot?** Funding that can leave fast is funding that can leave at the worst moment. Stability built on hot money is borrowed stability. This is true of a country financed by short-term foreign loans and of any enterprise financed by funding that can be withdrawn on demand.

The 1997 crisis is the clearest classroom for these questions because it shows, with real countries and real numbers, how an economy can look healthy on every surface metric and still be one broken peg away from collapse. The lesson is not "Asia was uniquely flawed." The lesson is that *financial structure is destiny*, and a peg plus short-term foreign-currency debt is a structure that pays you to ignore the risk right up until it ruins you.

To go deeper on the surrounding machinery: our piece on [Soros and the Bank of England in 1992](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday) shows the pure "attacking an overvalued peg" mechanism without the dollar-debt complication; [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) explains the global cost of money whose every shift ripples out to emerging markets, as it did in the 2013 taper tantrum; [who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system) maps the institutions, including the IMF, that decide who gets rescued and on what terms; and for a concrete, present-day example of a central bank managing a currency and credit under exactly these pressures, see [Vietnam's monetary policy, the State Bank, the dong, and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).

This article is educational, not investment advice. The point is not to predict the next crisis but to recognize its shape, because the shape, a fixed price defended with finite reserves, sitting on top of debt owed in a currency you do not earn, is older than 1997 and will outlast all of us.
