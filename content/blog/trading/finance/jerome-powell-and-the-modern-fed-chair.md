> **TL;DR** — The chair of the Federal Reserve can move trillions of dollars across global markets with a single sentence, and Jerome Powell's tenure shows that the job is really about communication, credibility, and crisis management more than about any one rate move.
>
> - The chair has only one vote out of twelve on the committee that sets interest rates, yet leads it and speaks for it, so the chair's words become policy before any rate actually changes.
> - The Fed's "dual mandate" is to keep prices stable and employment high, and the chair's whole craft is steering both without a steering wheel that responds instantly.

> - Because every word is parsed in real time, the chair's biggest power and biggest risk are the same thing: a sentence at a press conference can reprice the entire bond market in seconds.

The most powerful person in the world economy does not command an army, set a budget, or sign a law. They run a committee, give a press conference eight times a year, and choose their words very, very carefully. When the chair of the Federal Reserve speaks, traders in Tokyo, mortgage lenders in Texas, and finance ministers in Brazil all lean in, because a single phrase can move the price of trillions of dollars of bonds, push the dollar up or down against every other currency, and decide whether the monthly payment on a new home goes up or down. The diagram above is the mental model: five chairs over four decades, each handing the next one a reservoir of credibility that was either carefully refilled or quietly drained.

![Timeline of five Federal Reserve chairs from Volcker to Powell](/imgs/blogs/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair-1.png)

This post is about that job, and about the man who has held it through one of its most turbulent stretches: Jerome Powell. We will build everything from zero. By the end you should understand what the Fed actually is, why a committee of twelve people meeting in a marble room matters more to your finances than almost any politician, how a central banker's words become policy, and how Powell's specific decisions, from the pandemic "bazooka" to the fastest rate hikes in forty years, illustrate the genuine mechanics of the most powerful economic job on earth.

## Foundations: the Fed, the FOMC, and why words are policy

Before we can talk about the chair, we need to define the institution they chair, and a handful of terms the rest of the post turns on. None of this assumes any background.

**The Federal Reserve** (the "Fed") is the central bank of the United States. A *central bank* is the bank that other banks use, and the institution that controls a country's money supply and short-term interest rates. It is not a regular bank you can open an account at; it is the bank for banks, and the body that issues the national currency. The full mechanics of how the Fed conducts policy day to day are covered in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates); here we need only the outline.

**The FOMC** stands for the Federal Open Market Committee. This is the specific committee inside the Fed that sets interest-rate policy. It has twelve voting members and meets eight times a year. The chair of the Fed is also the chair of the FOMC, which is the source of most of the chair's power. We will return to its exact structure later, because one of the most common misconceptions is that the chair simply *decides* rates. They do not. They lead a vote.

**The policy rate** (formally the *federal funds rate*) is the single most important number the Fed controls. It is the interest rate at which banks lend money to each other overnight. That sounds obscure, but it sits at the bottom of a long chain: the overnight bank rate influences the rate on short-term government debt, which influences longer-term government debt, which influences mortgage rates, car-loan rates, credit-card rates, and the rate of return on a savings account. When you hear "the Fed raised rates by a quarter point," it means the FOMC voted to push this overnight rate up by 0.25 percentage points, and everything downstream drifts with it.

**A basis point** is one-hundredth of a percentage point. So 0.25 percent is "25 basis points," and a move from 5.00 percent to 5.25 percent is "a 25 basis point hike." Practitioners speak in basis points because the differences that matter are often small; you will see the abbreviation "bps."

**The dual mandate.** The US Congress gave the Fed two jobs, by law: keep prices stable (low, predictable inflation) and keep employment high (close to everyone who wants a job having one). Most central banks have a single mandate focused only on inflation; the Fed's is double. This matters enormously because the two goals can conflict. Fighting inflation usually means raising rates to slow the economy, which can throw people out of work. Supporting employment usually means cutting rates to speed the economy up, which can let inflation rise. The chair lives inside this tension.

**Inflation** is the rate at which prices rise over time. If a basket of goods that cost \$100 last year costs \$103 this year, inflation is 3 percent. The Fed targets 2 percent inflation per year, which it considers "stable enough that you don't have to think about it." When inflation runs hot, money loses value quickly, savings erode, and planning becomes hard.

**Central-bank independence.** The Fed is deliberately insulated from day-to-day politics. The chair and the governors are appointed by the president and confirmed by the Senate, but once in office they serve fixed terms and cannot be fired over a policy disagreement. The reason is brutal historical experience: politicians facing elections almost always want lower rates and a hotter economy now, even if it stores up inflation for later. An independent central bank can take the unpopular step of raising rates into a slowdown because it does not answer to the next election. This independence is the foundation of the chair's credibility, and, as we will see, it is also under constant pressure.

**Forward guidance.** This is the deliberate practice of telling markets in advance what the Fed expects to do, so that expectations do the policy work before any rate actually moves. If the chair says "we expect rates to stay near zero until inflation is clearly above target," lenders and investors immediately price that into everything, and long-term rates fall today, before the Fed has lifted a finger. Forward guidance is why **words are policy**. A modern central bank does not just set the overnight rate; it manages the entire expected *path* of that rate stretching years into the future, and it does that primarily by talking.

That last point is the thesis of this whole post, so let us make it concrete with a picture and then a number.

![Graph of how the Fed chair's words transmit through markets](/imgs/blogs/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair-2.png)

The chair speaks; the expected rate path is repriced; that ripples through Treasury yields, stock prices, and the dollar; and from there it reaches mortgages and, finally, real spending and hiring. The chair never touches your mortgage directly. They touch expectations, and expectations touch everything.

#### Worked example: how one sentence moves the bond value of \$1 trillion

This is the example that makes the whole job click, so we will go slowly.

A bond is a loan you make to a borrower (here, the US government) that pays you interest and returns your money on a set date. The key fact is that a bond's *price* moves opposite to interest rates. If rates in the market rise, newly issued bonds pay more, so older bonds paying less become worth less; their price falls. If rates fall, older higher-paying bonds become more valuable; their price rises.

The sensitivity of a bond's price to a change in rates is called its *duration*, measured in years. A 10-year Treasury bond has a duration of roughly 8 years. Duration has a simple, useful meaning: a bond with duration of 8 loses about 8 percent of its price for every 1 percentage point rise in rates, and gains about 8 percent for every 1 percentage point fall.

Now suppose the chair, at a press conference, says something more hawkish than expected, and the market revises up where it thinks rates will be over the next couple of years. Suppose that pushes the yield on 10-year Treasuries up by 0.20 percentage points (20 basis points) in the minutes after the remark. The arithmetic:

```
Price change  =  - duration  x  change in yield
              =  - 8 years    x  0.20%
              =  - 1.6%
```

Now apply that to a slice of the market. There is well over \$1 trillion of Treasury debt with roughly that maturity outstanding. A 1.6 percent decline in price on \$1 trillion is:

```
\$1,000,000,000,000  x  1.6%  =  \$16,000,000,000
```

That is \$16 billion of bond value erased (or created, if the move goes the other way) by one sentence, before the chair has changed a single actual rate. Multiply across all the maturities and all the other assets that move in sympathy, and a surprising press conference routinely moves *trillions* in total market value within an hour. **The intuition: the chair's real lever is not today's rate but the market's belief about every future rate, and belief reprices instantly.**

### A short history: why the job exists at all

The Federal Reserve is younger than people assume. The United States ran without a permanent central bank for most of the nineteenth century, which meant that when a panic hit, there was no institution that could create money on demand to stop a run. The banking system relied on private rescues; in the Panic of 1907, the financier J. P. Morgan personally organized a bailout of the New York banks because no public body could. The country decided it could not keep depending on a single rich man's willingness to act, and Congress created the Federal Reserve in 1913 to be a permanent, public lender of last resort.

The institution failed its first great test. In the early 1930s the Fed allowed the money supply to collapse and thousands of banks to fail, turning a stock-market crash into the Great Depression, a story told in [the 1929 crash and the Great Depression](/blog/trading/finance/wall-street-crash-1929-great-depression). That failure is not a footnote; it shaped every chair since. Ben Bernanke, a Depression scholar, once told Milton Friedman, "You're right, we did it. We're very sorry. But thanks to you, we won't do it again." When Bernanke flooded the system with money in 2008 and Powell did the same in 2020, they were consciously refusing to repeat the 1930s mistake of standing aside. The job exists because the absence of the job, twice over, was catastrophic.

### The transmission mechanism: from one rate to your whole life

We keep saying the policy rate "ripples downstream." It is worth being precise about *how*, because the chain of links is what makes a single overnight rate matter to a homebuyer. Economists call this chain the *transmission mechanism*, and it has several channels working at once:

- **The interest-rate channel.** Higher policy rates raise the cost of every loan tied to them: business borrowing, credit cards, adjustable mortgages. Costlier credit means less borrowing, less spending, and a slower economy, which cools inflation. This is the most direct channel and the slowest, often taking a year or more to fully bite.
- **The asset-price channel.** Higher rates make safe bonds more attractive, so money rotates out of stocks and riskier assets, lowering their prices. People who feel poorer (the *wealth effect*) spend less. The reverse happens when rates fall, which is why cutting rates tends to lift stock markets.
- **The exchange-rate channel.** Higher US rates draw global money into dollars, strengthening the dollar. A stronger dollar makes imports cheaper (helping cool inflation) but makes US exports more expensive abroad. This channel is how the chair's domestic decision spills across borders.
- **The expectations channel.** The one we keep returning to. If people believe the Fed is serious, they pre-emptively adjust prices and wages toward the target, doing the Fed's work for free. This is the fastest channel and the one driven purely by words.

The crucial, frustrating feature of all of this is that the channels work with what economists call *long and variable lags*. A rate change today might not show up in inflation for twelve to eighteen months, and nobody can say exactly how long. This is why the chair's job is closer to steering a supertanker than a car: you must turn the wheel long before the bend, based on a forecast of where the ship will be, knowing the turn will not register for a year. The 2021 "transitory" error was, at root, a misjudgment of timing in a system where timing is everything.

### Hawks, doves, and the language of the committee

Two pieces of jargon dominate every Fed story, and they are simple once defined. A *hawk* is someone who worries most about inflation and leans toward higher rates and tighter policy. A *dove* worries most about jobs and growth and leans toward lower rates and easier policy. Every FOMC member sits somewhere on this spectrum, and the chair's craft is partly reading where the committee's center of gravity sits and steering it.

"Hawkish" and "dovish" are also how markets describe the *tone* of any Fed communication. A statement that emphasizes inflation risks and keeps the door open to more hikes is "hawkish" and tends to push rates up and stocks down. One that emphasizes downside risks to growth and hints at cuts is "dovish" and tends to do the opposite. When you read that "Powell sounded hawkish at the press conference," it means his words tilted the expected rate path higher, with all the downstream effects the second figure traced. The entire art of forward guidance is choosing exactly how hawkish or dovish to sound, and to whom, on any given day.

## The job itself: setting expectations, managing crises, answering to Congress

With the foundations in place, we can describe what the chair actually does. The role has four faces.

**Face one: chief expectations manager.** The single most important thing a modern chair produces is not a rate decision but a *narrative* about the future of rates that markets and the public believe. If people believe the Fed will keep inflation near 2 percent, they behave in ways that make 2 percent inflation more likely: they do not demand huge wage increases to front-run price rises, businesses do not pre-emptively jack up prices, and the expectation becomes self-fulfilling. This is called *anchoring* inflation expectations, and it is the chair's most precious asset. The reverse is a nightmare: if people stop believing the Fed can control inflation, they act in ways that make inflation worse, and the central bank loses the cheapest tool it has. Volcker's entire 1980s campaign, told in detail in [Paul Volcker and the 1980 rate shock](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation), was ultimately about re-anchoring those expectations after they had come unmoored.

**Face two: crisis firefighter.** When the financial system seizes up, as it did in 2008 and in March 2020, the chair becomes the *lender of last resort*. That phrase means the Fed will lend money to solvent institutions that suddenly cannot borrow from anyone else, to stop a panic from cascading. The Fed can create money to do this, which gives it effectively unlimited firepower in a liquidity crisis. The hard part is judgment: lend too little and the panic spreads; lend too freely and you encourage reckless behavior in the future (the problem of *moral hazard*, where rescued actors take bigger risks next time expecting another rescue).

**Face three: democratic accountant.** Independence does not mean no oversight. The chair testifies before Congress twice a year (the "Humphrey-Hawkins" testimony) and is grilled regularly by politicians from both parties. The chair must defend the Fed's choices in public, in plain language, to people who often want opposite things. This is a genuine constraint: an institution that can create money but answers to no one would be intolerable in a democracy, so the chair spends real effort explaining and justifying.

**Face four: the lineage.** Every chair inherits the institution's accumulated credibility and either adds to it or spends it. The modern lineage runs Volcker → Greenspan → Bernanke → Yellen → Powell, and each handed the next a lesson as much as a balance sheet.

Volcker rebuilt credibility through pain, pushing rates near 20 percent and accepting a severe recession to prove the Fed would do whatever it took; the lesson he bequeathed was that credibility, once lost, can only be regained at enormous cost, so it must be guarded fiercely. Alan Greenspan spent nearly two decades as the "maestro," presiding over long expansions and cultivating an aura of cryptic infallibility; his lesson was double-edged, because his deft handling of the 1987 crash and the late-1990s boom built confidence, but his low rates and light-touch regulation are also blamed for inflating the housing bubble that burst in 2008. Ben Bernanke, a scholar of the Great Depression, met the 2008 collapse by inventing large-scale bond-buying, the policy we now call quantitative easing, and dragged the Fed across the line from a rate-setter into a buyer of trillions in assets; his lesson was that in a true crisis the old tools run out and the chair must be willing to do unprecedented things. Janet Yellen managed the slow, careful exit from the post-2008 emergency, lifting rates off zero by tiny increments and pioneering the modern reliance on explicit forward guidance; her lesson was patience and transparency. Powell inherited a normalizing economy and a toolkit forged by all four, and then faced a pandemic and the worst inflation in forty years, using QE at Bernanke's scale and rate hikes at Volcker's speed within a span of three years.

![Matrix comparing the recent Federal Reserve chairs](/imgs/blogs/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair-3.png)

Notice the pattern in that matrix: each chair is remembered for the tool their defining crisis forced on them. The threat picks the tool, and the tool becomes the legacy.

### Who Powell is, and why an "outsider" got the job

Jerome Powell is unusual among recent chairs in not being a professional economist. Greenspan was an economist by training; Bernanke and Yellen were distinguished academic economists. Powell is a lawyer by education and an investment banker and private-equity executive by career, who served as a Treasury official under President George H. W. Bush. He joined the Fed's Board of Governors in 2012 and was elevated to chair in early 2018.

Why does that background matter? Two reasons. First, Powell's instincts lean practical and markets-fluent rather than theoretical. He spent his private career watching how financial plumbing actually behaves under stress, which arguably served him well in the two liquidity crises of his tenure. Second, his communication style is deliberately *plainer* than his academic predecessors'. Greenspan was famous for opaque, hedged language; he once joked that if you understood what he said, he must have misspoken. Powell, by contrast, tries to speak in ordinary English at his press conferences, which is part of why his sentences carry so much market force: they are clear enough to be acted on instantly.

## The toolkit: what the chair can actually pull

We have mentioned rates, bond buying, and emergency lending. Let us lay the toolkit out as a stack, because the layering tells you something important about priority.

![Stack of the Federal Reserve chair's toolkit](/imgs/blogs/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair-5.png)

From bottom to top:

**Lender of last resort (emergency facilities).** The deepest, oldest tool. In a crisis, the Fed stands up special lending programs (called *facilities*) to make sure markets that have frozen can keep functioning. This is the firefighting tool, used rarely.

**The balance sheet (quantitative easing and tightening).** When the policy rate is already at zero and the economy still needs help, the Fed can buy government bonds and mortgage-backed securities in enormous quantities, creating new money to do so. This pushes down longer-term interest rates directly and pumps cash into the financial system. Buying is *quantitative easing* (QE); the reverse, letting bonds mature and shrinking the balance sheet, is *quantitative tightening* (QT). The full mechanics are in [quantitative easing explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money). The numbers are staggering: the Fed's balance sheet ballooned from under \$1 trillion before 2008 to roughly \$9 trillion at the 2022 peak.

**The policy rate (the federal funds rate).** The everyday tool. Eight times a year the FOMC chooses whether to raise it, cut it, or hold. Under Powell it traveled from near 0 percent up to roughly 5.25 to 5.5 percent and is the lever most people mean when they say "the Fed."

**Forward guidance (words and the dot plot).** On top sits the tool that steers all the others: communication. The chair's statements, press-conference answers, speeches, and the FOMC's published projections shape what markets expect, and expectations move asset prices *before* any rate changes. The *dot plot* is a chart the Fed publishes four times a year, in which each FOMC participant anonymously marks where they think the policy rate should be at the end of the next few years. It is the most-scrutinized chart in finance, because it telegraphs the committee's collective intent.

Why is guidance drawn on *top* of the stack? Because it acts first and acts on everything below it. A rate cut that markets fully expected does almost nothing on the day, because it was already priced in through guidance weeks earlier. A surprise in the chair's *tone* can move markets more than the rate decision itself.

#### Worked example: a 0.25 percent hike on a \$300,000 mortgage

Let us translate "the chair raised rates" into a household's monthly bill, because this is where the theory lands in real life.

Consider a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage of \$300,000. The monthly principal-and-interest payment depends on the rate. The standard formula is:

```
M = P x [ r(1+r)^n ] / [ (1+r)^n - 1 ]
```

where P is the loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12), and n is the number of monthly payments (360 for 30 years).

At a 6.50 percent annual rate, the monthly rate r is 0.0650 / 12 = 0.005417. Running the formula gives a monthly payment of about \$1,896.

Now suppose Fed actions push mortgage rates up by 0.25 percentage points to 6.75 percent. The new monthly rate is 0.0675 / 12 = 0.005625, and the payment rises to about \$1,946.

The difference is roughly \$50 a month. Over the 360 months of the loan:

```
\$50 x 360  =  \$18,000
```

So a single quarter-point move, the smallest standard increment the Fed uses, can add around \$18,000 to the lifetime cost of one ordinary mortgage. Multiply across the millions of new mortgages, car loans, and business loans written each year, and you see how a 25 basis-point decision is anything but small. **The intuition: the chair's smallest standard move quietly reshapes the budgets of millions of households who never watch a press conference.**

## Powell's tenure, act by act

Now we can walk the actual record. Powell's chairmanship is best understood as four distinct chapters, each a different test of the four faces of the job.

### 2018: the hikes, the clash, and the "pivot"

Powell took over in February 2018 with an economy that looked strong and a policy rate that had been creeping up off zero under Yellen. He continued the path, raising rates four times in 2018, to a range of 2.25 to 2.5 percent, and the Fed was also shrinking its balance sheet (QT) at the same time. The logic was textbook: a hot economy should be cooled gently to prevent inflation later.

Markets disagreed loudly. In the final quarter of 2018, the US stock market sold off sharply; the S&P 500 fell nearly 20 percent from its autumn peak to its December low. Investors feared the Fed was tightening too much, too fast, into a slowing global economy. Powell did not help by saying, in early October, that the balance-sheet runoff was on "automatic pilot," which markets read as the Fed ignoring the pain.

This was also the period of open political pressure. The sitting president publicly and repeatedly attacked Powell for raising rates, an extraordinary breach of the convention that politicians leave the Fed alone, and a direct test of the independence we described earlier. Powell did not resign and did not reverse course because of the tweets, which mattered for credibility. But by January 2019, with markets in distress and economic data softening, the Fed *did* change tack: Powell signaled patience, paused the hikes, and the Fed later cut rates three times in 2019. Markets called it "the Powell pivot." Whether it was a wise response to genuine data or a capitulation to market tantrums is debated to this day, and it is the first major critique of his record.

### March 2020: the bazooka

Then the pandemic hit, and the chair's crisis-firefighter face took over completely. In March 2020, as economies locked down, financial markets did not just fall; they *froze*. Even US Treasury bonds, normally the safest, most liquid asset on earth, became hard to trade as everyone scrambled for cash at once. This is the precise nightmare a lender of last resort exists to stop.

The Fed's response was the fastest and largest in its history. In a span of days and weeks it:

- Slashed the policy rate to near 0 percent.
- Announced effectively *unlimited* quantitative easing, committing to buy Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities in whatever quantity was needed to restore functioning markets.
- Stood up an alphabet soup of emergency lending facilities, reaching beyond banks to support corporate bonds, money-market funds, municipal debt, and small-business lending, some for the first time ever.

The scale was historic. The balance sheet jumped by trillions of dollars in months. Markets, which had been in free fall, stabilized and then recovered with startling speed. By this measure, March 2020 was Powell's finest hour: the financial system did not collapse, credit kept flowing, and a liquidity crisis was contained. The criticism, raised later, is that the support was kept too large for too long after the emergency had passed, which fed into the next chapter.

### 2021: the "transitory" misjudgment

As the economy reopened in 2021, prices started rising. Supply chains were tangled, demand snapped back faster than supply, government stimulus had put cash in people's pockets, and later the war in Ukraine pushed up energy and food. Inflation, which the Fed targets at 2 percent, climbed through 4, then 5 percent over the course of 2021.

The Fed's call, repeated through much of 2021, was that this inflation was *transitory*: a temporary side effect of reopening that would fade on its own as supply chains healed, so there was no need to slam on the brakes. On that judgment, the Fed kept the policy rate at zero and kept buying \$120 billion of bonds a month, continuing to stimulate an economy that was already running hot.

The call was wrong. Inflation did not fade; it accelerated and broadened from a few pandemic-scrambled categories into rents, services, and wages, the stickier kinds that do not just reverse. By late 2021 the Fed had quietly "retired" the word transitory, and by the time it acted, inflation had a serious head start.

![Before-and-after comparison of the 'transitory' call versus reality](/imgs/blogs/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair-4.png)

The before-and-after above captures the whole episode. This misjudgment is the central critique of Powell's tenure, and it is worth being fair about why it happened. Forecasting inflation in a once-in-a-century pandemic reopening was genuinely hard; the Fed was not alone in expecting the spike to fade; and acting too early risked choking off a fragile recovery. But the cost of being late was that the Fed then had to tighten far harder and faster than it would have, to prove it still meant business.

### 2022 to 2023: the fastest hikes since Volcker

When the Fed finally turned, it turned violently. Starting in March 2022, the FOMC raised the policy rate from near 0 percent to a range of roughly 5.25 to 5.5 percent by mid-2023, including several rare 75 basis-point hikes in a single meeting. Compressed into about sixteen months, this was the steepest tightening campaign since Volcker's in the early 1980s.

The purpose was not only to cool demand directly. It was, again, about *expectations*: the Fed needed to convince households, businesses, and markets that it would do whatever it took to bring inflation back to 2 percent, so that the expectation of high inflation did not become self-fulfilling. This is where the lineage matters most. Powell repeatedly invoked Volcker's example, signaling that he would accept economic pain rather than let inflation become entrenched. At the Jackson Hole conference in August 2022, in an unusually short and blunt speech, he warned explicitly of "some pain to households and businesses" ahead. Markets, which had been hoping for a softer line, fell sharply that day, a deliberate demonstration that the chair will use a hawkish tone as a tool.

Why the unusually large 75 basis-point steps? Because of the real-rate logic from our earlier example. For monetary policy to actually *restrain* the economy, the policy rate has to climb above the inflation rate, so that the real (inflation-adjusted) rate turns positive. In early 2022 the policy rate was near 0 percent while inflation was above 8 percent, meaning the real rate was around minus 8 percent, wildly stimulative at the exact moment the economy needed restraint. The Fed was, in effect, pressing the accelerator while the car was already speeding. To get the real rate positive, the Fed had to hike fast just to catch up to inflation, then keep going to get ahead of it. A long-standing rule of thumb in central banking, the *Taylor principle*, holds that when inflation rises by 1 percentage point, the policy rate should rise by *more* than 1 point, precisely so the real rate climbs and policy actually tightens. The 75 basis-point hikes were the arithmetic of catching up after starting a year too late.

Inflation, having peaked around 9.1 percent in June 2022 (the highest in roughly four decades), began a slow descent as the hikes worked their way through the economy. Whether the Fed could pull off a "soft landing", bringing inflation down without a recession, was the open question that defined the period. A soft landing means slowing the economy just enough to bring inflation down without tipping it into recession, the macroeconomic equivalent of stopping a heavy truck on a patch of ice: brake too softly and you do not stop, brake too hard and you skid. History offered little comfort, because most past inflation fights of that magnitude had ended in recession. That Powell's tightening brought inflation down substantially without an immediate sharp downturn was, by the standards of the historical record, a genuinely unusual outcome, though debate continued over how much of the disinflation was the Fed's doing and how much was supply chains simply healing on their own.

### 2023: the banking stress

One predictable consequence of raising rates that fast is that it strains anyone holding lots of older, lower-rate bonds, because those bonds lose value as rates climb (recall the duration example). In March 2023 this strain broke open: Silicon Valley Bank failed in a classic bank run, followed by other regional banks, and the tremor crossed the Atlantic to take down the long-troubled Credit Suisse. The full anatomy of that episode is in [the SVB and Credit Suisse bank runs of 2023](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs).

Here the chair had to do two contradictory things at once. The crisis-firefighter face wanted to calm the panic, so the Fed stood up a new emergency facility to lend banks cash against their bonds at full value, stopping the run. But the inflation-fighter face still needed rates high to finish the job on inflation. Powell threaded the needle: the Fed kept hiking (a 25 basis-point move came just days after SVB failed) while using the lending facility to contain the banking stress separately. It was a vivid lesson in the *financial-stability tradeoff*: the tool that fights inflation (high rates) can itself create instability, and the chair must sometimes wield two opposing instruments simultaneously.

## How the decision actually gets made

We keep saying "the Fed decided" or "Powell hiked," but the real machinery is a committee process, and understanding it corrects the biggest misconception about the job. Two figures tell the story: how a decision is produced, and who actually votes.

![Grid showing the flow from economic data to a market move](/imgs/blogs/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair-6.png)

A meeting is the end of a pipeline. Incoming data (the monthly jobs report, the consumer price index for inflation, GDP, and dozens of other series) flows to the Fed's professional staff, who prepare forecasts and briefings. The FOMC's voting members debate over a two-day meeting. They vote on the rate and each marks a dot on the dot plot. At precisely 2:00 pm Eastern on decision day, the *statement* is released, a short, carefully edited paragraph whose word changes from the previous meeting are dissected letter by letter. Half an hour later, at 2:30 pm, the chair holds a *press conference*, and this is often where the real market move happens, because the chair's tone and answers reveal the committee's leanings beyond the formal statement.

![Tree of the FOMC voting structure](/imgs/blogs/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair-7.png)

And here is who votes. The FOMC has twelve voting seats: the members of the Fed's Board of Governors in Washington (up to seven, including the chair and vice chair), the president of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York (who always votes, because New York is where the Fed actually trades in markets), and four of the remaining eleven regional Fed bank presidents, who rotate the vote among themselves year by year. The chair holds exactly *one* of those twelve votes.

So why is the chair so powerful if they have one vote? Because they set the agenda, frame the debate, write the statement, speak for the committee in public, and, by long convention, almost never lose a vote. A chair who was routinely outvoted would be seen as having lost control, which itself would damage credibility, so the chair invests heavily in building consensus before the vote ever happens. The power is persuasion and leadership, not a button. This is the single most useful thing to understand about the job: **the chair leads a vote, they do not issue a decree.**

## Influence: why every word travels the world

The chair's words do not stop at the US border. Because the dollar is the world's reserve currency, used to price oil, settle international trade, and denominated in most cross-border debt, US interest rates set the gravitational field for global finance. This is the subject of [who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system), and the Fed chair sits at its center.

When the Fed raises rates, money flows toward the higher returns available in dollar assets. That pushes the dollar up against other currencies, which sounds remote until you are a country that borrowed in dollars and now has to repay with a weaker local currency, or a company in Turkey or Argentina whose dollar debts suddenly cost far more in local terms. Powell's 2022 to 2023 hiking cycle sent the dollar to multi-decade highs and put real strain on emerging-market economies, even though Powell's mandate is purely domestic. The chair manages the US economy; the world feels every move. This is sometimes summarized in the old line that "the dollar is our currency, but it's your problem."

There is also a darker version of this dynamic. Many developing-country governments and companies borrow in dollars because dollar loans carry lower interest rates than loans in their own volatile currencies. That works fine until the dollar strengthens or US rates rise, at which point the debt becomes a trap: they earn revenue in a weakening local currency but must repay in an expensive, strengthening dollar. Repeated emerging-market crises, from Latin America in the 1980s to Asia in 1997, the subject of [the Asian financial crisis](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997), have been triggered or worsened by exactly this mismatch, and a Fed tightening cycle is often the spark.

#### Worked example: a Fed hike and \$50 billion of emerging-market dollar debt

Picture the position from the borrower's side, with round numbers. Suppose a developing country's companies collectively owe \$50 billion of debt borrowed in US dollars, and at the start of the year their local currency trades at 20 units per dollar. To repay that debt they need:

```
\$50,000,000,000  x  20 units/dollar  =  1,000,000,000,000 local units
```

That is 1 trillion local-currency units of repayment obligation. Now the Fed hikes aggressively; dollar assets become more attractive; global money rushes into the dollar; and the local currency weakens to 25 units per dollar, a 25 percent depreciation that is entirely ordinary during a sharp US tightening. The dollar debt has not changed by a single cent, but the local-currency cost of repaying it is now:

```
\$50,000,000,000  x  25 units/dollar  =  1,250,000,000,000 local units
```

The repayment burden jumped by 250 billion local units, a 25 percent increase, with no new borrowing and no change to the loan itself, purely because a committee in Washington raised US rates. **The intuition: the chair's domestic decision can quietly inflate the debts of borrowers on the other side of the planet who have no vote and no voice in the room.**

#### Worked example: real versus nominal, or why a 5 percent rate can still lose money

People obsess over the headline rate, but the number that actually matters to your purchasing power is the *real* rate, the rate after subtracting inflation. The headline number is the *nominal* rate. The relationship is simple:

```
real rate  =  nominal rate  -  inflation rate
```

Suppose you lock your savings into a one-year investment paying a nominal 5 percent, and over that year inflation runs at 4 percent. Your money grows in dollar terms:

```
\$10,000  x  1.05  =  \$10,500   (500 dollars more in your account)
```

But prices rose 4 percent, so what \$10,000 used to buy now costs \$10,400. Your real gain is only:

```
real rate  =  5%  -  4%  =  1%
```

In purchasing power you are up about \$100, not \$500. Now flip it: in 2021 and 2022, savers earned near 0 percent while inflation ran 7 to 9 percent. The real rate was deeply *negative*, around minus 7 percent, meaning cautious savers were quietly losing purchasing power fast even though their dollar balance never fell. **The intuition: the chair is really fighting over the real rate, and a high-looking nominal rate can still be punishingly low once inflation is subtracted.**

#### Worked example: \$10,000 in savings at 0.5 percent versus 5 percent

The chair's rate decisions land directly in your savings account, and the gap is larger than people expect because of compounding (earning interest on your interest).

Take \$10,000 left untouched for five years. In the near-zero-rate world of 2021, a savings account might pay 0.5 percent a year. After five years:

```
\$10,000 x (1.005)^5  =  \$10,253
```

You earned about \$253 over five years.

Now take the same \$10,000 in the higher-rate world of 2023, with the account paying 5 percent a year (a level that became available precisely because Powell hiked rates):

```
\$10,000 x (1.05)^5  =  \$12,763
```

You earned about \$2,763, more than ten times as much, on the same balance, purely because the policy rate was higher. **The intuition: when the chair moves the policy rate from near zero to 5 percent, ordinary savers go from earning almost nothing to earning thousands, and the difference compounds quietly year after year.**

#### Worked example: reading the dot-plot path priced into markets

Recall the dot plot, the chart where each FOMC participant marks where they expect the policy rate to be. Markets do not just read the *current* rate; they price in the entire expected path. Suppose at a given meeting the median dots imply a policy rate of 5.25 percent now, falling to 4.25 percent in one year and 3.25 percent in two years, as the Fed expects to cut once inflation is tamed.

A trader pricing a two-year investment cannot just use today's 5.25 percent. They roughly average the expected path. A crude approximation of the two-year rate is the average of the expected one-year rates:

```
year-1 expected rate  ~  5.25%  (high now, cuts come later)
year-2 expected rate  ~  4.25%
two-year rate  ~  (5.25% + 4.25%) / 2  =  4.75%
```

So even with the overnight rate at 5.25 percent today, a two-year Treasury might yield around 4.75 percent, *below* the current policy rate, because the market expects cuts. This is why short-term and longer-term rates can point in different directions, and why a single shift in the dots, say, the median moving up by 0.50 percent, instantly reprices every bond whose value depends on that expected path. **The intuition: the chair governs a curve of expected future rates, not a single number, and the dot plot is how the committee publishes that curve to the world.**

## Common misconceptions

The Fed chair is so visible, and so misunderstood, that a handful of myths get repeated constantly. Clearing them up is itself an education in how the job works.

**Misconception 1: "The chair sets interest rates by decree."** As the FOMC tree showed, the chair has one vote of twelve. They lead, persuade, and speak for the committee, but they cannot simply declare a rate. The power is agenda-setting and consensus-building, which is more subtle and, arguably, more durable than a dictator's button.

**Misconception 2: "The Fed prints money to fund the government."** The Fed creates money to buy assets (mostly government bonds) in the open market to influence interest rates, not to hand cash to the Treasury to spend. The distinction matters: the Fed buys *existing* bonds from investors to lower rates, and it can and does *sell* them back (or let them mature) to tighten. Government spending is decided by Congress, a separate branch entirely. The mechanics of money creation are laid out in [how money is created](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier).

**Misconception 3: "Raising rates is the chair punishing the economy."** Rate hikes are not punishment; they are an attempt to cool an economy running hotter than it can sustain without inflation. Inflation is itself a tax that hits everyone, especially people on fixed incomes and savers, so bringing it down protects the broad public even when the medicine (higher borrowing costs, slower hiring) is unpleasant.

**Misconception 4: "The chair controls inflation directly, like a thermostat."** The chair controls one overnight interest rate and the size of the balance sheet. Inflation is the product of millions of decisions about prices and wages, supply chains, energy markets, and government policy. The Fed influences inflation, with long and variable lags of many months, but it does not set it. The 2021 to 2023 episode is the cautionary tale: the Fed's tools work, but slowly and imprecisely.

**Misconception 5: "The chair works for the president."** The president appoints the chair, but once confirmed the chair serves a fixed four-year term and cannot be removed over a policy disagreement, and the broader board members serve fourteen-year terms. Independence is the whole point. When a president publicly pressures the chair to cut rates, as happened to Powell, the chair's willingness to ignore that pressure is precisely what preserves the institution's credibility.

**Misconception 6: "A press conference is just a formality after the real decision."** Often the opposite is true. The rate decision is frequently anticipated and already priced in, while the chair's *tone* at the press conference, an offhand answer about future risks, a refusal to rule out more hikes, is the genuinely new information. Markets routinely move more in the 2:30 pm press conference than at the 2:00 pm decision, which is why every word is scripted and every ad-lib scrutinized.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is one thing; the tape is another. Here are the named episodes where the chair's power was on naked display, drawn from Powell's own tenure.

**The fourth-quarter 2018 selloff and the Powell pivot.** As described above, Powell's "automatic pilot" remark in October 2018, combined with ongoing hikes, helped trigger a near-20 percent stock-market drop into Christmas Eve. The market's distress, plus softening data, prompted the January 2019 signal of patience and three rate cuts later that year. It is a textbook case of the chair's words causing a selloff, and then a change in words (and policy) reversing it, with the underlying economy barely changing in between.

**March 2020, the unlimited bazooka.** When markets froze at the pandemic's onset, the Fed's pledge of effectively unlimited bond buying and a battery of emergency facilities turned a free fall into a recovery within weeks. The phrase that did the heavy lifting was the open-ended *commitment*: by promising to buy "in the amounts needed," the Fed made the actual amount almost irrelevant, because no trader wants to bet against an institution with an unlimited balance sheet. This is forward guidance at its most powerful.

**Jackson Hole, August 2022, the hawkish shock.** Investors went into Powell's annual Jackson Hole speech hoping he would hint that the hiking cycle was nearly done. Instead he delivered a deliberately short, stern address warning of "pain" ahead and reaffirming the commitment to crush inflation. The S&P 500 fell over 3 percent that day. Nothing about actual policy changed at Jackson Hole; it was a speech. But the *tone* moved trillions, a pure demonstration that the chair uses words as a precision instrument, choosing brevity and bluntness specifically to deny markets the dovish comfort they wanted.

**The March 2023 banking stress and the split-screen response.** When SVB failed, the Fed simultaneously fought a fire and stayed on its inflation mission: a new lending facility to stop the bank run, and a 25 basis-point rate hike just days later. The market had to learn, in real time, that the Fed would not abandon the inflation fight just because rates had cracked a few banks, a hard lesson in the financial-stability tradeoff. The cousins of this episode appear across history, from the 2008 collapse traced in [the Lehman Brothers crisis](/blog/trading/finance/lehman-brothers-2008-financial-crisis).

**Every "dot plot" release.** Four times a year the dot plot drops, and markets convulse based on whether the median dot moved up or down by even a single notch. A shift of 25 or 50 basis points in where the committee thinks rates will be next year can move the whole bond market, because, as the worked example showed, longer-term rates are built from the expected path, not the current level.

## Critiques and risks

A fair account has to weigh the genuine criticisms of the modern chair's role, and of Powell specifically.

**The transitory call.** The single largest critique is that the Fed misjudged 2021 inflation as temporary, kept stimulating too long, and then had to tighten brutally to catch up, risking a recession it might have avoided with an earlier, gentler start. Defenders note the genuine difficulty of forecasting a pandemic reopening; critics note that the Fed's job is precisely to see such risks early.

**The market "put."** The name comes from options trading: a *put option* protects its holder against losses below a certain price, so a "Fed put" is the belief that the central bank effectively guarantees a floor under asset prices. Critics argue that the 2019 pivot, and the speed of the 2020 rescue, reinforced a belief that the Fed will always ride to the rescue when markets fall. If investors believe losses will be backstopped, they take more risk than they otherwise would, inflating asset prices and concentrating gains among the people who already own stocks and bonds, which raises hard questions about inequality and moral hazard. The counterargument is that the Fed responds to financial conditions because frozen markets choke off the credit that ordinary businesses and households depend on, not to bail out speculators; the 2020 facilities, for instance, were designed to keep companies able to make payroll, not to lift share prices. Where exactly the line sits between stabilizing the system and subsidizing risk-taking is one of the genuinely unresolved debates about the modern chair's role.

**Financial-stability tradeoffs.** Raising rates fast cracks the weakest links, as 2023's bank failures showed. The chair must constantly balance the inflation fight against the risk that the medicine breaks something in the financial plumbing.

**Political pressure and independence.** The open presidential attacks on Powell tested a norm that has protected the dollar's credibility for decades. Each time a chair is pressured to ease for political reasons, the institution's independence, and therefore its ability to make unpopular but necessary choices, is stressed.

**Communication as a double-edged sword.** The more the chair relies on words, the more a single clumsy phrase can do damage. The "automatic pilot" remark of 2018 is the cautionary example: a few words, not meant to roil markets, helped trigger a selloff. The power and the peril are the same instrument.

## When this matters to you, and further reading

You do not trade bonds for a living, so why should any of this change how you think? Because the chair's decisions reach into nearly every financial choice you make.

When you take out a mortgage or a car loan, the rate you are offered is downstream of the policy rate the chair leads. Locking a rate before an expected hike, or waiting for an expected cut, is a decision the chair's guidance helps you make. When you keep cash in savings, the difference between earning \$253 and \$2,763 over five years, as our worked example showed, is set by where the chair has put rates. When you hold a retirement account, the value of your bond holdings moves opposite to rates, and your stock holdings react to every shift in the expected rate path. And when you read that "the dollar is strong" or "emerging markets are under pressure," you are reading the global echo of a domestic decision made in a marble room in Washington.

The practical literacy is simple: learn to listen for *tone*, not just the rate. When the chair speaks, the question is rarely "did they hike or cut" (often already priced in) but "did they sound more worried about inflation or about jobs, and did they leave the door open to more moves or close it." That single read, the direction of the expected path, is what actually moves your mortgage, your savings, and your portfolio.

To go deeper from here, the natural next steps are the mechanics of [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) day to day, the bond-buying tool explained in [quantitative easing](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money), the historical campaign that built the modern chair's credibility in [Paul Volcker and the 1980 rate shock](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation), and the global stage the chair operates on in [who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system).

The enduring lesson of Powell's tenure is the one we started with. The most powerful economic actor on earth does not rule by decree. They lead a committee, manage expectations, fight the occasional fire, and answer to a legislature, and their single most potent tool is the careful, deliberate, world-moving sentence. In central banking, words are not commentary on policy. Words *are* policy, and the chair's whole craft is choosing them. That is why the same job that contained two once-in-a-generation crises also produced the "transitory" misstep: the instrument that grants the chair such power, the public's belief in their every word, is exactly the instrument that punishes them most when the words turn out to be wrong.
