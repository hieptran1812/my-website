> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The State Bank of Vietnam runs a different playbook from the Fed: it manages the dong inside a crawling band against the dollar and rations bank lending directly through annual credit-growth quotas, choices that flow straight from the monetary trilemma facing every open emerging economy.
>
> - Every open economy faces a trilemma: it can have a stable exchange rate, free movement of money across its borders, and its own independent interest-rate policy, but only two of the three at once.
> - Vietnam keeps a managed exchange rate and policy room, and pays for that by limiting how freely capital crosses its borders (capital controls).
> - The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) is a ministry-level government body, not an independent central bank like the Fed; its mandate is currency stability and supporting growth.
> - Its two signature tools are the daily USD/VND reference rate inside a trading band (recently plus or minus 5%) and an annual credit-growth quota that caps how much each commercial bank may grow its loan book.
> - The one fact to remember: Vietnam targets the quantity of credit (how many new loans get made) at least as much as the price of credit (interest rates), which is the opposite emphasis of the Fed.

Why does a Vietnamese exporter who just earned \$100,000 not know exactly how many dong that will turn into, even though the exchange rate barely moved last week? And why, in a fast-growing economy hungry for loans, can a healthy bank suddenly tell a creditworthy business "we have no room to lend you anything until January"? Both puzzles trace back to one fact: the institution at the center of Vietnam's money does not work the way the Federal Reserve does. It does not simply pick an interest rate and let markets sort out the rest. It manages the currency hands-on, and it hands each bank a yearly lending allowance.

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: an open economy wants three good things at once, can have only two, and Vietnam's particular choice of which two explains nearly everything else about how its money is run.

![Trilemma triangle with Vietnam's chosen two of three goals highlighted](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling-1.png)

We are going to build this up slowly and from scratch. By the end you will understand what a central bank is, what an exchange-rate regime is, why economists talk about an "impossible trinity," why Vietnam rations loans with quotas, and how all of this showed up in real episodes like the 2011 inflation scare and the 2022 pressure on the dong. No prior finance background is assumed. Every term is defined the first time it appears.

## Foundations: central banks, exchange rates, and the trilemma

Before we can say anything specific about Vietnam, we need four ideas: what a central bank does, what an exchange rate regime is, what foreign-exchange reserves are, and the trilemma that ties them together.

### What a central bank does

A **central bank** is the public institution that sits at the top of a country's money system. Ordinary banks (the ones where you keep your checking account) are called **commercial banks**. The central bank is the bank for those banks, and usually for the government too. It does roughly four things.

First, it is the **monopoly issuer of base money** -- the physical cash in your wallet and the electronic reserves that commercial banks hold at the central bank. No one else is legally allowed to create these. When people say a central bank "prints money," they mean it creates these reserves, usually by buying assets and paying for them with newly created central-bank money.

Second, it sets the **price of short-term money** -- the interest rate at which banks borrow from each other and from the central bank overnight. By raising or lowering that rate, it makes borrowing across the whole economy more expensive or cheaper. This is the Fed's main lever, and we will contrast it heavily with Vietnam's.

Third, it is the **lender of last resort**. If a healthy bank is hit by a sudden wave of withdrawals (a **bank run**), the central bank can lend it cash against good collateral so the bank does not collapse just because everyone showed up on the same day.

Fourth, in many countries it **manages the exchange rate** -- the price of the home currency in terms of foreign currency. The Fed mostly does not do this; the dollar floats freely. The State Bank of Vietnam does this every single day, and it is half of our story.

### Exchange-rate regimes: float, peg, and the managed middle

An **exchange rate** is simply the price of one currency in another. If one US dollar buys about 25,000 Vietnamese dong, we write that as USD/VND of around 25,000. (All the dong-per-dollar figures in this post are approximate and as-of recent years; treat them as illustrative, not live quotes.)

Countries choose how that price is set. There are three broad regimes, and they sit on a spectrum.

At one end is a **free float**. The market sets the price minute by minute, like the price of a stock. Nobody officially defends any level. The US dollar against the Japanese yen (USD/JPY) is a clean example: it can move two or three percent in a single day on news, and neither central bank steps in to stop it most of the time. Free floats are volatile but self-correcting; the price always reflects supply and demand right now.

At the other end is a **hard peg**. The government fixes the home currency to a foreign one at a stated rate and promises to trade unlimited amounts at that rate. Hong Kong famously pegs its dollar to the US dollar within a tight band. A peg gives businesses total certainty about the exchange rate, but it forces the country to give up control of its own interest rates (we will see exactly why in a moment).

In the middle sits the **managed float**, also called a **crawling band** or **crawling peg**. The central bank does not fix one number forever, but it does not let the market run free either. It publishes an official reference value and allows the market to trade only within a band around it -- say plus or minus a few percent -- and it nudges that reference value over time, usually letting it drift in one direction (the "crawl"). Vietnam runs exactly this kind of regime, and the next figure makes the contrast concrete.

![Before-after comparison of a free float versus Vietnam's managed crawl](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling-3.png)

The figure shows the difference cleanly. On the left, a free float: the market sets the price, big daily swings are normal, there is no official band, and the central bank rarely intervenes. On the right, Vietnam's managed crawl: the SBV posts a daily reference rate, a band caps how far the market price may move from it, the reference itself drifts upward (more dong per dollar) slowly over time, and the SBV actively trades dollars to keep the price inside the band.

Two more terms you will meet constantly. **Depreciation** is when a currency loses value gradually because of market forces -- the dong slips from 24,500 to 25,000 per dollar on its own. **Devaluation** is when the authorities deliberately and often suddenly reset the official rate weaker -- the SBV announces "from today, the reference rate is 3% higher." Same direction (the dong is worth less), but one is the market doing it and the other is the policymaker doing it on purpose. The reverse moves are **appreciation** (market-driven strengthening) and **revaluation** (official strengthening).

A point that trips up beginners: a number going *up* in the USD/VND quote means the dong is getting *weaker*, not stronger. USD/VND of 25,000 means it takes 25,000 dong to buy one dollar; if that becomes 26,000, the dollar now costs more dong, so each dong buys less of a dollar -- the dong has weakened. Currency quotes are stated as "how many of the home currency per one dollar," so for the dong, higher means weaker. Keep that straight and the rest of the post reads cleanly; the crawl drifting the reference rate *up* over time is the dong gently *weakening* over time.

### Foreign-exchange reserves: the war chest

To manage a currency, a central bank needs ammunition, and that ammunition is **foreign-exchange reserves** (FX reserves): a stockpile of foreign assets, mostly US dollars and dollar bonds, that the central bank owns. Vietnam's reserves have ranged in the rough neighborhood of \$80 billion to \$110 billion in recent years (an approximate, as-of figure).

Why does it need them? Because to stop the dong from falling, the SBV must create demand for dong. The way it does that is to sell some of its dollar reserves and buy dong in return. Every dollar it sells is a dollar of ammunition spent. A country with large reserves can defend its currency for a long time; a country with thin reserves runs out of ammunition and is eventually forced to let the currency fall. Reserves are, quite literally, how much currency defense a country can afford.

### Capital controls: the gate on cross-border money

The last foundation term is **capital controls**: rules that limit how freely money can move across a country's borders. In the United States you can wire a million dollars abroad to buy foreign stocks tomorrow, no permission needed -- that is free capital flow. In Vietnam, moving large sums in or out, converting big amounts of dong to dollars, or foreigners buying certain local assets, all run into limits, approvals, and frictions. Those frictions are capital controls. They are not unique to Vietnam; most emerging economies use some version. And as we are about to see, they are the price Vietnam pays to get the other two things it wants.

### The impossible trinity (the trilemma)

Here is the single most important idea in the post. Economists call it the **impossible trinity** or the **monetary trilemma**. An open economy would love to have all three of the following at once:

1. **A stable exchange rate** (businesses know what a dollar will cost next month).
2. **Free movement of capital across the border** (money can come and go freely).
3. **An independent monetary policy** (the central bank sets its own interest rates to suit the home economy).

The iron rule is: you can have any two, but never all three. Choosing two forces you to give up the third. Look again at the opening figure; that is what it is showing.

Why is it impossible to have all three? Walk through it. Suppose Vietnam wanted a fixed exchange rate AND free capital flows AND its own low interest rate. Money chases yield. If Vietnam's rate is lower than the dollar rate, investors pull money out of dong and into dollars to earn more -- with free capital flows, nothing stops them. That selling pushes the dong down, which breaks the fixed rate. To defend the rate, the SBV would have to raise its interest rate to match the dollar's, which means it just lost its independent policy. So fixed rate + free capital flows forces you to surrender independent policy. That is the trinity biting.

Different countries pick different corners. The United States picks free capital flows plus independent policy, and lets the exchange rate float -- the dollar goes wherever the market sends it. Hong Kong picks free capital flows plus a fixed rate, and surrenders independent policy -- its rates simply track US rates. Vietnam picks a stable (managed) exchange rate plus a meaningful amount of policy independence, and pays for that combination by restricting capital flows. The next sections are really just the detailed consequences of that one choice.

It is worth sitting with why each pairing forces the third to give. Take Hong Kong's choice -- fixed rate plus free capital. If Hong Kong tried to set its own low rate while the dollar paid more, money would flood out through the wide-open border into dollars, the fixed rate would crack, and the only way to stop it would be to raise rates back to the dollar's level. So free capital plus a fixed rate mechanically hands rate-setting to whoever issues the anchor currency. Now take the US choice -- free capital plus independent policy. The US can set whatever rate it likes and let capital roam freely, but the price is that the dollar's exchange rate then bounces around on its own; there is no way to also pin it. And Vietnam's choice -- managed rate plus independent policy -- only works because the capital border is *not* wide open. The controls are the thing that breaks the chain reaction, so the SBV can hold the rate and still set its own credit and rate conditions. Remove the controls and Vietnam would instantly face Hong Kong's bind. The trilemma is not a slogan; it is a logical constraint with a different bite in each corner.

## The State Bank of Vietnam: a government arm, not an independent referee

The **State Bank of Vietnam** (SBV) is Vietnam's central bank. But the most important thing to understand about it -- and the sharpest contrast with the Fed -- is its place in the government.

The Fed is **independent**: by law, the President cannot order it to cut rates, and its leaders serve fixed terms designed to insulate them from the election cycle. The idea is that politicians always want low rates and easy money before an election, so you take the punch bowl away from them by making the central bank answer to a mandate (stable prices, maximum employment) rather than to the government of the day.

The SBV is the opposite design. It is a **ministry-level body of the government** -- effectively a department of the executive branch, with its governor a member of the cabinet. It is not an independent referee standing apart from the state; it is an arm of the state's economic management. Its decisions on rates, the exchange rate, and credit are coordinated with the government's growth plans, not set in deliberate isolation from them.

This is not a value judgment; it is a design choice with trade-offs. An independent central bank is better at one specific job: credibly promising low inflation, because markets believe it will not cave to political pressure to inflate. A government-integrated central bank is better at coordinating monetary policy with industrial and growth policy, and at moving fast in a crisis without inter-agency friction. The cost is that its anti-inflation promises carry less automatic credibility, so it has to earn that credibility through actual results.

The SBV's mandate reflects this. It is charged with **currency stability** -- both keeping inflation in check and keeping the dong's external value steady -- and with **supporting economic growth and the banking system's safety**. Notice there is no single headline number like the Fed's 2% inflation target carved into its identity. Stability of the currency, internal and external, plus support for growth: that dual emphasis is why it reaches for tools the Fed never touches.

### The SBV's toolkit at a glance

Before we go deep on each tool, here is the whole kit laid out by what kind of lever it is and what it is for.

![Matrix of SBV tools by lever type, purpose, and who feels it](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling-2.png)

The matrix sorts six tools. Some are **price tools** -- they change the cost of money and let banks and borrowers respond: the refinancing rate (the price of cash the SBV lends to banks) and open-market operations (adding or draining daily liquidity). Some are **quantity tools** -- they set the volume directly, regardless of price: the FX trading band (which pins where the dong can trade), the credit-growth quota (which caps how much each bank can lend), the reserve ratio (which forces banks to park a slice of deposits idle), and FX intervention (selling or buying reserves to defend the band). The blend of price and quantity tools is what makes the SBV distinctive; a textbook modern central bank leans almost entirely on price (the policy rate), while the SBV keeps powerful quantity levers in active use.

We can also stack those same tools by how bluntly they bite, from the gentle price nudges on top to the hard volume caps at the bottom.

![Stack of the SBV toolkit from price levers on top to quantity levers at the base](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling-5.png)

Read the stack top to bottom. The refinancing rate and open-market operations sit on top: they whisper to the market by changing the cost of money. The reserve ratio sits in the middle: it locks away a slice of every bank's deposits (recently around 3%), shrinking how much they can lend without dictating the exact number. The FX band, FX intervention, and the credit ceiling sit at the base: these are the heavy quantity levers that set outcomes directly -- where the dong trades, how many reserves get spent, and how much credit the system may create this year. The deeper you go in the stack, the more the SBV is choosing the quantity itself rather than setting a price and watching the market respond.

## The managed dong: a daily reference rate and a band

Now the first signature tool, in detail. Every business day, the SBV publishes a **central reference rate** (sometimes called the central rate) for USD/VND -- the official anchor value for the dong against the dollar that day. Think of it as the SBV saying "today, par is about 24,200 dong to the dollar." The actual market is then allowed to trade only within a **band** around that reference -- in recent years widened to roughly plus or minus 5%. So if the reference is 24,200, the market may trade roughly between 22,990 (5% stronger) and 25,410 (5% weaker), and no further. The commercial banks quote rates to customers inside that band.

Two design features matter. First, the reference rate is reset daily and tends to **crawl** -- to drift gradually toward more dong per dollar (a slow, managed depreciation) -- rather than jump. This lets the dong adjust to a stronger dollar or to Vietnam's higher inflation without a destabilizing one-day shock. Second, the band gives the market room to breathe while capping the damage. The dong can wobble with supply and demand, but it cannot crater.

How does the SBV keep the market inside the band? Two ways. The gentle way is **moral suasion and signaling**: by where it sets the reference and what it says, it steers expectations. The forceful way is **FX intervention**: if the dong drifts toward the weak edge of the band, the SBV sells dollars from its reserves and buys dong, creating demand for dong that pushes the price back. If the dong is too strong (rare for Vietnam, but it happens), it does the reverse -- buys dollars and sells dong, which also happens to build up reserves.

Why does a stable dong matter so much here? Because Vietnam is an extraordinarily **trade-dependent economy**. Its exports plus imports together are larger than its entire annual output -- it is one of the most open economies in the world by that measure. Factories import components and raw materials priced in dollars, assemble them, and export finished goods priced in dollars. A wildly swinging currency would make every contract a gamble: an exporter who signs a deal today might find the dong has lurched by the time they get paid, wiping out their margin. A predictable, gently crawling dong lets the whole export machine plan. That stability is a public good the SBV is deliberately providing.

#### Worked example: an exporter paid \$100,000 and the band

Suppose you run a furniture factory near Ho Chi Minh City. You ship a container to a US buyer and invoice them \$100,000, payable in 60 days. You will eventually convert those dollars to dong to pay your workers and suppliers, who are paid in dong. How many dong will you get?

Today the central reference rate is, say, 24,200 dong per dollar. At exactly the reference, \$100,000 converts to:

```
100,000 dollars x 24,200 dong/dollar = 2,420,000,000 dong
```

That is 2.42 billion dong. But you will be paid in 60 days, and you do not know precisely where inside the band the dong will sit then. Here is where the band protects you. Even in a bad case where the dong has weakened all the way to the 5% weak edge of the band, the rate would be about 25,410 dong per dollar, giving you:

```
100,000 dollars x 25,410 dong/dollar = 2,541,000,000 dong
```

And in a case where the dong strengthened to the 5% strong edge (about 22,990), you would get:

```
100,000 dollars x 22,990 dong/dollar = 2,299,000,000 dong
```

So the band guarantees your \$100,000 lands somewhere between roughly 2.30 and 2.54 billion dong -- a spread of about plus or minus 5% around 2.42 billion. The intuition: the trading band turns an open-ended currency gamble into a bounded one, which is exactly what an exporter needs to plan payroll and pricing.

## The credit-growth ceiling: rationing loans by quota

Here is the tool that has no real counterpart at the Fed, and that surprises most people learning about Vietnam's system. Each year, the SBV sets a target for how fast total bank credit in the economy should grow -- often in the rough neighborhood of 14% to 16% -- and then it hands each individual commercial bank its own slice of that growth, a **credit-growth quota**, informally called the bank's "room." A bank may grow its loan book by, say, 14% this year, and no more. When it hits the cap, it must stop making new net loans, even to creditworthy borrowers, until the SBV grants more room or the calendar turns.

This is a **quantity tool**, and a blunt one. Most modern central banks influence lending only indirectly through the price of money: raise the policy rate, loans get more expensive, fewer people borrow, credit slows. The SBV does that too, but it also reaches in and sets the volume directly. The picture below shows how the quota rations credit across the system.

![Pipeline showing how the credit-growth ceiling rations new loans](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling-4.png)

Follow the flow. The SBV sets a system-wide credit-growth target -- say 14%. It then allots each bank its quota; a bank with a \$1 million-equivalent loan book gets room to add roughly \$140,000 of net new loans. The bank lends up to that cap. If it uses up the quota, it has no room for more loans and must wait. Whatever it does lend flows out into the economy as credit to firms and home-buyers. The quota is the throttle, and it bites before any interest rate would.

Why on earth would a central bank use a quantity tool instead of just moving rates? Three reasons, all rooted in Vietnam's situation.

First, **the price lever is weak when the financial system is bank-dominated and not very rate-sensitive.** In a deep market economy, a rate hike ripples instantly through bond yields, mortgage rates, and corporate borrowing. In Vietnam, a large share of credit is bank loans, many borrowers are not highly sensitive to small rate changes, and the bond and money markets are thinner. So nudging the rate up a bit may not slow a credit boom fast enough. A hard cap on loan growth slows it immediately and predictably.

Second, **it lets the SBV steer credit toward priorities and away from bubbles.** The SBV can give more room to banks that lend to manufacturing and exports and less to banks piling into property speculation. A pure rate tool cannot discriminate like that; it cools everything at once. Whether you think such steering is wise is a separate question, but it is a capability the quota provides.

Third, **it directly controls the trilemma pressure.** A credit boom funded by foreign money would push against the exchange rate; capping credit growth helps keep the whole system from overheating in a way that would threaten the managed dong. The quota and the FX band are two arms of the same stability mandate.

The cost is real and worth naming plainly. A quota is a rationing device, and rationing creates distortions. A bank that has hit its cap turns away good loans, so credit can be scarce for solid borrowers while the cap also fails to stop a determined bank from lending to riskier ones inside its room. The system can develop year-end scrambles, where banks rush to use or preserve room. And because the room is allocated administratively, it concentrates a lot of discretionary power in the SBV. Quantity tools are powerful precisely because they are blunt, and blunt instruments leave bruises.

#### Worked example: a bank with a \$1,000,000 loan book under a 14% quota

Let us make the quota concrete. Take a small commercial bank whose loan book at the start of the year totals the equivalent of \$1,000,000. (Real Vietnamese banks are far larger; we use a round number to see the mechanics.) The SBV grants this bank a credit-growth quota of 14% for the year. How much new lending may it book?

The cap applies to growth in the loan book, so:

```
New net lending allowed = 14% x 1,000,000 dollars = 140,000 dollars
```

So over the entire year, the bank may grow its loan book by at most \$140,000 of net new credit, taking it from \$1,000,000 to \$1,140,000. If a creditworthy business walks in during November asking for a \$200,000 loan and the bank has already used its \$140,000 of room, the answer is no -- not because the borrower is bad, but because the bank is full. The borrower must wait for next year's room or for the SBV to grant a mid-year increase.

Now suppose mid-year the SBV is happy with how this bank is lending and lifts its quota to 18%. The new ceiling becomes:

```
New net lending allowed = 18% x 1,000,000 dollars = 180,000 dollars
```

The bank suddenly has an extra \$40,000 of room (\$180,000 minus the original \$140,000) and can resume lending. The intuition: under a credit ceiling, a bank's ability to lend is set by an administrative allowance, not just by how many good borrowers show up or how much money it has -- the SBV is rationing the quantity of credit directly.

## The other tools: rates, reserves, open markets, and intervention

The dong band and the credit quota are the headline acts, but the SBV runs a fuller orchestra. These supporting tools will feel more familiar if you know how the Fed works; the difference is the market they operate in.

### Refinancing and discount rates

The **refinancing rate** is the interest rate at which the SBV lends short-term cash to commercial banks against collateral; the **discount rate** is a related rate for a specific kind of short-term lending. Together they set the floor cost of funding for banks. When the SBV wants to tighten, it raises these; cash from the central bank gets more expensive, banks pass that on, and credit cools. This is the closest analog to the Fed's policy rate -- but in Vietnam it shares the stage with the quantity tools rather than being the sole star.

### Reserve requirements

The **reserve requirement** (or reserve ratio) is the fraction of customer deposits a bank must hold idle as reserves rather than lend out. If the ratio is 3%, then for every 100 dong of deposits the bank must keep 3 dong parked and may lend the rest. Raising the ratio shrinks how much banks can lend from a given deposit base; lowering it frees up lending capacity. Many advanced central banks have let this tool wither (the US effectively set it to zero in 2020), but the SBV keeps it as an active lever because, again, it works on the quantity of credit directly.

The reserve ratio also interacts with the credit ceiling in a way worth spelling out, because the two can pull in the same direction. A higher reserve ratio takes deposits out of the lendable pool, so a bank that wanted to lend right up to its quota might find it does not even have the funds to do so. A lower reserve ratio frees up funding, which can help banks actually reach their allotted room. The SBV thus has two separate knobs on bank lending -- one that caps the *growth* of the loan book (the quota) and one that constrains the *funding* available to lend in the first place (the reserve ratio) -- and it can turn them together to tighten hard or ease decisively. Compare this with the Fed, which long ago concluded that reserve requirements were a clumsy, distortionary tool and abandoned them in favor of paying interest on reserves to steer rates. The two central banks reached opposite conclusions about the same tool precisely because one targets price and the other targets quantity.

### Open-market operations in a thinner market

**Open-market operations** (OMO) are the day-to-day plumbing: the central bank buys or sells short-term securities to add or drain cash in the banking system, steering the very-short-term interest rate. The Fed does this in the deepest, most liquid market on Earth. The SBV does it in a **thinner market** -- fewer instruments, fewer participants, less depth -- which means its OMO has a coarser, less precise effect on rates. Thin markets are part of why the SBV cannot rely on price tools alone the way the Fed can; the transmission from a small rate move to actual borrowing costs is weaker and less reliable.

### FX intervention: spending reserves to defend the band

The most dramatic supporting tool is direct **FX intervention** -- the SBV stepping into the currency market to defend the band. When the dong is sliding toward the weak edge, the SBV sells dollars from its reserves and buys dong. That creates demand for dong and supply of dollars, pushing the dong back inside the band. The diagram below traces the mechanism.

![Graph of the FX intervention mechanism from pressure to defended band](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling-7.png)

Read the causal chain. Outflow pressure builds (people want out of dong, perhaps because US rates rose and dollars now pay more). The dong drifts to the weak edge of the band. That triggers the SBV to intervene -- say it sells \$500 million of reserves. It uses those dollars to buy back dong, supporting the price. The band holds and the dong steadies. But notice the branch on the right: every intervention drains reserves, and reserves are finite. Intervention buys time and stability, but the ammunition is limited, which is why a long siege against a currency is so dangerous.

#### Worked example: the SBV sells \$500M to defend the band

Suppose pressure on the dong is mounting and the market price has reached the weak edge of the band. The SBV decides to intervene by selling \$500,000,000 of its dollar reserves and buying dong at the current rate of about 25,000 dong per dollar. Two things happen on its balance sheet at once.

First, its FX reserves fall by the amount sold:

```
Reserves spent = 500,000,000 dollars
```

If reserves were \$100 billion before, they are now \$99.5 billion -- a 0.5% draw. Do this many times and the war chest visibly shrinks.

Second, in buying dong it pulls dong out of the market. The amount of dong it absorbs is:

```
500,000,000 dollars x 25,000 dong/dollar = 12,500,000,000,000 dong
```

That is 12.5 trillion dong taken out of circulation. This is a subtle and important side effect: defending the currency by selling dollars also **tightens domestic money** -- it drains dong liquidity from the banking system, which by itself nudges domestic interest rates up. (The SBV can offset this with OMO if it wants to, an operation called sterilization, but the raw effect of selling reserves is to tighten.) The intuition: an FX intervention is not free -- it spends finite reserves and tightens domestic money at the same time, so a central bank cannot defend a currency forever without consequences at home.

## Where Vietnam sits in the trilemma, and how that differs from the Fed

Let us now place Vietnam precisely and contrast it with the United States.

Vietnam's chosen corner of the trilemma is: a **managed (stable) exchange rate** plus a **meaningful degree of monetary-policy autonomy**, paid for with **partial capital controls**. It does not have a fully free float; it does not have fully free capital movement. The capital controls are what let it hold both the managed dong and some room to set its own rates and credit conditions. If Vietnam threw the borders wide open to capital tomorrow, it would have to choose: either let the dong float freely (giving up exchange-rate stability) or let its rates be dictated by the dollar (giving up policy autonomy). The controls are the pressure-release valve that keeps both plates spinning.

I say "constrained" autonomy deliberately. Even with capital controls, the SBV is not fully free. Controls are leaky -- money finds ways around them -- so when the dollar's interest rate rises sharply, Vietnam still feels pressure to follow, lest the gap drive capital out faster than the controls can hold. So the SBV has *more* room than Hong Kong (which has none) but *less* room than the United States (which has full float-protected independence). It lives in the in-between, which is exactly where most successful emerging economies live.

The contrast with the Fed comes down to four differences, and they all reinforce each other.

| Dimension | The Fed (United States) | The State Bank of Vietnam |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary lever | Price: one overnight policy rate | Quantity: FX band and credit quota, plus a rate |
| Exchange rate | Free float; dollar goes where the market sends it | Managed crawl inside a daily reference band |
| Independence | Independent by law from the executive | Ministry-level arm of the government |
| Market depth | Deepest, most liquid markets on Earth | Thinner markets; weaker price transmission |
| Capital flows | Fully free | Partially controlled |
| Inflation framing | Explicit numeric target (2%) | Currency stability plus growth, no single headline number |

The deepest contrast is **price versus quantity**. The Fed targets the price of money (the rate) and trusts the deepest markets in the world to translate that one price into every loan, deposit, and asset value. To see how that propagation works, the companion post on [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) walks through the single-rate-to-everything mechanism. The SBV cannot trust thin markets to do that translation cleanly, so it reaches in and sets quantities directly -- where the dong trades, how much credit each bank may create. Same goal (a stable, growing economy), opposite instrument.

There is one more difference worth naming: **dollarization risk**. In economies where people have lost trust in the local currency, they start saving and even pricing things in dollars instead -- the local money gets displaced. Vietnam has historically had a meaningful undercurrent of this; households have at times preferred to hold dollars or gold as a store of value when they worried about the dong. A central bank facing that risk has an extra reason to keep the currency stable and to discourage dollar hoarding -- if confidence in the dong slips, people flee it, which is destabilizing in a way the Fed never has to worry about for the dollar. To understand how money and trust are created in the first place, the post on [how money is created by banks and central banks](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier) lays the groundwork.

#### Worked example: a saver choosing dong deposits at 6% versus holding dollars

Put yourself in the shoes of a Vietnamese saver with the equivalent of \$10,000. You have two simple options. Option A: convert to dong and put it in a one-year dong bank deposit paying 6% interest. Option B: keep it in dollars (in a dollar account or simply as cash), which pays you little or no interest.

Start with Option A. At a rate of 25,000 dong per dollar, your \$10,000 becomes 250,000,000 dong. A 6% deposit pays:

```
250,000,000 dong x 6% = 15,000,000 dong interest
```

So after one year you have 265,000,000 dong. The question is what that is worth back in dollars, which depends on where the dong sits in a year.

Case 1 -- the dong holds steady at 25,000. Your 265,000,000 dong converts back to \$10,600. You earned the full 6%. Dong deposit wins clearly over holding idle dollars.

Case 2 -- the dong depreciates 5% to about 26,316 per dollar (the crawl plus some pressure). Your 265,000,000 dong now converts to about \$10,070. You still came out slightly ahead of holding dollars, but the depreciation ate most of your 6% interest.

Case 3 -- the dong depreciates a sharp 8% to about 27,174 per dollar. Your 265,000,000 dong converts to about \$9,752 -- you actually lost money in dollar terms, despite earning 6% in dong. Holding dollars would have been better.

The intuition: a high dong interest rate only beats holding dollars if the dong does not depreciate by more than that rate -- which is exactly why keeping the dong stable is central to the SBV's job, because if savers expect big depreciation they flee into dollars no matter how high dong rates go.

This worked example also reveals why the trilemma is not abstract for ordinary people. The saver's decision is the trilemma in miniature: if capital were perfectly free and the dong were expected to fall, money would pour out of dong deposits into dollars, forcing the SBV to either jack up rates or let the dong drop. The capital controls slow that stampede; the managed band makes "the dong will not crater" a credible promise; together they let a 6% dong deposit remain attractive.

## How a weaker dong helps and hurts at the same time

One theme deserves its own section because it sits under every real episode: a weaker dong is neither simply good nor simply bad. It is a trade-off with a winner and a loser inside the same economy, and the SBV is constantly balancing the two.

When the dong weakens (more dong per dollar), Vietnam's exports get **more competitive**. A US buyer paying in dollars finds Vietnamese goods cheaper relative to goods from countries whose currencies did not weaken, so orders rise and exporters earn more dong per dollar of sales. This is **export competitiveness**, and for an export-led economy it is a powerful upside. A gently crawling, slowly weakening dong is partly a deliberate feature, not a bug -- it keeps Vietnamese factories price-competitive against regional rivals.

But the same weak dong makes **imports more expensive**, and Vietnam imports a great deal: machinery, fuel, raw materials, components. When those cost more in dong, the higher cost flows through into domestic prices. This is **imported inflation** -- inflation that arrives through the exchange rate rather than from domestic overheating. A factory that must import a machine, or a household that buys imported fuel, pays more when the dong is weak.

So the SBV faces a genuine tension: let the dong weaken to help exporters and you import inflation; hold the dong firm to contain inflation and you spend reserves and blunt export competitiveness. There is no free lunch. The crawling band is precisely the compromise -- a slow, managed weakening that supports exports without the sudden shock that would ignite inflation or panic savers.

#### Worked example: a 5% dong depreciation and a \$10,000 imported machine

A small manufacturer plans to import a packaging machine priced at \$10,000 from a foreign supplier, payable in dollars. At today's rate of 25,000 dong per dollar, the machine costs:

```
10,000 dollars x 25,000 dong/dollar = 250,000,000 dong
```

Now suppose the dong depreciates 5%. The new rate is about 26,316 dong per dollar (because a 5% weaker dong means it takes 25,000 / 0.95 = 26,316 dong to buy one dollar). The same \$10,000 machine now costs:

```
10,000 dollars x 26,316 dong/dollar = 263,160,000 dong
```

The price in dong rose by 13,160,000 dong -- about 5.3% more -- even though the dollar sticker price did not change at all. The manufacturer's cost went up purely because the dong got weaker. The intuition: a depreciation is a hidden price increase on everything a country imports, which is the mechanism by which a falling currency feeds domestic inflation -- and why the SBV cannot let the dong slide freely even when exporters would cheer it.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of confident-sounding beliefs about Vietnam's monetary system are wrong, and seeing why sharpens the whole picture.

**Misconception 1: "The dong is pegged to the dollar."** It is not pegged. A peg means a fixed rate the authorities defend at all costs (like Hong Kong's). The dong runs a *managed crawling band*: there is no fixed number, the official reference rate is reset daily and is allowed to drift weaker over time, and the market trades within a band around it. The difference matters because a peg gives up monetary autonomy entirely, while a crawling band deliberately keeps some flexibility. Calling it a peg misses the whole point of why Vietnam chose this regime -- to keep room to maneuver.

**Misconception 2: "The SBV just prints money freely to fund whatever it wants."** Money creation is constrained by the trilemma, not by appetite. If the SBV created dong recklessly, the extra dong would chase dollars, the currency would fall through the band, and the SBV would burn reserves defending it -- a hard, visible limit. Managing a currency means money creation is disciplined by the need to defend the band; "printing freely" would blow up the exchange-rate target the SBV is committed to.

**Misconception 3: "Credit ceilings are leftover central-planning relics with no economic logic."** They look like central planning, and they do carry distortions, but they are a rational response to Vietnam's specific situation: a bank-dominated financial system, thin markets where price tools transmit weakly, a recent memory of credit-fueled inflation and asset bubbles, and the trilemma pressure that an unchecked credit boom would put on the dong. The quota is a blunt instrument chosen because the precise instrument (the policy rate) does not bite hard or fast enough here. You can argue it should be phased out as markets deepen -- the SBV itself has signaled it wants to over time -- but that is different from saying it is illogical.

**Misconception 4: "A weaker dong is always bad for Vietnam."** A weaker dong helps exporters and is partly a deliberate policy to keep Vietnamese goods competitive. It is bad for importers and for inflation, and bad for savers holding dong. It is a trade-off, not a disaster, and a slow managed weakening is often exactly what the economy needs. The failure mode is not a weak dong; it is a *disorderly* collapse of the dong, which is what the band exists to prevent.

**Misconception 5: "The SBV setting credit quotas means the government decides who gets every loan."** The SBV sets each bank's *aggregate* growth ceiling, not individual loan decisions. Within its room, a bank still chooses its own borrowers on its own credit judgment. The quota controls the total volume of new credit and can tilt it toward priority sectors, but it is not a Soviet-style central allocation of every loan. It is a cap on the faucet, not a hand on each drop.

**Misconception 6: "Because the SBV is part of the government, monetary and fiscal policy are the same thing."** They are coordinated, not identical. Fiscal policy is the government taxing and spending; monetary policy is the SBV managing money, credit, and the currency. The SBV being a ministry-level body means the two are *closely coordinated* -- which has benefits in a crisis and costs for anti-inflation credibility -- but they remain distinct functions with distinct tools. The integration is the point of contrast with the Fed, not evidence that the two policies have merged into one.

## How it shows up in real markets

The mechanics become vivid in real episodes. The timeline below marks the milestones; then we walk through them. (Treat all rates, reserve levels, and inflation figures as approximate and as-of the years stated, using widely reported facts.)

![Timeline of dong and SBV policy milestones from 2011 to 2022](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling-6.png)

### 2011: high inflation and aggressive tightening

In 2011 Vietnam was running very hot. Inflation surged into roughly the high teens (around 18% at the peak), driven by years of rapid credit growth, loose money, and global commodity prices. The dong was under pressure and confidence was wobbling, with savers eyeing dollars and gold. The SBV responded with a sharp tightening: it pushed its policy rates up steeply and, crucially, leaned hard on the *quantity* side -- clamping down on credit growth and tightening the screws on bank lending. This is the post's whole thesis in action: facing an inflation that was fundamentally a credit-and-currency problem, the SBV did not rely on the rate alone; it choked off the volume of credit. The lesson is that in a bank-dominated economy with a managed currency, controlling the quantity of credit is the front line of inflation control, not an afterthought. Inflation came down over the following year, and the episode hardened the SBV's commitment to the quantity tools we have been describing.

### 2012: formalizing tiered credit quotas

In the aftermath of the 2011 overheating, the SBV leaned into the credit-quota system, allocating growth ceilings to banks in tiers based on their health and lending behavior. Stronger banks lending to priority sectors could be granted more room; weaker or riskier banks got less. This is the discriminating power of a quantity tool that a pure rate cannot match -- the SBV was not just slowing all credit, it was steering it. The lesson is that the credit ceiling is not only a brake; it is also a steering wheel, and the SBV uses it to shape *where* credit goes, not just how much there is in total. The trade-off, as always, is the concentration of discretionary power and the distortions that administrative allocation creates.

### 2015 to 2016: the shift to the daily reference-rate mechanism

For years before 2016, Vietnam ran a cruder system: the SBV would hold an official rate flat for long stretches and then occasionally devalue it in discrete steps. That approach had a flaw -- it built up one-way pressure. Everyone could see a devaluation coming, so they would bet against the dong ahead of it, forcing a bigger, more disruptive adjustment. Around the start of 2016, the SBV switched to the **daily central reference rate** mechanism we described: a rate reset every business day with reference to a basket of currencies and market conditions, allowed to crawl, with a trading band around it. This was a structural upgrade. By moving in small daily increments rather than rare large jumps, the SBV defused the one-way-bet dynamic and made the dong's adjustments smoother and less predictable to speculators. The lesson is that *how* you adjust a managed currency matters as much as *whether* you adjust it -- a continuous crawl is far more stable than a series of shocks, because it removes the easy, one-directional bet.

### 2022: global tightening, FX pressure, and a reserve drawdown

2022 was the hardest test of the modern system. The US Federal Reserve raised interest rates rapidly to fight its own inflation, which made the dollar much more attractive worldwide. Money flowed toward dollars everywhere, and emerging-market currencies came under pressure -- the dong included. Capital wanted out of dong and into higher-yielding dollars, pushing the dong toward the weak edge of its band. The SBV responded with the playbook from this post: it **intervened in the FX market**, selling a substantial chunk of its reserves to buy dong and defend the band, and reserves drew down meaningfully over the year. It also **widened the trading band** to roughly plus or minus 5%, giving the dong more room to absorb the pressure without breaking, and it raised policy rates to narrow the gap with dollar rates. This is the trilemma squeezing in real time: with the dollar rate up, Vietnam could not simply ignore it (the controls are leaky), so it had to spend reserves, widen the band, and tighten -- accepting some currency weakness and some domestic pain to avoid a disorderly collapse. The companion piece on [who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system) explains why a Fed decision in Washington reverberates straight into Hanoi like this. The lesson is that even a country with capital controls cannot fully decouple from the dollar; when the world's reserve currency tightens, every managed-currency central bank feels the pull and must spend reserves or accept weakness.

### 2022: banking-sector stress and the corporate-bond freeze

Layered on top of the FX pressure, 2022 also brought stress inside Vietnam's financial system. The country's corporate-bond market, which had grown quickly, seized up amid a crackdown on irregular issuance and a loss of investor confidence, and the property sector -- a big borrower -- came under strain. A run on one bank linked to a troubled property group tested the system's nerves. The SBV's response drew on its lender-of-last-resort role and its quantity levers: providing liquidity to stabilize the affected bank, while the broader credit-quota framework meant the system's overall credit growth was already being managed rather than left to run. The episode showed both the value and the limits of the toolkit: quantity controls and a hands-on central bank can contain a panic, but they cannot by themselves fix the underlying problem of credit that had flowed into speculative property and fragile bonds. The lesson is that managing the *quantity* of credit at the system level does not guarantee the *quality* of credit within it -- a ceiling caps how much gets lent, but not how wisely.

### The standing tension: competitiveness versus imported inflation

Running underneath all of these episodes is the permanent balancing act we worked through earlier: a weaker dong helps Vietnam's vast export sector but raises the cost of its vast imports and feeds domestic inflation. In good times the SBV lets the dong crawl gently weaker to keep factories competitive. In inflation scares like 2011 and pressure episodes like 2022, it has to lean against further weakness to protect prices and confidence, even at the cost of some export competitiveness and some reserves. There is no settled answer, only a continuous adjustment of the dial. The lesson is that for a trade-dependent emerging economy, the exchange rate is never just a number -- it is a live policy choice that picks winners (exporters or consumers) every single day.

## When this matters to you, and where to read next

If you never set foot in Vietnam, why care how its money is run? Three reasons.

First, **Vietnam is a template for how most of the world actually manages money.** The Fed's free-floating, rate-only, independent model is the exception, not the rule. The majority of countries -- across Asia, Latin America, Africa, and Eastern Europe -- run some version of a managed exchange rate with partial capital controls and active quantity tools, because they face the same trilemma Vietnam does. Understand Vietnam's logic and you understand the default playbook of emerging-market central banking.

Second, **if you invest in, source from, or sell to emerging markets, this is your risk map.** A manufacturer with a Vietnamese supplier, an investor in an emerging-market fund, or a company expanding into Southeast Asia is exposed to exactly the levers in this post: the crawl of the currency, the availability of credit (which can dry up when a bank hits its quota), and the reserve buffer that backs the whole thing. Knowing whether reserves are ample or thin, whether the band is being defended or widened, tells you how much currency risk you are really carrying.

Third, **the trilemma is a universal lens.** Once you see that no country can have a stable currency, free capital, and independent policy all at once, you can read any central bank's choices as a position in that triangle -- and predict the strains it will face. When the Fed hikes and an emerging currency wobbles, you will know exactly which corner is under pressure and what the central bank's likely response is.

For the price-targeting contrast, read [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) -- the cleanest example of the opposite design. To understand the raw material the SBV manages, read [how money is created by banks and central banks](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier). And to see why a decision in Washington ripples into Hanoi, read [who controls the world's money](/blog/trading/finance/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system).

The single thing to carry away: the State Bank of Vietnam targets the *quantity* of money and credit -- where the dong trades, how many reserves it spends, how much each bank may lend -- at least as much as the *price* of it. That choice is not a quirk or a relic. It is the logical consequence of an open, trade-heavy emerging economy picking its two prizes from the trilemma and paying for them honestly with the third.
