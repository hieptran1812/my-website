> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The financial system is a layered machine for moving money from the people who have it to the people who need it; real power belongs to whoever controls the unit of account and acts as lender of last resort.
>
> - Finance does four jobs: it moves payments, channels savings into investment, transfers risk, and discovers prices.
> - Money itself is a hierarchy. Central-bank reserves sit at the base as the safest settlement asset; bank deposits and "shadow money" stack on top, each a promise to deliver the layer below.
> - The cast is small: central banks, commercial banks, investment banks, asset managers, hedge funds and private equity, insurers and pension funds, exchanges and clearinghouses, regulators, and sovereign wealth funds.
> - True power is control of the currency, the payment rails, and the lender-of-last-resort backstop. That is why the Fed and other central banks sit at the apex, and why even a giant like BlackRock holds a different, softer kind of power.
> - The one fact to remember: one dollar of central-bank reserves can support several dollars of bank deposits, because banks create money when they lend.

Who actually controls the world's money? Most people picture a small room of bankers, or maybe a single famous investor, pulling levers behind a curtain. The honest answer is stranger and more interesting: there is no curtain, no single room, and no one person. What exists instead is a *machine* — a layered arrangement of institutions, each doing one job, connected by flows of money and promises. Once you can see the machine, the question "who has power?" stops being a conspiracy and becomes a structural fact you can reason about. The diagram above is the mental model: savers on one side, borrowers on the other, intermediaries in the middle, and a central bank sitting above the whole thing as the issuer of the currency and the backstop of last resort.

![Map of the financial system from savers through intermediaries to borrowers under a central bank](/imgs/blogs/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system-1.png)

This post is a map. By the end you will be able to name every major player, say whose money each one moves, identify the one lever that gives each its power, trace a single dollar from a paycheck all the way through the system and back, and understand why — when the machine seizes up, as it did in 2008 and again in 2020 — it is always the central bank that gets the call. We build it from zero. No finance background assumed. This is educational, not financial advice; nothing here is a recommendation to buy, sell, or do anything with your money.

## Foundations: what "the financial system" even is

Start with two kinds of people, because the entire edifice rests on them.

Some people, at any given moment, have more money than they need to spend right now. A household that earns more than it consumes, a company sitting on retained profits, a pension fund collecting contributions from workers decades before those workers retire — these are **savers**, the people with a *surplus*. Other people need more money than they currently have: a family that wants to buy a house today and pay for it over thirty years, a firm that needs to build a factory before it can earn the revenue to fund one, a government that wants to spend before tax season. These are **borrowers**, the people with a *deficit*.

Here is the problem the financial system solves. Savers and borrowers do not know each other, do not trust each other, want money for different lengths of time, and have wildly different appetites for risk. A saver wants their money safe and available; a borrower wants it for years and might not pay it back. Left to themselves, they cannot transact. The core job of finance is to stand between them and make the trade happen anyway. That job has a name: **intermediation** — channeling money from those who have it to those who need it, through an institution that sits in the middle and absorbs the mismatch. An **intermediary** is simply any institution that does this standing-in-the-middle: a bank, a fund, an insurer, an exchange.

Before we go further, here are the words this whole post leans on. I will define each precisely once, here, and then use them freely.

- **Money** is whatever a society accepts to settle a debt — to make a payment final, so the seller stops being owed anything. In a modern economy money is mostly not paper; it is numbers in bank accounts.
- **Credit** is money lent rather than owned outright — a promise to repay. When a bank "extends credit," it creates a loan: you get spendable money now in exchange for a promise to pay later.
- An **asset** is something you own that has value — cash, a house, a loan someone owes *you*, a share of a company. A **liability** is something you owe — a loan you must repay, a deposit a bank owes its customer. One person's asset is almost always another person's liability. Your bank deposit is *your* asset and the *bank's* liability; the bank owes you that money.
- **Liquidity** is how easily something can be turned into spendable money without losing value. Cash is perfectly liquid. A house is illiquid — selling it fast means selling it cheap. Liquidity is the single most important word in any financial crisis, as we will see.
- A **counterparty** is the other side of any financial deal — the person or institution on the opposite end of your trade or contract. **Counterparty risk** is the danger that *they* fail to do their part (don't pay, don't deliver) and leave you holding a loss.

Hold those six terms — money, credit, asset, liability, liquidity, counterparty — and the rest of the system is just these pieces arranged into ever larger structures.

Two more ideas make the foundations complete. The first is **maturity transformation**: intermediaries routinely take money that savers want back *soon* and lend it to borrowers for a *long* time. Your bank deposit is payable on demand; the 30-year mortgage it helps fund is not. The bank is, in effect, borrowing short and lending long, and pocketing the difference in interest rates as profit. This is genuinely useful — it lets society fund long-lived projects with short-term savings — but it is also the original sin that makes banks fragile: if the short-term savers all want their money back at once, the long-term loans cannot be called in to pay them. Almost every bank crisis is, at bottom, maturity transformation gone wrong.

The second is **leverage** — using borrowed money to amplify a position. If you buy a \$100,000 house with \$20,000 of your own money and an \$80,000 loan, you are leveraged 5-to-1: a 10% rise in the house price (to \$110,000) turns your \$20,000 stake into \$30,000 — a 50% gain on the money you put in, from a price move of only 10%. But a 10% fall does the reverse and wipes out half your equity. Leverage magnifies gains *and* losses symmetrically, and it is the single ingredient that turns an ordinary downturn into a crisis: when many institutions are leveraged against the same assets, a small price drop forces them all to sell at once. Keep maturity transformation and leverage in mind; together they explain why a system built to be useful is also built to be fragile.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000 paycheck becomes a loan becomes a deposit

Let us make intermediation concrete with the smallest possible flow. You get paid. Your employer sends \$1,000 into your checking account. That \$1,000 is now your **asset** (you own it) and your bank's **liability** (the bank owes it to you on demand).

The bank does not lock your \$1,000 in a vault and stare at it. Modern banks must hold *some* funds against deposits — for liquidity and to meet regulatory requirements — but they lend out most of what they take in. Suppose the bank keeps \$100 and lends \$900 to a borrower who wants to buy a used car. The dealer who sells the car receives \$900 and deposits it at *their* bank. Now there is a fresh \$900 deposit somewhere in the system — and that bank, in turn, keeps \$90 and lends \$810. The dealer's mechanic gets paid, deposits \$810, and the cycle continues: \$729, \$656, and on down.

Your single \$1,000 paycheck has rippled outward into a chain of loans and redeposits. The same dollars are being reused at each step. We will quantify exactly how far this goes in a later worked example, but the intuition is already here: **a deposit is not money sitting still — it is fuel that the banking system circulates as credit.** That circulation is intermediation in its rawest form.

## The four jobs of finance

People talk about "the financial system" as if it were one thing. It is really four jobs bundled together, and almost every institution exists to do one or two of them well. Naming the four jobs is the fastest way to stop being mystified by finance.

**Job one: payments.** The system has to let you move money to someone else and have that payment be *final* — settled, done, no longer owed. When you tap a card, the merchant needs confidence that the money is really coming. Behind the scenes, your bank and the merchant's bank settle the obligation, ultimately by moving **central-bank reserves** (we will define these precisely below) between themselves. The payment rails — the card networks, the wire systems, the interbank settlement systems — are infrastructure most people never see and never think about until they break. They are also, as we will see with SWIFT and sanctions, one of the deepest sources of power in the entire system.

**Job two: intermediation** — channeling savings into investment. This is the job we met in the foundations section: taking the surplus of savers and routing it to the deficits of borrowers who will (you hope) invest it productively. Banks do it by taking deposits and making loans. Bond markets do it by letting a company sell an IOU directly to thousands of investors. Stock markets do it by letting a company sell ownership stakes. Pension funds do it by collecting today's contributions and lending them to firms and governments. Every one of these is the same job in a different costume.

**Job three: risk transfer.** Not everyone wants the same risks. A farmer fears a bad harvest; a homeowner fears a fire; an airline fears the price of jet fuel jumping. The financial system lets these parties hand their risk to someone willing to bear it — for a price. Insurance is the oldest form: you pay a premium, and the insurer takes on your risk of fire or death or accident. **Derivatives** — contracts whose value derives from something else, like a futures contract on oil or an option on a stock — are the market version of the same idea: a way to buy or sell a specific risk without buying or selling the underlying thing. The airline can lock in its fuel cost; a speculator on the other side takes the price risk in hopes of profit.

**Job four: price discovery.** Markets do something subtle and underappreciated: they aggregate the opinions and information of millions of participants into a single number — a *price*. The price of a stock, a bond, a barrel of oil, or a currency is the system's best running guess about value, updated continuously as new information arrives. Price discovery is why a market crash is informative and not just frightening: prices are telling you that the collective judgment about the future just changed. Without functioning price discovery, capital flows to the wrong places and the intermediation job degrades.

These four jobs are not independent. A bank run (a payments and intermediation failure) destroys liquidity, which breaks price discovery, which triggers losses that demand risk transfer the system can no longer provide. When a crisis hits, all four jobs fail together. That interconnection is the whole reason a backstop has to exist — and the whole reason power concentrates where it does.

## The cast of players

Now meet the institutions. Every one of them is a specialized worker doing one or two of the four jobs. The trick to keeping them straight is to ask three questions about each: *what does it do, whose money does it move, and what power does that give it?* The figure below is the taxonomy — the family tree of who's who.

![Tree of financial-system player types branching into money issuers, credit intermediaries, risk holders, and infrastructure](/imgs/blogs/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system-2.png)

**Central banks** (the U.S. Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan, and their peers) sit at the top. A central bank *issues the currency* — it is the only institution that can create the base money everything else is denominated in. It sets the short-term interest rate that ripples through every loan in the economy. And it serves as **lender of last resort**: when no private institution will lend, the central bank can, because it can never run out of its own currency. Its money is its own creation — central-bank reserves and physical cash are *its* liabilities, owed to no one in any redeemable sense since 1971. Its power is the deepest in the system: control of the unit of account plus the backstop.

**Commercial banks** (JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, HSBC, your local credit union) take deposits from the public and make loans. The money they move is *depositors' money* — your checking and savings balances are the raw material. Their special power, which surprises most people, is that they *create money* when they lend: the act of making a loan conjures a new deposit into existence. We will prove this mechanically below. Banks are the workhorses of intermediation and the operators of the everyday payment rails.

**Investment banks** (the trading-and-advisory arms of firms like Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley) do not take retail deposits. They help companies and governments raise money by issuing stocks and bonds (the "underwriting" business), advise on mergers and acquisitions, and trade securities for clients and themselves. The money they move is *clients' capital and their own*. Their power is access — they are the gatekeepers between large borrowers and the capital markets, and they shape how new securities are priced and placed.

**Asset managers** (BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street, Fidelity, and thousands of smaller firms) invest other people's savings. They run mutual funds, index funds, and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) — pooled vehicles that buy hundreds of stocks or bonds on behalf of millions of small savers. The money is *clients' money*, held in the clients' name; the manager is an agent paid a fee. The three largest — BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street, the so-called **Big Three** — together manage on the order of \$20 trillion or more (approximate, as of the mid-2020s) and, crucially, *vote the shares* of the companies those funds own. That voting power is real but soft, as we will dissect later.

**Hedge funds and private equity** are the active-risk takers. A **hedge fund** is a pooled investment vehicle, usually for wealthy and institutional investors, that pursues returns using leverage (borrowed money), short selling (betting prices fall), and complex strategies. **Private equity** firms buy entire companies, often using heavy debt, aim to improve them, and sell them years later. Both move *their investors' money plus borrowed money*. Their power is concentration and agility — they can move fast and large, and at the extremes (a leveraged fund blowing up) they can transmit stress into the wider system.

**Insurers and pension funds** are the long-horizon money. **Insurers** collect premiums today and pay claims later; they invest the float (the premiums held in the meantime) in bonds and other assets. **Pension funds** collect contributions from workers and employers and invest them for decades to fund retirements. Both move *savers' and policyholders' money* and are among the largest, most patient suppliers of capital in the world — the natural buyers of long-dated bonds and infrastructure. Their power is the sheer size and stability of their capital.

**Exchanges and clearinghouses** are the market plumbing. An **exchange** (the New York Stock Exchange, Nasdaq, the CME) is a venue where buyers and sellers meet and prices are discovered. A **clearinghouse** stands between the two sides of every trade after it is struck, guaranteeing that each side gets what it is owed even if the other defaults — it becomes the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer, absorbing counterparty risk. Exchanges and clearers move *no one's money in particular*; they are agents and guarantors. Their power is control of the rails on which markets run.

**Regulators** (in the U.S., the Federal Reserve in its supervisory role, the SEC, the FDIC, the CFTC, and the OCC; the FCA and PRA in the U.K.; ESMA and national authorities in the EU) write and enforce the rules: capital requirements, disclosure, conduct, deposit insurance. They move no money; their power is the rulebook and the license to operate — they can shut an institution down.

**Sovereign wealth funds** are governments acting as investors. When a country runs large surpluses — from oil (Norway, Saudi Arabia, the Gulf states) or from trade (China, Singapore) — it parks the proceeds in a state-owned fund that invests globally. They move *national savings* and are among the largest single pools of capital on earth; their power is patient, strategic, and sometimes geopolitical.

## The hierarchy of money

Here is the idea that separates people who understand finance from people who merely use it: **money is not one thing — it is a hierarchy of promises, and the layers are not equally safe.** Most people treat the balance in their checking account, the cash in their wallet, and a dollar in a money-market fund as identical. They are not. They sit on different rungs of a ladder, and in a crisis the difference between the rungs is the difference between getting your money and not.

![Stack of the money hierarchy from central-bank reserves at the base up to deposits and shadow money](/imgs/blogs/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system-3.png)

At the very bottom — the safest, most final form of money — are **central-bank reserves**. These are accounts that commercial banks hold *at the central bank*. When Bank A pays Bank B, what ultimately moves is reserves: the central bank debits A's reserve account and credits B's. Reserves are the closest thing to "real" money in the system because they are the direct liability of the institution that can never run out of its own currency. Alongside reserves, physical cash (paper notes and coins) is also a direct central-bank liability — the same base layer, just in your pocket instead of in an account. Only the central bank can create this base money. No one else. This is the load-bearing fact of the whole system.

One rung up are **commercial-bank deposits** — the balance you see in your banking app. This is the money most people mean when they say "money," and in the U.S. it amounts to many trillions of dollars (on the order of \$18 trillion in broad money measures, approximate as of the mid-2020s). But a deposit is not base money. It is a *promise* by your bank to give you base money (cash, or a transfer settled in reserves) on demand. As long as the bank is sound and you don't all ask at once, the promise is as good as cash. When the bank is in doubt and everyone asks at once — that is a bank run, and the promise can break.

The top rung is what economists call **shadow money** — money-like instruments created outside the regulated banking system. The two big examples are **money-market fund (MMF) shares** and **repo**. A money-market fund pools investors' cash and buys very safe, very short-term debt, and lets investors treat their fund shares as if they were cash (you can redeem at, ideally, exactly \$1 per share). **Repo** — short for *repurchase agreement* — is a way to borrow cash overnight by temporarily selling a safe security (like a Treasury bond) and agreeing to buy it back tomorrow at a slightly higher price; the difference is the interest. Repo is the lifeblood of how big institutions fund themselves day to day, and it functions as money for the institutions that use it. But shadow money is the *least* backed layer: it is a promise resting on a promise, with no deposit insurance and no direct line to the central bank's backstop. When confidence wobbles, shadow money is the first rung to crack — which is precisely where the 2008 crisis began.

The hierarchy has a beautiful logic: each layer is a *promise to deliver the layer below it*. Your deposit is a promise to deliver reserves/cash. A money-market fund share is a promise to deliver deposits. Repo is a promise to return cash tomorrow. As you climb the ladder, you gain convenience and yield but lose the ironclad finality of base money. **Risk rises as you climb, because each higher layer can only honor its promise if the layer below it stays solid.**

#### Worked example: how \$1 of reserves backs several dollars of deposits

Return to your \$1,000 paycheck and let us finish the chain we started, now with the hierarchy in mind. Suppose every bank in the system keeps 10% of any deposit as reserves and lends the other 90%. (Real reserve ratios vary and the U.S. reduced required reserves to zero in 2020 — banks now hold reserves for liquidity and regulatory reasons rather than a fixed ratio — but 10% makes the arithmetic clean and the intuition correct.)

- Your \$1,000 deposit: the bank keeps \$100 in reserves, lends \$900.
- That \$900 is spent and redeposited: the next bank keeps \$90, lends \$810.
- The \$810 is redeposited: keep \$81, lend \$729.
- And so on: \$656.10, \$590.49, ...

Add up all the deposits this single \$1,000 of base money creates, and the sum is \$1,000 ÷ 0.10 = **\$10,000 of deposits** built on **\$1,000 of reserves**. The ratio (1 divided by the reserve fraction) is the **money multiplier**. With a 10% reserve fraction, \$1 of reserves can support up to \$10 of deposits across the system.

The intuition to carry away: **banks do not lend out a fixed pile of money — the act of lending creates new deposit money, and a small base of central-bank reserves can support a much larger superstructure of bank money.** This is also why the system is fragile: the superstructure is many times larger than its base, so a loss of confidence at the base can ripple violently upward. (For the full mechanics of money creation, see [how money is created](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier).)

## Who actually has power

We can now answer the title question precisely, not vaguely. Power in the financial system comes from controlling one or more of three things: **the currency** (the unit everything is priced in), **the payment rails** (how money actually moves and settles), and **the lender-of-last-resort backstop** (who can create money when everyone else is hoarding it). Whoever holds these holds real, structural power. Everyone else holds a narrower, more contingent kind.

The matrix below lays out each major player against three columns — what it does, whose money it moves, and its single power lever. Read it as the answer key to the whole post.

![Matrix of players by what they do, whose money they move, and their power lever](/imgs/blogs/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system-6.png)

**Why central banks sit at the apex.** A central bank controls all three power sources at once. It issues the currency, so every other instrument is denominated in *its* unit. It operates the ultimate settlement layer — reserves — so the entire payment system bottoms out in its ledger. And it is the lender of last resort: because it can create reserves at will, it can lend into a panic when no private actor will, putting a floor under the system. No private institution, however large, can do this. BlackRock cannot print dollars. JPMorgan cannot guarantee its own deposits without the government behind it. The central bank's power is not about being big; it is about being the *issuer and the backstop*. When the machine seizes, every other player ends up, directly or indirectly, asking the central bank for help.

**Why a giant asset manager has a different, softer power.** BlackRock manages something like \$10 trillion or more (approximate, as of the mid-2020s) — a staggering sum that dwarfs the balance sheets of most banks. So why isn't BlackRock the most powerful institution in finance? Because that \$10 trillion is *not its money*. It belongs to the pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and individual savers who are BlackRock's clients. BlackRock is an agent: it invests on instruction, charges a fee, and can be fired. Its power is real but indirect — it comes from *voting the shares* its funds hold (giving it a quiet say in corporate governance across thousands of companies) and from the influence its index decisions have on where capital flows. That is meaningful soft power. But it is not the power to create money or backstop a crisis. In a panic, BlackRock is a participant who needs liquidity, not a source of it. The distinction — controlling money versus managing money — is the most important one in this entire post.

This is why "who controls the world's money" has a layered answer. The central banks control the *substance* of money and the backstop. The big banks control the *creation* of everyday deposit money and the everyday rails. The asset managers control the *allocation* of an enormous pool of savings but not its substance. The regulators control the *rules*. No one controls all of it, and the part that matters most in a crisis — the ability to conjure money and settle finally — belongs to a public institution, not a private one.

## How money flows around the system

Let us trace the full loop, end to end, so the pieces lock together into a circuit. The pipeline below follows a single \$1,000 paycheck as it moves from a wage into a deposit, out as a loan, back as another deposit, and out again as the next loan.

![Pipeline of a 1000 dollar paycheck flowing through deposits and loans across banks](/imgs/blogs/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system-4.png)

Money enters your life as a payment: your employer's bank instructs a transfer, and \$1,000 of *deposit money* appears in your account, settled underneath by a movement of *reserves* between the two banks. That is the payments job and the base layer working together in one ordinary act you never think about.

Your \$1,000 now sits as a deposit — your asset, the bank's liability. The bank performs intermediation: it keeps a slice for liquidity and lends the rest to a borrower (say \$900 for a car). The borrower spends it; the seller redeposits it; another bank lends again. With each pass, the same base money supports another layer of deposits and another loan, exactly as the money-multiplier example showed.

Meanwhile, the savings that don't get lent by banks flow into the capital markets. Some of your money — through your retirement account — reaches an **asset manager**, who buys stocks and bonds on your behalf. The bonds were issued by companies and governments (intermediation through markets rather than banks); the stocks were once placed by **investment banks**; the trades execute on an **exchange** and settle through a **clearinghouse**. Some of your money, through your insurance premiums and pension contributions, reaches **insurers and pension funds**, who lend it long-term to fund mortgages, infrastructure, and corporate debt. Risk gets transferred along the way — the insurer takes your fire risk, a derivatives counterparty takes the airline's fuel risk.

Above all of it, the **central bank** sets the price of the base layer (the short-term interest rate) and stands ready to supply reserves if the system runs short. The **regulators** police capital and conduct so the whole thing stays within bounds. And every flow — wages, deposits, loans, bond coupons, insurance claims, share purchases — ultimately settles by moving reserves on the central bank's ledger. The circuit closes there. **Every dollar in the economy is, at bottom, a claim that resolves into central-bank money.** That is what it means to say the central bank sits at the apex: not that it touches every transaction, but that every transaction bottoms out in its unit and its ledger.

## How a central-bank rate change reaches your wallet

The most direct way ordinary people feel the central bank's power is through interest rates. When the Fed (or any central bank) changes its policy rate, it is setting the price of the base layer — the rate at which banks lend reserves to each other overnight. That single number then transmits, layer by layer, into the rates you actually pay and earn. The graph below traces the chain from one policy decision out to mortgages, business loans, and savings.

![Graph showing a policy rate change transmitting through bank funding into mortgages business loans and savings](/imgs/blogs/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system-5.png)

The mechanism: the policy rate sets banks' cost of funding. When it rises, banks' own borrowing gets more expensive, so they reprice everything — mortgages and business loans cost more, and savings accounts pay more. Higher borrowing costs cool spending and hiring; the economy slows and inflation (eventually) eases. When the policy rate falls, the chain runs in reverse: cheaper credit, more spending, more activity. The central bank is, in effect, adjusting the temperature of the whole economy through one dial — but the dial only works because every other rate is tethered, directly or loosely, to the base layer it controls. (For the machinery of how the Fed actually sets and enforces that rate, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).)

#### Worked example: how a 0.5% Fed rate change moves a \$300,000 mortgage payment

Suppose you are taking out a 30-year fixed mortgage of \$300,000, and the central bank's rate moves feed through to mortgage rates by about half a percentage point — from 6.5% to 7.0%. How much does that cost you?

The monthly payment on a fixed-rate mortgage follows the standard amortization formula. The numbers work out like this:

```
Loan: 300,000   Term: 30 years = 360 months
At 6.5% annual (0.5417% monthly): payment is about 1,896 per month
At 7.0% annual (0.5833% monthly): payment is about 1,996 per month
Difference: about 100 more per month
```

A half-point move adds roughly \$100 to the monthly payment — about \$1,200 a year, and over the full 30-year life of the loan, on the order of \$36,000 in extra interest. The borrower did nothing differently; a committee adjusted one number, and a household's housing cost moved by \$1,200 a year.

The intuition: **the central bank's policy rate is not a distant headline number — it is a multiplier on the largest debt most households will ever carry, and small moves in it move real monthly budgets.** That is the most tangible form the apex's power takes in an ordinary life.

#### Worked example: the bid-ask spread a saver quietly pays

Power and cost in finance are not always loud. Often the system charges you through a gap you never see itemized: the **bid-ask spread**. The *bid* is the price at which the market will buy from you; the *ask* (or *offer*) is the price at which it will sell to you. The ask is always higher. The difference is the spread, and it is the fee the market-maker — the intermediary standing ready to trade — earns for providing liquidity.

Say you want to buy a stock. The quote is \$100.00 bid / \$100.05 ask. You buy at the ask: \$100.05. If you turned around and sold immediately, you would get the bid: \$100.00. You are down \$0.05 a share the instant you transact — a 0.05% round-trip cost — without the price moving at all. On a \$10,000 purchase, that is \$5. Small. But now take a saver who trades a fund position frequently, or who buys an illiquid bond where the spread is not \$0.05 but \$2.00 on a \$100 bond — a 2% round-trip cost. On \$10,000, that is \$200, every round trip.

The intuition: **liquidity is never free; the bid-ask spread is the toll you pay an intermediary every time you cross the gap between buying and selling, and it is largest exactly where you can least afford it — in illiquid markets and in a panic.** Price discovery has a price, and savers pay it.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs are so widespread, and so wrong, that clearing them up is most of the value of understanding the system at all.

**"Banks just lend out the money people deposit."** This is the textbook story, and it is misleading. Banks do not wait to collect deposits and then lend a fixed pile. When a bank makes a loan, it creates a *new deposit* in the borrower's account out of thin air — the loan and the deposit appear simultaneously on the bank's balance sheet. Deposits do constrain a bank in practice (it needs reserves and funding to back its lending, and regulators cap how much it can lend relative to its capital), but the direction of causation is the reverse of the folk story: lending creates deposits, not the other way around. This is why the money multiplier described above is a *ceiling*, not a mechanical conveyor belt.

**"The central bank controls the money supply directly."** It controls the *base* and the *price* of money, not the total. The central bank sets the short-term interest rate and the supply of reserves, but the bulk of the money supply is *deposit* money created by commercial banks when they lend. If banks don't want to lend and borrowers don't want to borrow — as in a deep recession — the central bank can flood the system with reserves and the broad money supply still barely grows. It can lead the horse to water (cheap funding) but cannot force the drinking (lending and borrowing). This is why "printing reserves" does not mechanically produce inflation.

**"BlackRock / the big banks own and control everything."** Asset managers like BlackRock hold huge stakes, but those holdings belong to their clients — pensioners and savers — not to the firm. The manager votes the shares and earns a fee; it is an agent, not an owner. And it competes fiercely with Vanguard, State Street, and others; its clients can leave. Real control of *money* — the ability to create it and backstop it — sits with public central banks, not private asset managers. Conflating "manages a lot of money" with "controls the money" is the single most common error people make about who has power.

**"Cash and a bank deposit are the same money."** They sit on different rungs of the hierarchy. Cash is a direct claim on the central bank — base money. A deposit is a *promise* by a private bank to give you base money on demand. In normal times the promise is as good as cash. In a bank run, it is not: the bank may not be able to honor every promise at once, which is exactly why deposit insurance exists (in the U.S., the FDIC insures deposits up to \$250,000 per depositor per bank). The difference between the rungs is invisible until the day it is the only thing that matters.

**"Markets are rigged by insiders, so prices are fake."** Markets are imperfect, gamed at the edges, and occasionally seize up — but the core function of price discovery is real and mostly works. The price of a liquid stock or a Treasury bond reflects the aggregated, money-backed opinions of millions of participants who lose real money when they are wrong. That is a far more honest signal than any single expert's forecast. Treating all prices as fake throws away the system's most valuable output: a continuously updated, costly-to-fake estimate of value.

**"A financial crisis is caused by greed."** Greed is a constant; it does not explain timing. Crises are caused by *structural fragility* — too much leverage stacked on too thin a base of safe money, and too much reliance on the higher, shakier rungs of the money hierarchy (shadow money) that have no backstop. When confidence wobbles, the superstructure contracts faster than the base can support, and the system freezes at whatever layer was most overextended. Understanding the hierarchy of money tells you *where* a crisis will break before you know *when*.

**"The government and the central bank are the same thing."** They are deliberately separated, and the separation is load-bearing. The government (the Treasury or finance ministry) taxes, spends, and borrows by issuing bonds; it is run by elected politicians on a short electoral clock. The central bank issues the currency and sets interest rates; in most advanced economies it is *operationally independent*, insulated from day-to-day political pressure precisely so it can raise rates into a boom or hold a crisis line even when that is politically painful. The Treasury cannot create reserves; the central bank cannot levy taxes. They coordinate in a crisis — the Treasury guarantees, the central bank lends — but they are different institutions with different tools, and conflating them hides the most important checks-and-balances in the system.

**"More regulation always makes the system safer."** Regulation is necessary, but it is not free and it is not automatically protective. Rules push risk around rather than always eliminating it: tighter bank capital requirements after 2008 made banks sturdier, but they also pushed a great deal of lending into the lightly regulated shadow-banking layer — the very rung with no central-bank backstop. Good regulation targets the *fragility* (leverage, maturity mismatch, runnable funding) wherever it migrates; bad regulation just relocates the danger to where the supervisors aren't looking. The lesson of every crisis is that risk is conserved: clamp it in one place and it surfaces in another.

## How it shows up in real markets

The map is not academic. It comes alive — violently — in real episodes. Here are the ones that teach the mechanism best.

### 2008: the system froze at the repo and interbank layer

The Global Financial Crisis is usually told as a story about subprime mortgages, but mechanically it was a run on the *shadow money* layer of the hierarchy. Through the 2000s, banks and broker-dealers funded enormous holdings of mortgage securities by borrowing in the **repo** market — borrowing cash overnight against those securities, rolling the loan every single day. As long as everyone believed the collateral was safe, the rolling continued and the system hummed.

When mortgage securities turned out to be far riskier than rated, lenders in the repo market got scared. They demanded more collateral for the same cash (higher "haircuts"), then stopped lending against mortgage collateral at all. An institution that funds long-term assets with overnight borrowing has a fatal weakness: if the overnight funding doesn't roll, it must sell assets *immediately* to raise cash. Everyone selling at once crushed prices, which created more losses, which scared lenders more — a self-feeding spiral. Bear Stearns failed in March 2008; Lehman Brothers, which could not roll its funding, collapsed in September 2008. The interbank market — banks lending to each other overnight — seized up too, because no bank could tell which counterparty was about to fail. The lesson, straight from the hierarchy of money: **the crisis began at the top, least-backed rung (repo and shadow money) and only stabilized when the central bank extended its backstop downward to cover layers it had never insured before.** The Fed ended up lending against collateral and to institutions far outside its traditional perimeter — the lender of last resort, expanding its reach to refreeze a thawed system.

### 2020: the COVID dash for cash

In March 2020, as the pandemic hit, investors around the world did the same thing at the same time: they sold everything and ran for cash. This is the **dash for cash** — a stampede *down* the money hierarchy, from risky assets and even from normally-safe ones, toward the base layer of central-bank money. Astonishingly, even U.S. Treasury bonds — the safest, most liquid security on earth — saw their market briefly break down, because everyone was selling them to raise cash and there were not enough buyers to absorb the flood. Money-market funds faced heavy redemptions; the commercial paper market (short-term corporate IOUs) froze.

The Fed's response was the lender-of-last-resort playbook at maximum scale: it bought Treasuries and mortgage securities in enormous quantity to restore liquidity, opened facilities to backstop money-market funds and commercial paper, and extended dollar swap lines to other central banks so the *global* dollar system would not seize. Within weeks the dash subsided. The lesson: **in a true panic, everything that is not base money becomes suspect, and only the issuer of base money can satisfy a system-wide demand for it.** No private institution — not BlackRock, not JPMorgan — could have absorbed that demand. Only the entity that can create the base layer could.

### The Eurodollar system: dollars created outside America

Here is a fact that reshapes the map: most U.S. dollars are not in the United States, and many are not created by U.S. banks. A **Eurodollar** is simply a dollar-denominated deposit held at a bank *outside* the U.S. (the "Euro" prefix is historical and has nothing to do with the euro currency). A bank in London or Tokyo can take in dollar deposits and make dollar loans, creating dollar-denominated bank money entirely outside the Fed's direct domestic reach. The global dollar system is therefore vastly larger than the domestic U.S. money supply, and a huge amount of world trade and finance runs on these offshore dollars.

This matters for power. It means a dollar shortage can erupt *abroad* — foreign banks unable to roll their dollar funding — without any problem inside the U.S. The mechanism by which the Fed addresses this is **central-bank swap lines**: the Fed lends dollars to foreign central banks (taking their currency as collateral), who then lend those dollars to their own banks. In both 2008 and 2020 these swap lines were critical. The lesson: **the dollar's role as the world's reserve currency extends the Fed's reach — and its responsibilities — far beyond U.S. borders, because the world's offshore dollar system has no other lender of last resort.**

### SWIFT and sanctions: control of the rails as a weapon

Remember the payments job and the payment rails. **SWIFT** is the messaging network that banks worldwide use to instruct cross-border payments — not money itself, but the standardized messages that move money between banks. It is infrastructure, and infrastructure is power. When a country is cut off from SWIFT and from access to the dollar-clearing system, its banks lose the ability to settle international transactions in the dominant currencies, which is economically crippling.

This is exactly what happened to Iran (progressively, through the 2010s) and, most dramatically, to major Russian banks after the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, when Western governments disconnected them from SWIFT and froze a large share of the Russian central bank's foreign reserves held abroad. The mechanism is pure control-of-the-rails: the U.S. and its allies do not need to seize anyone's money: they need only deny access to the payment and settlement system that the global economy runs on. The lesson: **control of the rails — the payment and settlement infrastructure — is one of the deepest forms of power in the financial system, and in the dollar-centric world it is concentrated in the hands of the U.S. and the institutions it influences.** It is also why other countries are now investing heavily in building alternative rails.

### A bank run, mechanically: Silicon Valley Bank, 2023

A **bank run** is the hierarchy of money failing in real time. A bank funds long-term assets (loans, bonds) with short-term liabilities (deposits payable on demand). This works only because depositors don't all withdraw at once. When they fear the bank can't pay, the rational move for each depositor is to withdraw *first*, before the bank runs out — and that individual rationality produces collective catastrophe.

Silicon Valley Bank in March 2023 is the textbook modern case. SVB had taken in a flood of deposits and invested heavily in long-term bonds. When interest rates rose sharply in 2022, those bonds fell in value (bond prices fall when rates rise). Word spread among SVB's concentrated base of tech-startup depositors — many with balances far above the \$250,000 insurance limit — that the bank was sitting on large unrealized losses. They moved to withdraw. Crucially, modern runs happen at digital speed: depositors tried to pull roughly \$42 billion in a single day. To meet withdrawals, SVB had to sell its bonds at a loss, realizing the very losses depositors feared, which accelerated the run. The bank failed within about 48 hours. Regulators stepped in, guaranteed the deposits, and the Fed opened a facility letting banks borrow against their bonds at face value — again, the lender-of-last-resort backstop appearing the moment the deposit layer of the hierarchy cracked. The lesson: **a deposit is a promise that depends on confidence, and confidence is the most liquid and most fragile asset in the system; when it evaporates, only a credible backstop stops the cascade.**

### Bretton Woods to fiat: how the map itself was redrawn

The architecture we have been mapping is not eternal. It was deliberately built and then deliberately changed. The timeline below marks the four hinge points.

![Timeline of the money map from Bretton Woods 1944 through 1971 fiat to the 2008 and 2020 crises](/imgs/blogs/who-controls-the-worlds-money-global-financial-system-7.png)

In 1944, at **Bretton Woods**, the major economies built the postwar monetary order: the U.S. dollar was pegged to gold (\$35 an ounce), and other currencies were pegged to the dollar. The dollar became the anchor of the world precisely because it was, ultimately, redeemable in gold. In 1971, facing a drain on U.S. gold reserves, President Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility — the "Nixon shock" — and the world shifted to **fiat money**: currency that is money because the issuing state says it is and people accept it, backed by nothing but confidence and the central bank. This is the deepest change of all: after 1971, the base layer of the money hierarchy became a pure promise, with no commodity underneath. That is what makes the lender-of-last-resort role so total — a fiat central bank can never run out of its own currency, because there is nothing it must redeem the currency *into*. The crises of 2008 and 2020 then stress-tested this fiat, central-bank-anchored system to its limits, and each time the answer was the same: the central bank expanded its backstop. The lesson: **the modern system's apex sits with central banks not by accident but by design — the 1971 move to fiat made the currency-issuer the unconditional backstop, and every crisis since has confirmed it.**

## A few more worked examples to cement the mechanics

#### Worked example: a money-market fund "breaks the buck"

Recall that a money-market fund tries to keep its share price at exactly \$1.00, so investors treat it like cash. "**Breaking the buck**" means the share value falls below \$1.00 — the cash-like promise fails. It is rare and alarming because it shatters the illusion that this rung of shadow money is as safe as a deposit.

It happened in September 2008. The Reserve Primary Fund, a large money-market fund, held \$785 million of Lehman Brothers commercial paper. When Lehman failed, that paper was suddenly worth a fraction of face value. The fund's per-share value fell to about \$0.97 — it broke the buck. Here is the mechanism in numbers: if a fund holds \$1.00 of assets per \$1.00 share and 3% of those assets are wiped out, each share is now backed by only \$0.97. Investors who realized this raced to redeem at \$1.00 before the rest caught on — a run on the fund — forcing it to dump assets and crystallize losses. The panic spread to *other* money-market funds, threatening the entire commercial-paper market that companies rely on for short-term funding. The U.S. Treasury had to step in and temporarily guarantee money-market funds.

The intuition: **shadow money is money only as long as everyone believes it is; the moment a promise on the top rung of the hierarchy is broken, the run is instantaneous, because everyone is trying to get out at the same \$1.00 price before it falls.** A 3% loss on the assets triggered a system-threatening run — that is leverage of confidence, not of borrowing.

#### Worked example: tracing your \$1,000 paycheck into the capital markets

We traced \$1,000 through the banking layer. Now trace the part that flows into markets, to see the other half of intermediation. Suppose of your \$1,000, you save \$200 into a retirement account that buys a low-cost index fund.

- Your \$200 goes to an **asset manager** running the fund. The manager pools your \$200 with millions of other savers' contributions.
- The fund buys, say, \$120 of stocks and \$80 of bonds, in proportion to its mandate. Your money is now part-owner of hundreds of companies and a lender to dozens of governments and firms.
- The \$80 of bonds you (indirectly) bought might be U.S. Treasuries — meaning you are now lending to the federal government — or corporate bonds, meaning you are funding a company's factory.
- The \$120 of stocks trades on an **exchange** and settles through a **clearinghouse**, which guarantees the trade.
- For all this, the fund charges a fee — say 0.04% a year for a cheap index fund, which on \$200 is about \$0.08 a year. On an expensive actively managed fund charging 1%, it would be \$2.00 a year — 25 times more, for no guaranteed extra return.

The intuition: **the same act of saving routes your surplus to borrowers — but through markets and managers rather than a bank's loan officer, and the fee you pay the intermediary, however small it looks, compounds over decades.** A 0.96-percentage-point difference in annual fee, compounded over a 40-year working life, can consume a quarter or more of your final balance. Intermediation is never free, and the cost of the intermediary is one of the few things a saver can actually control.

## When this matters to you / further reading

This map is not trivia. It touches your life at specific, predictable points, and knowing where lets you reason instead of react.

It matters every time the central bank changes rates — because, as the \$300,000-mortgage example showed, that single number reprices your largest debts and your savings yield. It matters when you choose where to keep your money: a deposit under the insurance limit sits on a backstopped rung of the hierarchy; money in a fund or a fintech wrapper may not. It matters when you pick an investment: the fee you pay an intermediary, the bid-ask spread you cross, and the liquidity of what you buy are real costs that the system will quietly collect from you. And it matters when the news says "the markets froze" or "a bank failed" — because now you can ask the right question: *which rung of the money hierarchy cracked, and is the backstop reaching it?*

The single most useful idea to carry forward is the hierarchy of money: central-bank reserves at the base, bank deposits above, shadow money on top, each a promise to deliver the layer below, with risk rising as you climb. Almost every crisis is a story about the higher rungs losing faith in the lower ones, and almost every rescue is a story about the issuer of the base layer extending its backstop upward. Whoever controls that base layer and that backstop holds the real power. Today, by design since 1971, that is the central banks.
