> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — an individual bond has a *maturity date* — a day when it pays you back par and the loan is over — and that date acts like a magnet, pulling the bond's price home to par no matter how violently it swings in between. A bond fund or ETF has *no maturity date at all*, so its value just floats with interest rates forever, and a rate shock can leave it lower until rates reverse.
> - **An individual bond pulls to par.** Hold it to maturity and the interim price swings don't change your final payout — you get every coupon plus the face value back, absent default. The scary mid-life mark is a paper number you never have to accept.
> - **A fund's NAV floats.** It is a basket of bonds marked to market every day, with a *duration* but no maturity. When rates rise, the NAV falls by roughly duration × the rate move, and there is no maturity date to drag it back — only falling rates (or slowly compounding higher coupons) can.
> - **That is why "bond funds lost money in 2022."** A long-duration fund could drop ~20% in a year of rising rates — a real, marked loss on the screen. An individual bond held to maturity through the same year showed the same paper drop but still paid par at the end.
> - **ETFs add a second price.** A bond ETF has both a *NAV* (the value of the bonds inside) and a *market price* (what it trades for on the exchange). They usually hug each other, but in stress — most famously March 2020 — the price fell several percent *below* NAV, a discount that arbitrage and the Fed eventually closed.
> - **The trade-off is the whole decision.** Funds buy you diversification, liquidity, and tiny lot sizes for a small annual fee; individual bonds buy you a known maturity, a known payout, and full control. Neither is "better" — they fail in different ways.

You buy a 10-year bond and a 10-year bond fund on the same morning, put \$100,000 in each, and walk away. A year later rates have risen two percentage points. You log in. *Both* accounts are down about \$20,000. They look identical — same red number, same gut-punch. So you might reasonably conclude they are the same thing in two wrappers, and that the choice between them is just a matter of convenience.

They are not the same thing. They are two genuinely different animals that happen to look alike on a bad day. The difference is one word: **maturity**. The individual bond has a date — ten years out — when the issuer must hand you back the full \$100,000 of *face value* (the amount printed on the bond, also called *par* or *principal*). That date is a hard promise, and it acts like a magnet on the bond's price: as the maturity date approaches, the price is dragged back toward par, because on the final day the bond is worth exactly par and not a cent more or less. The fund has no such date. It is a basket of bonds that the manager keeps rolling over forever, so its value never gets pulled home to anything. It just floats wherever rates leave it.

![A bond matures and pulls home to par while a bond fund just floats with rates forever, shown as two paths after a rate shock where the bond price climbs back to par by maturity and the fund value stays down near the new lower level](/imgs/blogs/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for this entire post. After the same rate shock, the individual bond's price (the solid line) dips and then climbs steadily back to \$100 by its maturity, because the maturity date is reeling it in. The fund's value (the dashed line) dips and then just hovers near the new, lower level — it has no maturity to reel it back, so it stays down until rates themselves come back down. Same shock, two fates. Everything else in this post — NAV, duration, expense ratios, ETF premiums and discounts, the "bond funds lost money in 2022" debate — is a consequence of that single picture.

This is the most practical decision a bond investor actually faces, and it is widely gotten wrong, because the wrong question gets asked. The question is not "which one performs better?" — over long stretches they perform almost identically, because they hold the same kind of stuff. The question is "which failure mode can I live with?" An individual bond fails by *default* (the issuer doesn't pay) and by *illiquidity* (it's a pain to sell). A fund fails by *never recovering on its own* (no maturity date) and by *a small fee every single year*. We will build the whole thing from first principles, return again and again to one \$100,000 example, and end with the real episodes — 2022 and March 2020 — where these differences stopped being theoretical.

If you want the macro backdrop for *why* rates move in the first place, the sibling post on [interest rates, the price of money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable) is the place to start; here we take rate moves as given and ask what they do to the two ways you can own bonds.

## Foundations: the building blocks you need before the comparison makes sense

Before we can compare a bond to a fund, we have to be precise about a handful of terms. None of them are hard. Each is a name for something you can already picture.

A **bond** is a tradable loan. You hand an issuer (a government, a company, a city) money today; in return they promise you a fixed schedule of payments — a **coupon** (a fixed interest payment, usually paid every six months or once a year) and the **face value** (the principal, par, the amount printed on the bond, our standard \$1,000 or \$100,000) back on a specific day. That day is the **maturity**. The bond's whole identity is "this issuer will pay me this coupon until this date, then return my principal." We covered the parts in detail in [the anatomy of a bond](/blog/trading/fixed-income/anatomy-of-a-bond-par-coupon-maturity-issuer); the one part that matters most for *this* post is the maturity date.

The **coupon rate** is the fixed coupon as a percent of face value. A 4% coupon on a \$100,000 bond pays \$4,000 a year, every year, no matter what happens to interest rates afterward. That word *fixed* is the engine of everything: because the payment can't change, the *price* of the bond has to do all the adjusting when the world changes around it.

The **market interest rate** (or **yield**, or just "rates") is the return investors currently demand to lend for that length and risk. It floats with the economy, the central bank, and inflation. When people say "rates went up," this is the number that moved.

**Price and yield move in opposite directions.** This is the seesaw at the heart of all of fixed income, and it has its own post — [why bond prices move when rates move](/blog/trading/fixed-income/why-bond-prices-move-when-rates-move-and-by-how-much). The intuition: your bond pays a fixed \$4,000 a year. If new bonds start paying \$6,000 a year because rates rose, nobody will pay full price for your stingy old one — so its market price falls until its *effective* yield matches the new 6%. Rates up, price down. Rates down, price up. Always.

**Duration** is the single most important number in this post, so let's build it carefully. Loosely, duration is *how sensitive a bond's price is to a change in rates*, measured in years. A bond with a duration of 7 will lose roughly 7% of its value if rates rise 1%, and gain roughly 7% if rates fall 1%. The rule of thumb is:

$$\text{price change} \approx -\,\text{duration} \times \text{change in rate}$$

where the minus sign is the seesaw (rates up, price down). A 10-year bond has a duration of roughly 8–9 years (a little less than its maturity, because the coupons arrive earlier and shorten the average wait); a 2-year bond has a duration of roughly 2. Bigger duration means bigger swings. We give duration its own full treatment in [duration, the most important number in fixed income](/blog/trading/fixed-income/duration-the-most-important-number-in-fixed-income); here, just hold onto "duration is the size of the swing, in years."

**Net asset value (NAV)** is the per-share value of a fund. Take everything the fund owns (all its bonds, valued at today's market prices), subtract anything it owes, and divide by the number of shares. That's the NAV. It is recomputed — *struck* — once every business day for a mutual fund, and continuously for an ETF. NAV is to a fund what *price* is to a single bond: the number that moves when rates move.

A **bond fund** is a pool. Many investors put money in; a manager buys a big basket of bonds with it (often hundreds); the fund's value is the NAV of that basket; and crucially, **the manager keeps rolling the portfolio over** — when a bond matures or is sold, the cash buys a new bond, so the basket never matures. The fund holds a roughly constant *duration* but has no maturity date of its own. That "no maturity" is the entire difference from owning a single bond.

A **mutual fund** is the classic open-end pool: you buy and sell shares directly with the fund company, once a day, at that day's closing NAV. An **ETF** (exchange-traded fund) is a fund whose shares also trade on a stock exchange all day long, like a stock, at a *market price* set by buyers and sellers. So an ETF has two numbers — its NAV (the value of the bonds inside) and its market price (what it trades for) — and the relationship between them is its own small drama, which we'll get to.

The **expense ratio** is the fund's annual fee, quoted as a percent of assets. A 0.10% expense ratio on a \$100,000 holding costs you \$100 a year, skimmed quietly from the NAV — you never see a bill. Index bond ETFs charge as little as 0.03–0.05%; actively managed funds can charge 0.40–0.80% or more.

The **bid-ask spread** is the gap between the price a dealer will *buy* from you (the bid) and the price they'll *sell* to you (the ask). It's a hidden cost of trading. For a popular Treasury it's tiny; for an obscure corporate bond it can be a full percent or more, which matters enormously when we compare how easy each option is to trade.

With those nine terms — bond, coupon, face value, maturity, duration, NAV, fund/ETF, expense ratio, bid-ask spread — you have everything you need. Now let's watch the two animals behave.

## The one difference that explains everything: maturity pulls to par

Start with the individual bond, alone. Picture a 10-year bond with \$100,000 face value and a 4% coupon, bought at par. Every year it pays you \$4,000. In ten years it pays you back the \$100,000. That schedule is fixed and contractual.

Now rates rise. The bond's price falls — the seesaw. But here is the thing people miss: **the price was always going to end up at exactly \$100,000 on the maturity date, regardless of what rates did in between.** Why? Because on the final day, the bond is a claim on one payment — the \$100,000 par — due that same day. A claim on \$100,000 due today is worth \$100,000. There's no discounting left to do, no future to wait through. So as the maturity date approaches, the bond's price is mechanically dragged toward par. This is called the **pull to par**, and it is the most important and least appreciated fact about owning a bond directly.

The pull to par means the mid-life price drop is a *paper* loss. It's real in the sense that if you sold, you'd get less. It's unreal in the sense that if you *don't* sell, it evaporates on its own — the price climbs back to par as maturity nears, no matter what. You collected every \$4,000 coupon along the way, and you get your \$100,000 back at the end. The frightening number in the middle was a number you simply never had to accept.

#### Worked example: the same bond, two numbers

You buy the 10-year, 4% bond for \$100,000. One year later, rates have risen 2 percentage points. The bond now has 9 years left and a duration of about 7.5. Using our rule of thumb, the price falls by about 7.5 × 2% ≈ 15%, so it's now worth roughly \$85,000. Your statement shows a \$15,000 loss. Scary.

But trace the two paths forward. *Path A — you sell now:* you take \$85,000, lock in a \$15,000 loss, and walk away. *Path B — you hold to maturity:* you keep collecting \$4,000 a year for nine more years (\$36,000 of coupons), and on the maturity date the issuer hands you the full \$100,000 par. The \$85,000 mark in year one was a moment, not a destination. As long as the issuer doesn't default, you get *exactly* what you were promised the day you bought: every coupon and your principal back.

![Same bond shown two ways, on the left selling mid-life after a rate rise locks in a loss near eighty-five thousand dollars, on the right holding to maturity collects every coupon and returns the full one hundred thousand dollars of par with no loss absent default](/imgs/blogs/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds-5.png)

*The mid-life mark-to-market is a price you can refuse; the maturity payout is the price you actually get.*

The figure makes the asymmetry concrete: the left column is the scary version (sell, lock in −\$15,000); the right is the calm version (hold, collect everything, no loss). For an individual bond, you get to choose which column you live in — and the only thing that can take the right column away from you is *default*, the issuer failing to pay. That's why credit quality, not interest-rate swings, is the real risk of holding an individual bond to maturity. (We dig into default and how it's priced in [credit risk](/blog/trading/fixed-income/credit-risk-the-chance-you-dont-get-paid-back) and [credit spreads](/blog/trading/fixed-income/credit-spreads-pricing-the-probability-of-default).)

## A bond fund has no maturity, so its NAV just floats

Now the fund. A bond fund holds hundreds of bonds with a target duration — say a fund that keeps its duration around 7 years. When a bond in the basket gets close to maturity, the manager sells it and buys a longer one to keep the duration on target. **The portfolio never matures.** There is no day when the fund hands you back par, because the fund as a whole has no par and no maturity date. It is a perpetually rolling basket.

This changes everything about how rate moves affect you. When rates rise, the NAV falls by roughly duration × the rate move — same arithmetic as a single bond. But now there is *no pull to par* to undo it. The fund's bonds get rolled forward, the new bonds have their own future maturities, the duration stays around 7, and the NAV simply sits at the new, lower level. The only forces that can lift it back up are (a) rates falling again, or (b) the higher coupons the fund now earns on the bonds it buys, which compound the NAV upward slowly over time. There is no magnet pulling it home by a deadline, because there is no deadline.

![A bond fund's net asset value plotted against the market interest rate as a downhill straight line whose slope equals the negative of duration, with the 2022 move marked from end of 2021 at one hundred dollars and one and a half percent down to end of 2022 near eighty dollars and four and a half percent](/imgs/blogs/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds-2.png)

*A fund's value is a downhill function of the rate level; with no maturity, nothing drags it back up except rates falling again.*

The figure above is the centerpiece of this post: it plots a fund's NAV directly against the level of rates. It is a downhill line. Push the rate up, slide the NAV down; the steeper the line, the longer the fund's duration. There is no time axis pulling anything home — just the rate level and the NAV, locked together by duration. This is the picture of 2022: rates rose from roughly 1.5% to roughly 4.5% over the year, and a fund with a duration around 7 slid from \$100 of NAV toward \$80, a ~20% drop. The loss was real and marked-to-market on the screen every single day, because for a fund there is no "just hold to maturity" — there is no maturity to hold to.

#### Worked example: the \$100,000 fund through a +2% year

You put \$100,000 into a fund with a duration of about 7. Rates rise 2 percentage points over the year. The NAV falls by roughly 7 × 2% = 14%, so your holding is now worth about \$86,000. (A longer-duration fund — duration 10 — would fall ~20%, to ~\$80,000; this is exactly the 2022 experience for long-bond funds.) Along the way you collected distributions of roughly \$4,000 (the basket's coupons, around 4%), so your total value is about \$90,000 — down \$10,000 on the year.

Here is the part that diverges from the individual bond: there is no date on which this comes back automatically. If rates stay at the new level, your fund stays around \$86,000 of principal value indefinitely. It recovers only if (a) rates fall back, lifting the NAV, or (b) you wait many years while the fund reinvests at the new higher rates and the extra coupon income slowly compounds you whole — a process that takes roughly the fund's duration in years to play out. *A fund doesn't break the rate math; it just removes the deadline that would otherwise rescue you.*

This is the honest core of the "bond funds lost money in 2022" complaint, and the honest answer to it. The complaint is true: long-duration bond funds had their worst year in modern history, some down 30%+. But the lesson isn't "funds are broken." It's "a fund makes the duration risk *visible and permanent-until-rates-reverse*, whereas an individual bond lets you hide that same risk behind a maturity date." The risk is the *same risk*. The wrapper changes whether you're forced to look at it.

### The recovery horizon: why a fund heals in roughly its duration

There's a precise and reassuring fact buried in the fund's "no maturity" problem, and it's worth pulling out because it's the closest thing a fund has to a pull-to-par. After a one-time jump in rates, a bond fund's *total return* — price change plus reinvested income — climbs back to where it would have been (had rates never moved) after a number of years roughly equal to the fund's duration. The mechanism is the seesaw running in reverse: the same rate rise that cut your NAV also raised the yield on every bond the fund now buys, and that fatter income stream compounds until it has made up the principal loss.

This is not a coincidence; it's the deep meaning of duration. Duration is, formally, the holding period at which a bond's (or fund's) price loss from a rate rise is exactly offset by the extra reinvestment income that same rise produces — the "two faces of yield" from the sibling post on [reinvestment risk](/blog/trading/fixed-income/reinvestment-risk-and-the-two-faces-of-yield). For an individual bond, that breakeven horizon is its duration, and the maturity date is a hard backstop that guarantees par by the end. For a fund, the *same* breakeven math applies — the fund's total return recovers in about its duration — but there is no hard backstop date, because the fund keeps rolling. So a fund's recovery is real and roughly predictable, but it is a *statistical* recovery driven by income, not a *contractual* one delivered by a maturity date.

#### Worked example: the duration-7 fund's breakeven horizon

Your duration-7 fund drops ~14% when rates jump 2%. But it now earns ~2% more income per year on its bonds than before. Crudely, that extra ~2% a year on the (lower) NAV takes about seven years of compounding to rebuild the ~14% of lost principal — duration ≈ the recovery horizon. After about seven years, an investor who simply held through the shock and reinvested distributions ends up with roughly the *same* total wealth they'd have had if rates had never moved — and from there on, they're better off, because they're earning the higher rate. *A bond fund doesn't escape duration; it converts a rate shock into a temporary drawdown that income heals over roughly the fund's duration — slower and less certain than a maturity date, but real.*

## How a fund is actually built and rolled

To really understand the fund side, it helps to see the machine. Cash comes in from many investors and is pooled. The manager buys a diversified basket — often 100 to 1,000 individual bonds spread across issuers and maturities. Every business day, the fund values the basket at market prices and strikes the NAV. The bonds throw off coupons, which the fund either pays out to you as *distributions* or reinvests. And as bonds approach maturity, the manager sells them and buys fresh ones — the **roll** — keeping the basket's duration on target. The result is a portfolio that pays income, marks to market daily, and never reaches an end.

![How a bond fund is assembled and rolled, from investor cash pooled together to a manager buying a basket of about three hundred bonds, striking the daily net asset value, collecting coupons, paying distributions, and rolling maturing bonds into new ones so the portfolio keeps a constant duration and never matures](/imgs/blogs/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds-3.png)

*Coupons and maturing principal are continuously recycled into new bonds, which is why a fund keeps a duration but never reaches a maturity.*

The roll is the subtle, underappreciated step. It's what keeps the duration constant — and it's also what *helps* a fund recover after a rate shock. After 2022, bond funds were buying new bonds at 4–5% yields instead of the 1–2% yields of 2021. That higher income compounds the NAV upward over time. So a fund that fell 20% in 2022 wasn't doomed to stay down forever; the roll meant it was now earning much more income, which slowly heals the principal loss — over roughly the fund's duration in years. The maturity-less structure cuts both ways: no deadline to rescue you, but a continuously refreshing income stream that does the healing more gently.

#### Worked example: why a fund's distribution yield rose after the crash

Imagine the fund held \$100,000 of bonds yielding 2% at the start of 2021, paying \$2,000 a year. Through 2022, rates rose and the NAV fell to ~\$80,000 — but the manager kept rolling maturing bonds into new ones now yielding ~5%. A year or two later, the fund's bonds are throwing off roughly 5% on the *current* NAV. On \$80,000 of NAV at 5%, that's \$4,000 a year — double the old \$2,000 of income. The principal took the hit; the income engine got stronger. *The roll is the mechanism by which a bond fund, given enough time, turns a rate shock from a permanent loss into a temporary one — exactly the role maturity plays for a single bond, just slower and not guaranteed by a date.*

## ETF versus mutual fund: the second price, premiums, and discounts

Both mutual funds and ETFs are funds — same NAV, same duration, same rolling basket. The difference is *how you trade them*, and that difference creates a phenomenon mutual funds don't have: an ETF can trade at a price different from its NAV.

A mutual fund is simple. You place an order any time during the day; it executes at that day's closing NAV; you get exactly the NAV, no more, no less. There's no separate "market price" — the NAV *is* the price.

An ETF trades on an exchange all day, like a stock, at whatever price buyers and sellers agree on. That market price is *anchored* to the NAV by an arbitrage mechanism: large institutions called **authorized participants** can create new ETF shares by delivering the underlying bonds to the fund, or redeem ETF shares for the underlying bonds. If the ETF price drifts above NAV (a **premium**), they create shares and sell them, pushing the price down. If it drifts below NAV (a **discount**), they buy cheap ETF shares and redeem them for the more-valuable bonds, pushing the price up. In normal times this keeps the ETF price within a hair of NAV.

But the anchor is only as strong as the underlying bonds are tradable. And bonds — unlike stocks — don't all trade continuously. Many corporate and municipal bonds trade by appointment, a few times a week. When the underlying market freezes, the NAV (computed from stale or estimated bond prices) can lag reality, while the ETF — trading live on the exchange — reflects what buyers will *actually* pay right now. The result: in a panic, the ETF can trade at a meaningful discount to its stated NAV.

![In the March 2020 stress a bond ETF traded at a discount to its own net asset value, shown as the live exchange price diving about five percent below the NAV line at the peak of the panic before the Federal Reserve stepped in and the discount closed](/imgs/blogs/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds-4.png)

*The ETF's live price discovers the real, tradable value faster than a NAV built from bonds that have stopped trading; the gap is a stress signal, not a free lunch.*

The figure shows the classic shape from March 2020. The NAV (the lavender line) drifts down smoothly, because it's built from bond prices that are stale or modeled. The ETF's market price (the blue line) drops much harder and faster, opening a discount of several percent at the peak of the panic, because it reflects the price at which buyers would *actually* transact in a frozen market. Then the Fed announced it would buy corporate bonds (and bond ETFs), liquidity flooded back, and the discount snapped shut as the price rejoined the NAV.

There are two honest ways to read that discount, and both are true. One: the ETF "broke" — it traded below the value of its holdings. Two: the ETF was *more honest than the NAV* — it discovered the real, tradable price of the underlying market faster than the NAV could, because the NAV was anchored to bonds nobody was actually trading at those marks. The mainstream post-mortem leans toward the second reading: the ETF was a price-discovery tool doing its job in a market where the underlying had seized up. Either way, the practical lesson stands: **in a true liquidity crunch, the ETF price is what you can actually get, and it can be well below the comfortable NAV number.**

It's worth dwelling on why this is structurally inevitable for bond ETFs and not for stock ETFs. Stocks trade continuously on exchanges; a stock ETF's NAV is built from live, real prices, so the arbitrage that pins price to NAV always has an up-to-the-second NAV to pin to. Bonds are different: most individual bonds trade infrequently — a given corporate bond might change hands a few times a *week* — so the "market prices" used to compute a bond ETF's NAV are often estimates from pricing services, sometimes hours or a day stale. In calm markets that staleness is harmless. In a fast selloff, the staleness becomes a gap: the live ETF price is moving in real time while the NAV lags behind reality. The discount, then, is partly an illusion of measurement — the NAV is too high, not the ETF too low. This is why regulators and the ETF industry generally concluded that bond ETFs *performed their function* in March 2020, even as headlines screamed that they had failed. The honest summary: a bond ETF's price is a faster, more tradable truth than its NAV, and the gap between them is a thermometer for how frozen the underlying market is.

#### Worked example: selling a bond ETF at a discount in a panic

You own \$100,000 of a corporate-bond ETF. Its stated NAV is \$100,000, but the market is in crisis and the bonds inside have stopped trading. The ETF is quoting at a 4% discount — buyers will only pay \$96,000. If you *must* sell into that panic, you get \$96,000, not \$100,000; you've realized the discount as a real loss. If you can wait a few days for liquidity to return, the discount closes and you're back near \$100,000. The discount is a tax on selling into illiquidity — the same kind of tax an individual-bond holder pays as a wide bid-ask spread, just made visible as a price gap. *An ETF's premium/discount is the price of immediacy: in calm markets it's near zero, but in a freeze it can be several percent, and you pay it only if you insist on transacting right now.*

## Liquidity, diversification, and cost: the three real trade-offs

Strip away the drama and the choice between an individual bond and a fund comes down to three practical axes, plus the maturity-certainty axis we've already covered. Let's take them one at a time, because the popular intuition is wrong on at least one of them.

**Diversification.** This one favors the fund decisively, and it's the strongest argument for funds. An individual corporate bond is a bet on *one* issuer. If that company defaults and recovers only 40 cents on the dollar, you lose 60% of that position — a catastrophe if it was a big slice of your money. A fund holding 500 bonds barely notices a single default; one name going to zero costs the fund 0.2% of NAV. For Treasuries this matters little (the US government isn't going to default on a single bond), but for *corporate* or *municipal* or *emerging-market* bonds, where default is a live possibility, diversification is the difference between a survivable setback and a ruinous one. To get the same diversification with individual bonds, you'd need to buy dozens of them — tens of thousands of dollars each — which most investors simply can't afford. We cover how default losses actually work in [seniority, recovery, and the capital structure](/blog/trading/fixed-income/seniority-recovery-and-the-capital-structure).

The diversification gap is starkest exactly where it matters most: in high-yield (junk) bonds, where annual default rates can run several percent in a recession. A single junk bond is a coin-flip-ish bet that can lose you 50–70% if it goes bad; a junk-bond fund spreads that across hundreds of names, turning the gamble into a manageable, statistical drag. The lower the credit quality, the more the fund's diversification is worth. At the top of the quality ladder — US Treasuries — diversification is nearly free anyway (there's one issuer and it's risk-free), so the fund's advantage there is mostly about convenience and liquidity, not credit safety. This is the rule of thumb to carry: *the riskier the credit, the more a fund's diversification earns its fee; the safer the credit, the less it matters.*

**Liquidity.** This favors the fund too, and more than people expect. An ETF trades all day with a tight bid-ask spread; a mutual fund redeems at the daily NAV with no spread at all. An individual bond, by contrast, is genuinely hard to sell. Outside of Treasuries, the corporate-bond market is a dealer market with wide spreads and thin volume; selling a \$25,000 lot of an obscure bond can cost you a full percent or more in spread, and finding a buyer at all can take days. The catch — and this is the subtlety March 2020 taught — is that a bond ETF's liquidity is *better than its holdings' liquidity in calm times and can be worse-feeling in a crisis* (the discount). But day to day, for the ordinary investor, a fund is far easier to get into and out of than a pile of individual bonds.

**Cost.** This is the one axis where the individual bond wins, and it's why funds aren't free money. A fund charges an expense ratio — every year, forever, whether the fund went up or down. An individual bond has no ongoing fee: once you own it, you own it. The individual bond *does* cost you a bid-ask spread when you buy (and again if you sell early), but that's a one-time hit, not an annual drip. Over a long holding period, the fee compounds: a 0.50% expense ratio on a \$100,000 holding is \$500 a year, which over 20 years (with compounding) quietly eats roughly \$12,000–\$15,000. For cheap index ETFs at 0.05%, the fee is almost negligible (\$50 a year); for expensive active funds, it's a real drag.

![Individual bonds versus a fund or ETF compared across maturity certainty, diversification, liquidity, cost, and control, showing the individual bond winning on known maturity and full control while the fund wins on diversification and liquidity at the cost of an annual fee](/imgs/blogs/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds-6.png)

*An individual bond and a fund sit on opposite sides of every axis: the choice is a set of explicit trade-offs, not a winner and a loser.*

The matrix above is the whole decision on one screen. Read it as a set of trades, not a scoreboard. The individual bond gives you a *known maturity and payout* and *full control* over exactly what you hold, at the price of *poor diversification* and *clunky trading*. The fund flips every one of those: *strong diversification* and *easy liquidity*, at the price of *no maturity date* and a *small annual fee*. There is no universally right answer — there is the answer that fits what you're trying to do.

#### Worked example: the fee drag versus the spread, over 20 years

You're choosing between a single \$100,000 bond (no annual fee, but a 0.5% bid-ask spread to buy, costing \$500 once) and a bond fund charging a 0.20% expense ratio. The bond costs you \$500 up front and nothing after. The fund costs you nothing up front but \$200 in year one, and a bit more each year as the balance grows — call it roughly \$200–\$300 a year, about \$5,000 of fees over 20 years. So the fund is the more expensive option *if you'd hold the same bond to maturity anyway* and never need to trade. But if you'd otherwise have to sell that bond early (another 0.5% spread, \$500), or if you value the diversification and the ability to sell any day, the fund's \$5,000 over two decades buys real services. *The fee is the price of the fund's conveniences; whether it's worth it depends entirely on whether you'd use those conveniences.*

## The bridge: a bond ladder gives you both, by hand

If the choice feels like an unfair "pick your poison," there's a middle path that has existed for as long as bonds have: the **bond ladder**. A ladder is simply a row of individual bonds with staggered maturities — say one maturing each year for the next ten years. Each rung is a single bond with its own pull-to-par; together they give you a steady stream of maturity dates, so every year one bond matures and hands you par. You then reinvest that maturing principal into a new bond at the far end of the ladder (a new 10-year rung), keeping the structure rolling.

A ladder is, in effect, a do-it-yourself bond fund that *keeps* the pull-to-par. You get a measure of diversification (across maturities, and across issuers if you spread the rungs), you get a predictable annual stream of maturing cash you can spend or reinvest, and crucially, you never have to *sell* a bond into a bad market — you just wait for the next rung to mature. When rates rise, you're not stuck: the bonds maturing each year hand you cash to reinvest at the new, higher rates, so a ladder automatically captures rising rates without realizing losses. When rates fall, your longer rungs have already locked in the old higher coupons. The ladder smooths the reinvestment timing that a single bond gets wrong.

The cost of a ladder is effort and minimum size. Building a diversified ten-rung ladder of corporate bonds, with enough issuers per rung to be safe, can take dozens of bonds and hundreds of thousands of dollars — which is exactly the diversification problem a fund solves for a tiny fee. That tension is why the market invented defined-maturity ETFs (covered below): a way to buy each "rung" as a diversified, single-ticker fund that still matures and returns par in a target year. For most ordinary investors, a ladder of Treasuries (where you don't need issuer diversification, because there's only one issuer and it's the safest in the world) is the cleanest way to get a fund-like income stream while keeping the maturity-date guarantee.

#### Worked example: a five-rung Treasury ladder versus a fund

You have \$100,000 and want income plus the safety of maturity dates. *Ladder:* you buy five \$20,000 Treasuries maturing in one, two, three, four, and five years. Each year, the nearest one matures, hands you \$20,000 of par, and you reinvest it into a new 5-year Treasury at whatever rate prevails — so within five years your whole ladder is rolling at current rates. If rates rose to 5%, you're capturing 5% within a few years, with no realized loss, because you let bonds mature rather than selling them. *Fund:* you put \$100,000 in a short-Treasury fund; its NAV dips when rates rise and recovers as the fund rolls, and you can sell any day, but there's no maturity to hand you a fixed amount on a fixed date. *A ladder buys the pull-to-par and the rising-rate capture of individual bonds while spreading reinvestment across several dates — at the cost of the hands-on work a fund does for you for a few basis points a year.*

## A practical wrinkle: taxes and which account holds what

One more axis that quietly tips real-world decisions: taxes. The mechanics differ between the wrappers in ways worth knowing. An individual bond's coupons are taxed as ordinary income each year as you receive them; if you buy below par and hold to maturity, the pull-to-par gain may be taxed as well, on a schedule set by tax rules. A **municipal bond** — debt issued by a US state or city — pays coupons that are typically exempt from federal income tax, which is its whole appeal, covered in [municipal bonds, tax-free income](/blog/trading/fixed-income/municipal-bonds-tax-free-income-and-the-muni-market). With individual bonds you also control *when* you realize gains and losses: you can sell a loser to harvest a tax loss, or hold a winner to defer the gain, because you own specific lots.

A fund passes through the same coupon income as taxable distributions, but it also passes through the *fund's* realized capital gains — including gains the manager triggered by trading inside the basket, which you didn't choose and can't control. In a year where the fund did a lot of rolling, you can owe tax on gains even if your own shares are down. ETFs are generally more tax-efficient than mutual funds here, because the in-kind creation/redemption mechanism lets them flush low-basis bonds out without triggering taxable gains for shareholders — a structural advantage of the ETF wrapper that has nothing to do with bonds specifically. None of this changes the maturity story at the heart of the post, but it's a real reason a given investor might prefer individual munis in a taxable account and a cheap ETF in a tax-sheltered retirement account. (This is educational, not tax advice; the rules are intricate and depend on your situation.)

## Putting it all together: the \$100,000 head-to-head through a +2% year

Now we can run the full comparison the post has been building toward: \$100,000 in a single 10-year bond versus \$100,000 in a 10-year-duration fund, both taken through a year in which rates rise 2 percentage points.

![The running example of one hundred thousand dollars in a ten year bond versus a ten year duration fund through a year of rates rising two percent, showing both fall about twenty thousand dollars on paper but the bond returns to par at maturity while the fund recovers only if rates fall again](/imgs/blogs/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds-7.png)

*After the same +2% year, both fall roughly the same on paper, but only the bond is contractually pulled back to par by a maturity date.*

Read the matrix row by row. **After the +2% year**, both are marked down by roughly duration × 2%: the bond to ~\$83,000, the fund to ~\$80,000 — essentially the same hit, because they hold the same kind of risk. **Coupons** keep flowing on both: ~\$4,000 a year, and the fund's distributions even rise over time as it rolls into higher-yielding bonds. **The paper loss** is about the same on both — \$17,000–\$20,000, unrealized, marked to market. **If you must sell now**, you realize that loss on either one — identical outcomes, because in a forced sale there's no difference between selling a bond and selling a fund share. The divergence is only in the last row: **if you wait it out**, the bond climbs back to the full \$100,000 at maturity, every coupon paid, no loss (absent default) — while the fund recovers *only if rates fall again*, helped slowly by its rising coupon income, with no date that guarantees it.

That last row is the entire decision. The individual bond hands you a *guarantee* (par at maturity, absent default) in exchange for the things a fund does better (diversification, liquidity, small size). The fund hands you those conveniences in exchange for giving up the guarantee. If your need for the \$100,000 is tied to a specific date — a tuition bill, a house down payment, a pension liability — the individual bond's maturity date is genuinely valuable, because it lets you *ignore* the interim swings. This is exactly why pensions and insurers buy individual bonds and match their maturities to their obligations, a technique covered in [immunization and duration matching](/blog/trading/fixed-income/immunization-and-duration-matching-how-pensions-and-insurers-hedge). If your need is open-ended and you value diversification and the ability to trade, the fund's structure fits better.

#### Worked example: a tuition bill in exactly nine years

You'll owe \$100,000 of tuition in exactly nine years. *Option A:* buy a single high-quality 9-year bond for \$100,000 today. Whatever rates do over those nine years, the bond matures right when you need the money and pays you par — the interim price swings are irrelevant, because you're not selling. You've *immunized* the liability with a maturity date. *Option B:* put \$100,000 in a 9-year-duration bond fund. If rates happen to be high in year nine (NAV depressed) right when you need to sell, you could be short of \$100,000 — the fund has no maturity to guarantee the amount on your date. For a *dated* liability, the individual bond's maturity is the feature that matters most. *When you need a specific number on a specific date, a maturity date is worth more than diversification — which is why bond ladders and matched portfolios, not funds, are the classic tool for liability matching.* (A **bond ladder** is a row of individual bonds maturing in consecutive years, giving you a steady stream of maturity dates — the do-it-yourself way to get a fund's diversification while keeping the pull-to-par guarantee.)

## Common misconceptions

**"Bond funds lost money in 2022 because they're badly run — individual bonds were safe."** This is the most common and most misleading take. The individual bond was *not* safe from the rate move; it was marked down by exactly the same duration arithmetic — a 10-year bond fell ~15–20% in price right alongside the fund. The only difference is that the individual bond has a maturity date that *eventually* pulls the price back to par, so if you held to maturity you never *realized* the loss. The fund had the identical paper loss; it just shows it to you every day and has no maturity to erase it. The risk was the same; the wrapper changed whether you were forced to look.

**"A bond fund will eventually 'mature' and pay me back."** It won't. There is no maturity date for a standard bond fund or ETF — the manager rolls the portfolio forever. Your only exit is to *sell your shares* at whatever the NAV is that day. (The one exception is a small category of **defined-maturity** or **target-date bond ETFs**, which hold bonds all maturing in the same year and then liquidate — these deliberately give a fund a pull-to-par-like maturity. They are the exception that proves the rule.)

**"An ETF's price is always its NAV."** Usually, but not in stress. The arbitrage that keeps an ETF's market price near its NAV depends on the underlying bonds being tradable. When the bond market freezes — March 2020 being the textbook case — the ETF can trade at a several-percent discount to its stated NAV. If you're forced to sell into that, the discount is a real cost. The flip side: many analysts argue the ETF price was the *more accurate* number, discovering the real tradable value faster than a NAV built from stale bond marks.

**"Individual bonds have no risk if I hold to maturity."** They have no *interest-rate* risk to your final payout if held to maturity — but they still have **credit risk** (the issuer can default, and then you don't get par) and **inflation risk** (your fixed \$100,000 buys less in ten years if prices rise). The pull to par is a promise from the *issuer*, only as good as the issuer's solvency. For a US Treasury that's effectively ironclad; for a single corporate or muni bond, holding to maturity concentrates you in one issuer's ability to pay — which is exactly the risk diversification (a fund) is designed to spread.

**"Funds are more expensive, so individual bonds are cheaper."** Funds charge a visible annual fee; individual bonds charge an invisible bid-ask spread you pay when you trade. For a buy-and-hold-to-maturity Treasury investor, the bond is genuinely cheaper. But for a corporate or muni investor, the bid-ask spread on individual bonds is *wide* — often 0.5–1.5% per trade — and you pay it on the way in and again if you sell early. A cheap index ETF at 0.05% can easily be cheaper *and* better-diversified *and* more liquid than assembling the same exposure yourself. "Cheaper" depends entirely on what you're buying and how often you trade.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2022 bond bear market.** This is the defining recent example of the whole post. Over 2022, the Federal Reserve raised its policy rate from near zero to over 4% to fight inflation, and the 10-year Treasury yield rose from roughly 1.5% to roughly 3.9%. Long-duration bond funds — those holding 20-to-30-year Treasuries, with durations near 17 — fell roughly 30%, their worst year on record by a wide margin. The popular reaction was outrage at bond funds. But an investor who had instead bought a single 30-year Treasury at the start of 2022 had the *same* ~30% paper loss; they just had a maturity date 30 years out promising eventual par, so they could tell themselves the loss wasn't "real." The episode is the cleanest illustration that the duration risk is identical across the two wrappers — the maturity date changes only whether you're forced to realize it. (The macro story of why rates rose is in [what moves the yield curve](/blog/trading/fixed-income/what-moves-the-yield-curve-the-fed-growth-inflation-and-supply).)

**The March 2020 bond-ETF discount.** When COVID hit, the corporate-bond market seized up — dealers stopped making markets, and many bonds simply stopped trading. Large, liquid bond ETFs kept trading on the exchange, and their prices fell several percent *below* their stated NAVs — discounts of 4–5% on investment-grade corporate ETFs, and wider on high-yield. Critics said the ETFs "broke." Most post-mortems concluded the opposite: the ETFs were *price-discovery vehicles*, showing the real, tradable value of a frozen underlying market faster than the NAV could. The discounts vanished within weeks once the Fed announced it would buy corporate bonds and bond ETFs directly — the first time in history the Fed bought ETFs. The lesson: an ETF's liquidity is real but it is liquidity *for the wrapper*, and in a true freeze the price you can actually get may sit below the comfortable NAV. This is the practical edge case every bond-ETF holder should know exists.

**The SVB collapse and held-to-maturity accounting (2023).** Silicon Valley Bank held a large portfolio of long-duration Treasuries and mortgage bonds, bought when rates were near zero. When rates rose in 2022, those bonds fell sharply in market value — but the bank classified many of them as **held-to-maturity**, an accounting category that lets you carry a bond at cost (not market value) precisely because you intend to hold it to maturity and collect par. On paper, the bank looked fine; in reality it was sitting on a huge unrealized loss. When depositors fled and the bank was *forced* to sell those bonds before maturity, the paper losses became real and instantly, and the bank failed. This is the dark side of "hold to maturity erases the loss": the pull to par only works if you are *never forced to sell early* — and a forced seller (a bank facing a run, an investor facing an emergency) loses the maturity-date guarantee entirely. The full episode is in [SVB and Credit Suisse, the 2023 bank runs](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs).

**Defined-maturity (target-date) bond ETFs.** A small but growing category — funds that hold a basket of bonds all maturing in, say, 2030, then liquidate and return cash to holders that year. These were explicitly engineered to give a fund a *pull to par*: by holding bonds to a common maturity, the fund's value is dragged toward par as the target date approaches, just like a single bond, while still offering diversification across many issuers. They're the market's answer to "I want the diversification of a fund *and* the maturity certainty of a bond" — useful for building a diversified bond ladder for a dated goal. Their existence is the clearest proof that the maturity-versus-no-maturity distinction is the heart of the whole comparison: the only way to give a fund a pull to par is to give it a maturity date.

**Why pensions and insurers buy individual bonds.** Large institutions with *dated liabilities* — a pension that owes specific payments in specific years, an insurer that owes claims on a schedule — overwhelmingly hold individual bonds (or matched portfolios), not bond funds, for the core of their books. The reason is exactly the tuition example scaled up: they need a known amount on a known date, and a maturity date delivers that with certainty that a floating NAV cannot. This is the discipline of **immunization** — matching the duration and the maturity of your assets to your liabilities so that interim rate swings don't threaten your ability to pay on time. It's why the most sophisticated bond investors in the world often choose the "old-fashioned" individual bond over the convenient fund: when you have a date, the maturity date is the most valuable feature there is.

## When this matters to you, and where to go next

The practical takeaway is simple to state and easy to get wrong: **choose the wrapper by your failure mode, not by last year's return.** If you have a specific dollar amount you'll need on a specific date — a down payment, a tuition bill, a known liability — an individual bond (or a ladder of them, or a defined-maturity ETF) gives you a maturity date that lets you ignore the interim swings entirely, as long as you hold a credit you trust and won't be forced to sell early. If your need is open-ended, your amounts are modest, and you value diversification and the ability to trade any day, a low-cost bond fund or ETF gives you all of that for a small annual fee, at the cost of giving up the maturity-date guarantee.

The single mental model to keep: an individual bond is *pulled home to par by a deadline*; a fund *floats with rates forever*. Everything else — NAV, the 2022 losses, the March 2020 discount, expense ratios, premiums — falls out of that one difference. Both hold the same duration risk. Only one of them has a date that promises to make you whole.
