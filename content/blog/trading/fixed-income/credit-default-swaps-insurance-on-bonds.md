> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — a credit default swap (CDS) is insurance on a bond: the protection buyer pays a small periodic premium, and the protection seller pays out a large lump sum only if the borrower defaults — letting you trade a borrower's credit risk directly, without ever touching the bond.
> - A CDS has **two legs**: the **premium leg** (steady payments the buyer makes, quoted as a *spread* in basis points per year on the notional) and the **protection leg** (the single contingent payment the seller makes if a *credit event* — default, failure to pay, or restructuring — occurs).
> - The payout on default is roughly **(1 − recovery rate) × notional**. Buy \$10,000,000 of 5-year protection on Northwind at a 200 bp spread, and a default at 40% recovery pays you **\$6,000,000** against premiums of \$200,000 a year.
> - The **CDS spread** tracks the bond's **cash credit spread** almost step for step — they price the same default risk — and the small persistent gap between them is the **CDS-bond basis**.
> - You can use CDS to **hedge a bond you own**, to **short credit** without borrowing the bond, or to **express a view** on a borrower; there are **single-name** contracts and **index** contracts (CDX, iTraxx) that trade a whole basket at once.
> - The catch is **counterparty risk** — your insurance is only as good as the seller's ability to pay. In 2008, AIG sold protection it could not honor, and the cascade forced a ~\$182 billion federal rescue.

Imagine you own a corporate bond — a loan you made to a company called Northwind — and you lie awake worried that Northwind might go bankrupt and never pay you back. Now imagine you could buy an insurance policy on exactly that: you pay a small premium each year, and if Northwind defaults, the insurer hands you a check that covers your loss. You'd still hold the bond, still collect the coupons, but the nightmare scenario would be someone else's problem. That insurance policy exists. It's called a **credit default swap**, and it is one of the most important — and most misunderstood — instruments in all of finance.

The strange and powerful thing about a CDS is that you don't have to own the bond to buy the insurance. You can buy protection on Northwind even if you've never lent Northwind a cent — the same way you could (in principle) buy fire insurance on a stranger's house. That single fact turns a hedging tool into something far bigger: a way to *trade credit risk directly*, to bet for or against a company's survival, to short a bond you can't even borrow. It is what lets the market put a clean, continuous price on the question "how likely is this borrower to default, and how much would I lose if it did?"

![A credit default swap shown as insurance on a bond, with the protection buyer paying a periodic premium to the protection seller, who pays out a large lump sum only if the reference borrower defaults](/imgs/blogs/credit-default-swaps-insurance-on-bonds-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model to carry through the whole post. On the left, a **protection buyer** owns (or is betting against) Northwind's debt and wants insurance. They pay a steady premium — quoted as a *spread*, here 200 basis points a year on a \$10,000,000 notional — to a **protection seller**, usually a bank or an insurer, who collects that premium and takes on the credit risk. The question mark in the middle is the **credit event**: does Northwind default before the contract matures? If it does, the seller pays out — roughly the bond's loss, here \$6,000,000. If it doesn't, the seller keeps every premium and pays nothing. That asymmetry — small steady payments one way, one big contingent payment the other — is the whole machine. The rest of this post is about how each piece works, how the price is set, what you can do with it, and how the same elegant contract became a load-bearing wall in the 2008 financial crisis.

## Foundations: bonds, credit risk, and what "insurance on a loan" means

Before any of the machinery makes sense, we need a handful of terms, all built from zero. None are hard, but the CDS lives in the relationships between them.

A **bond** is a tradable loan. You hand a borrower money today, and in return you receive a fixed stream of **coupon** payments (the interest) plus the return of your **principal** — the amount you lent — at the end, called **maturity**. The borrower is the **issuer**. A **US Treasury** is a bond issued by the US government, treated as the closest thing to risk-free because the government can always print dollars to repay dollar debts. A **corporate bond**, like our fictional Northwind's, is a loan to a company, and a company can go bust.

That possibility — that the borrower won't pay you back in full and on time — is **credit risk** (also called *default risk*). It is the central thing a CDS is about. When a borrower can't meet its obligations, we say it **defaults**. Default rarely means you lose everything: in a bankruptcy, the company's assets get sold and creditors are paid back something. The fraction you get back is the **recovery rate**. If a bond recovers 40 cents on the dollar, the recovery rate is 40%, and your **loss given default** is the other 60%.

A **basis point** — written *bp* or *bps* — is one hundredth of a percent, 0.01%. Bond and credit people quote everything in basis points: a "200 bp spread" is 2.00%, and a move from 200 to 210 bps is "10 bps wider." A **spread**, in credit, is the *extra* yield a risky bond pays over a safe one of the same maturity. If a 5-year Treasury yields 4.0% and Northwind's 5-year bond yields 6.0%, Northwind's **credit spread** is 2.0%, or 200 bps — that 200 bps is the market's price for taking Northwind's default risk. Hold that number; it will come back.

The **notional** is the face amount the contract is written on — the size of the position. A CDS on a \$10,000,000 notional behaves as if it's insuring \$10,000,000 of Northwind debt, whether or not the buyer actually owns that much (or any).

Now, the word "swap." A **swap** is just a contract where two parties exchange streams of payments. In a CDS, the two streams are deeply lopsided: one party pays a small, regular premium; the other pays a large amount *only if* a specific bad event happens. It is structurally identical to an insurance policy — premiums in, a claim paid on a covered loss — which is why "insurance on a bond" is not a loose analogy but a precise description. The one twist that separates a CDS from ordinary insurance is that you needn't own the thing you're insuring. We'll return to why that matters enormously.

The party that pays the premium and receives protection is the **protection buyer**. The party that collects the premium and owes the payout is the **protection seller** — they are *short* the credit risk in the sense that they lose if the borrower deteriorates. The specific borrower the contract is written on is the **reference entity**, and the specific bond used to settle and to define a default is the **reference obligation**.

That last twist — that you needn't own what you're insuring — is worth pausing on, because it's the single fact that makes the CDS market what it is. Ordinary insurance forbids it: you can't buy fire insurance on a stranger's house, precisely because doing so would give you an incentive to see it burn (insurers call this "insurable interest"). A CDS deliberately removes that restriction. You can buy protection on a company you've never lent to, or sell protection on a company you'll never owe a cent. This is what turns the contract from a quiet bilateral insurance arrangement into a *market* — a place where credit risk is priced continuously by anyone with a view, where the total amount of protection outstanding can exceed the bonds that exist, and where a company's CDS spread becomes a public, real-time referendum on its survival. It is also, as we'll see, the property that let bets on subprime mortgages balloon far past the mortgages themselves in the run-up to 2008. The same feature that makes the market liquid and informative is the one that lets it amplify a shock.

#### Worked example: pricing the default risk without any derivative

Before we touch a CDS, let's see the credit spread it's built on. Suppose the 5-year Treasury yields **4.0%** and Northwind's 5-year bond yields **6.0%**. The credit spread is 6.0% − 4.0% = 2.0%, or **200 bps** — that 200 bps is everything the market is charging you to take on Northwind instead of the government. On a \$10,000,000 holding, that spread is worth 2.0% × \$10,000,000 = \$200,000 of *extra* yield per year — the bondholder's annual compensation for carrying Northwind's default risk. Put differently, if Northwind never defaults, you pocket an extra \$200,000 a year above what a Treasury would have paid; that bonus is your reward for being willing to lose money if it does.

Here is the key intuition that makes CDS click: that \$200,000 a year is *the price of insuring Northwind*. The yield premium and the insurance premium are two names for the same thing. If someone else would carry the default risk for you in exchange for roughly that same \$200,000 a year, you'd be left holding a near-risk-free Treasury-like return — you'd have transferred the dangerous part of the bond to them while keeping the safe part. That is exactly what a CDS does — and exactly why a CDS on Northwind tends to trade near **200 bps**. The whole derivative is just this insurance premium, peeled off the bond and given a market of its own.

*A credit spread is already an insurance premium hiding inside the bond's yield; a CDS just pulls that premium out and lets it trade on its own.*

## The two legs: premium in, protection out

Every CDS is two cash-flow streams running in opposite directions. Getting these straight is most of the battle.

The **premium leg** is what the protection buyer pays. It's quoted as the **CDS spread** — an annual rate in basis points applied to the notional — and paid in installments, almost always **quarterly**, on the standardized dates of March, June, September, and December 20th. (Standard dates matter: they let contracts written on different days net cleanly against each other, which is part of what makes the market liquid.) Our Northwind contract at a 200 bp spread on \$10,000,000 notional means 2.00% × \$10,000,000 = \$200,000 per year, split into four payments of \$50,000. The buyer keeps paying these until the contract matures *or* a credit event happens — whichever comes first. If Northwind defaults in year three, the premium leg stops there; the buyer doesn't pay for the years that never came. That "pay until it breaks, then stop" structure is exactly how an insurance premium works — you stop paying the moment the loss event you were insuring against occurs.

In modern CDS there's a small accounting wrinkle worth knowing, because it trips people up. Standardized contracts trade at **fixed coupons** (typically 100 bps for investment-grade names, 500 bps for high-yield), with the difference between the fixed coupon and the true market spread settled as an **upfront payment** at the start. So a contract whose fair spread is 200 bps but whose fixed coupon is 100 bps would have the buyer pay an upfront amount plus the 100 bp running coupon. This is plumbing, not economics — the total cost still works out to roughly the 200 bp spread — but it's why you'll see CDS quoted with both a "spread" and an "upfront." Throughout this post we'll keep things clean and talk in terms of the all-in spread.

The **protection leg** (also called the **contingent leg**) is what the protection seller pays — but only if a **credit event** fires. There is exactly one payment, and it lands once, on default. Its size is built to replace the bondholder's loss:

$$
\text{Protection payout} = (1 - R) \times \text{Notional}
$$

where $R$ is the recovery rate — the fraction of face value the defaulted bond is worth — and the notional is the contract size. The term $(1 - R)$ is the loss given default. At 40% recovery on our \$10,000,000 contract, the payout is (1 − 0.40) × \$10,000,000 = \$6,000,000. The logic is precise: the bond fell from \$10,000,000 of face value to \$4,000,000 of recovery value, a \$6,000,000 loss, and the protection leg pays exactly that loss. Insurance that pays your actual damages, no more and no less.

![A cash-flow timeline of a credit default swap showing steady quarterly premium payments out and a single large contingent protection payment in when the borrower defaults](/imgs/blogs/credit-default-swaps-insurance-on-bonds-2.png)

The timeline above shows the asymmetry that defines the instrument. Reading left to right: the contract starts, and the premium leg begins as a steady drip of small outflows — \$50,000 a quarter, \$200,000 a year. For two years nothing else happens; the buyer just pays. Then in year three Northwind defaults: the premium leg stops cold, and the protection leg fires exactly once with a single large inflow of \$6,000,000. Many small payments out, one big payment in. That shape — limited, known cost versus a large, contingent payoff — is why a CDS feels like insurance and behaves like an option on a company's survival.

What actually counts as a **credit event** is defined precisely by the contract (the standard ISDA documentation), because billions can hinge on whether one occurred. The three classic triggers are:

- **Bankruptcy** — the reference entity files for or is forced into insolvency.
- **Failure to pay** — it misses a required payment (above a minimum threshold) after any grace period.
- **Restructuring** — the terms of its debt are changed to creditors' detriment (maturity extended, coupon cut, principal reduced). Restructuring is the messy one; some contracts (especially US ones) exclude it, which is why you'll see "XR" (no restructuring) and "MR/MMR" (modified restructuring) variants.

Whether an event has occurred is not left to the two parties to argue. A **Determinations Committee** of dealers and investors votes on it, and settlement details are standardized so the market doesn't dissolve into litigation every time a company stumbles.

#### Worked example: the premium leg, payment by payment

Let's walk the premium leg in dollars. You buy \$10,000,000 of 5-year Northwind protection at a **200 bp** spread. Annual premium: 2.00% × \$10,000,000 = \$200,000. Paid quarterly, that's \$200,000 ÷ 4 = \$50,000 every quarter, on each of the four standard payment dates.

If Northwind survives all five years, you make 5 × \$200,000 = \$1,000,000 in total premiums and receive nothing — your insurance simply expired unused, like five years of fire insurance on a house that didn't burn. That \$1,000,000 is the most you can lose on the trade, and you knew it on day one; the cost of protection is bounded and fully predictable. If instead Northwind defaults exactly halfway through year three, you'll have paid roughly 2.5 × \$200,000 = \$500,000 in premiums before the leg stopped. (In practice there's also a small *accrued premium* settlement for the partial final quarter — you owe the few weeks of premium between the last payment date and the default — but \$500,000 is the clean figure to carry.)

*The premium leg is the running cost of the insurance; the longer the borrower survives, the more you pay for protection you never collect on.*

#### Worked example: the protection leg and the net result on default

Now the payoff. Northwind defaults at the half-year mark of year three. Its bonds, post-default, trade at **40 cents on the dollar** — recovery $R = 40\%$. The protection leg pays:

$$
(1 - 0.40) \times \$10{,}000{,}000 = 0.60 \times \$10{,}000{,}000 = \$6{,}000{,}000
$$

Against that, you paid about \$500,000 in premiums. Your **net** on the trade is \$6,000,000 − \$500,000 = \$5,500,000. If you were holding the actual Northwind bonds, that \$6,000,000 is almost exactly what the bonds *lost* (they fell from \$10,000,000 of face to \$4,000,000 of recovery value), so your insurance made you whole — the \$6,000,000 payout cancels the \$6,000,000 bond loss, and you're left having paid only the premiums for the peace of mind. If you *didn't* own the bonds, that \$5,500,000 is pure profit — you bet against Northwind and won, turning a \$500,000 stake into \$6,000,000 of payout.

*The payout is engineered to equal the bondholder's loss, which is what lets the same contract serve as a hedge for an owner and a bet for a speculator.*

There is one more wrinkle worth naming: how the payout is actually delivered. Under the older **physical settlement**, the protection buyer hands the seller a defaulted bond worth \$4,000,000 and receives the full \$10,000,000 face — netting the same \$6,000,000. Under modern **cash settlement** (now the default for most contracts), no bond changes hands: an **auction** sets the recovery price across the whole market, and the seller simply pays (1 − R) × notional in cash. The auction matters for a subtle but important reason — there are often *more* CDS contracts outstanding than there are bonds to deliver. If every protection buyer had to source a physical bond to hand over, they'd bid the price of the scarce defaulted bonds *up*, perversely shrinking the payout. The cash auction fixes a single market-wide recovery price so everyone settles on the same terms, no scramble for bonds required. We'll meet this "more contracts than bonds" problem again, in a much darker form, with AIG.

## How the price is set: the CDS spread and the cash spread

A CDS isn't priced by a committee; it trades, and its price is the **CDS spread** — the premium rate, in basis points, that makes the deal fair at that moment. When traders say "Northwind 5-year is at 200," they mean the spread is 200 bps a year. When the market gets more worried about Northwind, that spread *widens* (rises); when worry fades, it *tightens* (falls). The spread is a clean, continuously-quoted, real-time gauge of how likely the market thinks default is.

What anchors the spread to reality is a near-arbitrage with the bond market. Remember the credit spread baked into Northwind's bond yield — 200 bps of extra yield for carrying default risk? The CDS spread is the price of *shedding* that same risk. If the two diverged far, you could profit risk-free: buy the bond (collect 200 bps of credit spread) and buy protection (pay less than 200 bps), pocketing the difference while bearing no default risk. Arbitrageurs doing exactly that drag the two spreads together. So the CDS spread and the bond's cash credit spread should — and do — move almost in lockstep.

![A line chart showing the CDS spread and the bond's cash credit spread rising and falling together over time, with the small persistent gap between them labeled as the CDS-bond basis](/imgs/blogs/credit-default-swaps-insurance-on-bonds-3.png)

The chart above is the influence picture for this post: over time, as a credit scare flares and then fades, the **CDS spread** (solid line) and the **bond's cash credit spread** (dashed line) trace nearly the same path — both measure the same default risk, so they widen together and tighten together. The reason to draw them on one axis is the small, persistent gap between them. That gap has a name: the **CDS-bond basis**, defined as the CDS spread minus the cash spread. When the CDS trades a little *above* the cash spread, the basis is **positive**; when it trades below, the basis is **negative**.

The basis is usually small but rarely zero, because the arbitrage that would close it isn't free. Buying the bond ties up cash and balance sheet; borrowing to do it costs money; the bond can be hard to source or to short; CDS documentation covers events (like restructuring) that the cash bond doesn't. Those frictions leave a residual gap. In calm markets the basis is a few basis points. In a crisis — autumn 2008 most dramatically — the basis blew out massively negative: cash bonds were dumped at fire-sale yields (pushing cash spreads sky-high) while no one had the balance sheet to arbitrage them back, so CDS protection looked "cheap" relative to the bonds. The basis is, in that sense, a thermometer for how stressed the plumbing of the credit market is.

#### Worked example: reading a CDS-bond basis

Suppose Northwind's 5-year **cash credit spread** is **210 bps** (the bond yields 210 bps over the matching Treasury) while its 5-year **CDS spread** trades at **200 bps**. The basis is:

$$
\text{Basis} = \text{CDS spread} - \text{Cash spread} = 200 - 210 = -10 \text{ bps}
$$

A **−10 bp** basis. It says protection via CDS is slightly *cheaper* than the compensation you'd earn for the same risk in the cash bond. A so-called **negative-basis trade** harvests this: buy the Northwind bond (earn 210 bps over Treasuries) *and* buy CDS protection on it (pay 200 bps). You're now insured against default and still collecting 210 − 200 = 10 bps of "free" carry — about 0.10% × \$10,000,000 = \$10,000 a year on our notional, in theory near-riskless. The reason this isn't actually free money is everything the basis is made of: you've tied up cash buying the bond, you're paying to fund that purchase, and you're carrying the counterparty risk of the protection seller — frictions that, summed up, are *why* the basis was −10 bps in the first place. The market rarely hands out riskless profit; what looks like free carry is usually payment for a risk you haven't noticed yet.

*The basis is the small price the market charges for the friction of doing the arbitrage; when it gaps wide, it's signalling that the friction itself — funding, balance sheet, liquidity — has become the binding constraint.*

A spread can also be turned into a rough **default probability**, which is the deepest reason the CDS spread is so widely watched. A back-of-envelope relationship is:

$$
\text{Annual default probability} \approx \frac{\text{Spread}}{1 - R}
$$

At a 200 bp spread and 40% recovery, that's $0.0200 / 0.60 \approx 0.033$, or about a **3.3% annual** chance of default implied by the market. Compounded over the 5-year life of the contract, that works out to a cumulative default probability somewhere around 15% — the market, in effect, is saying Northwind has roughly a one-in-seven chance of failing over the next five years. (The exact version discounts each year's survival-weighted cash flows and accounts for the fact that a defaulted name can't default twice, but this captures the intuition: the spread is the loss-given-default times the default probability.) The relationship runs both ways, too — it's why the *recovery assumption* matters when you translate spreads into probabilities. The same 200 bp spread implies a *higher* default probability if you assume a *higher* recovery, because each default would pay out less, so more of them are needed to justify the same premium. Watch the spread, and you're watching the market's live, continuously-updated estimate of how likely a company is to fail — a number no rating agency, with its slow and discrete letter grades, can match for timeliness.

## Single-name versus index CDS

Everything so far has described a **single-name CDS** — one contract on one reference entity, Northwind. But the most-traded credit derivatives in the world are **index CDS**: a single contract that references a fixed *basket* of names at once.

The two big families are **CDX** (North American names, run by the index provider Markit/IHS) and **iTraxx** (European and Asian names). The flagship investment-grade contracts — **CDX IG** (125 North American investment-grade companies) and **iTraxx Europe** (125 European names, often quoted as the "Main") — let you buy or sell protection on an entire slice of the credit market in one liquid trade. There are high-yield versions too (**CDX HY**, **iTraxx Crossover**) for riskier names. The indices are reset every six months into a new "series," dropping names that have been downgraded or defaulted and adding fresh ones.

![A comparison matrix of single-name credit default swaps versus index credit default swaps across what they reference, liquidity, main use, and what happens on a default](/imgs/blogs/credit-default-swaps-insurance-on-bonds-4.png)

The matrix above lays the two side by side. A **single-name** CDS references one borrower; it's the surgical tool — hedge or short *this* specific company — but liquidity is thinner name by name and the bid-ask spread (the gap between the price you can buy and sell at) is wider, so getting in and out costs more. An **index** CDS references a fixed basket of 100–125 names; it's the most liquid credit product on earth, used to hedge or trade *market-wide* credit risk cheaply and instantly. The mechanics on a default differ too: in a single-name CDS the seller pays (1 − R) on that name; in an index, a defaulting name simply *drops out*, the seller pays (1 − R) on that one name's slice (1/125 of the notional for CDX IG, since the index holds 125 equally-weighted names), and the index continues trading on the surviving names with a slightly reduced notional. So a single default in a 125-name index is a small, contained event — about 0.8% of the basket — which is part of why indices feel so much steadier than holding any one name.

#### Worked example: buying index protection to hedge a portfolio

You run a \$125,000,000 portfolio of investment-grade corporate bonds — roughly the CDX IG basket. You're nervous about a broad credit selloff but don't want to sell 125 bonds (slow, costly, and you'd lose the yield you spent years building). Instead you buy \$125,000,000 of **CDX IG** protection, currently at a **65 bp** spread. Your cost: 0.65% × \$125,000,000 = \$812,500 a year — a small price for insuring the whole book.

If credit spreads blow out across the board — say the index spread doubles to 130 bps — the *value* of your protection jumps, roughly by the change in spread times the position's duration times the notional. With an index duration of about 4.5 years, a +65 bp move gains you about 0.65% × 4.5 × \$125,000,000 ≈ \$3,656,000 of mark-to-market — which offsets a large chunk of the loss on your bonds, whose prices fell as their spreads widened. One trade, the whole book hedged, for under \$1 million a year, and you never had to find buyers for 125 separate illiquid bonds in a falling market. That last point is the real magic: in a selloff, the cash bonds become hard to sell at any decent price exactly when you most want out, but the index stays liquid.

*An index CDS is the credit market's circuit breaker: it lets a manager dial portfolio-wide default risk up or down in a single liquid trade, instead of touching a hundred separate bonds.*

## Three things you can do with a CDS

The reason CDS matters isn't the contract — it's what the contract *unlocks*. There are three core uses, and they map onto three different kinds of market participant.

**First, hedge a bond you own.** This is the original, "insurance" use. You hold Northwind bonds, you like the coupon, but you want to neutralize the default risk. Buy matching CDS protection and your combined position becomes nearly credit-neutral: you keep the coupon, pay the premium, and if Northwind defaults the CDS payout offsets the bond's loss almost exactly.

![A before-and-after comparison showing a bond held alone exposed to a full default loss versus the same bond combined with matching credit default swap protection ending near credit-neutral](/imgs/blogs/credit-default-swaps-insurance-on-bonds-5.png)

The figure above contrasts the two states. On the left, the bond *alone*: you earn the coupon, but if Northwind defaults the bond crashes to 40 cents and you eat the full ~\$6,000,000 loss. On the right, bond *plus* CDS protection: the same default still costs the bond \$6,000,000, but the CDS pays you +\$6,000,000, so your net loss is near zero — you've kept the coupon minus the premium and shed the credit risk. The combined position behaves like a near-risk-free bond: you've stripped Northwind's default risk out and handed it to the protection seller.

**Second, short credit without borrowing the bond.** Here's where the CDS becomes radical. Suppose you think Northwind is in trouble and want to profit if it deteriorates. With the bond itself, shorting is a nightmare: you'd have to *borrow* a Northwind bond (often impossible — corporate bonds are illiquid and hard to locate), sell it, and hope to buy it back cheaper, all while paying borrow costs and posting margin. With a CDS, you simply **buy protection** on Northwind without owning any bond. If Northwind's spread widens, your protection gains value and you can sell it at a profit; if Northwind defaults, you collect the full payout. This is **shorting credit** cleanly, and it's the single biggest reason CDS exists as a *traded* market rather than a quiet insurance backwater.

**Third, express a view cheaply and precisely.** Because protection can be bought or sold without the underlying bond, a CDS lets you take a position on a borrower's creditworthiness — long or short — with far less capital than the cash market would require. Buy protection if you think a credit will worsen; sell protection (collect the premium) if you think it will hold up and you're willing to be the insurer. The precision is what professionals prize: with cash bonds, a view on a company's credit is tangled up with a view on interest rates, since a bond's price moves with both. A CDS isolates the *credit* component — it's close to a pure bet on default risk, with the rate risk stripped out. That makes it the natural instrument for **relative-value** trades, too: buy protection on the weaker of two similar companies and sell it on the stronger, profiting if the gap between them widens, with little exposure to the overall market. Hedge funds, in particular, use single-name and index CDS to bet on individual companies, on sectors, on the gap between sectors, and on the credit cycle as a whole. The flip side, of course, is leverage: because you put up only margin rather than the full notional, a view that goes wrong can cost you far more than you'd guess from the premium — the same property that makes CDS capital-efficient makes it dangerous in size.

#### Worked example: shorting a credit you don't own

You have no Northwind bonds and no way to borrow them, but you're convinced the company is deteriorating. You buy \$10,000,000 of 5-year protection at **200 bps**, costing \$200,000 a year. Six months later, Northwind reports terrible earnings and the market's fear spikes — its CDS spread widens from 200 to **400 bps**.

Your protection is now worth far more than you paid, because a new buyer would have to pay 400 bps for the same insurance you locked in at 200 — you're holding a contract that's cheap relative to where the market now prices the risk. The mark-to-market gain is roughly the spread change times the position's remaining risky duration times the notional. With about 4 years of remaining risky duration, that's 2.00% × 4 × \$10,000,000 ≈ \$800,000 of profit — against just \$100,000 of premium paid over the six months. You sell (unwind) the contract and book the gain, *without Northwind ever defaulting and without owning a single bond.* This is the crucial point that separates a CDS from ordinary insurance: you don't have to wait for the house to burn down. The protection itself trades, so a worsening of the borrower's prospects — short of any actual default — is enough to make you money.

*A CDS converts a credit opinion into a tradable position; you profit from the spread moving your way, not just from an actual default.*

## Counterparty risk: your insurance is only as good as the insurer

Here is the flaw at the heart of the elegant machine. A CDS protects you against the reference entity's default — but it introduces a *new* risk you didn't have before: the risk that **your protection seller can't pay** when the claim comes due. This is **counterparty risk**, and it's not a footnote; it's the reason CDS can amplify a crisis instead of dampening one.

Think it through. You buy protection on Northwind from a bank. Northwind defaults — exactly the scenario you insured against — and you turn to the bank for your \$6,000,000. If the bank is healthy, fine. But credit events cluster: companies don't default at random; they default in waves, during recessions and financial panics, *precisely when the banks and insurers who sold protection are themselves under maximum stress*. The very event that triggers your claim is the event most likely to have impaired the party who owes it. Insurance that fails exactly when you need it is worse than no insurance, because you paid premiums and made decisions believing you were covered.

The market has built defenses against this, and they're worth understanding because they're what stand between a single CDS failure and a chain reaction. **Collateral** (called *variation margin*) is the main one: as a CDS moves against the seller — as the reference entity's spread widens and the protection becomes more valuable — the seller must post cash collateral to the buyer, daily, to cover the growing exposure. This keeps the unpaid amount small even if the seller later fails, because most of what's owed is already sitting in the buyer's account. A second layer, **initial margin**, is a buffer posted at the start to cover the gap between the last collateral exchange and an actual default, since prices can jump faster than daily margining can keep up.

Since reforms after 2008, most standardized CDS are also **centrally cleared**: a **clearinghouse** (a central counterparty, or CCP) stands between buyer and seller, guaranteeing both sides and collecting margin from everyone, so the failure of one dealer doesn't directly take down its counterparties. The CCP novates the trade — legally, you no longer face Dealer X, you face the clearinghouse, which faces Dealer X separately — and it backs the guarantee with a mutualized **default fund** that all members contribute to. This is a genuine structural improvement: it turns a tangled web of bilateral exposures, where nobody can see who owes what to whom, into a hub-and-spoke system with a well-capitalized, transparent center. It is precisely the architecture that was *missing* in 2008.

But collateral itself has a sting in the tail — posting it *requires cash*, and demanding it from a stressed seller can be the push that topples them. That dynamic — collateral calls draining cash from a firm at the worst possible moment — is exactly what destroyed AIG, and it's a reminder that no amount of clever structuring makes credit risk disappear. It can only be moved, netted, and buffered. Someone, somewhere, is still bearing the loss when a borrower fails; the machinery just decides who, and how suddenly they find out.

#### Worked example: when the hedge isn't really a hedge

You "hedge" your \$10,000,000 Northwind bond by buying protection from Dealer X. You sleep soundly: bond loss on default would be \$6,000,000, and you're covered. Then a systemic crisis hits. Northwind defaults — and so does Dealer X, which had sold protection on dozens of names and can't honor any of them. Your claim is worth \$6,000,000 on paper; in the bankruptcy you recover, say, 10 cents on the dollar of *that*, or \$600,000.

Your realized loss: the \$6,000,000 bond loss minus the \$600,000 you actually collect = **\$5,400,000**, plus the premiums you paid for "protection." You were 90% *un*hedged at the moment it mattered. Had the contract been collateralized daily, Dealer X would have already posted most of that \$6,000,000 as the spread widened, and your loss would have been a fraction of this.

*A CDS doesn't remove credit risk; it transforms the reference entity's default risk into the seller's counterparty risk — and that's only an improvement if the seller is more reliable than the borrower, especially in a crisis.*

## The role CDS played in 2008

No instrument is more associated with the 2008 financial crisis than the credit default swap, and the reasons are worth understanding precisely, because the popular story ("derivatives blew up the world") is too vague to learn from. CDS did two distinct dangerous things in the 2000s.

**The first was AIG selling protection it could never pay on.** AIG Financial Products, a small London-based unit of the giant insurer, wrote roughly *half a trillion dollars* of CDS protection — much of it on **mortgage-backed securities** (bonds built from home loans) and the structured products on top of them. To AIG it looked like free money: collect premiums on highly-rated bonds that "would never default," post almost no capital against them. Crucially, AIG's contracts contained **collateral triggers**: if the insured securities were downgraded or their spreads widened, AIG had to post cash collateral. When US housing turned in 2007–2008 and mortgage securities collapsed, AIG faced collateral calls it could not meet — *tens of billions in cash it didn't have*. With AIG owing money to nearly every major bank on the planet, its failure threatened to cascade through the entire system, and the US government stepped in with a rescue that ultimately totaled around **\$182 billion**.

![A diagram of the 2008 credit default swap chain in which AIG sells protection on mortgage securities and synthetic collateralized debt obligations, housing falls, collateral calls overwhelm AIG, and a federal rescue follows](/imgs/blogs/credit-default-swaps-insurance-on-bonds-6.png)

The figure above traces the chain. AIG sells protection to banks (who used it to free up regulatory capital) and into **synthetic CDOs** — and that second word is the other dangerous thing CDS did. A **CDO** (collateralized debt obligation) was originally a pool of *real* bonds or loans, sliced into tranches of varying risk. A **synthetic CDO** contained no real loans at all — just a stack of CDS contracts, *bets* on whether a basket of mortgage bonds would default. Because you can write a CDS on a bond without owning it, the market could create synthetic exposure to subprime mortgages far in excess of the actual mortgages that existed. The same bad mortgage pool could be referenced by many synthetic CDOs at once. CDS let the *bet* on subprime grow vastly larger than the thing being bet on — so when subprime failed, the losses weren't capped at the size of the mortgage market; they were multiplied across every synthetic structure that had referenced it.

Put the two together and you get the crisis mechanism: when housing fell, mortgage defaults surged, the reference securities were downgraded, spreads blew out, collateral calls hit AIG, AIG couldn't pay, and the web of counterparty exposures meant AIG's failure would have detonated the banks it had insured. The contract that was supposed to *distribute* credit risk had instead *concentrated* it in one undercapitalized seller and *multiplied* it through synthetic structures.

#### Worked example: the leverage hidden in selling protection

See why selling protection felt like free money. AIG sells \$10,000,000 of protection on a mortgage security at a **50 bp** spread, posting essentially no capital. Income: 0.50% × \$10,000,000 = \$50,000 a year, for what looked like near-zero risk on an AAA-rated bond that "couldn't" default. Do that across \$500,000,000,000 of notional and you're collecting on the order of 0.50% × \$500,000,000,000 = \$2,500,000,000 — \$2.5 *billion* a year — against almost no capital set aside. To an executive reading the income statement, this is a money machine: enormous, steady premiums on assets the rating agencies swore were safe.

The catch is the downside no one budgeted for. If those securities fall and recovery is just 30%, the payout owed is (1 − 0.30) × \$500,000,000,000 = \$350,000,000,000 — \$350 billion against a firm that had set aside a rounding error. That is the tail: a number so large it can never be paid, hiding behind a stream of small premiums that looked riskless. And here is the part that actually killed AIG: it never even got to the defaults. Long before any actual default, the *collateral calls* — the daily demands to post cash as the insured securities were downgraded and their spreads widened — demanded tens of billions in cash up front. AIG had the income statement of a winner and the cash position of a firm about to fail, and the collateral clause turned a slow-motion paper loss into an immediate liquidity crisis.

*Selling protection is selling insurance against catastrophe: the premiums are steady and seductive, but the tail risk is enormous and shows up all at once — and writing it without capital is how a side bet sinks a parent company.*

## Putting the numbers together: the full Northwind CDS

Let's assemble everything into one picture — the running example, end to end, across the scenarios that matter.

![A table of the credit default swap payout math showing premiums paid versus payout received versus net result across no default and default scenarios at different recovery rates](/imgs/blogs/credit-default-swaps-insurance-on-bonds-7.png)

The table above is the whole economics of our Northwind trade on one page: \$10,000,000 of 5-year protection at a 200 bp spread, \$200,000 of premium a year. Read down the rows. If Northwind **never defaults**, you pay the full \$1,000,000 of premiums over five years and receive nothing — net −\$1,000,000, the cost of insurance you didn't need, and the *most* you can lose. If Northwind **defaults in year three at 40% recovery** (the base case), you'd paid about \$500,000 in premiums and receive \$6,000,000 — net +\$5,500,000. The bottom two rows show how recovery swings the payout: at **20% recovery** the payout is (1 − 0.20) × \$10,000,000 = \$8,000,000 (net +\$7,500,000), and at **70% recovery** it's only (1 − 0.70) × \$10,000,000 = \$3,000,000 (net +\$2,500,000). Lower recovery means a bigger payout, because the protection covers your *loss*, and a worse recovery is a bigger loss. Notice the shape of the whole table: a small, capped, known downside on the left, and a large, variable upside on the right — the exact payoff profile of buying insurance, or of buying an option. You pay a little, repeatedly, for the right to a big payment in a bad state of the world.

#### Worked example: the recovery rate is half the trade

Make the recovery sensitivity concrete. Same \$10,000,000 notional, same default in year three, but compare two worlds. In a senior, well-collateralized default the bond recovers **70%**: payout (1 − 0.70) × \$10,000,000 = \$3,000,000. In a deeply subordinated, asset-light default the bond recovers just **20%**: payout (1 − 0.20) × \$10,000,000 = \$8,000,000. Same default, same notional — but the payout differs by **\$5,000,000** purely because of recovery. Recovery, in turn, depends on what the company owns when it fails (a bank with loans recovers differently from a software firm with mostly people and goodwill) and on where the referenced bond sits in the capital structure — senior, secured debt recovers more than junior, unsecured debt. That's why a careful CDS buyer checks exactly which obligation the contract references before assuming it hedges their position.

This is why protection buyers care intensely about *which* bond and *which* seniority the contract references, and why the post-default **auction** that sets the market-wide recovery price is one of the most-watched events in the credit calendar.

*A CDS is a bet on two things at once — whether default happens and how little is recovered if it does — and the second can matter as much as the first.*

## Common misconceptions

**"A CDS is just gambling, with no real economic purpose."** The legitimate core is hedging: a bondholder, a bank, or a lender can shed credit risk they genuinely carry, exactly as a homeowner buys fire insurance. The fact that *some* participants use CDS to speculate doesn't erase that — and speculators provide the liquidity that makes hedging cheap. What's fair to say is that the *unfunded, naked* use (buying protection on credits you don't own, in size far exceeding the underlying bonds) can amplify rather than distribute risk. The tool is dual-use; 2008 was the dark side, not the whole story.

**"If I buy CDS protection, my bond is completely safe."** No — you've swapped one risk for another. You've removed the reference entity's default risk and taken on the *seller's* counterparty risk. If the seller fails when you need to claim — likely, since defaults cluster in crises — your "protection" can pay pennies. Collateral and central clearing shrink this risk a lot, but they don't make it zero.

**"The CDS spread and the bond spread are the same number."** They track each other closely because they price the same default risk, but they're not identical: the gap between them is the **CDS-bond basis**, and it can run several basis points in calm markets and blow out to dozens in a crisis. The basis is information, not noise — it reflects funding costs, balance-sheet scarcity, documentation differences, and how hard the bond is to source or short.

**"A default means you get your whole notional back."** You get (1 − R) times the notional, not the full notional. At 40% recovery you collect 60% of the notional, because the contract replaces your *loss*, and you'd have recovered the other 40% from the defaulted bond. The recovery rate, set by a post-default auction, is half the payoff and varies enormously by seniority — which is why two CDS on the same defaulted company can pay out very different amounts if they reference bonds of different seniority.

**"CDS pay out the instant a company looks shaky."** Only a defined **credit event** — bankruptcy, failure to pay, or (where included) restructuring — triggers the payout, and a **Determinations Committee** formally rules on whether one occurred. A falling stock price, a downgrade, or a scary headline does *not* trigger settlement. What those *do* move is the CDS spread (and thus the mark-to-market value of an existing contract) — which is how you profit from deterioration short of default, by selling the now-more-valuable protection.

**"Only banks and exotic funds touch CDS, so it doesn't affect me."** The credit conditions CDS spreads reflect — and sometimes drive — feed directly into what companies pay to borrow, which feeds into hiring, investment, and the broader economy. And in 2008, the failure of one CDS seller (AIG) was deemed serious enough to warrant \$182 billion of public money. CDS is plumbing; when it backs up, everyone gets wet.

## How it shows up in real markets

**AIG, 2008 — the canonical counterparty blow-up.** AIG Financial Products had written roughly \$500 billion of CDS protection, much of it on mortgage-linked securities, treating the premiums as nearly free income against AAA paper. The contracts' collateral triggers turned a paper problem into a cash crisis: as mortgage securities were downgraded and spreads widened through 2008, AIG owed tens of billions in collateral it didn't have. Because AIG had sold protection to nearly every major global bank, its failure threatened a chain reaction, and the US rescue eventually reached around \$182 billion. The lesson the post predicts exactly: a CDS transforms reference-entity risk into seller risk, and concentrating that seller risk in one undercapitalized firm, without margin, is how a side bet sinks the parent and threatens the system.

**The Greek sovereign CDS and the "credit event" fight, 2012.** When Greece restructured its government debt in 2012, imposing large losses on private bondholders, the question of whether this counted as a CDS *credit event* became a market-moving controversy. Initially the restructuring was structured to be "voluntary" — which would *not* trigger CDS — provoking fears that sovereign CDS was useless as a hedge. The Determinations Committee ultimately ruled that the use of "collective action clauses" to force holdouts *was* a credit event, and roughly \$3 billion net of CDS paid out via auction. The episode is the post's "what counts as a credit event" point in the wild: the precise contract language and the Committee's ruling, not the headline drama, decided who got paid.

**The "London Whale," 2012.** JPMorgan's Chief Investment Office, through a trader nicknamed the London Whale, built an enormous position in CDS *indices* (notably CDX investment-grade tranches), nominally to hedge the bank's credit exposure. The position grew so large it distorted the index's pricing, the hedge stopped behaving like a hedge, and unwinding it cost JPMorgan over \$6 billion. It's a clean illustration of two themes: index CDS as a portfolio-scale tool, and the danger when a "hedge" becomes a directional bet so big it moves the very market it trades in.

**The CDS-bond basis in autumn 2008.** During the post-Lehman panic, the CDS-bond basis for investment-grade credit went sharply *negative* — cash bonds traded at far wider spreads than the equivalent CDS protection. In theory this was free money (buy the bond, buy protection, harvest the gap), but no one had the balance sheet or funding to do the arbitrage, so the basis stayed dislocated for months. It's the textbook case of the post's point that the basis is a thermometer for funding stress: when the arbitrage that should close it can't be financed, the gap *is* the signal.

**Single-name CDS as an early-warning gauge.** Long before a troubled company formally defaults, its CDS spread typically blows out as the market reprices its survival odds — often faster and more visibly than its bond prices, because CDS is a cleaner, more liquid expression of pure credit risk. Spiking single-name spreads preceded the troubles at firms across multiple cycles, from financials in 2008 to various corporate stresses since. Watching the spread is watching the market's live default-probability estimate, which is why credit desks, rating analysts, and regulators all keep an eye on it.

**The shrinking single-name market.** A quieter real-market story: after 2008, regulation, central clearing, and the migration of activity into indices caused the *single-name* CDS market to shrink substantially from its mid-2000s peak, even as index CDS stayed deeply liquid. For many individual credits, protection became harder and costlier to buy. It's a reminder that liquidity is itself a risk: a hedge you can't put on (or can't unwind) at a reasonable price isn't much of a hedge — exactly the single-name-versus-index tradeoff the matrix above lays out.

## When this matters to you, and further reading

Most people will never trade a credit default swap. But the CDS market shapes the world you do live in. The spread on a company's CDS feeds into what it pays to issue debt, which feeds into whether it expands or lays off; the level of index CDS spreads is one of the cleanest real-time readings of how much stress is in the financial system; and the 2008 episode — where one CDS seller's failure justified \$182 billion of public money — is a permanent reminder that this plumbing can flood the whole house. If you ever read that "credit spreads are widening" or "the CDX is blowing out," you now know that's the market raising its estimate of how likely companies are to default, priced in the very instrument this post explained.
