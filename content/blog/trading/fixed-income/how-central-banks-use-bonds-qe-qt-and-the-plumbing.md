> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — the largest single owner of bonds on earth is a buyer who is not trying to make money: the central bank. It buys and sells Treasuries to set the price of money, and in a crisis it can buy without limit to keep the market from freezing.
> - The Fed has two jobs in the bond market: it pins the *short* rate by paying interest on bank reserves (the floor system), and in special times it buys *long* bonds outright to push down long yields and inject reserves — that second tool is **quantitative easing (QE)**.
> - **QE** is the Fed creating new bank reserves to buy Treasuries and mortgage bonds; **quantitative tightening (QT)** is the reverse, letting those bonds mature without replacing them so reserves drain out. QE grows the balance sheet; QT shrinks it.
> - The plumbing matters: bank **reserves**, the **repo** market, **interest on reserve balances (IORB)** and the **reverse repo (RRP)** facility are the pipes through which the policy rate is actually enforced. When a pipe clogs — as in September 2019 and March 2020 — rates spike and the Fed has to intervene.
> - QE works partly by lowering the **term premium** (the extra yield long bonds pay for tying up your money) and partly by signalling rates will stay low; both push investors out of safe bonds and into stocks, credit, and housing.
> - Running example: the Fed buys **\$500B** of Treasuries. We trace exactly what happens to the 10-year yield, to bank reserves, and to the Fed's balance sheet — and why the bond *seller* ends up holding cash they did not ask for.

Here is a fact that quietly reorganizes how you should think about the entire bond market: the single biggest holder of US government debt is not a foreign government, not a pension fund, not BlackRock. For most of the last fifteen years it was the Federal Reserve — the US central bank — which at its peak owned roughly **\$8.9 trillion** of Treasuries and mortgage bonds, more than a fifth of all the marketable Treasury debt in existence. And unlike every other buyer in this series, the Fed is not trying to earn a return. It buys bonds to *change their price on purpose*, because the price of bonds is the price of money, and the price of money is the lever the central bank pulls to steer the whole economy.

That is a strange kind of market participant. Everyone else you have met in this series — the insurer matching liabilities, the [pension fund buying safe income](/blog/trading/fixed-income/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income), the [corporate treasurer issuing debt](/blog/trading/fixed-income/who-issues-bonds-and-why-governments-companies-and-cities) — is a *price taker*: they accept the yield the market offers. The central bank is a *price maker*. It can create money out of nothing and use it to buy bonds, and when a buyer with an infinite balance sheet shows up, prices move. Understanding how it does this — through ordinary open-market operations, through the dramatic episodes of quantitative easing, and through the hidden plumbing of bank reserves and the repo market — is the difference between watching the bond market and understanding who is actually setting the tempo.

![A layered stack of the Federal Reserve balance sheet showing assets of Treasury securities and mortgage backed securities on one side and liabilities of bank reserves currency and the reverse repo facility on the other side balancing to the same total](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-use-bonds-qe-qt-and-the-plumbing-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: a central bank is, at bottom, a balance sheet. On the asset side it holds the bonds it has bought — Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities (MBS). On the liability side it holds the money it created to buy them — mostly **bank reserves** (digital balances banks keep at the Fed), plus physical currency and a facility called the reverse repo. Every action in this post — QE, QT, the repo market, the policy rate — is a movement on one or both sides of this balance sheet. Hold that picture: assets are bonds, liabilities are money, and the two sides always balance. (Everything here is educational, not investment advice; the aim is to understand the mechanism, not to predict the Fed's next move.)

## Foundations: the building blocks you need first

Let's assemble the vocabulary from zero, because central banking stacks several unfamiliar ideas on top of each other, and skipping any one of them makes the rest opaque. If you already know what a bond is, skim; if any of these terms is new, do not proceed past it.

**A central bank is the bank for the banks.** Ordinary people and companies keep their money at commercial banks — JPMorgan, Bank of America, your local credit union. Those commercial banks, in turn, keep *their* money at the central bank. In the US that central bank is the Federal Reserve (the "Fed"). The Fed issues the currency, holds the banks' accounts, and — crucially — has the unique power to create new money simply by typing it into existence. When the Fed "prints money," it almost never prints paper; it credits a digital balance. That power is what makes it different from every other actor in the bond market.

**Bank reserves are the banks' money at the Fed.** A *reserve* is a deposit a commercial bank holds in its account at the central bank. Think of it as the banks' checking account at the Fed. Reserves are the most basic form of money in the system — they are what banks use to settle payments with each other. If you wire \$1,000 from your Chase account to my Wells Fargo account, under the hood Chase's reserve balance at the Fed falls by \$1,000 and Wells Fargo's rises by \$1,000. Reserves are a *liability* of the Fed (the Fed owes the banks that money) and an *asset* of the banks. Remember this: when the Fed buys a bond, it pays by creating new reserves. That is the entire trick.

**The policy rate is the interest rate the central bank targets.** The Fed does not set the rate on your mortgage or your savings account directly. It sets a single very-short-term interest rate — the rate banks charge each other to borrow reserves overnight, called the **federal funds rate** — and lets that rate ripple out into every other rate in the economy. The Fed announces a *target range* for this rate (for example, 5.25% to 5.50%) and then uses its tools to keep the actual rate inside that range. The [mechanics of how the Fed sets this rate](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) deserve their own treatment; here, just hold that the policy rate is the *one price* the Fed controls most directly, and it is a very short-term one.

**A bond's yield moves opposite to its price.** This is the seesaw at the [heart of bonds](/blog/trading/fixed-income/price-and-yield-the-seesaw-at-the-heart-of-bonds): a bond pays fixed cash, so if you pay *more* for that fixed cash, your return — the *yield* — is *lower*. Pay less, yield higher. So when the Fed buys bonds and pushes their *prices up*, it pushes their *yields down*. "The Fed buying bonds lowers long-term interest rates" is exactly the same sentence as "the Fed buying bonds raises bond prices." Keep that equivalence in your pocket; we will use it constantly.

**Short rates and long rates are different things.** The Fed controls the *overnight* rate directly. But the rates that matter most for the real economy — 30-year mortgages, 10-year corporate borrowing, the discount rate that prices stocks — are *long-term* rates, set by the market's demand for and supply of long bonds. The Fed can *influence* long rates but does not set them by decree. The whole drama of quantitative easing is the Fed reaching past the short rate it controls and trying to push down the long rates it normally cannot.

**The term premium is the extra yield you demand for lending long.** Why does a 10-year bond usually yield more than a 1-year bond? Partly because the market expects short rates to be higher on average over those ten years, and partly because tying your money up for a decade is *risky* — rates could rise, inflation could surprise, you might need the cash. The *term premium* is the extra yield, above the average-expected-short-rate, that investors demand as compensation for that risk. It is the [reason the yield curve usually slopes up](/blog/trading/fixed-income/why-the-yield-curve-usually-slopes-up-term-premium-and-expectations). QE is, in large part, a machine for *compressing the term premium*: by taking long bonds out of the market, the Fed makes the remaining ones scarcer and reduces the risk the private sector must hold, so investors accept a smaller premium.

**Open-market operations are how the Fed transacts.** When the Fed wants to add or drain reserves, it buys or sells Treasuries in the open market — through a desk at the New York Fed that trades with a set of large dealers called *primary dealers*. Buying bonds *adds* reserves (the Fed pays by creating them); selling bonds *drains* reserves (the buyer pays the Fed, extinguishing them). "Open-market operations" (OMO) is the umbrella term for this buying and selling. QE is just open-market operations at enormous scale and at long maturities.

With those seven ideas in hand, here is the one sentence that motivates everything that follows: **a central bank steers the economy by controlling the price of money, and it does that by transacting in bonds — pinning the short rate through the interest it pays on reserves, and, when that is not enough, buying long bonds outright to push down long yields, inject reserves, and chase investors out of safety and into risk.**

## Two tools, two parts of the curve

It helps enormously to separate the Fed's two distinct jobs from the start, because beginners (and a surprising number of commentators) blur them together.

**Job one: pin the overnight rate.** Day in, day out, in normal times, the Fed's main job is to keep the federal funds rate inside its target range. In the modern system it does this not by carefully rationing the supply of reserves but by *paying interest on reserves*. We will unpack this "floor system" in detail below. This tool moves the *very short end* of the yield curve — overnight to a few months.

**Job two: influence the long end in special times.** Sometimes pinning the short rate is not enough. In a deep recession the Fed may cut the overnight rate all the way to zero and *still* find the economy starved of stimulus, because long-term rates — mortgages, corporate borrowing — remain stubbornly high. With the short rate at zero, the Fed has hit the "zero lower bound" and is out of conventional ammunition. So it reaches for an unconventional tool: it buys long-term bonds outright, in size, to push *long* yields down directly. That is **quantitative easing**. When the emergency passes, it reverses the process — **quantitative tightening**.

The crucial point: job one is about the *price* of overnight money and uses a *rate* (the interest it pays on reserves). Job two is about the *quantity* of long bonds it owns and works through the *size and composition of the balance sheet*. The first is conventional monetary policy; the second is "balance-sheet policy." A good cross-reference for how these fit into the full central-bank kit — rates, QE/QT, and forward guidance together — is the [central-bank toolkit overview](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance); this post zooms in on the *bond-market* mechanics of all of it.

#### Worked example: why the Fed needs a long-end tool at all

Suppose the economy is in a deep slump. The Fed cuts the overnight rate from 2% all the way to **0%** — the most it can conventionally do. Now consider a homeowner deciding whether to refinance a mortgage. Their mortgage rate is not the overnight rate; it tracks the **10-year Treasury yield**, which sits at, say, **3.0%**. Even with the overnight rate at zero, the 10-year is at 3%, so a 30-year mortgage costs maybe **4.0%**. That is still high enough to keep the housing market frozen.

The Fed has used up its conventional tool and the rate that actually matters — the long one — has barely moved. So it turns to QE: it announces it will buy, say, **\$80B of Treasuries and \$40B of MBS every month**. Over the following year, the 10-year yield drifts down from 3.0% to **1.5%**, and the 30-year mortgage rate falls from 4.0% to **2.7%**. *Now* refinancing makes sense, housing thaws, and the stimulus reaches the real economy.

*The intuition: the short rate the Fed controls and the long rate the economy feels can pull apart; QE is the tool that lets the Fed reach the long rate when the short rate is stuck at zero.*

## What quantitative easing actually is, step by step

The phrase "quantitative easing" sounds technical, even forbidding. The mechanic underneath is concrete and almost embarrassingly simple. Let's trace it dollar by dollar.

The Fed announces it will buy long-term bonds. Its trading desk at the New York Fed goes to the primary dealers and buys, say, \$1 billion of 10-year Treasuries. How does it pay? Not with money it had sitting around. It pays by **creating new bank reserves** — it credits the seller's bank with \$1 billion of brand-new reserves that did not exist a second earlier. On the Fed's balance sheet, *assets* rise by \$1B (the Treasuries it now owns) and *liabilities* rise by \$1B (the new reserves it owes the banking system). The balance sheet grows on both sides. No taxpayer money is spent; no Congressional appropriation is needed. The Fed has the unique legal power to expand its balance sheet at will, and QE is the exercise of that power.

![A pipeline showing the quantitative easing mechanism from the Federal Reserve buying bonds through primary dealers to long term yields falling reserves rising and investors rotating into riskier assets like stocks and credit](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-use-bonds-qe-qt-and-the-plumbing-2.png)

Read the pipeline above left to right. The Fed buys long bonds with newly created reserves; this does four things at once. First, it pushes *bond prices up and long yields down* directly — a giant price-insensitive buyer raises the price. Second, it *injects reserves* into the banking system, flooding banks with cash. Third, it removes *duration* (long-dated interest-rate risk) from the private market, *compressing the term premium*. And fourth — the one most people miss — it forces the bond *sellers* to do something with the cash: a pension fund that just sold its Treasuries to the Fed now holds reserves earning almost nothing, so it goes looking for yield, buying corporate bonds, stocks, or other assets. That rotation pushes *those* prices up too. Economists call this the **portfolio-balance channel**: QE works not only on the bonds the Fed buys, but on everything investors buy *instead*.

There are, then, several distinct transmission channels, and it is worth naming them so you can hear them in the commentary:

- **The duration / portfolio-balance channel.** By absorbing long bonds, the Fed reduces the supply the private sector must hold, lowering the term premium and chasing investors into riskier assets. This is the channel that lifts stocks and credit.
- **The signalling channel.** A central bank that has committed to buying bonds for a year is implicitly committing to keep short rates low for a long time — otherwise it would be fighting itself. So QE reinforces *forward guidance*: it tells the market "rates will stay low," which by itself pulls down long yields (a long yield is roughly the average of expected future short rates plus the term premium).
- **The liquidity / market-functioning channel.** In a crisis (March 2020 below), QE is less about stimulus and more about being the buyer of last resort when the market has frozen — restoring orderly trading so prices stop gapping.

#### Worked example: the Fed buys \$500B of Treasuries — trace everything

Here is our running example, and we will return to it. The Fed announces a \$500B Treasury purchase program. Walk through where every dollar goes.

**The Fed's balance sheet.** Assets rise by **\$500B** (Treasuries owned). Liabilities rise by **\$500B** (new reserves created). The balance sheet is now \$500B bigger on both sides. It still balances — it always does.

**Bank reserves.** The banking system now holds **\$500B more reserves** than before. These reserves are a hot potato: any individual bank can pass them to another bank by making a payment, but the *system as a whole* cannot get rid of them. The total quantity of reserves is fixed by the Fed; only the Fed can destroy them (by selling bonds back). So \$500B of new reserves sloshes around the banking system, pushing down the overnight rate unless the Fed actively pays banks to hold them (more on this in the plumbing section).

**The 10-year yield.** This is the tricky one, because the size of the move depends on conditions. A rough, oft-cited rule of thumb from the QE-era research literature is that large-scale asset purchases lowered the 10-year yield by **roughly 3 to 5 basis points per \$100B** of purchases, mostly through the term premium. (A *basis point*, "bp," is one hundredth of a percent — 0.01%.) So \$500B might lower the 10-year by roughly **15 to 25 bps** — call it **0.20%**. If the 10-year started at 2.50%, it might fall to about **2.30%**. That is a meaningful move: on a bond with a [duration](/blog/trading/fixed-income/duration-the-most-important-number-in-fixed-income) of about 9 years, a 0.20% yield drop is roughly a **1.8% price gain**. (These magnitudes are illustrative and vary enormously with the state of the market — early-crisis QE moves yields far more than QE into a calm market.)

**The seller.** Whoever sold the Fed its \$500B of Treasuries — pension funds, foreign central banks, dealers — now holds \$500B of cash (reserves, via their banks) instead of bonds. They did not get richer; they swapped a yielding asset for a non-yielding one. So they go shopping for yield, bidding up corporate bonds, equities, and other assets. The ripple spreads outward from Treasuries to everything.

*The intuition: a \$500B QE program is a single balance-sheet entry that simultaneously enlarges the Fed, floods the banks with reserves, nudges the 10-year down by tens of basis points, and pushes a wall of displaced cash into riskier assets.*

## What the Fed buys, and why composition matters

QE is not one undifferentiated act of "buying bonds." *What* the Fed buys, and at *which maturities*, changes the effect — and the Fed has used this deliberately.

**Treasuries versus MBS.** When the Fed buys **Treasuries**, it pushes down the whole Treasury curve and, through the portfolio-balance channel, every rate that is priced off Treasuries. When it buys **mortgage-backed securities** (bonds made of pooled home loans, covered in the [MBS post](/blog/trading/fixed-income/mortgage-backed-securities-bonds-with-negative-convexity)), it does something more targeted: it drives down the *mortgage spread* — the gap between mortgage rates and Treasury yields — and so lowers mortgage rates *directly*, beyond what the Treasury move alone would do. In 2008–2009 and again in 2020, MBS purchases were aimed squarely at reviving housing, because the Fed wanted the stimulus to reach homeowners, not just the Treasury curve. The trade-off is that the Fed buying MBS is a form of *credit allocation* — choosing to subsidize one sector (housing) over others — which is why it draws more political criticism than buying neutral Treasuries.

**Maturity matters too: Operation Twist.** In 2011 the Fed ran a program that was *not* QE at all in the balance-sheet sense: **Operation Twist**. Instead of buying bonds with new reserves (growing the balance sheet), it *sold short-dated* Treasuries and used the proceeds to *buy long-dated* ones — about **\$400B** worth — keeping the balance-sheet size unchanged while *lengthening* its average maturity. The goal was pure term-premium compression at the long end without injecting new reserves. Twist is the clean demonstration that QE's long-yield effect runs largely through *which* maturities the Fed holds (duration extracted from the market), not merely through *how big* the balance sheet is. It is QE's duration channel isolated from its reserve channel.

#### Worked example: MBS purchases versus Treasury purchases on a mortgage rate

Suppose the 10-year Treasury yield is **3.0%** and the typical 30-year mortgage carries a spread of **1.7%** over it, so mortgage rates are **4.7%**. The Fed wants to bring mortgages down by 1%.

*Path A — buy only Treasuries.* If \$500B of Treasury purchases lowers the 10-year by ~0.20%, mortgages fall to **4.5%** (spread unchanged at 1.7%). The effect on housing is modest.

*Path B — buy MBS too.* MBS purchases also compress the *mortgage spread* — say from 1.7% to **1.2%** — by directly bidding up mortgage bonds. Now mortgages = 2.80% (lower Treasury) + 1.2% (tighter spread) ≈ **4.0%**, a full 0.7% lower. *The intuition: buying Treasuries lowers all rates a little; buying MBS lowers mortgage rates specifically, which is why the Fed reaches for MBS when housing is the thing it most needs to revive.*

## Quantitative tightening: running the movie backward

If QE is the Fed creating reserves to buy bonds, QT is the Fed *un-creating* reserves by *not replacing* the bonds it owns as they mature. There are two ways to shrink a bond portfolio, and the distinction matters.

**Passive QT (roll-off).** The Fed simply lets its bonds mature and does *not* reinvest the principal. When a \$10B Treasury the Fed owns matures, the Treasury pays the Fed \$10B. In normal times the Fed would use that \$10B to buy a new Treasury, keeping its portfolio constant. Under QT it does *not* reinvest — so \$10B of reserves are extinguished as the Treasury raises the cash to pay the Fed (by selling new bonds to the public, who pay with reserves). The balance sheet shrinks by \$10B on both sides. This is the gentle, predictable form of QT, and it is what the Fed used in 2022–2024, capping how much it let roll off each month (e.g. \$60B of Treasuries and \$35B of MBS per month at the start).

**Active QT (outright sales).** The Fed could instead *sell* bonds outright before they mature, which drains reserves faster. The Fed has generally avoided this — selling long bonds at a loss into a market is disruptive and crystallizes losses — and stuck to passive roll-off. (It has *never* sold its MBS outright; it relies on roll-off, which is slow because mortgages prepay less when rates are high.)

![A before and after comparison of the Federal Reserve balance sheet under quantitative tightening showing the larger balance sheet with abundant reserves on the left shrinking to a smaller balance sheet with drained reserves and slightly higher yields on the right](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-use-bonds-qe-qt-and-the-plumbing-3.png)

The before-and-after above captures the essence: QT does to the balance sheet exactly what QE did, in reverse. Assets shrink as bonds roll off; reserves drain; the private sector must now *absorb* the duration the Fed is no longer holding, so the term premium drifts up and long yields drift higher — gently, all else equal. But "all else equal" is doing heavy lifting. QT runs in the background at a steady pace; meanwhile the economy, inflation, and rate-cut expectations move yields far more day to day. So QT's effect on yields is real but slow and easily swamped — it is the tide, not the waves.

The genuinely tricky part of QT is not yields; it is *reserves*. As reserves drain, the system moves from "way more reserves than banks need" toward "just enough" — and nobody knows exactly where "just enough" is until they cross it. Cross it and the plumbing seizes, which is exactly what happened in September 2019. To understand that, we have to open up the plumbing.

#### Worked example: how long does QT take to unwind \$1 trillion?

Suppose the Fed wants to shrink its balance sheet by **\$1 trillion** using passive roll-off, capped at **\$60B per month** of Treasuries plus **\$35B per month** of MBS — a \$95B monthly cap. Naively, \$1,000B ÷ \$95B ≈ **10.5 months**. But the MBS cap rarely binds: when mortgage rates are high, homeowners do not refinance, so prepayments are slow and MBS roll off at maybe **\$15–20B/month**, well below the \$35B cap. So real run-off might be \$60B (Treasuries) + \$18B (MBS) ≈ **\$78B/month**, stretching the \$1T unwind to **roughly 13 months** — and that assumes the Fed does not pause when reserves get scarce.

In practice the Fed slowed Treasury roll-off to **\$25B/month** in 2024 precisely to avoid draining reserves too fast. At \$25B + \$18B ≈ \$43B/month, the same \$1T takes **almost two years**. *The intuition: QT is deliberately slow and self-limiting — the Fed would rather take years than risk breaking the plumbing by draining reserves too quickly.*

## The plumbing: reserves, repo, IORB, and the RRP

This is the section beginners skip and practitioners obsess over. The "plumbing" is the set of pipes through which the policy rate is actually enforced and through which cash moves overnight between the banks, the Fed, and a cast of money-market players. If you understand four terms — reserves, repo, IORB, and the RRP — you understand why the Fed can keep the overnight rate pinned even with trillions of reserves sloshing around.

![A graph of the money market plumbing showing the Federal Reserve at the center with the interest on reserve balances rate as a floor under bank reserves the reverse repo facility catching money market funds and the repo market where dealers borrow cash against Treasuries](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-use-bonds-qe-qt-and-the-plumbing-4.png)

The graph above maps the plumbing. Start with the central problem. After years of QE, the banking system holds *trillions* of reserves — far more than banks need. In an old-fashioned "scarce reserves" system, the Fed controlled the overnight rate by carefully rationing reserves: too few and the rate spiked, too many and it collapsed toward zero. With trillions of excess reserves, that lever is gone — the rate would collapse to zero. So since 2008 the Fed has run a different regime.

**The ample-reserves "floor" system.** Instead of rationing reserves, the Fed sets a *floor* under the overnight rate by *paying interest on reserves*. The rate it pays is called **interest on reserve balances (IORB)**. Here is the logic: no bank will lend reserves to another bank overnight for *less* than it could earn risk-free by just leaving them at the Fed. So IORB sets a floor: the federal funds rate cannot fall much below IORB, because why would a bank lend below the rate it can get from the Fed itself? The Fed raises and lowers IORB to move the whole overnight complex up and down. This is the modern primary tool for "job one." It is why the Fed can keep the policy rate at 5.25% even with \$3 trillion of reserves in the system: it is not making reserves scarce, it is *paying* banks 5.4% to hold them.

**Repo: borrowing cash against Treasuries.** A *repurchase agreement* (repo) is a way to borrow cash overnight using Treasuries as collateral. You "sell" a Treasury today and agree to "repurchase" it tomorrow at a tiny bit more — the difference is the interest, the *repo rate*. Repo is the beating heart of the money market: it is how dealers, hedge funds, and money funds finance their bond positions and park cash safely overnight. The repo rate is *the* overnight secured rate, and the Fed watches it like a hawk. When repo spikes, it means cash has become scarce relative to Treasuries — a plumbing problem.

**The RRP: a floor for non-banks.** Here is a wrinkle. IORB is only available to *banks* — only banks have reserve accounts. But the money market is full of non-banks: money-market mutual funds, government-sponsored enterprises, foreign official accounts. They cannot earn IORB. Left alone, they might lend overnight at rates *below* the Fed's floor, dragging the policy rate down. So the Fed offers them a second floor: the **overnight reverse repo facility (RRP)**. Through the RRP, the Fed lets non-banks lend cash *to the Fed* overnight, secured by the Fed's Treasuries, at the **RRP rate** (set a bit below IORB). No money fund will lend to anyone else for less than it can earn lending to the Fed risk-free. So the RRP sets a *lower* floor, and IORB sets the *upper* floor, and the federal funds rate is corralled inside that narrow band. The RRP also acts as a giant *sponge*: when the system has too much cash, it pools in the RRP (which ballooned to **\$2.5 trillion** in 2022–2023); as QT drains cash, the RRP empties first, cushioning reserves.

So the four pipes fit together like this: **IORB** is the floor banks earn; the **RRP** is the floor non-banks earn; **reserves** are the cash banks hold; and **repo** is the open market where everyone borrows and lends overnight against Treasuries, with its rate normally sitting between the two Fed floors. The Fed moves the whole structure up and down by moving IORB and the RRP rate together.

#### Worked example: why the overnight rate cannot fall below the Fed's floor

Suppose IORB is **5.40%** and the RRP rate is **5.30%**. A money-market fund has **\$10 billion** of cash to place overnight. Its options: lend to a bank in the repo market, or lend to the Fed via the RRP at 5.30%. If a bank offered the fund only **5.20%**, the fund would refuse — it can get 5.30% from the Fed with zero credit risk. So no overnight lending happens below ~5.30%; the RRP rate is a hard floor for the fund.

Now from the bank's side: a bank with spare reserves can earn **5.40%** risk-free at the Fed via IORB. It will not lend those reserves to another bank for less than ~5.40%. So the overnight rate is boxed in: it cannot fall much below 5.30% (the non-bank floor) and tends to sit just below 5.40% (the bank floor). On \$10B overnight at 5.30%, the fund earns \$10,000,000,000 × 0.0530 ÷ 365 ≈ **\$1.45 million for one night**. *The intuition: by paying interest at two floors — IORB for banks, RRP for non-banks — the Fed can pin the overnight rate from above and below without rationing the quantity of reserves at all.*

#### Worked example: September 2019 — when reserves got too scarce

In September 2019, after a stretch of QT had drained reserves, something startling happened: the overnight repo rate, normally sitting near the Fed's ~2.25% floor, **spiked to nearly 10%** intraday on September 17. Cash had become genuinely scarce relative to the Treasuries that needed financing — a corporate-tax payment date and a wave of Treasury settlement had sucked cash out of the system on the same day, and reserves had fallen to around **\$1.4 trillion**, evidently below the "just enough" level.

The Fed reacted within a day: it began injecting cash through *open-market repo operations* — lending reserves to dealers against Treasuries — and soon restarted Treasury *bill* purchases to rebuild reserves (carefully calling it "not QE," since it was buying short bills to add reserves, not long bonds to ease policy). Reserves were rebuilt toward **\$1.6 trillion+** and repo calmed down. *The intuition: in an ample-reserves system the Fed must keep reserves comfortably above the scarcity point; September 2019 was the market discovering, painfully, that the Fed had let QT run a little too far.*

### The demand-for-reserves curve and the Standing Repo Facility

The September 2019 lesson is best understood through the *demand curve for reserves*, a picture every Fed watcher carries in their head. Plot the overnight rate (relative to IORB) against the quantity of reserves in the system. The curve is shaped like a flattened L. When reserves are *abundant*, the curve is flat: adding or removing reserves barely moves the rate, because banks are awash in cash and the rate just sits on the IORB floor. This is the comfortable "ample-reserves" zone the Fed wants to live in. But as QT drains reserves, you slide left along the flat part toward a *kink* — the point where reserves stop being abundant and start being scarce. Past the kink the curve turns sharply upward: now small reductions in reserves cause large jumps in the overnight rate. September 2019 was the system crossing the kink without warning, because the kink's exact location is *unobservable* until you hit it — it shifts with regulation, bank behavior, and the calendar.

That unknowability is why the Fed built a permanent backstop in 2021: the **Standing Repo Facility (SRF)**. The SRF is the mirror image of the RRP. Where the RRP lets the market *lend* cash to the Fed (draining excess), the SRF lets eligible counterparties *borrow* cash from the Fed overnight against Treasuries at a rate set just above the policy range. It puts a *ceiling* on the repo rate: no one will pay 10% for overnight cash in the open market when they can borrow from the Fed's SRF at, say, 5.5% against their Treasuries. With the RRP as a floor (a sponge for excess cash) and the SRF as a ceiling (a tap for scarce cash), the Fed has corralled the overnight rate from both sides — a far more robust plumbing than it had in 2019. The whole architecture is the institutional memory of that repo spike, designed so the same surprise can never seize the market again.

#### Worked example: how the Standing Repo Facility caps a spike

Imagine a future tax date drains cash and dealers suddenly need \$50B of overnight financing they cannot find. In 2019, with no SRF, the repo rate spiked toward 10% as desperate borrowers bid against each other. Now suppose the SRF rate is set at **5.50%** (just above a 5.25%–5.50% target range). A dealer who needs cash simply pledges \$50B of Treasuries to the Fed's SRF and borrows at 5.50%. No dealer will pay more than ~5.50% to anyone else — the Fed is always there at that rate. So instead of spiking to 10%, the repo rate is capped near **5.50%**. *The intuition: the SRF converts a potential plumbing crisis into a non-event by guaranteeing that overnight cash is always available from the Fed at a known ceiling rate — the Fed pre-commits to being the lender of last resort for the repo market.*

## March 2020: the dash-for-cash and the backstop

The most dramatic demonstration of the central bank as the bond market's backstop came in March 2020. It is worth walking through carefully, because it shows QE in its other guise — not as stimulus, but as the buyer of last resort that keeps the world's safest market from breaking.

In the early days of the COVID-19 panic, something happened that is not supposed to happen: **US Treasuries — the safest, most liquid asset on earth — sold off violently even as stocks crashed**. Normally, when stocks plunge, money rushes *into* Treasuries (a flight to safety) and Treasury prices rise / yields fall. In March 2020 the opposite occurred for several harrowing days: investors sold *everything*, including Treasuries, in a desperate scramble for cash. The 10-year yield, which had fallen to a record low near **0.5%** on March 9, *backed up* toward **1.2%** within days as the selling overwhelmed the market. Bid-ask spreads — the gap between the [price you can buy and sell at](/blog/trading/fixed-income/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income) — on even on-the-run Treasuries blew out to many times normal. The world's deepest market had become disorderly.

![A timeline of the March 2020 dash for cash showing the COVID panic the simultaneous selloff in stocks and Treasuries the Treasury market freezing and the Federal Reserve announcing unlimited bond purchases that restored function](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-use-bonds-qe-qt-and-the-plumbing-5.png)

The timeline above traces the episode. Why did Treasuries sell off in a panic? Several forces hit at once. Foreign central banks dumped Treasuries to raise dollars. Levered investors running "basis trades" (arbitraging tiny gaps between Treasury futures and cash bonds) were forced to unwind as margin calls hit, flooding the market with bonds. And everyone, everywhere, wanted *cash* — the most liquid thing of all — so they sold the most liquid asset they had, which was Treasuries. Dealers, whose balance sheets are capped by post-2008 regulation, could not absorb the flood. The market seized.

The Fed's response was overwhelming and fast. On **March 15, 2020** it cut rates to zero and announced **\$700B** of purchases. When that proved insufficient, on **March 23, 2020** it removed the cap entirely: it would buy Treasuries and agency MBS **"in the amounts needed to support smooth market functioning."** That phrase — *unlimited* — was the turning point. In the weeks that followed the Fed bought Treasuries at a staggering pace (well over **\$75B per day** at the peak), and the balance sheet exploded from about **\$4.2 trillion** to over **\$7 trillion** within months. The market healed: spreads normalized, the 10-year settled back down, and the panic-selling of Treasuries stopped because everyone knew there was now an infinite buyer.

This is the deepest lesson about the central bank and the bond market. In ordinary times the Fed *influences* the bond market at the margin. In a true liquidity crisis it *is* the bond market — the only balance sheet large enough to stand against a stampede. The credibility of that backstop is itself stabilizing: once the market believes the Fed will buy without limit, the incentive to panic-sell evaporates, and often the Fed ends up not needing to buy as much as it threatened. The backstop works partly by existing.

#### Worked example: why a forced seller breaks even a "risk-free" market

Imagine a hedge fund running a \$50B basis trade: it owns \$50B of cash Treasuries and is short \$50B of Treasury futures, earning a tiny spread, financed almost entirely with borrowed money (repo). The trade is nearly risk-free *if you can hold it to maturity*. But in March 2020, two things happened at once: repo financing got more expensive and harder to roll, and futures margin calls spiked. To raise cash, the fund had to *sell its \$50B of cash Treasuries — fast*.

Now multiply that by dozens of funds doing the same trade. Suddenly hundreds of billions of "safe" Treasuries are being dumped by forced sellers into a market where dealers cannot absorb them. The price falls, yields rise, and the safest asset on earth is briefly behaving like a risky one — not because anyone doubts the US will pay, but because *liquidity*, not credit, has vanished. The Fed's unlimited bid was the only thing big enough to be the buyer those forced sellers needed. *The intuition: even a default-free bond can crash in price when too many leveraged holders are forced to sell at once — and only a central bank's balance sheet is large enough to absorb that flood.*

## How QE/QT transmit to yields, the term premium, and risk assets

Let's pull the threads together and be precise about *how* balance-sheet policy reaches the prices you care about. A long yield can be decomposed into two parts:

$$ y_{\text{long}} \approx \underbrace{\text{average expected future short rate}}_{\text{expectations}} + \underbrace{\text{term premium}}_{\text{risk compensation}} $$

Here $y_{\text{long}}$ is the yield on a long bond (say the 10-year); the *expectations* piece is the market's forecast of where the overnight rate will average over the bond's life; and the *term premium* is the extra yield demanded for bearing the risk of holding long.

QE pushes down *both* terms. It lowers the **expectations** piece via signalling — committing to buy bonds signals rates will stay low, so the expected average short rate falls. And it lowers the **term premium** by absorbing duration: with the Fed holding trillions of long bonds, the private sector has less interest-rate risk to bear and demands less compensation for it. Empirical estimates attribute the bulk of QE's effect on the 10-year to the *term-premium* channel — QE's signature is a *compressed, sometimes negative term premium*, which is exactly what was observed through the 2010s.

From yields, the effect fans out to risk assets through the **portfolio-balance / reach-for-yield** channel we met earlier. When safe long bonds yield 1.5% instead of 3%, every yield-hungry investor — pension funds, insurers, savers — is pushed toward riskier assets to hit their return targets. That bids up corporate bonds (compressing [credit spreads](/blog/trading/fixed-income/credit-spreads-pricing-the-probability-of-default)), equities (a lower discount rate raises the present value of future earnings), and real estate. This is why QE eras coincide with rising stock and bond prices together, and why markets are so sensitive to any hint of "tapering" (slowing QE) or QT — the 2013 "taper tantrum," when the mere suggestion of slowing purchases sent the 10-year yield jumping ~100 bps, is the canonical example.

![A two line influence chart showing the Federal Reserve balance sheet expanding during quantitative easing phases and shrinking during quantitative tightening while the ten year Treasury yield falls during easing and rises during tightening over time](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-use-bonds-qe-qt-and-the-plumbing-6.png)

The centerpiece chart above is the single most important picture in this post — and it comes with a health warning: it is *illustrative and stylized*, drawn to show the *relationship*, not the exact historical path. It plots the Fed's balance sheet (blue) against the 10-year yield (red) over a stylized cycle. Read the phases. During **QE** phases the balance sheet expands and the 10-year yield trends *down* (and risk assets rally). During **QT** phases the balance sheet shrinks and the yield drifts *up*. The relationship is real but loose — in any given month, growth, inflation, and rate-cut expectations move the 10-year far more than the balance sheet does, which is why the two lines are correlated over phases but not pinned together day to day. The honest summary: QE/QT set the *backdrop* for yields and risk appetite; they do not set the daily price.

#### Worked example: how QE flows into stock prices through the discount rate

A stock is worth the present value of its future earnings, discounted at a rate built partly on the long Treasury yield. Take a simplified company expected to earn **\$5 per share next year**, growing **3%** forever. Using the Gordon growth model, its value is roughly earnings ÷ (discount rate − growth).

Before QE, suppose the discount rate is **8%** (a 5% long-yield-driven base plus a 3% equity risk premium): value ≈ \$5 ÷ (0.08 − 0.03) = \$5 ÷ 0.05 = **\$100**.

Now QE pushes the long yield down by 1.5%, dragging the discount rate to **6.5%**: value ≈ \$5 ÷ (0.065 − 0.03) = \$5 ÷ 0.035 ≈ **\$143**.

The *same earnings* are now worth **43% more** — purely because the rate used to discount them fell. *The intuition: QE lifts stocks not by improving company profits but by lowering the rate at which future profits are discounted; a small drop in long yields is a large jump in the present value of far-off cash flows.*

## The limits and side effects of QE

QE is powerful, but it is not free money and not without cost. A grown-up understanding includes its limits.

**Diminishing returns.** The first round of QE, deployed into a frozen 2009 market, had large effects because it restored function and shocked a market that did not expect it. By the third and fourth rounds, into calmer markets that already anticipated the Fed, each dollar of purchases moved yields far less. QE is most potent as a *crisis* tool (market functioning) and weakest as a routine *stimulus* tool into a calm market.

**It does not directly create lending.** A common misconception is that QE "floods the economy with money." It floods the *banking system with reserves*, but reserves are not loans. Banks do not lend out reserves to the public; reserves stay trapped inside the interbank system. QE works through *asset prices and expectations*, not by handing cash to consumers. That is why a decade of QE produced soaring asset prices but persistently *below-target* inflation — the money stayed in the financial system, lifting Wall Street more than Main Street, which is the root of the "QE worsens inequality" critique.

**Interest-rate risk lands on the central bank — and the taxpayer.** When the Fed owns trillions of long bonds bought at low yields, and then raises short rates above those bonds' coupons, it loses money: it pays more interest on reserves (IORB) than it earns on its old low-yielding bonds. In 2022–2023 the Fed swung from remitting tens of billions in profit to the Treasury each year to running an *operating loss*, accumulating a "deferred asset" of over **\$200 billion** by 2024. It is not insolvent — a central bank cannot run out of the money it creates — but the losses are real and ultimately fall on the public purse (forgone remittances to the Treasury).

**Exit is hard.** Unwinding QE (i.e. QT) risks the very plumbing problems we saw in September 2019, and tapering risks tantrums. The Fed has discovered that its balance sheet is far easier to expand than to shrink, and that a large balance sheet may be a semi-permanent feature of the modern system rather than an emergency measure to be fully reversed.

#### Worked example: the Fed's own interest-rate mismatch

Suppose the Fed holds **\$5 trillion** of Treasuries with an average coupon of **2.0%**, earning **\$100 billion/year**. It funds this with **\$3.5 trillion** of reserves on which it now pays IORB of **5.4%**, costing **\$189 billion/year**, plus RRP costs on top. The Fed is paying out far more than it earns — a negative carry of roughly **\$90+ billion/year** on this simplified slice.

In the QE years the math ran the other way: the Fed earned 2% on its bonds and paid near 0% on reserves, netting ~\$100B/year that it remitted to the Treasury. When short rates shot up, the carry flipped negative, and the Fed began recording losses. *The intuition: a central bank that buys long bonds with overnight money is running a giant maturity mismatch — profitable when short rates are low, loss-making when short rates rise above the bonds' coupons, with the swing landing on the taxpayer.*

## Common misconceptions

**"QE is printing money that causes inflation."** Mostly wrong, and the 2010s proved it. QE creates *bank reserves*, not cash in consumers' pockets. Reserves are trapped in the banking system and do not directly fund spending. A full decade of aggressive QE coincided with inflation running persistently *below* the Fed's 2% target. (The 2021–2022 inflation surge was driven mainly by pandemic supply shocks and large *fiscal* transfers — actual cash to households — not by QE per se.) The honest statement: QE inflates *asset* prices reliably and *consumer* prices only weakly.

**"The Fed sets mortgage and long-term rates directly."** No. The Fed sets the overnight rate directly and *influences* long rates — strongly via QE, weakly via expectations the rest of the time. The 10-year yield and the mortgage rate are set by the whole market's supply and demand. The Fed is the biggest single player, but it is one player; long yields routinely move *against* what the Fed wants when growth or inflation data surprise.

**"QE means the government is monetizing its debt."** Not in the way the phrase implies. The Fed buys Treasuries in the *secondary* market from private holders, not directly from the Treasury at issuance (which is generally prohibited). It is not directly financing the deficit; it is swapping existing Treasuries for reserves. The line between that and "debt monetization" can blur in extreme cases, which is why central-bank *independence* from the Treasury is guarded so carefully — see the [discussion of deficits, bond supply, and issuance](/blog/trading/macro-trading/deficits-debt-bond-supply-why-issuance-moves-yields).

**"A bigger Fed balance sheet always means lower yields right now."** No — the relationship holds over *phases*, not days. In the influence chart, balance sheet and yields move oppositely across QE/QT regimes, but on any given day growth and inflation news dominate. During 2022, the Fed was *shrinking* its balance sheet *and* the 10-year yield was rising sharply — but the yield was driven mostly by rate hikes and inflation, not QT. Correlation over years; noise over weeks.

**"The Fed can run out of money."** No. A central bank issues the currency; it cannot become insolvent in its own currency in the way a household or company can. It *can* run operating losses (it did in 2022–2024) and those losses have real distributional consequences (forgone remittances to the Treasury), but "the Fed is broke" is a category error. Its constraint is *inflation and credibility*, not solvency.

## How it shows up in real markets

**QE1 and the 2008–2009 backstop.** In late 2008, with credit markets frozen, the Fed launched its first large-scale asset purchases, eventually buying about **\$1.75 trillion** of Treasuries, agency debt, and MBS. The MBS purchases were especially targeted: by buying mortgage bonds directly, the Fed drove mortgage spreads down and mortgage rates from near 6.5% toward 5%, reviving a comatose housing market. QE1 is the cleanest example of QE-as-market-repair: deployed into a frozen market, it had large, measurable effects on the exact rates it targeted.

**The 2013 taper tantrum.** In May 2013, Fed Chair Ben Bernanke merely *suggested* the Fed might begin slowing ("tapering") its bond purchases later that year. He did not stop QE; he hinted at slowing it. The 10-year Treasury yield jumped from about **1.6% to nearly 3%** over the following months, and emerging-market currencies and bonds sold off hard as capital fled. The episode is the textbook demonstration of the *signalling* channel: it was not the actual quantity of bonds that moved the market, but the change in *expectations* about future Fed support. It also taught the Fed to communicate balance-sheet changes far more gingerly thereafter.

**September 2019 repo spike.** As covered above, QT plus a tax-date cash drain pushed reserves below the comfort level and the overnight repo rate spiked to near 10% intraday on September 17, 2019. The Fed restarted bill purchases and standing repo operations to rebuild reserves. The lesson, absorbed permanently: in an ample-reserves regime, the Fed must keep a generous buffer of reserves, and it later created a *Standing Repo Facility* (SRF) so that any future cash squeeze can be met instantly by the Fed lending against Treasuries — a permanent plumbing backstop.

**March 2020 unlimited QE.** The dash-for-cash and the Fed's "unlimited" pledge on March 23, 2020 is the defining modern example of the central bank as the bond market's backstop. The balance sheet went from ~\$4.2T to over \$7T in months; the world's safest market, which had briefly broken, was restored. The episode also showed QE's two faces clearly: the first weeks were pure *market-functioning* purchases (stop the freeze), which then shaded into *stimulus* QE as the crisis became a recession.

**2022–2024 QT and the negative carry.** When inflation surged, the Fed hiked rates from ~0% to ~5.4% and began QT, capping roll-off at \$95B/month before slowing it. Two consequences became visible textbook lessons: the Fed's carry went sharply negative (it paid more on reserves than it earned on its old low-coupon bonds, accumulating a deferred asset over \$200B), and the giant \$2.5T RRP balance acted as the intended sponge — draining first as QT removed cash, cushioning bank reserves for over a year before reserves themselves began to feel the squeeze.

**The Bank of England's 2022 gilt intervention.** Central banks beyond the Fed use the same playbook. In late September 2022, a UK fiscal announcement triggered a violent sell-off in long-dated gilts (UK government bonds) that threatened to blow up pension funds running leveraged liability-driven-investment (LDI) strategies — a forced-selling spiral much like a basis-trade unwind. The Bank of England, *in the middle of trying to do QT*, abruptly reversed and announced *emergency QE* — temporary unlimited long-gilt purchases — to halt the spiral and protect financial stability. It is the clearest recent proof that the backstop function overrides the policy stance: a central bank fighting inflation with QT will still flip to QE in an instant if the bond market threatens to break.

## When this matters to you

You will never directly trade with the Fed, but its bond-market footprint sets the backdrop for almost every financial decision you make. The mortgage rate you are quoted, the yield on your savings, the level of the stock market in your retirement account, the spread on corporate bonds in your bond fund — all sit on top of a long-yield curve that the central bank is leaning on, gently in normal times and with its full weight in a crisis. When you hear "the Fed is doing QE," translate it instantly to "the biggest buyer in the bond market is pushing long yields down and chasing everyone into risk." When you hear "QT," translate it to "that buyer is stepping back, and the private market has to absorb the duration again." And when a market panic hits and you wonder whether the safest assets will hold, remember March 2020: the floor under the bond market is, ultimately, a balance sheet that can be created without limit — which is both the system's greatest stabilizer and the reason its independence is worth guarding.
