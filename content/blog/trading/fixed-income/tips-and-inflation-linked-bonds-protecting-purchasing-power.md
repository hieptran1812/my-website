> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — a TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Security) is a US government bond whose principal grows with inflation, so it pays you back in dollars that keep their purchasing power instead of dollars eaten away by rising prices.
> - The **principal is indexed to the Consumer Price Index (CPI)**: every day it is multiplied by an *index ratio* that tracks inflation, and the bond's fixed coupon *rate* is paid on that ever-larger principal — so both your coupons and your final repayment grow with prices.
> - A TIPS pays a **real yield** — your return *above* inflation — while an ordinary Treasury pays a **nominal yield**. The gap between them is **breakeven inflation**: `nominal yield − real yield`, the inflation rate the market expects.
> - **TIPS beat nominal Treasuries when realized inflation comes in above the breakeven, and lag when it comes in below.** You are not betting that inflation will be high; you are betting it will be *higher than the market already expects*.
> - A **deflation floor** guarantees you get back at least your original \$1,000 par at maturity, even if prices fall for years — but the coupons can still shrink along the way.
> - The catch is a tax quirk called **phantom income**: in a taxable account you owe tax each year on the principal's inflation adjustment, even though you don't receive that cash until maturity. This is why TIPS are usually held in tax-sheltered accounts.

Here is a question that quietly ruins a lot of careful savers. Suppose you lend the US government \$10,000 for ten years and it promises to pay you back \$10,000 plus 4% interest a year. That sounds safe — the government always pays. But if prices rise 4% a year over that decade, the \$10,000 you get back at the end buys what about \$6,750 buys today. Your *dollars* came back in full; your *purchasing power* did not. You were repaid in shrunken money. The bond did exactly what it promised, and you still lost ground.

Inflation is the silent tax on every fixed promise. A nominal bond — the ordinary kind — promises you a fixed number of dollars, and says nothing about what those dollars will be worth. For most of financial history, that was the only kind of government bond you could buy, and lenders simply had to guess at future inflation and demand extra yield to cover it. Then, in 1981 in the United Kingdom and 1997 in the United States, governments started issuing a different animal: a bond that promises to protect your purchasing power directly. In the US these are called **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities**, or **TIPS**. Around the world the generic term is **inflation-linked bonds**, or **linkers**.

![A timeline showing a TIPS principal ratcheting up year by year with inflation while the fixed coupon rate is paid on the rising principal, so each coupon and the final repayment grow](/imgs/blogs/tips-and-inflation-linked-bonds-protecting-purchasing-power-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model to carry through the whole post. A TIPS starts life like any bond: you buy \$1,000 of face value (the *par* amount) and you are promised a fixed coupon rate. But then something different happens. Each period, the bond's principal is *re-indexed* upward by however much prices rose — so after a year of 3% inflation, your \$1,000 of principal becomes \$1,030. The fixed coupon rate is then applied to that larger principal, so your coupon grows too. By maturity, the principal has compounded up with the entire run of inflation over the bond's life, and that grown-up principal is what gets repaid. The coupon *rate* never moves; the *base it is paid on* moves, and that single design choice is what defends your purchasing power. The rest of this post unpacks every piece of that machine — how the indexing actually works day to day, what a "real yield" is, how to read the breakeven, the deflation floor, the tax trap, and the precise conditions under which a TIPS is a better deal than a plain Treasury, and when it is a worse one.

## Foundations: inflation, nominal vs real, and what "indexed to CPI" means

Before any of the machinery makes sense, we need a handful of terms, all built from zero. None are hard, but TIPS live in the relationships between them.

**Inflation** is the rate at which the general level of prices rises over time. If a basket of goods that cost \$100 last year costs \$103 this year, inflation was 3%. The US government measures this with the **Consumer Price Index**, or **CPI** — specifically a version called CPI-U (for all urban consumers), published monthly by the Bureau of Labor Statistics. The CPI is just a number: it was set to 100 in a base period and rises as prices rise. If the CPI goes from 300 to 309 over a year, prices rose 3%. TIPS are tied to this exact published index, which is what makes them objective: there is no judgment call, just a number the government already computes for other reasons.

A **bond** is a tradable loan. You hand a borrower money today, and in return you receive a fixed stream of **coupon** payments (the interest) plus the return of your **principal** — the amount you lent — at the end, called **maturity**. The borrower is the **issuer**. A **US Treasury** is a bond issued by the US government, treated as the closest thing to risk-free because the government controls the currency its debts are denominated in. The **par value** (or face value) is the headline amount printed on the bond — almost always \$1,000 in our examples — and it's the amount a plain bond repays at maturity.

Now the most important distinction in this entire post: **nominal versus real**.

A **nominal** quantity is measured in raw dollars, ignoring what those dollars are worth. A **real** quantity is measured in purchasing power — in actual goods you can buy — after stripping out inflation. If your savings grow 5% in dollar terms but prices rose 3%, your *nominal* return is 5% and your *real* return is roughly 5% − 3% = 2%. The 2% is what actually made you richer; the other 3% just kept you even with rising prices. A *nominal yield* is the dollar return a bond promises. A **real yield** is the return *above inflation* — the part that genuinely grows your purchasing power.

This is the whole point of TIPS. An ordinary Treasury quotes you a **nominal yield**: 4.3% in dollars, and whatever inflation does to those dollars is your problem. A TIPS quotes you a **real yield**: say 1.8% *on top of* whatever inflation turns out to be. The TIPS makes inflation the government's problem, not yours — it has promised you a return in purchasing power, and it adjusts the dollars to deliver it.

"**Indexed to CPI**" means the bond's principal is multiplied each day by an **index ratio** — the current CPI divided by the CPI on the bond's issue date. On day one the ratio is exactly 1.000, so the principal is \$1,000. After a year of 3% inflation the ratio is 1.030, so the principal is \$1,030. After another 2%, the ratio is 1.030 × 1.020 = 1.0506, so the principal is \$1,050.60. The ratio only ever reflects realized, published inflation — never a forecast. (One practical wrinkle: the CPI used is lagged about two to three months, because the data takes time to publish, but the idea is exactly as described.)

Two more terms and we have everything. A **basis point** — written *bp* or *bps* — is one hundredth of a percent, 0.01%; bond people quote everything in basis points, so a real yield of 1.8% is "180 bps." And **breakeven inflation** is the difference between a nominal yield and a real yield of the same maturity: it is the inflation rate at which a TIPS and a nominal bond would deliver the same return. Hold that term; it is the hinge of the whole post, and the next section is devoted to it.

It's worth asking, briefly, *why a government would issue these at all* — because the answer reveals what the instrument is really for. A government that issues nominal debt has a quiet incentive to let inflation run: inflation erodes the real value of what it owes, so high inflation is, in effect, a stealth default on nominal bondholders. By issuing TIPS, the government deliberately gives up that escape hatch — it promises to pay back real value no matter what prices do, which is a *credible commitment* to not inflate away its debt. In exchange, it usually borrows a little cheaper on the linkers (because it isn't charging itself an inflation risk premium), and it gets a market-based readout of inflation expectations for free. The saver, meanwhile, offloads inflation risk onto the issuer. TIPS are thus a risk-transfer trade: the party best able to bear inflation risk (the government, which controls the currency) takes it from the party least able to (the household saving for a real goal).

Throughout, we'll use one recurring instrument: **a 10-year \$1,000 TIPS with a 1% coupon rate**, bought at issue, alongside a real **10-year nominal Treasury yielding 4.3%**. Keep those two side by side; almost every example compares them.

#### Worked example: a nominal bond's hidden inflation loss

You buy a 10-year nominal Treasury: \$10,000 at a 4.3% coupon. Each year it pays 4.3% × \$10,000 = \$430, and at the end it returns your \$10,000. In *dollar* terms, lovely — you collected \$4,300 of coupons and got your principal back. Now suppose inflation ran 3% a year for that decade. Prices rose by a factor of 1.03^10 ≈ 1.344, so the \$10,000 you get back buys what \$10,000 ÷ 1.344 ≈ \$7,440 buys today. Your principal lost about a quarter of its purchasing power. Your *real* return on principal was negative; only part of that \$430-a-year coupon was real income, and the rest was just compensation for the erosion you were suffering.

*A nominal bond pays you back in full and in shrunken money at the same time — the dollars are all there, but they buy less, and that gap is exactly what TIPS were invented to close.*

## How the indexing actually works: the principal ratchets, the coupon rides

Let's slow down and watch the machine turn, because the mechanics are where TIPS first click. The fixed thing about a TIPS is its **coupon rate** — say 1%. The moving thing is its **principal**, which is re-indexed every day by the index ratio. The coupon you actually receive is the fixed rate applied to the *current indexed principal*, so as the principal climbs, the dollar coupon climbs with it.

Look again at Figure 1. At issue, principal is \$1,000.00 and the index ratio is 1.000. A year of 3% inflation lifts the ratio to 1.030, so the principal becomes \$1,030.00, and the first coupon is 1% of \$1,030 = \$10.30 — not 1% of the original \$1,000. A second year of 2% inflation lifts the ratio to 1.030 × 1.020 = 1.0506, principal \$1,050.60, coupon 1% of that = \$10.51. A third year of 2% takes the ratio to 1.0716, principal \$1,071.61, coupon \$10.72. At maturity, you are repaid the *indexed* principal — \$1,071.61 in this stub example, and far more over a real ten-year run of inflation — not the original \$1,000.

The elegance is that you never have to think about inflation at all. You bought a real yield of 1% (in this stylized case), and the bond's plumbing automatically tops up both your coupons and your principal so that 1% is delivered in purchasing power. If inflation is 3%, your nominal coupon and principal grow about 3%; if it's 8%, they grow about 8%; if it's 0%, they don't grow but you still get your 1% real. Inflation never touches the *rate*; it inflates the *base*, and the base carries the coupon up with it.

A few mechanical details make this concrete enough to trade. The index ratio isn't updated once a year — it's published *daily* by the Treasury, interpolated between monthly CPI readings, so a TIPS's principal creeps up a little every single day rather than jumping on coupon dates. Because of the publishing lag, the CPI that drives a given day's ratio is the level from about two to three months earlier; this lag is why a TIPS doesn't perfectly track *this month's* inflation print in real time, but it washes out over the bond's life. When you *buy* a seasoned TIPS in the secondary market, you pay for the accrued inflation: the quoted price is on the original face, and you settle on the **inflation-adjusted principal** (quoted price × index ratio) plus accrued interest. So a TIPS issued years ago at par might settle today at, say, \$1,180 of adjusted principal for every \$1,000 of original face — you're buying a principal that has already grown, and the deflation floor (which only ever restores the *original* \$1,000) is correspondingly far below where you bought. That last point matters: the deflation protection is valuable on a *new* TIPS and nearly worthless on a deeply seasoned one, a subtlety we return to under the floor.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000 TIPS through a year of 3% inflation

You buy one \$1,000 TIPS with a 2% coupon rate (paid in two semiannual installments of 1% each, as real TIPS do). Over the year, CPI rises 3%, spread as 1.5% in the first half and a further ~1.478% in the second (so the year compounds to 3%). Watch the two coupon dates:

- **Issue:** principal \$1,000.00, index ratio 1.0000.
- **Mid-year coupon:** the index ratio is now 1.0150, so the indexed principal is \$1,015.00. The semiannual coupon is 1% of \$1,015.00 = **\$10.15**.
- **Year-end coupon:** the ratio is now 1.0300, indexed principal \$1,030.00. The semiannual coupon is 1% of \$1,030.00 = **\$10.30**.

Over the year you collected \$10.15 + \$10.30 = **\$20.45** in coupons (versus \$20.00 on a non-indexed 2% bond), *and* your principal has grown to **\$1,030.00**. If you sold at par-equivalent, your position is worth \$1,030.00 plus the coupons — your purchasing power held, because the \$30 of principal growth exactly offset the 3% the dollar lost. By contrast, a nominal 2% bond would have paid \$20.00 and still been worth \$1,000 of face — \$30 poorer in real terms.

*The principal's inflation adjustment is the real protection; the growing coupon is just that adjustment showing up in the income too.*

## Real yields and nominal yields: two prices for the same horizon

Now we can be precise about what a TIPS *yields*. When you buy a TIPS, you are buying a **real yield** — the return you earn *over and above* inflation, whatever inflation turns out to be. When you buy a nominal Treasury of the same maturity, you are buying a **nominal yield** — a fixed dollar return that includes, baked in, the market's guess at inflation plus some compensation for the risk that the guess is wrong.

The cleanest way to see the relationship is the (slightly simplified) **Fisher equation**:

$$ (1 + y_{\text{nominal}}) = (1 + y_{\text{real}}) \times (1 + \pi^e) $$

where $y_{\text{nominal}}$ is the nominal yield, $y_{\text{real}}$ is the real yield (the TIPS yield), and $\pi^e$ is expected inflation. For the small numbers we deal with, this is well approximated by simple addition:

$$ y_{\text{nominal}} \approx y_{\text{real}} + \pi^e $$

In words: **the nominal yield is the real yield plus the inflation the market expects.** A 10-year nominal Treasury at 4.3% and a 10-year TIPS at 1.8% real are telling you, together, that the market expects about 4.3% − 1.8% = 2.5% average inflation over the next decade.

![A decomposition showing a nominal Treasury yield of 4.3 percent split into a real yield of 1.8 percent plus breakeven inflation of 2.5 percent, with the TIPS locking the real part and passing through realized inflation](/imgs/blogs/tips-and-inflation-linked-bonds-protecting-purchasing-power-2.png)

Figure 2 shows the decomposition. The top bar is the nominal yield, split into its two pieces: the blue part is the real yield (which is exactly what the TIPS hands you), and the amber part is the inflation the market has priced in. The middle row shows what the TIPS guarantees: the real yield is locked, and *whatever inflation actually turns out to be* is passed through on top via the principal adjustment. The bottom row is the punchline — the breakeven, which we turn to next.

The reason this matters is that **real yields, not nominal yields, are what actually price assets**. When economists and central bankers talk about how restrictive monetary policy is, they mean the real yield: a 5% nominal yield with 5% inflation is loose money (a 0% real cost of borrowing), while a 3% nominal yield with 1% inflation is tight (a 2% real cost). The TIPS market gives you a clean, traded, real-time measurement of real yields that you simply cannot get from nominal bonds alone — which is why this one instrument has outsized influence over everything from gold to the dollar to stock valuations. We have a sibling post entirely on that point: [real yields, the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything).

#### Worked example: backing out expected inflation from two yields

It's the morning, and you see two quotes: the 10-year nominal Treasury yields **4.30%** and the 10-year TIPS yields **1.80%** real. What does the market expect inflation to average over the next ten years? Subtract: 4.30% − 1.80% = **2.50%**. That 2.50% is the breakeven. Now use the exact Fisher form to check: (1 + 0.0180) × (1 + 0.0250) = 1.0180 × 1.0250 = 1.04345, so the exact nominal-equivalent is about 4.345% — within a rounding hair of the quoted 4.30%, the tiny difference being the compounding cross-term the simple subtraction ignores. For everyday reasoning, the subtraction is plenty: **breakeven ≈ nominal − real**.

*Two government bonds, read together, give you the market's inflation forecast for free — and that forecast is one of the most-watched numbers in all of macro.*

## Breakeven inflation: the number that decides who wins

The **breakeven inflation rate** is the most important concept in the TIPS world, so let's nail it down. It is the rate of *realized* inflation at which a TIPS and a nominal Treasury of the same maturity deliver the *same* total return. Above that rate, the TIPS wins; below it, the nominal bond wins. It's called the breakeven because it's the dividing line — the inflation outcome that makes you indifferent between the two bonds.

Concretely, breakeven = nominal yield − real yield = 4.30% − 1.80% = 2.50% in our running example. This is why buying a TIPS is **not** a bet that inflation will be high. It is a bet that inflation will be *higher than the breakeven the market has already priced in*. If the market expects 2.5% and you also expect 2.5%, the two bonds are a wash and you should pick on other grounds (taxes, the deflation floor, your need for certain real income). You only prefer the TIPS if you think inflation will beat 2.5%; you prefer the nominal bond if you think it will fall short.

This reframing trips up almost everyone the first time. People reason "inflation is going up, so I should buy TIPS." But if everyone can see inflation going up, the breakeven has already widened to price it, and you're paying for that expectation up front. The edge in TIPS comes only from a *surprise* — inflation exceeding what the curve already assumes. It is the same logic as any market: you don't make money being right about the consensus; you make money being right against it.

![An illustrative chart with breakeven inflation as a steady dashed line near 2.5 percent and realized CPI as a volatile dotted line that spikes above and falls below it, with the gap marking when TIPS win](/imgs/blogs/tips-and-inflation-linked-bonds-protecting-purchasing-power-4.png)

Figure 4 makes the bet visual. The dashed line is the breakeven — a fairly steady ~2.5% that the market priced into TIPS. The dotted line is *realized* CPI, which is volatile: it spikes well above the breakeven (say, during a supply shock) and then falls below it. Whenever realized CPI is above the dashed line, the TIPS holder is winning — collecting the overshoot that the nominal bondholder is not. Whenever it's below, the nominal bond is the better deal. The whole game of TIPS-versus-nominal is the area between those two lines.

There is a second, subtler reason the breakeven is not a pure inflation forecast: it also contains a small **inflation risk premium** and is distorted by the relative **liquidity** of the two markets. Nominal Treasuries are the single most liquid security on earth; TIPS are less liquid, so in a panic investors dump TIPS and the real yield jumps, which *narrows* the measured breakeven even if true inflation expectations haven't moved. This is exactly what happened in late 2008, and we'll return to it. For now, treat the breakeven as "expected inflation plus a small, usually-stable adjustment."

#### Worked example: the breakeven as a wager

You're choosing between the 10-year nominal at 4.30% and the 10-year TIPS at 1.80% real. The breakeven is 2.50%. Three scenarios over the decade:

- **Inflation averages exactly 2.50%.** The nominal pays 4.30% nominal = 1.80% real (4.30% − 2.50%). The TIPS pays 1.80% real (its real yield) + 2.50% inflation pass-through = 4.30% nominal = 1.80% real. **Identical.** That's the breakeven by definition.
- **Inflation averages 4.00%** (a 1.5-point surprise above breakeven). The nominal still pays 4.30% nominal = 0.30% real. The TIPS pays 1.80% real + 4.00% = 5.80% nominal = 1.80% real. The TIPS beat the nominal by **1.50 points of real return per year** — exactly the size of the surprise.
- **Inflation averages 1.00%** (a 1.5-point shortfall). The nominal pays 4.30% nominal = 3.30% real. The TIPS pays 1.80% real + 1.00% = 2.80% nominal = 1.80% real. The nominal beat the TIPS by **1.50 points** — again, the size of the surprise, in the other direction.

*Your gain or loss versus the nominal bond is, almost exactly, the amount by which realized inflation differs from the breakeven — TIPS turn an inflation surprise directly into a relative return.*

## When TIPS beat nominal Treasuries, and when they lag

We can now state the rule cleanly and then dramatize it. **TIPS outperform nominal Treasuries when realized inflation exceeds the breakeven; they underperform when realized inflation falls short of the breakeven.** The TIPS holder's real return is roughly constant (their real yield, locked in); the nominal holder's real return swings with inflation. So the comparison is really a comparison of one steady line against one wildly variable one.

![A two-column comparison showing that when inflation surprises low the nominal Treasury wins while when inflation surprises high the TIPS wins, with the real returns worked out for each case](/imgs/blogs/tips-and-inflation-linked-bonds-protecting-purchasing-power-5.png)

Figure 5 sets the two worlds side by side, both starting from the same 2.5% breakeven. On the left, inflation surprises low (1% realized): the nominal bond earns a real windfall of +3.3% while the TIPS quietly earns its +1.8% real and lags. On the right, inflation surprises high (5% realized): the nominal bond's real return turns negative at −0.7% while the TIPS again earns its +1.8% real — and this time that steadiness is a big win. The asymmetry is the point: the TIPS holder is never hurt in real terms by inflation, but they pay for that protection by giving up the windfall when inflation disappoints.

There's an important second-order effect hiding here, and it's about *risk*, not just expected return. Even if you think inflation will average exactly the breakeven, the TIPS is the lower-risk choice in real terms, because its real return doesn't depend on the inflation outcome at all. A nominal bond's real return is hostage to inflation; a TIPS's is not. For an investor whose liabilities are real — a retiree who needs to buy real groceries, a pension that owes inflation-linked benefits — the TIPS is the closer match even when its *expected* return is identical, because it removes a risk they actually care about. This is the deep reason linkers exist: they let you match real assets to real liabilities. We have a whole sibling post on how pensions and insurers do exactly this in [immunization and duration matching](/blog/trading/fixed-income/immunization-and-duration-matching-how-pensions-and-insurers-hedge).

![A matrix showing TIPS total return across five inflation scenarios from deflation to an 8 percent spike, with the TIPS real return staying near 1.8 percent while the nominal real return swings widely](/imgs/blogs/tips-and-inflation-linked-bonds-protecting-purchasing-power-7.png)

Figure 7 lays out the full table. Read down the "TIPS real return" column and it barely moves — pinned near +1.8% across every scenario, from deflation to an 8% spike, because that's what a real yield means. Read down the "Nominal real return" column and it swings from a +5.3% windfall in deflation to a −3.7% beating in the 8% spike. The "Who wins" column flips at the breakeven: below 2.5%, the nominal bond; above it, the TIPS. The table is the entire thesis of the post in one picture — TIPS trade away upside in benign inflation for a hard floor under your purchasing power in bad inflation.

#### Worked example: the same \$10,000 over a decade, two bonds, two inflation worlds

You put \$10,000 into each of our two bonds and hold for ten years. Nominal at 4.30%, TIPS at 1.80% real, breakeven 2.50%.

*World A — inflation averages 1% (low surprise).* The nominal compounds at 4.30%: \$10,000 × 1.043^10 ≈ **\$15,230** nominal. The TIPS earns 1.80% real plus 1% inflation ≈ 2.80% nominal: \$10,000 × 1.028^10 ≈ **\$13,180** nominal. The nominal bond ends with about \$2,050 more dollars. In real (today's-purchasing-power) terms, dividing each by 1.01^10 ≈ 1.105: the nominal is worth ≈ \$13,780 real, the TIPS ≈ \$11,930 real. **The nominal won.**

*World B — inflation averages 5% (high surprise).* The nominal still compounds at 4.30%: ≈ **\$15,230** nominal. The TIPS earns 1.80% real plus 5% ≈ 6.80% nominal: \$10,000 × 1.068^10 ≈ **\$19,300** nominal. Now deflate each by 1.05^10 ≈ 1.629: the nominal is worth ≈ \$9,350 real (you *lost* purchasing power), the TIPS ≈ \$11,850 real (you kept and grew it). **The TIPS won, and the nominal bond actually went backwards in real terms.**

*Notice the TIPS landed near \$11,900 real in both worlds, while the nominal bond's real outcome lurched from \$13,780 to \$9,350 — the TIPS bought you certainty about the thing you actually care about, your purchasing power.*

## Real yields, gold, and why TIPS move the wider world

We've been treating the TIPS real yield as a private property of one bond. It is much bigger than that. The real yield the TIPS market prints is, arguably, the single most important number in all of asset pricing, because *everything* is valued by discounting future cash flows, and the real yield is the real cost of waiting. When the real yield is high, a dollar promised far in the future is worth little today, so long-duration assets — growth stocks, long bonds, real estate, gold — are cheap. When the real yield is low or negative, that same future dollar is worth a lot today, so those assets get bid up. This is why a single TIPS yield ripples out into stock multiples, the dollar, and commodities — a thread we develop across the whole [cross-asset playbook](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything).

The cleanest, most-watched manifestation of this is the inverse dance between real yields and the price of **gold**. Gold is the purest test case because it has *no cash flow at all* — no coupon, no dividend, no earnings. Its only "yield" is whatever price appreciation you hope for. That means gold's chief competitor is the real return on safe assets: if you can earn a guaranteed +2% real in a TIPS, holding a metal that yields nothing has a real opportunity cost of 2% a year. But if the TIPS real yield is −1%, holding gold actually *beats* the safe real alternative even before any price gain — the opportunity cost has gone negative. So as real yields fall, the case for gold strengthens, and as they rise, it weakens.

![An illustrative two-line chart showing the real yield on inflation-protected bonds falling as the gold price rises and then reversing, an inverse relationship driven by the opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding asset](/imgs/blogs/tips-and-inflation-linked-bonds-protecting-purchasing-power-3.png)

Figure 3 sketches the relationship across an inflation cycle. The solid line is the TIPS real yield; the dashed line is the gold price. Watch them move as mirror images: as the real yield falls toward and below zero (early-to-mid cycle, when the central bank is cutting and suppressing rates), gold climbs; as the real yield rises again (late cycle, when the central bank is hiking to fight inflation), gold gives much of it back. The two lines cross because the relationship is genuinely inverse — *low or negative real yields are gold's best friend, and rising real yields are its worst enemy*. This is illustrative, not mechanical: gold also responds to the dollar, to geopolitics, and to central-bank buying, so the fit is loose in any given month. But over the medium term, the real yield is gold's gravitational center, which is why gold traders keep the 10-year TIPS yield on the same screen as the gold chart.

The same logic explains why TIPS real yields matter for the **dollar** and for **emerging markets**. A rising US real yield pulls global capital toward dollar assets (you get paid more, in real terms, to hold them), strengthening the dollar and tightening conditions everywhere — which is hard on commodities and on emerging-market borrowers with dollar debt. A falling real yield does the reverse, weakening the dollar and loosening global conditions. The TIPS market is, in this sense, a control panel for global liquidity: the real yield it prints is the price the whole world pays to defer consumption, and when that price moves, every other price moves with it.

#### Worked example: gold's opportunity cost flips with the real yield

You're deciding whether to hold \$10,000 in gold or in a 10-year TIPS, purely on opportunity cost. *Case 1: the TIPS real yield is +2.0%.* Holding gold instead means forgoing a guaranteed +2.0% real, or about \$200 of real purchasing power per year — gold has to appreciate at least 2% a year in real terms just to keep pace. The bar is high. *Case 2: the TIPS real yield is −1.0%.* Now holding the "safe" TIPS guarantees you *lose* 1.0% real, about \$100 a year of purchasing power. Gold, which merely holds its real value on average, beats that by a full point a year before any rally. The bar has gone negative, and capital floods toward gold. Nothing about gold itself changed between the two cases — only the real yield it competes against did. *Gold doesn't rally because investors suddenly love metal; it rallies because the real yield on its alternative collapsed, which is why the TIPS market, not the gold market, often leads the move.*

## The deflation floor: a free option you didn't pay for

Indexing cuts both ways. If the CPI can rise and lift your principal, it can also fall — *deflation* — and drag your principal down. During a deflationary stretch, the index ratio drops below 1.000, your indexed principal falls below \$1,000, and your coupons (a fixed rate on a shrinking base) shrink too. That's the symmetric, fair version of the deal.

But TIPS include a one-sided sweetener: the **deflation floor**. At maturity, you are repaid the **greater of the inflation-adjusted principal or the original par value**. In other words, no matter how much cumulative deflation has occurred over the bond's life, your principal repayment cannot fall below the \$1,000 you started with. The downside on principal is capped at zero; the upside is unlimited. This is, in effect, a free embedded put option on deflation that the Treasury hands you at no explicit charge.

![An illustrative chart where the inflation-adjusted principal dips below the 1000 dollar par line during deflation but the maturity payout is floored back up to 1000 dollars at par](/imgs/blogs/tips-and-inflation-linked-bonds-protecting-purchasing-power-6.png)

Figure 6 shows the floor at work. The dashed line is par, \$1,000. The solid curve is the inflation-adjusted principal, which dips below par as deflation sets in mid-life. Left alone, the bond would repay that lower adjusted figure — but the green dot at maturity is lifted back up to \$1,000 by the floor. You get your original par back even though the index ratio ended below 1.000. Two caveats keep this honest. First, the floor applies only to the **maturity** repayment of principal — if you *sell* before maturity, you sell at the market price, which can be below par during deflation, so the floor doesn't protect a mid-life sale. Second, the floor protects principal, not coupons; your coupons can still shrink while prices fall, because they're a fixed rate on the deflated principal.

The floor also matters for *newly issued* versus *seasoned* TIPS. A brand-new TIPS, issued at an index ratio of 1.000, has its full deflation protection ahead of it — the floor is "at the money." A seasoned TIPS that has already accrued years of inflation (index ratio well above 1.000) has a principal far above par, so deflation would have to be enormous and sustained to bring it back down to the \$1,000 floor; its embedded deflation option is deep out of the money and nearly worthless. This is why, after the deflation scare of 2008–2009, newly issued TIPS with fresh floors traded at a premium to older seasoned ones with the same maturity — the market was paying up for floor protection.

#### Worked example: the floor catches a deflationary decade

You buy a new \$1,000 TIPS at par (index ratio 1.000). Then the economy enters a Japan-style deflationary funk: prices fall a cumulative 6% over the bond's life, so by maturity the index ratio is 0.940 and the inflation-adjusted principal is \$1,000 × 0.940 = **\$940.00**. Without the floor, you'd be repaid \$940 — a \$60 loss of principal on top of the meager coupons. *With* the floor, you are repaid **max(\$940, \$1,000) = \$1,000.00**. The floor handed you \$60 of protection you never paid an explicit premium for. (Your coupons did shrink along the way — a 1% rate on a principal averaging below \$1,000 — but your principal came home whole.)

*The deflation floor turns a TIPS into a heads-you-keep-inflation-upside, tails-your-principal-is-protected instrument — the rare case in finance where you get an option for free.*

## The phantom income problem: taxed on money you haven't received

Here is the wrinkle that sends most TIPS into retirement accounts. The IRS treats the annual inflation adjustment to your principal as **taxable interest income in the year it accrues** — even though you don't actually receive that money until the bond matures. The grown-up principal is locked inside the bond; you can't spend it; but you owe tax on its growth right now. This mismatch — taxable income with no matching cash — is called **phantom income** (or "phantom" or "imputed" income).

This is genuinely strange and worth restating. On a TIPS in a taxable account, you owe federal income tax each year on two things: (1) the coupons you actually received, and (2) the increase in your indexed principal, which you did *not* receive. In a year of high inflation, that principal accrual can dwarf the small coupon — so your tax bill can exceed your actual cash income from the bond. You might have to come up with cash from elsewhere just to pay tax on phantom gains. (TIPS are exempt from *state and local* income tax, like all Treasuries, which softens the blow but doesn't fix the timing.)

The standard fix is simple: hold TIPS in a **tax-advantaged account** — a traditional IRA, a 401(k), or similar — where the annual accrual isn't taxed as it happens. For investors who want inflation protection in a taxable account without the phantom-income headache, the usual alternatives are **I Bonds** (US savings bonds whose inflation adjustment is tax-deferred until redemption, though with purchase limits) or a **TIPS mutual fund or ETF**, which distributes the accrued principal growth as a cash dividend each year — converting phantom income into real, spendable income that you're then taxed on, which at least matches the cash to the bill.

#### Worked example: the year the tax bill beats the cash

You hold \$100,000 of a 1%-coupon TIPS in a taxable account, and inflation runs 6% in a year. Your **cash** income is the coupon: 1% on a principal averaging ~\$103,000 ≈ **\$1,030**. But your **taxable** income is that \$1,030 coupon *plus* the principal accrual of about 6% × \$100,000 = **\$6,000** — a total of \$7,030 of taxable income. At a 32% marginal federal rate, your tax is 0.32 × \$7,030 ≈ **\$2,250**. So you received \$1,030 in cash and owe \$2,250 in tax — you're \$1,220 out of pocket on a bond that's supposedly paying you. You have to fund that gap from other money. *This is exactly why the conventional wisdom is to hold TIPS in a retirement account: in a taxable one, phantom income can turn a positive-yielding bond into a negative cash-flow position in any high-inflation year.*

## Duration, real duration, and how TIPS prices still move

It's tempting to think a TIPS is immune to the seesaw between bond prices and yields that governs every other bond — but it isn't. TIPS still have **duration**, the sensitivity of their price to a change in yield (we cover this in depth in [duration, the most important number in fixed income](/blog/trading/fixed-income/duration-the-most-important-number-in-fixed-income)). The crucial twist is *which* yield they're sensitive to. A nominal bond's price moves with the **nominal yield**; a TIPS's price moves with the **real yield**. So a TIPS has **real-rate duration** — when real yields rise, TIPS prices fall, and by roughly duration-times-the-yield-change, just like any bond.

This is why TIPS are not a perfect "inflation hedge" in the short run, even though they're a perfect inflation hedge if held to maturity. If inflation jumps but real yields also jump (for instance, because the Federal Reserve hikes aggressively to fight that inflation), a TIPS can *lose price* even as its principal accrues — the real-rate loss can swamp the inflation gain over short horizons. The inflation protection is delivered through the principal accrual and is guaranteed only if you hold to maturity; the day-to-day price is at the mercy of real yields. This subtlety burned a lot of investors in 2022, as we'll see.

#### Worked example: a TIPS that loses money in a year of high inflation

You own a 10-year TIPS with a real duration of about 8.5. Over a year, inflation runs a hot 7%, so your principal accrues +7% — a tailwind. But the Fed hikes hard and the real yield on your TIPS jumps from 0.0% to +1.5%, a move of +1.5 percentage points (150 bps). The price effect is roughly −duration × yield change = −8.5 × 1.5% ≈ **−12.75%**. Net for the year: +7% principal accrual − 12.75% price loss ≈ **−5.75%**. You held the canonical inflation hedge through a year of 7% inflation and still lost about 5.75% in market value. *Held to maturity, your real return is still locked at the real yield you bought; but mark-to-market, a TIPS is a real-rate bond first and an inflation hedge second, and a violent rise in real yields can overwhelm even a big inflation accrual.*

This two-factor view — that a nominal bond's price depends on the *nominal* yield, while a TIPS's depends on the *real* yield, and the two differ by the breakeven — is exactly what lets sophisticated investors trade inflation by itself. If you buy a TIPS and simultaneously *short* a nominal Treasury of the same duration, the two real-rate exposures cancel: when real yields move, both legs move together and net to roughly zero. What's left is a clean bet on the *breakeven* — you make money if realized and expected inflation rise, and lose if they fall, with the interest-rate risk hedged out. This long-TIPS/short-nominal package is called a **breakeven trade** (or "buying breakevens"), and it's how desks express a pure inflation view without taking a duration bet. The mirror image — short TIPS, long nominal — is "selling breakevens," a bet that the market has over-priced inflation. The mechanics are the same seesaw between price and yield covered in [why bond prices move when rates move](/blog/trading/fixed-income/why-bond-prices-move-when-rates-move-and-by-how-much); the trick is that combining two bonds lets you isolate the one factor you actually have a view on.

#### Worked example: a breakeven trade isolates the inflation bet

You think inflation will run hotter than the 2.5% the market has priced. You buy \$1,000,000 of 10-year TIPS and short \$1,000,000 of the duration-matched 10-year nominal. *Scenario 1: real yields rise 0.5% but the breakeven is unchanged.* The TIPS loses ≈ 0.5% × 8.5 ≈ \$42,500 in price; the short nominal *gains* a nearly identical amount because nominal yields rose the same 0.5% (real moved, inflation didn't). Net ≈ \$0 — the duration washed out. *Scenario 2: the breakeven widens 0.5% (inflation expectations rise) with real yields flat.* Now the nominal yield rises 0.5% (real flat + breakeven up 0.5%), so your short nominal *gains* ≈ \$42,500, while the TIPS price is unchanged (its real yield didn't move). Net ≈ **+\$42,500** — pure profit on the inflation view. *By pairing the two bonds, you converted a messy bond position with two risks into a clean instrument with one: a bet on inflation expectations alone.*

## Linkers around the world: the same idea, different plumbing

The US is a relative latecomer to inflation-linked bonds. The pioneer was the United Kingdom, which began issuing **index-linked gilts** in 1981, tied to the (now-deprecated) RPI and later RPI/CPI measures. The UK linker market is unusually large relative to the economy, because British pension funds have enormous *real* liabilities (inflation-linked pensions) and buy linkers to match them — a structural demand that, as we'll see, became dangerous in 2022.

The US Treasury launched **TIPS** in 1997. France issues **OATi** and **OAT€i** (linked to French and eurozone inflation respectively); Germany, Italy, and others issue euro-area linkers tied to the harmonized HICP index. Canada has **Real Return Bonds** (though it stopped issuing them in 2022), Japan issues JGBi, and emerging markets from Brazil to Mexico to Israel have deep linker markets — often *more* important domestically than in developed markets, because their populations have lived through high inflation and demand protection. The mechanics differ in the details (which index, what lag, how the floor works, whether coupons or principal are indexed), but the core idea is universal: a bond that promises a real return and lets the issuer, not the saver, carry inflation risk.

One structural variant worth knowing: most linkers, including TIPS, are **capital-indexed** — the principal accretes and the coupon rides on it, exactly as we've described. A few older designs are **interest-indexed** (only the coupon is adjusted; principal stays at par) or **current-pay**. Capital-indexed is now the global standard because it most cleanly delivers a constant real yield.

## Common misconceptions

**"TIPS are a bet that inflation will be high."** No — they're a bet that inflation will be *higher than the breakeven the market has already priced in*. If inflation comes in exactly at the breakeven, TIPS and nominal Treasuries deliver the same return. You only profit relative to nominal bonds from an inflation *surprise* to the upside. Buying TIPS because "inflation is rising" when everyone already sees it rising means you've paid for that expectation in a wider breakeven and earn nothing extra unless reality exceeds it.

**"TIPS always protect you from inflation."** Held to maturity, yes — your principal and coupons track CPI and your real yield is locked. But *in the short run, mark-to-market*, TIPS can fall in price even during high inflation if real yields rise (as in 2022). The protection comes through the principal accrual and is guaranteed only at maturity; the daily price is governed by real yields, and a TIPS is a real-rate bond first. Confusing the maturity guarantee with the daily price is the single most common and costly TIPS mistake.

**"The TIPS yield you see quoted is your total return."** The quoted yield is the *real* yield — your return *above* inflation. Your *nominal* total return is that real yield *plus* whatever inflation turns out to be. A TIPS quoted at "1.8%" is not paying you 1.8% in dollars; it's paying you 1.8% plus the CPI. A negative quoted real yield (which happened for much of 2020–2021) does not mean you lose money in dollars — it means you'll earn slightly less than inflation, which can still be a positive nominal return.

**"TIPS have no downside because of the deflation floor."** The floor only protects the *principal repaid at maturity* and only down to the original par. It doesn't protect you if you *sell* early (you sell at market price, which can be below par), it doesn't protect your coupons (which shrink in deflation), and it doesn't protect against a rise in real yields (which lowers the price). The floor is a nice free option on deflation, not a guarantee against any loss.

**"TIPS are tax-free."** They're exempt from *state and local* income tax, like all Treasuries — but not federal. Worse, the annual inflation adjustment to principal is federally taxable as it accrues, even though you don't receive the cash until maturity (the phantom-income problem). In a high-inflation year, the tax bill on a taxable-account TIPS can exceed the cash it pays you. This is why TIPS belong in tax-sheltered accounts for most investors.

**"A TIPS fund gives you the same protection as a TIPS held to maturity."** Not quite. An individual TIPS held to maturity gives you a *guaranteed* real return and the deflation floor. A TIPS fund or ETF never matures — it holds a rolling ladder of bonds — so it never delivers that hold-to-maturity guarantee; you're exposed to the fund's price (driven by real yields) whenever you sell. A fund is more convenient and solves phantom income via cash distributions, but it trades the maturity guarantee for perpetual real-rate risk.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2008 deflation scare and the breakeven collapse.** In the depths of the 2008 financial crisis, the 10-year breakeven inflation rate briefly went *negative* — the market was implicitly pricing in *falling* prices over the next decade. Part of that was genuine deflation fear, but a large part was a liquidity dislocation: terrified investors fled to the most liquid asset on earth, on-the-run nominal Treasuries, and dumped the less-liquid TIPS, spiking real yields and crushing measured breakevens far below any sane inflation forecast. The episode is the textbook illustration that the breakeven is *expected inflation plus a liquidity-and-risk wedge*, not a pure forecast — and that in a crisis, the wedge can dominate. It also made the deflation floor suddenly valuable: newly issued TIPS with fresh, at-the-money floors traded richer than seasoned TIPS for years afterward.

**The 2010–2021 era of negative real yields.** For much of the post-crisis decade, and especially during the pandemic, TIPS real yields fell below zero — the 10-year real yield bottomed around −1.2% in 2021. A negative real yield means investors were willing to *accept* a guaranteed loss of purchasing power, paying for the certainty of a known real outcome amid enormous uncertainty (and because the Fed's bond-buying, covered in [the central bank toolkit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance), suppressed real yields directly). This period is also the cleanest real-world demonstration of the gold relationship in Figure 3: as real yields ground lower and lower, gold — which pays no income and competes directly with real-yielding bonds — rose to record highs, because the opportunity cost of holding it had collapsed. When real yields are deeply negative, a metal that yields nothing isn't giving up much.

**2022: the year TIPS "failed" — and didn't.** 2022 is the most instructive episode for understanding what TIPS do and don't do. US inflation hit roughly 9% (a 40-year high), so a naive investor expected TIPS to soar. Instead, the Bloomberg US TIPS index fell about 12% on the year. What happened? The Fed hiked rates from near zero to over 4%, and real yields rocketed from deeply negative to clearly positive — the 10-year real yield rose well over two percentage points. For a TIPS with a real duration around 7–8, that real-rate move produced a double-digit *price* loss that overwhelmed the inflation accrual, exactly as in our duration worked example. TIPS did their job in the sense that they beat *nominal* Treasuries (which fell even harder, about −16% to −17% for long maturities, since they had no inflation accrual cushion at all). But the year was a brutal lesson that "inflation protection" is a hold-to-maturity property, not a short-run one — and that a TIPS is a real-rate instrument first.

**The UK 2022 gilt crisis and the leverage in linkers.** In September 2022, after a UK mini-budget spooked markets, yields on UK gilts — including index-linked gilts — spiked violently. British pension funds had used **liability-driven investment** (LDI) strategies, levering up their linker holdings to match their long-dated real liabilities. As linker prices crashed, the leverage triggered collateral calls; to meet them, funds had to sell *more* gilts, driving prices down further in a doom loop that forced an emergency Bank of England intervention. The episode is a reminder that inflation-linked bonds, despite their conservative purpose, sit inside leveraged structures whose failure can threaten financial stability — a theme we develop in [sovereign debt and the bond vigilantes](/blog/trading/macro-trading/sovereign-debt-and-the-bond-vigilantes).

**The TIPS-gold dance, illustrated.** The single most reliable relationship in the TIPS world is the inverse one between real yields and gold, sketched in Figure 3. Gold is the canonical asset that pays no cash flow at all, so its appeal rises and falls with the *real* return available on safe alternatives. When TIPS real yields fall (especially into negative territory), holding gold costs you almost nothing in forgone real income, and gold tends to rally; when real yields rise, the opportunity cost of holding a yield-less metal climbs and gold tends to fall. The relationship is illustrative rather than mechanical — gold also responds to the dollar, geopolitics, and central-bank buying — but real yields are the gravitational center, which is why gold traders watch the 10-year TIPS yield as closely as gold's own chart.

**Emerging-market linkers and lived inflation.** In countries that have actually lived through high inflation — Brazil, Mexico, Turkey, Israel — inflation-linked bonds are often a *larger* and more central part of the local debt market than in the US or Germany. Savers who remember their currency losing half its value in a year do not trust nominal promises, so governments must offer real protection to borrow long-term at all. These markets are the clearest demonstration of *why* linkers exist: the deeper a society's memory of inflation, the more it demands that the issuer, not the saver, carry inflation risk.

## When this matters to you, and further reading

For most individual investors, the practical takeaways are concrete. If you have a long-horizon, *real* goal — retirement spending, a future tuition bill, anything you'll pay for in tomorrow's prices — TIPS held to maturity (or a TIPS ladder) are the one instrument that locks in a known *purchasing-power* outcome, which no nominal bond can do. Hold them in a tax-advantaged account to dodge phantom income, or use I Bonds for the taxable-account version. Remember that you're not betting on high inflation, only on inflation beating the breakeven, and that a TIPS fund's price will gyrate with real yields in the short run even when it's doing its long-run job.

For understanding markets, the TIPS curve is one of the most information-rich objects in finance: it hands you the market's real yields, its inflation expectations (the breakeven), and — through the gold and dollar relationships — a window into global risk appetite, all at once.
