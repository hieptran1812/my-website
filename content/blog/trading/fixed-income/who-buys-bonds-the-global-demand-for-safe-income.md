> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Bonds are not bought because people *like* lending money. They are bought because the largest pools of money on earth are structurally forced to own safe income, and bonds are the only assets that fit. The demand side, not the supply side, is what makes the bond market the deepest market in the world.
>
> - There are really only seven kinds of bond buyer: **commercial banks, insurers, pension funds, central banks, foreign governments, mutual funds & ETFs, and households**. Each has a built-in reason it *must* hold bonds — not a preference, a requirement.
> - A bank holds Treasuries as **high-quality liquid assets (HQLA)** it can sell or pledge in seconds; an insurer holds long bonds to **match decades-long liabilities**; a pension uses bonds to **lock in funding (LDI)**; a central bank holds them as **FX reserves and as a policy tool**.
> - The world is **short of safe assets**. The supply of truly safe, deep, liquid bonds — above all US Treasuries — is smaller than the demand for them, which is why their yields sit lower than supply-and-demand for risky assets would suggest.
> - Roughly **\$27 trillion** of US Treasuries are outstanding (as of early 2025), and no single buyer owns more than about a fifth. The Fed, foreign holders, and a spread of domestic institutions each hold a large slice — which is exactly why the market is so hard to break.
> - When you understand *who* must buy bonds and *why*, you understand why a government can borrow trillions without running out of lenders, why falling rates can quietly bankrupt a bank (the SVB story), and why a trade surplus on the other side of the planet pushes your mortgage rate down.

Here is a fact that surprises almost everyone: the biggest market in the world is not the stock market. It is the bond market. Global bonds outstanding total well over \$130 trillion, dwarfing the roughly \$110 trillion of global equities — and the US Treasury market alone, at about \$27 trillion of marketable debt in early 2025, trades hundreds of billions of dollars on a quiet day. Stocks get the headlines. Bonds move the world.

But who actually *buys* all this debt? When the US Treasury auctions \$100 billion of new notes on a Tuesday afternoon, who shows up with the cash? It is tempting to picture rich individuals choosing bonds over stocks the way you might choose a savings account over a casino. That picture is almost entirely wrong. The bond market is not powered by people who *prefer* safety. It is powered by enormous institutions that are *structurally required* to own safe income — banks that must hold a liquid buffer by law, insurers that have promised to pay claims thirty years from now, pension funds that owe retirees a fixed monthly check, central banks sitting on trillions of foreign-currency reserves. For all of them, owning bonds is not a choice on a menu. It is part of the machinery of what they are.

The figure above is the mental model for this entire post: a small set of giant buyers, each with a concrete, built-in reason it needs bonds. Once you can name those seven buyers and the reason behind each one, the whole bond market stops looking mysterious. You will understand why a government drowning in debt never seems to run out of lenders, why a "safe" bank can collapse in a week, and why the dollar's role in global trade quietly finances the US deficit. Let us build it from zero.

## Foundations: the building blocks of bond demand

Before we meet the buyers, we need a shared vocabulary. The reader with no finance background needs four ideas in place: what a bond actually is, what "yield" means, what makes an asset "safe," and what "liquidity" means. A practitioner can skim this section; a beginner cannot proceed without it.

### What a bond is, in one sentence

A **bond is a tradable loan.** When you buy a bond, you are lending money to whoever issued it — a government, a company, a city. In return, the issuer promises two things: to pay you a stream of fixed interest payments (called **coupons**) along the way, and to pay back the original amount you lent (the **face value**, or **par**) on a fixed future date (the **maturity**). That is the entire contract. A 5-year, \$1,000 bond with a 4% coupon means: you lend \$1,000 today, you collect \$40 a year (4% of \$1,000) for five years, and at the end of year five you get your \$1,000 back.

The word that does the heavy lifting is *tradable.* Unlike a personal loan, a bond can be sold to someone else before it matures. That is what makes a bond an *asset* — a thing you can own, value, and resell — rather than just a private IOU. The bond market is simply the place where these tradable loans change hands.

We will use one recurring example throughout this post: **a 5-year \$1,000 par note with a 4% coupon.** Sometimes it is a US Treasury note (issued by the US government, considered the safest borrower on earth); sometimes it is a bond from a fictional company we will call **Northwind Corp** (a corporate borrower, riskier than the government). Keeping the same numbers lets the ideas compound.

### Yield: the return you actually earn

The **yield** of a bond is the annual return you earn if you buy it at today's price and hold it. If a \$1,000 bond pays a \$40 coupon and you buy it for exactly \$1,000, your yield is \$40 ÷ \$1,000 = 4%. Simple. But here is the twist that runs through all of fixed income: bond prices move, and **when the price changes, the yield changes the opposite way.** If that same bond's price falls to \$950, your \$40 coupon is now \$40 ÷ \$950 ≈ 4.2% — a higher yield, because you paid less for the same fixed payments. Price down, yield up. Price up, yield down. This seesaw is the heart of bond math (the next post in this series covers it in full), and for now you only need the direction: **price and yield move in opposite directions.**

One unit of jargon you will meet constantly: a **basis point** is one hundredth of a percentage point — 0.01%. So when someone says "the 10-year yield rose 40 basis points," they mean it went up by 0.40%, say from 4.10% to 4.50%. Traders quote bond moves in basis points (often written "bps") because the numbers are small and precision matters.

### What "safe" really means

When we call a bond "safe," we mean something specific: a high probability that you will get your coupons and your principal back, *in full and on time.* The risk that you will not is called **credit risk** or **default risk** — the chance the borrower cannot or will not pay. A US Treasury bond is considered the closest thing to "risk-free" because the US government has never failed to pay its dollar-denominated debt and can, in the last resort, create the dollars to do so. A bond from Northwind Corp is less safe, because a company can go bankrupt.

But "safe" has a second, subtler meaning that matters enormously for the buyers in this post. A safe asset is one whose value you do not have to *think* about. You can accept it as collateral, hold it as a buffer, or hand it to a counterparty, and everyone agrees on what it is worth without doing any analysis. Treasuries are "information-insensitive": nobody runs a credit check before accepting a Treasury bill. That property — being safe *and* universally accepted *without analysis* — is what makes a true safe asset so special and so scarce.

### Liquidity: how fast you can turn it into cash

**Liquidity** is the ease with which you can sell an asset quickly, in size, without moving its price. Cash is perfectly liquid. A house is illiquid — selling it takes months and the price depends on who shows up. US Treasuries are among the most liquid assets on earth: you can sell \$1 billion of them in minutes at a price within a hair of where they last traded, because there is always a buyer. This matters because several of our buyers — banks above all — do not just want safety; they need to be able to convert their bonds into cash *instantly* when depositors ask for their money. A safe asset that you cannot sell quickly is, for a bank, not safe at all.

### Price-insensitive versus price-sensitive buyers

One more distinction will pay off repeatedly, so let us name it now. Bond buyers fall into two camps by *how they react to the price.* A **price-insensitive buyer** buys (or sells) almost regardless of the yield, because their reason for holding bonds has nothing to do with the return: a bank meeting a regulatory buffer, a central bank running QE, a foreign reserve manager parking trade surpluses. They will own the bonds at 1% or at 5%, because owning them is the point. A **price-sensitive buyer** buys *more* when bonds look cheap (high yield) and less when they look expensive (low yield), because they are trying to earn a return: a bond fund chasing yield, a household deciding between bonds and stocks, a hedge fund running a relative-value trade.

This split matters because it explains the *resilience* of the bond market. The price-insensitive buyers form a stable floor of demand that shows up no matter what; the price-sensitive buyers are the ones who flex to clear whatever supply is left, at whatever yield it takes. When a government issues a flood of new debt, the price-insensitive base absorbs a chunk automatically, and the price-sensitive marginal buyers absorb the rest by demanding a slightly higher yield. The market clears on *price,* not on whether buyers exist. Keep this two-camp lens in mind as we meet each buyer — it is the single most useful way to predict how the buy side behaves under stress.

With those ideas — tradable loan, yield, safety, liquidity, and the price-sensitivity split — we can now meet the buyers and see exactly why each one needs what bonds provide.

## The seven buyers, and why each one structurally needs bonds

Almost every bond in the world is owned by one of seven kinds of buyer. They differ in *why* they hold bonds, *which* bonds they hold, and *how* they behave when prices move. Understanding those differences is the whole game, because a market dominated by buyers who *must* hold bonds behaves very differently from one dominated by buyers who *choose* to. Let us take them one at a time.

## Commercial banks: bonds as a liquid buffer and as collateral

Start with the buyer most people forget: the bank where you keep your checking account. A bank is, at heart, a machine that borrows short and lends long. It borrows from you — your deposit is a loan you make to the bank, repayable on demand — and it lends that money out for years as mortgages and business loans. That maturity mismatch is the bank's whole business model, and also its central danger: depositors can ask for their money back at any time, but the bank's loans are locked up for years.

To survive that mismatch, a bank must hold a slice of assets it can turn into cash *immediately* to meet withdrawals. After the 2008 financial crisis, regulators made this a legal requirement. Banks must hold enough **High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA)** — assets that can be sold or pledged for cash in a stress event without taking a big loss — to survive 30 days of heavy outflows. This rule is called the **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)**. And the gold standard of HQLA is, you guessed it, government bonds. Cash and central-bank reserves count as the top tier; US Treasuries and the safest agency mortgage bonds count as the next. So every large bank in the world is *legally obligated* to own a pile of Treasuries.

![A bank balance sheet showing assets including Treasuries as liquid buffer against deposit liabilities](/imgs/blogs/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income-5.png)

The figure shows why. On the left are the bank's assets: cash and reserves at the top (the most liquid), then Treasuries and agency mortgage bonds as the HQLA buffer, then the illiquid, higher-yielding loans that earn the bank its profit. On the right are the liabilities: deposits the bank owes you on demand, other borrowed funds, and a thin sliver of equity (the owners' capital, the cushion that absorbs losses). The Treasuries exist precisely to bridge the gap between "deposits I might have to repay tomorrow" and "loans I cannot get back for years."

But there is a second reason banks hold bonds, even more pervasive than the buffer: **collateral.** Modern finance runs on short-term borrowing called **repo** (short for *repurchase agreement*) — a loan where you hand over a bond as collateral and get cash, agreeing to buy the bond back the next day at a tiny premium. Trillions of dollars of repo trade every day, and the collateral that greases it is, overwhelmingly, Treasuries. A bank that owns Treasuries can always raise cash against them; a bank that owns only loans cannot. Treasuries are the chips in the casino of money markets.

#### Worked example: how much HQLA a bank must hold

Consider a mid-sized bank, "Lakeshore Bank," with \$100 billion of deposits. Regulators model a stress scenario in which a fraction of those deposits flee in 30 days — say a 10% "run-off" on stable retail deposits, more on flighty wholesale money. Suppose the modeled 30-day outflow comes to \$15 billion. Under the Liquidity Coverage Ratio, Lakeshore must hold at least \$15 billion of HQLA so it can cover that outflow without collapsing. If it holds \$5 billion in cash and reserves, it needs roughly \$10 billion more in Treasuries and top-rated agency bonds. That \$10 billion is *demand for bonds created by a rule,* not by any view on whether bonds are a good investment. Multiply this across thousands of banks and you get one of the largest, most reliable sources of bond demand on earth.

*The intuition: a bank does not buy Treasuries because it expects them to go up — it buys them because the law and the plumbing of money require a liquid, pledgeable buffer, and Treasuries are the only asset deep enough to be that buffer.*

### The SVB cautionary note: safe credit, dangerous rates

Here is the trap hidden inside that comfortable story. A Treasury bond has essentially *no credit risk* — the US government will pay you back. But it still has **interest-rate risk**: remember the seesaw, where price and yield move opposite ways. When interest rates *rise*, the price of existing bonds *falls*, because new bonds now pay more and nobody wants the old low-coupon ones at the old price. A 10-year Treasury bought at a 1.5% yield in 2021 was worth far less in 2022 after yields jumped above 4% — not because the government got riskier, but because rates rose. The bond was safe; the *price* was not.

This is exactly what sank **Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)** in March 2023. SVB had taken in a flood of deposits during the 2020–2021 tech boom and parked a huge share of them in long-dated Treasuries and agency mortgage bonds — the safest credits in the world. Then the Fed raised rates faster than at any time in forty years, and the market value of those bonds fell by tens of billions of dollars. On paper, SVB was sitting on enormous unrealized losses. When nervous depositors started pulling money out, SVB was forced to *sell* those bonds to raise cash — turning the paper loss into a real one, blowing a hole in its capital, and triggering a classic bank run. Within 48 hours, the bank was gone.

The lesson, which we will return to in the misconceptions section, is sharp: **"safe asset" means safe from default, not safe from price moves.** A bank that holds Treasuries to be liquid can still be destroyed by them if it holds the wrong *duration* and rates move against it. (Duration — a bond's sensitivity to rate moves — gets its own deep treatment later in this series; for now, just hold the idea that long bonds swing more than short ones.) This is the single most important nuance about bank bond-buying, and SVB is the textbook case. For the full story, see [SVB, Credit Suisse and the 2023 bank runs](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs).

## Insurers: matching long-dated liabilities

The next great buyer is the insurance industry, and it owns bonds for a completely different reason than banks do. A life insurer or an annuity provider sells a product that is, in effect, a promise to pay money far in the future. You pay premiums now; the insurer pays out decades from now — when you die, when your annuity starts, when a long-term claim comes due. The insurer's **liabilities** (what it owes) are therefore long-dated and highly predictable: it knows, from mortality tables and contract terms, roughly how much it must pay out in year 5, year 15, year 30.

The whole art of running an insurer is **asset-liability matching**: building a portfolio of assets whose cash flows arrive *exactly when the liabilities come due.* And what asset produces a known stream of cash flows on a known schedule for decades? A bond. A 30-year bond pays a fixed coupon every year and returns its principal in year 30 — a perfect building block for funding a claim due in year 30. Insurers are, by design, among the largest and most patient holders of long-dated bonds in the world.

![An insurer balance sheet comparison showing mismatched short assets versus matched long bonds funding long liabilities](/imgs/blogs/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income-2.png)

The figure makes the danger of *not* matching concrete. On the left is the mismatched insurer: its liabilities stretch out 30 years, but it foolishly holds only cash and short bills. Every time those bills mature, it must reinvest at whatever rate the market offers — and if rates fall, the income it earns may no longer be enough to fund the promises it made. On the right is the matched insurer: it holds 10-to-30-year bonds whose coupons and principal arrive as the claims fall due, locking in a yield today that funds the promise *regardless of where rates go.* This is why insurers reach for long bonds that almost nobody else wants — they have liabilities long enough to need them.

#### Worked example: funding a single future claim

Suppose our insurer must pay a \$1,000,000 claim in exactly 10 years. How does it fund that promise *today,* with certainty? It buys bonds whose value in 10 years will be \$1,000,000. The simplest version: buy a 10-year zero-coupon Treasury (a bond that pays no coupon, just returns face value at maturity) priced to yield, say, 4%. The price today of \$1,000,000 due in 10 years at 4% is \$1,000,000 ÷ (1.04)^10 ≈ \$1,000,000 ÷ 1.4802 ≈ **\$675,560.** The insurer invests \$675,560 today, holds the bond to maturity, and collects exactly \$1,000,000 in year 10 — fully funding the claim, no matter what rates do in between. The promise is *immunized.*

Now watch what happens if the insurer had instead bought a 1-year bill and planned to keep rolling it over. It puts in \$675,560 at 4% and after one year has \$702,582. But if rates then fall to 2%, the next nine years of rolling compound at only 2%, and the pot grows to roughly \$839,000 by year 10 — far short of the \$1,000,000 it owes. The mismatch left it exposed to falling rates, and falling rates broke the promise. The long bond removed that risk entirely.

*The intuition: an insurer buys long bonds not for yield but for certainty — locking a known cash flow against a known liability is the only way to keep a 30-year promise without betting on the path of rates.*

## Pension funds: liability-driven investing (LDI)

Pension funds are first cousins of insurers, and they own bonds for the same fundamental reason: they have promised someone a defined stream of future payments. A traditional **defined-benefit pension** promises a retiree a fixed monthly check for life. Those promised checks are the fund's liabilities — long-dated, predictable, and (crucially) more valuable when interest rates fall, for a reason worth pausing on.

Why are a pension's liabilities worth *more* when rates fall? Because the value today of a promise to pay money in the future is its **present value** — the future payments discounted back at the prevailing interest rate. When rates fall, the discount is gentler, so the present value of those distant promises *rises.* A pension that owes \$1 of payments in 20 years owes a bigger lump-sum-equivalent today when rates are 2% than when they are 5%. This means falling rates are *bad* for a pension's funding: its liabilities balloon. To protect itself, the pension wants to hold assets that *also* rise in value when rates fall — and long bonds are exactly that, because (the seesaw again) when rates fall, bond prices rise.

This insight gave rise to a strategy called **Liability-Driven Investing (LDI)**: a pension deliberately holds long-dated bonds (and interest-rate derivatives) so that when rates fall and its liabilities swell, its bond assets swell by a matching amount, leaving its *funding ratio* (assets ÷ liabilities) stable. The pension stops betting on rates entirely; it simply matches the rate sensitivity of its assets to that of its liabilities. LDI made pensions one of the largest structural buyers of long-dated government bonds, especially in the UK and Europe.

#### Worked example: why a pension matches duration, not just amount

A pension owes \$100 million of benefits with an average timing such that the liability has a **duration** of 18 years — meaning a 1% fall in rates raises the present value of those liabilities by roughly 18%, to about \$118 million. If the pension held only cash, its assets would not move, and a 1% rate drop would blow an \$18 million hole in its funding. So instead it buys long bonds with a matching 18-year duration. When rates fall 1%, those bonds rise about 18% in price — and the asset gain offsets the liability increase. The funding ratio stays put. The pension has *immunized* itself against rate moves by matching the duration of its assets to its liabilities, not merely the dollar amount.

*The intuition: a pension buys long bonds so that the same rate move that inflates what it owes also inflates what it owns — turning interest-rate risk from a threat into a wash.*

This same machinery had a famous failure, which we will revisit in the real-markets section: when UK gilt yields spiked in September 2022, LDI pensions that had *levered up* their bond exposure faced sudden collateral calls, were forced to dump gilts to raise cash, and pushed yields even higher — a doom loop that forced the Bank of England to intervene. The strategy that normally stabilizes pensions briefly destabilized the whole gilt market. For the policy angle, see [sovereign debt and the bond vigilantes](/blog/trading/macro-trading/sovereign-debt-and-the-bond-vigilantes).

## Central banks: reserves and the policy tool

Now we move from private buyers to official ones, and the logic shifts again. A central bank — the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan — holds bonds for two reasons, neither of which has anything to do with earning a return.

The first is **foreign-exchange reserves.** A central bank holds a stockpile of foreign assets — mostly other countries' government bonds — to defend its currency, to pay for imports in a crisis, and to maintain confidence. The world's reserve currency is the US dollar, so the dominant reserve asset is US Treasuries. When the People's Bank of China or the Bank of Japan holds "reserves," a large chunk of that is literally a portfolio of US government bonds. We will trace exactly how those reserves accumulate in the section on foreign governments — the two overlap, because a foreign central bank acting on behalf of its government is one of the biggest official Treasury buyers there is.

The second reason is the headline-making one: **monetary policy.** When a central bank wants to ease financial conditions and it has already cut its short-term policy rate to near zero, it can buy bonds directly in the open market — **quantitative easing (QE)**. By buying hundreds of billions of dollars of Treasuries and mortgage bonds, the central bank pushes their prices up and their yields down (the seesaw), lowering borrowing costs across the economy. The Fed's balance sheet swelled from under \$1 trillion before 2008 to roughly \$9 trillion at the 2022 peak, almost entirely through buying bonds. When it wants to tighten, it does the reverse — **quantitative tightening (QT)** — letting bonds mature without replacing them, shrinking its holdings.

This makes the central bank a uniquely powerful and uniquely *price-insensitive* buyer. A pension buys bonds to match liabilities; a bank buys them to satisfy a rule; but a central bank buys (or sells) them to *move the price on purpose.* It does not care what it pays — moving the price is the point. That is why QE is so potent and why the Fed's holdings show up as the single largest slice of Treasury ownership. For the mechanics, see [the central bank toolkit: rates, QE, QT, and forward guidance](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance) and [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).

#### Worked example: how QE moves a yield

Suppose 10-year Treasuries are trading to yield 3.0%, and the Fed announces it will buy \$80 billion of them a month. That is enormous, persistent, price-insensitive demand. As the Fed buys, prices rise and yields fall — say the 10-year drifts down to 2.5% over a few months. A 0.5% (50 basis point) drop in a 10-year yield translates, for a bond with about 8 years of duration, into roughly an 8 × 0.5% = **4% rise in price.** A holder of \$1,000,000 of those bonds sees their value rise about \$40,000. More importantly for the economy, every borrower whose rate is benchmarked to the 10-year — mortgages, corporate bonds — now pays less. The central bank used bond demand as a lever on the entire cost of credit.

*The intuition: a central bank is the one bond buyer that buys to change the price, not to live with it — which is precisely why its footprint dominates the ownership of the safe-asset market.*

It is worth separating the two central-bank motives cleanly, because beginners often blur them. **Reserve holding** is what a central bank does with *other countries'* bonds — a defensive stockpile of foreign safe assets (mostly US Treasuries) held to defend the currency and pay for imports. **QE** is what a central bank does with *its own country's* bonds — buying domestic government debt to push down domestic yields and ease financial conditions. The Bank of Japan holding US Treasuries is reserve management; the Bank of Japan holding Japanese government bonds is monetary policy. Both make the central bank a giant, price-insensitive bond owner, but for opposite-facing reasons — one looks abroad for safety, the other looks home to set the price of credit. A reserve manager wants the bonds to *stay valuable;* a QE central bank wants to *make* them more valuable. Holding both ideas at once is the key to reading central-bank balance sheets without confusion.

## Foreign governments: recycling trade surpluses into Treasuries

Some of the most consequential bond demand on earth comes from outside the country that issued the bonds. To see why, follow the dollars created by international trade.

When a country exports more than it imports — runs a **trade surplus** — it earns more foreign currency than it spends. A major exporter selling cars, electronics, and machinery to American consumers earns dollars. Those dollars pile up. The exporter's businesses sell them to their own central bank for local currency (so they can pay local wages and suppliers), and the central bank ends up holding a growing mountain of dollars as **FX reserves.** Now the central bank faces a problem: it cannot leave trillions of dollars sitting idle in a checking account. It needs to park them somewhere *safe, liquid, and dollar-denominated* — because the reserves must be deployable instantly in a crisis and must hold their value.

There is really only one market deep enough to absorb that much money safely: **US Treasuries.** No other asset is simultaneously safe, liquid, dollar-denominated, and *large enough* to soak up trillions without distorting the price. So the trade surplus loops straight back into Treasury demand. The dollars America sends abroad to buy goods come right back to finance the US government's borrowing. This is sometimes called the **dollar recycling** or **petrodollar/exporter** loop, and it is one of the deepest reasons the US can borrow so cheaply.

![A pipeline showing a trade surplus accumulating dollar reserves recycled by a foreign central bank into US Treasuries](/imgs/blogs/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income-7.png)

The figure traces the loop: an exporter sells to the US and earns dollars; the trade surplus means net dollars flow in; the central bank buys those dollars and builds reserves; it must park them safely; it buys US Treasuries because that is the only market big enough; and the extra demand presses US borrowing costs *down.* Notice the influence at the end of the chain — foreign demand for safety becomes lower interest rates for American homebuyers. A factory worker in an exporting nation is, indirectly, helping finance a mortgage in Ohio.

#### Worked example: a surplus becomes Treasury demand

Suppose an exporting nation runs a \$300 billion annual trade surplus with the US. Over a year, roughly \$300 billion of dollars accumulate in its system, much of which its central bank absorbs as reserves. If it parks, say, two-thirds of that in Treasuries, that is \$200 billion of *new, price-insensitive* Treasury demand in a single year — comparable in scale to a serious QE program, but coming from abroad and driven purely by trade flows. Stack a few large exporters together and you have a structural bid for Treasuries running into the hundreds of billions per year. This is why, for two decades, foreign official buyers were the marginal force keeping long-term US yields low — the so-called "global savings glut" hypothesis.

*The intuition: every dollar America spends on imports tends to come home as demand for its own government bonds, so the trade deficit and the ability to borrow cheaply are two sides of the same coin.*

It is worth flagging the honesty here: foreign demand can *fall* too. When a country needs to defend its own currency, it *sells* Treasuries to raise dollars — exactly the reverse of accumulation. So foreign official holdings, while huge, are not unconditional; they ebb and flow with trade balances and currency stress. As a share of the total, foreign official holdings have actually drifted down over the past decade as US issuance ballooned and domestic buyers took up more of the slack — a point the ownership figure below makes visible.

## Mutual funds, ETFs, and the vehicles that pool savings

The buyers so far are giant institutions buying for their own balance sheets. But a huge share of bond demand comes from a different channel: **pooled investment vehicles** — mutual funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) — that gather money from millions of ordinary savers and buy bonds on their behalf.

A **bond mutual fund** takes in money from thousands of investors, buys a diversified portfolio of bonds, and gives each investor a share of the whole. A **bond ETF** does the same but trades on a stock exchange like a single share, so you can buy "the entire US investment-grade corporate bond market" with one click. These vehicles exist because individual bonds are awkward for small savers: they trade in large minimum sizes (often \$1,000–\$10,000 or more per bond, and institutional lots far larger), in an opaque dealer market, with no easy way to diversify across hundreds of issuers. A fund solves all of that. The giant bond manager PIMCO built an empire on exactly this — see [PIMCO and the bond market](/blog/trading/finance/pimco-and-the-bond-market).

Funds are a fascinating *middle* kind of buyer. Unlike a bank (forced by a rule) or an insurer (forced by a liability), a fund buys bonds because its *investors* want bond exposure — for income, for diversification, for ballast against stocks. So fund demand is more **price-sensitive** and more **flow-driven**: when savers pour money into bond funds, the funds must buy bonds; when savers flee (often after a year of losses, as in 2022), the funds must sell, sometimes into a falling market. This makes funds a transmission belt that can *amplify* moves — a feature that matters in crises, when forced fund selling adds to the pressure on prices.

#### Worked example: building income with a bond ETF

Suppose you have \$50,000 to invest for income and you buy a broad bond ETF yielding 4.5%. The ETF holds thousands of bonds — Treasuries, agency mortgages, investment-grade corporates — so no single default can hurt you much. Your annual income is roughly 4.5% × \$50,000 = **\$2,250,** paid out monthly as the underlying bonds pay their coupons. You never picked a single bond, never called a dealer, never worried about a minimum lot size. The ETF did all of it, and your \$50,000 became part of the market's demand for thousands of individual bonds. One important wrinkle, which trips up beginners: unlike a single bond, the ETF has *no maturity date* — it perpetually rolls into new bonds, so its price keeps moving with rates forever. You get income and diversification, but you give up the "hold to maturity and get your money back" certainty of owning the bond directly.

*The intuition: funds and ETFs convert millions of small, scattered savers into a single large, professional bond buyer — democratizing access while adding a layer of flow-driven, price-sensitive behavior that pure liability buyers do not have.*

There is a deeper point about ETFs specifically that matters in a crisis. A bond ETF promises something almost magical: instant, stock-like liquidity in an underlying market (corporate bonds, especially) that is itself often *illiquid* — individual corporate bonds can go days without trading. In calm times this works beautifully, because authorized participants (large dealers) arbitrage any gap between the ETF's price and the value of its bonds. But in a panic, the underlying bonds may not trade at all, and the ETF's market price can swing away from the stale value of its holdings — sometimes trading at a *discount* to the bonds it owns. This happened in March 2020, when major investment-grade bond ETFs briefly traded several percent below the quoted value of their portfolios. The ETF was not "broken"; it was telling the truth about liquidity that the slow-moving underlying market was hiding. The lesson is that the *liquidity transformation* funds perform — turning illiquid bonds into liquid shares — is real and valuable but not free, and it is tested precisely when it is most needed.

## Households and retirees: income and capital preservation

The last buyer is the one the whole chain ultimately serves: the household. Some households buy bonds directly — a retiree who buys US Treasury bonds or savings bonds, a saver who builds a "ladder" of bonds maturing in successive years to fund living expenses. But far more households own bonds *indirectly,* through the very channels we have just described: their bank deposit (which the bank invests partly in bonds), their pension, their life insurance, their 401(k) bond fund.

Why do households want bonds at all? Two reasons, both timeless. The first is **income**: a bond pays a steady, predictable coupon — useful for a retiree who needs to replace a paycheck and cannot afford the swings of the stock market. The second is **capital preservation**: a bond promises to return your principal at maturity, so a saver who cannot tolerate losing money values the certainty. A 70-year-old does not want their savings to drop 40% in a stock-market crash the year they retire; bonds are the asset that lets them sleep.

![A pipeline showing household savings flowing through banks, pensions, insurers, and funds into the bond market and income returning](/imgs/blogs/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income-6.png)

The figure shows how a household's savings actually reach the bond market. Most people never buy a bond directly. Their monthly savings flow into intermediaries — a bank deposit, a 401(k), a pension contribution, an insurance premium. Those intermediaries pool thousands of savers into billions of dollars, hand the money to professional managers, who buy bonds at institutional scale and pricing, and the coupons flow back as income to the saver. The household is the source of the river; the institutions are the channels that carry it to the bond market. This is why "who buys bonds" has a layered answer: the *visible* buyers are banks, insurers, and funds, but the *ultimate* buyers are the millions of households whose savings they invest.

#### Worked example: a retiree's bond ladder

A retiree wants \$40,000 a year of dependable income and decides to build a **ladder** — buying bonds that mature in successive years so a chunk of principal comes back each year. Suppose they buy five bonds: one maturing in 1 year, one in 2, and so on to 5 years, each \$200,000 of face value, each paying a 4% coupon. Every year they collect 4% × \$1,000,000 = \$40,000 in coupons across the ladder, *plus* \$200,000 of returning principal as the nearest bond matures — which they reinvest into a new 5-year bond at the back of the ladder, keeping the structure rolling. The ladder gives them steady income, regular access to principal (in case they need cash or rates rise and they want to reinvest higher), and protection from having to sell at a bad time. It is the household-scale version of the asset-liability matching insurers and pensions do.

*The intuition: households want exactly what bonds provide — a known income and the return of their money — and even when they never buy a bond directly, their demand for safe income is the headwater of the entire bond market.*

## The safe-asset shortage: why the world wants more bonds than exist

We have now met all seven buyers. Step back and notice what they have in common: nearly all of them want the *same* thing — assets that are safe, liquid, and reliable. Banks want HQLA. Insurers and pensions want certain cash flows. Central banks and foreign governments want safe reserves. Funds and households want ballast. And the safest, most liquid, most universally accepted asset of all is the US Treasury.

This produces one of the most important and least-understood facts in finance: **the world is short of safe assets.** The demand for truly safe, deep, liquid bonds exceeds the supply of them. There simply are not enough Treasuries (and other top-tier safe bonds) to satisfy everyone who needs them for collateral, reserves, buffers, and matching. Economists call this the **safe-asset shortage,** and it has a measurable consequence: it pushes the *yield* on safe assets *down,* often below where ordinary supply and demand would put it. People pay a premium — accept a lower yield — for the privilege of holding something genuinely safe and liquid. That premium is sometimes called the **convenience yield** of Treasuries.

![A pyramid of the safe-asset hierarchy with US Treasuries at the apex and progressively less safe assets below](/imgs/blogs/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income-4.png)

The figure lays out the hierarchy. At the apex sit US Treasuries — the global reserve asset, the deepest market on earth, the benchmark everything else is priced against. Below them are other top sovereigns (German Bunds, Japanese government bonds, UK gilts) and US agency mortgage debt — safe, but smaller and less universally accepted. Below that, investment-grade corporate and supranational bonds — safe-ish, but carrying credit and liquidity risk. At the base sits the layer of **near-money**: bank deposits, money-market funds, and repo — instruments that *feel* like safe cash but are ultimately backed by the safe assets in the layers above them. When the supply of true safe assets at the top runs short, the financial system tries to *manufacture* more safety at the bottom — turning risky loans into "safe" tranches through securitization, for example. That manufacturing is exactly what failed in 2008, when supposedly safe mortgage-backed securities turned out not to be safe at all.

#### Worked example: the safe-asset premium in yields

Imagine two bonds, both due in 5 years, both highly likely to pay you back: a US Treasury note and a bond from a blue-chip company, Northwind Corp. Suppose Northwind is genuinely very safe — its real default risk is tiny. In a pure supply-and-demand world, both might yield, say, 4.5%. But the Treasury is also the world's premier collateral and reserve asset, wanted by every bank and central bank, so the rush of safety-and-liquidity demand bids its price *up* and its yield *down* to, say, 4.0%. The Treasury yields half a percent *less* than the equally-safe corporate — not because it is less risky in default terms, but because it carries a bonus of safety and liquidity that buyers pay for. That 0.5% gap is the convenience yield: the price of the "moneyness" of Treasuries. For deeper math on what real yields price, see [real yields: the variable that prices everything](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-yields-the-variable-that-prices-everything).

*The intuition: when the whole world needs the same safe asset and there is not enough of it, the asset's yield falls below what its risk alone would justify — scarcity of safety is itself a force on interest rates.*

## Who actually owns the Treasury market

It helps to see the buy side as a whole. The US Treasury market — roughly \$27 trillion of marketable debt in early 2025 — is owned by a spread of holders, with no single one dominating. The figure below shows illustrative shares; the exact numbers shift quarter to quarter, but the *shape* — diversified, no dominant buyer — is the durable truth.

![A grid showing illustrative ownership shares of the US Treasury market across the Fed, foreign holders, banks, pensions, funds, and households](/imgs/blogs/who-buys-bonds-the-global-demand-for-safe-income-3.png)

On the official side (left), the Federal Reserve holds roughly a sixth of the market through its QE portfolio, slowly running off under QT; foreign official holders — other central banks' reserves — hold another large slice; foreign private investors hold more. On the private and domestic side (right), banks and money-market funds hold a big chunk as HQLA and collateral, pensions and insurers hold a steady share for liability matching, mutual funds and ETFs hold a chunk on behalf of savers, and households hold the rest directly. *(Shares are illustrative and rounded for clarity; consult the US Treasury's TIC data and the Fed's Flow of Funds for precise, current figures.)*

The single most important takeaway from this picture is *diversification of demand.* Because the buyer base is split across price-insensitive official holders (the Fed, foreign central banks) and price-sensitive private ones (banks, funds, households), the market almost always clears: when one buyer steps back, another steps in. When the Fed sold bonds in QT, domestic banks, funds, and households absorbed much of the supply (at higher yields). This deep, varied demand is *why* the US can issue trillions of dollars of debt without failing to find lenders — and why the "who will buy all this debt?" panic that recurs every few years has, so far, always been answered. For the supply side of that question, see [deficits, debt, and bond supply](/blog/trading/macro-trading/deficits-debt-bond-supply-why-issuance-moves-yields).

#### Worked example: absorbing a Treasury auction

Suppose the Treasury auctions \$50 billion of new 10-year notes on a Wednesday. Who shows up? Primary dealers (big banks legally required to bid) take down a slice and redistribute it. Foreign central banks submit bids through the Fed's facility. Pension funds and insurers buy to extend duration. Bond funds buy to deploy investor inflows. Households buy small amounts directly through TreasuryDirect. No single buyer takes \$50 billion; the auction is filled by a *mosaic* of demand. If demand is strong, the auction "stops" at a low yield (high price) and is called well-bid; if weak, it stops at a higher yield (a "tail") and signals soft demand. Traders watch the **bid-to-cover ratio** — total bids divided by the amount sold — as a thermometer of demand; a 2.5x cover means \$125 billion of bids chased \$50 billion of bonds. The mosaic of buyers is what makes the auction work.

*The intuition: the Treasury market never relies on one buyer — its strength is a diversified base of holders with different motives, so the bid is deep and resilient even when any single buyer retreats.*

## Common misconceptions

**"Bonds are bought by people who are scared of stocks."** This is the most common and most misleading picture. The dominant bond buyers are not nervous retail investors choosing safety over growth; they are institutions *required* to hold bonds by law (banks), by the structure of their liabilities (insurers, pensions), or by their role in the monetary system (central banks, foreign reserves). The buy side is driven far more by *obligation* than by *sentiment.* This is why bond demand can stay strong even when bonds look unattractive on a pure return basis — the buyers are not choosing.

**"Treasuries are completely safe, so a bank that holds them can't get in trouble."** Treasuries are safe from *default* — you will get your money back. But they are not safe from *price moves.* When rates rise, the market value of existing bonds falls, and a bank that must sell them to meet withdrawals can be forced to realize huge losses. That is precisely what destroyed SVB in 2023: the credit was impeccable, the *duration* was deadly. "Safe asset" means safe from credit risk, not from interest-rate risk.

**"The government will eventually run out of people to buy its debt."** This fear surfaces every few years, yet it keeps being wrong, because it misunderstands the demand side. The buyer base is enormous, diversified, and structurally hungry for safe assets — and much of it (banks needing HQLA, foreign central banks recycling trade surpluses) buys regardless of the yield. The real constraint is *price,* not *quantity*: if demand softens, the government still sells its bonds, just at a higher yield. "Running out of buyers" almost never happens; "buyers demand a higher yield" happens all the time. The genuine risk is fiscal credibility, not a literal absence of lenders — see [sovereign debt and the bond vigilantes](/blog/trading/macro-trading/sovereign-debt-and-the-bond-vigilantes).

**"Foreign countries buy our bonds as a favor, and could 'dump' them to hurt us."** Foreign central banks buy Treasuries because *they have no better option* for parking trillions of dollars of trade-surplus reserves safely and liquidly — not as a favor, and not easily reversible. Dumping a large Treasury holding would crater the price of the very bonds the seller still holds, strengthen the seller's own currency (hurting its exporters), and leave it with dollars it must reinvest *somewhere.* The "nuclear option" of dumping Treasuries is far more self-harming than commonly assumed, which is why it almost never happens at scale.

**"Buying a bond fund is the same as owning a bond."** A single bond has a maturity date: hold it to the end and you get your principal back, full stop. A bond *fund or ETF* never matures — it perpetually rolls into new bonds, so its price moves with rates forever and there is no date on which you are guaranteed your money back. In a rising-rate year like 2022, bond *funds* fell in price and stayed down, frustrating savers who thought "bonds are safe." Both have their place, but the "hold to maturity, get your money back" certainty belongs to the individual bond, not the fund. See [bond funds and ETFs vs owning individual bonds](/blog/trading/fixed-income/bond-funds-and-etfs-vs-owning-individual-bonds) later in this series.

**"Bond demand is steady and boring."** Most of it is — but the *price-sensitive, flow-driven* slice (funds, households, levered strategies) can swing violently and amplify crises. When savers flee bond funds after losses, the funds must sell into a falling market; when levered pension LDI strategies face collateral calls, they dump bonds at the worst moment. The boring buyers (banks, insurers, foreign reserves) provide a stable floor; the flow-driven buyers provide the drama. Confusing the two is how people get surprised by bond-market crises.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2023 SVB collapse — safe credit, fatal duration.** Silicon Valley Bank held a large share of its assets in long-dated US Treasuries and agency mortgage bonds — the safest credits available. When the Fed raised rates from near zero to over 5% across 2022–2023, the market value of those long bonds fell sharply, leaving SVB with roughly \$15 billion of unrealized losses on its securities. When uninsured tech-startup depositors got nervous and began pulling money, SVB had to sell bonds to raise cash, crystallizing the losses, wiping out its capital, and triggering a run that ended the bank in 48 hours in March 2023. The lesson is the cleanest illustration of this post's central nuance: banks buy Treasuries as a liquid buffer, but a buffer of long-duration bonds can itself become the source of failure when rates rise. It also reshaped how regulators think about unrealized losses on bank bond portfolios.

**The UK gilt / LDI crisis of September 2022 — when liability matching levered too far.** UK defined-benefit pensions used Liability-Driven Investing to match their long-dated liabilities with long gilts and interest-rate derivatives — sensible in principle. But many had *levered* that exposure to do more with less capital. When the UK government announced a large unfunded tax-cut package, gilt yields spiked violently (prices crashed), and the levered LDI funds faced enormous collateral calls almost overnight. To raise cash, they were forced to *sell gilts* — pushing yields even higher, triggering more collateral calls: a self-reinforcing doom loop. The Bank of England had to step in and buy gilts to halt the spiral. The episode is a vivid lesson that the stabilizing buyers of bonds can, when levered, briefly become the most destabilizing sellers.

**The global savings glut and two decades of low US yields.** From the early 2000s, large exporting economies ran persistent trade surpluses and recycled the dollars into US Treasuries — exactly the loop in figure seven. Then–Fed Chair Ben Bernanke named this the "global savings glut": a wall of foreign official demand for safe dollar assets that helped hold US long-term yields low even as the Fed raised short-term rates (Alan Greenspan's famous "conundrum" of 2005, when long yields refused to rise). For years, foreign central banks were the marginal buyer setting the long end of the US curve — a striking demonstration that bond demand is global, and that a trade surplus on one side of the world finances borrowing on the other.

**The 2020 "dash for cash" — when even the safest market seized up.** In March 2020, as the pandemic hit, investors scrambled for cash and, unusually, sold *Treasuries* to raise it — even as everything else was falling, the safe asset was being dumped because it was the easiest thing to sell. Liquidity in the world's deepest market briefly evaporated; dealers could not absorb the selling. The Fed responded with the largest bond purchases in history, buying over \$1 trillion of Treasuries in a matter of weeks to restore function. The episode showed both the fragility of even the safe-asset market under extreme stress *and* the role of the central bank as the buyer of last resort that backstops it.

**The post-2022 QT handoff — domestic buyers replacing the Fed.** As the Fed shifted from QE to quantitative tightening in 2022, it stopped being a net buyer and began letting bonds roll off — withdrawing its huge, price-insensitive bid just as the Treasury was issuing record amounts of new debt. The market's response was instructive: rather than failing, it *re-priced.* Domestic banks, money funds, pensions, and households absorbed the extra supply, but demanded higher yields to do so, contributing to the sharp rise in long-term rates through 2023. It was a real-time demonstration of the demand-side truth: the market does not run out of buyers, it adjusts the price until the new mix of buyers clears the supply.

**Japan's pensions and insurers and the hunt for yield.** Japanese life insurers and the giant Government Pension Investment Fund (GPIF) hold vast portfolios of bonds to match their long-dated liabilities. For years, with Japanese government bond yields pinned near zero, these institutions reached abroad — buying US Treasuries and other foreign bonds (hedged and unhedged) to find any yield at all. Their flows are large enough to move global bond markets, and shifts in their hedging costs (driven by the gap between US and Japanese rates) can swing demand for Treasuries by tens of billions. It is a reminder that the liability-matching buyer is global, patient, and large enough that its allocation decisions ripple across borders.

## When this matters to you

Once you can name the seven buyers and the reason each one needs bonds, a lot of financial news decodes itself. When you read that "the Fed is doing QT," you now know it means the largest price-insensitive buyer is stepping back, so yields face upward pressure. When you read that a bank "had to sell its bond portfolio at a loss," you recognize the SVB pattern — safe credit, dangerous duration. When you read that "foreign demand at the auction was weak," you understand a piece of the dollar-recycling loop just wobbled. And when a pundit warns that the government will "run out of buyers," you know the more accurate framing is that buyers will demand a higher yield.

It also touches your own money more directly than it seems. Your bank deposit is partly invested in the very Treasuries this post describes. Your pension or 401(k) almost certainly owns bonds on your behalf, matched to the income you will need decades from now. The mortgage rate you are quoted is anchored to the 10-year Treasury yield, which is set in part by foreign central banks recycling trade surpluses thousands of miles away. You are already, whether you knew it or not, both a bond buyer and a beneficiary of the global demand for safe income.

The next step is to understand *how that demand sets the price.* The buyers in this post all care intensely about one thing — the yield they earn — and that yield is set by the seesaw between price and yield that we keep gesturing at. That mechanism is the subject of the next post: [price and yield: the seesaw at the heart of bonds](/blog/trading/fixed-income/price-and-yield-the-seesaw-at-the-heart-of-bonds). For the policy lens on how the price of money is set, see [interest rates: the price of money, the master variable](/blog/trading/macro-trading/interest-rates-the-price-of-money-master-variable); for the allocation lens on bonds as the portfolio's risk-free anchor, see [government bonds: the risk-free anchor and duration](/blog/trading/cross-asset/government-bonds-the-risk-free-anchor-and-duration); and for the heavy math of how a bond's price is computed from its cash flows, see [bond pricing](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/bond-pricing).
