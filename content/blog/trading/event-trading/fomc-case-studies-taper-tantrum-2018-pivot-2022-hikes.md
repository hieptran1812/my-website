> **TL;DR** — The same Federal Reserve can crash markets or rescue them; what decides the sign and size of the move is never the headline rate change alone but the gap between what the market had already priced and what the Fed actually signaled, filtered through the regime you are in.
>
> - **What the events are:** four dated FOMC moments — the 2013 taper tantrum, the December 2018 hawkish hike, the 2022 hiking shock, and the September 2024 first cut.
> - **How the markets reacted:** bonds repriced +138bp on a 2013 hint; the S&P fell 19.8% in Q4 2018 into a January pivot; 2022 was the rare year stocks, bonds and crypto all fell together; the 2024 cut barely moved the tape because it was fully priced.
> - **The trade / what flips the sign:** trade the surprise, not the decision — and ask what regime you are in, because the same hawkish Fed that crashed stocks in 2018 had no obligation to rescue them in 2022.
> - **The one number to remember:** −19.8% — the cost of a Fed that would not pivot, Q4 2018.

On December 24, 2018 — Christmas Eve, a holiday-shortened half-session — the S&P 500 fell 2.71%. It was the worst Christmas Eve in the history of the index. Five days earlier, on December 19, Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell had walked to the podium after his committee raised interest rates for the fourth time that year, and told the world two things the market did not want to hear: the projections still penciled in two more hikes for 2019, and the shrinking of the Fed's balance sheet was on "auto-pilot." Stocks had been clinging to the hope that Powell would blink. He did not blink. By the close on December 24 the S&P 500 sat 19.8% below its September high, a hair from the 20% line that defines a bear market, and the financial press was openly asking whether the Fed had just broken the economy.

Then, on January 4, 2019, at an academic panel in Atlanta, Powell blinked. He used the word "patient." He said the Fed was "listening carefully" to markets and was prepared to adjust the balance-sheet runoff. The S&P 500 rose 3.4% that day and never looked back: the index climbed roughly 30% over 2019 and printed new all-time highs. Same chair, same committee, same dual mandate — a complete reversal in the market's fortunes, driven entirely by what the Fed *signaled* about its reaction function.

That whiplash is the whole subject of this post. The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) is the single most powerful price-setting body on Earth, and its eight scheduled meetings a year are the heaviest events on the macro calendar. But the lesson of the four episodes below is not "the Fed moves markets." Everyone knows that. The lesson is *how* it moves them: the reaction is a function of expectations, signal, and regime, and you can read it forensically, asset by asset, if you know what to look for.

We are going to dissect four episodes that, together, cover the full range of what the Fed can do to markets. The 2013 taper tantrum shows how a *hint* — not even a decision — can reprice the entire bond market. December 2018 shows the cost of a Fed that will not pivot and the violence of the relief when it finally does. The 2022 shock shows the rare regime where the Fed *wants* asset prices lower and there is no rescue coming. And the September 2024 first cut shows that even a large, dramatic decision can move nothing if it was fully priced. Each is dated to the day, each is broken down by asset, and each ends in a single repeatable lesson. By the end you should be able to look at any future FOMC meeting and ask the three questions that actually determine the reaction.

## Foundations: how to read an FOMC case study

Before we walk through any episode, we need a shared vocabulary. Every case study below answers the same four questions in the same order, so let us define them from zero.

### What the FOMC is and what it controls

The FOMC is the Federal Reserve's monetary-policy committee. Eight times a year it sets the **federal funds rate** — the interest rate at which banks lend reserves to each other overnight — by announcing a target range (for example, 5.25%–5.50%). That overnight rate is the anchor for every other interest rate in the economy: mortgages, corporate bonds, credit cards, the discount rate used to value stocks. When the Fed moves its target, it is moving the price of money for everyone. (For the mechanics of how the Fed actually pins the rate inside its range, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).)

The Fed also controls the **balance sheet**: by buying bonds (quantitative easing, or QE) it pushes long-term yields down and floods the system with reserves; by letting bonds roll off (quantitative tightening, or QT) it does the reverse. Balance-sheet policy is the slower, heavier lever, and as you will see in the taper tantrum, even a *hint* about it can move markets violently. We lean on the [QE versus QT explainer](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets) for that mechanism rather than re-deriving it here.

The FOMC operates under a **dual mandate**: maximum employment and stable prices, where "stable prices" has meant a 2% inflation target since 2012. Those two goals usually point the same way — when the economy overheats, both inflation and employment run hot, and the Fed tightens; when it weakens, both cool, and the Fed eases. The dangerous moments are when they *conflict*: high inflation alongside a slowing economy forces the Fed to choose, and which goal it prioritizes is the deepest driver of every reaction in this post. In 2018 the two were aligned and benign; in 2022 they were in open conflict, and the Fed chose to fight inflation at the cost of employment and asset prices. Keep the mandate in view: it is the constraint that decides whether the Fed is *allowed* to be your friend.

Each scheduled meeting follows a fixed choreography. The statement and rate decision are released at 2:00 pm Eastern; on four of the eight meetings a year, the release also includes the Summary of Economic Projections (the dot plot and the committee's growth, unemployment, and inflation forecasts). At 2:30 pm the Chair begins a press conference that runs roughly 45 minutes. This timing matters for trading: the algorithmic, headline-driven move happens in milliseconds at 2:00 pm as machines parse the statement against the prior one word-by-word, but the *human* repricing — the part that often sets the day — comes during the presser, when an unscripted answer reveals how the committee is actually thinking.

### What is "priced," and the three-way surprise

Here is the single most important idea in event trading, and it is counterintuitive: **markets do not react to the Fed's decision. They react to the surprise.** Price already contains the consensus. If the entire world expects a 25 basis point hike (a basis point, or "bp," is one-hundredth of a percentage point — so 25bp is 0.25%), and the Fed hikes 25bp, the rate change is *already in the price* and the decision itself moves nothing. What moves the tape is the difference between what was expected and what was delivered.

But "the surprise" is richer than just the rate number, because an FOMC announcement has three distinct channels, and a trader has to track all three:

- **The rate decision itself** — did they hike, cut, or hold, and by how much, relative to what futures markets priced in beforehand?
- **The Summary of Economic Projections (the "dot plot")** — each quarter, the committee publishes anonymous dots showing where each member expects rates to be at the end of the next few years. The dots are how the Fed signals the *path*, and a hawkish or dovish shift in the dots can dwarf the decision. (For the path-pricing mechanics, see [terminal rate and rate-cut cycles](/blog/trading/macro-trading/terminal-rate-and-rate-cut-cycles-pricing-the-path).)
- **The press conference** — the Chair takes questions for 45 minutes, and a single phrase ("a long way from neutral," "patient," "we are not thinking about thinking about raising rates") can reprice trillions. The statement is scripted; the presser is where the surprises hide.

So when we say "what the market expected versus what the Fed signaled," we mean all three: decision, dots, and tone. A Fed can hike exactly as expected and still be wildly hawkish if the dots shift up and the Chair sounds determined. That is precisely what happened in December 2018.

### The reaction function

The **reaction function** is the market's model of *how the Fed will respond to incoming data* — when it will hike, when it will cut, what it cares about. In calm times, the market believes the Fed will ease whenever risk assets fall hard enough; this belief is the famous "Fed put." When the reaction function is stable and dovish, bad news for the economy is good news for stocks, because it brings cuts forward. When the reaction function flips — when the Fed makes clear it will *not* rescue stocks because it is fighting inflation — the put is gone, and the same bad economic news that used to rally stocks now crushes them. The 2022 episode below is the cleanest example of a regime where the put was suspended. We point readers to a sibling piece in this series on forward guidance and the Fed put for the full anatomy of that belief; here we just need the concept.

### The cross-asset map

Finally, every FOMC move propagates across asset classes in a roughly predictable way, and the four episodes will use the same map each time:

- **Bonds** are the first responders. Treasury yields are *literally* the price of the rate path, so they reprice instantly and mechanically on any shift in the expected path. A more-hawkish-than-expected Fed lifts yields (bond prices fall); a more-dovish Fed drops them.
- **Stocks** care about two things at once: the discount rate (higher rates lower the present value of future earnings, which hits long-duration growth and tech hardest) and the growth outlook (will tighter policy cause a recession?). So stocks can fall on a hawkish Fed for *either* reason.
- **The dollar** strengthens when the Fed is more hawkish than other central banks (higher US rates attract capital) and weakens on dovish surprises. The dollar is the global financial system's plumbing; a surging dollar tightens conditions everywhere. (See [trading the dollar](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile) for the DXY mechanics.)
- **Crypto** behaves as a high-beta, long-duration risk and liquidity asset: it tends to rally when the Fed adds liquidity and sell off hard when the Fed drains it. In 2022 it traded almost exactly like leveraged Nasdaq.

With that map fixed, we can walk through the four episodes. Each one is the same skeleton — what was priced, what the Fed signaled, the cross-asset reaction, the repeatable lesson — applied to a different regime.

## Episode 1 — The 2013 taper tantrum: a hint repriced the bond market

The taper tantrum is the purest illustration in modern markets of "the surprise is in the *signal*, not the decision," because the Fed did not change a single rate. It only hinted at the *future* path of its bond-buying — and the bond market threw a tantrum.

### What was priced

By spring 2013 the US economy was four years past the financial crisis and the Fed was running its third round of QE: it was buying \$85 billion of bonds a month, and the market had come to treat that flood of buying as a permanent fixture. The 10-year Treasury yield sat at 1.66% at the start of May. Investors had piled into long-duration bonds, mortgage REITs, and emerging-market debt — all the trades that work when the Fed is anchoring long yields near record lows and is expected to keep doing so. The consensus, in short, was: *QE forever, low rates for years.*

### What the Fed signaled

On May 22, 2013, in testimony to Congress, then-Chair Ben Bernanke said that if the data continued to improve, the Fed could "in the next few meetings" begin to *taper* — to slow — its bond purchases. Note what this was *not*: it was not a rate hike, not a halt to QE, not even a confirmed plan. It was a conditional hint that the pace of *buying* might slow at some unspecified future point. Then on June 19, at the FOMC meeting, Bernanke went further and laid out a tentative timeline for winding down QE through 2014.

That was enough.

![The 2013 taper tantrum 10-year Treasury yield with Bernanke May testimony and June FOMC annotated](/imgs/blogs/fomc-case-studies-taper-tantrum-2018-pivot-2022-hikes-2.png)

### The cross-asset reaction

- **Bonds (the epicenter):** the 10-year Treasury yield ripped from 1.66% in early May to 1.94% by May 21, then to 2.31% on the June FOMC, and onward to 2.99% by early September and 3.04% by year-end. That is +138 basis points in seven months on nothing but a change in the *expected supply* of future bond demand. The repricing was mechanical: if the Fed is going to buy fewer bonds, the market clears at a higher yield.

  Why so violent for a mere hint? Two reinforcing forces. First, *positioning*: years of QE had herded investors into the same crowded long-duration trades, often levered, on the assumption that the Fed had their back. When the assumption cracked, everyone tried to reduce duration at once, and there were no natural buyers — the marginal buyer (the Fed) was the one stepping back. Second, *convexity and hedging*: as yields rose, holders of mortgage-backed securities saw their bonds' effective duration extend (homeowners stop refinancing when rates rise, so the bonds last longer), forcing them to sell *more* Treasuries to stay hedged, which pushed yields higher still, which extended duration further. This convexity feedback loop is a recurring accelerant in bond selloffs, and it turned a hint into a 138bp move.
- **Stocks:** the S&P 500 wobbled — a quick 5–6% pullback in late May and June — but recovered within weeks and finished 2013 up roughly 30%. Equities care about the rate path, but a faster economy that *justifies* tapering is, on balance, fine for earnings. The tantrum was a bond story, not an equity story.
- **The dollar and emerging markets:** the dollar firmed and the real pain showed up offshore. Countries that had financed themselves cheaply during QE — the "Fragile Five" of India, Indonesia, Brazil, Turkey, and South Africa — saw their currencies and bonds sold hard as global capital rushed back toward rising US yields. This is the dollar-plumbing channel: when US yields jump, money comes home.
- **Crypto:** essentially a non-event. In 2013 Bitcoin was a tiny, idiosyncratic market driven by its own dynamics; it had not yet become the macro liquidity asset it is today. We note this precisely because it changes by 2022.

#### Worked example: the taper tantrum on a bond position

Say you held a \$500,000 position in 10-year Treasuries at the start of the tantrum, the kind of allocation a conservative income investor might own. To translate a yield move into money, we use **DV01** — the dollar value of a one-basis-point change in yield. For a 10-year bond, DV01 is roughly \$8.60 per \$10,000 of face value per basis point, so on \$500,000 that is about \$430 per basis point.

The math, step by step:

- Yield move: +138bp (1.66% rising to 3.04%).
- DV01 on the position: about \$430 per basis point.
- Price loss: 138 × \$430 ≈ −\$59,300 in pure DV01 terms; using the more conservative early-tantrum DV01 of roughly \$430 over the sharpest +110bp leg, the mark-to-market loss was about −\$47,300.
- On the \$500,000 position, that is roughly a 9–12% drawdown — from a "safe" bond holding, in seven months, with no rate hike at all.

The intuition: a "risk-free" bond is only risk-free if you hold it to maturity; mark-to-market, a hint about future Fed buying cost this holder the better part of a year's worth of expected coupon income.

#### Worked example: the offshore spillover on an EM bond fund

The dollar-plumbing channel had a second victim. Say you held \$20,000 in an emerging-market local-currency bond fund in May 2013, attracted by yields of 6–8% while US rates were near zero. As US yields rose, capital fled EM, and a typical fund of this kind fell roughly 12–15% from May to September 2013 between the bond losses and the currency depreciation. The math:

- Position: \$20,000 in a local-currency EM bond fund.
- Combined bond-plus-FX drawdown over the tantrum: about −13%.
- Mark-to-market loss: \$20,000 × −0.13 = **−\$2,600**, leaving about \$17,400.

The intuition: a US monetary signal radiates outward through the dollar, so a hint delivered in Washington was felt hardest in Jakarta and Istanbul — the carry trades funded by cheap dollars unwind first when those dollars get more expensive.

### The repeatable lesson

**Communication is policy.** The Fed did not tighten in 2013 — it talked about *eventually* tightening — and the bond market did the tightening for it. Ever since, central bankers obsess over forward guidance precisely because the taper tantrum showed that a poorly-telegraphed signal can move markets harder than an actual decision. For a trader, the lesson is to watch the *path*, not just the present setting: the entire value of a long bond is a bet on where rates go, so any credible information about the future repricing it instantly.

There is a trading corollary the Fed itself learned. When it actually began tapering in December 2013 — the real decision — markets barely flinched, because by then it had spent six months carefully telegraphing the plan. The *anticipated* event was a non-event; the *surprise hint* six months earlier was the explosion. That asymmetry is the heart of event trading and we will see it invert in 2024: the surprise, not the action, is what you trade.

## Episode 2 — December 2018: the hawkish hike, the Q4 selloff, and the January pivot

If 2013 was a bond story, December 2018 is the canonical *equity* story — and the canonical story of the Fed put. It is the episode every macro trader keeps in their back pocket, because it shows both the cost of a Fed that will not pivot and the violence of the relief when it finally does.

### What was priced

Through 2018 the Fed under its new Chair, Jerome Powell, had been hiking steadily — four times that year — and shrinking its balance sheet on a pre-set schedule. By December, markets were nervous. Global growth was slowing, the trade war with China was escalating, and credit spreads were widening. The hope priced into stocks was straightforward: the Fed would hike once more in December and then *signal a pause* — acknowledge the deteriorating backdrop and take its foot off the brake. Markets wanted Powell to show he was watching them.

### What the Fed signaled

On December 19, 2018, the FOMC hiked 25bp to a 2.25%–2.50% range — as expected. But everything *around* the decision was hawkish relative to that hope:

- The dot plot still projected **two more hikes in 2019**, only a modest trim from three.
- Powell said the balance-sheet runoff was on **"auto-pilot"** and "not... something that we're going to be changing" — explicitly closing the door on the easing the market wanted.
- Earlier in the autumn he had said rates were "a long way from neutral," a phrase the market read as a commitment to keep going.

The decision matched expectations; the *signal* was far more hawkish than priced. The Fed put looked dead.

The intraday microstructure of December 19 is itself a lesson. Stocks were actually *up* roughly 1.5% in the half hour after the 2:00 pm statement — the hike was priced, and the dots had been trimmed from three to two, which the first algorithmic read treated as a modest dovish surprise. Then Powell took the podium at 2:30 pm. Asked about the balance sheet, he gave the "auto-pilot" answer; asked whether the Fed would adjust, he sounded unbothered by the market's distress. The S&P 500 round-tripped its entire gain and closed down 1.54% — a swing of roughly 3% from the intraday high to the close, all of it driven by *tone* during the presser, not by the statement. This is the clearest single example of why you trade the whole event and not just the 2:00 pm headline: the number was fine; the Chair's body language was the trade.

![S&P 500 same-day move on three FOMC days colored by sign](/imgs/blogs/fomc-case-studies-taper-tantrum-2018-pivot-2022-hikes-3.png)

### The cross-asset reaction

- **Stocks:** the S&P 500 reversed an early gain to close down 1.54% on the decision day, then kept falling. This was the tail end of a brutal quarter: from its September 20 high of 2,930.75 the index slid to 2,351.10 on December 24 — a 19.8% peak-to-trough drawdown — with that Christmas Eve session alone down 2.71%, the worst in the index's history. The Nasdaq, heavier in long-duration growth names, fell harder (−2.17% on the decision day).
- **Bonds:** here is a tell. The 10-year yield actually *fell* about 4bp on the decision day and kept falling through the quarter. Why, if the Fed was hawkish? Because the bond market was pricing not the hike but the *consequences*: a Fed tightening into a slowdown raises the odds of a policy error and a recession, which means *lower* rates later. Bonds rallied as a recession hedge even as the Fed hiked — a classic late-cycle signature.
- **The dollar:** firm but not explosive; the move was dominated by the equity-and-recession story, not a rate-differential story.
- **Crypto:** Bitcoin was already deep in its own 2018 bear market (down ~80% from its 2017 peak) and the macro risk-off did it no favors, but the crypto market was still small enough that it was a sideshow to the equity rout.

There was also a structural amplifier in 2018 that is easy to forget: the explosion of systematic, volatility-targeting strategies. Risk-parity funds, volatility-control funds, and trend-followers all mechanically *sell* into rising volatility — when the VIX spikes, their models cut equity exposure, and that selling pushes prices down and volatility up, triggering more selling. December 2018 was thin, holiday-shortened liquidity meeting forced mechanical selling, which is why a "normal" 1.5% decline on the decision day metastasized into a near-bear-market over five sessions. The Fed lit the match; the market's own plumbing poured on the fuel. (See [following the flows](/blog/trading/macro-trading/following-the-flows-positioning-cot-dealer-hedging) for how dealer hedging and systematic flows amplify these moves.)

![The Q4 2018 S&P 500 drawdown from the September high to the Christmas Eve low](/imgs/blogs/fomc-case-studies-taper-tantrum-2018-pivot-2022-hikes-4.png)

### The pivot

Then the reaction function flipped back. On January 4, 2019, Powell — sharing a stage with his predecessors Yellen and Bernanke — struck a completely different tone: the Fed would be "patient," was "listening carefully" to markets, and would not hesitate to adjust the balance-sheet runoff "if needed." Stocks rose 3.4% that day. Over the following weeks the Fed flattened its projected rate path, and by July 2019 it delivered the first *cut* of the cycle. The S&P 500 rallied roughly 30% across 2019. The Fed put was alive after all.

![Before and after of the December 2018 hawkish hike and the January 2019 pivot](/imgs/blogs/fomc-case-studies-taper-tantrum-2018-pivot-2022-hikes-5.png)

#### Worked example: the Q4 2018 drawdown on an equity position

Say you held a \$40,000 long position in an S&P 500 index fund at the September 20, 2018 high. You did nothing — you just rode the quarter. The math:

- Peak value: \$40,000 at 2,930.75 on September 20.
- Trough: the index fell to 2,351.10 on December 24, a −19.8% move.
- Mark-to-market loss: \$40,000 × −0.198 = **−\$7,920**, leaving you with about \$32,080.
- The single Christmas Eve session (−2.71%) alone cost roughly \$40,000 × −0.0271 ≈ −\$1,084 — but on the already-shrunken balance nearer \$32,000 it was closer to −\$880.

And the recovery: had you held through the January pivot and the rest of 2019, the position would have not only recovered but ended the year well above where it started. The intuition: the entire \$7,920 drawdown was the market pricing a Fed that would not pivot — and almost all of it reversed the moment the Fed said one word, "patient."

### The repeatable lesson

**The Fed put exists, but its strike price is conditional.** In 2018 the put was struck somewhere around a 20% drawdown plus visible credit stress; once markets hit it, the Fed pivoted. The lesson is *not* "stocks always bounce when they fall 20%." It is that the Fed will rescue markets *when doing so does not conflict with its mandate.* In 2018, inflation was at target, so easing was free — Powell could blink at no cost to his inflation credibility. Remember that condition, because in 2022 it did not hold, and the put did not appear.

## Episode 3 — 2022: the everything-down year and the fastest hiking cycle in 40 years

2022 is the episode that breaks the "Fed always rescues stocks" instinct. It was the year the reaction function inverted completely: with inflation at a 40-year high, the Fed *wanted* financial conditions to tighten, *wanted* asset prices lower, because that was the transmission mechanism for cooling demand. There was no put. And so 2022 produced the rare outcome where stocks, bonds, and crypto all fell hard at the same time — the diversification that is supposed to save a portfolio failed precisely when it was needed.

### What was priced

Coming into 2022, the market believed the Fed's own framing: inflation was "transitory," supply-chain noise that would fade, and rate hikes would be slow and gradual — a few 25bp moves to a low terminal rate. The whole structure of risk assets, from megacap tech to crypto to long bonds, was built on cheap money continuing. (The full macro arc of this misjudgment is told in [the 2021–2023 inflation and the fastest hiking cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle).)

### What the Fed signaled

Inflation refused to fade. By mid-2022, headline CPI was running above 9% year-over-year, the highest since 1981. The Fed abandoned "transitory," abandoned "gradual," and embarked on the most aggressive tightening in four decades:

- **March 16, 2022:** liftoff, +25bp to a 0.25%–0.50% range — the first hike of the cycle.
- Then the pace exploded: +50bp in May, and **four consecutive +75bp hikes** in June, July, September, and November 2022 — a cadence the Fed had not used since 1994.
- The target range went from near zero to 4.25%–4.50% by December 2022, and on to a terminal 5.25%–5.50% by July 2023 — roughly **+525bp in 16 months.**
- Crucially, Powell killed the put explicitly. At Jackson Hole in August 2022 he warned of "some pain to households and businesses" and said the Fed would "keep at it until the job is done." This was a deliberate signal: *do not expect us to rescue you.* That eight-minute speech — unusually short, deliberately blunt — knocked the S&P 500 down 3.4% the same afternoon, because it removed the last sliver of hope that the Fed would pivot to protect markets.

The deeper point of 2022 is that the Fed was using *financial conditions* as its transmission mechanism. The Fed does not control inflation directly; it controls the overnight rate and tries to tighten financial conditions — higher yields, lower stocks, a stronger dollar, wider credit spreads — which in turn cool demand. In 2018 a tightening of conditions was an unwelcome side effect the Fed wanted to limit. In 2022 it was the *goal*. When falling stock prices are the policy *working as intended*, there is no level at which the Fed rides to the rescue, because the rescue would undo the policy. This single inversion — asset-price weakness as a feature, not a bug — is what made 2022 unlike any episode in a generation.

![The 2022 fed funds hiking cycle step chart with the run of 75 basis-point hikes annotated](/imgs/blogs/fomc-case-studies-taper-tantrum-2018-pivot-2022-hikes-6.png)

### The cross-asset reaction

This is where the cross-asset map matters most, because *everything* moved the same direction:

- **Stocks:** the S&P 500 fell about 19% for the year, with the long-duration Nasdaq down roughly 33%. The damage was concentrated in the highest-multiple, longest-duration names, exactly as the discount-rate channel predicts: when the risk-free rate goes from 0% to 5%, the present value of a dollar of earnings a decade away collapses. Note the FOMC days were not uniformly red — the March 2022 liftoff day actually *rose* 2.24%, because the hike was fully priced and the clarity was a relief. The damage came from the *path*, not any single meeting.
- **Bonds:** this is the historic part. The 10-year Treasury yield rose from about 1.5% to nearly 4.3%, and the Bloomberg US Aggregate bond index fell roughly 13% — its worst year in modern history. Bonds, the asset you hold to *diversify* against an equity selloff, fell *with* stocks. In a rate-shock regime, stocks and bonds correlate positively, because the rate going up is the thing hurting both. (See [when correlations go to one](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis) for why diversification fails exactly when you need it.)
- **The dollar:** the DXY surged to a two-decade high near 114.8 in late September 2022 (from about 95.7 at the end of 2021) as the Fed out-hiked everyone, the dollar's safe-haven bid kicked in, and the dollar-smile dynamic took hold. A 20%+ dollar move tightened conditions across the entire globe and helped break the British gilt market in late September (the LDI pension crisis) and pushed the yen toward 152, forcing Japan's first FX intervention since 1998. The dollar is the world's funding currency, so a surging dollar is a global margin call: anyone who borrowed dollars to buy anything else suddenly owed more, and had to sell to repay.
- **Crypto:** Bitcoin behaved exactly like leveraged Nasdaq, falling from a 2021 year-end close of about \$46,306 to about \$16,548 by the end of 2022 — a −64% year — with the liquidity drain compounded by the collapses of Terra/Luna, Three Arrows, and FTX. Crypto had finally become a macro liquidity asset, and when the Fed drained liquidity, it drained the hardest. (See [crypto as a macro liquidity asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/crypto-as-a-macro-liquidity-asset).)
- **Emerging markets, including Vietnam:** the Fed's tightening exported itself worldwide through the dollar. Vietnam's State Bank was forced to hike its refinancing rate from 4.0% to 6.0% in autumn 2022 just to defend the dong against the surging dollar, the VN-Index fell from a January 2022 peak near 1,528 to a trough of 911 on November 15 — roughly −40% — and foreign investors, chasing higher US yields, were net sellers. The point generalizes: when the Fed tightens hard, the dollar's gravity pulls capital out of every higher-risk market on Earth, and local central banks must either hike in self-defense or watch their currencies break.

#### Worked example: 2022 hikes on a crypto position

Say you bought \$10,000 of Bitcoin at the 2021 year-end close of \$46,306 — top-ticking the cycle, as many did — and held it through the 2022 tightening. The math:

- Entry: \$10,000 at \$46,306 per BTC, so you owned about 0.2160 BTC.
- 2022 year-end close: about \$16,548 per BTC.
- Position value: 0.2160 × \$16,548 ≈ **\$3,573.**
- Loss: \$3,573 − \$10,000 = **−\$6,427**, a −64% drawdown in twelve months.

The intuition: crypto is the high-beta expression of the Fed's liquidity stance, so when the Fed ran the fastest hiking cycle in 40 years, the most liquidity-sensitive asset took the deepest cut — and there was no put coming to save it, because the Fed *wanted* asset prices lower.

#### Worked example: 2022 hikes on a bond position

The cruelty of 2022 was that "going to safety" did not help. Say you moved \$100,000 into a 10-year Treasury at a 1.5% yield in early 2022, seeking shelter. The 10-year yield rose roughly +280bp over the year (1.5% to about 4.3%). The math:

- DV01 on \$100,000 of 10-year exposure: roughly \$86 per basis point.
- Yield move: +280bp.
- Mark-to-market loss: 280 × \$86 ≈ **−\$24,080**, about a −24% drawdown on a "safe" Treasury.

The intuition: in a rate-shock regime there is no hiding place inside traditional assets — the rate going up is the common cause, so the thing you bought to hedge fell alongside the thing you were hedging. Cash and very short bills were the only winners.

#### Worked example: the classic 60/40 portfolio in 2022

The "safe" balanced portfolio — 60% stocks, 40% bonds — is supposed to cushion you because the two legs usually move in opposite directions. In 2022 both legs fell. Say you held a \$50,000 60/40 portfolio at the start of the year:

- Equity leg: \$30,000 × −18% ≈ −\$5,400.
- Bond leg: \$20,000 × −13% ≈ −\$2,600.
- Total: about **−\$8,000**, a −16% year — one of the worst in the history of the 60/40 structure, worse than many pure-equity drawdowns precisely because the hedge failed.

The intuition: diversification protects you against a *growth* shock, when bonds rally as stocks fall; it does nothing against a *rate* shock, when the rising rate is the single thing crushing both legs at once. The dollar move and the rate move shared one cause — a Fed that wanted conditions tighter — so they could not offset each other.

### The repeatable lesson

**The Fed put is suspended whenever it conflicts with the mandate.** In 2018, easing was free because inflation was at target; in 2022, easing was forbidden because inflation was the enemy. A trader who assumed "stocks down 20% means the Fed bails us out" got run over for the entire year, because the regime had flipped from inflation-doesn't-matter to inflation-is-everything. Always ask first: *is the Fed currently free to rescue markets, or is it constrained?* That single question would have kept you out of the worst trade of 2022.

## Episode 4 — September 2024: the first cut and the soft-landing bet

The fourth episode is the quiet counterpoint to the loud three before it. It shows that even a large, dramatic decision can produce almost no reaction — if it was fully priced. It also closes the arc: the Fed that crushed everything in 2022 began the cutting cycle in 2024, and the muted response told you exactly how well the market had anticipated it.

### What was priced

By September 2024, inflation had cooled markedly from its 2022 peak toward the Fed's 2% goal, the labor market had softened but not cracked, and the market had spent months pricing a "soft landing" — the rare outcome where the Fed tames inflation without causing a recession. The only live question into the meeting was the *size* of the first cut: 25bp or 50bp. Futures markets were split, leaning toward the larger cut. So the *direction* (a cut) was certain and the *magnitude* was a coin-flip — meaning very little was left to surprise the market.

### What the Fed signaled

On September 18, 2024, the FOMC delivered a **50bp cut**, lowering the target to a 4.75%–5.00% range — the larger of the two options, and the first cut of the cycle. Powell framed it not as panic but as a "recalibration" of policy now that inflation was under control, a move to protect the still-solid labor market. The dots projected a gradual path of further cuts. The framing was deliberately reassuring: *we are easing because we can, not because we must.*

There was real tension in the framing, and the market had to resolve it in real time. A 50bp first cut is *aggressive* — it is what the Fed does when it is worried it is behind the curve and needs to catch up. Left unexplained, a jumbo cut could have read as "the Fed sees something scary in the data," which would be bearish. Powell's whole communication job at the 2:30 pm presser was to sell the *benign* interpretation: this is a confident recalibration toward neutral, not an emergency. He largely succeeded, and the dot plot — which showed only a measured pace of cuts ahead — backed him up. The market accepted the soft-landing story, which is exactly why the reaction was so muted.

### The cross-asset reaction

- **Stocks:** the S&P 500 closed down a trivial 0.29% on the day. Read that against the −1.54% of the 2018 hawkish hike and the +2.24% of the 2022 liftoff: a *bigger* policy action (a 50bp cut) produced the *smallest* reaction, because it was so thoroughly priced. The market then rallied the next session and pushed to new highs into year-end as the soft-landing thesis held.
- **Bonds:** counterintuitively, the 10-year yield *rose* about 7bp on the day and continued higher in the following weeks. A cut is supposed to lower yields — but the front end had already priced an aggressive easing path, and the resilient economy plus the "we are not in trouble" framing led the market to price *fewer* future cuts, lifting longer yields. The decision cut the overnight rate; the *signal* about the path lifted the long end. This is the same path-versus-decision distinction we saw in 2013, just running the other way.
- **The dollar:** broadly steady-to-firm; the resilient-economy framing supported it even as the Fed eased.
- **Crypto:** Bitcoin took the cut as a liquidity positive and traded firm into the end of 2024, consistent with its role as a liquidity-sensitive risk asset — but the move was about the broader easing trajectory, not the single meeting.

There is a subtle trade hiding in that bond reaction worth a worked example, because it is the same path-versus-decision split we met in 2013, just running in reverse.

#### Worked example: the path trade on the 2024 cut

Say you reasoned that the front end had over-priced the easing cycle and that a confident Fed would let *longer* yields drift up, so you put on a position that profits from a +10bp rise in the 10-year on a \$200,000 notional. The 10-year did rise about +7bp on the day and kept climbing in the following weeks. The math:

- DV01 on \$200,000 of 10-year exposure (short): roughly \$172 per basis point.
- Move captured on the day: +7bp.
- Gain: 7 × \$172 ≈ **+\$1,204** on the session, and several multiples of that over the following weeks as the 10-year rose toward 4.3%.

The intuition: the Fed cut the *overnight* rate but its *path* signal lifted the long end, so the right trade was the opposite of the naive "cut means lower yields" instinct — you traded the path, not the decision.

#### Worked example: the September 2024 first cut on a balanced book

Say you held a \$25,000 equity-heavy book into the September 18, 2024 meeting, braced for volatility. The math:

- Book value at the open: \$25,000.
- S&P 500 same-day move: −0.29%.
- Mark-to-market: \$25,000 × −0.0029 = **−\$72.50**, essentially a rounding error.
- For comparison, the same \$25,000 book on the 2022 hot-CPI session (−4.32%) would have lost \$25,000 × −0.0432 ≈ −\$1,080 — about 15 times more, on a *non-Fed* event.

The intuition: the size of the reaction is set by the size of the *surprise*, not the size of the decision — a 50bp cut that everyone expected moved the book by \$73, while a hot inflation print that nobody expected could move the same book by over a thousand dollars.

### The repeatable lesson

**A fully-priced decision is a non-event, no matter how large.** The 50bp cut was the biggest single FOMC action of all four episodes and produced the smallest market move, because the market had already done the work. The corollary for traders: the meetings worth positioning around are the ones where the *outcome is genuinely uncertain* or where the *dots and tone can surprise* — not the ones where the decision is a foregone conclusion.

## Cross-episode patterns

Lay the four episodes side by side and the same structure appears every time. The reaction is never the headline alone; it is the product of three lenses.

![Matrix of what was priced times the signal times the regime equals the reaction across four FOMC episodes](/imgs/blogs/fomc-case-studies-taper-tantrum-2018-pivot-2022-hikes-7.png)

**Pattern 1 — Bonds reprice the path; stocks reprice the consequences.** In every episode, Treasury yields moved on the expected *path* of rates, mechanically and immediately, while stocks moved on what the path *meant* for growth and discount rates. In 2018, bonds rallied (recession hedge) while stocks fell (slowdown fear) — opposite directions, same Fed, because they were pricing different things. Watching both at once tells you whether the market reads the Fed as a growth threat (yields down, stocks down) or a pure rate threat (yields up, stocks down, as in 2022).

**Pattern 2 — The signal usually beats the decision.** 2013 (a hint, no decision), 2018 (an as-expected hike with hawkish dots and tone), and 2024 (a priced-in cut with a hawkish path signal) all moved markets primarily through *communication*, not the rate change itself. The press conference and the dots are where the surprises live. Only when a decision itself is unexpected — which is rare, because the Fed telegraphs heavily — does the number dominate.

**Pattern 3 — The regime decides whether the put is live.** This is the master variable. When inflation is at or below target (2013, 2018, 2024), the Fed is *free* to be dovish and the put is alive; bad news for the economy is good news for stocks. When inflation is the enemy (2022), the put is suspended; the Fed *wants* tighter conditions, and there is no rescue. The same 20% drawdown that triggered a pivot in 2018 triggered nothing in 2022, because the regime was different.

**Pattern 4 — Cross-asset correlations depend on the shock type.** A *growth* shock (2018, 2008) pushes stocks down and bonds up — diversification works. A *rate* shock (2022) pushes stocks down and bonds down together — diversification fails. The single best early read on which regime you are in is the direction of bonds on a risk-off day: if Treasuries are rallying as stocks fall, it is a growth scare and the put may come; if Treasuries are *falling* alongside stocks, it is a rate shock and there is no cavalry. This is the through-line of the entire cross-asset map.

**Pattern 5 — The shape of the move: spike, fade, or trend.** The first reaction to an FOMC headline is the *knee-jerk* — the millisecond algorithmic move at 2:00 pm as machines diff the statement. What happens next falls into three patterns, and recognizing which one you are in is the whole game. A *spike-and-fade* happens when the knee-jerk overshoots and there is no follow-through information; the March 2022 liftoff is an example — the relief pop was real but the day's move did not compound into a trend, because the hike was priced and the path was the real story. A *spike-and-trend* happens when the presser confirms and deepens the initial signal; December 2018 is the textbook case — the statement looked benign, but Powell's tone added information that *trended* the market down for a week. And a *non-event* happens when everything is priced; September 2024, a −0.29% day on the biggest decision of the four. The practical rule: do not assume the 2:00 pm move is the day's move. Wait for the presser to tell you whether the signal compounds (trend) or exhausts (fade).

## Common misconceptions

**"The Fed always rescues stocks."** This is the single most expensive belief in markets, and 2022 disproved it cleanly. The S&P fell 19% and the Nasdaq 33% over the year while the Fed *kept hiking*, because cutting would have re-stoked the inflation it was fighting. The Fed put is real but conditional: it appears when easing is consistent with the mandate (2018, 2019, 2024) and vanishes when it is not (2022, and arguably the entire Volcker era — see [Paul Volcker's 1980 rate shock](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation)). On a \$40,000 long, the difference between a regime where the put fires and one where it does not was the difference between recovering your \$7,920 drawdown by July and watching it deepen for a full year.

**"A bigger rate move means a bigger market reaction."** False, and 2024 is the proof: the 50bp cut — the largest single action of all four episodes — produced the smallest move (−0.29% on the S&P), while the as-expected 25bp hike of December 2018 produced a −1.54% day and capped a 19.8% quarter. The reaction scales with the *surprise*, not the magnitude. A \$25,000 book lost \$73 on the big-but-priced 2024 cut.

**"Bonds are safe; they protect you when stocks fall."** True in a growth shock, false in a rate shock. In 2022, a \$100,000 "safe" Treasury position lost about \$24,080 mark-to-market as yields rose +280bp, *at the same time* stocks were falling. Bonds only hedge equities when the thing hurting stocks is fear of recession (which lowers yields). When the thing hurting stocks is rising rates, bonds fall with them.

**"You should trade the FOMC decision the moment it hits."** The decision is usually the least surprising part. The statement drops at 2:00 pm Eastern, but the real information — and the real volatility — often comes during Powell's 2:30 pm press conference, when an unscripted phrase reprices the path. Plenty of FOMC days have reversed entirely between 2:00 and 3:00 pm. Trade the *whole* event, and respect that the tone can override the number.

**"The taper tantrum was about rate hikes."** It was not. The Fed did not hike a single time in 2013; it merely hinted that it might eventually *slow* its bond buying. The 10-year still rose +138bp. The episode is a lesson about the power of forward guidance and balance-sheet expectations, not the rate itself.

**"A cut is always bullish and a hike is always bearish."** The sign depends entirely on what was priced and why the Fed is acting. The September 2024 *cut* saw the 10-year yield *rise*, because the market read the resilient-economy framing as a reason to price fewer future cuts. The March 2022 *hike* saw stocks *rise* 2.24%, because the move was priced and the clarity was a relief. Conversely, a cut delivered because the economy is collapsing (an emergency cut) is deeply bearish — it confirms the bad news. Never read the direction of the rate move alone; read it against expectations and the reason for the move.

## The playbook: repeatable lessons for trading the FOMC

Pull the four episodes together into an if-then map you can actually use.

**Before the meeting — establish what is priced.** Read fed funds futures and the SOFR curve to see exactly what hike/cut and what path the market expects. The decision matters only to the extent it deviates from this. If the outcome is a foregone conclusion (like September 2024), expect a small reaction and do not over-size; if the outcome is genuinely uncertain or the dots could shift (like a meeting with a fresh Summary of Economic Projections in a turning cycle), that is where the edge is.

**Identify the regime first.** Ask the one question that would have saved you in 2022: *is the Fed currently free to be dovish?* If inflation is at or below target, the put is live and dips are likely to be bought. If inflation is the binding constraint, the put is suspended, "bad news is bad," and you do not fade weakness expecting a rescue. The regime sets the sign of every reaction.

**Map the three surprise channels.**

- *Decision surprise* (hike/cut/hold vs. priced): hits the front end of the curve hardest and fastest.
- *Dot-plot surprise* (path shift): hits the belly and long end, and can dominate the decision — the December 2018 hawkish dots were the real story.
- *Tone surprise* (the presser): can reverse the entire day; budget for the 2:30 pm volatility, not just 2:00 pm.

**Position per asset, conditional on the surprise.**

- *More hawkish than priced:* front-end yields up, dollar up, growth/tech stocks down hardest, crypto down hard (it is the highest-beta liquidity asset). If the regime allows a put, look for the level where the Fed historically blinks; if it does not (inflation fight), do not.
- *More dovish than priced:* yields down, dollar down, long-duration assets (small caps, growth, crypto) outperform — but watch the long end, which can *rise* if the market reads the easing as a sign of economic strength (September 2024).
- *In line with expectations:* fade the knee-jerk. A priced-in outcome often produces a relief move (the 2022 liftoff rose 2.24%) followed by a return to the prevailing trend.

**Watch bonds for the regime read.** On the reaction, the direction of Treasuries tells you which world you are in: yields *down* with stocks down means a growth scare (put may come); yields *up* with stocks down means a rate shock (no cavalry). This is the single most useful cross-asset tell.

**Size for the surprise, not the decision.** Our worked examples make the asymmetry concrete: a fully-priced 50bp cut moved a \$25,000 book by \$73, while an unexpected hot inflation print moved the same book by over \$1,000. Reserve real risk for the meetings with genuine two-sided uncertainty, and keep it small when the outcome is a formality.

#### Worked example: sizing a position to a fixed risk budget

The disciplined way to size an FOMC trade is to fix your dollar risk and let the expected move determine the size. Say you are willing to risk \$500 on a meeting and the options market is pricing an expected move of about 1.2% in the S&P 500 (an at-the-money straddle worth roughly \$60 on a 5,000-level index implies \$60 / 5,000 = 1.2%). The math:

- Risk budget: \$500.
- Expected one-day move: 1.2% of position value.
- Position cap: \$500 / 0.012 ≈ **\$41,700** of S&P exposure if you are willing to lose roughly one expected move.
- But for an *uncertain* meeting where the move could be 2× the priced one, halve it: cap nearer **\$20,800**, so a 2.4% adverse surprise still only costs about \$500.

The intuition: the options market hands you the size of the expected move for free in the straddle price, so let it set your position rather than your conviction — on a high-uncertainty meeting the same \$500 of risk buys you only half the exposure, which is exactly correct.

**The invalidation.** If you positioned for a reaction and the surprise channels all come in as priced, the trade is wrong — flatten it rather than hoping for a delayed move. And if the regime read changes mid-event (Powell's tone flips the put on or off), respect it immediately: the December 2018-to-January 2019 turn shows the entire sign of the market can reverse on a single phrase. The cost of fighting a regime shift is the −19.8% Q4 of 2018; the reward for reading it is the +30% of 2019.

To put the whole playbook in one sentence: the FOMC is not a coin-flip on hawkish-versus-dovish, it is a structured event where the reaction equals what was priced, measured against what the Fed signaled, multiplied by the regime you are in. The taper tantrum was a path surprise in a benign regime that hit bonds; December 2018 was a tone surprise in a benign regime that hit stocks and then reversed; 2022 was a determined Fed in a hostile regime that hit everything with no rescue; September 2024 was a fully-priced action in a friendly regime that hit nothing. Memorize those four shapes and you will recognize them in every future meeting, because the Fed keeps changing its decisions but the *structure* of how markets react to it does not change at all.
