> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — One US data surprise does not hit "the market"; it travels down a *chain* of asset classes, and each link reacts with its own size and its own sign.
>
> - The chain is: **surprise → rate expectations (front-end Treasuries) → the dollar → equities → gold → crypto → emerging markets and the VN-Index.** Price already holds the consensus; only the surprise moves anything.
> - The reaction map is *not* "everything moves together." On the hot Sep-13-2022 CPI the S&P fell **−4.32%**, the dollar *rose* **+1.4%**, gold slipped **−0.4%**, and Bitcoin crashed **−9.4%** — one number, four different signs and sizes.
> - Each asset has a different **beta to the surprise**: crypto is the biggest mover (leverage + pure liquidity), the dollar is the smallest (it *is* the rate gauge). Knowing the betas tells you which asset best expresses your view.
> - The one number to remember: **−4.32% (S&P) versus −9.4% (BTC) on the same print** — roughly a 2× crypto amplifier.

At 8:30 a.m. Eastern on September 13, 2022, the Bureau of Labor Statistics published a single line: consumer prices rose 8.3% over the prior year, against a consensus of 8.1%. Two tenths of a percentage point hotter than expected. In the next six and a half hours, that one line did very different things to very different markets. The S&P 500 closed down **4.32%** — its worst session since June 2020. The Nasdaq fell **5.16%**. But the US dollar index *rose* **1.4%**, gold slipped a modest **0.4%**, and Bitcoin — trading around the clock, with no closing bell to hide behind — cratered roughly **9.4%**.

Stop and look at those signs. The same print that *destroyed* stocks *lifted* the dollar. The same print that barely dented gold *gutted* crypto. If markets simply "moved together," none of that would happen. They do not move together. They move along a chain, and the sign and size of the move depend on *where in the chain* each asset sits and *how leveraged* its reaction is. That chain — how one print hits every market — is the subject of this post, and it is the payoff of everything else in this foundations track. Once you can see the chain, a confusing wall of red and green tickers turns into a single causal story you can read in order.

The picture below is the map we will spend the rest of the article filling in. Keep it open as a reference: surprise at the top, then the relay through rate expectations, then the dollar, then the assets that are all priced *against* rates and the dollar.

## Foundations: the transmission chain

Before we trace a single episode, we need to agree on what a "surprise" is and what the links of the chain actually are. None of this requires finance background — we build each term from zero.

### Markets trade the surprise, not the number

A price you see on a screen is a bet about the future that *thousands* of participants have already placed. By the time a number is released, the *expected* value of that number is already baked into prices. Economists publish a **consensus** — the median forecast — ahead of every major release. If the actual figure lands exactly on consensus, in theory nothing should move, because nothing was learned. What moves price is the **surprise**: the gap between the actual print and the consensus.

> The surprise = actual − consensus. Price already contains the consensus. Only the surprise is new information, and only new information moves markets on the release.

So on September 13, 2022, the number was not "8.3% inflation." The *number that mattered* was "+0.2 percentage points hotter than the 8.1% everyone penciled in." Two tenths. That is the spark. Everything downstream is the chain reaction.

### The chain has six links

A US data surprise — especially an inflation or jobs surprise — propagates through markets in a roughly fixed order, because each asset is priced off the link before it. Here are the six links, defined from scratch.

1. **The front-end rate channel.** The fastest-moving thing is the market's guess about what the Federal Reserve will do with interest rates. That guess lives in **short-dated Treasury yields** — especially the **2-year Treasury yield**, which is essentially the market's forecast of the average Fed policy rate over the next two years. A hot inflation print says "the Fed will have to keep rates higher for longer," so the 2-year yield jumps *within milliseconds*. This is the first link, and it is the engine for everything else.

2. **The dollar channel.** Money flows to where it earns the most, adjusted for risk. If US short-term rates just rose relative to Europe's or Japan's, holding dollars suddenly pays more. Global capital rotates *into* dollars, and the **dollar index (DXY)** — the dollar measured against a basket of major currencies — rises. So a hot US print, via higher US rates, tends to *strengthen* the dollar.

3. **The discount-rate channel (equities).** A stock is a claim on a company's future cash flows. To value those future dollars today, you *discount* them by an interest rate — future money is worth less when rates are higher, because you could otherwise earn that rate risk-free. When the front-end rate jumps, the discount rate on every stock jumps, and the present value of those future cash flows *falls*. That is why a hot inflation print, in a regime where the Fed is fighting inflation, *sinks* equities even though it lifts the dollar.

4. **The real-yield channel (gold).** Gold pays no interest and generates no cash flow. Its main competitor is a safe bond that *does* pay interest after inflation — the **real yield** (the nominal yield minus expected inflation). When real yields rise, holding gold has a higher *opportunity cost*, so gold tends to fall. A hot print can cut both ways for gold (it raises nominal yields, which hurts, but it is itself an inflation signal, which can help), which is exactly why gold is often the *smallest* and most ambiguous mover.

5. **The liquidity / high-beta channel (crypto).** Bitcoin and the broader crypto complex have no cash flows to discount and no central bank. What they have is extreme sensitivity to *financial conditions* — how much cheap, abundant money is sloshing through the system. When a hot print tightens the expected path of policy (less liquidity ahead) and lifts the dollar (the global cost of money), the most liquidity-sensitive, most leveraged asset moves the *most*. Crypto is the amplifier at the end of the chain.

6. **The foreign-flow channel (emerging markets and Vietnam).** Emerging and frontier markets like Vietnam are not priced directly off US data — but they are priced off the *dollar* and *US yields*, which the US print just moved. A stronger dollar and higher US yields pull global capital *home* to the US, so foreign investors *sell* emerging-market stocks and currencies. In Vietnam this shows up as **foreign outflows from the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE)** and pressure on the **dong (VND)**, and the **VN-Index** tends to react with a *lag* of a few sessions.

### What "beta to the surprise" means

The single most useful idea in this whole article is **beta to the surprise**. Borrowed from statistics, *beta* here just means: for a given size of surprise, how big is this asset's reaction? A high-beta asset swings a lot per unit of surprise; a low-beta asset barely flinches.

Crucially, beta is *not* the same as direction. Two assets can have opposite signs but similar magnitudes, or the same sign but wildly different magnitudes. On the hot September 2022 print, equities and crypto both fell — same sign — but Bitcoin fell roughly twice as far. The dollar rose — opposite sign — but only by 1.4%, the smallest move of the lot. Beta captures the *size*; the channel captures the *sign*.

#### Worked example: one surprise, four signs, on a \$10,000 stake

Say you held \$10,000 in each of four assets going into the September 13, 2022 CPI, and you simply rode the same-day move.

- Stocks (S&P 500 tracker), −4.32%: \$10,000 × (−0.0432) = **−\$432**.
- Bitcoin, −9.4%: \$10,000 × (−0.094) = **−\$940**.
- Gold, −0.4%: \$10,000 × (−0.004) = **−\$40**.
- A *long dollar* position (long DXY), +1.4%: \$10,000 × (+0.014) = **+\$140**.

Four positions, the *same* trigger, and the dollar position made money while the other three lost it — and crypto lost more than twice what stocks did. The takeaway in one line: a single print does not have a single sign; it has a *map* of signs, and your job is to read the map, not to guess "risk on or risk off."

### The chain has a clock, not just an order

The six links do not fire all at once — they fire in a *sequence measured in milliseconds to days*, and that timing is itself tradable. At the instant of release, the fastest, most automated markets re-price first: fed funds futures and the 2-year Treasury move in *microseconds*, because algorithms parse the headline number against the consensus and adjust before a human can read the screen. The dollar follows within the same second, because FX is nearly as automated and is mechanically tied to the rate differential. Equity index futures jump in the same breath, but the *cash* equity market — individual stocks — needs the 9:30 a.m. open to fully express the move if the print landed at 8:30. Gold and crypto, both traded continuously, re-price immediately, with crypto continuing to grind for hours because it never closes.

Then come the slower links. Emerging-market equities in their own time zones cannot react until *their* sessions open — Vietnam's market, eleven to twelve hours ahead of New York, often reacts the *next* Vietnamese trading day, and the full foreign-flow effect plays out over *several* sessions. So the same print is "instant" for the 2-year, "this second" for the dollar, "this morning" for US stocks, and "this week" for the VN-Index. The chain is not just an order of *causes*; it is a timeline of *when each market gets the memo*. That gap between the fast links and the slow links is where the lead/lag trades in the playbook live.

### Why the signs flip across assets

Beginners expect a "big day" to be uniformly red or uniformly green. The reason it never is comes down to a simple split: some assets are priced *as* the cost of money, and some are priced *against* it. The dollar and front-end yields *are* the cost-of-money variables — when money gets more expensive (a hot print), they go *up*. Stocks, gold, and crypto are all assets you discount *using* the cost of money, or that *compete* with interest-bearing safe assets — when money gets more expensive, their value goes *down*. That is the whole reason a hot print is green for the dollar and red for everything else: you cannot simultaneously make money more expensive *and* make the things priced against expensive money worth more. The sign flip is not noise; it is arithmetic.

## Rates first: the 2-year prices the Fed path

Everything starts with the front end of the yield curve, so we start there too.

When traders process a hot inflation print, the very first thing they re-price is the *Federal Reserve's reaction function* — the rule that links incoming data to future rate decisions. Higher inflation than expected means a higher probability the Fed hikes more, or holds high rates longer. The market expresses that instantly in **fed funds futures** and in the **2-year Treasury yield**, which together encode the expected policy path. (The mechanism by which the Fed actually sets that rate is covered in [How the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates); here we care about how the *expectation* moves on the print.)

On September 13, 2022, the 2-year yield jumped about **+18 basis points** (a basis point is one hundredth of a percentage point) in a single session, while the 10-year rose only about +6bp. That tells you the surprise hit the *near-term* policy expectation hardest — the market decided the Fed would have to do more *soon*. The front end leads; the long end follows more reluctantly, because long rates also embed growth and term-premium considerations.

Why does an 18bp move on a yield matter so much? Because bonds have **duration** — a measure of how much a bond's price moves when its yield moves. A rough rule: a 2-year note has a duration near 2, so a +18bp yield move costs about 0.18% × 2 ≈ 0.36% of price. That sounds small until you remember the sums involved and the *leverage* downstream.

#### Worked example: an 18bp 2-year move on a \$1,000,000 position

A government-bond desk holds \$1,000,000 face of 2-year Treasuries. The position's **DV01** — dollars lost per 1bp rise in yield — is roughly \$1,000,000 × 0.0001 × 2 (duration) = \$200 per basis point.

- On a +18bp move: 18 × \$200 = **−\$3,600** on the day.
- Scale that to a \$50,000,000 book and the same +18bp print is 18 × (\$50,000,000 × 0.0001 × 2) = 18 × \$10,000 = **−\$180,000**.
- A desk that *anticipated* the hot print and was *short* the 2-year (positioned for yields to rise) instead *makes* the \$3,600 per million.

The lesson: the front-end rate move looks tiny in percentage terms but is the *origin* of the whole chain — and on a large or leveraged book it is real money. Every other asset's move is, in a sense, a *levered echo* of this first 18bp.

The reason rates lead is plumbing: the front-end rates market is the deepest, fastest, most algorithmic market on earth, and it is the most *direct* read on the Fed. Stocks, the dollar, gold, and crypto all have to ask "what does this print mean for the Fed?" — and the 2-year has already answered. (For the Fed-watching mechanics behind this, see [Trading the dollar: DXY, carry and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile), which leans on the same rate-differential logic.)

There is a second, subtler thing the front end tells you: not just *whether* the Fed path moved, but *which part* of it. On September 13, 2022, the 2-year jumped 18bp while the 10-year rose only 6bp. The 2-year mostly reflects the *near-term* policy path — the next year or two of Fed meetings — while the 10-year blends the policy path with longer-run growth and inflation expectations and a term premium. When the 2-year out-moves the 10-year on a hot print, the **yield curve flattens** (or, as in late 2022, inverts further): the market is saying "the Fed will hike *now*, which slows the economy *later*, so long-term growth and rates should actually be *lower*." That curve shape is itself a recession signal, and it feeds back into equities through the growth channel. (The slope-and-inversion mechanics are unpacked in [Reading the yield curve: slope, inversion, recession](/blog/trading/macro-trading/reading-the-yield-curve-slope-inversion-recession).)

The microstructure of the front-end move on a print is worth picturing concretely. In the seconds after 8:30 a.m., the 2-year future can gap by several basis points on the first transaction, blow through several price levels as resting orders get hit, and then chop violently as dealers re-hedge and the second wave of participants (those who waited to read the *internals* of the report — core vs. headline, shelter vs. goods) join in. The headline number moves price first; the *composition* of the report can move it *again* a minute later. This is why a print that is hot on the headline but soft on the closely-watched core can produce a sharp initial spike that *fades* within minutes — the chain's first link reverses, and every downstream asset follows the reversal.

## The dollar: rate differentials pull capital

The second link is the dollar, and it follows almost mechanically from the first.

Currencies are *relative* prices — the dollar's value is always measured against *other* currencies. The dominant driver over short horizons is the **interest-rate differential**: the gap between what you earn holding dollars and what you earn holding euros, yen, or pounds. When the US 2-year yield jumps on a hot print but German and Japanese yields do not, the *reward* for holding dollars rises relative to those currencies. Global capital chases that reward, buying dollars and selling the others, and the **dollar index (DXY)** climbs.

On September 13, 2022, DXY rose **+1.4%** — a large daily move for a major currency index, where 0.3% is an ordinary day. That move was the front-end rate jump, expressed in FX. And because so much of the world's debt, trade, and commodity pricing is denominated in dollars, a stronger dollar tightens *global* financial conditions: it makes dollar debt harder to service, makes imports priced in dollars more expensive, and pulls capital out of riskier corners of the world. The dollar is the *transmission belt* from a US data point to the rest of the planet.

Notice the dollar's beta, though: +1.4% is the *smallest* of the major moves on that day. That is not a coincidence. The dollar is close to the *source* of the impulse — it is essentially the rate differential wearing a currency costume — rather than a leveraged bet stacked on top of it. The further down the chain you go, the more the move compounds; the dollar sits near the top, so its percentage move is modest even though its *consequences* are enormous.

The dollar's sign can also flip depending on *which* macro story is in charge — the so-called **dollar smile**. When the US is the lone strong economy, good US data lifts the dollar (the right side of the smile). When the world is panicking, the dollar rises as the safe haven *regardless* of US data (the left side). Only in the calm middle does good news abroad weaken the dollar. The September 2022 episode sits firmly on the right side: hot US inflation, hawkish Fed, dollar up.

The dollar's power in the chain comes from a fact that has nothing to do with the United States' size and everything to do with *plumbing*: the dollar is the world's funding and invoicing currency. Roughly half of all cross-border bank lending and trade invoicing is in dollars; most commodities are priced in dollars; and a vast quantity of debt issued by companies and governments *outside* the US is denominated in dollars. So when the dollar strengthens, a Brazilian company with dollar debt and real revenue suddenly owes *more* in local-currency terms; an importer paying for oil in dollars pays *more*; a fund that borrowed cheap dollars to buy higher-yielding assets abroad sees its funding cost rise. A stronger dollar is a *global margin call* — it tightens conditions for every dollar-borrower on earth. That is the precise mechanism by which a US inflation print, via the front-end rate and then the dollar, reaches markets that never traded a single US Treasury.

#### Worked example: a dollar move sizing a \$50,000 currency hedge

A US-based fund holds \$50,000 of European stocks and worries a hot CPI print will strengthen the dollar against the euro, eroding the dollar value of those shares. It hedges by going short the euro (long the dollar) in size matched to the position.

- A hot print lifts the dollar 1.4% (the September 2022 magnitude). The European shares, *measured in dollars*, lose roughly \$50,000 × 1.4% = **−\$700** from the currency move alone, before any change in the stocks' local price.
- The matched long-dollar hedge gains roughly \$50,000 × 1.4% = **+\$700**, offsetting the currency drag.
- Net currency impact after the hedge: about **\$0** — the fund kept its equity view and shed the dollar risk it did not want.

The intuition: the dollar leg of the chain is small in percentage terms but reaches *into* every foreign position you hold, so a 1.4% dollar move is a real \$700 swing on a \$50,000 foreign book — which is exactly why cross-asset traders watch DXY on every US print.

## Equities: discount rate plus growth

The third link is equities, and here the *sign* is the part beginners find most counterintuitive.

A share of stock is a claim on a stream of future cash flows — earnings, dividends, buybacks — stretching out for decades. To turn those future dollars into a price today, you discount them at a rate that rises with interest rates. When the front-end rate jumped on the hot September 2022 print, the discount rate on *every* future dollar of corporate earnings rose, and the present value of those earnings *fell*. That is the **discount-rate channel**, and it is why a hot inflation print *sank* stocks: −4.32% on the S&P, −5.16% on the Nasdaq.

Why did the Nasdaq fall *more* than the S&P? Duration again — this time *equity* duration. Tech and growth companies earn most of their cash flows *far in the future*; those distant cash flows are the most sensitive to the discount rate, exactly like a long-maturity bond. So growth stocks have higher beta to a rate surprise than value stocks or dividend payers, whose cash flows are nearer-term. The Nasdaq is a long-duration index; the Dow (−3.94% that day) is shorter-duration. Same print, different equity betas.

There is a second channel layered on top: **growth**. A stock is worth more if the company will *earn* more. A hot inflation print, in the 2022 regime, did not just raise the discount rate — it raised the odds the Fed would tighten so hard it caused a recession, which would *shrink* future earnings. So both terms of the valuation moved against stocks at once: higher discount rate *and* lower expected growth. That double hit is why the equity reaction was so violent.

The regime matters enormously here. In a *deflation-scare* regime — say early in a downturn when the fear is too-*little* growth — a hot inflation print can *rally* stocks, because it signals the economy is not collapsing. The channel (discount rate vs. growth) is fixed, but which term dominates depends on what the market is afraid of *that day*. In 2022 the fear was inflation and over-tightening, so good-news-on-the-economy was bad-news-for-stocks. Always name the regime before you predict the sign. ([Risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates) maps these regime shifts in more detail.)

Inside the equity market, the rate surprise does not hit every stock equally — it *re-sorts* them, and the sorting is information. On a hot print, the heaviest selling concentrates in the longest-duration corners: unprofitable growth, speculative tech, anything valued on cash flows a decade out. The shortest-duration corners — energy, banks (which can earn *more* from higher rates), cash-rich value names, dividend payers — hold up far better and sometimes rise. This **sector dispersion** is why two investors can both "own stocks" into the same print and have wildly different days: a portfolio of profitless tech can fall twice as far as a portfolio of energy and banks on an identical CPI surprise. The cross-asset chain has a *within-asset* echo: duration governs beta inside equities just as it governs beta across the whole map.

The other thing equities do that the cleaner links often do not is *fade and reverse*. The cash equity market is full of discretionary humans, systematic funds, and dealer hedging flows that interact in the hours after a print. A violent knee-jerk down can be bought back if positioning was already bearish (there was no one left to sell), or if the *internals* of the report were softer than the headline, or if a Fed speaker walks back the hawkish read that afternoon. The November 2022 cool print is a famous case of the *trend* version — the rally extended for days — while plenty of hot prints have produced a sharp morning plunge that recovered most of its losses by the close as the initial overshoot faded. The chain tells you the *initial* sign and relative size; whether it becomes a fade or a trend is the second decision, and it hinges on positioning and follow-through, not on the print itself.

#### Worked example: a hot-CPI equity day across position sizes

Suppose three investors each held S&P 500 exposure into the September 13, 2022 print and took the full −4.32% close-to-close.

- A \$25,000 retirement account: \$25,000 × (−0.0432) = **−\$1,080** in one session.
- A \$250,000 brokerage account: \$250,000 × (−0.0432) = **−\$10,800**.
- A trader who held the *Nasdaq* (−5.16%) on a \$25,000 stake: \$25,000 × (−0.0516) = **−\$1,290** — \$210 worse than the S&P holder on the identical print, purely because of the higher equity duration.

The intuition: the same surprise costs the long-duration (Nasdaq) holder more than the broad-index holder, because distant cash flows discount harder — equity "duration" is as real as bond duration.

## Gold: real yields and the dollar pull in opposite directions

The fourth link is gold, and gold is special because *two* of its drivers point in *opposite* directions on a hot print, which is why it is often the *smallest* and most ambiguous mover.

Gold pays no coupon, no dividend, no rent. Holding it earns you nothing except price change. So gold's biggest enemy is a *safe, interest-bearing* alternative — specifically, the **real yield**, the return on an inflation-protected Treasury (the nominal yield minus expected inflation). When real yields rise, the opportunity cost of holding a zero-yield rock goes up, and gold tends to fall. A hot inflation print raises *nominal* yields, which usually drags real yields up too — bad for gold.

But a hot inflation print is also, definitionally, *more inflation*, and gold is widely held as an inflation hedge. That second channel pushes gold *up*. And the *dollar* channel pushes it down again: gold is priced in dollars worldwide, so a stronger dollar makes gold more expensive for foreign buyers and tends to lower the dollar price.

Add it up on September 13, 2022: real yields up (bearish), dollar up sharply (bearish), inflation signal up (bullish). The bearish forces won, but only narrowly — gold fell just **−0.4%**, a rounding error next to the −9.4% in Bitcoin. That near-cancellation is the *signature* of gold's low beta to inflation surprises: its drivers fight each other. (The master-signal framing of real yields is developed in [Real vs nominal inflation: real yields, the master signal](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

The practical upshot: do not expect gold to be a clean expression of an inflation view. If you are *sure* a print will be hot, gold is a *muddy* trade — the dollar and real-yield channels will partly eat your thesis. The clean expressions are elsewhere on the chain.

There is a useful diagnostic hiding in gold's ambiguity. Because gold sits at the intersection of the real-yield and dollar channels, its reaction *tells you which channel is dominant* on a given print. If a hot print sends gold sharply *lower*, the real-yield and dollar channels are firmly in control — a textbook hawkish reaction. If a hot print sends gold *higher* despite rising yields, the market is treating the inflation signal as the dominant story (or fearing the Fed is *behind the curve* and losing control of inflation), which is a very different, more dangerous regime. So even though gold is a poor *vehicle* for an inflation trade, it is an excellent *gauge* of how the market is interpreting the print — watch it to read the regime, not to express the view.

## Crypto: the high-beta amplifier

The fifth link is crypto, and it is the loudest one on the chain.

Bitcoin has no cash flows to discount, no central bank, no earnings, no coupon. What it has — over the 2020s, increasingly — is a tight link to global **liquidity** and **financial conditions**. When money is cheap and abundant, the most speculative, most leveraged assets fly; when policy tightens and the dollar strengthens, those same assets fall the hardest. Crypto sits at the *very end* of the transmission chain, downstream of rates, the dollar, *and* risk appetite, and it carries the *most leverage* in its market structure (perpetual futures, high-leverage offshore venues, liquidation cascades). All of that makes its **beta to the surprise the highest of any major asset**.

On the hot September 2022 print, Bitcoin fell roughly **−9.4%** — more than double the S&P's −4.32%. On the cool November 2022 print it rose about **+10.0%**, versus the S&P's +5.54%. Same direction as equities each time (crypto is a risk asset, not a hedge), but a *much* bigger swing. (The macro-liquidity framing — why crypto behaves like a leveraged bet on financial conditions — is laid out in [Crypto as a macro-liquidity asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/crypto-as-a-macro-liquidity-asset).)

Two structural features amplify the move further. First, **24/7 trading**: crypto has no closing bell, so a print released at 8:30 a.m. ET keeps moving the price all night while equity futures gap and reopen — the *measured* same-day move captures the full reaction without the muting effect of a market close. Second, **liquidations**: leveraged long positions get force-closed when price drops past their margin, and those forced sales push price *further* down, triggering more liquidations — a cascade that turns a −4% impulse into a −9% reality.

The liquidation cascade deserves a closer look, because it is the single biggest reason crypto's beta is so high. A large share of crypto trading happens on *perpetual futures* — leveraged contracts with no expiry — often at 5×, 10×, or higher leverage. When a trader is long Bitcoin at 10× leverage, a 10% drop *wipes out* their entire margin, and the exchange automatically closes the position by *selling* into the market. That forced sale pushes price down a little more, which crosses the liquidation level for the *next* leveraged long, whose position is also sold, and so on. The result is a self-reinforcing spiral: a modest macro impulse at the top of the chain (a hot print, a stronger dollar) becomes a violent downdraft once the leverage in the system unwinds. The same mechanism runs in reverse on a cool print — short liquidations ("short squeezes") amplify the rally. This is why crypto's reaction is not just *bigger* than equities' but *jumpier*, with sharp spikes and air pockets that the smoother, lower-leverage equity market rarely shows.

There is a regime caveat, because crypto's link to macro is not constant. In 2022, crypto was tightly coupled to the Nasdaq and to liquidity — it behaved like a leveraged tech stock. In other periods, idiosyncratic crypto events (an exchange collapse, an ETF approval, a halving) dominate, and the macro beta temporarily weakens. The late-2023 cool print, where Bitcoin rose only +1.5% against the S&P's +1.91%, is a case where crypto's own drivers were in the driver's seat and the macro amplifier was muted. The chain still applies — crypto moved the *right direction* — but the size depended on whether macro or crypto-native catalysts were dominant *that week*.

![Ranked diagram of asset sensitivity showing crypto highest beta and the dollar lowest](/imgs/blogs/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market-5.png)

#### Worked example: crypto's amplifier on a \$10,000 stake, both directions

Take the two 2022 prints and a \$10,000 stake in each of Bitcoin and the S&P 500.

- Hot Sep-13 print — Bitcoin −9.4%: \$10,000 × (−0.094) = **−\$940**. S&P −4.32%: \$10,000 × (−0.0432) = **−\$432**. Crypto lost **\$508 more** on the identical trigger.
- Cool Nov-10 print — Bitcoin +10.0%: \$10,000 × (+0.10) = **+\$1,000**. S&P +5.54%: \$10,000 × (+0.0554) = **+\$554**. Crypto gained **\$446 more**.
- Round trip across both prints: Bitcoin −\$940 then +\$1,000 = **+\$60**; the S&P −\$432 then +\$554 = **+\$122**. The crypto path is far wilder even when the destination is similar.

The intuition: crypto roughly *doubles* the equity reaction in both directions, so if you are confident in the *direction* of the surprise, crypto gives you the most bang — and the most pain when you are wrong.

## Emerging markets and Vietnam: foreign flows follow the dollar

The sixth and final link reaches the markets that have *nothing directly* to do with US inflation: emerging and frontier markets, and Vietnam in particular.

A US CPI print does not mention the dong, HOSE, or the VN-Index. But it moves the *dollar* and *US yields*, and those are the two variables that govern global capital flows. Here is the chain into Vietnam, link by link:

1. The hot US print lifts US yields and the dollar (links 1 and 2 above).
2. A stronger dollar and higher US yields make US assets *relatively* more attractive and emerging-market assets *relatively* riskier. Global funds **reduce emerging-market and frontier exposure** — they sell EM and frontier stocks and repatriate to dollars.
3. In Vietnam, this is **net foreign selling on HOSE**. Foreign investors are a minority of daily turnover but a powerful *signal* and a real source of supply; their persistent selling weighs on the index.
4. The selling, plus the global dollar pull, puts **pressure on the dong**. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) may respond by tightening policy or selling FX reserves to defend the currency band — which itself tightens domestic liquidity and pressures stocks.
5. The **VN-Index falls — but with a lag** of a few sessions, because the transmission is indirect (it runs through flows and FX, not through a direct re-pricing of US rates) and because the Vietnamese market trades in a different time zone and is dominated by domestic retail investors who react more slowly to a US macro signal.

This is exactly the dynamic that played out across 2022. As the Fed hiked and the dollar surged, the SBV was forced to *raise* its refinancing rate from 4.0% to 6.0% in the autumn (a +200bp defensive move) to protect the dong, the dong weakened toward 23,600+ per dollar, foreign investors sold, and the VN-Index — which had peaked near 1,528 in January 2022 — bottomed around **911 on November 15, 2022**, a roughly 40% drawdown. The US inflation fight, transmitted through the dollar, reached all the way to Ho Chi Minh City. (The mechanics of foreign flows and the index effect in Vietnam are detailed in [Foreign flows, ETFs and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)

It is worth being precise about *why* Vietnam reacts with a lag rather than instantly, because the lag is the tradable feature. Three forces stretch the reaction across days instead of seconds. First, the **time zone**: HOSE trades while New York sleeps, so a US 8:30 a.m. print lands in the middle of the night in Hanoi and cannot be priced until the next Vietnamese session. Second, the **indirectness**: Vietnam is not re-pricing US rates directly — it is re-pricing the *consequences* of a stronger dollar (foreign selling, dong pressure, possible SBV tightening), and those consequences accumulate over sessions as foreign funds actually execute their selling and as the central bank actually responds. Third, the **investor base**: Vietnam's market is dominated by domestic retail investors, who react more slowly and less mechanically to a US macro signal than the algorithms that move the 2-year Treasury. The combination produces a transmission that is real but *delayed and diffuse* — the print's energy reaches Vietnam, but spread over a week rather than concentrated in a single afternoon.

The dong itself is the conduit, and the SBV's choices shape how the pressure lands. Vietnam runs a managed exchange rate, so when the dollar surges the SBV faces a trilemma: let the dong weaken (imported inflation), sell FX reserves to defend it (finite ammunition), or raise rates to make the dong more attractive (which tightens domestic credit and pressures stocks). In 2022 it used a mix of all three, and the rate hikes in particular fed *back* into the equity channel — higher domestic rates raise the discount rate on Vietnamese stocks just as higher US rates raise it on US stocks. So the US inflation print reaches the VN-Index by *two* paths at once: the foreign-flow path (selling) and a domestic-rate path (the SBV's defensive hikes). (For how the SBV's tools work, see [Vietnam monetary policy: the State Bank, the dong, the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).)

The lag is the *tradable* feature here. A US CPI surprise that moves the dollar today is *information* about the pressure on Vietnamese assets *over the next several sessions* — the chain has a built-in delay you can see coming.

![The Vietnam lag chain from a US CPI surprise through the dollar and foreign outflows to a delayed VN-Index drop](/imgs/blogs/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market-7.png)

#### Worked example: a foreign-outflow day on a \$8,000 VN-stock position

Suppose a US CPI print comes in hot, the dollar jumps, and over the next two sessions HOSE sees heavy net foreign selling that drags the VN-Index down 2%. You hold the equivalent of \$8,000 in Vietnamese blue chips.

- Day-of US print: the dong weakens and US yields rise, but the VN-Index barely moves — the lag. Your position: roughly **flat**.
- Next two sessions: foreign selling and dong pressure pull the index −2%. \$8,000 × (−0.02) = **−\$160**.
- If the dong *also* weakened 1% against the dollar over the window and you measure in dollars, add roughly \$8,000 × (−0.01) = **−\$80** of currency drag, for about **−\$240** total in dollar terms.

The intuition: a Vietnamese investor feels the US print *late and indirectly* — through foreign flows and the exchange rate — so the same hot print that hit US stocks instantly arrives in Hanoi as a slow, two-part squeeze (price and currency) over the following sessions.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Theory is cheap; the chain earns its keep when you line up *dated* episodes and watch the signs flip in lockstep. We compare three real CPI prints across the same five assets. This grid is the centerpiece of the post — study it before reading on.

![Heatmap of same-day cross-asset moves across three CPI prints for stocks Nasdaq Bitcoin dollar and gold](/imgs/blogs/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market-4.png)

### Episode 1 — the hot print: September 13, 2022 (Aug CPI 8.3%)

Already dissected above, this is the canonical "good-news-is-bad-news" inflation-regime reaction. Surprise: +0.2pp *hot*. The chain fired in textbook order: 2-year yield +18bp, dollar +1.4%, S&P −4.32%, Nasdaq −5.16%, gold −0.4%, Bitcoin −9.4%. Every risk asset down, the dollar up, the front-end yield up. The *sign pattern* — green dollar, red everything-else — is the fingerprint of a hawkish inflation surprise.

### Episode 2 — the cool print: November 10, 2022 (Oct CPI 7.7%)

Two months later, the mirror image. October CPI printed 7.7% against a 7.9% consensus — a *cool* surprise of −0.2pp. The chain ran in reverse: the 2-year yield *fell* 33bp, the 10-year fell 28bp, the dollar *dropped* 2.1%, and risk assets exploded higher — S&P **+5.54%** (one of its best days of the era), Nasdaq +7.35%, gold +2.8%, Bitcoin +10.0%. Same chain, every sign flipped. This is the cleanest proof that the transmission map is symmetric: a cool print is just a hot print with the arrows reversed.

#### Worked example: the cool-print mirror on a \$10,000 stake

Same \$10,000-per-asset setup as the hot-print example, but on the cool November 10, 2022 print.

- Stocks (S&P +5.54%): \$10,000 × (+0.0554) = **+\$554**.
- Bitcoin (+10.0%): \$10,000 × (+0.10) = **+\$1,000**.
- Gold (+2.8%): \$10,000 × (+0.028) = **+\$280**.
- Long dollar (DXY −2.1%): \$10,000 × (−0.021) = **−\$210** — the *only* loser, because the dollar moves *opposite* to risk on a cool print.

The intuition: flip the surprise and you flip every sign on the map at once — the dollar leg that *made* money on the hot print *loses* it on the cool one, and crypto again leads the pack with a \$1,000 swing.

### Episode 3 — the late-cycle cool print: November 14, 2023 (Oct CPI 3.2%)

A year on, inflation had fallen from 8% toward 3%, and the surprise was milder: October 2023 CPI 3.2% vs. 3.3% expected — cool, but only −0.1pp. The chain still fired, but *softer* and with a revealing twist. The 2-year fell 22bp, the 10-year fell 19bp, the dollar fell 1.5%, the S&P rose a modest +1.91%, the Nasdaq +2.37%, Bitcoin a muted +1.5% — *but* the **Russell 2000** (small-cap, the most rate-sensitive equity index) surged **+5.44%**.

Two lessons hide in that twist. First, beta is *relative to the surprise size*: a −0.1pp surprise produced roughly a third of the move a −0.2pp surprise did — smaller spark, smaller fire. Second, *within* equities, the most rate-sensitive segment (small caps) had the highest beta, mirroring how Nasdaq out-moved the Dow on the hot print. Notice too that Bitcoin's +1.5% *under*-shot the S&P this time — a reminder that crypto's amplifier depends on the *kind* of catalyst (a pure-liquidity, risk-appetite catalyst lights crypto up; a narrow rate-relief catalyst in late 2023, when crypto had its own idiosyncratic drivers, did not).

Reading the three rows of the heatmap top to bottom, you see the chain's discipline: the dollar is always the *opposite* color from stocks, the magnitudes shrink as the surprise shrinks, and crypto is usually — though not always — the most saturated cell in the row.

## Common misconceptions

The transmission chain corrects a handful of beginner mistakes that cost real money.

### Misconception 1: "On a big macro day, everything moves together."

No. The defining feature of the chain is that **signs differ across assets**. On September 13, 2022, the dollar rose 1.4% while the S&P fell 4.32% — *opposite directions on the same print*. If you believed "everything moves together," a long-dollar hedge against your stock book would have looked like a contradiction; in fact it was the *only* green position on the screen. The correct statement is that *risk assets* move together (stocks, crypto, EM all fell), while the *dollar and front-end rates* move opposite to them.

### Misconception 2: "Gold is the obvious inflation trade."

On a hot inflation print, gold fell **−0.4%** — barely a hedge at all. Gold's real-yield and dollar channels (both bearish on a hot print) fight its inflation-hedge channel (bullish), so the net move is small and unreliable. Over *long* horizons gold may hedge inflation; on the *day* of a hot surprise it is one of the *worst* expressions of the view. The clean inflation-surprise trades live in rates and the dollar, not gold.

### Misconception 3: "Crypto is a hedge / it does its own thing."

On every CPI print in our sample, Bitcoin moved the *same direction* as equities — down on the hot print, up on the cool ones — just *more*. Crypto is not a hedge against a macro-tightening shock; it is the *high-beta version* of the risk-on/risk-off trade. Treating it as uncorrelated to macro is how leveraged crypto longs got liquidated in 2022.

### Misconception 4: "Vietnam is insulated from US data."

The VN-Index fell roughly 40% in 2022 *largely because of* the US inflation fight, transmitted through the dollar and foreign flows. Vietnam is not priced off US CPI directly, but it is priced off the dollar and US yields that US CPI moves — so it is *indirectly* exposed, with a lag. "Local market, local drivers" ignores the foreign-flow channel that dominates at turning points.

### Misconception 5: "The first move is the whole move."

The same-day numbers in this post are *knee-jerk* reactions. Many of them faded or reversed over subsequent sessions as positioning, second prints, and Fed commentary re-shaped the path. The chain tells you the *direction and relative size* of the *initial* reaction; it does not promise the move persists. Reaction first, then fade or trend — always.

### Misconception 6: "If I get the number right, I get the trade right."

You can forecast the print perfectly and still lose, for two reasons. First, what matters is the surprise versus *consensus*, not the absolute number — a "high" inflation print that is *lower* than the feared whisper number can rally risk. Second, the *reaction* depends on the regime and on positioning, which can flip the sign you expected. In late 2023, several hot-ish prints produced muted or even positive equity reactions because the market had already swung to "the Fed is done, disinflation is winning." Getting the economics right is necessary but not sufficient; you also have to get the *consensus* and the *regime* right. The number is the input; the reaction function is the trade.

## The playbook: trading the transmission chain

Here is how a practitioner turns the map into positions. The organizing principle: **you have one view on the surprise; the chain tells you which asset best expresses it.**

### Step 1 — form a view on the surprise, not the number

You are never betting on "inflation." You are betting on *actual vs. consensus*. Know the consensus cold, and form a probability over hotter / in-line / cooler. If you have no edge on the surprise, you have no event trade — sit out. The edge is in the *gap*, and the gap is what the market has *not* priced.

### Step 2 — pick the asset by beta and clarity of channel

Once you have a directional view on the surprise, choose where to express it:

- **Cleanest, most direct:** the front-end rates trade (short the 2-year if you expect a hot print). It is the source of the chain, so it has the least cross-current noise — but it requires futures and DV01 sizing.
- **Cleanest FX expression:** long the dollar (DXY) for a hot print, short for a cool one. The dollar's channel (rate differentials) is unambiguous and the move is modest, so the *risk* is contained.
- **Highest reward (and risk):** crypto, the end-of-chain amplifier. If you are confident in the *direction*, Bitcoin roughly doubles the equity reaction. If you are unsure, its leverage and liquidation cascades will punish you.
- **Avoid for inflation views:** gold. Its channels fight, so the move is small and ambiguous — a muddy way to express a clean view.
- **Lagged / indirect:** EM and the VN-Index. A hot US print that moves the dollar is a *several-session* signal for Vietnamese assets — useful for positioning, not for a same-day scalp.

#### Worked example: expressing a dovish (cool-CPI) view in BTC vs. the S&P

You expect a *cool* print and want to be long risk. You can deploy \$5,000 in each of two expressions and compare, using the November 2022 cool-print betas.

- S&P 500 expression (+5.54%): \$5,000 × (+0.0554) = **+\$277**.
- Bitcoin expression (+10.0%): \$5,000 × (+0.10) = **+\$500**.
- Same \$5,000 risked, same correct view: the crypto expression returned **\$223 more** because of its higher beta — but a *wrong* view (a hot print) would have cost roughly twice as much in crypto too.

The intuition: pick the asset whose beta matches your *conviction* — high beta (crypto) when you are sure, low beta (the dollar) when you want the cleanest, most contained expression of the same directional call.

### Step 3 — exploit the lead/lag

The chain is *ordered*, which creates tradable lead/lag relationships:

- **Rates lead everything.** Watch the 2-year tick on the print; it tells you the sign before the equity cash market even opens.
- **The dollar leads EM.** A sharp DXY move today is a *forward indicator* of foreign-flow pressure on Vietnam and other EM/frontier markets over the next few sessions. You can position in VN exposure *ahead* of the lagged reaction.
- **Don't chase the knee-jerk in the loudest asset.** Crypto's first spike is often an over-shoot driven by liquidations; the *fade* is frequently a better entry than the spike.

### Step 4 — size for the regime and define invalidation

The *same* chain has *opposite* signs in different regimes. In an inflation-fighting regime (2022), hot = risk-off. In a soft-landing or growth-scare regime, a hot print can be *neutral or risk-on*. Before the print, write down: "If hot, I expect X because the regime is Y." Your **invalidation** is a move that contradicts the chain — e.g., a hot print where stocks *rise* and the dollar *falls* tells you the regime has shifted and your map is stale; cut the trade. (For the broader regime-rotation framework that decides which sign is in force, see [Risk-on, risk-off: the cross-asset rotation](/blog/trading/cross-asset/risk-on-risk-off-the-cross-asset-rotation).)

Finally, size *down* into the print and *up* after. Pre-print, the surprise is unknown and the move can be violent in either direction — small size or defined-risk options. Post-print, once the chain has revealed the sign, you can add to the move with more confidence (and a real stop at the invalidation level).

### A pre-print checklist you can run in two minutes

The chain becomes a routine when you reduce it to a short checklist run *before* every major release:

1. **What is the consensus, and how wide is the forecast range?** A tight range means the market is confident and a surprise will hit harder; a wide range means a big number may already be partly priced.
2. **What regime am I in?** Write one sentence: "Hot is bad for stocks because the fear is over-tightening," or "Hot is fine because the fear is recession." This single sentence fixes the *sign* of the whole map.
3. **Which link is cleanest for my view?** Rates for the purest expression, the dollar for a contained one, crypto for maximum beta, and *not* gold for an inflation view.
4. **Where is the lag trade?** If I expect a dollar move, what does that imply for EM and the VN-Index over the next few sessions, and can I position ahead of it?
5. **What is my invalidation?** A specific cross-asset pattern that says my map is wrong — e.g., a hot print where the dollar *falls* and stocks *rise* means the regime flipped; I am out.

Run those five questions and you have converted a wall of tickers into a single, ordered, sign-aware plan. The trader who skips the checklist sees chaos; the trader who runs it sees a chain.

#### Worked example: a defined-risk options expression into the print

Rather than hold spot through a print, a trader buys a defined-risk options position to cap the downside of being wrong on the surprise. Suppose she is mildly bearish equities into a CPI release and buys S&P put exposure for a premium of \$300 on a notional she has sized to her account.

- If the print is hot and the S&P falls 4% (a 2022-style reaction), the puts might be worth, say, \$1,500 — a gain of \$1,500 − \$300 = **+\$1,200**.
- If the print is cool and the S&P *rallies*, the puts expire worthless and the loss is capped at the **−\$300** premium, no matter how far the rally runs.
- The asymmetry — risk \$300 to make \$1,200 — is the point: into an event whose *direction* you cannot be sure of, defined-risk caps the cost of being wrong while keeping the upside of being right.

The intuition: when the surprise itself is a coin-flip, you do not size up on spot — you pay a known premium to convert an unknown, two-sided event move into a one-sided, capped-loss bet.
