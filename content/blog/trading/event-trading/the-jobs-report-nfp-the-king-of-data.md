It is the first Friday of the month. At 8:29:59 ET the screens are still. The S&P 500 future sits flat, the 2-year Treasury yield has not moved in twenty minutes, the dollar is asleep. Then the clock turns over to 8:30:00, the Bureau of Labor Statistics releases the Employment Situation report, and a single number â€” **+517,000 jobs** when the market expected about 187,000 â€” detonates across every asset on the board at once. Within the first second the 2-year yield is up double digits in basis points, the dollar is bid, stock futures are sliding, and gold is selling off. No human read the report and decided to sell. The number hit the wire, algorithms parsed the headline against the consensus, and the entire price of money in the United States re-rated before a person could finish reading the first sentence.

That was the morning of February 3, 2023. It is one of the cleanest examples in modern markets of what this whole series is about: markets do not trade the number, they trade the **surprise** â€” the gap between what actually printed and what was already in the price. And of all the scheduled numbers that hit that wire every month, the jobs report is the one that most reliably moves everything at once. Inflation (CPI) is the king of data, but employment is the crown prince, and on many months it is the one that matters more, because the labor market sits at the center of both things the Federal Reserve is legally required to care about: stable prices and maximum employment.

Why does a single jobs number reprice the *entire* rate curve in seconds, rather than just nudging a stock or two? Because the price of every financial asset on earth is, at bottom, a discounted stream of future cash flows â€” and the discount rate is set by the path of Fed policy. When a payroll print changes the market's best guess of where the Fed funds rate will be in six months, it changes the discount rate applied to every bond, every stock, every currency cross at once. The 2-year Treasury yield is the market's purest, most liquid bet on that policy path, which is why it moves first and hardest. The dollar follows because higher US rates pull global capital toward dollar assets. Stocks follow because a higher discount rate shrinks the present value of future earnings â€” and because, late in a cycle, the same strong economy that lifts rates also raises the odds of a recession down the road. One number, one re-rated discount rate, every asset repriced. That is the mechanism this entire post is built to help you read.

This post is the **structural primer**. By the end you will be able to open the report and read it like a trader â€” not just the headline payroll figure that the news leads with, but the two surveys it is built from, the wage numbers buried inside, the unemployment rate that comes from a completely different count, the revisions that quietly rewrite last month, and the statistical machinery (seasonal adjustment, the birth/death model) that explains why the number is noisier than it looks. *How to trade it* â€” sizing, the spike-fade-trend, the options around it â€” is a sibling post; here we build the anatomy so that when you read that one, every term already means something.

## Foundations: what the jobs report actually measures

Let us define everything from zero, because the single biggest mistake beginners make is treating "the jobs report" as one number. It is not. It is a package of distinct measurements, and a trader's edge comes from knowing which one moved and why.

**Who releases it, and when.** The report is the *Employment Situation*, published by the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), a part of the US Department of Labor. It comes out at **8:30 a.m. Eastern Time, usually the first Friday of the month**, and it covers the *previous* month. The January report lands in early February. The release time is identical to CPI, PPI, retail sales and GDP â€” 8:30 ET is the standard slot for top-tier US data, an hour before the stock market opens, while the bond and futures markets are already trading. That timing is why the first move always shows up in rates and futures, not in cash equities.

**Nonfarm payrolls (NFP) â€” the headline.** The number the world calls "the jobs report" is *nonfarm payrolls*: the net change in the number of paid jobs in the US economy in the prior month, **excluding farm workers, private household employees, the self-employed who run unincorporated businesses, and the military.** When a headline says "the economy added 200,000 jobs," that 200,000 is the change in nonfarm payrolls from one month to the next. "Nonfarm" sounds quaint, but it exists because farm employment is wildly seasonal and hard to measure, so it was carved out decades ago to leave a cleaner signal. The figure is reported in *thousands* â€” a print of "+200k" means 200,000 net new jobs.

How big is a "good" number? It depends entirely on the moment in the cycle, and this is where beginners need a reference point. In the post-pandemic snapback of 2021, the economy was adding an *average* of about 606,000 jobs a month as it clawed back the millions lost in 2020. That pace was never sustainable â€” it was a rehiring boom, not a steady state. By 2022 the average had fallen to about 377,000, by 2023 to 251,000, by 2024 to 186,000, and into 2025 toward roughly 110,000 a month. The chart below makes the trajectory unmistakable: the labor market has been cooling, year after year, even though every one of those figures is still a *positive* jobs gain. This matters for reading any single print, because the market judges a number against the recent run-rate and the consensus, not against zero. In 2021, a +250k month would have been a shockingly weak miss; in 2025 the same +250k would be a blowout. The "neutral" payroll number â€” the pace that roughly matches population and labor-force growth, neither tightening nor loosening the labor market â€” drifts over time and is currently estimated somewhere around **70k to 100k a month**, far below the 200k+ that felt normal a few years ago.

![Average monthly US payroll gain by year from 2021 to 2025 showing a cooling labor market](/imgs/blogs/the-jobs-report-nfp-the-king-of-data-2.png)

**The establishment survey vs the household survey.** Here is the fact that unlocks the whole report. The payroll number and the unemployment rate come from **two different surveys, of two different populations, counting two different things.**

- The **establishment survey** (officially the Current Employment Statistics, or CES) asks **businesses and government agencies** â€” roughly 119,000 worksites covering about a third of all nonfarm jobs â€” "how many people were on your payroll in the pay period that includes the 12th of the month?" This survey produces *payrolls* and *average hourly earnings*. It counts **jobs**, so a person holding two jobs is counted twice.
- The **household survey** (the Current Population Survey, or CPS) calls about **60,000 households** and asks, for each adult, "did you work, were you looking for work, or were you out of the labor force?" This survey produces the **unemployment rate**, the **participation rate**, and the broader U-6 measure. It counts **people**, so a person with two jobs is one employed person.

That single design difference â€” one survey counts jobs at firms, the other counts people at home â€” is the reason the two halves of the report can flatly contradict each other in any given month, and why a sophisticated reader never quotes one without glancing at the other.

**The unemployment rate (U-3).** This is the share of the **labor force** that is unemployed. The labor force is everyone who is either working or actively looking for work. So the rate is: (people unemployed and looking) Ã· (people working + people unemployed and looking). The crucial subtlety â€” which we will return to â€” is that someone who gives up looking for a job **drops out of the labor force entirely** and stops being counted as unemployed. The headline rate that gets quoted is the **U-3** measure; it is the official, narrow definition.

**U-3 vs U-6.** Because U-3 ignores discouraged workers and the underemployed, the BLS also publishes broader measures, the broadest of which is **U-6**. U-6 adds back people who want a job but have stopped looking ("marginally attached"), plus people working part-time who would prefer full-time work ("part-time for economic reasons"). U-6 is always meaningfully higher than U-3 â€” often by several percentage points â€” and the **gap between them** tells you about labor-market slack that the headline hides. When U-6 widens relative to U-3, the job market is softening beneath a calm-looking headline.

**Average hourly earnings (AHE).** This is the establishment survey's wage number: the average pay per hour across private-sector workers, reported as a month-over-month percent change and a year-over-year percent change. It is the report's **inflation signal**, and in a world where the Fed is fighting inflation, AHE can matter more than the payroll headline itself. The logic: wages are both a cost to businesses (which can get passed into prices) and income to households (which can fund more spending), so fast wage growth is a sign that inflation has a self-sustaining engine.

**The participation rate.** This is the share of the working-age population that is *in* the labor force â€” working or looking. It measures **labor supply**: how many people are even available to be hired. A falling unemployment rate looks great until you notice it fell because people *left* the labor force rather than because they *found jobs* â€” and the participation rate is how you catch that.

**Revisions.** Every release revises the **prior two months'** payroll figures as more survey responses arrive, and once a year the whole series is **benchmarked** to near-complete tax records. The first print you trade is an estimate built from an incomplete sample. We will spend a whole section on this because it is the part beginners ignore and pros watch hardest.

**The birth/death model.** The establishment survey samples existing firms; it cannot survey businesses that were *born* (newly opened) or *died* (closed) since the sample was last refreshed. To estimate the jobs created and destroyed by those uncounted new and dying firms, the BLS adds a statistical adjustment called the **birth/death model**. At economic turning points it can be wrong in a big way, because it assumes business formation behaves roughly as it did in the recent past.

**Seasonal adjustment.** Hiring is intensely seasonal â€” retailers staff up before December, schools hire in autumn, construction slows in winter. To make month-to-month numbers comparable, the BLS *seasonally adjusts* the data, stripping out the predictable calendar pattern so that what is left is the genuine underlying change. The headline payroll number is always the seasonally adjusted figure. The adjustment is essential, but it is itself an estimate, and unusual years (a pandemic, a strange winter) can distort it.

Hold those definitions. The rest of the post takes them apart one at a time and shows you what each does to the tape.

## 1. The two surveys, and why they disagree

Start with the split, because it is the foundation everything else sits on. The establishment survey and the household survey are not two views of the same data; they are two different measurements that happen to be released on the same morning.

![Two-column comparison of the establishment payroll survey versus the household survey](/imgs/blogs/the-jobs-report-nfp-the-king-of-data-3.png)

The **establishment survey** is large and statistically tight. It samples around 119,000 worksites â€” businesses and government agencies â€” and asks how many people were on the payroll. Because the sample is big and drawn from firms (which keep precise payroll records), the resulting payroll number is relatively low-noise month to month. The 90% confidence interval on the monthly payroll change is roughly **Â±130,000 jobs**. Sit with that: when the headline reports "+150,000," the honest statistical statement is "somewhere between about +20,000 and +280,000, with 90% confidence." A print that "beats" by 30k is statistically a tie. Traders react to it anyway â€” because the *market's expectation* moved â€” but you should never mistake a small beat for a real signal about the economy.

The **household survey** is smaller â€” about 60,000 households â€” and therefore noisier. With a sample that size, the month-to-month change in the number of employed people has a confidence band of several hundred thousand. The household survey's value is not its monthly precision; it is that it asks about **people** and so can measure things the payroll survey cannot: who is unemployed, who has stopped looking, who is working part-time involuntarily. The unemployment rate, despite being noisier in any single month, is the report's most economically meaningful number over time, because it maps directly onto the Fed's "maximum employment" mandate.

Now the disagreements. They happen for structural reasons:

1. **Jobs vs people.** A gig worker who drives for a ride-share company in the morning and waits tables at night holds two jobs. The establishment survey, counting jobs, registers both. The household survey, counting people, registers one employed person. In an economy where multiple-job-holding rises, payrolls can grow faster than household employment for no sinister reason.
2. **Coverage.** The establishment survey misses the self-employed, unincorporated business owners, farm workers and unpaid family workers. The household survey includes them. So in a period when self-employment booms (say, a wave of new sole proprietorships), household employment can rise while payrolls look flat.
3. **Sample size and noise.** The smaller household sample swings more month to month. A scary one-month jump in the household-survey unemployment count is often just noise; the trend over three to six months is the signal.

#### Worked example: when the two surveys disagree

Say the establishment survey reports **+180,000 payroll jobs**, a solid number, but the household survey shows employment *falling* by 50,000 and the unemployment rate ticking up from 4.0% to 4.1%. A naive reader sees "+180k jobs, good" and stops. A trader reads the conflict: payrolls are still growing, but the household side hints the labor market is softening at the margin. Suppose you run a \$50,000 portfolio that is half in rate-sensitive growth stocks. If the bond market decides the household-survey weakness is the real signal â€” that the Fed will cut sooner â€” 2-year yields might fall 8bp and your rate-sensitive sleeve (\$25,000) could rally about 1.0% on the day, a gain of roughly \$250, even though the "headline" was strong. **Intuition:** the surveys disagree on purpose; the trade lives in which one the market chooses to believe today.

This is why the professional habit is to read both. In most months they agree and the distinction is academic. But at turning points â€” exactly when it matters most â€” they diverge, and the divergence *is* the information. The household survey often shows softening before the payroll headline rolls over, because firms cut hours and stop hiring before they fire, and people feel the labor market loosen before it shows up in net payrolls.

### The statistical machinery behind the number

Two pieces of machinery sit between the raw surveys and the headline you trade, and both can distort the print in ways that matter at exactly the wrong moments. A reader who does not know they exist will be repeatedly fooled.

**Seasonal adjustment.** Hiring follows a powerful calendar rhythm. Retailers add hundreds of thousands of workers before the December holidays and shed them in January. School systems hire in late summer and pause over the breaks. Construction hires in spring and slows in deep winter. Tax-preparation firms staff up in February and March. If you reported the *raw* (not seasonally adjusted) numbers, January would always look catastrophic â€” the economy "loses" over two million jobs every January as holiday-temporary workers roll off â€” and nobody could tell a genuine slowdown from the normal post-holiday unwind. So the BLS applies *seasonal adjustment*: a statistical model that learns each month's typical calendar pattern from years of history and strips it out, leaving the underlying change. The headline payroll number, the unemployment rate, AHE â€” all the figures you trade are seasonally adjusted.

The catch is that seasonal adjustment is itself an estimate, and it assumes the calendar pattern is stable. When something breaks the pattern â€” a pandemic that scrambles the entire seasonal profile, an unusually warm or cold winter that shifts construction hiring, a holiday calendar quirk â€” the adjustment can over- or under-correct, injecting "phantom" jobs into one month and pulling them from another. This is why analysts sometimes glance at the *non-seasonally-adjusted* series or at year-over-year changes (which net out the seasonal effect a different way) when they suspect the adjustment is misbehaving. For the years right after 2020, the seasonal factors were visibly distorted because the model was still digesting the pandemic's bizarre pattern, and several "strong" or "weak" prints in that era were partly seasonal-adjustment artifacts rather than real economic signals.

**The birth/death model.** The establishment survey can only sample firms it knows about. It has no way to directly survey a business that opened last month (it was not in the sample frame yet) or one that closed (it stopped responding â€” but did it die, or just fail to respond?). New businesses create jobs that the sample misses; dying businesses destroy jobs the sample cannot see. To plug that gap, the BLS adds a modeled estimate called the *net birth/death adjustment*: a statistical guess at the net jobs created by business formation minus business closure, based on historical patterns.

In normal times the birth/death adjustment is a quiet, sensible correction. At economic turning points it can be badly wrong, and in a predictable direction. The model assumes business formation behaves roughly as it did in the recent past. When the economy turns *down*, more firms are failing and fewer are being born than the model assumes â€” so it *adds back* jobs that the real economy was not creating, making the late-cycle prints look stronger than the truth. Those phantom jobs then get stripped out in the annual benchmark revision. The August 2024 benchmark that erased ~818,000 jobs was, in large part, the birth/death model being unwound: it had been crediting the economy with new-business jobs that the tax records showed never materialized. The lesson for a trader is structural: be most skeptical of strong payroll prints *late in a cycle*, because that is exactly when the birth/death model flatters the number and the eventual revisions tend to be negative.

#### Worked example: the birth/death adjustment flattering a print

Suppose a headline reads **+150,000** jobs, and a footnote shows the net birth/death adjustment contributed +130,000 of that. In other words, almost the entire "gain" is *modeled*, not surveyed â€” the actual sampled firms barely grew. A trader who reads only the headline books a solid number; a trader who reads the birth/death line sees a report that is far weaker beneath the headline and likely to be revised down. If you are short \$100,000 of the 2-year Treasury expecting yields to rise on a "strong" number, the +150k headline might briefly push yields up 6bp in your favor (DV01 on a 2-year â‰ˆ \$190/bp, so +6bp â‰ˆ **+\$1,140**), but as the desk reads the birth/death detail and fades the move, yields round-trip and you give the **\$1,140** back by the close. **Intuition:** the headline can be mostly model; the surveyed jobs are the real signal, and the birth/death line tells you how much of the print to trust.

## 2. The headline number and revisions: why last month gets rewritten

The headline payroll figure is the number the algorithms parse first and the news leads with. It is also, paradoxically, the **least reliable** part of the report. Understanding why is the single biggest upgrade a beginner can make to their reading of jobs day.

![The same payroll month printed three times: advance, revised, and benchmark](/imgs/blogs/the-jobs-report-nfp-the-king-of-data-5.png)

Each month's payroll figure is published **three times**, and it usually changes between them:

1. **The advance (first) print.** Released about five days after the month ends, built from roughly 70% of the survey sample that has responded by the deadline. This is the headline you trade on jobs Friday. It is an estimate from an incomplete sample.
2. **Two monthly revisions.** The next two Employment Situation reports each refile that month's number as more responses arrive. So *every* jobs report you read contains, buried below the headline, a revision to the prior two months. The headline grabs attention; the revision quietly rewrites history.
3. **The annual benchmark.** Once a year, the BLS realigns the entire payroll series to the **Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW)** â€” counts derived from actual unemployment-insurance tax records covering nearly every job. This is as close to ground truth as the data gets, and it can move the level by hundreds of thousands of jobs across a year.

The revisions are not small. A first print can be revised by 50,000 to 80,000 jobs in either direction over the following two months â€” comparable to the size of the "surprise" that moved markets in the first place. And the benchmark can be enormous: in August 2024, the BLS preliminary annual benchmark revised away roughly **818,000 jobs** from the twelve months through March 2024, meaning the economy had been adding meaningfully fewer jobs all along than the monthly headlines had suggested. Traders who had read every strong headline at face value had been reading a labor market that, on the revised data, was already cooling.

There is a systematic reason revisions tend to lean *negative* near a slowdown and *positive* near a recovery. The birth/death model assumes business formation behaves as it recently has. When the economy turns down, more firms are dying and fewer being born than the model assumes, so the model adds back jobs that did not exist â€” and later revisions strip them out. Near a recovery, the opposite: the model under-counts the burst of new business formation, and revisions add jobs back. This is why a savvy reader treats strong late-cycle prints with suspicion and watches whether the **revision trend** is consistently negative.

#### Worked example: a headline that gets revised away

Suppose this month's report shows **+250,000** jobs â€” a clear beat â€” and on the day your rate-sensitive book rallies. But buried in the same release, last month's print is revised **down by 80,000** (from +200k to +120k) and the month before that down another 30,000. Net two-month revision: **âˆ’110,000**. The "real" recent run-rate is far softer than the headline implied. Now run the money on a \$30,000 rate-sensitive book. The knee-jerk on the +250k headline might lift it 1.2% (+\$360) in the first minutes. But as the desk digests the âˆ’110k revision, the bond market unwinds the move; by the close your book gives back most of it and finishes up only 0.3%, or about +\$90 â€” a swing of roughly \$270 from high to settle on the same report. **Intuition:** the headline set the knee-jerk; the revision set the fade. Read the revision before you chase the print.

This is why the professional checklist puts "the two-month revision" near the top, right under the headline-versus-consensus comparison. A +250k headline with a +50k net upward revision is a genuinely strong report. The same +250k headline with a âˆ’110k downward revision is a *weak* report wearing a strong headline. The market often takes a few minutes to do that arithmetic â€” which is precisely the window where the spike fades or extends.

## 3. Wages (average hourly earnings): why the Fed watches them

For most of the 2010s, when inflation ran below the Fed's 2% target, the payroll headline was the star and wages were a footnote. From 2021 onward, with inflation the dominant macro problem, **average hourly earnings became one of the most market-moving lines in the entire report** â€” sometimes more than payrolls. To read jobs day in an inflation regime, you have to understand why.

The Fed's logic runs through what economists call the **wage-price spiral**. When wages rise faster than productivity, businesses face higher labor costs. They have two choices: absorb the cost (squeezing profit margins) or pass it into prices (fueling inflation). Higher prices then push workers to demand higher wages to keep up â€” and the loop feeds itself. Services inflation in particular is mostly labor cost, because a haircut, a restaurant meal, or a nursing-home bed is mostly somebody's wages. So when the Fed says it is watching the labor market for inflation, it largely means it is watching **wage growth**, and AHE is the monthly read on it.

This is the deeper reason the jobs report sits where it does in the hierarchy of data â€” second only to CPI, and on many months ahead of it. The Federal Reserve operates under a **dual mandate**, written into law: maximum employment *and* stable prices. CPI speaks directly to half of that mandate (prices); the jobs report speaks to the *other* half (employment) and, through wages, *also* to the price half. No other single release touches both legs of the Fed's job at once. That is why a jobs report can move markets even on months when inflation is the headline obsession: a hot wage number is an inflation signal, a rising unemployment rate is a recession signal, and the payroll trend is a growth signal â€” three of the Fed's most important inputs, delivered in one 8:30 release. When traders call CPI the king and NFP the crown prince, the point is not that employment is less important; it is that on any given month, *whichever of the two mandate halves the market is most anxious about* determines which release dominates. In an inflation panic, CPI rules; in a growth scare, NFP rules. The two trade places as the regime shifts.

It helps to know that AHE is not the only wage gauge, and the others exist precisely because AHE has flaws. The **Employment Cost Index (ECI)**, released quarterly, holds the mix of jobs constant so it is not distorted by composition shifts, and the Fed arguably trusts it more for the trend. The **Atlanta Fed Wage Growth Tracker** follows the same individuals over time, capturing the raises that job-switchers get (which AHE can miss). But none of those is released monthly with the jobs report, and none moves the tape in real time. On the first Friday, AHE is the wage number the algorithms parse and the desks trade, flaws and all â€” which is exactly why a careful reader keeps the quarterly measures in the back of their mind to sanity-check a surprising monthly AHE print.

AHE is reported two ways, and both matter:

- **Month-over-month (MoM):** the change from last month, often around +0.2% to +0.4% in a normal economy. Annualize a +0.4% monthly print (roughly Ã—12) and you get nearly +5% â€” too hot for a 2% inflation target. A +0.5% MoM print is a genuine wage-inflation scare.
- **Year-over-year (YoY):** the change versus twelve months ago, which smooths out the monthly noise. The Fed's rough comfort zone for YoY wage growth, consistent with 2% inflation, is around **3.0% to 3.5%** (2% inflation plus ~1â€“1.5% productivity). Wage growth running at 5% YoY is a flashing red light; at 3% it is consistent with target.

The trading consequence: in an inflation regime, a hot AHE print can flip the market's read of an otherwise-soft report. A weak payroll number that you would expect to rally bonds can instead see yields *rise* if AHE came in hot, because the Fed cares more about the inflation signal than the growth signal when inflation is the enemy. This is the reaction function at work â€” covered in depth in [the reaction function: why the same number moves differently](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) â€” and it is why you cannot read NFP without reading AHE in the same glance.

#### Worked example: the wage-inflation scare

Say a report shows payrolls roughly in line (+160k vs +170k expected â€” a tiny miss) but **AHE at +0.5% MoM**, well above the +0.3% expected. In a normal regime the small payroll miss might lift stocks slightly. In an inflation regime, the +0.5% wage print dominates: the market reprices the odds that the Fed stays higher for longer, 2-year yields jump, and the S&P sells off 1.5% on the day. On a \$20,000 long stock position, a âˆ’1.5% day is **âˆ’\$300**. The same report, in a year where inflation was already dead, might have produced a *flat-to-up* tape because the market would have ignored the wage line and focused on the soft payrolls. **Intuition:** the wage number is the dial that decides whether soft jobs are read as "Fed can ease" (good) or "inflation is sticky" (bad). One AHE decimal can flip the sign.

There is a measurement wrinkle worth knowing: AHE can be distorted by **composition effects**. In early 2020, average hourly earnings *spiked* â€” not because anyone got a raise, but because the pandemic laid off millions of low-wage service workers, mechanically raising the *average* of who was left. When low-wage workers returned, AHE "fell" for the same compositional reason. So in turbulent periods, read AHE alongside the Employment Cost Index (a quarterly, composition-controlled wage measure) rather than trusting the monthly print alone. But on a normal jobs Friday, AHE is the wage signal the market trades.

## 4. The unemployment rate, participation, and the Sahm rule

Now to the household survey's marquee output. The unemployment rate is the number politicians quote and the number the Fed's "maximum employment" mandate points at â€” but it is also the number most likely to mislead a beginner, because of *why* it moves.

![US unemployment rate from 2020 to 2025 with the 3.4% low and the 4.3% Sahm trigger marked](/imgs/blogs/the-jobs-report-nfp-the-king-of-data-4.png)

Recall the definition: unemployment rate = (unemployed and actively looking) Ã· (labor force), where the labor force = employed + (unemployed and looking). The trap is the denominator. The rate can fall for a **good** reason (people found jobs) or a **bad** reason (people gave up looking and left the labor force, so they stop counting as unemployed). It can rise for a **bad** reason (people lost jobs) or a **benign** reason (people who had given up start looking again, re-entering the labor force faster than they find work). You cannot read the rate's direction without reading the **participation rate** alongside it.

Chart the rate over 2020â€“2025 and the story is vivid. It spiked to **14.8% in April 2020** as the pandemic shut the economy, fell through the recovery to a 53-year low of **3.4% in January 2023** (the tightest labor market in living memory), and then drifted up through 2024. The August 2024 report (covering July) put it at **4.3%** â€” still low by historical standards, but the *rate of change* set off an alarm that has its own name.

**The Sahm rule.** Named after economist Claudia Sahm, it is an empirical recession indicator: historically, when the **three-month average of the unemployment rate rises 0.5 percentage points or more above its low of the prior twelve months**, a recession has either begun or is imminent. Every US recession since the 1970s has tripped it, with no false positives in that window. In August 2024, the move from the ~3.5% low to a 4.3% three-month average crossed that 0.5pp threshold â€” and the market noticed instantly. The rule's power is that it captures *acceleration*: it does not care that 4.3% is low in absolute terms; it cares that unemployment rose half a point off its bottom, because that pace of deterioration historically feeds on itself (laid-off workers spend less, which causes more layoffs).

This is the regime flip that makes the jobs report so treacherous to trade. For most of 2022â€“2023, a *rising* unemployment rate was good news for stocks â€” it meant the overheated labor market was cooling, inflation pressure was easing, and the Fed could stop hiking. But once the Sahm rule trips, the market's reaction function flips: a rising unemployment rate stops meaning "inflation relief" and starts meaning "recession," and now both stocks *and* yields fall together (yields fall because the market prices Fed cuts; stocks fall because earnings are at risk). We will see exactly this in the August 2024 episode below.

#### Worked example: the Sahm rule and a recession hedge

Suppose you hold a \$10,000 recession-hedge position in long-dated Treasuries (say, a long-bond ETF) precisely because you expect the labor market to crack. The unemployment rate, having bottomed near 3.4%, prints its third consecutive rise and the three-month average crosses 0.5pp above the low â€” the Sahm rule trips. The market reprices toward faster Fed cuts; long yields fall ~15bp on the day, and a long-duration bond position can rise roughly 2% on a move like that. On \$10,000 that is about **+\$200** in a single session, and the position keeps working if the slowdown narrative builds. Meanwhile a friend's \$10,000 of cyclical stocks falls 1.8% (âˆ’\$180) on the same recession scare. **Intuition:** once the unemployment trend trips the Sahm rule, the bond hedge and the stock long move in *opposite* directions on the same report â€” that divergence is the whole reason to hold the hedge.

Participation deserves a last word. If unemployment falls but participation falls with it, the "improvement" is people leaving the workforce â€” not a strong labor market. If unemployment rises *and* participation rises, the labor market may actually be healthy (more people are being drawn in to look for work). The cleanest bullish labor signal is rising participation *and* falling unemployment: more people looking, and more of them finding work. Read the two together, always.

The U-6 measure adds the last layer of texture. Recall that U-6 includes the people who want a job but have stopped looking, plus those working part-time who would prefer full-time. When a recession begins, U-6 typically rises *before* U-3, because the first thing employers do is cut hours and stop hiring â€” moving full-time workers to part-time and pushing discouraged would-be workers out of the official count â€” long before the layoffs that push up the narrow rate. So a widening U-6-minus-U-3 gap is an early-warning gauge of slack that the headline rate masks. In the strong 2022â€“2023 labor market the gap was historically narrow; watching it start to widen is one of the first quiet signs that the cracks are forming.

It is also worth knowing that the jobs report does not live in isolation; it is the marquee member of a family of labor data that traders cross-check. The **JOLTS** report (Job Openings and Labor Turnover Survey) measures job *openings* and the *quits rate* â€” and openings are a leading indicator, because firms stop advertising jobs before they start cutting them. JOLTS openings peaked around 12 million in early 2022, an unprecedented level signaling a desperately tight market, and their steady decline since was an early sign of cooling that preceded the slowdown in payrolls. **Weekly initial jobless claims**, released every Thursday, are the highest-frequency labor read available; a healthy economy runs around 200,000â€“220,000 a week, and a sustained move toward 300,000+ is a recession-warning sign that arrives weeks before it shows up in the monthly unemployment rate. A trader reading the jobs report holds all three in view: claims for the real-time pulse, JOLTS for the leading signal, and the Employment Situation for the comprehensive monthly verdict. When all three point the same way, the signal is strong; when they conflict, the conflict itself is the information.

## 5. Hot vs cool: how the market judges a jobs surprise

We now have all the parts. The question a trader answers in the first seconds is binary: was this report **hot** (strong labor market) or **cool** (weak labor market) relative to what was priced â€” and which way does that push the Fed? But the *sign of the trade* depends on the regime, and that is the part beginners get backwards.

![Decision-flow checklist combining headline, revisions, wages, unemployment and participation into a hot-or-cool verdict](/imgs/blogs/the-jobs-report-nfp-the-king-of-data-7.png)

Here is the core logic, in two regimes:

**Inflation regime (good-news-is-bad-news).** When the Fed is hiking to fight inflation, a strong labor market is *bad* for risk assets, because it means the Fed must stay tight. So:
- **HOT report** (big payroll beat, low/falling unemployment, hot wages): yields **up**, dollar **up**, stocks **down**, gold often down. The market prices fewer cuts / more hikes.
- **COOL report** (payroll miss, rising unemployment, soft wages): yields **down**, stocks **up**, dollar down. The market prices the Fed easing â€” and as long as the cooling is *gentle*, stocks celebrate the relief.

**Growth-scare regime (bad-news-is-bad-news).** Once cooling tips into fear of recession â€” the Sahm rule trips, claims spike, the report is *alarmingly* weak â€” the sign flips:
- **ALARMINGLY WEAK report**: yields **down** (Fed will cut hard) but stocks **down too** (earnings and jobs are at risk). The "Fed will ease" good news is overwhelmed by "the economy is breaking" bad news.

So there are not two outcomes but three, and the third â€” the recession scare â€” is the one that catches people who learned the simple "weak jobs = stocks up" rule in the 2022â€“2023 regime. The variable that decides which regime you are in is *what the market is most afraid of right now*: too much inflation, or too little growth. That fear is the reaction function, and it is the single most important thing to identify before the print.

How big is the move, and where does it land first? On a top-tier surprise, the first-second reaction is concentrated in **rates and FX**, because those markets trade around the clock and are wide awake at 8:30 ET while the US stock market is still 60 minutes from its open. The 2-year Treasury yield is the purest expression of "what will the Fed do," so it moves first and cleanest; the dollar moves with it (higher US yields pull capital toward dollar assets); stock-index *futures* react in the same instant; and cash equities only re-rate at the 9:30 open, by which time the rates and FX markets have already digested the number. A genuine blowout or shock can move the 2-year yield 15â€“25bp, the dollar index over 1%, and the S&P 1â€“2% on the day. A surprise within the statistical noise band moves little.

The shape of the move over the first hour is its own tell, and it follows a recognizable arc: the **spike**, the **fade or trend**. In the first milliseconds, algorithms that parse the headline against the consensus fire the knee-jerk â€” pure surprise, headline versus expectation, no nuance. In the next several minutes, human traders and slower models read the *internals*: the two-month revision, the AHE line, the unemployment-rate trend, the birth/death footnote, the participation number. If the internals *agree* with the headline (a strong headline with upward revisions and hot wages), the move **extends** into a trend that can run all session. If the internals *contradict* the headline (a strong headline gutted by a downward revision or a soft wage print), the spike **fades** and often fully reverses within 30 to 60 minutes â€” and the fade, not the spike, is the real signal. This is why the structural reader treats the first knee-jerk as a hypothesis, not a conclusion: the headline sets the spike, the internals set whether it lasts. The precise mechanics of *sizing into* that arc â€” where to place the entry, how wide to set the stop given the Â±130k noise, how to use options to define risk through the print â€” are the trading sibling's job; here, the structural point is that the *internals* (revisions, wages, the unemployment trend) determine whether the knee-jerk on the headline extends or reverses, which is exactly why you read them in the order the checklist above prescribes rather than the order the news presents them.

#### Worked example: reading hot vs cool on the same headline

Two months, identical +220k headline (consensus +180k, a 40k beat). Month A: unemployment ticks *down*, AHE +0.4% MoM, prior two months revised *up* +30k. This is unambiguously HOT â€” in an inflation regime, sell stocks. On a \$25,000 S&P long, a âˆ’1.0% day is **âˆ’\$250**. Month B: same +220k headline, but unemployment ticks *up* from 4.0% to 4.2%, AHE soft at +0.1%, and the prior two months revised *down* âˆ’90k. The headline says strong; the internals say cooling. The market fades the knee-jerk and stocks finish *up* 0.8% â€” on the same \$25,000 that is **+\$200**. **Intuition:** the headline was identical; the internals flipped the sign and the dollar outcome by \$450. The report is never just the headline.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Theory is cheap; the tape is the proof. Two dated episodes show both regimes and how the same instrument â€” payrolls â€” moved markets in opposite directions depending on what the market feared.

![Cross-asset reaction to the February 3 2023 payroll blowout: S&P down, 2-year yield up, dollar up](/imgs/blogs/the-jobs-report-nfp-the-king-of-data-6.png)

### February 3, 2023: the +517k blowout (inflation regime, good-news-is-bad)

The January 2023 report (released February 3) was a once-in-a-cycle shock. Consensus expected about +187,000 payroll jobs. The print: **+517,000** â€” nearly triple the estimate â€” and the unemployment rate fell to **3.4%**, a 53-year low. This landed in the heart of the inflation-fighting regime: the Fed had been hiking aggressively and the market was hoping for signs the economy was slowing enough to let it stop. The blowout said the opposite â€” the labor market was still red-hot, which meant the Fed could keep going.

The cross-asset reaction was textbook good-news-is-bad-news: the S&P 500 fell **âˆ’1.04%** on the day, the 2-year Treasury yield (the maturity most sensitive to Fed policy) jumped **+18bp**, and the dollar index rose **+1.2%**. Strong jobs, falling stocks, rising yields, rising dollar â€” the entire risk-off-on-strong-data pattern, in one session.

#### Worked example: trading the Feb-2023 blowout

You walk in long \$25,000 of the S&P 500 and you also run a \$1,000,000 short in the 2-year Treasury (a bet that yields rise / prices fall). On the âˆ’1.04% S&P day, the equity long loses **âˆ’\$260** (\$25,000 Ã— âˆ’0.0104). The 2-year short, meanwhile, gains on the +18bp yield jump: the DV01 (dollar value of a 1bp move) on \$1,000,000 of a 2-year note is roughly \$190 per basis point, so +18bp â‰ˆ **+\$3,420** to the short. Net across the two positions: âˆ’\$260 + \$3,420 â‰ˆ **+\$3,160** on the day. **Intuition:** the same hot number that hurt the stock long *paid* the rates short, which is exactly why traders express a jobs view in rates, not only in equities â€” the rate curve is where the surprise lands first and cleanest.

The structural lesson from Feb-2023 is that the headline really was the story (a +330k surprise is far outside the Â±130k noise band, so it was a real signal), and the market read it through the inflation-regime reaction function: strong economy â†’ Fed stays tight â†’ sell risk, buy the dollar, push yields up.

### August 2, 2024: the +114k miss and the 4.3% Sahm trigger (growth-scare regime, bad-news-is-bad)

Eighteen months later the regime had flipped. Inflation had cooled substantially; the market's fear had migrated from "too much inflation" to "is the economy breaking?" The July 2024 report (released August 2) printed **+114,000** payrolls against about +175,000 expected â€” a clear miss â€” and, more importantly, the unemployment rate rose to **4.3%**, crossing the **Sahm rule** threshold off the cycle low.

In the *old* regime, a soft jobs report with rising unemployment would have rallied stocks (Fed relief). Instead, because the Sahm trigger flipped the market into recession-fear mode, the reaction was bad-news-is-bad-news: the S&P 500 fell **âˆ’1.84%** on the day, and the 2-year yield *dropped* **âˆ’28bp** as the market suddenly priced aggressive Fed cuts. Stocks down *and* yields down â€” the recession-scare signature, the opposite of the Feb-2023 pattern even though both were "surprising" jobs reports.

And it did not stop at the US close. The weak NFP, landing two days after the Bank of Japan had hiked rates, became the spark for the **August 5, 2024 yen-carry unwind** â€” a global de-leveraging cascade in which the Nikkei fell **âˆ’12.4%** (its worst day since 1987), the S&P fell another **âˆ’3.0%**, the VIX spiked to an intraday **65.73**, and Bitcoin dropped **âˆ’15%**. The mechanics of that cascade belong to a sibling post on carry unwinds, but the trigger is the point here: a single soft US jobs print, read through a fragile growth-scare reaction function, can be the match that lights a global risk-off fire.

#### Worked example: the Aug-2024 recession scare across a book

You run a \$40,000 book: \$25,000 in S&P 500 and \$15,000 in long-dated Treasuries as a recession hedge. On August 2, the âˆ’1.84% S&P day costs the equity sleeve **âˆ’\$460** (\$25,000 Ã— âˆ’0.0184). But the âˆ’28bp drop in the 2-year (and a comparable rally further out the curve) lifts the long-bond sleeve roughly +1.5%, or about **+\$225** on \$15,000. Net: âˆ’\$460 + \$225 â‰ˆ **âˆ’\$235** â€” a real loss, but the bond hedge cut it by nearly half. A book that had been 100% equities (\$40,000) would have lost about **âˆ’\$736** on the same âˆ’1.84% day with no offset. **Intuition:** in a growth-scare regime the bond hedge earns its keep precisely on the days the stocks hurt, because the report pushes the two in opposite directions.

Put the two episodes side by side and the whole thesis of this series is visible in one instrument: in Feb-2023 strong jobs sent stocks down and yields up; in Aug-2024 weak jobs sent stocks down and yields down. Same report, opposite reactions â€” because the regime, and therefore the reaction function, had flipped. The number never tells you the sign; the regime does.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs trip up nearly every newcomer to jobs day. Each is corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "The headline payroll number is everything."** It is the *least* reliable single number in the report. Its 90% confidence band is about Â±130k, so a "beat" of 30k is statistically a tie, and the figure gets revised twice â€” by 50â€“80k on average and, at the August 2024 benchmark, by **âˆ’818,000** across a year. The internals (the two-month revision, wages, the unemployment trend) routinely flip the market's read of the same headline, as the hot-vs-cool worked example showed: an identical +220k printed once as a sell and once as a buy. Read the whole report, not the first line.

**Myth 2: "A falling unemployment rate is always good."** The rate can fall because people *stopped looking* and left the labor force, which is a *weakening* signal dressed as strength. You only know which by reading the **participation rate** alongside it. A rate that falls on rising participation is healthy; a rate that falls on falling participation is people giving up.

**Myth 3: "Strong jobs are good for stocks."** Only in a growth-scare regime. In an inflation-fighting regime, strong jobs are *bad* for stocks because they keep the Fed tight â€” exactly what happened on **February 3, 2023**, when a +517k blowout sent the S&P **âˆ’1.04%** and the 2-year yield **+18bp**. The sign of the reaction is set by what the market fears, not by whether the number is "good" in everyday terms.

**Myth 4: "Weak jobs are good for stocks (the Fed will cut)."** Only while the cooling is *gentle*. Once it tips into recession fear â€” the Sahm rule trips â€” weak jobs become bad for stocks too: on **August 2, 2024**, a +114k miss with unemployment at 4.3% sent the S&P **âˆ’1.84%** *and* the 2-year yield **âˆ’28bp**. Stocks and yields fell together: bad-news-is-bad-news.

**Myth 5: "The unemployment rate and payrolls always agree."** They come from two different surveys counting two different things (jobs vs people), so they routinely diverge â€” especially at turning points, which is exactly when the divergence carries the most information. Strong payrolls with a rising unemployment rate is a classic late-cycle conflict, and the household survey often softens first.

## The playbook: how to read NFP like a trader

You will not trade the print well until the next sibling post (sizing, the spike-fade-trend, options around the event), but you can already *read* it correctly, which is most of the battle. Here is the structural playbook â€” the if-then map you run on the first Friday.

**Before the release â€” know three things:**
1. **The consensus.** Find the median forecast for payrolls, the unemployment rate, and AHE. Price already contains the consensus; only the surprise moves the tape. Write down the consensus so you are reacting to the *gap*, not the level.
2. **The regime.** Ask the single most important question: is the market more afraid of inflation or of recession *right now*? That answer sets the sign of every reaction. (See [inflation and the Fed reaction function](/blog/trading/macro-trading/inflation-and-the-fed-reaction-function-dot-plot) for how to read the regime, and [why news moves markets: the surprise framework](/blog/trading/event-trading/why-news-moves-markets-the-surprise-framework) for the surprise mechanics.)
3. **Where this print sits in the calendar.** Is FOMC next week? Is the market on a knife-edge about the next decision? An NFP that lands right before a Fed meeting moves more, because it is the last big labor read the committee sees. (The full release order is in [the macro calendar: CPI, NFP, FOMC, PMI](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi).)

**At 8:30 ET â€” read in this order (not the news's order):**
1. **Headline vs consensus.** Is the surprise outside the Â±130k noise band? If not, the headline alone is not a tradeable signal.
2. **The two-month revision.** Net up or net down? A strong headline with a big downward revision is a weak report. Read this *before* you react to the headline.
3. **Average hourly earnings (MoM and YoY).** In an inflation regime, a hot AHE can flip the whole read. +0.5% MoM is a wage-inflation scare; ~0.2â€“0.3% is benign.
4. **The unemployment rate and its trend.** Is it rising or falling, and is the three-month average approaching the Sahm threshold (0.5pp above the 12-month low)? Crossing it flips the regime.
5. **Participation.** Did the unemployment rate move because of jobs or because of people entering/leaving the labor force?

**The if-then map (sign depends on regime):**
- **Inflation regime, HOT report** â†’ yields up, dollar up, stocks down. Express the view in **rates** (short the front end) for the cleanest fill; the equity reaction is the second-order echo.
- **Inflation regime, COOL report (gentle)** â†’ yields down, stocks up, dollar down. The Fed-relief rally; small caps and rate-sensitive sectors lead.
- **Growth-scare regime, ALARMINGLY WEAK report (Sahm trips)** â†’ yields down *and* stocks down. Risk-off; the bond hedge offsets the equity loss, and a global carry unwind is the tail risk.

**The invalidation â€” what tells you you read it wrong:** if the knee-jerk reverses and fully fades within the first 30â€“60 minutes, the internals contradicted the headline (a revision, a wage line, a participation quirk the algorithms over- or under-weighted), and the *fade* is the real signal. If the first move *extends* through the hour, the report agreed with itself across surveys and internals, and the trend is your friend. Either way, your pre-written consensus and regime call are what let you tell a real signal from noise inside the chaos.

**Sizing note:** because the first print is statistically noisy (Â±130k) and the spike can violently reverse on the revision, the structural discipline is to size *for* the noise â€” never bet the headline as if it were precise, and never fade or chase before you have read the two-month revision and the wage line. The number that earns the trade is rarely the one in the headline.
