Now we put the engine back together and drive it. We will take one event — the monthly US Consumer Price Index, released by the Bureau of Labor Statistics at 8:30 a.m. Eastern — and trade a full day around it, minute by minute. To keep it honest we use two real prints as our branches. On **September 13, 2022**, CPI came in hot (8.3% versus an 8.1% consensus) and the S&P 500 fell **−4.32%**, its worst CPI day in over two years. On **November 10, 2022**, CPI came in cool (7.7% versus 7.9%) and the S&P rose **+5.54%**, one of its best days of the decade. Same report. Same agency. Inflation was historically high both days. The outcomes were mirror images. The only thing that differed was the *sign of the surprise* — and that is the whole game.

The reason we run two branches instead of one is that a single walkthrough teaches you what happened; two mirror-image walkthroughs teach you the *structure* underneath. If you only studied the hot day, you might walk away thinking "high inflation crashes stocks." Run the cool day right beside it and that lesson dissolves — inflation was high both days, and stocks did opposite things. What is left, once the level cancels out, is the surprise. Everything in this post is built to make that one idea concrete: not as a slogan, but as a sequence of decisions you make across a real trading day, with real numbers, against two real prints. By the end you should be able to take any future CPI date on the calendar, write the plan the night before, and trade it with the same eight-step process — because the process is the part that does not change.

## Foundations: the tools we'll apply

Before the timeline, a one-line refresher on each tool, in the order we use them. If any of these is fuzzy, the linked pillar post unpacks it from zero. The point of this section is not to re-teach — it is to give you the *checklist* in plain language, so you can see how the pieces snap together over the course of one day.

- **The surprise framework.** Price already contains the consensus. Only the difference between the *actual* number and the *consensus* — the surprise — moves price on release. A CPI of 8.3% is not bullish or bearish on its own; 8.3% *against an 8.1% forecast* is a hot surprise, and that two-tenths miss is what the market trades. This is the foundation everything else rests on. See [why news moves markets: the surprise framework](/blog/trading/event-trading/why-news-moves-markets-the-surprise-framework).

- **What's priced in.** The consensus you read on the calendar is the economists' average. The *whisper* — where positioning and the options market actually sit — can differ. The market is positioned for an outcome, and the surprise is measured against *that* position, not the headline forecast. The gap between consensus and whisper is why an "in-line" print can still move things.

- **The expected move.** The options market quotes, in advance, how big it thinks the day will be. An at-the-money straddle priced at \$60 on a \$5,000 S&P index implies a roughly **±1.2%** move. That number is your ruler: it tells you how to size, where a "normal" reaction ends and a "trend" begins, and whether you're being paid enough to hold options through the event. See [the expected move: pricing event risk with options](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-expected-move-pricing-event-risk-with-options).

- **The reaction function.** This decides the *sign*. In the 2022 inflation regime, the market's single question was "how hard will the Fed have to squeeze?" — so hot inflation meant more hikes, which was bad news for stocks (good-news-is-bad, the hallmark of a tightening regime). In a growth-scare regime the same hot print might be shrugged off entirely, or a cool print might be read as a recession signal and *sell* stocks. The same number, the same surprise sign, can produce opposite outcomes in different regimes — which is why you can never trade a print on a rule like "hot is bad for stocks" without first asking what the market is afraid of *right now*. Always name the regime first; the regime is the lens through which the surprise is read. See [the reaction function: why the same number moves differently](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently).

- **Spike, fade, or trend.** The first move is the **knee-jerk** — algorithms reading the headline in milliseconds. Then one of two things happens: the move **fades** (the knee-jerk was an overreaction and price reverts) or it **trends** (the move was real and continues all session). Telling them apart in the first thirty minutes is the core skill. See [the anatomy of a news reaction: spike, fade, trend](/blog/trading/event-trading/anatomy-of-a-news-reaction-spike-fade-trend).

- **Cross-asset transmission.** One print hits every market at once, in a fixed causal order: rates move first (they are the most direct read on the Fed), then the dollar, then equities, then gold and crypto take their cue from real yields and risk appetite. The assets confirm or contradict each other — a stock rally with the dollar also rallying is suspicious. See [cross-asset transmission: how one print hits every market](/blog/trading/event-trading/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market).

- **The plan.** Before the print you write an **if-then map**: if hot, do this in each asset; if in-line, do that; if cool, do the other. The plan turns a chaotic thirty seconds into a lookup. Your hands already know the answer because you wrote it down when you were calm.

- **Risk.** Every position is sized to a fixed dollar loss against the expected move, with a hard stop and a max-loss-per-event budget. The single rule that keeps you in the game: never widen a stop or add to a loser in the heat of the release.

That is the toolkit. Now the day.

## Stage 1 — T-1: what's priced, the expected move, and the if-then plan

The work that decides whether you win a CPI day is done the *day before*, when nothing is happening and your head is clear. By 8:30 the next morning it is far too late to think.

**Step one: find the consensus and the whisper.** The calendar says the consensus for the Aug-2022 CPI (released Sep 13) is **8.1% year-over-year headline, 6.1% core**. But the consensus is just the economists' average — the number Bloomberg and Reuters publish from a survey of forecasters. It is necessary but not sufficient. We also want the *whisper* — where the market is actually leaning and, crucially, how it is *positioned*. These are two different things. The consensus is what analysts say; the whisper is what traders have actually done with their capital. When they diverge, the surprise is measured against the positioning, not the published forecast, because it is the positioned crowd that has to react.

In early September 2022 the tape had rallied for weeks on a hopeful narrative that inflation had peaked over the summer. Gasoline prices had fallen sharply from their June highs, and that fed a story that the worst was behind us. Positioning had leaned toward a cool number — funds had been adding risk, short-dated call buying had picked up, and the put-call ratio had drifted toward complacency. That matters enormously: the market was not neutral going into the print. It was leaning bullish, and leaning bullish into a binary event is the most dangerous place to be, because a disappointment forces a one-way scramble for the exit. A merely in-line number would have disappointed the optimists; a hot number would hit a crowd positioned exactly the wrong way and turn a normal selloff into a stampede as longs were stopped out. Knowing this *before* the print is what lets you size the hot-branch hedge correctly — you are not hedging against the number, you are hedging against the *crowd's forced unwind* if the number disappoints. This is the difference between the consensus on the calendar and what's truly *priced in* — covered in depth in [consensus, expectations, and "priced in"](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in).

**Step two: read the expected move.** We look at the S&P options expiring that day. The at-the-money straddle — the call plus the put at the current index level — is quoting around \$60 with the index near 5,000. The expected move is approximately the straddle price divided by the index: 60 / 5000 = **1.2%**. So the options market is telling us, before the print, that it expects roughly a ±1.2% S&P day. For Bitcoin, the same math on a \$2,400 straddle at a \$60,000 price gives **±4%**.

Why does the straddle encode the expected move? Because a straddle pays off in *either* direction — you make money if the market moves enough up *or* down, and the option sellers price it to break even against the move they expect. If they thought the day would be quiet, they would sell the straddle cheaply; if they thought it would be wild, they would demand a fat premium. So the straddle price *is* the market's consensus estimate of the day's magnitude, expressed in dollars. Dividing by the index level converts it to a percentage. This is the single most useful number you can pull before any event, because it is forward-looking — it is the market's bet on volatility *tomorrow*, not a measurement of volatility yesterday.

These numbers are our ruler, and they do double duty. First, as a *fade-vs-trend* threshold: a move that stays *inside* ±1.2% is "priced" — the options market expected this much movement, so it is likely noise and a candidate to fade. A move that blows *through* ±1.2% is a surprise the options market underpriced — the realized move exceeded what was implied, which signals a genuine repricing and a candidate to trend. Second, as a *sizing* input: you set stops at the edge of the expected-move band and size so a full adverse move costs only your planned risk. We use both jobs below.

**Step three: size to the expected move.** This is where the plan becomes money.

#### Worked example: sizing to the expected move
You want to risk a fixed **\$500** on your core S&P position for this event. The expected move is ±1.2%, and you set your stop just beyond it — say at a 1.2% adverse move, because if price breaches the full expected move against you, your read on the branch was wrong. Position size = risk ÷ stop distance = \$500 ÷ 0.012 = **\$41,667**, which you round to a **\$41,000** position. Now the arithmetic is fixed before the print: if the S&P moves the full expected 1.2% against you, you lose your planned \$500 and you are out — no improvising. If it moves 1.2% in your favor, that \$41,000 position gains about \$492. The expected move did three jobs at once: it set your stop, it set your size, and it set your target. **Sizing to the ruler the options market already quotes turns "how much should I trade?" into a one-line calculation.**

**Step four: write the if-then map.** This is the heart of the plan. We write, for each asset, exactly what we will do under each branch — *before* the print. The figure below is that map in its decision form.

![The 8:30:00 decision tree routing a print versus consensus to hot in-line or cool branches with the pre-set action per asset](/imgs/blogs/event-driven-trading-capstone-a-full-cpi-day-walkthrough-4.png)

Read the figure top to bottom. The 8:30:00 print compares to consensus and routes down one of three branches. Each branch already carries the action per asset, written the night before:

- **If HOT (above 8.1% / 6.1%):** short S&P futures, long the dollar, short gold, short Bitcoin, expect yields up. The bias is risk-off. Only fade the first spike if there is no follow-through.
- **If IN-LINE (right at consensus):** expect a volatility crush and chop. The big options premium that was priced for the event evaporates the moment the uncertainty resolves. Fade the first spike, trade small, and wait for a real trend to declare itself.
- **If COOL (below consensus):** long S&P, short the dollar, long gold, long Bitcoin, expect yields down. The bias is risk-on. This is a trend day — do not fade it.

Notice we have not predicted the number. We have prepared for all three outcomes. That is the entire philosophy of [building an event-day trading plan](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-expected-move-pricing-event-risk-with-options): you cannot know the print, but you can know your response to every version of it.

**Step five: set the risk budget.** We decide that this single event can cost us no more than a fixed dollar amount across all positions — say \$500 on the core trade plus a small budget for the hedge — and we will not exceed it no matter what the tape does. The point of a per-event budget is that it caps the damage from being wrong about the branch *before* you are in the heat of the moment, when the temptation to "give it more room" is strongest and most expensive. A trader without a pre-set budget will, on the worst day, hold a losing position too long, average down, and turn a planned \$500 loss into a \$3,000 one. The budget is the circuit breaker.

We also pre-write the invalidation: if the S&P breaks back through the expected-move band in the wrong direction after we enter, we are wrong about the branch and we exit, no debate. Invalidation written in advance is the difference between a disciplined exit and a hopeful hold. With the consensus, the whisper, the expected move, the sizing, the if-then map, and the risk budget all written down, the night-before work is done. We sleep. The morning is now a lookup — and that is exactly the state you want to be in when an event detonates the tape at 8:30:00.

## Stage 2 — 8:30:00 ET: the print and the knee-jerk

At 8:30:00.000 the BLS releases the number. The release is structured data delivered to the wire services and parsed by headline-reading algorithms in single-digit milliseconds. These algorithms do not read the press release — they read a machine-readable feed, compare the headline and core figures to the pre-loaded consensus, and fire orders before a human has finished reading the first sentence. The market gaps. This is the **knee-jerk**, and which branch we are on is decided in this first second, by machines, on the surprise sign alone. A human cannot compete with the algorithms on speed, and trying to is how you get run over. Your edge is not speed; it is the *plan* — you already decided what to do on each branch, so you do not need to react in milliseconds. You need only to recognize, within a few seconds, which branch the gap confirmed, and then execute the column you wrote the night before.

The scatter below makes the point: the surprise sign, plotted against the same-day S&P move, *is* the branch.

![Scatter of CPI surprise in percentage points against S&P 500 same-day move with the hot and cool branch points highlighted](/imgs/blogs/event-driven-trading-capstone-a-full-cpi-day-walkthrough-5.png)

The green region (top-left) is the cool branch: a downside surprise, stocks up. The red region (bottom-right) is the hot branch: an upside surprise, stocks down. The two highlighted points are our two days. There is no fourth quadrant in this regime — hot never rallied stocks, cool never crashed them. The sign of the surprise routes the whole day, and it is fixed before you can blink.

### The hot branch — September 13, 2022

The number prints: **8.3% headline versus 8.1% consensus; core 6.3% versus 6.1%.** Both above. Worse, the *core* (which strips out food and energy and is what the Fed watches most) was hot — and core rising while headline was supposed to be falling is the ugly combination. The surprise is **+0.2pp hot** on a market that was leaning bullish. The knee-jerk is violent: S&P futures gap down, the dollar gaps up, two-year yields jump. Our plan says: this is the HOT branch. We do not think. We execute the hot column.

#### Worked example: the pre-set hot-branch hedge pays for the long
The night before, our plan held a core **\$25,000** long S&P position (a swing trade we did not want to close) and, anticipating event risk, a pre-set **\$20,000** short S&P futures hedge to be active only on a hot print. The hot branch fires. By the close the S&P is **−4.32%**. The long position loses 4.32% of \$25,000 = **−\$1,080**. But the short hedge *gains* 4.32% of \$20,000 = **+\$864**. Net for the day: −\$1,080 + \$864 = **−\$216**. Without the hedge we would have lost \$1,080; with the pre-set hedge from the plan we lost \$216 — an 80% reduction in the drawdown, and we are still positioned for the longer-term swing. **The hedge was not a panic trade at 8:31; it was a line in the if-then map, sized and ready the night before.**

The knee-jerk on the hot branch did not fade. It trended all day, because the surprise hit a market positioned the wrong way and the pain compounded as longs were forced out. The mechanism is a feedback loop: the initial gap stops out the leveraged longs, their forced selling pushes price lower, which stops out the next layer of longs, and so on. A surprise that lands on crowded positioning does not just move price once — it sets off a cascade as the positioning unwinds. By the close the S&P had given back **−4.32%**, the Nasdaq **−5.16%**, the dollar had jumped **+1.4%**, and Bitcoin had bled roughly **−9.4%**. Every risk asset fell together. That is the signature of a real cross-asset move, not a head-fake.

#### Worked example: the dollar leg as a separate position
Suppose, beyond the equity hedge, your plan held a **\$30,000** long-dollar position (via a dollar-index proxy) sized for the hot branch — a clean expression of "hot CPI means more Fed hikes means a stronger dollar." The dollar index rose **+1.4%** on Sep 13. Gain = 1.4% of \$30,000 = **+\$420**. On the cool branch the dollar would have *fallen* −2.1%, so the same \$30,000 position would have lost 2.1% × \$30,000 = **−\$630** — which is exactly why this position is in the hot column only and is flipped to a *short* dollar on the cool branch. The dollar is the cleanest single-asset expression of the CPI surprise because it sits directly downstream of the rate move, with less of the noise that clutters equities. **A long-dollar position on a hot print is, in effect, a leveraged bet on the Fed reaction function — which is why it paid \$420 here while the stock book was bleeding.**

### The cool branch — November 10, 2022

Now rewind and run the other branch. Eight weeks later the number prints: **7.7% headline versus 7.9% consensus; core 6.3% versus 6.5%.** Both *below*. The surprise is **−0.2pp cool**, and this time the market was nervous, braced for another hot number after months of them — positioning was defensive, the crowd was short or in cash, and the put-call ratio was elevated. A cool surprise into a defensive crowd produces the mirror of the hot-into-bullish stampede: a violent short-covering rally as everyone who was positioned for bad news scrambles to chase the move. The knee-jerk is explosive in the other direction: S&P futures rocket up, the dollar collapses, yields crater. Our plan says: COOL branch. We execute the cool column — long stocks, short dollar, long gold, long Bitcoin.

The magnitude of the cool-day rally (+5.54%, larger than the hot day's −4.32%) is itself a positioning tell. Relief rallies are often bigger than the selloffs that precede them, because a defensive crowd chasing a turn has more buying to do than a complacent crowd has selling. The same surprise size in points (0.2pp) produced a *larger* equity move on the cool side precisely because the crowd was leaning the other way. This is the surprise framework and the positioning lens working together: the move is the surprise *times* how wrong-footed the crowd was.

#### Worked example: the cool-branch long and the crypto kicker
On the cool branch our **\$25,000** S&P long runs with the move: +5.54% of \$25,000 = **+\$1,385**. We also held a **\$10,000** Bitcoin position as the high-beta risk expression; Bitcoin rose roughly +10% on the cool print, so +10% of \$10,000 = **+\$1,000**. Combined, the two risk longs made +\$1,385 + \$1,000 = **\$2,385** on the day. Contrast that with the hot branch, where the same \$25,000 long *lost* \$1,080. **One report, two prints two months apart, and a \$3,465 swing on the same \$25,000 position — entirely because the surprise sign flipped.** That gap is the whole reason we trade the surprise and not the number.

## Stage 3 — 8:30 to 9:00: the core read and fade-vs-trend

The first second decides the branch. The first *thirty minutes* decide whether you fade or trend — and that is where most of the money is made or lost.

The knee-jerk overshoots constantly. The headline-reading algorithms fire on the top-line number in milliseconds, but the top line is not the whole story. Humans — and the slower, smarter models — then read the *internals* and decide whether the knee-jerk was justified or an overreaction. The internals for CPI are the **core** (ex food and energy, which strips out the two most volatile components so the underlying trend shows through) and, increasingly, the **supercore** (core services excluding housing), which the Fed watches as the stickiest, most labor-driven measure of underlying inflation. There is also the **shelter** component, which lags real-time rents by many months and so can keep the headline elevated long after the actual housing market has cooled. A sophisticated read in the 8:30–9:00 window asks not just "was the headline hot?" but "was it hot *because of* a sticky core, or *because of* a one-off energy spike that the Fed will look through?" Those two hot prints route to very different reactions.

The headline can be hot while core is cooling, or vice versa, and the market cares far more about core because the Fed cares far more about core. The 8:30–9:00 window is when the market re-reads the print through the internals and decides to keep going or to reverse. This is the most information-dense thirty minutes of the trading day, and it is where preparation separates the trader who calmly executes a plan from the one who is frantically reading headlines and clicking the wrong way.

Here is the decision rule, tied directly to our expected-move ruler:

- **If the knee-jerk move is inside the ±1.2% expected move and the internals contradict the headline, fade it.** The options market priced this much movement; the print is "in the range"; the knee-jerk is noise. Fade back toward the pre-print level.
- **If the knee-jerk blows through ±1.2% and the internals *confirm* the headline, trend it.** The surprise was bigger than the options market priced and the internals agree. This is a real repricing; ride it, do not fight it.

On **September 13, 2022**, the internals were brutal — core *rose* to 6.3% when it was supposed to ease to 6.1%, supercore was firm, and the move had already blown well past the ±1.2% expected move within the first hour. The S&P was down more than 3% before lunch, nearly *three times* the full expected move, which is the loudest possible signal that the options market had underpriced the event and a real repricing was underway. Headline and core agreed: this was hot and getting worse. That is the textbook trend signal — internals confirming the headline, realized move exceeding the implied move, every cross-asset confirming. Fading it would have meant standing in front of a −4.32% freight train. The plan's hot-branch instruction — "trend, do not fade unless no follow-through" — was correct, and the follow-through was overwhelming.

The cool branch on **November 10, 2022** was the same logic in reverse. Core *fell* to 6.3% versus a 6.5% consensus — the first clear sign in months that the underlying trend was bending lower, not just the volatile headline. The internals confirmed the cool headline, the move blew through the expected move to the upside (+5.54% is more than four times a 1.2% expected move), and every asset confirmed: yields collapsed, the dollar fell, gold rose. Trend, do not fade. A trader who tried to fade the +1% knee-jerk at 8:32, reasoning "stocks can't rally on 7.7% inflation," would have been short into a +5.54% rocket. The internals and the broken expected-move ceiling said *trend*, and the level of inflation was irrelevant.

#### Worked example: fading a false in-line spike
Say a different, in-line print arrives where headline matches consensus but the first algo read spikes the S&P +0.9% in two minutes — inside the ±1.2% expected move. The internals are mixed, not confirming. You take a **\$15,000** fade short against the spike with a tight stop 0.4% above (a \$60 risk on a 0.4% adverse move on \$15,000). Price reverts to flat within the hour as the vol crush sets in. You cover for a 0.7% gain: +0.7% of \$15,000 = **+\$105** on \$60 of risk. The trade worked because an in-line print inside the expected move, with non-confirming internals, is the canonical fade. **The expected move told you the spike was "in the range," and the internals told you it was noise — so you faded the crowd's overreaction instead of joining it.**

This window is also where the **vol crush** does its work. Before the print, options carried a fat premium for the event's uncertainty; the instant the number resolves, that uncertainty is gone and option prices collapse even if the underlying barely moves. On an in-line print, an option buyer can be *right about direction and still lose* because the premium evaporated faster than the move paid. That asymmetry — covered in [event volatility: implied vs realized and the vol crush](/blog/trading/event-trading/event-volatility-implied-vs-realized-and-the-vol-crush) — is why we sized the night before and why we lean toward *selling* premium into an in-line print and *buying* the direction (not the option) on a confirmed trend.

## Stage 4 — the US session: the cross-asset map plays out

By mid-morning the branch is set and the fade-vs-trend question is answered. Now the move propagates across every asset in a fixed causal order, and the assets confirm each other. This is where the cross-asset map we built pays off as a *coherence check*: if the assets are not all telling the same story, the move is suspect, and you should distrust it.

The reason the order is fixed is that the assets are not reacting independently — they are reacting to the *same* underlying driver, the Fed reaction function, but at different removes from it. Treasury yields are the most direct read on the Fed, so they move first and most cleanly. The dollar sits one step downstream (it follows the rate differential). Equities sit further out still (they care about rates *and* growth *and* earnings, so their move is noisier). Gold and crypto sit at the far end, reacting to real yields and the overall risk mood. Knowing this chain lets you use the cleaner, faster-moving assets to confirm or doubt the noisier ones. When the two-year yield is screaming "hot" but equities are only drifting, the equity move has further to go. When equities are crashing but yields are flat, something other than CPI is driving stocks and you should be careful.

The transmission order is always the same:

1. **Rates move first.** Treasury yields are the most direct read on what the Fed will do. A hot print pushes the two-year yield up (the market prices more hikes); a cool print pushes it down (fewer hikes, sooner cuts). On the hot day the two-year jumped about **+18bp**; on the cool day the ten-year *collapsed* **−28bp** and the two-year **−33bp**. Rates lead because they are the cleanest expression of the Fed reaction function.
2. **The dollar follows rates.** Higher US yields make dollar assets more attractive, so the dollar rises on a hot print (**+1.4%** on Sep 13) and falls on a cool one (**−2.1%** on Nov 10). The dollar is the hinge between US rates and everything global.
3. **Equities take the cue.** Higher rates lower the present value of future earnings and signal a tighter Fed, so stocks fall on hot, rise on cool. The S&P's −4.32% and +5.54% are the headline, but the *internals* of the equity move matter: on the cool 2023 print, small-cap Russell 2000 rose **+5.44%** because small caps are the most rate-sensitive part of the market.
4. **Gold and crypto react to real yields and risk appetite.** Gold fell slightly on the hot print (higher real yields hurt the zero-yield metal) and rose **+2.8%** on the cool one. Bitcoin, the highest-beta risk asset, fell roughly **−9.4%** hot and rose roughly **+10%** cool — it amplifies the equity signal.

The figures below show the two complete cross-asset days side by side. First the hot branch.

![Bar chart of the cross-asset reaction to the hot August 2022 CPI showing stocks crypto down dollar up](/imgs/blogs/event-driven-trading-capstone-a-full-cpi-day-walkthrough-2.png)

Every risk asset is red, the dollar is the lone green bar, and Bitcoin is the deepest red — exactly the risk-off signature. Now the mirror.

![Bar chart of the cross-asset reaction to the cool October 2022 CPI showing stocks crypto up dollar down](/imgs/blogs/event-driven-trading-capstone-a-full-cpi-day-walkthrough-3.png)

The chart flips: every risk asset is green, the dollar is the lone red bar. The two figures are near-perfect mirror images — which is the whole point. The *level* of inflation was high on both days; the *sign of the surprise* produced opposite, internally-coherent cross-asset days.

The coherence is the trader's confirmation. On the hot day, if stocks had been falling but the dollar had *also* been falling, you would distrust the equity move — something would be off, because a real hot-CPI selloff has the dollar rising with yields, not falling. The fact that rates, the dollar, stocks, gold, and crypto all told the same risk-off story is what let you hold the trend with conviction through the entire session rather than taking a quick scalp and getting out. Conviction comes from confirmation, and confirmation comes from the assets agreeing.

When the cross-asset story fractures — stocks down but the dollar also down, or yields up but gold also up — that is your signal that the move is positioning-driven noise, not a real repricing, and you tighten or exit. A classic example: a hot print where stocks gap down but yields *fall*. That combination makes no sense for a hot CPI (hot should push yields up), so the equity selloff is probably about something else — a positioning unwind, a separate headline — and fading it is reasonable. The cross-asset map is, in effect, a lie detector for any single asset's move. How crypto specifically slots into this map is unpacked in [how crypto reacts to macro news](/blog/trading/event-trading/how-crypto-reacts-to-macro-news), and the full mechanism in [trading the CPI release: a cross-asset playbook](/blog/trading/event-trading/trading-the-cpi-release-cross-asset-playbook).

#### Worked example: the cross-asset bond leg in dollars
Suppose alongside the equity trade you held a **\$1,000,000** notional two-year Treasury position into the cool Nov-2022 print. The two-year yield fell **−33bp** that day. A two-year note has a DV01 (dollar value of a one-basis-point move) of roughly **\$190** per million notional. A −33bp fall in yield is a *rise* in price, so the gain ≈ 33 × \$190 = **+\$6,270** on the \$1,000,000 position. On the hot Sep-2022 print, the two-year rose **+18bp**, a loss of 18 × \$190 = **−\$3,420**. **The bond leg is not a sideshow — it moved more dollars than the stock leg on a notional basis, and it led the whole cross-asset cascade because rates are the market's direct vote on the Fed.**

## Stage 5 — T+1: the lagged VN-Index reaction

The US CPI prints at 8:30 a.m. Eastern, which is roughly 7:30 or 8:30 p.m. in Vietnam depending on daylight saving — the HOSE exchange is long closed. So Vietnam cannot react in real time. Instead, the shock arrives the *next session*, transmitted through the dollar and foreign equity flows. This lag is a feature, not a bug for the prepared trader: it means a Vietnamese trader who watched the US CPI reaction the previous evening already knows which way the foreign flows will lean at the open the next morning. The information is free and the reaction is delayed — a rare combination that rewards anyone paying attention to the US calendar.

Why do foreign flows matter so much for the VN-Index specifically? Because foreign investors, while a minority of total turnover, are the *marginal* price-setter at the index level — they concentrate in the large-cap blue chips that dominate the VN-Index weighting, and their buying and selling moves those heavyweight names. When foreign funds turn net sellers, the index-heavy stocks lead the market down regardless of what domestic retail is doing. And foreign flows are not random: they are driven by the global cost of capital, which is set in Washington and expressed through the dollar and US Treasury yields. A hot US CPI that lifts US yields makes the risk-free US alternative more attractive and the dong-denominated Vietnamese trade less so, so foreign money rotates out. This is the channel that connects a number released in the evening in Vietnam to the VN-Index open the next morning.

![Transmission chain from US CPI to dollar and US yields to foreign flows on HOSE to the VN-Index the next session](/imgs/blogs/event-driven-trading-capstone-a-full-cpi-day-walkthrough-6.png)

Trace the chain in the figure. A hot US CPI pushes the dollar up and US yields up overnight. A stronger dollar pressures the dong (USD/VND rises), and the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) must defend the currency band — which it did aggressively in autumn 2022, hiking its refinance rate from 4.0% to 6.0% to protect the dong. Higher US yields also make Vietnamese equities relatively less attractive to foreign investors, so foreign funds *sell* on HOSE the next session. That net outflow is what actually moves the VN-Index, because foreign flows are the marginal price-setter at the index level. The result: the VN-Index falls at T+1.

The 2022 episode is the textbook case. As the US hiked and the dollar surged through autumn 2022, the VN-Index fell from its January peak near 1,528 all the way to a **trough of 911 on November 15, 2022** — roughly a −39% drawdown — with the SBV forced into +200bp of rate hikes (refinance rate from 4.0% to 6.0%) to defend the dong. Note the timing: the VN-Index troughed on November 15, just *days after* the cool US CPI of November 10 turned the global tide. That is not a coincidence. The cool US print weakened the dollar, eased the pressure on the dong, and let foreign flows stabilize — and the VN-Index bottomed almost immediately. The same dollar-and-flows channel that drove Vietnam down all autumn turned and lifted it the moment the US inflation surprise flipped cool. The lag from the US shock to the VN reaction is exactly the dollar-and-flows channel in the figure, working in both directions.

It is worth sitting with that symmetry. A Vietnamese trader did not need to forecast Vietnamese inflation or guess the SBV's next move to anticipate the November 2022 turn. They needed only to watch the US CPI reaction on the evening of November 10, see the dollar collapse −2.1% and US risk assets rocket, and conclude that foreign selling pressure on HOSE was about to ease. The signal was in the dollar, hours before the HOSE open. The full mechanism, including the SBV's reaction function and the credit-ceiling tool, is in [Vietnam events: the SBV, VN CPI, and how the VN-Index reacts](/blog/trading/event-trading/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts), with the flow mechanics in [foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam) and the domestic-liquidity backdrop in [liquidity and the margin cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam).

#### Worked example: the VN T+1 leg in dollars
You hold a **\$8,000**-equivalent VN-Index basket (priced in dong, but you measure your book in dollars). The US CPI prints hot in the evening; the dollar jumps and US yields rise. The next session, foreign funds sell HOSE and the VN-Index opens down and closes **−2%**. Your basket loses 2% of \$8,000 = **−\$160** on the price move. But there is a second leg: the dong weakened roughly 0.5% against the dollar that day, so converting your dong holdings back to dollars costs another ~0.5% of \$8,000 = **−\$40**. Total dollar loss: −\$160 − \$40 = **−\$200**. **A Vietnamese position takes a double hit from a hot US print — the index falls on foreign selling *and* the currency translation works against a dollar-based book — which is why the dollar leg of the cross-asset map matters as much in Hanoi as on Wall Street.** The flip side: a cool US print sends foreigners *back* into HOSE the next session, and the VN-Index tends to gap up at the open.

This is the elegant part of the whole system. The VN-Index reaction is not a separate event to study — it is the *same* CPI surprise, arriving one session later through the dollar-and-flows channel. If you understood the US cross-asset map, you already understood the Vietnam leg; you just had to wait one session and watch the dollar.

## The two branches compared

Put the two days next to each other, asset by asset, and the symmetry is the lesson. Same report, same agency, both showing historically high inflation, eight weeks apart — and a near-perfect mirror across every market.

| Asset | Hot print (Sep 13, 2022) | Cool print (Nov 10, 2022) |
|---|---|---|
| Surprise | +0.2pp above consensus | −0.2pp below consensus |
| S&P 500 | **−4.32%** | **+5.54%** |
| Nasdaq | −5.16% | +7.35% |
| Dow | −3.94% | +3.70% |
| 10-year yield | +6bp | **−28bp** |
| 2-year yield | +18bp | −33bp |
| US dollar (DXY) | +1.4% | −2.1% |
| Gold | −0.4% | +2.8% |
| Bitcoin | ≈ −9.4% | ≈ +10.0% |
| Branch behavior | trend, no fade | trend, no fade |
| VN-Index (T+1 channel) | foreign selling, index down | foreign buying, index up |

A few things to read off the table. First, the *direction flips on every single line* — there is no asset that went the same way on both days, which is exactly what a coherent reaction-function regime produces. When one driver (the surprise sign) sets the whole board, every asset inverts together; if some assets had *not* flipped, it would mean a second driver was muddying the signal. Second, the *magnitudes are not symmetric*: the cool day's S&P move (+5.54%) was larger than the hot day's (−4.32%), because the cool print hit a market braced for bad news and the relief rally was outsized — the positioning amplifier we discussed. Third, the *bonds led both days* — the yield moves were the cleanest, earliest expression of the Fed repricing, and everything else followed, which is why a disciplined trader watches the two-year yield as the tell for the whole complex. Fourth, *Bitcoin amplified the equity move both times* (−9.4% vs +10%), confirming its role as the highest-beta risk asset on the board: when risk is on, Bitcoin rises more than stocks; when risk is off, it falls more. Fifth, *gold played defense*, barely moving on the hot day (−0.4%) but rallying +2.8% on the cool day — gold cares about real yields, which fell hard on the cool print, so the cool day was unambiguously good for it while the hot day was a wash between higher nominal yields and safe-haven demand.

The deeper point: if you had spent both mornings asking "is inflation high?" you would have been paralyzed, because the answer was "yes" both times. The market never asked that. It asked "was the print hot or cool *versus what we already priced*?" — and that single question, answered in the first second, set the sign of every line in the table. That is the surprise framework, in full color, on two real days. ## Common misconceptions

A quick recap of the biggest traps the series corrected, each with a number to make it stick.

**"A high inflation number is bad for stocks."** No — the *surprise* is what matters, not the level. Inflation was 8.3% on the hot day and 7.7% on the cool day; stocks fell on the higher-but-as-feared and... also fell. Wait — they fell on 8.3% and *rose* on 7.7%. The lower number rallied stocks **+5.54%** and the higher number crashed them **−4.32%**, but only because 8.3% was *above* its 8.1% consensus and 7.7% was *below* its 7.9% consensus. The level told you nothing; the surprise told you everything. Trade the gap to consensus, never the absolute number.

**"The first move tells you the direction for the day."** Often, but not always. On a confirmed trend (hot internals, move blows through the expected move) the first move is the day. But on an in-line print inside the expected move, the first move is frequently a fade — algorithms overshoot on the headline, then humans re-read the internals and reverse. We saw the fade pay **+\$105** on a \$15,000 position when the knee-jerk reverted. The first *second* sets the branch; the first thirty *minutes* decide fade-or-trend.

**"You should buy options before a big event to catch the move."** Dangerous. The vol crush means an option can lose money even when you call the direction right, because the event premium collapses the instant the number resolves. On an in-line print, an at-the-money straddle bought the day before can be down 30–50% by 9:00 a.m. even if the underlying is roughly flat. Buy the *direction* on a confirmed trend; sell premium into an in-line print; never buy lottery options into the crush.

**"Vietnam moves on its own domestic news."** Partly, but the VN-Index is heavily steered by foreign flows, and foreign flows are steered by the US dollar and US rates — which are steered by US CPI. The 2022 drawdown to **911** was, in large part, the US tightening cycle transmitted through the dollar to foreign selling on HOSE. The US calendar is, indirectly, a Vietnamese calendar.

**"If I have a good plan I will win every event."** No — a good plan makes your *expectancy* positive and your *losses bounded*, not your win rate 100%. On the hot day even a well-hedged book still lost \$216. The plan's job is not to eliminate losses; it is to keep them small and pre-decided so that the winners (like the +\$2,385 cool-branch day) outweigh them over many events. Process, not prophecy. The trader who internalizes this stops trying to predict the print — an impossible task — and instead competes on the one thing they can control: the quality and discipline of their response to each branch. Over a year of CPI, NFP, and FOMC prints, the disciplined responder with bounded losses and a few outsized winners compounds; the predictor who occasionally nails a print but lets a few losses run does not. The edge is structural, not psychic.

**"The vol crush only matters for option traders."** It matters for everyone, because it shapes the *shape* of the day. On an in-line print, the collapse of event premium removes a major source of demand for hedges, and the unwinding of those hedges can itself create a small mechanical drift. More importantly, the vol crush is *why* in-line prints tend to chop and fade rather than trend — the uncertainty that was holding the market in a tense, wide range resolves, and price settles back toward where it was. Even if you never touch an option, understanding that an in-line print deflates the day tells you to expect a fade, trade smaller, and not chase the first spike.

## The same day, a different event

We walked CPI, but the entire point of the capstone is that the *workflow* — not the specific release — is the edge. Swap CPI for any other macro event and the eight stages are identical; only the inputs change. Two quick translations make this concrete.

**The jobs report (NFP), first Friday, 8:30 ET.** Same clock as CPI, same pre-positioning the night before, same expected-move ruler, same if-then map. What changes is the surprise variable: instead of inflation versus consensus, it is *payrolls and the unemployment rate* versus consensus. And the reaction function can be more complex, because the labor market feeds both the Fed (too-hot jobs means more hikes, bad for stocks) *and* the growth story (too-weak jobs means recession, also bad for stocks). That is why a strong jobs print can be bad-news-is-bad in one regime and a weak print can be bad-news-is-bad in another. On **February 3, 2023**, a blowout +517k payrolls print (versus ~187k expected) sent the S&P **−1.04%** and the two-year yield up **+18bp** — too hot, more Fed. On **August 2, 2024**, a weak +114k with the unemployment rate jumping to 4.3% sent the S&P **−1.84%** and the two-year *down* **−28bp** — recession scare, the opposite kind of bad news. Same report, opposite surprise directions, both negative for stocks, because the regime decided how each surprise was read. The eight steps did not change; only the regime lens did. The hands-on version is in [trading the NFP release: a cross-asset playbook](/blog/trading/event-trading/trading-the-nfp-release-cross-asset-playbook).

**The FOMC decision, eight times a year, 2:00 p.m. ET.** Now the clock moves to the afternoon and there are *two* events stacked: the 2:00 statement-and-dots, then the 2:30 press conference. The expected move is larger and the fade-vs-trend question gets a second act — the market often gaps one way on the statement, then completely reverses during the chair's press conference. The surprise variable here is not a single number but the *gap between the statement, the dot plot, and the chair's tone* versus what was priced. On **December 19, 2018**, a hike with a still-hawkish dot plot, against a market begging for a dovish pivot, sent the S&P **−1.54%** and was part of a brutal Q4 2018 drawdown of nearly −20%. On **March 16, 2022**, the first hike of the cycle — exactly as expected — *rallied* the S&P **+2.24%** on relief and clarity. Same kind of event, opposite reactions, because one delivered a hawkish surprise into a dovish-positioned market and the other removed uncertainty that had been weighing on the tape. The full anatomy is in [trading the FOMC decision: statement, dots, presser](/blog/trading/event-trading/trading-the-fomc-decision-statement-dots-presser).

The lesson of both translations is the same: the calendar, the priced expectation, the expected move, the plan, the reaction, the cross-asset confirmation, the risk, and the review are *invariant*. The release is just which numbers go into the slots. Master the workflow on CPI and you can trade any scheduled macro event with the same machinery.

## The master playbook

Here is the whole series on one card. Run these eight steps around *every* macro event — CPI, NFP, FOMC, PCE, PMI, even an earnings print or a geopolitical shock. The event changes; the workflow does not.

![The master playbook eight-step workflow from calendar to priced to expected move to plan to reaction to cross-asset to risk to review](/imgs/blogs/event-driven-trading-capstone-a-full-cpi-day-walkthrough-7.png)

Walk the card left to right, top to bottom:

1. **Calendar.** Know the exact date, time, and consensus for every event on the horizon. Surprises only happen against scheduled expectations; an event you didn't know was coming is one you can't trade. Start every week by marking the calendar — the foundation of [the global economic calendar: what actually moves markets](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets).
2. **What's priced.** Find the consensus *and* the whisper. Read where positioning is leaning so you measure the surprise against the real expectation, not just the headline forecast. A market leaning one way amplifies a surprise in the other.
3. **Expected move.** Read the at-the-money straddle to get the options market's quoted move (±1.2% on a normal S&P CPI day). This is your ruler for sizing, stops, and the fade-vs-trend line.
4. **The plan.** Write the if-then map: the per-asset action under hot, in-line, and cool, with entries, stops, and invalidation — all before the print. Your hands should never improvise at 8:31.
5. **Reaction.** At the print, identify the branch in the first second from the surprise sign, then decide fade-or-trend in the first thirty minutes using the internals and the expected-move ruler. Reaction beats number.
6. **Cross-asset.** Confirm the move across rates, the dollar, gold, and crypto. If the assets all tell the same story, hold with conviction; if the story fractures, the move is noise — tighten or exit.
7. **Risk.** Honor the pre-set stop and the max-loss-per-event budget. Never widen a stop, never add to a loser, never revenge-trade after a bad print. The one rule that keeps you in the game.
8. **Review.** After the close, log the print, the surprise, the branch, your trades, and the outcome. Update the playbook. Every event is a data point that makes the next one easier to read.

That is the entire series, distilled. The eight steps are the same whether you are trading the 8:30 CPI on Wall Street, the FOMC at 2:00 p.m., the NFP on the first Friday, or watching the VN-Index gap at the HOSE open the morning after a US print. The event is just the input. The workflow is the edge.
