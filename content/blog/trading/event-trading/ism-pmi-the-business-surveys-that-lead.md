It was a first-of-the-month Monday, just after 10:00 a.m. New York time. For three weeks the tape had been pricing a soft landing: stocks grinding higher, the front end of the bond curve drifting toward rate cuts, copper firm, the dollar soft. Then a single line crossed the wire — the ISM Manufacturing PMI had printed below 50. Not a catastrophe, not a crash number, just a survey index that had slipped under the line that separates an economy that is growing from one that is shrinking.

The reaction was out of proportion to the move in the index itself. Within minutes, the cyclical corner of the equity market — industrials, materials, banks, small caps — went red while defensive sectors barely moved. Copper sold off. The Australian dollar, which trades like a bet on global growth, dropped. Two-year yields fell as traders pulled forward the timing of the next rate cut. The narrative did not change because the economy had collapsed overnight; it changed because a **timely, forward-looking survey** had said, before any of the slow official statistics could confirm it, that the momentum was turning. The morning's headlines went from "soft landing" to "recession watch" on the strength of one diffusion index.

That is the whole point of this post. The ISM and PMI surveys are not the economy. They are a fast, cheap, monthly **opinion poll of the people who actually place the orders** — and because they are fast and because they ask about the future, they tend to move *before* the hard data. Learn to read them and you get an early seat. Misread them — confuse a small-but-noisy manufacturing print for the whole economy — and you will trade the wrong recession at the wrong time.

By the end of this post you should be able to do four things in the seconds after a PMI crosses the wire: place the level against 50 to name the regime, read the direction to gauge the momentum, decide whether the print is the small early factory signal or the broad services signal, and check the prices-paid internal for the separate inflation read. Each of those four reads points to a different asset and a different trade, and the art is knowing which one matters most *today*, given what the market is currently afraid of. We will build all of it from the ground up — the arithmetic of the diffusion index, the reason it leads, the trap of manufacturing versus services, the sub-indices that carry the real signal, the global PMIs that move commodities — and then put it back together as a checklist you can actually run live.

## Foundations: what a diffusion index is

Let us define every term from zero, because the survey's whole reputation rests on a piece of arithmetic that sounds trivial and turns out to be powerful.

**PMI** stands for **Purchasing Managers' Index**. A purchasing manager is the person inside a company who decides what to buy and when — raw materials, components, inventory, services. They sit at the very front of the production line, so they feel a change in demand before it shows up in shipped goods, before it shows up in revenue, and long before it shows up in a government statistic. If orders are slowing, the purchasing manager is the first to know, because they are the one who stops placing them.

**ISM** stands for the **Institute for Supply Management**, a US trade body that has run its manufacturing survey since 1948 — one of the longest-running and most-watched economic series in the world. ISM publishes two headline surveys each month: the **Manufacturing PMI** (released on the first business day of the month) and the **Services PMI**, sometimes called the non-manufacturing or business-activity index (released a couple of business days later). Separately, the data company **S&P Global** (which absorbed the IHS Markit surveys) runs its own family of PMIs for the US and for dozens of countries around the world, including the closely watched manufacturing and composite PMIs for China, the euro area, the UK and Japan. The methodology is similar; the panels and timing differ. When traders say "PMI" they mean this whole family of surveys; when they say "ISM" they mean specifically the two US ones.

**The diffusion index and the 50 line.** Here is the engine. Each month the survey asks a panel of purchasing managers, for each of several activities (new orders, production, employment, supplier deliveries, inventories, prices, and so on), a single deliberately simple question: *is this better, the same, or worse than last month?* The survey does not ask "by how much." It only asks for the **direction** of the change. To turn those votes into a number, the survey takes the percentage of respondents saying "better," adds **half** of the percentage saying "the same," and ignores those saying "worse":

```
diffusion index = (% better) + 0.5 x (% same)
```

If exactly as many firms are improving as are deteriorating, the index sits at **50**. More firms growing than shrinking pushes it above 50; more shrinking than growing pulls it below. That single threshold — **50 = the breakeven between expansion and contraction** — is the most important number in the entire release. Everything a trader does with a PMI is, at bottom, a measurement of where the index sits relative to 50 and which way it is moving.

**Expansion vs contraction.** A reading above 50 means the sector is *expanding* — not that everything is wonderful, just that more firms reported improvement than deterioration. A reading below 50 means *contraction* — more firms are getting worse than better. A reading of 55 is a healthy, solidly growing economy; a reading of 48 is a mild contraction; a reading of 42 is the kind of number you see in a recession. The historical record matters: ISM Manufacturing has bottomed near 30 in the worst recessions and peaked in the low 60s in the hottest booms, so the practical range you will live in is roughly 42 to 63, with 50 as the dividing line.

**The sub-indices.** The headline PMI is itself a weighted composite of several of those activity questions, but three sub-indices carry information the headline hides: **new orders** (the demand the firm has just won — the most leading of all), **prices paid** (what firms are paying for inputs — an early read on inflation), and **employment** (hiring intentions — a preview of the official jobs report). We will spend a full section on these, because the professional read of a PMI is almost always a read of the internals.

**Manufacturing vs services.** Manufacturing — factories and goods producers — is only about a tenth of US output and employment, but it is intensely **cyclical**: it swings hard with the inventory cycle and turns early, which is exactly why a small sector gets an outsized amount of attention. Services — finance, healthcare, retail, technology, hospitality — is the other roughly seventy-plus percent of the economy and is where most people actually work. A core lesson of this post is that the headline-grabbing manufacturing PMI is the *early* signal, but the services PMI is the *broad* signal. Confusing the two is the single most common PMI mistake.

**Soft data vs hard data.** Finally, the distinction that explains the survey's power. **Hard data** are the actual, measured outcomes: GDP, industrial production, retail sales, payrolls. They are accurate but slow — GDP for a quarter arrives weeks after the quarter ends and gets revised twice. **Soft data** are surveys and sentiment: PMIs, consumer confidence, regional Fed business surveys. They are less precise but **fast and forward-looking**. A PMI for June is out on the first business day of July; the official June industrial-production figure arrives in the middle of the month and the June GDP contribution arrives much later. The survey trades on that timing edge: it is the market's best same-week guess at what the slow statistics will eventually confirm.

**Why the survey, and not just the spending?** A natural objection from a beginner is: if we want to know whether the economy is growing, why not just look at how much firms are actually spending and producing? The answer is that the actual spending arrives too late and too noisily to trade. By the time a firm's orders show up as shipped goods, recorded revenue, and a line in the national accounts, two or three months have passed and the moment for an early trade is gone. The survey skips the whole accounting chain. It asks the person who *makes the spending decision* what they are seeing right now and turns that into a number you can have in your hand the same morning the month ends. You give up precision — a survey cannot tell you that output rose 1.3% — and you buy timeliness, which for a trader is usually the better deal. The market does not pay you for being right eventually; it pays you for being right early.

**Who is on the panel, and what they are asked.** The ISM Manufacturing panel is made up of purchasing and supply executives at hundreds of US manufacturing firms, spread across the major industry groups roughly in proportion to their contribution to GDP. Each respondent answers the same short set of direction questions every month — better, same, or worse — for new orders, production, employment, supplier deliveries, inventories, customers' inventories, prices, backlog of orders, new export orders and imports. ISM publishes each of those as its own diffusion index, plus the headline composite. The Services survey works the same way with a panel drawn from non-manufacturing industries and a slightly different question set built around business activity. The consistency of the question is what makes the series so valuable across decades: the same simple comparison, asked of the same kind of person, month after month since 1948, gives you a clean, apples-to-apples read on the direction of the economy that almost no other indicator can match.

## How a diffusion index is built

Let us make the arithmetic concrete, because the diffusion construction has two consequences traders exploit and beginners miss.

Say a hundred purchasing managers respond this month. Forty say business is **better** than last month, thirty-five say it is **the same**, and twenty-five say it is **worse**. Plug those into the formula: the "better" share contributes its full 40 points, the "same" share contributes half of 35, which is 17.5, and the "worse" share contributes nothing. The diffusion index is 40 + 17.5 = **57.5**. Because 57.5 is above 50, the report reads as **expansion**, and a fairly strong one.

Now notice the two things that make a diffusion index special.

**First, only direction is counted, not magnitude.** A firm that doubled its orders and a firm that nudged them up one percent both vote the same way: "better." A firm whose business cratered and one that softened slightly both vote "worse." This is a feature, not a bug. It makes the index a clean measure of **breadth** — how widely growth is shared across firms — rather than a measure of how *big* the growth is at any one firm. Breadth turns out to be a wonderful leading indicator, because turning points in the cycle show up first as a *shift in how many* firms are improving, before they show up as a shift in *how much* output is produced.

**Second, the index is bounded between 0 and 100 and centered on 50.** If every single firm said "worse," the index would be 0; if every firm said "better," it would be 100. Neither extreme ever happens — even in the deepest recession some firms are growing and some are shrinking in any boom — so the practical band is narrow. That is why a one-point move in a PMI is genuinely meaningful and a three-point move is a big deal. A reader used to stock prices that move several percent a day has to recalibrate: in PMI-land, the difference between 49 and 51 is the difference between a contracting and an expanding sector.

There is a third, subtler property worth internalizing: because the index measures the *share* of firms moving in each direction rather than the *size* of their moves, it is naturally **mean-reverting** and it cannot run away from itself. A booming economy cannot push the index much past the low 60s, because there is a ceiling on how many firms can simultaneously report improvement — you eventually run out of firms that are not already growing. The same is true at the bottom. This is why extreme readings are so informative: a PMI in the low 60s is almost mechanically unsustainable and tends to roll over, while a PMI in the low 40s is almost mechanically a trough that tends to recover. Seasoned traders treat the extremes as *contrarian* setups for the direction trade — not because the economy magically reverses, but because the diffusion math itself bounds how far breadth can stretch before it snaps back.

One more practical note on construction: the ISM applies seasonal adjustment to its sub-indices, and the headline composite is an equal-weighted blend of five of them (new orders, production, employment, supplier deliveries, and inventories). That weighting is why a moving sub-index can drag the headline in a direction the casual reader does not expect — a long supplier-delivery time, for instance, mechanically *lifts* the headline even though it usually signals supply stress rather than booming demand. Knowing which gears turn the headline is part of reading the print like a professional rather than a headline-skimmer.

#### Worked example: turning survey votes into the headline number

Suppose next month the votes shift: only 30% say "better," 40% say "the same," and 30% say "worse." The new diffusion index is 30 + (0.5 x 40) = 30 + 20 = **50.0** exactly — the breakeven. The index fell from 57.5 to 50.0, a drop of 7.5 points, even though a plurality of firms are still doing fine. What changed was **breadth**: the share of firms reporting improvement collapsed from 40% to 30%. In money terms, imagine you run a \$50,000 portfolio tilted toward cyclical stocks that historically lose about 1.5% on a PMI print this weak in a slowing economy: that is roughly −\$750 on the day. The lesson: the headline can swing sharply on a shift in how *many* firms are growing, not how *fast* — read it as a breadth gauge, not a growth-rate gauge.

## The 50 line as a regime signal: read level and direction

A purchasing-managers' index is a two-dimensional read disguised as one number. You read the **level** (above or below 50) for the *regime*, and you read the **direction** (rising or falling) for the *momentum*. Beginners read only the level; the edge is in reading both at once.

![ISM Manufacturing PMI from 2020 to 2025 with the 50 line marking expansion versus contraction](/imgs/blogs/ism-pmi-the-business-surveys-that-lead-2.png)

The level tells you which world you are in. Above 50, more firms are growing than shrinking; the sector is expanding and, all else equal, that is supportive of risk assets, of cyclical earnings, of commodity demand. Below 50, the sector is contracting; that is the territory where the recession trade starts to get pricing power. The 50 line is not a magic switch — an economy at 49.8 and one at 50.2 are essentially identical — but the market treats a clean break of 50 as a **regime label**, because crossing it changes the story analysts tell and the positioning that follows the story.

The direction tells you where the level is heading next, and this is where the real information lives. Consider two readings that have the same headline implication on the surface but opposite trading implications:

- A PMI of **48 that is rising** — say it printed 46, then 47, then 48 — is *still in contraction* but *recovering*. Demand is coming back; the trough is probably in. This is often a **buy-the-cyclicals** setup, because the market pays for the change long before the level gets back above 50.
- A PMI of **52 that is falling** — say it printed 56, then 54, then 52 — is *still in expansion* but *deteriorating*. The momentum is rolling over. This is often a **reduce-risk** setup even though the level still says "expansion."

The trader's compression of all this is a single rule: **a 48 turning up beats a 52 turning down.** The level names the regime; the direction names the next move. Markets, which are discounting machines, frequently pay more for the *change in the rate of change* than for the level itself — which is why a PMI that misses expectations but is clearly bottoming can rally the very assets a naive level-reading would say to sell.

There is a useful third dimension hiding inside "direction," and that is **momentum versus expectations**. A trader reading the print is really comparing three things at once: where the index sits (level), where it sat last month (direction), and where the *market thought it would print* (the consensus). The reaction on the day is driven mostly by the third comparison — the surprise — because the consensus is already in the price. But the durability of the reaction is driven by the first two. A print that beats consensus *and* extends an improving trend is the most powerful combination, because the knee-jerk and the trend point the same way. A print that beats consensus while the underlying trend is still rolling over often produces a sharp pop that fades within a day or two, as the market remembers that one good month does not reverse a deteriorating sequence. Separating the *surprise* (which sets the immediate move) from the *trend* (which sets whether the move sticks) is the difference between trading the headline and trading the data.

It also pays to watch the **rate of change of the rate of change** at the turns. The most valuable moment in the whole cycle is the **inflection**: the month the second derivative flips, when a series that had been falling at an accelerating pace suddenly falls more slowly, or a rising series stops accelerating. Those inflections rarely make the headlines — the level is still ugly or still glowing — but they are where the smart money positions, because by the time the level confirms the turn the easy part of the move is already gone. The diffusion index, being a breadth measure, is unusually good at flagging these inflections early, because breadth turns before magnitude does.

![The PMI scale showing above 50 as expansion below 50 as contraction and rising versus falling as accelerating versus slowing](/imgs/blogs/ism-pmi-the-business-surveys-that-lead-3.png)

#### Worked example: the level says one thing, the direction says another

You are short a \$40,000 basket of cyclical industrial stocks going into a release, betting the slowdown continues. The PMI prints **49.0** — below 50, technically contraction, so your headline thesis looks right. But the prior two prints were 47.2 and 48.1, so this is the *third consecutive rise* and the new-orders sub-index has just popped back above 50. The market reads the **direction**, not your level: cyclicals rally 2.5% on the "trough is in" interpretation. Your short loses 2.5% of \$40,000 = **−\$1,000** despite the index being below 50. The intuition: when level and direction disagree, the market usually trades the direction — fight that at your own cost.

## The survey leads the hard data

Now to the property that makes these surveys worth a trader's attention in the first place: **they lead.** Because a PMI is built from a forward-looking question asked of the people closest to demand, and because it is published before the slow official statistics, it tends to turn a month or two ahead of the hard data it is meant to anticipate. Line the survey up against actual GDP growth and the relationship is unmistakable — the survey bends first.

![ISM Manufacturing PMI plotted against annual real GDP growth showing the survey turning ahead of the hard data](/imgs/blogs/ism-pmi-the-business-surveys-that-lead-4.png)

The mechanism is simple supply-chain physics. A purchasing manager who senses softening demand cuts new orders *this month*. Those cut orders mean less production *next month*, which means lower revenue and shipments *the month after*, which finally shows up in the quarterly GDP number *months later*. The survey catches the decision at the head of that chain; the hard data records the consequence at the tail. By the time GDP confirms a slowdown, the PMI has usually been warning about it for two or three months — and the market, which front-runs the confirmation, has often already moved.

This is why the soft-data-versus-hard-data gap is itself a tradable signal. When surveys roll over while the hard data still looks fine, you are watching the early-warning system flash before the official statistics catch up. The honest caveat: the survey is noisy. It throws *false* signals too — manufacturing PMI has dipped near or below 50 several times in the past two decades without a recession following, because manufacturing is a small, volatile slice of a services-dominated economy. So the survey is an *early* signal, not an *infallible* one. The discipline is to treat a sub-50 manufacturing print as a reason to *check* the services print and the new-orders internals, not as a recession verdict on its own.

There is a feedback loop in the gap worth understanding. When the surveys and the hard data disagree for a stretch, the market is forced to take a side, and which side it takes tells you the prevailing narrative. In a confident, late-cycle market the crowd tends to dismiss soft survey weakness as "just sentiment" and trust the strong hard data — until the hard data finally rolls over and the dismissal looks foolish in hindsight. In a fearful, fragile market the crowd does the opposite, treating a single weak survey as confirmation of the recession it already fears and selling aggressively even as the hard data holds up. The same gap, in other words, gets traded in opposite directions depending on the regime. That is the reaction function at work again: it is never just the number, it is the number filtered through what the market is primed to believe.

It also matters *how* a PMI leads. The survey does not lead by a fixed number of months; the lead time stretches and shrinks with the cycle. Near a sharp turning point — a sudden demand shock — the survey can lead by only a few weeks, because everyone is reacting at once and the hard data catches up fast. In a slow, grinding deceleration the survey can lead by a quarter or more, drifting lower month after month while GDP stays stubbornly positive on the strength of past momentum. A trader who treats the lead as a rigid clock will be early too often; the better mental model is that the survey marks the *change in trajectory* and the hard data confirms the *arrival*, with a variable gap in between that you size your conviction around rather than betting the house on a precise timetable.

#### Worked example: pricing the lead time into a bond position

The manufacturing PMI rolls from 53 to 51 to 49 over three months while GDP, reported with a lag, still shows the prior quarter at +2.3%. You believe the survey is leading and the economy is slowing faster than the bond market has priced, so you buy \$1,000,000 of the 2-year Treasury, betting yields fall as the market pulls forward rate cuts. Over the next two months the hard data confirms the slowdown and the 2-year yield falls 25 basis points. With a 2-year DV01 of roughly \$190 per basis point, a 25bp rally is worth about 25 x \$190 = **+\$4,750** on your position. The intuition: the survey's lead time *is* the trade — you are paid for being early to the confirmation that the slow data eventually delivers.

## Manufacturing vs services: services is most of the economy

Here is the section that separates people who read PMIs well from people who get faked out by them. The headline that hits the wire on the first of the month is the **manufacturing** PMI, and it gets the splashy coverage. But manufacturing is only about a tenth of US output and a similar share of jobs. The economy you actually live in — the one that determines whether the consumer is spending, whether the labor market is holding up, whether a recession is genuinely arriving — is the **services** economy, which is seventy-plus percent of GDP.

![Manufacturing PMI versus services PMI with services shown as the much larger share of the economy](/imgs/blogs/ism-pmi-the-business-surveys-that-lead-5.png)

So why does a small, volatile factory survey move markets at all? Two reasons. First, **it turns early.** Manufacturing is the most cyclical part of the economy, driven hard by the inventory cycle — firms over-order in booms and slash orders in slowdowns, which amplifies and *accelerates* the swings. That makes manufacturing the canary: it dips into contraction before the broad economy does and climbs out before it does. Second, **history and habit.** The ISM Manufacturing series goes back to 1948 with a long, well-studied relationship to the cycle, so traders are conditioned to react to it.

But the trap is this: a sub-50 manufacturing PMI generates recession headlines, and yet if the **services** PMI is still comfortably above 50, the broad economy is almost certainly still growing. The years after the 2021 boom are the textbook case — US manufacturing slid into a long stretch below 50 while services stayed above it and the overall economy kept expanding and adding jobs. Anyone who read the manufacturing print as a recession signal and sold the broad market got run over by an economy that was, in fact, still growing.

The real recession tell is the **services** PMI breaking below 50, ideally alongside a weakening employment sub-index. Services contracting means the consumer and the labor market are cracking, and *that* is the print that gives the recession trade genuine pricing power. The professional read is a two-step: watch manufacturing for the **early turn**, but trust services for whether the **whole economy** is actually expanding or contracting.

A useful way to combine the two is the **composite PMI**, which blends manufacturing and services into one number weighted by their economic shares. Because services dominates the weighting, the composite tracks the broad economy far better than the manufacturing headline alone, and S&P Global publishes a flash composite mid-month for the major economies that gives the earliest read of all. When manufacturing and services are pointing the same way, the composite simply confirms the trend with extra conviction. The interesting and tradable situations are the **divergences** — manufacturing sub-50 while services holds, or, more ominously, services starting to slip toward manufacturing's weakness rather than manufacturing climbing toward services' strength. The direction of convergence is the signal: a manufacturing recession that *spreads* to services is the genuine downturn, while a manufacturing recession that stays contained and eventually recovers toward services is a mid-cycle wobble that the broad market shrugs off.

It is worth being honest about *why* manufacturing is so volatile relative to its size, because that is what makes it both useful and dangerous. Goods demand is lumpy and storable: when sentiment dips, firms and households can simply postpone buying a car, a machine, or a refrigerator, and they can run down inventory instead of reordering. That postponement shows up immediately and violently in the factory survey. Services demand — a haircut, a doctor's visit, a meal, a software subscription — is harder to postpone and impossible to inventory, so it is far smoother. The factory survey therefore *exaggerates* the cycle: it overshoots on the way down and on the way up. That exaggeration is exactly what makes it lead, and exactly what makes a single sub-50 print such a frequent false alarm. The skill is using the loud, early, exaggerated factory signal to *raise your attention* and the quiet, broad, smooth services signal to *make the call*.

#### Worked example: a services beat lifts the broad market

The manufacturing PMI has been stuck at 47 for months and the bears are loud about recession. Then the **services** PMI, expected to slip toward 51, instead prints **54** with a firm new-orders and employment read. The "manufacturing recession is spreading" thesis is wrong: the seventy-percent of the economy that matters is accelerating. The broad market — say your \$30,000 long in a total-market index fund — rallies 1% on the relief that the consumer is holding. That is +1% of \$30,000 = **+\$300** on the day. The intuition: a weak factory survey alone is not a recession; the services survey is the one that decides whether the whole economy is contracting.

## The sub-indices: new orders, prices paid, employment

If the headline PMI tells you the regime, the **sub-indices** tell you what is coming next — and reading them is where amateurs and professionals diverge. The headline is one number on the screen; the survey buries its best forward signals in the internals. Three of them carry most of the information.

![The PMI sub-indices new orders prices paid and employment and what each one foreshadows](/imgs/blogs/ism-pmi-the-business-surveys-that-lead-6.png)

**New orders — the leading sub-index.** Orders are the demand a firm has just won, which becomes the production and revenue of the coming months. New orders is the most forward-looking part of an already-forward-looking survey: it is the leading edge of the leading indicator. The killer signal is a **divergence**: when the headline PMI is holding up but new orders have dropped below 50, the slowdown is already in the pipeline even though the current activity looks fine. Watch new orders relative to inventories, too — orders falling while inventories rise is the classic warning that production cuts are coming, because firms are sitting on goods they cannot sell.

**Prices paid — the inflation tell.** This sub-index measures what firms are paying for their inputs, and it is one of the earliest reads on inflation the market gets each month. Input costs feed into the producer price index and, with a lag, into the consumer price index weeks later. A sudden jump in prices paid is a flashing light that inflation pressure is building in the pipeline — and the bond market reacts immediately, because it implies a stickier inflation path and a tighter central bank. Crucially, the prices-paid signal is *orthogonal* to the growth signal: you can get a weak headline (growth scare) with a hot prices-paid (inflation scare) in the same report, which is the stagflation-lite combination that is genuinely hard to trade because it pushes growth assets and rate-sensitive assets in conflicting directions.

**Employment — the jobs preview.** The employment sub-index captures firms' hiring intentions, which previews the official monthly jobs report (non-farm payrolls, NFP). A soft ISM employment reading a few days before payrolls Friday is an early hint that the jobs report may disappoint, and traders who watch it get a small head start on positioning for a weak (or strong) NFP. It is an imperfect preview — the survey covers a different universe than the payroll count — but it is one more forward-looking shard, and combining the ISM Manufacturing and Services employment sub-indices sharpens the read.

Two more internals deserve a mention because they catch experienced traders off guard. **Supplier deliveries** measures how long it takes suppliers to deliver — and counterintuitively, a *slower* delivery time pushes the index *up*, because slow deliveries usually mean demand is so strong that suppliers cannot keep up. In a normal expansion that is a healthy signal; during the 2021 supply-chain crunch it was the opposite, a sign of broken logistics rather than booming demand, and it artificially inflated the headline. So a rising headline driven mostly by lengthening supplier deliveries should be read with suspicion. **Backlog of orders** is the other one: a growing backlog means firms have more work than they can complete, which supports future production, while a shrinking backlog warns that the order book is thinning out even if current production looks fine. Reading new orders against backlog together gives you a cleaner picture of the demand pipeline than either alone.

The single most powerful internal relationship for spotting a turn is **new orders versus inventories**. When new orders are rising while inventories are falling, firms are selling faster than they can restock, which forces them to ramp production — bullish for the coming months. When new orders are falling while inventories are rising, firms are stuck with goods they cannot move, which forces them to cut production — bearish. Some traders track the new-orders-minus-inventories spread as a leading indicator of the leading indicator, because it captures the inventory cycle that drives so much of manufacturing's volatility. A widening positive spread is an early all-clear; a widening negative spread is an early warning that the factory floor is about to slow regardless of what the current headline says.

#### Worked example: prices paid jumps and the front end reprices

Going into the release, the bond market is comfortable that inflation is fading and the central bank will cut soon. Then the headline PMI is roughly in line, but the **prices-paid** sub-index jumps sharply — input costs are reaccelerating. The market reprices the inflation path: the 2-year yield, which tracks the expected policy path, rises 10 basis points as rate-cut odds get pushed back. You are long \$1,000,000 of the 2-year note. With a DV01 of roughly \$190 per basis point, a +10bp move is 10 x \$190 = **−\$1,900** against you. The intuition: prices paid can move the front end of the curve all by itself, even when the growth headline is unremarkable — read the internals, not just the headline.

## Global PMIs: China as a commodity and EM signal

The PMI family is global, and one foreign survey deserves its own paragraph for traders: **China's manufacturing PMI** (both the official government series and the private Caixin/S&P Global series). China is the **marginal buyer** of much of the world's industrial commodities — copper, iron ore, oil, and the inputs to construction and manufacturing. When the Chinese factory survey slips below 50, it signals that the single largest source of incremental commodity demand is softening, and the price impact ripples outward fast.

The transmission is direct. A sub-50 China manufacturing PMI pressures **copper** (often called "Dr. Copper" for its diagnostic read on global growth), pressures **oil**, and pressures the **commodity currencies** — most of all the **Australian dollar**, which trades almost as a leveraged bet on Chinese demand because so much of Australia's export economy is iron ore and coal sold into China. A weak China PMI is also one of the earliest tells for **emerging-market risk** broadly, because a slowing China drags on the trade partners and commodity exporters across the developing world. So even a US-focused trader watches the China PMI: it is the early signal for the commodity complex and for the global growth impulse that feeds back into everything from cyclical equities to the dollar.

China actually has *two* manufacturing PMIs, and the gap between them is informative. The **official** PMI, published by the National Bureau of Statistics, surveys a larger panel that skews toward big state-owned enterprises. The private **Caixin** PMI (compiled by S&P Global) surveys a smaller, more export-oriented and private-sector panel that skews toward smaller firms. When the two diverge, you are learning something about *which part* of the Chinese economy is moving: a soft Caixin with a firmer official PMI suggests the export and small-business sector is hurting while state-supported activity holds up, and vice versa. Traders watch both, and a sub-50 reading confirmed across both panels is a stronger signal than either alone. Because China's official PMI is released at the very end of the month — often the first major global data point of the new month, hours before anyone else's — it frequently sets the tone for commodities and Asian risk before the Western session even opens.

The euro-area and UK PMIs matter too, mostly through the **currency and rates** channel rather than commodities. A run of sub-50 euro-area composite PMIs weakens the case for the European Central Bank to stay tight, which pressures the euro and can lift the dollar by default. Japan's PMIs feed into the Bank of Japan debate and, through it, into the yen and the global carry trade. The point of the global PMI family is that growth is not a single national story: a coordinated downturn — China, Europe, and the US all rolling over together — is a far more dangerous setup for risk assets than any one country slipping, because it removes the offsetting regions that usually cushion a local slowdown. Lining up the PMIs across the major economies on one screen is how a macro trader takes the temperature of the *global* cycle, not just the domestic one.

#### Worked example: a weak China PMI hits a commodity position

The official China manufacturing PMI prints **49.2**, below 50 and below expectations, confirming the factory sector is contracting. Copper sells off on the demand read and the Australian dollar drops with it. You hold a \$10,000 commodity-exposed position — say a copper-and-base-metals ETF — which falls 3% on the print. That is 3% of \$10,000 = **−\$300** on the day, and if you were also long AUD, that position bleeds in sympathy. The intuition: China's PMI is the world's commodity demand gauge — when it goes sub-50, the copper-and-AUD complex usually feels it before the US data does.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Numbers make this concrete. The ISM Manufacturing PMI's path over the 2020–2025 stretch is a clean tour of every regime a trader will face.

**The COVID crater (April 2020): 41.5.** When the pandemic shut the economy, ISM Manufacturing plunged to **41.5** — deep contraction, more firms shrinking than growing by a wide margin. The market had already crashed in March, so by the time this print landed it confirmed rather than caused the move, but it marked the bottom of the activity cycle. Within months the index was screaming back, a reminder that PMIs are mean-reverting and that the *direction* off a trough is the signal, not the level at the trough.

**The reopening boom (March 2021): 63.7.** As stimulus and reopening collided with constrained supply, ISM Manufacturing ripped to **63.7** — one of the hottest prints in the series' history. More than that, the **prices-paid** sub-index was on fire, which in hindsight was an early, ignored warning of the inflation wave that would dominate the next two years. A trader reading the internals saw the inflation tell in the survey months before the consumer price index made it undeniable. This is the survey's lead property in its purest form.

**The slow roll-over (June 2022): 53.0.** By mid-2022 the index had cooled to **53.0** — still expansion, but clearly decelerating from the boom. This is the "52 turning down" regime: the level still said growth, but the direction said the momentum was rolling over, and the market was already rotating out of cyclicals as the Federal Reserve hiked aggressively into the slowdown.

**The manufacturing recession (2022–2023): sub-50 for over a year.** Then ISM Manufacturing fell below 50 and *stayed* there for an unusually long stretch — bottoming around **46** — the longest sub-50 run in years. And here is the lesson that pays for the whole post: **there was no broad US recession.** Services stayed above 50, the labor market kept adding jobs, and the overall economy kept growing. The manufacturing survey was in a genuine recession; the economy was not. Traders who read the sub-50 factory print as a recession signal and stayed bearish on the broad market missed a substantial equity rally. The factory survey was the early turn; the services survey was the truth.

The episode is worth sitting with because it is the cleanest real-world refutation of the most common PMI mistake. Month after month the manufacturing headline printed in the mid-to-high 40s, the recession headlines wrote themselves, and the permabears pointed at the chart as proof. And month after month the consumer kept spending, services kept expanding, and payrolls kept growing — because the part of the economy that was contracting was the small, cyclical, goods-producing slice, while the seventy-percent services majority was fine. The pandemic had pulled forward an enormous amount of goods buying (everyone bought a desk, a bike, a sofa) and then demand rotated back toward services (travel, dining, experiences), which is exactly the kind of demand the goods-heavy manufacturing survey is worst at capturing. A trader who understood the survey's structural blind spot saw the sub-50 manufacturing prints for what they were — a sector rotation, not a recession — and stayed constructive while the headline-readers stayed scared.

The cross-asset pattern across these episodes is consistent. When a sub-50 manufacturing print lands in a **growth-scare regime** — the market already nervous about a slowdown — it hits the cyclical and commodity complex hardest: industrials, materials, banks and small caps underperform defensives; copper and oil soften; the commodity currencies fall; bonds get bid as rate-cut odds rise; gold and defensives hold up. The same number in a **goldilocks regime** — where a slight cooling is read as relief that the central bank can stop hiking — can do the opposite and rally stocks. As always, **the reaction function decides the sign**: the regime you are in determines whether a soft survey is bad news or good news.

The same regime logic explains why the prices-paid component was the most important number in the 2021 boom even though the growth headline was the eye-catcher. In an **inflation regime**, the market's dominant fear is that the central bank will be forced to hike harder and longer, so the input-cost read in the survey carries more weight than the growth read. A hot prices-paid print in 2021 was unambiguously bad for both bonds and the more speculative end of the equity market, because it pulled forward the tightening that eventually arrived in 2022. By 2023 and 2024, with inflation cooling, the market's fear had rotated back toward growth, so a *cooling* prices-paid print became good news — it cleared the runway for rate cuts. Same sub-index, opposite trade, because the regime had flipped. The discipline is to ask, before every release, which axis the market is most afraid of right now — growth or inflation — and read the matching part of the survey first.

Vietnam offers a smaller-scale version of the same dynamic for local traders. S&P Global publishes a Vietnam manufacturing PMI, and as an export-driven, manufacturing-heavy economy, Vietnam's print is unusually sensitive to global goods demand — it tends to weaken when the US and European PMIs roll over, because Vietnamese factories are filling those foreign order books. A sub-50 Vietnam PMI alongside soft US and China prints is an early warning for the export cycle that feeds the VN-Index's industrial and export names, and it interacts with foreign-flow dynamics: a global growth scare that shows up in the PMIs often coincides with foreign investors trimming emerging-market and frontier exposure, which pressures the index through the flow channel as much as through the earnings channel. The lesson generalizes — wherever you trade, find your local PMI and read it against the global ones, because no economy's cycle is an island.

#### Worked example: a sub-50 ISM in a growth scare

The market is in a growth scare — three weeks of recession chatter, cyclicals already wobbly. ISM Manufacturing was expected to hold 50.5 but prints **49.0**, a clean break below the line. You hold a \$25,000 book tilted to cyclical and small-cap stocks. On a risk-off PMI miss in this regime, that book falls about 2% on the day: 2% of \$25,000 = **−\$500**. Meanwhile a hedge — long the 2-year note — gains as yields fall, partly offsetting the loss. The intuition: in a growth scare, the break of 50 is the trigger, but the *regime* is what turns a half-point miss into a real move in your cyclical exposure; size the surprise risk before the print, not after.

## Common misconceptions

**"A sub-50 PMI means recession."** This is the big one, and it is wrong as stated. The print that goes sub-50 and grabs the headlines is usually **manufacturing**, which is only about a tenth of the economy. The 2022–2023 stretch is the proof: ISM Manufacturing sat below 50 for over a year, bottoming near 46, with **no broad recession** — because services stayed above 50 and the labor market kept growing. The corrected rule: a sub-50 *manufacturing* PMI is a yellow flag worth checking; a sub-50 *services* PMI, especially with weak employment internals, is the print that actually signals broad contraction.

**"Higher is always better."** No — you must read level *and* direction. A PMI of 58 that has fallen from 62 is **decelerating** and often bearish for the assets that were pricing the boom; a PMI of 48 that has risen from 44 is **recovering** and often bullish even though it is still in contraction. A 48 turning up beats a 52 turning down. The level names the regime; the change names the trade.

**"The headline is the whole story."** The headline is a weighted composite that hides the most useful signals. A headline that holds at 51 while **new orders** quietly drop to 47 is telling you the slowdown is already in the pipeline. A roughly in-line headline with a **prices-paid** jump of several points can move the 2-year yield 10 basis points all by itself. Read the internals.

**"PMI is hard data, so it is precise."** PMI is **soft data** — a survey of opinion about direction, not a measured count of output. That is the source of both its strength (it is fast and forward-looking) and its weakness (it is noisy and throws false signals). Manufacturing PMI has dipped near or below 50 several times in the past two decades without a recession following. Treat it as an early warning to investigate, not a verdict.

**"Only the US ISM matters."** For commodity and EM traders, **China's** manufacturing PMI is often the more important number on the day. China is the marginal buyer of industrial commodities, so a sub-50 China print pressures copper, oil and the Australian dollar — sometimes more than a US print does. A US-centric trader who ignores the China PMI is blind to the global growth impulse that feeds back into cyclicals and the dollar.

**"ISM and S&P Global PMI are the same number, so I only need one."** They are different surveys with different panels and have, at times, told genuinely different stories about US manufacturing in the same month — one printing above 50 while the other prints below. When they disagree, the honest answer is that the truth is somewhere in between and the signal is weaker than either alone suggests. The professional habit is to look at both, and to put more weight on the *direction they agree on* than on the exact level of either. Treating one survey's headline as gospel and ignoring a contradicting survey is how you end up over-confident in a number that the other panel is quietly disputing.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Pull it together into a checklist you can run in the seconds after the number crosses the wire. Read it in this order.

![The trader four step PMI checklist level direction services over manufacturing and prices paid](/imgs/blogs/ism-pmi-the-business-surveys-that-lead-7.png)

**1. Level versus 50 — the regime.** Where does the print sit relative to the line? Above 50 is expansion, below is contraction. The single most important event is a *clean break through 50* in either direction, because that is the moment the narrative and the positioning flip. Note the consensus going in: the market has already priced the *expected* level, so only the **surprise** (actual minus consensus) moves the tape on release.

**2. Direction — the momentum.** Compare to last month and the three-month trend. Is this print accelerating or decelerating? A rising series toward 50 from below is a recovery story; a falling series toward 50 from above is a deterioration story. When level and direction disagree, the market usually trades the **direction**.

**3. Services over manufacturing — the breadth.** Do not let a weak factory print fool you. Check the services PMI: if services is comfortably above 50, the broad economy is still growing regardless of the manufacturing headline. Reserve your recession trade for a sub-50 *services* print with weak employment internals.

**4. Prices paid — the inflation tell.** This is a separate axis. A jump in prices paid lifts 2-year yields and the dollar and pushes rate-cut odds out, even if the growth headline is unremarkable. A drop in prices paid eases the rate path and supports the rate-cut trade. In a stagflation-lite report — weak growth, hot prices — the growth and inflation signals conflict, and you size down because the cross-asset reaction is muddled.

**The cross-asset map.** A **beat in a growth scare** is risk-on: cyclicals, small caps, copper and the commodity currencies rally; the recession trade unwinds. A **sub-50 miss in a growth scare** is risk-off: cyclicals and copper fall, bonds get bid, defensives and gold outperform. **Hot prices paid** is its own trade: 2-year yields up, dollar up, rate-cut odds down. And do not skip **China's PMI**: a sub-50 China manufacturing print pressures copper, oil and the Australian dollar and is an early EM-risk tell.

**Sizing and timing.** ISM is a **scheduled monthly print**, so you position *before* the release for the regime you are in and size your exposure for the surprise risk around the number. The first move after the release is the **knee-jerk**; whether it becomes a trend depends on the *direction* of the series, not the single data point. A one-off print that contradicts a clear three-month trend is often faded; a print that confirms and extends the trend is often the start of a move. As with every event, respect the reaction function: the same sub-50 number is risk-off in a growth scare and a relief rally in a regime where a cooling economy means the central bank can finally pause.

A few risk rules keep you out of trouble around the print. First, because PMI surprises move several assets at once and in correlated ways, do not stack the same view across cyclicals, copper, and the Australian dollar and call it diversified — it is one bet in three costumes, and a single bad print can hit all three. Size the *theme*, not each leg separately. Second, the front-end bond reaction to a prices-paid shock is fast and can overshoot, so if your trade is a rates trade, decide your invalidation level in basis points before the release and respect it; a +10bp move you were willing to ride can become a +25bp move that breaks your risk budget if the survey reprices the whole rate path. Third, the surveys are most tradable when the calendar is otherwise quiet — a PMI that lands in the same week as a central-bank meeting or a major inflation print will be drowned out, so weight your conviction toward the prints that have the tape to themselves. Finally, remember that the survey's job is to be *early*, which means it will sometimes be early and *wrong*; budget your position so that a false signal is a paper cut, not a wound, and let the trend confirmation tell you when to press.

#### Worked example: building the trade from the four reads

Run the checklist on a live print. Consensus for ISM Manufacturing is 50.0; it prints **48.5** (a downside surprise), the prior two months were 51.0 and 49.5 (**falling** direction), services that week printed a soft **50.5** (broad economy barely growing), and **prices paid** ticked *down* (no inflation worry). Reads one through three say risk-off and slowing; read four says the bond market can rally without an inflation block. So you trim a \$20,000 cyclical long — taking a roughly 2% hit, −\$400 — and add a \$1,000,000 long in the 2-year, which gains about +\$190 per basis point as yields fall 8bp, for about **+\$1,520**. Net, the bond leg more than covers the equity loss. The intuition: the four reads do not just predict the direction — they tell you *which asset* expresses the view most cleanly, and here the soft growth with no inflation block made the front end the better trade.
