> **TL;DR** — China is the world's biggest commodity buyer and a deflationary force, so a soft China PMI or a negative China CPI print sinks copper, the Australian dollar, and emerging markets — and hits Vietnam directly through trade and the dong.
>
> - The events to watch: China CPI/PPI, the manufacturing PMI, credit / total social financing (TSF), quarterly GDP, and the People's Bank of China's LPR (loan prime rate) and RRR (reserve requirement ratio).
> - The cross-asset map: weak China data → lower copper/iron ore/oil → weaker AUD and EM FX → pressure on the VND and Vietnamese exporters → lower VN-Index. A *side-channel* runs the other way: Chinese overcapacity exports cheap goods, which *cools* Western inflation.
> - The trade: read three signals together — PMI direction, the credit impulse, and the daily yuan fix — and lean long or short the China-beta basket (copper, AUD, EM, VN) accordingly. The sign flips when stimulus actually shows up in the credit data, not when it's merely announced.
> - The one number to remember: China's CPI went **negative (−0.3%)** in July 2023 — while US CPI was still near 3% on its way down from a 9% peak. Two economies, opposite problems.

In the summer of 2023, the world's two largest economies were fighting opposite wars. In Washington, the Federal Reserve was still wringing its hands over inflation that had peaked at 9.1% the year before and was grinding only slowly back toward target. In Beijing, the People's Bank of China had the reverse nightmare: prices were *falling*. China's consumer price index printed **−0.3% year-over-year in July 2023** — the first outright deflation since the early-pandemic shock. Factory-gate prices (the producer price index, or PPI) had already been negative for months and would stay negative for the better part of two years.

That single divergence — the West too hot, China too cold — is the master key to trading China. When a smart trader in New York sees a soft Chinese manufacturing PMI cross the wires at 9:45 p.m. Eastern (China releases in the morning, Beijing time), the reflex is not to think about Chinese stocks, which most Western desks barely touch. The reflex is to check **copper, the Australian dollar, and the emerging-market complex** — because China is the marginal buyer of the world's industrial metals, the AUD is the most liquid liquid proxy for Chinese demand, and a China growth scare is an EM growth scare. And for anyone trading Vietnam, the chain runs one step further: China is Vietnam's single largest trading partner and the source of most of its imported inputs, so a weaker yuan and a colder Chinese factory floor land squarely on the dong and on HOSE-listed exporters.

This post is about reading that chain. We will build the toolkit from zero — what the PBoC actually controls and why it is so different from the Fed, what each China data release measures and how to read it, why China's recent problem has been *deflation* rather than inflation, and how that one fact ripples out through commodities, currencies, emerging markets, and Vietnam. Then we will trace the real 2023-24 episodes with numbers, kill a few stubborn misconceptions, and end with a concrete playbook. Let us start with the map.

![China transmission map fanning out to commodities, the Aussie dollar, emerging markets, and Vietnam, with a deflation side-channel](/imgs/blogs/china-pboc-and-the-worlds-second-economy-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one picture. A China data point or PBoC move on the left fans out: into commodities (demand pull), into the Aussie dollar and the EM complex, and — one rung further down the supply chain — into Vietnam. The dashed channel along the bottom is the part most beginners miss: China does not only *pull* commodities up when it grows; it also *pushes* global goods prices down by exporting its overcapacity. Hold both of those in mind at once and you understand why China can be bullish for copper and bearish for Western inflation in the very same year.

## Foundations: how China steers its economy

Before we can trade China, we have to define the institutions and the data, because almost none of it works the way the US system works. If you map Beijing onto Washington one-for-one, you will misread every print.

**The People's Bank of China (the PBoC)** is China's central bank. Like the Fed or the European Central Bank, it sits at the top of the monetary system. Unlike them, it is *not* independent — it operates under the State Council, which is to say under the direct authority of the government and the Communist Party. When you read a PBoC decision, you are reading a *policy of the state*, not the judgment of a politically insulated committee. That matters for trading: the PBoC's job is not narrowly "2% inflation and maximum employment." It is to deliver the growth target the government has set, manage financial stability, and keep the currency where the state wants it. Multiple objectives, one institution.

**The LPR — the Loan Prime Rate.** This is the closest thing China has to a headline policy rate, and it is genuinely odd by Western standards. The LPR is the rate at which the best-rated corporate borrowers can get loans, published monthly in two tenors: a 1-year LPR (the benchmark for most business loans) and a 5-year LPR (the benchmark for mortgages). It is set as a quoted average submitted by a panel of large banks, but in practice it tracks the PBoC's own medium-term lending facility rate, so a cut in the LPR is a deliberate easing signal. When you read "China cut the 5-year LPR by 10 basis points," translate it as "Beijing just made mortgages cheaper to try to stabilise the property market."

**The RRR — the Reserve Requirement Ratio.** This is the share of deposits that banks must hold in reserve rather than lend out. Cut the RRR and you free up hundreds of billions of yuan for banks to lend — it is a *quantity* lever, not a price lever. The Fed essentially stopped using reserve requirements as an active tool decades ago; the PBoC uses RRR cuts as a regular, headline-grabbing easing move. A 25-basis-point RRR cut can release on the order of 500 billion yuan of liquidity into the banking system. So when China eases, you watch *two* dials: the LPR (price of credit) and the RRR (quantity of credit).

**Total social financing (TSF) and the credit impulse.** Because the PBoC steers credit volume so directly, the most important monthly number out of China is arguably not a price at all — it is *how much new credit the economy created.* Total social financing is China's broad measure of all new financing to the real economy: bank loans, corporate bonds, shadow-banking credit, government bonds, everything. The *credit impulse* is the change in the flow of new credit relative to GDP — essentially, is the firehose of new lending accelerating or stalling? The credit impulse has historically led Chinese activity (and global commodity demand) by six to nine months, which is why it is one of the most-watched leading indicators on macro desks.

**The managed yuan.** The Chinese yuan (the renminbi, RMB; the offshore-traded version is "CNH," the onshore is "CNY") is *not* a free-floating currency. Each morning the PBoC publishes a "fixing" — a reference rate against the US dollar — and the market is then allowed to trade within a band of roughly ±2% around that fix for the day. By nudging the daily fix, the PBoC sends a powerful signal: a weaker fix says "we are comfortable with a softer yuan" (often an easing or pro-export signal), while a stronger-than-expected fix says "we are defending the currency." Because so many Asian currencies — including the Vietnamese dong — trade in the yuan's gravitational field, the fix is a market event in its own right.

**China CPI, PPI, and the PMI.** China's National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) publishes the consumer price index (CPI, the cost of a consumer basket) and the producer price index (PPI, factory-gate prices) monthly. The PPI is especially important for the rest of the world because it is the leading edge of *exported* deflation: when Chinese factory-gate prices fall, the price of the goods China ships abroad tends to fall too. The **PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index)** is a monthly survey of factory managers; a reading above 50 signals expansion, below 50 signals contraction. China actually has *two* manufacturing PMIs — the official NBS PMI (which skews toward large, state-owned firms) and the private Caixin PMI (which skews toward smaller, more export-oriented firms). When they disagree, the disagreement itself is information.

**GDP targets and the property drag.** China sets an annual GDP growth *target* — "around 5%" in recent years — and then deploys policy to hit it. This is the opposite of the West, where GDP is an outcome, not a goal. The big structural story behind the 2023-24 deflation is the **property sector**: real estate and its supply chains had grown to roughly a quarter of Chinese GDP, fuelled by debt, and when the property bubble deflated (the collapse of developers like Evergrande and Country Garden), it took household confidence, construction demand, and a huge slug of commodity demand down with it. A property-led slowdown is *disinflationary* — it destroys demand for steel, copper, and cement — which is precisely why China's recent problem has been falling prices rather than rising ones.

With the vocabulary in place, let us go deep on each piece, starting with the central bank that does not look like any other.

## 1. The PBoC toolkit: why it is not the Fed

The single biggest mistake Western traders make with China is to wait for "the rate decision" the way they wait for the FOMC. China does not work like that. The Fed turns essentially *one* dial — the federal funds rate, the price of overnight money — and lets the market figure out the rest. The PBoC pulls several overlapping levers at once, and crucially, it controls the *quantity* of credit, not just its price.

![Side-by-side comparison of the Fed's single policy-rate dial versus the PBoC's four levers](/imgs/blogs/china-pboc-and-the-worlds-second-economy-3.png)

The contrast in the figure is the heart of it. On the left, the Fed: one main dial (the fed funds rate), with quantitative easing and tightening reserved for crises, and after that the market and the banks do what they like with free-floating dollars. On the right, the PBoC: the LPR (price of credit), the RRR (quantity of credit), the managed yuan (the exchange rate), and *window guidance* — direct, often informal instructions to the big state-owned banks about who to lend to and how much. Because the PBoC controls both the price and the volume of credit, *and* the currency, reading China means watching quantities — RRR cuts, TSF, the credit impulse — as much as the headline rate.

Why does this matter for the reaction? Because the transmission is slower and chunkier. When the Fed cuts 25 basis points, the entire US yield curve and the dollar reprice within milliseconds, because everything is market-priced. When the PBoC cuts the RRR by 25 basis points, the *effect* shows up weeks later in the loan data — and the market often shrugs on the announcement because it has learned that an announced easing is not the same as actual credit hitting the economy. This is the trap that snared so many commodity bulls in 2023-24: every few weeks Beijing announced *something*, the headlines screamed "China stimulus," copper popped — and then the credit impulse data came out flat, and the pop faded.

The second reason this matters is the currency. Because the yuan is managed, a PBoC that wants to ease faces a constraint the Fed never does: if it cuts rates aggressively while the Fed is holding rates high, capital wants to flee China for higher US yields, and the yuan comes under devaluation pressure. So the PBoC eases *cautiously*, often leaning on RRR cuts and window guidance (which do not widen the rate gap with the US) rather than big LPR cuts (which do). For a trader, the daily yuan fix is the tell: a string of weaker fixes says the PBoC is choosing growth over the currency, which is risk-on for commodities but a warning shot for every Asian currency that competes with China — the dong included.

There is a layer of plumbing worth knowing, because it is where the PBoC actually injects money. Two tools sit underneath the LPR. The **medium-term lending facility (the MLF)** is the PBoC's main channel for lending one-year money to the big banks at a rate it sets; for years the MLF rate was the de facto anchor that the LPR tracked, so an MLF rate cut was the real easing signal and the LPR cut that followed a few days later was the echo. The **open-market operations (OMOs)** — chiefly seven-day reverse repos, where the PBoC lends short-term cash against collateral — are how it manages day-to-day liquidity, and the seven-day reverse-repo rate has been increasingly emphasised as the *primary* policy rate in the PBoC's ongoing reforms. The practical upshot for a trader is that China is mid-transition between a quantity-led framework (RRR, MLF volumes, window guidance) and a more conventional price-led one (a single short-term policy rate), and during that transition you have to watch *more* instruments, not fewer, to know whether Beijing is easing or tightening.

The third reason the PBoC is different: it does not telegraph. The Fed gives you a calendar of eight scheduled meetings a year, a published statement, a press conference, and a dot plot of where each official sees rates going. The PBoC gives you a monthly LPR fixing on a set date and otherwise moves when it decides to — an RRR cut can drop on a Friday evening with little warning, and "window guidance" by definition never appears on a calendar at all. So you cannot trade China policy the way you trade an FOMC meeting, by buying volatility into a known event time. You trade China policy by reading the *conditions* — a weak credit impulse, a sliding PMI, a property market in distress — that make easing likely, and by watching the daily fix and the state-bank lending behaviour for confirmation that it has begun.

#### Worked example: pricing the slow transmission of an RRR cut

Say you run a \$50,000 position in a basket of copper miners, and the PBoC announces a 25bp RRR cut on a Friday. The headline reads "China unleashes ~500 billion yuan of liquidity." Your basket spikes +3% on the open Monday, taking your position to \$51,500 — a \$1,500 paper gain. But the *credit impulse* is what actually drives copper demand, and it shows up only when the freed-up reserves turn into new loans, six to nine months out. Two weeks later the monthly TSF print lands soft — new lending barely rose — and the basket gives back the entire move plus a little, settling at \$49,800, a −\$200 loss versus where you started. The lesson in dollars: you got paid \$1,500 for the *announcement* and gave back \$1,700 when the *data* disconfirmed it. Trade the credit impulse, not the press release.

## 2. China's data: CPI, PPI, PMI, credit, and GDP

Now the releases themselves — what each one measures, what the market watches, and how it reacts.

**CPI and PPI: the deflation signal.** China's CPI measures consumer prices; its PPI measures factory-gate (producer) prices. Through 2023-24, both told the deflation story. CPI hovered around zero and dipped negative; PPI was *deeply* negative for over a year. The market reaction function for a soft China CPI is the mirror image of the US: a low-or-negative print is *bad* news, because it confirms weak domestic demand and a stalling economy. A negative China CPI tends to push copper and the AUD *down* and to weigh on EM, because it says "the world's biggest industrial consumer is not consuming." This is the opposite of the US, where in the 2022-23 inflation regime a *low* CPI print was *good* news (it meant the Fed could stop hiking) and rallied everything.

![China CPI versus US CPI year-over-year, with China near and below zero while US spiked to a 40-year high](/imgs/blogs/china-pboc-and-the-worlds-second-economy-2.png)

The chart makes the divergence concrete. The US line (red) blows out to a 9.1% peak in mid-2022 and then grinds down toward the 2% target. The China line (blue) does the opposite: it drifts down from a modest start, kisses the zero line, and dips *below* it in mid-2023 — the −0.3% deflation print. Same calendar, opposite problems. For a global macro trader, this single chart explains why "China data" and "US data" require completely different reaction functions: a cool number is a *buy* signal in the US disinflation regime and a *sell* signal in the China deflation regime.

**The PMI: the timeliest growth read.** Because the PMI is a survey, it lands fast — the official NBS manufacturing PMI prints on the last day of each month, before the hard data. A reading above 50 means factory managers see expansion; below 50 means contraction. Through much of 2023-24 the official PMI oscillated *around* 50 — sometimes a hair above, often below — which is the statistical signature of an economy stuck in low gear. The market watches not just the level but the *direction* (is it rising or falling?) and the *internals* (the new-orders and new-export-orders sub-indices, which lead the headline). A soft PMI hits the same China-beta basket: copper, the AUD, EM equities and FX.

A subtlety that separates a sophisticated China-watcher from a novice: China publishes *two* manufacturing PMIs, and they sample different economies. The official NBS PMI surveys a large panel that skews toward big, state-owned enterprises and heavy industry; the private **Caixin** PMI surveys a smaller, more export-oriented and private-sector-heavy set of firms. When the two diverge — say the official PMI prints 49.5 (state-led heavy industry struggling) while Caixin prints 51.0 (private exporters doing fine) — the *gap* is the signal: it tells you whether the weakness is concentrated in the state-driven, property-linked old economy or in the private, export-driven new economy. Through 2023-24 the official PMI was frequently the weaker of the two, consistent with the property-and-construction drag being the epicentre of the malaise. A trader who reads only the headline that crosses first misses this; reading both, and the sub-indices beneath them, is how you tell a broad slowdown from a sector-specific one.

There is also a timing edge in the PMI calendar. The official PMI lands on the last calendar day of the month and the Caixin manufacturing PMI a day or two later, both *before* the month's hard data (industrial production, retail sales, fixed-asset investment, which arrive mid-month). So the PMI is your earliest monthly read on the direction of Chinese activity — and because it lands in the Asian morning, copper, iron ore and the AUD have already repriced on it by the time the US session opens. The new-export-orders sub-index deserves special attention for anyone trading Vietnam: it is a direct read on whether China's export machine is accelerating, which bears on how fiercely Chinese exporters will compete with Vietnamese ones in the months ahead.

**Credit / total social financing: the leading indicator.** As we said, TSF and the credit impulse lead activity by six to nine months. The reaction here is subtle: a strong TSF print can rally commodities *even when* the contemporaneous activity data is soft, because the market trades the *forward* implication. Conversely, a soft TSF print after a wave of stimulus announcements is the classic "stimulus underwhelmed" disappointment that fades a commodity rally.

**GDP: the scorecard against the target.** China's quarterly GDP is judged against the official "around 5%" target. A print *below* target raises the odds of more stimulus (which can be risk-on for commodities, if the market believes the stimulus will be effective) but also confirms weak demand (risk-off). The net reaction depends on which the market is more focused on — the disappointment or the policy response. In a credible-stimulus regime, a soft GDP print can rally the China-beta basket on stimulus hopes; in a "stimulus-fatigue" regime like 2023-24, the same soft print just confirms the malaise and sells off.

#### Worked example: a soft China PMI hitting a copper position

You hold a \$10,000 position in copper (via a futures-tracking ETF or a miner basket). Overnight, China's official manufacturing PMI prints 49.0 against a 50.1 consensus — a clear miss, back into contraction. Copper is the most China-sensitive liquid commodity, so the knee-jerk is sharp. The basket opens −4% on the China-demand scare. Your position: \$10,000 × (−4%) = **−\$400**, leaving you at \$9,600. If the move extends to −6% by the close as the AUD and EM sell off in sympathy and momentum funds pile on, that is \$10,000 × (−6%) = **−\$600**, leaving \$9,400. The intuition: a single sub-50 PMI is a vote that the marginal buyer of copper is buying less, and copper trades the vote immediately.

## 3. The commodity channel: China as the marginal buyer

Here is the fact that anchors the entire commodity complex: China consumes roughly **half of the world's copper, iron ore, aluminium, and steel**, and is the largest crude-oil importer. It is not the only buyer, but it is the *marginal* buyer — the swing demand that sets the price at the edge. When Chinese construction and manufacturing accelerate, China bids for the last available ton of copper and the price rises; when China slows, that marginal bid disappears and the price falls. This is why copper is nicknamed "the metal with a PhD in economics" — or, more precisely these days, a PhD in the *Chinese* economy. Copper's price is one of the cleanest real-time reads on Chinese industrial demand available anywhere.

![Copper and WTI crude oil prices from 2020 to 2025, both tracking the China demand cycle](/imgs/blogs/china-pboc-and-the-worlds-second-economy-4.png)

The chart shows copper (amber, left axis, dollars per pound) and WTI crude (blue dashed, right axis, dollars per barrel) over 2020-25. Copper rockets from roughly \$2.80/lb in the COVID-trough year of 2020 to \$4.23 in 2021 on post-pandemic reopening and China-stimulus hope — then *stalls* and drifts sideways around \$3.85-\$4.20 through the 2023-24 deflation, exactly when the much-hyped China stimulus failed to translate into real demand. Oil tells a related story, spiking with the 2022 reopening and energy shock and then easing back as global growth — China's especially — disappointed. The flat copper "shelf" of 2023-24 is the price chart of a stimulus that underwhelmed.

The transmission to a trader's screen is direct. China data is released in the Asian morning, hours before US markets open. So a soft China print first hits the commodities that trade nearly around the clock — copper, iron ore (on the Singapore and Dalian exchanges), oil — and the currencies of commodity exporters. By the time New York wakes up, the move is already in the tape, and US-listed miners (the big diversified names) gap to match. The cleanest way to read overnight China risk is therefore to glance at the copper and iron-ore tape and the AUD before the US open: they have already voted.

There is an important nuance. Not all commodities are equally China-sensitive. The *industrial* metals — copper, iron ore, aluminium, zinc — are the purest China plays, because China's demand share is enormous and concentrated in construction and manufacturing. Oil is China-sensitive but more diversified (China is the biggest importer, but US and global demand matter a lot too), so it reacts to China but less violently. Gold is barely a China-growth play at all — it trades on real yields, the dollar, and safe-haven demand, so a China growth scare can even *help* gold (risk-off bid) while it crushes copper. Knowing which commodity is the clean China proxy (copper) and which is muddied (oil) and which is orthogonal (gold) is half the battle.

Iron ore deserves its own paragraph because it is the *most* concentrated China bet of all — even more so than copper. China imports the overwhelming majority of the world's seaborne iron ore (chiefly from Australia and Brazil) and turns it into the steel that builds apartments, bridges, and rail. Because steel demand is dominated by *construction*, and construction is dominated by the property sector, iron ore is effectively a leveraged bet on Chinese property. When Evergrande and Country Garden imploded and new-home starts collapsed, iron ore demand took a direct hit that copper — which has more diversified end-uses across electrification, grids, and electronics — partly escaped. So within the industrial-metals complex there is a hierarchy of China-sensitivity: iron ore (pure construction/property), then copper (construction plus the electrification structural story), then aluminium and zinc. A trader who wants the cleanest expression of "Chinese property is dying" shorts iron ore and the big iron-ore miners; a trader who wants "Chinese industrial demand is soft but electrification is a structural offset" prefers copper.

The around-the-clock nature of these markets is itself a tradeable feature. Because iron-ore futures trade on the Singapore and Dalian exchanges during Asian hours, and copper trades on the LME and Shanghai, the metals tape *is* the live China-demand read while New York sleeps. When a China data point disappoints overnight, the sequence is usually: iron ore and Dalian commodities drop first → copper follows → the AUD weakens → EM FX softens → US-listed miners gap lower at the New York open to catch up. A trader who checks the overnight iron-ore and copper moves before the US bell has, in effect, already seen how the China-beta complex intends to trade that day.

#### Worked example: the Aussie dollar as a China proxy

Australia digs up the iron ore and coal that China's steel mills consume, so the Australian dollar is the most liquid, around-the-clock proxy for Chinese industrial demand — you can trade China through the AUD when the metals markets are thin. Suppose you are short a \$20,000 AUD/USD position (you expect the Aussie to fall on weak China data). China's industrial-production and retail-sales data dump comes out overnight, both missing badly. AUD/USD drops 2% on the China-demand read. Your *short* gains: \$20,000 × 2% = **+\$400**. Had you been *long* the AUD into that data — the wrong side — you would have lost \$20,000 × (−2%) = **−\$400**. The intuition: the AUD is a currency you can trade 24 hours a day, so when China prints weak in the Asian morning, the AUD is often the first and most liquid place the market expresses it.

## 4. The deflation export: China as a global disinflationary force

Now the side-channel — the part that runs *opposite* to the demand story and confuses people the most. China does not only pull commodity prices *up* when it grows. It also pushes consumer-goods prices *down* across the world, because it is the world's factory and it has built far more manufacturing capacity than its own slowing economy can absorb.

![Flow showing Chinese overcapacity driving factory-gate prices down, cheap exports, and global goods disinflation](/imgs/blogs/china-pboc-and-the-worlds-second-economy-5.png)

The flow lays out the mechanism. **Overcapacity**: Chinese factories — especially in electric vehicles, solar panels, steel, and chemicals — were built out for the 8% growth era, and now domestic demand has slowed to around 5% and the property bust has gutted construction. That leaves enormous idle capacity. **PPI falls**: with too much supply chasing too little demand, factory-gate prices go negative and *stay* negative for over two years. **Price war**: firms slash prices to move inventory rather than idle the plant. **Cheap exports**: the surplus output is shipped abroad, often below what Western producers can match. **Global goods disinflation**: importing countries see the price of their *goods* (as opposed to services) fall, which cools their headline CPI. For the Fed and the ECB, fighting their own inflation, this is the "easy button" — China's deflation is quietly doing some of their disinflation work for them.

This is why China can be simultaneously bullish for one trade and bearish for another. Weak China growth → less demand for copper (bearish metals). But weak China growth → overcapacity and cheaper Chinese exports (disinflationary for the West, which is *bullish* for US bonds, because cooler inflation means the Fed can ease sooner). A trader who only knows the commodity-demand channel will be baffled when a string of weak China data coincides with a *rally* in long-dated US Treasuries. The deflation-export channel is the missing link.

There is also a political dimension that a trader must price. When China dumps cheap EVs and solar panels abroad, the importing countries do not just enjoy lower prices — they retaliate with **tariffs**. The EU's tariffs on Chinese EVs and the US's tariffs on Chinese goods are direct consequences of the overcapacity-export dynamic. So the deflation-export channel carries a tail risk: it invites trade barriers, which can abruptly redirect Chinese export flows toward *other* markets — including Vietnam, which can be hit with a flood of cheap Chinese goods that undercut its own producers. The same channel that helps Western consumers can hurt Vietnamese ones.

The deeper engine behind China's deflation is not just factory overcapacity — it is a *demand* shortfall rooted in household behaviour. When the property bust wiped out a huge chunk of Chinese household wealth (most family savings were tied up in apartments), households reacted the way people do after a wealth shock: they cut spending and *saved* more. A rising savings rate in the face of falling confidence is textbook disinflation — too little demand chasing too much supply. China's policymakers leaned on the *supply* side (build more, lend to producers) far more than the *demand* side (transfer cash to households, build a stronger social safety net so people feel safe spending), which is precisely why the stimulus kept failing to lift prices: it added capacity to an economy whose problem was insufficient consumption. For a trader, the tell that China's deflation is *ending* will be a turn in the consumption and household-confidence data — not another supply-side stimulus headline.

This is also why the deflation-export channel has staying power. As long as Chinese households under-consume and the state keeps the factories running, China will keep producing more than it can absorb and shipping the surplus abroad at low prices. That makes "China exports disinflation" a *structural* tailwind for Western goods prices, not a one-quarter blip — and a structural reason the Fed's last mile of disinflation was easier than the inflation hawks feared. The risk to that benign reading is the political backlash: if tariffs wall off the major Western markets, China's surplus has to go somewhere, and the somewhere is often smaller open economies like Vietnam, Thailand, and Mexico — turning a global disinflation gift into a localised competitive shock.

#### Worked example: China deflation as a tailwind for a US bond position

You hold a \$500,000 position in 10-year US Treasuries. China's PPI prints deeply negative again, and a string of soft China data reinforces the "China is exporting disinflation" narrative; the market grows more confident that US goods inflation will keep cooling and the Fed can ease. The 10-year yield falls 9 basis points on the day. The duration of a 10-year Treasury is roughly 8 years, so the price gain is approximately duration × yield change = 8 × 0.09% = **+0.72%**. On \$500,000 that is \$500,000 × 0.72% = **+\$3,600**. The same China weakness that cost the copper trader money just *made* the bond trader money — because the deflation-export channel runs opposite to the demand channel. The intuition: weak China is bearish for things China *buys* (copper) and bullish for things that benefit from cooler Western inflation (long bonds).

## 5. The Vietnam channel: China as Vietnam's biggest partner

For anyone trading the VN-Index, China is not a distant abstraction — it is the country next door, Vietnam's single largest trading partner, and the source of most of the imported inputs that Vietnamese factories turn into exports. So China transmits into Vietnam through several channels at once, and a China event is a Vietnam event.

![Branching flow of how a China slowdown or yuan move hits Vietnamese inputs, exporters, the dong, and the VN-Index](/imgs/blogs/china-pboc-and-the-worlds-second-economy-6.png)

The graph traces the channels. A China slowdown or a yuan devaluation splits into three first-order effects. **Inputs**: Vietnam imports a huge share of its raw materials, components, and machinery from China, so a cheaper yuan makes Vietnamese inputs cheaper (a margin tailwind) but a China slowdown can disrupt the supply chain. **Export rivalry**: Vietnam and China compete in many of the same export categories — textiles, footwear, electronics assembly — so a weaker yuan makes *Chinese* goods cheaper on world markets and Vietnamese goods relatively *less* competitive. **China demand**: Vietnam sells agricultural goods (fruit, rubber, cashews), tourism, and some manufactured goods *to* China, so a China slowdown cuts that demand. These converge on **VND pressure** (the State Bank of Vietnam often lets the dong slide to track the yuan and keep exports competitive) and **squeezed exporters**, which together feed into **the VN-Index and foreign flows** — when the regional risk picture sours, foreign investors sell HOSE.

The currency link deserves emphasis because it is the most direct. The State Bank of Vietnam (the SBV) manages the dong against a basket, but the yuan looms large in that basket because China is the dominant trade partner. When the PBoC lets the yuan weaken, the SBV faces a choice: hold the dong firm and let Vietnamese exporters lose competitiveness to China, or let the dong slide to keep pace. In practice the SBV usually allows a managed depreciation. You can see this in the data: the dong drifted from roughly 22,790 per dollar at end-2021 to about 25,485 by end-2024 — a steady ~12% depreciation over three years, tracking broad dollar strength and the need to stay competitive as the yuan softened. A weaker dong is a tailwind for Vietnamese exporters' reported earnings but a headwind for foreign investors, whose dollar-denominated returns get eaten by the currency slide — which is one reason foreign investors net *sold* roughly 90 trillion dong of Vietnamese stocks in 2024.

The supply-chain story has a brighter side worth naming. The "China + 1" trend — global firms diversifying manufacturing *out* of China to reduce concentration risk — has made Vietnam a major beneficiary, pulling in factories for electronics, textiles, and footwear. So while a China slowdown hurts Vietnam in the short term through the channels above, the structural relocation of supply chains toward Vietnam is a long-run tailwind. The trader's job is to separate the cyclical China-slowdown hit (sell the dong, sell exporters on a bad China print) from the structural China+1 beneficiary story (a multi-year reason to own Vietnamese industrial and logistics names).

There is one more wrinkle that catches foreign investors off guard: the foreign-flow feedback loop. Vietnam is still classified as a *frontier* market by the major index providers (with an aspiration to be upgraded to *emerging* market status), which means a large slice of the foreign money in Vietnamese equities arrives through funds that treat the whole emerging/frontier-Asia complex as one risk bucket. When a bad China print sours sentiment on emerging Asia broadly, those funds de-risk the bucket — and Vietnam, as one of the more liquid frontier names, gets sold alongside China even though its domestic story is independent. This is how a Chinese data miss thousands of kilometres away shows up as net foreign selling on HOSE: not through any direct economic link on that day, but through the portfolio plumbing of funds that lump Vietnam in with the China/EM trade. The ~90 trillion dong of net foreign selling in 2024 was partly this dynamic — a strong dollar and a soft China/EM tape pulling money out of the whole regional bucket, Vietnam included.

The flip side is the upgrade catalyst. If and when Vietnam is reclassified from frontier to emerging market, it would attract a wave of passive and active EM money that currently cannot or does not hold it — a structural inflow that runs *opposite* to the cyclical China-risk-off outflows. So for a Vietnam trader, China is the near-term weather (a bad print means dong pressure and foreign selling) while the index-upgrade path and the China+1 relocation are the long-term climate (multi-year inflows and factory build-out). Confusing the weather for the climate — panic-selling the structural story on a cyclical China scare, or ignoring China risk because the long-term story is good — is the classic Vietnam-investor mistake.

#### Worked example: a yuan devaluation pressuring the VND and a VN-exporter position

You hold a \$8,000 position (in dollar terms) in a Vietnamese exporter listed on HOSE. The PBoC sets a sharply weaker yuan fix, signalling it will let the currency slide to support exports. Two things happen to your dollar-denominated position. First, the stock itself: a weaker yuan makes the company's Chinese competitors cheaper, so the market marks the exporter down 3% on competitiveness fears — that is \$8,000 × (−3%) = **−\$240**. Second, the SBV lets the dong follow the yuan down by 1.5% to stay competitive, and since your position is measured in dollars, the currency move costs you another \$8,000 × (−1.5%) = **−\$120** in translation. Total hit: −\$240 − \$120 = **−\$360**, leaving \$7,640. The intuition: for a foreign holder of a Vietnamese exporter, a yuan move hits you *twice* — once through the stock's competitiveness and once through the dong's depreciation against your home currency.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Theory is cheap; let us walk the actual 2023-24 tape, where the deflation thesis and the "stimulus that underwhelmed" thesis played out in real prices.

**The 2023 deflation scare (July 2023).** In early July 2023, China's June CPI came in at 0.0% year-over-year, and the July print would land at **−0.3%** — outright deflation, the first since 2021. PPI was already running around −5% year-over-year. This was the moment the market's China narrative flipped from "reopening boom" (the post-COVID hope of early 2023) to "structural deflation and a property bust." The cross-asset reaction was textbook for the *demand* channel: industrial metals sagged, copper stuck on its \$3.85-\$4.00 shelf instead of breaking higher, the AUD weakened, and EM equities underperformed developed markets. Meanwhile the *deflation-export* channel quietly supported the disinflation trade in the West — one more reason the market grew confident through late 2023 that US inflation was beaten and the Fed was done hiking.

**The property-crisis drag (2021-2024).** The slow-motion collapse of China's property developers is the structural backdrop to the whole period. Evergrande, the most indebted developer in the world, defaulted in late 2021 and was ordered liquidated by a Hong Kong court in early 2024. Country Garden, once China's largest developer by sales, slid into distress through 2023. Because property and its supply chains had grown to roughly a quarter of Chinese GDP, this was not a sector story — it was a *macro* story. It gutted demand for steel and copper (bearish industrial metals), crushed household confidence (China's consumers started *saving* rather than spending, which is deeply disinflationary), and left local governments — which had funded themselves by selling land to developers — fiscally hamstrung. Every "China stimulus" headline of 2023-24 has to be read against this backdrop: the policy was pushing against a deflating debt-fuelled property bubble, which is like pushing on a string.

**The stimulus that underwhelmed (2023-24).** Through 2023 and into 2024, Beijing rolled out a steady drip of easing — RRR cuts, modest LPR cuts (including a notable 5-year LPR cut to support mortgages), and various "support measures." Each time, the China-beta basket — copper, the AUD, EM equities, the big diversified miners — would pop on the headline, and each time it would fade as the credit-impulse and activity data failed to confirm a real acceleration. The copper "shelf" in the chart above is the visual record of this: a market repeatedly *trying* to price a China recovery and repeatedly being disappointed. The September 2024 stimulus package — a more comprehensive monetary-plus-fiscal effort, with RRR cuts and signals of larger fiscal support — produced the sharpest of these pops, a genuine multi-day rip in Chinese equities and the China-beta complex, before the market again demanded to see the fiscal follow-through.

**The yuan and the dong (2022-2024).** As the Fed hiked aggressively in 2022-23 and the PBoC eased, the rate gap pushed the yuan weaker, and the SBV let the dong follow — the dong moving from ~23,633 per dollar at end-2022 to ~25,485 by end-2024. The SBV had its own drama: in autumn 2022, to defend the dong amid the global dollar surge and a domestic banking scare, it *hiked* its refinancing rate from 4.0% to 6.0% in two moves — and the VN-Index, already battered, bottomed at **911 on November 15, 2022**, roughly 40% below its early-2022 peak near 1,530. As the global dollar pressure eased in 2023, the SBV reversed course, cutting the refinancing rate back to 4.5% across three moves from April to June 2023 to support growth. This is the SBV reacting to the same global forces — the Fed, the dollar, the yuan — that China's events set in motion.

Put the dong's path in dollar terms to feel why foreigners were selling. The dong depreciated from roughly 22,790 per dollar at end-2021 to 25,485 by end-2024 — about a 12% slide over three years. For a foreign investor holding Vietnamese stocks, that currency move is a straight subtraction from dollar returns *before* the stocks even move. The VN-Index itself went from 1,498 at end-2021 to 1,267 at end-2024, a roughly 15% decline *in dong terms*; layer the ~12% currency depreciation on top, and the dollar-terms loss for an unhedged foreign holder was on the order of 25% over the period. That compounding of an index decline and a currency slide — both driven in part by the strong-dollar, soft-China backdrop — is exactly why foreign money kept leaving even when Vietnamese valuations looked cheap on local metrics.

#### Worked example: the dong drag on a foreign holder's VN-Index position

You are a US-based investor with a \$30,000 position tracking the VN-Index, entered at end-2021. Over the next three years the index falls ~15% in dong terms, taking the local-currency value of your holding to roughly \$30,000 × (1 − 0.15) = \$25,500 *if the currency had been flat*. But the dong also depreciated ~12% against the dollar, so the dollar value is further cut: \$25,500 × (1 − 0.12) ≈ **\$22,440**. Your total dollar loss is \$30,000 − \$22,440 = **−\$7,560**, about −25%, even though the index "only" fell 15% locally. The intuition: for an unhedged foreign holder, the dong's managed depreciation is a second, hidden loss stacked on top of whatever the stocks do — which is why China-driven dong weakness shows up as relentless foreign outflows.

#### Worked example: sizing the China-deflation regime trade across the basket

Suppose in mid-2023, reading the deflation print, you put on a coordinated "short China-beta" book: short \$10,000 of copper, short \$20,000 of AUD/USD, and short \$15,000 of an EM-equity ETF — \$45,000 of notional risk expressing one view. Over the following month the deflation narrative deepens: copper falls 5% (+\$500 on the short), the AUD falls 3% (\$20,000 × 3% = +\$600), and the EM ETF falls 4% (\$15,000 × 4% = +\$600). Total: \$500 + \$600 + \$600 = **+\$1,700** on \$45,000 of risk, a +3.8% book return. The intuition: when one macro fact (China deflation) drives several assets the same way, you size *the theme* across correlated expressions rather than betting the whole stake on one — but you also recognise the correlation means a sudden credible-stimulus headline could move all three against you at once.

## Common misconceptions

**"China stimulus always lifts commodities."** This is the costliest myth of the 2023-24 cycle. It *used* to be reliable — the 2008-09 and 2015-16 Chinese stimulus waves were huge, credit-fuelled, and construction-led, and they sent copper and iron ore soaring. But the 2023-24 stimulus was different: it was *cautious* (constrained by the weak yuan and a debt-laden financial system), and crucially it was pushing against a *deflating property bubble* rather than inflating a new one. Announced easing repeatedly failed to show up in the credit-impulse data, and the commodity pops repeatedly faded. The corrected rule: stimulus lifts commodities only when it actually expands credit and construction — watch the credit impulse and the property data, not the press release.

**"A weak China is bad for all assets."** No — it depends entirely on the channel. Weak China is bad for things China *buys* (copper, iron ore, the AUD, EM growth) but can be *good* for things that benefit from the disinflation China exports (US Treasuries, anything that rallies when the Fed can ease). In the worked example above, the same weak China data that cost the copper trader money made the bond trader +\$3,600. Always ask: is this asset on the *demand* channel or the *deflation-export* channel?

**"China's CPI doesn't matter to US markets."** It matters enormously — just indirectly. A US trader who ignores China CPI is missing one of the cleanest forward reads on US *goods* inflation. When Chinese PPI is deeply negative, the price of imported goods into the US tends to fall, cooling US core-goods CPI, which feeds the Fed's reaction function. The 2023-24 US disinflation was helped along by China's exported deflation. China CPI is a US bond-market input, not just a China story.

**"The yuan is a free-floating currency you can trade like the euro."** It is not. The PBoC sets a daily fix and confines trading to a ±2% band, and it will intervene — through the fix, through state banks, through capital controls — to keep the currency where the state wants it. Trading the yuan against the PBoC is trading against an entity with effectively unlimited reserves and the legal power to change the rules. The yuan *fix* is a signal to read, not a market to fight. (Express the China view through liquid proxies — the AUD, copper, EM — not through a leveraged bet on the managed CNH.)

**"China and Vietnam are basically the same trade."** They share a border and a supply chain, but they are *opposite* sides of several trades. A weaker yuan that makes Chinese exports cheaper makes Vietnamese exporters *less* competitive — that is a China-up, Vietnam-down relationship. And the China+1 supply-chain relocation is a structural *transfer* of manufacturing from China *to* Vietnam — long-term, what hurts China's export share can help Vietnam's. Treating them as one beta is how you end up with a portfolio that is accidentally doubled-up on a single regional bet while missing the relative-value trade between them.

## The playbook: how to trade China's impact

Pull it together into an if-then map. The discipline is to read three China signals *together*, not one at a time, and then lean the China-beta basket accordingly.

![Decision flow combining PMI direction, credit impulse, and yuan fixing into a lean on commodities, AUD, EM, and Vietnam](/imgs/blogs/china-pboc-and-the-worlds-second-economy-7.png)

The decision flow is the routine. Three inputs feed the read: **PMI direction** (above or below 50, and rising or falling), the **credit impulse** (is TSF accelerating or stalling?), and the **yuan fixing** (a weaker fix signals easing and pro-export intent; a stronger fix signals defence of the currency). The key question is whether all three point the same way. If China is genuinely re-accelerating — PMI rising above 50, credit impulse turning up, yuan stable — lean *long* the China-beta basket: copper, the AUD, EM equities, Vietnamese exporters, with a stronger dong. If China is weak or deflating — PMI sub-50, credit impulse stalling, yuan being allowed to slide — lean *short* the basket: copper, the AUD, EM; fade Vietnamese bounces; expect the dong under pressure. When the three signals *disagree*, size down — that is the market's way of telling you the regime is in transition.

The concrete rules:

**Watch quantities, not just the rate.** Because the PBoC steers credit volume directly, the LPR/RRR announcement is the *least* informative China event. The credit impulse and TSF — the actual lending data — are what move commodity demand. Position around the data, not the press conference.

**Trade China through liquid proxies, in the right window.** China data lands in the Asian morning, hours before the US open. The cleanest, most liquid ways to express a China view are copper, the AUD, and broad EM — all of which trade around the clock and have already moved by the time New York opens. Use the overnight copper and AUD tape as your read on how the US session will open.

**Separate the demand channel from the deflation-export channel.** Before you put on a trade, decide which channel your asset is on. Copper, AUD, EM, Vietnamese exporters: *demand* channel (long China strength). US Treasuries, Western disinflation trades: *deflation-export* channel (benefit from China weakness). The same China print can be a buy in one book and a sell in the other.

**Fade the announcement, respect the data.** Announced stimulus that is not confirmed by the credit impulse is a fade. A *genuine* turn in the credit impulse, the property data, and the PMI together is a trend — that is when you stop fading the pops and start trading the China recovery as real.

**For Vietnam, split cyclical from structural.** On a bad China print, the cyclical trade is to expect dong pressure and a competitiveness hit to exporters, and to watch foreign flows turn negative. But hold the structural China+1 story separately: the multi-year relocation of supply chains to Vietnam is a reason to own Vietnamese industrial, logistics, and electronics names through the cyclical noise.

**Mind the tail risks.** The deflation-export channel invites tariffs (EU/US barriers on Chinese EVs and goods), which can abruptly redirect Chinese export flows — including toward Vietnam, hurting its producers. And a *disorderly* yuan devaluation (rather than the managed slide) is a global risk-off trigger, because it forces every Asian currency, the dong included, to follow and can spark capital flight. Size for the gap risk around major China policy meetings and the daily fix.

**The invalidation.** This whole framework assumes China stays a *deflationary, demand-disappointing* force. The trade flips when the data shows a real, credit-confirmed re-acceleration — a credit impulse that turns up *and stays up*, a PMI that holds above 50, property prices that stabilise, and CPI that climbs durably back toward 2%. When that happens, the China-beta basket goes from "fade the rallies" to "buy the dips," and the deflation-export tailwind for Western bonds reverses. Watch the credit impulse and the property data for the regime change; they will turn before the headline GDP does.

#### Worked example: position sizing around a China GDP print

You expect a soft China Q-on-Q GDP print and want to express it, but you respect the gap risk that a simultaneous stimulus announcement could whip the basket. You decide to risk 1% of a \$200,000 account — \$2,000 — on a short copper trade. Copper sits at \$4.20/lb; you judge a bad-GDP move at −5% and a stimulus-surprise stop at +4%. To cap the loss at \$2,000 on a +4% adverse move, your position size is \$2,000 ÷ 4% = **\$50,000** of notional copper. If the print is soft as expected and copper falls 5%, you make \$50,000 × 5% = **+\$2,500**; if a surprise stimulus headline lifts copper 4% and stops you out, you lose \$50,000 × 4% = **−\$2,000**, exactly your budgeted 1%. The intuition: around binary China events, size from the *stop* (the stimulus-surprise risk), not from the target — the managed, policy-driven nature of China means a single headline can reverse the basket in seconds.
