It is 4:05 p.m. on a Thursday in late October. The U.S. stock market closed at 4:00 sharp, and the indices ended the day quiet — the S&P 500 barely moved. Then a single mega-cap technology company releases its quarterly results in the after-hours session. The headline numbers look fine: earnings per share beat the consensus estimate, revenue came in a hair above the street. For about ninety seconds the stock ticks up. Then the company's chief financial officer opens the conference call and walks the analysts through *next* quarter's outlook — and the guidance is soft. Cloud growth is decelerating. Capital spending is climbing faster than revenue. The forward number lands below what the most optimistic desks had penciled in.

The stock drops 9% in the after-hours tape. And here is the part that turns a single company's bad night into everyone's problem: this one stock is worth more than 6% of the entire S&P 500. As its after-hours price collapses, S&P 500 index futures — which trade nearly around the clock — start sliding in sympathy. By the time you wake up the next morning, futures are pointing to a sharply lower open for the *whole market*, on the strength of one company's guidance call. Funds that own index ETFs, pension portfolios, the 401(k) of someone who has never heard of this company's cloud segment — all of them are about to feel a single CFO's cautious tone.

This is the puzzle this post exists to answer. Earnings are supposed to be the most *idiosyncratic*, company-specific news there is — the opposite of a macro release like CPI or the jobs report that hits every asset at once. So why does one company's earnings call move the entire index overnight, like a macro event? The answer is **concentration**: when a handful of names make up a third of the index, their earnings stop being single-stock stories and become index-level shocks. And the season's opening act — bank earnings — is read as something even more macro: a real-time scan of credit, lending, capital markets, and the consumer, i.e. the whole economy. By the end of this post you will read the earnings calendar like a macro calendar, understand why mega-cap and bank prints move the tape, know why guidance beats the headline, and see how Vietnam's earnings season swings the VN-Index.

This is a post in *Trading the News: How Markets React to Macro Events*. Most posts in this series take apart a scheduled government release — a CPI report, a Fed decision — and trace how stocks, bonds, the dollar, gold and crypto move on the surprise. Earnings season is different in one respect and identical in another. Different: the "release" is a private company's results, not a government statistic. Identical: the market has already priced a consensus, and only the *surprise* — against both the reported number and, crucially, the forward guidance — moves price. If you have not yet internalized that idea, read [Why news moves markets: the surprise framework](/blog/trading/event-trading/why-news-moves-markets-the-surprise-framework) and [Consensus, expectations and "priced in"](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) first; this post leans on both.

## Foundations: how earnings season works

Before we go deep, we need a shared vocabulary. Every term below gets used throughout the post, so we define each one from zero, with an everyday picture before any math. If you have never read a quarterly report, this section is the load-bearing wall.

### What earnings season is

Publicly traded companies in the United States are required to report their financial results every three months — a **quarter**. The reporting clusters into a few-week window after each quarter ends (the quarters end in March, June, September and December), and that window is called **earnings season**. Four times a year, over roughly five or six weeks, the bulk of the market's companies open their books and tell investors how the last three months went.

Picture a school where every student's report card arrives in the same two weeks, four times a year. Most report cards matter only to that student's family. But a few students are so dominant — the school's star athletes, the ones whose performance the whole town follows — that *their* report cards make the local newspaper and move the mood of the entire school. The stock market works the same way. Most earnings reports move one stock; a few move the whole market.

### EPS: the headline number

The number that gets the headline is **earnings per share**, abbreviated **EPS**. It is the company's profit (net income) for the quarter, divided by the number of shares outstanding. If a company earned \$10 billion of profit and has 10 billion shares, its EPS is \$1.00. EPS is the single figure analysts forecast most intently and the wires splash across the screen the instant results hit.

A crucial fact for a trader: EPS describes the quarter that *just ended*. It is **backward-looking**. By the time it is reported, that quarter is history — the company already lived it. We will see that markets, which price the *future*, often care far more about what management says comes *next* than about the quarter that just closed.

One refinement you will hear in every earnings discussion: companies report two versions of profit. **GAAP** EPS follows the official accounting rules and includes one-off items — restructuring charges, asset write-downs, stock-based compensation, legal settlements. **Adjusted** (or "non-GAAP") EPS strips those out to show the "underlying" business. Analysts mostly forecast and compare against the adjusted number, so when you read "beat by 5 cents," it is almost always adjusted EPS versus adjusted consensus. The gap between GAAP and adjusted can be large and is itself a signal: a company that "beats" on adjusted EPS while bleeding on GAAP is excluding a lot, and skeptical investors notice. For a macro/index trader the distinction matters less than guidance, but it is why two headlines about the same report can disagree on whether it was a beat.

### Consensus, the beat, and the miss

Just as a CPI report has a consensus forecast, every company's EPS has one too. Banks and research firms employ equity analysts who model each company and publish an estimate; the wires (Bloomberg, Refinitiv, FactSet) average those estimates into the **consensus**. When the actual EPS comes in above consensus, the company **beats**; below, it **misses**; bang on, it's **in line**.

> **earnings surprise = actual EPS − consensus EPS**

This is the exact same surprise framework that drives every macro reaction. The market has already priced the consensus into the stock. Only the *surprise* — the gap between the reported number and what was expected — is new information. A beat is a positive surprise on the trailing quarter; a miss is a negative one.

### The whisper number: the bar the stock actually has to clear

Here the story gets subtle, and it is the source of more "wait, it beat — why is it down?" confusion than anything else in markets. There is the *published* consensus, and then there is the **whisper number** — the unofficial, higher (or lower) figure that active traders and the buy side actually expect after a stock has run up into the print. When a stock has rallied 30% into earnings, the market has effectively priced a result *above* the published consensus. To rally further, the company has to beat not the published number but the whisper. Beat the published consensus, miss the whisper, and the stock falls. We return to this repeatedly; it is the key to the "beat but down" puzzle.

### Guidance: the forward number

Most companies, especially in the U.S., don't just report the past quarter — they offer **guidance**: management's own forecast for next quarter's or next year's revenue and earnings. Guidance is the *forward* number, and because markets price the future, guidance frequently matters *more than* the reported EPS. A company can post a great quarter and crater on a weak outlook, or post a soft quarter and rally on a confident one. We will give this its own section, because it is where most of the "irrational" earnings reactions come from.

### After-hours and pre-market: when the reaction happens

U.S. companies almost always report either *after* the 4:00 p.m. ET market close or *before* the 9:30 a.m. open, precisely so the market has digesting time and the reaction doesn't whipsaw the regular session. So the first reaction happens in the **after-hours** (post-close) or **pre-market** (pre-open) session — thinner, more volatile trading where the stock can gap 10% before most people are even watching. Crucially, **index futures** (S&P 500, Nasdaq-100) trade nearly 24 hours, so a mega-cap's after-hours move flows straight into index futures and sets the tone for the next day's open across the whole market.

### The bank-earnings kickoff

By tradition and by their fiscal calendars, the big U.S. banks — **JPMorgan, Wells Fargo, Citigroup**, soon joined by Bank of America, Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley — report *first* each season, roughly two weeks after the quarter ends. That timing makes their results the season's opening read. And because banks lend to, take deposits from, process payments for, and underwrite securities across the entire economy, their numbers are treated as a **scan of the whole economy** — not just four bank stocks. The kickoff sets the macro mood for everything that follows.

### The Magnificent 7 and index concentration

A stock-market index like the S&P 500 is **market-cap weighted**: each company's influence on the index is proportional to its total market value. A \$3-trillion company moves the index far more than a \$30-billion one, even though both are "one of the 500." Over the 2020s, a cluster of mega-cap technology companies — nicknamed the **"Magnificent 7"** (Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Nvidia, Meta and Tesla) — grew so large that together they reached roughly **34%** of the S&P 500 by the end of 2024. Seven names; a third of the index. That concentration is the entire reason a single tech earnings report can behave like a macro event, and we will do the math on it.

It helps to be precise about what "weight" means here. A company's weight in a cap-weighted index is its market value divided by the total market value of every index member. If the whole S&P 500 is worth, say, \$45 trillion and one company is worth \$3.15 trillion, that company's weight is \$3.15T / \$45T = 7%. When a stock's price moves, its market value moves proportionally, and its contribution to the index changes by its weight times its own percentage move. That is the one equation behind everything in this post, and it is why weight — not headlines, not narratives — decides which earnings reports are macro events. A name can be a household brand with millions of customers; if its index weight is 0.1%, its earnings barely register at the index level. A name almost no consumer interacts with directly can be 7% of the index and move the benchmark on a guidance line.

### NVIDIA: the late-cycle macro event

One mega-cap deserves a special mention. Because of its fiscal calendar, **NVIDIA reports later than its mega-cap peers** — typically weeks after the others, near the very end of earnings season. As the dominant supplier of AI chips, its results are read as a referendum on the entire AI-capital-spending wave, so its guidance moves not just its own stock but the whole semiconductor complex and, by weight, the index. NVIDIA earnings have become a scheduled, circled-on-the-calendar macro event in their own right — the season's grand finale.

### Vietnam's earnings calendar

Vietnam's listed companies also report quarterly, with a similar rhythm: most file within about a month of quarter-end, and a full-year audited report follows. The **VN-Index** — the benchmark for the Ho Chi Minh City exchange (HOSE) — is, like the S&P 500, top-heavy, but with a different tilt: **banks and real estate** dominate its weight. So in Vietnam, the prints that move the index are the big bank results (Vietcombank, BIDV, Techcombank and peers) and the big property developers. We devote a full section to how Vietnam's season swings the VN-Index.

With the vocabulary in place, we can now build the central claim: in a concentrated market, a few earnings reports are macro events.

## The earnings calendar: banks first, mega-caps mid, NVDA last

The macro calendar has a fixed cadence — CPI mid-month, jobs first Friday, the Fed eight times a year (covered in [The global economic calendar](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets) and the macro-trading series' [macro calendar](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi)). Earnings season has its own cadence, and once you see it you can mark the same dates on your calendar every quarter.

**Week 1 — the bank kickoff.** Roughly two weeks after the quarter closes, the money-center banks report. This is deliberately early relative to the rest of the market, and it gives traders the first hard read on the economy since the quarter ended. The mood set here — are credit losses rising? is the consumer spending? are deal pipelines alive? — carries into the rest of the season.

**Weeks 2–3 — the broad market.** The bulk of the index reports in a torrent: hundreds of companies across every sector. This is peak volume by company count, but most of these are single-stock stories. A mid-cap industrial beating by a penny moves its own stock and is invisible to the index. The signal-to-noise here is low for an index trader; the action is bottom-up, stock by stock.

**Weeks 3–4 — mega-cap tech, the macro week.** Then the giants report, usually clustered in a single week: Microsoft, Alphabet, Apple, Amazon, Meta. This is the week that matters most for the *index*, because these five alone are a huge share of the S&P 500's weight. A surprise here doesn't move one stock — it moves the benchmark. This is the macro week of earnings season.

**Late — the NVIDIA event.** Weeks after its peers, NVIDIA closes the season. By the time it reports, the market has digested everyone else, so NVDA gets the spotlight to itself, and its read on AI demand sets the tone for the entire technology complex into the next quarter. Because its results land on a quiet night with no competing mega-cap prints, the reaction is undiluted, and the options market routinely prices one of the largest single-stock expected moves of any company in the index into its report. Desks talk about "NVDA night" the way they talk about CPI day — a scheduled, binary risk event that can swing index futures by itself.

Notice the cadence: the season opens with a *macro* read (the banks, scanning the economy), runs through a long middle of *single-stock* noise, then closes with the heaviest *index-weight* prints (mega-cap tech and NVDA). For an index trader, the front and the back of the season carry almost all the systematic risk, and the middle is where stock-pickers earn their keep. Knowing which phase you are in tells you whether to trade the index or to trade names.

The cover figure above lays this calendar out as a timeline and tags which prints move the whole index. The key mental shift is this: **the earnings calendar is a macro calendar in disguise.** A handful of dates — bank kickoff, mega-cap week, NVDA — carry index-level risk, and you can circle them in advance exactly as you'd circle a CPI or FOMC date.

#### Worked example: the cost of being un-hedged on mega-cap week

Say you run a \$50,000 portfolio that simply tracks the S&P 500 through an index ETF, and you are not watching the earnings calendar. Mega-cap week arrives. One name with a 7% index weight reports a weak outlook after the close and falls 10% in after-hours; index futures gap down 0.7% overnight on that alone.

- Index drag from the one stock: 7% weight × (−10%) = **−0.70%** on the index.
- Your portfolio is \$50,000; a −0.70% index move ≈ \$50,000 × 0.0070 = **−\$350** of paper loss, gapped open overnight before you can react.
- If a *second* mega-cap also disappoints the same week — say another 6%-weight name down 8%, adding 6% × (−8%) = −0.48% — the combined drag is about −1.18%, or **−\$590** on the \$50,000.
- Had you known mega-cap week was the macro week, you might have trimmed exposure or bought a cheap index put — a \$120 hedge that caps the \$590 overnight gap is cheap insurance.

The intuition: an index fund is *not* diversified away from earnings risk during mega-cap week — a third of it reports in a few sessions, and the gaps happen overnight when you can't trade.

## Why a mega-cap is a macro event: the concentration math

Here is the heart of the post, and it is just one multiplication. The reason a single mega-cap earnings report behaves like a macro release is that, in a market-cap-weighted index, **a stock's effect on the index equals its weight times its own move.**

![Concentration math showing a 7 percent weight stock down 10 percent dragging the index 0.7 percent](/imgs/blogs/earnings-season-as-a-macro-event-3.png)

Walk through the figure. Take one mega-cap with a 7% index weight. After the close it reports weak guidance and falls 10%. The drag it puts on the index, mechanically, before any other stock so much as ticks, is:

> index move = stock weight × stock move = 7% × (−10%) = **−0.70%**

That is a macro-sized move from one company. For comparison, a hot CPI surprise in the 2022 inflation regime moved the S&P about −4% (see [CPI: the report that moves the world](/blog/trading/event-trading/cpi-the-report-that-moves-the-world)), and an ordinary Fed decision moves it a percent or two. A single mega-cap miss can deliver a meaningful fraction of a CPI day's move — by itself.

Now contrast that with a small company. A 0.02%-weight name (a typical small-cap that just barely makes the index) falling the same 10% contributes 0.02% × (−10%) = **−0.002%** to the index. That is two-thousandths of a percent — completely invisible. The index does not notice. This is precisely *why* most earnings reports are single-stock stories: the math drowns them out.

The difference between the two is the whole point. The mega-cap and the small-cap can have *identical* business news — both down 10% on a guidance miss — and one is a macro event while the other is noise, purely because of weight. Concentration converts idiosyncratic news into systematic risk.

There is a second-order effect that makes mega-cap week even more potent: **correlation through the index itself.** When a 7%-weight name gaps down, the index and the index futures fall, and that drags on *every other stock in the index* through index-tracking flows. Passive funds and ETFs that hold the S&P 500 see net redemptions or rebalancing pressure, and they sell every constituent proportionally — including the 493 companies that did nothing wrong that night. So a single mega-cap miss doesn't only subtract its own weight; it transmits a mild sympathy sell across the whole tape. In a market where passive ownership is enormous, the mechanical plumbing amplifies the concentration math. The reverse is true on a strong mega-cap print: the index gaps up, passive inflows lift everything, and a single guidance beat can float all 500 boats. This is also why the breadth of the market (how many stocks are participating) can be terrible while the index sits at a record high — the index is the seven, and the seven are the index.

A natural question is whether you can sidestep this by holding an **equal-weighted** index instead, where every company is 1/500 ≈ 0.2% regardless of size. You can, and it genuinely dilutes single-name earnings risk: in an equal-weight S&P, a mega-cap down 10% drags the index only 0.2% × (−10%) = −0.02%, a hundredth of the cap-weight drag. The trade-off is that you also give up the upside concentration that drove the cap-weighted index's returns for a decade. Most investors hold the cap-weighted version, knowingly or not, which is exactly why mega-cap earnings nights are an index-level event for the typical portfolio.

### Why concentration grew so extreme

For most of stock-market history, the top handful of companies were maybe 15–18% of the S&P 500. By the end of 2024, the Magnificent 7 had reached **~34%** — close to double the historical norm. This happened because a small group of technology platforms compounded earnings at a far faster rate than the rest of the market for a decade, and a cap-weighted index mechanically lets winners grow their share. The figure below makes the lopsidedness concrete.

![Stacked bar showing the Magnificent 7 at 34 percent of the S&P 500 and the other 493 stocks at 66 percent](/imgs/blogs/earnings-season-as-a-macro-event-2.png)

Seven stocks on one side of the bar, 493 on the other, and the seven take a third of the whole. The trading consequence is direct: **the dispersion of the index has collapsed onto a few earnings calls.** You can no longer say "the S&P is broadly diversified, so no single company can move it much." That was true in 2005. In a 34%-concentration market, the seven mega-cap earnings reports are, collectively, the single largest scheduled risk event of the quarter for an index investor — more than CPI, arguably rivaling the Fed.

#### Worked example: turning a stock move into an index move and into dollars

You hold \$25,000 in an S&P 500 index fund. The largest constituent — call its weight 7% — reports after the close and gaps down 9% on soft AI-cloud guidance.

- Index drag: 7% × (−9%) = **−0.63%**.
- Dollar impact on your fund: \$25,000 × 0.0063 = **−\$157.50**.
- Now suppose instead you held \$25,000 *directly in that one stock* — the move is the full −9%: \$25,000 × 0.09 = **−\$2,250**.
- The index "diluted" the single-stock −\$2,250 down to −\$158 by spreading the 7% weight across your holding. That dilution is real protection — but only because you held the index, not the name.

The intuition: a cap-weighted index passes through a fraction of each stock's move equal to its weight, so a 34%-concentrated index passes through a *large* fraction of just seven companies' earnings nights.

## Bank earnings as an economy scan: credit, NIM, the consumer

The mega-cap math explains why tech prints move the index. Bank earnings move markets for a different reason: not weight (banks are a modest share of the S&P), but *information*. The big banks sit at the center of the economy's plumbing, so their quarterly lines double as a real-time scan of conditions that no government statistic reports this fast.

![Bank earnings scan diagram linking credit losses net interest margin trading revenue and the consumer to an economy-wide read](/imgs/blogs/earnings-season-as-a-macro-event-4.png)

The figure maps the four windows. Read them as macro indicators, because that is how the market reads them.

**Credit losses.** Banks must set aside reserves for loans they expect to go bad — **loan-loss provisions** — and they report **charge-offs** (loans written off) and **delinquencies** (loans falling behind). When a bank *raises* its provisions, it is telling you, in real time, that its credit models see more defaults coming. A surprise jump in provisions is one of the earliest macro warning signs of a slowdown, and it can hit the whole financial sector and risk sentiment broadly. It is, in effect, the bank's private forecast of the economy, released a quarter ahead of the official data.

**Net interest margin (NIM).** A bank earns the spread between what it charges on loans and what it pays on deposits; that spread, scaled, is the **net interest margin**. NIM tells you about the rate environment and about loan demand — is the bank actually lending, and at what spread? Falling loan balances signal that businesses and households are borrowing less, a leading indicator of slowing activity. (For *why* rates set NIM, see [How the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).)

**Trading and investment-banking revenue.** The Wall Street side of a universal bank — trading desks and the deal/IPO underwriting business — is a pulse on capital-markets activity. Strong trading revenue means volatility and volume (good for the banks, often a sign of stress elsewhere); a rebound in IPOs and M&A advisory means corporate confidence is returning. This line reads the *financial* economy.

**The consumer.** Through credit-card spending, deposit balances, and payment rates, the big consumer banks see Main Street's behavior almost in real time. Card spending up and savings buffers intact say the consumer is healthy; rising card balances with falling payment rates say households are stretching. Because consumer spending is roughly two-thirds of U.S. GDP, this is among the most-watched reads of the whole season.

Put together, the bank kickoff is treated as a macro data point: *the market reacts to JPM, WFC and C as if a new economic indicator had been released*, because in a real sense it has. A cautious tone in week one can cap the whole season; a confident one can give it a tailwind.

Why is the bank read often *faster* and *cleaner* than the official government statistics? Because banks see transactions, not surveys. The government's consumer-spending and labor data are collected, aggregated and published with a lag of weeks to months, and revised afterward. A big consumer bank sees its own cardholders' spending in real time, across tens of millions of accounts, and management is describing what they saw through the end of last week on the call. When a bank CEO says "we are seeing the lower-income consumer pull back on discretionary spending," that is a forward-looking, high-frequency read that the official retail-sales report won't confirm for another month. Traders price it immediately. The same goes for credit: a bank's delinquency and provisioning trends lead the official default and bankruptcy statistics, because the bank is the first to see a borrower fall behind.

A few words on how to read the provisioning line specifically, because it is the most macro-loaded number banks report. Under modern accounting, banks reserve for *expected* lifetime losses, not just losses already incurred — so the provision is explicitly a forecast. When a bank builds reserves, it is marking down its own view of the economy; when it *releases* reserves (takes some back into income), it is marking up that view. A quarter where a bank "beats EPS" largely because it *released* reserves is a lower-quality beat than one driven by genuine revenue growth, and sharp analysts discount it. Conversely, a bank that misses EPS purely because it *built* reserves ahead of a feared downturn may be sending a more important macro signal than the headline miss suggests. The provisioning line is where the bank tells you what it really thinks happens next.

#### Worked example: a credit-loss warning hitting a financials position

You hold \$20,000 in a financial-sector ETF. On the season's first morning, the largest bank reports a quarter where EPS technically beat — but it sharply *raised* loan-loss provisions and warned about consumer credit, and on the call management sounded defensive about the next two quarters.

- The "beat" is ignored; the market trades the forward credit warning. The bank's stock falls, and the whole sector follows: say financials drop 3% on the day.
- Dollar impact: \$20,000 × (−0.03) = **−\$600**.
- Note the trailing EPS *beat* did not save it — provisions are a forward statement about the economy, and the market priced that, not last quarter's profit.
- Had the warning instead been *reassuring* (provisions flat, consumer healthy), the same position might have risen 2%: \$20,000 × 0.02 = **+\$400**, and the whole season would open with a risk-on tone.

The intuition: bank earnings are priced as a macro scan, so the *guidance and provisioning* — the forward economic signal — move the sector and the mood, not the backward EPS line.

## Guidance beats the headline: why a stock can fall on a beat

Now to the single most confusing thing about earnings reactions, and the one that separates people who trade earnings from people who get run over by them. **A stock can beat on EPS and revenue and still fall hard.** Newcomers see "Company X beats earnings" on the headline, expect the stock up, and watch it drop 8% — and conclude markets are irrational. They are not. They are doing exactly what they always do: pricing the *future* off the *surprise versus expectations*, and the relevant expectation is the forward one.

![Before and after diagram showing an EPS and revenue beat overridden by weak guidance so the stock falls](/imgs/blogs/earnings-season-as-a-macro-event-5.png)

The figure lays out the trap. On the left, what the *headline* says: EPS beat (\$1.10 reported versus \$1.00 consensus), revenue beat too. The naive read — "a beat means the stock rises" — fires. On the right, what the *market* prices: the company guided *next* quarter below the whisper number, the stock had already run up into the print so the bar was higher than the published consensus, and so the stock falls 8% despite the beat. Guidance won.

Three forces conspire here, and they layer on top of the basic surprise framework from [Consensus, expectations and "priced in"](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in):

1. **The reported quarter is already history.** EPS describes a quarter the company has already lived. Markets discount the future; once the number is out, attention jumps immediately to the outlook. A backward beat with a forward cut is a *net negative* surprise on the thing that matters.

2. **The whisper sets the real bar.** When a stock has rallied into earnings, the run-up *is* the market pricing a result above the published consensus. The published "beat" was already expected and more. Beating the published number while missing the whisper is, functionally, a miss.

3. **Positioning amplifies it.** If everyone is long into the print and leaning on a great quarter, a soft guide triggers a rush for the exits — the "long squeeze" of an over-owned name. The first sellers don't wait to debate valuation; they sell the gap. (For the general mechanic, see [Positioning and the pain trade](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade) and the macro-trading [flows piece](/blog/trading/macro-trading/following-the-flows-positioning-cot-dealer-hedging).)

The professional takeaway is blunt: **on earnings, read the guidance line first and the EPS headline second.** The reaction is the trade, not the headline — exactly the series' core lesson, applied to a corporate print.

It is worth being concrete about *where* guidance lives, because it is rarely in the press-release headline. The reported EPS and revenue hit the wire at the close; the stock often ticks on that for a minute. Then, at the top of the hour, management holds a **conference call** with analysts, opening with prepared remarks (which contain the guidance) and then taking questions. The single most violent part of an earnings reaction frequently comes *during* that call — not on the press release — when the CFO gives the forward outlook or the CEO answers a pointed question about a softening trend. A stock can be up 3% on the headline at 4:05 and down 7% by 5:15 because of one sentence about next quarter's margins. This is why experienced earnings traders don't fire on the press-release number; they wait for the guidance and the tone of the call, because that is where the surprise that actually matters is revealed.

There is also a *regime* dimension to how guidance is punished or rewarded, exactly as there is for macro surprises (the series calls it [the reaction function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently)). In a richly valued, momentum-driven market where the mega-caps have run hard, the bar is brutal: anything short of a strong guide gets sold, because the price already embeds optimism. In a beaten-down, fearful market, the same soft guide might be shrugged off — expectations were already on the floor, so there is little optimism left to disappoint. The identical guidance can be a −8% night in one regime and a non-event in another. Always ask not just "what did they guide?" but "what had the market already priced, and what mood is it in?"

#### Worked example: a beat-but-weak-guidance gap on a single-stock position

You hold \$10,000 in a single mega-cap stock into its earnings. It beats EPS and revenue — but cuts next-quarter revenue guidance below the whisper, and the stock had rallied 20% in the month before the print.

- The market trades the forward cut: the stock gaps down 8% in after-hours.
- Dollar impact: \$10,000 × (−0.08) = **−\$800**, gapped open before the regular session even starts.
- If you had instead owned \$10,000 of the *index* and this stock were 6% of it, your exposure to this single gap would be 6% × (−8%) = −0.48%, or just \$10,000 × 0.0048 = **−\$48**, not the full −8%.
- The lesson in dollars: a concentrated single-stock bet pays the full −\$800; the index holder pays a sliver — about −\$48.

The intuition: the headline beat is a backward-looking fact the market already priced via the run-up; the forward guidance is the new information, and it sets the sign of the move.

## Vietnam: earnings season and the VN-Index

Everything above transfers to Vietnam, with one important twist in *which* sectors carry the index. The VN-Index, like the S&P 500, is concentrated — but its giants are **banks and real-estate developers**, not technology platforms. So Vietnam's earnings season swings the index through the bank and property prints.

![VN-Index year-end levels from 2020 to 2024 as bars colored by yearly direction](/imgs/blogs/earnings-season-as-a-macro-event-6.png)

The chart traces the VN-Index year-end levels: about 1,103 at the end of 2020, a boom to 1,498 in 2021, a brutal drop to 1,007 in 2022 (the index troughed near 911 that November), a recovery to 1,130 in 2023, and 1,267 by the end of 2024. The 2022 collapse is the clearest illustration of how Vietnam's earnings-and-sector machinery works: it was a property-and-credit story. A real-estate and corporate-bond stress event hit developer earnings and the banks exposed to them, and because property and banks dominate the index, the whole VN-Index fell roughly 34% on the year. (For why money rotated out, see [Liquidity and the margin cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)

A few specifics that make Vietnam's season its own animal:

- **Banks lead.** Vietnamese banks (Vietcombank, BIDV, VietinBank, Techcombank, VPBank and peers) are the heaviest single block in the VN-Index. Their quarterly results — credit growth against the SBV's credit ceiling, NIM, and provisioning against real-estate and corporate-bond exposure — are the equivalent of the U.S. bank kickoff, but with even more index weight behind them. A strong bank-earnings season is often what powers a VN-Index rally.

- **Real estate is the swing factor.** Property developers are the other heavy block, and their earnings (and write-downs) are volatile and tightly linked to the bond market and the SBV's credit policy. When developers report well, the index has a tailwind; when they take write-downs, the drag is index-level.

- **Foreign flows interact with the prints.** Foreign investors are a swing buyer/seller on HOSE, and their reaction to earnings amplifies moves. In 2024, foreigners net-sold roughly 90 trillion VND, a persistent headwind even when domestic earnings were decent. How index inclusion and ETF flows move the same names is the subject of [Foreign flows, ETFs and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam), and which sectors lead a recovery is covered in [Sector rotation explained: leaders and laggards](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards).

- **The reporting calendar.** Vietnamese companies generally file quarterly results within about 30 days of quarter-end, with audited full-year numbers later. The clustering is looser than the U.S. but the principle holds: the bank and property prints carry the index, and the rest is mostly single-stock noise.

A second VN-specific lens is the **credit ceiling**. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) hands each bank an annual cap on how much its loan book may grow — the *credit ceiling* — and that quota is the binding constraint on bank earnings. A bank can have strong demand and healthy margins, but if it has used up its credit-growth quota, its earnings growth stalls until the SBV grants more room. So a Vietnamese bank's earnings call is read partly as a read on *policy*: how much credit growth was allowed, how much is left, and whether the SBV is loosening or tightening. This couples Vietnam's earnings season tightly to its monetary policy in a way the U.S. doesn't replicate; the mechanism is laid out in [Vietnam's monetary policy: the State Bank, the dong and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling). When the SBV cut its policy rate three times in 2023 (from a refinancing rate of 6.0% back to 4.5%), it eased the pressure on both bank funding costs and property developers' debt loads — and the VN-Index recovered from its 2022 trough partly on that earnings relief.

A third feature is the role of **retail and margin**. Vietnam's market is far more retail-driven than the U.S., and domestic individual investors trade heavily on margin. That makes earnings reactions sharper and more prone to overshoot: a strong bank print can trigger a margin-fueled chase, and a property write-down can trigger forced margin selling that cascades through the index. The margin cycle's amplifying effect is exactly the dynamic covered in [Liquidity and the margin cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam) — earnings provide the spark, leverage provides the accelerant.

#### Worked example: a strong VN bank print lifting a position

You hold the equivalent of \$8,000 in a basket of Vietnamese bank stocks. A bellwether bank reports strong credit growth within its SBV ceiling, an expanding NIM, and falling provisions — a clean macro signal that credit stress is easing.

- The bank's stock and its peers rally; say the \$8,000 basket rises 5% on the print and the follow-through.
- Dollar impact: \$8,000 × 0.05 = **+\$400**, lifting the basket to \$8,400.
- Because banks are the heaviest VN-Index block, the same print also lifts the index itself — a single-sector print behaving like a macro event, exactly as a mega-cap does in the U.S.
- If a major *property* developer had instead announced large write-downs the same week, the property block could drop 6%, and an \$8,000 property position would lose \$8,000 × 0.06 = **−\$480**, dragging the index the other way.

The intuition: in Vietnam the concentration tilt is banks and property, so *their* earnings are the macro-event prints, just as mega-cap tech is in the U.S.

## How it reacted: real episodes

The series rule is to ground every claim in real, dated market behavior. Two patterns recur every earnings season, and they map cleanly onto the mechanics above.

### A mega-cap guidance gap moving index futures overnight

The defining modern earnings episode is the recurring one in the hook: a single mega-cap reports after the close, beats the headline, then guides softly — and S&P 500 and Nasdaq-100 futures slide overnight, gapping the whole market lower at the open. This has played out repeatedly across the Magnificent 7 era. A cloud-growth deceleration at one of the hyperscalers, a soft iPhone or services guide, a capital-spending number that climbs faster than revenue — any of these, on a name worth 6–7% of the index, translates mechanically into a multi-tenths-of-a-percent index gap before the cash market even opens.

The reaction has a characteristic shape that maps directly onto [the anatomy of a news reaction: spike, fade, trend](/blog/trading/event-trading/anatomy-of-a-news-reaction-spike-fade-trend). The **knee-jerk** is the after-hours gap as algorithms and fast money trade the guidance line. Then comes the **fade or trend** decision in the next session: if the guidance scare was idiosyncratic to one company, the rest of the market often shrugs and the index recovers; if the soft guide is read as a *signal* about the whole sector (all the hyperscalers are slowing AI capex), the move trends and pulls the other mega-caps with it. The single-stock-versus-macro distinction *is* the fade-versus-trend call.

#### Worked example: sizing an index lean off a mega-cap gap

Index futures are pointing to a −0.7% open after one mega-cap's overnight guidance gap. You run \$40,000 and want to express a view that the scare is idiosyncratic and the index will fade the gap (recover) during the cash session.

- If you buy the dip with \$40,000 of index exposure at the gapped-down open and the index recovers 0.5% intraday, you make \$40,000 × 0.005 = **+\$200**.
- If you are wrong and the soft guide trends — the other mega-caps follow and the index falls another 1% — you lose \$40,000 × 0.01 = **−\$400**.
- A disciplined plan caps the loss: set the invalidation at the session low and size so a −1% adverse move is a tolerable −\$400, not a −\$2,000 catastrophe.

The intuition: trading the gap is a bet on *single-stock versus macro* — fade if the miss is one company's problem, respect the trend if it is the whole sector's.

### The bank-earnings kickoff setting the season's tone

The second recurring episode is the bank kickoff coloring the entire season. When the money-center banks open the season with rising loan-loss provisions and cautious commentary on the consumer, the financial sector sells off and the macro mood turns defensive — risk-off bleeds into the broad tape even before mega-cap week. When the banks instead report resilient credit, healthy consumer spending, and a reviving deal pipeline, the season opens with a tailwind and the market is primed to reward the rest of the reports. The kickoff is the season's weather forecast.

This is why a bank "beat" with a *raised provision* can still sink the sector: the market is reading the provisioning as a forward macro warning, exactly as it reads a hot CPI surprise as a forward inflation warning. The asset that reacts is not just the four banks — it is the whole financial sector, and via sentiment, risk assets broadly. How that risk-on/risk-off mood propagates across assets is the subject of [Risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates).

## Common misconceptions

**"A beat means the stock rises."** The single most expensive myth. A beat on the *trailing* quarter is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. The stock prices the *forward* guidance against the *whisper*, after accounting for how far it ran into the print. Beat the published consensus, miss the whisper or guide softly, and the stock falls — an 8% drop on a "beat" is routine, as our \$10,000 → −\$800 example showed. Read the guidance line before you cheer the headline.

**"Earnings are a single-stock story, so they don't move the index."** True for a small-cap; catastrophically false for a mega-cap. The index move equals weight times the stock's move, and at 34% concentration a few names carry the index. A 7%-weight stock down 10% is −0.70% on the S&P before anything else trades — a macro-sized move from one company.

**"Bank earnings only matter to bank investors."** Banks are a modest *weight* in the S&P, but they carry outsized *information*. Their provisions, NIM and consumer data are read as a real-time economy scan, so a cautious kickoff can cap a whole season and drag risk sentiment well beyond the financial sector.

**"NVIDIA is just one tech stock."** As the AI-capex bellwether, NVDA's guidance is read as a referendum on the entire AI investment wave; it moves the semiconductor complex and, by its index weight, the benchmark. Its earnings date is a circled macro event, not a routine single-stock print.

**"In Vietnam, tech moves the index like in the U.S."** No — Vietnam's concentration tilts to **banks and real estate**. The VN-Index's macro-event prints are the big bank and property results, not technology. The 2022 −34% index year was a property-and-credit story, not a tech one.

**"Earnings season is irrelevant to bonds, gold and crypto."** Less true than it looks. A risk-off earnings shock — a cautious bank kickoff or a mega-cap-led index drop — bleeds across assets exactly like a macro surprise: bonds can catch a flight-to-safety bid, gold can firm, and crypto, which trades as a high-beta risk asset, often falls with the Nasdaq on a bad mega-cap night. The transmission is the same risk-on/risk-off rotation the series describes everywhere else; earnings are just another trigger for it. A trader who watches only individual stocks during the season misses that a concentrated index shock is a cross-asset event.

**"The reported quarter is what you trade."** The reported quarter is the *least* tradeable part, because it is the part most fully priced. The new information is the guidance and the tone of the call against the whisper and the regime. Trading the headline EPS is trading yesterday's news; the edge is in the forward read.

## The playbook: how to trade earnings season as a macro event

Pull the pieces into a single read. The figure below is the trader's decision sequence — top-down from the kickoff tone to the index lean, with the Vietnam parallel.

![Trader earnings-season read from bank kickoff tone to mega-cap guidance to the index lean with the Vietnam path](/imgs/blogs/earnings-season-as-a-macro-event-7.png)

**1. Mark the calendar like a macro calendar.** Circle three dates each quarter: the bank kickoff (week 1), mega-cap week (weeks 3–4), and the NVDA date (late). These carry index-level risk. The hundreds of mid-cap reports in between are bottom-up noise for an index trader.

**2. Read the kickoff for the macro tone.** When the banks report, read the *forward* lines — provisions, consumer health, loan demand, deal pipeline — not the backward EPS. A cautious kickoff is a risk-off lean for the whole season; a confident one is a tailwind. Position the *mood*, not the four bank stocks.

**3. On mega-cap week, weight guidance over the headline.** For each giant, your read is: what is the whisper, how far has the stock run, and what is the *forward* guide? Beat-but-soft-guide is a sell setup; in-line-but-strong-guide is a buy setup. Translate each name's likely move into an index drag (weight × move) so you know what the benchmark does.

**4. Price the event with options if you can.** A stock's options imply an **expected move** into earnings — roughly the at-the-money straddle price as a percent of the stock. If the implied move is ±8% and you think the guidance scare is overdone, that asymmetry is your edge. (The full mechanic is in [The expected move: pricing event risk with options](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-expected-move-pricing-event-risk-with-options) and [Event volatility: implied vs realized and the vol crush](/blog/trading/event-trading/event-volatility-implied-vs-realized-and-the-vol-crush).)

**5. Take the index lean off the mega-cap gap.** When a mega-cap gaps overnight, decide single-stock versus macro: if idiosyncratic, fade the index gap; if a sector-wide signal, respect the trend. Size so the invalidation (the session high/low) is a tolerable loss — our \$40,000 lean risked −\$400, not the account.

**6. In Vietnam, watch the bank and property prints.** Apply the same logic to the VN-Index, but the macro-event prints are the heavy bank and real-estate names. Strong bank credit growth and falling provisions lift the index; property write-downs drag it. Layer in foreign flows, which amplify the reaction.

**7. Respect the overnight gap risk.** The defining hazard of earnings is that the move happens in thin after-hours/pre-market trading, gapping past your stops. An index fund is *not* hedged against mega-cap week. If you can't tolerate an overnight gap, trim exposure or buy a defined-risk hedge into the calendar's circled dates.

#### Worked example: a full mega-cap-week position plan

You manage \$30,000 indexed to the S&P 500 and the macro week is approaching. The two largest constituents (a combined ~12% of the index) report midweek; their options imply ±7% moves.

- Downside scenario: both guide soft and average −8%. Index drag = 12% × (−8%) = −0.96%; dollar impact \$30,000 × (−0.0096) = **−\$288** overnight.
- You buy a one-week index put costing, say, \$150 that pays off if the index falls more than ~0.8%. In the downside case it offsets most of the \$288 drag; in the upside case you lose only the \$150 premium.
- Upside scenario: both guide strong and rally; your index holding gains, the put expires worthless (−\$150), and you keep the rest.
- Net: you converted an uncapped overnight gap into a known \$150 cost — cheap insurance against the quarter's largest scheduled risk.

The intuition: mega-cap week is a macro risk event, so you hedge it like one — define the cost, cap the gap, and let the upside run.
