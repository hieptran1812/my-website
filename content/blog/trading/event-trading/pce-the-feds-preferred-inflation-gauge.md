> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When the Fed says it targets "2 percent" inflation, it does not mean the CPI that leads the evening news. It means **2% on core PCE**, a gauge most retail traders never look at — broader in scope, slower-moving, and usually a few tenths of a point below CPI.
>
> - **What it is**: the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index, published by the BEA at the end of each month. *Core PCE* strips food and energy; it is the number the FOMC's official 2% target is written against.
> - **How the market reacts**: usually a shrug. PCE lands roughly two weeks *after* CPI and after PPI, so trading desks have already nowcast it to within a rounding error. A \$25,000 book might move ±\$75 on PCE day versus ±\$1,080 on a hot CPI day.
> - **The trade**: PCE rarely surprises — so when it *does* diverge from the path CPI implied, that gap is genuine new information about policy, and it moves the Fed (and the front end of the curve) even when stocks barely twitch.
> - **The one number to remember**: at the 2022 inflation peak, headline CPI hit **9.1%** while core PCE peaked at just **5.6%** (February 2022). Same economy, two very different rulers — and the Fed steered by the lower one.

## A target most people are watching the wrong way

In June 2022, the US Consumer Price Index hit 9.1% year-over-year — a four-decade high. It was on every front page, every cable chyron, every dinner-table conversation. Petrol was the headline; eggs were the punchline. If you had asked any trader, any commentator, any politician "how high is inflation right now?", they would have said 9.1%, and they would have been quoting CPI.

Here is the strange part. The number the Federal Reserve actually targets — the one that determines whether interest rates go up or down, the one written into the FOMC's own Statement on Longer-Run Goals — was not 9.1%. It was core PCE, and at its peak four months earlier it had reached just 5.6%. By the time CPI was screaming 9.1%, core PCE was already pulling back. Same country, same prices, same shopping baskets — and a gap of three and a half percentage points between the gauge everyone watched and the gauge the central bank steered by.

That gap is the whole subject of this post. Most people, including a lot of experienced traders, treat CPI as *the* inflation number and never realise the Fed has been quietly looking at something else the entire time. They react hard to CPI and ignore PCE — which is half right, because CPI *is* the market-moving print on the day. But it leaves them blind to the gauge that sets policy, and blind to the one situation where PCE matters enormously: when it tells a different story than CPI did. This post will teach you what PCE is, why it differs from CPI, why it usually runs below it, why the Fed prefers it, and — most useful for a trader — why its reaction on the tape is usually a non-event, except when it isn't.

![CPI versus PCE comparison matrix of scope weights healthcare publisher and what the Fed targets](/imgs/blogs/pce-the-feds-preferred-inflation-gauge-1.png)

The matrix above is the whole post in one frame: two gauges that measure the same idea — the rising cost of living — but with different rulers, and only one of them is what the Fed targets. Keep it in mind as we build each row up from scratch. For the report that *does* move the tape on the day, this post is a companion to the deep-dive on CPI elsewhere in this series; here we focus on the quieter, more powerful gauge sitting behind it.

## Foundations: what PCE measures and how it differs from CPI

Let us define every term from zero, because the entire edifice rests on a handful of words that sound interchangeable but are not.

**Inflation** is the rate at which the general level of prices rises over time. If a basket of goods and services costs 3% more this year than last, inflation is 3%. To measure it, a statistical agency picks a basket, prices it month after month, and reports how fast the total cost climbs. The trouble — and the source of every difference we are about to discuss — is that *which basket you pick, how you weight it, and where you get the prices* all change the answer.

**CPI** is the Consumer Price Index. It is published by the **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)**, an arm of the Department of Labor. CPI measures the change in prices that *urban consumers pay out of pocket* for a fixed basket of goods and services. It is released around the middle of each month, for the prior month, and it is the number markets trade and newspapers print. There is also a *core* CPI, which strips out food and energy (the two most volatile components) to reveal the underlying trend.

**PCE** is the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index. It is published by the **Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA)**, an arm of the Department of Commerce, as part of the same monthly report that gives us personal income and consumer spending. PCE measures the change in prices of *all goods and services consumed by households* — and crucially, "consumed by households" includes things bought *on your behalf* by someone else, which we will unpack in a moment. PCE is released near the *end* of each month, for the prior month — typically two weeks or so after the CPI covering the same period.

**Core PCE** strips food and energy out of PCE, exactly as core CPI does for CPI. Core PCE is the single most important inflation number in American finance, because it is the gauge the Federal Reserve's 2% target is officially written against. When a Fed official says "we are not yet confident inflation is moving sustainably toward 2 percent," the 2 percent they mean is core PCE.

So far we have two agencies, two indices, and two "core" versions. Now the four substantive differences, which are the meat of this section.

**Difference 1 — scope (whose spending counts).** CPI counts only what urban consumers pay *directly, out of their own pockets*. PCE counts *all* personal consumption, including spending made on a household's behalf by third parties. The biggest example is healthcare: when your employer's insurance plan or the government's Medicare program pays a hospital bill for you, *you* never see that money — but the care was consumed by a household, so PCE counts it. CPI counts only the co-pay and premium you personally wrote a cheque for. PCE's basket is therefore *broader*: it captures roughly the full universe of household consumption in the national accounts, where CPI captures the narrower slice that hits consumers' wallets directly.

**Difference 2 — weights (fixed versus chained).** This is the most technical difference and the most consequential, so we will spend real time on it below. In short: CPI uses *fixed weights* updated slowly, so it implicitly assumes you keep buying the same basket even as relative prices change. PCE uses *chained weights* updated every month, which capture the fact that when beef gets expensive, shoppers buy more chicken. That behaviour — *substitution* — systematically pulls the PCE number lower than CPI.

**Difference 3 — healthcare treatment.** Because of the scope difference above, healthcare carries a much heavier weight in PCE (around 17% of the index) than in CPI (around 8%). And medical prices in PCE are dominated by what Medicare and insurers actually pay, which tends to rise slowly and administratively, not by the sticker prices consumers face. Heavier weight on a slow-rising category is another force pulling PCE below CPI.

**Difference 4 — timing.** CPI lands mid-month; PCE lands at the end of the month. By the time PCE prints, the CPI for the same period is two weeks old, the PPI (producer prices) has also printed, and the components that feed PCE are largely known. This is the single most important fact for a trader: **PCE is the last of the inflation prints to arrive, so it is the one with the least surprise left in it.**

Hold those four differences — scope, weights, healthcare, timing — in your head. The rest of the post is just consequences of them.

It is worth pausing on *why two agencies measure the same thing at all*, because the answer explains a lot of the personality difference. The BLS is a labour-market agency; its CPI was born to adjust wages, Social Security benefits, tax brackets, and contracts for the cost of living. That mandate makes CPI a *consumer's-eye* index: it cares about what a household pays out of pocket, it is fixed and predictable enough to write into a cost-of-living adjustment clause, and once published it is essentially never revised, because you cannot rewrite the inflation figure that already adjusted last month's pension cheque. The BEA, by contrast, is the national-accounts agency; it builds GDP. Its PCE price index exists to deflate the consumption part of GDP — to separate "people bought more stuff" from "the same stuff cost more." That mandate makes PCE an *economy's-eye* index: it cares about total consumption regardless of who paid, it gets revised as better source data arrives, and it slots neatly into the GDP machinery. Two different jobs, two different indices — and the Fed, which thinks about the whole economy rather than any single household's budget, naturally gravitated to the economy's-eye one.

A second subtlety: the food-and-energy distinction (headline versus core) is *orthogonal* to the CPI-versus-PCE distinction. You can have headline CPI, core CPI, headline PCE, and core PCE — four numbers, two axes. Headline-versus-core is about *which components* you include; CPI-versus-PCE is about *which methodology and scope*. When this post compares "headline CPI" to "core PCE" — as the first chart does — it is varying both axes at once, which is why that particular gap looks so large. The cleanest like-for-like comparison, core CPI to core PCE, isolates the methodology gap alone, and that is the ~0.3–0.4 percentage-point wedge we will keep returning to.

## 1. PCE versus CPI: scope, weights, and why PCE runs lower

Let us look at the two gauges side by side over the recent inflation cycle, because the picture makes the relationship obvious.

![Core PCE versus headline CPI from 2021 to 2026 with the 2 percent target line](/imgs/blogs/pce-the-feds-preferred-inflation-gauge-2.png)

The orange line is headline CPI; the blue line is core PCE. Three things jump out. First, they move together — both ramped up through 2021, both peaked in 2022, both glided back toward the green dashed 2% line. They are measuring the same underlying inflation. Second, at the peak they were *miles* apart: CPI topped out around 9.1% in mid-2022 while core PCE peaked at 5.6% in February 2022. Third, as inflation cooled, the gap narrowed — by 2024–25 both gauges were clustered in the high-2s to low-3s, much closer together. The gap is widest when inflation is volatile (food and energy spiking pushes *headline* CPI far above *core* PCE) and narrows when things calm down.

Now, that chart compares *headline* CPI to *core* PCE, which is a bit apples-to-oranges — headline includes the volatile food and energy that core strips out, which is why the 2022 gap is so dramatic. The cleaner comparison is core-to-core: core CPI versus core PCE. Even there, PCE almost always prints lower, just by a smaller margin — historically about 0.3 to 0.4 percentage points in a typical year. Why? The three structural drivers we previewed above.

![Three drivers that pull PCE below CPI substitution scope and weights and healthcare](/imgs/blogs/pce-the-feds-preferred-inflation-gauge-3.png)

**The substitution effect.** This is the chained-weights story. Say the price of beef jumps 20% in a month. A fixed-weight index like CPI keeps the same amount of beef in its basket and records the full 20% hit, because by construction it assumes you buy the same beef regardless of price. But real shoppers do not behave that way; faced with dear beef, many buy more chicken. A *chained* index like PCE updates its weights to reflect that shift, so the beef price spike gets a smaller weight after the substitution and the recorded inflation is lower. Economists call CPI's method a Laspeyres-type formula (fixed-basket) and PCE's a Fisher-type formula (chained, blending current and prior weights). The practical effect: chaining shaves a couple of tenths of a percentage point off the inflation reading every year, purely from capturing that switching behaviour. This is sometimes called *substitution bias* in CPI — the suggestion that CPI overstates the true cost-of-living increase because it ignores how people adapt.

To see the chaining effect in arithmetic, take a tiny two-good basket: beef and chicken, each \$100 of monthly spending to start, so \$200 total. Beef then rises 20% to \$120 while chicken holds at \$100. A *fixed-weight* index keeps the original quantities, so it values the new basket at \$120 + \$100 = \$220 and records 10% inflation (\$220 ÷ \$200 − 1). But suppose households respond to dear beef by shifting spending — buying less beef and more chicken — so that the actual basket consumed is now \$90 of beef and \$110 of chicken. A *chained* index, which updates to that new mix, captures a smaller effective price increase because consumers steered toward the good that did not rise. The recorded inflation comes out a few tenths lower than the fixed-weight 10%. Scale that one substitution up across thousands of goods every month and you get the persistent wedge: chaining mechanically subtracts a couple of tenths of a percentage point a year from the inflation reading, simply by acknowledging that people are not robots buying the identical cart regardless of price.

**Broader scope and monthly reweighting.** Because PCE covers the full sweep of household consumption — including the big third-party-paid categories — the fast-rising items that dominate a consumer's out-of-pocket experience get diluted by a much larger denominator. And because PCE reweights every single month rather than only updating its expenditure weights periodically the way CPI does, it adapts faster to the changing mix of what people actually buy. There is a subtle naming trap here worth flagging: CPI does eventually update its weights too (it moved to annual updating, and historically updated every couple of years), but it applies those weights in a fixed-basket Laspeyres frame, so it still does not capture *within-period* substitution the way PCE's monthly chaining does. The difference is not "fixed versus updated weights" but "fixed-basket versus chained formula" — a distinction that sounds pedantic until you realise it is worth a few tenths of a point of measured inflation every year.

**Healthcare coverage.** This deserves its own emphasis because it is the largest single source of the gap. In PCE, medical care is about 17% of the index, and most of that is priced off what insurers and the government actually reimburse providers — Medicare reimbursement rates, in particular, which are set administratively and tend to crawl rather than leap. In CPI, healthcare is only about 8% of the basket, and it is priced off what consumers pay directly. So PCE puts more than twice the weight on a category whose measured prices rise slowly. That structurally drags the whole PCE index below CPI.

Add the three together and you get the persistent wedge: in a normal year, core PCE prints roughly 0.3 to 0.4 percentage points below core CPI. When food and energy are wild, the *headline* gap blows out further still.

#### Worked example: the CPI–PCE gap on a real cost-of-living basket

Say a household's annual cost of living is \$50,000. In June 2022, headline CPI said prices were rising at 9.1% a year and core PCE said the underlying rate was about 5.6%. Translate both into dollars on that \$50,000 basket:

- At 9.1% (CPI): \$50,000 × 0.091 = **\$4,550** of added annual cost.
- At 5.6% (core PCE): \$50,000 × 0.056 = **\$2,800** of added annual cost.
- The gap between the two gauges: \$4,550 − \$2,800 = **\$1,750** per year on this one household.

That \$1,750 difference is not a rounding error — it is the difference between "inflation is a five-alarm fire" and "inflation is serious but cooling," depending entirely on which ruler you pick up. The intuition: the gauge you choose changes the size of the problem by thousands of dollars, which is exactly why it matters *enormously* which one the Fed targets.

## 2. Why the Fed targets PCE and not CPI

If CPI is the number everyone watches, why on earth does the central bank target the obscure one? The Fed actually explained itself, and the reasoning is worth knowing because it tells you what the Fed thinks "inflation" *is*.

Back in February 2000, the Fed shifted the inflation measure it emphasised in its forecasts from CPI to PCE. In its own words at the time, the PCE price index was preferable on three grounds, and they map almost exactly onto the differences we just built up.

**Reason 1 — broader, more representative coverage.** The Fed wants to gauge inflation across the *whole* economy's consumption, not just the slice consumers pay for directly. Because PCE includes the third-party-paid spending (employer health plans, Medicare, and so on), it captures a fuller picture of what is actually being consumed and priced. To a central bank trying to read economy-wide price pressure, a broader basket is a better thermometer.

**Reason 2 — the chained formula tracks the true cost of living better.** The Fed accepts the economists' argument that fixed-weight indices overstate cost-of-living increases by ignoring substitution. PCE's chained methodology, which updates weights to reflect how households actually shift their spending, gives — in the Fed's judgement — a more accurate read on the real change in the cost of living. The central bank would rather not over-tighten policy in response to an inflation number it believes is biased upward.

**Reason 3 — revisability and consistency.** PCE can be revised as better source data arrives, and its historical weights get updated, so the back-history stays internally consistent with the latest understanding of consumption patterns. CPI, by contrast, is essentially never revised once published — good for a contractual benchmark, less good for a forecaster who wants the most accurate picture of the past. PCE's revisability means the Fed's models are working with the best available estimate, not a frozen first print.

There is a fourth, more practical reason that follows from all this: because PCE is part of the national income and product accounts, it ties cleanly into the rest of the data the Fed cares about — GDP, consumer spending, personal income. CPI is a standalone price survey; PCE is woven into the whole macro fabric. For a central bank that thinks in terms of aggregate demand and the output gap, a price index that lives inside the GDP accounts is simply more useful.

The upshot is concrete and worth memorising: the FOMC's *Statement on Longer-Run Goals and Monetary Policy Strategy* — the document that defines the target — says the Committee judges that **2 percent inflation, as measured by the annual change in the price index for personal consumption expenditures, is most consistent over the longer run with the Fed's mandate.** Not CPI. PCE. And in practice, *core* PCE, because the Fed watches the core series to see through volatile food and energy and read the underlying trend.

A short history clarifies how recent this all is. The Fed switched its forecast emphasis to PCE in February 2000, but it did not put a *number* on the target until January 2012, when then-Chair Ben Bernanke announced the explicit 2% PCE goal in the first formal longer-run statement. Before 2012 the target was implicit — the Fed pursued "price stability" without naming a figure. So the precise object the entire modern rates market now obsesses over — 2% on the PCE price index — has only been written down for a little over a decade. The 2% level itself is a judgement call, not a law of nature: it is meant to be high enough to keep the economy clear of deflation (which is far harder to escape than mild inflation) and low enough that prices stay effectively stable for planning purposes. Other major central banks landed on similar numbers — the ECB targets 2% on the HICP, the Bank of England 2% on UK CPI — which is partly convergence on the same economic logic and partly central banks copying each other's homework.

One more refinement matters for reading Fed communications. In August 2020 the Fed adopted *flexible average inflation targeting* (FAIT), under which it aims for inflation that *averages* 2% over time — meaning after a period of undershooting (as in the 2010s, when core PCE sat stubbornly below 2%), the Fed would tolerate a period of *overshooting* to make up the average. This is why, even as core PCE ran hot in 2021, the Fed was initially slow to react: its own framework told it to let inflation run above target for a while to compensate for the prior decade's shortfall. The framework arguably contributed to the Fed being "behind the curve" in 2021–22. By 2024–25, with the overshoot long since delivered, the Committee was back to treating 2% as a ceiling-ish goal to return to from above. The point for a trader: the *number* (2% core PCE) is fixed, but the *framework* around it — how symmetric, how forward-looking, how tolerant of overshoot — shifts, and those shifts change how the Fed reacts to the very same PCE print.

For the mechanism that links this inflation gauge to the actual policy rate — how a core-PCE reading translates into a hike, a hold, or a cut, and how the dot plot encodes that — see the dedicated post on [inflation and the Fed's reaction function](/blog/trading/macro-trading/inflation-and-the-fed-reaction-function-dot-plot), and for the plumbing of how the Fed moves the rate at all, [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).

#### Worked example: 2% target versus a 3% PCE print on your cash

Why does the Fed obsess over a single percentage point? Because that point is your money. Say you hold \$100,000 in cash earning nothing (or earning a rate that does not keep up with prices). Take the two cases:

- If core PCE runs at the Fed's 2% target: your cash loses \$100,000 × 0.02 = **\$2,000** of purchasing power over the year.
- If core PCE instead prints 3%: your cash loses \$100,000 × 0.03 = **\$3,000** of purchasing power.
- The extra erosion from that one percentage point of overshoot: \$3,000 − \$2,000 = **−\$1,000** in real terms, every year, on this balance.

The Fed's entire job is to keep that erosion anchored at the \$2,000 line rather than letting it drift to \$3,000 or \$5,000. The intuition: a "small" miss on the inflation gauge is a four-figure tax on every \$100,000 of savings, which is why the target is defended so fiercely — and why the gauge it is defined on is not an academic footnote. (For the deeper logic of why *real* returns are what matter, see [real versus nominal inflation and real yields](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal).)

## 3. Core PCE and supercore PCE: the policy variable

The Fed does not just target PCE rather than CPI. It looks *inside* PCE, at successively narrower cuts, to find the cleanest signal of where inflation is heading. Understanding these cuts is understanding what the Fed is really reacting to on any given month.

**Headline PCE** is the whole index — everything households consume. It is the most comprehensive but the noisiest, because food and especially energy prices swing wildly month to month on weather, geopolitics, and harvests. A central bank cannot set a multi-year interest-rate path off a number that lurches 1.5 percentage points because oil spiked.

**Core PCE** strips food and energy. This is the workhorse. By removing the two most volatile categories, core PCE reveals the *persistent* trend in prices — the part that reflects genuine, broad-based demand pressure rather than a one-off supply shock. When you read that "the Fed is watching core PCE," this is almost always the series meant. It is the official target gauge in practice.

**Supercore PCE** — more precisely, *core services excluding housing* — is the narrowest cut, and it became famous in late 2022 when Fed Chair Jerome Powell singled it out in a speech. The logic: goods prices had started falling, and housing inflation (a huge, lagging component) was expected to roll over with a delay because of how rents feed into the index slowly. So Powell argued the cleanest read on whether inflation was *truly* beaten lay in the prices of services *other than* housing — things like haircuts, restaurant meals, medical services, financial advice. Those prices are driven heavily by wages, and wages are the stickiest, most demand-sensitive part of inflation. If supercore is still hot, the labour market is still too tight, and the Fed cannot relax. Supercore PCE is the gauge-inside-the-gauge-inside-the-gauge.

It helps to see the nesting as a set of Russian dolls, each one stripping out a category the Fed cannot control or that muddies the underlying signal. Headline PCE is the outer doll — comprehensive but noisy. Core PCE removes food and energy — the volatile, supply-driven shell. Supercore removes housing on top of that — the large, mechanically-lagging component that the Fed knows is already in the pipeline. What is left in the innermost doll is the part of inflation most tightly bound to the labour market and aggregate demand, the part interest rates can actually bite. The narrower the cut, the more it reflects *demand* rather than *supply* — and demand is what the Fed's tools work on.

A concrete example of why the housing strip matters: rents in the official indices are measured with a long lag, because the surveys capture the average rent across all existing leases, not just newly signed ones. So when market rents stop rising (as they did in 2023), the official shelter component keeps printing high for many months as old leases roll over at the new, lower market rate only gradually. The Fed *knows* this lag exists, so it does not want to keep policy tight just because a mechanically-lagging shelter number is still elevated. Supercore lets it look past the lag to the prices that move in real time.

Why does this nesting matter to a trader? Because on PCE release day, the headline number is largely known (the market nowcast it from CPI and PPI), so the *trade* — if there is one — is usually in the composition: did core come in line but supercore surprise hot? Did a quirky component like portfolio-management fees (which are tied to equity-market levels and feed PCE financial services) swing the core figure in a way CPI did not capture? The headline is old news; the internals occasionally are not. The professionals on rates desks do not even glance at the headline year-over-year print when PCE crosses — they go straight to the month-over-month core and supercore figures and compare them to their nowcast, because that is the only place a surprise can hide.

#### Worked example: a supercore surprise that reprices the curve

Suppose on PCE day the headline and core both land exactly on the nowcast — a yawn. But supercore (core services ex-housing) comes in at +0.45% month-over-month versus an expected +0.30%. That is a meaningful upside surprise in the stickiest category, and it implies the Fed will hold rates higher for longer. Say the front end reacts: the 2-year Treasury yield rises 8 basis points. On a \$1,000,000 position in the 2-year (DV01 — the dollar value of a one-basis-point move — is roughly \$190 for a 2-year note):

- Yield move: +8bp. Price falls (yields up means bond prices down).
- Dollar impact: 8 × \$190 = **−\$1,520** on a \$1,000,000 long 2-year position.
- Had you been *short* the 2-year (betting yields would rise) at the same size: **+\$1,520**.

So even on a "muted" PCE day where the headline did nothing, a hot supercore internal moved a million-dollar front-end position by over fifteen hundred dollars. The intuition: when PCE matters, it usually matters through the composition, not the headline — the surprise hides one level down.

## 4. Why the PCE reaction is muted: CPI and PPI already nowcast it

Now we get to the trader's core insight, the one that separates people who understand the inflation calendar from people who just react to whatever crosses the wire. On most release days, **PCE barely moves markets** — and there is a precise, mechanical reason.

![The inflation release calendar showing CPI then PPI then PCE arriving last](/imgs/blogs/pce-the-feds-preferred-inflation-gauge-5.png)

Walk the calendar left to right. Around the 10th to 15th of each month, **CPI** drops. This is the big one — the whole market is positioned for it, options price an expected move into it, and the surprise versus consensus drives a violent repricing across stocks, bonds, the dollar, gold and crypto. (For exactly how one print radiates across every asset, the cross-asset-transmission post in this series walks the chain step by step.) A day or so after CPI, **PPI** — the Producer Price Index — prints. PPI matters here for a specific, slightly nerdy reason: several of the components that feed the PCE calculation are sourced not from CPI but from PPI. Healthcare prices, airfares, and portfolio-management and investment-advice fees in PCE are built largely from PPI inputs rather than CPI inputs.

So here is what happens on trading desks in the days between PPI and the end-of-month PCE release. Analysts take the relevant CPI components, swap in the relevant PPI components, apply the known PCE weighting scheme, and produce a **nowcast** — a forecast of the PCE number built almost entirely from data that has *already been published*. These nowcasts are good. Typically they pin core PCE to within about five hundredths of a percentage point (0.05pp) of the eventual print. By the time the official BEA number lands at month-end, the market already knows what it is going to say to a near-certainty.

It is worth being precise about *which* CPI components map to *which* PCE components, because that mapping is the whole nowcasting trade. A large share of core PCE — the goods categories, most of core services like restaurants and recreation — is built directly from the corresponding CPI source data, so for those pieces the CPI print *is* the PCE print, just reweighted. A smaller but important share is built from PPI instead: medical care services (priced off what providers are paid, which is a producer price), airfares, and the financial-services line including portfolio-management fees. A handful of components come from neither and are estimated by the BEA from other sources. A desk's nowcast takes the CPI-sourced pieces from the mid-month CPI, the PPI-sourced pieces from the PPI a day or two later, applies the PCE weights, and adds an estimate for the residual. Because the CPI-and-PPI-sourced share is the overwhelming majority of the index, the nowcast is tight — and the *residual* pieces are exactly where the occasional divergence comes from.

A number you already know cannot surprise you. And without surprise, there is no repricing — recall the iron law of event trading, that only the gap between actual and expected moves price (the [consensus and "priced in"](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) post is the full treatment). PCE arrives with its surprise already wrung out by CPI and PPI two weeks earlier. That is why PCE day is, on the tape, usually a non-event. The Cleveland Fed even publishes a running "inflation nowcast" for both CPI and PCE that anyone can read for free, which tells you how thoroughly the surprise has been engineered out of the release before it happens: the central bank's own regional branch will tell you, in advance, roughly what the number is going to be.

![CPI day versus PCE day before and after comparison of timing surprise move and options](/imgs/blogs/pce-the-feds-preferred-inflation-gauge-6.png)

The before-after frame sharpens the contrast. On CPI day (left), the timing is "first," the surprise is often large, the move is violent (the S&P fell 4.32% on the hot August-2022 CPI and rose 5.54% on the cool October-2022 print), and options had priced a 1.0–1.5% expected move that then collapses in a vol crush. On PCE day (right), the timing is "two weeks later," the surprise is tiny because of the nowcast, the move is a fraction of CPI day, and — the one caveat, in lavender — a genuine *divergence* from the CPI-implied path can still move the Fed and the curve.

#### Worked example: a muted PCE day versus a CPI day on the same book

Put numbers on "muted." Say you run a \$25,000 equity book that roughly tracks the S&P 500.

- On a violent CPI day — the −4.32% of 13 September 2022 — your book moves \$25,000 × 0.0432 = **−\$1,080** in a single session.
- On a typical PCE day where the index moves about ±0.3% (because the number was already nowcast), your book moves \$25,000 × 0.003 ≈ **±\$75**.
- Ratio: \$1,080 ÷ \$75 ≈ **14×**. CPI day delivers roughly fourteen times the P&L swing of an ordinary PCE day on the identical position.

That is the whole reason PCE rarely makes the front page of the markets section. The intuition: if you are budgeting risk around the inflation calendar, CPI is the event to hedge and PCE is the one you can usually sleep through — *usually*.

## 5. When PCE matters: divergence from the CPI path

"Usually" is doing a lot of work in that last sentence, and the exception is where the trader's edge lives. PCE rarely surprises — but when it *diverges* from what CPI implied, that divergence is pure, uncontaminated new information about the gauge the Fed actually targets. And the Fed reacts to it even when the stock market did not.

How can PCE diverge from a nowcast built off CPI and PPI? Because of the very structural differences we spent section 1 on. The categories where PCE is built differently from CPI — healthcare priced off Medicare and insurer reimbursements, the financial-services line driven by portfolio-management fees that move with the equity market, the different weights, the chaining — are exactly the categories where the nowcast can be wrong. Most months these are quiet. But in a month where, say, Medicare reimbursement rates reset, or equity markets rallied hard (lifting the portfolio-management fee component of PCE services), or the chaining bit harder than usual, PCE can print a few hundredths or even a tenth of a point away from where CPI pointed. That gap is the signal.

![The trader PCE decision tree of confirm leading to muted and diverge leading to Fed moving](/imgs/blogs/pce-the-feds-preferred-inflation-gauge-7.png)

The decision tree is the entire playbook in one picture. Core PCE prints. You ask one question: did it *confirm* the path CPI and PPI implied, or did it *diverge*? If it confirms — landing within a few hundredths of the nowcast — there is no new information, you fade any knee-jerk, and you do not pay up for event risk. If it diverges — hotter or cooler than CPI implied — you have learned something the Fed will act on, and the trade is in the front end of the rate curve and the dollar, not necessarily in equities at all.

Why the front end and not stocks? Because a PCE divergence is a *policy* signal, and policy lives in short-term interest rates. If core PCE comes in two tenths hotter than the CPI path suggested, the market shaves its rate-cut expectations: maybe it had two cuts priced for the year and now prices one and a half. That repricing shows up immediately in the 2-year yield and Fed funds futures, and it can ripple out to the 10-year and the dollar. Equities might shrug — they already digested the inflation story on CPI day — while bonds quietly reprice the entire path of policy. This is the classic asymmetry: PCE is boring for the equity day-trader and occasionally critical for the rates trader.

#### Worked example: a PCE divergence that reprices two Fed cuts

Suppose the market enters PCE day expecting two 25bp Fed cuts over the next year. Core PCE prints two tenths above the CPI-implied path — a real divergence in the gauge that sets policy. The market reprices to one and a half cuts. That shift lifts yields across the curve; say the 10-year Treasury yield falls... no — *rises*, because fewer cuts means higher rates — by 15 basis points. You are short a \$500,000 position in the 10-year (positioned for higher yields), with a DV01 of roughly \$430 per basis point:

- Yield move: +15bp (fewer cuts now priced).
- Dollar impact on the short: 15 × \$430 = **+\$6,450** on the \$500,000 position.
- Note the equity book that day might have moved a trivial ±\$75 on \$25,000 — the action was entirely in rates.

So a PCE print that the stock desk barely noticed handed the rates desk a \$6,450 gain on half a million dollars of duration. The intuition: PCE's reaction is muted *on average*, but the average hides a fat tail — the divergence days — where the gauge the Fed targets says something CPI did not, and that is precisely when PCE is worth more than CPI.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Abstract differences are easy to nod along to and hard to believe. So here are the dated, real episodes that show the CPI–PCE relationship doing exactly what we described.

**The 2022 peak: 9.1% CPI, 5.6% core PCE.** The marquee episode is the 2022 inflation peak itself. Headline CPI peaked around 9.1% year-over-year in June 2022 — the number that defined the era. Core PCE, the Fed's actual gauge, had already peaked months earlier at **5.6% in February 2022** and was drifting lower by mid-year. Two things are striking. First, the sheer size of the gap: three and a half percentage points between the headline everyone quoted and the core gauge the Fed steered by. Most of that gap was food and energy (which headline CPI includes and core PCE excludes), but a persistent chunk was the structural wedge — chaining, scope, healthcare — that keeps PCE below CPI even core-to-core. Second, the *timing*: core PCE peaked four months before headline CPI, because core strips out the volatile energy that kept the headline climbing into the summer. A Fed watching core PCE saw the peak forming in late winter; a public watching headline CPI did not feel the peak until midsummer.

![Core PCE alone with the 2 percent target band showing the road back toward target](/imgs/blogs/pce-the-feds-preferred-inflation-gauge-4.png)

**The disinflation back toward 2%.** The second episode is the long glide down, shown in the chart above with the 2% target band shaded green. From the 5.6% peak, core PCE fell steadily: to roughly 4.9% by end-2022, the low-4s through mid-2023, around 3.0% by the end of 2023, and down to the high-2s by 2024. But notice what the chart shows in 2024–25: the descent *stalls* just above the target band, hovering in the 2.6–3.0% range rather than settling at 2.0%. This is the famous "last mile" of disinflation — the part where the easy gains (goods deflation, supply chains healing) are exhausted and what remains is sticky services inflation driven by wages, which is exactly the supercore problem from section 3. The Fed's caution through 2024–25 about cutting rates "too soon" was, in plain numbers, a refusal to declare victory while *this specific gauge* sat above its band. Every Fed communication in that period was, at root, a statement about where the blue line in that chart was relative to the green band.

**Why the day-of reactions were quiet.** Across all of these months, the *PCE release days themselves* were mostly unremarkable on the tape. The disinflation story was real and important, but it was being told first by CPI two weeks earlier each month. By the time PCE confirmed it, the market had already moved. The big inflation-day reactions of the era — the −4.32% and +5.54% S&P sessions — were all *CPI* days, not PCE days. PCE was the gauge that mattered for *policy* and the gauge that barely registered for *intraday P&L*, simultaneously. That dual nature is the entire point.

**How the gauge drove the rate decisions.** It is worth connecting the blue line in that chart to the actual policy path, because the two move together with a lag. As core PCE climbed from 1.6% in early 2021 toward its 5.6% peak, the Fed went from zero rates to the fastest hiking cycle in four decades — lifting the funds-rate ceiling from 0.25% in March 2022 to 5.50% by July 2023. As core PCE then ground down from 5.6% toward the high-2s, the Fed first paused, holding at 5.50% through most of 2024, and then began cutting in September 2024, taking the ceiling back down toward 4.50% by the end of that year. Every one of those moves was, at root, a read on where core PCE sat relative to the 2% target and where it was heading. A trader who plotted core PCE and the funds-rate ceiling on the same chart would have seen the policy path as essentially a lagged, smoothed function of the gauge. That is what it means to say the Fed "targets" core PCE: the gauge is the input and the rate is the output, with the dot plot as the bridge between them — the mechanism the [Fed reaction-function post](/blog/trading/macro-trading/inflation-and-the-fed-reaction-function-dot-plot) lays out in full.

**A note on Vietnam and other economies.** This whole CPI-versus-PCE distinction is a peculiarly American piece of plumbing — it exists because the US happens to produce two high-quality consumption price indices from two different agencies. Most other central banks, including the State Bank of Vietnam and the ECB, target a CPI-type measure (the ECB targets the Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices, the HICP). So when you trade non-US inflation prints, the gauge *is* the CPI-type number — there is no quieter PCE-equivalent sitting behind it. The PCE lesson is US-specific, but the deeper lesson — *know which exact index your central bank targets, because that is the one that sets policy* — travels everywhere.

Vietnam is a clean illustration of the general rule. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) and the National Assembly frame the country's inflation goal in terms of the GSO's headline CPI, with an annual target band (in recent years around 4–4.5%). There is no PCE-equivalent and no "core versus headline" obsession at the centre of policy — the headline CPI *is* the gauge, full stop. Vietnamese CPI ran far cooler than US CPI through the 2022 shock, averaging around 3.15% for 2022 and 3.25% for 2023, which is part of why the SBV could pivot to *cutting* its refinancing rate in 2023 (from 6.0% down to 4.5% across three moves) even as the Fed was still hiking. A trader watching Vietnam does not need to learn a second gauge — but does need to know that the SBV's reaction function is driven by that headline CPI plus the exchange rate and credit growth, not by anything resembling core PCE. The structural takeaway transfers: identify the exact index the relevant central bank wrote its mandate against, because every other inflation number is just commentary.

It is also worth knowing the *historical* size of the US wedge, not just the 2022 snapshot. Over the two decades before the pandemic, core PCE ran below core CPI by roughly 0.3 to 0.4 percentage points in a typical year — remarkably stable. That stability is what lets the nowcast and the Fed's models lean on it: the gap is not random, it is a structural feature you can budget for. When you see a financial commentator say "CPI is at 3.2% so the Fed is far from its 2% goal," the more accurate translation is "core PCE is probably around 2.8%, so the Fed is closer than the CPI headline implies" — and that 0.4-point translation is, more often than not, the difference between a market pricing the Fed as on-hold versus pricing the first cut.

## Common misconceptions

A few myths cause real money to be lost or left on the table. Each is corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "CPI is the Fed's inflation target."** This is the big one, and it is simply false. The Fed's 2% target is defined on **core PCE**, as stated in the FOMC's own longer-run goals document. CPI is the market-moving print and the public's inflation number, but it is *not* the official target. The practical consequence: when CPI is at 3.2% and core PCE is at 2.8%, the Fed is closer to its goal than the CPI headline suggests. A trader who only watches CPI consistently misreads how close the Fed is to cutting. The gap is not cosmetic — recall it can be 0.3–0.4 percentage points core-to-core, and far wider at the headline level.

**Myth 2: "PCE and CPI should agree, so a gap means one is wrong."** No — the gap is *by design* and is structural. Chaining, broader scope, and heavier healthcare weighting mean PCE is *built* to run below CPI. A persistent 0.3–0.4 percentage-point wedge core-to-core is normal and expected, not an error in either series. Treating the gap as a signal that "CPI is overstated" or "PCE is missing something" misunderstands that they are answering slightly different questions.

**Myth 3: "PCE day is a big inflation-trading event."** For equities and crypto, almost never. Because PCE is nowcast to within ~0.05pp from the earlier CPI and PPI, the typical PCE day moves a \$25,000 book by about ±\$75 versus the ±\$1,080 a hot CPI day delivers — roughly a 14-to-1 ratio. The traders who circle PCE on their calendar as a "big event" are usually rates traders watching for a *divergence*, not equity traders expecting fireworks.

**Myth 4: "Core strips food and energy because those don't matter."** Of course food and energy matter — they are a huge part of a household's actual cost of living. Core strips them not because they are unimportant but because they are *volatile and supply-driven*, and the Fed's tools (interest rates) work on demand, not on the price of crude oil. Core PCE isolates the part of inflation the Fed can actually influence. The 2022 episode proves the point: core PCE peaked in February while headline kept climbing into the summer on energy — the Fed needed to see through that noise to read the underlying trend.

**Myth 5: "If headline CPI is at target, the Fed will cut."** Not necessarily. The Fed wants *core PCE* sustainably at 2%, and it pays special attention to *supercore* (services ex-housing). In 2024–25, headline numbers flirted with target while core PCE stalled in the high-2s and supercore stayed sticky — and the Fed kept rates restrictive far longer than headline-watchers expected, precisely because the gauge it targets was not yet home.

## The playbook: how to trade PCE day

Here is the if-then map. Treat PCE day as the *confirmation* event in the inflation cycle, with one high-value exception.

**Before the release — set your baseline.** The single most useful preparation is to read the PCE *nowcast*. Several banks and the Cleveland Fed publish a core-PCE nowcast in the days after PPI prints, built from the already-released CPI and PPI components. That nowcast is your consensus. Because it is usually accurate to ~0.05pp, your expected move on PCE day should be small — a fraction of what you would budget for CPI. Do not pay rich options premium for a PCE straddle the way you might for CPI; the expected move is structurally tiny because the surprise has already been wrung out.

**Scenario A — PCE confirms the CPI path (the base case, ~80% of months).** The print lands within a few hundredths of the nowcast. There is no new information. The play: do nothing, or fade any algorithmic knee-jerk in the first minute or two, which tends to revert because there is no fundamental surprise to sustain it. Do not initiate fresh macro risk on a confirming PCE print — you would be paying spread and slippage for zero edge. This is the day to sit on your hands.

**Scenario B — PCE diverges hot (the high-value case).** Core or supercore PCE prints meaningfully *above* the CPI-implied path — say two tenths hot. This is genuine new information about the gauge the Fed targets, and it tilts policy toward higher-for-longer. The play is in **rates and the dollar, not equities**: the front end (2-year yield, Fed funds futures) reprices fewer cuts, the curve can flatten, and the dollar tends to firm. As the worked example showed, a 15bp move on a \$500,000 10-year position is ~\$6,450 — meaningful — while the equity book barely moves. Position the surprise in the asset that prices policy.

**Scenario C — PCE diverges cool.** The mirror image: core/supercore prints *below* the CPI path. The market prices *more* cuts; front-end yields fall, the dollar softens, and rate-sensitive equities (small caps, long-duration tech) can catch a bid even though the broad index is quiet. Again, the cleanest expression is in rates.

**How to read the wire in real time.** When the BEA release crosses at 8:30 ET (or 10:00 ET depending on the schedule), do not anchor on the headline year-over-year number that the news algorithms blast out — that figure was knowable two weeks ago. Go straight to two things: the *month-over-month core PCE* (reported to one decimal, e.g. +0.2% or +0.3%) and, if you can get it quickly, the *core-services-ex-housing* (supercore) month-over-month. Compare both to your nowcast, not to the prior month and not to the CPI you already traded. A core that prints +0.3% against a +0.2% nowcast is a hot divergence even if the year-over-year figure looks unremarkable; a core at +0.1% against a +0.2% nowcast is a cool divergence. The rounding matters: because the numbers are reported to a single decimal, a print that "rounds up" or "rounds down" relative to the nowcast can itself move markets, which is why desks track the unrounded estimate. Also watch for *revisions* to the prior month — because PCE is revisable, a downward revision to last month plus an in-line current month can be a net dovish surprise, the kind of thing CPI (which is not revised) can never deliver.

**Sizing and risk.** Because the base case is a non-event, your default position size around PCE should be *small* relative to CPI. Reserve real risk for the divergence scenarios, and let the nowcast tell you which scenario you are in within seconds of the release — compare the print to the nowcast, not to last month's level or to CPI. The invalidation is simple: if the print confirms the nowcast, any position predicated on a divergence is wrong immediately and should be cut. A practical discipline: decide *before* the release exactly what core and supercore numbers would count as "hot divergence," "cool divergence," and "confirmation," and what you will do in each case. Writing those three lines down in advance stops you from inventing a story to justify trading a print that, on the numbers, was a non-event — the most common way money is lost around PCE.

**The cross-asset summary.** On a confirming PCE day: equities flat, bonds flat, dollar flat, crypto flat — a true non-event. On a hot divergence: bonds down (yields up), dollar up, rate-sensitive equities and crypto down, gold often down (higher real yields). On a cool divergence: the reverse — bonds up, dollar down, risk assets and gold up. But the magnitudes are almost always smaller than the equivalent CPI surprise, because PCE is the second print, not the first. The one structural exception to "smaller" is when PCE *reveals* something CPI could not have — a healthcare or financial-services swing — in which case the rates reaction can be as large as a CPI day's, concentrated entirely in the front end.

The meta-lesson for the whole inflation complex: **CPI is the trade, PCE is the truth.** CPI moves the tape because it arrives first and carries the surprise; PCE sets policy because it is the gauge the Fed defined its mandate against. A complete inflation trader watches CPI for the intraday move and PCE for the divergence that quietly redraws the rate path.
