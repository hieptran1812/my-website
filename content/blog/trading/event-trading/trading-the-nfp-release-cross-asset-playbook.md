> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — On the first Friday of the month at 8:30 a.m. Eastern, the US jobs report drops and the tape lurches. You do not trade the payroll number; you trade the *surprise* against what was priced — and, before that, you name the *regime*, because jobs is the one report where the SIGN of the reaction flips depending on what the market is afraid of.
>
> - **What NFP is:** the monthly US Employment Situation report from the Bureau of Labor Statistics. The marquee line is **nonfarm payrolls** (how many jobs the economy added), alongside the unemployment rate, average hourly earnings, and revisions to the prior two months.
> - **How the market reacts:** the regime decides the sign. In an *inflation regime* a strong jobs print is bearish for stocks (good news is bad — more Fed). In a *growth-scare regime* a weak jobs print is bearish (bad news is bad — recession). The cross-asset map (stocks, crypto, dollar, gold, bonds) all hinge on which fear is in charge.
> - **The trade:** write the if-then map before 8:30, size to the expected move (roughly **plus or minus 0.8%** on the S&P on a normal jobs day), watch the *internals* — average hourly earnings, the unemployment rate, and the two-month revision can reverse the headline knee-jerk — then decide fade or trend.
> - **The one fact to remember:** the same direction of surprise produced bearish stocks in two opposite regimes. A **+517k blowout** on Feb 3, 2023 sent the S&P **−1.04%**; a **weak +114k** on Aug 2, 2024 sent it **−1.84%**. Strong jobs and weak jobs both crashed stocks — for opposite reasons.

Two jobs Fridays, eighteen months apart, tell you almost everything about how this release trades — and why so many beginners get it exactly backwards.

On **February 3, 2023**, the Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that the US economy had added **517,000** jobs in January. The consensus among economists was roughly **187,000**. That is not a small beat — it is a blowout, nearly three times what was expected, and the unemployment rate fell to **3.4%**, a fifty-three-year low. On the surface this was spectacular news about the economy. By the close, the S&P 500 was down **−1.04%**, the two-year Treasury yield had jumped **+18 basis points**, and the dollar had climbed **+1.2%**. The economy was roaring. Stocks fell anyway.

On **August 2, 2024**, the same agency reported that the economy had added just **114,000** jobs in July against a consensus near **175,000** — a clear miss — and the unemployment rate had risen to **4.3%**, tripping a recession warning indicator that markets watch closely. This was bad news about the economy. By the close, the S&P 500 was down **−1.84%**, the two-year yield had collapsed **−28 basis points**, and over the following session the move metastasized into a global carry-trade unwind that drove Japan's Nikkei down **−12.4%** in a single day. The economy was weakening. Stocks fell anyway.

Same report. Same agency. Same 8:30 a.m. release time. One print was a monster beat, the other a clear miss. They were opposites — and both produced a falling stock market. If you had walked into either morning with the simple rule "strong jobs is good for stocks, weak jobs is bad," you would have been wrong both times, in opposite directions. The market was not asking "are jobs good or bad?" It was asking a sharper, regime-dependent question — and learning to ask that same question before the print is the entire game.

Put the two outcomes side by side and the puzzle becomes a thesis. A blowout strong print and a clear weak print produced the same direction of move in stocks — both down — because the regime, not the number, set the sign.

## Foundations: how to prepare for the jobs report

Before we trade a single share, we have to build the right mental model, because the jobs report has a trap that the inflation report does not. With CPI, hot is (almost always) bad for stocks and cool is good — the sign is stable across most regimes. With jobs, the sign *itself* depends on what the market is afraid of this month. Get the regime wrong and you will fight the tape with perfect logic and lose money. So we build up from zero, one term at a time.

### What the jobs report actually is, and who cares

The US **Employment Situation** report — universally called **"NFP"** after its headline line, **nonfarm payrolls** — is released by the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) on the first Friday of each month at **8:30 a.m. Eastern**, covering the prior month. It is built from two separate surveys. The **establishment survey** (a poll of employers) produces the headline payroll number: the net change in the count of jobs on company payrolls, excluding farm work, the military, and a few other categories. The **household survey** (a poll of people) produces the **unemployment rate**: the share of the labor force that is looking for work but does not have it.

The report carries several numbers traders watch at once:

- **Nonfarm payrolls** — the net jobs added (in thousands). The headline. "+250k" means a quarter-million net new jobs.
- **The unemployment rate (U-rate)** — the percentage of the labor force unemployed. A separate survey, so it can disagree with payrolls in any given month.
- **Average hourly earnings (AHE)** — wage growth, month-over-month and year-over-year. This is the inflation read inside the jobs report: fast wage growth can mean more inflation pressure.
- **Revisions** — the BLS revises the prior *two* months every release. A headline beat with deep downward revisions can be a net negative.
- **Labor-force participation** and the **average workweek** — second-order context that desks fold into the wage and slack picture.

Why does a count of jobs move global markets? For the same reason CPI does: the **Federal Reserve**, the US central bank. The Fed has a dual mandate — stable prices *and* maximum employment. The jobs report is the market's monthly read on *both* halves of that mandate at once. A hot labor market can mean wage-driven inflation that forces the Fed to keep rates high; a collapsing labor market can mean recession, which forces the Fed to cut. Interest rates are the price of money, and the price of money is the gravity that pulls on every asset on Earth. So NFP is not really a story about jobs. It is the market's monthly preview of *what the Fed will be pushed to do* — and which half of the mandate is in the driver's seat. (For the mechanism that turns this data into a rate decision, see the macro-trading companion on [the macro calendar: CPI, NFP, FOMC, PMI](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi).)

This dual-mandate framing is exactly why jobs is harder than CPI. CPI speaks to only one half of the mandate — prices — so its sign for risk assets is usually stable. Jobs speaks to *both* halves, and which half the market is listening to changes from quarter to quarter. When inflation is the binding constraint, the market hears the jobs report through the price-stability mandate (a strong labor market threatens more inflation). When recession is the binding constraint, the market hears the same report through the employment mandate (a weakening labor market threatens the economy). The report does not change; the channel the market routes it through does. That routing is what we call the regime, and naming it is the whole preparation.

### The two surveys, and why the report is noisy

It helps to know that the headline payroll number and the unemployment rate come from *different* surveys, because that single fact explains a lot of jobs-day chop. The **establishment survey** samples about 120,000 businesses and government agencies; it produces payrolls, average hourly earnings, and the average workweek. The **household survey** samples about 60,000 households; it produces the unemployment rate and the participation rate. Two surveys, two methodologies, two sampling errors — which is why payrolls can print strong while the unemployment rate rises in the same month, sending a genuinely mixed signal.

The headline payroll figure also carries a large statistical confidence band: the BLS itself states that the monthly change has a 90% confidence interval of roughly **±130k**. In plain numbers, a reported +150k month could in truth be anywhere from about +20k to +280k. That is enormous relative to the moves traders fade and trend. It is the deep reason the *reaction* matters more than the *number*: the market is reacting to a noisy estimate that will itself be revised twice. A disciplined jobs-day trader treats any single print as one noisy draw from a distribution, not as gospel — and sizes accordingly.

### The consensus, the whisper, and why the number itself moves nothing

Markets are forward-looking. In the weeks before a jobs report, professional forecasters publish payroll estimates, and the median becomes the **consensus** — the number "everyone" expects. There is often a **whisper** too: the figure that active desks are really positioned for, which can drift from the published consensus in the days before the print (it firms up after the ADP private-payrolls report and the JOLTS job-openings data earlier in the week). The crucial fact is the same one that governs every macro release:

> The consensus is already baked into prices before 8:30.

That means the consensus number itself moves nothing on release. The only thing that can move price at 8:30:00 is the gap between what prints and what was priced. We call that gap the **surprise**:

> surprise = actual payrolls − consensus payrolls

If consensus is +180k and the print is +180k, the surprise is zero, and in theory the headline should move little (it is already in the price). If the print is +250k, that is a **strong** surprise of +70k. If the print is +114k against a +175k consensus — as in August 2024 — that is a **weak** surprise of −61k. Hold that distinction: a +250k print can be a *weak* surprise if consensus was +320k, and a +120k print can be a *strong* surprise if consensus was +50k. The level is not the signal. The surprise is.

### The if-then map: your real preparation

The single most useful thing you can do before a jobs print is not to predict the number — nobody can do that reliably — but to write down, in advance, what you will do in each scenario. That is the **if-then map**: for each branch (strong / in-line / weak), what each asset does and what trade you take. You build it cold, before the adrenaline, so that at 8:30:00 you are not deciding anything; you are merely checking which branch the data lit up. The if-then map figure at the top of this post is exactly that artifact, and we will fill in every branch by the end.

### Hot/strong vs cool/weak, and the regime switch

A **strong** print means more jobs (and/or faster wages, lower unemployment) than priced — a tight labor market. A **weak** print means fewer jobs (and/or rising unemployment, soft wages) than priced — a loosening labor market. So far this mirrors CPI's hot/cool framing. Here is where jobs diverges and where the trap lives.

Whether strong is *good* or *bad* for stocks depends entirely on the **regime** — what the market is most afraid of right now:

- **Inflation regime.** The market's dominant fear is that inflation stays high and the Fed keeps rates "higher for longer." In this regime, a **strong** jobs print is *bad* for stocks: a hot labor market means wage pressure and a Fed that will not ease. This is **good-news-is-bad**. A **weak** print is *good*: it cools the inflation fear and pulls Fed cuts forward. This was the world of 2022 and into 2023.
- **Growth-scare regime.** The market's dominant fear is that the economy is tipping into recession and the Fed is too late to cut. In this regime, a **weak** jobs print is *bad* for stocks: it confirms the recession is here. This is **bad-news-is-bad**. A **strong** print is *good*: it says the economy is fine and the Fed has room to ease gently. This was the world of August 2024.

This is the heart of the whole post and the reason jobs is harder to trade than CPI. The deeper mechanism — why the same number can move differently depending on what the market fears — is the subject of the companion post on [the reaction function: why the same number moves differently](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently). Read it for the theory; here we will operationalize it for jobs specifically.

How do you actually know which regime you are in *before* the print, when nobody rings a bell? You read three tells in the days ahead of jobs Friday. First, **what's leading the macro narrative**: if every headline is about sticky inflation and Fed hikes, you are in an inflation regime; if the headlines are about layoffs, soft survey data, and "will the Fed cut in time," you are in a growth scare. Second, **the shape of the yield curve and the level of real yields**: a deeply inverted curve with high real yields says the market still fears a restrictive Fed (inflation regime); a curve that has started to steepen as the front end prices cuts says the recession story is winning. Third, and most directly, **how stocks have been reacting to recent data**: if good data has been *hurting* stocks lately, that is the live signature of an inflation regime; if good data has been *helping* stocks, the growth scare is in charge. You do not need to be certain — you need to know which way to lean, and you confirm it in real time by watching whether stocks and yields agree or disagree on release.

#### Worked example: mispricing the regime

Suppose it is an **inflation regime** (the market fears higher-for-longer). A strong jobs print lands — a big beat. A trader who naively believes "strong economy equals good for stocks" buys \$25,000 of an S&P 500 ETF at the print. But in an inflation regime the strong print is *bad* news (more Fed), and the S&P falls **−2.0%** over the session as yields rise.

- Position: long \$25,000 of the S&P 500 ETF.
- Move against the trade: −2.0%.
- Profit and loss: \$25,000 × (−0.020) = **−\$500.**
- The error was not the entry timing or the size. It was reading good-news-is-good in a good-news-is-bad world.

The intuition: in jobs trading, naming the regime *before* you read the number is worth more than any view on the number itself — get the regime wrong and a correct call on the data still loses \$500.

### The headline-vs-internals trap (the second move)

Here is the most common way to get whipsawed on a jobs day. The headline payroll number hits the wire at 8:30:00 and the algorithms trade it in milliseconds — they parse the single payroll figure and fire. But the *real* information is often in the **internals**, which human desks read in the next two to three minutes:

- **The two-month revision.** The BLS revises the prior two months every report. A headline +250k that comes with a combined **−90k** revision to the prior two months is really a +160k month of underlying job growth — far weaker than the headline. The knee-jerk buys the +250k; the second move sells once the revision is digested.
- **Average hourly earnings.** A strong payroll number with *soft* wage growth is less inflationary than it looks; a weak payroll number with *hot* wages is more inflationary. In an inflation regime, AHE can matter more than the headline.
- **The unemployment rate.** Because it comes from a different survey, the U-rate can contradict payrolls. A +200k payroll print alongside an unemployment rate that *rose* two-tenths is a mixed, often net-negative report.

The lesson: the headline knee-jerk is frequently *wrong*, and the desk that waits ninety seconds to read the internals can fade the algos. This is the single biggest edge available to a patient discretionary trader on jobs day — and the single biggest trap for the impatient one.

### Why jobs is the trickiest of the big three

Among the marquee releases, CPI has the most stable sign (hot is usually bad), and the FOMC decision is largely about the *statement and dot plot* rather than a single surprise number. Jobs sits in between and is the hardest to trade for three reasons: the **sign flips with the regime**; the **headline routinely lies relative to the internals**; and the report is **noisy** (monthly payrolls have a confidence band of roughly ±100k around the published figure, and they get revised). That noise is precisely why the *reaction* matters more than the number, and why sizing for a two-sided whipsaw is not optional.

## What's priced in, and building the if-then map

Now we go deep on the five things that actually drive a jobs-day P&L: what's priced before the print, the headline knee-jerk, the internals second move, the cross-asset map, and the fade-vs-trend decision with sizing.

### Before the print: read the positioning, not just the consensus

By Thursday night, the consensus is set and the market is positioned for it. Two pre-prints shape the whisper during the week: the **ADP** private-payrolls report (Wednesday) and **JOLTS** job openings (earlier in the week). A hot ADP can pull the whisper above consensus; a soft JOLTS can pull it below. The expected move quoted by the options market — roughly **±0.8%** on the S&P on a normal jobs day, smaller than CPI's ±1.2% because jobs is noisier and the Fed weighs inflation more heavily — tells you how big a reaction is "normal."

Positioning is the other half. If the market is heavily leaning one way into the print — say, everyone is short bonds expecting a strong number — then a weak print triggers an outsized move as that crowded position unwinds. The cleanest way to think about this is the **pain trade**: the move that hurts the most people, which is often the move that happens, because crowded positions amplify the surprise in the opposite direction. (We treat this fully in [positioning and the pain trade](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade).)

There is one more piece of pre-print discipline that beginners skip: **deciding in advance whether you even want to hold risk through the print.** For most traders most of the time, the correct answer on jobs Friday is to be flat or small into 8:30 and let the report happen, then trade the *reaction* with information you did not have before. Holding a directional position through a ±0.8% binary event without an edge on the number is not trading — it is gambling on a coin you cannot weight. The professional move is to walk into the print with a written map, little or no risk on, and the patience to act on the second move. The amateur move is to have a strong opinion about the number and a large position to match. The two episodes that anchor this post both punished the amateur move on opposite prints.

#### Worked example: the Feb-2023 cross-asset trade

It is February 3, 2023, an inflation regime. You have written your if-then map: *if strong, stocks down, front-end yields up, dollar up.* The print lands at +517k versus +187k expected — a massive strong surprise. You execute two legs of the strong branch.

- **Leg 1, equity short via a small long that you should not be holding:** suppose you were caught long \$30,000 of the S&P 500 ETF into the print. The S&P falls −1.04%. P&L: \$30,000 × (−0.0104) = **−\$312.**
- **Leg 2, the front-end short you *did* put on:** you are short \$1,000,000 face of the two-year Treasury, which has a DV01 (dollar value of one basis point) of roughly **\$190 per basis point**. The two-year yield rises +18bp, so the bond's price *falls*, and your short gains: \$190 × 18 = **+\$3,420.**
- Net across the two legs: −\$312 + \$3,420 = **+\$3,108.**
- The equity leg lost \$312 because you were wrong-footed long; the rate leg made \$3,420 because the strong print did exactly what a strong print does to the front end in an inflation regime.

The intuition: the cleanest expression of "strong jobs in an inflation regime" was not equities at all — it was the **front end of the curve**, where +18bp on a \$1,000,000 position printed +\$3,420 while the stock leg barely moved.

### The knee-jerk: algos trade the headline payroll

At 8:30:00.000, the headline payroll number crosses the wire and the algorithms fire. They are not reading the unemployment rate or the revision; they are reading the single payroll figure relative to consensus, and they trade it in microseconds — futures, Treasuries, and FX all gap in the same instant. This is the **knee-jerk**: the first, mechanical move driven by the headline alone. It is fast, it is violent, and it is often *wrong* about the report's true message because it ignores the internals.

The jobs-day clock has five phases, and the knee-jerk is only the second of them. Pre-positioning happens before 8:30. The knee-jerk is 8:30:00. The internals read is 8:30 to roughly 8:35. Then comes the fade-or-trend fork, and finally the settle into the close.

![Five-phase jobs-day timeline from pre-positioning through the headline knee-jerk and internals read to fade or trend and settle](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-nfp-release-cross-asset-playbook-3.png)

For a beginner, the most important thing to understand about the knee-jerk is what you *cannot* do during it. You cannot out-execute the algos in the first few hundred milliseconds — they are faster than any human and any retail order route. So the knee-jerk is not where your edge is. Your edge is in the *next* phase, where the internals get read and the move either confirms or reverses. The general microstructure of this spike-then-fade-or-trend sequence — how the first move resolves into a reversion or a continuation — is the subject of [the anatomy of a news reaction: spike, fade, trend](/blog/trading/event-trading/anatomy-of-a-news-reaction-spike-fade-trend). Here we apply it to jobs.

### The internals second move: AHE, the U-rate, and revisions

This is where jobs days are won and lost. The knee-jerk trades the headline; the **second move** trades the internals once desks have parsed them, and it frequently reverses the first reaction.

Take a strong-looking print: payrolls +250k versus +180k expected. The algos buy the beat. The futures spike up. But over the next ninety seconds, three things become clear: the prior two months were revised down by a combined −90k; average hourly earnings came in soft; and the unemployment rate ticked *up* a tenth. The real report is much weaker than the headline. The desks sell into the algos' spike, and the move reverses. The trader who waited and read the internals fades the knee-jerk for a clean trade; the trader who chased the headline is now offside.

![Before-and-after of a strong headline reversing once downward revisions and soft wages are read in the internals](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-nfp-release-cross-asset-playbook-4.png)

The reverse trap exists too: a *weak* headline (+120k versus +180k) that comes with *upward* revisions to the prior months and hot wages is less dovish than it looks, and an initial down-spike in yields can reverse upward. The rule is simple to state and hard to obey: **do not commit full size on the headline alone; wait for the internals.** Average hourly earnings and the revision are the two numbers that most often flip the sign of the second move.

#### Worked example: fading the headline on weak internals

It is an inflation regime. Payrolls print +250k against +180k expected — a beat. The S&P futures spike **+0.6%** in the first thirty seconds as algos buy the strong headline (good-news-is-bad would say this *should* be a sell, but the knee-jerk is mechanical and overshoots). You wait. The internals hit: a combined **−90k** downward revision and soft wages. You short \$20,000 of the S&P ETF at the top of the spike. The market reverses and closes **−1.0%** from your entry as desks digest that the labor market is softer than the headline.

- Entry: short \$20,000 of the S&P 500 ETF at the spike high.
- Move in your favor after the reversal: −1.0% (price falls, your short gains).
- P&L: \$20,000 × 0.010 = **+\$200.**
- Had you chased the +0.6% knee-jerk *long* with the same \$20,000, you would have lost roughly \$20,000 × (0.006 + 0.010) ≈ **−\$320** as the spike reversed past your entry.

The intuition: the ninety seconds you spend reading the revision is the edge — fading the algos' headline overshoot turned a +\$200 trade for the patient desk into a −\$320 loss for the impatient one.

### The cross-asset map: how each market moves, and how the sign flips

A jobs print does not hit one market; it hits the whole screen at once, because every asset is pricing the same thing — the future path of the Fed — through a different lens. Here is the per-asset transmission, with the regime dependence made explicit. (The general mechanism of one print rippling across markets is covered in [cross-asset transmission: how one print hits every market](/blog/trading/event-trading/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market).)

- **Bonds (the front end, the 2-year).** This is the *source* of the move. The two-year Treasury yield is the market's bet on where the Fed funds rate will be over the next couple of years, so it reacts most directly and most cleanly to a jobs surprise. A **strong** print pushes the front-end yield *up* (more Fed, or less easing) regardless of regime; a **weak** print pushes it *down*. On Feb 3, 2023 the 2y rose +18bp on the +517k blowout; on Aug 2, 2024 it fell −28bp on the weak +114k. The front end is where the surprise shows up most reliably.
- **Stocks (S&P 500).** The highest-volume reaction and the one whose *sign flips with the regime*. In an inflation regime, strong jobs → stocks down (rates up, valuations compress). In a growth-scare regime, weak jobs → stocks down (recession). The most rate-sensitive corners — long-duration tech, small caps — move most.
- **The US dollar (DXY).** The dollar tracks the rate path. A strong print that lifts US yields *relative to other countries* lifts the dollar (a wider rate gap pulls capital into dollars); a weak print that pulls US cuts forward weakens it. Feb 2023: DXY +1.2%. Aug 2024: DXY −0.4%. The dollar usually moves first and cleanest because it is a direct rate-differential trade.
- **Gold.** Gold pays no interest, so it competes with the *real* (inflation-adjusted) yield on cash and bonds. A strong print that pushes real yields up makes gold *less* attractive (gold down); a weak print that pulls real yields down lifts gold. Gold's reaction is therefore a real-yield trade, and it can decouple from the nominal-yield move when inflation expectations shift.
- **Crypto (Bitcoin).** The highest-beta read on global liquidity. Bitcoin behaves like a leveraged risk asset on jobs day: it amplifies the stock direction, often by several multiples. In the August 2024 cascade, Bitcoin fell roughly **−15%** as the growth scare and carry unwind hit risk assets across the board. (On why crypto trades as a macro-liquidity asset, see the macro-trading note on [crypto as a macro-liquidity asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/crypto-as-a-macro-liquidity-asset).)

A note on markets outside the US, because the US jobs report is a *global* event, not just an American one. Because the dollar and US yields are the backbone of global finance, the NFP reaction radiates outward overnight. By the time Asian markets open, they are trading the same Fed-path repricing the US set in motion. Vietnam's VN-Index, for example, does not react to US payrolls directly, but it inherits the consequences: a strong US print that lifts the dollar pressures the dong and can accelerate foreign selling on the Ho Chi Minh exchange, while a weak US print that pulls forward Fed cuts eases dollar pressure and gives the State Bank of Vietnam more room to keep policy loose. The August 2024 cascade hit emerging-market equities and currencies broadly as the global carry unwind forced selling everywhere. The lesson is the same at every latitude: the US jobs report sets the global cost of money for the month, and every market downstream prices off that — which is why even a domestically-focused trader anywhere on Earth should know which way the first Friday broke.

The figure below shows the actual cross-asset reaction to the +517k Feb 2023 blowout: stocks down, front-end yield up, dollar up — the textbook signature of a strong print in an inflation regime.

![Cross-asset reaction bars for the Feb 2023 jobs blowout with S&P down, 2-year yield up, dollar up](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-nfp-release-cross-asset-playbook-5.png)

The key insight is that the **front end and the dollar tell you the regime-independent part of the story** (strong → yields and dollar up, weak → down), while **stocks and crypto tell you the regime-dependent part** (whose sign flips). If you ever see stocks and the front end disagree about whether a print is "good" — stocks rallying while yields surge on a strong print — that is the market telling you it is in a growth-scare regime where strong jobs are *welcome*. Reading the cross-asset disagreement is how you confirm which regime you are in, in real time.

Why is the front end so much cleaner than equities? Because the two-year Treasury is almost a pure bet on the average Fed funds rate over the next two years, and the jobs report is, to first order, information about that path. Equities have to translate the rate-path change into a valuation change *and* an earnings-expectation change *and* a risk-appetite change — three channels that can partially cancel, which is exactly why the equity sign is regime-dependent and the front-end sign is not. When you want to express a high-conviction view on a jobs surprise with the least regime ambiguity, the front end is the instrument. Equities are where the regime debate plays out; the two-year is where the surprise gets priced.

#### Worked example: the dollar leg of a strong print

You expect a strong jobs print in an inflation regime and want to express it in the dollar rather than equities, because the dollar's sign does not depend on the regime. You buy **\$100,000** of dollar exposure (long DXY via a futures or ETF proxy). The print lands strong and the dollar rallies +1.2%, as it did on Feb 3, 2023.

- Position: long \$100,000 of dollar (DXY) exposure.
- Move in your favor: +1.2%.
- P&L: \$100,000 × 0.012 = **+\$1,200.**
- Compare the equity leg on the same print: the S&P fell only −1.04%, so a \$100,000 equity short would have made \$1,040 — but with far more regime risk, since a growth-scare reading could have sent stocks *up* on the same strong number.

The intuition: the dollar earned \$1,200 with a sign you could rely on regardless of regime, while the equity leg's \$1,040 came with the risk that the whole sign was wrong — on jobs day, the cleanest dollar of profit often comes from the cleanest instrument.

### Fade vs trend, and sizing for the whipsaw

After the internals are read, you face the fork: **fade** the move (bet it reverts) or **trend** with it (bet it continues). The decision rule is built around the expected move and the regime:

- **Fade** when the move is *inside* the expected move and the surprise is small or in-line. A +0.4% wobble on an in-line print is noise; the volatility crush after the event usually pulls it back. Fade small, with tight risk.
- **Trend** when the surprise is large *and* regime-confirming. A blowout strong print in a clear inflation regime, or a recession-signal weak print in a growth-scare regime, is not noise — it confirms the dominant fear, and these moves run for hours and sometimes days. **Do not fade a regime-confirming trend.** That is the cardinal sin of jobs-day trading: standing in front of a move that the whole macro narrative is pushing.

A useful way to make the fade-or-trend call concrete is to compare the realized move to the expected move in real time. If, fifteen minutes after the print, the S&P has moved less than one expected move (under ±0.8%) and the surprise was small or contradicted by the internals, you are in fade territory — the market is chopping and the post-event volatility crush favors mean reversion. If the S&P has already moved *more* than one expected move and the surprise was large and regime-confirming, you are in trend territory — the market has decided, and the move that ran past the expected move on a clean surprise tends to keep running into the close. The dangerous middle — a move right around one expected move with mixed internals — is where you should do nothing. Not every jobs print is a trade. The discipline to pass on the ambiguous ones is what keeps the whipsaw from grinding you down over a year of first Fridays.

Sizing is non-negotiable because jobs is a two-sided whipsaw. You size to the expected move and to a fixed dollar risk, then cut that in half because the headline-vs-internals reversal can stop you out on both sides of the same print. The reason for halving is mechanical: on a normal directional event you might lose your full stop once if you are wrong; on a jobs print with a headline-vs-internals reversal, you can be stopped on the knee-jerk *and* on the reversal — two stops in five minutes from one report. Sizing as if you will only be tested once is how an account bleeds out on jobs Fridays even when the directional calls are mostly right.

#### Worked example: sizing to the expected move

The S&P 500 ETF trades at \$500 a share and the options market implies a jobs-day expected move of **±0.8%**. You decide your maximum acceptable loss on this event is **\$400**. How big a position can you take if your stop is set at the expected move?

- Expected-move dollar swing per share: \$500 × 0.008 = **\$4.00** per share.
- You want max loss = \$400, with the stop one expected-move away (\$4.00 per share).
- Shares: \$400 ÷ \$4.00 = **100 shares.**
- Position size: 100 × \$500 = **\$50,000.**
- If the trade goes one expected move against you and stops out: 100 × \$4.00 = **\$400**, exactly your budget.

The intuition: sizing to the expected move converts a vague "don't risk too much" into a hard number — a \$400 risk budget on a ±0.8% event is a \$50,000 position, and on a noisy jobs day you might halve even that to survive the two-sided whipsaw.

The full per-asset decision tree — each asset's strong response, weak response, and the level that *invalidates* the trade — looks like this:

![Per-asset jobs-day tree for stocks crypto dollar gold and bonds with strong weak and invalidation in each node](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-nfp-release-cross-asset-playbook-6.png)

The invalidation line in each node is the discipline. For stocks, the invalidation is the tape closing *opposite* the surprise — if a strong print closes the S&P green in an inflation regime, your bearish read was wrong and you are out. For the dollar, it is the DXY falling on a clear strong print. For bonds, it is yields falling on a clear strong print. Writing the invalidation *before* the print is what stops you from turning a small, defined loss into a large, hopeful one.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Now the dated case studies, with real numbers, that prove the regime thesis. Two jobs prints, both bearish for stocks, for opposite reasons.

### February 3, 2023 — the +517k blowout (strong → stocks down)

Coming into early 2023, the market was squarely in an **inflation regime**. CPI was still running hot, the Fed was hiking, and the dominant fear was higher-for-longer rates. Then the January jobs report landed: **+517,000** payrolls against a consensus near **+187,000**, with the unemployment rate at **3.4%**, a fifty-three-year low. On any naive reading this was a phenomenal economy.

The market read it as bad news, because in an inflation regime a red-hot labor market means the Fed cannot ease and may have to do more. The reaction:

- **S&P 500: −1.04%** on the day. Stocks fell on a blowout jobs number.
- **2-year Treasury yield: +18 basis points.** The front end repriced for a Fed that would stay restrictive.
- **US dollar (DXY): +1.2%.** A wider rate gap pulled capital into dollars.

This is the cleanest illustration of **good-news-is-bad**: the economy was strong, and precisely *because* it was strong, the Fed-sensitive assets sold off. The bar chart at the top of the post — the two prints side by side — shows this −1.04% next to the August 2024 number, and the lesson lands immediately: a monster beat was bearish.

#### Worked example: the strong-print short on the front end

You are positioned for the Feb 2023 strong scenario. You are short **\$1,000,000** face of the two-year Treasury (DV01 ≈ **\$190 per basis point**), and you are also long a modest \$30,000 of the S&P that you intended to flatten before the print but did not.

- Front-end short: the 2y yield rises +18bp, the bond price falls, your short gains \$190 × 18 = **+\$3,420.**
- Stranded equity long: the S&P falls −1.04%, costing \$30,000 × (−0.0104) = **−\$312.**
- Net: +\$3,420 − \$312 = **+\$3,108.**
- The discipline lesson: had you flattened the \$30,000 long as your map instructed, the trade would have been a clean +\$3,420 instead of +\$3,108.

The intuition: in an inflation regime the front-end short *is* the strong-jobs trade — \$3,420 of the \$3,108 net came from +18bp on the 2-year, and the stray equity long was just leakage.

### August 2, 2024 — the weak +114k and the carry cascade (weak → stocks down)

By the summer of 2024, the regime had flipped to a **growth scare**. Inflation had cooled enough that the market's dominant fear was no longer the Fed staying high — it was that the Fed had stayed high *too long* and the economy was rolling over. Into that fear came the July jobs report: just **+114,000** payrolls against a consensus near **+175,000**, and the unemployment rate jumped to **4.3%**. That rise tripped a widely-watched recession indicator (the Sahm rule, which flags recession when the unemployment rate's three-month average rises half a point above its prior-year low). The reaction:

- **S&P 500: −1.84%** on the day. Stocks fell on a *weak* jobs number — the opposite cause from 2023, the same direction of outcome.
- **2-year Treasury yield: −28 basis points.** The front end repriced hard for emergency cuts.
- **US dollar (DXY): −0.4%.** A softer dollar as US cuts were pulled forward.

But the story did not end on Friday. The weak US print combined with a Bank of Japan rate hike from days earlier (July 31) to detonate a **carry-trade unwind** over the weekend and into Monday. The yen-funded carry trade — borrowing cheap yen to buy higher-yielding assets worldwide — went into reverse as the yen surged. The cascade on Monday, August 5:

- **Nikkei: −12.4%** — Japan's worst single day since the 1987 crash.
- **S&P 500: −3.0%** on the Monday, on top of Friday's −1.84%.
- **VIX (the fear gauge): an intraday spike to 65.73**, from a close near 23 the prior Friday — a once-a-decade volatility event.
- **Bitcoin: roughly −15%**, behaving as the highest-beta risk asset on the screen.

This episode is the mirror image of February 2023, and together they make the thesis undeniable: a weak print crashed stocks in a growth-scare regime exactly as a strong print crashed them in an inflation regime. The deeper mechanics of how a single weak print fed a global leverage unwind are exactly the carry-unwind dynamics described in the macro-trading post on [carry-trade unwinds: 1998, 2008, 2024](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks).

The cascade is worth dwelling on because it teaches the single most underappreciated jobs-day lesson: **a data surprise can be the spark for a fire that was already built.** The fuel here was the yen carry trade. For years, traders had borrowed yen at near-zero rates and bought higher-yielding assets — US tech, emerging-market bonds, anything with a yield above Japan's. As long as the yen stayed weak and the assets rose, the trade printed money. Then two things happened within days: the Bank of Japan hiked on July 31, lifting the cost of the yen funding leg, and the weak US jobs print on August 2 pulled forward expectations of US rate cuts, narrowing the yield gap that made the carry trade worthwhile. The yen surged from above 161 per dollar in early July toward 142 by August 5 — a violent move for a major currency. Every carry position was suddenly underwater on both legs at once, and the forced unwind fed on itself: selling assets to repay yen pushed the yen higher, which deepened the losses, which forced more selling.

The point for a jobs-day trader is humility about scale. The +114k print, on its own, "deserved" something like the −1.84% same-day S&P move. The −3.0% Monday, the −12.4% Nikkei, and the VIX spike to 65.73 were not the print's doing alone — they were the print acting as the trigger on a pre-loaded leverage position. You cannot predict which jobs print will be a trigger, but you can respect the possibility every time by sizing for a tail and by watching the assets that reveal hidden leverage: the yen, the VIX, and credit spreads. When those start to move violently on a jobs print, you are no longer in a normal reaction — you are in a deleveraging, and the rules change. In a deleveraging, correlations go to one, "safe" diversifiers stop diversifying, and the only thing that helps is having had less risk on beforehand.

#### Worked example: the weak-print equity loss

You walked into August 2, 2024 long \$25,000 of the S&P 500 ETF, not having flattened, and not having recognized the growth-scare regime. The weak +114k print sends the S&P down −1.84% on the day.

- Position: long \$25,000 of the S&P 500 ETF.
- Day-one move: −1.84%.
- Day-one P&L: \$25,000 × (−0.0184) = **−\$460.**
- If you held through Monday's additional −3.0%: \$25,000 × (−0.030) = a further **−\$750**, for a two-day total near **−\$1,210.**
- A \$25,000 Bitcoin position, at roughly −15% over the same window, would have lost \$25,000 × (−0.15) = **−\$3,750** — the highest-beta asset took the worst of it.

The intuition: the same long that *would* have been right in a growth-scare regime *if* the print had been strong was catastrophically wrong on a weak print — a −\$460 day-one loss that compounded toward −\$1,210, and far worse in crypto.

### What the two episodes prove together

Lay the two prints side by side, as the cover bar chart does: a +517k blowout sent the S&P −1.04%; a weak +114k sent it −1.84%. One was the strongest jobs surprise in years, the other a clear miss. Both were bearish for stocks. The only thing that explains both is the **regime**: in an inflation regime the market punishes strength; in a growth-scare regime it punishes weakness. If you are not asking "which regime?" before "strong or weak?", you are guessing.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of myths cost beginners real money on jobs day. Each is corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "Strong jobs is good for stocks."** This is the big one, and it is *regime-dependent*. In the February 2023 inflation regime, the strongest jobs surprise in years (+517k) sent the S&P **−1.04%**. Strong was bad. The rule "strong economy → buy stocks" would have lost you money on exactly the day it felt most obviously right. Always name the regime first.

**Myth 2: "Weak jobs is good for stocks because it means rate cuts."** Also regime-dependent and also wrong in the wrong regime. In the August 2024 growth scare, a weak +114k print sent the S&P **−1.84%** on the day and another **−3.0%** the following Monday. When the market fears recession, weak jobs confirm the fear faster than the promise of cuts can offset it. Bad news is bad when the dominant fear is recession.

**Myth 3: "Trade the headline payroll number."** The headline is the *least* reliable part of the report. A +250k headline with a −90k two-month revision and soft wages is a weak report wearing a strong mask. The knee-jerk buys the headline; the second move sells the internals. The patient desk that waited ninety seconds to read the revision turned a chased +0.6% spike into a +\$200 fade while the impatient one lost roughly **−\$320**.

**Myth 4: "The reaction is over in the first minute."** On jobs day, the most important move is often the *second* one, and the largest move can come *days* later. The August 2024 weak print's worst damage — Nikkei −12.4%, VIX to 65.73 — landed the following Monday as the carry trade unwound. Sizing as if the event resolves in one minute is how you get run over by the trend that follows.

**Myth 5: "Crypto is uncorrelated, so it's a safe place to hide on jobs day."** Bitcoin is the *highest-beta* read on global liquidity, not a hedge. In the August 2024 cascade it fell roughly **−15%** — far more than the S&P's −3.0% — because it amplifies the risk-on/risk-off swing rather than dampening it. On a macro-shock jobs day, crypto is the accelerator, not the brake. The uncorrelated story works only on quiet days when no macro shock is forcing a global repricing of risk; on exactly the days that matter most — the violent jobs prints — crypto's correlation to equities jumps toward one and its higher beta makes it the worst, not the best, place to be hiding.

**Myth 6: "The unemployment rate confirms the payroll number."** They come from different surveys and routinely disagree. A +200k payroll print alongside an unemployment rate that *rose* is a genuinely mixed report, and the market often weights the rising unemployment rate more heavily because, in a growth scare, the U-rate is the recession tell. In July 2024, payrolls were merely soft at +114k, but it was the U-rate jumping to 4.3% — tripping the Sahm recession signal — that turned a soft report into a market event. Read both surveys, and when they conflict, ask which one the current regime cares about most.

## The playbook: how to trade the jobs report

Here is the full if-then, in the order you execute it. The sequence matters: regime first, surprise second, internals third, sizing throughout.

![Five-step jobs-day playbook pipeline from naming the regime to sizing for whipsaw to never fading a regime-confirming trend](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-nfp-release-cross-asset-playbook-7.png)

**Step 1 — Name the regime.** Before anything, decide what the market is most afraid of right now. Is the dominant fear inflation and higher-for-longer (an inflation regime), or recession and a Fed that is too late (a growth scare)? The fastest live read is the cross-asset disagreement: in an inflation regime, strong data lifts yields and *hurts* stocks; in a growth scare, strong data lifts yields and *helps* stocks. This single choice sets the sign of everything that follows.

**Step 2 — Read the surprise, not the number.** Compute actual minus consensus. A +250k print is *weak* if consensus was +320k and *strong* if consensus was +50k. Only the surprise is new information; the consensus is already in the price.

**Step 3 — Size for the whipsaw.** Use the expected move (roughly ±0.8% on the S&P on a normal jobs day) as your risk yardstick. Set a fixed dollar risk budget, derive the position from it, then cut it in half because the headline-vs-internals reversal can stop you out on both sides of the same print. A \$400 risk budget on a ±0.8% event is a \$50,000 S&P position before halving.

**Step 4 — Wait for the internals.** Do not commit full size on the headline alone. Read average hourly earnings, the unemployment rate, and especially the two-month revision. The second move — driven by the internals — frequently reverses the knee-jerk. This ninety-second patience is your edge over the algos.

**Step 5 — Fade or trend, and never fade the regime.** Fade a small, in-line wobble inside the expected move; the post-event volatility crush will help you. Trend with a large, *regime-confirming* surprise — a strong blowout in an inflation regime, or a recession-signal weak print in a growth scare — because those moves run. The cardinal rule: **never fade a print that confirms the dominant regime fear.** That is the move with the whole macro narrative behind it.

The per-asset positions, given a directional surprise and a named regime:

- **Bonds (2y front end):** the cleanest, most regime-independent trade. Strong → short the front end (yields up, price down); weak → long it. Feb 2023: +18bp; Aug 2024: −28bp. Invalidation: yields move *opposite* the surprise on a clear print.
- **Stocks (S&P 500):** the regime-dependent core. Inflation regime: strong → short, weak → long. Growth scare: strong → long, weak → short. Invalidation: the tape closes opposite your read.
- **US dollar (DXY):** tracks the rate path. Strong → long dollar; weak → short. Invalidation: dollar moves opposite a clear strong/weak print.
- **Gold:** a real-yield trade. Strong (real yields up) → short; weak (real yields down) → long. Invalidation: gold rises while real yields rise.
- **Crypto (Bitcoin):** the highest-beta expression of the stock direction. Use it to *amplify* a high-conviction risk view, with smaller size because the beta cuts both ways. Invalidation: BTC fades the stock direction.

Tie it back to the two episodes. February 2023: inflation regime, strong surprise → short stocks, short the front end (the front-end short made +\$3,420 on \$1,000,000 at +18bp), long the dollar. August 2024: growth scare, weak surprise → short stocks (a long lost −\$460 on \$25,000 day one), long the front end, and respect that the move can cascade for days. Both times, the trader who named the regime first, read the surprise second, waited for the internals, and refused to fade the regime-confirming trend was on the right side. The number was never the point. The reaction was.

If you internalize one habit from this entire playbook, make it the order of operations: **regime, then surprise, then internals, then size, then fade-or-trend.** Beginners reverse it — they start with a guess about the number, build a position, and only afterward wonder what regime they are in. That order is exactly backwards, and the two episodes show the cost: a perfectly correct read on the data ("the economy is strong") lost money in February 2023 because the regime made strength bearish, and a perfectly correct read ("the economy is weakening") lost money in August 2024 because the regime made weakness bearish too. The data was never wrong. The framing was. Jobs day rewards the trader who knows that the labor market and the stock market are answering different questions, and who reads them in the right order.

One last reminder about expectations management. Over a full year of first Fridays, you will not have a strong, tradable view on most of them. Many jobs prints land near consensus with mixed internals and produce a chop that is not worth the risk. The edge does not come from trading every report; it comes from being prepared for every report and acting decisively only on the few where the regime is clear, the surprise is large, and the internals confirm the headline. The map is the work. The trade is the easy part — and most months, the right trade is no trade.
