> **TL;DR** — The Fed only decides on rates eight times a year, but it *talks* almost every week, and the talk moves markets as much as the decisions. Minutes, speeches, testimony, and Jackson Hole all reprice the rate path between meetings, then a ten-day blackout slams the comms shut right before each decision.
>
> - The between-meeting calendar is dense: the **minutes** drop three weeks after each decision, a near-daily stream of **Fedspeak** runs through the cycle, **Congressional testimony** lands twice a year, and **Jackson Hole** is the August set-piece.
> - The reaction depends on **who is speaking** and whether they *change the path*. The Chair and the rotating **voters** move rates; a non-voting regional president moves them far less. The same hawkish line is a trade when the Chair says it and noise when a non-voter says it.
> - The trade is to **weight every message by speaker and by new information**, ride the set-piece events, and **fade the tape's overreaction** to a non-voter. Into the blackout, markets drift quietly because the last guidance is already priced.
> - The one number to remember: in 2013 Chair **Bernanke merely hinted** at slowing bond buys and the 10-year Treasury yield rocketed from **1.66% in May to 3.04% by year-end** — a hint, not a hike, repriced the entire bond market.

In May 2013 the Federal Reserve did not raise interest rates. It did not cut them. It did not change a single word of policy. The fed funds rate stayed pinned near zero, where it had sat since the financial crisis. And yet over the following seven months, the most important price in global finance — the yield on the 10-year US Treasury note — nearly doubled, climbing from about 1.66% to 3.04%. Mortgage rates jumped. Emerging-market currencies cracked. Stocks wobbled. Trillions of dollars of bonds repriced.

What happened? On May 22, 2013, in testimony before Congress, Chair Ben Bernanke was asked whether the Fed might slow the pace of its bond-buying program. He answered, in effect, *maybe, in the next few meetings, if the data cooperate.* That was it. No decision, no date, no number. A hint. The bond market took that hint and ran, repricing the entire expected path of policy on the strength of one carefully hedged sentence. Markets later named the episode the **taper tantrum**, and it became the cleanest demonstration in modern history of a simple, under-appreciated truth: **the Fed moves markets far more often through its words than through its decisions.**

Most beginners learn that markets care about the eight scheduled FOMC meetings a year — the days the committee announces a rate decision. That is true, but it badly undersells how the rate path actually gets set. Between those eight decisions, the Fed releases the *minutes* of each meeting three weeks later, sends officials out to give speeches almost every business day, testifies before Congress twice a year, and gathers for an annual symposium at Jackson Hole that has launched more than one regime change. And for ten days before every decision, all of that goes dark in the **blackout period**. This post is the trader's map of that between-meeting communications cycle: what each piece is, how the market parses it, why *who* is speaking matters as much as *what* they say, and how you trade the whole thing.

## Foundations: the between-meeting communications cycle

Before we trade any of it, we need the vocabulary. The Federal Open Market Committee — the FOMC — is the Fed's rate-setting body. It meets eight times a year, roughly every six weeks, and at each meeting it decides the target range for the federal funds rate (the overnight rate that anchors all other rates). For the mechanics of *how* the Fed actually sets and transmits that rate, see the companion piece on the [central bank toolkit: rates, QE, QT, and forward guidance](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance). Here we are interested in everything that happens *between* those eight decisions.

**The statement and the presser.** On decision day, at 2:00 PM Eastern, the FOMC releases a short policy *statement* and, since 2011, the Chair holds a *press conference* half an hour later. Four times a year (March, June, September, December) the meeting also includes the **Summary of Economic Projections**, which contains the famous **dot plot** — each official's anonymous projection for where rates will be at the end of the next few years. The decision day itself is covered in the macro series' note on [trading the FOMC statement, presser, and dot plot](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi); this post starts the morning after.

**The minutes.** Roughly three weeks after each meeting, the Fed publishes the **minutes** — a detailed, several-thousand-word account of the *discussion* that produced the decision. The minutes are not a transcript (the full transcript is released only after five years), but they are far richer than the statement. They tell you who worried about what, how close the committee was to a different decision, and which way the internal debate was tilting. Because they arrive three weeks late, the *decision* in the minutes is old news. The *debate* is not.

**Fedspeak.** Between meetings, individual Fed officials — the Chair, the other governors in Washington, and the twelve regional Federal Reserve Bank presidents — give speeches, sit for interviews, and appear on panels. Traders call this stream **Fedspeak**. On a busy week there might be five or six separate appearances. Each one is an opportunity for an official to nudge the market's expectations, and the market hangs on every adjective. The whole point of Fedspeak, from the Fed's side, is **forward guidance**: shaping expectations about future policy without having to actually change rates today.

**Hawks and doves.** Officials sit on a spectrum of policy stance. A **hawk** worries most about inflation and tends to want rates higher and held there longer. A **dove** worries most about unemployment and growth, and tends to want rates lower and cut sooner. These are not insults — they describe a genuine difference in how each official weighs the Fed's two legal goals (its **dual mandate**: stable prices *and* maximum employment). When a known hawk sounds dovish, or a known dove sounds hawkish, that *change in tone* is itself a tradable signal, because it suggests the whole committee may be shifting.

**Voters versus non-voters.** Here is the crucial wrinkle most beginners miss. Not every official who speaks gets to *vote*. The FOMC has twelve voting seats: the seven Washington governors, the president of the New York Fed (always a voter), and four of the remaining eleven regional presidents, who **rotate** the vote among themselves each year. So in any given year, several regional presidents are giving speeches but *cannot vote on the decision*. Their words still carry information — they reflect the internal debate — but they carry less weight than a voter's, and far less than the Chair's.

**Congressional testimony.** Twice a year, by law, the Fed Chair testifies before Congress to deliver the *Monetary Policy Report* — once to the House, once to the Senate, on consecutive days. These hearings (still informally called the **Humphrey-Hawkins testimony** after the 1978 law that created them) are set-piece events. The Chair's prepared remarks are scripted, but the question-and-answer session is live and unpredictable, and it has produced some of the largest between-meeting market moves in history. The 2013 taper tantrum started in exactly such a hearing.

**Jackson Hole.** Every August, the Federal Reserve Bank of Kansas City hosts an economic symposium at Jackson Hole, Wyoming. It is not an FOMC meeting and produces no decision, but the Chair's keynote speech there has become a venue for signaling big shifts in the framework. Markets treat Jackson Hole as a scheduled, high-variance event — a place where the Fed lays out the *philosophy* that will drive the next several decisions.

**The blackout.** Finally, for the ten days before each meeting — from the second Saturday before the decision through the Thursday after — Fed officials observe a self-imposed **blackout** (formally, the "external communications blackout"). No speeches, no interviews, no new guidance on policy. The idea is to prevent any official from moving markets, or appearing to leak the decision, in the run-up to the vote. The practical effect for traders is profound: in the blackout, the *last* piece of guidance is already priced, and the market drifts into the decision on positioning and incoming data rather than fresh Fed words.

Put it all together and you get a repeating six-week cycle: decision → minutes (+3 weeks) → a stream of speeches → blackout (−10 days) → next decision. The rate path — the market's best guess at where rates are headed, which you can read directly off [fed funds futures](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) — gets nudged at *every* stage, not just on the eight decision days.

**Why "the path" is the thing that moves, not "the rate."** This is the mental model that ties the whole post together, so it is worth stating plainly. The Fed sets *one* number on decision day: the target range for the overnight rate. But almost nothing in the economy is priced off the overnight rate directly. Your mortgage is priced off a 10- or 30-year rate. A company's borrowing cost is priced off a multi-year corporate yield. A stock's valuation depends on a discount rate stretching out for decades. All of those long-term rates are built from the market's *expectation of the entire future sequence of overnight rates* — the path — plus a risk premium. So when the Fed *signals* that the path will be higher or lower than the market thought, every long-term rate reprices immediately, before the Fed has changed anything today. That is the whole trick of forward guidance: by moving expectations of the path, the Fed moves long rates without spending a single decision. The 2013 taper tantrum is the purest example — the overnight rate did not budge, but the *expected path* of bond-buying changed, and the 10-year repriced +138 bp.

**What the dual mandate has to do with all this.** Congress gave the Fed two goals: stable prices (operationalized as 2% inflation) and maximum employment (a longer-run unemployment rate the Fed estimates around 4%). Every official is, in effect, trading off those two goals in their own head, and where they land determines whether they sound hawkish or dovish. A hawk puts more weight on the inflation half of the mandate; a dove puts more weight on the employment half. When you read a speech or the minutes, you are really reading *how the committee is currently weighting the two halves of the mandate* — and a shift in that weighting, even with no change in the rate, is what reprices the path. This is why "is the data good or bad?" is the wrong question; the right question is "which half of the mandate is the Fed prioritizing right now, and which way does this news bend *that*?" The companion piece on [the reaction function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) develops this at length.

### The 2013 taper tantrum: a hint, not a hike

The taper tantrum is the foundational case, so let us put real numbers on it before going deeper. In late 2012 the Fed was buying \$85 billion of bonds a month under its third quantitative-easing program (QE3), holding long-term rates down. "Tapering" meant *slowing* those purchases — not selling bonds, not even stopping buying, just buying *less* each month. It is a second-derivative idea: the Fed would still be adding stimulus, just at a decelerating pace.

On May 22, 2013, Bernanke told the Joint Economic Committee of Congress that "in the next few meetings" the Fed *could* begin to taper if the labor market continued to improve. The 10-year yield, which had been around 1.66% at the start of the month and 1.94% the day before, began to climb immediately. By the June 19 FOMC press conference, where Bernanke laid out a fuller taper timeline, the 10-year sat at 2.31%. By early September it was 2.99%. By year-end, 3.04%.

![The 2013 taper tantrum showing the 10-year Treasury yield climbing from 1.66 to 3.04 percent](/imgs/blogs/fed-speak-minutes-and-the-blackout-period-2.png)

That is a **+138 basis point** move in the most-watched long rate on the planet — and the Fed never touched the policy rate. The first actual taper did not come until December 2013, and the first rate hike not until December 2015, two and a half years later. The market repriced the *path* on words alone. This is the entire thesis of the post in one episode: between meetings, communication *is* policy.

#### Worked example: the taper tantrum on a long bond position

Suppose in early May 2013 you owned a \$500,000 position with the interest-rate sensitivity of a 10-year Treasury. The relevant risk measure is **DV01** — the dollar change in your position's value for a one-basis-point move in yield. A 10-year note has a modified duration of roughly 8.6 years, so DV01 ≈ \$500,000 × 8.6 × 0.0001 = about **\$430 per basis point**.

- The yield rose from **1.66% to 3.04%**, a move of **+138 basis points**.
- Bonds fall when yields rise, so a long position *loses* on a yield jump.
- Loss ≈ \$430/bp × 138 bp = **−\$59,340** over the full move.
- Even just the May-22-hint-to-June-FOMC leg, +65 bp (1.66% to 2.31%), cost ≈ \$430 × 65 = **−\$27,950**.

A single hedged sentence in Congressional testimony, with no policy change, cost a long bondholder nearly **\$60,000 on a half-million-dollar position**. That is why bond traders parse Fedspeak word by word.

## The minutes: what the three-week-old detail reveals

The minutes seem like they should be useless. They come out three weeks after the meeting, and by then everyone already knows what the committee decided — the statement, the dot plot, and the press conference all landed on decision day. So why do markets routinely move on the minutes, sometimes by 10 to 20 basis points in the 2-year yield within minutes of the 2:00 PM release?

Because the *decision* is old news but the *debate* is new. The statement is a terse paragraph hammered out by consensus; it tells you the outcome but hides the disagreement. The minutes pull back the curtain. They tell you how the committee got there, how unanimous or fractured it was, and which risks it is most focused on — which is the best available read on what it will do *next*.

![How the market parses the FOMC minutes for dissents, count words, and risk language](/imgs/blogs/fed-speak-minutes-and-the-blackout-period-4.png)

Traders mine the minutes for three things.

**Dissents.** When an official votes against the decision, the minutes record it and explain why. A hawkish dissent (someone wanted to hike when the committee held) signals there is pressure inside the room for tighter policy than the statement implies. A dovish dissent signals the opposite. A *string* of dissents tells you the consensus is fragile and a turn may be near. Markets price the next decision partly on how lonely or crowded the Chair's position looks.

**The count words.** The minutes are written in a peculiar, quantified dialect. Instead of naming officials, they describe how many held each view using a calibrated ladder of words: roughly, "**a couple**" or "**a few**" (the smallest group) < "**some**" < "**several**" < "**many**" < "**most**" < "**almost all**" < "**all**." When the minutes say "*several* participants" thought a cut might soon be appropriate, that is a smaller faction than "*many* participants." Veteran Fed watchers literally diff the count words against the previous meeting's minutes: if "*a few*" wanting cuts becomes "*several*" wanting cuts, the dovish faction is growing, and the path reprices lower. These words are chosen deliberately by the staff precisely *because* the market reads them this way.

**The risk language.** The minutes describe how the committee sees the balance of risks — whether the bigger danger is that inflation reaccelerates (upside risk) or that the economy weakens (downside risk). A shift in that framing, even with no change in the decision, tells the market which way the committee will lean at the next fork.

Here is how a Fed watcher actually reads a paragraph of minutes. Suppose the previous meeting's minutes said: "*A few* participants noted that it might become appropriate to reduce the policy rate later this year if inflation continued to ease." Then the new minutes say: "*Several* participants noted that it might become appropriate to reduce the policy rate at an upcoming meeting." Two things just changed. First, the *count word* climbed from "a few" to "several" — the dovish faction grew. Second, the *timing language* sharpened from "later this year" to "an upcoming meeting" — the urgency rose. Neither sentence announces a cut. But together they tell the market the committee is closer to easing than it was six weeks ago, and the front end of the curve will reprice lower on the release. A trader who diffs the two paragraphs side by side has the edge over one who only reads the headline summary on the wire.

The reverse pattern is just as tradable. If "several participants" worried about upside inflation risk becomes "most participants" worried about it, the hawkish faction has grown, the count word has climbed, and the path reprices *higher*. The minutes are, in this sense, a slow-motion vote count — a way to see how the internal balance of power is shifting between the eight formal decisions. That is information you simply cannot get from the one-paragraph statement, and it is why a document about a three-week-old meeting can still move billions of dollars of bonds the moment it hits the wire at 2:00 PM.

#### Worked example: a hawkish minutes surprise on a short-dated position

Say the minutes reveal that *more* officials than expected wanted to keep rates high — the count word moved from "several" to "most" — and the market reprices the near-term path *up* by 12 basis points in the 2-year yield. You hold a \$1,000,000 position with the rate sensitivity of a 2-year Treasury. A 2-year note has modified duration ≈ 1.9 years, so:

- DV01 ≈ \$1,000,000 × 1.9 × 0.0001 = about **\$190 per basis point**.
- The 2-year yield repriced **+12 bp** on the hawkish surprise.
- A long 2-year position loses: \$190/bp × 12 bp = **−\$2,280**.
- The S&P, which dislikes a higher path, might also slip ~0.5%; on a \$25,000 equity book that is another **−\$125**.

Three-week-old minutes, no policy change, and a careful reader of the count words still booked a **\$2,280** loss on the unprepared long. The lesson: the minutes are old on the surface and fresh underneath.

It is worth contrasting this with how the market treats the *same* committee's actual decision. On the FOMC decision in [December 2018](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently), the committee hiked and the dots kept two more hikes pencilled in for 2019 — more hawkish than the market wanted — and the S&P reversed to close **−1.54%**. The minutes three weeks later then *re-litigated* that same decision, and the market moved again as it learned how divided the room had been. One meeting, two separate repricings, three weeks apart.

## Fedspeak: reading individual officials

If the minutes are the committee's collective voice three weeks late, Fedspeak is the live, individual voice in real time. Between meetings, officials are out almost daily, and a single sentence from the right person can shift the rate path more than a full economic release. But the operative phrase is *the right person*. Fedspeak is not one signal — it is a dozen overlapping signals of wildly different weight, and the skill is in the weighting.

![Hawk to dove spectrum of Fed officials with voter status flags](/imgs/blogs/fed-speak-minutes-and-the-blackout-period-3.png)

**Weight by seniority.** The Chair sits at the top. The Chair's words are the closest thing to official guidance there is between meetings; the Chair both shapes and reflects the committee consensus, and markets treat a Chair remark as near-policy. For the role and how it has evolved, see [Jerome Powell and the modern Fed chair](/blog/trading/finance/jerome-powell-and-the-modern-fed-chair). Below the Chair, the other Washington-based governors and the New York Fed president carry heavy weight (all are permanent voters). Below them sit the regional presidents, whose weight depends heavily on whether they vote this year.

**Weight by vote.** This is the part that separates pros from beginners. In any given year, only four of the eleven non-New-York regional presidents vote. A hawkish speech from a *voting* regional president is a real input to the next decision. The identical speech from a *non-voting* president is mostly information about the internal debate — useful, but not a vote that will be cast. Markets that overreact to a non-voter's hawkish soundbite frequently give the move back within a day, which is one of the cleaner fade setups in the comms calendar.

**Weight by stance.** Knowing each official's place on the hawk-dove spectrum lets you grade their remarks for *surprise*. A famously hawkish president sounding hawkish is not news; the market has already discounted it. The same president sounding *dovish* — softening, acknowledging downside risk, opening the door to cuts — is a genuine surprise, because it suggests even the hawks are shifting. The signal is in the *deviation from each speaker's known prior*, not the absolute hawkishness of the words. This is the surprise framework applied to people instead of data prints: see [why news moves markets: the surprise framework](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) for the general principle.

**Weight by new information.** Finally, ask whether the remark actually *changed* anything. Most Fedspeak is a faithful restatement of the last meeting's message — officials are, on purpose, singing from the same hymn sheet. A speech that repeats the consensus moves nothing even if it sounds dramatic in a headline. A speech that *breaks* from the consensus — a first official floating a cut, a hawk conceding on inflation — is the one that reprices the path. The headline algorithms that trade the wire in milliseconds cannot always tell the difference; the human edge is knowing when a "hawkish" headline is just last month's message recycled.

### The microstructure of a speech: how the move actually happens

It helps to understand the mechanics of a Fedspeak move, because the *shape* of the reaction is what you trade. When an official speaks, the prepared text and the headlines hit news wires (Bloomberg, Reuters) as machine-readable flashes. Algorithmic systems scan those flashes for keywords — "inflation," "patient," "data-dependent," "restrictive," "cut," "pain" — and fire orders in milliseconds, before a human has finished reading the sentence. This produces the *spike*: an instant, often violent first move on the headline keyword alone.

But the keyword algorithms are crude. They cannot reliably tell whether "the Fed may need to do more" is a *new* hawkish signal or the speaker's standard line for the past six months, and they do not know whether the speaker is a voter. So the spike frequently *overshoots*. Then, over the next minutes to hours, human traders and slower models digest the full context — who said it, whether it was new, how it fits the consensus — and the move either *fades* (if the spike overreacted to a non-event) or *trends* (if the message genuinely repriced the path). This spike-then-fade-or-trend pattern is the same anatomy that governs reactions to data prints, covered in detail in the series note on [the anatomy of a news reaction: spike, fade, trend](/blog/trading/event-trading/anatomy-of-a-news-reaction-spike-fade-trend); Fedspeak just substitutes a person's words for a number.

The practical implication: the *first* print is the algorithm's guess, not the market's considered view. If you have done the speaker-weighting homework in advance, you can often tell within seconds whether the spike is a real path repricing to follow or an overreaction to fade. The reader who knows that the speaker is a non-voting hawk repeating last month's line is positioned to fade the algorithmic spike before the slower money figures it out.

### A worked illustration of the spectrum

Make the spectrum concrete with the kind of cast you actually track. Through 2022–23, a few regional presidents built reputations as the committee's most hawkish voices — repeatedly pushing for higher rates and a higher terminal level. Others, particularly some governors and a couple of regional doves, were the first to talk about slowing the pace of hikes and eventually cutting. The Chair sat in the center, setting the consensus the others orbited.

Now apply the weighting. When the hawkish camp said "higher for longer" in early 2023, it was no surprise — that was their known stance, and they were often non-voters that year, so the path barely moved. But when the *Chair* shifted in late 2023 from "we are not thinking about cuts" to acknowledging that the committee had *discussed* the timing of cuts, *that* was a path-repricing event, because it came from the one voice that always votes and always sets the consensus. Same topic — rate cuts — wildly different market impact, decided entirely by *who* and *whether it was new*. Grading speakers this way is the core skill of trading the comms cycle.

#### Worked example: fading a non-voting hawk

A non-voting regional president gives a speech at 9:00 AM and says inflation is "not yet beaten" and the Fed "may need to do more." Bond-trading algorithms sell the front end on the hawkish keyword, and the 2-year yield ticks up; a small rate-sensitive position you hold drops about 1% — call it a **\$10,000** notional overreaction in your book that is now showing a paper loss.

- You recognize the speaker is a **non-voter** this year and a **known hawk** — so the speech is neither a vote nor a surprise.
- You judge the move an overreaction and fade it: you add to the position at the dip.
- Over the next session the move reverts ~1% as the market re-weights the speaker.
- Recovery on the faded \$10,000 ≈ +1% = **+\$100**, plus you avoided selling the bottom.

Small in dollars, but it is a repeatable edge: the comms calendar serves up several "non-voting hawk spooks the tape" setups a quarter, and grading the speaker correctly is the whole trade.

## Jackson Hole and testimony: the set-piece events

Most Fedspeak is low-stakes background noise. But two recurring events are different in kind: they are *scheduled*, *high-variance*, and reliably move every asset class. Treat them the way you treat an FOMC decision or a CPI print — as a calendared event with an [expected move](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) you can size around.

**Congressional testimony** comes twice a year and is genuinely two-sided. The prepared statement is released before the Chair speaks and is usually a careful restatement of the policy stance — low surprise. The danger and opportunity are in the **Q&A**, where members of Congress ask unscripted questions and the Chair answers live. There is no teleprompter and no committee to hide behind. The 2013 taper tantrum *began* in exactly this setting: Bernanke's "next few meetings" line was an answer to a question, not a line in his prepared remarks. Traders watch testimony Q&A the way they watch a press conference — for the off-script sentence that reveals something the statement smoothed over.

The reason testimony is so reliably market-moving is the format itself. The Chair must answer, on the record and on live television, questions from politicians who have every incentive to pin down a clear answer — about inflation, jobs, the path of rates, financial stability, even fiscal policy. The Chair tries to stay on message, but over two days and dozens of questions, an off-script clarification or a candid admission almost always slips out. Traders run the Q&A like a press conference: the prepared statement is the floor, and the surprises live in the answers. A single phrase — "we are not on a preset course," or "we have a ways to go" — can shift the path because it reveals how the Chair is *currently* leaning between the two halves of the mandate. And because testimony spans two consecutive days (House then Senate), the market often gets a second crack: a phrase that moved markets on day one gets re-tested on day two, sometimes walked back, sometimes reinforced.

**Jackson Hole** is the August symposium, and over the past fifteen years its keynote has become a venue for the Fed to signal *framework* changes rather than tactical ones. In 2010, Bernanke used it to pre-announce QE2. In 2020, Powell used it to unveil "average inflation targeting," a new framework that promised to let inflation run hot to make up for past undershoots. And in 2022, Powell used it to do the opposite — to slam the door on hopes of an early "pivot" to rate cuts. Because Jackson Hole sets *philosophy*, its moves can be larger and more durable than a routine speech: the market is not repricing one meeting but the whole reaction function. It is also unusually well-telegraphed as a *date* — markets know the symposium is coming in late August every year, and the Chair's speech time is published in advance — so the options market builds an expected move around it just as it does around an FOMC decision. That predictability is exactly what makes it a sizing decision rather than a surprise: you cannot know what the Chair will say, but you always know *when*, so you can choose your exposure into it deliberately.

### The 2022 Jackson Hole "pain" speech

The August 2022 keynote is the modern case study. Through that summer, markets had convinced themselves the Fed was about to "pivot" — that with inflation seeming to peak, rate cuts might come in early 2023. Stocks had rallied through July and August on that hope. Powell's job at Jackson Hole was to kill it.

He did, in a famously short eight-minute speech. He warned that bringing inflation down "will also bring some **pain** to households and businesses," that "the historical record cautions strongly against prematurely loosening policy," and that the Fed would "keep at it until the job is done." There was no new decision, no new number, no change to the rate that day. It was pure forward guidance — a verbal reset of the path.

The S&P 500 fell about **3.4%** that session, one of the worst days of the year, and the selloff extended into the following weeks. The market was not reacting to a hike; it was reacting to the *removal of the pivot hope* it had priced in. The reaction function had not changed — the Fed was still in inflation-fighting mode — but the market's *guess* about the path had drifted too dovish over the summer, and Powell yanked it back in eight minutes.

#### Worked example: Jackson Hole 2022 on a stock book

You hold a \$25,000 diversified US equity book into the August 2022 Jackson Hole speech, and you did *not* trim ahead of it.

- The S&P fell about **−3.4%** on the day of the speech.
- Loss on the book: \$25,000 × −3.4% = **−\$850** in a single session.
- Had you instead trimmed to \$15,000 of exposure into the known set-piece event, the loss would have been \$15,000 × −3.4% = **−\$510** — you would have saved **\$340** for the cost of carrying less risk over one speech.
- A trader who *expected* a hawkish reset and ran a small short into it would have *made* roughly the same \$850 per \$25,000 notional.

The point is not that you must predict the speech. It is that Jackson Hole is a *known* high-variance date, so you size your book deliberately around it rather than getting caught flat-footed by a "speech" that behaves like a policy decision.

## The blackout: why markets drift quietly into a meeting

For ten days before each decision, the Fedspeak stream goes silent. From the second Saturday before the meeting through the Thursday after it, officials give no speeches, no interviews, and no policy guidance. This is the **blackout period**, and it changes the texture of the tape in a way that is easy to feel and easy to trade around once you know it is there.

![Blackout window timeline showing officials going silent ten days before the FOMC decision](/imgs/blogs/fed-speak-minutes-and-the-blackout-period-6.png)

Think through what the blackout does to the flow of information. In the weeks *after* a meeting, new Fed words arrive constantly, and each one can jolt the rate path. As the calendar approaches the blackout, officials get a *last word* — a final round of speeches in which they make sure the market is positioned where they want it before the gate comes down. Once the blackout begins, no new guidance can arrive. The last guidance is therefore the *final* guidance, and it is already priced.

The consequence is a characteristic **low-comms drift**. With the largest single source of surprise switched off, the market into the meeting moves mostly on two things: incoming economic data (a CPI or jobs print can still land in the blackout) and *positioning* — traders squaring up and pre-positioning for the decision. Absent fresh Fed words, realized volatility in rates often *falls* through the blackout, then spikes back up the instant the 2:00 PM statement lifts it. This is why the implied-vs-realized [volatility "crush"](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) is so reliable around FOMC: the event injects a burst of uncertainty into an otherwise quiet, guidance-starved window.

There is one well-known escape hatch. If something material changes during the blackout — a market dislocation, a shock data print — the Fed can communicate through a trusted journalist, the so-called "**Fed whisperer**," in a Wall Street Journal article that everyone knows to read as semi-official. This is rare and reserved for cases where the Fed feels the market is mispriced going into a decision and silence would let a bad surprise build. When it happens, it is a tell that the leadership is uncomfortable with where expectations sit. The most-cited case was June 2022, when, days before the meeting and inside the blackout, a hot CPI print landed and a WSJ article appeared signaling the Fed was likely to hike 75 bp rather than the 50 bp it had previously guided. The market repriced almost instantly — and the Fed did indeed hike 75 bp. The "whisper" let the Fed avoid surprising the market with a larger-than-guided move while the blackout kept officials formally silent.

**Why the blackout sets up the vol crush.** There is a deeper reason the blackout matters for anyone who trades options around the Fed. The price of an option contains an *implied volatility* — the market's estimate of how much the underlying will move. As a known event like an FOMC decision approaches, implied volatility *rises*, because everyone knows a big move is possible on decision day, and they bid up the options that would pay off on that move. But through the blackout, *realized* volatility — how much prices actually move day to day — tends to *fall*, because the largest source of surprise (Fed words) is switched off. So you get a widening gap: implied vol climbing into the event while realized vol drifts lower. Then the decision lands, the uncertainty resolves in a single afternoon, and implied vol *collapses* — the famous "**vol crush**." A trader who bought options into the blackout expecting a big move can be right about the direction and still *lose*, because the vol they paid for evaporated the moment the statement removed the uncertainty. This is why the [expected move](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) priced into options is the right yardstick: it tells you how big a move the options market already pays for, so you know whether you are buying or selling expensive insurance into the decision.

#### Worked example: the vol crush on an FOMC straddle

Say you buy an at-the-money straddle (a call plus a put at the same strike) on an index ETF the week before an FOMC decision, paying a premium that implies an expected move of about ±1.5% by expiry. The decision lands and the index moves +0.8% — a real move, in your favor on the call.

- Your straddle needed roughly a **±1.5%** move just to break even on the premium you paid.
- The actual move was **+0.8%** — *less* than the breakeven.
- On a \$10,000 straddle position, the post-event vol crush plus the sub-breakeven move could leave the straddle worth ~\$8,400, a loss of about **−\$1,600** despite correctly guessing the direction.
- Had you instead *sold* the straddle into the inflated pre-event vol, the crush would have worked *for* you: roughly **+\$1,600** as the premium decayed.

The intuition: into a known event you are not betting on direction, you are betting on whether the move beats the *priced-in* move — and the blackout's quiet realized vol is exactly what inflates the premium you pay or collect.

#### Worked example: trading the blackout drift and the decision-day snap

You are running a \$1,000,000 rate position into a meeting and want to think about risk across the blackout and the decision.

- Through the **10-day blackout**, with no Fed words, the 2-year yield is calm: it drifts within roughly ±5 bp. On your ~\$190/bp DV01 position, that is daily P&L noise of about **±\$950** — quiet.
- Then the decision lands and the market re-prices the path by, say, **15 bp** in one afternoon: \$190/bp × 15 bp = **±\$2,850** in a single session.
- The single decision-day move (\$2,850) is larger than the *entire* ten-day blackout's accumulated drift — proof the variance is bunched at the unblackout, not spread across it.
- So you carry *less* risk into the 2:00 PM release and *more* during the quiet blackout drift, where the move is small and mean-reverting.

The blackout is the calm before the scheduled storm. Knowing exactly when the storm hits is half of sizing it.

## How to trade the comms cycle

Pulling the pieces together, here is the operating model. The mistake the comms cycle punishes is treating every Fed headline as equally important. The skill is a fast, disciplined *weighting* of each message, then a decision to follow it or fade it.

![Trader's comms-cycle playbook weighting messages by speaker and fading non-voter overreactions](/imgs/blogs/fed-speak-minutes-and-the-blackout-period-7.png)

**Step 1 — identify the message type.** Is this a *decision* (highest weight), the *minutes* (read for dissents and count words), a *set-piece* (Jackson Hole or testimony — high variance, size around it), or *routine Fedspeak* (most of the stream)? The type sets your baseline attention.

**Step 2 — weight by speaker.** Chair and permanent voters move the path; rotating voters move it less; non-voters move it least. Before you react to any speech, know whether the speaker votes this year. A non-voter's bombshell is usually a fade candidate; the Chair's offhand remark usually is not.

**Step 3 — grade for surprise.** Compare the message to the speaker's known stance *and* to the last meeting's consensus. A hawk sounding hawkish or a speech that merely restates the message is no surprise — it should move little, and if the tape overreacts, fade it. A speaker breaking from their prior, or the consensus shifting, is the real signal — follow it.

**Step 4 — position around the blackout.** Carry lighter risk into the decision day, where the variance is concentrated; the blackout drift itself is quiet and mean-reverting. Keep an eye out for a "Fed whisperer" article during the blackout — it is a rare but loud tell.

**Step 5 — set the invalidation.** Every comms trade needs a stop. If you faded a non-voter and the Chair then *confirms* the hawkish message the next day, your premise (it was noise) is wrong — cut it. If you followed a perceived dovish pivot and the minutes contradict it three weeks later, the path you traded has been revised — adjust.

The asset map across the cycle is consistent with the rest of this series. Hawkish surprises (a higher path) push short-dated yields up, the dollar up, and rate-sensitive stocks (small caps, long-duration tech) down; gold tends to soften on higher *real* yields. Dovish surprises do the reverse. The front end of the curve (the 2-year) is the cleanest expression because it is the part of the curve most directly tied to the expected Fed path — which is precisely what the comms cycle is constantly repricing. For how that path interacts with the rest of the curve, see [reading the yield curve: slope, inversion, and recession](/blog/trading/macro-trading/reading-the-yield-curve-slope-inversion-recession).

![Long rates jumped while the policy rate sat still in 2013](/imgs/blogs/fed-speak-minutes-and-the-blackout-period-5.png)

That 2013 chart is the whole thesis in one picture: the dashed policy rate never moved off 0.25%, yet the solid 10-year line climbed +138 bp on communication alone. The Fed sets the *front* of the curve directly; it sets the *rest* of the curve through words. The comms cycle is how those words get delivered.

### The same cycle runs at every central bank

The Fed is the most-watched central bank, but the comms-cycle logic is universal — every major central bank publishes accounts of its meetings, sends officials out to speak, observes a quiet period before decisions, and has its own set-piece communications. Reading them is the same skill ported to a different cast.

The **European Central Bank** publishes "accounts" of its Governing Council meetings (its version of minutes) about four weeks later, and its officials span a hawk-dove spectrum sorted partly by country — the northern-European "hawks" historically more inflation-averse, the southern "doves" more growth-focused. The ECB observes a quiet period before its decisions, exactly like the Fed blackout. The **Bank of England** is unusually transparent: it publishes the *individual vote split* of its nine-member committee *with the decision*, so the "dissent count" is instant rather than three weeks late — a vote of 7–2 to hold versus 5–4 tells you immediately how close a turn is. The **Bank of Japan**, after decades at the zero bound, moves markets enormously on subtle shifts in the language of its statements and the governor's press conference, because its policy turns are rare and slow — the yen-carry unwind of August 2024 was triggered partly by a BoJ hike and hawkish guidance colliding with weak US data.

For **Vietnam**, the channel is different in form but identical in logic. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) does not hold eight scheduled press conferences a year; it communicates through policy-rate announcements, credit-growth ("credit ceiling") guidance, and signals about defending the dong. When the SBV raised its refinancing rate from 4.0% to 6.0% in autumn 2022 to defend the currency, then cut it back to 4.5% across three moves in 2023, the *guidance* around those moves steered the VN-Index and foreign flows as much as the rate changes themselves. The mechanics are covered in [Vietnam's monetary policy: the State Bank, the dong, and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling) and the [reaction function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) pieces; the point here is that the "read the words, weight by speaker, watch the set-pieces" discipline travels to any central bank you trade.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Let us close the loop with the two marquee cases, side by side, because they bracket the range of what between-meeting communication can do.

**2013 taper tantrum — a hint moves the whole bond market.** The trigger was Bernanke's May 22 testimony answer and the June 19 press conference. No policy change occurred for months. Yet the 10-year yield went from **1.66% (May) → 2.31% (June 19 FOMC) → 2.99% (early September) → 3.04% (year-end)**, a **+138 bp** repricing of the long end. The policy rate stayed at 0.25% the entire time. Emerging markets that had relied on cheap dollar funding — the "fragile five" currencies — sold off hard as the global cost of capital reset. The episode is the canonical proof that *forward guidance is policy*: the Fed changed the price of money across the world by signaling a future change in the *pace* of bond buying. A 10-year holder with a \$430/bp DV01 lost roughly **\$59,000 per \$500,000** of exposure over the full move — all of it on words.

**2022 Jackson Hole — eight minutes erase the pivot.** The trigger was Powell's August 2022 keynote. Again, no decision that day. But by warning of "pain" and rejecting an early loosening, Powell removed the rate-cut hope the market had rallied on all summer, and the S&P 500 fell about **−3.4%** in the session — a **−\$850** hit per **\$25,000** of equity exposure — with the weakness extending for weeks. The reaction function had not changed; the market's *path estimate* had drifted too dovish, and a set-piece speech snapped it back. It is the cleanest example of a "speech" trading like a policy decision, and of why you size your book around known set-piece dates.

**The cross-asset spread in each case.** The two episodes also show how a comms shock radiates across assets, which is the part a single-asset trader misses. In 2013, the taper hint hit *everything* priced off the global cost of capital: US mortgage rates jumped, which cooled the housing recovery; rate-sensitive equity sectors (utilities, REITs, anything valued on a low discount rate) underperformed; and crucially, capital that had flowed into higher-yielding emerging markets while US rates were near zero began to reverse. Currencies like the Indian rupee, Indonesian rupiah, Turkish lira, Brazilian real, and South African rand — the "fragile five" with large external funding needs — sold off hard as the dollar funding they relied on suddenly looked more expensive. A single Fed sentence tightened financial conditions on three continents.

In 2022, the Jackson Hole reset hit the equity complex hardest where valuations were most stretched on a low-rate assumption — long-duration tech and unprofitable growth names fell more than the index. The dollar firmed (a higher US path pulls capital toward dollar assets), gold softened on the higher real-yield implication, and crypto, which through this cycle traded as a high-beta liquidity asset, sold off alongside the Nasdaq. The lesson repeats across both: a comms event reprices the *path*, the path is the discount rate for *every* asset, so the whole cross-asset map moves together — risk-off on a hawkish reset, risk-on on a dovish one. For how that rotation works mechanically, see [risk-on, risk-off: how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates).

#### Worked example: the same comms shock across a mixed book

Say you hold a \$100,000 book on the 2022 Jackson Hole day, split \$40,000 US equities, \$30,000 long-duration tech, \$20,000 in a 10-year bond position (\$430/bp DV01 scaled down to about \$170/bp at this size), and \$10,000 in crypto. A hawkish reset hits all four legs at once:

- US equities **−3.4%**: \$40,000 × −3.4% = **−\$1,360**.
- Long-duration tech, higher beta, say **−4.5%**: \$30,000 × −4.5% = **−\$1,350**.
- The 10-year yield backs up ~10 bp on the higher path: \$170/bp × 10 bp = **−\$1,700** on the bond leg.
- Crypto, high-beta to liquidity, say **−5%**: \$10,000 × −5% = **−\$500**.
- Total one-day hit ≈ **−\$4,910** on a \$100,000 book — about **−4.9%**, *worse* than any single leg, because the comms shock correlated everything to one factor.

The intuition: a path-repricing event is a *single-factor* shock, so diversification across asset classes does not save you the way it does on idiosyncratic news — all the legs move together, which is exactly why you trim ahead of a known set-piece.

Both episodes share a structure: a scheduled communications event, no change in the policy rate, and a large repricing of the *path*. That is the signature of the between-meeting cycle.

## Common misconceptions

**"Only the decision matters."** The single most expensive myth. The 2013 taper tantrum repriced the 10-year by **+138 bp** with the policy rate frozen at 0.25%, and the 2022 Jackson Hole speech cost the S&P **−3.4%** with no decision at all. The rate path is repriced at *every* stage of the cycle — minutes, speeches, testimony, Jackson Hole — not only on the eight decision days. If you only watch decision days, you miss most of the year's Fed-driven moves.

**"The minutes are stale — they came out three weeks ago, so they're priced in."** The *decision* is priced in; the *debate* is not. The market regularly moves 10 to 20 bp in the front end on the minutes because of what they reveal about dissents, count words, and risk balance — the best available read on the *next* decision. A hawkish count-word shift can cost a long 2-year **\$2,280 per \$1,000,000** of exposure even though the decision itself was old news.

**"Every Fed speech matters equally."** No. Weight by speaker (Chair > voters > non-voters), by stance, and by whether the message is new. A non-voting hawk repeating the consensus moves the path far less than the Chair breaking from it. Markets that overreact to a non-voter's soundbite often give the move back within a day — which is why grading the speaker is itself an edge.

**"The blackout means nothing happens before a meeting."** The blackout silences *Fed officials*, not the *market*. Economic data still prints, positioning still shifts, and the market drifts — quietly, because the largest source of surprise is switched off. Realized volatility usually *falls* through the blackout, then snaps higher the instant the statement lifts it. The calm is the setup, not the absence of risk.

**"Jackson Hole is just an academic conference."** It produces no decision, true — but the Chair's keynote has launched QE2 (2010), a new inflation-targeting framework (2020), and the hawkish "pain" reset (2022). It sets *philosophy*, which drives many decisions, so its moves can be larger and more durable than a routine speech. Treat it as a calendared high-variance event.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Here is the if-then map for the between-meeting cycle, asset by asset.

**The setup.** Mark the full cycle on your calendar, not just the eight decisions: the minutes date (+3 weeks from each meeting), the blackout window (−10 days to each meeting), the twice-yearly testimony, and Jackson Hole in August. Know which officials vote *this* year and where each sits on the hawk-dove spectrum. The front end of the curve (the 2-year) and fed funds futures are your cleanest gauges of the repricing.

**If the minutes come in hawkish** (more dissents/count-words toward tightening than expected): 2-year yield up, dollar up, rate-sensitive equities (small caps, long-duration tech) down, gold soft on higher real yields. Express it short the front end or long the dollar. *Invalidation:* if the next round of Fedspeak walks it back, the path revision was a head-fake — exit.

**If the minutes come in dovish:** the mirror image — 2-year down, dollar down, rate-sensitive equities up, gold firmer. *Invalidation:* a hawkish speech from the Chair after the minutes overrides them.

**If a *voting* official or the Chair delivers a surprise** (breaks from the consensus or their own prior): follow the move — it is a genuine input to the next decision. Size it as a real path repricing, with a stop at the level that says you misread the message.

**If a *non-voting* official spooks the tape** (a hawkish or dovish soundbite that is not new and not a vote): fade the overreaction. Add against the move and target a partial reversion as the market re-weights the speaker. *Invalidation:* the Chair or a voter *confirms* the message within a day — then it was real, cut the fade.

**Into Jackson Hole or testimony:** treat it like an FOMC decision. Trim exposure ahead of the event so a hawkish or dovish reset does not catch you over-sized; the [expected move](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) priced into options tells you how big the market thinks the swing could be. After the event, trade the *change in framework*, not the headline adjectives.

**Through the blackout:** carry lighter risk into the 2:00 PM decision, where the variance is concentrated, and expect quiet, mean-reverting drift in the days before. Watch for a "Fed whisperer" article — it is a rare but loud signal that the leadership wants to move expectations before the vote.

**Sizing and risk.** Because the front end moves on path repricing, a \$1,000,000 2-year position carries roughly \$190/bp of DV01 — a 15 bp decision-day swing is ±\$2,850, while a full taper-tantrum-scale 138 bp event on a 10-year \$500,000 position (≈\$430/bp DV01) is nearly \$60,000. Size each comms trade so the *worst plausible* repricing for that event type is a loss you can carry, and concentrate risk on the high-variance set-pieces and decision days rather than the quiet blackout drift.

The through-line: the Fed talks far more than it acts, and the talk sets the path. Learn to weight the words by *who* says them and *whether they are new*, ride the set-pieces, fade the non-voters, and respect the calm of the blackout — and the between-meeting cycle stops being noise and becomes a calendar of tradable events.
