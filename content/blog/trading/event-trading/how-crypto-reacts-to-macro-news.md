> **TL;DR** — Since 2021-22, Bitcoin and Ether do not trade as "digital gold." They trade as the highest-beta risk asset on the macro board: a leveraged Nasdaq that reacts to CPI and the Fed harder than stocks, only faster and around the clock.
>
> - The print they react to is the same one stocks react to — a US inflation or Fed surprise that moves the expected path of liquidity. Crypto has no earnings to anchor it, so it is *pure* liquidity-and-risk sentiment.
> - The reaction map: on a hot print crypto sells off in the *same direction* as the Nasdaq but roughly **twice the size**, because it trades 24/7 and runs on perpetual-futures leverage that cascades through liquidations.
> - The trade: size a crypto position for about **2x the equity move** on the same number, watch funding and open interest for a crowded long, beware the weekend gap, and always check whether today is a macro day or a crypto-idiosyncratic day.
> - The one number to remember: on the hot Sep-13-2022 CPI the S&P fell **−4.32%** while Bitcoin fell about **−9.4%** — the same print, roughly a **2x** crypto amplifier.

At 8:30 a.m. Eastern on September 13, 2022, the Bureau of Labor Statistics published one line: consumer prices rose 8.3% over the prior year, against a consensus of 8.1%. Two tenths hotter than expected. By the closing bell, the S&P 500 was down **4.32%** — its worst session since June 2020 — and the Nasdaq had fallen **5.16%**. Those are big, frightening numbers for a stock index. But there was no closing bell for crypto. Bitcoin, trading around the clock with nowhere to hide, dropped roughly **9.4%** on the same number. Ether fell harder still.

Stop and sit with that. The *identical* piece of news — two tenths of a percentage point of inflation surprise — knocked the S&P down a bruising 4.32% and knocked Bitcoin down more than double that. If you held \$10,000 of S&P that day you lost about \$432. If you held \$10,000 of Bitcoin you lost about \$940. Same news, same direction, roughly twice the damage. That 2x is not a coincidence of one bad day. It is the central fact about how crypto reacts to macro news, and it is what this post is about.

The story most people still tell about Bitcoin — "an uncorrelated inflation hedge, digital gold" — is exactly backwards for how it has traded since 2021. The figure below is the mental model we will spend the rest of the article unpacking: a macro surprise hits crypto through the *same* risk-off channel that hits stocks, but then leverage and liquidations bolt on an extra amplifying loop that equities simply do not have. Keep it open as a map.

## Foundations: crypto as a macro asset

Before we trace any episode, we need to agree on what crypto *is* in the modern macro plumbing, and define every term from zero. None of this requires a finance background — we build each idea with an everyday picture first, then go deep.

### What "high-beta" means

The single most important word in this post is **beta**. Borrowed from statistics, beta here just measures *how much a thing moves when the broad market moves*. If the whole risk market drops 1% and an asset drops 1%, its beta is 1.0. If it drops 2%, its beta is 2.0 — it is a *high-beta* asset, an amplifier. If it only drops 0.5%, its beta is 0.5 — a *low-beta* asset, a shock absorber.

Say you have two cars on the same bumpy road. One has soft suspension (low beta): the road jolts, the cabin barely shakes. The other is a go-kart with no suspension at all (high beta): the same bump throws you out of your seat. Crypto is the go-kart. The "road" is the global appetite for risk, and crypto feels every bump magnified.

The Nasdaq is already a higher-beta version of the S&P 500 — it is concentrated in long-duration growth and tech stocks that swing more than the broad market. Crypto sits one rung *above* the Nasdaq on the same ladder. The right one-line model is: **crypto trades like a leveraged Nasdaq, only more so.** Everything else in this post is an explanation of *why* that one line is true.

### Risk-on, risk-off

Markets cycle between two moods. **Risk-on** is the mood where investors want to *own* risky things — stocks, junk bonds, emerging markets, crypto — because the future looks easy: cheap money, growth, low fear. **Risk-off** is the opposite — investors *dump* risky things and crowd into safe ones (cash, US Treasuries, sometimes the dollar) because the future looks scary: tightening money, recession, high fear.

A macro surprise is mostly a *risk-mood switch*. A hot inflation print in 2022 flipped the switch to risk-off — it told the market the Federal Reserve would have to keep money tight and expensive for longer. When the switch flips off, the highest-beta asset falls the most. That asset, repeatedly, has been crypto.

### Real-yield sensitivity (no earnings to anchor it)

A stock has an *anchor*: the company behind it earns cash. Even in a sell-off, a profitable business has a floor of value — its earnings, its dividends, its book value. A bond has an even harder anchor: a contractual stream of coupons and a maturity date. These anchors dampen how far the price can swing on a single macro number.

Bitcoin and Ether have **no cash flows and no central issuer**. There are no earnings to discount, no coupons, no maturity. So what *is* their price? It is, almost entirely, a bet on **financial conditions** — how cheap and abundant money is, and how willing people are to hold a volatile, speculative store of value. The technical handle for the master gauge is the **real yield**: the interest a safe bond pays *after* subtracting expected inflation. When real yields rise (safe money pays more), the opportunity cost of holding a zero-yield speculative asset goes up, and crypto falls. When real yields fall (safe money pays nothing), crypto soars. With no earnings anchor, crypto's price is freer to swing on the same macro number than a stock's — that is the first reason its beta is bigger.

> A stock is a claim on earnings discounted by rates. Bitcoin is *only* the discount-rate-and-liquidity part with the earnings removed — so it amplifies the exact channel that moves stocks, with no anchor to slow it down.

### The Bitcoin–Nasdaq correlation regime since 2021

"Correlation" measures whether two things move *together*. A correlation of +1 means they move in lockstep; 0 means no relationship; −1 means they move oppositely. For most of Bitcoin's early life its correlation to stocks was near zero — it really did look uncorrelated. That changed decisively in 2021-22.

As crypto matured into a multi-trillion-dollar asset class, it filled with the same players who trade stocks: macro hedge funds, leveraged trading desks, and retail investors using the same risk budget for their tech stocks and their tokens. Those players treat crypto as the spicy end of their risk book. So when they de-risk on a hot CPI, they sell tech *and* crypto together. Bitcoin's rolling correlation to the Nasdaq, near zero before 2020, ran *strongly positive* through 2022 — often 0.5 to 0.8 on a rolling basis, the tightest it had ever been. Crypto and the Nasdaq became, for macro purposes, the same trade with different leverage.

### Perpetual futures, funding rate, open interest, liquidation

Here is the machinery that makes crypto's reaction bigger than a "leveraged Nasdaq" cliché — the part that has no equity equivalent at retail scale. Four terms, defined from zero:

- **Perpetual futures ("perps")** are the dominant way crypto is traded for size. A perpetual future is a derivative contract that tracks the price of Bitcoin but, unlike a normal futures contract, *never expires* — you can hold it forever. Crucially, perps let you trade with **leverage**: post \$1,000 of margin and control \$5,000, \$10,000, even \$50,000 of Bitcoin exposure. Most crypto volume is leveraged perp volume, not spot buying.
- **Funding rate** is the price of keeping a perp open. Because a perp never expires, exchanges use a small periodic payment — usually every 8 hours — to keep its price glued to spot. When most traders are *long* (betting up), the funding rate goes *positive* and longs pay shorts. So a crowded long position has a running *cost*: you bleed funding every 8 hours just to hold it. Positive funding is a tell that the market is crowded and leaning one way.
- **Open interest (OI)** is the total dollar value of all perp contracts currently open. High open interest means a lot of leveraged bets are stacked up. Rising OI into a rally means the move is being *bought on leverage* — fuel that can reverse violently.
- **Liquidation** is what happens when a leveraged bet goes wrong. If you control \$5,000 with \$1,000 of margin (5x) and the price moves against you by 20%, your \$1,000 of margin is wiped out, and the exchange *force-closes* your position by selling it into the market — whether you like it or not. You do not get a margin call and a polite phone call; the engine sells instantly.

Put those together and you have a machine that turns a small dip into a large one. The figure below traces the loop.

![The perpetual futures liquidation machine showing crowded longs funding liquidations and forced selling](/imgs/blogs/how-crypto-reacts-to-macro-news-3.png)

A crowded long book (high OI, positive funding) sits on a hair trigger. A hot CPI nudges price down a little; that little dip liquidates the most-leveraged longs; their positions are *force-sold* into the market, pushing price down more; that next leg liquidates the next tier of longs; and around it goes. This is a **liquidation cascade**, and it is why a 4% equity day can be a 9% — or a 15% — crypto day. Equities have nothing like this at retail scale: a stock investor who is wrong simply holds a losing position; nobody force-sells it at 2 a.m.

### The beta ladder inside crypto

Crypto is not one beta — it is a *ladder* of betas, and the higher you climb, the more violent the macro reaction. Bitcoin is the lowest rung: the largest, most liquid, most institutionally-owned token, so it has the *smallest* macro beta within crypto (though still roughly 2x the S&P). Ether sits a rung higher — it amplifies whatever Bitcoin does, typically moving 1.2-1.5x a Bitcoin move on a risk day. Above Ether come the large-cap "altcoins," then the mid-caps, then the small, illiquid, heavily-leveraged tokens at the top of the ladder, which can move 3-5x a Bitcoin move on the same macro print and lose 90%+ in a true risk-off flush.

The mechanism is the same one we built above, just dialed up: thinner liquidity, more concentrated leverage, and no institutional base to absorb selling. So when you ask "how does crypto react to macro news," the honest answer is "it depends which rung." A hot CPI that takes Bitcoin down 9% can take an illiquid alt down 25%, because the same forced-selling cascade hits a far shallower order book. The practical rule: the further down the liquidity ladder you go, the harder you must shrink position size into a macro event, because the beta — and the cascade risk — compounds with every rung.

#### Worked example: the same print up and down the ladder

On a hot CPI that takes Bitcoin **−9%**, picture three positions of \$5,000 each.

- Bitcoin (beta ~2x S&P): −9% × \$5,000 = **−\$450**.
- Ether (≈1.3x Bitcoin on the day): −11.7% × \$5,000 = **−\$585**.
- A mid-cap alt (≈2.5x Bitcoin, thinner book): −22.5% × \$5,000 = **−\$1,125**.

The intuition: the same news, the same \$5,000, and the loss more than doubles as you climb the beta ladder — so "crypto exposure" is meaningless without specifying *which* crypto.

### Spot price versus the perp: the basis tell

One more piece of plumbing worth knowing. Crypto trades in two parallel venues: **spot** (you buy the actual coin) and **perps** (you trade the leveraged derivative). On a macro shock these two can briefly *diverge* — the leveraged perp, where the panic and the liquidations happen, can trade below spot during a down-cascade (or above it in an up-squeeze). The gap between them is the **basis**, and it is a real-time gauge of where the leveraged stress is. A perp trading at a steep discount to spot during a sell-off is the visible footprint of a liquidation cascade dragging the derivative below the cash market; when the basis snaps back toward zero, the forced selling has usually exhausted. Watching basis alongside funding and open interest gives you a near-live readout of the leverage machine in action.

### Why "digital gold" became "leveraged Nasdaq"

The original Bitcoin pitch was *digital gold*: a fixed-supply, apolitical store of value that should hold up when fiat money is debased — an inflation hedge uncorrelated to stocks. (For that origin story, see [Bitcoin and the cypherpunk vision](/blog/trading/crypto/bitcoin-and-the-cypherpunk-vision).) That thesis may still prove right over decades. But on the *event-day* horizon this series cares about — how it trades when a print hits the tape — it is wrong today. Since 2021 the marginal buyer and seller of Bitcoin has been a leveraged risk trader, not a gold bug. So crypto reacts to a macro surprise like the highest-beta member of the risk family, not like gold. We will see the evidence below; for now, hold the model: **same channel as stocks, no earnings anchor, plus a leverage cascade — therefore a bigger move.**

The macro-policy mechanism behind all of this — exactly *how* a rate decision reaches crypto through liquidity and real rates — is laid out in the companion piece [how monetary policy moves crypto](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-crypto-liquidity-real-rates) and in [crypto as a macro-liquidity asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/crypto-as-a-macro-liquidity-asset). This post is the *event-day* lens: what happens in the minutes and hours after the print, and how you trade it.

## Why crypto's beta is higher than equities'

We now have all the pieces to answer the headline question precisely. On the *same* macro surprise, why does crypto move more than stocks? Three compounding reasons.

### Reason 1 — no earnings anchor, pure liquidity sentiment

We covered the principle above; here is what it does to the number. A stock's price is *future earnings discounted by rates*. A macro print moves the discount rate, but the earnings part is sticky — the company still sells the same widgets next quarter regardless of one CPI report. That sticky earnings base damps the swing. Crypto's price has *no* earnings term. It is the discount-rate-and-liquidity bet, full stop. Move the macro variable and the *entire* price reacts, with nothing holding the floor. That alone makes crypto's macro beta a multiple of an index's.

#### Worked example: the same print, two betas

On the hot Sep-13-2022 CPI, the S&P fell **−4.32%** and Bitcoin fell about **−9.4%**.

- A \$10,000 S&P position: −4.32% × \$10,000 = **−\$432** on the day.
- A \$10,000 Bitcoin position: −9.4% × \$10,000 = **−\$940** on the day.
- The ratio of losses: \$940 ÷ \$432 ≈ **2.18x**.
- Put differently, to risk the *same* \$432 in crypto you could hold only about \$4,600 of Bitcoin, not \$10,000.

The intuition: the same news cost the Bitcoin holder more than twice as much, so equal-dollar exposure is not equal risk — crypto carries roughly double the macro-event risk per dollar.

### Reason 2 — the leverage and liquidation amplifier

The earnings-anchor argument explains maybe a 1.3-1.5x beta. The *rest* of the gap — getting you from "a bit more than the Nasdaq" to "twice the S&P" — is the leverage machine. When a print pushes price down through a dense band of leveraged longs, the forced selling from liquidations *adds* to the fundamental sell-off. The first leg is the macro reaction (high-beta, ~1x to 1.5x the equity move); the second leg is the cascade (liquidations selling into a falling market). Stack them and you get the 2x — or, on a really crowded day, much worse.

#### Worked example: 5x leverage meets a −15% day

Take the August 2024 episode (more on it below), when Bitcoin fell about **15%** in the cascade. Say you held a 5x-leveraged long: \$1,000 of your own margin controlling \$5,000 of Bitcoin exposure.

- A 5x position is liquidated when price moves against you by about 1/5 = **20%** (the move that wipes your margin), but in practice maintenance buffers trigger it sooner.
- The −15% move = −15% × \$5,000 = **−\$750** of mark-to-market loss on the position.
- That −\$750 against \$1,000 of margin is a **−75%** hit to your capital — you are perilously close to liquidation, and any intraday spike past −20% force-closes you and you lose the entire \$1,000.
- A spot (un-leveraged) holder of the same \$5,000 of Bitcoin lost \$750 too — but kept the position and lived to see the rebound; the 5x trader was sold out at the bottom.

The intuition: leverage does not just multiply your gains and losses, it converts you into a *forced seller* at the worst moment — and millions of such forced sellers are what make the crypto move bigger than the stock move.

### Reason 3 — 24/7 with no circuit breakers

US stock exchanges close at 4 p.m. and have **circuit breakers** — automatic trading halts at −7%, −13%, and −20% that pause the market to let panic cool. Crypto has neither a closing bell nor a market-wide halt. A shock can run, uninterrupted, all night and all weekend. There is no pause to let liquidity refill the order book. So the same panic that a stock market would *spread over* a halt-punctuated session, crypto expresses in one continuous, un-braked slide. That is the third multiplier on the beta. The chart below shows the three CPI days side by side — the same prints that moved the S&P moved Bitcoin in the same direction every time, but bigger.

![Bitcoin versus S&P 500 same-day move across three CPI release days as grouped bars](/imgs/blogs/how-crypto-reacts-to-macro-news-2.png)

Look at the pattern, not just the hot day. On the cool Oct-2022 print the S&P jumped **+5.54%** and Bitcoin jumped **+10.0%** — again roughly 2x, but to the *upside*. On the milder cool Oct-2023 print the S&P rose **+1.91%** and Bitcoin rose a more modest **+1.5%** (small prints produce small, noisier crypto moves, and that day the rate-sensitive rally concentrated in small-cap stocks — the Russell 2000 was up **+5.44%**). The headline takeaway holds: when the macro surprise is large, crypto's reaction is a roughly 2x amplified version of the equity reaction, in the same direction.

### The dollar channel: crypto's hidden third leg

There is a fourth amplifier that is easy to miss. A hot US print does not only tighten the expected path of rates — it strengthens the **dollar**, because higher US rates pull global capital into dollar assets. (The mechanism is in [trading the dollar: DXY, carry, the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile).) Bitcoin is priced in dollars, and it is globally fungible, so a stronger dollar is a direct headwind: the same coin is worth fewer dollars when the dollar itself is gaining. On the hot Sep-13-2022 CPI the dollar index rose **+1.4%** *and* Bitcoin fell **−9.4%** — the two moves reinforce each other. On the cool Nov-10-2022 CPI the dollar fell **−2.1%** *and* Bitcoin rose **+10.0%**. So part of crypto's outsized macro reaction is that it gets hit by *both* the rate-expectations channel and the dollar channel at once, pulling the same direction. A risk asset that is also a dollar-denominated global asset feels the squeeze twice.

## The 24/7 problem: instant reaction and the weekend gap

Crypto's around-the-clock trading is a double-edged sword for an event trader, and it deserves its own section because it changes *when* and *how* you can act.

### Crypto reacts to a US print instantly

A US CPI or jobs report drops at 8:30 a.m. Eastern. Stock futures react instantly, but the cash stock market does not open until 9:30. Bitcoin, by contrast, is *already* a live, fully-open market at 8:30 — there is no "pre-market" versus "regular hours" distinction. So crypto often shows you the cleanest, fastest read on the print: within seconds of the release the perp price has repriced, funding has flipped, and you can see the knee-jerk before US stocks have even opened. For a trader watching the reaction function unfold, crypto is frequently the *first* tape to read. (On how that first knee-jerk then fades or trends, see [the anatomy of a news reaction](/blog/trading/event-trading/anatomy-of-a-news-reaction-spike-fade-trend).)

### The weekend macro-gap problem

The flip side is brutal. Markets do not only move on scheduled 8:30 prints. They move on *unscheduled* shocks — a surprise central-bank statement, a geopolitical event, a bank failure, a sovereign headline — and those can land at any hour, including Saturday at 2 a.m. when every stock exchange on Earth is closed.

When that happens, crypto trades *through* the shock and gaps, while equities sit frozen until Monday's open. The figure below contrasts the two tracks.

![The 24/7 weekend macro gap with crypto trading continuously while equities stay closed](/imgs/blogs/how-crypto-reacts-to-macro-news-5.png)

This is why crypto is sometimes called the market's "**weekend risk barometer**." If something scary breaks on a Saturday, the only liquid market large enough to register the fear is crypto. So crypto can gap 8-10% over a weekend on a macro or geopolitical shock, fully pricing it, while the S&P 500 only finds out on Monday and gaps to catch up. The classic real-world version: when Silicon Valley Bank failed over a weekend in March 2023 and a major stablecoin briefly de-pegged, crypto whipsawed violently in real time across Saturday and Sunday while stock traders could do nothing but watch the futures.

#### Worked example: the weekend gap on a position

Say you hold \$20,000 of Bitcoin into a Friday close and a macro shock breaks Saturday night.

- Crypto gaps **−8%** over the weekend: −8% × \$20,000 = **−\$1,600**, and there is no stock-market halt to slow it.
- You *can* trade out Saturday — but the order book is thin on a weekend, so selling \$20,000 into the gap might cost you another **0.5%** in slippage = about **−\$100** extra.
- A friend holding \$20,000 of an S&P ETF over the same weekend shows **\$0** change on their screen until Monday — then the ETF gaps down to catch up.
- Net: the crypto holder absorbs roughly **−\$1,700** in real time over a weekend; the equity holder's loss is *deferred*, not avoided, but they at least cannot panic-sell at 3 a.m. into no liquidity.

The intuition: 24/7 trading means crypto never gets to "pause and breathe" — weekend and overnight shocks hit it in full and immediately, so you must size and de-leverage *before* the weekend, not after.

## The leverage and liquidation amplifier, in detail

We have named the machine; now let us watch it run, because the cascade is the single most important thing to understand about a crypto event-day crash.

### How a cascade builds

Picture the perp order book before a hot print, on a day when the market has been grinding higher. Open interest is at a record, funding has been persistently positive (everyone is long and paying to stay long), and leverage is stacked in tiers — some traders at 3x, some at 5x, plenty of degens at 25x or more. Each tier has a *liquidation price* a fixed distance below the current price.

Now the print lands hot. The first, fundamental sell-off pushes price down a couple of percent — enough to hit the liquidation prices of the most-leveraged tier (the 25x crowd). Their longs are force-sold. That selling pushes price down further, into the next tier's liquidation prices (the 10x crowd), who are force-sold in turn. Each wave of forced selling is *fuel* for the next wave. The market is briefly a machine that sells *because* it is falling — the opposite of a healthy market where lower prices attract buyers. This is why crypto crashes are so fast and so deep relative to the news that triggered them: a 2% fundamental move can detonate a 10% liquidation cascade.

Trace it tier by tier with round numbers, Bitcoin at \$60,000. A 25x long is liquidated on roughly a −4% move (\$57,600); a 10x long on roughly −10% (\$54,000); a 5x long on roughly −20% (\$48,000). The hot print drives the first −4%, wiping the 25x crowd; their forced selling drags price toward −6 or −7%; that does not yet hit the 10x tier, but a *second* shock — a follow-on data point, a large stop, thin liquidity — can carry it to −10%, detonating the 10x crowd and so on down the ladder. Whether the cascade stops at the 25x tier or chains all the way to the 5x tier depends entirely on *how much leverage was stacked and how thin the order book is* — which is why the same-size macro surprise can produce a −5% day one month and a −15% day another. The trigger is the news; the *magnitude* is the leverage map.

There is also an exhaustion signal. As each tier is liquidated, open interest *falls* — those contracts are closed by force. So a sharp drop in OI during a sell-off is the visible footprint of the cascade clearing out the weak hands. Once OI has been flushed and funding has flipped from positive to neutral or negative, the fuel is largely spent, and the move frequently finds a floor and snaps back — the violent over-shoot partially retraces. Reading OI and funding *during* the move tells you roughly where in the cascade you are: still building, or burning out.

### Funding and OI are the gauges you watch

You can *see* the danger before it fires. Two dials:

- **Funding rate**: persistently and strongly positive funding means longs are crowded and paying up — a hot print into that setup has maximum cascade fuel. Strongly negative funding means shorts are crowded — and a *cool* print can detonate a "short squeeze" cascade to the upside instead.
- **Open interest**: record-high OI means a lot of leverage is stacked; a sharp *drop* in OI during a sell-off is the visible signature of liquidations clearing out (the cascade in progress). When OI has been flushed lower, the fuel is spent and the move often exhausts.

The asymmetry to remember: when funding is one-sided, the surprise that goes *against* the crowd produces the violent move. A hot print into a crowded long book is the textbook setup for a down-cascade; a cool print into a crowded short book is the textbook setup for an up-squeeze.

#### Worked example: reading funding before a CPI

Suppose Bitcoin is \$60,000, open interest is at a record, and funding is **+0.05% per 8 hours** (strongly positive — crowded longs).

- That funding costs a long **0.05% × 3 = 0.15% per day**, or about **0.15% × \$10,000 = \$15 per day** on a \$10,000 long — a running bleed that pressures weak hands to bail on any dip.
- If a hot CPI prints and price dips just **−3%** (−\$1,800 on \$60,000), it triggers the top tier of leverage; their forced selling can extend the move to **−9%** (−\$5,400) within an hour.
- On your \$10,000 spot position that −9% is **−\$900** — but you saw the crowded-long, positive-funding setup beforehand and could have trimmed or hedged.

The intuition: positive funding plus record open interest is a loaded spring; the macro print is just the finger that pulls the trigger, and the size of the move is set by how much leverage was stacked, not only by how hot the number was.

## CPI versus FOMC: which one crypto cares about, and why

Not all macro events move crypto equally. The two heavyweights are **CPI** (the monthly inflation report) and the **FOMC** (the Federal Reserve's interest-rate decision). Crypto cares about both, but for slightly different reasons.

### CPI: the surprise machine

CPI is the **Consumer Price Index** — the headline measure of how fast US prices are rising, released monthly at 8:30 a.m. Eastern by the BLS. (For the full calendar mechanics, see [the macro calendar: CPI, NFP, FOMC, PMI](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-macro-calendar-cpi-nfp-fomc-pmi).) CPI matters to crypto because it is the *biggest source of surprise* about the future path of money. The Fed has told you roughly what it plans to do; CPI tells you whether it will be *forced* to do more or less. A hot CPI says "tighter for longer" — risk-off, crypto down. A cool CPI says "the Fed can ease up" — risk-on, crypto up.

CPI is also the cleanest *high-beta* event because it is a pure number-versus-consensus surprise — there is no nuance, just hotter or cooler than expected — so the reaction function is sharp and the crypto amplifier is at full strength. The three CPI days we keep returning to are the canonical examples. The chart below isolates Bitcoin's reaction on those three days so you can see the sign flip cleanly.

![Bitcoin same-day move on three CPI days colored by sign showing the hot day down and cool days up](/imgs/blogs/how-crypto-reacts-to-macro-news-4.png)

The hot Aug-2022 print (red) sent Bitcoin **−9.4%**; the cool Oct-2022 print (green) sent it **+10.0%**; the milder cool Oct-2023 print nudged it **+1.5%**. Hot inflation, crypto down; cool inflation, crypto up — the sign of the surprise sets the sign of the crypto move, and the magnitude of the surprise sets the magnitude.

#### Worked example: the cool-CPI upside

It cuts both ways — that is the whole point of high beta. On the cool Oct-2022 CPI Bitcoin rose about **+10.0%**.

- A \$10,000 Bitcoin position on that day: +10.0% × \$10,000 = **+\$1,000** in a single session.
- A \$10,000 S&P position that same day: +5.54% × \$10,000 = **+\$554**.
- Again the ratio is about **1.8x** — crypto amplified the *good* news just as it amplifies the bad.

The intuition: high beta is symmetric. Crypto is not "the asset that crashes on macro"; it is "the asset that does whatever the Nasdaq does, twice" — which is wonderful on a cool print and terrible on a hot one.

### FOMC: the path matters more than the move

The FOMC decision is different in flavor. The rate decision itself is usually *expected* — by the time the Fed moves, the market has priced it, so the decision is rarely the surprise. (See [trading the FOMC: statement, presser, dot plot](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-fomc-statement-presser-dot-plot).) The surprise lives in the *guidance*: the dot plot (the committee's projection of the future rate path) and the press conference tone. Crypto, being a pure bet on the future *path* of liquidity, can react sharply to a hawkish or dovish *path* even when the rate move itself was a foregone conclusion.

The cleanest example is December 19, 2018: the Fed hiked as expected, but kept signaling two more hikes for 2019 — *hawkish* relative to a market begging for a pause. Stocks reversed to close **−1.54%**, capping a brutal Q4 that drew the S&P down **−19.8%** from its September high. Crypto, already deep in its own bear market, was crushed in the same risk-off tape. The lesson: on FOMC, crypto trades the *path*, not the headline rate — a dovish surprise about the path can rip crypto higher even on a rate hike, and a hawkish path can sink it even on a cut. Because crypto has the longest "duration" (it is *all* future, no present cash flow), it is the most sensitive asset on the board to a shift in the expected path of policy.

The contrast with March 16, 2022 makes the point. That day the Fed delivered its *first* hike of the entire cycle — the start of the most aggressive tightening in forty years, which should be terrible for the highest-beta liquidity asset. Yet stocks *rallied* **+2.24%** (the Nasdaq **+3.77%**) and crypto rallied with them. Why? Because the hike was fully expected, and the *path* the Fed laid out was, on the day, read as "clarity at last" rather than "worse than feared." The market had been terrified of an even more hawkish surprise; getting the expected hike removed an uncertainty. This is the reaction-function lesson in its purest crypto form: it was not the rate move that set the sign, it was the *surprise relative to the path the market had already priced*. Crypto, with no earnings to muddy the signal, traded the path almost mechanically.

There is one more wrinkle specific to FOMC days. The decision and statement land at 2:00 p.m. Eastern, then Chair Powell's press conference runs from 2:30. Crypto often whipsaws *twice*: a knee-jerk on the 2:00 statement, then a second, sometimes larger move during the 2:30 presser as the tone of the answers shifts the perceived path. Because crypto is open and liquid through both, it frequently leads the round-trip — spiking on the statement, reversing on the presser — faster than the cash equity market can. An event trader watching the FOMC in crypto is watching the most reactive instrument on the board re-price the path in real time.

### The FOMC versus a soft NFP

Jobs data rounds out the macro trio. The monthly **non-farm payrolls (NFP)** report — first Friday, 8:30 a.m. Eastern — moves crypto through the same growth-and-liquidity channel, with a twist: the *sign* of a strong jobs number flips with the regime. In the 2022-23 inflation fight, a *blowout* jobs number was *bad* for risk because it meant the Fed could stay tight — the +517k January-2023 report (vs ~187k expected) sent the S&P **−1.04%** and the 2-year yield up 18bp, and crypto sold off in sympathy. But by August 2024 the regime had flipped to a growth scare: the *weak* +114k July report (vs ~175k expected, unemployment jumping to 4.3%) was *also* bad for risk, this time because it raised recession fear — the S&P fell **−1.84%** and crypto fell with it, then far harder when that print fed into the August-5 carry cascade. The lesson for crypto is the same as for stocks: a jobs number is only "good" or "bad" relative to *what the market currently fears most* — too-hot inflation or too-cold growth — and crypto amplifies whichever fear is in control.

## When crypto decouples: idiosyncratic events

The whole thesis so far is "crypto = high-beta macro." But there are days when crypto *ignores* the macro tape entirely and trades on its own news. Knowing the difference is the most important judgment call in crypto event trading, because using the macro playbook on an idiosyncratic day will get you run over.

### Crypto-idiosyncratic shocks

These are events *internal* to crypto that have nothing to do with CPI or the Fed:

- **Exchange or counterparty failures.** When FTX — then the second-largest crypto exchange — collapsed into bankruptcy in November 2022, Bitcoin fell about 20% over a week on a macro-quiet stretch. That was not a Fed move; it was a *crypto solvency* shock, a run on a counterparty. The macro reaction function was irrelevant; the driver was contagion fear inside crypto.
- **Regulatory and structural shifts.** The approval of US spot Bitcoin ETFs in January 2024 was a structural, crypto-specific catalyst — it opened a TradFi on-ramp for institutional money and reshaped who buys Bitcoin and how. (See [Bitcoin ETFs and the TradFi bridge](/blog/trading/crypto/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge).) That moved crypto on its own clock, not the macro calendar's.
- **Protocol failures and de-pegs.** The Terra/LUNA collapse in May 2022 vaporized tens of billions in days — a purely internal stablecoin-design failure.

On these days the BTC–Nasdaq correlation *breaks*: crypto can crash while stocks are flat, or rip while stocks are quiet. The macro betas we computed do not apply.

### The ETF-flow channel: a new macro linkage

The January 2024 launch of US spot Bitcoin ETFs did more than provide a one-off catalyst — it built a *new permanent channel* between macro and crypto. Before the ETFs, institutional money reached Bitcoin awkwardly, through offshore exchanges and futures. After them, a pension fund or wealth manager can buy Bitcoin in the same brokerage account as their stocks, and those ETFs publish *daily flows* — how much money went in or out. Those flows have become a macro read in their own right: on risk-on days the ETFs see net inflows that add a steady, price-insensitive *buyer* to the market; on risk-off days the flows reverse and that buyer disappears or turns seller. The deeper consequence is that the ETF wrapper *tightens* crypto's tie to the macro tape, because the people allocating to those ETFs are the same asset allocators rotating between stocks, bonds, and crypto on the same risk signals. The TradFi bridge that the ETFs built (detailed in [Bitcoin ETFs and the TradFi bridge](/blog/trading/crypto/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge)) is, for macro purposes, a wider pipe carrying the *same* risk-on/risk-off flows that move every other asset — which is one more reason crypto's macro beta is unlikely to fall back toward zero.

### Stablecoins: the liquidity that the cascade runs on

One last piece of crypto-native plumbing matters on a macro day: **stablecoins** — tokens pegged to the dollar that serve as the cash leg of crypto trading. When a macro shock hits and traders de-risk, they sell coins *into* stablecoins; the total stablecoin supply and the size of balances sitting on exchanges are a rough gauge of dry powder. A growing stablecoin supply is fuel for the next risk-on leg; a shrinking one signals capital leaving the system entirely. In a severe risk-off event the danger doubles if a stablecoin's peg wobbles — as a major one briefly did during the March-2023 banking weekend — because the *cash* leg of the market becomes uncertain at the exact moment everyone is rushing to it. So the same 24/7, leverage-driven structure that amplifies a macro move also runs on a dollar-proxy whose stability is itself macro-sensitive. It is amplifiers all the way down, which is precisely why crypto's reaction to a macro print is the biggest on the board.

### The decouple check

So before you apply the high-beta macro playbook, ask one question: **is today driven by a macro print, or by a crypto-specific story?** If a CPI or FOMC is the news, trade the macro beta — crypto will amplify the equity move. If the news is an exchange failure, a hack, an ETF decision, or a regulatory ruling, throw the macro map out and trade the crypto story on its own terms. The same week can contain both: a hot CPI on Tuesday (macro day, ~2x equity beta) and an exchange scare on Thursday (idiosyncratic, decoupled). Reading *which regime you are in* is the whole game. The cross-asset version of this judgment — when everything correlates versus when it does not — is developed in [the reaction function: why the same number moves differently](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently).

## How it reacted: real episodes

Enough principle. Here are dated episodes with real numbers, the kind you can check, that prove the thesis.

### The three CPI days: BTC versus S&P

We have used these throughout; here they are as a clean ledger of crypto's ~2x macro beta.

- **September 13, 2022 (hot, +0.2pp).** Aug CPI printed 8.3% vs 8.1% expected. S&P **−4.32%**, Nasdaq **−5.16%**, the dollar (DXY) **+1.4%**, Bitcoin about **−9.4%**. The same risk-off switch that hurt stocks gutted crypto at roughly double the rate. This was a textbook hot-print, crowded-long down-cascade.
- **November 10, 2022 (cool, −0.2pp).** Oct CPI printed 7.7% vs 7.9%. S&P **+5.54%** (one of its best days since 2020), Nasdaq **+7.35%**, the 10-year Treasury yield fell 28bp, the dollar **−2.1%**, Bitcoin about **+10.0%**. Crypto amplified the relief rally — but note this landed in the same week as the FTX collapse, a vivid reminder that macro and idiosyncratic forces can collide.
- **November 14, 2023 (mild cool, −0.1pp).** Oct CPI printed 3.2% vs 3.3%. S&P **+1.91%**, the rate-sensitive Russell 2000 small-cap index **+5.44%**, the 10-year yield fell 19bp, Bitcoin a modest **+1.5%**. A smaller surprise produced a smaller, noisier crypto move — high beta still applies, but small prints get lost in crypto's own volatility.

The consistent message across all three: crypto moved in the *same direction* as stocks every time, and on the two large-surprise days it moved roughly *twice as far*.

### August 2024: the carry-unwind cascade, Bitcoin −15%

The most violent recent demonstration of crypto's amplifier was not even a US-data day in isolation — it was a global de-leveraging that crypto expressed in full. On July 31, 2024 the Bank of Japan hiked rates; on August 2 a weak US jobs report (+114k vs ~175k expected, unemployment up to 4.3%) triggered a recession scare; together they unwound the enormous **yen carry trade** — borrow cheap yen, buy risk assets — and forced a worldwide scramble to sell. (The mechanics are in [carry-trade unwinds: when leverage breaks](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks).)

On August 5, 2024 the Nikkei fell **−12.4%** (its worst day since the 1987 crash), the S&P fell **−3.0%**, and the VIX — the stock market's fear gauge — spiked intraday to **65.73**. Bitcoin? It fell about **−15%**, far more than the S&P's −3%, as the global leverage flush detonated crypto's own liquidation cascade on top of the macro sell-off. This is the amplifier at full power: a global risk-off shock, expressed through crypto's leverage machine, produced a crypto move five times the size of the S&P's.

#### Worked example: the Aug-2024 flush on a leveraged book

Recall the 5x trader from earlier, now with the real numbers.

- \$5,000 of Bitcoin exposure on \$1,000 margin (5x), into the −15% Aug-5 cascade.
- −15% × \$5,000 = **−\$750** mark-to-market — a **−75%** hit to the \$1,000 margin.
- Most exchanges would have force-liquidated this position during the intraday plunge (which over-shot the close), so the realized outcome was likely a **total loss of the \$1,000** margin.
- The spot holder of the same \$5,000 lost the \$750 on paper but recovered much of it as Bitcoin bounced in the following sessions, just as the Nikkei rebounded **+10.2%** the very next day.

The intuition: in a macro cascade the leveraged trader does not just lose more — they get *removed from the game* at the lows, while the un-leveraged holder survives the round trip. Leverage converts volatility into permanent loss.

### The 2022 macro bear: crypto's worst year was the Fed's fastest hiking year

Zoom out from single days to the whole 2022 cycle and the thesis gets its strongest evidence. 2022 was the fastest Fed hiking year in four decades — the policy rate went from near zero to over 4% as the Fed fought inflation. If Bitcoin were "digital gold, an inflation hedge," 2022 — a year of 40-year-high inflation — should have been its best year. Instead it was its *worst*: Bitcoin fell from a 2021 year-end close of about \$46,306 to a 2022 close of about \$16,548, a drop of roughly **64%**, tracking the tightening of money with brutal fidelity. The chart below puts the whole arc in one frame.

![Bitcoin year-end price from 2018 to 2025 with the 2022 rate-hike bear marked](/imgs/blogs/how-crypto-reacts-to-macro-news-6.png)

The shape tells the macro story exactly. Bitcoin soared in 2020-21 on zero rates and pandemic liquidity, collapsed in 2022 as the Fed drained that liquidity at record speed, and recovered through 2023-24 as the hiking cycle ended and a rate-cutting path came into view. Crypto's multi-year price is a *photograph of financial conditions* — which is precisely what you would expect from a high-beta, no-earnings, pure-liquidity asset, and precisely *not* what you would expect from an inflation hedge.

#### Worked example: the 2022 bear on a buy-and-hold position

Say you bought \$10,000 of Bitcoin at the 2021 year-end close and held through the 2022 macro bear.

- 2021 close ≈ **\$46,306**; \$10,000 ÷ \$46,306 = about **0.216 BTC**.
- 2022 close ≈ **\$16,548**; 0.216 BTC × \$16,548 = about **\$3,573**.
- Loss = \$3,573 − \$10,000 = about **−\$6,427**, a **−64%** drawdown — in a year inflation hit a 40-year high.
- For comparison, gold (the real inflation hedge) was roughly *flat* in 2022, ending near where it started. Bitcoin did the opposite of what a hedge would do.

The intuition: across a full tightening cycle, Bitcoin traded as the *most* rate-sensitive risk asset, not as a hedge — the clearest possible refutation of the "digital gold on macro days" myth.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about crypto and macro are not just wrong but expensively wrong. Each is corrected with a number.

### "Bitcoin is an inflation hedge"

The single most damaging myth. In 2022, US inflation hit a 40-year high — and Bitcoin fell about **64%** (\$46,306 → \$16,548 year-end). A hedge *rises* when the thing it hedges gets worse. Bitcoin did the exact opposite: it fell *with* stocks on hot CPI (−9.4% on Sep 13, 2022) because the market read hot inflation as "tighter money ahead," and tighter money is poison for the highest-beta liquidity asset. Over a multi-decade horizon the digital-gold thesis may still hold; on the event-day horizon, hot inflation is *bad* for Bitcoin, not good.

### "Bitcoin is uncorrelated, a diversifier"

It *was* — before 2021. Since then its rolling correlation to the Nasdaq has run strongly positive (often 0.5-0.8), and in the August 2024 global risk-off it fell **−15%** the same day the S&P fell **−3%** and the Nikkei **−12.4%**. When you most want diversification — a crisis — crypto correlates *up* with stocks and falls harder, exactly like every other risk asset in a panic. (This "correlations go to one" behavior is general; see [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis).)

### "Crypto is too big to be moved by one CPI report"

Size does not buy immunity from macro; it *increases* the macro linkage, because a bigger market attracts the same institutional risk traders who treat crypto as the spicy end of their book. On the hot Sep-2022 CPI a multi-hundred-billion-dollar Bitcoin still dropped **−9.4%** on two tenths of an inflation surprise. The leverage machine means the move can be larger, not smaller, as the market grows — more open interest is more cascade fuel.

### "A bigger move means crypto is a better trade on the print"

A bigger move means bigger *risk*, not free money. Crypto's ~2x beta cuts both ways and its leverage can wipe you out before your thesis plays out — the 5x trader in our example was *right* that the macro bear would end, but got liquidated at the lows and never collected. Higher beta demands *smaller* position size for the same risk, not the same size for a bigger payday.

### "If stocks are closed, crypto is safe over the weekend"

Backwards. Because crypto is the only large market open on weekends, it absorbs *all* of a weekend shock in full and immediately — an 8% weekend gap is real money lost in real time (−\$1,600 on \$20,000), whereas the stock holder's loss is merely *deferred* to Monday. The 24/7 market is more exposed to off-hours shocks, not less.

## The playbook: how to trade a macro print in crypto

Everything above converges on a concrete event-day routine. The figure summarizes the four checks; the text gives the if-then map.

![The crypto event-day playbook with four checks size leverage weekend gap and the decouple test](/imgs/blogs/how-crypto-reacts-to-macro-news-7.png)

### Before the print

1. **Run the decouple check first.** Is today actually a macro day? If the dominant news is a CPI, an FOMC, or a major US jobs report, the macro playbook applies. If it is an exchange scare, a hack, an ETF ruling, or a regulatory headline, *stop* — trade the crypto story, not the macro beta.
2. **Read the consensus and the implied move.** Crypto options price an expected move too. If the S&P's expected move into the print is ±1.2%, budget roughly **±2.4% to ±2.5%** for Bitcoin — about double — and more for Ether and alts. Do not size a crypto position as if it moved like the S&P.
3. **Check funding and open interest.** Strongly positive funding + record OI = a crowded long, maximum down-cascade risk on a hot print. Strongly negative funding = a crowded short, up-squeeze risk on a cool print. The crowded side is the side that gets hurt by a surprise against it.
4. **De-leverage into the event, especially into a weekend.** The surest way to be removed from the game is to hold high leverage through a print or over a weekend. Cut leverage *before*, not after.

### The surprise scenarios (the if-then map)

- **Hot CPI (inflation above consensus), inflation-fighting regime → risk-off.** Crypto falls, roughly 2x the equity reaction, with cascade risk if longs were crowded. The trade is short / reduce / hedge; the violent version is a liquidation cascade, so respect the downside tail.
- **Cool CPI (inflation below consensus) → risk-on.** Crypto rallies, roughly 2x the equity reaction, with squeeze risk if shorts were crowded. The trade is long / add; the upside can over-shoot on a short squeeze.
- **Hawkish FOMC path (higher-for-longer dots, hawkish presser) → risk-off.** Crypto, the longest-duration asset, falls hardest on a hawkish *path* even if the rate move was expected.
- **Dovish FOMC path (cuts signaled, dovish presser) → risk-on.** Crypto rallies hardest; the path matters more than the headline rate.

### Sizing, the fade, and the invalidation

- **Size for 2x.** The cardinal rule: equal *dollars* in crypto is roughly *double* the macro-event risk of the same dollars in the S&P. To risk the same amount, hold about half the dollar exposure.
- **Mind the spike-then-fade.** The first move after a print is the knee-jerk; crypto's knee-jerk over-shoots more than equities' because of the cascade, and often partially *retraces* once liquidations clear and OI is flushed. Watch for OI collapsing during the move — that is the cascade exhausting, and frequently the local bottom or top.
- **Invalidation = the decouple.** Your macro trade is invalidated the moment a crypto-idiosyncratic story takes over (an exchange wobble, a de-peg). If correlation to stocks breaks intraday, exit the macro thesis — the map no longer applies.

#### Worked example: sizing a CPI trade for equal risk

You normally trade a \$25,000 S&P position into a CPI with an expected move of about ±1.2% (≈ ±\$300 of risk).

- To take the *same* ±\$300 of risk in Bitcoin, where the expected move is about ±2.4%: \$300 ÷ 2.4% = about **\$12,500** of Bitcoin — half the dollar size.
- If you instead matched dollars (\$25,000 of Bitcoin), your risk on the print would be ±2.4% × \$25,000 = about **±\$600** — double the risk you intended.
- And if you used 3x leverage on that \$25,000 of crypto exposure, a hot-print cascade of −9% = **−\$2,250** of loss on perhaps \$8,300 of margin — a **−27%** capital hit from one CPI report.

The intuition: the entire crypto event-day discipline reduces to one sentence — because the beta is double, the position must be half, and the leverage must be lower than your instinct says.
