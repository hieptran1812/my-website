> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The VN-Index reacts to *two* forces at once: **local Vietnam events** (the State Bank of Vietnam's policy rate, the credit-growth ceiling, VN CPI, and the managed USD/VND band) and **global events** (the Fed, the dollar, and US yields, which drive foreign money in and out of HOSE). You have to read both.
>
> - **Local:** the SBV is not the Fed. It steers the market with a *credit-growth ceiling* (room tín dụng) as much as with the refinancing rate, and it *manages* the dong in a trading band rather than letting it float. When VN CPI runs hot or the dong is under pressure, the SBV tightens — and Vietnamese stocks lose fuel.
> - **Global:** a strong dollar and high US yields pull foreign funds *out* of HOSE and push USD/VND up against the band, which forces the SBV to defend. So a US event you never heard about reaches the VN-Index a few sessions later through **foreign flows and the dong**.
> - **The amplifier:** Vietnam's **margin cycle**. Retail investors trade on broker leverage, so a dip that triggers margin calls can cascade into a crash — that is how the VN-Index fell about **−40%** in 2022.
> - **The one number to remember:** in 2024 foreigners net-sold roughly **−90 trillion VND (~\$3.6bn)** of HOSE stocks while the dollar stayed strong — Vietnam's clearest example of "the Fed sets the tide, local fundamentals set the boat."

In the first week of January 2022 the VN-Index touched an all-time high near **1,530**. Vietnamese retail brokerage accounts were opening at a record pace, margin balances at the big brokers were at all-time highs, and the financial press was full of stories about a new generation of "F0" investors — first-time traders who had discovered the market during the pandemic. It felt unstoppable.

By **November 15, 2022**, the same index closed near **911** — a fall of roughly **−40%** in ten months. That was not a slow grind. It was a series of violent down-weeks where the index fell the daily limit on many of its biggest stocks, margin calls forced brokers to dump shares into a market with no buyers, and the whole thing fed on itself. The proximate triggers were textbook Vietnam: the State Bank of Vietnam (the SBV, Vietnam's central bank) hiked its policy rate by **+200 basis points** in two moves that autumn to defend a dong that was sliding against a surging US dollar; a corporate-bond scandal froze credit; and the margin that had fueled the rally now worked in reverse.

Here is the part most beginners miss: the spark came from *outside* Vietnam. The US Federal Reserve was hiking the fastest in forty years, the dollar index (DXY) hit a multi-decade high near **114.8** in late September 2022, and global funds were pulling money out of every emerging and frontier market to sit in dollars earning 4%+. Vietnam imported that tightening through its currency and its foreign flows, and the SBV had little choice but to respond. To trade Vietnamese events well, you have to hold both pictures in your head at once — the local calendar *and* the global one. This post builds that dual model from zero.

## Foundations: the Vietnam event toolkit

Before we can trade a Vietnamese event, we need the vocabulary. Vietnam's market machinery looks superficially like the US one — there is a central bank, an inflation number, a stock index — but several pieces work *differently* enough that copying a US playbook will get you hurt. Let's define each piece from the ground up.

**The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV).** This is Vietnam's central bank, the rough equivalent of the US Federal Reserve. But it sits inside the government rather than operating at the same arm's-length independence the Fed has, and crucially it has a different *toolkit*. The Fed sets one main interest rate (the federal funds rate) and lets the market sort out how much credit flows. The SBV does set interest rates too, but it *also* directly controls how much each bank is allowed to lend. That second tool — the credit ceiling — is the single most important thing to understand about Vietnamese monetary policy, so we will give it its own definition. (For the full mechanism, the companion post [Vietnam monetary policy: the State Bank, the dong, and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling) goes deeper; here we focus on how it moves the market.)

**The refinancing rate and the discount rate.** The SBV's headline policy rate is the **refinancing rate** — the interest rate at which the SBV lends short-term funds to commercial banks against collateral. There is also a slightly lower **discount rate** (a cheaper, more restricted facility). When you read "the SBV hiked rates," it usually means the refinancing rate moved. Higher policy rates make money more expensive throughout the banking system, which cools borrowing, lending, and — eventually — the appetite to buy stocks on credit. The refinancing rate is the Vietnamese cousin of the fed funds rate, but as we will see it is *not* the SBV's main lever.

**The credit-growth ceiling ("room tín dụng").** This is the SBV's signature tool and it has no clean US equivalent. Every year the SBV sets a target for total credit growth in the economy — say "credit may grow 14–15% this year" — and then *allocates* that growth to individual banks as a quota. A bank that has used up its quota literally cannot make new loans until the SBV grants it more "room" (room tín dụng, where "room" is the English loanword used in Vietnamese finance). The SBV can tighten by holding the ceiling low or refusing top-ups; it can ease by raising the ceiling or granting mid-year extensions. For the market, credit room is *liquidity itself*: more room means more money sloshing through the economy and, indirectly, into stocks. When you trade Vietnam, you watch the credit-room announcements as closely as the rate.

**The managed USD/VND band.** The Vietnamese dong (VND) does **not** float freely like the US dollar, the euro, or the yen. The SBV publishes a **central reference rate** for USD/VND each day and allows the market rate to trade within a band around it (the band has been widened over the years, to roughly ±5% in recent years). The SBV defends the band by buying or selling US dollars from its foreign-exchange reserves and by adjusting domestic interest rates. So the dong is a *managed* currency: it can weaken, but in controlled steps, not in a free-falling crash. When global pressure builds, the SBV spends reserves and tightens policy to keep the dong inside the band — and that defense is exactly what transmits global stress into the local stock market.

**VN CPI (the GSO inflation print).** Vietnam's Consumer Price Index is released monthly by the **General Statistics Office (GSO)**, usually near the end of each month. It measures the year-over-year change in a basket of consumer prices — the same idea as US CPI, but the basket is heavily weighted toward **food and foodstuffs** (which can be over a third of the basket) plus housing, transport, and education. That food weight matters: a pork-price spike or a rice shock can move VN CPI more than it would move US CPI. The government runs an annual CPI *target*, typically around **4%**, and the SBV's room to cut rates evaporates when CPI presses toward or past that line.

**Foreign flows on HOSE.** HOSE is the Ho Chi Minh City Stock Exchange, home of the VN-Index (Vietnam's main large-cap benchmark). "Foreign flows" means the net buying or selling by foreign investors — global funds, frontier-market ETFs, and regional money managers. Vietnam is classified as a **frontier market** by major index providers (with a long-running campaign to be upgraded to *emerging* market status), so it sits in a relatively small, flighty bucket of global capital. When global risk appetite is high, that money flows in; when the dollar is strong and US yields are high, it flows out. Foreign investors own a minority of the market, but they trade the most liquid blue chips and they move at the margin — which is exactly where prices are set.

**The margin cycle.** Vietnamese equity trading is dominated by *retail* investors, and a large share of them trade on **margin** — borrowing from their broker to buy more stock than their cash allows. When prices rise, margin balances rise (people borrow more to chase the rally); when prices fall enough, brokers issue **margin calls** forcing investors to either add cash or sell. Because so much of the market is leveraged the same way, a fall can trigger a wave of forced selling that drives prices down further, triggering *more* margin calls — a cascade. This is the amplifier that turns an ordinary Vietnamese correction into a −40% crash. The companion post [Liquidity and the margin cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam) covers the mechanics in detail.

**Why the dong is managed, not floating.** It is worth pausing on *why* Vietnam manages its currency. A floating currency would let the dong absorb global shocks by weakening freely — but Vietnam is a small, open, export-and-import-heavy economy, and a freely crashing currency would import inflation (everything bought in dollars gets more expensive), scare away the foreign direct investment that drives Vietnamese manufacturing, and destabilize the banking system, which carries dollar liabilities. So Vietnam chooses *managed stability*: it accepts that defending the band sometimes means tightening policy at the worst possible moment for the stock market. That trade-off — currency stability bought with domestic tightening — is the structural fact behind almost every big VN-Index event.

**The Vietnam event calendar.** Knowing *when* these events land is half the battle, because the calendar is messier than the US one. US releases are clockwork: CPI at 8:30 ET on a known day, the jobs report on the first Friday, the FOMC eight times a year on a published schedule. Vietnam is looser. The GSO releases CPI near the *end* of each month (often with the quarterly GDP print bundled in for quarter-end months), but the exact day and time are less rigidly fixed than the US 8:30-ET ritual. The SBV does **not** meet on a fixed eight-times-a-year schedule with a press conference and a dot plot; it announces rate and policy changes *when it decides to act*, sometimes with little warning, often effective the next business day. The credit-growth ceiling is typically set at the **start of the year** and revisited mid-year, but the timing of any top-up is at the SBV's discretion. And because Vietnam imports global events, the *US* calendar — FOMC dates, US CPI days, US jobs Fridays — is effectively part of the Vietnamese calendar too, feeding through with a lag. The practical upshot: a Vietnamese event trader keeps three calendars open at once (the GSO data schedule, the SBV's irregular announcement risk, and the full US macro calendar), and treats the SBV's lack of a fixed schedule as a *standing tail risk* rather than a series of neatly datable events.

**The market's structure.** One more piece of context shapes how all these events transmit: the *composition* of the VN-Index. It is heavily weighted toward **banks, real estate, and a handful of large consumer and industrial names** — sectors that are unusually sensitive to credit and rates. That is not an accident; it reflects an economy where bank lending and property development are the engine. The consequence for an event trader is that the VN-Index has a *high beta to domestic liquidity*: an SBV rate cut or a credit-room expansion hits the biggest index weights (the banks and developers) most directly, which is why Vietnamese policy events move the index so forcefully. A US S&P 500 driven by technology mega-caps reacts to a rate move through the discount-rate channel; the VN-Index reacts through both the discount rate *and* the direct effect on its bank-and-property core.

With those definitions and the calendar in hand, the rest of the post is about *how* these pieces move the index, in what order, and how to trade it. Let's take the local channels first, then the global ones, then put them together in real episodes.

## 1. The SBV: the refinancing rate plus the credit ceiling

The first thing to internalize is that the SBV moves the market with **two hands**, not one. The Fed effectively has one hand (the policy rate, plus its balance sheet). The SBV has the policy rate *and* the credit ceiling, and the second hand often matters more.

Start with the rate. Here is the SBV refinancing-rate path through the cycle that defined the 2020s.

![SBV refinancing rate step chart showing the plus 200 basis-point 2022 defense and the three 2023 cuts to 4.5 percent](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts-2.png)

Read the steps left to right. The SBV held the refinancing rate at **4.0%** through the pandemic to support a recovering economy. Then, in the autumn of 2022, it hiked twice — to **5.0%** on September 23 and to **6.0%** on October 25 — a **+200bp** move in five weeks. That was not because Vietnamese inflation had exploded (it had not, as we will see); it was a *currency-and-stability defense* against a surging dollar and a domestic credit scare. Then, as the global storm passed and Vietnam's own growth slowed, the SBV reversed course aggressively in 2023: it cut three times between April and June (to 5.5%, then 5.0%, then **4.5%**), a **−150bp** easing, and has held 4.5% since.

Notice the *speed*. The Fed took most of 2022 to hike 425bp in 25-to-75bp steps. The SBV did its entire +200bp in two moves over five weeks, then unwound 150bp of it over three months the next spring. Vietnamese policy is *lumpier and faster* than US policy because the SBV is reacting to currency pressure and credit conditions that can flip quickly, not steering a slow-moving \$27-trillion economy. For a trader, that means VN rate events are *bigger surprises* relative to how often they happen — there is no slow telegraphed glide path with a dot plot.

Now the second hand: the credit ceiling. This is the lever with no chart, because it is not a single number — it is a quota system. When the SBV announces the year's credit-growth target and the bank-by-bank allocations, the market reads it as a liquidity signal. A generous ceiling (say 15–16%) plus willingness to grant mid-year top-ups means banks can keep lending, businesses can keep borrowing, and some of that credit finds its way (directly and indirectly) into the equity market. A stingy ceiling or a refusal to top up means the credit taps are closing — and Vietnamese stocks, which run on domestic liquidity, lose their bid.

Here is why this matters more than it sounds. In a country where the corporate bond market is thin and capital markets are young, **bank credit is the dominant source of financing for the whole economy.** When the SBV controls how fast bank credit can grow, it is controlling the economy's master liquidity dial directly, rather than nudging it through interest rates and hoping. For an event trader, the implication is concrete: an SBV credit-room announcement can move the market *as much as* a rate decision, and sometimes more — especially for the bank and real-estate stocks that are the biggest weights in the VN-Index.

It is worth being precise about *why* the credit ceiling exists, because it reveals the SBV's whole philosophy. In a fully market-based system, a central bank that wanted to cool lending would raise rates and let banks decide individually how much to lend at the new, higher cost. That works when the transmission from policy rate to bank lending is reliable. In Vietnam, the SBV has historically distrusted that transmission — banks chasing market share have been willing to lend aggressively even when rates rose, fueling credit booms (and asset bubbles, especially in property) that the rate tool could not contain. The credit ceiling is the SBV's answer: instead of *pricing* credit and hoping the quantity follows, it caps the *quantity* directly. For a trader this has a sharp implication — you cannot infer Vietnam's monetary stance from the policy rate alone. The rate can be held flat while the SBV tightens *hard* by squeezing the credit ceiling, or eases by loosening it. Reading only the rate is reading only one of the SBV's two hands.

This also explains a recurring Vietnamese market pattern: the **mid-year credit crunch and the year-end rally**. Banks that grow loans fast in the first half of the year can hit their annual ceiling by summer and be forced to slow new lending — a liquidity squeeze that often coincides with a soft patch in the market. Then, if the SBV grants a mid-year or fourth-quarter top-up, credit reopens and the market can rally into year-end on the renewed flow. An event trader who knows the credit-room rhythm watches for the squeeze and positions for the top-up, treating the ceiling as a *seasonal liquidity clock* layered on top of the rate cycle.

#### Worked example: an SBV +100bp hike and a margined position's funding cost

Say you hold a **500,000,000 VND** Vietnamese equity position (about **\$20,800** at a USD/VND of 24,000), and you funded **300,000,000 VND** of it on broker margin, putting up **200,000,000 VND** of your own cash. Margin loans in Vietnam are priced off the banking system's cost of funds, so when the SBV hikes, your broker's margin rate follows.

- Suppose your margin rate starts at **13%** per year. On a **300,000,000 VND** loan that is `300,000,000 × 0.13 = 39,000,000 VND` of annual interest (about **\$1,625** at 24,000).
- The SBV hikes the policy rate **+100bp** and your broker passes it through, lifting your margin rate to **14%**. Now the annual interest is `300,000,000 × 0.14 = 42,000,000 VND` (about **\$1,750**).
- The hike alone added `42,000,000 − 39,000,000 = 3,000,000 VND` per year (about **\$125**) to your carrying cost — for holding the *same* position.
- Worse, the hike is the kind of news that *also* pushes the stock down. If your 500,000,000 VND position falls just −5% (−25,000,000 VND, about **−\$1,040**), you have lost more than eight years of the extra interest in a single repricing — and you are now closer to a margin call.

The intuition: an SBV hike hits a leveraged Vietnamese investor *twice* — it raises the cost of the borrowed money and it lowers the value of what the money bought.

## 2. VN CPI and the SBV's tolerance band

If the SBV's job were only to defend the currency, life would be simple. But like every central bank it also has to keep inflation in check — and in Vietnam, inflation is measured by the GSO's monthly CPI print. Here is the crucial fact for event traders: **Vietnamese inflation has been remarkably tame** through the same years that US inflation exploded.

Recall the US story: CPI ran to **9.1%** in mid-2022, the worst in forty years, which is *why* the Fed hiked so violently. Now look at Vietnam. VN CPI averaged about **3.2%** in 2020, **1.8%** in 2021, **3.2%** in 2022, **3.3%** in 2023, and **3.6%** in 2024 — never breaching the government's roughly **4%** ceiling. Vietnam did *not* have a US-style inflation crisis.

That single comparison rewires how you read Vietnamese events. When the SBV hiked +200bp in autumn 2022, it was **not** fighting runaway domestic inflation the way the Fed was. VN CPI in 2022 was around 3.2% — comfortably inside target. The SBV hiked to defend the dong and stabilize a banking system rattled by a bond scandal. This is the opposite of the US, where the rate hikes *were* the inflation fight. Misreading this — assuming "SBV hiked, so Vietnam must have an inflation problem" — leads you to the wrong trades.

So what is VN CPI's role for a trader? It is the **constraint**, not usually the trigger. Think of the ~4% target as a fence around the SBV's freedom to cut:

- When VN CPI is well below 4% (as in 2021's 1.8% or much of 2023–24), the SBV has *room to ease* — it can cut rates and grant credit room to support growth without breaching its mandate. That is bullish for the VN-Index because it means cheaper money and more liquidity.
- When VN CPI presses *toward* 4% — driven by, say, a pork-price spike, an electricity-tariff hike, an oil shock, or a sharply weaker dong feeding through to import prices — the SBV's hands are tied. It cannot cut even if growth is weak, because cutting would risk breaching the inflation target. That removes the bullish "rate-cut hope" that often supports the market.

Because the CPI basket is so food-heavy, the *internals* matter. A trader watching a VN CPI release should look past the headline to the **food and foodstuffs** component (the biggest weight) and the **transport** component (which tracks the global oil price). A benign headline that is actually being held down by a one-off can mask building pressure; a scary headline driven by a temporary food spike may be one the SBV looks through.

There is also a powerful *feedback loop* between VN CPI and the currency that ties this section to the next one. A weaker dong makes imports — fuel, raw materials, machinery — more expensive in dong terms, which feeds into VN CPI a few months later. So a strong dollar does not just pressure the dong directly; it threatens to *import inflation*, which in turn limits the SBV's ability to cut rates to support the economy. The currency channel and the inflation channel are the same channel viewed from two angles.

#### Worked example: a weaker dong imports inflation for a VN importer

A Vietnamese company imports **\$5,000** worth of components per month — a tiny, illustrative slice of a real importer's bill. Watch what happens to its dong costs as the SBV lets the dong slide under dollar pressure.

- At a USD/VND of **23,600** (roughly the 2022 year-end level), that \$5,000 bill costs `5,000 × 23,600 = 118,000,000 VND`.
- The dollar keeps surging and USD/VND drifts to **25,400** (roughly the 2024 year-end level). The same **\$5,000** bill now costs `5,000 × 25,400 = 127,000,000 VND`.
- The dong cost rose by `127,000,000 − 118,000,000 = 9,000,000 VND`, or **+7.6%**, even though the dollar invoice never changed. In dollars the bill is still **\$5,000**; in dong it is **+7.6%** more expensive.
- Multiply that across every imported input in the economy and you see the mechanism: a +7.6% currency move quietly lifts the cost of imported goods, which shows up in VN CPI with a lag — and that is exactly what limits the SBV's room to cut.

The intuition: for a managed-currency, import-heavy economy, the exchange rate is a back-door inflation lever — which is why the SBV cannot ignore the dollar even when domestic inflation looks calm.

## 3. The FX channel: how the dollar reaches Vietnam

We have now seen both local hands. The third section is the bridge to the *global* world, and it is the single most important transmission mechanism for a Vietnamese event trader to understand. It runs through the currency.

![The FX channel: a strong dollar and high US yields pressure the dong, force the SBV to defend, drain liquidity, and push the VN-Index down](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts-4.png)

Walk the chain left to right. It starts *outside* Vietnam, with a **strong dollar** — usually because the Fed is hiking, US yields are high, or global investors are fleeing to the safety of dollars in a risk-off episode. (The macro-trading companion [Trading the dollar: DXY, carry, and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile) unpacks *why* the dollar moves; here we take it as the input.)

A strong dollar means **USD/VND pressure**: the dong tends to weaken, drifting toward the top of the SBV's trading band. The SBV cannot just shrug. Letting the dong blow through the band would import inflation, spook foreign investors, and destabilize banks. So the SBV **defends** — it sells US dollars from its FX reserves to meet demand, and/or it raises domestic interest rates to make holding dong more attractive and to slow the outflow. Both defenses **drain local liquidity**: selling dollars pulls dong out of the banking system, and higher rates make credit scarcer and more expensive. Less liquidity and higher rates mean a higher discount rate for stocks and less money available to buy them — so the **VN-Index falls**, often with the margin cycle amplifying the move.

This is exactly the chain that played out in 2022. Let the data show it.

![USD/VND versus the dollar index DXY 2021 to 2024 on a dual axis, the dong weakening as the dollar strengthens](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts-5.png)

The two lines move together. As the dollar index (DXY, the amber dashed line) climbed from about **95.7** at the end of 2021 to **103.5** at the end of 2022 and on to **108.5** at the end of 2024, USD/VND (the blue line) climbed right alongside it — from about **22,790** to **23,633** to **25,485**. The dong weakened roughly **12%** against the dollar across those three years. That is not a coincidence; it is the FX channel operating. The dong did not crash — the SBV's management kept the moves orderly — but the *direction* was set in Washington, not Hanoi.

Why does this matter so much more for Vietnam than for, say, the US stock market? Because the US is the *issuer* of the world's reserve currency. When the dollar strengthens, US assets become *more* attractive to global money, not less. Vietnam is on the other side of that trade: when the dollar strengthens, frontier-market assets like Vietnamese stocks become *less* attractive, because a foreign investor's dong-denominated returns get eaten by the currency loss when converted back to dollars. The strong dollar is a headwind for Vietnam at the exact moment it is a tailwind for the US — which is why "US stocks down, VN-Index down" and "US stocks up, VN-Index up" is far from guaranteed. The *currency* is the hinge.

#### Worked example: the 2022 crash on a real VN portfolio, anchored in dollars

Put yourself in early 2022 holding a **100,000,000 VND** Vietnamese equity portfolio. At the year-start USD/VND of roughly **24,000**, that is about **\$4,167**.

- The VN-Index falls about **−40%** from its January high to the November trough. Your portfolio tracks it: `100,000,000 × (−0.40) = −40,000,000 VND`. You are left with **60,000,000 VND**.
- In dollars at the start: about **\$4,167**. After the −40%: `60,000,000 ÷ 24,000 = \$2,500`. A loss of about **\$1,667** in local terms.
- But a *foreign* investor felt it worse, because the dong *also* weakened. Convert the ending 60,000,000 VND at the weaker year-end rate of **23,633**: `60,000,000 ÷ 23,633 = \$2,539`. The currency move was modest in 2022, so the foreign loss is close to the local loss here — but in a year where the dong slides hard, the currency loss *stacks on top of* the price loss.
- Stack it the worst way: a −40% price fall *plus* a −10% currency depreciation compounds to `(1 − 0.40) × (1 − 0.10) − 1 = −46%` in dollar terms — your **\$4,167** becomes about **\$2,250**.

The intuition: a foreign investor in Vietnam runs *two* risks at once — the stock risk and the currency risk — and in a dollar-strength crisis they tend to bite together, which is exactly why foreigners sell first and ask questions later.

## 4. Foreign flows: how the Fed drives the HOSE bid

Section 3 explained the currency channel. The *flow* channel is its twin, and it is the one you can watch in real time on the tape every single day. Foreign investors publish their net buying and selling on HOSE daily, and that number is one of the most-watched figures in the Vietnamese market.

Here is the logic, building on the frontier-market point above. Global funds allocate a slice of their portfolio to frontier and emerging markets. That slice is *discretionary* and *small* — it is the first thing trimmed when global conditions tighten and the first thing added when they loosen. The master switch for that decision is, again, the Fed and the dollar:

- When the Fed is **cutting**, the dollar is **weak**, and US yields are **low**, global money goes hunting for higher returns in riskier places — including Vietnam. Foreigners turn net **buyers** of HOSE. Their buying lifts the blue chips that dominate the VN-Index, and because they buy the most liquid names, their flow sets the marginal price.
- When the Fed is **hiking**, the dollar is **strong**, and US yields are **high** (you can earn 5% risk-free in Treasuries), the calculus flips. Why take Vietnamese frontier-market risk *plus* dong currency risk to maybe earn a bit more, when a US Treasury pays 5% in the safest currency on earth? Foreigners turn net **sellers**, and their selling is a persistent headwind for the index.

This is why a Fed event you might think has nothing to do with Vietnam — a hawkish FOMC statement, a hot US CPI print, a blowout US jobs report — can pressure the VN-Index a few sessions later. The US event moves the dollar and US yields *instantly*; that changes the global allocation math; foreign funds adjust their HOSE positions over the following days; and the index feels it with a lag. Vietnam reacts *last* in the global chain, through this flow pipe.

Look at what foreigners actually did.

![Foreign net flow on HOSE by year in trillion VND, red for net selling and green for net buying, 2020 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts-6.png)

The pattern is striking. Foreigners net-sold HOSE in **2020 (−19trn VND)**, heavily in **2021 (−62trn)**, again in **2023 (−25trn)**, and most dramatically in **2024 (−90trn VND)**. The one big net-*buying* year was **2022 (+29trn)** — and that is the exception that proves the rule. Foreigners bought into the *panic* of late 2022, when Vietnamese valuations had collapsed to multi-year lows and frontier-market value hunters stepped in near the bottom. The other four years they followed the dollar: as the dollar stayed strong and US yields stayed high through 2023 and especially 2024, foreigners kept pulling money out, and the **−90trn VND** of net selling in 2024 is the loudest single piece of evidence in this whole post that the Fed sets Vietnam's tide.

A subtlety worth flagging: net *selling* by foreigners in 2024 did **not** crash the VN-Index — it closed 2024 at **1,267**, *up* from 1,130 in 2023, despite the record foreign outflow. That is because *domestic* investors absorbed the selling. This is the dual-force model in action: the global force (foreign flows) was a headwind, but the local force (resilient domestic liquidity, a stable 4.5% policy rate, tame inflation) was a tailwind, and the local force won that year. You cannot predict the VN-Index from foreign flows alone, any more than from local news alone. You read both.

It helps to be precise about *why* foreign flows punch above their weight. Foreign investors own a minority of the Vietnamese market by value, so on a static ownership basis you might dismiss them. But three things make their flow the marginal price-setter. First, they concentrate in the **most liquid blue chips** — the large-cap banks, the consumer giant, the industrial leaders — which are exactly the heavyweight components of the VN-Index, so their buying and selling moves the index more than their headline ownership share suggests. Second, their flow is **directional and persistent**: when the global allocation switch flips, they do not buy one day and sell the next; they sell (or buy) steadily for weeks or months, creating a one-way pressure that the market has to absorb. Third, their flow carries **information** for domestic investors — a sustained run of foreign selling is read as a signal about global conditions, which can shape domestic sentiment and, in a fragile margin environment, tip the balance toward forced selling. So foreign flows matter not because foreigners own the most, but because they trade the index's core, move in persistent waves, and signal the global tide to everyone else.

There is also a frontier-market wrinkle that makes Vietnam's flows lumpier than a typical emerging market's. Because Vietnam sits in the relatively small frontier bucket of global indices (with a years-long campaign to be upgraded to *emerging* status), a handful of dedicated frontier funds and ETFs represent an outsized share of the foreign flow. When those vehicles see redemptions — often because *their* investors are de-risking globally on a Fed or dollar move — they have to sell Vietnamese blue chips mechanically to meet the redemptions, regardless of how cheap those stocks have become. That mechanical, valuation-insensitive selling is part of why foreign outflows can persist even when Vietnamese fundamentals look fine. The flip side is the upgrade story: a reclassification to emerging-market status would pull a far larger pool of passive money toward Vietnam, and the *anticipation* of that upgrade is itself a recurring flow catalyst that an event trader tracks.

For the *mechanics* of how frontier-market index inclusion and ETF flows move specific stocks, the companion post [Foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam) is the deep dive; here the point is the macro driver behind the flow.

#### Worked example: 2024's foreign selling against a blue-chip position

Frame 2024's roughly **−90 trillion VND** of foreign net selling — about **\$3.6bn** at a USD/VND of 25,000 — against a single investor's holding.

- You hold **\$8,000-equivalent** of Vietnamese blue chips — the large-cap banks and consumer names that foreigners trade most. At 25,000 that is `8,000 × 25,000 = 200,000,000 VND`.
- Persistent foreign selling is a slow, grinding headwind on exactly these names. Say it contributes to a **−10%** drawdown in your blue-chip basket over a stretch of the year: `200,000,000 × (−0.10) = −20,000,000 VND`, about **−\$800**.
- Now compare two investors holding the *same* basket. The domestic investor's loss is the **−\$800** equivalent (−20,000,000 VND). The foreign investor, who also has to convert back to dollars at a year-end rate that weakened toward **25,485**, eats a small extra currency loss on top — turning a −10% local move into roughly −11% in dollar terms.
- The asymmetry is the point: the foreign investor's *total* return is `stock return − currency depreciation`, so when the dollar is strong they need Vietnamese stocks to *outperform* just to break even after FX — which is precisely why they would rather sell and sit in 5% Treasuries.

The intuition: foreign selling is not a one-day shock like a US CPI print; it is a tide that pulls at the blue chips for months, and it turns on the dollar, not on anything happening in Vietnam.

## 5. The margin cycle: Vietnam's home-grown amplifier

The four channels above explain the *direction* of VN-Index moves. The margin cycle explains their *violence*. It is the reason Vietnamese corrections so often turn into crashes, and it is the most distinctly *local* feature in this whole post.

Recall the setup: the Vietnamese market is dominated by retail investors, and a large share of them buy on broker margin. In an up-market this is a turbocharger. Prices rise, investors' collateral is worth more, so brokers extend more margin, so investors buy more, so prices rise further. Margin balances at the brokers climb to records right alongside the index — which is exactly what happened into the 2022 peak.

Now run it in reverse. Prices fall. Leveraged investors' collateral is worth less, so they breach their **maintenance margin** — the minimum equity the broker requires. The broker issues a **margin call**: add cash within a day or two, or the broker sells your shares to bring the loan back in line. In a healthy market, individual margin calls are absorbed quietly. But when the *whole* market is leveraged the same way and prices fall *together*, the calls hit *everyone at once*. Forced selling drives prices down further, which triggers *more* margin calls, which forces *more* selling — a self-reinforcing cascade. Vietnamese stocks have daily price limits (a stock can only fall so far in one session, around ±7% on HOSE), so a real cascade shows up as a stock locked **limit-down** with no buyers for days — a queue of forced sellers and an empty bid.

This is the machinery behind the 2022 −40%. The autumn rate hikes and the bond scandal were the trigger, but the *depth and speed* came from the unwind of record margin. A market that climbs on leverage falls on leverage, and Vietnam's retail-and-margin structure makes that dynamic unusually fierce. (Again, [Liquidity and the margin cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam) is the dedicated treatment.)

For an event trader the practical implications are sharp. First, the margin cycle is a *gauge*: when aggregate broker margin is at record highs, the market is fragile, and any negative catalyst — an SBV hike, a hot CPI print, a wave of foreign selling, a global risk-off day — has a much bigger downside than the same catalyst would in a deleveraged market. Second, it interacts with *every* other channel: an SBV hike does not just raise discount rates, it raises the cost of the margin that is propping up prices, accelerating the unwind. Third, it makes the *bottom* violent and the *recovery* sharp — once the forced selling exhausts itself and margin is wrung out, the market can rebound fast because the overhang of leveraged sellers is gone. The 2023 recovery off the 911 trough had exactly that character.

#### Worked example: a margin call cascade on a leveraged position

You hold **1,000,000,000 VND** of Vietnamese stock (about **\$41,700** at 24,000), funded with **600,000,000 VND** of your own equity and **400,000,000 VND** of broker margin. Your broker requires you to keep equity of at least **30%** of the position value (a maintenance margin).

- Start: position **1,000,000,000 VND**, loan **400,000,000 VND**, equity `1,000,000,000 − 400,000,000 = 600,000,000 VND` → equity ratio `600 / 1,000 = 60%`. Comfortable.
- A bad event hits and your stocks fall **−25%**. Position value: `1,000,000,000 × 0.75 = 750,000,000 VND`. Your loan is still **400,000,000 VND** (debt does not fall). Equity: `750,000,000 − 400,000,000 = 350,000,000 VND` → ratio `350 / 750 = 46.7%`. Still above 30%, but the cushion has shrunk fast.
- Another **−25%** as the cascade deepens. Position: `750,000,000 × 0.75 = 562,500,000 VND`. Equity: `562,500,000 − 400,000,000 = 162,500,000 VND` → ratio `162.5 / 562.5 = 28.9%`. **Below 30% — margin call.** You must add cash or the broker sells.
- If you cannot add cash, the broker liquidates into a falling, limit-down market — at whatever price clears. Your **600,000,000 VND** of starting equity (about **\$25,000**) is now about **162,500,000 VND** (about **\$6,770**), a **−73%** wipeout of *your money* from a −44% fall in the *stock* — leverage cut both ways.

The intuition: leverage means the stock does not have to go to zero to wipe you out, and the forced selling that saves the broker is exactly what drives the next investor's stock below *their* margin line — which is how one −7% session becomes a market-wide cascade.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Theory assembled, let's run the three episodes that define modern Vietnamese event trading. Each one shows the dual-force model — local plus global — producing a different outcome.

![VN-Index year-end levels 2020 to 2024 with the 2021 high, the 2022 close, and the November 2022 trough](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts-3.png)

**2022 — the −40% crash (global trigger, local amplifier).** The VN-Index entered 2022 near its all-time high of **1,528** (January 6) and ended the year at **1,007**, having bottomed near **911** on November 15 — roughly **−40%** peak-to-trough. The chain ran exactly as Section 3 describes. *Globally*, the Fed hiked 425bp through the year and the DXY hit ~114.8 in late September, pulling foreign money out of frontier markets and pressuring the dong. *Locally*, the SBV defended the dong with **+200bp** of hikes (to 6.0%) in September–October, a domestic corporate-bond scandal froze a key funding channel for real-estate developers, and the record margin built into the rally unwound in a forced-selling cascade. The remarkable detail: VN CPI was only ~3.2% that year — Vietnam crashed its market *without* an inflation crisis, because the trigger was the dollar and the amplifier was leverage. This is the single best case study of "the Fed sets the tide" in a frontier market.

**2023 — the recovery (local easing wins).** With the global storm easing and Vietnam's own growth slowing, the SBV pivoted hard. It **cut three times** between April and June 2023 — refinancing rate from 6.0% back down to **4.5%**, a −150bp reversal — and granted more credit room to support the economy. VN CPI stayed tame (~3.3% average), giving the SBV the freedom to ease. The VN-Index recovered from the 911 trough to close 2023 at **1,130**, up about +12% on the year. The recovery was sharp precisely because the margin overhang had been purged in the 2022 crash: a deleveraged market with falling rates and reviving credit is a market with room to run. Note the *order of operations* — the SBV could only cut because inflation was inside target. Had VN CPI been at 9% like US CPI, the SBV would have been trapped, and the recovery would have looked very different.

**2024 — the resilient grind (global headwind, local tailwind).** This is the most instructive year for the dual-force model. *Globally*, the dollar stayed strong (DXY back up to ~108.5 by year-end), US yields stayed high, and foreigners net-sold a **record ~−90 trillion VND (~\$3.6bn)** out of HOSE — the heaviest foreign outflow on record. By the foreign-flow channel alone, you would have predicted a down year. *Locally*, though, the SBV held the policy rate steady at 4.5%, inflation stayed tame at ~3.6%, domestic credit kept flowing, and Vietnamese retail and institutional buyers absorbed everything the foreigners sold. The result: the VN-Index *rose* to **1,267**, up about +12% on the year, in the teeth of record foreign selling. The lesson is the thesis of this whole post — **you cannot trade Vietnam off either force alone.** In 2024 the local tailwind beat the global headwind. In 2022 the global trigger plus the local amplifier overwhelmed everything. Reading only one side gets the year wrong.

Step back and notice the *shape* of the three years together. 2022 was the configuration where every force pointed the same way — down — and the leverage amplifier turned alignment into a crash. 2023 was the configuration where the global force eased *and* the local force eased (the SBV cutting into tame inflation) at the same time, producing a clean recovery. 2024 was the configuration where the two forces *opposed* each other, and the outcome was a grind higher with elevated volatility as the local bid fought the foreign offer to a draw-and-then-some. Those are the three states an event trader maps every new setup onto: forces aligned bearish (cut risk hard), forces aligned bullish (press the trend), forces in conflict (size down and let the dollar break the tie). The episodes are not three random years; they are the three states of the model.

There is also a quieter lesson in the 2023 → 2024 transition about *sequencing*. The SBV cut to 4.5% in mid-2023 and then *held* — it did not keep cutting into 2024 even though foreign selling was a drag, because the dollar was strong and cutting further would have widened the rate gap with the US and pressured the dong harder. So the *global* dial (the strong dollar) constrained the *local* dial (the SBV's freedom to cut) — the two forces are not even fully independent. A weak dollar gives the SBV room to ease aggressively; a strong dollar takes that room away. When you read a Vietnamese rate decision, always ask what the dollar was doing, because the dollar is often the reason the SBV did — or did not — move.

#### Worked example: the 2024 paradox — who lost and who didn't

In 2024 foreigners net-sold about **−90trn VND (~\$3.6bn)** yet the VN-Index rose about **+12%**. Trace the money with a stylized pair of investors.

- A **domestic** investor with a **500,000,000 VND** index-tracking position (about **\$20,000** at 25,000) earned roughly the index return: `500,000,000 × 0.12 = +60,000,000 VND`, about **+\$2,400**. They *bought what the foreigners sold* and were paid for it.
- A **foreign** investor holding the same **\$20,000-equivalent** earned the +12% in dong but then converted back to dollars at a rate that weakened from ~24,269 to ~25,485, a **−4.8%** currency loss. Net dollar return: `(1 + 0.12) × (1 − 0.048) − 1 ≈ +6.6%`, about **+\$1,320** — still positive, but the currency clawed back over a third of the gain.
- That gap — **+\$2,400** for the local versus **+\$1,320** for the foreigner on the *same stocks* — is the foreign-flow channel in one number: the dong's depreciation is a tax on foreign returns, and it is exactly why foreigners were sellers even in an up year.
- The capital didn't vanish; it *changed hands*. The **~\$3.6bn** foreigners pulled out was matched by domestic buying, which is why the index could rise despite the outflow.

The intuition: foreign selling moves *who owns* the market more than it moves *the level* of the market — as long as domestic liquidity is there to take the other side.

## Common misconceptions

**"The VN-Index only follows local news."** This is the costliest error a Vietnamese investor makes, and it is the whole reason this post exists. The global force — the Fed, the dollar, US yields — reaches the VN-Index through foreign flows and the dong, on a lag. The clinching evidence is 2024: foreigners net-sold a record **−90trn VND** purely because the dollar was strong and US Treasuries paid 5%, *nothing* to do with Vietnamese fundamentals. If you only watched Hanoi, you would have been baffled by the relentless foreign selling pressure all year. You have to watch Washington too.

**"The SBV is just Vietnam's version of the Fed, so I'll read it like the Fed."** No. The SBV steers with a **credit-growth ceiling** as much as with rates, and it **manages the dong in a band** rather than letting it float. A credit-room announcement can move the market as much as a rate decision — and there is no equivalent in the US playbook. Reading the SBV as a Fed clone makes you miss its most powerful lever.

**"The SBV hiked +200bp in 2022, so Vietnam must have had an inflation crisis."** Vietnam's CPI averaged about **3.2%** in 2022 — inside the ~4% target the entire time. The hikes were a **currency-and-stability defense** against a surging dollar and a domestic bond scare, not an inflation fight. This is the exact opposite of the US, where the hikes *were* the inflation fight (US CPI hit 9.1%). Confusing the two leads you to expect Vietnamese rate cuts to wait for inflation to fall, when in fact the SBV's constraint was the currency.

**"Foreign selling means the VN-Index must fall."** Not necessarily — and 2024 is the proof. Foreigners net-sold a record **−90trn VND** *and the index rose +12%*, because domestic buyers absorbed the selling. Foreign flows move *who owns* the market at the margin; whether the index falls depends on whether the *local* force (domestic liquidity, the policy rate, credit room, inflation) is a tailwind or a headwind at the same time. One force does not override the other; they net out.

**"A managed currency means the dong is safe, so I can ignore FX."** The dong is *managed*, not *fixed* — it weakened roughly **12%** against the dollar from 2021 to 2024 in controlled steps. For a foreign investor that depreciation is a direct drag on dollar returns; for a domestic investor it is a back-door inflation tax that constrains the SBV's ability to cut rates. "Managed" means *orderly*, not *static* — and the orderly slide is itself a transmission channel for global stress.

## The playbook: how to trade Vietnamese events

The dual-force model turns into a concrete checklist. The rule that ties it together: **go with the trend only when the local dials and the global dial agree; when they fight, size down and wait for the FX band and foreign flows to settle.**

![The Vietnam trader's event checklist of five dials: SBV rate, credit room, VN CPI, the Fed and dollar, and foreign flows plus the margin cycle](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-events-sbv-vn-cpi-and-how-vn-index-reacts-7.png)

**The five dials to watch.**

1. **The SBV refinancing rate.** Hikes drain liquidity and lift the discount rate (bearish); cuts add fuel (bullish). The SBV moves in fast, lumpy steps, so a move is a bigger surprise than a Fed move. The level has been **4.5%** since mid-2023 — a cut from here would be a strong bullish signal; a surprise hike would signal currency stress.
2. **The credit-growth ceiling (room tín dụng).** A generous annual target plus mid-year top-ups is bullish (more liquidity); a stingy ceiling or a refusal to top up is bearish. Watch especially the banks and real-estate names, which live and die on credit room.
3. **VN CPI (GSO, monthly).** Treat it as the *constraint* on the SBV, not usually the trigger. Below ~4%, the SBV has room to ease (bullish optionality). Pressing toward 4% ties the SBV's hands (removes rate-cut hope). Watch the food and transport internals, not just the headline.
4. **The Fed, the dollar (DXY), and the US 10-year yield.** This is the global dial. A strong/rising dollar and high US yields pull foreign money *out* of HOSE and pressure the dong — a multi-session headwind. A weakening dollar and falling US yields bring foreigners *back* — a tailwind. This is where Vietnam imports global events, on a lag.
5. **Foreign flows and the margin cycle.** Track daily net foreign buy/sell on HOSE and aggregate broker margin balances. Record-high margin = a fragile market where any negative catalyst has outsized downside. Persistent foreign selling = a grinding headwind that domestic buyers may or may not absorb.

**The if-then map.**

- **Both forces bullish** (SBV cutting or holding low + tame CPI + weak dollar + foreigners buying): the strongest setup. Trend-follow the VN-Index and the high-foreign-ownership blue chips. This is the 2023-recovery configuration.
- **Both forces bearish** (SBV hiking + dollar surging + foreigners selling + high margin): the danger configuration — the 2022 setup. Reduce gross exposure, cut margin to near zero, and respect that the margin cycle can turn a correction into a cascade. Do not "buy the dip" into a leveraged, foreign-selling, rate-hiking market.
- **Forces in conflict** (local tailwind, global headwind — the 2024 case): the hardest tape. The index can grind higher on domestic liquidity even as foreigners sell, but the foreign-flow headwind caps the upside and adds volatility. Size down, favor names with *low* foreign ownership (less exposed to the foreign-selling pipe), and let the dollar and the FX band tell you which force is winning.

**Sizing and risk around the events.** Vietnamese policy events (SBV rate decisions, credit-room announcements) are *lumpy* and less telegraphed than Fed events, so the headline surprise can be large — keep position sizes modest into a scheduled SBV meeting or a credit-room announcement window. The single most important risk discipline in Vietnam is **margin control**: because the margin cycle is the amplifier, your own leverage is the variable you most control. In the danger configuration, the trader who has already cut margin survives the cascade; the trader at maximum margin gets liquidated at the bottom. And always anchor your foreign-flow read to the dollar: if the DXY is breaking higher, assume the foreign-selling headwind persists regardless of how good the local story looks.
