> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Dozens of economic data points print every week, but only a handful actually move markets; build a watchlist that knows the difference and ignores the rest.
>
> - The calendar is **tiered**: tier-1 (US CPI, FOMC, NFP) reliably moves the tape; tier-2 (PCE, PPI, retail sales, ISM, JOLTS, GDP) matters in context; tier-3 (the rest) is mostly noise.
> - Tier-1 dominates because it **shapes the path of the Fed** — the world's most important price (the cost of dollars). A single hot CPI moved the S&P 500 **−4.32%** in one session; a typical tier-3 day moves it less than ±0.2%.
> - US data lands on a fixed clock: **8:30 ET** (CPI, NFP, PPI, retail sales), **10:00 ET** (ISM, JOLTS, sentiment), **2:00 ET** FOMC statement + **2:30 ET** press conference. The global layer adds ECB/BoJ/BoE/PBoC decision days and Vietnam's SBV/GSO releases.
> - The one number to remember: tier-1 prints can move the S&P **1–5% in a day**; tier-3 prints move it a fraction of one percent. Prep a plan for tier-1, glance at tier-2, ignore tier-3.

On Tuesday, September 13, 2022, one number printed at 8:30 a.m. New York time and erased roughly **\$1.5 trillion** of US stock-market value before lunch. The number was the August Consumer Price Index — the headline measure of US inflation. Economists had penciled in 8.1% year-over-year. It came in at **8.3%**. Two-tenths of a percentage point hotter than expected does not sound like much. But that single surprise told the market the Federal Reserve would have to keep raising interest rates harder and longer than priced. The S&P 500 fell **−4.32%** — its worst day since June 2020. The Nasdaq fell **−5.16%**. The US dollar index jumped **+1.4%**. Bitcoin dropped roughly **−9.4%**. The two-year Treasury yield, the part of the bond market most sensitive to the Fed, leapt **+18 basis points**.

Here is the part most beginners miss. That same week, the calendar also carried the NFIB small-business optimism index, the August federal budget statement, weekly jobless claims, wholesale inventories, and the August Producer Price Index. Combined, those five releases moved the S&P 500 by a rounding error. **One print dwarfed an entire week of "economic data."** That asymmetry — a tiny number of releases doing almost all of the work — is the single most useful thing a new trader can learn about the economic calendar.

This post teaches you to build a watchlist around that asymmetry. We will define the **tiered calendar** (what is tier-1 and why), the **release clock** (when each print drops, to the minute), the **monthly cadence** (NFP on the first Friday, CPI mid-month, FOMC eight times a year), the **global 24-hour layer** (Tokyo to London to New York), and the **Vietnam layer** (the State Bank of Vietnam, GSO inflation, and why the US dollar and US yields end up driving the VN-Index through foreign flows). The goal is not to memorize every acronym. It is to know, at a glance, which releases deserve a trading plan and which deserve a shrug.

Why does this matter so much for a beginner? Because the economic calendar is the single most over-consumed, under-understood tool in retail trading. New traders discover the calendar, see a wall of three-star events, and conclude that "the market" is an impossibly complex machine reacting to a firehose of numbers. The truth is the opposite: the machine has a handful of master switches, and almost everything else is decoration. Learn the switches and the calendar shrinks from an intimidating spreadsheet into a short, predictable list of dates you can plan your month around. Markets are not reacting to *everything*; they are reacting, overwhelmingly, to a few things — and you can name all of them.

## Foundations: how to read an economic calendar

Open any economic calendar — the ones on Investing.com, Forex Factory, Trading Economics, or your broker's research tab — and you will see a table that refreshes all week. Each row is one scheduled data release. Before we rank them, you need the vocabulary, because every column on that table is a word a trader uses dozens of times a day. We will define each from zero.

**The release (or "print").** A single scheduled publication of an economic statistic: the May jobs report, the April CPI, the Q1 GDP estimate. Markets call it "the print" because in the old days the number literally printed on a wire-service ticker. Each release covers a *reference period* (a month, a quarter) and is published on a fixed future date and time announced weeks ahead. That predictability is the whole reason a calendar exists.

**Consensus (or "expectations," "the survey").** The median forecast of economists polled before the release — say, by Bloomberg, Reuters, or Dow Jones. This is the most important word on the page. **Markets do not trade the number; they trade the difference between the number and the consensus.** The consensus is already baked into prices the moment it forms. A jobs report of +200,000 is bullish, bearish, or a non-event depending entirely on whether the survey said +100,000, +200,000, or +350,000.

**Prior (and revision).** The "prior" is last period's value for the same series. The "revision" is a correction to a previously published number — statistical agencies refine their estimates as more survey responses arrive. Revisions matter more than beginners think: in August 2024 the US jobs data was revised down by a startling **−818,000** over a twelve-month span, reshaping the whole narrative about the labor market. When you read a release, read the revision to the prior month right next to the new headline.

**The surprise.** The plain arithmetic that drives the reaction:

```
surprise = actual − consensus
```

A *positive* surprise means the number beat expectations; a *negative* surprise means it missed. Crucially, "beat" does not mean "good for stocks." In the 2022–2023 inflation regime, a hot inflation surprise was *bad* for stocks (it meant more Fed hikes), so good-news-for-the-economy was bad-news-for-the-market. The sign of the surprise tells you the direction of the *force*; the *regime* tells you the direction of the *price*. We unpack that in the macro-calendar deep dive linked at the end; here, just hold onto the equation.

**Release time and the embargo / lock-up.** Every US release has a fixed publication minute (8:30 a.m. Eastern for most). Reporters get the data early in a secure "lock-up" room — the **embargo** — where they prepare stories but cannot transmit until the clock hits release time. At that instant, the data floods the wires and prices move within milliseconds. The embargo is why the reaction is so violent and so simultaneous across every screen on earth.

**Seasonal adjustment.** Many series are reported "seasonally adjusted" (SA) — statistically smoothed to strip out predictable calendar patterns (retail spikes every December, construction slows every winter). The headline number you trade is almost always the SA figure. The unadjusted ("NSA") number exists for context but is not what moves the tape.

**The importance rating (colored stars / bull heads).** Calendars rank each release with one to three stars (or one to three little bull heads, or a red / orange / yellow dot). Three stars / red = high impact; one star / yellow = low impact. This is the calendar's built-in tiering, and it is a decent first filter. But it is crude — it does not adapt to the regime. A jobs number is a permanent three-star event, yet in a quiet expansion it can be a non-event, while a normally one-star regional manufacturing survey can matter enormously the week the market is hunting for recession signals. Use the stars as a starting point, then apply the tiering logic below.

To make the surprise concrete, walk through the three best-documented CPI prints of the recent cycle. In each, the surprise was tiny in absolute terms but huge in consequence. August 2022 CPI: consensus 8.1%, actual **8.3%** — a +0.2pp *hot* surprise that sent the S&P **−4.32%**. October 2022 CPI: consensus 7.9%, actual **7.7%** — a −0.2pp *cool* surprise that sent it **+5.54%**. October 2023 CPI: consensus 3.3%, actual **3.2%** — a mild −0.1pp cool surprise that sent it **+1.91%**, and sent the rate-sensitive Russell 2000 small-cap index up a remarkable **+5.44%**. Three prints, three surprises of two-tenths of a percent or less, three of the most violent index sessions of the period. The level of inflation barely changed in any of them; what moved was the gap between the number and what the market had already priced. That is the entire mechanism, and it is why the consensus column is the one you read first.

With the vocabulary in hand, we can now rank the releases — because the entire skill of "trading the news" rests on knowing which rows on that table are worth your attention.

## 1. The tiers, and why tier-1 dominates

Sort every recurring release into three buckets by how reliably it moves global markets.

**Tier-1 — the market-movers (US CPI, FOMC, NFP).** These three are the heart of the calendar. They reliably produce the largest moves across every asset class, and a single surprise from any of them can set the tone for weeks. If you trade around news at all, you build a plan for these.

**Tier-2 — matters in context (Core PCE, PPI, retail sales, ISM PMIs, JOLTS, GDP).** Genuinely important data that can move markets meaningfully — but usually only when it confirms or contradicts the tier-1 narrative, or when the market is starved for a signal. The Fed's *preferred* inflation gauge is actually Core PCE (more on that below), yet PCE moves markets less than CPI because CPI prints two weeks earlier and largely tells you what PCE will say. Tier-2 releases reward attention; they rarely demand a pre-built plan.

**Tier-3 — mostly noise (housing starts, trade balance, factory orders, wholesale inventories, the Beige Book, most regional Fed surveys).** Useful to economists building models, nearly irrelevant to a same-day trader. On a typical day, a tier-3 surprise moves the S&P 500 by a fraction of one percent — often inside the normal intraday wiggle, which means the "reaction" is statistically unmeasurable. Note them, do not trade them.

Why does tier-1 dominate so completely? Because all three feed the one variable that prices every asset on the planet: **the path of the Federal Reserve's policy rate**, which is the price of dollars. Stocks are valued by discounting future cash flows at an interest rate; bonds *are* interest rates; the dollar's exchange rate is set by rate differentials; gold competes with the real yield on cash; crypto trades as a high-beta liquidity asset. Move the expected Fed path and you move all of them at once. CPI tells the Fed about inflation (half its mandate). NFP tells the Fed about employment (the other half). FOMC *is* the Fed telling you what it will do. That is the entire reason these three sit on top of the pyramid — they are the inputs and the output of the single most important reaction function in finance.

It helps to know what each tier-1 print actually measures, because the market does not react to the headline alone — it reads the *internals*. The CPI report is not one number; it is a basket. The headline includes food and energy (volatile); the *core* strips those out to reveal the underlying trend; and within core, traders zoom in on **shelter** (the largest, stickiest component) and **core services ex-housing** (the "supercore" the Fed obsesses over for signs of wage-driven inflation). A headline that lands in line but a hot supercore can still sink stocks. The jobs report is the same: the **headline payroll count** is the marquee number, but the **unemployment rate** and **average hourly earnings** (wage inflation) often matter more, and the **revisions** to the prior two months can flip the entire story. The FOMC decision is rarely about the rate move itself — that is usually fully priced — but about the **forward guidance**: the statement's wording, the dot plot's projected path, and the chair's tone in the presser. In every case, the surprise lives in the details, which is exactly why these three demand a prepared plan rather than a glance.

There is a deeper reason the level of a number matters far less than its surprise. Markets are forward-looking auctions: every participant has already positioned for the consensus, so the consensus is *in the price* before the release. Only the gap between the actual and the consensus is new information, and only new information can move price. This is why a "great" jobs report of +250,000 can crash stocks (if the survey said +350,000) while a "weak" +100,000 can rally them (if the survey said +50,000). A beginner reads the headline and asks "is this good or bad for the economy?" A trader reads the headline against the consensus and asks "is this better or worse than what was already priced?" — and then, separately, "what does the regime say this surprise means for the Fed?" Those are the two questions every tier-1 reaction answers, and getting them in the right order is the whole game.

It also helps to hold the **cross-asset reaction map** in your head, because a tier-1 print moves every asset class at once, in a coordinated pattern. In the 2022–2023 inflation regime, a *hot* CPI sent the same signal everywhere: stocks down (higher discount rate, more hikes), the dollar up (higher US rates attract capital), Treasury yields up (the bond market prices more hikes), gold roughly flat-to-down (real yields rose), and Bitcoin sharply down (a high-beta liquidity asset draining as financial conditions tighten). The September 13, 2022 print is the textbook case: S&P **−4.32%**, dollar **+1.4%**, two-year yield **+18bp**, Bitcoin **−9.4%** — every asset moved the way the "tighter Fed" thesis predicts. A *cool* print reverses every leg: on November 10, 2022, the S&P rose **+5.54%**, the dollar fell **−2.1%**, the ten-year yield fell **−28bp**, and gold rose **+2.8%**. When you can predict not just the direction of stocks but the *whole* coordinated move across six markets, you understand the tier-1 reaction — and you can express it in whichever instrument you trade.

The pyramid figure above makes the structure concrete: a narrow, high-impact tier-1 cap on top, a wider tier-2 band, and a broad tier-3 base of releases you mostly ignore — all feeding the Fed reaction function off to the side.

#### Worked example: a CPI-day vs a tier-3-day risk budget on a \$50,000 account

Say you run a \$50,000 trading account and your rule is to risk at most 1% of equity (\$500) on any single idea. On a CPI day, the realistic one-day swing in a broad US equity position is 1–5%; you size *down* so that even a −4% adverse move stays inside your risk budget. To cap the loss at \$500 against a possible −4% move, your position can be at most \$500 ÷ 0.04 = **\$12,500** of equity exposure — a quarter of the account, not the whole thing. On a tier-3 day (say, factory orders), the realistic swing attributable to the print is ±0.2%; the same \$500 risk budget would "allow" a \$250,000 position, which is absurd and far beyond the account. The point is not to lever up on quiet days — it is that the *event* sets the position size: a tier-1 print forces you to trade smaller, and a tier-3 print is not worth trading at all. **Let the calendar size the trade, not the other way around.**

## 2. The US release clock — 8:30, 10:00, and 2:00 Eastern

US data does not trickle out randomly. It lands on a clock so precise you can set your day by it. Knowing the clock means you are never blindsided and you are at the desk for the moment that matters.

![US release clock timeline with 8:30 10:00 and 2:00 ET blocks](/imgs/blogs/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets-2.png)

**8:30 a.m. Eastern — the heavy hitters.** This is the single most important minute in the macro day. The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) releases CPI, the monthly jobs report (NFP), and PPI at 8:30. The Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) releases GDP and the PCE inflation report at 8:30. The Census Bureau releases retail sales and durable goods at 8:30. US equity futures, Treasuries, and FX all trade in the pre-market at this hour, so the *knee-jerk* reaction is instantaneous — bonds and the dollar reprice in milliseconds, an hour before the stock market's cash session even opens.

**9:30 a.m. Eastern — the equity open.** US cash stocks open an hour after the 8:30 data. The first hour of trading is where an 8:30 surprise gets fully expressed in equities, and it is the highest-volume, highest-volatility window of the day.

**10:00 a.m. Eastern — the surveys.** The second cluster. The Institute for Supply Management (ISM) releases its manufacturing and services PMIs at 10:00. The Census Bureau releases JOLTS (job openings) and new-home sales at 10:00. The University of Michigan releases its consumer-sentiment survey at 10:00. These are soft / survey data — important context, mostly tier-2.

**2:00 p.m. Eastern — the Fed.** On the eight scheduled FOMC decision days a year, the Federal Reserve releases its policy statement (and, four times a year, the "dot plot" of rate projections) at exactly 2:00 p.m. This is the highest-stakes single release on the calendar.

**2:30 p.m. Eastern — the press conference.** Thirty minutes after the statement, the Fed chair begins a televised Q&A. This is its own event: the statement moves price at 2:00, and the presser frequently moves it the *other* way at 2:30 as the chair adds nuance. A trader who exits at 2:15 thinking "the move is done" is often whipsawed at 2:35.

Who releases what is worth committing to memory, because the source tells you the type of data: **BLS** = labor + consumer prices (CPI, NFP, PPI); **BEA** = the national accounts (GDP, PCE); **Census** = activity (retail sales, durable goods, housing, JOLTS); **ISM** = the business surveys (PMIs); **the Fed** = policy (FOMC, the minutes, the Beige Book). When you see a release on the calendar, identifying its agency instantly tells you which tier it sits in.

The microstructure of the reaction is worth understanding, because it dictates how you trade — or whether you trade at all. At exactly 8:30:00.000, the data hits the wires and algorithms parse it in microseconds. The first move — the **knee-jerk** — happens faster than any human can click. By the time a retail trader has read the number, the bond, FX, and equity-futures markets have already gapped to a new level. Chasing that first tick is how beginners get filled at the worst possible price. What happens *next* is where the opportunity lives, and it usually takes one of two shapes. Sometimes the knee-jerk **fades**: the market overshoots on the initial algorithmic spike, then mean-reverts as humans digest the internals and decide the move was overdone. Other times the knee-jerk becomes a **trend**: the surprise is large and confirms a regime shift, so the initial move extends for hours or days as positioning unwinds. Knowing which regime you are in — fade or trend — is the core read of event trading, and it is why "the reaction is over once the number prints" is one of the most expensive beliefs a new trader holds.

The pre-market versus cash-session split matters too. An 8:30 print lands a full hour before US stocks open at 9:30, but futures, Treasuries, and FX trade through it. So the *price discovery* happens in those markets first, and the stock market merely "catches up" at the open — which is why the 9:30–10:30 window is the single most volatile hour on a data day. A 2:00 FOMC decision, by contrast, lands *during* the cash session, so stocks reprice instantly and then re-reprice at 2:30 when the presser starts. Matching the release time to which market reacts first — and when — is the difference between trading the event and being run over by it.

#### Worked example: a tier-1 reaction on \$20,000 vs a tier-3 day

Take the September 13, 2022 hot-CPI session: the S&P 500 fell **−4.32%**. On a \$20,000 S&P-tracking position (an index fund or futures notional), that one print cost 0.0432 × \$20,000 = **−\$864** in a single session — and the bulk of it happened in the first 90 minutes after the 9:30 open. Now take an average tier-3 day where the relevant print (say, the trade balance) moves the index ±0.2%. On the same \$20,000, that is 0.002 × \$20,000 ≈ **±\$40** — and you cannot even be sure the \$40 came from the print rather than from background noise. The tier-1 move is **more than twenty times** the tier-3 move on identical capital. That ratio is why you set an alarm for the 8:30 CPI and let the trade-balance print pass without looking up.

## 3. The monthly cadence — when each tier-1 print recurs

The three tier-1 events recur on a rhythm you can mark on a wall calendar months ahead. There is never a surprise about *when* they land — only about *what they say*.

![Monthly US data cadence calendar grid marking NFP CPI FOMC PCE](/imgs/blogs/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets-5.png)

**NFP — the first Friday.** The BLS Employment Situation report ("non-farm payrolls," or NFP) lands at 8:30 a.m. on the **first Friday of every month**, covering the prior month. It reports how many jobs the economy added, the unemployment rate, and average hourly earnings (a wage-inflation read). Because it is the first major data of the month and speaks to half the Fed's mandate, it routinely sets the tone for the weeks that follow.

**CPI — mid-month.** The BLS releases the Consumer Price Index at 8:30 a.m., usually around the **10th to 14th** of the month, covering the prior month. It is the headline inflation number and, in an inflation-fighting regime, the single most powerful release on the calendar. PPI (producer prices) and retail sales typically print within a day or two of CPI, forming a mid-month "inflation-and-spending" cluster.

**FOMC — eight times a year.** The Federal Open Market Committee meets and announces its rate decision **eight times a year** (roughly every six weeks), at 2:00 p.m. on the second day of a two-day meeting. Four of those eight come with the Summary of Economic Projections, including the dot plot. The dates are published a year in advance.

**PCE — near month-end.** The BEA releases the Personal Consumption Expenditures price index at 8:30 a.m. **near the end of the month**, covering the prior month. Core PCE is the Fed's *preferred* inflation gauge — but because CPI prints two weeks earlier and the market can largely infer PCE from the CPI and PPI components, PCE usually generates a smaller reaction. It is the most-watched tier-2 release.

A few things sharpen this rhythm. The FOMC's eight dates fall into two kinds: the four meetings that come with the **Summary of Economic Projections** (the dot plot, every March, June, September, and December) carry far more weight than the four "interim" meetings, because the dots show each official's projected rate path and reveal whether the committee is shifting hawkish or dovish. Three weeks after every FOMC, the **minutes** of that meeting are released at 2:00 p.m. — a tier-2 event that occasionally moves markets if it reveals a more divided or more hawkish committee than the statement let on. Between meetings, individual Fed officials give speeches that can move rate expectations, which is why the calendar's "Fed speak" rows matter more in the run-up to a decision. Layered on top is the weekly **initial jobless claims** print every Thursday at 8:30 — normally tier-3 background noise, but it becomes tier-2 the moment the market is hunting for the first crack in the labor market, as it was through mid-2024.

The cadence is also why traders speak of the **"blackout period."** In the roughly ten days before each FOMC meeting, Fed officials stop giving speeches and interviews — they go silent. That silence concentrates all the information flow into the data releases during the blackout (a CPI or jobs report that lands in the blackout window has outsized power to shape the decision, because the Fed cannot push back verbally). Knowing the blackout is in effect tells you the next CPI is the last big input the committee will weigh, which raises its stakes. None of this requires guessing; it is all printed on the calendar a year ahead. The skill is reading the rhythm, not predicting the dates.

#### Worked example: sizing into the monthly cluster

You manage a \$50,000 account and you want exposure to a continued disinflation trend, but you are wary of event risk. The calendar tells you the mid-month CPI lands Tuesday the 12th, PPI Wednesday the 13th, and FOMC the following Wednesday. You decide to hold only a \$10,000 core long through CPI (risking 4% × \$10,000 = **\$400** to a worst-case −4% CPI shock, inside your \$500 budget), then *add* a second \$10,000 tranche only if CPI confirms the trend — bringing total exposure to \$20,000 *after* the biggest binary risk has passed. If CPI surprises hot and the S&P drops −3%, you lose 0.03 × \$10,000 = **\$300** on the core but were never carrying the full \$20,000 into the landmine. **Scaling around the cadence turns a known event date into a risk-management tool rather than a coin flip.**

## 4. The global layer — the 24-hour clock and the other central banks

US data drives global markets, but the US is not the only game, and the market never sleeps. Macro risk circles the planet every day in a relay, and the major central banks each have decision days that move their own currency blocs.

![24-hour global macro clock from Tokyo to London to New York](/imgs/blogs/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets-4.png)

The clock runs like a relay race. **Tokyo** opens around 7–8 p.m. New York time (the prior evening, US-clock): this is when the Bank of Japan (BoJ) announces decisions and when Japanese and Chinese data print, in thinner liquidity. **London** opens around 3 a.m. New York time: the European Central Bank (ECB) and Bank of England (BoE) announce here, and euro-area and UK data drop as FX and bond desks wake up. **New York** opens with the 8:30 data and the 9:30 equity bell: the deepest liquidity and the biggest moves of the day. Then the baton passes back to **Tokyo**, where Asia reacts overnight to whatever New York did — and the loop repeats. Liquidity peaks in the **London–New York overlap (roughly 8:00–11:00 a.m. ET)**, which is also when most tier-1 US data lands. That overlap is no accident in your P&L: it is when the most participants are at their desks to react.

The major non-US central banks each anchor a bloc:

- **ECB (European Central Bank)** — sets rates for the euro area; its decision days (roughly every six weeks, with a 8:15 a.m. ET statement followed by a presser) move EUR/USD, European bonds (Bunds), and European equities.
- **BoJ (Bank of Japan)** — the world's anchor of cheap funding for decades; a BoJ move is outsized because the yen funds the global "carry trade." When the BoJ hiked on July 31, 2024, it helped trigger a violent unwind days later (covered below).
- **BoE (Bank of England)** — sets UK rates; moves GBP and Gilts; "Super Thursday" bundles the decision, minutes, and forecasts.
- **PBoC (People's Bank of China)** — sets China's rates and the daily yuan fixing; less transparent and less scheduled than the others, but China data and PBoC moves ripple straight into commodities, Asian equities, and — as we will see — Vietnam.

A word on *why* these four are not interchangeable. The Fed is tier-1 globally because the dollar is the world's reserve currency and US Treasuries are the world's risk-free benchmark — every other asset is priced relative to them. The ECB is tier-1 for the euro bloc and, through EUR/USD, for global FX. The BoJ punches above its economy's weight because of the carry trade: decades of near-zero Japanese rates made the yen the world's funding currency, so a BoJ surprise transmits into *every* leveraged position globally, not just Japanese assets. The BoE is mostly a UK story. And the PBoC is a special case: China does not run a transparent, scheduled, market-driven policy framework the way the Western central banks do, so its moves are harder to anticipate and the reaction is often a guessing game about intent. For a trader, the ranking is roughly: a Fed surprise moves everything; an ECB or BoJ surprise moves a lot; a BoE or PBoC surprise moves its own bloc and bleeds outward. Knowing this hierarchy tells you how much screen time each decision day deserves.

Why does a Tokyo central bank matter to a New York stock trader? Because of the **carry trade**: borrow in a cheap currency (the yen, at near-zero rates) and invest in higher-yielding assets worldwide. When the BoJ unexpectedly hikes and the yen surges, that funding gets unwound all at once, forcing selling across global risk assets. The mechanism is covered in the carry-trade deep dive linked at the end; for the calendar, the lesson is that a BoJ decision day belongs on your watchlist even if you only trade US stocks.

The 24-hour structure also explains a pattern that confuses newcomers: why a market can "gap" overnight. If a US trader goes to sleep with the S&P at one level and wakes to find futures sharply lower, the cause is almost always something that printed in the Tokyo or London session — a hot European inflation number, a surprise PBoC move, a BoJ decision, or a geopolitical headline that broke while New York slept. The gap is not random; it is the rest of the world's reaction to data that landed on *their* clock. A trader who only watches the US calendar is repeatedly surprised by these gaps; one who watches the full 24-hour clock sees them coming. The thin-liquidity hours matter here, too: a surprise that lands in the Tokyo session, when fewer participants are at their desks, can move price *further* on *less* news than the same surprise would in the deep London–New York overlap, because there are fewer buyers to absorb the selling. Liquidity is not constant around the clock, and the same headline can have a very different price impact depending on which session it hits.

It is worth being concrete about the global release times, because they are as fixed as the US clock. The ECB announces its rate decision at **2:15 p.m. Frankfurt time (8:15 a.m. ET)**, followed by a press conference 45 minutes later — so on an ECB day, the euro and European bonds move before the US even opens, and a hawkish or dovish ECB can set the tone for the US session that follows. The BoE's "Super Thursday" decision lands at **noon London time (7:00 a.m. ET)**. The BoJ has no fixed minute — it announces when its meeting concludes, usually in the late Tokyo morning, which adds a layer of timing uncertainty that itself moves the yen. China's data — GDP, CPI, PMIs — prints in the Asian morning and ripples into commodities and emerging-market currencies before London opens. The practical upshot: a global watchlist is not just "more events," it is a sequence, and the order in which they land each day shapes how the next session opens.

#### Worked example: a DXY move on a CPI day and a \$5,000 USD/VND-exposed position

On the September 13, 2022 hot-CPI session, the US dollar index (DXY) rose **+1.4%** as the market priced a more aggressive Fed. Suppose you hold \$5,000 of USD-denominated assets while your home currency and expenses are in Vietnamese dong (VND) — a common position for someone saving in dollars but living in Vietnam. A +1.4% DXY move broadly translates into the dollar strengthening against most currencies; against the dong, your \$5,000 stake gains roughly 0.014 × \$5,000 = **+\$70** of relative purchasing power versus VND on the day (the dong tends to weaken when the dollar broadly strengthens). The same logic runs in reverse on the cool-CPI session of November 10, 2022, when DXY fell **−2.1%**: a \$5,000 dollar position lost about 0.021 × \$5,000 = **−\$105** of relative value versus stronger non-dollar currencies. A US inflation print you might think is "not your market" reprices your dong-versus-dollar exposure within minutes — that is why DXY belongs on a Vietnam-based trader's screen.

## 5. The Vietnam layer — the SBV, GSO CPI, and the dollar's long reach

For a Vietnam-based reader, the calendar has a local layer most global guides skip entirely — and a crucial twist: Vietnam's own data matters, but the biggest driver of the VN-Index on any given week is often a *US* number, transmitted through foreign capital flows.

**The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) — Vietnam's FOMC.** The SBV sets Vietnam's policy rates (the refinancing and discount rates), manages the dong's exchange rate, and — uniquely — sets a **credit-growth ceiling** that caps how much banks may lend each year. Unlike the Fed's calendar-scheduled meetings, SBV moves are less predictable in timing, often announced as needed. But when they come, they are tier-1 for Vietnamese assets. The mechanism (rates, the dong, the credit ceiling) is covered in the Vietnam-monetary-policy deep dive linked at the end.

![SBV refinancing rate step chart hikes in 2022 then three cuts to 4.5 percent](/imgs/blogs/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets-6.png)

The step chart above traces the SBV's refinancing rate as exactly the kind of tier-1 policy event a Vietnam trader watches. Through 2020–2021 the rate sat at 4.0%. Then in autumn 2022, as the Fed hiked aggressively and the dollar surged, the SBV raised the rate **+200 basis points** in two moves (to 5.0% on September 23 and 6.0% on October 25) — not because Vietnamese inflation was out of control (it ran near 3%), but to *defend the dong* against a roaring dollar. As US pressure eased in 2023, the SBV reversed course with three cuts back to **4.5%** (April–June 2023) and has held there since. That sequence is the single clearest illustration of the post's thesis: **a US tier-1 force (the Fed path) drove a Vietnamese tier-1 policy response.**

**GSO inflation (Vietnam CPI).** The General Statistics Office (GSO) of Vietnam publishes the country's CPI monthly. Vietnamese headline inflation has been notably tame — averaging roughly 1.8% in 2021, 3.2% in 2022, 3.3% in 2023, and 3.6% in 2024 — kept under a government target near 4–4.5%. Because it rarely surprises violently, GSO CPI is more tier-2 for markets than the US CPI is, but it directly conditions how much room the SBV has to cut.

**Credit growth and FX — the levers that actually move the VN-Index.** Two things drive Vietnamese stocks more than the CPI print does: the **credit-growth ceiling** (loosen it and liquidity floods into property and equities; tighten it and the market starves) and the **USD/VND exchange rate** (a weakening dong forces the SBV to tighten, which drains liquidity). The dong has steadily depreciated — from roughly 22,790 per dollar at end-2021 to about 25,485 by end-2024 — and each leg of dollar strength has coincided with SBV defense and VN-Index pressure.

**Why DXY and US yields drive the VN-Index through foreign flows.** Vietnam is a frontier-to-emerging market that depends on foreign capital. When US yields rise and the dollar strengthens (a hot US CPI, a hawkish FOMC), global investors pull money *out* of riskier emerging markets and *into* dollar assets. Foreign investors were **net sellers of roughly 90 trillion dong** of Vietnamese stocks in 2024 — the heaviest foreign outflow on record — as the dollar stayed strong and US yields stayed high. The VN-Index round-trip tells the story: it peaked near **1,528 in January 2022**, then collapsed to a trough of **911 on November 15, 2022** (down roughly −39%) as the Fed hiked and the dollar surged, before recovering to about **1,267 by end-2024**. The transmission and the index-effect of foreign ETF flows are covered in the foreign-flows deep dive linked at the end. The headline for the calendar: **a Vietnam trader must watch the US tier-1 releases as closely as the local ones, because the dollar and US yields are the upstream cause of the foreign flows that set the VN-Index.**

Trace the chain link by link, because it is the most important transmission a Vietnam-based trader can internalize. A hot US CPI prints at 8:30 ET — which is 8:30 in the *evening* in Vietnam, after the Ho Chi Minh City exchange has closed. Overnight, US yields rise and the dollar strengthens. The next morning, with the dong now under more depreciation pressure, two things happen: foreign funds that benchmark to emerging-market indices trim their Vietnam allocations (selling pressure on the VN-Index), and the SBV faces a harder choice between defending the dong (tightening, which drains local liquidity) and supporting growth (easing, which weakens the dong further). The VN-Index opens lower not because anything changed in Vietnam, but because something changed in Washington the night before. This is why a Vietnamese trader who checks only the GSO calendar is genuinely trading blind — the upstream cause of half the index's big moves is a US release they never see on a local calendar.

The credit-growth ceiling deserves special emphasis because it is the lever with no equivalent in developed markets. Each year the SBV sets a target for how fast total bank lending may grow — say, 14–15% — and allocates quotas to individual banks. When the ceiling is raised mid-year (or quotas are topped up), it is a direct liquidity injection: more lending means more money chasing property and stocks, and the VN-Index tends to rally on the announcement. When the ceiling is hit and lending freezes, the market starves. So a Vietnamese event trader watches three local "prints" that have no US analog — the annual credit-growth target, mid-year quota adjustments, and the USD/VND reference rate the SBV sets each morning — alongside the US tier-1 calendar that drives the foreign-flow tide.

#### Worked example: an SBV 50bp cut and a \$10,000-equivalent VN bank-stock position

Suppose you hold the dong-equivalent of **\$10,000** in a basket of Vietnamese bank stocks, and the SBV announces a surprise **−50 basis-point** cut to the refinancing rate. Bank stocks and the broad VN-Index are highly rate-sensitive: lower policy rates cut banks' funding costs, loosen liquidity, and lift loan demand. A clean, single-day relief rally on a dovish surprise of this size has historically run on the order of +2–4% for rate-sensitive Vietnamese names. Taking +3% as a central estimate, your \$10,000 position gains 0.03 × \$10,000 = **+\$300** on the day. If instead the dong is under pressure and the SBV is *forced to hike* 50bp to defend it (as in autumn 2022), the same basket can fall −3% or worse, costing roughly 0.03 × \$10,000 = **−\$300** — and the autumn-2022 drawdown to the 911 trough turned that into far more for anyone holding through it. **SBV decisions are Vietnam's FOMC: a single announcement reprices your whole local book.**

## How it reacted: tier-1 vs tier-3 — the size gap

Numbers make the asymmetry undeniable. The chart below assembles the biggest single-day S&P 500 moves by event type from real, dated sessions, against a typical tier-3 day.

![Bar chart of biggest single-day S&P 500 move by event type tier-1 versus tier-3](/imgs/blogs/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets-3.png)

Read the bars left to right. The cool CPI of **November 10, 2022** sent the S&P **+5.54%** — its best day in over two years — as a −0.2pp downside inflation surprise told the market the Fed could ease off. The hot CPI of **September 13, 2022** sent it **−4.32%**. A weak NFP on **August 2, 2024** (+114k vs ~175k expected, unemployment jumping to 4.3%) sent it **−1.84%** on recession fear. A hawkish FOMC on **December 19, 2018** sent it **−1.54%**. A blowout NFP on **February 3, 2023** (+517k vs ~187k) sent it **−1.04%** as strong jobs meant a higher-for-longer Fed. And a typical tier-3 day sits near **+0.18%** — inside the noise.

The gap is the lesson. The two CPI bars alone span more than ten percentage points of single-day range. The tier-3 bar is a sliver. No amount of attention to factory orders would have earned or saved you what one CPI print did.

Notice also that the *same* event type produced wildly different signs depending on the regime. Both the November 2022 (+5.54%) and September 2022 (−4.32%) bars are CPI days — the difference is entirely the surprise direction in an inflation-obsessed regime: a cool print meant fewer hikes (risk-on), a hot print meant more hikes (risk-off). The two NFP bars carry the same lesson with a twist: the February 2023 blowout (+517k) *fell* −1.04% because strong jobs meant higher-for-longer, while the August 2024 weak print (+114k, unemployment 4.3%) fell *harder* at −1.84% — but for the opposite reason, recession fear. In one window, strength was bad because it meant more Fed; in the next, weakness was bad because it meant a hard landing. The event type tells you the *size* of the likely move; only the regime tells you the *sign*. This is why memorizing "hot CPI = stocks down" as a permanent rule is a trap — it was true in 2022, but in a different regime a cool-enough-to-cut print can rally stocks and a too-hot print can do the same if the fear was deflation. Always anchor the sign to what the market is afraid of this month.

The deepest episodes layer the tiers. Consider the **August 2024 carry cascade**. The setup was a global one: the BoJ hiked on July 31 (a Tokyo tier-1 event), then the weak US NFP on August 2 (a New York tier-1 event) stoked recession fears. The combination forced a violent unwind of the yen carry trade on August 5. The Nikkei fell **−12.4%** — its worst day since the 1987 crash. The S&P 500 fell **−3.0%**. The VIX (the market's "fear gauge") spiked intraday to **65.73** from a close near 23 days earlier. Bitcoin fell roughly **−15%**. Two tier-1 events on opposite sides of the globe, two days apart, cascaded into a worldwide risk-off. Then the Nikkei rebounded **+10.2%** the next session — a textbook spike-then-reversal that punished anyone who chased the panic. The episode is the perfect proof of the 24-hour clock and the global layer working as one system.

#### Worked example: the carry-cascade on a \$25,000 global-equity book

You hold a \$25,000 globally diversified equity book on August 2, 2024: say \$15,000 in US stocks and \$10,000 in Japanese stocks. Over August 5, the US sleeve falls −3.0% (0.03 × \$15,000 = **−\$450**) and the Japan sleeve falls −12.4% (0.124 × \$10,000 = **−\$1,240**), for a one-day mark-to-market loss of about **−\$1,690**, or −6.8% of the whole book. The investor who knew the BoJ-plus-NFP setup was a tier-1 double-event and had trimmed the Japan sleeve ahead of it took a fraction of that. The investor who panic-sold the Japan sleeve at the August 5 low then missed the **+10.2%** rebound the next day — turning a paper loss into a realized one. **Two tier-1 events two days apart moved a quiet book by nearly 7% in a session; the calendar told you the risk was coming.**

## Common misconceptions

**"More data means more to trade."** No. A heavy calendar week is mostly tier-3 filler. One CPI print out-moves a dozen second-tier releases combined. On September 13, 2022, the week's other five releases barely registered while one CPI moved the S&P −4.32%. Quantity is not signal; the tier is.

**"A strong economy is always good for stocks."** Regime-dependent. In the 2022–2023 inflation fight, a *strong* jobs report was *bad* for stocks because it meant a higher-for-longer Fed: the +517k blowout NFP on February 3, 2023 sent the S&P **−1.04%** and the two-year yield **+18bp**. Good-news-is-bad-news only flips back to good-news-is-good-news once inflation is no longer the binding constraint. Always ask what the market fears *this* month before you guess the sign.

**"PCE is the Fed's favorite, so it must move markets most."** It is the Fed's preferred gauge, but CPI moves markets more because CPI prints ~two weeks earlier and the market can largely infer PCE from the already-released CPI and PPI components. By the time PCE lands near month-end, most of its information is stale. Importance to the Fed and impact on the tape are not the same thing.

**"The reaction is over once the number prints."** Often false. The first move is the **knee-jerk**; then comes the **fade** or the **trend**. On FOMC days the 2:00 statement and the 2:30 press conference frequently push price in *opposite* directions. A trader who books the knee-jerk and walks away at 2:15 is regularly whipsawed at 2:35.

**"Vietnamese stocks only care about Vietnamese data."** The opposite is often true. The dollar and US yields — set by US tier-1 prints — drive the foreign flows that move the VN-Index. The 2024 record **−90 trillion dong** of net foreign selling tracked dollar strength far more than any GSO CPI print. A Vietnam trader who ignores the US CPI is trading half-blind.

**"If I can't predict the number, I can't trade the event."** You do not need to predict the number — almost no one can, consistently — to trade the event well. The edge in event trading is **preparation, not prediction**: knowing the consensus, pre-defining your action for the hot / in-line / cool scenarios, sizing for the worst case, and placing the stop before the print. You react to the surprise with a plan already written, rather than improvising in the chaos of the first 90 seconds. Traders who try to forecast the CPI to two decimals lose; traders who prepare three branches and execute the right one win.

**"Bigger surprises always mean bigger moves."** Usually, but not linearly, and sometimes not at all. A huge surprise that the market had *whispered* about (positioning already leaned that way) can produce a muted move, while a modest surprise that catches everyone offside can detonate. The reaction depends on the surprise *relative to positioning*, not just the surprise relative to consensus. The September 18, 2024 FOMC is a clean example: the Fed cut **−50bp** — a large, somewhat unexpected move — yet the S&P closed only **−0.29%** on the day, because so much easing was already priced and the cut was read as insurance rather than panic. Size of surprise is one input; what was already in the price is the other.

## The playbook: building your event watchlist

Here is how to turn all of this into a working watchlist you can run every week.

![Watchlist decision tree sorting releases by tier into prep ignore](/imgs/blogs/the-global-economic-calendar-what-actually-moves-markets-7.png)

**Step 1 — pull the week ahead and triage by tier.** Every Sunday, open an economic calendar and filter to the highest-impact (three-star) events. For each, ask the question in the decision figure above: *is it tier-1?* If yes (US CPI, FOMC, NFP — or an SBV / major-central-bank decision day), it gets a plan. If it is tier-2, note it as a context check. If it is tier-3, ignore it — no new risk goes on for a trade-balance print.

**Step 2 — write down the consensus and the scenarios for each tier-1 event.** You cannot trade a surprise you have not defined. For the CPI, write the consensus (say, 3.1% headline, 3.5% core) and three scenarios: *in line* (likely a fade / non-event), *hot* (in the current regime, risk-off — stocks down, dollar and yields up), *cool* (risk-on — stocks up, dollar and yields down). Decide your action for each *before* the print, because you will not think clearly in the first 90 seconds after 8:30.

**Step 3 — size for the event, not the average day.** As the worked examples showed, a tier-1 print can move a broad position several percent in a session. Set the position so the worst-case scenario stays inside your risk budget. On a \$50,000 account risking 1% (\$500), a possible −4% CPI shock caps the position at \$12,500 — trade smaller into the landmine. There is a market-implied way to estimate the worst-case move so you are not guessing: the **expected move** baked into options prices, which the options market publishes for you every day. The shortcut is that the at-the-money straddle price (the cost of buying both a call and a put at the current level, expiring just after the event) is roughly the market's one-standard-deviation expected move.

#### Worked example: using the option-implied expected move to size a CPI trade

Say the S&P 500 is at 5,000 the day before a CPI print, and the at-the-money straddle expiring the next day costs **\$60** (in index points). The implied expected move is \$60 ÷ 5,000 = **1.2%** — meaning the options market thinks there is roughly a two-thirds chance the index lands within ±1.2% by the close, and a one-third chance it moves *more*. On a \$15,000 position, a 1.2% move is 0.012 × \$15,000 = **±\$180**, but you must plan for the tail: a hot-CPI shock can be 3–4× the implied move, so the realistic worst case is closer to \$540–\$720. For Bitcoin the same day, with BTC at 60,000 and its straddle at **\$2,400**, the implied move is \$2,400 ÷ 60,000 = **4.0%** — more than three times the S&P's — so a \$10,000 BTC position carries an implied ±\$400 and a realistic tail near ±\$1,200. **The straddle price is the market handing you the event's risk budget; read it before you size, and let the most volatile asset get the smallest position.**

**Step 4 — respect the clock and the fade.** Be at the desk for 8:30 (data) or 2:00 (FOMC). Expect a knee-jerk, then watch for the fade or the trend; do not assume the first tick is the last word, and on FOMC days do not leave before the 2:30 presser.

**Step 5 — set the invalidation and walk away.** Define, in advance, the price level or the time at which you are wrong and exit. The whole edge of event trading is preparation; once the plan is set and the stop is placed, the discipline is to *not* improvise in the chaos.

**Step 6 — for Vietnam, watch the upstream cause.** A Vietnam-based trader runs two layers: the local watchlist (SBV decisions, GSO CPI, credit-growth and USD/VND headlines) *and* the US tier-1 watchlist, because the dollar and US yields drive the foreign flows that set the VN-Index. When the US CPI prints hot and DXY jumps, expect foreign-selling pressure on the VN-Index regardless of what GSO said.

To make this concrete, here is what one week on the watchlist looks like. On Sunday you pull the calendar and find: Tuesday — US CPI (tier-1, three stars); Wednesday — US PPI and retail sales (tier-2); Thursday — weekly jobless claims (tier-3 unless the labor market is the story) and the ECB decision (tier-1 for euro assets); Friday — nothing major. Plus, all month: an SBV that could adjust the dong reference rate any morning. Your week is now triaged. Monday you write the CPI plan: consensus, three scenarios, position sizes, stops. Tuesday at 8:30 ET you are at the desk; you let the knee-jerk happen, watch whether it fades or trends, and execute the branch you prepared. Wednesday you *glance* at PPI to see whether it confirms Tuesday's CPI signal, but you do not build a separate trade for it. Thursday you note the jobless claims in passing and pay real attention to the ECB at 8:15 ET if you hold any euro exposure. Friday you rest. Everything else that printed that week — and there will be a dozen tier-3 releases — you let scroll past. That is the entire discipline in practice: a handful of planned events, and a wall of ignored noise.

The biggest behavioral trap is the opposite of laziness — it is *over-engagement*. New traders, eager to "use" the calendar, try to trade every three-star event and end up overtrading the noise, paying spreads and commissions on reactions that were always going to be inside the day's normal range. The calendar's real value is as much about what it tells you to *ignore* as what it tells you to watch. A disciplined event trader does very little most days and a lot on the three or four days a month when a tier-1 print actually lands. The patience to wait for those days — and to size up only then — is the edge.

The discipline is simple to state and hard to keep: **know what is tier-1, prep a plan for it, and ignore the noise.** Dozens of prints will scroll past your calendar every week trying to convince you they matter. A small handful actually do. Trade those, and let the rest go by.
