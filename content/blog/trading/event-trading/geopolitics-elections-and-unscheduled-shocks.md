> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Half of what moves markets is on a calendar; the other half is not. Wars, oil shocks, OPEC cuts, surprise elections and debt-ceiling standoffs arrive with no release time, and when they hit, the market does the same reflexive thing every time: it runs to safety.
>
> - **What it is:** an *unscheduled shock* is news that markets could not have timed in advance, so unlike a CPI print there is no consensus already in the price to fade against — the whole move is surprise, which is why these events produce the biggest, fastest gaps.
> - **How the market reacts — the cross-asset map:** the **flight to safety** (also called *risk-off*). Money rushes OUT of stocks, crypto and emerging markets and INTO four classic safe havens — US Treasuries, gold, the US dollar and the Japanese yen — all at once. Oil is the special case: a supply shock sends oil *up* while it sends stocks *down*.
> - **The trade:** the edge is not in predicting the shock — you cannot — it is in the **reflex and the classification**. Size down, do not chase the gap, position for safety, and above all decide whether this is a *fade* (most geopolitical scares revert in days to weeks) or a *regime change* (oil, inflation and policy shocks that reset the level for years).
> - **The one number to remember:** since 1945, the median S&P 500 selloff around a major geopolitical shock has been recovered within **weeks, not months** — the headline that feels like the end of the world usually is not. The exceptions are the ones that change the price of oil or the path of rates.

It is a quiet Sunday evening. Markets are closed. Somewhere in the world, over the weekend, something breaks — an invasion, a missile strike on an oil field, a coup, a surprise election result that no poll saw coming. There was no 8:30 a.m. release time for it, no economists' consensus number printed in a calendar, no countdown. It simply happened, and now a few hundred million people who are long stocks are going to wake up to a market that has already decided what it thinks.

By the time futures reopen Sunday night, the tape has lurched. Equity index futures are gapping down. Crude oil is up several dollars a barrel. Gold is bid. Treasury futures are bid — meaning yields are falling. The dollar is firmer, and against almost everything the Japanese yen has strengthened. None of these moves were coordinated by anyone. No committee voted. They are the *same reflex* that markets have shown for as long as there have been markets: when the world gets scary and the future gets less certain, capital sprints out of anything that can fall and into the handful of assets it trusts to hold value when everything else is on fire. Traders call this the **flight to safety**, or **risk-off**, and it is the single most important pattern in this entire post.

This is the half of event trading that has no schedule. Everywhere else in this series we have studied the *calendar* — the CPI print, the jobs report, the FOMC decision — events whose power comes from the *surprise* against a known consensus at a known time. Unscheduled shocks are the mirror image. There is no consensus baked into the price because nobody knew the event was coming, so the entire move is raw surprise hitting an unprepared market. That makes these events more violent, harder to fade, and far more dangerous to the careless. But it also means the edge is different. You cannot forecast a war. What you *can* do is recognize the reflex the instant it fires, classify the shock correctly, and know — from a hundred years of history — which shocks fade and which ones change everything.

## Foundations: the anatomy of an unscheduled shock

Before we can trade these events, we need to be precise about what they are and why they behave so differently from the scheduled releases. Let us build every term from zero.

### A shock versus scheduled data

Recall the central idea of this whole series: **markets trade expectations, not facts**. A CPI print at 8:30 a.m. moves the market only by its *surprise* — the gap between the actual number and the consensus economists already had in the price. If everyone expected 3.2% and it prints 3.2%, the market barely twitches, because the number was already *in* the price. The companion post on [why news moves markets](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) builds this out: price already contains the consensus, so only the surprise moves it.

An **unscheduled shock** breaks that frame. It is a piece of news that markets could not have put on a calendar and could not have polled economists about: a war that starts on a Thursday, a refinery that explodes, an OPEC meeting that surprises with a deeper cut than briefed, an election that flips against every poll, a debt-ceiling negotiation that suddenly looks like it might actually fail. Because nobody scheduled it, there is **no consensus already priced in**. The market was sitting at one level assuming the world was normal, and now the world is not normal. The *entire distance* between the old price and the new one has to be travelled in one move, because the old price reflected a world that no longer exists.

That is why shocks gap. A scheduled release pushes price by the small surprise around a known number; an unscheduled shock revalues an asset from "world is fine" to "world is not fine" with nothing in between. The companion piece on [liquidity and gaps around news](/blog/trading/event-trading/liquidity-and-gaps-around-news) explains the plumbing of *how* that gap forms — market makers pull their quotes and price jumps through an empty order book. Here we care about *why the destination is where it is*: the flight to safety.

### The flight to safety, or risk-off

The **flight to safety** (the words traders actually shout are *risk-off*) is the coordinated, reflexive rotation of capital away from assets that can fall and toward assets believed to protect value in a crisis. Its mirror image is **risk-on**: the calm-times rotation back into stocks, credit and growth. The macro-series post on [risk-on, risk-off and how money rotates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/risk-on-risk-off-how-money-rotates) maps the full cycle; for shocks, the only direction that matters in the first hours is *off*.

Here is the everyday version. Say you keep your savings spread across a small business you part-own, a friend's promising startup, and a stack of government savings bonds. One night you hear that a hurricane is heading for your town. What do you do? You do not call your friend to buy *more* of the risky startup. You pull cash, you hold the safe bonds, you batten down. You move toward what is certain and away from what is fragile, and you do it *fast*, before you even know how bad the storm will be. A market full of investors does exactly the same thing on a geopolitical shock, except it happens in seconds and across trillions of dollars. The fragile assets — stocks, crypto, emerging markets — get sold. The certain ones get bought. That is the flight to safety, and the assets on the receiving end have a name.

### Safe havens: the four classic ones

A **safe haven** is an asset that investors buy *because* everything else is falling — its appeal goes *up* when risk goes up. There are four classic ones, and you should know not just their names but *why* each one earns the title:

- **US Treasuries** (US government bonds). When investors are scared, they want to lend to the borrower least likely to default: the US government, which issues the world's reserve currency and can always print to repay. So in a crisis, money floods into Treasuries, which pushes their *price up* and their *yield down*. (Bond prices and yields always move opposite — the macro series on [reading the yield curve](/blog/trading/macro-trading/reading-the-yield-curve-slope-inversion-recession) explains the mechanism.) A falling 10-year yield in a panic is the single clearest fingerprint of risk-off.
- **Gold**. Gold has no government, no CEO, no earnings, and no counterparty who can fail to pay you. It is just metal that has been treated as money for five thousand years. In a crisis — especially one that threatens the value of paper money or the stability of governments — gold is the asset of last resort that does not depend on anyone else keeping a promise.
- **The US dollar (USD)**. The dollar is the world's reserve currency: most global trade, debt and reserves are denominated in it. In a panic, everyone scrambles for dollars to pay debts and hold cash, so the dollar tends to *strengthen* in a crisis even when the crisis is American — a phenomenon the macro series calls the [dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile).
- **The Japanese yen (JPY) and Swiss franc (CHF)**. The yen is a *funding currency*: for years investors borrowed cheap yen to buy higher-yielding assets elsewhere (the *carry trade*). When risk-off hits, those trades unwind, the borrowed yen gets bought back, and the yen surges. The Swiss franc is a haven for the older reason — a politically stable, well-run economy that the world trusts.

The rotation diagram at the top of this post is the whole reflex in one picture: red arrows of money leaving stocks, crypto and emerging markets; green arrows of money flooding into those four havens. Memorize the directions. They are the same in almost every shock.

### The risk premium

Why does this rotation happen at all? Because of the **risk premium** — the extra return investors demand for holding something risky instead of something safe. A stock is worth the present value of its future earnings, discounted by a rate that includes a chunk for risk. When a shock makes the future more uncertain, investors demand a *bigger* risk premium: they will only hold the risky asset at a *lower* price. So the price of risky assets falls not because the earnings changed but because the *discount on those earnings* rose. The flight to safety is, mechanically, the risk premium repricing higher across the entire risky universe at once. When you hear a trader say "the risk premium just jumped," they mean exactly this: fear got more expensive to ignore.

### An oil supply shock: the special case

Most of the time the flight to safety is clean — risk down, havens up. But there is one shock that breaks the symmetry, and it is the most economically important of all: the **oil supply shock**.

Oil is not just another commodity. It is the input to nearly everything: transport, manufacturing, food (fertilizer and shipping), heating. A **supply shock** is anything that suddenly removes barrels from the market — a war in a producing region, an OPEC+ production cut, an embargo, a pipeline or refinery hit. Less oil for the same demand means the price of oil jumps. And because oil feeds into the cost of almost everything, a higher oil price pushes up *inflation* — which, as we will see, traps central banks and can change the entire market regime. So an oil supply shock sends oil *up* even as it sends stocks *down*, and that combination is uniquely dangerous. We will spend a full section on it.

### Headline risk, the fade, and the regime change

Two more terms, and then we can trade. **Headline risk** is the danger that a single news headline — not a number, not a trend, just a sentence on a wire — moves your position violently before you can react. It is the defining hazard of unscheduled shocks: you cannot hedge a headline you do not know is coming.

And the most important distinction in the entire post: the **fade** versus the **regime change**. A *fade* is a shock whose market impact reverses within days to weeks because it did not actually change the economic fundamentals — most geopolitical scares are fades. A *regime change* is a shock that resets the level of something permanent — the price of oil, the path of interest rates, the structure of trade — so the move *sticks* for months or years. Knowing which one you are looking at is the whole game. Trade a fade as if it were a regime change and you sell the bottom; trade a regime change as if it were a fade and you buy a falling knife all the way down. We will build a decision tool for telling them apart.

## The taxonomy of shocks

Unscheduled shocks are not random noise. They fall into four recognizable families, and each family has its own characteristic *examples* and its own characteristic *half-life*. Knowing which family you are in is the first step to knowing whether to fade or respect the move.

![Matrix of four shock families geopolitical oil and OPEC elections and policy and fiscal brinkmanship with examples and typical duration](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks-3.png)

### 1. Geopolitical and war shocks

These are the headline-grabbers: wars, invasions, terror attacks, coups, assassinations, sudden escalations between nuclear powers. The 1990 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, the September 11 attacks in 2001, the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, recurring flare-ups in the Middle East — all geopolitical shocks. The reflex they trigger is the textbook flight to safety: equities down, gold and Treasuries up, dollar firm, oil up *if* the conflict touches an oil-producing region.

The crucial empirical fact about pure geopolitical shocks — the one we will hammer in the misconceptions section — is that **they usually fade**. A war in a region with no oil and no major trade route is terrible for the people in it but, coldly, has little durable effect on the discounted future earnings of American companies. The market sells first on fear, then realizes the fundamentals did not actually move, and buys back. The exception is precisely when the conflict *does* move a fundamental — most often the price of oil.

### 2. OPEC and oil supply shocks

OPEC (the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries) and its broader alliance OPEC+ collectively control a large share of the world's oil exports, and they meet to set production targets. When they surprise the market with a deeper-than-expected cut — or when a war removes a major producer's barrels — the oil price gaps higher. The 1973 Arab oil embargo quadrupled the oil price and is the archetype; the 2022 invasion of Ukraine spiked crude above \$120 a barrel intra-year.

Oil shocks are the family most likely to be a **regime change** rather than a fade, because oil feeds straight into inflation, and inflation drives interest rates, and interest rates reprice every asset on earth. A pure geopolitical scare can fade in a week. An oil shock that lifts inflation can change the rate path for a year. This is why the macro-signals post on [commodities as macro signals](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) calls oil the most macro of all commodities — it is the bridge from a geopolitical headline to your mortgage rate.

### 3. Elections and policy uncertainty

Elections are the strange members of the family: the *date* is known, but the *outcome* is not, and sometimes the outcome is a genuine shock. The 2016 Brexit referendum, the same year's US presidential election, surprise results in major economies, sudden tariff announcements, regulatory shifts — these are policy shocks. They differ from wars because there is usually a *pre-event drift* (markets position cautiously as the date approaches) followed by a sharp *relief or fear move* once the result is known. We will dig into the structure of these in their own section because it is genuinely different from the rest.

### 4. Fiscal brinkmanship: debt ceilings and shutdowns

The last family is self-inflicted: governments threatening not to fund themselves. In the US, two recurring versions matter. A **government shutdown** happens when Congress fails to pass funding bills and non-essential government operations stop. A **debt-ceiling standoff** is far more serious: the debt ceiling is a legal cap on how much the US Treasury can borrow, and if Congress refuses to raise it, the government could in theory *default* on its debt — fail to pay bondholders. The 2011 and 2023 debt-ceiling fights both rattled markets; the 2011 episode contributed to the first-ever downgrade of the US credit rating.

Fiscal brinkmanship is mostly *noise that fades* — historically a deal always lands at the last minute and markets recover. But it carries a fat tail: a true US default would not be a fade, it would be the regime change to end all regime changes, because Treasuries are the safe haven, and a default would poison the very asset everyone runs to. That asymmetry — usually noise, occasionally catastrophic — is what makes these episodes so nerve-wracking to trade.

#### Worked example: a geopolitical gap and a small hedge

You are long a broad equity position worth **\$25,000** going into a weekend. Over the weekend a geopolitical shock hits, and futures reopen with equities gapping down 3%. Your position takes a hit of 3% × \$25,000 = **−\$750** before you can do anything — that is the headline risk of holding risk over a weekend. Now suppose you had been holding a small **\$10,000** gold position as insurance, and gold catches the flight to safety, rising 2%: that is +2% × \$10,000 = **+\$200**. Your net hit shrinks from −\$750 to −\$750 + \$200 = **−\$550**. The hedge did not make you money — it softened the blow. The lesson: a haven sleeve does not need to be large to take the edge off a gap, because in a true risk-off event the havens rise *while* the risk assets fall, so a little protection works exactly when you need it most.

## The flight-to-safety mechanism: what gets bought and why

We have named the four havens. Now let us be precise about the *mechanics* of the rotation — what actually happens to each asset in the first minutes and hours of a shock, and why. This is the pattern you are training your reflexes to recognize.

### Stocks and crypto: the high-beta sell-off

Equities fall first and hardest because they are the purest expression of "the future will be good." A shock makes the future less certain, the risk premium jumps, and the discounted value of all those future earnings drops. Within equities, the *highest-beta* names fall most: small caps, unprofitable growth companies, anything leveraged. **Beta** is a measure of how much an asset moves relative to the broad market; high beta means it amplifies both up and down moves.

Crypto, and Bitcoin especially, behaves like *very* high-beta risk in a shock. Despite the "digital gold" narrative, in an acute risk-off event Bitcoin almost always falls *with* stocks, often more. In the August 2024 cascade Bitcoin dropped about **−15%** in a day while the S&P 500 fell 3%. The macro framing — that crypto trades as a [macro liquidity asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/crypto-as-a-macro-liquidity-asset), not an independent haven — explains why: when investors scramble for cash and safety, the most speculative asset is the first thing sold.

### Treasuries: yields fall as money floods in

The clearest haven signal is the bid for government bonds. As money pours into Treasuries their price rises and their yield falls. This is why, in a classic risk-off, you see the 10-year Treasury yield *drop* even as stocks crater. It is also a *return* of capital, not a return *on* capital — investors are not buying Treasuries for the yield, they are buying them to park money somewhere it cannot vaporize.

There is one critical exception, and it is the fiscal-brinkmanship case: if the shock is *about Treasuries themselves* — a debt-ceiling default scare — then Treasuries can sell off instead of rally, because the safe asset is the thing under threat. Always ask: is the shock *external* to the safe haven (most cases, haven rallies) or *about* the safe haven (rare, haven wobbles)?

#### Worked example: the flight to Treasuries

You own a **\$500,000** position in 10-year Treasuries. A geopolitical shock triggers a flight to safety and the 10-year yield rallies (falls) by 20 basis points (0.20%). To translate a yield move into dollars, we use **DV01** — the dollar change in the position's value for a 1-basis-point move in yield. For a 10-year Treasury, DV01 is roughly \$0.86 per \$1,000 of face value per basis point, so on \$500,000 of face the DV01 is about 500 × \$0.86 ≈ **\$430 per basis point**. A 20-basis-point yield *fall* lifts the price (price moves opposite to yield), so your gain is 20 bp × \$430/bp = **+\$8,600**. While your stocks were bleeding, your Treasuries were the asset that paid you — that is the whole point of holding the haven. The intuition: in a risk-off event the bond rally is not a side bet, it is the structural offset to the equity drawdown, which is why a balanced book hurts far less than an all-stock one.

### Gold and the dollar: the two stores of value

Gold and the dollar both rally in a shock, but for different reasons, and they sometimes compete. Gold rises because it is the no-counterparty store of value — it cannot default and does not depend on any government. The dollar rises because the world needs dollars to settle debts and because US Treasuries (the safest large asset) are dollar-denominated, so buying the haven *is* buying dollars. In most shocks both rise together. Occasionally — especially when the *dollar itself* is the worry (a US-centered fiscal crisis) — gold rises while the dollar wobbles, which is gold doing its oldest job: hedging the currency.

### The yen: the carry unwind

The yen's haven behavior is the most mechanical and the most violent. For years, with Japanese interest rates near zero, investors borrowed yen cheaply and bought higher-yielding assets around the world — the **carry trade**. That works beautifully in calm times and unwinds catastrophically in a shock: when risk-off hits, the borrowed positions are sold and the yen is bought back to repay the loans, which *strengthens* the yen, which forces *more* unwinding, in a feedback loop. The macro series traces these episodes across [1998, 2008 and 2024](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks); the August 2024 cascade was a textbook one, with the yen surging from about 162 to the dollar to 142 in days as the carry trade blew up and dragged global stocks down with it.

### Emerging markets and Vietnam: the far end of the rotation

Notice that emerging markets sit on the *red* side of the rotation diagram, right next to crypto. There is a reason. When a global shock hits, foreign capital that had been chasing higher returns in smaller, riskier markets rushes home to the dollar and to Treasuries. The result is a brutal one-two punch for emerging economies: their stock markets fall *and* their currencies weaken at the same time, because the foreign money leaving has to sell local stocks and then sell the local currency to buy dollars. A risk-off event is, for an emerging market, a *capital-flight* event.

Vietnam is a clean example. The VN-Index is heavily influenced by foreign flows on the Ho Chi Minh exchange, and the State Bank of Vietnam manages the dong against the dollar. In a global risk-off shock, foreign investors net-sell Vietnamese stocks and the dong comes under pressure as dollars leave — exactly the dynamic that played out across 2022–2024, when sustained foreign net selling and a strong dollar weighed on the index. The defensive lesson is structural: an emerging-market equity position is *long* the global risk premium, so it falls harder than US stocks in a shock and needs a larger haven offset, not a smaller one. A trader who treats a VN-Index position as if it were as resilient as the S&P in a panic is under-hedged exactly when the gap is widest.

### The volatility spike

All of this shows up in one number: **volatility**. The VIX index — often called the "fear gauge" — measures the market's expectation of near-term S&P 500 volatility, derived from options prices. In calm times it sits around its long-run average of roughly 19.5; in a shock it explodes, because everyone rushes to buy options as insurance and that demand drives the index up. Crucially, **shocks, not slow trends, produce the biggest volatility spikes** — a grinding bear market might keep the VIX in the 20s for months, but an acute shock launches it into the 40s, 60s, or higher in a single session.

![Bar chart of crisis VIX peaks LTCM 1998 GFC 2008 COVID 2020 and yen carry 2024 against the long-run average of 19.5](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks-2.png)

Look at the peaks. The 1998 LTCM crisis (a hedge-fund blowup and Russian default) took the VIX to about **45.7**. The 2008 global financial crisis peaked near **80.9**. The COVID crash in March 2020 hit **82.7**. The August 2024 yen-carry unwind spiked the VIX intraday to **65.7**. Against a normal reading near 19.5, these are 2× to 4× normal — and every one of them was an acute shock, not a slow grind. When you see the VIX gapping toward 40, you are not looking at a trend; you are looking at a shock, and the flight to safety is already underway. The vol-crush mechanics of *how* that spike resolves are covered in the companion post on [event volatility, implied versus realized](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/volatility-surface); for shocks, the takeaway is simpler — a vertical VIX is the alarm bell of risk-off.

## Oil shocks: supply versus demand, and the inflation channel

Oil deserves its own section because it is the shock that most often turns a fade into a regime change. To trade it you have to distinguish two completely different kinds of oil move that look identical on a chart but mean opposite things.

### Supply shocks versus demand shocks

A **supply shock** removes barrels from the market: a war in a producing region, an OPEC+ cut, an embargo, a hurricane that shuts Gulf refineries. Supply falls, demand stays put, so the price jumps. Critically, a supply shock pushes oil *up* while it is *bad* for growth — higher energy costs are a tax on every consumer and business. So a supply shock is the dangerous combination: prices up, growth down.

A **demand shock** is the opposite cause: the economy weakens, people and factories use less oil, so the price falls. A demand-driven oil *drop* is usually a *symptom* of a slowing economy, not good news. And a demand-driven oil *rise* — oil climbing because the global economy is booming — is benign, even bullish, because it confirms growth.

This is why you can never read the oil price in isolation. Oil up because supply was cut (war, OPEC) is risk-off and inflationary. Oil up because demand is booming is risk-on and benign. Oil down because demand collapsed is risk-off and recessionary. The macro post on [commodities as macro signals](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) builds the full decoder; for shocks, the one to fear is the *supply* shock, because it is the one that drives inflation.

![Line chart of WTI crude oil annual average from 2019 to 2025 with the 2022 supply-shock spike annotated](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks-4.png)

The oil chart is the shock barometer in one line. WTI crude averaged about \$57 a barrel in 2019, collapsed to \$40 in the 2020 demand shock (COVID killed demand), recovered to \$68 in 2021 as the economy reopened, then *spiked to a \$95 annual average in 2022* — with intra-year prints above \$120 — on the Russia-Ukraine supply shock, before settling back toward \$65–\$78 in the calmer years that followed. That 2022 spike is the fingerprint of a supply shock: a fast, war-driven jump in the price of the one input that feeds every other price.

#### Worked example: an oil supply shock on an energy position

You hold a **\$10,000** position in energy producers, and a supply shock sends WTI from \$75 to \$95 a barrel. That is a move of (\$95 − \$75) ÷ \$75 = +26.7% in the oil price, call it **+27%**. Energy producer stocks are highly sensitive to the oil price; assume your position roughly tracks it, so you gain about +27% × \$10,000 = **+\$2,667**. The same shock that gapped the broad market *down* 3% gapped your energy sleeve *up* nearly 27%, because a supply shock raises the revenue of the companies that sell the now-scarcer barrel. The intuition: in an oil supply shock, energy is the one equity sector that is *long* the shock, which is exactly why energy is the classic hedge against geopolitical and inflation risk in a stock portfolio.

### The inflation channel: why an oil shock can reset the regime

Here is the mechanism that turns an oil shock from a one-day move into a regime change. It runs as a chain, and each link is unavoidable.

![Pipeline of the oil-shock inflation channel from supply cut to oil up to headline inflation up to central-bank dilemma to stagflation risk](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks-5.png)

A supply cut raises the oil price. Oil feeds directly into **headline inflation** — the cost of fuel, shipping and anything made or moved with energy, which is almost everything. As headline inflation rises, the central bank faces a brutal **dilemma**: its job is to fight inflation by raising interest rates, but the same oil shock that raised inflation is *also* slowing the economy (energy is a tax on growth), and raising rates into a slowdown risks a recession. Hike to fight inflation and you deepen the slowdown; cut to support growth and you let inflation run. There is no clean move.

When prices are rising *and* growth is falling at the same time, you get the worst macro environment there is: **stagflation** (stagnation plus inflation). It is the nightmare of the 1970s, when oil shocks drove inflation into double digits while economies stalled. Stagflation is poison for a standard portfolio because it hurts *both* stocks (slowing growth, rising discount rates) *and* bonds (rising inflation erodes their fixed payments) at once — the two assets that usually offset each other both fall. This is why an oil supply shock is the most feared shock of all: it can break the very diversification that protects you in every other crisis. The macro post on [the Fed's reaction function](/blog/trading/macro-trading/inflation-and-the-fed-reaction-function-dot-plot) traces exactly how the central bank navigates this trap.

## Elections and policy uncertainty: the pre-event drift and the relief or fear move

Elections are the one family where the *date* is on the calendar even though the *shock* is not, and that gives them a distinctive two-part structure you can actually trade around.

### The pre-event drift

As a major election or referendum approaches, markets do not sit still. Uncertainty about the outcome is itself a cost: investors demand a higher risk premium for holding assets whose value depends on the result. So you often see a **pre-event drift** — a cautious de-risking into the date, with volatility (the VIX, and the options market's implied move) ramping up as the event nears. The post on [the expected move and pricing event risk with options](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-expected-move-pricing-event-risk-with-options) explains how the options market literally prices in the size of the expected swing; for elections, that implied move can be very large because the outcomes are binary and far apart.

### The relief or fear move

Then the result lands, and one of two things happens. If the outcome is roughly what markets feared *or* simply removes the uncertainty, you typically get a **relief move** — even a "bad" but *known* result is often better than continued uncertainty, so markets can rally simply because the binary resolved. This is the same dynamic as the FOMC: the macro post on [trading the FOMC statement](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-fomc-statement-presser-dot-plot) notes that markets frequently rally on a hawkish-but-expected decision because the *uncertainty* lifted. If the outcome is a genuine surprise that markets had not priced — a true upset — you get a **fear move**, a sharp risk-off gap as the market scrambles to reprice a future it had not prepared for.

The 2016 Brexit referendum is the archetype: markets had drifted assuming "Remain" would win, the surprise "Leave" result triggered a violent overnight fear move (the pound crashed, global equities gapped down), and then — tellingly — much of the global equity damage *faded within days* because the durable economic effects were slow, regional and uncertain. That is the election shock in miniature: a sharp surprise move, then a fade, *unless* the result changes a durable fundamental like trade or rates.

#### Worked example: fading a faded shock

A surprise election result gapped your **\$20,000** equity position down sharply on the open, but over the following two weeks the market realized the durable economic damage was limited and the position recovered. You had classified it correctly as a *fade*, held through the panic, and even added a little near the lows. The position has now recovered 4% from where you re-risked: +4% × \$20,000 = **+\$800**. The discipline that produced that \$800 was not a forecast — you did not know the result either — it was the *classification*: recognizing that a policy surprise with slow, uncertain real-economy effects is a fade, not a regime change, and therefore the panic was an opportunity rather than a reason to sell. The intuition: most of the money in shock trading is made not by predicting the shock but by correctly judging whether the crowd's panic will reverse.

### Tariffs and policy by headline

A modern variant worth flagging: **policy by headline**. Trade policy, tariffs and regulatory threats now arrive as social-media posts and off-the-cuff announcements, with no schedule at all. These behave like miniature election shocks — a sharp risk-off move on the headline, then a fade *if* the policy turns out to be a negotiating posture, or a regime change *if* it durably raises the cost of goods (which feeds inflation, just like an oil shock). The same supply-side inflation channel applies: a tariff is, economically, a tax that raises prices, so a serious tariff regime can lift inflation expectations and pressure the rate path. The decoder is the same one you use for oil: does this headline change a *durable* fundamental, or is it noise that will fade?

## Fade versus regime change: most shocks fade, some reset everything

We now reach the most important judgment in the entire post. Every unscheduled shock forces you to answer one question: *is this a fade or a regime change?* Get it right and the shock is a gift — you fade the panic and re-risk into the recovery. Get it wrong in the dangerous direction and you sell the exact bottom, or you buy a falling knife that keeps falling for a year.

### Why most shocks fade

The base rate strongly favors the fade. Decades of data on geopolitical shocks tell the same story: the market sells reflexively on the headline, and then, within days to weeks, it recovers — because the headline did not actually change the discounted future earnings of the companies you own. A war in a faraway region, a terror attack, a political crisis, a debt-ceiling standoff that gets resolved — these are genuinely frightening, but they rarely alter the fundamental machinery of the economy. The fear premium that spiked the risk premium drains back out, and prices recover. This is not optimism; it is arithmetic. If the earnings did not move, the price has to come back.

### What makes a shock a regime change

A shock becomes a regime change when it durably alters one of a small number of fundamentals:

- **The price of oil** (and therefore inflation). The 1973 oil embargo did not fade — it reset the price of energy and ushered in a decade of stagflation.
- **The path of interest rates.** Anything that durably changes inflation changes what the central bank must do, and the rate path is the discount rate on every asset on earth. The 2022 inflation shock — partly oil-driven — was a regime change: the Fed hiked from near zero to over 5%, and *that* repriced everything, far more durably than any single geopolitical headline.
- **The structure of trade or the financial system.** A true sovereign default, a banking system seizure, a permanent rupture in global trade — these reset the structure, not just the mood.

The test is mechanical, and it is the one the playbook will use: **does this shock change oil, inflation/rates, or earnings durably?** If yes, respect it as a regime change. If no — if it is pure fear that leaves the economic machinery intact — it is a fade.

#### Worked example: sizing the fade-versus-regime bet

Two shocks hit on different weekends. Shock A is a flare-up in a region with no oil and no major trade route; your **\$50,000** equity book gaps down 4% to a paper loss of −4% × \$50,000 = **−\$2,000**. You judge it a fade (no change to oil, rates or earnings), hold, and add. Shock B is an OPEC+ supply cut that spikes oil and lifts inflation expectations; the same book gaps down 4% (−\$2,000) but now you judge it a *regime change* (oil → inflation → rates), so you do *not* add — you trim and raise the haven sleeve. Same −\$2,000 paper loss, opposite correct responses. Had you treated Shock B like a fade and bought the dip, you would have ridden the position lower as the rate-path repricing ground on for months; had you treated Shock A like a regime change and dumped it, you would have sold the bottom of a move that recovered in a fortnight. The intuition: the dollar size of the gap tells you nothing about what to do — only the *classification* does.

## How it reacted: real episodes

Theory is cheap. Let us pin the framework to dated episodes with real numbers, so the pattern is concrete.

### Oil spiking on a supply shock: 2022

The clearest modern oil supply shock was the February 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine. Crude oil — already elevated on the post-COVID demand recovery — spiked above \$120 a barrel intra-year, and the annual average WTI price jumped to about \$95, up from \$68 the year before. This was not a demand boom; it was barrels removed by war and sanctions. And it did exactly what the inflation channel predicts: it poured fuel on an already-hot inflation, which forced the most aggressive Fed hiking cycle in forty years. That combination — supply-shock oil plus the rate response — was a *regime change*, not a fade. Anyone who treated the 2022 selloff as a quick geopolitical dip to buy got run over for most of the year, because the durable fundamental (rates) kept repricing assets lower. The gold chart shows the other side of the same era.

![Line chart of gold annual average from 2019 to 2025 with the 2023 to 2025 crisis and central-bank bid annotated](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks-6.png)

Gold's path through the shock years is the flight to safety made visible. It sat around \$1,400 in 2019, jumped to \$1,770 in the 2020 COVID panic, held near \$1,800 through 2021–2022, and then — as wars, central-bank buying and a persistent flight to safety stacked up — climbed steeply to a \$2,388 average in 2024 and toward \$3,000 in 2025. Gold does not earn anything, pay anything, or grow anything; it rose because in an era of geopolitical shocks and currency anxiety, the world wanted the asset with no counterparty. When you see gold breaking to new highs, the market is telling you the risk premium is structurally elevated.

### The classic fades: 1990 Kuwait and 9/11

Two older episodes are worth knowing because they are the textbook proof that most geopolitical shocks fade. When Iraq invaded Kuwait in August 1990, oil spiked and equities sold off on the threat to Middle Eastern supply — a genuine supply-shock fear. Yet within months, as the supply disruption proved manageable, both oil and equities had largely round-tripped. The September 11, 2001 attacks closed US equity markets for several days; when they reopened, stocks fell sharply on the shock — and then, within roughly a month, the S&P 500 had recovered to near its pre-attack level. Neither event changed the durable earnings power of the American corporate sector, so the fear premium that spiked the risk premium drained back out. The shocks were horrifying; the market impact faded. That is the base rate the misconceptions section is built on, and it is why "sell everything on the headline" is a losing rule.

### The VIX panic spikes

The volatility chart earlier in this post is the catalog of acute shocks. The LTCM crisis and Russian default of 1998 (VIX ~45.7), the 2008 global financial crisis (~80.9), the COVID crash of March 2020 (~82.7), and the August 2024 yen-carry unwind (~65.7) are the four biggest panic spikes of the modern era — and every one of them was a *shock*, an acute break, not a slow bear market. The August 2024 episode is especially instructive: it began with a Bank of Japan rate hike and a weak US jobs print, which together triggered a violent carry-trade unwind. The Nikkei fell **−12.4%** in a single day — its worst since the 1987 crash — the S&P 500 dropped 3%, the VIX spiked intraday to 65.7, and Bitcoin fell 15%. And then? It largely *faded*: the Nikkei rebounded **+10.2%** the very next day, and US stocks recovered within weeks, because the underlying US economy had not actually broken — it was a leverage-and-positioning unwind, not a regime change.

### The flight to gold and Treasuries

In each of those panics the haven side of the rotation fired exactly as the reflex predicts. Money fled into Treasuries (yields fell), into gold, into the dollar, and into the yen. The August 2024 yen surge — from about 162 to 142 to the dollar — was the carry unwind buying back borrowed yen, the single most mechanical haven move there is. The point of cataloging these is not the individual numbers but the *consistency*: the rotation map at the top of this post is not a theory, it is what happens, episode after episode, decade after decade.

### A debt-ceiling scare

The fiscal-brinkmanship family shows the fade with a fat tail. The 2011 US debt-ceiling standoff rattled markets through the summer and culminated in the first-ever downgrade of the US credit rating by a major agency; equities sold off sharply. The 2023 standoff produced another tense round of headline-driven volatility. In both cases a last-minute deal landed, the threatened default did not happen, and markets recovered — the episodes were, in the end, fades. But the *reason* they were frightening is the tail: a genuine US default would not be a fade, because it would damage the safe haven itself. That asymmetry is why a sophisticated trader treats a debt-ceiling scare as *probably* noise to fade, while keeping the position small enough to survive the small chance it is not.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs about shock trading are not just wrong but expensive. Here are the ones to unlearn, each corrected with the evidence.

### Misconception 1: "Sell everything on the headline"

The most common and most costly instinct. The data says the opposite: **most geopolitical selloffs reverse within weeks.** The reflexive flight to safety overshoots, because fear is not a fundamental — it is a premium that drains back out once the market realizes the discounted earnings did not move. The August 2024 panic that gapped the Nikkei down 12.4% rebounded 10.2% the *next day*. Selling everything into the headline is, statistically, selling the bottom. The disciplined move is to size down *before* you are forced to, then look to *fade* the panic — unless the shock changed oil, rates or earnings.

### Misconception 2: "Bitcoin is digital gold, so it will rise in a crisis"

In an acute risk-off event, Bitcoin trades as *high-beta risk*, not as a haven. It fell **−15%** in the August 2024 cascade while gold rose. Crypto behaves like a [macro liquidity asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/crypto-as-a-macro-liquidity-asset): when investors scramble for cash and safety, the most speculative asset is sold first. Bitcoin may be a long-run store of value in some monetary debasement story, but in the first hours of a shock it is on the *red* side of the rotation, not the green.

### Misconception 3: "A higher oil price is good news because the economy must be strong"

Only if the oil rise is *demand*-driven. A *supply*-driven oil spike — war, OPEC cut — is unambiguously bad: it is a tax on growth and a push on inflation at the same time. You must always ask *why* oil is moving. Oil up on a booming economy is benign; oil up because a war removed barrels is the stagflation trigger. The 2022 supply spike was the bad kind, and it helped force the rate regime that crushed both stocks and bonds.

### Misconception 4: "The dollar falls when there's a crisis in America"

Counterintuitively, the dollar usually *rises* even in an American crisis, because the world needs dollars to settle debts and the safest large asset (Treasuries) is dollar-denominated, so fleeing to safety *is* buying dollars. This is the left side of the [dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile): the dollar strengthens both when the US economy is booming *and* when the world is in a panic, and it only weakens in the calm middle. The rare exception is a crisis *about* the dollar itself — a US fiscal default scare — where gold can rise while the dollar wobbles.

### Misconception 5: "A government shutdown is a market crisis"

Government shutdowns are mostly noise. Non-essential operations pause, the data calendar gets disrupted, but the economic machinery and the Treasury's ability to pay its debts are untouched, so markets have historically shrugged them off. The dangerous cousin is the *debt-ceiling* fight, which threatens an actual default on the debt — that is the one with the fat tail. Conflating the two leads you to panic over the harmless event and underestimate the dangerous one.

## The playbook: how to trade and survive the unscheduled

You cannot forecast a war, an OPEC surprise, or an upset election. So the playbook is not about prediction — it is about *reflex, classification, and survival*. Here is the if-then map, in the order you execute it when a shock hits.

![Decision flow of the unscheduled-shock playbook from classify fade or regime change to size down to re-risk or stay defensive](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks-7.png)

### Step 1: Size down first, before you classify anything

The first move when a shock hits is *not* to be clever — it is to reduce risk. Do not feed a market order into the gap (the [liquidity vacuum](/blog/trading/event-trading/liquidity-and-gaps-around-news) will give you a terrible fill). Cut your most fragile, highest-beta exposure, raise some cash, and respect the volatility spike: position sizes that were fine at a VIX of 15 are reckless at a VIX of 50, because the same number of shares now swings two or three times as many dollars. Survival comes before opportunity. The shock will still be there in an hour for you to analyze; your blown-up account will not be.

### Step 2: Classify — fade or regime change?

Now run the one test that matters. Ask: **does this shock durably change oil, inflation/rates, or earnings?**

- **No** → it is most likely a *fade*. Pure geopolitical fear, a resolved debt-ceiling fight, an election surprise with slow real-economy effects. The base rate says it reverses in days to weeks.
- **Yes** → it is a *regime change*. An oil supply shock that lifts inflation, a true rate-path shift, a structural rupture. The move will stick for months.

This single call drives everything that follows. The companion post on [the reaction function](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-reaction-function-why-the-same-number-moves-differently) hammers the same lesson for scheduled data: the *regime* decides the sign and durability of the move, not the headline itself.

### Step 3: Position for safety while you wait

While the shock is live and unclassified, lean into the flight-to-safety map. The defensive posture is the same in every shock: less stocks and crypto, more cash, and a sleeve of havens — Treasuries, gold, and (if you trade FX) long dollar and long yen. The havens are not a return bet; they are the structural offset that lets your book survive a drawdown intact enough to act when the dust settles. Recall the Treasury worked example: a 20-basis-point flight-to-safety rally on a \$500,000 10-year position paid **+\$8,600** exactly while equities were bleeding. That is what a haven sleeve is for.

### Step 4: If it is a fade — re-risk into the fear

If your classification says *fade*, the panic is an opportunity, not a threat. As the risk premium drains back out and volatility starts to recede, add back the risk you cut, into the fear, near the lows. This is the unglamorous source of most shock-trading profit: not predicting the event, but having the discipline to buy the reflexive overshoot when the crowd is selling the bottom. The fade worked example showed it — a \$20,000 position re-risked near the lows and recovered 4% paid **+\$800**, purely on correct classification.

### Step 5: If it is a regime change — stay defensive, do not buy the dip

If your classification says *regime change*, do the opposite. Hold the havens, keep the hedges, and *do not buy the dip* until the new regime is fully priced. In a regime change the first dip is not the bottom — it is the first step of a repricing that can grind for months, as the 2022 oil-and-rates shock showed. Buying a falling knife in a regime change is how disciplined traders blow up: they apply the fade playbook (which is correct 80% of the time) to the 20% of shocks where it is exactly wrong.

### The if-then summary

| If the shock... | Classification | The position |
|---|---|---|
| Is pure geopolitical fear, no oil/rates effect | Fade | Size down, then re-risk into the panic as vol recedes |
| Cuts oil supply and lifts inflation | Regime change | Stay defensive; do not buy the dip; hold havens |
| Is a resolved debt-ceiling/shutdown | Fade (fat tail) | Fade it small; keep size survivable for the tail |
| Is an election/policy upset, slow real effects | Fade | Re-risk into the fear move as uncertainty clears |
| Durably shifts the rate path or trade structure | Regime change | Respect it; reprice your whole book to the new level |

### The invalidation

Every plan needs a line where you admit you were wrong. For a *fade* trade, the invalidation is simple: if the shock starts feeding a durable fundamental — oil keeps climbing, inflation expectations break higher, the central bank signals a changed path — then you misclassified, and a fade just became a regime change. Cut the re-risk and go back to defense. For a *regime-change* call, the invalidation is the mirror: if oil reverses, inflation expectations settle, and the rate path stabilizes, the regime change was a fade after all, and you re-risk. The discipline is not in being right the first time; it is in updating the classification the moment the evidence changes, because the cost of clinging to the wrong call through a regime change is the kind of loss that ends careers.

### Surviving the cross-asset correlation trap

One final survival note. In a severe shock, the diversification you counted on can vanish: as the cross-asset post on [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis) explains, in a true panic almost every risk asset falls together — stocks, credit, EM, crypto — because everyone is selling everything to raise the same cash. The hedge that worked in normal times may correlate to your book exactly when you need it not to. This is why the *true* havens matter: Treasuries, gold and cash are the assets whose correlation to risk goes *negative* in a crisis, which is precisely what diversification is supposed to do. Build your defense around assets that decouple when it counts, not around a basket of risk assets that merely *looked* different in calm times.
