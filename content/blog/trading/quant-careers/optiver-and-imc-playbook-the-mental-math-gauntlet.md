> - **The full loop:** mental-math test, then an online assessment, then trading games (market-making sims), then a superday on the desk. The internship is the real interview, and conversion is the main full-time path.

Maya has eight minutes. The proctor's instructions were three sentences long: no calculator, no paper, the timer starts when you click *begin* and does not stop. On her screen is a single arithmetic question — `47 × 53` — and a box for the answer. Below it, a counter she is trying very hard not to look at: **80 questions remaining.** She does the multiplication the way she learned it in school: forty-seven times fifty is two thousand three hundred fifty, forty-seven times three is one hundred forty-one, add them, two thousand four hundred ninety-one. She types it, hits enter, and the next question appears. The counter now says 79. She glances at the timer despite herself. Seven minutes, fifty-one seconds left. She just spent nine seconds on one question, and she has seventy-nine to go. The math is suddenly obvious in a way that has nothing to do with the math: at nine seconds a question, she will answer about fifty-three of the eighty before time runs out, and she needs sixty or more correct to have a chance. She is going to fail this test not because she cannot multiply, but because she is too slow — and somewhere in Amsterdam, an algorithm is about to reject her before a single Optiver employee has read her name.

This is the mental-math gauntlet, and it is the front door at two of the most respected market-making firms in the world: **Optiver** (founded 1986, Amsterdam) and **IMC Trading** (founded 1989, Amsterdam). It is the most distinctive filter in quant recruiting, and it is widely misunderstood. People who have not taken it assume it is a trivia hurdle — a hazing ritual that screens for nerds who memorized times tables. People who have taken it, and especially the traders who *designed* it, will tell you something stranger and more interesting: it is one of the most predictive single-hour signals a market maker has ever found for who will be good at the actual job. The test is the job, compressed into eight minutes.

This post is the firm-playbook layer for Europe's market makers. We will not re-derive the arithmetic tricks from scratch — those live in the [mental-math and arithmetic-speed interview post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/mental-math-arithmetic-speed-quant-interviews), and we will link out to the [market-making games](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) and [estimation / Fermi](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/estimation-fermi-problems-quant-interviews) posts where the technique belongs. Here we answer the questions those posts do not: *why* a speed-math test predicts trading ability, what the rest of the Optiver and IMC loop looks like, what the culture and the comp are actually like, and — most usefully — exactly how to train so that you walk into that eight-minute window as the prepared tail of the distribution rather than the unlucky middle. Figure 1 is the map: the loop you are trying to survive.

![A pipeline diagram of the Optiver and IMC hiring loop with five stages from application through mental-math test, online assessment, trading games, superday, and offer, with a loop-back edge from the math test to the application for candidates below the bar](/imgs/blogs/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet-1.png)

Read that pipeline left to right and the firm's priorities are obvious. The mental-math test sits second — right after you apply, before any human is involved — because it is cheap to administer to thousands of people and it correlates with the thing the firm cares about. Everything downstream (the online assessment, the trading games, the superday) is more expensive and more human, so the firm only spends it on people who already cleared the cheap, predictive filter. If you understand nothing else about recruiting at a European market maker, understand this: **the gauntlet is an efficiency machine, and you are competing against the math, not against a person, in round one.** Our recurring character Maya — a math undergrad aiming at a trading seat — will walk the whole loop with us, and by the end she will be the prepared tail of that distribution instead of the casualty in the opening paragraph.

## Foundations: why a market maker tests mental math

Before we dissect the test, we need the vocabulary of the job it is screening for. If you are new to markets, build these definitions slowly — every claim about *why* the test works rests on them.

**The bid, the ask, and the spread.** For any tradable thing — a stock, an index future, a stock option — there is at any instant a highest price someone will *buy* at (the **bid**) and a lowest price someone will *sell* at (the **ask**, or offer). The ask is higher than the bid; the gap between them is the **bid-ask spread**. If a future is bid 99 and offered at 101, the spread is two points. The spread is the price of immediacy: if you must trade *right now*, you cross it.

**A market maker** is the firm quoting both of those prices at once, all day, on thousands of instruments. Optiver and IMC are market makers in **listed derivatives** — exchange-traded options and futures. "Listed" just means standardized and traded on a public exchange, as opposed to bespoke contracts negotiated privately. A market maker stands ready to buy at the bid and sell at the ask simultaneously. When one customer sells to it at 99 and another buys from it at 101 a moment later, it has bought low and sold high without taking a directional view, and it pockets the spread. That spread, captured across an enormous number of trades, is the business. (We dissect the unit economics of this in the sibling post on [how quant firms actually make money](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money) — here we only need that the spread is the prize.)

**The mid.** The midpoint between the bid and the ask — `(bid + ask) / 2` — is the market maker's best estimate of the instrument's *fair value*. If a fair value is 100 and you want to quote a two-point-wide market around it, you show 99 bid, 101 ask. The whole craft of market making is (1) estimating the mid quickly and accurately, and (2) deciding how wide a spread to charge and which way to lean it.

**Edge.** "Edge" is the expected profit on a single transaction, computed *before* you know the outcome. A market maker's gross edge is roughly half the spread: if your fair value is right and you quote a two-point spread, every fill earns you about one point in expectation, because on average you capture half the round-trip spread. Edge is always an *expectation* over many repetitions, never a guarantee on any one trade. This reframing is the soul of the industry: a market maker does not predict where the price goes; it has a small positive expected value per quote and the patience and volume to let the law of large numbers do its work.

**Why speed is itself edge.** Here is the part outsiders miss. In a market maker's world, the fair value is not standing still — it moves continuously as the underlying market moves. The instant news hits, or the index ticks, your old quote of "99 bid, 101 ask" is *stale*: the fair value might now be 102, and your offer at 101 is now a gift to anyone fast enough to take it. Being filled on a stale quote is called **adverse selection** — you get picked off precisely when you least want to be, by someone who knows something (or computed something) faster than you. So a market maker who can re-price a hundred options *faster* — who can recompute the mid and re-quote before the slow traders react — loses less to adverse selection and captures more spread. **Speed of accurate computation is not a nice-to-have; it is, quite literally, the edge.** The mental-math test is a direct, if crude, measurement of exactly this: how fast can you produce a correct number under pressure?

**Calibration.** The second thing the test secretly measures. **Calibration** means knowing how confident you should be — and acting accordingly. A well-calibrated trader who says "I'm 90% sure the fair value is near 100" is right about 90% of the time when they say that. On the mental-math test, calibration shows up as the decision of whether to answer a hard question or skip it: a calibrated test-taker who is unsure of a digit either fixes it fast or moves on, rather than burning fifteen seconds and getting it wrong anyway. As we will see, the *scoring* of the test rewards calibration directly, because a wrong answer is worth nothing and the clock you spent on it is gone forever.

**Quoting reflexes.** Put speed and calibration together under a clock and you get the reflex the whole loop is hunting for: see a situation, compute a fair value, commit to a number, and update the instant new information arrives — without freezing, without second-guessing into paralysis, and without the panic that makes people careless. A market maker does this thousands of times a day. A candidate who can do 70 correct mental-math questions in 8 minutes has demonstrated, in the cheapest possible way, that they have the raw reflex. That is *why* a speed-math test is round one. It is a low-resolution X-ray of the exact skill.

With that vocabulary, the rest of this post is just detail: what the test looks like, how the firm scores it, what comes after it, and how you train so the X-ray comes back clean.

## The mental-math test, dissected

Let us be precise about the artifact, while flagging clearly what is documented and what is reasonable inference. The widely reported format — across efinancialcareers write-ups, interview-guide sites, and candidate accounts — is **on the order of 60-80 questions in 8 minutes, no calculator and no scratch paper, with a pass bar somewhere around 70-85% correct.** The exact question count, time limit, and cutoff vary by firm, by year, and by role, and the firms do not publish them, so treat any single precise number as illustrative rather than gospel. Optiver's version is the famous one; IMC runs a close cousin, often paired with a coding screen. What does not vary is the *shape* of the thing: many questions, very little time, and a bar high enough that you cannot clear it by going slow and careful.

The questions are not advanced. There is no calculus, no probability theorem, no clever number theory. That is the point — and the trap. They are deliberately *elementary*, which lulls untrained candidates into thinking the test is easy right up until the timer turns the elementary into the impossible. Figure 3 breaks the question bank into the four families you will see, with the fast trick for each.

![A tree diagram showing the four families of questions on the mental-math test — arithmetic, fractions and decimals, sequences, and estimation — each with a fast trick, branching from a root node labeled 80 questions in 8 minutes with no calculator](/imgs/blogs/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet-3.png)

**Family 1 — arithmetic.** Two-digit and three-digit multiplication, addition, subtraction, and percentages: `47 × 53`, `8% of 250`, `1244 + 879`, `36 × 25`. The school method works but is too slow; the fast methods are pattern-based. `47 × 53` is `(50−3)(50+3) = 2500 − 9 = 2491` by difference of squares. `36 × 25` is `36 × 100 / 4 = 900`. `8% of 250` is `8 × 250 / 100 = 20`, or better, `250 × 0.08 = 20` seen at a glance. The skill is recognizing which shortcut applies in under a second.

**Family 2 — fractions and decimals.** Convert `3/8` to a percentage; what is `7/16` as a decimal; `0.625` as a fraction. The fast method is to *memorize* the common conversions cold — `1/8 = 12.5%`, so `3/8 = 37.5%`; `1/16 = 6.25%`, so `7/16 = 43.75%` — and to anchor everything off halves and quarters you know by reflex. A market maker reads option prices in fractions and decimals all day; this family is the most job-shaped of the four.

**Family 3 — sequences.** Find the next term: `2, 6, 18, 54, ?` (ratio of 3, answer 162); `1, 4, 9, 16, ?` (squares, answer 25); `3, 5, 9, 17, ?` (differences double, answer 33). The trick is to test the two cheapest hypotheses first — constant difference, then constant ratio — and only then look for second-order patterns. Most sequences on a speed test are arithmetic, geometric, or squares; do not over-think them.

**Family 4 — estimation.** `812 / 39`, `√150`, `19 × 21`. Here you are often not even asked for the exact answer — you are asked to pick the closest of several, or to get within a tolerance. `812 / 39 ≈ 800 / 40 = 20`, then nudge up slightly to ~21. `√150` is between `√144 = 12` and `√169 = 13`, closer to 12.2. The skill is *bracketing*: bound the answer fast, then refine only as much as the question demands. This family is the closest cousin of the [estimation and Fermi-problem](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/estimation-fermi-problems-quant-interviews) round you will meet later in the loop.

Notice what unites all four: in each, there is a slow correct method everyone knows and a fast correct method you have to *train into a reflex*. The test does not separate people who can do arithmetic from people who cannot — almost every candidate can do the arithmetic given a minute. It separates people who have built the reflexes from people who have not. That is a much more useful thing to measure, and it is the whole design.

#### Worked example: a batch of real-style questions, worked the fast way

Let us put Maya at the keyboard and walk a representative batch the way a trained candidate sees them. Each line is one question; the bracket shows the reflex, not the longhand.

- `48 × 52` → difference of squares: `(50−2)(50+2) = 2500 − 4 = 2496`. *(~3 seconds, no carries.)*
- `15% of 80` → `10% is 8, half of that is 4, so 8 + 4 = 12`. *(~3 seconds.)*
- `7/8 as a percent` → `1/8 = 12.5%`, so `7/8 = 100% − 12.5% = 87.5%`. *(~2 seconds, memorized.)*
- `624 / 8` → `624 / 8 = (640 − 16)/8 = 80 − 2 = 78`. *(~4 seconds.)*
- `2, 5, 11, 23, ?` → differences are `3, 6, 12` — they double — next difference `24`, so `23 + 24 = 47`. *(~5 seconds.)*
- `√90` → between `9 (=√81)` and `10 (=√100)`, nearer 9.5; answer ≈ `9.5`. *(~3 seconds.)*
- `34 × 11` → multiply-by-11 trick: `3_(3+4)_4 = 374`. *(~2 seconds.)*
- `0.375 as a fraction` → `0.375 = 3/8` (recognized from the 1/8 = 0.125 anchor). *(~2 seconds.)*

That is eight questions in roughly **24 seconds**, about 3 seconds each, all correct. Maya in the opening paragraph spent 9 seconds on one. The difference between the two Mayas is not intelligence; it is whether `48 × 52` triggers "difference of squares" automatically or triggers "okay, forty-eight times fifty…". At 3 seconds a question she answers all 80 in 240 seconds with four minutes to spare, leaving room to double-check; at 9 seconds a question she runs out of time at question 53. *The test is won in the gap between knowing a method and having a reflex.*

#### Worked example: the EV math — how many of 80 must Maya answer to clear ~80%?

The scoring is where calibration becomes a number. Take a clean, illustrative model: the test has 80 questions, a wrong answer scores zero (no penalty, but no credit and the time is spent), and the firm's pass bar this cycle is **80% correct of the questions you answer** — call it the *accuracy bar* — combined with answering *enough* questions that the bar is meaningful. Different firms phrase the cutoff as percent-of-attempted or raw-correct; we will use percent-of-attempted because it makes the speed-vs-accuracy trade explicit.

Suppose Maya, drilled, has a steady-state of **3.5 seconds per question** and an accuracy of **92%** at that pace. In 8 minutes = 480 seconds she answers `480 / 3.5 ≈ 137` — but the test only has 80, so she answers all 80 with time to spare. At 92% accuracy she gets `0.92 × 80 ≈ 74` correct, which is `74/80 = 92.5%` of attempted. She clears the 80% bar comfortably. Good.

Now suppose untrained Maya runs at **8 seconds per question** with 90% accuracy (slower people are often *more* careful, which is the cruel twist). In 480 seconds she answers only `480 / 8 = 60` of the 80. At 90% she gets `0.90 × 60 = 54` correct. As a fraction of *attempted* that is `54/60 = 90%` — she clears the accuracy bar! But as a fraction of the *whole test* it is `54/80 = 67.5%`, and if the firm scores raw-correct-out-of-80 (many do), 67.5% is below an 80% raw bar and she fails. **This is the central tension of the test: accuracy alone does not save you if you are slow, because the unanswered questions count as wrong against the full denominator.**

Where is the break-even? If the bar is 80% of all 80 questions = 64 correct, and accuracy is 90%, Maya must *answer* at least `64 / 0.90 ≈ 71` of the 80. At 71 answered in 480 seconds she has `480 / 71 ≈ 6.8` seconds per question. So the honest target is: **answer ~71+ of 80 at ~90% accuracy, which demands under ~6.8 seconds per question.** Push accuracy to 95% and the required answered-count drops to `64 / 0.95 ≈ 68`, buying you ~7 seconds each. *The EV of the test rewards the joint product of speed and accuracy — and because slowness silently converts unanswered questions into wrong ones, the cheapest lever for most candidates is raw speed, not squeezing the last 3% of accuracy.*

#### Worked example: a quoting reflex — mid, spread, and skew in five seconds

To see why this test predicts trading ability rather than test-taking ability, watch the same reflex do the actual job. A customer asks Maya — now a junior trader — for a market in a stock-index option. Her screens say the theoretical fair value is **4.20**, the option is moderately liquid, and her current inventory is slightly long this option (she already owns some, so she would rather sell than buy more).

She must produce a two-sided quote in about five seconds. The reflex:

1. **Mid.** Fair value is 4.20. That is her center.
2. **Spread.** Liquidity is moderate, so she charges a moderate width — say 0.10 wide, 0.05 on each side. A tight market (0.04 wide) wins more flow but earns less per fill and bleeds more to adverse selection; a wide market (0.20) is safer but rarely gets hit. She picks 0.10 as the calibrated middle.
3. **Skew.** She is long inventory, so she leans the quote to encourage selling *to her customers* and discourage buying *from her*. She shades the whole market down by 0.02: instead of `4.15 bid / 4.25 ask` she shows `4.13 bid / 4.23 ask`. Now her ask is more attractive (she is offering cheaper, inviting buyers to lift her offer and reduce her long), and her bid is less attractive (she is bidding lower, discouraging more sellers from adding to her long).
4. **Quote.** "`4.13 bid at 4.23`." Done, in about five seconds.

Every one of those steps is an arithmetic operation under time pressure: halving the spread, adding and subtracting from the mid, adjusting by the skew. The trader who computes `4.20 − 0.05 − 0.02 = 4.13` in half a second and the one who computes `48 × 52` in three seconds are using the *same muscle*. The mental-math test measures that muscle directly, which is why a candidate's score genuinely forecasts how they will perform on a quoting screen — and, the firms believe, on a real desk. (The full art of quoting games is in the [market-making games interview post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews); here the point is only that the gauntlet and the desk run on one engine.) *Quoting is mental math with money attached, and that is precisely why the cheap test predicts the expensive job.*

## Why a speed-math test actually predicts trading ability

It is worth lingering on the skeptic's objection, because it is the most common misconception and the firms have a real answer to it. The objection: "Plenty of brilliant traders are mediocre at arithmetic, and plenty of arithmetic savants are terrible traders. So how can this test predict anything?"

The answer is that the test is not measuring arithmetic in isolation. It is measuring four things at once, and all four transfer to the desk:

**Speed under pressure.** Already covered — the desk re-prices continuously, and slow re-pricing is adverse selection. The test puts you under a clock that feels exactly like the clock on a moving market. A candidate who melts down when the timer is visible will melt down when the market gaps.

**Calibration and triage.** The test forces hundreds of tiny decisions: do I trust this answer, or check it? Do I attempt this hard one or skip to bank an easier point? That is the same triage a trader does across a book of hundreds of options: which quotes do I tighten, which do I pull, where do I spend my limited attention. The score rewards good triage and punishes the candidate who sinks fifteen seconds into one stubborn question — exactly as the market punishes a trader who fixates on one position while the rest of the book goes stale.

**Composure.** A surprising amount of what separates the pass from the fail is emotional, not cognitive. The visible counter dropping from 80 induces panic, and panic causes careless errors that a calm person would never make. The desk is the same: the difference between a good trader and a blown-up one is often composure when the screen turns red. The test is a cheap composure screen.

**Numerical fluency as a substrate.** Finally, fluency with numbers is genuinely load-bearing in market making. A trader who has to *think* about whether `3/8` is bigger than `0.4` is carrying a tax on every decision. (It is: `3/8 = 0.375 < 0.4`.) The fluent trader has that comparison for free and spends their cognition on the things that actually matter — the risk, the flow, the skew. The test ensures you arrive with the substrate already installed.

There is a small piece of math that makes the calibration point sharp, and it is worth doing because it is the same math the desk lives on. Suppose Maya is mid-test, deciding whether to attempt a question she is only 60% sure of. If she attempts and is right she banks one point; if she attempts and is wrong she banks zero and has burned, say, six seconds she could have spent on two near-certain questions worth roughly `2 × 0.95 = 1.9` expected points. So the expected value of attempting the shaky question is about `0.60 × 1 = 0.6` points for the time of one slot, versus an expected `0.95` points if she spends that same slot on an easy one she has not reached yet. The calibrated move is to *skip the shaky one and bank the sure thing* — and the test scores exactly that decision. A trader does the identical calculation across a book all day: do I keep fighting this one hard position, or pull the quote and redeploy my attention to three easy spreads I can capture cleanly? The candidate who instinctively maximizes expected points-per-time-slot on the test is rehearsing the core risk-allocation decision of the desk. That is not a coincidence; it is the whole reason the firms trust the screen. The deeper version of this expected-value reasoning is the subject of the [decision-making-under-uncertainty interview post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/decision-making-under-uncertainty-quant-interviews), but the test surfaces it in its rawest form.

None of this means the test is a *perfect* predictor — no single screen is, and the firms know it, which is why the loop continues past it. It means the test is a cheap, decently-correlated *first* filter, and in a funnel with thousands of applicants per seat, a cheap decent filter at the top is worth enormous amounts of saved interviewer time. The cost asymmetry is the whole argument: a human interviewer-hour is expensive and scarce, while an automated eight-minute test costs the firm essentially nothing to administer to ten thousand people. If the test even *weakly* correlates with on-desk performance, using it to rank the funnel before spending interviewer time is overwhelmingly positive expected value for the firm — which is the same logic a market maker uses to decide which trades are worth its limited risk budget. That is the honest case for the gauntlet, and it is a good one.

## The rest of the loop

Clearing the gauntlet gets you *in the door*; it does not get you the job. The full loop, as reported for Optiver and IMC, runs roughly as in Figure 1: **mental-math test → online assessment → trading games → superday → offer.** Let us walk the stages after the math.

**The online assessment.** Beyond the pure speed test, both firms run further online rounds that mix more math with logic and pattern questions — more sequences, more estimation, sometimes light probability and brain-teasers, sometimes a personality or situational-judgment component. **IMC in particular pairs the math with a coding screen** (HackerRank-style algorithmic problems), reflecting its more engineering-forward identity; a candidate aiming at IMC should be ready for a clean data-structures-and-algorithms round in addition to the arithmetic. (The coding technique lives in the sibling [coding-interview post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/coding-interview-quant-data-structures-algorithms); here, just know it is part of the IMC path more than the Optiver trading path.)

**The trading games.** This is where the loop gets fun and where the EV mindset takes over from raw speed. Both firms run **market-making simulations**: you are given an instrument with some hidden or partially-known value, and you must make two-sided markets while other people (interviewers or other candidates) trade against you. You quote, they hit or lift, information arrives, and you must update your fair value and re-quote — under time pressure, with real-feeling P&L. There are also dice games, betting games, and information-revelation games. None of them are trivia. They test exactly the quoting reflex from the worked example above: estimate a fair value, charge a calibrated spread, lean on inventory and information, update fast, and keep your composure when a trade goes against you. The firms watch *how you reason*, not just whether you "win" — a candidate who articulates their EV out loud and updates cleanly on new information scores well even on a losing round. (We will not re-derive the game theory; the [market-making games post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) does that in full.)

A concrete flavor of these games, to make the connection to the math test explicit: an interviewer says "I am rolling three fair six-sided dice and summing them — make me a market on the total." Maya must compute the fair value (the expected sum is `3 × 3.5 = 10.5`) and then *charge a spread around it* that protects her from the interviewer, who may know something she does not or may be about to give her information by trading. She might quote "`9 bid at 12`," a three-wide market centered on 10.5. If the interviewer lifts her offer at 12, that is a signal the true value is probably high (why else pay 12 for something worth 10.5 on average?), and she should revise her fair value *up* and re-quote — exactly the adverse-selection update from the foundations section. Every move is arithmetic under pressure plus a calibrated read of what a trade *tells* her. The candidate who freezes on `3 × 3.5` has failed before the interesting part begins; the candidate who has the fair value instantly and spends her cognition on the spread and the update is the one the desk wants. The mental-math reflex is the price of admission to the game, not the game itself — which is, again, why the test sits first.

**The superday.** The final stage is an on-site (or full remote-day) with multiple back-to-back rounds: more math, more games, a probability or brain-teaser round, technical depth appropriate to the role, and a fit conversation with people from the desk you would join. By this point the firm is no longer asking "can this person compute?" — you proved that in round one — but "do we want this person making real risk decisions next to us, and will they learn fast?" Optiver's superday leans toward the trading reflex and risk intuition; IMC's blends in more of the engineering conversation.

**The internship is the real interview.** The single most important strategic fact about the loop is this: at both firms, as across the top of the industry, **the internship is the primary path to a full-time seat, and conversion is the point.** A strong summer intern who clears the bar largely converts to a return offer; full-time-direct hiring exists but is narrower. So if you are a student, your target is not "a full-time Optiver job in two years" — it is "an Optiver *internship* next summer," because that internship *is* the extended interview that produces the full-time offer. Aim the whole campaign at the intern seat.

## Culture and offices: Amsterdam, Chicago, Sydney

Both firms were born in Amsterdam and both carry a distinctly European-trading-floor culture that differs in flavor from the New York hedge-fund world. Understanding it helps you decide whether you actually want this and helps you read the room in the fit rounds.

**Optiver** — founded 1986 in Amsterdam, with major offices in **Chicago, Sydney, and Shanghai**, and roughly **2,100 employees** as of 2024 (per the firm's own reporting). It is a listed-derivatives market maker through and through: its identity is built around being fast, accurate, and risk-aware in options and futures across global exchanges. The culture is famously merit-driven and direct — the Dutch directness is real — and it prizes people who can make confident decisions under uncertainty and own the outcome. The mental-math test is a cultural artifact as much as a screen: it signals that this is a place that values speed, precision, and a certain comfort with being measured.

**IMC Trading** — founded 1989 in Amsterdam, with offices in **Chicago and Sydney**. IMC is the close sibling: also a listed-derivatives market maker, also Amsterdam-rooted, also speed-math-first. Its self-presentation leans more *tech-forward* — IMC talks about itself as a technology-driven trading firm, which is why the coding screen features more prominently in its loop. If Optiver's archetype is the quick-witted trader, IMC's archetype is the engineer who also thinks like a trader. The two firms compete for overlapping talent and recruit from the same campuses, which is exactly why this post treats them together.

The office geography matters for your application strategy. The Amsterdam headquarters are the cultural core; Chicago is the U.S. listed-derivatives hub (close to the CME and CBOE); Sydney covers the Asia-Pacific session. If you have a visa or relocation constraint, check which office hires for your target role *before* you apply — the loop is the same, but the seats and work authorization differ by location. ## Compensation: what the seats actually pay

Now the honest comp picture, with all its conditionality. Market-maker comp is bonus-driven, highly variable, and survivorship-biased; the figures below are reported ranges (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, efinancialcareers, the "Young & Calculated" 2026 quant-pay survey, and firm pages), and they should always be read with their caveats attached. A great year is not the median, and the people quoting the biggest numbers are the ones who survived the filter.

**Internships first, because the internship is the seat.** Internship pay at this tier is high precisely because the internship is the real interview and the firms compete hard for the top candidates. As of the 2026 cycle, **IMC interns are reported up to roughly \$5,800/week**, and IMC conversion packages — the full-time total comp offered to interns who convert — are reported around **\$425,000** total comp. **Optiver PhD interns** are reported around **\$80,000–\$90,000 for an 8-week placement** (that is the specialized PhD-research internship; the standard trading internships are paid on a comparable high-weekly basis to peers in the space). For reference across the industry, Jane Street interns run around an annualized \$300,000 (~\$4,500–\$6,000/week), and Citadel/Citadel Securities interns are reported at \$4,300–\$5,800/week for 2026 plus sign-on and corporate housing. The pattern is consistent: a summer at a top market maker pays like a senior engineering salary, prorated — because the firm is paying for a 10-12 week live audition for a seat worth far more.

**Full-time, new grad.** Top-tier market-maker and prop new-grad trading seats across the industry land first-year totals broadly in the **\$450,000–\$650,000 on-target** range (base in the **\$250,000–\$375,000** band plus a sign-on of **\$50,000–\$200,000** and a performance bonus), with the IMC conversion figure of ~\$425,000 sitting squarely inside that picture. These are *on-target* numbers for strong performers at the strongest firms; they are not a floor, and they are not guaranteed.

**The bonus does not repeat — say it out loud.** The single most important thing to internalize about this comp is that **base salaries are relatively flat and the bonus is the lever, and the bonus tracks your P&L contribution and does not repeat automatically.** A trader who has a great year on a great seat might post a large bonus; the same trader on a quieter book the next year posts a much smaller one. The headline "market makers make \$X" figures describe the strong years of the survivors. The industry is also somewhat up-or-out: not everyone who joins a trading seat keeps it. We dissect the comp-trajectory math in detail in [how quant firms actually make money](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money); here the point is to read every comp number with its conditionality stapled to it.

One more honest note on selectivity. Top market-maker intern and grad programs are extremely competitive — commonly described as low-single-digit-percent acceptance, with thousands of applicants per opening. The mental-math test is the first place that funnel narrows hard. That is not a reason to despair; it is a reason to *prepare*, because preparation is the highest-EV thing you control. Which brings us to the plan.

## How to train for the gauntlet

The best news in this entire post: **the test is trainable, and it is trainable on a known curve.** The wall is speed, speed is built by drilling, and drilling responds to practice the way a muscle responds to lifting. You do not need to be a savant; you need to be the prepared tail of the distribution in Figure 2, and that is a matter of weeks of disciplined reps, not innate gift.

![A data chart showing an illustrative score distribution on the mental-math test, with most candidates clustered near 50 percent correct, an amber middle band, and a green pass band at roughly 70 to 85 percent correct that only the drilled tail of candidates reaches](/imgs/blogs/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet-2.png)

That distribution is illustrative — the firms do not publish their score histograms — but its shape is well-attested by candidate reports: a broad mass of untrained people in the 45-60% band, where speed (not arithmetic ability) is the wall, and a smaller, well-prepared tail that clears the 70-85% pass band. The entire goal of your training is to move yourself from the gray middle into the green tail. Here is the program.

**The tool.** Use a free timed-arithmetic drill — **Zetamac** is the canonical one in this community, with configurable operations and a default 120-second sprint; *Arithmetic* and similar apps work too. Set it to the operations on the test (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) and number ranges that match (two- and three-digit). Track one headline metric every session: **correct answers per fixed time window.** This is your single source of truth.

**The four drills, rotated.** Map your practice to the four families from Figure 3:

1. **Arithmetic speed** — daily Zetamac sprints. This is the bread-and-butter, the largest share of your time.
2. **Fraction and decimal conversions** — flashcards of the common conversions (`1/8`, `1/16`, `1/12`, `1/3`, `1/6`, `1/7`, `1/9`, `1/11`) until they are instant. This is pure memorization and pays off fast.
3. **Sequences** — a short daily set; train the "difference, then ratio, then second-order" triage so you stop over-thinking them.
4. **Estimation** — daily bracketing drills: division to two significant figures, square roots between perfect squares, percentages of round numbers. This also feeds the later Fermi round.

**The technique layer.** Speed without tricks plateaus fast. Learn and *automate* the standard reflexes: difference of squares for near-equal factors, multiply-by-11 and by-25 and by-5 shortcuts, percentage decomposition (`15% = 10% + 5%`), and the fraction anchors above. The goal is for the trick to fire *before* you consciously decide to use it. Figure 5 shows the gap you are trying to close on a single representative problem.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting two methods for computing 47 times 53, with the slow longhand method on the left taking four error-prone steps and about twelve seconds, and the fast difference-of-squares method on the right reaching the same answer of 2491 in one step and about four seconds](/imgs/blogs/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet-5.png)

That left-versus-right gap, multiplied across 80 questions, is the entire test. Closing it for *every* question type is what eight weeks of drilling buys you.

#### Worked example: Maya's eight-week training plan with a target curve

Let us make the plan concrete and measurable, because "practice mental math" is useless advice and "hit these numbers by these dates" is a plan. Maya starts at an untrained baseline and drills 20-30 minutes a day, six days a week. Her target metric is **correct answers in a single 8-minute, 80-question session**, and her secondary metric is **per-question accuracy**. The illustrative curve she should aim to track is in Figure 4.

![A data chart showing an illustrative eight-week training curve, with correct answers per eight-minute session rising from about 40 at week zero to over 70 by week eight on the left axis, and per-question accuracy on the right axis dipping slightly as speed is pushed before recovering from about 80 percent to about 94 percent](/imgs/blogs/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet-4.png)

Reading the curve as a plan:

- **Week 0 (baseline):** ~40 correct in 8 minutes, ~81% accuracy. This is a typical untrained-but-capable starting point. Do not be discouraged — this is the *gray middle*, and everyone starts here.
- **Weeks 1-2 (speed shock):** push pace hard on Zetamac. Correct-count jumps to ~48-54 because you are simply attempting more questions; accuracy may *dip* to ~80-82% as you trade carefulness for speed. This dip is expected and temporary — Figure 4 shows it explicitly. The fast early gains come from learning to *not slow down*.
- **Weeks 3-5 (technique consolidation):** the tricks become reflexes; correct-count climbs to ~59-66 and accuracy *recovers and surpasses* baseline (~85-90%) because the fast methods have fewer error-prone steps. This is the grind phase where most of the durable improvement happens.
- **Weeks 6-8 (calibration and ceiling):** correct-count grinds from ~66 to **70+** and accuracy settles around **92-94%**. The gains slow — you are approaching your personal ceiling — but you cross the ~70-correct pass band around week 8. You also start doing *full 8-minute simulations* under realistic conditions (no paper, timer visible) to train composure, not just speed.

The arithmetic of the plan ties back to the EV worked example: at ~70 correct of 80 with ~93% accuracy, Maya is answering ~75 of the 80 and getting ~70 right, which is `70/80 ≈ 87.5%` raw and well over `70/75 ≈ 93%` of attempted — clearing both a percent-of-attempted bar and a raw-correct bar comfortably. The total investment was roughly `6 days × 8 weeks × 25 minutes ≈ 20 hours`. *Twenty hours of focused drilling is the difference between the casualty in the opening paragraph and the prepared tail of the distribution — which is an absurdly high return on time for a seat worth hundreds of thousands of dollars, and it is the single highest-EV move a candidate for these firms can make.*

A final training note on composure: practice with the timer *visible* and the counter dropping, because the panic those induce is part of the test, and you want to have already desensitized yourself to it before it counts. Train the emotion, not just the arithmetic.

## Common misconceptions

The mental-math gauntlet attracts more folklore than almost any other part of quant recruiting. Here are the four myths that most reliably sink candidates, named and corrected.

**Myth 1: "It is just arithmetic — if you can multiply, you can pass."** This is the most dangerous myth because it feels true and is false. Almost everyone *can* do the arithmetic given a minute; almost no one can do 70 of it correctly in 8 minutes without training. The test does not measure whether you can multiply — it measures whether multiplication is a *reflex* under a clock. Treating it as "just arithmetic" is exactly how a capable person ends up in the gray middle of Figure 2. It is a speed-and-composure test wearing an arithmetic costume.

**Myth 2: "You can use a calculator / scratch paper / take your time."** No. The format is explicitly no-calculator, typically no scratch paper, and hard-timed. The constraints *are* the test — they are what force the speed-and-calibration measurement. Candidates who prepare assuming they will have aids walk in and discover that their plan to "work carefully" collides with a timer that does not care. Prepare under the real constraints: no aids, visible clock, full speed.

**Myth 3: "Optiver and IMC are basically the same, so one prep fits both identically."** They are close siblings and the math prep *does* transfer, but they are not identical. IMC leans harder on a **coding screen** as part of the loop, reflecting its tech-forward identity; Optiver's path emphasizes the trading reflex and risk intuition more. A candidate targeting IMC should add clean data-structures-and-algorithms practice to the arithmetic drilling; a candidate targeting Optiver should weight the trading-games and quoting-reflex preparation. Figure 6 is the map of where they overlap and where they diverge — prepare for the union, not the assumption that they are one firm.

**Myth 4: "Speed alone wins — just answer as fast as humanly possible."** This one fails on the EV math. Speed with low accuracy is a trap, because a wrong answer scores zero and you spent the time anyway. The optimum is the *joint* product of speed and accuracy, and as the worked example showed, the cutoff is usually high enough on *both* axes that you cannot brute-force it with reckless speed. The trained candidate is fast *and* accurate; the reckless one answers 80 questions and gets 55 right. Calibration — knowing when to commit and when to double-check — is what separates them, and it is exactly the calibration the desk will demand of you later.

A bonus myth worth puncturing: **"the test is a hazing ritual with no real predictive value."** We spent a whole section on why this is wrong. The test correlates with the real reflexes of market making — speed under pressure, calibration, composure, numerical fluency — which is why a firm full of brilliant traders keeps using it. It is crude, but it is not arbitrary.

## How it plays out in the real world

Let us return Maya to the keyboard, eight weeks after the opening paragraph, and watch the loop play out the way it does for a prepared candidate.

She applied to both Optiver and IMC — for **internships**, deliberately, because she understood that the summer seat is the real interview and the conversion path to full-time. She targeted the offices where she has work authorization and checked the role requirements before applying rather than after.

The mental-math test arrives as round one, as it does for everyone. This time the counter dropping from 80 does not induce panic, because she has run dozens of full 8-minute simulations with the timer visible. `48 × 52` fires "difference of squares" before she finishes reading it; `7/8` is `87.5%` without computation; `812 / 39` is "about 20, nudge to 21." She answers ~76 of the 80, gets ~71 right, and clears the bar in the green tail of Figure 2. An algorithm in Amsterdam advances her, and now a human will read her name.

The online assessment follows. At Optiver it is more math and logic; at IMC it additionally includes a coding screen, for which she practiced clean algorithmic problems because she read Myth 3 and did not assume the two firms were identical. She clears both.

The trading games come next, and here the EV mindset she built for the test pays a second dividend. In the market-making sim she quotes a fair value, charges a calibrated spread, leans her market on her inventory and on the information that arrives, updates cleanly when a trade goes against her, and — crucially — narrates her reasoning out loud so the interviewers can see the calibration. She does not "win" every round, but she reasons like a trader in all of them, which is what they are scoring.

The superday is the last stage: back-to-back rounds of math, games, a probability puzzle, and a fit conversation with people from the desk. By now the firm is not asking whether she can compute — round one settled that — but whether they want her making real risk decisions beside them. She is calm, she is calibrated, she is clearly trainable. She gets an internship offer.

That summer is the real interview. She does the live audition, contributes, learns the system, and converts to a full-time return offer with a package in the reported ~\$425,000 range for IMC conversions — read, as always, with its conditionality: the bonus tracks contribution and does not repeat automatically, and a strong first year is not a promise of a strong second. But the seat is hers, and it started with twenty hours of drilling that moved her from the gray middle of a distribution into its green tail. The firm runs a cheap, decently-predictive filter at the top of a funnel of thousands; you respond by spending the twenty highest-EV hours of your candidacy turning yourself from the gray middle of the distribution into the green tail. That is the same expected-value reasoning the job will demand of you every day on the desk. The test is not separate from the work; it is a low-resolution sample of it.
