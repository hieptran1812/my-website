It is a Thursday morning, and **Wei** — a fourth-year computer-science PhD whose dissertation is on representation learning, three months into his first quant job — is standing in front of six people, defending a signal he is genuinely proud of. He has spent five weeks on it: a feature built from the dispersion of analyst-revision timing, cleaned point-in-time so it never peeks at the future, fit with a model whose hyperparameters he chose on a *separate* slice of history. In his backtest the signal earns an information coefficient of about 0.04 and a Sharpe ratio near 1.6 after costs. He is ready. Then a senior researcher across the table asks, quietly: "How many distinct configurations did you try before you landed on this one?" Wei feels his stomach drop, because the honest answer is "maybe forty." And forty trials, he knows, is enough to make a Sharpe of 1.6 look real when the *deflated* number — the Sharpe you would expect from the luckiest of forty random tries — might be closer to 0.5. The room is not hostile. It is exactly the opposite: everyone wants the signal to be real, and the only way to find out is to attack it as hard as the market eventually will. Wei takes a breath and says, "Let me show you the out-of-sample slice I held back, and the deflated Sharpe accounting for the trials." That sentence is why he was hired.

That single moment is the entire identity of a systematic research fund in miniature. Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw are not built around one genius with one signal; they are built around a *process* for turning hypotheses into edges and then refusing to believe any of them until the data has been given every chance to say no. The job is not "be right about the market." The job is "be ruthlessly honest about whether you are right, on a research question where almost everything that looks like signal is noise." This post is the complete playbook for *that* role at *these two firms*: what a systematic research fund is and how it gets paid, what a research scientist actually does all day, the rigor-and-defend culture, the interview loop with worked numbers, how the two firms differ, the compensation with its honest caveats, and exactly how to prepare. Figure 1 is the whole research workflow on one page — the loop Wei lives inside.

![A seven-stage pipeline showing the systematic research workflow from hypothesis through data, signal, backtest, out-of-sample validation, production, and decay monitoring, with a loop back to re-research](/imgs/blogs/two-sigma-and-de-shaw-the-systematic-research-powerhouses-1.png)

Notice that the pipeline does not end at "production." It ends at "monitor / decay" and loops back to "hypothesis," because every signal a systematic fund ships is dying from the moment it goes live — as more capital finds the same edge, the edge thins. The job is therefore never finished, and that single structural fact shapes everything: the hiring bar, the culture, the comp, and the kind of person who thrives here. Hold that loop in mind; we will return to it repeatedly.

## Foundations: what a systematic research fund does and how it is paid

Before any of the research makes sense, you need a compact vocabulary and one structural fact about the business. None of it is hard, but it is load-bearing for everything below, so let us build it from zero.

**A systematic fund trades by rule, not by gut.** A *discretionary* manager looks at a company, forms a view, and places a bet because they believe it. A *systematic* (or *quantitative*) fund instead writes down a rule — a mathematical model that maps data to positions — and lets the rule trade, automatically, across thousands of instruments at once. Two Sigma (founded 2001, New York City) and D.E. Shaw (founded 1988 by David E. Shaw) are the canonical *academic* systematic funds: they are run like research laboratories whose product is a portfolio of statistical edges. The edge is not "we know where Apple is going." The edge is "we have found hundreds of small, durable statistical regularities, and combined carefully, they add up."

**A signal is a forecast of future return.** The atom of systematic investing is the **signal** (also called an *alpha* or a *factor*): a number, computed from data, that predicts the relative return of an asset over some horizon. A classic example is *momentum* — the empirical regularity that assets which have outperformed recently tend to keep outperforming for a while. A signal takes inputs (prices, fundamentals, text, satellite imagery, anything) and outputs a forecast: "this stock is mildly more attractive than that one over the next two weeks." No single signal is strong; a good one barely beats a coin flip. The art is combining many weak-but-independent signals into a portfolio whose aggregate forecast is reliable. The mechanics of *building* one signal are the subject of the sibling deep-dive on [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research); here we care about why these firms organize their whole company around producing them.

**Alpha is the return you earn that the market did not hand you.** If you just buy the whole market, you earn the market's return — that is *beta*, and it is free. **Alpha** is the excess return that comes from skill: from holding the right things at the right times, net of the market move. A systematic fund sells alpha. Its entire promise to investors is "we will deliver returns that do not simply track the stock market, so you are paying us for genuine edge, not for exposure you could buy for nearly nothing in an index fund." This is why out-of-sample honesty is existential for them: a signal that only *looked* like alpha in the backtest, but was really fitted noise, delivers beta-or-worse in production and destroys the promise.

**Out-of-sample is the only honest test.** Here is the deepest idea in the whole field, and the one the entire culture is built to defend. When you build a signal, you fit it on historical data — the **in-sample** period. Of course it looks good on the data you fit it to; you *chose* it because it looked good there. The only meaningful question is how it performs on data it has never seen: the **out-of-sample** period. A signal that earns a Sharpe of 3.0 in-sample and 0.4 out-of-sample is not a good signal that got unlucky — it is an *overfit*, a pattern your model memorized from noise that does not generalize. Distinguishing real edge from overfitting is the central skill of the job, and we will return to it with numbers. The discipline that protects against it — purged cross-validation, holdout periods, the deflated Sharpe ratio — lives in the sibling posts on [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research) and [overfitting, purged CV, and the deflated Sharpe](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/overfitting-purged-cv-deflated-sharpe-quant-research).

**Three numbers describe a signal's quality.** You will hear them constantly, so define them now.
- The **information coefficient (IC)** is the correlation between your forecast and the realized return. It is almost always small — an IC of 0.03 to 0.05 is *good* in equity signals. A signal that predicts perfectly would have IC of 1.0; nobody is close. The point is that a tiny edge, applied across thousands of names thousands of times, compounds.
- The **Sharpe ratio** is return per unit of risk: the average excess return divided by its standard deviation, annualized. A standalone signal with a Sharpe of 1.0 is solid; a combined book targeting a Sharpe of 2.0+ is excellent. The Sharpe is what investors ultimately pay for, because it measures how *reliable* the return is, not just how big.
- **Turnover** is how much you trade to maintain the signal — how fast the positions churn. High turnover means high transaction costs, which can eat a signal that looks profitable on paper. A signal with a great gross Sharpe and ruinous turnover is worthless net of costs. Every backtest at these firms is run *net of realistic costs*, because a paper edge that cannot survive trading frictions is not an edge.

**Capacity is how much money the signal can absorb before it stops working.** A signal that works on \$10 million may collapse at \$10 billion, because the very act of putting that much capital to work moves the prices the signal is trying to exploit. **Capacity** is the dollar size at which a signal's edge degrades to nothing. This matters enormously at Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw precisely because they are *large* — managing roughly \$70 billion and \$65 billion respectively (as of 2026). A brilliant high-capacity signal is worth vastly more to a \$70 billion fund than a brilliant tiny-capacity one, which shapes what research is valued: long-horizon, broad, scalable signals over fast, narrow, capacity-constrained ones.

**These are hedge funds, paid 2-and-20 on outside money.** Here is the structural fact that distinguishes these firms from a proprietary trading shop like Jane Street. A *prop firm* trades its own capital. Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw, by contrast, are **hedge funds**: they manage *other people's* money — pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, wealthy individuals — and charge a fee. The classic hedge-fund fee is **"2-and-20"**: a *management fee* of about 2% of assets per year (charged regardless of performance) plus a *performance fee* of about 20% of the profits. (Real fees at the top funds vary and are often negotiated differently — some charge a pass-through expense model instead of a flat 2% — but 2-and-20 is the canonical mental model, so we will use it.) This single fact explains the firms' obsession with rigor: when you manage outside money, a blown-up signal does not just cost you — it costs your investors, who will redeem, and your reputation, which is the franchise. The discipline is not academic fussiness; it is the business model.

**The research-scientist role is the heart of the firm.** At a prop trading shop, the central figure is the *trader*. At these systematic funds, the central figure is the **quantitative researcher** or **research scientist**: a person whose job is to generate research ideas, build and test signals, defend them, and ship the survivors into the production book, then monitor them as they decay. Around that core sit *quant developers* and *engineers* who build the data platform, the backtesting infrastructure, and the live trading systems — because a signal is worthless if it cannot be computed reliably on petabytes of data and executed without bugs. The role taxonomy across the industry is laid out in [the four paths](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer); here we zoom all the way in on the research scientist, because that is the role these two firms are most famous for and the one where a research background pays off most.

To summarize the foundations as one sentence: *a systematic research fund is a laboratory that manufactures small, scalable statistical edges from data, sells the aggregate as alpha to outside investors for a fee, and survives only by being more honest about overfitting than anyone else in the building.* Everything else — the hiring, the culture, the comp — falls out of that.

## The research-scientist role and the data/ML lens

So what does Wei actually *do* on a Tuesday? Figure 2 lays out the five things the role demands; the role is the intersection of all five, and a candidate who is brilliant at four and weak at one is a weak hire.

![A five-row by two-column matrix showing what a research-scientist role demands — math, statistics and ML, engineering, rigor, and communication — against what each looks like in a strong hire and why each is load-bearing](/imgs/blogs/two-sigma-and-de-shaw-the-systematic-research-powerhouses-2.png)

Read that matrix as a single claim: *the job is a five-legged stool, and the stool falls over if any leg is missing.* Let us walk each leg, because understanding what the role requires is the key to both deciding whether you want it and preparing for it.

**Math: the substrate.** A research scientist needs comfortable fluency in linear algebra (a portfolio is a vector; a covariance matrix is the risk model; signal combination is often a regression or an optimization), probability (everything is a distribution; every forecast is conditional), and the basics of optimization (you are constantly solving "maximize expected return subject to a risk budget and trading-cost penalty"). You do not need to be a research mathematician, but you need the math to be *transparent* to you so it is not where your effort goes — your effort should go to the research judgment. If the math is the part you are shaky on, the sibling math series is where to build it; for a research scientist the highest-leverage topics are probability, linear algebra, and statistical estimation.

**Statistics and ML: the lens.** This is the leg that most distinguishes these firms from a market-maker. A research scientist sees the world through a statistical lens: *given this data, what can I actually conclude, and how confident should I be?* The modern version adds machine learning — gradient-boosted trees, regularized regression, sometimes deep learning on alternative data (text, images, sequences). Two Sigma in particular is known for an AI/ML emphasis. But — and this is the part beginners miss — the ML here is *adversarial against yourself*. In a Kaggle competition, more model capacity usually helps, because the test set is drawn from the same distribution as the training set. In markets, the future is *not* drawn from the same distribution as the past (regimes change, edges get arbitraged away), and more model capacity usually *hurts* by fitting noise. So the ML skill that matters is not "can you train a big model" — it is "can you tell when a fit is real versus when it is luck," which is a statistics skill more than an engineering one. The full pipeline — financial ML done with purged cross-validation so the model never trains on data that leaks into the test set — is the subject of the sibling post on [the financial ML pipeline](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/financial-ml-pipeline-purged-cv-quant-research).

**Engineering: the multiplier.** A signal that lives only in a notebook that you cannot rerun is not a research result; it is an anecdote. Research scientists at these firms write real code — vectorized, tested, reproducible — that runs over enormous datasets and integrates with a shared research platform. The engineering bar is serious: you will be expected to write code a colleague can read and a system can run in production without you babysitting it. This is *not* the kernel-bypass, lock-free, microsecond-shaving engineering of an HFT shop (that is the [Jump and HRT](/blog/trading/quant-careers/jump-and-hrt-playbook-the-low-latency-systems-bar) world, and it is a different bar). It is the engineering of *correct, scalable, reproducible research*: distributed data processing, clean interfaces, version control, and the discipline that someone can rerun your experiment in two years and get the same number. The engineering is a multiplier on the research — a brilliant idea you cannot implement cleanly at scale is worth less than a good idea you can.

**Rigor: the discipline.** This is the leg the whole culture is built around, so it gets its own section below. In short: the willingness and the methodological toolkit to *kill your own idea* — to hold out data, account for the trials you ran, deflate your Sharpe, and report the honest number even when it is disappointing.

**Communication: the seal.** The best signal in the world delivers zero dollars if you cannot get it into the book, and getting it into the book at these firms means *defending it* to skeptical, sharp colleagues who will attack it. You must be able to explain your hypothesis, your methodology, your results, and — crucially — the ways your result *could* be wrong, clearly and without ego. A research scientist who cannot communicate is like a researcher who never publishes: the work might be brilliant, but it does not compound into the firm.

#### Worked example: evaluating a signal — what "good" actually looks like

Let us make the role concrete with the numbers Wei stares at. He has built a candidate signal on a universe of about 2,000 liquid US stocks, forecasting two-week relative returns. His backtest reports:

- **Information coefficient: 0.04.** The rank correlation between his forecast and the realized two-week return is 0.04. Beginners see 0.04 and think "that is basically zero." It is not. Across 2,000 names, rebalanced every two weeks for years, an IC of 0.04 that is *stable* is a genuinely useful signal — recall that even the best equity signals live around 0.03 to 0.05. The lesson: *in this game, a tiny but durable edge is the whole prize; nobody is hunting for IC of 0.5.*
- **Sharpe ratio: 1.6, net of costs.** Standalone, after realistic transaction costs, the signal earns about 1.6 units of return per unit of risk. A standalone Sharpe of 1.6 is good — not spectacular, but a real contributor to a combined book that targets, say, 2.0+ by stacking many such signals whose errors partly cancel.
- **Turnover: 180% per month.** The book churns nearly twice a month. That is *moderately high* — it means costs matter a lot, which is exactly why the Sharpe must be computed net of costs, not gross. If the gross Sharpe were 2.5 and the net were 1.6, the 0.9 gap *is* the cost of turnover, and a slightly slower (lower-turnover) version of the signal might net more.

Now the question that decides everything: **does it hold out-of-sample?** Wei fit the signal on data through 2022 and held back 2023–2024 entirely. On that untouched slice, the IC is 0.035 and the net Sharpe is 1.3. The numbers *degraded* — they always do, because in-sample is optimistic by construction — but they degraded *gracefully*, from 1.6 to 1.3, not catastrophically from 1.6 to 0.2. That graceful degradation is the signature of a real edge. *The grading is never "how high is the in-sample Sharpe"; it is "how little does the edge degrade when it meets data it has never seen."*

The full toolkit for this evaluation — IC, Sharpe, turnover, breakeven cost, the works — is drilled in the sibling post on [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research). What matters for *this role* is that the daily work *is* this evaluation, run over and over, with the firm's money and reputation on the line.

## The rigor culture: publish, defend, and kill your own ideas

If you remember one thing about the culture of these firms, make it this: **the highest virtue is intellectual honesty, and the most respected act is killing your own idea before the market does.** This sounds like a platitude until you see how it reshapes daily life.

**Why rigor is the whole business.** Return to the structural fact: these are hedge funds managing tens of billions of *outside* money at roughly 2-and-20. A signal that looks like a Sharpe of 3.0 but is really overfit noise will, in production, lose money or merely track the market — and when it does, investors redeem, the performance fee evaporates, and the franchise erodes. The cost of a *false positive* (shipping an overfit signal) is therefore enormous, and the cost of a *false negative* (killing a good signal because you were too strict) is merely an opportunity cost. When false positives are far more expensive than false negatives, the rational culture is one that is *aggressively skeptical* of every result — which is precisely what you find. The skepticism is not personality; it is the economically correct response to the asymmetry.

**The publish-and-defend norm.** Research at these firms is reviewed the way a scientific paper is reviewed. You write up your hypothesis, methodology, and results, and you present them to colleagues whose explicit job in that meeting is to find the flaw. Did you peek at the future (look-ahead bias)? Did you survivorship-bias your universe (only test on stocks that still exist)? How many configurations did you try before this one (the multiple-testing problem)? Is the Sharpe deflated for those trials? Does it survive realistic costs and capacity? This is uncomfortable the first time and energizing once you internalize that *everyone is on the same side* — they all want the signal to be real, and the only way to know is to attack it. A researcher who takes the critique personally, or who cannot defend their choices, struggles here regardless of raw talent. A researcher who *invites* the attack and has already done it to themselves thrives.

**Killing your own idea is a skill, not a defeat.** New researchers are emotionally attached to their signals; they have spent weeks on them. The senior researchers are not, and the gap is the whole maturity curve of the job. A senior researcher runs the kill-tests *first* — the holdout, the trials count, the cost sensitivity — and reports a *dead* signal with the same equanimity as a live one, because a fast, honest kill frees them to work on the next idea instead of nursing a corpse. The sibling post on [the quant research write-up and killing ideas](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/quant-research-writeup-killing-ideas) is the technique manual; the cultural point here is that *the ability to kill your own idea quickly and cleanly is among the most valued traits in the building*, and it is exactly what the interview is built to detect.

**The engineering-meets-science blend.** The rigor is not only statistical; it is also engineering rigor. A result you cannot reproduce is not a result. So the culture prizes clean, version-controlled, tested research code, point-in-time data hygiene (your data as it *was known at the time*, never revised-in-hindsight), and reproducible pipelines. This is where the "serious engineering bar" lives — not in latency, but in *reproducibility at scale*. A signal that ran once on a hand-massaged dataset and cannot be regenerated is treated with suspicion, correctly.

#### Worked example: the overfitting trap — in-sample vs out-of-sample and the deflated Sharpe

This is the worked example that *is* the culture. Figure 3 draws it; let us put the numbers behind the picture.

![A line chart of cumulative strategy return over sixty months showing an in-sample fitted curve that looks like a Sharpe of three collapsing to a flat out-of-sample line, annotated with the overfitting cliff and a deflated Sharpe near point five](/imgs/blogs/two-sigma-and-de-shaw-the-systematic-research-powerhouses-3.png)

Suppose Wei, in his eager early days, tries a *different* approach than the careful signal above. He has a flexible model and a rich dataset, and he searches hard: he tries roughly **40 distinct configurations** — different features, lookbacks, model hyperparameters — and reports the *best one he found*. That best configuration shows an **in-sample Sharpe of 3.0**. He is thrilled. A Sharpe of 3.0 would be one of the best equity signals on earth.

Here is the trap. When you try 40 configurations and report the best, you are not measuring the quality of a signal — you are measuring the *luckiest of 40 noisy draws*. Even if every one of those 40 configurations were pure noise with a true Sharpe of zero, the *maximum* of 40 noisy backtest Sharpes will look impressively positive, simply because you cherry-picked the extreme. This is the **multiple-testing problem**, and the correction for it is the **deflated Sharpe ratio**: an adjustment that asks "given that I ran N trials, how high would the best Sharpe be *by chance alone*, and is my observed Sharpe meaningfully above that bar?"

With about 40 trials, the chance-expected best Sharpe is far from zero — and once Wei deflates his 3.0 for the 40 trials, the honest number drops to roughly **0.5**. Then he does the only test that cannot be gamed: he runs the chosen configuration on the **2023–2024 holdout he never touched**. Out-of-sample, the realized Sharpe is **0.4**. The deflated estimate (≈0.5) *predicted* the out-of-sample collapse (≈0.4); the raw in-sample 3.0 was a mirage. That is the cliff in Figure 3: the blue in-sample curve climbing smoothly, the dashed line promising it would continue, and the red out-of-sample line going flat the moment the holdout begins.

The discipline that prevents this — counting your trials honestly, deflating, holding out data, using purged cross-validation so no information leaks from test into train — is the subject of the sibling posts on [overfitting and the deflated Sharpe](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/overfitting-purged-cv-deflated-sharpe-quant-research) and [the financial ML pipeline](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/financial-ml-pipeline-purged-cv-quant-research). The cultural lesson is the one Wei learned in the review room: *the in-sample number is not evidence of edge; it is evidence of how hard you searched. Only the out-of-sample number, deflated for your search, is real.*

## The interview loop, decoded stage by stage

Now the process. The systematic-research loop differs from a market-maker's games-and-mental-math loop in one decisive way: the center of gravity is a **research case** — a take-home or live exercise where you build and defend a signal — because that is the most faithful possible miniature of the job. Figure 4 maps the loop.

![A seven-stage pipeline of the research-scientist interview loop running from screen through research case, coding, ML and statistics, math, and team fit to an offer, with a loop back to redo the case](/imgs/blogs/two-sigma-and-de-shaw-the-systematic-research-powerhouses-4.png)

**Stage 0 — screen.** A resume, ideally with a research signal: publications, a strong PhD or master's in a quantitative field, competition results (Kaggle, ICPC, Putnam, IMO/IOI), or a demonstrable open-source research project. Many candidates first hit an **online assessment** — a timed coding or quantitative test. The screen is coarse; it gets you into the loop and then your pedigree mostly stops mattering, because every later stage measures what you can *do*, not what you *have*.

**Stage 1 — the research case (the center of the loop).** This is the stage that defines these firms. You are given a dataset and a question — often a take-home that runs several days, sometimes a shorter live exercise — and asked to build a predictive signal and *write it up*. What is being graded is almost never "how high is your backtest Sharpe." It is your *research judgment*: Did you state an economic hypothesis (a *reason* the edge should exist) before fitting, or did you data-mine blindly? Did you guard against look-ahead and survivorship bias? Did you hold out data and report out-of-sample honestly? Did you account for the configurations you tried? Can you articulate *how your result could be wrong* and what you would test next? A candidate who reports a modest, well-defended signal with clean out-of-sample discipline beats a candidate who reports a spectacular, undefended, obviously-overfit one — every time. The case is the single highest-leverage thing to prepare for, and the way to prepare is to *actually do it*: build a real signal on real data and write it up as if defending it.

**Stage 2 — coding.** A round (or rounds) of algorithmic data-structures-and-algorithms problems plus, often, a more applied exercise: clean up some messy data, vectorize a slow computation, implement a backtest correctly. The bar is "writes correct, clean, reasonably efficient code" — not the C++-low-latency depth of an HFT shop, but real software competence, frequently in Python (with C++ valued for the heavier infrastructure roles). The general coding-prep map is in [the four paths](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer).

**Stage 3 — ML and statistics.** The round that most distinguishes these firms. Expect conceptual and applied questions on overfitting, the bias-variance tradeoff, cross-validation (and *why naive k-fold leaks information in time series* — the purged-CV idea), regularization, model selection, hypothesis testing, and how to tell a real fit from a lucky one. They are probing the *lens* from the role section: do you reflexively think about generalization and multiple testing, or do you treat ML as a leaderboard-climbing exercise? A candidate who says "I'd use a holdout and deflate for the number of models I tried" is speaking the firm's native language.

**Stage 4 — math.** Probability and linear algebra, sometimes a short proof or a derivation. Conditional probability, expectations, covariance and its matrix algebra, a regression derived from first principles, occasionally a clean brain-teaser. The depth is real but it is *applied* — they want to see that the math is transparent to you, not that you can prove the Riemann hypothesis. The technique drills live across the [math-for-quants](/blog/trading/quant-careers/do-you-need-a-phd-the-backgrounds-that-get-hired) prep ecosystem; for research, probability and linear algebra carry the most weight.

**Stage 5 — team fit.** Not a soft formality. The interviewers are forming a judgment about whether you are someone they want in the review room: curious, honest about uncertainty, quick to update, not territorial about your ideas. You demonstrate this through *how you behave in the technical rounds* — a candidate who says "good point, my holdout was too short, here's how I'd fix it" is showing the exact trait a publish-and-defend culture needs. Treat every round as partly a fit round.

The loop in Figure 4 has a "redo the case" edge back to the start for a reason: a weak research case sinks an otherwise strong candidate, and a strong one carries you. *The research case is to a systematic fund what the trading game is to a market-maker — the one stage that most faithfully simulates the job, and therefore the one that decides offers.* The broader interview-process strategy across firms is in the sibling guide on [the quant interview process](/blog/trading/quant-careers/do-you-need-a-phd-the-backgrounds-that-get-hired); here the point is that the research case is the hinge.

#### Worked example: Wei (CS-PhD) chooses this path — and why the PhD premium shows up here

Let us make the central career claim of this post concrete, because it is the reason a research-minded reader is here. **Wei** has a CS PhD. He could go to a big-tech ML team, a startup, or a quant fund. Why does the PhD premium show up *most* on this path?

Compare two candidates for the same research-scientist seat: Wei (PhD, 5 years past undergrad, including 4 doing supervised research) and **Sam** (strong CS bachelor's, 2 years as a software engineer). On raw coding, Sam may be *better* — two years shipping production software is real practice. On math, they may be comparable. The gap opens on exactly the two legs the job is built around: **rigor** and **the statistical lens**.

Wei has spent four years doing the thing the job *is*: forming a hypothesis, designing an experiment to test it, watching most of his hypotheses die, learning to *want* to find the flaw in his own work, and defending results to a skeptical advisor and skeptical reviewers. That is not a credential; it is *4,000+ hours of supervised practice at the exact skill the interview tests*. When the senior researcher asks Wei "how many configurations did you try," Wei's instinct — built over years — is to have already deflated for it. Sam, brilliant but new to research, has to learn that instinct on the job, which is why a research background commands a premium *here specifically*, where the daily work is research, far more than it does at a market-maker or an HFT shop, where it is largely irrelevant.

Now the honest part, because this series does not gatekeep: **the PhD is not required.** It is *common* for research-scientist roles at Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw, not mandatory, and there are excellent research scientists with master's degrees or even bachelor's who built the rigor instinct another way — through serious independent research, a research-heavy internship, or sheer obsessive self-directed work. The premium attaches to *the rigor instinct and the statistical lens*, and a PhD is the most reliable (and most expensive, in years) way to acquire them — not the only way. The full breakdown of which backgrounds get hired, and when a PhD is worth the years, is the subject of the sibling post on [whether you need a PhD](/blog/trading/quant-careers/do-you-need-a-phd-the-backgrounds-that-get-hired). The takeaway for Wei: *the PhD pays off here because the job is research, and the PhD is years of paid practice at research — but it is the skill that gets hired, not the diploma.*

## Two Sigma vs D.E. Shaw: same DNA, different stories

These two firms are siblings — both academic systematic powerhouses, both research-scientist cultures, both managing tens of billions of outside money — but they are not identical, and a candidate should understand the differences. Figure 5 puts them side by side.

![A five-row by two-column matrix comparing Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw across founding and base, scale, core focus, culture, and recent strategic moves](/imgs/blogs/two-sigma-and-de-shaw-the-systematic-research-powerhouses-5.png)

**Founding and lineage.** **D.E. Shaw** is the elder, founded in **1988 by David E. Shaw** — a computer scientist who left a Columbia faculty position to apply computational methods to markets. It is, in a real sense, the *original* quant fund: the firm that proved you could hunt for statistical anomalies systematically and at scale, and whose alumni seeded a remarkable fraction of the industry (the "quant king" lineage is not marketing; the firm's research-first DNA propagated outward for decades). **Two Sigma** is younger, founded in **2001 in New York City**, and grew up in the era of cheap distributed computing and, later, modern machine learning — which shows in its self-presentation as an AI/ML-and-engineering company that happens to invest.

**Scale.** Both are large: Two Sigma manages roughly **\$70 billion** with around **2,000 staff**; D.E. Shaw manages roughly **\$65 billion** (figures as of 2026, from public reporting; treat as approximate and time-varying). The scale is not a vanity number — it is *why long-horizon, high-capacity signals are prized*. A signal that only works on \$50 million does almost nothing for a \$70 billion book; the research that gets rewarded is research that *scales*.

**Core focus and edge.** Two Sigma leans into **AI/ML and distributed computing** as its identity — large-scale data engineering, machine-learning models, a platform-heavy approach. D.E. Shaw is associated with **mathematical models and computational anomaly hunting** — the original deep-quant approach — and is famously secretive and rigorous. In practice the two overlap enormously (both do plenty of ML, both do plenty of classical statistics), and the difference is more flavor and emphasis than a wall.

**Recent moves.** The notable strategic difference: D.E. Shaw has *added discretionary* capability — its **Cogence Fund** brings human-judgment investing alongside the systematic core — which is a deliberate reminder that "systematic" and "discretionary" are not enemies; large firms increasingly run both. Two Sigma's recent identity has leaned hard into its engineering-and-AI platform. For a candidate, the practical read is: both are research-scientist homes; if you are AI/ML-and-platform-flavored, Two Sigma may feel native; if you are deep-math-and-anomaly-flavored, D.E. Shaw may; but the hiring bar and the daily rigor are recognizably the same animal, and you should apply to both.

**How they compare to the rest of the landscape.** It helps to triangulate. Against [Citadel](/blog/trading/quant-careers/citadel-and-citadel-securities-the-pod-shop-and-the-market-maker) — a multi-strategy *pod shop* where portfolio managers run accountable books with sharp-elbowed, high-turnover incentives — Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw are *collaborative research labs*: less month-to-month book-level accountability terror, more long-horizon collective research. Against [WorldQuant's alpha-factory model](/blog/trading/quant-careers/worldquant-and-the-alpha-factory-model) — which scales alpha generation across a distributed network of researchers each producing many formulaic alphas — Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw concentrate deeper, fewer, more rigorously-defended research efforts in-house with a heavier engineering platform. The taxonomy of these archetypes is derived in [the firm archetypes post](/blog/trading/quant-careers/citadel-and-citadel-securities-the-pod-shop-and-the-market-maker); the point here is that "systematic research powerhouse" is a specific niche — outside money, deep in-house research, long horizons, publish-and-defend — and these two firms define it.

## Compensation: the band, the conditionality, and how it flows to a researcher

Now the money, with the honesty this series demands. Figure 6 puts the firm scale next to the QR comp band.

![A two-panel chart with firm assets under management of about seventy billion for Two Sigma and sixty-five billion for D.E. Shaw on the left, and reported quant-researcher total compensation by level on the right with amber error bars showing the spread](/imgs/blogs/two-sigma-and-de-shaw-the-systematic-research-powerhouses-6.png)

**The reported band.** Quant-research compensation at the top systematic funds tracks the broader QR market. As reported on levels.fyi (2025) and aggregated in this series' data appendix (as of June 2026): a **new-grad / L1** researcher lands roughly **\$300k–\$336k** total; the **QR median** sits around **\$250k–\$325k** depending on firm and level; a **mid-career** researcher around 4 years of experience can reach roughly **\$575k** (a structure like \$175k base + ~\$400k bonus is reported in the pod-shop QR world); and **senior** researchers reach roughly **\$642k**, with **top reported** numbers around **\$721k+**. These are *reported ranges*, not guaranteed offers, and they vary by year, firm, level, and — above all — performance. The amber error bars in Figure 6 are the point: comp is a *band*, not a point, and where you land in the band is mostly the bonus.

**The base is flat-ish; the bonus is the lever.** As across the industry, the *base salary* is relatively stable and modest-by-finance-standards (the H1B-disclosed bases at the top firms cluster around \$200k–\$300k); the **bonus** is the variable that ties your total comp to your research's contribution to the firm's P&L. A strong year can multiply your total; a quiet year can leave you near base. And — the single most important caveat in this entire post — **the bonus does not repeat automatically.** A researcher who earns \$700k in a year where their signals contributed strongly might earn \$350k the next year if those signals decayed (remember the loop in Figure 1 — signals *always* decay) and the new ones have not yet landed. Anyone quoting you a steady "\$600k a year" is describing a survivor in a good stretch, not a guaranteed annuity.

**Survivorship bias, stated plainly.** The headline comp numbers describe *the people who survived the filter*. Top research seats are extremely selective — thousands apply per seat — and the funds run an up-or-out reality where researchers whose signals do not earn their keep over time are managed out. The \$700k senior number is real for the researchers who got there; it is silent about the larger number who washed out, switched firms, or landed well below the headline. Read every comp figure in this post through that lens: it is the *survivor's* number, and the survivor is selected.

#### Worked example: 2-and-20 economics and how comp flows to a researcher

Let us trace the dollars from investor to researcher, because understanding the *flow* explains why comp is shaped the way it is. Take a stylized fund — call it a \$50 billion book (between the two firms' scale) — and use the canonical 2-and-20 fee model (real fees differ, but this builds the intuition).

- **Management fee:** 2% of \$50 billion = **\$1 billion per year**, charged regardless of performance. This funds the firm's operations — salaries, the data, the compute, the buildings — which is why *base* salaries can be paid even in a flat year.
- **Performance fee:** suppose the fund returns a strong 15% net to investors in a given year. The *gross* profit before the performance fee is larger; on a simplified basis, 20% of the profits is the firm's performance fee. If gross profit attributable to the fee is, say, \$10 billion in profit, the 20% performance fee is roughly **\$2 billion** that year. This is the pool from which the big *bonuses* are paid — and it is *entirely* dependent on performance. In a flat or down year, this pool shrinks toward zero, and so do bonuses.

Now Wei's slice. Suppose Wei's research contributed signals responsible for, say, \$200 million of the fund's profit in a strong year. His comp is *not* a fixed fraction of that — comp at these firms is determined by a holistic review of contribution, seniority, and the firm's overall results — but his bonus is *driven* by it. If his contribution lands him at a senior-band total of roughly **\$650k** that year (base ~\$200k + bonus ~\$450k), the bonus is the part that came from the performance pool. The next year, if his signals decayed (the loop) and contributed \$40 million instead of \$200 million, his bonus compresses — perhaps a total of **\$350k** (base ~\$200k + bonus ~\$150k). *Same researcher, same talent, half the comp — because the performance pool is the lever, and the lever is the firm's P&L, which is the sum of decaying signals.* This is the literal mechanism behind "the bonus does not repeat," and it is why a research scientist's career is a sequence of *new* signals, not a single annuity-paying one.

The intuition to carry: *base is paid out of the steady management fee; the bonus is paid out of the volatile performance fee; so your total comp is as volatile as the firm's returns, and your job security is as durable as your ability to keep producing new edge as the old edge decays.*

## How to prepare for a research-scientist role

Preparation for this path looks different from preparing for a market-maker's games loop, because the loop is different. Figure 7 is the fit-and-prep checklist.

![A five-row by two-column matrix asking whether a systematic research fund is for you and giving the concrete prep action for each row, covering enjoyment of the work, tolerance for rigor, the right reps, the portfolio to show, and a red flag](/imgs/blogs/two-sigma-and-de-shaw-the-systematic-research-powerhouses-7.png)

**First, decide if you want it.** The fit questions in the left column of Figure 7 are not rhetorical. Do you genuinely enjoy hunting for structure in data — the long, mostly-failing search for a regularity that holds up? Are you *glad*, not crushed, to kill your own idea when the data says no? Are you comfortable defending your work to people whose job is to attack it? If yes, this is one of the best jobs in the world for a research-minded person. If the idea of spending weeks on a signal that then dies in the holdout sounds miserable, a market-maker's faster-feedback world or a discretionary seat may suit you better, and that is real information, not a failing.

**Build one real signal, end to end.** The single highest-leverage preparation is to *do the job*: take a public dataset (equity prices, fundamentals, a Kaggle financial dataset, anything with a time dimension), form an economic hypothesis, build a signal, backtest it net of costs, hold out data, and evaluate it honestly with IC, Sharpe, and turnover. Do not stop at "it works in-sample." Run the holdout. Count your trials. Deflate. This is exactly the research case the interview will hand you, and having done it once — *honestly, including the part where it disappointed you* — is worth more than any amount of reading. The how-to is in [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research) and [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research).

**Drill the rigor toolkit until it is reflexive.** Purged cross-validation (so test data never leaks into training in a time series), the deflated Sharpe ratio (so you deflate for the trials you ran), look-ahead and survivorship bias (so your data is point-in-time and your universe includes the dead companies). These are the things the ML/stats round probes and the research case grades. They live in [the financial ML pipeline](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/financial-ml-pipeline-purged-cv-quant-research) and [overfitting and the deflated Sharpe](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/overfitting-purged-cv-deflated-sharpe-quant-research). The goal is that "how many trials did you run, and have you deflated for them" is a question you ask *yourself* before anyone asks you.

**Write it up and defend it out loud.** A signal you cannot explain and defend is half a research result. Write a short, honest report on your practice signal — hypothesis, method, results, *and the ways it could be wrong* — and then explain it to someone (a friend, a rubber duck, a mirror) as if they are trying to find the flaw. The communication leg of the role is graded in the fit round and in how you handle the research case discussion. The technique manual is [the quant research write-up](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/quant-research-writeup-killing-ideas).

**Show a clean, reproducible repo.** The engineering leg is real. Your practice signal should live in a version-controlled repository that someone else could clone and rerun to get the same numbers. That single artifact demonstrates the engineering bar, the reproducibility rigor, and your communication all at once — it is the most efficient thing you can build to show, not tell.

**Cover the math and coding floors.** You do not need to over-invest here relative to the research case, but you cannot be weak: probability and linear algebra fluency for the math round, clean DSA-level coding for the coding round. If a PhD or research background already gives you the research instinct, the marginal hour is often best spent shoring up the coding floor (many researchers under-practice clean, efficient code) rather than re-deriving statistics you already know.

The shape of the prep, in one line: *do the job before the interview does — build, hold out, deflate, write up, defend, and ship a clean repo — because the research case is the hinge and the only way to prepare for it is to have already done it.*

## Common misconceptions

Five myths sink more research-track candidates than any skill gap. Name them, correct them.

**Myth 1: "You must have a PhD."** *False, but with a real kernel.* A PhD is *common* for research-scientist roles at Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw — more common here than anywhere else in quant — but it is *not mandatory*. What is mandatory is the *rigor instinct and the statistical lens* (the two legs from the role section), and a PhD is the most reliable, most expensive way to acquire them, not the only way. Excellent research scientists exist with master's and bachelor's degrees who built that instinct through serious independent research, a research-heavy internship, or obsessive self-directed work. The diploma is a strong *signal* of the skill; the firms hire the skill. Treat the PhD as "the highest-probability path to the instinct," not "the gate." The full backgrounds breakdown is in [whether you need a PhD](/blog/trading/quant-careers/do-you-need-a-phd-the-backgrounds-that-get-hired).

**Myth 2: "It is just Kaggle for money."** *Dangerously false, and it sinks ML-strong candidates.* Kaggle and finance research share tools (gradient boosting, cross-validation) but differ in the one way that matters: in Kaggle, the test set is drawn from the *same distribution* as the training set, so more model capacity usually helps and the leaderboard is the truth. In markets, the future is *not* drawn from the past — regimes change, edges get arbitraged away — so more capacity usually *hurts* by fitting noise, and the in-sample "leaderboard" is a *trap*. A candidate who treats the research case like a Kaggle competition (maximize the backtest Sharpe, throw capacity at it) will produce exactly the overfit corpse from the Figure 3 worked example and get rejected. The skill is adversarial-against-yourself generalization, which is a statistics-and-rigor skill, not a leaderboard skill.

**Myth 3: "One genius signal makes the fund."** *False.* The romantic image of a lone genius with one world-beating signal is almost the opposite of how these firms work. No single signal is strong (recall: IC of 0.04 is *good*); the edge comes from *combining hundreds of weak, partly-independent signals* so their errors cancel and the aggregate forecast is reliable, then *replacing them as they decay*. The fund is a research *factory*, not a single oracle. Chasing one perfect signal is both bad research (you will overfit hunting for it) and a misread of the business. The realistic mental model is "many small, decaying edges, continuously renewed" — which is exactly why the loop in Figure 1 never closes.

**Myth 4: "Discretionary investing is dead."** *False, and D.E. Shaw's Cogence Fund is the counterexample.* The narrative that systematic has "won" and human judgment is obsolete is overstated. Large firms increasingly run *both* — D.E. Shaw added discretionary capability deliberately — because systematic and discretionary edges are partly *uncorrelated*, and a firm that captures both has a more robust return stream than one betting everything on one approach. For a candidate, the lesson is that "systematic vs discretionary" is not a war you must pick a side in; the best firms are converging on "both, where each is strongest."

**Myth 5: "The in-sample Sharpe is the result."** *The single most expensive myth, and the one the whole culture exists to refute.* A high in-sample Sharpe is *not* evidence of edge — it is evidence of how hard you searched. Only the out-of-sample number, deflated for the number of trials you ran, is real. A candidate or researcher who reports a raw in-sample 3.0 without the holdout and the trials-deflation has not produced a result; they have produced a question. Internalizing this *before* the interview is the difference between sounding like a researcher and sounding like someone who will overfit the firm's money.

## How it plays out in the real world

Stitch the pieces into a realistic arc. Wei, our CS-PhD, applies to both Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw (and a couple of pod shops and a market-maker, because a smart funnel diversifies — the funnel math is in [the firm archetypes and application strategy](/blog/trading/quant-careers/citadel-and-citadel-securities-the-pod-shop-and-the-market-maker)). He clears the online assessment on the strength of clean coding. The research case is where he shines: he forms an economic hypothesis *first*, builds a modest signal, holds out two years of data, reports an honest out-of-sample Sharpe of 1.3 (degraded gracefully from 1.6 in-sample), and — crucially — opens his write-up with the *ways it could be wrong* and how he would test them. In the ML/stats round he reflexively talks about purged CV and deflating for trials. In the fit round, when challenged on a weak point, he says "you're right, my holdout was short, here's how I'd extend it." He gets an offer near the new-grad band, around **\$300k–\$336k** total, with the understanding that the bonus — and thus his real comp — will track his research contribution.

Year one, Wei ships two small signals into the combined book and earns a bonus that puts him comfortably above base. Year two, one of those signals decays (the loop), his contribution dips, and his bonus compresses — a concrete, slightly painful lesson in why the bonus does not repeat. Year three he lands a higher-capacity, longer-horizon signal that scales into the \$65–70 billion book, his contribution jumps, and his comp moves toward the mid-career band around **\$575k**. By year five, as a survivor, he is in the senior conversation — roughly **\$642k–\$721k+** in a strong stretch — fully aware that the number is a *survivor's* number and that a quiet year would compress it sharply. Along the way the thing that made him valuable was never one brilliant signal; it was the *renewable* ability to generate, defend, and ship new edge as the old edge decayed, plus the engineering to do it reproducibly at scale and the honesty to kill what did not work.

Two contrasts make the arc legible. **Sam** (the strong bachelor's-degree engineer from the worked example) took the same loop and stumbled on the research case — not on the coding, which he aced, but on the rigor: he reported a spectacular in-sample Sharpe without a holdout, could not say how many configurations he tried, and treated the reviewer's pushback defensively. He was not rejected for lack of talent; he was rejected for lack of the *rigor instinct* the case is built to detect. He could acquire it — through a research-heavy internship or a serious self-directed project — and reapply, which many do. And **Maya** (the math-undergraduate aiming at trading from the sibling posts) found, on reflection, that she *preferred* the fast feedback of a market-maker's seat to the long, mostly-failing search of research, and went to [Jane Street](/blog/trading/quant-careers/jane-street-playbook-culture-ocaml-and-the-trading-games) instead — a correct decision, not a worse one. The lesson the three of them teach together: *the systematic research powerhouses select hard for a specific temperament — the joy of rigorous, mostly-failing, honestly-defended research — and the right move is to figure out whether that is you before you spend the years.*

A few honest realities to round it out. The selectivity is severe; these are among the hardest seats in quant to land, and a single strong application is a low-probability bet — which is why a diversified, well-prepared funnel beats a single dream-firm shot. The work is genuinely intellectual and genuinely frustrating: most of your hypotheses will die, and the satisfaction is in the *honesty* of the search, not in being right often. The comp is excellent for survivors and volatile for everyone, because it is bonus-driven and the bonus tracks decaying P&L. And the PhD premium is real *here* in a way it is not elsewhere — not because of the diploma, but because the job *is* research and a PhD is years of practice at research. If that bargain appeals to you, you are exactly who these firms are built to hire.
