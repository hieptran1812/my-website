## Foundations: why poker is the perfect training for market making

Before any of the poker-trading mapping makes sense, you need a handful of definitions. If you already work in markets, skim. If you are brilliant but new to finance, read this slowly, because every clever thing later in the post is just a re-application of these six ideas.

**What a market maker is.** Most people picture a trader as someone who *predicts* — who decides a stock will go up and buys it. A market maker does something different and, in a sense, humbler. A market maker continuously offers to *both* buy and sell an instrument, posting a price it will pay (the **bid**) and a slightly higher price at which it will sell (the **ask** or **offer**). The gap between them is the **spread**. When a buyer hits the ask and, a moment later, a seller hits the bid, the market maker has bought low and sold high and pocketed the spread without ever having an opinion about where the price is going. SIG does this in **options** — contracts whose value depends on an underlying stock, an interest rate, and the market's expectation of future volatility — across thousands of names. The market maker's profit is the spread, repeated millions of times, minus the cost of the inventory risk it takes on along the way.

**Expected value (EV).** This is the single most important concept in the entire industry, and it is exactly the concept poker teaches. The EV of a bet is the probability-weighted average of its outcomes. If a bet pays you +\$100 with probability 0.30 and costs you \$30 with probability 0.70, its EV is `0.30 × (+\$100) + 0.70 × (−\$30) = +\$30 − \$21 = +\$9`. A positive-EV bet is one you want to make *on average, repeated many times*. A market maker does not need to be right on any single trade. It needs each quote to carry positive expected value, and it needs to make that quote enough times that the law of large numbers turns the small per-trade edge into reliable profit. This is the spine of the whole *Breaking Into Quant* series: **the job is a probabilistic edge — and so is getting it.**

**Implied probability.** A price is a probability statement in disguise. If a contract pays \$1 when an event happens and \$0 otherwise, and it trades at \$0.40, the market is *implying* a 40% probability of that event. Reading prices as probabilities — and probabilities as prices — is the bilingual fluency every options trader needs, and it is *identical* to the skill of reading pot odds in poker, as we will see.

**Pot odds.** In poker, the **pot** is the money already in the middle of the table. When it is your turn and you face a bet, you compare the price you must pay to call against the total you stand to win. If the pot is \$80 and you must call \$20, you are risking \$20 to win \$100 (the \$80 already there plus your own \$20 call becomes the pot you can win, and against the bettor you are getting \$80-to-\$20, i.e. 4-to-1). Pot odds convert directly into a **break-even probability**: at 4-to-1, you break even if you win one time in five, i.e. 20% of the time. If your hand wins *more* than 20% of the time, calling is +EV. That conversion — price-to-call into break-even probability — is the same arithmetic an options trader does to read whether a quote is rich or cheap.

**Variance, bankroll, and risk of ruin.** Here is where poker stops being a cute analogy and becomes a genuinely deep teacher. A poker player with a real edge still loses individual hands and even loses for whole sessions, because outcomes are *noisy*. The spread of those outcomes is **variance**. Your **bankroll** is the total money you can afford to put at risk. **Risk of ruin** is the probability that a string of bad luck wipes you out *before* your edge has time to show up. The entire discipline of bankroll management exists because of one brutal fact: **a positive-EV strategy bet too large will still go bankrupt with near-certainty.** A trader who internalizes that is safe; one who only learned "take the +EV bet" is a time bomb. SIG cares about this more than almost anything, which is why one of our four worked examples is dedicated entirely to it.

**Edge.** Any reason you expect positive EV. In poker, your edge is being a better decision-maker than the table — better at reading ranges, sizing bets, and folding when you are beat. On a market-making desk, the edge is the spread plus your skill at managing inventory and reading whether incoming order flow is *informed* (someone who knows something you don't) or *noise* (someone rebalancing a portfolio). In both arenas, edge is small, probabilistic, and only pays out over a large number of repetitions — which is precisely why both arenas reward the same temperament.

Now the thesis of the whole post: **poker is a fast, cheap, emotionally honest simulator of the market maker's job.** A real trade gives you feedback over minutes to days and risks real firm capital. A poker hand gives you the same decision structure — incomplete information, an implied-probability calculation, a sizing decision, a read on opponents, and an outcome that is noisy relative to the decision — in ninety seconds, for chips. You can run hundreds of reps in an evening. That density of feedback, on the exact cognitive skill the job requires, is why SIG built its training and screening around the game. It is not that they want to hire poker pros. It is that poker is the best available gymnasium for the muscle they need.

## The poker-to-trading mapping, concept by concept

Let's walk Figure 1 row by row, because each row is a real correspondence a SIG trader uses every day.

**Pot odds become implied probability.** When Maya faces a \$20 call into a \$100 pot, she computes a 20% break-even probability and asks "does my hand win more often than that?" When a SIG trader looks at an option quoted at \$0.40 that pays \$1 if a stock finishes above a strike, she computes a 40% implied probability and asks "is the true probability higher or lower than that?" If she thinks the true probability is 45%, the option is cheap and she wants to buy it (and quote accordingly); if she thinks it is 35%, it is rich and she wants to sell it. The arithmetic is identical. The discipline of *never confusing the price you're offered with the true probability* is the entire game, in both rooms.

**Position becomes information and inventory.** In poker, **position** means where you sit relative to the action: acting *last* in a betting round is a structural advantage because you see what everyone else does before you commit. More information, same decision — strictly better. On a desk, the analog is twofold. First, **inventory**: the positions you are already holding shape what you want to do next, exactly as your stack size and prior bets shape your poker decisions. Second, **information edge**: a market maker who can see more of the order book, or who is faster, is "in position" relative to slower participants. A trader who has learned to value position in poker has internalized that *information has a price* — and that you should pay up for it and exploit it when you have it.

**Bet sizing becomes quote sizing.** This is the most underrated correspondence and the one SIG watches most closely at the table. A weak poker player bets the same amount regardless of how strong their hand is; a strong one **sizes to the situation** — big with a big edge, small or checking when thin, and never so big that one loss is catastrophic. A market maker does exactly this with quote size: show more size (a willingness to trade a larger quantity) when your edge is large and your inventory is comfortable, show less when your edge is thin or your inventory is already lopsided. The skill is not "bet big" or "bet small"; it is **matching the size to the edge**, which is the Kelly intuition we will make precise in Worked Example 3.

**Reading the table becomes reading order flow.** A good poker player puts opponents on a **range** — the set of hands they could plausibly hold given how they've bet — and updates that range as new cards and bets arrive. A market maker does the same with order flow: is the size hitting my bid coming from an informed participant who knows the stock is about to drop (in which case I should widen my spread and pull back), or from an index fund mechanically rebalancing (in which case it's harmless and I should keep quoting tight)? Both are exercises in **Bayesian updating** on incomplete information: start with a prior, watch the actions, revise. SIG's games test whether you update at all, and whether you update *correctly* rather than emotionally.

The phenomenon a market maker fears most is the poker concept of being **picked off**: trading against someone who knows more than you do. In poker, when a tight, conservative player suddenly bets big into you, the disciplined read is "their range is now very strong, so I should fold marginal hands" — you respect the information their action reveals. On a desk, when an unusually large, aggressive order sweeps your quote in one direction, the disciplined read is identical: "this flow may be informed, so I should widen my spread and reduce my size before I get run over." The trader who keeps quoting tight into informed flow is the poker player who keeps calling big bets with mediocre hands — bleeding money to someone with a better read. This is why a market maker's spread is not greed; it is the toll that uninformed traders pay so that the maker can afford to lose to the informed ones. Internalizing *why* the spread exists — as protection against adverse selection — is one of the things SIG's poker labs teach better than any lecture, because you feel it the first time a "passive" opponent traps you.

**Bankroll rules become position-sizing rules.** Finally, the table teaches the meta-skill: survive. A poker player who plays \$5/\$10 stakes on a bankroll meant for \$1/\$2 will bust on normal variance no matter how good they are. A trader who sizes positions so that a routine bad week could blow up the book is, likewise, doomed regardless of edge. SIG's whole risk culture is downstream of this poker lesson: **the first job is not to maximize EV, it is to stay in the game long enough for your EV to compound.** Hold that thought — it is the punchline of the entire post, and Figure 6 will draw it.

#### Worked example: Maya's pot-odds call, mapped to an options quote

Maya is in a hand. The pot is \$80. Her opponent bets \$20, making the total pot \$100, and it costs her \$20 to call. The pot odds are \$100-to-\$20, or **5-to-1** counting her own call as part of what she wins back — but the cleaner way SIG wants her to think is: *she risks \$20 to win the \$80 already in the pot plus the \$20 bet, so she's getting \$100 for \$20, and her break-even probability is `\$20 / (\$80 + \$20 + \$20) = \$20 / \$120 ≈ 16.7%`.* (The denominator is the final pot if she calls: the \$80 + the opponent's \$20 + her \$20.) So Maya needs her hand to win **more than about 17% of the time** for the call to be +EV.

She estimates her hand wins about 30% of the time at showdown. Since `30% > 17%`, the call is positive expected value. Let's verify the EV directly. If she calls \$20: with probability 0.30 she wins the \$100 pot (net +\$100, since her \$20 is part of the \$120 final pot and she takes the whole thing — her profit is the \$100 that wasn't hers); with probability 0.70 she loses her \$20 call. So `EV = 0.30 × (+\$100) + 0.70 × (−\$20) = +\$30 − \$14 = +\$16`. Calling is worth about +\$16 in expectation. Folding is worth \$0. **She calls.**

Figure 4 draws this decision as the tree SIG wants in your head: size the pot, convert the call price into a break-even probability, compare it to your true equity, and let that single inequality decide. The numbers in the figure use a cleaner \$80 pot with a \$20 call so the break-even lands at a round 20%, but the structure is identical: the decision is never "do I have a good hand?" — it is "does my equity beat the price I'm being offered?" Notice what the tree does *not* contain: any reference to how the last hand went, how you feel, or whether you "deserve" to win this one. The entire computation is forward-looking and unemotional, which is exactly the discipline a market maker needs when a position is moving against her and the temptation is to act on the sting rather than the math.

![A decision tree for a pot-odds call: the spot with an eighty-dollar pot and twenty-dollar call, the price-to-call converting to a twenty percent break-even probability, the comparison against your hand equity, and two leaves — call when equity exceeds twenty percent and fold when it is below](/imgs/blogs/sig-susquehanna-playbook-poker-game-theory-and-ev-4.png)

Now the trading mirror. A SIG trader sees a binary-style option that pays \$1 if a stock closes above \$50 at expiry, quoted at \$0.17. That \$0.17 price is the market's **implied probability**: 17%. The trader's own model says the true probability is 30%. The option is *cheap* — she's being offered the same 17% break-even that Maya was, on an event she believes is 30% likely. So she buys it (and, as a market maker, quotes a bid near \$0.17 to accumulate the position). Her per-contract EV is `0.30 × (\$1 − \$0.17) + 0.70 × (−\$0.17) = 0.30 × \$0.83 − 0.70 × \$0.17 = \$0.249 − \$0.119 = +\$0.13`. Positive, for the same reason Maya's call was: **her true probability beats the implied probability baked into the price.**

*The poker table and the options screen are running the exact same arithmetic — break-even probability from the price, true probability from your read, and act only when your read beats the price.*

## The EV and decision-theory interview

SIG's interview loop, drawn in Figure 2, is engineered end to end to test one trait: **clear expected-value thinking under uncertainty, with calibration and composure.** It is not a knowledge test. You cannot cram facts and pass it. Let's walk the stages.

![A seven-stage pipeline of the SIG interview loop: resume screen, mental-math test, EV and probability puzzles, trading games, superday on-site, offer, and the education program, with a loop-back edge from games to puzzles](/imgs/blogs/sig-susquehanna-playbook-poker-game-theory-and-ev-2.png)

**Stage 1 — the resume screen.** Like every top market maker, SIG filters first on background and signal. Strong candidates come from math, computer science, statistics, physics, and engineering, and a **competition signal** — USAMO/IMO, Putnam, ICPC, a strong Kaggle or trading-competition placement — is a powerful tailwind because it directly demonstrates speed and pressure tolerance. A PhD is *not* required for the trading track; SIG hires plenty of strong undergraduates. The screen is selective: top market-maker programs are widely described as admitting low-single-digit percentages of applicants, with thousands competing per seat. Treat the screen as a real filter, not a formality.

**Stage 2 — the timed mental-math test.** This is the gauntlet SIG shares with [Optiver and IMC](/blog/trading/quant-careers/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet): roughly **60–80 questions in about 8 minutes, no calculator, no scratch paper**, a mix of multiplication, division, decimals, fractions, percentages, and negative numbers. The pass bar is commonly reported around **70–85% correct**. It is not testing whether you *can* do arithmetic — it's testing whether you can do it *fast, accurately, and calmly under a ticking clock*, because that is the literal sensation of quoting a market while orders fly. Speed here is trainable: the firms that use this screen reward weeks of focused drilling. The technique lives in the sibling [mental-math interview post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/mental-math-arithmetic-speed-quant-interviews); the point for *this* post is that SIG uses it as a calibrated proxy for desk readiness, not as a hazing ritual.

**Stage 3 — EV and probability puzzles.** Here the loop turns toward decision theory proper: expected-value problems, conditional-probability questions, fair-value and no-arbitrage puzzles, and the classic brain-teasers (dice, coins, urns, gambits). These are not asking for memorized formulas; they're asking whether you can *set up* an EV calculation cleanly, reason about a random process, and arrive at a defensible number. The technique is covered deeply in [the expected-value techniques post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/expected-value-techniques-quant-interviews) and [the decision-making-under-uncertainty post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/decision-making-under-uncertainty-quant-interviews); the meta-skill SIG is scoring is whether you reach for an EV framework *by reflex* when handed an uncertain decision.

**Stage 4 — the trading games.** This is SIG's signature, and where the poker culture becomes literal. The games take several forms: market-making simulations (you must quote a two-sided market on the value of some uncertain quantity — the number of red cards left in a deck, the sum of a pair of dice rolled out of sight, the population of a country — and you trade against the interviewer); dice and betting games; and, yes, sometimes actual poker. What every game shares is that it tests EV thinking, **calibration** (does your quoted range actually contain the truth at the rate you imply?), **Bayesian updating** (do you revise your market sensibly as information arrives?), and **grace under pressure** (do you tilt after a loss?). They are explicitly *not* trivia. We work a market-making round in Worked Example 2.

**Stage 5 — the superday.** The on-site (or virtual on-site) is a sequence of 45–60-minute rounds with different people, mixing more math, more games, deeper probability, and **light behavioral** conversation. The behavioral piece is real and easy to underrate (it's one of our common misconceptions): SIG is choosing people they'll spend a months-long training program and years on a desk with, so they screen for honesty about uncertainty, coachability, and the ability to disagree and update without ego.

**Stage 6 — the offer**, and **Stage 7 — the education program**, which is distinctive enough that it gets its own section below.

Note the **loop-back edge** in Figure 2 from the games stage to the puzzles stage: the interview is genuinely iterative within a superday, and a candidate who stumbles on one game but reasons cleanly afterward can recover. Calibration and composure are scored across the whole loop, not on a single hand.

#### Worked example: a SIG-style market-making game, scored on EV

Maya is asked to make a market on the **sum of two six-sided dice** that the interviewer has rolled but not shown her. The interviewer will then choose to *buy from her at her ask* or *sell to her at her bid*, and any sane interviewer will trade against her whenever her quote is off.

First, the fair value. The expected sum of two dice is `2 × 3.5 = 7`. So Maya's market should be **centered on 7**. Now the width. If she quotes a one-wide market, "6 at 8" (bid 6, offer 8), she is saying she'll buy at 6 or sell at 8. The interviewer, knowing the true expected value is 7, will lift her offer of 8 only if the true sum is *above* 8 and hit her bid of 6 only if it's *below* 6 — but the interviewer also knows the actual roll. The skill SIG is testing: Maya should quote a **tight enough market to look confident, wide enough to protect against being picked off by someone who knows more than she does.** Against an informed counterparty (the interviewer *saw the dice*), a too-tight market is a trap.

Suppose Maya quotes **"6 at 8."** The interviewer rolled an 11. They lift her offer at 8: Maya is now *short* the dice-sum at 8, and it settles at 11, so she loses `11 − 8 = \$3`. Had she quoted wider — "5 at 9" — she'd have sold at 9 and lost only \$2; tighter — "6.5 at 7.5" — she'd have lost \$3.5. The lesson the game teaches in real time: **width is the price of protection against informed flow.** A market maker's spread exists precisely because some of the people trading against her know more than she does, and the spread has to be wide enough that her wins on the uninformed traders cover her losses to the informed ones.

Now score the EV of the *strategy*, not the single outcome. Across all 36 equally likely dice outcomes, the sum is below 6 in 10 cases, between 6 and 8 in 16 cases, and above 8 in 10 cases. With a "6 at 8" market against an interviewer who only trades when it's profitable for them, Maya gets picked off on the 10 low and 10 high outcomes and trades flat on the middle. Widening to "5 at 9" cuts both the frequency and the magnitude of being picked off, at the cost of trading less often. *The right width maximizes EV given that your counterparty is informed* — and articulating that tradeoff out loud is what actually earns the points, far more than the specific number she quotes.

*In a trading game, the interviewer is grading the reasoning, not the roll: a candidate who explains why she widened her market against an informed counterparty has already demonstrated the core market-making instinct.*

## The structured education program: SIG teaches the craft

Here is where SIG genuinely differs from the "throw you in and see if you sink" reputation that clings to parts of this industry. SIG runs one of the most structured **education programs** for new traders in the business, and it is a real part of the value proposition — and of the pitch you should understand as a candidate. Figure 5 lays out the arc.

![A four-stage timeline of the SIG education program for new traders: classroom decision theory in weeks one to four, poker labs in weeks four to eight, trading simulations in weeks eight to sixteen, and a mentored live book from month four onward](/imgs/blogs/sig-susquehanna-playbook-poker-game-theory-and-ev-5.png)

**Classroom decision theory (early weeks).** New hires start with structured instruction in probability, expected value, options theory (what a call and a put are, how they're priced, what "the greeks" mean — delta, gamma, vega, theta), and the firm's house decision framework. This is deliberate: SIG does *not* assume you arrive knowing how to price an option. It assumes you arrive able to *think*, and it teaches the domain knowledge on top. That is the entire reason a PhD is not required — the firm front-loads the finance.

A quick orientation on that options vocabulary, since it's the domain layer SIG teaches and it makes the poker analogy even tighter. A **call** option is the right to buy a stock at a fixed **strike** price; a **put** is the right to sell at a strike. An option's price has a hard part and a soft part. The hard part is arithmetic — given a model, how much should this contract be worth? The soft part is the input that arithmetic can't give you: **implied volatility**, the market's expectation of how much the stock will move. A SIG options market maker is, in large part, *making a market in volatility* — quoting a two-sided price on how turbulent the future will be, exactly as a poker player quotes a willingness to bet on an uncertain outcome. The **greeks** measure how the option's value responds to each input: **delta** to the stock price, **gamma** to the *rate of change* of delta, **vega** to volatility, **theta** to the passage of time. A trader manages a book of thousands of these and keeps the net risk inside limits — which is just inventory management, the poker "position" concept at industrial scale. The reassuring part for a candidate: none of this is on the interview. SIG teaches it here, in the program, because they hired you for the decision engine, not the vocabulary.

**Poker labs (the middle weeks).** The poker that screened you in becomes a teaching tool. New traders study poker *as decision theory under uncertainty*: computing equities, sizing bets to edge, putting opponents on ranges, understanding why a +EV call can still be a bad call if it threatens your stack. The labs make the abstractions concrete in a way a lecture can't, because you feel the variance in your gut, lose hands you "should" have won, and learn viscerally that **process and outcome are different things** — a lesson that is otherwise extremely hard to teach a smart person who is used to being right.

**Trading simulations (scaling up).** Next, the trainees quote simulated markets: they post two-sided prices, get hit and lifted, accumulate inventory, manage the risk of a lopsided book, and watch a simulated P&L move. This is the bridge from games to the desk — same decisions, larger and more realistic, still without firm capital at stake.

**The mentored live book (month four and beyond).** Only after months of this do new traders begin managing a small amount of *real* risk, under a mentor, with daily feedback. The progression is gradual and supervised by design. The firm has invested heavily in training you by this point and wants you to succeed; the brutal "P&L-or-out in your first quarter" caricature does not describe how a structured program like SIG's actually onboards a new trader. (It is still a performance culture — long-run P&L absolutely matters — but the *ramp* is real.)

Why does this matter to you as a candidate? Two reasons. First, it changes what you should optimize for in prep: you do **not** need to arrive an expert in options pricing, because they will teach it; you *do* need to arrive with the raw decision-making engine, because they cannot teach calibration and composure as easily as they can teach the greeks. Second, it should shape your questions in the interview. Asking a thoughtful question about the education program signals that you understand SIG's actual value proposition — the teaching — and that you intend to be coached, which is exactly the temperament they're selecting for.

## The compensation: what SIG actually pays, honestly

Let's talk money, with the honesty the rest of this series insists on. Comp at top market makers is **bonus-driven, highly variable, and survivorship-biased** — and SIG is no exception. The reported numbers describe the people who cleared the filter and are performing; they are not a median over everyone who started, and a strong year is emphatically not a guaranteed year.

For a new-grad trader at a top-tier market maker (the cohort that includes SIG, Jane Street, Optiver, IMC, and the prop firms), reported figures for 2025–2026 cluster as follows (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, H1B base disclosures, the *Young & Calculated* 2026 quant-pay survey): **base salary roughly \$250k–\$375k**, a **sign-on bonus roughly \$50k–\$200k**, and a first-year performance bonus that pushes **on-target first-year total comp to roughly \$450k–\$650k**. For context, Jane Street has publicly quoted an "annualized equivalent of \$300k" base across its entry roles, and Five Rings and Jane Street have led H1B base disclosures around \$300k. SIG sits in this same band as one of the largest options market makers; treat any single number as illustrative of the range, not a quote for a specific seat.

The crucial honesty point — the same one every post in this series hammers — is the **shape** of that comp, not the headline. Base is relatively flat; **the bonus is the lever, and the bonus tracks your P&L contribution and does not repeat automatically.** A trader who has a \$1M year because their book and the market cooperated can have a \$300k year next year if the seat changes or the volatility that fed their edge evaporates. The senior survivor numbers in this industry are real — strong traders 5+ years in can clear seven figures, and the rare outliers reach far higher — but they sit on top of a distribution whose lower tail includes people who washed out, switched firms, or simply landed well below the headline. When someone tells you "everyone at SIG makes \$600k by year five," they are describing the survivors and ignoring the filter.

#### Worked example: Maya's first-year comp, and why year two is not a repeat

Maya gets the SIG offer. Suppose, illustratively and within the reported band, it's a **\$300k base**, a **\$100k sign-on bonus** (often paid partly up front, partly after a year), and a target first-year performance bonus of **\$150k**. Her on-target first-year total is `\$300k + \$100k + \$150k = \$550k`. That is a genuinely large number for a 22-year-old, and it's why these jobs are so sought after.

Now run year two honestly. The **sign-on does not repeat** — that \$100k was a one-time hook. So even if her base rises modestly to \$320k and she earns the *same* \$150k performance bonus, year-two total is `\$320k + \$150k = \$470k` — about **15% lower** than year one, purely because the sign-on is gone. And the performance bonus is not fixed: if her desk has a quiet year, that \$150k could be \$60k, dropping year two toward \$380k; if she's excelling on a hot book, it could be \$300k+, pushing toward \$620k. The variance is the point.

Compare the EV-versus-certainty tradeoff against, say, a big-tech software offer of a steady \$250k that reliably grows. Maya's SIG path has a **higher expected value and far higher variance**. Whether that's the right choice is the same EV-under-variance decision the trader makes on the desk — and it depends on her risk tolerance, not on which headline number is bigger. *The honest way to read a quant offer is as a distribution, not a point: the sign-on inflates year one, and the bonus is a bet on a seat, not a salary.*

## How to prepare with a poker mindset

Preparation for SIG is not memorization; it is *training the decision engine* the firm screens for. Here is the playbook, organized around the poker mindset, and summarized in Figure 7.

![A five-row two-column matrix titled is SIG for you and how to prep, pairing fit signals like enjoying bets on incomplete information and thinking in pot odds with prep actions like drilling EV puzzles, the timed mental-math test, playing small-stakes poker, quoting honest ranges, and reading the Kelly and EV posts](/imgs/blogs/sig-susquehanna-playbook-poker-game-theory-and-ev-7.png)

**Drill mental math to reflex.** The timed screen is the most trainable stage and the most common point of failure. Practice the [arithmetic-speed techniques](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/mental-math-arithmetic-speed-quant-interviews): multiplication shortcuts, fraction-to-decimal conversions, percentage estimation, and — critically — doing it *under a clock* so the time pressure feels normal. Aim to comfortably clear the 70–85% bar in 8 minutes before the screen, not during it.

**Make EV your reflex.** Work through expected-value and probability problems until setting up an EV calculation is automatic. Use [the expected-value techniques post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/expected-value-techniques-quant-interviews) and [the decision-making-under-uncertainty post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/decision-making-under-uncertainty-quant-interviews) as your problem sets. The goal is that when anyone hands you an uncertain situation, your first instinct is to enumerate outcomes, attach probabilities, and compute the weighted average — including the ones where you have to estimate the probabilities yourself.

**Actually play poker — for the math, not the gambling.** Play small-stakes poker (the stakes should be small enough that you genuinely don't care about the money, because the point is the decisions, not the dollars) and study the underlying math: pot odds, equity, ranges, bet sizing, and bankroll management. The value is not becoming a winning player; it is building genuine, gut-level intuition for the exact decision structure SIG screens for. A candidate who can talk fluently about why a particular call was +EV but still a mistake given stack sizes has a real edge in the games round.

**Practice calibrated honesty.** When you quote a range — in a game or in life — make it a range you'd actually trade on. Interviewers test calibration by pushing: "you said 30%, would you bet on that?" The honest, calibrated answer ("yes, at those odds I'd take that bet; here's my reasoning, and here's what would change my mind") beats false confidence and beats hedging-everything timidity. Practice stating numbers you can defend and updating them gracefully when challenged.

**Internalize sizing and risk of ruin.** This is the deepest piece and the thing that separates a candidate who *gets it* from one who has merely memorized "+EV good." Read [the Kelly criterion post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews) and learn, cold, why bet *size* matters as much as bet *edge* — why even a positive-EV strategy bet too large goes bankrupt, and how Kelly sizing maximizes long-run growth. Worked Examples 3 and 4 below are your drills.

#### Worked example: Kelly sizing on a real edge

Maya has a genuine edge: a repeatable bet that wins **60% of the time at even money** (win \$1 for every \$1 risked, lose the \$1 otherwise). Her per-dollar EV is `0.60 × (+\$1) + 0.40 × (−\$1) = +\$0.20` — a strong 20-cent edge per dollar. The question is *how much of her bankroll* to put on each bet to grow her money fastest over many repetitions.

The **Kelly criterion** gives the growth-optimal fraction. For an even-money bet (payout odds `b = 1`) with win probability `p = 0.60` and loss probability `q = 0.40`, the Kelly fraction is `f* = p − q/b = 0.60 − 0.40/1 = 0.20`. So Maya should bet **20% of her current bankroll** on each repetition. Not more — and this is the counterintuitive part that SIG's poker labs drill in. Figure 3 plots why.

![A chart with two curves over bet-size fraction: expected log-growth per bet peaking at the 20 percent full-Kelly point then falling, and an illustrative probability-of-ruin curve climbing steeply as bet size exceeds Kelly](/imgs/blogs/sig-susquehanna-playbook-poker-game-theory-and-ev-3.png)

Figure 3 shows the long-run growth rate (the quantity Kelly maximizes — the expected logarithm of your wealth per bet) as a function of how large a fraction you bet. It rises, peaks exactly at the 20% full-Kelly point, and then **falls** as you bet more — even though the per-bet *dollar* EV keeps rising with size. Meanwhile the illustrative probability-of-ruin curve climbs steeply past Kelly. The two facts together are the whole lesson: **past the Kelly fraction, betting bigger makes your money grow *slower* in the long run while making catastrophe *more* likely.** More size is not more edge; it's more variance for less growth.

If Maya has a \$100,000 bankroll, full Kelly says bet \$20,000 per round. Many experienced players and desks deliberately bet **half-Kelly** (here, \$10,000) because it sacrifices only a little long-run growth for a large reduction in the gut-wrenching swings — a tradeoff worth understanding cold before you walk into the games round. *Kelly turns "I have an edge" into "here is exactly how much to bet on it" — and its most important output is the ceiling, not the floor: it tells you when you are betting too much.*

#### Worked example: when the higher-EV play is the wrong play

Now the example that, more than any other, is what SIG is checking you understand. Maya is offered a one-time bet: **wager any fraction of her \$100,000 bankroll; with probability 0.60 it triples (she gets 3× her stake back), with probability 0.40 she loses the stake.** The per-dollar EV is `0.60 × (+\$2) + 0.40 × (−\$1) = \$1.20 − \$0.40 = +\$0.80` per dollar — a *massive* +80% edge. The naive "take the +EV bet, and bet big because the edge is huge" instinct screams: **bet everything.**

Watch what "bet everything" does. If Maya wagers the full \$100,000: 60% of the time she ends with \$300,000, but **40% of the time she ends with \$0 — broke, out of the game, with no bankroll to ever exploit her edge again.** A 40% chance of total ruin is not a risk a market maker takes, no matter how positive the EV, because *ruin is absorbing*: once you're at zero, every future +EV opportunity is worthless to you. This is exactly the **risk of ruin** versus expected value tension, and it's why the kit's punchline is "a +EV bet is not automatically the right bet."

What *should* she bet? Kelly again. For this bet, the growth-optimal fraction is well under half her bankroll, and even that is aggressive given the chunky downside; a disciplined trader sizes it so that a loss is a setback, not an extinction. Figure 6 makes the point with simulated paths.

![A chart of two simulated bankroll paths on a log scale over two hundred repeated positive-EV bets: a green Kelly-sized path that compounds upward and a red over-bet path that spikes then collapses on a normal losing streak](/imgs/blogs/sig-susquehanna-playbook-poker-game-theory-and-ev-6.png)

Figure 6 runs the *same* positive edge and the *same* sequence of outcomes through two strategies: a Kelly-sized bettor (green, betting the growth-optimal fraction each round) and an over-bettor (red, betting more than double Kelly to chase the headline EV). Same edge, same luck — and the green line compounds steadily upward while the red line spikes early, then a perfectly ordinary losing streak craters it toward zero. **The over-bettor and the Kelly bettor had identical EV per bet. Only the sizing differed, and the sizing decided who survived.** That is the single most important thing SIG is checking you understand, and it is the deepest gift the poker table gives a trader.

*The highest-EV play is wrong whenever it risks ruin, because ruin ends the only game that matters — the long one. SIG would rather hire the candidate who folds the +EV bet that could bust them than the one who shoves every edge.*

## Common misconceptions

**"SIG is just gambling — it's a casino with extra steps."** This is exactly backwards, and the confusion is the whole reason SIG uses poker as a teaching tool rather than, say, roulette. A casino sells **negative-EV** bets to people who don't size them. SIG is the *house* in the sense that matters — it trades only when it has a positive-EV edge (the spread, plus its read), and it sizes positions so variance can't kill it. Poker is the perfect training precisely *because* it is the one "gambling" game where a disciplined, mathematical player has a genuine long-run edge over the table, won through EV thinking and bankroll management rather than luck. The skill SIG teaches is the literal opposite of gambling: it's how to *be* the disciplined edge, not the sucker funding it.

**"You have to be a poker pro to get hired."** No. SIG wants the *thinking* poker trains, not a tournament résumé. Plenty of successful SIG traders were mediocre poker players who understood the decision theory cold. Showing up able to compute pot odds, reason about ranges, and articulate why a +EV call can still be a mistake matters far more than your lifetime poker winnings. The firm teaches the rest in its education program. (Conversely, a strong poker background with *no* ability to explain the underlying math is not the signal they want, either.)

**"+EV always means take the bet."** This is the single most dangerous misconception, and Worked Example 4 exists to kill it. A positive-EV bet sized too large will bankrupt you with near-certainty over enough repetitions — the higher-EV play is the *wrong* play whenever it carries a real risk of ruin. Sizing is not a footnote to edge; it is co-equal with edge. A candidate who says "it's +EV, so I take it" without asking "how much, given my bankroll?" has failed the deepest test in the loop. The right reflex is always two questions: *do I have an edge*, and *how much can I bet on it without risking the game itself*.

**"The behavioral round doesn't matter — it's all just math."** It matters more than candidates expect. SIG is choosing people to put through a months-long education program and to trust with real capital on a collaborative desk for years. They screen for honesty about uncertainty (do you state calibrated ranges or bluff confidence?), coachability (will you take instruction in the program?), and emotional regulation (do you tilt after a loss?). A candidate who is brilliant at the math but defensive, ego-driven, or unable to say "I don't know, but here's my best estimate and my confidence in it" is a real risk on a trading floor, and the behavioral round is where that shows up.

**"A PhD or a finance degree is required."** Neither. The trading track hires heavily from strong undergraduates in math, CS, stats, physics, and engineering, and it teaches the finance (options pricing, the greeks, the firm's frameworks) in the education program. A competition background (Putnam, IMO, ICPC, Kaggle) is a far stronger signal than a finance degree, because it demonstrates the speed and pressure tolerance the job actually requires. Don't self-select out because you "don't know finance" — that's the part they expect to teach.

## How it plays out in the real world

Let's make it concrete with a realistic composite arc, grounded in the reported facts.

Maya applies to SIG in the fall of her senior year, targeting the **internship** first — because at top market makers the internship is the real interview and most full-time seats come from intern conversion, a pattern this whole series emphasizes. She clears the resume screen on her math major plus a strong Putnam showing. She drills the mental-math screen for three weeks and clears the ~75% bar comfortably. The probability and EV puzzles go well because she's made EV-setup a reflex. In the trading games she quotes a market on a hidden quantity, gets picked off once, *visibly updates and widens her market while explaining why* — and that composure-plus-reasoning, more than any single answer, is what gets her to the superday. The behavioral conversation is relaxed; she asks a genuine question about the education program. She gets an intern offer.

The summer internship pays at the top-market-maker intern rate (the cohort that includes Jane Street interns at roughly an annualized \$300k, and IMC interns up to around \$5,800/week with conversion packages reported near \$425k total comp — SIG sits in this competitive band). It *is* the interview: she spends the summer in a structured program, quoting simulated markets, and converts to a full-time return offer. Her first-year full-time comp lands in the reported band — base in the low-to-mid \$300k range, a sign-on, and a target bonus that puts her on-target first-year total in the \$450k–\$650k range, with all the conditionality of Worked Example 2.

Then the real arc begins, and it is *not* the headline curve. She spends months in the education program before touching meaningful risk. Her first year on a live book is humbling: she learns that being right on the math and wrong on the sizing loses money, that a quiet-volatility quarter can flatten even a good trader's P&L, and that the variance she studied in poker labs feels very different when it's the firm's capital and her bonus. The ones who thrive are the ones who internalized the deepest lesson early — **survive first, optimize EV second** — and who treat each year's bonus as a draw from a distribution, not a salary they're owed.

A few honest cautions that the headline numbers hide. The job is genuinely high-variance in comp, and the lower tail is real: not everyone clears the bar, seats change, and the volatility that feeds an edge can dry up for a year. The skills, while elite, are somewhat firm- and product-specific and don't always port cleanly elsewhere. And the selection is brutal at the front: thousands apply per seat, and the acceptance rate at top market-maker programs is widely described as low single digits or below. SIG's structured program and teaching culture make the *ramp* more humane than the caricature, but the *filter* is as sharp as anywhere in the industry. Go in with the EV-under-variance frame — on the application as much as on the desk — and you'll read the whole thing correctly.
