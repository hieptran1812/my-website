> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — At senior level, communication is not a soft add-on you tolerate around the real work; it is the *mechanism* by which your correct work turns into capital, headcount, and adoption. A brilliant analysis nobody can follow, trust, or act on is worth almost nothing — and the senior quant is precisely the person who converts a true result into a decision the firm actually makes.
>
> - **Correct-but-unadopted earns nothing; correct-and-adopted earns everything.** The gap between those two is communication: leading with the result, stating limitations before the room finds them, separating evidence from advocacy, and being someone a skeptical room can trust to do all three. The work does *not* speak for itself.
> - **Defending an idea well is mostly about conceding, not winning.** The senior who names the three real holes in their own proposal *before* the skeptical PM does buys the trust that lets the rest of the argument land. You separate "here is what I actually know" from "here is what I hope," and you let the idea win or lose on the merits.
> - **Mentoring is multiplication, not charity.** A senior who spends roughly **8 hours a week** ramping two juniors unlocks more finished output than the same 8 hours spent working alone — and the leverage compounds as the juniors ramp. The math is in this post.
> - **The one idea to remember:** reputation is the product of *being right × being clear × being trusted*, and a product is killed by any one factor going to zero. Being right but unreadable, or clear but untrustworthy, multiplies to nothing. Get all three and influence **compounds** like capital — roughly a 30x gap over a decade in the illustrative model below.

Maya — the same Maya from across this series, now five years into a trading seat and starting to be handed real responsibility — once watched a signal die in a room. It was not her signal. It belonged to a junior researcher on an adjacent desk, and it was, as far as anyone could later tell, *correct*. The factor was real, the out-of-sample work was honest, the edge would probably have made money. But the researcher could not get it across the line in the allocation meeting. He opened by walking the room through the eleven feature variants he had tried, in the order he had tried them. He buried the one number that mattered — the out-of-sample Sharpe — in a table on the ninth slide. When the portfolio manager asked the obvious skeptical question ("is this just the post-2020 regime?"), he got defensive and oversold, insisting the edge was robust when he plainly had not tested that regime. The PM, who had seen a hundred over-eager juniors, quietly stopped trusting the rest of the presentation. The idea was tabled "to revisit later." It never came back. A correct signal earned the firm exactly zero dollars because the person who found it could not turn correct work into an adopted decision.

That is the subject of this post, and it is the senior skill that almost nobody screens for and almost everybody underrates. The spine of this series has been that the job is a probabilistic edge — and so is getting it. Here is the senior expression of that idea: **a correct result has positive expected value only if it gets acted on, and communication is the conversion rate from correctness to action.** A true signal that dies in committee has the same P&L as a false one. The senior quant is the person whose conversion rate is high — whose write-ups get read and believed, whose proposals win capital, whose juniors get better because of them, and whose name on a memo makes the room lean in rather than brace for a sales pitch.

![Two paths for the same correct signal: on the left it is buried, overclaimed, and dies in committee for zero capital; on the right the result leads, objections are pre-answered, and it wins capital, headcount, and adoption](/imgs/blogs/communication-and-influence-writing-presenting-mentoring-1.png)

This is not about being a slick presenter, and it is emphatically not about spin. The opposite, in fact: the most persuasive thing a senior quant does is concede the weaknesses of their own idea out loud, because in an environment that settles every argument with money, *trust is the scarce resource* and honesty is how you earn it. This post covers the four places communication becomes leverage — the research write-up, defending an idea to a skeptical room, mentoring juniors as multiplication, and building a reputation that compounds — and it grounds each in the kind of concrete numbers this series runs on. By the end you should see why, at senior level, the sentence "but the work is correct" is the *beginning* of the job, not the end of it.

## Foundations: why communication is leverage

Before the techniques, a few ideas need to be defined cleanly from zero, because the whole argument rests on them and they are constantly muddled — especially by smart technical people who have spent their lives being rewarded purely for getting the right answer.

**Correct vs adopted.** A piece of work is *correct* if its conclusion is true: the signal really does have an edge, the risk model really does capture the tail, the trade really is mispriced. A piece of work is *adopted* if the firm actually acts on it: capital gets allocated, the system gets built, the process gets changed. These are different properties, and — this is the load-bearing point — **only adopted work has value.** A correct-but-unadopted result is, from the firm's profit-and-loss statement, indistinguishable from no work at all. It cost the salary and the months; it returned nothing. The conversion from correct to adopted runs entirely through other people's heads, and the only tool you have for changing what is in someone else's head is communication.

**Leverage.** In this series we have used *leverage* in the trading sense — borrowing to size a position larger than your capital. Here it means the more general thing: a multiplier on your own effort. A junior's output is roughly their own hours times their own skill — they *add*. A senior's output is their work *times* how many other people act on it and how many other people they make better — they *multiply*. Communication is the lever. The same eight hours of analysis, communicated so a room can act on it, can move a hundred million dollars of capital; communicated badly, it moves nothing. The lever is the difference, and at senior level the lever is most of the job.

**Trust as the multiplier.** Here is the piece that technical people resist hardest, because it feels unfair. In a room deciding whether to risk the firm's capital on your idea, the decision is not made on the merits of the idea alone — it is made on the merits of the idea *as filtered through how much the room trusts you to have done the work honestly.* Nobody in an allocation meeting can re-derive your backtest live. They are taking your word for a great deal of it. So the question they are actually answering is not "is this signal real?" but "do I believe this person when they tell me this signal is real, and do I believe they have told me the holes?" Trust is the multiplier on every claim you make. A trusted person saying "Sharpe 1.1, here are the three weaknesses" gets a pilot allocation. A distrusted person saying "Sharpe 2.5, it's bulletproof" gets shown the door — and *should*, because the second person is either fooling themselves or fooling you, and a desk cannot afford either. We covered the discipline of not fooling yourself in [intellectual honesty and killing your own ideas](/blog/trading/quant-careers/intellectual-honesty-and-killing-your-own-ideas); this post is about the other half — how you communicate that honesty so that other people can rely on it.

**Signal vs advocacy.** A claim can carry two very different things: *evidence* (what the data actually shows) and *advocacy* (what you want the room to do about it). Junior communication fuses them — every sentence is simultaneously a fact and a sales pitch, so the listener can never tell where the evidence stops and the wanting begins. Senior communication separates them ruthlessly: "Here is what I measured. Here is what I do not know. Here is what I therefore recommend, and here is the bar at which I would change my mind." A reader who can separate your evidence from your advocacy can *trust your evidence*, because they can see you are not bending it to serve the recommendation. This separation is the single most reusable move in everything that follows.

**Why a desk amplifies all of this.** In most jobs, communication failures are slow and forgiving. A muddled memo gets a polite "thanks, we'll consider it" and the cost is diffuse. On a desk the feedback is fast and denominated in dollars and in *attention*, which is the genuinely scarce resource. Senior people are time-starved; a portfolio manager might give your idea four minutes before deciding whether it is worth forty. If your first thirty seconds do not land the result, you have spent your budget reconstructing context and the idea dies of attention starvation — not because it was wrong, but because nobody got to the part where it was right. The market punishes correct-but-unadopted work exactly as hard as it punishes incorrect work, and that symmetry is what turns communication from a nicety into an edge. We return to the firm-by-firm reality of this in the section on how it plays out; first, the four skills.

## The research write-up: lead with the result, then earn the depth

The research write-up — a memo, a slide deck, a pull-request description for a signal, a take-home case — is where most quants do most of their communicating, and it is where the biggest, cheapest gains are available. The default a technical person reaches for is the *chronological* write-up: I did this, then I tried that, then I found this problem, then I fixed it, and here, at the end, is the conclusion. That structure is natural because it mirrors how you actually did the work. It is also exactly backwards for the reader, who does not care how you spent your week — they care what they should *do*, and they want to know in the first ten seconds whether this is worth the next forty minutes.

![A five-layer stack for a research write-up: the result and the call on top, then evidence, then method, then limitations, then the explicit recommendation, so a busy reader can act in seconds or dig as deep as they want](/imgs/blogs/communication-and-influence-writing-presenting-mentoring-2.png)

The senior structure inverts the chronology. **Lead with the result and the recommendation.** The first sentence states the call and the one number that supports it: "I recommend a small live pilot of a 5-day equity reversal signal; it posts an out-of-sample Sharpe of 1.1 net of 5 basis points round-trip cost." Everything else in the document exists to let a reader who wants to go deeper *verify* that sentence, in descending order of how many readers will reach each layer. Below the result sits the *evidence* — the two or three plots that would actually change a skeptic's mind (the OOS equity curve, the information coefficient, the turnover), not the eleven plots you happened to generate. Below that, the *method* — the data range, the train/test split, how costs were applied — in enough detail that a colleague could reproduce it, because reproducibility is what separates a result from a rumor. Below that, the *limitations*, stated by you, in writing: where the edge breaks, which regimes you did not test, why you would not size it large yet. And at the bottom, the *explicit recommendation*: the ask, the size, the review date, and the bar at which you would kill it.

The genius of this structure is that it serves every reader simultaneously. The PM who has four minutes reads the first sentence and the recommendation and is done. The skeptic reads down into the evidence and the limitations. The colleague who has to maintain the signal reads the method. Nobody is forced to read the whole thing to extract the part they need, and — crucially — the part they need is *findable*, because the document is organized by importance-to-the-reader, not by chronology-of-the-author. This same discipline is what the research case in a quant interview is testing; we cover the interview version in [the research case and take-home: how to ace it](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-research-case-and-take-home-how-to-ace-it), and the production version in [the research write-up and killing ideas](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/quant-research-writeup-killing-ideas).

There is a deeper reason to lead with the result, and it has to do with trust. When you bury the conclusion and walk the reader through your process first, you are implicitly asking them to trust that the journey ends somewhere good — and a skeptical reader will not extend that credit. They will assume you buried the result because the result is weak. (Often they are right; juniors bury weak results by reflex.) Leading with the number signals confidence and respect for the reader's time, and it lets the reader spend their skepticism where it belongs — on the evidence — rather than on the suspense.

#### Worked example: a rambling summary rewritten result-first

Here is the before, drawn almost verbatim from the kind of summary the junior in the opening gave Maya's room. I have compressed it, but the shape is faithful:

> "Over the past three weeks I explored a number of cross-sectional equity signals. I started with a momentum factor, which looked promising in 2019 but degraded, so I moved to a mean-reversion construction. I tried several lookback windows — 3-day, 5-day, 10-day — and several universes. The 5-day version on the liquid large-cap universe was the most interesting. I also experimented with volatility scaling and with a sector-neutral version. After accounting for transaction costs, which I estimated at various levels, the results were generally encouraging, and I think there may be something tradeable here, though more work is needed."

A busy reader gets to the end of that and knows almost nothing actionable. What is the number? Is it net of costs? What is being recommended? How big? The reader has to *reconstruct* the point from a chronology, and most readers will not bother — they will skim, miss the (buried, hedged) conclusion, and move on. Worse, the hedging ("generally encouraging," "may be something," "more work is needed") fuses evidence and advocacy into a fog where the reader cannot tell what is actually known.

![A rambling chronological summary on the left, where the reader must reconstruct the conclusion, versus a result-first summary on the right that states the call and the number in sentence one so the reader can act in ten seconds](/imgs/blogs/communication-and-influence-writing-presenting-mentoring-3.png)

Now the after — same work, same honesty, inverted structure:

> "**Recommendation: pilot a 5-day cross-sectional reversal signal at 5% of book risk, review in one quarter.** It posts an out-of-sample Sharpe of **1.1**, net of 5 bps round-trip cost, on the held-out 2023–2025 window I never fit on. **The two weaknesses I would size around:** the edge concentrates in the highest-volatility decile, and I have not tested it through a sharp liquidity-crunch regime — there was none in the hold-out — so I would cut the pilot if realized volatility spikes past the 90th percentile. Method, the full feature search (I tried 9 variants; this haircuts the Sharpe — see the deflation note), and reproduce steps are below."

Notice what changed and what did not. The *work* is identical; nothing was added or removed from the analysis. But now the reader knows the call, the number, the size, the two real holes, and the kill bar in the first four sentences. The nine variants are disclosed (honesty), but as a *method footnote with a deflation caveat*, not as the headline narrative. The hedging fog is gone, replaced by a clean separation: here is what I measured (Sharpe 1.1), here is what I do not know (the liquidity-crunch regime), here is what I therefore recommend (a small, kill-barred pilot). A skeptical PM can act on the after in ten seconds and dig into the evidence if they want to; the before would have been tabled.

*The single highest-return edit you can make to any research write-up is to delete the chronology, find the one sentence that states the call and the number, and move it to the top — the same work, reordered for the reader instead of the author, is the difference between adopted and ignored.*

A few more write-up disciplines worth naming, because they recur:

**Be your own harshest critic on paper.** The limitations section is not a confession you are dragged into; it is your single most credibility-building move, and you should write it *first*, before the rosy parts. A reader who sees that you have already found the three holes in your own idea stops looking for the ones you are hiding — because the evidence in front of them says you do not hide holes. A write-up with no limitations section reads as either naive (you did not look) or dishonest (you looked and buried), and a senior reviewer assumes the worse of the two. Stating "here is where this breaks" up front is how you spend a little credibility to buy a lot.

**One number per claim, and the number is real.** Vague intensifiers ("strong," "robust," "significant") are advocacy wearing the costume of evidence. Replace each with the quantity it is standing in for: not "robust out of sample" but "Sharpe 1.1 OOS vs 1.4 in-sample, a 21% haircut." A number can be checked, argued with, and trusted; an adjective can only be believed or disbelieved, and a skeptic disbelieves.

**Write for the smartest reader on a different team.** The right audience model is someone brilliant but unfamiliar with your corner — they will catch a logical gap instantly but will not know your desk's shorthand. So you define your terms on first use and you skip the in-group jargon, not because the reader is slow but because jargon is a tax on adoption: every undefined term is a place the reader either stops to ask or quietly stops trusting. This is the same standard we hold this whole series to.

## Defending an idea to a skeptical room

The write-up is asynchronous; defending an idea is live, and live is harder because you cannot edit, the room can interrupt, and your own ego is in the chair with you. This is the allocation meeting, the research review, the risk committee — the moment where a correct idea either wins capital or dies. The instinct of a junior is to treat it as a debate to be won: marshal every argument, deflect every objection, never concede. That instinct is exactly wrong, and watching it fail is what taught Maya the senior version.

![A six-step pipeline for defending an idea: anticipate the objections, concede the weak parts first, present only the decision-changing evidence, separate signal from advocacy, recommend the explicit action, and win or lose on the merits, with a loop edge feeding a fair objection back into a stronger idea](/imgs/blogs/communication-and-influence-writing-presenting-mentoring-4.png)

The senior approach has a shape, and the shape is the figure above. **Anticipate the objections.** Before the meeting, you sit down and write the three questions a skeptic *will* ask — the regime you did not test, the cost assumption that flatters you, the crowdedness of the trade — and you have an honest answer ready for each. Most of the value is in the writing-them-down, because half of them turn out to be objections you cannot yet answer, which tells you what to go test before the meeting rather than getting caught flat in it. **Concede the weak parts first.** This is the counterintuitive core. You open by naming the real holes yourself: "Two things to flag before I make the case — I haven't tested this through a liquidity crunch, and the edge concentrates in high-vol names." Conceding first does three things at once: it disarms the skeptic (they cannot land a blow you have already landed on yourself), it demonstrates that you have done the adversarial work, and it earns the trust that makes the rest of your evidence credible. The room stops auditing you for hidden weaknesses and starts evaluating the actual idea.

**Present only the decision-changing evidence.** A skeptical room has limited attention; spending it on the eleven plots that confirm what nobody disputed is a waste. You show the two or three that a skeptic *would* demand — the OOS curve, the cost-sensitivity, the regime breakdown — and you stop. **Separate signal from advocacy explicitly.** Out loud, you mark the boundary: "Here is what the data shows. Here is what I'm extrapolating. Here is what I genuinely don't know." A room can act on evidence it can see you are not inflating. **Recommend the explicit action,** with a size and a kill bar, so the decision is concrete rather than "interesting, let's think about it" (the phrase where ideas go to die). And then — the hardest part — **let it win or lose on the merits.** If the room kills it for a reason you cannot answer, that is the system working; you keep the trust, which is worth more than this one allocation, and the loop edge in the figure carries the fair objection back into a stronger version of the idea. The person who fights a losing position to the death loses the position *and* the trust; the person who says "that's a fair point, let me go test it and come back" keeps the trust and usually comes back stronger.

This is the live-room application of the [intellectual honesty](/blog/trading/quant-careers/intellectual-honesty-and-killing-your-own-ideas) discipline: killing your own idea privately is the research skill, and being visibly willing to let the room kill it is the *communication* skill that makes your surviving ideas believable.

#### Worked example: anticipating the three objections to a proposal

Wei — the CS-PhD from across this series, now a senior researcher — is about to propose that his pod allocate to a new options-flow signal. Before the meeting, he does the senior thing: he writes the three objections he is certain to face, and grades his own answer to each.

**Objection 1: "Is this just the 2023–2024 regime?"** Wei's honest answer: *partially.* He has 2021–2025 data, and the edge is stronger in the latter half. His pre-answer: "It's present across the full sample at a Sharpe of 0.8, and stronger recently at 1.3 — I'm recommending we size to the *full-sample* number, 0.8, not the flattering recent one, precisely because I can't rule out that the recent strength is regime." He has turned a weakness into evidence of his honesty, and pre-committed to the conservative number.

**Objection 2: "What's the capacity? Will it decay when we size up?"** Wei's honest answer: *limited and uncertain.* The signal trades names with modest liquidity. His pre-answer: "Capacity is maybe \$50M before market impact eats half the edge — I've modeled impact at 2x our usual cost assumption and the Sharpe holds at 0.6, so I'm proposing we cap the pilot at \$20M, well inside that, and re-measure impact live before scaling." He has named the constraint and pre-sized inside it.

**Objection 3: "How crowded is this? Are other pods already in it?"** Wei's honest answer: *unknown, and that's the real risk.* His pre-answer: "I can't observe other funds' books, so I can't rule out crowding — that's the one I'm least able to defend. My mitigation is the small pilot size and a tight kill bar: if the realized edge comes in below half the backtest in the first month, that's my signal that it's crowded and I cut it." He has openly conceded the objection he cannot fully answer, and attached a concrete plan to it.

Walk into the meeting having done this, and three things happen. First, when the PM raises objection 1, Wei has already raised it himself and pre-committed to the conservative size — the PM's skepticism has nowhere to land. Second, the *quality* of having an honest, pre-sized answer to all three signals that Wei has done the adversarial work, which is exactly the trust that gets the allocation. Third, on objection 3 — the one he cannot fully answer — his openly conceding it is *more* persuasive than a confident dodge would have been, because it tells the room he is not hiding the other risks either. The proposal that openly carries its three weaknesses gets the pilot; the proposal that pretends to have none gets the audit.

*Anticipating objections is not about having a slick answer for everything — it is about discovering, before the room does, which of your weaknesses you genuinely cannot defend, conceding those openly, and pre-sizing the idea so that the weaknesses you cannot rule out cannot ruin you.*

#### Worked example: Wei defends a strategy to a skeptical PM and wins the allocation

Now the live version, played out. Wei is in the room. The PM — call her Dana, fifteen years on the buy side, professionally allergic to over-eager researchers — has four minutes of attention to give and has killed nine of the last ten ideas brought to her.

Wei opens, not with his method, but with the call and the concession: "I'm recommending a \$20M pilot on an options-flow reversal signal, full-sample Sharpe 0.8 net of costs. Before I make the case, two flags: I can't rule out that its recent strength is regime, so I've sized to the full-sample number not the recent one; and I can't observe crowding, which is the risk I can least defend, so the pilot is small with a tight kill bar."

Dana, who was leaning back, leans forward. In thirty seconds Wei has done something the last nine researchers did not: he has told her the number, the ask, and the two things *she* was going to attack — before she attacked them. There is nothing for her skepticism to do but evaluate the actual idea. She asks objection 2, the capacity question. Wei has the pre-answer ready: impact modeled at 2x, Sharpe holds at 0.6, pilot capped well inside capacity. She pushes on the cost assumption; Wei shows the one plot that matters — Sharpe vs assumed cost — and notes the edge survives to 3x costs. He does not show the other eight plots. She asks the regime question again, harder; Wei does not get defensive, he says "that's exactly why I'm using the 0.8 and not the 1.3 — if you think even 0.8 is regime-contaminated, the honest move is a smaller pilot, and I'd take a \$10M version happily." He has just *offered to make his own ask smaller* in response to a fair objection, which is the single most trust-building thing he could do.

Dana approves the pilot — at \$15M, splitting the difference, with the kill bar Wei proposed. Here is the part that matters for the rest of Wei's career: the allocation is the smaller prize. The larger prize is that Dana now trusts Wei. The next idea he brings, she gives six minutes instead of four, and starts from "Wei doesn't oversell," which is worth more than any single pilot. Compare the counterfactual: had Wei walked in selling a Sharpe of 1.3 as bulletproof and dodging the crowding question, Dana would have killed it in two minutes *and* discounted his next three ideas. The way he won was by being willing to lose honestly — and the trust he banked compounds, which is the subject of the last section.

*You do not defend an idea by being unbeatable; you defend it by being so visibly honest about its weaknesses that the room trusts your account of its strengths — and the trust you bank in a single honest defense is worth more than the allocation you are defending.*

## Mentoring as multiplication

Everything so far has been about converting *your* correct work into adopted decisions. Mentoring is the move that breaks the one-person ceiling entirely: instead of communicating your conclusions, you communicate your *judgment*, so that other people produce correct, adopted work without you in the loop. This is where a senior stops adding and starts multiplying, and it is the most undervalued lever in the building, because its payoff is delayed and diffuse while its cost is immediate and concrete.

The objection technical people raise — and it is the third "misconception" we tackle below — is that mentoring is a distraction from real work. The hours you spend reviewing a junior's backtest or explaining why their feature is leaking are hours you are not spending on your own P&L. For a junior, that arithmetic is correct: their leverage is low, so every hour given away is an hour lost. For a senior, the arithmetic flips, and the reason is the multiplication math.

![A chart comparing team output against hours per week spent mentoring: working alone, mentoring hours are pure loss and output falls; with mentees, each ramped junior adds output so eight hours of teaching unlocks far more than eight hours spent solo](/imgs/blogs/communication-and-influence-writing-presenting-mentoring-5.png)

#### Worked example: the multiplication math of mentoring

Take a senior researcher with a fixed budget of roughly **40 productive hours a week** — that is the real constraint; you cannot manufacture more, and the people who try, burn out. Measure output in a deliberately rough unit: *finished, reviewable analyses per week*, illustrative, call it a "unit."

**Working entirely alone**, every one of those 40 hours produces output at 1 unit per hour, and that is the ceiling: 40 units a week, full stop, forever, because there is exactly one of you. Any hour spent not-working-alone — say, teaching — is pure loss on this curve: spend 8 hours mentoring and you are down to 32 units of your own output. That is the amber line in the figure, sloping *down*, and it is the line a junior correctly lives on.

**Now spend 8 of the 40 hours mentoring two juniors.** Ramping and sustaining one junior costs roughly 4 hours a week of your time — code review, a weekly one-on-one, unblocking them when they are stuck. So 8 hours supports about 2 juniors. Your own output drops to 32 units (the hours you gave away are real). But each *ramped* junior, once they are productive, contributes roughly 6 units a week of their own finished work — work that genuinely lands, because your review made it land. Two juniors at 6 units is 12 units. So total team output is `32 + 12 = 44 units`, versus the **32** you would have produced if you had hoarded those same 8 hours for solo work. The 8 hours of teaching did not cost you 8 units; it *net* gained you 12, because it was levered across two other people's full-time output instead of spent on your own marginal hour.

The curve keeps climbing as you add mentees, with diminishing returns — eventually your review capacity saturates and a tenth mentee gets sloppy review that produces leaking backtests, which is worse than none. The sweet spot in the illustrative model is around 8–12 hours a week, two or three juniors, where you are still giving each enough attention to make their output trustworthy. Past that you are a bottleneck, not a multiplier. But the shape is the entire point: **for someone with a senior's leverage, hours invested in ramping juniors return more total output than the same hours spent working alone** — and that is before you count the compounding, where this year's mentee becomes next year's mentor.

*Mentoring is not charity subtracted from your real work; for anyone with enough leverage to review others credibly, it is the single highest-return use of a marginal hour, because it is the only use that multiplies across other people's full-time output instead of adding to your own.*

There is a craft to the mentoring itself, and it is — unsurprisingly for this post — mostly about communication. **The feedback that lands** is specific, attached to the work, and separates the error from the person. "This backtest leaks: you computed the volatility scaling using the full sample including the test period, so the test isn't really out of sample — re-run it with the scaling fit only on the training window and let's see if the Sharpe survives" is feedback a junior can act on and learn from. "This isn't rigorous enough" is feedback that only produces anxiety. The first names the exact error, explains why it matters, and gives the fix; the second is a vague verdict that teaches nothing. The difference is the same result-first, evidence-not-advocacy discipline from the write-up section, applied to a person.

**Teach the judgment, not the answer.** The junior who is handed the fixed backtest learns nothing; the junior who is asked "what would make you doubt this result?" and walked through how a senior would attack it learns the move and uses it next time without you. This is the difference between giving someone a corrected analysis and giving them the *kill-your-own-idea reflex* — and the second is what actually multiplies, because it removes you from the loop. The best mentoring makes itself unnecessary. A junior you have to re-correct on the same error every week is a junior you are *adding* to; a junior who has internalized the reflex and now catches it themselves — and catches it in *their* juniors — is a junior you have *multiplied* through.

**Protect their reputation while it forms.** A senior with the trust we have been discussing can spend a little of it sponsoring a junior — putting a junior's good result in front of the room with "this is solid work, I've reviewed it" — which both gets the junior's work adopted and teaches the junior, by example, what a credible presentation looks like. This is the link between mentoring and reputation: your trust is a resource you can *lend* to people whose work deserves it, and doing so both grows them and signals to the room that your judgment about people is as reliable as your judgment about signals.

## Reputation that compounds

The final form of communication leverage is the slowest and the largest: reputation. Everything above — the clear write-up, the honest defense, the feedback that lands — is a single transaction. Reputation is the *integral* of those transactions over a career, and it has a property that makes it the most valuable asset a senior owns: it **compounds**.

![A chart of an influence index over ten years: being right plus clear plus trusted compounds at roughly 30 percent a year to about 18x, while being merely right without clarity or trust stays nearly flat, and the widening gap is labeled as the whole career](/imgs/blogs/communication-and-influence-writing-presenting-mentoring-6.png)

Here is the model, and it is worth stating as something close to an equation because it makes the failure modes obvious. Your influence — your ability to get your ideas acted on — is a *product*:

`influence ≈ being right × being clear × being trusted`

It is a product, not a sum, and that is the whole insight. A sum is forgiving: if one term is weak, the others compensate. A product is brutal: if any single term goes to zero, the whole thing is zero. Being *right* but unreadable (clarity ≈ 0) multiplies to nothing — that was the junior in the opening. Being right and clear but *untrusted* (you oversold once, or shaded a result, and the room caught it) multiplies to nothing — your clearest memo gets read as a sales pitch. Being clear and trusted but *wrong* multiplies to nothing, and fast, because the market settles it in dollars. You need all three above zero even to be in the game.

But when all three are positive, something better than addition happens: they *compound across time.* Each correct, clearly-communicated, trusted call does not just earn its own allocation — it raises the *base* from which the next call starts. The PM who trusted you last quarter gives your next idea more attention, less skepticism, and a faster yes; that faster yes lets you run more ideas; more ideas at a high trust level builds more reputation; and around it goes. The blue line in the figure is this compounding — modeled, illustratively, at about 30% a year, reaching roughly **18x** the starting influence over a decade. The amber line is the person who is merely right — correct work that never compounds because it is never clearly communicated or trusted, so each call is forgotten and the base never rises. It crawls along near flat. The gap between the two lines at year ten is not a rounding error; **it is the entire difference between a senior quant and a clever individual contributor who plateaus**, and it is built one honest, clear, correct transaction at a time.

#### Worked example: the compounding arithmetic of being trusted

Make the compounding concrete with attention as the currency, since attention is what actually scales. Suppose every time you bring an idea to the allocation room, the trust you have built determines how much attention it gets, and attention roughly determines the odds it gets a fair hearing and an allocation.

Maya, early on, brings ideas that get **4 minutes** of attention each — the default for an unknown. At 4 minutes, a complex idea often dies of attention starvation before its merits land; say **1 in 4** of her good ideas gets adopted. She brings 8 good ideas in a year; 2 get adopted. Those 2 adoptions, presented honestly and proven right, each nudge her trust up — and trust buys attention. The next year, her ideas get **6 minutes**, the room starts from "Maya doesn't oversell," and **1 in 2** of her good ideas now lands. Same 8 good ideas, now 4 adopted. Each of *those* compounds her trust again; by year three she gets **10 minutes**, a near-default-yes on a credible ask, and **3 in 4** land — 6 adopted out of 8.

Look at the trajectory: **2, then 4, then 6** adopted ideas a year, from the *same* underlying rate of good ideas. Her research skill did not triple; her *conversion rate from correct to adopted* tripled, because trust compounded into attention compounded into adoption. Meanwhile the equally-talented researcher who never built the trust — who oversold once, or whose write-ups stayed muddy — is still getting 4 minutes and converting 1 in 4, year after year, watching correct ideas die in committee and wondering why the less-brilliant Maya keeps getting the capital. The answer is in the figure: he is on the flat amber line, and Maya is on the compounding blue one. Same correctness, wildly different careers, and the difference is entirely communication and the trust it builds.

*Reputation is the only asset on a desk that compounds rather than resets each year — and because it is a product of being right, clear, and trusted, the way to grow it is not to be more brilliant but to never let any one of those three factors slip to zero in front of a room that remembers.*

The corollary is sobering and worth stating plainly: because trust is the compounding factor, it is also the one you can destroy fastest. A reputation built over five years of honest, clear, correct calls can be badly damaged by *one* episode of overselling a result you knew was shaky, because the room recalibrates from "this person tells me the holes" to "this person hides holes," and that single recalibration discounts every future claim. The math of compounding cuts both ways: the same multiplier that grows trust over years collapses it in an afternoon. This is the deepest reason the intellectual honesty discipline and the communication discipline are the same discipline viewed from two angles — honesty is what keeps the trust factor positive, and trust is what makes everything else compound.

## How to build the skill

None of this is innate, and none of it is taught in a quant curriculum, which is exactly why it is such a durable edge — the supply of brilliant analysts vastly exceeds the supply of brilliant analysts who can get their work adopted. Here is how you build it deliberately, roughly in order of return on effort.

**Rewrite your own summaries result-first, every time, until it is automatic.** This is the single highest-return habit and it costs nothing. Every memo, every Slack update, every PR description: write it however it comes out, then find the one sentence that states the conclusion and the number, and move it to the top. Delete the chronology. Do this for three months and it becomes the way you think, not just the way you write — and thinking result-first makes you a better researcher too, because it forces you to know what your result actually is.

**Pre-write the three objections before every presentation.** Before any meeting where you are asking for something, write the three hardest questions a skeptic will ask and your honest answer to each. As in Wei's example, the value is partly in the answers and largely in discovering which objections you *cannot* answer — those are the things to go test, or to concede openly, before you walk in. This converts the terror of being caught flat-footed into a rehearsed, honest readiness.

**Practice conceding out loud.** The instinct to defend is strong and physical; you have to override it deliberately until the override becomes the new instinct. In low-stakes settings — a team meeting, a code review of your own work — practice the sentence "that's a fair point, here's the weakness you're pointing at, and here's what I'd do about it." It feels like losing and it is actually winning, but you will not believe that until you have done it twenty times and watched your credibility go *up* each time.

**Seek the feedback that lands, and learn to give it.** Ask the senior people whose write-ups get adopted to tear up one of yours — not to bless it, to break it, the same [adversarial review](/blog/trading/quant-careers/intellectual-honesty-and-killing-your-own-ideas) you would apply to a signal. Then, when you start mentoring, give that same specific, error-not-person, here-is-the-fix feedback forward. The fastest way to learn what good communication is, is to have your bad communication specifically diagnosed by someone who does it well.

**Read the writers in your own firm whose memos get acted on.** Every firm has a few people whose write-ups consistently win allocations and get forwarded. Read them not for the content but for the *structure*: how fast do they get to the number, where do they put the limitations, how do they phrase the ask? You are reverse-engineering the local dialect of adopted-correctness, and it varies by firm — Jane Street's collaborative culture rewards a different register than a sharp-elbowed pod shop.

**Start mentoring before you feel ready,** because the leverage compounds and the skill of explaining your judgment is also the skill of *clarifying* your judgment to yourself. The first junior you ramp will teach you, by their confused questions, exactly which parts of your own reasoning are actually muddled. There is no faster way to find the holes in your own thinking than to be forced to make it clear to someone else.

## Common misconceptions

**"The work speaks for itself."** This is the most expensive belief a talented technical person can hold, and the opening of this post is what it costs. Work does not speak; *people* speak, and people only act on work they can follow and trust. The history of every field is littered with correct results that were ignored for years because the person who found them could not get them adopted, and discovered later by someone who could. On a desk the lag is not years, it is one allocation meeting, and the cost of correct-but-unadopted work is identical to the cost of being wrong: zero P&L and wasted months. The work never spoke for itself anywhere; the people who believe it did are the people whose work happened to be communicated for them by someone else.

**"Confidence persuades."** A junior watches a confident person command a room and concludes that confidence is the lever. It is not — *calibrated* confidence is, and the two are opposites in practice. Overconfidence (selling a shaky result as bulletproof) works exactly once, until the result fails and the room recalibrates you as someone who oversells, at which point your confidence becomes a *negative* signal. What actually persuades a sophisticated room is the opposite of bravado: it is the visible willingness to state your uncertainty precisely — "I'm confident in the 0.8, I'm genuinely unsure about the crowding" — because that calibration is what lets the room trust you on the parts where you *are* confident. Confidence without calibration is noise; calibration is the signal, and it usually sounds humbler than the junior expects.

**"More detail is clearer."** Technical people equate completeness with clarity and so they include everything — every variant tried, every plot generated, every caveat — believing that more information helps the reader. It does the reverse. A reader's attention is finite and a skeptic's patience is short; burying the three decision-changing plots among eleven confirming ones does not add rigor, it *hides* the rigor, because the reader cannot find it. Clarity is not maximal information; it is the *right* information in the right order, which usually means ruthless deletion. The senior write-up is shorter than the junior one and far more is adopted from it, because every sentence that survived earns its place by changing what the reader should do.

**"Mentoring is a distraction from real work."** Covered by the multiplication math above, but worth naming as the myth it is. For a junior, low-leverage, the arithmetic genuinely says hoard your hours. For a senior, the arithmetic flips: an hour spent ramping a junior who then produces 6 units a week of trustworthy work is the highest-leverage hour available, returning more than the same hour spent on your own marginal analysis. The people who treat mentoring as charity they are too busy for are leaving their largest multiplier unused — and, not coincidentally, they are the senior individual contributors who plateau while the multipliers become the ones the firm builds teams around.

**"Communication is a soft skill, separate from the technical work."** This framing — soft skills vs hard skills — is the root error, and it is worth killing explicitly. At senior level communication is not adjacent to the technical work; it is the *completion* of it. An analysis is not done when it is correct; it is done when it is adopted, and adoption runs entirely through communication. Treating the writing-up and the defending as an afterthought to the "real" modeling is like treating the execution of a trade as an afterthought to the alpha — the alpha you cannot execute is not alpha, and the result you cannot get adopted is not a result. The senior quant has internalized that the technical work and the communication of it are one task with two halves, and the second half is where most of the value is captured or lost.

## How it plays out in the real world

The named firms in this series reward communication and influence in concrete, observable ways, even though almost none of them screen for it explicitly at the junior level.

At **Jane Street**, the collaborative, low-ego culture is a communication culture by design. The firm is famous for its internal "tech talks" and for a writing-heavy, peer-review-style research process where ideas are expected to be defensible to a skeptical, friendly room. The OCaml functional-programming bent is partly aesthetic, but it is also about *legibility* — code and reasoning that others can read and trust. A researcher who can write a clear, honest case and concede its weaknesses fits the culture and rises; one who cannot, even if brilliant, struggles to get ideas adopted in a place that decides collaboratively. The path to senior at Jane Street runs directly through being someone whose write-ups the room trusts.

At a **pod shop like Citadel**, the dynamic is sharper-elbowed and more explicitly about influence with the portfolio manager who owns the book and the capital. As we covered in [owning P&L and owning research: the accountability ladder](/blog/trading/quant-careers/owning-pnl-and-owning-research-the-accountability-ladder), the researcher whose signals get into the PM's book is the one who can defend them in exactly the result-first, objection-anticipating, honestly-conceding way Wei used. The high-accountability culture means trust is earned and lost fast and visibly — and the researchers who build the compounding trust are the ones who get more capital routed to them over time, which is the entire game in a pod. The comp follows directly: a mid-level pod-shop QR around 4 years in might post roughly **\$575k** (base ~\$175k + bonus ~\$400k, as reported on levels.fyi for 2025), and the bonus — the variable lever — tracks how much of *your* research the PM trusted enough to size, which tracks your influence.

At the **systematic funds — Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw** — the research-scientist culture is the most write-up-driven of all. Long-horizon signals are evaluated over months, decisions are made by committee and by senior researchers reading each other's work, and a researcher's reputation is built almost entirely through the written and presented record. This is the environment where the compounding-reputation model is most literal: your influence is the integral of every clearly-communicated, honest, correct call you have made, and the senior researchers are precisely the ones with the longest such record. It is also the environment where the "more detail is clearer" myth does the most damage, because the volume of written research is enormous and the scarce resource is senior attention — the researcher whose memos get read and forwarded is the one who learned to lead with the result.

Across all of them, the senior-level reality matches the comp data in the appendix to this series. The progression from a first-year total around **\$450k–\$650k** to a five-year-survivor band of **\$800k–\$1.2M** (standard) and **\$2M–\$4M** (strong), as reported across levels.fyi and the 2026 quant-pay surveys, is not primarily a progression in technical skill — the technical gap between a strong second-year and a strong sixth-year is real but modest. The gap that the comp curve is actually paying for is *leverage*: the senior's correct work gets adopted, multiplied through juniors, and trusted on sight, so the *same* unit of analysis from a senior moves far more capital than from a junior. The strong-band outliers are, almost without exception, people who are not just right but *influential* — whose ideas the firm acts on at scale, and who have built teams that multiply them. That influence is built out of exactly the four skills in this post, and it is why communication, the thing nobody screens for, is the thing the senior comp curve is quietly rewarding.

![A grid mapping four communication failure modes to their fixes: jargon to define-on-first-use, burying the result to lead-with-the-result, overclaiming to separate-evidence-from-advocacy, and no-limitations to state-the-limits-first](/imgs/blogs/communication-and-influence-writing-presenting-mentoring-7.png)

The four failure modes in the figure above are the ones to audit yourself for, because each is a place the senior comp curve is quietly *not* rewarding you. Jargon costs you readers; burying the result costs you attention; overclaiming costs you trust; omitting limitations costs you both trust and the chance to be the one who found the hole. Each fix costs minutes and converts the same correct work from unread to acted-on — which is to say, each fix is a free lift on the only conversion rate that determines whether your correctness becomes capital.

## When this matters / Further reading

This skill starts mattering the moment your work begins depending on other people acting on it — which is earlier than most people think. The first research case you present in an interview is already testing it; the take-home is graded as much on the write-up as on the analysis. Your first ramp-up, your first proposed signal, your first allocation ask — all of them are communication problems wearing technical clothing. And it matters *most* at exactly the transition this Track is about: the step from a clever individual contributor to a senior whose leverage comes from getting correct work adopted, multiplying through juniors, and building the trust that compounds. If you are aiming at that transition, this is the skill to deliberately build, because it is the one the curriculum skipped and the comp curve rewards.

The honest caveat: none of this works as technique divorced from substance. Result-first writing of a wrong result just gets the wrong result adopted faster, which is worse. Conceding weaknesses you have not actually found is theater the room will see through. Mentoring without judgment to transmit is just supervision. Communication is the *lever*; it multiplies whatever is on the other end, and if the other end is sloppy or dishonest work, the lever multiplies the damage. So this post sits on top of the [intellectual honesty](/blog/trading/quant-careers/intellectual-honesty-and-killing-your-own-ideas) discipline and the technical foundation, not instead of them. Be right first; then be clear and trusted, so that being right finally pays.
