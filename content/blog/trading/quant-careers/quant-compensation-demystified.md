> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Quant comp is bonus-driven, variable, and back-loaded; the headline number you hear is the strong-year survivor's pre-tax, pre-deferral total, not the cash that lands in your account.
>
> - A package has four parts: a guaranteed **base** (~250k-375k USD), a one-time **sign-on** (50k-200k), a variable **year-end bonus** (0 to millions), and a **deferred** slice that vests over 3-4 years.
> - The bonus tracks *your* P&L contribution and does **not** repeat: a 1.5M USD year can be 300k the next year on the exact same seat.
> - Deferred comp is a promise, not cash; leave early and you forfeit every unvested tranche.
> - The one number to remember: **base barely moves with seniority; the bonus is the entire lever, and the lever swings both ways.**

## A new grad reads two offers backwards

Maya has two offers on her desk. She is a math undergrad who spent two summers grinding trading games, and she has done well: a market maker offered her a package the recruiter described as "775k," and a systematic fund offered her one the recruiter described as "450k." She tells her friends she is taking the 775k. Obviously. It is 72% more money.

She is reading both numbers backwards, and it will cost her.

The "775k" is a base of 275k, a one-time sign-on of 150k, and a *projected* first-year bonus of 350k that the recruiter quoted as if it were guaranteed and as if it would repeat. The sign-on lands once and never again. The 350k bonus is an on-target estimate that assumes she contributes strongly to P&L in her very first year — before she has learned the systems, before she has been allocated real risk. A more honest year-one number is the base plus the sign-on plus a realistic first-year bonus of maybe 100k-150k, and a more honest year-*two* number is the base plus a bonus that depends entirely on what she did, with the sign-on gone. The "450k" offer, by contrast, is a base of 225k and a bonus of 225k where the firm defers only a small slice and the comp pool is famously stable year to year.

Run it forward two years and the gap she thought she saw inverts. The "775k" can be a 540k year one (base + sign-on + a real first-year bonus) and a 375k year two (base + a modest bonus, sign-on gone). The "450k" can be a 450k year one and a 460k year two. By the end of year two the "lower" offer has paid her more *and* paid her more predictably. Maya took the bigger headline and chased a number that was never going to land.

This post is the antidote to that mistake. We will build quant compensation from zero — every component, what is guaranteed and what is not — then show how it scales by level and by firm type, why the bonus does not repeat, how deferred comp vests and can be forfeited, why the headline is not the take-home, and how survivorship bias inflates every comp story you have ever heard. Figure 1 is the mental model to carry through all of it: a comp package is a stack, and only the top of the stack is guaranteed cash this year.

![Stacked diagram of a quant comp package showing base salary, one-time sign-on, variable year-end bonus, and a deferred slice, with guaranteed parts at the top and variable, conditional parts below](/imgs/blogs/quant-compensation-demystified-1.png)

The thesis is simple and it is the opposite of what the recruiter implies: **understand the structure before you chase the headline.** A quant offer is not a salary. It is a portfolio of promises with very different probabilities attached, and the biggest, brightest promise is the one least likely to pay what you were told.

## Foundations: the components of quant comp

Let us define every piece from the ground up, because the entire industry's comp confusion comes from collapsing five very different things into one number.

**Base salary.** This is the guaranteed cash you are paid every pay period regardless of how you, your desk, or the firm perform. At top quant firms in 2026, base for a new grad runs roughly **250,000 to 375,000 USD** — already high relative to most industries, because firms compete hard for the top of the technical talent pool and want your downside to feel safe. Jane Street, for example, has quoted an "annualized equivalent of \$300,000" base across its quant trader, quant researcher, ML, and FPGA roles (as reported on firm pages and levels.fyi, 2025). The crucial property of base: it is **flat-ish**. It does not scale much with seniority. An eight-year veteran does not have a 2M USD base — they have a base of maybe 250k-350k and a bonus that dwarfs it. Hold that fact; it is the load-bearing fact of this entire post.

**Sign-on bonus.** A one-time cash payment to get you in the door, typically **50,000 to 200,000 USD** for a top new-grad offer. It exists to win the bidding war and to compensate you for things like leaving an unvested equity package at a prior employer or relocating. The defining property: it lands **once**, almost always in year one. A recruiter who folds a 150k sign-on into a "775k package" is quietly telling you a number that is structurally impossible to repeat in year two, because the sign-on is gone. Sign-ons also frequently carry a **clawback**: if you quit within 12 months, you may have to pay it back.

**Year-end bonus.** This is where quant comp is made and where it diverges from a normal salaried job. The bonus is **variable** — it can range from zero to many times your base — and it is paid once a year based on a backward-looking assessment of your contribution. At a trading desk it tracks the **P&L** (profit and loss) you generated or contributed to. At a research shop it tracks the value of the signals you shipped. The bonus is the lever: it is why a strong mid-level quant can clear 1M+ USD while their base sits at 200k. It is also why comp is so misunderstood, because the bonus is the part people quote, the part that does not repeat, and the part most subject to survivorship bias.

**Deferred compensation.** Firms rarely pay the entire bonus in cash now. A slice — often 20% to 40% of a large bonus — is **deferred**: promised to you but paid out over the next three to four years, and contingent on you still working there when each tranche vests. Deferral is a retention tool. It ties your future self to the firm: walk away and you leave the unvested portion on the table. We will spend a whole section on this, because it is the single most misunderstood line in a quant offer.

**Profit share / payout percentage.** At some firms — especially pod shops and certain prop desks — senior people are paid not a discretionary bonus but a **percentage of the P&L they directly generate**. A portfolio manager (PM) at a multi-strategy fund might keep, say, 10-20% of the net profit on their book after costs. This is the most direct, most variable, and most brutal form of comp: in a great year the payout is enormous, and in a losing year it can be **zero**, and a deep enough drawdown can end the seat entirely.

**Vesting and clawback** are the two pieces of fine print that turn a promise into a conditional promise. **Vesting** is the schedule on which deferred comp becomes truly yours — typically in equal tranches over three or four years. **Clawback** is the firm's right to take back comp already paid (or cancel unvested comp) under certain conditions: you leave inside a window, you violate a non-compete, your book later turns out to have had a problem, or you are terminated for cause. A "775k offer" with 40% of the bonus deferred over four years and a clawback on early exit is a fundamentally different financial object than 775k of cash in your bank account this December.

Here is the whole package as one picture. Only the top — base, and the one-time sign-on — is money you can count on this year. Everything that makes the headline big lives in the variable, deferred, conditional layers below.

The mental shorthand: **base is what you live on; bonus is what you negotiate the rest of your life around; deferral is the leash; the payout percentage is the seat.** Every other concept in this post is a consequence of those four sentences.

One more piece of mechanics, because it explains why the bonus feels so opaque: **how the bonus is actually decided.** At a discretionary firm — most prop shops and systematic funds — there is a comp process each year, sometimes informally called the comp meeting, where managers assess each person's contribution and the firm's overall profitability and then assign a bonus. It is not a formula you can audit. Your bonus depends on the P&L you generated or contributed to, the firm's total P&L pool that year (a great personal year inside a bad firm year still pays less), your manager's read of your trajectory, and what the firm thinks it must pay to retain you. This is why two people with similar P&L can get different bonuses, and why the same person's bonus swings year to year even when their effort does not. At a **payout-percentage** firm — pod shops, some PM seats — the discretion is replaced by an explicit formula: you keep a contractual cut of your book's net P&L after costs, which is more transparent but also more unforgiving, because a flat or negative book pays a flat or negative number with no manager there to round it up.

The practical upshot of the comp process is that **the bonus is a judgment about a backward-looking year, made under a firmwide budget constraint, that resets annually.** It is not a salary, it is not an entitlement, and it is not yours to forecast as a straight line. A senior quant learns to treat each year's bonus as an independent draw from a distribution whose mean depends on skill and seat but whose realization depends on opportunity, the firm's year, and luck.

A note on honesty before we go further. Every dollar figure in this post is **illustrative and dated**. The bands come from reported 2025-2026 ranges on levels.fyi, Glassdoor, H1B disclosures, efinancialcareers, and the "Young & Calculated" quant-pay survey. They are *reported ranges among people who are still in the seat*. They are not promises, they are not medians for everyone who applies, and they will be stale within a year or two. When you see a precise-looking number here, read it as "roughly, as of 2026, for survivors" — never as "this is what you will make."

## Comp by level: the bands, and the conditionality hiding inside them

The first thing people want is the number-by-experience curve. Here it is — with the asterisk that makes it honest stapled directly to it.

![Grouped bar chart of total quant comp in USD millions by experience level, showing poor, standard, and strong-year bands for new grads, mid-level, five-year survivors, and eight-year survivors, with the strong-standard gap widening sharply with seniority](/imgs/blogs/quant-compensation-demystified-2.png)

Reading the bands as of 2026:

- **New grad (0-2 years):** first-year total comp typically **450k-650k USD** on-target at the top tier (Jane Street, Citadel Securities, Two Sigma, Jump, HRT) — base 250k-375k, sign-on 50k-200k, plus a first-year bonus. A poor outcome is closer to 450k, a strong one toward 650k.
- **Mid-level (2-5 years):** the bands fan out. A pod-shop quant researcher around four years in might be near **575k** (base 175k + bonus 400k). A big-prop quant trader two-and-a-half years into a *strong seat* might hit **1.5M** (base 200k + bonus 1.3M). The standard mid-market band is roughly **375k-500k**; a strong outcome **650k-820k**; an outlier strong seat well over a million.
- **Five-year mark (survivors):** standard **800k-1.2M**, poor **475k-625k**, strong **2M-4M**.
- **Eight-year mark:** standard **1.6M-3.8M**, poor **0.9M-1.8M**, strong **8M+**. Top-tier quant traders five to seven years in occasionally report **8M-12M**; a ten-year top-prop trader example is base 250k + bonus 2.75M = **3M**.

Two features of that chart matter more than any single number.

First, **the spread explodes with seniority.** At the new-grad stage, poor and strong are 450k and 650k — a 1.4x ratio. At eight years, poor and strong are roughly 1.3M and 8M — a 6x ratio, *same title, same firm*. As you climb, your comp becomes a leveraged bet on your own performance. The base barely moves; the bonus, which is now most of the package, swings enormously. This is the deep structure: **seniority does not raise your floor much; it raises your ceiling a lot, and the distance between floor and ceiling is your P&L.**

Second — and the red box on the chart says this for a reason — **these bands only show survivors.** Everyone in the chart is still in a quant seat at that experience level. The people who washed out at year three, switched into a non-quant role, or landed at a smaller firm far below the headline simply are not in any of the three bands. The chart cannot show them, because comp surveys can only sample the people who are still there to answer. We will return to this in the survivorship section; for now, internalize that the "5-yr standard 800k-1.2M" band is conditional on having survived to year five in the seat, which is itself far from guaranteed.

#### Worked example: decomposing Maya's two offers into year-1 and year-2 reality

Let us do Maya's mistake properly, with numbers, and flag that every figure is illustrative. Offer A is the "775k" from the market maker; offer B is the "450k" from the systematic fund.

Offer A, year 1: base **\$275,000** + sign-on **\$150,000** + a *realistic* first-year bonus. The recruiter projected the bonus at 350k, but that is an on-target number for someone already contributing to P&L, not a first-year ramp. Say a real first-year bonus is **\$120,000**. Year-1 total: 275 + 150 + 120 = **\$545,000**. Not 775k — but still excellent.

Offer A, year 2: base **\$275,000** + bonus. The sign-on is gone. If Maya ramps well and contributes, her bonus might grow to **\$200,000**, giving **\$475,000**. If her seat is quieter or her contribution is middling, the bonus might be **\$100,000**, giving **\$375,000**. The point: year two is base-plus-bonus, with the 150k sign-on permanently removed, so even a *better* year on the bonus line can show a lower total than year one.

Offer B, year 1: base **\$225,000** + sign-on **\$50,000** + first-year bonus **\$175,000** = **\$450,000**. The fund's comp pool is stable and the bonus is less P&L-whippy. Year 2: base **\$225,000** + bonus **\$235,000** = **\$460,000** (sign-on gone, bonus grows modestly with tenure).

Two-year totals: Offer A pays 545 + 425 (taking the quieter year-2) = **\$970,000**; Offer B pays 450 + 460 = **\$910,000**. If year 2 at A is the better 475, A pays 1.02M. The offers are *much closer than 775 vs 450 implied*, A carries far more variance, and the "72% bigger" framing was an illusion created by counting a one-time sign-on and an optimistic, non-repeating bonus inside a single headline.

*The lesson Maya needed: decompose every offer into guaranteed-this-year, variable-this-year, and one-time, then run it forward two years before you compare — the headline collapses one-time and conditional money into a number that does not exist in year two.*

## Comp by firm type: same stage, different shape

Two quants at the same experience level and similar total comp can have radically different comp *shapes*, and the shape tells you what you are actually signing up for. The shape is the ratio of guaranteed base to variable bonus, how the bonus is determined, and what happens to it in a bad year.

![Stacked bar chart of mid-level quant total comp in USD millions split into base and variable bonus across four firm types, prop or market maker, pod shop, systematic fund, and HFT firm, annotated with the bonus share of total for each](/imgs/blogs/quant-compensation-demystified-3.png)

Reading the four archetypes at a mid-level seat (illustrative, 2026):

- **Prop firm / market maker** (Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, IMC, Citadel Securities): trades the firm's own capital as a collaborative book. A strong mid-level seat might be base **200k** + bonus **1.1M** = **1.3M**, with the bonus around **85%** of total. The bonus comes from a firm P&L pool, allocated by your contribution. Highly bonus-heavy, highly P&L-linked, but the *drawdown is shared by the firm* — a bad year shrinks your bonus, it does not fire you outright. This is the seat with the biggest strong-year upside relative to a stable floor.
- **Pod shop** (Citadel, Millennium, the multi-strats): allocates outside investor capital to many independent PM books. A mid-level QR might be base **175k** + bonus **400k** = **575k**, bonus around **70%**. The defining feature is the **payout percentage**: as you become a PM, your comp is an explicit cut of *your book's* net P&L, and a deep enough drawdown triggers a **stop-out** that closes your book and ends the seat. Highest accountability, highest career variance.
- **Systematic fund** (Two Sigma, D.E. Shaw, WorldQuant): long-horizon, research-driven, paid from a comp pool funded by management and performance fees on investor capital. A mid researcher might be base **225k** + bonus **350k** = **575k**, bonus around **70%** but *base-heavier in feel* and pool-based rather than directly P&L-linked. The bonus is more stable and more removed from any single day's trading, because you are several layers from the dollars and the firm smooths across a large research effort.
- **HFT firm** (Jump, HRT, Tower): ultra-low-latency systems shops where the edge is engineering. A mid dev or QT might be base **250k** + bonus **650k** = **0.90M**, bonus around **72%**. Comp is strong and bonus-heavy but tends to be team-and-system-attributed rather than tied to one person's daily P&L, because the P&L comes from infrastructure many people built.

The payout-percentage structure deserves a closer look, because it is where comp gets both most lucrative and most brutal. When a pod-shop quant becomes a PM, they are typically given a book — a capital allocation, say 200M USD — and a mandate, and they are paid a **percentage of the net P&L** that book generates after the costs charged against it (data, financing, a share of infrastructure, sometimes the cost of the analysts on the pod). A common shape is a payout in the low-to-mid teens of percent. If the PM makes 30M USD net on the book, a 12% payout is 3.6M; if they make nothing, the payout is roughly nothing above a small base. And there is a hard floor in the other direction: most pods run a **drawdown stop-out**, often around a 5-10% loss on allocated capital, at which point the book is cut or closed and the PM may be let go. So the payout percentage is a call option on your book's P&L written against the risk of losing the seat entirely — enormous upside, a zero in a flat year, and a career-level downside if the book draws down. This is the most direct line between "the dollars I generate" and "the dollars I take home" anywhere in the industry, and it is why pod PMs both make the most eye-watering numbers and have the shortest, most volatile tenures.

The chart's claim is not "prop pays more" — note that pod and systematic are nearly identical totals here. The claim is that the **shape** differs: prop and HFT lean bonus-heavy and closely P&L-linked; systematic leans toward a stable pool; pod ties you most directly to your own book and its drawdown. When you compare two offers, compare shapes, not just totals — a base-heavier, pool-based offer is a *different risk profile* than a bonus-heavy, P&L-linked one, even at the same number. The firm-archetypes post in this series goes deep on what those structures feel like to work inside; here the point is only that **the comp shape is a direct readout of the firm's risk model.**

#### Worked example: Maya the trader and Wei the researcher compare comp shape

Maya joins a prop market maker; Wei, a CS PhD, joins a systematic fund. Both are three years in. Both report a "good year" around **750k USD** total. They sound identical. They are not.

Maya's 750k is base **200k** + bonus **550k**. Her bonus is an explicit function of the P&L she contributed on her options book. In a strong year the desk made money and her slice was large. The structure: roughly 27% guaranteed, 73% variable, and the variable part is a fairly direct readout of *this year's* trading. Next year, if the volatility regime that fed her book flips, her bonus could fall to 200k and her total to **400k** — a 47% drop on the same seat, with her base unchanged.

Wei's 750k is base **240k** + bonus **510k**, where the bonus is his slice of a firmwide comp pool funded by fees on the fund's investor capital. His signals contributed, but the pool smooths across hundreds of researchers and a long-horizon book. The structure: roughly 32% guaranteed, 68% variable, but the variable part is *less whippy* because it is pool-based and removed from any single strategy's monthly P&L. Next year, even if one of his signals decays, the pool and his tenure cushion the swing; his total might be **680k** — an 9% drop, not a 47% one.

Same headline, very different second moments. Maya bought a higher ceiling and a wider distribution. Wei bought a narrower distribution and more comp stability, at the cost of the top-end blowout year Maya can have. Neither is "better"; they are different bets, and the comp shape is how you read which bet you are taking.

*The lesson: total comp is the first moment; comp shape — base/bonus split and how the bonus is set — is the variance, and at quant pay levels the variance is the part that actually determines how your career feels.*

## The bonus that does not repeat

This is the single most expensive misunderstanding in the industry, so it gets its own section and its own chart. **The year-end bonus is reset to zero every January.** It is not a salary component that accrues. It is a backward-looking payment for the value you contributed in the year just ended, and next year it is recomputed from scratch against next year's contribution. A monster year does not raise a baseline. It pays you once.

![Stacked bar chart comparing year-1 and year-2 total comp for the same trader on the same seat, with a flat 200k base both years, a 1.3M bonus in year 1 shown in green and a 100k bonus in year 2 shown in red, annotated that total comp fell roughly 80 percent because the bonus resets to that year's P&L](/imgs/blogs/quant-compensation-demystified-4.png)

#### Worked example: the 1.5M that became 300k on the same seat

A trader — call him a mid-level QT at a big prop firm — has a strong year. The volatility was high, his book caught the moves, and his P&L contribution was large. His comp: base **\$200,000** + bonus **\$1,300,000** = **\$1,500,000**. He tells everyone he meets that he "makes 1.5M." The internet repeats it. Recruiters quote it. It becomes a data point in the folklore that "quants make millions."

The next year, the volatility regime that fed his book collapses. He does nothing wrong — he trades the same way, manages risk well — but the opportunity simply is not there, and his P&L contribution is a fraction of last year's. His comp: base **\$200,000** + bonus **\$100,000** = **\$300,000**.

His base did not change. His seat did not change. His skill did not change. His total comp fell **80%**, from 1.5M to 300k, because the bonus is a function of *this year's P&L*, and this year's P&L was small. If he had spent the 1.5M year as if it were his new salary — bought the apartment, took on the payments — he would now be in real trouble. This is why experienced quants treat a big bonus as a windfall to be saved and invested, not as a raise: the bonus does not repeat automatically, and the firm makes no promise that it will.

*The lesson: a single bonus number tells you what one specific year was worth, not what you "make." Annualizing a strong bonus into a salary is the classic trap; the right mental model is "I have a modest guaranteed base plus a lottery ticket whose expected value is high but whose realization is enormously path-dependent."*

There is a subtler version of this trap on the way *up*, too. When you are recruited, the recruiter may quote your potential employer's *strong-year* bonus as your "comp." When you accept, your first-year bonus is usually a ramp number — far below the strong-year figure — because you spent half the year learning the systems. So year one underperforms the headline (you have not ramped), and any later strong year overperforms it (and then does not repeat). The headline is an average of moments that may never coincide in a single calendar year for you.

## Deferred comp and vesting: a promise with a leash

When the bonus is large, the firm does not hand you all of it in cash. A slice — commonly **20% to 40%** of a big bonus — is **deferred** and paid out over the following three to four years, vesting in tranches, each contingent on you still being employed when that tranche vests. This is universal at the senior end and common even at mid-level for the variable portion above some threshold.

Deferral does three things for the firm and one thing to you. For the firm: it **retains** you (you have unvested money you would forfeit by leaving), it **aligns** you (some deferred comp is tied to the fund's later performance, so you care about the long-run book), and it **smooths** the firm's cash outflow. To you, it means a material chunk of your "comp this year" is not spendable this year, is at risk if you leave, and may be at risk if the firm later claws it back.

![Timeline of deferred compensation vesting, showing a 200k grant at year zero with nothing vested, 50k vesting at year one and year two, an early exit at year two-and-a-half forfeiting the remaining 100k unvested plus garden leave, versus staying to year four to vest the full amount](/imgs/blogs/quant-compensation-demystified-5.png)

The figure shows the mechanics. A **grant** of deferred comp is awarded at year 0 — say 200k USD — but **zero of it is yours yet.** It vests in tranches: 50k at year 1, 50k at year 2, and so on, over a three-to-four-year schedule. Each tranche becomes truly yours only when it vests, and only if you are still employed. If you leave at year 2.5, you have vested 100k and you **forfeit the remaining 100k entirely** — plus you likely owe a **garden leave** or non-compete period (often 3-12 months) during which you are paid your base but cannot work for a competitor, which has its own cost.

#### Worked example: what Wei forfeits by leaving early

Wei has been at his systematic fund for two and a half years. He has received deferred-comp grants each year, and his **total unvested balance** is **\$240,000** across overlapping schedules: roughly 80k from his year-1 bonus deferral (vesting through year 4), 90k from year 2 (vesting through year 5), and 70k from year 3's partial-year grant (vesting through year 6). A competitor offers him a seat with a 200k sign-on and a higher base.

Wei must do the math the recruiter will not do for him. By leaving now, he **forfeits the entire \$240,000 of unvested deferred comp** — that money evaporates the day he resigns. He also faces a **6-month garden leave**: paid his current base (say 120k for the half year) but unable to start at the new firm, so he loses six months of the new firm's bonus-generating clock. The new sign-on is 200k. Net first-pass: he gives up 240k of forfeited deferral to receive a 200k sign-on — a **40k loss before he has earned a dollar at the new place** — plus the dead time of garden leave, partly offset by his base during it.

Does that mean he should never move? No. If the new seat genuinely raises his expected annual comp by, say, 150k per year for the next several years, the one-time 40k forfeiture and the garden-leave drag are recouped within the first year and the move is strongly positive in expectation. The point is that **deferred comp and garden leave are a real, quantifiable cost of switching that the headline sign-on hides**, and the only honest way to evaluate a move is to net the forfeited unvested balance and the dead time against the lifetime uplift — not to look at the sign-on alone.

*The lesson: deferred comp is a retention leash priced in dollars. It does not make a move wrong, but it does mean the true cost of leaving is "unvested balance forfeited + garden-leave opportunity cost," which can be six figures and which the new firm's shiny sign-on is partly designed to make you forget.*

The clawback wrinkle deepens this. Some firms can claw back comp **already paid** — not just cancel unvested tranches — if you leave for a competitor inside a window, breach a non-compete, or if your book is later found to have had a risk or compliance problem. Read the actual offer document. The phrases that matter are "deferred," "vesting schedule," "forfeiture on termination," "garden leave," "non-compete," and "clawback." A 1M USD "comp" with 400k deferred over four years and a clawback on early exit is a very different object from 1M in cash, and the difference is exactly the thing the headline number is designed to elide.

## Why the headline is not the take-home

Stack the effects we have built and the gap between the number you hear and the money you can spend becomes large and predictable.

![Before-and-after comparison contrasting a 775k headline number against take-home reality, showing how the one-time sign-on, roughly 30 percent deferral, P&L-linked bonus, and 40 to 50 percent taxes reduce it to roughly 400k of cash in hand in year one](/imgs/blogs/quant-compensation-demystified-6.png)

Walk the left column to the right column, using Maya's "775k" as the worked case:

- **The headline counts a one-time sign-on as if annual.** Strip the 150k sign-on out of the recurring picture: it is real money in year one and gone after. The recurring number is closer to 625k on-target, not 775k.
- **It assumes the strong bonus repeats.** As we have seen, the projected 350k bonus is an on-target, strong-contribution figure, not a first-year ramp and not a guarantee. Year one is realistically lower.
- **It ignores the deferred slice.** If ~30% of the bonus is deferred over three to four years, then ~30% of the bonus portion is *not spendable this year* and is at risk if she leaves. On a 350k bonus that is ~105k locked up.
- **It is pre-tax.** At top-bracket federal plus a high-tax state (NYC, where most of these seats are), the marginal tax rate on this income is roughly **40-50%**. Nearly half of every additional dollar of comp goes to taxes. Cost of living in those cities is also high.

Run the cash-in-hand math for year one. Gross spendable-this-year comp ≈ base 275k + sign-on 150k + (first-year bonus 120k of which ~30k is deferred, so ~90k cash) = **~515k gross cash**. Apply a blended ~42% effective tax: take-home ≈ **~300k**, with another ~30k of deferred bonus locked up to vest later. The "775k" turned into roughly **300k of after-tax cash in hand** in year one, plus a deferred balance she cannot touch and a bonus that may not repeat. That is not a swindle — 300k after tax at 22 is extraordinary — but it is **less than half the headline**, and the entire industry's comp folklore is built on quoting the headline.

The location effect compounds the tax one. Most top seats are in New York or Chicago, both high-cost cities, and the marginal tax rate differs meaningfully between them — a New York City quant pays city income tax on top of state and federal, while a Chicago quant pays Illinois state tax but no city income tax. On a large bonus, that difference can be several percentage points of the whole package, which on a seven-figure number is six figures of after-tax cash. It does not change the ranking of two offers at the same firm, but it absolutely changes the comparison of two offers in different cities, and the headline never mentions it. The same 1M USD package buys a very different life net of tax and rent in two different cities, and an honest comparison normalizes for both. The mistake is to compare two headline numbers as if a dollar in one city equals a dollar in another; it does not, and at quant comp levels the gap is large enough to flip which offer is actually better.

*The take-home rule: from any quoted quant number, mentally subtract the one-time sign-on, haircut the bonus to a realistic and non-repeating level, lock up the deferred slice, and tax the rest at ~40-50% adjusted for the city. What survives, net of rent, is roughly what you live on.*

## Survivorship bias: why every comp story you hear is inflated

Now the deepest distortion, the one that makes "everyone makes 600k by year five" feel true when it is not. **Survivorship bias** is the error of drawing conclusions from the things that made it through a filter while ignoring the things that did not. Comp folklore is almost pure survivorship bias.

Think about *who* generates the comp stories you hear. The person who landed a top seat and had a great year posts their number, tells their friends, shows up on levels.fyi. The person who washed out at year two, or never converted their internship, or took a 250k seat at a smaller firm and is quietly fine but unremarkable, generates no story. The data you absorb is filtered three times over: by who got hired (the funnel is brutally selective — top programs are often described as low-single-digit-percent admit rates, thousands of applicants per seat), by who survived the up-or-out reality of the first few years, and by who chooses to broadcast. Each filter biases the visible numbers upward.

There is a vivid historical analogue that quants love: in World War II, analysts examined returning bombers to decide where to add armor, and the planes came back peppered with bullet holes on the wings and fuselage but rarely on the engines. The naive conclusion was to armor the wings. The statistician Abraham Wald pointed out the opposite: armor the engines, *because the planes hit in the engines did not come back to be measured.* Comp surveys are the returning bombers. The "5-yr standard is 800k-1.2M" band is computed from the people still in a quant seat at year five — the survivors. The ones whose engines got hit, who washed out or left the industry, are not in the dataset, so the visible band is systematically higher than the true distribution over everyone who started.

This is why the honest version of "everyone clears a million" is: **among the people who got hired at a top firm and survived to a senior seat and chose to share, many clear a million — and that is a heavily filtered subset of everyone who set out to become a quant.** The headline is real for the survivors and misleading as a forecast for you, because you do not yet know whether you will pass all three filters. The expected value of a quant career is genuinely high; the *distribution* is wide and the left tail (wash-out, mediocre seat, a regime that never rewards your book) is real and underreported.

It helps to put rough numbers on the filter, flagged heavily as illustrative. Suppose a cohort of strong applicants targets top quant seats. Of those who apply, a small fraction are hired — the funnel is widely described as low-single-digit-percent at the top programs. Of those hired, some fraction wash out, switch into adjacent roles, or land at smaller firms in the first few years — the up-or-out reality. Of the survivors, the comp distribution at five years is the band on Figure 2. So the comp you should attach to "I am going to try to become a quant" is not the survivor band; it is the survivor band *times the probability of surviving each filter*, blended with the much lower outcomes of the people who did not survive. The expected value of attempting a quant career is still genuinely high — base alone, from year one, beats almost any other entry-level path, and the right tail is spectacular. But the *variance* is enormous and the visible numbers report only the right side of the distribution. This is precisely the series' recurring spine: getting and keeping the job is itself a probabilistic edge, and the honest quant prices their own career the way they price a trade — by the whole distribution, not the best-case headline.

The career-strategy consequence is concrete: **do not size your life off the strong-survivor number, and do not let a recruiter anchor your self-worth or your offer comparison on it.** Size your life off your base. Treat bonuses as windfalls. Diversify the human capital risk by becoming genuinely good (skill is the one thing that moves you up the survivor curve). And when you compare offers, compare the *structure and the floor*, because the ceiling is a survivorship-inflated story.

## How to read a comp number honestly

Put it all together into a procedure. When anyone — a recruiter, a friend, a forum post — quotes you a quant comp number, run it through this filter before you believe anything about it. The single most useful move is to score each component along four questions, because each component answers them differently, and the answers are exactly what the headline hides.

![Matrix scoring each comp component against four questions, whether it is guaranteed, whether it is variable with P&L, whether it is paid later, and whether it can be clawed back, showing base salary as fully guaranteed while bonus, deferred comp, and profit share are variable, deferred, and conditional](/imgs/blogs/quant-compensation-demystified-7.png)

The matrix is the whole post in one grid. Read each row — base, sign-on, year-end bonus, deferred bonus, profit-share/payout — against four columns: is it guaranteed, is it variable with your P&L, is it paid later, and can it be taken back. Only the base row is green across the board: fully guaranteed, not variable, paid biweekly, not clawed back. As you move down the rows the green turns to amber and red — the bonus is not guaranteed and is heavily variable, the deferred bonus is paid over years and forfeited on exit, the payout percentage is fully variable and can be clipped by a drawdown. The grid makes the structural truth visual: the components that make the number big are exactly the components that score worst on certainty. A "775k" is mostly built from the red rows.

**1. Decompose it into the four components.** Ask explicitly: how much is base, how much is sign-on, how much is target bonus, how much is profit-share/payout? A single number is a marketing object; the decomposition is the financial object. If they will not break it down, that itself is information.

**2. Separate one-time from recurring.** Pull the sign-on out. It is real, it matters for year one, and it is gone after. Your recurring comp is base + bonus, full stop.

**3. Haircut the bonus to a realistic, first-year-ramped, non-repeating level.** The quoted bonus is usually an on-target or strong-year figure. Your year-one bonus is a ramp number. And no bonus is promised to repeat. Build your expectation around a *distribution* of bonus outcomes, not the top of the range.

**4. Find the deferral and the vesting schedule.** What fraction is deferred? Over how many years? What is forfeited if you leave, and when? This converts "comp" into "spendable-this-year cash" plus "a conditional future claim."

**5. Identify the comp shape and the firm's risk model.** Bonus-heavy and P&L-linked (prop/HFT) versus pool-based and smoother (systematic) versus payout-percentage with a stop-out (pod). The shape tells you your variance, and at these levels variance is the story.

**6. Tax it and locate it.** Apply ~40-50% to the marginal dollars and remember the cost of living of the city the seat is in. After-tax cash in a high-cost city is the number that touches your actual life.

**7. Discount the whole thing for survivorship.** Remember you are hearing a filtered, survivor-skewed sample. Weight the floor and the structure more than the ceiling, because the ceiling is the part most inflated by who chooses to talk.

Do all seven and a "775k" resolves into something like "a ~275k base I can count on, a 150k one-time sweetener, a bonus that is realistically ~120k in year one and a wide distribution after, ~30% of which is locked up for years, taxed at ~42%, in a high-cost city, quoted to me at the strong-survivor end." That is a fantastic offer. It is also not 775k, and now you can compare it honestly to the "450k" that turned out to pay more by year two.

## Common misconceptions

**"Everyone makes a million by year five."** This is survivorship bias wearing a number. Among people who got hired at a top firm and survived to a senior seat, many do clear seven figures; that is a triple-filtered subset (hired, survived, broadcasting) of everyone who set out to become a quant. The standard five-year survivor band is roughly 800k-1.2M, the poor band 475k-625k, and an unknown number washed out entirely and appear in no band. The expected value is high; the guarantee is not.

**"The base is the main number."** Backwards. At quant firms the base is the *small, stable* part — 250k-375k for a new grad and not climbing dramatically with seniority. The bonus is the lever and the bulk of senior comp. A senior quant with a 300k base and a 2.7M bonus does not "make 300k"; the base is almost a rounding-error on their package. Read the bonus and the payout structure, not the salary line, to understand the comp.

**"The bonus repeats — it's basically my new salary."** No. The bonus is reset to zero every year and recomputed from that year's P&L contribution. A 1.5M year can be a 300k year on the same seat with no change in skill, because the opportunity changed. Annualizing a strong bonus into a lifestyle is the most common way quants get into financial trouble despite huge headline numbers.

**"Deferred comp is guaranteed — it's money I've earned."** It is *promised*, not guaranteed and not yet yours. It vests in tranches over three to four years, you forfeit every unvested tranche if you leave, garden leave and non-competes add dead time, and clawbacks can in some cases reach money already paid. Until a tranche vests with you still employed, it is a conditional claim, not cash.

**"A bigger headline offer is the better offer."** Only after you decompose both. A higher headline built on a one-time sign-on and an optimistic, non-repeating bonus can pay *less* over two years than a lower headline with a stable, base-heavier structure — exactly Maya's inversion. Compare structures and floors, not headlines.

**"Comp scales smoothly and predictably with experience."** The floor scales modestly; the ceiling scales explosively, and the gap between them is your performance and your firm's opportunity set. At eight years, the strong band is roughly 6x the poor band at the same title. Quant comp at the senior end is a leveraged bet on yourself, not a step-function salary ladder.

## How it plays out in the real world

Stitch the pieces onto real, named structures, all from reported 2025-2026 ranges and flagged illustrative.

At a **prop market maker** like Jane Street, a new-grad quant trader sees a base near the "annualized \$300k equivalent" the firm quotes across roles, plus a sign-on, plus a first-year bonus, landing in the 450k-650k first-year band. levels.fyi reports Jane Street quant trader base around 300k with a 75th percentile near 407k and a 90th near 512k as total figures climb with the bonus; quant researcher figures range from an L1 around 307k up toward 565k at higher levels. The structure is bonus-heavy and tied to the firm's collaborative P&L pool, with the drawdown shared rather than landing on one person — the highest-upside, firm-cushioned shape.

At **Citadel**, the distinction between the pod hedge fund and **Citadel Securities** the market maker matters for comp shape. levels.fyi reports Citadel quant researcher around a 325k median, an L1 near 336k climbing to an L3 near 642k, with top reported figures past 721k. On the pod side, comp increasingly becomes a payout percentage of *your book's* P&L as you become a PM — the most direct, highest-variance structure, with a drawdown stop-out that can end the seat. Citadel Securities H1B base disclosures sit around 257k; Five Rings and Jane Street lead at roughly 300k base.

At **HFT** shops like **Jump** and **HRT**, comp is strong and bonus-heavy but more system-and-team-attributed, because the edge lives in infrastructure many engineers built. At **systematic funds** like **Two Sigma** (~70B USD AUM) and **D.E. Shaw** (~65B USD AUM), the researcher's bonus is a slice of a comp pool funded by management and performance fees on investor capital — smoother, more removed from any single strategy's monthly P&L, base-heavier in feel. **WorldQuant**'s alpha-factory model spreads research across a distributed network with its own comp structure.

Internships deserve a line here because, as the recruiting posts in this series stress, **the internship is the real interview and the main door to a full-time seat.** Internship pay is itself eye-watering — Jane Street interns at an annualized ~300k (roughly \$4,500-6,000/week), Citadel and Citadel Securities at \$4,300-5,800/week for 2026 plus a 15k-25k sign-on and corporate housing, IMC up to ~\$5,800/week with conversion packages around 425k total comp, Optiver PhD interns ~80k-90k for an eight-week placement, XTX AI-research interns up to ~35k/month. The strategic point: full-time seats largely come from intern conversion, so the highest-expected-value comp move early on is to win the internship, because that is where the full-time offer — and the comp trajectory — is actually decided.

It is worth tracing one realistic arc end to end, illustratively, because the bands on Figure 2 are snapshots and a career is the path through them. A strong new grad joins a prop market maker at a 540k first-year total (base 290k + sign-on 130k + ramped bonus 120k). Year two, sign-on gone, base 290k + a bonus that grew with their ramp to 280k = 570k. Year three is a strong market year and their book contributed: base 290k + bonus 760k = 1.05M — the year they tell everyone about. Year four the regime quiets: base 290k + bonus 240k = 530k — lower than year three despite no drop in skill, the bonus-does-not-repeat reality. Years five through eight, if they survive and grow into owning more risk, the bonus trends up and gets lumpier, landing them somewhere in the wide five-to-eight-year survivor band, with a deferred balance always trailing behind them as a retention leash. That path — up, down, lumpy, back-loaded, with a flat base underneath the whole time — is what a quant career's comp actually looks like, and it is nothing like a smooth salary ladder. The person who understood the structure saved their year-three windfall and was unbothered by year four; the person who annualized year three into a lifestyle had a bad year four.

Across all of these, the pattern is the same one we built from zero: a modest, stable base; a variable bonus or payout that is most of the package and tracks your contribution; a deferred slice with a vesting leash; and a survivorship-inflated folklore that quotes the strong-year survivor's pre-tax, pre-deferral total as if it were everyone's spendable salary. The firm that pays you 1.5M in a great year is the same firm that pays you 300k in a quiet one, and both numbers are true.

## When this matters and further reading

Comp structure matters most at exactly the moments it is easiest to get wrong: when you are choosing between offers, when you are deciding whether to switch firms, when you are deciding how much of a big year to spend, and when you are negotiating. In every one of those moments the headline number is the enemy of a good decision, because it collapses guaranteed, variable, one-time, deferred, and conditional money into a single figure engineered to look as large as possible.

Carry four things out of this post. First, **base is what you live on; the bonus is the lever and most of the package, and it does not repeat.** Second, **deferred comp is a promise with a leash — vesting, forfeiture, garden leave, and clawback are real dollar costs of leaving.** Third, **the headline is roughly double the after-tax, post-deferral cash that lands**, so always decompose, haircut, defer, and tax before you believe a number. Fourth, **every comp story you hear is survivorship-filtered**, so weight the floor and the structure over the ceiling. Do that and you will never again take the "775k" over the "450k" without first checking which one actually pays you more.
