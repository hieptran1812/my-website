> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Your resume's only job is to **not get filtered**. It rarely wins the offer — it survives a ten-second, often half-automated screen so the live loop can decide. Optimize for *signal density*, not completeness.
>
> - **Rank your signals, then front-load them.** The hierarchy, top to bottom: a hard competition result (IMO / IOI / Putnam / ICPC / a Kaggle medal) → research and publications → strong projects with *measurable outcomes* → relevant internships → GPA and coursework at a top program → generic skills and soft claims. Put the top of that list in the top third of the page.
> - **A strong bullet = action + tool + quantified result.** "Worked on an ML project" is filtered; "Built a momentum signal in Python, Sharpe 1.4 out-of-sample" is read. Every bullet should survive the question *"so what — by how much?"*
> - **Tailor by role.** The same line is weighted differently by a QT, a QR, and a QD desk. Reorder your top three bullets for the column you're aiming at.
> - **The one number to remember:** a single strong, *quantified* project can roughly **double** your screen-pass rate (illustrative) — projects are the great equalizer, and "no projects" is one of the five instant auto-rejects.

A recruiter at a top market maker — I'll call her the screener — has roughly **four hundred** resumes in the queue for a single summer trading internship, and a coffee that's going cold. She is not reading them. She is *filtering* them. For each PDF she gives it something between **six and ten seconds**: her eyes hit the top third of the page, hunt for one or two hard signals — a competition result, a named firm, a quantified line, a GPA at a program she recognizes — and her hand is already moving to *forward* or *reject* before she's consciously decided. Before she even opened the stack, an applicant-tracking system had already silently dropped a third of the submissions for missing keywords or a format it couldn't parse. The candidates in that dropped third never find out. They will tell their friends the market is brutal, and they'll be right, but they'll have the wrong reason.

This is the single most misunderstood document in the entire job search. People treat the resume like an autobiography — a complete, fair account of everything they've done, so the reader can appreciate the full picture. The screener has no interest in the full picture and no time to appreciate anything. She is running a *filter*, and the filter has a brutally simple objective function: in ten seconds, is there enough hard signal here to justify spending the firm's next-stage time on this person? Everything you put on the page either raises that signal density or dilutes it. There is no neutral line. A line that doesn't help is actively hurting, because it's occupying space and attention that a stronger line could have used.

So here is the reframe this whole post is built on, and it's the spine of the series too — *both the job and getting the job are expected-value problems under uncertainty.* Your resume is not a pitch and it's not a brag sheet. It's a **bid for the scarce resource of the screener's attention**, and the way you win that bid is by maximizing the probability that, in her ten-second pass, she finds the one or two signals that make forwarding you the obviously correct call. Figure 1 is the whole strategy in one picture — the signal hierarchy, which is also the cover of this post. Read it once before we go deep, because every section below is an elaboration of it.

![Vertical stack of resume signals from strongest at top to weakest at bottom, with competition wins and research at the top, strong projects and internships in the middle, and GPA coursework and generic skills at the bottom](/imgs/blogs/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen-1.png)

Notice what the hierarchy says and what it doesn't. It does *not* rank you as a person. It ranks your **signals by how cleanly and quickly they prove the thing the firm is trying to buy.** A competition medal sits at the top not because the firm worships olympiads but because it's the single cleanest, most instantly-legible public proof of exactly what the loop tests. A line that says "team player, detail-oriented, fast learner" sits at the bottom not because those traits are bad but because the line *proves nothing* — anyone can type it, so it carries zero information and the screener has trained herself to skip it. Your job is to fill the top third of your page with lines from the top of this hierarchy, and to ruthlessly cut or compress everything from the bottom.

This is the second post in Track C of the series, the recruiting-mechanics track, and it sits between two siblings you should read alongside it: [the quant recruiting calendar and the funnel](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-quant-recruiting-calendar-and-the-funnel) tells you *when* to throw your resume into the pipe and how many of you survive each stage, and [online assessments and screens decoded](/blog/trading/quant-careers/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded) is what happens to you *immediately after* your resume passes — the OA that's waiting on the other side of the forward button. The resume is the gate between them. Let's open it.

## Foundations: what the screen is actually doing

Before we can optimize the resume we have to be precise about the machine it's feeding, because almost every resume mistake comes from imagining the wrong reader. The reader is not a hiring manager carefully weighing your potential. The reader is a *filter*, and the filter has two stages — one made of software, one made of an exhausted human — and your page has to survive both. Figure 4 draws the whole path.

![Pipeline of the resume screen path showing submit one-page PDF flowing through an ATS keyword and format filter, a recruiter ten-second human read, forward to hiring manager, and online assessment, with a silent auto-reject branch for failing any step](/imgs/blogs/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen-4.png)

Let me define every piece of that machine from zero, because each one changes what you should do.

**The ATS (Applicant Tracking System).** Most large firms route applications through software that parses your PDF into structured fields — name, education, skills, experience — and lets recruiters search and filter the pile. Two things matter for you. First, the ATS **parses your file**, and a clever multi-column layout with text in graphics, tables, or sidebars can parse into garbage — your beautifully designed resume becomes a scrambled mess in the recruiter's view, or worse, an empty record. Second, recruiters often **search and filter by keyword**: if the role wants C++ and your resume never says "C++" (because you wrote "low-level systems programming"), a keyword filter can skip you. The ATS is not usually a hard auto-rejecting robot at top quant firms — that's an over-told myth — but it *is* a parsing-and-search layer that punishes weird formatting and missing keywords. The defensive move is boring and effective: a clean, single-column, standard-section PDF with the role's actual nouns in it.

**The ten-second human read.** This is the real filter at most quant firms, and it's the one this post mostly optimizes for. A recruiter or a junior member of the desk gives each surviving resume a single, fast pass. The cognitive reality of that pass is well-documented in eye-tracking studies of recruiters: the gaze lands on the top of the page, scans the most recent and most prominent items, and forms a forward/reject lean in **under ten seconds** — often closer to six. They are not reading sentences. They are *pattern-matching for signal*. This is why position on the page is load-bearing: a brilliant project buried at the bottom of page one (or, fatally, on a page two that no one scrolls to) is, for screening purposes, invisible.

**Signal versus noise.** This is the core concept, so slow down on it. A **signal** is any line that gives the reader real, hard-to-fake information about whether you can do the job — a Codeforces rating, a quantified project result, a competition placement, a named internship, a GPA at a known-hard program. **Noise** is any line that gives the reader no information they didn't already assume — "proficient in Microsoft Office," "passionate about markets," "strong communication skills," an objective statement, a list of every language you've touched once. The screener's whole skill is separating the two at speed, and she does it by *signal density*: how much real information is packed per square inch of the top third of your page. A resume can be completely truthful and still fail because it's truthful *noise* — every line accurate, none of them informative. The single best mental edit you can make is to read each line and ask: *"What does this prove that the reader didn't already assume? And by how much?"* If the answer is "nothing," cut it or quantify it.

**The screen as a probability filter, not a quality judge.** Here is the EV framing that should calm you down. The screen is *not* trying to find the best candidate — it can't, in ten seconds. It's trying to cheaply raise the *probability* that the people it forwards are worth the next stage's expensive time. It is tuned for **low false-positive cost**: forwarding a few mediocre resumes is fine; the firm will catch them in the OA. So the screen errs toward rejecting anything *ambiguous*, because an ambiguous resume has a worse expected value-per-second than a clearly-strong one sitting right behind it in the queue. This is why "make the reader work to find your signal" is fatal: the reader won't work, because the next resume might be clearer and the clock is running. Your resume doesn't have to be the *best* in the pile. It has to clear the bar of "obviously worth ten more minutes" inside ten seconds. That's a much more achievable target, and it's entirely about presentation of signal, not about being a genius.

A few more terms you'll need below. **QT, QR, QD** are the three core seats: **Quant Trader** (calibration, speed, markets intuition, EV under pressure), **Quant Researcher** (finding and validating signals, the science of not fooling yourself), and **Quant Developer** (fast, correct, production systems). **Signal density** is information-per-inch on the page. **The funnel** is the sequence apply → screen → OA → interviews → offer, with savage drop-off at each stage; the resume governs the apply → screen step, and only that step. **Survivorship bias** is the trap of reading advice only from people who got in and concluding the path is simple. Hold onto the funnel idea especially — because the most important thing to internalize is that the resume's job is *narrow*. It does not win the offer. It buys you the *right to be measured by the loop*, and that's all. Optimizing it for anything more than "survive the screen" is wasted effort.

## The signal hierarchy, by role

Figure 1 ranked the signals in general. But the ranking *shifts by role*, and tailoring to that shift is where most of the easy edge lives. The same line — say, "fast mental arithmetic" or "published a paper on diffusion models" — lands as a top signal for one desk and near-noise for another. Figure 3 is the role-by-signal matrix.

![Matrix of resume signals as rows competition wins, mental math, coding, research out-of-sample, and strong projects against role columns QT, QR, and QD, with each cell graded from top signal to minor depending on how that role weights the signal](/imgs/blogs/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen-3.png)

Read the matrix column by column, because each column is a different reader looking for different proof.

**Quant Trader (QT).** The market makers — Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, IMC, Akuna, Citadel Securities — screen for raw problem-solving, calibration, and speed. So the top signals for a QT resume are, in order: a **competition result** that proves raw reasoning under a clock (a math olympiad, Putnam, a strong trading-game finish like a good IMC Prosperity placement), then anything that proves **mental-math and EV intuition** (a poker record, a quant trading competition, a relevant project), then strong quantitative projects. Heavy C++ or a deep research paper is *nice* but not the headline — a QT screener barely weights your distributed-systems internship. If you're aiming at trading, your top line should be your hardest competition result or your most markets-flavored project, full stop.

**Quant Researcher (QR).** The systematic funds and pod-shop research desks — Two Sigma, D. E. Shaw, WorldQuant, the research seats at the pods — screen for the science of finding real effects and *not fooling yourself*. So the top signals are: **research and publications** (especially anything empirical or ML-flavored), then **strong projects with out-of-sample discipline** — a backtest you built with proper [purged cross-validation](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/financial-ml-pipeline-purged-cv-quant-research), a Kaggle medal (which is verifiable proof you can model noisy data without overfitting the leaderboard), a real signal you researched and *killed*. Competition results still help a lot, especially Kaggle. Mental-math speed barely registers here — a QR screener doesn't care that you can multiply two-digit numbers in your head. If you're aiming at research, lead with your strongest research artifact and your most rigorous project.

**Quant Developer (QD).** The low-latency and infrastructure desks — Jump, HRT, Citadel Securities, the dev seats everywhere — screen for engineering ability. Top signals: a high **competitive-programming rating** (a 2000+ Codeforces, an ICPC regional or World Finals appearance), strong **systems projects** with measurable engineering outcomes (a low-latency matching engine with benchmarks, a meaningful open-source contribution to a performance-sensitive C++ project), and **coding-depth** evidence. Research papers are neutral-to-mildly-negative ("does this person want to build, or to publish?"). Mental math is irrelevant. If you're aiming at dev, lead with your rating and your hardest systems project.

Notice the structural lesson: **competition wins and strong quantified projects are the two signals that sit near the top of *every* column.** They're the universal currency. Everything else is role-conditional. So if you're going to invest in building one signal, a competition result or a strong project is the highest-EV bet because it transfers across all three doors — and if you're going to *tailor* a resume, the move is to reorder your top three bullets so the role's top-signal row leads. We'll make this concrete with Maya and Wei shortly.

One more honesty note on **GPA and coursework**, which the matrix leaves out because it's a *qualifier*, not a top signal. A high GPA at a known-hard program (the kind that front-loads real probability, [linear algebra](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/probability-spaces-random-variables-math-for-quants), and proof-based math) is a real positive signal — it's a credible, if noisy, proxy for "this person can handle hard quantitative work." It belongs near the top of your page *if it's strong*. But it's a *supporting* signal, not a headline: a 4.0 with no projects and no competition result is a weaker resume than a 3.5 with a Kaggle medal and a quantified backtest, because the GPA proves general capability while the project proves the *specific* capability the job tests. Lead with the specific.

## Anatomy of a strong bullet: action + tool + quantified result

The single most common reason a *qualified* candidate fails the screen is not a weak background — it's weak *bullets*. They did real, impressive work and then described it in lines that prove nothing. Fixing your bullets is the highest-leverage hour you can spend on your resume, and the formula is mechanical. A strong bullet has three parts: an **action** (what you did, as a strong verb), a **tool or method** (how — the specific tech, which doubles as a keyword), and a **quantified result** (the outcome, as a number the reader can grade). Figure 2 puts the weak and strong versions of the same work side by side.

![Before-after figure with a weak-bullet column on the left showing vague lines like worked on a machine-learning project and used Python for data analysis, and a strong-bullet column on the right showing the same work quantified as built a momentum signal in Python Sharpe 1.4 out-of-sample and ranked top 2 percent of 4300 in a Kaggle competition](/imgs/blogs/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen-2.png)

Look at the transformation, because it's the same project both times. "Worked on a machine-learning project" tells the reader a *topic* and nothing else — no action of yours, no method, no outcome, no scale. It's the resume equivalent of "stuff happened." Now: "Built a momentum signal in Python; Sharpe 1.4 out-of-sample on 5 years of daily equities data." Same project. But now the reader knows you *built* it (not "was on a team that touched it"), in *Python* (a keyword and a tool claim), and that it *worked* — Sharpe 1.4 is a number she can grade, "out-of-sample" tells her you have the discipline that separates real research from leaderboard-chasing, and "5 years of daily equities" gives scale. That single bullet now does the work of proving raw skill *and* the specific quant-research virtue of out-of-sample honesty. It's a top-of-hierarchy signal where the weak version was noise.

The mechanical edit, line by line:

- **Lead with a strong, specific verb.** Built, designed, implemented, optimized, derived, traded, benchmarked, shipped. Not "worked on," "helped with," "was responsible for," "involved in." Passive and vague verbs are a tell that you didn't own the work.
- **Name the tool or method.** "in Python / C++ / PyTorch," "via vectorization," "using a purged walk-forward backtest." This is where your ATS keywords live *naturally* — you don't keyword-stuff, you just describe what you actually used, and the keyword falls out.
- **End with a quantified result.** This is the part everyone skips and the part that matters most. A number — a Sharpe, a latency, a runtime cut, a placement, an accuracy, a scale — turns a claim into evidence. If you genuinely can't quantify the *outcome*, quantify the *scale* or the *input*: "processed 2 TB of tick data," "across 4,300 entrants," "in a 48-hour competition." A number anywhere in the bullet beats no number.

The discipline that makes every bullet strong is to subject each one to two questions: **"So what?"** (does the reader care about this outcome?) and **"By how much?"** (is there a number?). If a bullet survives both, keep it. If it fails "so what," cut it. If it fails "by how much," go find the number — and the number almost always exists; people just don't bother to dig it out. You ran the backtest; you *have* the Sharpe. You optimized the code; you *know* it got faster, you just have to go measure by how much. The number is the difference between a line the screener skims past and a line that stops her thumb on the forward button.

#### Worked example: rewriting a weak bullet into a strong, quantified one

Let me walk one bullet through the whole transformation, the way I'd do it editing a real resume, so you can see the signal density climb at each step.

Maya — our recurring character, a math undergrad aiming at trading — has this on her draft: **"Did a research project on options pricing."** Let's grade it. So what? She did *a project*, on *a topic*. By how much? No number, no action, no method. As a signal it's nearly worthless — it tells the screener that Maya has heard of options, which the screener already assumed from the fact that Maya applied to a trading firm. In the ten-second read, this line is *skipped*. It occupies a row and earns nothing.

Now we dig. I ask Maya what she actually did. It turns out she **implemented a binomial-tree options pricer in Python, validated it against the Black–Scholes closed form, and built a small tool that computed implied volatility from market quotes for 200 listed options** — and she found her implied-vol surface had a clear smile that matched the textbook. Every one of those facts is a signal she left on the cutting-room floor.

The rewrite: **"Built a binomial-tree options pricer in Python (500 lines), validated to within 0.3% of the Black–Scholes price, and back-solved implied volatility across 200 listed options to reconstruct the volatility smile."** Grade it again. So what? She *built* a pricer, *validated* it (proving she checks her own work — a calibration virtue), and recovered a *real market structure*. By how much? 500 lines (scale), 0.3% (precision — a number a quant immediately respects), 200 options (scale). This bullet now proves she can code, that she understands the pricing math, that she validates against ground truth, and that she's touched real market data. It went from skipped to *forwarded* — the same project, the same Maya, a completely different signal.

Here's the EV of that edit. Maya did the work either way; the rewrite cost her twenty minutes of remembering and measuring. If that single bullet lifts her per-application screen-pass probability even from an illustrative 20% to 30%, then across 25 applications she goes from `25 × 0.20 = 5` expected interviews to `25 × 0.30 = 7.5` — two and a half extra shots at the loop, for twenty minutes of work, on a project she'd already finished. That is the highest hourly-rate work in the entire job search.

*A weak bullet hides the work you actually did; the rewrite isn't embellishment, it's the simple act of putting the numbers back in that were true the whole time.*

## What auto-rejects you

There's an asymmetry in the screen that you have to respect: a strong signal *raises* your odds, but a single flaw can *zero* them, instantly, before any signal is even read. The screener is filtering at speed, and certain flaws are so cheap to detect and so predictive of "not worth the time" that hitting one ends the read on the spot. Figure 6 is the auto-reject list and why each one is fatal.

![Grid of the auto-reject list with two columns, the flaw and why it kills you, listing typos and broken formatting, fluff and buzzwords, no real projects, and irrelevance and padding, each paired with the reason a screener instantly rejects on it](/imgs/blogs/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen-6.png)

Walk the list, because each entry is a thing you can fully control, which means each is an own-goal if you trip it.

**Typos and broken formatting.** This is the most avoidable and the most fatal, and quant firms are *less* forgiving of it than most industries — for a good reason. The job is precision under pressure: a trader who fat-fingers an order or a researcher who flips a sign in a backtest costs real money. A typo on the one document you had unlimited time to polish is read as direct evidence that you're careless with detail. It doesn't matter that it's "just a typo" — the screener isn't grading your spelling, she's reading the typo as a *signal about how you work*, and it's a terrible one. Broken formatting (a layout that parses into garbage in the ATS, inconsistent fonts, misaligned dates, a PDF that's actually an image) sends the same message and can also literally make your resume unreadable. The fix is non-negotiable: proofread it three times, have two other people read it, and run it through the actual application portal to see how it parses.

**Fluff and buzzwords.** "Passionate about financial markets." "Results-driven team player." "Excellent communication and analytical skills." "Synergy." These lines are pure noise — zero information — and worse, they're a *negative* signal, because their presence tells the screener you don't understand what this resume is for. The space they occupy is space a real signal could have used, and the ten-second clock is running. A resume thick with fluff reads as a resume that has nothing harder to say. Cut every line that you could copy onto a stranger's resume without it becoming false. If "detail-oriented" would be equally true on anyone's page, it proves nothing on yours.

**No real projects.** This one is subtle because it's an absence, not a flaw, but it's just as fatal — and it's the most fixable. A resume with coursework and a GPA but *no projects* is a resume of pure potential with nothing to verify. The screener has only your claim that you can do the work and no evidence. Compare that to the resume right behind it in the queue that has one quantified project, which is *proof*. The project-less resume loses that comparison every time, because the screen rewards verifiable signal and a project is the cheapest verifiable signal a student can build. We'll spend the next section on why projects are the great equalizer; for now, internalize that *zero projects* is close to an auto-reject for anyone past their first year, no matter how high the GPA.

**Irrelevance and padding.** A resume padded to fill space with high-school awards, unrelated retail jobs given full bullet treatment, a list of forty technologies, or a second page of low-signal filler *dilutes your signal density* — it forces the screener to wade through noise to find your two real signals, and she won't wade, she'll reject. Relevance is a ruthless filter: every line competes for space against your strongest lines, and a weak-but-true line that pushes a strong line lower on the page is actively costing you. This is why the one-page constraint is a *gift*, not a limitation — it forces the density that the screen rewards. If it doesn't raise your signal for *this role*, it doesn't go on the page.

The meta-point: these aren't five different problems, they're one problem — **anything that lowers signal density or signals carelessness ends the read.** The screen is a low-tolerance filter precisely because the firm can afford to be: there are four hundred resumes and three forward slots, so it can reject on the faintest negative signal and still fill the funnel. Your defense is to give it *no* reason to reject, then *one clear reason* to forward.

#### Worked example: how a single strong project changes the pass odds

Let me price the project effect directly, because it's the most actionable number in this post, using the illustrative model in Figure 5.

![Bar chart of illustrative resume-screen pass rates rising from a generic resume with no projects at about 10 percent, through relevant coursework only, one strong quantified project, a second strong project, and a competition result at about 65 percent, flagged illustrative](/imgs/blogs/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen-5.png)

These are illustrative teaching rates — a model of *relative* lift, not measured per-firm data (there's no public dataset of resume-screen pass rates, but the qualitative direction is well-established across this series). Read them as "roughly how much each signal moves the needle," not as promises.

Start with a resume that has a solid GPA, relevant coursework, and **no projects**: an illustrative **10%** screen-pass rate. The screener has nothing to verify, so the resume loses most head-to-head comparisons. Now add **one strong, quantified project** — Maya's options pricer, say. The pass rate jumps to an illustrative **38%**, roughly *3.8×*, and most of that jump comes from a single line, because that line converts the resume from "claims of potential" to "evidence of skill." Add a **second** strong project (a different domain — a systems project, or a backtest) and it climbs to ~**48%**: diminishing returns, because the screener already believes you can build, so the second project mostly adds breadth. Add a **competition result** on top and it reaches ~**65%**, because the competition is the cleanest signal of all.

Now the EV. Suppose Maya applies to **25** firms. With the no-projects resume at 10%, she expects `25 × 0.10 = 2.5` first-round interviews — thin enough that a single bad week of variance leaves her with nothing. With one strong project at 38%, she expects `25 × 0.38 = 9.5` interviews — *nearly four times* the shots at the loop, from building **one** project. And the project does double duty: the work of building it is the same work that makes her perform once she's in the room. The lift is enormous and the cost is one project. This is why "no projects" is an auto-reject and "one strong project" is the single best return on effort available to a student: the first project is worth more than the GPA, the coursework, and the soft-skills section combined.

*The first strong project doesn't just add a line — it changes the kind of document you're submitting, from a claim of potential into a piece of evidence, and the screen rewards evidence by a multiple.*

## Tailoring by role

We saw in the matrix that each desk weights signals differently. Tailoring is the act of *acting* on that — reordering and re-emphasizing your resume so the top third speaks to the specific column you're applying to. It's the cheapest edge in the screen, because it costs almost nothing (you're not adding work, just resequencing it) and it directly raises the probability the screener finds her top-signal in the first two seconds.

Tailoring does **not** mean having ten wildly different resumes or lying about your background. It means having one strong base resume and a small number of *role variants* that reorder the same true content. The mechanics:

**Reorder your top three bullets to lead with the role's top-signal row.** For a QT application, your competition result and your most markets-flavored project go first. For a QR application, your research and your most rigorous out-of-sample project go first. For a QD application, your Codeforces rating and your hardest systems project go first. Same lines, different order — and since the screener reads top-down and quits early, order is most of the battle.

**Trim the skills section to the role.** A QD resume should foreground C++, systems, and performance tooling; a QR resume should foreground Python, the ML stack, and statistics; a QT resume can keep the skills section short and let the competition and games signals carry it. Listing every language you've touched is a padding auto-reject *and* it dilutes the keyword match — a focused skills line that mirrors the role's stack parses cleaner in the ATS and reads cleaner to the human.

**Mirror the role's nouns.** If the posting says "C++," your resume should say "C++," not "low-level systems programming." If it says "alpha research," your project bullet should use the word "signal" or "alpha." This isn't keyword-stuffing — it's describing your real work in the role's own vocabulary, which helps the keyword search find you and helps the human pattern-match you to the role instantly.

The honest limit on tailoring: it reorders and re-emphasizes; it cannot manufacture a signal you don't have. If you have no research and you're applying to a research-scientist seat, tailoring won't save you — you need the underlying signal (and the "build your signal" section below is for exactly that case). Tailoring is a multiplier on signal you already possess, not a substitute for it. But for a candidate with a real, mixed background — which is most strong candidates — the reorder is free EV, and skipping it is leaving interviews on the table.

#### Worked example: ranking a candidate's signals to fit one page

Tailoring and the one-page constraint collide in a concrete problem: you have *more* true, decent content than fits on one dense page, and you have to *cut*. Let me work it as a ranking problem, because that's what it is.

Maya's raw inventory, for a QT application, contains: (1) a top-500 Putnam finish; (2) the binomial-tree options pricer project; (3) a momentum-signal backtest with Sharpe 1.2 out-of-sample; (4) a 3.9 GPA in math at a strong program; (5) a software-engineering internship at a mid-size tech firm; (6) president of the math club; (7) a part-time tutoring job; (8) "skills: Python, C++, R, SQL, LaTeX, Git, Excel, Bloomberg"; (9) a high-school national-olympiad bronze; (10) "passionate about quantitative trading."

A page fits maybe seven or eight real lines of signal once you account for the education block and headers. So we rank by the QT column of the matrix and cut from the bottom.

**Keep, top to bottom:** (1) the Putnam finish — top QT signal, leads the page; (3) and (2) the two quantified projects — the backtest first (markets-flavored, has a Sharpe) then the pricer; (4) the GPA and program — strong supporting signal, lives in the education block at the top; (9) the high-school olympiad — a real competition line, kept but compressed to one line since it's older; (5) the SWE internship — kept but trimmed to one quantified bullet, because for QT it's a *supporting* signal (proves she can ship), not a headline.

**Cut or compress:** (6) math-club president becomes a half-line under activities at most, or goes — it's a soft signal that doesn't prove the job; (7) the tutoring job is cut entirely (irrelevant, would be padding); (8) the skills list is *trimmed* to "Python, C++, SQL" — the three that matter for QT — because eight tools is padding and dilutes the match; (10) "passionate about quantitative trading" is *deleted on sight* — pure fluff, an auto-reject trigger.

The result is a one-page resume whose top third is Putnam → backtest → pricer → GPA, which is exactly the QT top-signal sequence from the matrix. Maya didn't lose anything real; she *concentrated* her signal by cutting the noise that was diluting it. The same inventory, ranked for a QR application, would lead with the backtest and the pricer (research-flavored) and demote Putnam slightly; for QD, it would foreground the C++ skill and the SWE internship and the pricer's engineering. One inventory, three rankings, three dense pages.

*Fitting one page isn't about cramming — it's about ranking your signals by the role and cutting from the bottom until only the lines that prove the job remain.*

## Projects: the great equalizer

I've called projects "the great equalizer" twice now, so let me make the full case, because for anyone without a top-school name or a competition medal, projects are the most reliable way past the screen — and they're available to everyone, requiring only time and skill, not pedigree or luck.

Here's the mechanism. The screen is trying to verify the skill the job needs, and most resume lines are *unverifiable claims*: a GPA is a noisy proxy, a school name is a correlation, "proficient in Python" is an assertion. A **project with a measurable outcome is verifiable evidence.** It says: here is a thing I built, here is what it does, here is the number that proves it works, and (crucially) you can check it — the code is on GitHub, the result is reproducible, the competition placement is public. A project converts your resume from a *claim about your potential* into a *demonstration of your skill*, and the screen rewards demonstration over claim by a wide margin (Figure 5's 10%-to-38% jump is that reward).

This is why projects equalize. A candidate from a non-target school with no referral faces a brutal screen *on pedigree alone* — the screener has no school name to anchor on and no warm intro to trust. But a strong, public, quantified project is **school-independent**: it doesn't matter where you learned to build a low-latency order-book matching engine with benchmarked throughput, only that you did and anyone can verify it. The project substitutes directly for the pedigree the candidate lacks. The same logic runs through the [do-you-need-a-PhD question](/blog/trading/quant-careers/do-you-need-a-phd-the-backgrounds-that-get-hired): the credential is a proxy, and a verifiable artifact proves the underlying skill the proxy was only ever standing in for.

What makes a project *strong* (as opposed to a tutorial you followed):

- **It's relevant to the role.** A QT project touches markets, EV, or trading games; a QR project touches signals, backtests, or modeling with out-of-sample rigor; a QD project touches performance, systems, or low-latency. A generic to-do app proves nothing for any of these.
- **It has a measurable outcome.** A Sharpe, a latency in microseconds, a throughput, an accuracy, a runtime reduction, a competition placement. The number is what makes it evidence rather than a claim.
- **It's verifiable.** Public code, a writeup, a reproducible result, a public leaderboard rank. A private claim ("I built a trading bot that made money") is nearly worthless to a screener because it can't be checked and everyone says it.
- **It shows judgment, not just effort.** The best QR projects show you *killed* a bad idea or caught your own overfitting — the [kill-your-idea discipline](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/quant-interview-process-strategy-how-to-prepare) that research actually rewards. A writeup that says "this signal looked great in-sample but decayed out-of-sample, so I cut it" is a *stronger* signal than one that claims a magic Sharpe, because it proves the calibration the job needs.

The strategic read: if you're missing the top-of-hierarchy signals (no competition medal, no publications, not from a target school), **the highest-EV thing you can build is a project**, because it's the one top-half signal that's purely a function of effort and skill. You can't retroactively win an olympiad, and a PhD is a five-year detour, but you *can* spend two months building a benchmarked matching engine or a rigorous backtest, and that single artifact can roughly double your screen-pass rate. Projects are the door that's open to everyone, and far too few people walk through it.

## How to build your resume signal

Everything above is diagnostic — how the screen reads you. This section is prescriptive — what to do about it, in priority order, depending on where you're starting. The organizing principle is the hierarchy: **build the highest signal you don't yet have, then present it densely.**

**If you have strong signals but a weak resume (the most common case):** you're leaving interviews on the table for free. Do the bullet rewrite on every line — action + tool + quantified result, subjected to "so what / by how much." Reorder to lead with your top-signal-for-the-role. Cut the fluff and the padding ruthlessly. Trim to one dense page. Run it through an actual ATS portal to check parsing. This is a weekend of work and it can double your pass rate without you acquiring a single new skill, because the signal was already there — you just buried it. Most "my resume isn't getting responses" problems are this problem.

**If you have weak signals (early, or non-traditional):** build a project, then another, then chase a competition if you're young enough. A project is two months and roughly doubles your odds (Figure 5). A second project adds breadth. A competition result — a Codeforces grind to 2000+, a Kaggle medal, a strong showing in a public trading game like IMC Prosperity — is the strongest single line you can add and the work to earn it is the same work that makes you pass the loop. These are the signals that move you from the bottom of the hierarchy to the top, and they're available to anyone with time and discipline.

**If you have the signals and the resume but no responses:** the problem is probably *targeting or timing*, not the document — which means it's a [recruiting-calendar-and-funnel](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-quant-recruiting-calendar-and-the-funnel) problem, not a resume problem. Are you applying when the firm is actually recruiting (quant timelines are early and brutal)? Are you applying to enough firms (the funnel math demands volume)? Are you applying to the *right* seat for your signals? A great resume thrown into a closed pipe or at the wrong door gets the same silence as a bad resume. Diagnose which problem you actually have before you keep rewriting bullets that are already strong.

The format checklist, because presentation is half the screen and it's fully controllable:

- **One page.** For students and early-career, always. The constraint forces density. A second page is read by almost no one and signals you can't prioritize.
- **One column, standard sections, parseable PDF.** Education, experience, projects, skills. No graphics-as-text, no sidebars that scramble in the ATS. Pretty is worthless if it parses to garbage.
- **Reverse-chronological, most impressive items highest.** The screener reads top-down and quits early; spend your best position on your best signal.
- **Numbers everywhere.** Every bullet should have a quantity if you can find one. The number is the signal.
- **Proofread three times, then have two people read it.** A typo is an auto-reject in a precision industry. This is the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy.

#### Worked example: Maya and Wei each prioritize their one-pager

Let me close the prescriptive section by running both recurring characters through their resume decisions, because they start from opposite ends of the hierarchy and the *same principle* routes them to different pages.

**Maya — math undergrad, aiming at QT — has a competition and a project.** Her signal inventory tops out with the Putnam finish (a top-of-hierarchy competition result) and two quantified projects. Her resume problem is *not* acquisition — she has strong signals — it's *presentation and ranking*. So her playbook is: lead the page with Putnam, follow with the markets-flavored backtest (Sharpe quantified) and the options pricer, anchor the education block with her GPA-at-a-strong-program, trim the SWE internship to one quantified line, and delete the fluff and the eight-item skills list down to three. Her top third now reads Putnam → backtest → pricer → GPA, which *is* the QT top-signal sequence. With a strong-but-unranked resume her illustrative screen-pass rate might be 38%; with the competition leading and the bullets quantified, it climbs toward the ~60–65% band. Across 20 applications that's the difference between ~7.6 and ~13 expected interviews — from a weekend of reordering and rewriting, zero new skills. Maya's lesson: *when your signals are strong, the entire game is ranking and density.*

**Wei — physics PhD, aiming at QR — has papers.** His signal inventory tops out with two published papers (top-of-hierarchy research) and the five-year research training that *is* the QR job. His resume problem is the opposite of Maya's in one respect and the same in another. Same: he must quantify and rank — lead with the publications, then his most rigorous empirical project, framed in out-of-sample / model-validation language that a QR screener immediately respects. Different: his risk is that a PhD resume often *reads as academic* — dense with publication-list formatting, jargon-heavy, no engineering or markets signal — and a QR screener is also checking "can this person code cleanly and resist overfitting?" So Wei's playbook adds: surface a concrete coding signal (a quantified data-pipeline or modeling project with a real result), translate his research into the *role's* nouns (signal, backtest, out-of-sample, validation), and cut the fully-academic lines that don't prove the job. He should also keep it to **one page** — the academic-CV instinct is to list everything, and a six-page CV is an auto-reject for an industry seat. With a raw academic CV his illustrative pass rate at QR seats might be 30% (strong research, but reads academic and unranked); with the translation and the coding signal surfaced, it climbs toward the ~50–60% band. Wei's lesson: *when your signal is real but speaks the wrong dialect, the game is translation — say your research in the language the desk screens for, and prove the skills the PhD didn't.*

The two playbooks are mirror images of the same rule. Maya ranks abundant strong signals; Wei translates and de-academicizes a strong-but-mismatched one. Both are doing the identical underlying thing: **finding their top-of-hierarchy signal for the target role and front-loading it on one dense, quantified, parseable page.**

*Whether your problem is ranking abundance or translating a mismatch, the move is the same — find the line that most cleanly proves the job, and put it in the first thing the screener reads.*

## Common misconceptions

Four beliefs cause more self-inflicted screen failures than any actual skill gap. Each one is a way of optimizing the resume for the wrong objective.

**Myth 1: "Length equals depth — a longer resume shows I've done more."** Reality: length is *negatively* correlated with passing the screen. The screen rewards signal *density*, and a second page dilutes density and signals you can't prioritize. The ten-second read happens in the top third of page one regardless of how many pages follow — so everything past that top third is mostly unread, and everything that pushes your best signal *out* of that top third is actively hurting you. A dense one-pager beats a comprehensive three-pager every time, because the screen isn't measuring how much you've done, it's measuring how quickly it can find proof that you can do the job. The one-page constraint is a forcing function for the density the screen wants. (The PhD academic-CV instinct to list every paper, talk, and poster is the most common version of this mistake.)

**Myth 2: "List every skill — more keywords, more matches."** Reality: a skills list of twenty technologies is *padding*, and padding lowers signal density and triggers the irrelevance auto-reject. It also *weakens* your keyword match, counterintuitively: a focused skills line that mirrors the role's actual stack parses cleaner and pattern-matches harder than a wall of forty tools the reader has to wade through. And listing a skill you can't defend in the interview is a *liability* — if "expert in C++" is on your page and you fumble a basic C++ question, you've converted a neutral line into a negative one. List the skills that are real, relevant to the role, and defensible. Three sharp skills beat twenty fuzzy ones.

**Myth 3: "A finance or business degree is the best preparation, so I should foreground it."** Reality: for QT/QR/QD seats, a finance degree is one of the *weaker* signals, and foregrounding "finance coursework" over a quantitative project actively hurts you. The screen tests math, code, and calibration — the feeder fields are math, CS, physics, statistics, EE, and ORFE — and a finance degree teaches the *institutions* of markets, which the job assumes you'll absorb on the desk in a week. So a resume that leads with "Corporate Finance, Financial Modeling, Valuation" coursework is leading with low-signal-for-this-role content. Lead instead with the quantitative project, the competition, the math, the code. (This connects directly to [the backgrounds that actually get hired](/blog/trading/quant-careers/do-you-need-a-phd-the-backgrounds-that-get-hired): the credential is a proxy, and the *quantitative* proxies are the ones the screen weights.)

**Myth 4: "GPA is everything — a perfect GPA guarantees the screen."** Reality: GPA is a *supporting* signal, not a sufficient one. A high GPA at a hard program is a real positive — keep it near the top — but it's a noisy proxy for general capability, and the screen prefers *specific*, *verifiable* proof of the job's skills. A 4.0 with no projects loses to a 3.5 with a Kaggle medal and a quantified backtest, because the projects prove the thing the job tests and the GPA only correlates with it. Treat GPA as a credential-strength multiplier that supports your real signals, not as the headline. If your GPA is your *only* strong line, you don't have a GPA problem — you have a project problem, and the fix is to go build one.

The thread through all four: each myth optimizes the resume for the wrong objective — completeness, keyword count, credential prestige, academic perfection — when the *only* objective is **maximizing the screener's ten-second probability of finding hard, role-relevant, verifiable signal.** Every time you catch yourself adding a line "to be thorough" or "to look impressive," check it against that objective. If it doesn't raise signal density for this role, it's not helping you, no matter how true or impressive it is in the abstract.

## How it plays out

Let me ground all of this in what the page actually looks like and what actually happens to it, generic-versus-tailored, with no real personal data. Figure 7 is the layout contrast.

![Before-after figure contrasting a generic one-pager that leads with an objective paragraph soft skills every tool ever used and coursework with projects buried, against a tailored signal-dense one-pager that leads with a competition result and GPA, three quantified project bullets, a trimmed role-relevant skills line, and one dense page with no filler](/imgs/blogs/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen-7.png)

**The generic one-pager** — the left column — is what most applicants submit, and it fails not because it's false but because it's *organized for the wrong reader*. It opens with an objective statement ("Seeking a challenging role in quantitative finance...") that the reader skips, follows with a soft-skills list that proves nothing, lists every language and tool ever touched (padding that dilutes the match), dumps coursework with no outcomes, and — fatally — buries the actual projects low on the page or on a page two. A screener gives it ten seconds, finds no hard signal in the top third, and rejects. The candidate may be perfectly qualified; the resume just never *showed* it in the window the screener was looking.

**The tailored one-pager** — the right column — is organized for the ten-second read. The top line carries the strongest role-relevant signal (a competition result, with GPA-at-a-strong-program in the education block right below). Three quantified project bullets sit in the top half, each action + tool + number. The skills line is trimmed to the role's actual stack. The whole thing is one dense page, no filler, no objective statement, no fluff. The screener's eye lands on the competition result, then the quantified projects, and her hand is on *forward* before she's finished the first project bullet. Same candidate, same true content — but the second page front-loads the signal the screen is hunting for, and it survives.

This is the entire job of the resume, made literal. Watch how it fits the funnel: of four hundred submissions, the ATS parse-and-keyword layer and the ten-second human read together forward perhaps a few dozen to the next stage. Your resume's role in that whole machine is *one filter* — the apply → screen step — and its success condition is binary: forwarded, or silently rejected. It does not get you the job. It does not even get you the interview, really — it gets you the *OA*, the [online assessment](/blog/trading/quant-careers/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded) that's the next filter, where your actual skill starts to be measured. The resume just buys you the right to be measured. That's why the honest framing is so liberating: you don't need a perfect resume, you need one that *clears the bar of obviously-worth-the-next-step inside ten seconds* — a much lower, much more controllable target than "be the best candidate."

And the comp reality, since the series is honest about it: the seats this resume is competing for are extraordinarily well-paid — reported new-grad on-target total comp at the top tier runs roughly **\$450k–\$650k**, with internships at Jane Street annualizing near **\$300k** and Citadel/Citadel Securities interns at **\$4,300–\$5,800/week** for 2026 (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, firm pages, as of 2025–2026; these are survivorship-biased and bonus-driven, and the bonus does not repeat). Which is exactly *why* the screen is so brutal: those numbers draw thousands of applicants per seat, the firm can reject on the faintest negative signal and still fill the funnel, and the resume is the first place that volume gets cut. The headline comp is the reason the ten-second filter exists. Respect the filter, and you get the shot at the comp; ignore it, and you join the silent third the ATS dropped before a human ever looked.

#### Worked example: pricing the whole resume as one EV decision

Let me close by pricing the resume effort itself as an expected-value decision, because that's the spine of this series — *getting the job is an EV calculation under uncertainty, and so is deciding how much to invest in each step.*

Maya is deciding how to spend her next two weekends. Option A: send her current, un-tailored, fluff-heavy resume to 25 firms and move on to interview prep. Option B: spend the two weekends doing the full resume rewrite — quantify every bullet, rank for the role, cut the padding, fix the format — before sending to the same 25 firms.

Under Option A, her illustrative screen-pass rate is ~20% (strong content, poor presentation), so she expects `25 × 0.20 = 5` first-round interviews. Under Option B, the rewrite lifts her to an illustrative ~38% (the same content, now presented densely and ranked), so she expects `25 × 0.38 = 9.5` interviews. The rewrite *costs* two weekends and produces **4.5 additional expected shots at the loop** — and interviews are the scarce resource, because once Maya's in the room her trading-game and mental-math skills convert at a healthy rate. There is almost no other two-weekend investment in the entire job search with that return, because it's pure *presentation* leverage on signal she already has: she's not acquiring skill, she's un-burying it.

Now the second-order point that keeps the resume in its proper place. Even at Option B's 38%, the resume is *only* moving the apply → screen step. Beyond it sits the OA filter, the interview loop, the offer — each with its own conditional probability. So the right amount to invest in the resume is "enough to stop being the bottleneck, then stop." A *perfect* resume that lifts her from 38% to 42% is worth far less than the two weekends it'd take, because the marginal interviews are small and her time is now better spent on the OA and the loop, which are the next filters. The resume's EV is high right up until it's no longer your bottleneck — then the EV of *more* resume work collapses, and you should redeploy to the next stage of the funnel.

*The resume is the highest-EV thing to fix when it's broken and a near-zero-EV thing to keep polishing once it works — its whole value is in clearing one filter, so optimize it to clear, then move to the next one.*

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you catch yourself treating the resume as a *brag sheet* instead of a *filter-survival document* — the moment you start adding lines to look thorough, impressive, or complete, when the only question that matters is whether a tired recruiter can find one hard, role-relevant, verifiable signal in the top third of your page in under ten seconds. The resume's job is narrow and honest: it does not win the offer, it does not even win the interview, it just buys you the right to be measured by the loop. Optimize it for that one job — signal density, quantified bullets, ranked-for-the-role, one parseable page, zero fluff — and then stop, because past the point where it clears the screen, more polish is wasted effort that belongs on the next filter.

So: **rank your signals against the hierarchy, front-load the top ones for the role you're targeting, make every bullet action-plus-tool-plus-number, cut everything that doesn't prove the job, and proofread it like a precision instrument — because in this industry, it is one.** If your signals are strong, the whole game is ranking and density (Maya). If your signal is real but speaks the wrong dialect, the game is translation (Wei). If your signals are weak, the game is building one project, because that single artifact roughly doubles your odds and is open to everyone. And if your strong, dense resume still gets silence, the problem isn't the document — it's targeting or timing, and you should read the funnel.
