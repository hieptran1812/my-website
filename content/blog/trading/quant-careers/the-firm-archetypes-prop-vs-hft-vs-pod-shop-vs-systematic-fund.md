> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — "Quant firm" is not one job. It is four very different businesses, and the difference that matters most to you is whose money is being traded.
>
> - **Prop firms and market makers** (Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, IMC) trade their *own* capital with no outside clients. The edge is the bid-ask spread, the culture tends to be collaborative, and your downside is cushioned by the firm's book.
> - **HFT firms** (Jump, HRT, Citadel Securities) also trade own capital, but the edge is *speed* — they win races measured in microseconds, which makes the job an engineering arms race.
> - **Multi-strat pod shops** (Citadel, Millennium, Point72) manage *outside* money by handing each portfolio manager an independent book with a hard drawdown limit. The pay can be enormous, but a stop-out can end your seat in a single bad month.
> - **Systematic / quant hedge funds** (Two Sigma, D.E. Shaw, WorldQuant) also manage outside money, charge a fee, and pay research from a pool — long horizons, lower variance, more patience.
> - **The one fact to remember:** own-capital firms keep the edge and share the risk; outside-money firms charge a fee and push the risk down to you. That single split predicts your culture, your comp shape, and how you get fired.

Two new graduates, call them **Maya** and **Wei**, finished the same elite math program with nearly identical GPAs. Maya is fast, calibrated, and loves the adrenaline of a live market; she takes an offer from a famous proprietary trading firm. Wei is patient, obsessive about clean data, and would happily spend three weeks chasing down why a backtest looks too good; he takes a seat at a systematic hedge fund. A year later, over coffee, they compare notes and discover they barely have the same profession.

Maya spends her day quoting two-sided markets on a desk full of people who openly debate ideas, and her bonus is a slice of the *whole firm's* trading profit — when she has a quiet quarter, the desk carries her. Wei spends his day building features and running purged cross-validation, his work flows into a shared signal stack, and his bonus comes out of a discretionary pool funded by the fees the fund charges its investors. Neither of them, it turns out, understood at the offer stage how different "a job in quant" can be.

The mistake almost everyone makes when breaking in is to treat the top firms as a single ranked list — Jane Street above Citadel above Two Sigma, as if they were colleges. They are not interchangeable. They are *different businesses with different customers, different risks, and different ways of paying you*. This post is the map. Figure 1 is the whole argument on one page: four archetypes, and the handful of attributes that actually separate them.

![A six-row by five-column comparison grid of four quant firm archetypes showing capital source, edge, holding period, risk to you, and example firms](/imgs/blogs/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund-1.png)

If you remember nothing else, remember the top row of that grid: **whose money is being traded.** Everything else — the culture, the comp shape, the way you can lose your seat — flows downstream from that one fact.

## Foundations: own capital vs outside money, and why it changes everything

Before we can tell the archetypes apart we need a small vocabulary. None of these terms are hard, but they are the load-bearing ideas for the rest of the post, so let us build each from zero.

**Proprietary capital ("prop").** A firm trades *prop* when the money at risk is the firm's own — its partners' and its accumulated profits. There are no outside investors to answer to, no clients whose money you are managing. Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, and IMC are all, at their core, prop firms. The crucial consequence: a prop firm keeps **100% of its trading profit** (minus costs and the bonus pool it pays you) because it never has to hand a cut to an investor. It also eats 100% of the losses, which is exactly why it can afford to be patient with an individual trader having a bad month.

**AUM — assets under management.** When a firm manages money for *other people*, the size of that pile is its AUM. Two Sigma's roughly **\$70 billion** and D.E. Shaw's roughly **\$65 billion** are AUM figures: that is other people's money, parked at the fund to be invested. A prop firm, by contrast, has no AUM in the same sense — it is not managing anyone else's money, so the number that describes it is its trading capital, which firms keep quiet. This is why you will never find a clean "AUM ranking" that puts Jane Street next to Two Sigma. They are not measured on the same axis.

**LP — limited partner.** The "other people" whose money fills a fund's AUM are its limited partners: pension funds, university endowments, sovereign wealth funds, funds-of-funds, and wealthy individuals. They are *limited* partners because their liability is limited to what they invested and they do not run the fund. The fund's managers are the *general* partners (GPs). When you read that a hedge fund "raised capital," it means LPs committed money. LPs can also pull their money out — *redemptions* — which is a pressure a prop firm simply does not face.

**The fee model: "2-and-20" vs the prop payout.** How does a fund that manages outside money get paid? The classic hedge-fund structure is **2-and-20**: a **2% management fee** charged annually on AUM regardless of performance, plus a **20% performance fee** (also called *carry* or *incentive fee*) on the profits the fund generates. On \$10 billion of AUM, the 2% management fee alone is **\$200 million a year** before the fund makes a single dollar of profit for its investors. (Many large funds have moved to *pass-through* expense models where the actual costs of running the strategy — data, compute, salaries — are billed to the fund instead of a flat 2%, but the shape is the same: investors pay to be there, and the firm takes a cut of gains.) A prop firm has **no management fee and no performance fee**, because it has no investors. Instead, it keeps the trading profit and pays its traders a **bonus that is a share of P&L** — profit and loss. Hold onto that contrast; it is the engine behind the whole comp section later.

**P&L.** Profit and loss — the dollars a strategy, a desk, or a trader makes or loses over a period. Almost every comp number in quant is ultimately a function of P&L. "Your bonus is tied to your P&L contribution" is the single most important sentence about pay in this industry.

**The pod / book / risk-limit model.** A *multi-strat* (multi-strategy) fund does not run one big strategy. It hires many **portfolio managers** (PMs), gives each one a slice of capital to run independently — that slice and the positions in it are the PM's **book** — and constrains each book with a hard **risk limit**. The defining feature is the **drawdown stop-out**: if a PM's book loses more than a set fraction of its capital (often something like 5%–10%, varying by firm and seat), the firm forcibly cuts the PM's risk, and a deep enough drawdown means the PM — and often the whole pod's team — is let go. This is the "tight leash" you hear about at Citadel and Millennium. It is a feature, not a bug: it is how the *fund* keeps its own drawdown small even when individual PMs blow up, because the PMs are deliberately built to be uncorrelated with one another.

**Deferred comp.** Big bonuses at hedge funds and some prop firms are often not paid all at once. A chunk is **deferred** — paid out over the following two, three, or four years, and frequently *forfeited if you leave* before it vests. Deferred comp is a retention tool: it raises the cost of quitting and lets the firm claw back pay if your later years go badly. When someone quotes you a headline number, always ask what fraction is cash now versus deferred over years.

**Redemptions and lock-ups — why outside money behaves differently.** Here is a subtle consequence of LP capital that affects you even though it never appears in your offer letter. Because LPs can ask for their money back, a fund that has a bad stretch can face **redemptions** — investors pulling capital — which shrinks the AUM, shrinks the fee revenue, and shrinks the comp pool, sometimes forcing the fund to *deleverage* (cut positions) at the worst possible time. Funds defend against this with **lock-ups** (LPs commit for a year or more) and **gates** (limits on how fast money can leave), but the pressure is real and it has no analogue at a prop firm. A prop firm's capital cannot redeem — it is the firm's own — so a prop desk can sit through a drawdown that would trigger redemptions at a fund. This is one more reason the own-capital archetypes feel structurally calmer to work at: there is no investor who can yank the rug at the bottom.

**The edge.** A firm's *edge* is the durable reason it makes money — the thing competitors cannot easily copy. For a market maker the edge is the **bid-ask spread** captured across enormous volume. For an HFT firm the edge is **speed** — being first to react. For a multi-strat the edge is the **diversification across many uncorrelated PMs**. For a systematic fund the edge is **predictive signals** that forecast returns over days to months. Same industry, four completely different edges. If you want the full unit-economics of each engine, the sibling post [how quant firms actually make money](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money) derives them dollar by dollar; here we care about what each edge means for *you* as an employee.

With that vocabulary in hand, the rest of the post is a tour of the four archetypes — what each is, how it is capitalized, how it makes money, what its culture feels like, what risk it puts on you, and who tends to thrive there. Then we will turn the lens around and ask the question that actually matters: which one should *you* aim for?

## Archetype 1: the prop firm / market maker (own capital, collaborative)

Start with the friendliest archetype, the one that produced Maya's job. A **proprietary trading firm that acts as a market maker** stands ready, all day, to both buy and sell a security, quoting a **bid** (the price at which it will buy) and an **ask** (the price at which it will sell). The gap between them is the **spread**. When a buyer hits the firm's ask and, moments later, a seller hits its bid, the firm has bought low and sold high and pocketed the spread — without ever needing to predict which way the price was going. Do that across millions of instruments and millions of fills a day and the tiny per-trade edge compounds into serious money.

**How they are capitalized.** Own capital, full stop. Jane Street (founded 2000, NYC), Optiver (founded **1986**, Amsterdam), Susquehanna / SIG (founded **1987**, Bala Cynwyd PA, **3,000+** employees), and IMC (founded 1989, Amsterdam) all trade their partners' and accumulated money. There are no LPs, no AUM, no management fee, no 2-and-20. The flip side of having no investors is that there is no fee revenue cushioning a bad year — the firm lives entirely on its trading profit. But because the firm owns the risk, it can spread a single trader's bad month across the whole book rather than firing the trader on the spot.

**How they make money.** The spread, captured at scale, net of **adverse selection** — the risk that the person trading against you knows something you don't, so your "buy low" fills are systematically the ones about to go lower. A good market maker manages adverse selection with inventory limits, fast risk models, and pricing that widens when the market gets dangerous. The edge per trade is minuscule; the volume is gigantic.

**The culture.** This is the archetype most associated with a *collaborative, puzzle-loving* culture. Jane Street is famous for OCaml (a functional programming language), interview puzzles, trading games, and an EV-first decision culture; SIG trains new traders with **poker** and decision theory; Optiver is known for its brutal **mental-math test** (roughly 80 questions in 8 minutes, no calculator). The common thread is that these firms select for *fast, calibrated thinking under uncertainty* and then put you on a desk where the firm's P&L — not your personal book — is the thing being optimized. You are graded, but you are graded as part of a team that shares the upside and absorbs the downside together.

**The risk to you.** Lowest of the four archetypes, for a structural reason: your downside is the firm's downside. A quiet quarter does not flatten your book and walk you out, because there is no individual stop-out leash the way there is at a pod. You can still be managed out for sustained underperformance — these are not charities — but the *month-to-month* risk that a drawdown ends your seat is far lower than at a pod shop. That is the single biggest reason risk-averse strong candidates gravitate here.

**Comp shape.** Flat-ish base, large P&L-driven bonus. Jane Street quotes an "annualized equivalent" base around **\$300k** across its quant-trader, quant-research, ML, and FPGA roles; levels.fyi reports Jane Street quant-trader base around **\$300k** with the average near **\$309k**, the 75th percentile near **\$407k**, and the 90th near **\$512k** (reported 2025). In a strong seat, a big-prop quant trader 2–3 years in can reach roughly **\$1.5M** total (base around \$200k plus a bonus around \$1.3M), and 5-year survivors in strong seats reach \$2M and well beyond — with the loud, important caveat that **the bonus does not repeat automatically**. A \$1.3M bonus year can be a \$300k bonus the next year if the seat or the P&L changes. Always read a headline number as *that year's* number, not an annuity.

**Who thrives here.** Fast, calibrated decision-makers who like markets and like working alongside other sharp people. If you light up at a market-making game — quoting a price, getting hit, updating — and you would rather share a book than be measured alone, this is your archetype. The interview reflects it: expect the [market-making games](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) and the mental-math and probability rounds, not a take-home research case.

One nuance worth flagging, because it confuses candidates: a market maker does not "predict the market." It is largely *direction-agnostic*. Its profit comes from the spread and from managing inventory, not from a view on where prices go — which is why the interview tests calibration and EV reasoning rather than macro opinions. If you walk into a Jane Street or Optiver superday with a thesis on where rates are headed, you have misread the job. The skill being tested is: *given a price someone is willing to trade against you, what is the fair value, how wide should you quote, and how should you update when you get hit?* That is a probability-and-EV skill, not a forecasting one, and it is precisely why this archetype recruits so heavily from competition math and decision-theory backgrounds rather than from finance majors.

#### Worked example: prop-firm economics — why "the firm keeps the edge" still pays you well

The thing newcomers find confusing about prop firms is: *if there is no management fee and no carry, where does my bonus come from?* The answer is the trading P&L itself, and the math is worth doing.

Suppose a prop firm's desk captures an average net edge of **0.2 cents** (a fifth of a cent, after adverse selection) on each share traded, and the desk does **100 million** shares a day. Daily P&L is `0.002 USD × 100,000,000 = \$200,000` a day. Over roughly 250 trading days that is **\$50 million** a year of gross trading profit from that one desk — and the firm keeps essentially all of it, because there is no investor to pay a cut to.

Now the bonus. There is no fixed formula, but a useful way to reason about it: prop firms typically pay out a meaningful slice of the P&L pool to the people who generated it, after the firm covers its costs and its capital. If a five-person desk generated \$50M and the firm allocated, say, **20%** of that to the desk's bonus pool, that is **\$10M** split across the desk — and a strong individual contributor on that desk earning, say, a \$2M share is suddenly very plausible. Notice what is happening: the *absence* of a management fee and carry is exactly why the prop firm can pay generous P&L shares — there is no LP standing between the dollars you earned and the dollars you take home. The firm keeps the edge, but the edge is large and undiluted, so your slice can be too.

*A prop firm doesn't charge a fee, so it never dilutes the edge — which is precisely why a strong trader's P&L share can rival a hedge-fund payout without any outside investor in the way.*

## Archetype 2: the HFT firm (own capital, but the edge is speed)

The second archetype looks like the first from the outside — own capital, no clients, market-making-adjacent — but its edge is so different that it deserves its own box, and it changes who gets hired. **High-frequency trading (HFT)** firms win on **latency**: they react to information faster than anyone else. When a price moves on one exchange, the firm that updates its quotes on the other exchanges first either captures the trade or avoids being picked off. The edge is *being first*, measured in microseconds and increasingly nanoseconds.

**How they are capitalized.** Own capital, like the prop archetype. Jump Trading (founded 1999, Chicago), Hudson River Trading / HRT (founded 2002, NYC), and **Citadel Securities** (the market-making arm, distinct from the Citadel hedge fund) trade their own money. No LPs, no AUM.

**How they make money.** The same spread-capture and short-horizon trading as a market maker, but won through *speed*. The infrastructure is the strategy: co-located servers in the exchange's data center, microwave and laser links between cities, FPGA hardware that reacts in nanoseconds, kernel-bypass networking, and lock-free C++ that never pauses. HRT puts it bluntly: "engineering excellence drives everything." Jump is famously secretive and runs a deep systems / kernel-bypass / lock-free bar.

**The culture.** Engineering-first. The math still matters, but the binding constraint is *can you make the system faster and more reliable than the competitor's system*, so these firms hire and promote strong systems engineers, hardware people, and quants who can write production C++. HRT lets candidates pick a C++ or Python track; Jump and Citadel Securities lean hardest on C++ depth (the memory model, undefined behavior, templates, cache behavior, lock-free data structures) and real-time systems design. If you want the flavor of that interview, the sibling on [C++ for low-latency quant interviews](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/cpp-for-low-latency-quant-interviews) is the technical companion to this section.

**The risk to you.** Medium, and of a particular kind. There is usually no individual drawdown stop-out the way a pod has — you are an engineer or a quant contributing to a firm-owned strategy, not running a personal book that gets flattened. The real risk is *competitive and technological*: the edge is an arms race, and a strategy that prints today can be a net loser tomorrow when a competitor gets faster. Your job security tracks the firm's ability to stay at the frontier. Teams whose strategies get out-competed get reorganized.

**Comp shape.** Very strong, base similar to prop, bonus tied to the strategy's P&L. H1B base disclosures put Jane Street and Five Rings near **\$300k** base and Citadel Securities near **\$257k**; total comp for a productive low-latency quant or engineer at a top HFT firm sits comfortably in the high-six to low-seven figures in good years, with the same warning as everywhere: the bonus tracks the strategy, and strategies decay. Figure 4, later, places the HFT comp shape next to the others.

**Who thrives here.** People who genuinely love systems — who find joy in shaving a microsecond off a hot path, who think in cache lines and lock-free queues, who want the satisfaction of a strategy whose edge is *measurable in how fast your code runs*. If you are a competitive programmer or a systems person more than a markets person, HFT is often the best fit of the four.

#### Worked example: pod-shop math — the payout, the stop-out, and expected tenure

We will detour into the pod archetype's math here because it is the sharpest contrast to the prop and HFT worlds, and because Maya's friend who took a pod offer needs to understand it before she signs.

Suppose a PM at a multi-strat is given a **\$500M** book and a **10% drawdown stop-out** — lose 10% of allocated capital (a \$50M peak-to-trough loss) and the firm flattens the book and likely ends the seat. The PM's payout is a **percentage of the net P&L of their own book** — commonly quoted in the **10%–20%** range, net of the costs the firm charges the pod (financing, data, infrastructure, a share of overhead).

Say the PM has a good year and the book earns **\$40M** net of costs, at a 15% payout. The PM's pay is `0.15 × 40,000,000 = \$6M`. That is the dream number you hear quoted. But now model the *risk*. Pod books are deliberately run at high turnover and tight risk so they stay uncorrelated; a strategy that targets, say, a Sharpe ratio of 2 still has a real probability of a 10% drawdown in any given year. If — illustratively — there is a **25%** annual probability of hitting the stop-out, then expected tenure before a stop-out is roughly `1 / 0.25 = 4 years`, and over a multi-year horizon a *majority* of PMs will eventually be stopped out at least once. When that happens, the payout for that year is **\$0**, the deferred comp from prior years may be at risk, and the PM is job-hunting.

So the honest expected value is two-sided: a fat right tail (the \$6M years) and a hard left tail (the zero-and-fired years). The pod model pays you for *owning* that variance. Compare that to Maya's prop desk, where a bad month is absorbed by the firm's book and she keeps her seat.

*A pod payout is a call option on your own book — huge upside in a good year, but the stop-out is the strike where it expires worthless and takes the seat with it.*

## Archetype 3: the multi-strat pod shop (outside money, independent books, stop-outs)

Now the archetype that produces the biggest numbers *and* the highest personal risk. A **multi-strategy pod shop** is a hedge fund that raises a large pool of **outside LP capital** and deploys it not through one strategy but through **dozens or hundreds of independent portfolio managers**, each running their own book under a tight risk leash. Citadel (founded 1990 by Ken Griffin), Millennium (founded 1989), and Point72 are the canonical examples.

**How they are capitalized.** Outside money. Citadel runs roughly **\$65B** of AUM; Millennium roughly **\$70B**. That money comes from LPs and is the fund's to invest. Because it is outside money, the fund charges fees — modern multi-strats typically use a *pass-through* model where the actual costs of running the platform (data, compute, technology, and the PMs' own comp) are billed to investors, on top of a performance fee. LPs accept this because the multi-strat product they are buying is *low-volatility, market-neutral returns* — a smooth equity curve, not a moonshot.

**How they make money — and the magic of the structure.** This is the most important idea in the archetype. The fund does not need any single PM to be brilliant. It needs **many PMs whose books are uncorrelated with each other**, each running market-neutral (long some names, short others, net flat to the broad market). When you sum many uncorrelated positive-expectancy books, the *noise cancels* and the *edge compounds*, producing a high Sharpe ratio at the fund level even though each individual book is only modestly good. The fund's real product is diversification and risk management, not any one signal. The statistical-arbitrage engine underneath this is derived in [how quant firms actually make money](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money).

Figure 3 shows the structure: one big AUM pool at the top, a central risk-and-financing desk that polices everyone, and PMs below it — each with a hard drawdown limit and their own analysts and engineers. The red node is the failure mode that defines the culture.

![A tree diagram of a multi-strat pod shop with the fund at the top, a central risk desk and two portfolio-manager pods below it each with a drawdown limit, and analyst and engineer nodes under each pod plus a stop-out node](/imgs/blogs/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund-3.png)

**The culture.** High-accountability, sharp-elbowed, hire-and-fire. Because each PM is a measurable profit center with a stop-out, the culture is far more *individually competitive* than a prop firm's shared-book collaboration. PMs guard their signals; teams form and dissolve around books; a pod that hits its drawdown limit is dissolved and its people are out. Citadel's culture is widely described as high-pressure and high-turnover precisely because the structure is built to be ruthless about risk. This is not a moral judgment — it is the design that lets the fund deliver smooth returns to its LPs — but it is a very different daily experience from a collaborative prop desk.

**The risk to you.** The highest of the four archetypes, and it is the structural centerpiece of the whole post. The drawdown stop-out means a *single bad stretch* can end your seat, regardless of your talent or your prior track record. Markets do not care that you are smart; a regime shift can flatten a perfectly reasonable book. The famous compensation is, in large part, the price the fund pays you to *bear that variance on its behalf*. If you cannot stomach the idea that a 10% drawdown could end your job in a month, the pod archetype is the wrong seat for you no matter how big the number on the offer.

**Comp shape.** The fattest right tail of any archetype and the hardest left tail. A pod-shop QR around 4 years of experience might be near **\$575k** (base around \$175k plus a bonus around \$400k) in a standard year; a successful *PM* with a sizable book can clear many millions in a good year, as the worked example showed. But the same PM stopped out earns the base and little or no bonus, and may forfeit deferred comp and lose the seat.

**Who thrives here.** People who want **autonomy and ownership of risk**, who are emotionally durable, and who are confident enough in their own edge to bet their career on it. If you want to run your own book, be paid directly for your own P&L, and you can sleep through a drawdown, the pod is where the uncapped upside lives. If you need stability or hate being measured alone, avoid it. Figure 2 makes the contrast with the prop archetype explicit.

![A before-and-after comparison showing a prop firm trading its own capital as one collaborative book on the left versus a pod shop trading outside money through independent PM books with stop-outs on the right](/imgs/blogs/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund-2.png)

## Archetype 4: the systematic / quant hedge fund (outside money, long-horizon signals)

The fourth archetype is Wei's world, and it is the one most people picture when they hear "quant." A **systematic quant hedge fund** manages **outside LP money** using **predictive signals** held over days to months, built and run by **research scientists** rather than fast-twitch traders. Two Sigma (founded 2001, NYC, roughly \$70B AUM, ~2,000 staff), D.E. Shaw (founded **1988** by David E. Shaw, roughly \$65B AUM — the original "quant king"), and WorldQuant are the archetypes.

**How they are capitalized.** Outside money, charged a fee — historically the **2-and-20** model, though large funds vary the exact terms. The 2% management fee on tens of billions of AUM is, by itself, hundreds of millions of dollars a year of stable revenue that funds the research operation regardless of performance. That stable fee base is *why* these firms can afford long-horizon research that may take a year to pay off — a luxury a pod's stop-out clock does not allow.

**How they make money.** Forecasting. A systematic fund builds **signals** — statistical predictors of future returns — and trades on them across hundreds or thousands of names. The holding period (days to months) is far longer than a market maker's seconds or an HFT firm's microseconds, which means the edge comes from *being right about the future*, not from being fast or from the spread. The enemy is **signal decay**: every signal weakens as capital and competitors crowd into it, so the research engine must constantly generate new ones. WorldQuant industrializes this with its **alpha-factory** model — a distributed network of researchers producing formulaic alphas on the firm's BRAIN / WebSim platform. The craft of building one of these signals is the subject of [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research).

**The culture.** Research-scientist culture: closer to a top ML lab than a trading floor. Long horizons, deep collaboration on a shared signal stack, emphasis on out-of-sample discipline and avoiding overfitting, and a higher density of PhDs than any other archetype (common, though not required — more on that myth below). The pace is slower and more deliberate than a pod or a trading desk. If you would rather spend three weeks getting the cross-validation right than make a fast call under fire, this is your fit.

**The risk to you.** Medium, and gentler than a pod's. There is usually no individual drawdown stop-out firing you in a month; you are a researcher contributing to a shared, diversified book funded by a stable fee base. The risk is slower: your *signals decay*, your research has to keep producing, and a fund-level bad stretch can shrink the comp pool. But the fee cushion and the team structure make month-to-month job risk far lower than a pod's.

**Comp shape.** Strong but lower-variance than a pod or a strong prop seat. A systematic QR's comp is a slice of a discretionary pool funded by the fund's fees, tied to the contribution of the signals you built. levels.fyi (2025) reports Citadel QR median around **\$325k** (L1 around \$336k rising to L3 around \$642k, top reported around \$721k) and Jane Street QR median around **\$250k** (L1 around \$307k up toward \$565k). The numbers are excellent; they simply do not have the pod's two-sided fat tails. Figure 5 traces how a dollar of LP capital becomes a dollar of your bonus.

![A five-layer stack showing money flowing from LP capital at the top through fees, the firm, a discretionary comp pool, down to a researcher's bonus at the bottom](/imgs/blogs/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund-5.png)

**Who thrives here.** Patient researchers who love clean data and statistical rigor, who can kill their own ideas when the out-of-sample evidence says to, and who want a collaborative, lower-variance seat. If you came from a research PhD and the idea of a 10% stop-out clock makes you queasy, the systematic fund is usually the right archetype.

#### Worked example: systematic fund — how 2-and-20 on AUM flows to a researcher's bonus

Wei wants to understand where his bonus actually comes from, because at the offer stage it felt like magic money. Let us trace it.

Take a systematic fund with **\$10B** of AUM. The **2% management fee** is `0.02 × 10,000,000,000 = \$200M` a year, charged regardless of performance. Now suppose the fund returns **12%** gross to investors in a year — `0.12 × 10B = \$1.2B` of profit — and charges a **20% performance fee** on it: `0.20 × 1,200,000,000 = \$240M`. So the firm's fee revenue that year is roughly `200M + 240M = \$440M`.

Out of that, the firm pays its costs (data licences, compute, real estate), keeps a profit for the partners, and funds a **discretionary comp pool** for researchers and staff. Suppose — illustratively — the firm sets aside **30%** of fee revenue for the research comp pool: `0.30 × 440M = \$132M`, split across, say, 150 researchers. The *average* is `132M / 150 ≈ \$880k`, but it is allocated by contribution, not evenly: a researcher whose signals drove a large share of the P&L might get \$2M, while a junior researcher in a quiet year might get \$300k.

Notice how *many layers removed* Wei's bonus is from the dollars he helped earn — LP capital, then fees, then the firm's pool, then his slice. That distance is exactly why systematic-fund comp is **lower-variance** than a pod's: Wei is not paid a direct cut of his own book's P&L the way a pod PM is; he is paid a *share of a pooled, fee-funded pot*, which smooths out both his good years and his bad ones. He gives up the pod's uncapped upside in exchange for not getting flattened by a single drawdown.

*In a systematic fund your bonus is a share of a fee-funded pool, not a cut of your own P&L — which is why it is steadier than a pod's payout and why the stable 2% fee, not your last trade, is what keeps the lights on.*

## A note on the fifth option: bank systematic desks

Before we compare, one more seat deserves a mention because many strong candidates end up there: the **systematic / quantitative trading desk inside an investment bank** (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, and others). These desks do quant market-making, electronic execution, and systematic strategies, but inside a *bank's* balance sheet and regulatory regime. The work can be genuinely quant, the brand is portable, and the entry bar is sometimes a notch lower than the elite prop and pod firms.

The defining difference is *whose balance sheet and whose rules*. A bank is a regulated deposit-taking institution, so a systematic desk inside it trades the bank's capital under capital-adequacy rules (Basel-style risk-weighted-asset charges), the Volcker constraints on proprietary risk, and an internal risk-and-compliance apparatus that a prop firm simply does not carry. That overhead has two effects on you. First, it *caps the edge* — a strategy that consumes a lot of regulatory capital has to clear a higher hurdle to be worth running, so the most aggressive trades a prop firm would happily take never get put on at a bank. Second, it *caps your comp* — bank pay is benchmarked to the bank's pay bands and the city's market, not to a slice of undiluted P&L, and large bonuses are heavily deferred in stock that vests over years. The trade-offs in one line: comp is typically *lower* and *more capped* than at a top prop or pod, the regulatory and capital constraints are heavier, and the culture is more corporate.

What you get in return is a softer floor and a more forgiving ramp. Bank desks rarely have an individual stop-out that fires you in a month; the brand opens doors at the buy-side later; and the structured training and broader exposure (sales-and-trading, structuring, electronic execution) can be a genuinely good education. For many people a bank desk is an excellent *first* quant seat — a place to build a track record and a network before jumping to a prop firm or a fund where the tails are fatter. It is not its own column in the figures only because its economics are a regulated, capped blend of the market-maker and systematic archetypes: own-capital market-making like a prop firm, but inside the fee-and-salary culture of a large institution.

## What each archetype means for your career and your risk

Now zoom out. Once you know whose money a firm trades, you can predict almost everything else about what working there will feel like. Figure 4 makes the comp shape concrete; read it as *shapes*, not as promises about any individual.

![A stacked bar chart of mid-level total compensation by archetype, splitting base salary from on-target bonus, with poor-year downside markers showing the pod shop collapsing to zero on a stop-out](/imgs/blogs/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund-4.png)

Three patterns are worth naming explicitly.

**Base is small and similar; the bonus is the lever.** Across all four archetypes, the base salary sits in a narrow band — roughly **\$175k–\$250k** for a mid-level quant, anchored by Jane Street's ~\$300k for its broad roles and Citadel Securities' ~\$257k base disclosure. The base is the *contractual, repeatable* part of your pay. The bonus is where the archetypes diverge by an order of magnitude, and the bonus is **variable, P&L-driven, and does not repeat automatically**. This is the single most misunderstood fact about quant comp: a quoted "\$1.5M total" is a *bonus year*, and the next year could be a third of that if the seat or the market changes.

**The variance of the bonus is the price of the upside.** Look at the amber downside markers in Figure 4. The prop and systematic archetypes keep a floor even in a bad year — the firm's book or the fee base carries you. The pod archetype's downside is **zero bonus and a lost seat** on a stop-out. That is not a flaw in the pod model; it is the deal. The pod pays the fattest right tail *because* it makes you bear the hardest left tail. Higher expected value usually comes bundled with higher variance, and a senior quant evaluates a seat the way they evaluate a trade: not on the headline number, but on the *distribution* of outcomes.

**Deferred comp changes the real number.** A large fraction of big bonuses — especially at funds — is deferred over multiple years and forfeited if you leave early. So a \$2M "total comp" might be \$700k cash this year and \$1.3M vesting over three more years contingent on you staying. That deferral is also what makes a **non-compete** or **garden leave** expensive: if leaving forfeits unvested comp *and* costs you six months of paid-but-idle garden leave, the true cost of switching firms can be well over a million dollars. We will not derive non-compete math here, but file it under "the headline number is not the number."

#### Worked example: Maya and Wei choose an archetype — the EV / variance tradeoff

Bring our two graduates back and make them choose between two concrete offers, so the abstract "variance" becomes dollars.

**Maya's choice — prop desk vs pod seat.** Maya has two offers. The **prop desk** offers a base of \$200k and a bonus that, modeling her uncertainty, is roughly: \$400k in a bad year (30% chance), \$900k in a normal year (50%), \$1.6M in a great year (20%). Expected bonus: `0.30 × 400 + 0.50 × 900 + 0.20 × 1600 = 120 + 450 + 320 = \$890k`, so expected total ≈ **\$1.09M**, and crucially the *worst case keeps her seat*. The **pod seat** offers a base of \$175k and a payout that is binary-ish: 25% chance of a stop-out (bonus \$0 and the seat gone), 50% chance of a \$700k bonus, 25% chance of a \$3M bonus. Expected bonus: `0.25 × 0 + 0.50 × 700 + 0.25 × 3000 = 0 + 350 + 750 = \$1.1M`, expected total ≈ **\$1.27M** — *higher EV than the prop desk* — but with a one-in-four chance the year ends with no bonus and a job search.

The EV says pod; the variance says it depends on Maya. If she has savings, no dependents, and the emotional durability to ride a stop-out, the pod's extra ~\$180k of expected value plus its uncapped tail is worth it. If a forced job search next year would be financially or psychologically ruinous, the prop desk's lower-variance \$1.09M is the better *risk-adjusted* choice even though its EV is lower. This is literally the [Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews) logic applied to a career: you do not maximize raw EV, you maximize EV *subject to not being ruined*.

**Wei's choice — systematic fund vs pod.** Wei is a patient researcher who would be miserable on a stop-out clock. Even though a pod might offer him a higher headline number, his *realized* EV at a pod is lower than it looks, because the variance interacts badly with his temperament: stressed researchers produce worse research, and a researcher who is great at out-of-sample discipline is wasting that skill in a seat that rewards fast aggressive risk-taking. The systematic fund's steadier, fee-funded pool is both the better fit *and* — for him specifically — plausibly the higher realized EV, because he will do his best work there.

*Matching an archetype is an EV-under-constraint problem: the right seat is the one that maximizes your expected comp subject to your personal ruin threshold and the temperament that lets you do your best work.*

## Matching yourself to an archetype

So how do you actually choose? Stop ranking firms by prestige and start matching the *archetype* to your skills and your tolerance for variance. Figure 7 lays out which temperament thrives where; find the row that sounds most like you and read across.

![A matrix of five temperaments as rows against four firm archetypes as columns, with green cells marking where each temperament thrives](/imgs/blogs/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund-7.png)

A few honest heuristics:

- **If you make fast, calibrated decisions and love markets** — prop / market maker. You will thrive in trading games and on a collaborative desk, and you get the lowest personal risk of the four.
- **If you love systems, latency, and C++** — HFT. The edge is your code, and the satisfaction is concrete and measurable.
- **If you want autonomy, want to own your own risk, and can stomach a stop-out** — pod shop. The uncapped upside lives here, and so does the hardest downside.
- **If you are a patient researcher who can kill your own ideas** — systematic fund. Lower variance, deep research, and a fee-funded floor under your comp.
- **If you want a strong, lower-pressure first quant seat with a portable brand** — bank systematic desk, then re-evaluate the buy-side once you have a track record.

The role you want also interacts with the archetype: a **trader** fits a prop or pod; a **researcher** fits a systematic fund or a pod's research seat; a **developer / low-latency engineer** fits an HFT firm best. The sibling [four paths — trader, researcher, developer, engineer](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer) maps the roles in detail; the point here is that the *firm archetype* and the *role* are two separate axes, and you are choosing a cell in their grid, not a point on a prestige line.

## Common misconceptions

This corner of finance is choked with confidently-wrong folklore. Four myths in particular cost people good decisions.

**Myth 1: "A hedge fund and a prop firm are the same thing."** They are opposites on the axis that matters most. A prop firm trades its *own* money with no clients; a hedge fund manages *outside* LP money and charges fees. That single difference cascades into everything: whether there is a management fee, whether you are paid from P&L directly or from a fee-funded pool, whether a drawdown ends your seat, and what the culture feels like. Calling Jane Street and Citadel "both hedge funds" is like calling a restaurant and a catering company "both kitchens" — true in the most superficial sense and useless for any decision.

**Myth 2: "Pods are safe because the big firms are huge and stable."** The *fund* is stable precisely *because* the individual *seats* are not. The whole multi-strat design is to keep the fund's drawdown tiny by aggressively cutting any PM who draws down — the firm's stability is *purchased with* your seat's fragility. A \$70B fund can be rock-solid while having one of the highest individual turnover rates in finance. Confusing the firm's stability with your job's stability is the single most expensive mistake people make taking a pod offer.

**Myth 3: "Bigger AUM means a better seat."** AUM measures the *size of outside money managed*, which is a fact about the *firm's product*, not about your comp or your fit. A prop firm with no AUM at all can pay you more than a \$70B fund, because it keeps the undiluted edge. And within a fund, more AUM can even *hurt* a research seat: capacity constraints and signal decay mean a strategy that worked at \$5B may be crowded and thin at \$50B. AUM is a vanity metric for the candidate; the metrics that matter are the comp *distribution* and the risk to your seat. Figure 6 makes the point that AUM is only even *defined* for the outside-money archetypes — prop and HFT firms have none.

![A bar chart comparing assets under management for D.E. Shaw, Citadel, Millennium, and Two Sigma, around 65 to 70 billion dollars each, annotated with founding years, with prop and HFT firms noted as having no AUM](/imgs/blogs/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund-6.png)

**Myth 4: "HFT is dying."** People have been declaring HFT dead since spreads compressed in the early 2010s. What actually happened is that the *easy* latency edge got competed away and the survivors got *better*: faster hardware, smarter signals, more asset classes (futures, options, crypto), and global expansion. Citadel Securities, Jump, and HRT are larger and more profitable than ever. The arms-race nature of the edge is real — and it is exactly why these firms keep hiring strong engineers — but "the arms race is hard" is not the same as "the business is dying." If anything, the rising bar makes the engineering talent *more* valuable, not less.

**Myth 5 (bonus): "The headline comp number is what you'll make."** Covered above, but it bears repeating because it underlies all the others: a quoted total is a *bonus year*, much of the bonus may be *deferred and forfeitable*, and the loud \$600k-by-year-5 figures are **survivorship bias** — they describe the people who survived the up-or-out filter, not the median entrant. Read every comp number with its conditionality attached.

## How it plays out at real firms

To make the archetypes concrete, here is how the named firms sort into them and what that means in practice — all figures as of 2026, drawn from levels.fyi, Glassdoor, H1B disclosures, firm pages, and the "Young & Calculated" 2026 quant-pay survey, and flagged illustrative where they are round estimates.

**Prop / market maker.** **Jane Street** (f. 2000, NYC) — OCaml, puzzles, trading games, a strong intern pipeline, QT base around \$300k. **Optiver** (f. 1986, Amsterdam, ~2,100 staff) — listed-derivatives market making and the legendary 8-minute mental-math test. **SIG / Susquehanna** (f. 1987, ~3,000 staff) — poker-based training and a structured new-trader education program. **IMC** (f. 1989, Amsterdam) — HackerRank-style and mental-math screens. New-grad totals at this tier commonly land **\$450k–\$650k** on-target (base \$250k–\$375k plus a sign-on of \$50k–\$200k), and the internship — Jane Street interns earn an annualized ~\$300k — *is* the real interview, because most full-time seats are intern conversions.

**HFT.** **Jump Trading** (f. 1999, Chicago) — secretive, deep C++ / kernel-bypass / lock-free bar, plus Jump Crypto. **HRT** (f. 2002, NYC) — "engineering excellence drives everything," candidate-choice of C++ or Python plus algorithms, probability, and systems design. **Citadel Securities** — the market-making arm (distinct from the Citadel hedge fund), ~\$257k base disclosed, 2026 intern pay of \$4,300–\$5,800/week plus a \$15k–\$25k sign-on and corporate housing. The interview leans hardest on systems and C++ depth.

**Multi-strat pod shop.** **Citadel** (f. 1990, Ken Griffin) and **Millennium** (f. 1989) — ~\$65B and ~\$70B AUM respectively, PMs own books, hard stop-outs, high-accountability culture and high turnover. **Point72** rounds out the set. A pod QR around 4 YoE near \$575k in a standard year; a successful PM in the millions in a good year, zero-and-fired on a stop-out.

**Systematic fund.** **Two Sigma** (f. 2001, ~\$70B AUM, ~2,000 staff) — AI/ML, distributed computing, research-scientist culture. **D.E. Shaw** (f. 1988, ~\$65B AUM) — the original quant fund, math-models plus computational anomaly hunting, recently adding discretionary strategies. **WorldQuant** — the alpha-factory / distributed-researcher model on its BRAIN / WebSim platform. levels.fyi (2025) puts QR medians around \$250k–\$325k with seniors well into the \$600k–\$720k range.

**The broader ecosystem.** Beyond the headline names sit **DRW, Tower Research, Five Rings, Akuna Capital, Old Mission, XTX Markets, Radix**, and the bank systematic desks. Five Rings and Jane Street lead H1B base disclosures near \$300k. These firms span the same four archetypes — many of "the next tier" pay and treat their people superbly, and a non-headline firm in the *right archetype for you* will beat a headline firm in the wrong one.

A closing reality check on selectivity and comp: top intern and grad programs are extremely selective — commonly described as low-single-digit-percent acceptance, with thousands of applicants per seat — and the comp tails you hear quoted are survivorship-biased. The mid-market standard band is closer to **\$375k–\$500k**, strong years **\$650k–\$820k**; 5-year survivors range from \$475k–\$625k (poor) to \$800k–\$1.2M (standard) to \$2M–\$4M (strong). The headline outliers are real, but they are the right tail of a wide distribution, not the median.

## When this matters / Further reading

This map matters the moment you have more than one option — an offer to weigh, an internship to target, a recruiter to answer. The wrong question is "which firm is the most prestigious?" The right questions are: *Whose money do they trade? What is their edge? How does a bad year end — with the firm carrying me, or with a stop-out carrying me out the door? And which of those deals fits my skills and my tolerance for variance?* Answer those four, and you have replaced a prestige ranking with an EV-under-constraint decision — which is exactly how the senior quant you are trying to become thinks about every trade *and* every career move.

A practical next step: when you get an offer, do not just read the number. Ask what fraction of comp is base versus bonus, how much of the bonus is deferred and over how long, whether your seat has an individual drawdown stop-out, and what happened to the last three people in your role. The answers tell you which archetype you are *really* joining, regardless of the brand on the door.
