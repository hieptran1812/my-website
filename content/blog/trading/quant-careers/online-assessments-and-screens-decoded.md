> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Between your application and the first human conversation sit one or more automated screens, and every one of them is a pass/fail gate with a hard cutoff, not a ranked competition where finishing 200th out of 5,000 helps you.
>
> - **Four screen types dominate:** the timed mental-math test (market makers, roughly 80 questions in 8 minutes, pass bar around 70-85% correct), the coding online assessment (HackerRank or Codility, often 2-4 algorithm problems in 60-120 minutes, graded by hidden test cases), the probability and statistics screen, and the occasional game-based or take-home assessment.
> - **The trap is always time, and the scoring is always a cutoff.** You are not trying to be the best applicant. You are trying to clear the bar reliably — to get above the line nine times out of ten — and then stop spending effort there.
> - **Every one of these is trainable, and the highest-leverage move is the boring one:** two weeks of targeted daily drilling moves your probability of clearing a typical screen from a coin-flip to 90 percent or more.
> - **The one number to remember:** a 74% on a screen with a 75% cutoff is rejected in exactly the same silent, automated way as a 30%. Near-misses do not earn partial credit at the gate. Calibrate your prep to clearing the line, not to impressing a human who is not yet reading you.

Maya is twenty-two, a math undergraduate aiming at a trading seat, and she is staring at a timer that reads `00:47`. Forty-seven seconds. On her screen are three coding problems. She has finished one cleanly. The second compiles but two hidden test cases are red — she suspects an integer overflow she did not handle. The third she has not opened. She had ninety minutes and she spent thirty-eight of them on the first problem, polishing it long past the point where it already passed every test, because polishing felt like progress. The clock does not care that she understood all three problems. It does not care that with another twenty minutes she would have cleared the bar. At `00:00` the assessment submits whatever is on the screen, an automated grader runs the hidden tests, and a number lands in a recruiting system she will never see. If that number is below a threshold she was never told, she gets a templated rejection email three weeks later, and no human at the firm will ever have read a single word she wrote.

This is the part of quant recruiting that almost nobody prepares for correctly, because it is invisible. The trading games, the superday, the famous Jane Street EV puzzles — those are the parts people talk about, because a human was in the room and the candidate has a story to tell. The online assessment is the opposite: it is a machine, it is silent, and most candidates who fail it never learn why. They assume they were not smart enough. Usually they were plenty smart enough and they were filtered out by a process they did not understand — by misallocating time, by chasing a perfect solution instead of a passing one, by treating a pass/fail gate as if it were a competition to be won.

This post decodes those gates. We will cover what each screen actually tests, how it is scored (the cutoff, the auto-grading, the partial credit, the anti-cheat), where the time-pressure trap hides in each one, and a concrete, measurable plan to clear each one reliably. The throughline of this whole series applies here with unusual force: **getting the job is a probabilistic edge, and so is clearing a screen.** Your goal is not a perfect score. Your goal is to push your probability of getting above the line high enough that the gate stops being a coin-flip — and then to spend your remaining preparation time on the parts of the loop where a human is actually keeping score.

## Foundations: what an online assessment is and how it is scored

Before we take the screens one at a time, we need a shared vocabulary, because the language of recruiting hides the mechanics. Let us build it from zero.

**An online assessment — an OA — is any automated, unproctored-or-lightly-proctored test you take alone on a computer, on a clock, that produces a score a recruiting system uses to decide whether you advance.** The phrase "online assessment" usually means the coding test specifically, but I am going to use "screen" as the umbrella term for the whole family — the mental-math test, the coding OA, the probability screen, the game — because they share the same essential nature. They are the automated stage of the funnel. No human is watching you take them. A human may glance at the output afterward, but the gating decision is made by a number crossing a threshold.

**The funnel.** A quant firm opening a single intern or new-grad seat will receive applications from thousands of people. As reported across the industry, the top intern and graduate programs are extremely selective — commonly described as low-single-digit-percent or below acceptance rates, comparable to or tighter than the most selective university admissions. The firm cannot put thousands of people in front of a trader for forty-five minutes; a trader's time is the firm's scarcest resource. So the firm interposes cheap, scalable, automated filters that cost the firm almost nothing per candidate and shrink the pool to something humans can handle. That is the entire reason screens exist. They are not there to find the best candidate. They are there to *cheaply remove most candidates* so that human attention is spent on a survivable number.

![A seven-stage pipeline showing the recruiting funnel from application through a resume screen, a first online screen, a second screen, a human phone round, a superday, and finally an offer, with a loop edge marking that failing a screen returns you to the applicant pool.](/imgs/blogs/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded-2.png)

Figure 2 shows where the screens sit. You apply. An applicant-tracking system (ATS) and possibly a resume screen do a first cheap filter on keywords and credentials — that is its own subject, covered in the sibling post on [the resume that passes the screen](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-quant-resume-that-passes-the-screen). Then come the automated tests: usually one, sometimes two, occasionally a game. Only after you clear those does a human pick up the phone. The screens are the moat around the human stage.

**A "hard cutoff" — the single most important concept in this whole post.** Most screens are scored against a threshold. Above the line, you advance. Below it, you are rejected. The threshold is a number the firm chose to make the pool the right size, and it is usually not disclosed to you. This has three brutal consequences that you must internalize:

1. **A screen is pass/fail, not ranked.** You are not competing to finish first. There is no prize for the highest score. The candidate who clears the bar by one point and the candidate who clears it by forty points both advance to the same phone screen, on equal footing, with the screen score mostly forgotten. The screen has done its only job — it sorted you into "advance" or "out."

2. **A near-miss is a total miss.** If the cutoff is 75% and you score 74%, you are rejected by the same silent, automated email as someone who scored 30%. The gate does not award sympathy for how close you came. This is the opposite of how school trained you, where a 74 is a passing grade and one point matters little. At the gate, the one point is everything, and then it is nothing.

3. **Because it is a cutoff, your job is reliability, not maximization.** The right mental model is a probability. If your true skill puts you at the bar on a good day and below it on a bad day, your *probability of passing* might be 50%. Every hour of prep that moves you from "at the bar" to "comfortably above it" raises that probability toward certainty. Once your probability of clearing is high — say 90%, clearing it nine attempts out of ten — further prep on that screen has sharply diminishing returns, and your time is better spent elsewhere in the loop.

![An illustrative histogram of applicant scores on a timed screen, with a hard cutoff line at seventy-five percent: the red mass of applicants below the line is filtered out and the green mass at or above the line advances, annotated to show that a seventy-four percent is rejected exactly like a thirty percent.](/imgs/blogs/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded-3.png)

Figure 3 makes the cutoff concrete with an illustrative score distribution. The applicant pool — smart people, most of them under-prepared for the specific format — piles up well below the bar. The cutoff is a vertical wall. Everyone to its left is gone, regardless of how close. The figure is illustrative; the exact shape and the exact cutoff vary by firm, role, and year, and firms do not publish these. But the *structure* — a pool peaking below the bar, a hard wall, near-misses rejected like far-misses — is real and is the thing to understand.

**Auto-grading.** Most screens are graded by a machine. A mental-math test is multiple-choice or fill-in, graded instantly. A coding OA runs your submitted code against a battery of hidden test cases and counts how many pass. A probability screen is multiple-choice or numeric-answer, graded by exact match. This matters because the grader is literal and merciless: it does not read your intent, it does not give you credit for an almost-right answer, and it does not care how elegant your reasoning was. On a coding OA the grader does not read your code at all in most cases — it runs it and checks outputs. Your beautiful, well-commented solution that has one off-by-one error scores the same as a pile of garbage that happens to produce the right outputs. Conversely, ugly code that passes all the tests gets full marks.

**Partial credit — and this is the nuance that saves candidates.** Many people believe a coding OA is all-or-nothing per problem. It usually is not. On most platforms, each problem has a set of hidden test cases, and your score for that problem is the fraction of those test cases your code passes. A solution that handles the normal cases but times out on the large inputs might pass, say, 6 of 10 test cases and earn 60% of that problem's points. This changes the optimal strategy completely. It means a *working brute-force solution that you can write quickly* is often worth more points than a half-finished optimal solution that does not run. It means you should almost always get *something* submitted for every problem rather than perfecting one. We will return to this — it is the single biggest strategic lever on a coding OA, and most candidates get it exactly backward.

**Time pressure — the trap baked into every screen.** Every screen is short relative to its content. The mental-math test gives you about six seconds per question. The coding OA gives you maybe thirty minutes per problem, which sounds generous until you have spent ten of them reading and re-reading and another ten down a wrong path. The probability screen gives you a few minutes per question, enough to solve it if you know the method and not nearly enough to derive the method from scratch. The clock is not an accident — it is the test. The firm is not asking "can you solve this?" It is asking "can you solve this *fast and under pressure*?", because that is what the job demands. A market maker who is right but slow is dead. The clock is a feature.

**Anti-cheat and proctoring.** Most screens are unproctored or lightly proctored, and firms increasingly use detection rather than prevention. Coding platforms log your keystrokes and paste events: a giant block of code appearing instantly, with no incremental typing, flags as a likely paste from an outside source. Some platforms run plagiarism detection across all submissions for a given problem, so two candidates who submit suspiciously similar non-obvious solutions both get flagged. Some firms use webcam proctoring for the assessment, recording you and using software to flag eyes leaving the screen or a second face appearing. The rise of AI coding assistants has pushed firms to add follow-up technical conversations where you must explain and extend the code you submitted — so even if you cheat past the OA, you collide with a human who asks you to walk through "your" solution and you cannot. **The honest framing: the screen is a filter to get you to a human, and the human will find out. Cheating the OA mostly buys you a more humiliating rejection later, plus the real risk of a permanent ban from the firm and sometimes a shared blocklist across firms.** Treat the screen as the practice that earns the human round, not as an obstacle to defeat.

With the foundations in place — funnel, hard cutoff, auto-grading, partial credit, time pressure, anti-cheat — let us take the four screen types one at a time.

## The mental-math test: speed and calibration under a six-second clock

The mental-math test is the signature screen of the listed-derivatives market makers — Optiver, IMC, SIG, Akuna, sometimes Jane Street and Citadel Securities at the early stages. The canonical format, most associated with Optiver, is roughly **80 questions in 8 minutes, no calculator, no scratch paper**, with a reported pass bar around **70-85% correct**. That is about six seconds per question, including reading the question, and you are expected to leave many unanswered — finishing all 80 is rare and not the point.

**What it tests.** Not trivia, and not advanced math. The questions are arithmetic, fractions and decimals, percentages, simple sequences, and rough estimation: things like `47 × 53`, `8% of 250`, `3/8 as a percent`, `812 ÷ 39`, `what comes next in 2, 6, 18, 54`. Any single question is trivial with a calculator and ten seconds. The test is not measuring whether you *can* do arithmetic — every applicant can. It is measuring two things that are genuinely predictive of trading: **raw speed** (how many correct answers can you produce per minute when the clock is screaming) and **calibration** (do you know which questions to skip, do you guess intelligently when partway there, do you avoid the trap of sinking thirty seconds into one hard question and starving the next five easy ones). A market maker quoting a two-sided market does exactly this all day — fast approximate arithmetic, knowing when an answer is good enough, knowing when to pass. The test is a proxy for the reflexes the desk needs. The sibling post on the [Optiver and IMC playbook](/blog/trading/quant-careers/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet) goes deep on the firms; for the technique itself, the quant-interview series has a dedicated post on [mental-math arithmetic speed](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/mental-math-arithmetic-speed-quant-interviews).

**How it is scored.** Usually correct-minus-nothing or correct-minus-penalty. Pure correct-count rewards attempting everything; a wrong-answer penalty (some tests subtract for wrong answers to discourage random guessing) rewards calibration — only answer when you are confident or when an educated guess has positive expected value. You generally do not know which scheme you face, so the safe default is: answer when you are confident, skip when you are not and there is a penalty, and on a no-penalty test, guess on anything you can narrow down. The pass is the cutoff — above the bar you advance, below it you are out, and a 69% on a 70% bar is a reject.

**The time-pressure trap.** It is the sunk-question. You hit a question you *almost* have — you can feel the answer — and you spend fifteen seconds clawing at it. In those fifteen seconds, two or three easy questions went unanswered. The math is unforgiving: at six seconds per question, fifteen seconds on one question is the budget for two and a half questions. The correct discipline is brutal — **if a question is not yielding in your time budget, you abandon it instantly and move on.** Speed comes not from solving hard questions fast but from not getting trapped by them at all.

#### Worked example: how many of 80 to clear the bar, and what speed that demands

Maya is prepping for Optiver. The reported pass band is roughly 70-85% correct. The question she actually needs answered is concrete: *how many questions must I get right, and how fast must I go?* Let us do the arithmetic, because this is exactly the kind of calibration the test rewards.

Take the conservative end: assume the bar is **80% correct of attempted**, or in some interpretations 80% of a target like 60-70 questions. The cleaner way to think about it is in terms of *correct answers in the 8 minutes*, because that is what actually lands. Suppose, illustratively, the bar is around **64 correct answers** (treating ~80% of an ~80-question test as the target, with the understanding that firms set their own threshold).

She has 8 minutes = 480 seconds. To get 64 correct she must, accounting for the questions she will get wrong or skip, attempt perhaps 70. That is 480 ÷ 70 ≈ **6.9 seconds per attempted question, including reading it.** Now the calibration math. If her per-question accuracy when she answers is 92%, then attempting 70 yields about 64 correct — she clears. If her accuracy is only 85%, attempting 70 yields about 59 correct — she misses. To recover, she must attempt more: at 85% accuracy she needs to attempt 64 ÷ 0.85 ≈ **76 questions** to expect 64 correct, which drops her to 480 ÷ 76 ≈ 6.3 seconds each. The lesson lands hard: **a drop in accuracy must be paid for with even more speed, and the two trade against each other.** The candidates who pass have pushed *both* up — they are fast *and* accurate — which is why the test predicts the job. An untrained strong student typically lands near 40-50 correct: fast enough mentally, but not trained on the specific format, and not calibrated on skipping. Eight weeks of daily drilling routinely moves that to 70+.

*The bar is not "do arithmetic"; it is "produce N correct answers in 480 seconds," and that is a speed-times-accuracy product you raise by drilling both factors at once.*

**How to clear it.** The training is unglamorous and effective. Use a fixed-settings arithmetic drill (Zetamac is the standard) and do timed sessions every day. Track one number: correct answers in the session. Push for speed first — accuracy recovers with familiarity, but speed only comes from repetition. Learn the specific tricks: round-and-adjust for multiplication (`47 × 53 = 50 × 50 + 50 × 3 − 3 × 47`… or faster, `(50−3)(50+3) = 2500 − 9 = 2491`), anchor fractions on halves and quarters, bracket estimation answers between powers you know. The detailed technique lives in the [mental-math post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/mental-math-arithmetic-speed-quant-interviews); the point here is that this screen responds to drilling more reliably than any other, because it is pure trainable skill with a known format.

## The coding online assessment: correctness under a clock, graded by hidden tests

The coding OA is the screen for developer, engineer, and many trader and researcher roles — and the firms that lean hardest on it are the engineering-heavy ones (HRT, Jump, Citadel Securities) and the market makers' developer tracks (IMC famously runs a strong coding screen). The format is usually a HackerRank or Codility assessment: **2 to 4 algorithmic problems in 60 to 120 minutes**, your choice of language from a list (commonly Python, C++, Java; Python is fine for most OAs because they test algorithms, not low-latency C++ — that depth comes in the later human round, covered in the [Jump and HRT playbook](/blog/trading/quant-careers/jump-and-hrt-playbook-the-low-latency-systems-bar)).

**What it tests.** Data structures and algorithms — the competitive-programming-adjacent core. Arrays and hashing, two pointers, sliding window, binary search, sorting, stacks and queues, recursion, dynamic programming, graph traversal (BFS and DFS), and the complexity analysis that tells you whether your approach will finish in time on the largest input. The problems are usually medium-difficulty by LeetCode's taxonomy — harder than warm-ups, rarely the gnarliest hard problems. The depth comes from doing them *fast and correctly under time pressure*, which is a different skill from solving them with unlimited time. The full technical treatment is in the quant-interview series post on [coding interviews and data structures and algorithms](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/coding-interview-quant-data-structures-algorithms); here we focus on the screen mechanics and strategy.

**How it is scored.** Hidden test cases. Each problem has a battery of tests you cannot see — small cases, large cases, edge cases (empty input, single element, maximum size, duplicates, negatives, overflow boundaries). Your score on the problem is typically the fraction of those tests your code passes, and your total is the sum across problems. **This is the partial-credit reality and it dictates strategy.** A solution that is correct on small inputs but O(n²) when O(n) was needed will pass the small test cases and *time out* on the large ones — earning, say, 50-70% of that problem rather than zero. So a fast, simple, correct-but-slow solution banks real points. The platform also typically shows you the *sample* test cases passing or failing as you go, but hides the rest, so passing the visible samples does not mean full marks.

**The time-pressure trap, and it has two heads.** The first is the one that got Maya in the intro: **over-investing in one problem.** She polished problem one past full marks while problems two and three sat untouched. On a partial-credit OA, the marginal value of the 95th percentile polish on a problem you have already solved is *zero* — you cannot score above 100% — while the marginal value of a quick brute-force attempt on an unopened problem is large. The second head is **the opposite failure: diving into code before understanding the problem,** then realizing 15 minutes in that you misread a constraint, and either restarting or shipping something wrong. Both failures come from not budgeting time per problem deliberately.

![A six-stage pipeline showing the time budget for one coding online assessment problem: read and restate in three to five minutes, plan the approach in five to seven minutes, code it in twelve to fifteen minutes, test edge cases in five to seven minutes, submit when hidden tests pass, then move to the next problem, with a loop edge for re-reading when the plan stalls.](/imgs/blogs/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded-4.png)

Figure 4 lays out a disciplined per-problem time budget. On a 90-minute, three-problem OA, you have roughly 30 minutes per problem. The temptation is to spend all 30 coding. The discipline is to spend it in proportions: a few minutes reading and restating the problem and noting the constraints (the input sizes tell you the required complexity — `n ≤ 10⁵` screams "O(n) or O(n log n), not O(n²)"); a few minutes planning the approach and confirming it will fit in time *before you write a line*; the bulk coding it cleanly; and a real block at the end testing edge cases against the grader. The plan-before-code step is the highest-leverage habit, because catching a wrong approach on paper costs two minutes and catching it in code costs twenty.

#### Worked example: one OA problem worked inside a 30-minute budget

Wei, a CS PhD student aiming at a research-and-engineering role, opens problem two of a three-problem OA. He has budgeted 30 minutes. The problem: *given an array of up to 100,000 integers and a target, return the indices of two numbers that sum to the target.* Classic two-sum. Here is how the budget plays out, minute by minute.

**Minutes 0-3, read and restate.** He reads it twice. Key facts he writes down: array up to 100,000 elements (so `n` can be 10⁵), integers can be negative, exactly one valid pair is guaranteed, return the indices not the values. The constraint `n = 10⁵` is the tell: a nested-loop O(n²) approach would be about 10¹⁰ operations, which at roughly 10⁸ simple operations per second is around 100 seconds — far over the typical 1-2 second time limit. So the large hidden tests *will* time out on brute force. He needs O(n).

**Minutes 3-8, plan.** The optimal approach: walk the array once, and for each element `x`, check whether `target − x` has already been seen, using a hash map from value to index. If yes, return the two indices; if no, store `x → its index` and continue. Time O(n), space O(n). He confirms the edge cases he will need to handle: duplicate values (store the first index, the hash map naturally handles the lookup), the element pairing with itself (the "already seen" check prevents using the same index twice because he checks before inserting). He is confident the approach is correct and fits. Five minutes well spent.

**Minutes 8-22, code.** He writes it cleanly — a single pass, a dictionary, an early return. About fourteen minutes including a couple of small typo fixes. He does not get clever; clever is how bugs enter under pressure.

**Minutes 22-28, test.** He runs the sample cases (pass), then manually constructs edge cases against the grader's custom-input box: an array with the answer at the very end, an array with duplicate values where the pair is two equal numbers, negative numbers summing to a negative target. All pass. He checks for integer overflow — in Python, irrelevant; in C++ or Java he would have widened to 64-bit for the sum. 

**Minutes 28-30, submit and bank.** All hidden tests pass. Full marks on problem two. He moves to problem three with 60 minutes of the original 90 already spent and a clean 30 left — exactly on budget.

Contrast this with the failure mode: a candidate who dives straight to code at minute 0, writes the nested-loop O(n²) version (which is *easier* to write and feels productive), passes the visible samples, submits, and earns 60% because the large hidden tests time out. They left 40% of the problem on the table by skipping the three-minute read that would have revealed `n = 10⁵`.

*The read-and-plan minutes feel like not-working, but they are where the points are won — on this problem, the difference between 60% and 100% was a constraint Wei read in the first three minutes and the other candidate never did.*

![A before-and-after comparison of the same two-sum problem solved two ways: the brute-force nested-loop version is order n squared, correct on small inputs but times out and fails the large hidden test cases, while the optimal hash-map version is order n and passes all hidden test cases for full points.](/imgs/blogs/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded-5.png)

Figure 5 makes the brute-force-versus-optimal tradeoff concrete on exactly this problem. Both approaches are *correct* — they return the right answer. But "correct" is not the bar; "passes the hidden tests within the time limit" is the bar. At `n = 100` both finish in milliseconds. At `n = 100,000` the brute force is doing about 10 billion operations and times out, while the hash-map version does about 100,000 operations and finishes instantly. The grader does not award points for correctness-in-principle. It awards points for tests passed. This is *the* coding-OA lesson: the constraint sizes are not flavor text, they are the spec for which complexity class you must hit.

**Languages.** Use the language you are fastest and most correct in for the OA, unless the role specifically demands otherwise. For algorithmic OAs, Python is often the best choice even for C++ shops, because you write it faster and with fewer bugs, and the OA is testing algorithms, not microsecond latency. The low-latency C++ depth (memory model, undefined behavior, lock-free, cache effects) is tested later, in a human round, not the screen — see the [Jump and HRT playbook](/blog/trading/quant-careers/jump-and-hrt-playbook-the-low-latency-systems-bar). One caveat: if the role's OA itself imposes tight time limits calibrated for compiled languages, Python's slowness can cause a *correct* O(n) solution to time out on the largest tests; in that case know your language's fast-IO idioms and consider C++ for the heavy problem.

## The probability and statistics screen: decompose, do not derive

Some firms — especially on the research and trading tracks — include a probability and statistics screen, either as its own timed online test or folded into a broader quantitative assessment. The format is typically **5 to 15 questions in 20 to 45 minutes**, multiple-choice or numeric-answer, auto-graded by exact match.

**What it tests.** The probability and statistics that underpin trading: expected value, conditional probability and Bayes' rule, basic combinatorics, variance and standard deviation, the properties of common distributions, and elementary inferential statistics — hypothesis tests, p-values, confidence intervals. The questions are not research-grade; they test whether you have the working fluency to reason about uncertainty quickly. The pattern that recurs is *expected value under uncertainty*, which is the spine of the whole quant job. The math itself is built from first principles in the [math-for-quants probability series](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/probability-spaces-random-variables-math-for-quants); the interview-style technique is in the quant-interview posts, and for the statistics specifically there is a dedicated post on [hypothesis testing and p-values](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/hypothesis-testing-pvalues-quant-interviews).

**How it is scored.** Right or wrong per question, summed, against a cutoff. Because there are few questions, **each one is worth a lot and a careless slip is expensive** — getting 3 of 5 wrong-by-arithmetic when you understood all 5 can sink you. This screen punishes the same thing the mental-math test punishes — careless errors under time pressure — but on conceptual problems where a single sign error or a flipped conditional changes the answer entirely.

**The time-pressure trap.** It is *deriving* instead of *recognizing*. A trained candidate sees "two dice, probability the sum is 7" and instantly recalls there are 6 favorable outcomes of 36, so 1/6 — three seconds. An untrained candidate enumerates the outcomes — ninety seconds, and a real chance of miscounting. The screen rewards having the standard results and methods pre-loaded so you recognize the structure and apply a known method, rather than reconstructing the method from scratch under the clock. The fix is having drilled enough problems that the patterns are automatic.

#### Worked example: a probability screen question, worked the fast way

Wei hits this question on a research-track screen: *A test for a rare condition is 99% accurate, meaning it correctly returns positive for 99% of people who have the condition and correctly returns negative for 99% of people who do not. The condition affects 1 in 1,000 people. You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the condition?* He has about three minutes. The naive instinct says "99%." The correct answer is far lower, and this is a Bayes problem — the classic base-rate question that quant screens love because it separates people who *recognize the structure* from people who *trust the surface number.*

He decomposes it with the natural-frequencies method, which is faster and less error-prone than the formula under pressure. Imagine **100,000 people.**

- Of these, **1 in 1,000 has the condition** = 100 people have it; 99,900 do not.
- Of the **100 who have it**, the test catches 99% = **99 true positives.**
- Of the **99,900 who do not**, the test wrongly flags 1% = **999 false positives.**
- Total positives = 99 + 999 = **1,098.** Of those, the ones who truly have the condition = 99.

So the probability of actually having the condition given a positive test is **99 / 1,098 ≈ 9.0%.** Despite the "99% accurate" headline, a positive test means only about a 9% chance you have the condition — because the condition is so rare that false positives from the huge healthy population swamp the true positives. Wei rounds to "about 9%," picks that multiple-choice answer, and moves on in under two minutes. The candidate who answered "99%" failed not the arithmetic but the *recognition* — they did not see that the base rate dominates.

This is the same engine that runs hypothesis testing: a p-value is the probability of seeing data this extreme *if the null hypothesis were true*, and the base-rate trap above is exactly why a low p-value does not by itself mean your hypothesis is probably true — the prior matters. That connection is the subject of the [hypothesis-testing post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/hypothesis-testing-pvalues-quant-interviews), and it is also why the same firms test both: they want people who do not confuse `P(positive | sick)` with `P(sick | positive)`, because confusing those on a trading signal loses money.

*The screen is not testing whether you can do Bayes' rule; it is testing whether you reach for it automatically when you see a base rate — recognition beats derivation when the clock is running.*

**How to clear it.** Drill standard problems until the patterns are reflexive: a few expected-value problems, a few conditional-probability and Bayes problems, a few distribution and variance problems, a few hypothesis-test and p-value problems every day. Keep a running list of the standard results you should never have to re-derive (the sum-of-two-dice distribution, the expected number of trials to first success in a geometric setting, the variance of a sum of independent variables, the structure of a Bayes update). The goal is that the screen presents *no new derivations* — only recognition and quick application.

## Game and take-home assessments: calibration and craft on a longer clock

The fourth family is the least standardized and the most varied: **game-based assessments and take-home tasks.** They show up less often than the first three, but they are growing, and they reward a different mix of skills.

**Game-based assessments** are timed, interactive simulations — often a market-making game (you are shown a fair value with noise and asked to quote bid and ask, then the market moves and you must update and manage a position), a betting or dice game, or a logic-and-pattern game. Some firms run these as an automated online stage; others, like Jane Street and SIG, run the richer versions live in the human rounds (the [SIG poker-and-EV playbook](/blog/trading/quant-careers/sig-susquehanna-playbook-poker-game-theory-and-ev) covers the philosophy). What the game tests is **expected-value thinking, calibration, and updating under uncertainty** — can you quote a market with positive EV, do you widen your spread when you are less sure, do you update correctly when new information arrives, do you avoid going on tilt after a loss. The scoring is usually your simulated P&L or a calibration score, against a threshold. The interview-technique side — how to actually play a market-making game — is in the quant-interview series; the career-layer point here is that these reward *process*, not a single outcome, so a controlled, well-calibrated player beats a lucky aggressive one over enough rounds.

**Take-home assessments** are longer tasks you do over 2 to 5 days, most common on the research track: a small data set, a question ("find a predictive signal," "build and evaluate a simple strategy," "analyze this and write up what you find"), and a deliverable — code plus a short write-up. The scoring is *not* automated; a human reads it. What they evaluate: the soundness of your method, whether you avoided overfitting and respected out-of-sample discipline, the clarity of your code, and — critically — **whether you can honestly assess and even kill your own idea.** A take-home that breathlessly claims a Sharpe of 5 with no skepticism scores worse than one that finds a modest, honest signal and clearly states its limitations. The research craft — building a signal, backtesting it without fooling yourself, the write-up — lives in the quant-research posts; the take-home is your audition for exactly that craft. Treat the write-up as a research note: state the question, the method, the result, the caveats, and what you would do next.

**The time-pressure trap differs here.** For games it is *tilt* — overreacting to a bad outcome and abandoning a sound process. For take-homes it is the *opposite of the OA trap*: you have days, and the danger is over-engineering — a sprawling, unfocused submission — or worse, p-hacking your way to an impressive-looking result that does not survive scrutiny. The discipline is to scope tightly, be honest, and ship a clean, defensible piece of work.

#### Worked example: a two-week practice plan with a target pass-probability curve

Maya has two weeks until her first wave of screens: a mental-math test and a coding OA. She wants a *measurable* plan, framed the way this whole series frames everything — as raising her probability of clearing each bar. Here is the plan, with the target curve.

**The daily allocation (about 90 minutes total).**

- **Mental-math: 20 minutes.** Two 8-minute Zetamac sessions at fixed settings, with a 4-minute review of which question families slowed her down. Track: correct answers per session.
- **Coding OA: 50 minutes.** Two timed medium DSA problems, strictly clocked at ~25 minutes each, *with the read-plan-code-test budget*, and self-scored on whether all her own edge cases pass. Track: problems where every test passes on the first submit.
- **Probability: 20 minutes.** Five problems — one EV, one Bayes, one distribution, one combinatorics, one hypothesis-test — from a quant problem set. Track: number right with no careless slip.

**The target curve.** She models her probability of clearing each bar over the two weeks. On day 0, untrained on the format, she is near a coin-flip — call it 45-50% — even though she is a strong student, because the format is unfamiliar and her calibration (when to skip, how to budget time) is undeveloped. The early gains are fast: in the first week she fixes the obvious gaps (learns the multiplication tricks, stops over-investing in one OA problem, pre-loads the standard probability results), and her pass probability climbs steeply. The second week is a grind against the rare failures — the edge case she keeps missing, the question family that is still slow — and the curve flattens as it approaches her ceiling.

![Two illustrative curves rising over a fourteen-day practice plan: the probability of passing a coding online assessment, where all hidden tests pass, and the probability of clearing the mental-math bar, both starting near fifty percent and crossing a ninety percent target reliability line around day twelve.](/imgs/blogs/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded-6.png)

Figure 6 shows the curve. Both screens start near a coin-flip and cross the **90% target line — clearing the bar nine attempts out of ten — around day 12.** That 90% is the goal, and the reason is the EV framing: at 50% pass probability the screen is pure variance and a bad day ends her candidacy at a firm she may not get another shot at for a year; at 90% the screen is nearly a formality and a bad day is survivable. The flattening of the curve after day 12 is the signal to *stop* — the marginal hour of mental-math drilling once she is reliably at 90% buys almost nothing, and that hour is far better spent preparing for the human rounds where a person is keeping score and the EV of preparation is higher. The whole point of treating the screen as a probability is to know when you have done enough.

*Calibrate prep to the bar: drive each screen's pass probability up to ~90% and then move on — over-preparing a gate you already clear nine times out of ten is spending your scarcest resource, time, where it has the least expected value.*

## How to train to clear each screen

Pulling the four plans together into one measurable regimen. The unifying principle: **each screen maps to one specific drill that builds the exact skill it tests, and you train to a *reliability* target, not a *maximum* score.**

![A four-by-four prep matrix: rows for the daily drill, the core skill, the practice resource, and the bar to hit, across columns for the mental-math test, coding OA, probability and statistics screen, and game or take-home, showing each screen mapped to its targeted drill.](/imgs/blogs/online-assessments-and-screens-decoded-7.png)

Figure 7 is the checklist. Read it as: for each screen, here is the daily drill, the skill it builds, the resource, and the bar.

- **Mental-math.** Drill: 20 minutes of timed arithmetic sprints daily (Zetamac at fixed settings is the standard). Skill: speed times accuracy, plus the calibration to skip. Bar: 70+ correct in an 8-minute session, hit consistently across several sessions, not once on a lucky run. The "consistently" is the reliability target — one good session is variance; ten in a row is skill.
- **Coding OA.** Drill: two timed medium DSA problems a day, strictly clocked, using the read-plan-code-test budget. Skill: recognizing the pattern and implementing it cleanly within the time limit. Resource: LeetCode medium in timed mode; do them *on a clock*, because untimed practice trains a different skill. Bar: all hidden tests pass within budget — and, because partial credit is real, also practice the meta-skill of getting *something* submitted for every problem rather than perfecting one.
- **Probability and statistics.** Drill: five mixed problems a day (EV, Bayes, distribution, combinatorics, hypothesis test). Skill: decompose to basic rules and recognize the structure fast. Resource: a quant probability problem set; keep a list of standard results you should never re-derive. Bar: right *and* with no careless slip, because few questions means each one is heavily weighted.
- **Game and take-home.** Drill: mock market-making games with a friend or a simulator, plus one practice take-home worked end to end as a research note. Skill: calibration and updating for games; honest, scoped craft for take-homes. Bar: defensible and calibrated — quote with positive EV and widen when unsure; for take-homes, an honest modest result beats an over-fit impressive one.

Two regimen-level rules tie it together. First, **practice on a clock.** Every one of these screens is a speed test as much as a skill test, and untimed practice builds the wrong muscle — it teaches you to solve the problem, not to solve it in six seconds or twenty-five minutes. Second, **measure your pass probability, not your effort.** The metric is not "hours studied"; it is "fraction of timed sessions where I cleared the bar." When that fraction reaches roughly 90% on a screen, you are done with that screen and your time moves elsewhere.

## Common misconceptions

The screens are surrounded by folklore, most of it wrong and most of it expensive. Here are the four that cost candidates the most.

**Myth 1: "The OA ranks you, so I should aim for the highest possible score."** No. Almost every screen is a hard pass/fail cutoff (Figure 3). Above the line you advance; below it you are out; the magnitude above the line is mostly irrelevant. The candidate who clears the bar by one point sits in the same phone screen as the one who aced it. This myth is expensive because it leads to *over-investing* — polishing a solved OA problem to perfection while another problem sits unopened, or grinding a mental-math drill from 85 correct to 90 when you needed 70 and your time was better spent on the parts of the loop a human actually scores. Calibrate effort to clearing the bar reliably, then stop.

**Myth 2: "A partial solution is worthless, so I should perfect one problem rather than attempt all of them."** Backward. On most coding OAs, scoring is per-hidden-test, so a working-but-slow brute force banks real points (often 50-70% of a problem), and you score the sum across problems. The candidate who fully solves one of three problems and leaves two blank often loses to the candidate who gets partial credit on all three. *Always get something submitted for every problem.* The corollary on the mental-math and probability screens: an educated guess on a no-penalty test has positive expected value, so do not leave answerable questions blank out of perfectionism.

**Myth 3: "Brute force is fine — the grader just checks correctness."** No. The grader checks correctness *within a time limit*, and the large hidden test cases are designed to time out a sub-optimal complexity class (Figure 5). The input constraints in the problem statement are the spec for which complexity you must hit: `n ≤ 10⁵` means O(n) or O(n log n) will pass and O(n²) will time out on the big tests. Brute force is a fine *fallback* to bank partial credit when you cannot find the optimal approach — but it is not "fine" as a plan, because the points you leave on the table are the ones the cutoff is built to filter on. Read the constraints; they tell you what passing requires.

**Myth 4: "I'm strong, I can wing it without preparing for the format."** This is the most common and the most costly, because it is half-true. You *are* strong — that is not the question. The screen does not test whether you can do arithmetic, write a two-sum, or apply Bayes' rule with unlimited time; of course you can. It tests whether you can do those things *fast, under a clock, in an unfamiliar format, with no second chances*. A brilliant student who has never done a timed Zetamac session lands near 45 correct, not 70 (Figure 6), and a brilliant coder who has never practiced the read-plan-code-test budget burns 38 minutes on problem one and leaves problem three blank (the intro). The format is a skill, the skill is trainable, and two weeks of drilling moves your pass probability from a coin-flip to 90%. Winging it is choosing to leave that on the table — and the screen is the cheapest stage to lose at, because no human ever even read you.

## How it plays out in the real world

Concretely, here is what the screens look like across the named firms, with the honest caveats.

**The market makers (Optiver, IMC, SIG, Akuna, Citadel Securities, often Jane Street).** The mental-math test is the opener for the European market makers especially. Optiver's is the famous one — roughly 80 questions in 8 minutes, no calculator, reported pass band around 70-85%. IMC pairs a mental-math screen with a HackerRank-style coding screen, so a developer-track candidate there faces both gates. SIG's culture is decision-theory and poker, and while much of its game-playing is in the human rounds, the early screens still test speed and EV. These firms run the screen *first* because the trait it proxies — fast, calibrated arithmetic under pressure — is the core trading reflex, and they would rather lose a slow-but-deep candidate than spend a trader's hour on someone who cannot quote fast. The internship is the real interview at all of them, and conversion to full-time is the main path — so the screen you are clearing is most valuably the *internship* screen.

**The engineering-heavy firms (HRT, Jump, Citadel Securities developer track).** The coding OA carries more weight, and it is harder — closer to competitive-programming difficulty, sometimes with a tighter time limit that favors compiled languages. HRT lets you pick C++ or Python, and the OA is the gate before the deep C++ and systems-design human rounds where the low-latency bar actually lives. Jump is secretive and systems-heavy; expect the screen to test real algorithmic depth, not warm-ups. For these firms, treat the OA as serious and practice the hard problem in a fast language.

**The systematic research firms (Two Sigma, D.E. Shaw, WorldQuant).** The mix shifts toward probability and statistics screens and take-home research tasks. A research-track candidate is more likely to face a quantitative screen and then a take-home signal or backtest case than a pure mental-math sprint. WorldQuant's alpha-factory model in particular involves working on its BRAIN/WebSim platform, so the audition is research-shaped. The take-home is where the [research craft](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/coding-interview-quant-data-structures-algorithms) — and especially the discipline of *killing your own idea* — gets tested before any human conversation.

**The honest realities.** A few that candidates need to hear plainly. First, **the screens are genuinely high-variance for an under-prepared person and low-variance for a prepared one** — which is exactly why preparation has such high expected value. Second, **a rejection at the screen stage is almost never personal and almost never explained;** the automated email tells you nothing, and you should not read it as a verdict on your ability, only as a signal that you were below a cutoff you can train to clear next cycle. Third, **the screen is the cheapest stage to fail and the cheapest to fix** — no human read you, so there is no relationship burned, and the fix is pure trainable drilling, unlike the harder-to-improve human rounds. Fourth, on the comp side: clearing these screens is the gate to a market where, as of 2026, top-tier new-grad total compensation runs roughly \$450,000 to \$650,000 on-target (base \$250,000-\$375,000 plus a sign-on of \$50,000-\$200,000, per ranges reported on levels.fyi and the 2026 quant-pay surveys) — but remember the survivorship caveat that runs through this whole series: those figures describe the people who cleared the filter, bonuses do not repeat automatically, and a strong year is not the median. The screen is the first filter on the way to that distribution; clearing it does not put you at its top, it puts you in the running.

#### Worked example: the expected value of two weeks of screen prep

Let us close with the EV math that justifies everything above, because this series insists every decision be framed as expected value. Maya is applying to 8 firms this cycle. Suppose, illustratively, that without format-specific prep her probability of clearing each firm's screen is 45% (she is strong but unpracticed at the format), and with two weeks of targeted drilling it rises to 90% (Figure 6).

Treat each firm's screen as roughly independent. The probability of clearing *at least one* screen — getting at least one foot in a human door this cycle — is `1 − (probability of failing all of them)`.

- **Without prep:** probability of failing one screen is 0.55, so failing all 8 is `0.55⁸ ≈ 0.0084`. Probability of clearing at least one ≈ **99.2%.** And the *expected number* of screens cleared is `8 × 0.45 = 3.6`.
- **With prep:** failing one is 0.10, failing all 8 is `0.10⁸ ≈ 0.00000001`. Clearing at least one ≈ **100%.** Expected number cleared = `8 × 0.90 = 7.2`.

At first glance "clear at least one" barely moves — 99.2% to ~100% — and a careless reader might conclude prep does not matter. That reading is wrong, and seeing why is the whole point. The screens are *not* the prize; they are gates to the human rounds, and **more shots on goal is the real payoff.** Prep doubles the expected number of human-round opportunities from 3.6 to 7.2. If each human-round opportunity converts to an offer with some probability `c`, expected offers go from `3.6c` to `7.2c` — prep *doubles your expected offers* by doubling the number of doors you walk through, even though "at least one door" was nearly certain either way. And because the screens are the *cheapest* stage to improve (pure trainable drilling, two weeks, no relationship risk), the EV per hour of screen prep at the start of a cycle is enormous — far higher than, say, the marginal hour of polishing a resume that already passes. That is why screen prep, despite being the unglamorous, invisible part of recruiting, is among the highest-EV things a candidate can do.

*The screens look like a near-certain gate you will clear anyway, but their real value is in expectation: prep does not just get you through one door, it doubles the number of human conversations you reach, and human conversations are where offers are made.*

## When this matters and further reading

The online assessment is the part of quant recruiting that punishes the unprepared and rewards the deliberately-practiced more than any other stage, precisely because it is invisible, automated, and pure-skill. It matters most at the start of a recruiting cycle — when you are applying broadly and every clearable screen is a human conversation you would otherwise never have had — and it matters most for strong candidates who are tempted to wing it, because they are the ones leaving the most expected value on the table. Internalize the four ideas: the screens are hard pass/fail cutoffs not rankings; partial credit is real so attempt everything; the time pressure is the test not an accident; and you train to a reliability target of clearing the bar nine times out of ten, then move on. Do that, and the gate that filters out most of your competition becomes, for you, a formality.
