> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — You can be a brilliant mathematician or a world-class programmer and still flunk a quant interview in the first five minutes, because the room assumes a layer of markets literacy that nobody put on your syllabus.
>
> - A **market** is a sorted ledger of standing orders. The highest buy price is the **bid**, the lowest sell price is the **ask**, and the gap between them — the **spread** — is the market maker's edge. The size resting at each price is the **depth**.
> - A **market order** takes liquidity now and pays the spread plus **slippage** as it walks the book; a **limit order** rests until the price reaches it. Knowing which to use, and what it costs, is the whole game in a market-making interview.
> - **Fair value** is what a thing is worth given everything you know; **no-arbitrage** says two ways to get the identical payoff must cost the same, or a free-money machine exists. Put-call parity is the cleanest example.
> - The three instruments to own are **equities, futures, and options** — just enough of each, not a derivatives course. And the number to remember: **a positive gross edge can go negative once transaction costs scale with turnover**, which is why execution literacy is not optional.

He had a perfect transcript — a competition medalist, a clean proof of a hard combinatorics lemma on the whiteboard ten minutes earlier, the kind of raw horsepower the firm hires for. Not because he couldn't compute the expected value — he could do that in his head — but because he did not know what "your market" *meant*. He did not know that the right answer is a two-sided quote: a price he'd buy at, a price he'd sell at, with a spread in between. He had never seen an order book. He thought "the market" was a vague gestalt of CNBC tickers, not a concrete, sortable list of who-wants-to-buy-and-sell-at-what. The math was never the problem. The problem was that he had spent four years on the analysis and zero hours on the thing the analysis is *for*.

This is the most common, most fixable, and most quietly fatal gap in quant recruiting. But both quietly assume you already know what a market *is* — what a bid and an ask are, why a quote has two sides, what it costs to trade. Almost nobody teaches that floor explicitly, because to people inside the industry it is so obvious it feels like air. So a generation of brilliant outsiders walks into superdays having out-prepped everyone on the hard parts and tripped on the easy one.

This post is that floor. It is not a derivatives course — we will not price an option with a stochastic differential equation, and we will link out the moment a topic deserves a whole post of its own. It is the practical minimum: what a market actually is, how the order book works, how a trade executes and what it costs you, what fair value and no-arbitrage mean, and the three instruments every quant should be able to talk about without flinching. Read that stack and the whole idea clicks. Once you can read it, "what's your market?" stops being a riddle and becomes the most natural question in the world.

## Foundations: what a market actually is

Strip away the noise and a market is a mechanism for two strangers who will never meet to agree on a price. Let us build every piece from zero.

**Buyers, sellers, and price discovery.** At any instant there are people who want to *own* a thing (buyers) and people who want to *get rid of* it (sellers). A buyer has a maximum price they'll pay; a seller has a minimum they'll accept. **Price discovery** is the process by which all those private maximums and minimums get revealed and matched into actual transactions. The market is the place where that happens. The "price" of a stock you see quoted is just the most recent point where a buyer's maximum met a seller's minimum — the last trade — surrounded by a live cloud of *other* people's standing maximums and minimums, waiting.

**The order book.** That cloud, made concrete, is the **order book**: a continuously updated, sorted list of every resting buy order and every resting sell order, organized by price. Buy orders are **bids**; sell orders are **asks** (or **offers**). The book sorts bids from highest to lowest and asks from lowest to highest, because those are the orders most likely to trade next. Every exchange in the world — for stocks, futures, options, crypto — runs some version of this book. It is the atom of modern markets, and Figure 1 is its anatomy.

**The bid, the ask, and the spread.** The **best bid** is the highest price any buyer is currently willing to pay. The **best ask** is the lowest price any seller is currently willing to accept. The ask is always at least as high as the bid — if a buyer's price ever reaches a seller's price, they trade immediately and both orders leave the book. So at rest there is always a gap, the **bid-ask spread**. In Figure 1 the best bid is 100.00 and the best ask is 100.02, so the spread is two cents. If you want to **buy right now**, you pay the ask (100.02). If you want to **sell right now**, you receive the bid (100.00). The spread is the toll for the privilege of trading *immediately* instead of waiting.

**Liquidity and depth.** A market is **liquid** when you can trade a meaningful size quickly without moving the price much. Liquidity has two visible dimensions in the book: a *tight* spread (the best bid and ask are close together) and *deep* size (lots of shares resting at each level). A penny-spread stock with thousands of shares at every price is liquid; a thing with a fifty-cent spread and ten shares at the top is illiquid. **Depth** is that second dimension — the quantity sitting at each price level. Figure 1 shows 500 shares at the best bid, 400 at the best ask, and progressively more as you go deeper. Depth is what determines how much you can trade before you start pushing the price against yourself, which is the heart of the slippage story later.

**The market maker.** Someone has to be standing on *both* sides of that spread — quoting a bid and an ask at the same time, ready to buy from sellers and sell to buyers. That someone is a **market maker**. It posts the 100.00 bid and the 100.02 ask simultaneously. If a seller hits its bid (it buys at 100.00) and a moment later a buyer lifts its ask (it sells at 100.02), the market maker has bought low and sold high without forming any opinion about where the price is headed. It captured the two-cent spread for *providing liquidity* to both sides. That, repeated millions of times a day, is one of the four engines that funds this whole industry — we cover all four in [how quant firms actually make money](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money). For now, hold this: the spread is not a fee the exchange charges. It is the market maker's compensation, and it is earned, not free, because of a risk we will meet shortly.

**Edge, expected value, and P&L.** Three words you'll hear constantly. **Edge** is the expected profit on a single transaction, measured *before* you know how it turns out. **Expected value (EV)** is the probability-weighted average outcome — the thing you maximize when you can't control luck. **P&L** is profit and loss, the money a strategy actually made. The reframing that defines the industry: a quant does not predict outcomes. A quant finds a *positive expected value* and repeats it enough times that the law of large numbers turns a tiny edge into a real fortune. Markets literacy is the vocabulary in which all of that EV thinking is expressed — you cannot reason about the EV of a quote if you don't know what a quote is.

**The tick and the units.** One more primitive that trips people up: prices move in discrete steps called **ticks**, not continuously. A liquid US stock ticks in pennies (\$0.01); a Treasury future ticks in fractions of a point; a foreign-exchange pair ticks in fractions of a **pip**. The tick size matters because it sets the *minimum possible spread* — a stock that ticks in pennies can never have a spread tighter than one cent, so for the most liquid names the spread is *pinned at one tick* and competition happens entirely on the time dimension of the queue, not the price. When you hear a trader say "it's a one-tick-wide market, all the edge is in queue position," this is what they mean: everyone quotes the same price, so the only way to capture the spread is to be earlier in line. Quantities, meanwhile, are quoted in **shares** for stocks, **contracts** for futures and options (each contract usually controls 100 shares of the underlying, for equity options), and **notional** (the dollar face value) for many over-the-counter instruments. Mixing up shares and contracts is a classic rookie error that an interviewer will catch instantly.

With those primitives, the rest of the post is just four deeper looks: the order book up close, fair value, the instruments, and execution costs.

## The order book and microstructure: where price meets reality

**Market microstructure** is the study of how trading actually happens at the level of individual orders — the rules of the book, who gets filled first, what a trade costs in practice. You do not need to be a microstructure researcher to clear an interview, but you need the working model, because the market-making games are *built* on it.

**Limit orders vs market orders.** This is the single most important distinction, and the candidate who froze did not know it. A **limit order** says "buy at most X" or "sell at least Y" — it names a price and waits. It joins the book at its price level and rests there until either the market reaches it (and it fills) or you cancel it. A limit order is *patient*: it gives you price control but no guarantee of execution. A **market order** says "buy now" or "sell now, whatever the price" — it does not name a price; it grabs whatever liquidity is currently resting on the other side. A market order is *impatient*: it guarantees execution but surrenders price control. The person who posts a limit order is **providing liquidity** (they add to the book and wait); the person who sends a market order is **taking liquidity** (they remove from the book). Market makers live on the providing side; people who need to get in or out *now* live on the taking side.

**The matching engine and price-time priority.** When your order reaches the exchange, a piece of software called the **matching engine** decides what trades against what. The rule is almost universally **price-time priority**: better prices match first, and among orders at the same price, the one that arrived first matches first. So if you post a bid at 100.00 and someone else also bids 100.00 a second later, you are ahead of them in the queue — when a seller comes along to hit the 100.00 bid, your order fills before theirs. This is why latency matters so much for some firms: at the same price, being first in line is worth real money, and the race to be first is an entire business model (the low-latency firms in the [Jump and HRT playbook](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money)). Figure 2 traces the whole path: you decide, the order routes to the engine, the engine matches it (instantly for a market order, eventually for a limit order), and you get a fill and a confirmation.

**Partial fills and queue position.** Two consequences of the matching rules surprise newcomers. First, your order can **partially fill**: if you send a limit order to buy 1,000 shares at 100.00 but only 300 shares of sellers show up at that price, you get 300 shares and the remaining 700 stay resting in the book. Your "fill" is not all-or-nothing. Second, your **queue position** — where you sit in line at a given price — is itself a valuable, perishable asset. If you've been resting at the front of the 100.00 bid for a while, you're near the front of the queue and likely to fill soon; if you cancel and re-post (say, to nudge your price), you lose that position and go to the back of the line. A market maker's profitability often hinges on getting and keeping good queue position cheaply, which is a subtle, latency-sensitive game that the [low-latency firms](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money) have industrialized. You don't need to master it to be literate, but you should know it exists, because "why don't you just always quote the best price?" has a real answer: because re-quoting costs you your place in line.

**Hidden liquidity and the limits of the visible book.** The book in Figure 1 shows the *displayed* orders. But not all liquidity is visible: some venues allow **hidden** or **iceberg** orders, where only a small portion of a large order is shown and the rest fills quietly as the visible part is consumed. And a large amount of trading happens off the public exchanges entirely, in **dark pools** and other venues, precisely so large players can move size without revealing it and getting front-run. The practical takeaway: the visible book is a *partial* picture of supply and demand, and a sophisticated trader reasons about the liquidity that *isn't* shown as well as the liquidity that is. This is why "the screen says there are only 400 shares on the offer" is not the same as "only 400 shares are available" — there may be an iceberg behind it. You don't need to trade dark pools to be literate; you need to know the visible book under-states reality.

![A seven-stage pipeline showing a trade executing: you decide buy or sell and limit or market, the order routes to the exchange, the matching engine applies price-time priority, a market order takes liquidity immediately while a limit order rests until hit, then one or more fills are reported and a confirmation returns your average price](/imgs/blogs/the-markets-knowledge-every-quant-needs-2.png)

**Walking the book and slippage.** Here is where the order book stops being a static picture and starts costing you money. Suppose you send a market order to buy 1,000 shares against the book in Figure 1. There are only 400 shares at the best ask of 100.02. Your order takes all of them, then keeps going — it climbs to the next level, 100.03, and takes 600 of the 800 shares there. You bought 400 at 100.02 and 600 at 100.03. You did **not** get the price on the screen. The difference between the best ask you saw and the average price you actually paid is **slippage**, and it grows with order size. **Market impact** is the closely related idea that your own trading pushes the price: by consuming the resting liquidity, you've moved the best ask up to 100.05 for the next person — possibly including the next *you*. Figure 3 makes this exact, walking a buy order from 400 shares (no slippage) to 2,000 shares (meaningful slippage) against the very book in Figure 1.

#### Worked example: reading an order book and finding where your market order fills

Maya is handed Figure 1's book and asked: "You send a market order to buy 1,000 shares. What's your average fill price, and what's your slippage versus the screen?"

The ask side, top to bottom, is 400 shares at \$100.02, then 800 at \$100.03, then 1,500 at \$100.05. A market buy takes the cheapest liquidity first:

- It takes all **400 shares at \$100.02**, costing 400 × \$100.02 = \$40,008.
- It still needs 600 more, so it takes **600 of the 800 shares at \$100.03**, costing 600 × \$100.03 = \$60,018.
- Total cost: \$40,008 + \$60,018 = **\$100,026** for 1,000 shares.
- Average fill price: \$100,026 ÷ 1,000 = **\$100.026 per share**.

The screen showed a best ask of \$100.02. Maya actually paid \$100.026 on average. Her **slippage is \$0.006 per share** — six-tenths of a cent — which on 1,000 shares is \$6. Tiny here. But push the size to 2,000 shares and she'd take all 400 at 100.02, all 800 at 100.03, and 800 at 100.05, for an average of **\$100.036** — slippage of 1.6 cents, or \$32, and she'd have moved the best ask all the way to \$100.05 for everyone behind her.

*The price on the screen is the price for the first share, not the thousandth; the book tells you the rest, and a market order pays whatever the book demands.*

![A chart of price paid per share against order size: a green dashed line marks the best ask at 100.02, a red step curve shows the marginal price rising as the order walks each book level, and a blue curve shows the average fill price climbing from 100.02 at 400 shares to 100.036 at 2,000 shares, with the slippage at 1,000 and 2,000 shares annotated](/imgs/blogs/the-markets-knowledge-every-quant-needs-3.png)

**The spread as the market maker's edge.** Now flip to the other side of the trade. Every time Maya pays the ask and someone else hits the bid, the market maker on the other side captures the spread. It is not winning a bet on direction; it is collecting a toll for standing in the middle. But — and this is the nuance that separates someone who has *read about* markets from someone who *understands* them — the spread is not free money, because of **adverse selection**. When someone trades against your quote, *they chose to*. Sometimes they trade for innocent reasons. But sometimes they trade because they know something you don't: faster data, news, a better model. They only lift your ask when your ask is stale and about to look cheap; they only hit your bid when it's about to look expensive. You get filled exactly when you least want to be. The spread has to be wide enough to pay for the trades where you got picked off. That is why market making is a skill and not an ATM, and why the market-making games test whether you *update your quote* when someone trades against you.

#### Worked example: the spread as the market maker's edge, times volume

A market maker quotes a one-cent-wide market on a liquid stock: bid \$50.00, ask \$50.01. On average it captures about **half the spread per share** it trades — half a cent — because it doesn't always get both sides, and adverse selection eats into the rest. Suppose on a typical day it trades **40 million shares** of this name across both sides.

- Gross capture: 40,000,000 shares × \$0.005 per share = **\$200,000 per day** on this one stock, before costs.
- Now adverse selection: say 15% of that volume is "toxic" — informed flow that, on average, costs the maker 1.5 cents per share when it gets run over. That's 6,000,000 shares × \$0.015 = **\$90,000** of losses.
- Net edge on the day: \$200,000 − \$90,000 = **\$110,000** on one stock. Multiply across thousands of names and you have a market-making business.

The lesson for Maya in an interview: when she quotes a tight market and the interviewer *keeps hitting her bid*, that is the simulated informed trader. The right response is not to hold her quote like a deer in headlights — it is to **lower her market**, because the repeated selling is information.

*The spread is the market maker's revenue per trade and adverse selection is its cost of goods sold; the business only works when the spread, times enormous volume, exceeds what the sharks take.*

## Fair value and no-arbitrage: the two ideas a quote is built on

Every quote you ever make rests on two ideas. **Fair value** is your best estimate of what a thing is worth given everything you know. **No-arbitrage** is the constraint that, if two different ways of getting the *identical* payoff cost different amounts, someone will buy the cheap one and sell the expensive one until the prices converge — so in a functioning market, such free-money machines don't persist. Fair value tells you where to center your quote; no-arbitrage tells you which prices *cannot* be independent of each other.

**Fair value, concretely.** In the widget game from the intro — value uniform between \$0 and \$100 — the fair value is the expected value, \$50. A trader's market is centered there: maybe bid \$48, ask \$52, a four-dollar spread to cover the risk that the interviewer (who may know the true value) is going to pick the trader off. As information arrives — a hint, a partial reveal, a counterparty who keeps buying — the fair value estimate moves, and the quote moves with it. Fair value is not a single mystical number handed down by the market; it is *your posterior*, updated as you learn, and a good trader's quote is a live readout of that posterior. This is exactly the EV-and-updating skill the [trading game](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-trading-game-and-mental-math-rounds-what-theyre-really-testing) is testing.

**No-arbitrage, concretely.** The cleanest example in all of finance is **put-call parity**, and it is worth knowing cold because it shows up in interviews constantly and because it teaches the no-arbitrage *style of reasoning* better than anything else. Briefly: a **call option** is the right (not obligation) to *buy* a stock at a fixed **strike** price K. A **put option** is the right to *sell* at K. Put-call parity says that holding a call and shorting a put with the same strike and expiry gives you the *exact same payoff as owning the stock itself* (financed by borrowing the strike). Therefore their prices must satisfy an identity. If they don't, you can build the synthetic stock cheaply and sell the real one (or vice versa) for riskless profit. Figure 5 shows the four prices that no-arbitrage pins together; the full derivation lives in the dedicated [put-call parity post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/put-call-parity-no-arbitrage-quant-interviews).

![A graph showing put-call parity as a no-arbitrage identity: a long call and a short put combine with lending cash that grows to the strike to form a synthetic stock, which must equal the price of the real stock, with arrows showing the four prices forced into one relationship](/imgs/blogs/the-markets-knowledge-every-quant-needs-5.png)

#### Worked example: a put-call-parity no-arbitrage calculation

Wei is asked the classic screen: "A stock trades at \$100. A three-month call struck at \$100 costs \$6. A three-month put struck at \$100 costs \$5. The three-month interest rate is about 1%. Is there an arbitrage?"

The parity relationship is: **Call − Put = Stock − present value of the strike.** Let's check both sides.

- Left side (the options): Call − Put = \$6 − \$5 = **\$1**.
- Right side (stock minus discounted strike): the present value of \$100 received in three months at 1% is about \$100 ÷ 1.01 ≈ **\$99.01**. So Stock − PV(strike) = \$100 − \$99.01 = **\$0.99**.

The two sides are \$1.00 and \$0.99 — essentially equal, a one-cent gap that the bid-ask spread would swallow. **No arbitrage.** The prices are consistent. Now change one number: suppose the call cost \$7 instead of \$6. Then the left side is \$2.00 while the right is \$0.99 — a **\$1.01 mispricing**. Wei would sell the (now expensive) call, buy the put, buy the stock, and borrow the discounted strike: a package that is riskless and locks in roughly a dollar per share. He'd do it in size until other people noticed and the prices snapped back into line.

*No-arbitrage is not a theory you believe in; it is a force, because the moment two equivalent payoffs diverge in price, a crowd of Weis pours in until they agree again.*

**Why no-arbitrage is the most useful idea you'll learn.** Notice what the put-call-parity argument did *not* require: any opinion about where the stock is going, any model of volatility, any forecast at all. It is a **relative** statement — given the price of three of the four things, the fourth is *determined*, full stop, on pain of free money. This is the entire spirit of derivatives pricing and a huge fraction of quant trading: you rarely need to know the absolute right price of anything; you need to know that *these prices must be consistent with each other*, and you trade the inconsistency. Market makers in options don't forecast the stock — they keep the whole surface of option prices internally consistent and arbitrage-free, and collect the spread for doing so. The skill the interview is probing with a parity question is whether you can reason in *relative, no-arbitrage* terms rather than reaching for a forecast you don't have. A candidate who answers "well, I'd need to predict the stock" has missed the entire point; the candidate who says "I don't need to — the four prices are locked together" has demonstrated the single most important mode of thought in the field.

This is the deep reason markets literacy matters even for the most theoretical researcher. A signal that "predicts" a price relationship which *no-arbitrage already pins down* is not a signal — it's a discovery that you've mismeasured something. Wei has watched promising backtests evaporate because the "edge" was really an arbitrage that transaction costs and the parity relationship had already closed. Knowing what *must* hold tells you where there's no point looking for alpha, which is half of knowing where there *is*.

## The instruments every quant should know

You do not need a derivatives textbook. You need the practical floor on three instruments, because between them they cover almost every interview prompt and most desks. Figure 4 is the whole map: what each is, what moves its price, how much leverage it carries, and the one risk that defines it.

![A four-row grid mapping the three instruments — equities, futures, options — across columns for what it is, its price driver, its leverage, and its one defining risk, showing equities as a share of a company with no leverage, futures as a promise to trade later with high margin leverage, and options as a right to buy or sell with embedded leverage from the premium](/imgs/blogs/the-markets-knowledge-every-quant-needs-4.png)

**Equities (stocks).** A share of stock is a fractional ownership claim on a company. Its price is driven by expected future earnings, interest rates (which discount those earnings), and supply-and-demand flows. Equities are the simplest instrument: no leverage when bought with cash, no expiry, and the worst case is the price going to zero (you can't lose more than you put in, unless you short). Most quant interviews use a stock as the generic "thing being traded" in a market-making game precisely because it has no extra moving parts. If you understand the order book on a stock, you understand the mechanics for everything else.

**Futures.** A futures contract is a standardized promise to buy or sell something — an index, oil, a Treasury bond, gold — at a set price on a set future date. Its price tracks the current **spot** price of the underlying plus a **carry** term (interest, storage, dividends foregone). The defining feature is **leverage via margin**: you don't pay the full notional value up front, only a margin deposit (often a few percent), so a small move in the underlying is a large percentage move on your deposit. The defining risk is the **margin call**: if the position moves against you and your margin runs low, the exchange demands more cash immediately, and if you can't post it, your position is liquidated at the worst possible moment. Futures are how desks take leveraged, capital-efficient directional and hedging positions. For the macro and rates context — why Treasury futures move the way they do — the [fixed-income](/blog/trading/fixed-income) and macro series go deep; here, just know the shape.

**Options.** An option is the *right, but not the obligation*, to buy (a **call**) or sell (a **put**) the underlying at the **strike** price, on or before expiry. You pay a **premium** for that right. The price of an option depends on four things: the current spot price, the strike, the time to expiry, and — uniquely — the **volatility** of the underlying. That last one is the whole reason options are their own discipline: an option is fundamentally a bet on *how much the price moves*, not just *which way*. The defining risk is that the premium decays to zero: if you buy an option and the move you paid for doesn't happen by expiry, you lose the entire premium. You don't need to price options from scratch to be markets-literate — but you must know what a call and a put are, what "strike" and "premium" and "volatility" mean, and that options carry embedded leverage. The full treatment is in [options theory](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/options-theory).

**Long, short, and notional.** Three words that cut across all three instruments. You are **long** something if you own it and profit when it rises; you are **short** if you've sold something you don't own (borrowing it to deliver) and profit when it falls. Going short is how a quant expresses a *negative* view or hedges, and it's central to the market-neutral strategies the research desks run — long the cheap names, short the expensive ones, so the market's overall direction cancels out. **Notional** is the total face value of a position: 200 shares of a \$100 stock is \$20,000 notional; one S&P 500 future might be \$250,000 of notional controlled by a \$15,000 margin deposit. The gap between notional and the cash you put up *is* the leverage, and it's why a futures position five percent of the way to a margin call feels very different from a cash stock position down five percent — same price move, wildly different consequence. When an interviewer asks you to size a position, they want a number in notional and an awareness of the leverage behind it.

What unites all three for a quant is that each trades on an order book, each has a bid-ask spread, each can be hit by slippage, and each is pinned by no-arbitrage relationships to the others (an option to the stock, a future to the spot). The instruments differ in their risk and their leverage, but the *market mechanics* — the thing this post is about — are the same across all of them. Learn the book once and you've learned it for everything. The deeper rates and macro context for futures lives in the [fixed-income series](/blog/trading/fixed-income); the full options treatment is in [options theory](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/options-theory); here you have exactly the floor, and the floor is enough to walk into any interview.

## Execution, slippage, impact, and transaction costs

Here is the topic that separates a backtest from a P&L, and it is the one Wei underrated for two years. **Transaction costs** are everything you lose between deciding to trade and the trade being done. They come in layers:

- **The spread.** Every time you take liquidity, you cross the bid-ask spread — you buy at the ask and would have to sell at the bid. On a round trip (buy then sell) you pay the full spread.
- **Slippage and market impact.** As we saw, a large order walks the book and pays a worse average price than the screen showed, and it pushes the price against itself. The bigger you trade relative to the available depth, the worse this gets.
- **Explicit fees.** Exchange fees, clearing fees, regulatory fees, the broker's commission. Small per share, but they add up at high volume.
- **Opportunity cost.** If you use patient limit orders to avoid the spread, you risk *not filling* and missing the move entirely — a real cost that doesn't show up on any invoice.

The discipline of trading large size cheaply — slicing a big order into small pieces over time, using algorithms like **VWAP** (volume-weighted average price) and **TWAP** (time-weighted) and **POV** (percent of volume) to blend into the market without showing your hand — is **execution**, and it is a whole research area covered in [execution algorithms](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/execution-algorithms-vwap-twap-pov-quant-research). The floor you need: trading is not free, the cost scales with how fast and how big you trade, and **a strategy's gross return and its net return can be wildly different**.

#### Worked example: transaction-cost drag turning a positive gross edge negative

Wei builds a signal. In his backtest — which assumes he trades at the mid-price for free — it earns a **gross edge of 5 basis points per trade** (a basis point is one-hundredth of a percent, so 5 bps is 0.05% of the notional). It's a fast signal, so he wants to trade it often. The question that determines whether he has a strategy or a mirage: what does it cost to trade?

- Suppose the realistic round-trip cost — half-spread each way plus fees plus a little slippage — starts around **3 bps per trade** at low turnover, but rises as he trades faster (his own impact grows): roughly 3 bps + 0.014 bps per round-trip-per-year of turnover.
- At a modest **72 round trips per year**, cost ≈ 3 + 0.014 × 72 ≈ **4 bps**, so net edge ≈ 5 − 4 = **1 bp per trade**, and the annualized net return peaks around **+0.7%** — positive, but a far cry from the gross.
- Push turnover to **143 round trips per year**, cost ≈ 3 + 0.014 × 143 ≈ **5 bps**, exactly equal to the gross edge: **net return crosses zero**.
- Trade *faster than that* and the gross edge is unchanged at 5 bps but the cost exceeds it: the strategy **loses money**, even though the signal "works" in every cost-free backtest.

Figure 6 plots all of this: gross return climbing linearly with turnover, the cost drag rising faster, and the net return arching up, peaking, and diving below zero.

![A chart of annualized return against turnover: a blue gross return line rises linearly, an amber transaction-cost drag line rises faster, and a green net return curve arches up to a peak around plus 0.7 percent near 72 round trips then crosses zero at about 143 round trips into a red shaded region where the edge is killed](/imgs/blogs/the-markets-knowledge-every-quant-needs-6.png)

*A backtest that ignores transaction costs is not optimistic, it is wrong; the costs are not a haircut on a good strategy, they are the thing that decides whether the strategy exists at all.*

This is why a researcher who can't reason about execution will ship a strategy that dies on contact with the market. It is also why the desk respects the execution and microstructure people, and why "what does it cost to trade this?" is a reflex question on every research review. Markets literacy is not a junior topic you graduate from — it is the reality check that runs underneath every senior decision. The proper backtesting discipline, including realistic cost modeling, is in the research series under [backtesting done right](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/execution-algorithms-vwap-twap-pov-quant-research).

## Why this shows up in interviews and on the desk

Now the payoff. Why does any of this matter for *getting hired* and for *doing the job*?

**In interviews, the market-making game assumes all of it.** When an interviewer says "make me a market on X," they are testing — in one compact exercise — whether you understand bid/ask/spread, whether you can compute a fair value, whether you widen your spread when you're uncertain and tighten it when you're confident, and crucially whether you *update* when they trade against you (adverse selection, live). A candidate who has never seen an order book has to reconstruct the entire concept under time pressure while also doing the EV math. A candidate who is markets-literate spends all their cognitive budget on the *decision* — the quote, the update, the sizing — because the mechanics are automatic. That is a five-rounds-deep advantage. The mechanics of these games, round by round, are in [the trading game and mental-math rounds](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-trading-game-and-mental-math-rounds-what-theyre-really-testing) and the technique in [market-making games](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews).

**On the desk, it's the shared language.** Whether you end up a trader, a researcher, or a developer, the people around you will speak in bids, offers, fills, spreads, slippage, and basis points all day. A researcher who hands a portfolio manager a signal without a cost estimate gets sent back. A developer building an order-routing system who doesn't understand price-time priority will build the wrong thing. A trader who doesn't feel the book in their bones will get picked off. Markets literacy is the substrate everyone stands on — it is the difference between participating in the conversation and translating it in your head.

#### Worked example: the interview EV of being markets-literate

Suppose Maya is interviewing at four market-making firms, each with a multi-round superday that includes a market-making game. Estimate, roughly, what the literacy is worth.

- Without it, she fumbles the game in maybe **3 of 4** superdays — she gets the EV math right but freezes on the mechanics, signaling she's "not a markets person." Say her per-firm offer probability is **8%**.
- With a weekend of focused literacy — reading a live book, paper-trading a few orders, drilling put-call parity — she clears the game cleanly and her per-firm probability rises to, say, **18%**.
- Expected offers without: 4 × 0.08 = **0.32**. Expected offers with: 4 × 0.18 = **0.72**.
- The lift is **0.40 expected offers** from a single weekend, on top of all the months she spent on the hard math. The marginal return on the *easy* gap is enormous precisely because almost nobody fills it.

*The cheapest edge in quant recruiting is the markets floor, because it takes a weekend to acquire and a lifetime of math prep is wasted without it.*

## How to build markets literacy fast

The good news is that this is the *fastest* part of quant prep to acquire, because it's concrete and bounded. Here is the efficient path.

1. **Watch a live order book for ten minutes.** Open any platform that shows the depth-of-book (Level 2) for a liquid stock or a futures contract. Watch the bids and asks update. See the spread tighten and widen. Watch a big print eat through levels. Ten minutes of this teaches more than any chapter, because the picture in Figure 1 becomes *motion*.
2. **Place a few tiny paper trades.** Most brokers offer a paper-trading (simulated) account. Send a market order and a limit order. Watch your market order fill instantly at the ask; watch your limit order rest and either fill or not. Feel the difference between taking and providing liquidity. This makes Figure 2 muscle memory.
3. **Learn put-call parity cold.** Spend one focused hour on the [put-call parity post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/put-call-parity-no-arbitrage-quant-interviews). It is the cleanest no-arbitrage argument and it shows up constantly. If you can derive it on a whiteboard, you understand the *style* of reasoning the whole field uses.
4. **Read one page on each instrument.** You already have it — Figure 4 is the map. Know what a stock, a future, and an option are; know the one risk that defines each. Don't go deeper until a role demands it.
5. **Run a tiny backtest with realistic costs.** Take any simple signal, backtest it twice — once frictionless, once with a few basis points of cost per trade — and watch the net return collapse the way Figure 6 shows. This single exercise inoculates you against the most common research mistake.
6. **Play a market-making game and lose.** Find a friend or an online sim, make markets, and let them pick you off when your quote is stale. Losing to adverse selection *once* teaches it permanently. Pair this with the [trading game post](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-trading-game-and-mental-math-rounds-what-theyre-really-testing).

## Common misconceptions

Markets literacy is surrounded by myths that technical people, in particular, tend to carry. Each one is worth naming and killing.

**Myth 1: "Price equals value."** No. **Price** is where the last trade happened — where one buyer's maximum met one seller's minimum at one instant. **Value** is what a thing is *worth*, which is an estimate, a posterior, a thing reasonable people disagree on. The entire game of trading exists in the gap between them: you buy when you believe value exceeds price and sell when you believe price exceeds value. If price always equaled value, there would be no trade and no industry. A market maker's quote isn't a claim that price equals value — it's a two-sided bet centered on the maker's *estimate* of value, with a spread to cover being wrong. Confusing the two is the tell of someone who has never actually traded.

**Myth 2: "The spread is a fee."** It is not a fee the exchange or a broker charges; it is the market maker's *earned compensation* for providing liquidity and bearing adverse selection. You don't pay the spread to a middleman who pockets it risk-free — you pay it to the entity that took the other side of your trade and now holds the risk of being picked off. The distinction matters because it explains *why* spreads are wide on illiquid or volatile things (more adverse-selection risk to compensate) and tight on liquid, calm ones. A fee would be constant; the spread is a price for risk, and it moves.

**Myth 3: "You need deep finance for a quant role."** For most trading and development roles, you need the floor in this post plus the math and the technique — not a finance degree, not the CFA, not a derivatives course. Firms hire mathematicians, physicists, and computer scientists *specifically* and teach them the markets layer, because the markets floor is the small, learnable part and the quantitative ability is the rare part. The backgrounds that actually get hired — and the (non-)role of a PhD — are covered in the [backgrounds post](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-quant-curriculum-map-what-to-learn-in-what-order). What you *can't* skip is the floor, because the floor is what lets your math be *useful*.

**Myth 4: "Options are too advanced to bother with."** A common defensive crouch from people strong in CS or pure math: options feel like a separate, scary discipline, so they skip them entirely and hope the interview doesn't go there. But the *floor* on options is small — call, put, strike, premium, the fact that an option is a bet on volatility, and that put-call parity ties options to the stock. That floor takes an afternoon and shows up in interviews constantly because options are where the cleanest no-arbitrage arguments live. You don't need to price a barrier option with Monte Carlo. You need to not freeze when someone says "the call is trading at six and the put at five — is that consistent?"

**Myth 5: "Markets literacy is a one-time checkbox."** Tempting to think you read this post, clear the floor, and never think about microstructure again. But the floor is the *reality check* that runs under every senior decision: every research idea has to survive a transaction-cost estimate; every system has to respect how the book actually works; every quote has to account for adverse selection. The depth you add over a career is large, but it's all built on this floor, and the senior people are the ones who never forgot it. The floor doesn't expire; it deepens.

## How it plays out in the real world

Concretely, here is how markets literacy shows up across the firms and the careers in this series.

**At the market makers (Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, IMC, Citadel Securities).** The interview *is* a markets-literacy test wearing a probability costume. Optiver and IMC front-load a brutal mental-math screen (around 60–80 questions in 8 minutes, no calculator), then move to market-making games and probability. SIG, famously, uses poker and game-theory framing — decision-making under uncertainty with real stakes. Jane Street's games are EV-and-updating exercises where the order book is implicit in every prompt. Across all of them, a candidate who is markets-fluent saves their working memory for the *decision* — the quote, the update, the size — while the fumbling candidate burns it reconstructing what a bid is. The firm-by-firm interview shape is detailed across the series; the unifying fact is that the markets floor is *assumed*, never taught.

**On a trading desk.** A junior trader's first months are largely about internalizing the book until it's reflexive — feeling when the market is thin and they should widen, feeling when a counterparty's persistent buying is information and they should lift their fair value. The math that got them hired is necessary but invisible; the visible daily skill is markets fluency under pressure. A trader who is great at probability but slow on the book gets run over, because in the moment there's no time to derive — only to act.

**On a research desk.** Wei's reality: a signal is worthless until it survives realistic transaction costs, and a "signal" that merely re-discovers a no-arbitrage relationship is no signal at all. Researchers at systematic funds like Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw spend enormous effort on *execution-aware* research — building cost models, estimating capacity, and slicing orders so their own trading doesn't destroy the edge (the lesson of Figure 6, industrialized). A researcher who can't reason about microstructure ships strategies that look brilliant in the backtest and bleed in production. The reviewers know this, which is why "what does this cost to trade, and at what capacity?" is the first question in every research review.

**For developers and engineers.** The people building matching-engine-adjacent systems, order routers, and market-data pipelines need the microstructure floor to build the *right* thing — to respect price-time priority, to model the book correctly, to understand why a microsecond of latency is worth real money at firms whose edge is being first in the queue. A developer who treats the market as a black box will get the data model wrong in ways that cost the desk money.

Across every path, the pattern is the same: the markets floor is the smallest, most learnable part of becoming a quant, and skipping it is the most common self-inflicted wound. The candidate from the intro had everything except a weekend's worth of this, and it cost him the offer.
