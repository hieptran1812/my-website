> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In your first 90 days at a quant firm you have something dangerous: real access to systems and capital, and almost none of the judgment that is supposed to govern that access. Your job is not to be impressive. Your job is to protect the downside — don't blow up, don't lose trust — while quietly compounding credibility. It is a pure expected-value asymmetry problem.
>
> - **The downside dwarfs the upside early.** A flashy week-two bet that goes right buys you a little credibility; the same bet going wrong, or one hidden error, can cost you the seat. When the downside is career-scale and the upside is small, the correct play is almost always the patient one.
> - **Trust is the currency, and it compounds.** Scope — bigger size, less supervision, eventually your own book or system — is *unlocked* by reliability, not granted by tenure. A small task done well earns a bigger one. The whole ramp is a loop that converts trust into scope.
> - **The rookie failure modes are not technical.** The careers that end early die from overconfidence, hiding errors, struggling silently, oversizing, and misreading the desk — not from being bad at math. Every one of them trades a small visible cost now for a large hidden cost later.
> - **The one number to remember:** in the first 90 days, the asymmetry can be roughly **+2 vs −10** — a flashy move's upside is a couple of credibility points, its downside is a career-scale loss. You do not win this period by maximizing; you win it by not losing.

It is 9:14 on a Tuesday morning, and **Maya** is one keystroke away from a mistake that could follow her for years.

She is nine days into her first job as a quant trader at a top market-making firm — the kind of place where, two weeks ago, she was a candidate sweating through a mental-math screen, and where now she has a login that can move real size in a real market. Her onboarding buddy stepped away to grab coffee. On her screen is a ticket for a hedge the desk wants put on: a few hundred contracts, routine, the kind of thing the senior next to her does without thinking. Maya has watched it done a dozen times. She is *almost* sure she has the side right — buy, not sell — and *almost* sure of the size. The order entry box is filled in. Her cursor is over the submit button. A small, ambitious voice says: *do it yourself, show them you can, don't be the rookie who needs hand-holding.* A second, quieter voice says: *you are about to send a live order into a real market with money you do not yet fully understand, and you are only ninety percent sure.*

The gap between those two voices is the entire subject of this post. Because here is the thing the ambitious voice does not know yet: if Maya sends that order and she is right, almost nothing happens — the hedge goes on, nobody notices, she saved her buddy ninety seconds. If she sends it and she is *wrong* — wrong side, extra zero, wrong symbol — she has just done real damage with real money on day nine, and the story the desk tells about Maya for the next two years is *the one who sent a live order alone before she was ready.* The upside is tiny. The downside is enormous. This is not a hard decision once you see the asymmetry. Maya pulls her cursor back, waits the ninety seconds, and asks her buddy to check the ticket when he gets back. He glances at it, says "side's right, but that's double the size we want," fixes it, and sends it. She just avoided being a cautionary tale, and nobody will ever know — which is exactly how a good first 90 days is supposed to feel.

Figure 1 is the shape of those 90 days. Notice what it is *not*: it is not a steady climb where you do a little more each week until you are running the desk. It is a deliberate, staged ramp — weeks of pure observation, then shadowing, then a tiny supervised mandate, then owning one small thing — and the speed of the ramp is governed not by the calendar but by how much trust you have earned.

![A four-stage timeline of the first ninety days at a quant firm: weeks one to two observing the desk and learning the systems with no live risk, weeks three to six shadowing a senior, weeks seven to ten a first tiny supervised mandate, and weeks eleven to thirteen owning one small thing end to end](/imgs/blogs/your-first-90-days-ramping-without-blowing-up-1.png)

The thesis of this post is one sentence, and it is the opposite of what most ambitious new hires believe: **in your first 90 days, your job is to protect the downside while compounding credibility — not to prove you are brilliant.** You were *already* judged brilliant; that is why you have the seat. Thousands of people who are also brilliant did not get it. The firm is not waiting to find out whether you are smart. It is waiting to find out whether you are *safe* — whether it can hand you more access without lying awake about it. That is a different test, and most of the way people fail it has nothing to do with intelligence. We will build the idea from zero, walk the ramp week by week, do the EV math that makes the patient play obviously correct, and name the specific rookie mistakes that end careers in the first quarter. The good news is that this is the most *learnable* period of the whole career, because the right behaviors are simple. The hard part is having the discipline to do the simple thing when an ambitious voice in your head is telling you to do the flashy one.

## Foundations: what the first 90 days are actually for

Before any of the tactics make sense, you need a small, precise vocabulary and one core idea. None of it is hard, but each piece is load-bearing, and most new hires struggle precisely because they are missing one of these. Assume you are brilliant but have never worked at a firm that hands new people real risk — we will build each idea from zero.

**What "ramping" means.** Ramping is the controlled process of going from zero access to real ownership. At a quant firm this is unusually high-stakes because the systems you touch move money, sometimes a lot of it, sometimes in milliseconds. A new software engineer at a normal tech company can ship a bug and roll it back; a new quant trader can send a wrong order and the loss is instant and permanent. So firms ramp you in stages, each one widening what you are allowed to touch, and each gated by whether you have shown you can handle the previous stage. The ramp is the firm's risk-management process applied to *you*.

**Scope.** Scope is the size and autonomy of what you are allowed to do. On day one your scope is roughly zero — you can read, watch, and run things in a sandbox, but you cannot move real size alone. By the end of a good first year your scope might be a small book you manage, or a system you own and deploy. Scope is the thing that grows over a career, and the entire junior-to-senior arc is, in one frame, the steady expansion of scope. The first 90 days are where that expansion *starts* — and where it can stall for years if you mishandle it.

**Trust.** Trust is the firm's belief that you will do the right thing when no one is watching — that you will size correctly, flag your errors, ask before you guess, and not blow up the seat. Trust is the *currency* that buys scope. You do not get a bigger mandate because you have been there longer; you get it because the people who would be blamed if you blew up have decided they can stop watching you so closely. Trust is slow to build and fast to destroy, which is the asymmetry this whole post is about.

**Credibility.** Credibility is the reputation layer on top of trust: not just "she won't blow up" but "she's good, her judgment is sound, give her the interesting work." Trust gets you scope; credibility gets you the *good* scope — the desks, the projects, the mentors worth learning from. Both compound, and both can be cratered by a single bad event early, which is why protecting them is the dominant strategy in the first quarter.

**Expected value (EV) and the asymmetry.** EV is the average outcome of a decision, weighting each possible result by its probability. If a bet wins +10 with probability 0.5 and loses −2 with probability 0.5, its EV is 0.5 × 10 + 0.5 × (−2) = +4 — a good bet. EV is the spine of this entire career series, and the core idea of *this* post is a particular shape of EV: in your first 90 days, the EV of trying to look impressive is *negative*, because the payoffs are wildly asymmetric. The upside of a flashy early move is small — a little credibility — and the downside is huge — lost trust, a damaged reputation, sometimes the seat itself. When the downside dwarfs the upside, the correct play is to minimize the chance of the bad outcome, *even at the cost of some upside.* If you have read [Kelly criterion and sequential betting](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews), you already know the deep version of this: avoiding ruin matters more than capturing gains, because you cannot compound from zero. Your career is a sequential bet, and the first 90 days are where the ruin probability is highest.

**Blowing up.** "Blowing up" is the trader's term for a catastrophic, often irrecoverable loss — the account goes to zero, the seat is gone. In the first-90-days context it has a softer but just as final meaning: blowing up your *credibility*. You don't have to lose the firm money to blow up early. Hiding a small error, repeatedly oversizing, freezing and hiding it when you're lost — these blow up the *trust* account, and a blown trust account can be just as career-ending as a blown trading account, because nobody will give you scope again.

**Why the downside is asymmetric specifically *for you*, specifically *now*.** Two facts stack. First, you have access disproportionate to your judgment: the firm gave you the keys before you fully understand the building, by necessity, because the only way to learn is with some real exposure. Second, you have no track record to absorb a bad event. A senior who makes one mistake has years of reliability buffering the story; a new hire who makes one *visible, trust-destroying* mistake has nothing to weigh against it, so that one event *becomes* the story. The same mistake costs a rookie far more than a veteran. That is the asymmetry, and it is why the first quarter is the highest-variance, highest-stakes stretch of the entire career — not because the work is hard, but because the cost of a misstep is at its lifetime maximum while your ability to avoid one is at its lifetime minimum.

Hold those ideas — ramp, scope, trust, credibility, EV asymmetry, blowing up — and the rest of this post is detail.

## The disciplined move vs the rookie move

Almost every first-90-days decision reduces to the same fork, and Figure 2 lays the two branches side by side. On the left is the rookie move: oversize to look impressive, hide a small error and hope nobody notices, struggle silently for days rather than ask, act on a guess about how a system works. On the right is the disciplined move: size small and earn the right to size bigger, flag the error in minutes and own the fix, ask one sharp question after a genuine attempt, confirm the system on a tiny test before acting on real size.

![A before-and-after comparison: on the left the rookie move shows oversizing to look impressive, hiding a small error, struggling silently, and acting on a guess; on the right the disciplined move shows sizing small to earn bigger size, flagging the error in minutes, asking one sharp question after a real attempt, and confirming the system on a tiny test first](/imgs/blogs/your-first-90-days-ramping-without-blowing-up-2.png)

What makes the left column so seductive is that *each item looks locally rational* to an ambitious person. Oversizing looks like confidence. Hiding the small error looks like sparing everyone a fuss over something trivial. Struggling silently looks like self-reliance. Acting on a guess looks like initiative. The rookie move is not stupidity — it is ambition pointed in the wrong direction. The reason the right column wins is not that it is more virtuous; it is that it has *better EV under the asymmetry.* Sizing small caps your downside. Flagging the error converts a hidden time bomb into a thirty-second non-event. Asking after a real attempt buys judgment cheaply. Confirming on a tiny test trades a few minutes for the elimination of a catastrophic tail. The disciplined move is, in every case, the one that protects the downside — which is the whole game right now.

The deeper point: these are not two personality types. They are two *policies*, and you choose your policy fresh on every decision. A brilliant, ambitious person who has internalized the asymmetry will pick the right column every time, not because they are timid but because they can do the EV in their head. Let's make that EV explicit.

#### Worked example: the EV-asymmetry of a flashy early bet

Maya, day nine, faces the choice from the opening. Frame it as a bet. There is a hedge to put on. Two policies:

**Policy A — the flashy move.** Send the order herself, alone, while her buddy is away. She is, say, **90%** confident she has it exactly right. If she's right (probability 0.90), the payoff is small: she saved ninety seconds and looks a touch more capable. Call that **+2 credibility units.** If she's wrong (probability 0.10) — wrong size, wrong side — she has put real, mistaken risk into the market on day nine with no supervision. That is a trust-destroying event; the story sticks. Call that **−10 credibility units** (a career-scale loss, capped only because she's unlikely to be literally fired for one supervised-context error, but it dominates her reputation for a long time).

EV of Policy A = 0.90 × (+2) + 0.10 × (−10) = 1.8 − 1.0 = **+0.8**.

**Policy B — the patient move.** Wait ninety seconds, have her buddy check the ticket, then send. If the ticket was right (0.90), payoff is roughly **+2** anyway — the hedge goes on, and quietly asking for a check on a live order on day nine is itself a small *credibility gain*, not a loss, because it signals exactly the safety the firm is testing for. If the ticket was wrong (0.10), her buddy catches it — which is what happened — and the payoff is **+1**: the error never reaches the market, and she just demonstrated good instincts. There is no −10 branch at all.

EV of Policy B = 0.90 × (+2) + 0.10 × (+1) = 1.8 + 0.1 = **+1.9**.

Policy B has more than double the EV of Policy A — and, more importantly, *it has no ruin tail.* Even if you think the numbers are off, the structure is robust: Policy A carries a small chance of a catastrophic outcome in exchange for a tiny expected gain; Policy B removes the catastrophe for a ninety-second cost. Figure 3 draws this as bars. The flashy bet's upside is +2 and its downside is −10; the patient play's upside is +3 and its downside is a bounded, recoverable −2. The reason the patient play wins is not that its average is higher — it's that *the patient play cannot ruin you, and the flashy one can.*

![A bar chart comparing two ways to spend the first ninety days: the flashy early bet has a small plus-two upside in green and a career-scale minus-ten downside in red, while the patient play has a plus-three upside and a bounded minus-two recoverable downside, showing the downside dwarfs the upside early on](/imgs/blogs/your-first-90-days-ramping-without-blowing-up-3.png)

*The lesson: when the downside is career-scale and the upside is a couple of points, you do not maximize — you protect the tail, and ninety seconds is a cheap price for removing a ruin branch.*

The numbers above are illustrative; nobody literally scores credibility in units. But the *shape* is exactly right, and once you can see the shape, the supposedly hard "should I just do it myself?" decisions of the first quarter become easy. You are almost never trading away meaningful upside by being careful. You are trading away the *appearance* of boldness — and that appearance is worth far less than you think, especially to people who have watched a hundred bold rookies and know exactly how that movie ends.

## The ramp curve, week by week

The ramp in Figure 1 is not arbitrary. Each stage exists to let you build a specific capability while your exposure to catastrophic mistakes stays bounded. Here is what each stretch is actually for, and how to spend it well.

**Weeks 1–2: Observe.** You have almost no live access, and that is correct — you don't yet know enough to use it safely. Your entire job is to *learn the systems and read the room.* Learn how orders are entered, how positions are monitored, what the dashboards mean, where the kill switch is, what the daily rhythm of the desk is, who does what. On the research side, learn the data pipeline, the backtester, the code review norms, where the gotchas live. This is also when you read the *culture* — more on that below — because the first two weeks are the only time you get to observe before people have formed an opinion of you. The rookie mistake here is having loud opinions before you understand the context: the new hire who, on day three, suggests the desk is "doing risk wrong" has just told everyone they don't yet know what they don't know. The disciplined move is to take notes, ask clarifying questions, and bank observations for later when you've earned the standing to act on them.

**Weeks 3–6: Shadow.** Now you sit with a senior and watch real decisions get made in real time. This is the highest-leverage stretch of the entire ramp, and most new hires waste it by watching *what* the senior does instead of asking *why.* The value is not in memorizing the senior's actions — those are situation-specific and will be useless next week. The value is in extracting the senior's *judgment*: why did they size that position the way they did? Why did they pass on that trade that looked good to you? Why did they re-run that backtest with a different split? Each "why" is a piece of the judgment you don't yet have and the firm is paying for you to acquire. The goal of shadowing is to build the internal model that lets you eventually make those calls yourself. A senior who is good at being shadowed will narrate their reasoning; a great shadow makes that easy by asking the right "why" at the right moment and never the "what" that the documentation already answers.

**Weeks 7–10: First mandate.** You get a tiny, supervised piece of real work — a small position to manage, a small signal to build and check, a small system change to make. The size is deliberately small because the point is not the P&L; the point is to see how you behave with real skin in the game. Do you size to your actual edge or to your ego? Do you flag the first thing that goes sideways, or sit on it? Do you follow the process even when nobody's checking? A lead is double-checking your work at this stage, which is the safety net — and the worst thing you can do is treat the safety net as permission to be sloppy. The mandate is small, but how you handle it is the single biggest determinant of how fast the next stage arrives.

**Weeks 11–13: Own a small thing.** You take full ownership of one small loop end to end — you do it, you monitor it, you flag and fix your own errors, and the lead's double-check fades from "before every action" to "spot-checks." This is the first real taste of scope, and it is also the first time the training wheels are genuinely off for one narrow thing. The skill that matters most here is *knowing when you're about to hit a cliff and asking before you go over it* — the difference between "I'm not sure about this edge case, can I run it by you?" and silently shipping something you didn't fully understand. Owning a small thing well is the proof-of-concept the firm needs before it gives you a bigger thing.

The crucial thing to understand about this curve: **it is a default, not a guarantee, and it runs at the speed of trust.** A new hire who flags errors, asks well, and never oversizes will often find the stages compress — they're owning a small thing by week eight because they've shown they're safe. A new hire who hides errors and oversizes will find the stages *stretch* — they're still being double-checked at month four because nobody can stop watching. The calendar in Figure 1 is the median path; your behavior moves you up or down it.

#### Worked example: a 90-day ramp plan with weekly milestones

Let's make the plan concrete. Maya, the new quant trader, writes herself a ramp plan on day one — not because anyone asked, but because having explicit milestones is itself a credibility signal and a way to measure whether she's on track. Here is her plan, with one measurable milestone per stretch:

```python
ramp_plan = {
    "Weeks 1-2 (Observe)": {
        "milestone": "Can explain the full order lifecycle and "
                     "find the kill switch without help.",
        "do": "Shadow the dashboards, map who-does-what, take notes.",
        "avoid": "Loud opinions before I understand the context.",
    },
    "Weeks 3-6 (Shadow)": {
        "milestone": "Can predict the senior's next move and explain "
                     "why correctly 8 times out of 10.",
        "do": "Ask 'why' on every non-obvious call; log the answers.",
        "avoid": "Asking 'what' that the docs already answer.",
    },
    "Weeks 7-10 (First mandate)": {
        "milestone": "Handle a tiny position/signal; flag every error "
                     "within minutes; zero hidden mistakes.",
        "do": "Size small, follow the process, surface friction fast.",
        "avoid": "Oversizing or sitting on a small mistake.",
    },
    "Weeks 11-13 (Own a thing)": {
        "milestone": "Own one small loop end to end; lead moves from "
                     "checking every action to spot-checks.",
        "do": "Run the loop, ask before any cliff, own the fixes.",
        "avoid": "Silent struggle that turns into a blowup.",
    },
}
```

Notice three things about this plan. First, every milestone is *observable* — "can explain the order lifecycle," "predict the senior's move 8 of 10," "zero hidden mistakes" — not vague like "get comfortable." Second, every stretch has an explicit *avoid*, because knowing the failure mode is half of dodging it. Third, the plan ramps *capability and ownership*, never *size for its own sake.* Maya is not trying to be running a big book by day 90; she is trying to be *completely trustworthy* with a small one, which is what unlocks the big one later.

*The lesson: write the plan down, make the milestones measurable, and define success as "demonstrably safe with a small thing," not "impressively large." The size comes later, automatically, once the trust is there.*

## Shadowing and the first small mandate

The two middle stages deserve a closer look, because this is where most of the *learnable* skill of the first 90 days lives.

**Shadowing is about stealing judgment, not actions.** Picture two new researchers shadowing the same senior. The first one watches the senior reject an idea and writes down "rejected idea X." Useless — next week there's a different idea. The second one watches the same rejection and asks, "what made you kill that one so fast?" and the senior says, "the in-sample Sharpe was great but the turnover meant it'd die after costs, and I've been burned by that exact shape three times." *Now* the second researcher has a transferable piece of judgment — a pattern-match for a whole class of dead ideas — that they'll use for years. The art of shadowing is converting the senior's specific actions into general principles, and the tool for that conversion is the well-aimed "why."

This connects directly to how the work actually gets done day to day, which we cover in [a day in the life of a quant trader](/blog/trading/quant-careers/a-day-in-the-life-quant-trader) and in [the research workflow in production, from idea to live signal](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-research-workflow-in-production-from-idea-to-live-signal). Shadowing is your chance to see those workflows from the inside before you're responsible for them. Spend it extracting the *reasoning*, not memorizing the *recipe*.

**The first small mandate is a controlled experiment — on you.** When you get your first real piece of work, the firm already knows the work is small enough that you can't do much damage. What it's measuring is your *behavior under real stakes*, which is information it cannot get any other way. Three behaviors are under the microscope:

- **Sizing.** Do you size to your actual, demonstrated edge — which is small, because you're new — or do you size to how confident you *feel*? New hires systematically oversize because they confuse the intensity of their conviction with the quality of their edge. The right move is to size *smaller than feels right*, because your calibration isn't proven yet and a too-small position costs almost nothing while a too-big one can hurt. This is the desk-level version of the discipline covered in [risk discipline and not blowing up](/blog/trading/quant-careers/risk-discipline-and-not-blowing-up): the position size is the one variable that, set wrong, can end you regardless of how good the idea was.
- **Error handling.** When — not if — something goes sideways, what do you do in the first sixty seconds? Flag it, or freeze? This is the single most-watched behavior of the entire ramp, and we'll do its math in a moment.
- **Process adherence.** Do you follow the process when the lead isn't looking? Run the OOS check even though you're sure? Do the code review even though it's a one-line change? The first mandate is where the firm learns whether your discipline is real or performative.

The reason the mandate is *small* is so that the firm can learn all of this *cheaply.* A blown small mandate teaches everyone what they needed to know at low cost. A blown large mandate would teach the same thing at high cost — which is exactly why you don't get a large one yet. The mandate's size is the firm's risk budget for finding out who you are.

## Asking questions well — and the kind that erode trust

New hires are told "there are no dumb questions," and it is a kind, well-meaning lie. There are no dumb questions in the sense that you should never suffer in silence to protect your ego. But there absolutely are questions that *cost you credibility* and questions that *build it*, and learning the difference is one of the highest-return skills of the first quarter.

**The question that builds trust** has three properties: you made a genuine attempt first, you've narrowed the question to something specific, and you can't resolve it from the documentation or by experiment in reasonable time. "I traced the order flow and I understand it up to the point where it hits the risk check, but I can't tell from the code whether the check blocks or just warns on a soft-limit breach — which is it?" That question signals competence: you did the work, you got specific, you hit a real wall. The senior answers it in ten seconds and *thinks more of you*, not less.

**The question that erodes trust** has the opposite properties: no attempt, no specificity, and the answer is sitting in the docs you didn't read. "How does the order system work?" on day five, when there's a wiki page titled exactly that, tells the senior you'd rather spend their time than your own — which is precisely the wrong signal during the period when they're deciding how much of their attention you're worth. Repeated, it's corrosive: each one says "I'm a tax on your time," and the senior starts routing around you.

The discipline, then, is not "ask fewer questions" — it's **"try first, get specific, then ask fast."** The "try first" caps the time you waste. The "get specific" makes the question cheap to answer and signals competence. The "then ask fast" is the crucial counterweight: once you've made a real attempt and hit a genuine wall, *ask immediately* — do not let pride turn a ten-minute question into a three-day silent struggle. There's a window, and on a quant desk it's often measured in minutes for trading questions and a couple of hours for research questions. Past that window, the cost of being stuck exceeds the cost of "looking like you needed help," and the EV flips hard toward asking.

One more nuance, because it's where smart people specifically go wrong: **the smartest new hires are the most prone to the silent-struggle failure**, because they've spent their whole lives being the person who figures it out alone, and asking feels like an admission they're not that person anymore. Reframe it. On a quant desk, *the cost of you being stuck is the firm's cost, not your private burden.* An hour you spend stuck is an hour of the firm's expensive new hire producing nothing, and possibly an hour during which a small problem is quietly growing into a big one. Asking the right question fast isn't weakness — it's you correctly minimizing the firm's cost, which is exactly the judgment they hired you for.

## Owning mistakes: the trust multiplier

Here is the most counterintuitive and most important idea in the entire post: **flagging your own error, fast, is one of the strongest positive credibility signals you can send — and hiding even a small one is among the most career-ending.** It feels backwards. Surely the error itself is the bad thing, and admitting it just makes it worse? No. On a desk that handles real money, *everyone makes errors* — seniors included — and the firm knows it. What the firm cannot tolerate is an error it *doesn't know about*, because an unknown error is an uncontrolled risk, and uncontrolled risk is the one thing a risk-management business exists to eliminate. So the firm's true preference ordering is: (1) no error, best; (2) error, flagged immediately, fine — the system catches it; (3) error, hidden, catastrophic — the system is blind to it and you've proven you'll keep it blind. The gap between (2) and (3) is enormous, and it is entirely under your control.

#### Worked example: the cost of hiding a small error vs flagging it

Wei, the new quant researcher, finds at 4 p.m. that a feature he shipped into a shared research dataset two days ago has a subtle bug — a small look-ahead leak that slightly inflates any backtest using it. The damage so far is small: two colleagues have run backtests on it, and the inflation is minor. He faces two policies.

**Policy A — flag it now.** He posts in the team channel immediately: "Heads up — feature `X` I committed Tuesday has a look-ahead leak, here's the fix, and here are the two backtests that touched it so you can re-run." Cost: thirty minutes of his time and a small, brief embarrassment. Payoff: the leak is contained within hours, the two colleagues re-run, no bad result reaches anyone important, and — critically — Wei has just demonstrated the *exact* behavior the firm most wants to see in a researcher. Net credibility: roughly **+3**. Yes, *positive*, because flagging a self-inflicted error fast is a stronger signal of trustworthiness than never having the error at all is of competence.

**Policy B — quietly fix it and say nothing.** He pushes a silent fix, tells himself the inflation was tiny and the colleagues' backtests "probably don't matter." Cost now: zero, and a moment of relief. But the expected cost *later* is severe. Suppose there's a 30% chance the leak gets discovered — git history, a colleague noticing their numbers shifted, the bug recurring. If discovered, the finding is no longer "Wei made a small mistake" (forgivable) but "Wei *hid* a mistake that contaminated shared data" (a trust-destroying event, because now every result Wei has ever produced is suspect). Call that discovered-cost **−15 credibility.** EV of Policy B = 0.70 × 0 + 0.30 × (−15) = **−4.5**, and the −15 branch is the kind that gets people quietly managed out.

The math: Policy A is **+3**, Policy B is **−4.5** — a swing of **7.5 credibility units**, in favor of the option that feels worse in the moment. And the asymmetry is even starker than the average suggests, because Policy A has no bad tail at all, while Policy B's whole expected cost lives in a catastrophic tail. Flagging converts a hidden time bomb into a controlled, even *credibility-positive*, event.

*The lesson: the error is rarely what ends you; hiding it is. Flagging fast is not damage control — it is one of the highest-return moves available to a new hire, because it proves the one thing the firm most needs to know about you.*

There's a cultural tell here worth internalizing. Watch how seniors talk about their own mistakes on your desk. At a healthy quant firm, they talk about them *openly* — "I screwed up the hedge ratio last week, here's what I learned" — precisely because the culture has figured out that surfacing errors fast is how a risk business survives. If you're on a desk where seniors openly own their errors, that's your signal that flagging is not just safe but expected. Match it.

## The rookie failure modes — and the fix for each

Pull the failure modes together. Figure 5 lays out the five ways a strong new hire blows up early, why each one ends careers, and the cheap disciplined fix for each. The unifying pattern: **every failure mode trades a small visible cost now for a large hidden cost later.** That's exactly the trade the EV asymmetry tells you never to make.

![A grid of five rookie failure modes with why each one ends careers and the fix: overconfidence and oversizing fixed by sizing to your actual edge, hiding errors fixed by flagging in minutes, silent struggle fixed by trying then asking one sharp question fast, acting on a guess fixed by confirming on a tiny test first, and misreading the desk fixed by watching norms in weeks one to two and matching the tempo](/imgs/blogs/your-first-90-days-ramping-without-blowing-up-5.png)

**Overconfidence (oversizing).** The failure: you confuse the strength of your conviction with the quality of your edge, and you put on too big a position or push too large a change. Why it ends careers: one bad fill on an oversized position can erase months of carefully built credibility in an afternoon, and the story becomes "reckless." The fix: size to your *demonstrated* edge, which is small because you're new — deliberately smaller than feels right. A too-small position costs you almost nothing; a too-big one can end you.

**Hiding errors.** The failure: you sit on a small mistake hoping it'll wash out. Why it ends careers: a hidden small loss, when discovered, becomes "she hides things" — which makes every number she's ever produced suspect and is functionally unhirable. The fix: flag it in minutes and own the fix loudly. (See the worked example above — this is the single highest-return habit.)

**Silent struggle.** The failure: you're stuck and you grind in silence for days rather than ask, because asking feels like weakness. Why it ends careers: it doesn't, usually, in one shot — but it bleeds your productivity, lets small problems grow, and signals you can't calibrate when to escalate. The fix: try genuinely, then ask one sharp, specific question *fast.* The window is minutes-to-hours, not days.

**Acting on a guess.** The failure: you're not sure how a system behaves, but you act anyway — send the order, run the job, ship the change — on a guess. Why it ends careers: a wrong assumption about a system that touches real capital can cause real, immediate damage. The fix: confirm on a tiny test first. Send one contract, not five hundred. Run the job on one symbol, not the universe. Trade two minutes for the elimination of a catastrophic tail.

**Misreading the desk.** The failure: you don't read the culture — you're too loud, too quiet, too casual, too formal, you interrupt the wrong person at the wrong moment. Why it ends careers: it doesn't directly, but it burns the goodwill you'll need to *get scope*, because scope is granted by people who like working with you. The fix: spend weeks 1–2 watching the norms before you assert yourself, and match the desk's tempo and register.

#### Worked example: Maya and Wei, two new hires, two ramps

Make it concrete with both characters running their first quarter in parallel — Maya as a new quant trader, Wei as a new quant researcher.

**Maya (new QT).** Her trading desk is fast and verbal. In weeks 1–2 she watches the order flow, maps the dashboards, finds the kill switch, and — crucially — notices that the desk's norm is to *say your size out loud* before sending anything non-trivial, a habit she adopts. Week 9, she's on her first small mandate: a tiny hedging book, double-checked by a lead. A quote comes in that *looks* like easy edge, and her conviction is high. The rookie move is to size it up to make her first week of P&L look good. Instead she sizes it *small* — to her demonstrated edge, which is barely-established — and says her size out loud as the desk does. The senior nods. The trade works; the P&L is unimpressive but clean. Two weeks later the *same shape* of quote comes in, she's now seen it work, she sizes a notch bigger, and the lead has stopped checking that category of trade. **That notch is scope, and she earned it by not grabbing for it the first time.** By day 90, Maya's lead describes her in exactly the words that unlock the next stage: "completely safe, never surprises me, ready for a real book."

**Wei (new QR).** His research group is quieter and asynchronous. In weeks 3–6 he shadows a senior by reading her killed-idea writeups and asking, on each, *why* she killed it — building the pattern-library of dead-idea shapes. Week 8, on his first small mandate, he finds the look-ahead bug from the earlier worked example. The rookie move is to fix it quietly. Instead he flags it in the channel within thirty minutes, posts the fix and the affected backtests, and takes a moment of small embarrassment. The senior's private reaction is *relief* — this is a researcher who will surface contamination, the one thing she most fears in a teammate. **That flag bought Wei more trust than three clean weeks would have.** By day 90, Wei is owning a small signal end to end, and his senior has moved from reviewing every commit to spot-checking — because she's learned she doesn't have to watch him to know he'll tell her the truth.

*The lesson: Maya earned scope by not oversizing the first chance she got; Wei earned it by flagging an error he could have hidden. Neither did anything flashy. Both did the disciplined thing, and both got the same payoff — the firm stopped watching, which is the definition of trust converting to scope.*

## How trust converts into scope

Step back and look at the whole machine. Figure 4 draws the loop: a small task done reliably earns a bigger mandate; the bigger mandate done reliably earns trust; trust earns ownership; and each reliable cycle loops back to *widen the next grant.* This is the engine of the entire junior-to-senior career, and the first 90 days are where you start it turning.

![A pipeline showing how trust converts into scope as a loop: a small task with close supervision done reliably earns a bigger mandate with less hand-holding, which done reliably makes you trusted so the lead stops double-checking, which earns ownership of a book or system, and each reliable cycle loops back to widen the next grant](/imgs/blogs/your-first-90-days-ramping-without-blowing-up-4.png)

The crucial properties of this loop:

**It is gated by reliability, not time.** You do not advance because the calendar advanced. You advance because you proved, on the previous step, that you can be trusted with the next one. This is why two people who started the same day can have wildly different scope at month six — one ran the loop, the other stalled it by being unreliable in small things.

**It compounds.** Each cycle makes the next grant bigger, because trust accumulates. The new hire who is meticulously reliable about a tiny mandate in month one is the one running a real book by month nine, because each reliable cycle bought a slightly bigger one, and the bigger ones bought bigger still. Compounding is why the *early* cycles matter so much more than they appear — a small reliability edge early, compounded across many cycles, becomes an enormous scope gap later. This is the career-shaped version of the compounding logic in [decision-making under uncertainty](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/decision-making-under-uncertainty-quant-interviews): small, consistent, positive-EV decisions, repeated, dominate occasional big swings.

**It can run backwards.** A blown trust event doesn't just fail to advance you — it can *demote* you, sending the lead back to checking every action, shrinking your scope while they rebuild confidence. This is why the failure modes are so costly: they don't just cost you the next grant, they can cost you grants you already had. The loop is not a ratchet; trust can be withdrawn.

**The output is autonomy.** The thing you're actually accumulating, cycle by cycle, is the right to act *without being watched.* Early on, everything you do is double-checked. The whole arc of the career is the steady removal of that supervision until you're the one whose judgment is trusted and whose work others shadow. Scope, in the end, is just trust made operational.

Figure 6 makes the stakes of running the loop well visible over the first 90 days. The disciplined ramp is a slow, monotone climb — credibility compounding a few points a week, never given back. The reckless ramp spikes early on a flashy win, looks like it's winning around week six, and then a hidden error surfaces around week nine and the curve craters *below where it started* — because trust, unlike a trading P&L, does not mean-revert quickly once it's broken. By week 13 the steady line is far ahead, and the gap is the whole point.

![A line chart of credibility versus week over the first ninety days: the disciplined ramp climbs slowly and monotonically to about eighty-five by week thirteen, while the reckless ramp spikes early on a flashy win to about sixty, then craters below its starting point around week nine when a hidden error surfaces, showing credibility compounds slowly and craters fast](/imgs/blogs/your-first-90-days-ramping-without-blowing-up-6.png)

## Reading the room and desk culture

One thread that runs underneath everything above and deserves its own moment: **culture is a real input to your ramp, and it varies a lot by firm and by desk.** The first 90 days are when you read it, and misreading it is the quiet failure mode that doesn't show up in any P&L but caps how much scope you'll be granted.

The variation is large. A Jane Street trading pod and a Two Sigma research group and a Jump low-latency team are three genuinely different cultures, and the behavior that earns trust in one can read as wrong in another. On a fast verbal trading desk, you're expected to speak up, say your size out loud, and have a view; staying silent reads as not engaged. In a quiet asynchronous research group, the norm might be thoughtful written communication and you signal seriousness by writing a careful note, not by talking over people in a meeting. A low-latency engineering team may prize precise technical correctness and have little patience for hand-waving. None of these is the "right" culture; they're different, and your job in weeks 1–2 is to *read which one you're in* before you assert a default that may be wrong.

Concrete things to read in the first two weeks: How do people ask each other questions — in a channel, by tapping a shoulder, by booking time? How direct is the feedback — blunt or cushioned? What's the tempo — interrupt-driven or deep-focus? How do seniors treat their own mistakes? Who actually makes decisions versus who has the title? What's the register — jokey, formal, terse? You're not trying to become a different person; you're trying to *not send the wrong signal by accident* while you build the standing to be more yourself. The new hire who reads the desk well gets the benefit of the doubt; the one who misreads it spends goodwill they didn't know they had and then can't understand why the scope isn't coming.

This is also why the firm-specific playbooks earlier in this series matter as onboarding prep: knowing that a culture runs on functional programming and puzzles, or on poker-style EV, or on engineering excellence, tells you in advance what "good" looks like there. But the playbook is a prior; the first two weeks are where you update it with the reality of *your specific desk*, which always has its own local norms.

## How to plan your first 90 days

Here is the concrete checklist, organized by phase. Figure 7 captures it as a matrix — for each phase, the one thing to do and the one thing to avoid. The do-column protects the downside and compounds trust; the avoid-column is the cheap mistake that quietly erodes it.

![A matrix self-checklist for the first ninety days with four phases and two columns for what to do and what to avoid: weeks one to two observe by learning systems and mapping the desk while avoiding loud opinions, weeks three to six shadow by asking why behind each call while avoiding asking what the docs answer, weeks seven to ten first mandate by sizing tiny and flagging every error while avoiding oversizing or hiding mistakes, weeks eleven to thirteen own a thing by running the loop and asking before any cliff while avoiding silent struggle](/imgs/blogs/your-first-90-days-ramping-without-blowing-up-7.png)

**Before you start (week 0).**
- Re-read the firm playbook for your firm so you know what "good" looks like there.
- Decide, in advance, your default policy: *protect the downside, compound credibility, never chase the spike.* Write the EV asymmetry on a sticky note if you have to.

**Weeks 1–2 (Observe).**
- Learn the systems cold: order lifecycle, dashboards, the kill switch / emergency stop, the data pipeline, the code review process. You should be able to *explain* them, not just recognize them.
- Read the desk culture: how people ask questions, how direct the feedback is, the tempo, who decides, how seniors treat their own errors.
- Take notes. Bank observations. Resist the urge to have loud opinions before you understand the context.

**Weeks 3–6 (Shadow).**
- Ask "why," not "what." Extract the senior's *judgment*, not their *actions.*
- Keep a running log of the principles you're learning — the dead-idea shapes, the sizing heuristics, the gotchas — so they compound.
- Build a relationship with at least one senior who'll answer your specific questions; be worth their time by always trying first.

**Weeks 7–10 (First mandate).**
- Size *smaller than feels right.* Your edge is small because you're new; size to it, not to your conviction.
- Flag every error within minutes. Make this reflexive. It is the highest-return habit you have.
- Follow the process when no one's watching — the OOS check, the code review, the size-out-loud. The mandate is where the firm learns if your discipline is real.

**Weeks 11–13 (Own a thing).**
- Run one small loop end to end, including monitoring and fixing your own errors.
- Develop the skill of *sensing a cliff and asking before you go over it.* This is the difference between owning a thing safely and a quiet blowup.
- Start the trust loop deliberately: do the small thing so reliably that the next, bigger grant becomes obvious.

**Throughout.**
- Default to the disciplined column in Figure 2 on every decision.
- When in doubt about a live action, confirm on a tiny test first.
- Treat your credibility like a capital account you're not allowed to draw down — because that's exactly what it is.

## Common misconceptions

**"You have to prove yourself fast."** This is the single most dangerous myth of the first quarter, because it points your ambition at exactly the wrong target. You do not have to prove you're *brilliant* — the firm already concluded that, which is why you have the seat over the thousands who didn't. What you have to prove is that you're *safe.* And safety is proven slowly and quietly, by not blowing up, by flagging errors, by sizing small, by asking well — not by a flashy early win. The new hire who's trying to prove themselves fast is the one who oversizes, hides the error, and craters their credibility in week nine (the red line in Figure 6). The patient one, who's just trying to be completely trustworthy with a small thing, is the one running a real book by month nine. Fast-proving is negative EV; you can't compound from a blowup.

**"Asking questions makes you look weak."** Backwards, with a nuance. The *right* questions — specific, after a real attempt, asked fast — make you look *competent*, because they signal you did the work and hit a genuine wall. It's the *wrong* questions (no attempt, no specificity, answer is in the docs) that erode credibility. So the fix is not "ask fewer questions" — it's "try first, get specific, then ask fast." And the silent-struggle alternative is far more damaging than any question: an hour you spend stuck is the firm's expensive new hire producing nothing while a small problem quietly grows. Asking the right question fast is you minimizing the firm's cost — which is the judgment they hired you for, not a weakness.

**"Hide small errors — no one needs to know about every little thing."** This is the myth that quietly ends the most careers, because it feels so reasonable. But on a desk that handles real money, the firm's true preference is: best is no error, *fine* is an error flagged immediately, and *catastrophic* is an error hidden — because a hidden error is an uncontrolled risk, the one thing a risk business cannot tolerate. The worked example put numbers on it: flagging a self-inflicted bug fast is roughly **+3** credibility (yes, positive), while hiding it is **−4.5** with a catastrophic tail, because if it's discovered you're no longer "someone who made a mistake" (forgivable) but "someone who hides mistakes" (unhirable). The error rarely ends you; hiding it does.

**"Move fast and break things."** This works at a consumer software company where a broken build is rolled back in minutes. It is *catastrophically* wrong at a quant firm, where "things" are live orders and real capital and a broken thing can be an instant, permanent loss. The first-90-days motto is the opposite: **move carefully and break nothing,** especially while your judgment hasn't caught up to your access. The disciplined hire confirms on a tiny test before acting on real size — sends one contract, runs the job on one symbol — trading a couple of minutes for the elimination of a catastrophic tail. Speed comes *later*, once you've earned the judgment that makes fast safe. Early on, slow is fast, because not-blowing-up is the prerequisite for everything else.

**"The ramp is the same length for everyone."** No — the ramp runs at the speed of trust. The calendar in Figure 1 is the median path. Behave well — flag errors, size small, ask sharply — and the stages compress; you're owning a small thing by week eight. Behave badly — hide, oversize, guess — and they stretch; you're still being double-checked at month four. Your behavior is the throttle.

## How it plays out in the real world

The patterns above are not abstract; they show up in concrete, recognizable ways across the named firms, and the honest version is more reassuring than the myth.

**The structured-program firms make the ramp explicit.** Susquehanna (SIG) is famous for running a structured education program for new traders, complete with poker and decision-theory training, precisely because the firm has decided that judgment and EV discipline are *teachable* and that rushing new traders onto real size is bad risk management. If you join a firm like that, the ramp is formalized — and the worst thing you can do is treat the structure as an obstacle to your impatience rather than as the firm's accumulated wisdom about how not to blow up new traders. The structure *is* the asymmetry, institutionalized.

**The intern-conversion pipeline is a pre-ramp.** As reported across levels.fyi and firm pages, most full-time seats at the top firms come from intern conversion — Jane Street, Citadel/Citadel Securities, IMC, Optiver all run heavy intern programs (Jane Street interns earn an annualized rate around \$300k; Citadel Securities interns roughly \$4,300–\$5,800/week for 2026, plus a sign-on and corporate housing). The relevant point for *this* post: if you converted from an intern, your first 90 days as a full-timer build on a relationship and a body of trust you already started accruing as an intern — which is one more reason the internship is the real interview. The trust loop in Figure 4 began before your start date.

**The comp tells you the stakes — and why the firm is cautious with you.** A new-grad total comp at a top firm runs roughly **\$450k–\$650k** on-target in year one (base of perhaps \$250k–\$375k plus a sign-on and bonus, as reported on levels.fyi and the 2026 quant-pay surveys; figures illustrative and survivorship-biased). That number tells you two things. First, the firm is making a large bet on you, which is exactly why it ramps you carefully — a \$500k-a-year new hire who blows up a seat is an expensive lesson. Second — and this is the honest part — *that comp is not a reward for being impressive in your first quarter.* The bonus component, which is the lever, is tied to contribution that you mostly haven't made yet in 90 days; your first-year number is largely the firm's investment in your *potential*, conditional on you not blowing up. The way you turn that potential into a *repeating* number in year two and beyond is by running the trust-to-scope loop well, starting now. A great year-one comp that you torch with a year-one blowup doesn't repeat.

**The up-or-out reality makes the downside real.** The honest, widely-reported version of the quant career is that it has real attrition — not everyone who starts survives to the senior comp numbers that get quoted ("everyone makes \$600k by year five" is survivorship bias; it describes the people who survived the filter). Some of that attrition happens *early*, in the first year, and a meaningful fraction of it traces to exactly the failure modes in Figure 5 — the new hire who hid an error, who repeatedly oversized, who couldn't be trusted with scope. The reassuring flip side: the failure modes are *behavioral and avoidable*, not measures of raw talent. The people who wash out early are rarely the ones who weren't smart enough; they're the ones who couldn't, or wouldn't, protect the downside. You control that.

**Real desks really do run the trust loop.** The promotion-to-scope mechanism in Figure 4 is not a metaphor — it's how desks actually operate. A new quant trader genuinely starts with tight limits and a lead double-checking actions, and those limits genuinely widen as reliability is demonstrated. A new researcher genuinely has their commits reviewed before they move to spot-checks. The firms differ in the formality and the vocabulary, but the loop is universal, because it's just risk management applied to people: you grant access in proportion to demonstrated reliability. Understanding that the loop is *the* mechanism — and that you start it turning in your first 90 days — is the difference between a new hire who's anxiously trying to look impressive and one who's calmly, deliberately compounding trust.

## When this matters / Further reading

This post matters most in exactly one window — the first three months of a new quant seat — but the discipline it teaches is the spine of the whole career. The first 90 days are the highest-variance, highest-stakes stretch you'll face, not because the work is hardest then, but because your access is at a lifetime maximum relative to your judgment, and the cost of a misstep is correspondingly large. Internalize the asymmetry — small upside, career-scale downside — and the supposedly hard decisions become easy: protect the downside, flag your errors, size small, ask well, read the room, and let trust compound into scope. You don't win this period by being brilliant. You were already brilliant. You win it by being *safe* long enough for the firm to learn it can stop watching you.
