> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The trading-game and mental-math rounds look like a quiz and a calculator test, but they are neither: they are the cheapest way a firm has ever found to measure calibration, expected-value reasoning, Bayesian updating, composure, and risk control — the five things that make a profitable trader.
>
> - **The market-making game is a loop, not a question.** You quote a two-sided market on an unknown, someone trades against you, news arrives, you re-quote, and you manage the inventory that piles up. You are scored on how you run the loop, not on whether you nailed the final number.
> - **Width is a confidence meter.** The single most-watched signal is how wide you quote: a calibrated trader makes a wider market when they know less and a tighter one when they know more. Always quoting tight is not aggressive — it is a tell that you do not understand adverse selection.
> - **The mental-math sprint measures speed *and* triage, not arithmetic.** A wrong answer scores zero and the clock you spent on it is gone, so the round secretly grades the same EV-under-a-clock reflex the desk runs on. Estimation rounds grade structured decomposition: a defensible range beats a confident wrong number.
> - **The one thing to remember:** these rounds reward *good process under uncertainty even when the outcome is bad luck* and punish *lucky right answers reached by bad process*. Get that, and you know exactly what to practice.

Maya is in the fourth round of her superday, and the interviewer has just asked her to make a market. "How many windows," he says, leaning back, "are in New York City? Make me a market — a bid and an ask — and I'll trade against you." Maya, who has drilled mental math for two months and feels sharp, blurts the first credible-sounding pair that comes to mind: "Eight million bid, twelve million ask." The interviewer smiles. "I sell you a hundred contracts at eight million." A pause. "Now — a developer publishes a report saying there are roughly forty-five thousand buildings in Manhattan alone, averaging eight floors. Same market?" Maya freezes. She has just been *lifted on her bid* — sold to at eight million — which means the interviewer thinks the true number is *higher* than eight million, and the new fact he handed her supports exactly that. Her bid was too low, and she got picked off for it. The right move is to raise her whole market immediately and probably widen it, because she has just learned she was badly miscalibrated. Instead she says "uh, yeah, same market, eight–twelve," and the interviewer writes a single word on his pad. The word is not *wrong*. The word is *passive*.

That moment — not the windows number, the *response to being hit* — is the entire round. Maya did not fail because eight million was a bad guess; a Fermi estimate of windows in NYC genuinely lands in the millions and any number in that zone is defensible. She failed because she treated the game as a trivia question with a hidden right answer, when it was a live test of whether she updates on new information, manages the risk of a position she just took on, and has the composure to move her market when the evidence moves. The interviewer was never scoring her arithmetic. He was scoring the five reflexes that make a trader, and the game is a near-perfect instrument for measuring all five at once. Figure 1 is the loop she was actually being graded on.

Read that loop and the whole genre of trader interviews stops being mysterious. The trading game and its faster cousin, the mental-math sprint, are not two unrelated hazing rituals — they are two probes into the same engine. This post is the meta-layer on those rounds: not *how to compute `47 × 53` fast* (that technique lives in the sibling [mental-math and arithmetic-speed post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/mental-math-arithmetic-speed-quant-interviews)) and not *the catalog of market-making game variants* (that drill material lives in the [market-making games post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews)), but the thing those posts assume you already understand: **what the interviewer is actually scoring, and why.** Get the *why* and the *how* becomes obvious — and your practice stops being arithmetic flashcards and starts being calibration training. Maya, our recurring math undergrad aiming at a trading seat, will walk every round with us, and by the end she will know exactly what that one-word note meant and how to earn a better one.

## Foundations: what a market is, and what "making a market" means

Before we can say what the rounds test, we need the vocabulary of the job they screen for. Build these slowly if you are new to markets — every later claim rests on them.

**The bid, the ask, and the spread.** For any tradable thing there is, at any instant, a highest price someone will *buy* at (the **bid**) and a lowest price someone will *sell* at (the **ask**, or offer). The ask is always above the bid; the gap between them is the **bid-ask spread**, and its size in points is the **width**. If something is "99 bid at 101," the bid is 99, the ask is 101, and the market is two wide. The spread is the price of immediacy: if you must trade *this instant*, you cross it and pay it.

**Making a market.** To **make a market** in something is to quote both a bid and an ask at the same time, committing to buy at your bid or sell at your ask if someone wants to trade. A market maker is a firm that does this all day across thousands of instruments. The two things that can happen to your quote have names: you get **hit** when someone *sells* to you at your bid (you bought), and you get **lifted** (or your offer is *taken*) when someone *buys* from you at your ask (you sold). In an interview game, the interviewer is the counterparty: they decide whether to hit your bid, lift your offer, or pass — and their choice is itself information.

**Fair value and the mid.** Your **fair value** is your single best estimate of what the thing is truly worth. The **mid** is the midpoint of your quote, `(bid + ask) / 2`, and it should sit at your fair value. If you think windows in NYC number about ten million, your mid should be ten million; quoting "8 bid, 12 ask" centers your mid at ten million with a two-million-wide market around it. Everything in market making is (1) estimating the fair value and (2) choosing a width and a lean around it.

**Edge and expected value (EV).** **Edge** is the expected profit on a single transaction, computed *before* you know the outcome. **Expected value** is the average payoff weighted by probabilities: if a bet pays `+\$10` with probability 0.6 and `−\$10` with probability 0.4, its EV is `0.6 × 10 − 0.4 × 10 = +\$2`. A market maker's gross edge per fill is roughly *half the spread*: if your fair value is right and you quote a two-wide market, every fill earns about one point in expectation, because on average you capture half the round-trip spread. Edge is always an expectation over many repetitions, never a promise on any single trade. This is the soul of the business: a market maker does not predict direction; it captures a small positive EV per quote and lets volume and the law of large numbers do the rest. (We dissect the unit economics of this in the sibling post on [how quant firms actually make money](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money) — here we only need that the spread is the prize.)

**Adverse selection.** Here is the concept that the whole game turns on. The fair value does not stand still — it drifts as information arrives. When someone trades against you, *they chose to*, and often they chose because they know or computed something you have not. So your fills are not a random sample of the world: you tend to get *hit* right before the price falls and *lifted* right before it rises. Getting filled by someone better-informed is **adverse selection**, and it is the market maker's central enemy. The deep consequence: **a fill is not just a trade, it is evidence.** If you keep getting hit on your bid, the market is telling you your bid is too high relative to where the fair value is actually heading. The trader who hears that and the trader who does not are separated by exactly this concept.

**Calibration.** **Calibration** means your stated confidence matches reality: when a well-calibrated person says "I'm 80% sure," they are right about 80% of the time. In market making, calibration is expressed *as the width of your quote*. If you are very sure of the fair value, you quote a tight market (small width) because you are confident you are not about to be picked off. If you are unsure, you quote a wide market, because the width is your protection against being wrong. **Width is calibration made visible**, and it is the single signal the interviewer watches most closely.

**Inventory and risk.** Every fill leaves you holding something — a **position**, or **inventory**. Get hit repeatedly and you are **long** (you own a growing pile); get lifted repeatedly and you are **short** (you owe a growing pile). Inventory is risk: if you are long and the price falls, you lose. So a market maker manages inventory by **skewing** the quote — leaning it to encourage the trades that flatten the position and discourage the ones that grow it — and by **sizing**, never letting any one position get big enough to blow up the book. **Risk control** is the fifth thing the game tests: a candidate who happily accumulates an enormous one-sided position because each individual trade "had edge" has failed to understand that ruin is forever and EV is meaningless if you are not around to collect it. (The math of how big to bet lives in the [Kelly-criterion post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews); here we need only that *size is a decision, and an uncapped one is a failure*.)

With that vocabulary, the thesis of the whole post is one sentence: **the trading game and the mental-math sprint are cheap instruments for measuring calibration, EV reasoning, updating, composure, and risk control — and they reward good process under uncertainty over lucky outcomes.** The rest of this post is detail: how each round works, what scores well versus poorly, and how to practice the thing being measured rather than the thing it looks like.

## The market-making game, step by step

Let us walk the canonical version slowly, because every misconception about these rounds comes from skipping a step. The interviewer picks something with an unknown numeric value — the number of windows in NYC, the number of piano tuners in Chicago, the sum of a hidden pair of dice, the population of a country, the value of a made-up security whose payoff is defined by a rule. They ask you to *make a market*: quote a bid and an ask. Then they trade against you, repeatedly, sometimes feeding you new information between trades. The game runs for several rounds. At the end, your profit-and-loss matters a little; *how you played* matters enormously.

**Step 1 — Estimate a fair value.** You form a rough best guess of the true number. For a Fermi-style quantity you decompose it (we do this in detail below); for a dice or card game you compute the expectation under the rules. This is where mental math shows up — but note it is *in service of* the quote, not the point of the round.

**Step 2 — Choose a width.** Around your fair value you pick a spread. This is the calibration decision, and it is the heart of the game. Quote too tight and you will be picked off by an interviewer who knows the answer better than you (adverse selection). Quote too wide and you signal you have no idea — and in some firms' scoring, an absurdly wide market is treated as a refusal to commit, which is its own failure. The right width *reflects how uncertain you genuinely are*. Figure 3 makes the tradeoff concrete: there is an EV-maximizing width, and it sits in the calibrated middle.

![A data chart showing net expected value per round as a function of quoted spread width, with a red too-tight zone where the curve is negative from being picked off, a green calibrated zone where the curve peaks around three points wide, and an amber too-wide zone where expected value fades toward zero from lack of flow](/imgs/blogs/the-trading-game-and-mental-math-rounds-what-theyre-really-testing-3.png)

The curve in Figure 3 is illustrative, not firm data — there is no published EV-vs-width function — but its *shape* is forced by the two opposing forces the foundations named. Widen your quote and you earn more per fill (you keep about half a bigger spread) but get filled less often and bled less by informed flow; tighten it and you trade constantly but earn almost nothing and get picked off. The product of "edge per fill" and "probability of a good fill," minus the adverse-selection cost, peaks at a calibrated middle width — around three points wide on a fair value near 100 in this illustration. The lesson the interviewer wants you to demonstrate is that **you are quoting at the peak because you understand both forces, not because you are timid or reckless.**

**Step 3 — Get hit or lifted, and read it.** The interviewer trades. *Which side they took is information.* If they hit your bid (sold to you), they think the value is at or below your bid; if they lift your offer (bought from you), they think it is at or above your offer. A single trade is weak evidence; repeated trades on the same side are strong evidence. The candidate who treats each fill as a neutral event has missed the entire game.

**Step 4 — Update.** Now you re-price. You move your mid toward where the fills are pushing it, and — crucially — if the fills suggest you were systematically wrong, you *widen* (you now know you know less than you thought) and *skew* (you lean away from the side you keep getting hit on). New external information (the interviewer hands you a fact) gets folded in the same way: it shifts your fair value and possibly your confidence. This is Bayesian updating in the wild, and we have a whole sibling post on the formal version, [decision-making under uncertainty](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/decision-making-under-uncertainty-quant-interviews); in the game you do it by feel, but the feel must be *directionally correct*.

**Step 5 — Manage inventory.** Every fill changed your position. If you have been hit three times you are long three lots; if the value is drifting against you, that is a growing loss. You skew your quote to attract the offsetting trade and you keep your size bounded. A candidate who lets the interviewer load them up with an enormous one-sided position — because "each trade had edge" — gets marked down hard, because they have shown they do not respect risk.

Then the loop repeats. Notice that **nothing in this loop is a trivia question.** The windows-in-NYC number is just the MacGuffin; the interviewer could run the identical game on the sum of two hidden dice and learn the same things about you. What is being measured is your behavior *inside the loop*: do you size your width to your uncertainty, do you read the fills, do you update in the right direction, do you stay composed when you are clearly being picked off, and do you cap your risk. #### Worked example: a full market-making game, with running EV and P&L

Let us put Maya back at the table and play a clean dice-style market-making game end to end, tracking her P&L the way the interviewer does. The hidden security pays out the value of a number the interviewer has fixed but not revealed; call the true value **V = 50** (Maya does not know this). Maya's prior is that V is "somewhere around 50, give or take 10." Each round, Maya quotes a market; the interviewer trades one lot against the side that is profitable for them given the true V.

**Round 1.** Maya quotes `45 bid / 55 ask` — a ten-wide market reflecting her honest uncertainty. The mid is 50, dead on, but she does not know that. The interviewer, knowing V = 50, sees that her bid of 45 lets them sell to her at 45 something worth 50 (a 5-point gift to them) and her offer of 55 lets them buy at 55 something worth 50 (also a 5-point gift to them). Either trade loses Maya 5. The interviewer hits her bid: Maya buys one lot at 45. Her position is **long 1 at 45**; marked at the true 50 she is up `+5` on paper, but she cannot see that — she only knows she got hit, weak evidence her bid was a touch high.

Hold on — that seems backwards, so read it carefully, because it is the crux. When your mid is *exactly right*, every fill the counterparty takes is one *you* profit from, because they are crossing your spread to reach a fair mid. Maya bought at 45 something worth 50; she is up 5. **A correct mid means your spread is pure edge.** The interviewer trades anyway (in a real game they might be probing, or might themselves be uncertain), and Maya banks `+5` of expected edge on the fill. Running P&L (marked to the true value): **+5**.

**Round 2.** Maya, having been hit once, makes a small, sensible update: maybe her bid was slightly high, so she shades down a touch and quotes `44 bid / 54 ask`, still ten wide. The interviewer hits her bid again: Maya buys a second lot at 44, worth 50, another `+6`. She is now **long 2**, running P&L **+11**. But notice the trap forming: she keeps getting hit on the *bid*, which a less-careful player would read as "I should keep lowering my market." In this game her mid is actually fine and she is *making* money, so lowering slowly is harmless — but the same pattern, with a different true V, is exactly how adverse selection bleeds a bad player dry. We will see that in the next worked example.

**Round 3 — news arrives.** The interviewer says: "A reliable source tells you V is an even number between 48 and 52." This sharpens Maya's uncertainty dramatically: V is now almost pinned to 48, 50, or 52, with a mean near 50 and a tiny spread. The *calibrated* response is to **tighten her market** — she now knows a great deal — and **center it at 50**. She quotes `49 bid / 51 ask`, a two-wide market. This is the single most important move in the game: her width collapsed from ten to two *because her information improved*. An interviewer watching this thinks, "good — width tracks knowledge." The interviewer lifts her offer: Maya sells one lot at 51, worth 50, banking `+1`. She is now **long 1** (sold one of her two), running P&L **+12**.

**Round 4 — manage inventory and close.** Maya is long 1 at an average cost well below fair value, sitting on a profit, with a sharp two-wide market. She skews very slightly to encourage being lifted again (to flatten her remaining long) — say `49 bid / 50.5 ask` — inviting the interviewer to buy her last lot near fair value. The interviewer lifts it: she sells her last lot at 50.5, worth 50, `+0.5`. Position flat, running P&L **+12.5**, and — far more important — she played every step correctly: wide when uncertain, tight when informed, skewed to manage inventory, calm throughout.

The number 12.5 is almost beside the point. *What the interviewer wrote down was that Maya's width tracked her uncertainty, she folded the news into a re-quote instantly, and she flattened her position at the end rather than carrying risk for no reason.* That is a strong pass, and it would have been a strong pass even if a run of bad luck had left her P&L slightly negative — because the round scores the process, and her process was sound.

#### Worked example: why widening after repeated hits is correct (adverse selection)

Now run the cruel version, the one that separates players. Same setup, but this time the true value is **V = 42** and Maya does not know it; her prior is again "around 50, give or take 10." She opens with `45 bid / 55 ask`.

The interviewer, knowing V = 42, sees that Maya's *bid* of 45 lets them sell her at 45 something worth only 42 — a 3-point profit for them, a 3-point loss for Maya. They hit her bid. Maya is now **long 1 at 45**, marked at 42, **−3**. She reads it as a single weak signal and shades down a little: `44 bid / 54 ask`. The interviewer hits her again (44 still beats 42 for them): **long 2**, average 44.5, marked 42, running P&L about **−5**. Hit a third time at, say, 43: **long 3**, running P&L about **−6**.

Here is the fork in the road. The **bad player** treats each hit as noise, keeps quoting roughly the same market, and keeps getting hit — accumulating a large long position into a fair value that is below all of his fill prices, bleeding a little more each round. He has been *adversely selected*: the interviewer is trading against him precisely because his bid is too high, and he is not listening. The **good player** notices the pattern — *I keep getting hit on the bid and never lifted on the offer* — and draws the correct inference: **my whole market is too high, and I am more uncertain than I thought.** So she does two things at once. She **moves her mid down** (toward where the fills are pushing it — maybe to 42 or 43) and she **widens** (from ten to, say, fourteen, because being repeatedly wrong is evidence she knows less than she assumed) and she **skews** (lowers the bid more than the offer, so she stops attracting more sells). She might re-quote `36 bid / 50 ask`, then narrow as the fills confirm the new level. The bleeding stops, because she is no longer offering to buy at prices above fair value. Figure 2 contrasts the two responses side by side.

![A before-and-after figure contrasting a bad player who keeps quoting the same market after being hit twice and bleeds, with a good player who treats the repeated hits as adverse-selection evidence and widens and skews the quote to stop the bleeding](/imgs/blogs/the-trading-game-and-mental-math-rounds-what-theyre-really-testing-2.png)

The counterintuitive lesson, and the one interviewers most want to see you grasp: **widening your market after being repeatedly hit is not retreating — it is correct.** Repeated one-sided fills are a Bayesian signal that you were miscalibrated, and the right response to "I am more wrong than I thought" is "lower my confidence," which *is* a wider quote. A candidate who tightens after being picked off (because they feel they must "win the next one back") has it exactly backwards and is told, gently, that they would have blown up a real book. *The width of your quote should rise the moment the fills tell you that you understand the value less well than you believed — listening to your own fills is the whole skill.*

Figure 6 traces the running P&L of this kind of game through the moment that gets graded. The line drifts up while the quote is calibrated, dips in round 4 when a stale quote gets hit repeatedly (the pick-off), and recovers from round 5 onward once the player widens and skews — exactly the update Figure 2 describes. The trace is illustrative, not firm data; what matters is *where the graded moment sits*. The interviewer is not watching the final P&L number — they are watching whether you recognized the drawdown for what it was (adverse selection, not bad luck) and corrected it. The endpoint is the outcome; the bend at round 5 is the process.

#### Worked example: a Fermi estimate decomposed — windows in Manhattan

The estimate at the top of a market-making game (the fair value) is itself a graded skill, and it gets its own dedicated round at many firms: the **Fermi** or estimation question. Let us decompose Maya's windows-in-Manhattan number properly, because *how she gets there* is what scores. The answer nobody knows exactly; the structure is the point. (The full technique catalog is in the [estimation and Fermi-problems post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/estimation-fermi-problems-quant-interviews); here we use one to show the *thinking* the interviewer is grading.) Figure 5 lays out the factor tree.

![A tree diagram decomposing windows in Manhattan into a product of three factors — number of buildings, floors per building, and windows per floor — each with a bounded estimate and a sanity-check, multiplying to roughly eleven million](/imgs/blogs/the-trading-game-and-mental-math-rounds-what-theyre-really-testing-5.png)

Maya thinks aloud, decomposing into a product of factors she can each defend:

- **Buildings in Manhattan.** Manhattan is about 23 square miles, densely built. She brackets: tens of thousands, not thousands and not hundreds of thousands. She lands on **≈ 47,000 buildings** — a figure she can sanity-check by noting that's a few thousand buildings per square mile in a dense grid, which feels right.
- **Floors per building.** A few skyscrapers have 50–100 floors, but the *vast* majority are low- and mid-rise. The average is dragged down by the many small buildings. She estimates **≈ 8 floors** average — flagging that the distribution is heavily skewed and the average is what matters for a total.
- **Windows per floor.** A typical floor's perimeter is on the order of 200 feet, with a window roughly every 7 feet, giving about **≈ 30 windows per floor** — and she notes this is rough because interior offices and glass facades cut both ways.

Multiply: `47,000 × 8 × 30 ≈ 11,000,000`. So Maya's estimate is **about 11 million windows in Manhattan**, and for all of NYC she would scale up by the other four boroughs. The exact number is unknowable; *that is not what is being graded.* What scores is that she (1) decomposed a hopeless question into three tractable factors, (2) bounded each factor with a sanity-check rather than pulling a number from nowhere, (3) flagged the biggest source of error (the skewed floor distribution), and (4) arrived at a single defensible figure she could then *make a market around* — say `8 million bid / 14 million ask`, a wide market reflecting genuine uncertainty. A candidate who just says "um, a billion?" with no structure fails not because a billion is wrong (it is) but because there is *no reasoning to evaluate*. *A Fermi answer is a product of bounded guesses, and the interviewer grades the factor tree, not the final digit.*

## Width as a calibration signal

Of everything in the game, width deserves its own section, because it is the signal that most cleanly separates people who get the round from people who do not — and because the most common piece of bad advice ("always quote tight to look aggressive") is dangerously wrong.

Recall that your width *is* your stated confidence. So watch what width communicates across the arc of a game. Early, when you have only a rough prior, a wide market is *correct and expected* — it says "I have a rough idea, and I am honest about the range." As information arrives (you get fills, the interviewer hands you facts), your uncertainty shrinks, and your width should shrink with it. By the endgame, with a sharp picture, a tight market is correct. **A player whose width tracks their information across the game has demonstrated calibration in the most direct way the interviewer can observe it.** A player who quotes the same width throughout — whether stubbornly tight or stubbornly wide — has shown the opposite: that their quote is a habit, not a measurement.

The two failure modes are symmetric. The **always-tight** player quotes a two-wide market on windows-in-NYC when they have no idea, gets picked off mercilessly, and reads as someone who does not understand adverse selection — they think tightness signals confidence when it actually signals recklessness. The **always-wide** player quotes a hundred-wide market even after being handed sharp information, and reads as someone who refuses to commit — and in several firms' explicit scoring, a market so wide it would never trade is treated as a non-answer, the quoting equivalent of "I don't know." The skill is the dial in between, and the tell of a strong candidate is that the dial *moves* as the game unfolds.

There is a subtler point that separates good from excellent. Width should respond not just to *how much* you know but to *how much the counterparty might know that you don't*. If you are making a market on something where the interviewer plausibly has private information — a real security, a quantity they clearly know exactly — you should quote *wider* than your own uncertainty alone would suggest, because the adverse-selection risk is higher. This is why real market makers widen their quotes around news events and earnings: not because their own estimate got worse, but because the population of people trading against them got more informed. An interview candidate who articulates "I'm widening here because *you* might know something I don't" has shown a level of understanding that earns the best note on the pad.

## The mental-math sprint and what speed proves

The trading game's faster cousin is the timed mental-math screen — most famously Optiver's roughly 80 questions in 8 minutes, no calculator, no scratch paper, with a reported pass bar around 70–85% correct (the exact format is unpublished and varies, so treat any single number as illustrative). On its face this looks like the *opposite* of the trading game: pure arithmetic, a right answer per question, no judgment. That appearance is the trap, and seeing through it is the point of this section. (The arithmetic *tricks* — difference of squares, fraction anchors, the eleven-times shortcut — live in the [mental-math post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/mental-math-arithmetic-speed-quant-interviews) and the [expected-value-techniques post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/expected-value-techniques-quant-interviews); here we ask only what the round *measures*.)

What the sprint actually measures is, once again, the five-trait engine — just compressed and accelerated:

**Speed under pressure is literally edge.** On a real desk the fair value moves continuously; a stale quote is a gift to anyone fast enough to take it. A trader who re-prices a hundred options *faster* loses less to adverse selection and captures more spread. So speed of accurate computation is not a nice-to-have — it is the same quantity as edge, measured directly. The sprint is the cheapest possible X-ray of it.

**Triage is calibration.** This is the part outsiders miss. Because a wrong answer scores zero *and the clock you spent on it is gone forever*, the sprint forces hundreds of tiny EV decisions: do I trust this answer or check it? Do I attempt this hard one or skip to bank an easier point? That is the identical triage a trader does across a book of hundreds of options — which quotes to tighten, which to pull, where to spend limited attention. The score rewards good triage and punishes the candidate who sinks fifteen seconds into one stubborn question, exactly as the market punishes a trader who fixates on one position while the rest of the book goes stale.

**Composure is the same composure.** A surprising amount of the pass/fail line is emotional, not cognitive. The visible counter dropping from 80 induces panic, and panic causes careless errors a calm person would never make. The desk is identical: the difference between a good trader and a blown-up one is often composure when the screen turns red. The sprint is a cheap composure screen that costs the firm nothing to administer to thousands of people.

**Numerical fluency is the substrate.** A trader who has to *think* about whether `3/8` is bigger than `0.4` is paying a tax on every decision. (It isn't: `3/8 = 0.375 < 0.4`.) The fluent trader gets that comparison for free and spends cognition on the things that matter — the risk, the flow, the skew. The sprint ensures you arrive with the substrate installed.

#### Worked example: the EV of attempting versus skipping a question

Make the triage concrete, because this is where the mental-math round secretly *is* an EV problem. Suppose the sprint is scored as raw-correct (a wrong answer is worth zero, same as a skip, and there is no penalty), Maya runs at a baseline of **3.5 seconds per question** with **94% accuracy** on questions she's confident about, and she has 60 seconds left with maybe 17 questions reachable at that pace. She hits one she finds hard: she estimates she'd take **12 seconds** on it and be only **50%** likely to get it right.

Compare the two options by EV in units of *expected correct answers*. **Attempt the hard one:** it costs 12 seconds and yields `0.50` expected correct — and those 12 seconds are 12 seconds not spent on easy questions. In 12 seconds at 3.5s each she could instead answer `12 / 3.5 ≈ 3.4` easy questions at 94%, yielding `3.4 × 0.94 ≈ 3.2` expected correct. So the hard question's *opportunity-adjusted* EV is `0.50` versus `3.2` for the easy ones it displaces. **Skipping the hard one is worth about 2.7 more expected-correct answers.** The arithmetic is overwhelming: at a sprint pace, a hard low-probability question is a terrible trade, and a well-calibrated test-taker skips it without a flicker of ego. *The mental-math round is a continuous EV optimization disguised as arithmetic — and the candidates who clear it are the ones who feel, in their gut, the opportunity cost of every second.*

That worked example is the bridge between the two rounds. The trader sizing a quote and the test-taker deciding whether to attempt a question are running the same computation: *what is the expected value of this action, accounting for what it costs me elsewhere?* The firm runs the sprint because it is a cheap, scalable, weirdly predictive proxy for that one reflex.

## Estimation and Fermi rounds: structured thinking made visible

The estimation round — windows in NYC, piano tuners in Chicago, golf balls in a 747, the daily revenue of a coffee shop — sits between the game and the sprint. It is slower than the sprint (you talk through it) and it lacks the adversarial fill-and-update loop of the game, but it shares the DNA of both: it is graded on *structured reasoning under uncertainty*, never on the final number. We worked one above (windows in Manhattan); here we name what scores.

**Decomposition into a product of bounded factors.** The strong answer breaks an unanswerable question into 3–5 factors you can each estimate within a reasonable range, then multiplies (or adds) them. "Windows = buildings × floors × windows-per-floor" is a decomposition; "a billion, I dunno" is not. The interviewer is checking whether you can impose structure on chaos — exactly the skill a researcher uses to frame a vague trading idea into a testable hypothesis, which is why the same round appears in research interviews too. (That framing skill is the heart of the [building-an-alpha-signal post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research).)

**Bounding each factor with a sanity-check.** A good estimator does not just assert "8 floors average" — they justify it ("most buildings are low-rise, a few are towers, the average is dragged down") and ideally bracket it ("definitely more than 3, definitely less than 20"). This is calibration again: each factor is itself a little market, quoted with a width that reflects how sure you are.

**Flagging the dominant source of error.** The best answers identify *which factor matters most* and where the biggest uncertainty lives. In the windows problem, the floor-count average is the shakiest factor (the distribution is wildly skewed), so a strong candidate says so. This is risk awareness: knowing where your estimate is most fragile is knowing where you would lose the most if you are wrong.

**Updating when handed new information.** Often the interviewer feeds you a fact mid-estimate ("actually, assume the average building is 12 floors") to see if you fold it in cleanly and revise the product, rather than clinging to your first answer. This is the same updating reflex as the game's "news arrives" step. A candidate who recomputes smoothly passes; one who ignores the new fact or gets flustered does not.

Notice the unifying thread across all three rounds, which is the entire thesis of this post: **none of them has a hidden right answer you are being tested against. All three test whether you reason well under uncertainty.** The windows number, the dice value, the arithmetic answer — those are scaffolding. The interviewer is reading your calibration, your EV sense, your updating, your composure, and your risk awareness off of *how you behave*, and the specific quantity is interchangeable.

## The behaviors interviewers reward versus punish

We can now be very concrete about what earns the good note and what earns the passive one. Figure 7 is the checklist; the prose below explains the *why* behind each line, because the behaviors only make sense once you see them as expressions of the five traits.

![A matrix figure with rows for narrating the reasoning, sizing width to uncertainty, updating on every fill, capping the risk, and composure under fire, with one column showing what scores well versus poorly and another column giving a concrete practice drill for each](/imgs/blogs/the-trading-game-and-mental-math-rounds-what-theyre-really-testing-7.png)

**Reward: narrate your reasoning. Punish: go silent.** The interviewer cannot grade a process they cannot see. The single highest-leverage behavior is to *think aloud* — "my fair value is around X because…, I'll quote a wide market because I'm unsure, you hit my bid so I'll lower my mid and widen." Silence reads as either having no process or being unable to function under pressure. This is why Maya's "uh, yeah, same market" was marked passive: she went quiet at the exact moment the interviewer wanted to hear her update.

**Reward: width that tracks uncertainty. Punish: a fixed width.** Already covered at length — a moving dial is calibration made visible; a habit is its absence.

**Reward: update on every fill and every fact. Punish: re-post the stale quote.** A fill is evidence; a fact is evidence. The candidate who re-quotes identically after being hit three times has announced that they do not hear the market. The candidate who says "you keep hitting me, so I'm lowering and widening" has announced that they do.

**Reward: bounded size and active inventory management. Punish: accumulating a huge one-sided position.** Letting the interviewer load you up because "each trade had edge" is the classic risk-control failure. Even a sequence of positive-EV trades can be ruinous if they are all correlated and unbounded — the EV is irrelevant if a bad draw takes you out. Skewing to flatten and capping your size shows you respect ruin.

**Reward: composure. Punish: tilt.** Getting picked off is *part of the game* — it is supposed to happen, and the interviewer is partly testing whether being wrong rattles you. The candidate who calmly says "okay, I was off, here's my revised market" scores far above the one who panics, tightens to "win it back," and digs deeper. Tilt — letting a loss distort your next decision — is the cardinal sin, in the game and on the desk.

A useful mental model: the interviewer is not your adversary trying to bankrupt you, even though they are trading against you. They are a sensor, and your job is to *emit a clean signal of good process* so the sensor reads it. Everything above is about making your reasoning legible and your decisions sound, so that even a round where the dice go against you reads as a strong pass.

## How to practice these rounds

The good news embedded in this whole analysis: because the rounds test *trainable* reflexes rather than fixed talent, deliberate practice moves the needle a lot. Practice the thing being measured, not the thing it looks like.

**For the mental-math sprint, build raw speed first.** Speed is the wall for most candidates, not knowledge — almost everyone can do the arithmetic given a minute. Drill a timed arithmetic trainer (the widely-used free one is Zetamac, configurable to the sprint's number ranges) for 20–30 minutes daily. Track your score per session; most untrained people start near 30–40 correct in the standard window and can climb to 70+ over six to eight weeks. Layer in the fast methods (difference of squares, fraction anchors, percent shortcuts) only once the basic speed is there — and crucially, *practice the triage*: deliberately skip questions that feel slow, to train the EV reflex of banking easy points.

**For the trading game, practice quoting and updating with a partner.** Have a friend pick a hidden number or quantity, quote them a market, and let them trade against you and feed you news. The skills you are drilling: setting a width that matches your honest uncertainty, *narrating* your reasoning aloud, reading which side they trade and updating your mid and width accordingly, and managing the position you accumulate. Play it on a phone call so that thinking-aloud becomes a habit and you are comfortable making decisions without visual aids — many superday games happen over a call.

**For estimation, build a decomposition habit.** Take a Fermi question a day and force yourself to write the factor tree before guessing — buildings × floors × windows, customers × visits × spend, and so on. Bound each factor, flag the shakiest one, multiply, and state a market around the result. The goal is for "decompose into a product of bounded factors" to become automatic.

**For all three, train calibration explicitly.** This is the meta-skill, and it is the most neglected. Keep a small log of predictions with confidence levels — "70% sure this estimate is within 2x" — and check whether your 70%s come true 70% of the time. Most people are wildly overconfident; closing that gap is exactly what makes your quoted widths correct. The [decision-making-under-uncertainty post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/decision-making-under-uncertainty-quant-interviews) has the formal tools, and the [Kelly-criterion post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews) has the sizing math that turns calibration into a position.

A note on time horizon, because the funnel matters: these rounds appear at the market-making and trading-focused firms (Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, IMC, Akuna, Citadel Securities) and they are mostly screening for *trading* seats, where the internship is the real interview. Budget six to eight weeks of daily practice before your first superday, and remember that the cheap rounds (the sprint especially) come *first* in the loop — clearing them is the price of admission to the rounds where you can actually show your judgment.

## Common misconceptions

**"It's about getting the right number."** The deepest and most common error, and the whole post is its refutation. There is no hidden right answer you are scored against in the game or the estimation round; the quantity is scaffolding for measuring your process. Maya's eight-million windows guess was *fine*; her failure to update on being lifted was the problem. The interviewer would happily pass a candidate whose final number was off by a factor of two but whose reasoning was impeccable, and fail one who landed near the true value by luck with no structure. *Process over outcome* is the literal scoring rubric.

**"Tighter is always better — it shows confidence."** No — quoting tight when you are uncertain is not confidence, it is a failure to understand adverse selection, and it gets you picked off. The correct width *reflects your uncertainty*: wide when you know little, tight when you know a lot, moving as your information moves. A candidate who quotes a two-wide market on windows-in-NYC reads as someone who does not know the value can be against them. Calibration, not aggression, is the virtue.

**"Mental math means being a human calculator."** The sprint is not testing whether you can compute — it is testing speed-with-triage under pressure, which is the desk's edge-and-attention-allocation reflex in miniature. A "calculator" who computes every question slowly and accurately fails the round, because slowness silently converts unanswered questions into wrong ones. The skill is the *EV of where you spend each second*, not the raw arithmetic.

**"You should stay quiet and think, then give your best answer."** The opposite of what scores. Silence gives the interviewer nothing to grade and reads as either no-process or paralysis. Thinking aloud — narrating your fair value, your width, your update — is the single highest-leverage behavior, because the round is scoring your *process* and your process is invisible unless you voice it. The candidates who narrate clearly while staying calm get the best notes.

**"The games are mostly luck, so they're unfair."** Luck is real on any single round, which is exactly why the firms grade *process* and run *many* rounds. Over a multi-round game, a player with sound process and calibrated widths will, in expectation, do better — and more importantly, the interviewer can read the process directly regardless of how the dice fell. A lucky win with bad process scores worse than an unlucky loss with good process. The round is engineered specifically to see through luck.

**"These rounds are unique hazing rituals with no real-world analog."** They are the *opposite* of arbitrary — each round is a deliberately faithful, compressed simulation of the actual job. Quoting under uncertainty, getting picked off, updating on news, managing inventory, allocating attention under a clock: that is a market maker's Tuesday. The firms use these rounds *because* they predict on-desk performance, which is why a strong score genuinely forecasts a strong trader.

## How it plays out at the real firms

These rounds are concentrated at the trading-and-market-making firms, and the flavor differs in instructive ways. **Jane Street** (founded 2000, NYC) is the archetype of the EV-and-games culture: its interviews lean heavily on trading games and probability puzzles, framed explicitly around expected value and betting, with a collaborative tone — the interviewer often *helps* you reason, because they are scoring how you think with information, not whether you crack it alone. We dissect that culture in the sibling [Jane Street playbook](/blog/trading/quant-careers/jane-street-playbook-culture-ocaml-and-the-trading-games). **Optiver and IMC** (Amsterdam, founded 1986 and 1989) open with the brutal timed mental-math sprint — the speed filter — before they will spend a human minute on you, and the [Optiver and IMC playbook](/blog/trading/quant-careers/optiver-and-imc-playbook-the-mental-math-gauntlet) walks that gauntlet in detail. **SIG (Susquehanna)** (founded 1987) is the decision-theory house: it uses *poker* as trader training and frames everything around EV and calibration, so its games skew toward betting and probability under incomplete information.

The comp on the other side of these rounds is real and worth naming, with its conditionality. A new-grad trading seat at a top market maker runs, as reported on levels.fyi and in 2025–2026 surveys, a base around 250k–375k USD with a sign-on of 50k–200k, landing first-year totals commonly in the 450k–650k USD range on-target — Jane Street quotes an "annualized equivalent of 300k" base across its roles. But the honest framing the rest of this series insists on applies here too: those numbers are *survivorship-biased* and *bonus-driven*. The bonus is the lever and it does not repeat automatically — a strong year is not the median, and many candidates who clear these rounds still wash out of the up-or-out reality. The rounds are selective in the extreme: top intern and grad programs are widely described as low-single-digit-percent acceptance, with thousands applying per seat. The point of mastering the rounds is not the headline comp; it is getting through the cheapest, most predictive filter into a seat where your judgment can compound.

There is one structural fact about how these rounds sit in the loop that changes how you should prepare. The cheap rounds come *first* — the mental-math sprint is administered to thousands before any human is involved, precisely because it is cheap and predictive. The expensive rounds — the live trading games, the superday — come later and only for people who already cleared the cheap filter. So the EV of your prep is front-loaded: drilling the sprint clears the gate that everything else depends on, and the trading-game judgment matters most once you are in the room with a human reading your process. We map the full sequence in the [interview-loop-round-by-round post](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-interview-loop-round-by-round).

Maya, by the way, got a second superday at a different firm a month later. Same windows-style question, same lift on her bid, same incoming fact. This time she said it out loud: "You lifted my bid, which tells me you think it's worth more than I quoted — and that fact you just gave me agrees. I'm raising my whole market to twelve bid at eighteen, and I'm keeping it wide because you clearly might know more than I do." The interviewer wrote a different word on the pad. The arithmetic had not changed at all. The *process* had, and the process was the only thing ever being graded.
