> **TL;DR** — A firm move is a one-time tax on your career, paid in forfeited unvested deferred comp, a garden-leave gap, and ramp time; you should only pay it for a genuinely better seat or to escape a broken one, never for a marginal headline bump.
>
> - The headline number is gross; the realized number is what you keep after you subtract the deferred comp you walk away from, the months you are out of the bonus cycle, and the slow first year while you relearn everything.
> - A move resets your tenure, your vesting clock, and your firm-specific context to zero. It does NOT reset your skills, your track record, or your reputation, which travel with you for better or worse.
> - Quant is a tiny world. Your old manager and a former teammate are two hops from the next desk you want, and the back-channel reference they give is decided the day you leave, not the day you apply.
> - The one number to remember: a forfeited \$300,000 deferred slice can turn a "+15% raise" into a first-year pay cut. Run the ledger before you feel the offer.

A few years into a solid run, Wei got the call every mid-level quant eventually gets. A competing pod shop wanted him, and the recruiter led with the number: a fifteen percent bump on his current total comp, structured as a higher base and a fatter sign-on. He was flattered, a little bored at his current seat, and the math seemed obvious. More money, a fresh start, a clean fifteen percent. He said yes over a weekend.

What the recruiter did not put on the table, because it was not theirs to mention, was the \$300,000 of unvested deferred comp sitting in Wei's account at his old firm. Three years of bonus, paid out on a schedule, most of it still in flight. The moment he resigned, it was gone. Forfeited. Not negotiated, not bought out by the new firm, simply erased. On top of that came a three-month garden-leave gap with no bonus accrual, and a first year at the new seat where he was the slowest person on the desk because he did not yet know the systems, the data, or the people. When the dust settled, Wei's "fifteen percent raise" was a meaningful pay cut for the first two years. He had paid the full move tax for a marginal headline bump, which is the single most common and most expensive mistake in a quant career.

This post is the math of that decision. Not a motivational take on loyalty or ambition, but the actual ledger: every cost of a move, every benefit, what resets and what does not, and a framework for deciding which side of the line a given offer falls on. Figure 1 is the whole post in one picture: a move pays a real, lumpy, one-time tax, and it is only worth paying when the new seat genuinely outweighs that tax.

![Two-column comparison of switching firms versus staying, listing the costs a move pays and the compounding a stay keeps](/imgs/blogs/staying-vs-switching-the-firm-hopping-math-1.png)

The thesis, stated once so it anchors everything below: **switch for a genuinely better seat or a broken situation, not for a marginal headline bump that the deferred-comp forfeiture erases.** Everything else in this post is the arithmetic and the judgment behind that one sentence.

## Foundations: the true cost and benefit of a move

Before we can compute anything, we need to define the terms precisely, because the entire mistake people make lives in the gap between a few words that sound interchangeable but are not. We will build each one from zero.

**Total comp, headline vs realized.** Total compensation is base salary plus sign-on plus year-end bonus. When a recruiter quotes you a number, they quote the *headline*: the on-target, everything-goes-well, gross figure. The *realized* number is what actually lands in your account after the frictions of the move are subtracted. For a quant who stays put, headline and realized are roughly the same. For a quant who moves, they can diverge by hundreds of thousands of dollars in the first year. Almost every bad firm-hopping decision is a case of someone optimizing the headline while ignoring the realized.

**Deferred comp.** This is the load-bearing concept of the entire post, so we will be careful. At nearly every serious quant firm, your bonus is not paid to you all at once. A portion is paid in cash now, and a portion is *deferred*, meaning it is promised to you but paid out over the next three to four years on a vesting schedule, conditional on you still working there when each slice vests. A vesting schedule is just a calendar of dates on which a chunk of previously-earned-but-withheld money becomes legally yours. The firm does this deliberately. It is a retention tool. Each year you stay, you earn a new bonus, and a new deferred tail starts; so at any moment, a healthy chunk of several years of past bonuses is sitting unvested in your account. The moment you resign before a slice vests, you *forfeit* it. You do not get paid out, you do not get a pro-rated portion, it simply evaporates. This forfeited unvested deferred comp is the single largest and most overlooked cost of a move. People call it **golden handcuffs** for exactly this reason: the more successful you have been, the more deferred comp is in flight, and the more expensive it is to leave.

**Non-compete and garden leave.** A non-compete is a clause in your contract that says you cannot go work for a competitor for some period after you leave, often three to twelve months in this industry. **Garden leave** is the gentler-sounding cousin: you remain technically employed, you are paid your base salary, but you are sent home and barred from working, often so the firm can protect its strategies while your knowledge goes stale. The practical effect on your ledger is the same. For the length of the gap, you earn base salary but no bonus, no P&L participation, no deferred accrual. If your comp is bonus-heavy, as quant comp is, a six-month garden leave can cost you a large fraction of a year's real earnings even though you are nominally "still paid."

**Ramp time.** When you start a new seat, you are not productive on day one. You do not know the codebase, the data pipelines, the risk limits, the desk's idiosyncratic conventions, who to ask when something breaks, or which of the firm's thousand internal tools does what. **Firm-specific context** is the accumulated, undocumented knowledge of how this particular place actually works, and it is worth a great deal of P&L. At your old seat you had years of it; at the new seat you start at zero. The time it takes to climb back to full productivity is the *ramp*, typically six to twelve months for a researcher or trader, and during that ramp your bonus-generating output is below your eventual steady state. That gap is a real cost even though no one writes you an invoice for it.

**Expected value, applied to a career.** The spine of this whole series is that the job is a probabilistic edge, and so is every decision about the job. **Expected value (EV)** is the probability-weighted average of all outcomes of a choice. For a career move, the EV is the realized comp and career trajectory across the plausible futures at the new seat, minus the realized comp and trajectory you would have had by staying, net of the move tax. A move is *positive EV* when, across the realistic range of outcomes, you come out ahead after paying the full cost. A *marginal* headline bump, the kind Wei chased, is almost always negative EV once the tax is in the equation, because the bump is too small to clear the forfeited deferred comp. We will compute several of these explicitly. If the EV framing is unfamiliar, the dedicated treatment is in [expected-value techniques](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/expected-value-techniques-quant-interviews) and the decision-under-uncertainty material in [decision making under uncertainty](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/decision-making-under-uncertainty-quant-interviews).

**The vesting overhang, made concrete.** It helps to picture the deferred-comp situation as a rolling stack rather than a single number. Suppose each year your bonus is split so that part pays now and part defers over the next three years in equal slices. After a few years on the job, you are simultaneously carrying the unvested tails of several past bonuses: the recent year's bonus is mostly still unvested, the prior year's is partly unvested, and so on. The sum of those tails is your *vesting overhang*, and it grows as you become more senior and your bonuses get larger. The cruel arithmetic of golden handcuffs is that the overhang is largest exactly when you are most employable, because a great recent year both makes you attractive to other firms and stacks a large fresh deferred tail you would forfeit by leaving. The firm designed it that way on purpose. The practical upshot: never estimate your forfeiture from memory or vibes. Pull up your actual vesting schedule, sum the unvested slices, and put that exact number at the top of the ledger, because it is almost always larger than you think and it is the number that most often flips a move from positive to negative.

**What resets and what does not.** Hold this distinction in your head for the rest of the post, because it is the whole game. A move *resets* the things that are tied to a building: your tenure, your vesting clock, your firm-specific context. A move does *not* reset the things that are tied to you: your skills, your track record, and your reputation. The first group is why a move is expensive. The second group is why a move can still be worth it, because if a better building lets your portable skills produce more, you win, and if it does not, you have paid the tax for nothing.

With these terms defined, we can stop hand-waving about "more money" and start running ledgers.

## The costs of switching: the full ledger

Let us itemize every line on the cost side, because the mistake is almost always one of omission. People remember the headline gain and forget two or three of the costs entirely. Figure 2 puts them in order as a waterfall: the headline at the top, each cost knocking it down, and the small bar of realized value at the bottom.

![Waterfall chart showing a 690k headline reduced by forfeited deferred comp, a garden-leave gap, and ramp drag down to 255k net first-year value, below a 600k stay reference line](/imgs/blogs/staying-vs-switching-the-firm-hopping-math-2.png)

**Cost 1: forfeited unvested deferred comp.** This is almost always the biggest line and the one people underweight the most, because it feels like money you do not have yet. But you earned it. It is yours, contingent only on showing up. Walking away from \$200,000 to \$400,000 of unvested deferred comp is exactly as real as writing a check for that amount, and a rational new employer knows this, which is why a serious offer for a senior person sometimes includes a *buyout* or *make-whole* that compensates you for what you forfeit. If the new firm will not buy out your forfeited deferred, that is information: either they do not value you that highly, or the seat is not as strong as the headline suggests. The cost does not disappear just because no one mentions it. It is the first thing you should put on the ledger, and you should size it precisely by reading your own vesting schedule.

**Cost 2: the garden-leave / non-compete gap.** During a garden leave you collect base salary, which in this industry is the small part of your comp, and forgo the bonus, which is the large part. As a rule of thumb, in a strong seat the bonus can be several times the base, so a quarter to a half of a year spent on garden leave can cost you a quarter to a half of a year's *total* earnings even though your base keeps arriving. There is a second-order cost too: a long non-compete means your market knowledge goes stale, so the firm that hired you partly bought a depreciating asset, which can soften your bargaining position and your year-one expectations.

**Cost 3: ramp time.** For the first six to twelve months at the new seat you are below your steady-state productivity, which for a bonus-driven role means below your steady-state comp generation. You are learning the systems instead of producing P&L or shipping research. This is a real, if fuzzy, drag, and you should estimate it as a fraction of a year's bonus rather than pretend it is zero.

**Cost 4: lost firm-specific context, beyond the ramp.** Some context never fully transfers. The relationships you built, the trust you accumulated with risk and with management, the specific data and infrastructure you knew cold, the institutional memory of why things are the way they are. You re-earn all of it from scratch, and some of it you simply never rebuild to the same depth in the same time. This is hard to put a number on, but it is the reason a person who is genuinely great at firm A can look merely good at firm B for a surprisingly long time.

**Cost 5: option value and optionality you give up.** At your current firm you may be on a known path: a promotion in sight, a book about to be handed to you, a mentor who is actively investing in you. A move can reset all of that to "prove yourself first." You are trading a known, partly-vested option for an unknown new one. Sometimes the new option is better. But you should not pretend the old one was worth zero.

**Cost 6: the risk that the new seat is worse than advertised.** Everything on the ledger so far is a cost you can size in advance. This one is the cost you cannot, and it deserves a line of its own. A new seat is, to you, an unknown distribution: the manager you have not worked under, the data and infrastructure you have not touched, the strategy whose true capacity you cannot verify from the outside, the team whose dynamics are invisible until you are inside them. The headline assumes everything goes well. The realistic ledger should haircut the expected upside by the probability that the seat turns out worse than the pitch, because pitches are written by people who want you to say yes. A useful discipline is to ask, for any quoted on-target number, what the *poor* outcome looks like and how likely it is. At your current seat you already know your poor-outcome distribution; at a new seat you are guessing, and the guess should be conservative. This is the difference between the headline EV and the risk-adjusted EV, and it is the reason a small bump is not just low-reward but also higher-variance than staying.

A subtle point ties these six costs together: they are *correlated*, not independent. A move that goes badly tends to go badly on several lines at once. A weak new seat means a slower ramp, a smaller realized bonus, a higher chance you are looking again within two years, and therefore a second move tax close behind the first. The downside is fatter than a naive sum of independent costs would suggest, which is one more reason to demand that the benefit be large and structural before you pay it.

Now the worked example that ties the costs together. This is Wei's move from the hook, computed line by line.

#### Worked example: the full move ledger, net EV

Wei is a mid-level quant earning \$600,000 total comp at his current pod shop, anchored to the reported 2025 to 2026 mid-level band of roughly \$575,000 to \$820,000. The competing offer leads with a fifteen percent bump, which on paper is \$690,000 headline total comp. Wei does the headline math in his head and likes it. Then he runs the full ledger.

- **Headline at the new seat:** \$690,000. This is the gross, on-target number, before any friction.
- **Minus forfeited unvested deferred comp:** \$300,000. Wei has three years of bonus tails vesting over the next few years, and roughly \$300,000 of it is unvested. The new firm offered no make-whole. He walks away from all of it. Net so far: \$390,000.
- **Minus the garden-leave gap:** \$75,000. A three-month garden leave during which he collects base but loses three months of bonus accrual, costed at roughly \$75,000 of foregone bonus. Net so far: \$315,000.
- **Minus ramp drag in year one:** \$60,000. For the first six to twelve months he is below his steady-state output while he learns the new systems and data; he books this as roughly \$60,000 of foregone year-one bonus. Net realized first-year value: **\$255,000.**

So the "+15% raise" is, in realized first-year terms, about \$255,000 against the \$600,000 he would have made by staying. That is not a fifteen percent gain. It is roughly a fifty-eight percent first-year *cut*. The forfeited deferred comp alone, \$300,000, is twice the size of the entire \$90,000 headline bump that tempted him. The move is sharply negative EV in year one and only climbs back toward break-even over several years, and only if the marginal bump holds, which for a bonus-driven role it may not.

*A headline bump is gross and immediate; the move tax is real and front-loaded, so a small raise is almost always a first-year pay cut once you subtract what you forfeit.*

The point of the ledger is not that moving is bad. It is that the costs are large, lumpy, and front-loaded, while the benefit of a marginal bump is small and spread out. The arithmetic only flips when the benefit is not marginal, which is the next section.

## The benefits of switching: when a move is genuinely better

If a move only ever cost, no one would ever move, and people who never move can stall just as badly as people who hop constantly. The benefit side of the ledger is real and sometimes enormous. The discipline is to demand that the benefit be *structural*, not cosmetic, before you pay the tax. Here is what a genuinely better seat buys you.

**Benefit 1: more capital, a bigger book.** Your realized comp at most quant firms is some function of the P&L you generate, and your P&L is, very roughly, your edge times the capital you are allowed to deploy. If you have a real edge and your current firm caps your capital, a seat that hands you a materially larger book can multiply your payout even at the same edge and the same percentage cut. This is the cleanest positive-EV reason to move: the same skill, applied to more capital, simply produces a bigger number. A two-times increase in book size on a stable edge can double your bonus, which dwarfs any forfeited deferred comp.

**Benefit 2: a better strategy, a higher ceiling.** Some seats have a low ceiling regardless of how good you are: a saturated strategy, a desk that is being wound down, a firm whose edge is decaying. A seat with a higher ceiling, a less crowded strategy, a growing rather than shrinking pool, compounds for years. You are not buying a one-time bump; you are buying a steeper trajectory, and trajectory compounds.

**Benefit 3: a better seat in the org, a real mentor.** Who you work under in your first formative years is one of the largest determinants of how fast you grow. A seat under a genuinely great PM or researcher who invests in you can be worth more than a large pay bump, because it raises your skill, and skill is the portable asset that travels with you forever. The [ecosystem-and-how-to-choose-your-firm](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-wider-ecosystem-and-how-to-choose-your-firm) post goes deep on evaluating seats; the short version is that the mentor and the strategy matter more than the logo.

**Benefit 4: escaping a broken situation.** Sometimes the move is not about upside at all; it is about cutting a loss. A toxic manager, a desk that is being defunded, a firm in decline, a seat where you have hit a hard ceiling with no path up, a culture that does not fit you. Staying in a broken situation has its own quiet cost: stalled growth, a flat or declining trajectory, the slow erosion of your skills and your reputation. Escaping a genuinely broken seat is positive EV even at a flat headline, because the alternative is decay. The trap to avoid here is calling ordinary boredom or impatience "broken." A real broken situation is structural; a mood is not.

**Benefit 5: optionality and a reset of a bad first impression.** Occasionally the most valuable thing a move buys is a clean slate on a reputation that, through no great fault of yours, calcified at your current firm. Early-career people sometimes land in a seat where they were typecast: pegged as the junior who once made a mistake, slotted into work below their level, defined by a first manager's narrow read of them. Firm-specific reputation, unlike industry reputation, does not fully travel, and that cuts both ways. A move can shed a stale internal label and let you be evaluated fresh on current ability. This is real, but it is also the benefit most often invoked as a rationalization, so hold it to a high bar: a genuine typecasting problem is one where you have tried and failed to change the internal narrative, not one where you simply have not asked for more responsibility yet.

It is worth naming the size asymmetry between the cost side and the benefit side, because it is the crux of the whole decision. The costs are mostly *additive and bounded*: a forfeiture of a known amount, a gap of a known length, a ramp of a known span. The best benefits are *multiplicative and unbounded*: more capital times a stable edge, a higher ceiling that compounds for years, a mentor who raises your skill permanently. This is why a move for a structurally better seat can win by a wide margin while a move for a bump almost always loses. You are comparing a fixed, certain tax against either a small fixed bump, which loses, or a compounding multiplier, which can win enormously. The arithmetic rewards patience for the multiplier and punishes impatience for the bump.

Figure 3 shows the difference in shape between a marginal move and the trajectory it produces. The switcher who chases a small bump falls behind for the first two years, paying the move tax, and only catches back up around year four or five if the bump holds. A genuinely better seat, by contrast, would pull the switch line *above* the stay line, because more capital or a higher ceiling raises the slope, not just the intercept.

#### Worked example: a move that is clearly positive EV

Maya is a researcher four years into her career, earning \$575,000 total comp at a firm that caps her at a fixed research budget and a strategy she has largely tapped out. A systematic fund offers her a seat with a materially larger book and a less crowded strategy, plus a partial make-whole for her forfeited deferred. Run the ledger.

- **Forfeited unvested deferred comp:** \$200,000, of which the new firm makes her whole for \$120,000 in additional sign-on. Net forfeiture: \$80,000.
- **Garden-leave gap:** \$50,000 of foregone bonus over a two-month garden leave.
- **Ramp drag:** \$50,000 of foregone year-one output.
- **The upside:** her edge is intact, and the new seat gives her roughly double the effective capital on a strategy with more room. On a stable edge, her bonus participation roughly doubles. Where she would have earned about \$575,000 by staying, her on-target at the new seat is roughly \$1,000,000 once she ramps, and the trajectory keeps climbing because the strategy is not saturated.

Year one: \$1,000,000 headline, minus \$80,000 net forfeiture, minus \$50,000 gap, minus \$50,000 ramp, is roughly \$820,000 realized, already well above the \$575,000 she would have made by staying. And year two onward, with the move tax fully paid and the deferred tail now building at the new firm, she is at roughly \$1,000,000 and rising against a stalled \$575,000-ish at the old seat. The forfeited deferred is real, but it is dwarfed by a payout that doubled because the seat was structurally better. This is what a positive-EV move looks like: the benefit is structural, not a fifteen percent headline, and it more than clears the tax.

*When a move buys more capital or a higher ceiling, the upside is multiplicative and compounding, which is the only kind of benefit large enough to be worth the front-loaded tax.*

Notice the difference between Maya and Wei. Wei chased a *bump*: the same kind of seat, fifteen percent more on the headline, additive and one-time. Maya chased a *seat*: more capital and a higher ceiling, multiplicative and compounding, with the new firm willing to share the forfeiture cost. That is the line between a trap and a genuinely better move, and it is almost never about who quotes the bigger headline.

## The reputation network and the small-world problem

Here is the part of the calculus that does not show up on any comp ledger and yet outweighs all of it over a full career: quant is a tiny industry, and your reputation is the asset that travels with you forever.

Think about the actual size of the world. The set of serious quant firms is small. Each one hires a small number of people per year. The senior people have mostly worked with, or one hop from, each other. Specialist recruiters in this niche know nearly everyone. The practical consequence is that you are almost never more than two hops from any desk you might want to join: your old manager knows someone there, or a former teammate moved there, or the same recruiter staffs both firms. This is the **small-world problem**, and Figure 4 traces it.

![Network graph showing a person's exit terms propagating through an old manager, a former teammate, and a specialist recruiter to the next hiring desk, which decides the offer](/imgs/blogs/staying-vs-switching-the-firm-hopping-math-4.png)

What flows along those two hops is not your resume. It is the *back-channel reference*: the unofficial, off-the-record read that the hiring desk gets by calling someone who actually worked with you. And the content of that back-channel reference is determined not by how good you are at the job, but by **how you leave.** Did you give honest notice, transition your work cleanly, avoid trashing your firm on the way out, and behave like a professional during the awkward weeks between resignation and departure? Or did you ghost, take a strategy with you, badmouth your old desk, or burn the manager who hired you? The first costs you nothing and pays dividends for a decade. The second can quietly close doors you will never even know existed, because the rejection arrives as a polite "we went another direction," not as "your old manager said you torched the place."

This is why **how you leave matters more than where you go.** A clean exit is one of the highest-EV, lowest-cost moves in your entire career: it preserves the back-channel reference, keeps the network warm, and means that the next time you want to move, the small world works *for* you instead of against you. People who hop constantly and leave a trail of singed bridges find, around year eight, that the small world has quietly priced them as a flight risk and a difficult colleague, and the offers stop matching their skill.

There is a flip side worth stating plainly: the network also means that a strong reputation compounds. If you are known as someone who is sharp, honest, and good to work with, you get the inbound calls, the warm intros, the seat that never gets posted publicly. The same two-hop network that punishes a bad exit rewards a good one. Your reputation is portable in both directions, which is exactly why it is the most valuable thing you own and the thing you should protect most carefully when you move.

The small world also reshapes how you should think about *frequency*, not just etiquette. Each move is a fresh entry in a record that the network keeps. One or two well-reasoned moves over a decade read as a person who grew and chose deliberately. Five moves in six years read as a flight risk, regardless of how good each individual reason was, because the pattern is what the back-channel transmits, not the footnotes. A hiring desk weighing two equally-skilled candidates will quietly prefer the one whose history suggests they will stay long enough to ramp, contribute, and let their deferred comp vest, because that desk is about to pay the ramp cost on whomever they hire and does not want to pay it twice. So the firm-hopping math has a meta-layer: even a string of individually-positive moves can be collectively negative if the frequency itself prices you as someone who will not stick. The discipline is to move rarely and decisively, so that each move is legible to the network as a step up rather than a symptom.

A final, often-missed point about the network: it does not only carry *whether* you were good, it carries *how* you operated. Did you share credit or hoard it? Did you help teammates or compete against them? Did you handle a bad month with composure or with blame? These behavioral signals travel along the same two hops as your skill, and over a full career they compound into a reputation that either opens the best seats to you or quietly closes them. The people who reach the top seats are very often not the single most talented individuals but the ones whose reputation for being both excellent and good to work with made every door open on the first knock. You are building that reputation every day, and a move is simply a moment when the network reads the most recent chapter of it.

#### Worked example: the counteroffer math and why accepting one often backfires

When Wei resigned, his current firm came back within forty-eight hours with a counteroffer: they would match the new firm's headline, taking him from \$600,000 to \$690,000 to keep him. For a moment this looked like the best of both worlds, more money and no move tax. Let us run the math, and then the part that is not math.

The math first. Staying on a counteroffer means: no forfeited deferred comp (he keeps the \$300,000 in flight), no garden-leave gap, no ramp, and a \$90,000 raise. On the pure ledger, the counteroffer dominates both staying at \$600,000 and moving for a realized \$255,000 in year one. If comp were the only variable, take the counteroffer every time.

But comp is not the only variable, and counteroffers carry two hidden costs that frequently make them negative EV despite the higher headline. First, the **trust cost**: you have just told your firm you were willing to leave. Even when they smile and match, you are now, quietly, marked as a flight risk. When the next promotion or the next prized book is allocated, the manager remembers that you nearly walked, and the person who never threatened to leave gets the nod. The raise is real; the discount on your future optionality is also real and usually larger. Second, the **root-cause cost**: a counteroffer pays you more to do the same job in the same situation. If you were looking because the seat was genuinely capped or the situation was broken, the counteroffer fixes the headline and fixes none of the actual problem, so you are back on the market in eighteen months, now with a tarnished trust account *and* a burned offer at the firm you turned down. Industry lore, widely repeated though hard to source precisely, is that a large share of people who accept counteroffers leave within a year or two anyway.

So the honest read: a counteroffer is rarely the win it looks like. If the only thing that was ever wrong was the number, a counteroffer can work, but you should know you have spent trust capital to get it. If anything structural was wrong, the counteroffer is a trap that resets nothing and costs you your standing at both firms.

*A counteroffer raises the headline while quietly debiting your trust account and leaving the real problem unsolved, which is why the bigger number so often backfires.*

## What a move resets and what it does not

We can now state the reset distinction precisely, because it is the cleanest way to decide whether a move is worth the tax. Figure 5 lays out the four things at stake and which side of the line each falls on.

![Matrix showing tenure and vesting reset to zero on a move, firm-specific context mostly resets, while skills and reputation travel with you intact](/imgs/blogs/staying-vs-switching-the-firm-hopping-math-6.png)

**Resets to zero: tenure and the vesting clock.** The day you start at the new firm, your tenure is zero. Your seniority-for-the-purpose-of-promotion clock restarts. Your deferred-comp vesting starts fresh, which means you are once again in the early, golden-handcuff years where a lot is unvested and leaving again is expensive. This is pure cost, paid in full on day one.

**Resets, mostly: firm-specific context.** You relearn the systems, the data, the people, the conventions. Some of it comes back fast; some never fully does. This is the ramp tax, and it is real for six to twelve months.

**Travels with you, fully: your skills.** Everything you actually know how to do, the math, the coding, the market intuition, the research discipline, the ability to size a bet and not blow up, comes with you intact. No one can forfeit your skills. This is why skill is the asset to invest in above all others: it is the one thing a move cannot reset, and it is the thing that determines your ceiling at every firm.

**Travels with you, for better or worse: your reputation and track record.** Your reputation is portable, and so is the record of how you have behaved. A great track record and a clean exit history open doors; a pattern of hopping and burning bridges closes them. Unlike skill, reputation can be *damaged* by a move, specifically by how you handle the exit, which is why the small-world section matters so much.

The decision rule falls right out of this picture. A move is worth it when the gain from the things that travel with you, your skills applied to a better seat, more than covers the loss from the things that reset, your tenure, vesting, and context. A marginal headline bump does not change what your skills can produce; it only resets the clocks, so it is pure cost. A structurally better seat lets your portable skills produce more, so it can clear the reset cost handsomely. You are, in effect, asking one question: *does the new building let the portable me earn enough more to pay for resetting the building-specific me?*

## The counteroffer question, in full

We ran the counteroffer math above; here is the decision logic around it, because counteroffers are where people make the most emotionally-driven mistakes. A counteroffer arrives at the worst possible moment for clear thinking: you are flattered, your current firm is suddenly attentive after months of taking you for granted, and the path of least resistance, stay, now pays more. Three rules keep you honest.

First, **decide your reservation number before you ever resign.** A reservation number is the comp at which you are genuinely indifferent between staying and leaving. Set it privately, in advance, based on the seat and the situation, not on whatever the negotiation produces. If your current firm would only pay you fairly under the threat of departure, that itself is information about how they value you, and it is rarely good information.

Second, **separate the comp problem from the situation problem.** If you were looking only because of money, a counteroffer can in principle solve it, at the cost of trust capital. If you were looking because of the seat, the manager, the strategy, or the culture, a counteroffer solves none of that and you should decline it. The most expensive counteroffer mistake is accepting one that fixes the number while leaving the broken thing broken, because you pay the trust cost and remain unhappy.

Third, **respect the small world on the way out either way.** If you accept the counteroffer, you have turned down a firm that made you an offer in good faith, and that firm remembers. If you decline it and leave, your current firm remembers how you handled the negotiation. Both audiences are two hops from your future. Behave like the professional you want the back-channel to describe.

## How to decide: a stay-vs-switch framework

Put it all together as a repeatable process, so you run the ledger before you feel the headline rather than after. Figure 6 lays out the framework as a pipeline, with a loop edge for the case where the answer comes out marginal and you should re-assess the seat rather than the number.

![Pipeline diagram of a stay-versus-switch decision: name the reason, score the seat, model the ledger net of the move tax, check reputation, then decide, with a loop back to re-assess if marginal](/imgs/blogs/staying-vs-switching-the-firm-hopping-math-5.png)

**Step 1: name the honest why.** Before anything else, write down the real reason you are looking. Is it a structurally better seat, more capital or a higher ceiling? Is it a broken situation you need to escape? Or is it just more money, boredom, or the flattery of being wanted? Be brutally honest, because the rest of the framework only works if the input is true. "Just more money" and "I am bored" are almost never sufficient reasons on their own.

**Step 2: score the seat, not the logo.** Evaluate the new seat on the axes that actually compound: capital you will deploy, the strategy and its ceiling, the manager and mentorship, the fit, and the trajectory. A bigger logo with a worse seat is a bad trade. Score the seat as if the comp were identical to your current seat, so you are not fooled by the headline.

**Step 3: model the ledger, net of the move tax.** Compute realized, not headline. Take the new headline, subtract forfeited unvested deferred comp, subtract the garden-leave or non-compete gap, subtract the ramp drag. Compare that realized number, across several years, against what you would realize by staying, including the deferred slices that will vest if you stay. If the new firm offers a make-whole for your forfeited deferred, count it; if they refuse, treat the refusal as a signal about how much they value you.

**Step 4: check the reputation cost.** Can you exit cleanly? Will you burn a manager who invested in you, or a firm you turned down? The two-hop network is keeping score, and a move that is positive on the comp ledger can still be negative once you account for a damaged back-channel reference.

**Step 5: decide on the number, not the feeling.** If the seat is structurally better and the ledger is net-positive after the tax, go, and leave clean. If the ledger is marginal, do not move; loop back and ask whether the seat is genuinely better, because a marginal ledger almost always means you were chasing a headline. The discipline is the same one the senior trader applies to a position: size to the edge, not to the excitement.

#### Worked example: Maya weighs a competing offer against her unvested comp

A year after her good move, Maya is courted again, this time by a brand-name firm leading with a twenty percent headline bump, from her current \$1,000,000 to \$1,200,000. She is happy where she is, but twenty percent is real money, so she runs the framework rather than her feelings.

- **Step 1, the why:** honestly, it is mostly the headline and the prestige of the name. Her current seat is excellent, her mentor is investing in her, and her trajectory is steep. There is no broken situation.
- **Step 2, score the seat:** the new seat offers similar capital and a similar strategy ceiling, under a manager she has not met, at a firm whose culture she does not know. On the axes that compound, it is roughly a lateral move, possibly a downgrade given the unknown manager.
- **Step 3, model the ledger:** she has \$250,000 of unvested deferred comp at her current firm, vesting over the next three years, and the new firm offers no make-whole. The move costs \$250,000 forfeited, plus a \$60,000 garden-leave gap, plus \$70,000 of ramp drag. Year one realized: \$1,200,000 minus \$250,000 minus \$60,000 minus \$70,000, which is \$820,000, *below* the \$1,000,000 she would make by staying. The twenty percent headline is a roughly eighteen percent first-year cut.
- **Step 4, reputation cost:** leaving now would burn the mentor who has been actively building her, which damages exactly the back-channel reference she will want later.
- **Step 5, decide:** the seat is at best lateral, the ledger is negative for two years and only marginal thereafter, and the reputation cost is real. She declines, and she does it graciously, keeping the door open and the network warm.

*A twenty percent headline against \$250,000 of unvested deferred comp and a steep current trajectory is a trap, and the framework surfaces it before the flattery does.*

The framework did the one thing that matters: it made Maya compute realized value net of the move tax, and weigh the seat rather than the logo, *before* the headline could hijack her decision. That is the entire skill.

## Common misconceptions

**"Always hop for more money."** The single most expensive belief in this post. It optimizes the headline and ignores the forfeited deferred comp, the gap, and the ramp, all of which are front-loaded and frequently larger than the bump. A marginal hop is usually a first-year pay cut, as Wei learned for \$300,000. Hop for a structurally better seat or to escape a broken one; do not hop for a number that the move tax erases. The people who compound the fastest are usually not the fastest hoppers; they are the ones who let deferred comp vest, build firm-specific context, and move decisively only when the seat is genuinely better.

**"Loyalty is rewarded."** This one is half-true and dangerous in its other half. Staying *can* compound, through vesting, tenure, trust, and a path that opens up, and a firm that values you will reward continuity. But loyalty for its own sake, staying in a capped seat or a broken situation out of sentiment or inertia, is its own quiet cost: stalled growth and a flat trajectory. The correct stance is neither reflexive loyalty nor reflexive hopping. It is to stay while staying compounds and to move decisively when a structurally better seat appears. Loyalty is a strategy, not a virtue, and like any strategy it should be run only while it is positive EV.

**"A counteroffer is a win."** It looks like one, more money with no move tax, but it usually debits your trust account, marking you as a flight risk, and it solves nothing if the reason you were looking was structural rather than monetary. A large share of counteroffer-accepters leave within a year or two anyway, having spent trust capital and burned the offer they declined. Treat a counteroffer as information about how your firm values you under threat, not as a victory.

**"Your network does not matter; my work speaks for itself."** In a normal-sized industry, maybe. In quant, which is tiny, your reputation is two hops from every desk you want, and the back-channel reference often decides the offer before your work is ever evaluated. How you leave each firm follows you for a decade. Your work does speak, but it speaks *through* the network, and a damaged reputation can mute even excellent work. Protect the network as carefully as you protect your skills, because it is the other asset that travels with you.

**"The deferred comp does not count, it is not in my account yet."** It is earned, contingent only on showing up, and walking away from it is exactly as real as writing a check. Underweighting it is the precise mechanism by which a "+15% raise" becomes a pay cut. Read your own vesting schedule and put the real, unvested number on the ledger before you do anything else.

## How it plays out in the real world

The mechanics above are not abstract; they are how comp and contracts are actually structured at the named firms, as of 2026.

**Deferred comp is standard and substantial.** At the top prop shops and multi-strat pods, a meaningful slice of the bonus is deferred and vests over three to four years. As reported on levels.fyi and in the Young and Calculated 2026 quant-pay survey, mid-level total comp runs roughly \$575,000 to \$820,000 for standard-to-strong performers, and at the five-year mark survivors land roughly \$800,000 to \$1.2M standard, with strong performers reaching \$2M to \$4M. Because base salaries are flat-ish and the bonus is the lever, a large fraction of those headline numbers is variable and partly deferred, which means a successful person always has a large unvested tail in flight. The more successful you have been, the more golden the handcuffs, and the larger the forfeiture line on any move.

**The bonus does not repeat, which compounds the move risk.** A \$1.3M year at a strong pod-shop seat, base \$200,000 plus bonus \$1.1M, can be a \$300,000 year if the seat or the P&L changes. This matters doubly for a switcher: you are leaving a known deferred tail for an unknown future bonus at a new seat, and the new firm's headline is on-target, not guaranteed. The asymmetry, a certain forfeiture against an uncertain future bonus, is exactly why the move tax should be weighted heavily.

**Non-competes and garden leave are real and enforced, especially at the secretive HFT shops.** Firms like Jump and Hudson River Trading, which compete on low-latency edge and guard their strategies fiercely, are known for taking non-competes and garden leave seriously. A multi-month gap with base-only pay is a normal cost of moving between such firms, and it belongs on every ledger. The pod shops and prop firms vary, but the structural feature, you are out of the bonus cycle during the gap, is widespread.

**Make-wholes exist for people firms genuinely want.** A serious offer for a senior or high-demand person will sometimes include a buyout of forfeited deferred comp, structured as additional sign-on. Maya got a partial one; many people get none. Whether the new firm offers it is one of the cleanest signals of how much they actually value you, and it is a fair thing to ask for directly in [negotiation](/blog/trading/quant-careers/negotiation-offers-and-the-comp-ladder).

**The small world is not folklore; it is structural.** The serious quant firms number in the dozens, the senior populations overlap heavily, and specialist recruiters span the niche. Five Rings and Jane Street lead H1B base disclosures near \$300,000, Citadel Securities near \$257,000, and the people at those firms know the people at the others. A clean exit is therefore not just etiquette; it is the maintenance of an asset that determines which seats you can reach for the rest of your career. The full picture of how the firms relate, and how to choose among them, is in [the wider ecosystem and how to choose your firm](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-wider-ecosystem-and-how-to-choose-your-firm), and the structure of the comp itself is in [quant compensation demystified](/blog/trading/quant-careers/quant-compensation-demystified).

## When this matters / Further reading

This decision arrives at predictable moments: the first competing offer two or three years in, the counteroffer the week you resign, the recruiter call when you are bored, the moment a mentor leaves and you wonder whether to follow. In every one of them, the move that feels obvious, take the bigger headline, is usually the one the ledger reverses once you subtract forfeited deferred comp, the gap, and the ramp. Run the framework before you feel the offer, weigh the seat rather than the logo, protect the small-world network by leaving clean, and remember which assets travel with you and which reset to zero. The senior quant has internalized exactly this discipline in their career that they apply to a position: size to the edge, not to the excitement, and never let a marginal headline override the real number.
