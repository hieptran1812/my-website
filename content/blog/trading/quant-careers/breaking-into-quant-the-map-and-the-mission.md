Maya is sitting in a glass room on the 40th floor of a Manhattan tower, and she is sweating. Not from the temperature — the air conditioning is aggressive — but because a trader she met twenty minutes ago has just said the words "I'll make you a market in the number of red cars that drive past this building in the next hour" and is now waiting, pen tapping, for her two-sided quote. Maya is a math undergraduate. She has solved harder problems than this on paper. But on paper there was no clock, no opponent who would hit whichever side of her quote was wrong, and no salary riding on whether she sounded calm. Her brain is doing two things at once: estimating the true number, and estimating *how confident she actually is*. The gap between those two estimates is the whole game. Quote a tight market and she looks sharp but bleeds if she is wrong; quote a wide one and she looks timid but stays safe. Somewhere in between is the answer that maximizes her expected value. She takes a breath and says, "Forty at fifty." The trader smiles, says "I sell you fifty at forty," and the round is on.

That moment — a smart technical person, mid-game, doing expected-value arithmetic under social and financial pressure — is the entire industry in miniature. The job of a quant is to find a small statistical edge in markets and harvest it carefully, again and again, while controlling risk so a bad run does not end the game. The job of *getting* the job turns out to be the same shape: you are estimating odds, sizing bets, and managing risk, except the bets are job applications and interview answers and the runway you have before your savings or your patience runs out.

This is the opening post of a long series about that journey — from a technical outsider who has never heard the word "vega" to a senior quant who owns a book of risk and mentors the next batch of Mayas. Figure 1 is the map of the whole trip. Notice that it is not a straight line. Every stage feeds back into the one before it: a failed interview loop sends you back to build foundations, a first taste of the desk reshapes how you'd apply next time. The path is a pipeline with feedback, and the people who navigate it best treat each stage as a probabilistic bet rather than a pass-or-fail gate.

Let's build the vocabulary first, because almost everything that follows depends on it.

## Foundations: What a quant actually is and what "edge" means

A **quant** — short for "quantitative analyst," though almost no one says the long form — is a person who uses mathematics, statistics, and code to make money in financial markets. That is the whole definition. The word covers a trader who prices options on a screen, a researcher who hunts for predictive signals in years of price data, a developer who writes the C++ that fires an order in microseconds, and a scientist who builds machine-learning models of the market. They share a method: decisions driven by data and probability rather than gut feeling or a story about the economy. Where they differ is which part of the machine they operate, and that difference is so important that an entire later post is devoted to it — see [the four role archetypes](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer).

To say what these people *do*, you need to know what kind of firm they do it at. There are two broad families.

A **market maker** is a firm (or a person at a firm) that continuously offers to both buy and sell an asset, quoting a **bid** (the price at which it will buy) and an **ask** or **offer** (the price at which it will sell). The difference between them is **the spread**. If a market maker quotes a stock "100.00 bid, 100.04 ask," anyone who wants to sell immediately gets \$100.00 and anyone who wants to buy immediately pays \$100.04. The market maker pockets the \$0.04 spread for providing the convenience of an instant trade. It does not care whether the stock goes up or down; it cares about capturing that spread thousands of times a second while not getting badly run over when the true price moves. Jane Street, Optiver, IMC, SIG, Citadel Securities, and Hudson River Trading are, at their core, gigantic, sophisticated market makers. They are **proprietary trading firms** ("prop firms"): they trade their own capital, keep their own profits, and answer to no outside investors. There are no client fees and no fund to manage — the firm *is* the money.

The other family is the **hedge fund**. A hedge fund manages other people's money — from pension funds, endowments, wealthy individuals — and charges a fee for the privilege, classically "2 and 20" (2% of assets per year plus 20% of the profits). A **quantitative** or **systematic** hedge fund makes its decisions with models and code rather than a manager's discretion. Two Sigma, D.E. Shaw, and WorldQuant are systematic funds. Citadel is a particularly important hybrid: its hedge-fund arm is a **multi-strategy "pod" shop**, meaning it is organized into many semi-independent teams called **pods**, each run by a **portfolio manager** (PM) who is handed a slice of capital, a risk budget, and a simple mandate — make money or lose your seat. (Citadel Securities, the market maker, is a legally separate company with separate hiring; people confuse the two constantly.)

Now the words that carry the whole series.

**Alpha** is return that is not explained by simply taking on market risk. If the stock market rises 8% and your strategy returns 8%, you produced no alpha — you just rode the wave (that ride is called **beta**). If you returned 12% with the same risk, the extra 4% is alpha: a genuine edge, something you knew or did that the market did not price in. Alpha is the thing quant researchers spend their lives hunting, and it is frustratingly scarce and quick to decay once others find it too.

**Edge** is the more general word. Your edge is any repeatable reason you expect to come out ahead. A market maker's edge is the spread plus superior speed and inventory management. A researcher's edge is a signal that predicts tomorrow's returns slightly better than chance. A poker player's edge is reading opponents. Edge is almost never large per instance — a market maker might expect to earn a fraction of a cent of true value on each share — but it is *positive and repeatable*, and repeated across enormous volume it compounds into the comp numbers we'll discuss.

**Expected value** (**EV**) is how you measure edge. The EV of a bet is the probability-weighted average of its outcomes: multiply each possible payoff by its probability and sum them. A bet that wins \$100 with probability 0.6 and loses \$100 with probability 0.4 has an EV of `0.6 × 100 − 0.4 × 100 = +\$20`. A quant takes bets with positive EV and skips or fades bets with negative EV, and crucially *sizes* each bet by how confident they are. EV is the native language of the trading floor and the interview room alike. If you want the formal machinery, the interview series has a dedicated treatment in [expected-value techniques for quant interviews](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/expected-value-techniques-quant-interviews), and the underlying probability is built from scratch in [probability spaces and random variables](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/probability-spaces-random-variables-math-for-quants).

**P&L** stands for "profit and loss" — literally the money your trading or your strategy made or lost over some period. A trader's P&L is the scoreboard. It is what bonuses are paid on, what gets a pod more capital or shuts it down, and what someone means when they say "I was up two million last quarter." P&L is realized EV: if your EV process is sound, your P&L should be positive on average over enough trades, even though any single day or trade can go either way.

Three more terms you'll meet constantly, because they shape both the work and the comp. A **pod** is the unit of organization at a multi-strategy fund like Citadel: a small team, led by a portfolio manager, that's given a slice of capital and a risk budget and runs its own book of strategies. Pods are largely autonomous and largely accountable — a pod that makes money gets more capital, and a pod that loses past its risk limit can be unwound and its people let go, sometimes within months. That accountability is the engine behind the next term. **Up-or-out** is the industry's blunt career physics: at most top firms you either keep growing your contribution and move up, or your seat eventually goes to someone who will. There is no comfortable plateau where you coast for twenty years; the same competitive pressure that makes the comp large makes the tenure precarious. And **deferred compensation** is how firms keep their best people from walking out the door with a great year's bonus: a large chunk of a senior quant's bonus is often paid out over several following years and forfeited if they leave early — golden handcuffs that make the headline comp number and the *cash-in-hand* number two different things. When you read a comp figure later in this post, remember it may be partly deferred and partly conditional on staying.

The last foundational idea is the one that ties the job to the job hunt: **the difference between a good decision and a good outcome.** In a world of randomness, a good decision — a positive-EV bet, correctly sized — can lose, and a bad decision can win. Amateurs judge themselves by outcomes and ride an emotional roller-coaster. Professionals judge themselves by the *quality of the decision* and trust that good decisions win over many repetitions. This is the single most important mental habit in the industry, and it is exactly the habit that keeps a job-seeker sane through a string of rejections that were never personal to begin with. Hold onto it; the next section is built on it.

## The two EV games: the markets and getting hired

Here is the thesis of the series stated plainly: **the discipline that makes you good at markets is the same discipline that makes you good at getting hired.** Both are games of positive expectancy under uncertainty, and both reward process over outcome, honest calibration, and ruthless risk control. Figure 2 lays the two arenas side by side.

![A two-column matrix comparing edge in markets versus edge in getting hired across four rows: positive expectancy, process over outcome, calibration, and risk control](/imgs/blogs/breaking-into-quant-the-map-and-the-mission-2.png)

Walk down the four rows.

**Positive expectancy.** A trader only puts on a position when the expected value is positive after costs. A job-seeker should only spend a scarce resource — an application, a referral, a week of prep — when the expected return beats the cost. Applying to a firm where you have a real shot is positive EV; spraying a hundred identical resumes at firms whose bar you cannot yet clear is negative EV, because each application costs attention and the marginal one near-certainly fails.

**Process over outcome.** A trader who loses on a well-reasoned, well-sized trade did nothing wrong; the loss was variance. A candidate who gives a sharp, well-calibrated interview answer and still gets rejected — because another candidate was slightly stronger, or the firm filled the seat — also did nothing wrong. If you let a rejection convince you that you are not good enough, you will under-apply next round and tank your own EV. Judge the loop, not the result.

**Calibration.** This is the deep one. Calibration means your stated confidence matches reality: when you say you are 70% sure, you are right about 70% of the time. In a trading game, a calibrated player quotes a tight market when they genuinely know the answer and a wide one when they do not — and says so. Interviewers at Jane Street and SIG are explicitly testing this; they would rather hear "I'm only about 60% on this, here's my reasoning" than false certainty. In the job hunt, calibration means honestly assessing which firms and roles you are actually competitive for, so you neither waste shots on impossible seats nor sell yourself short on winnable ones.

**Risk control.** A trader caps the downside before chasing the upside — position limits, stop-losses, never betting the firm on one idea. A job-seeker does the same with their runway: don't quit your job and burn six months of savings on a single dream firm, don't let one rejection from one firm define the season, and keep a portfolio of applications so no single "no" is fatal. Risk control is what lets you stay in the game long enough for your positive EV to play out.

The symmetry runs deeper than a clever analogy. The reason these four habits show up in both arenas is that both are **repeated games under uncertainty with asymmetric, fat-tailed payoffs** — a single great trade or a single great offer can make a year, while a single catastrophic loss or a burned-out, demoralized season can end one. In games shaped like that, the optimal policy is never to maximize the best case; it is to maximize the probability-weighted outcome while keeping the worst case survivable. That is why a market maker quotes a slightly-too-wide market when uncertain rather than a thrillingly tight one, and why a candidate keeps applying methodically rather than staking everything on one firm and one shot. The firms are explicitly hiring for people who already think this way, which is the deepest reason the job hunt rewards exactly the temperament the job demands: the interview is, in part, a live audition for whether you run this policy under pressure.

Let's make the markets side concrete, because the candidates reading this need to feel what a "small edge, repeated" actually means.

#### Worked example: the EV of a simple market-making quote

Maya is back in the trading game, but now we'll put numbers on it. Suppose the "asset" is the number of jellybeans in a jar, and the *true* number — which neither player knows exactly — is 1,000. Maya has to make a two-sided market: she quotes a bid and an ask, and the interviewer will either buy from her at her ask or sell to her at her bid, choosing whichever side is profitable for them given their own estimate.

Say Maya quotes "990 bid, 1,010 ask" — a 20-wide market centered on her best guess of 1,000. The interviewer, who is testing her, "sells" her 1 unit at her bid of 990, betting the true number is below 990. When the jar is revealed at 1,000, Maya bought something worth 1,000 for 990, so she made `1,000 − 990 = +10`. Good outcome. But suppose instead the true number had been 950: now Maya bought at 990 something worth 950 and lost `950 − 990 = −40`.

Her EV depends on her quote width and her accuracy. If her estimate of 1,000 is unbiased and the true value is symmetric around it with a typical error of, say, ±30, then a *wide* market (say 50-wide: 975 / 1,025) almost always traps the counterparty into a bad trade, earning Maya positive EV — but no experienced interviewer will trade against a market that wide, so she captures nothing and looks timid. A *tight* market (say 4-wide: 998 / 1,002) gets plenty of action but barely covers her estimation error, so she earns close to zero EV and risks real losses when her guess is off. The art — and the thing being graded — is choosing the width that maximizes `(probability of getting traded) × (EV per trade)`. A market maker on a real desk faces this exact tradeoff billions of times a year, except their edge per trade is fractions of a cent and their volume is astronomical. *A market maker does not need to be right by a lot; they need to be right by a little, on purpose, over and over — and that "on purpose" is the entire craft.*

If the mechanics of that game are new to you, the interview series drills them directly in [market-making games in quant interviews](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews). For this series, the point is the shape of the thinking, not the tricks.

Now flip the same EV lens onto the job hunt, with Wei. Wei is a fifth-year CS PhD whose dissertation is on probabilistic models — exactly the profile systematic funds covet for research-scientist roles. Wei is deciding how many firms to apply to and how hard to prepare. That is also an EV problem, and we'll work it fully in the funnel section. For now, hold the symmetry in your head: Maya quoting a market and Wei choosing an application strategy are running the same algorithm.

## Who the top firms are (a first map)

You cannot navigate a landscape you can't name. So here is the first map of the major players, with what each one actually is. Treat this as a survey; later posts in Track B give each firm its own playbook. All firm facts below are public, as of June 2026, from firm pages, Wikipedia, and standard industry reporting.

**Jane Street** (founded 2000, New York). A proprietary trading firm and market maker, enormous in ETFs and options. Culturally distinctive: it runs much of its system in **OCaml**, a functional programming language, and its interviews are built around puzzles, probability, and trading games rather than rote finance. It is famous for a strong, collaborative culture and a powerful intern pipeline. If you've heard exactly one quant firm's name, it's probably this one.

**Optiver** (founded 1986, Amsterdam, with Chicago, Sydney, and Shanghai offices; about 2,100 employees as of 2024). One of the oldest names, a listed-derivatives market maker, and the source of the legendary **mental-math test** — roughly 80 arithmetic questions in 8 minutes, no calculator, no scratch paper. If you can't add, subtract, multiply, and estimate at speed, Optiver's front door is closed; the interview series has a whole post on building that speed in [mental math and arithmetic speed](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews).

**Susquehanna (SIG)** (founded 1987, Bala Cynwyd, Pennsylvania; 3,000-plus employees across 14 offices). An options market maker with a famous quirk: it teaches new traders **poker**, deliberately, because poker is decision-making under uncertainty with money on the line — the purest training ground for EV thinking and calibration. SIG runs a structured education program for incoming traders.

**IMC Trading** (founded 1989, Amsterdam, with Chicago and Sydney offices). A market maker whose screening leans on HackerRank-style coding plus mental math. A close cousin of Optiver in style and geography.

**Jump Trading** (founded 1999, Chicago). High-frequency and low-latency, trading futures, options, and crypto (via Jump Crypto). Secretive by design, with a deep bar in C++, systems programming, kernel bypass, and lock-free data structures. Jump is where the engineering *is* the edge.

**Hudson River Trading (HRT)** (founded 2002, New York). High-frequency, with the explicit motto that "engineering excellence drives everything." Ultra-low-latency across equities, futures, and options. Notably candidate-friendly in one respect: its interviews often let you pick your language (C++ or Python) and mix algorithms, probability, and systems design.

**Citadel** (founded 1990 by Ken Griffin). The multi-strategy **pod** hedge fund described earlier — high accountability, sharp elbows, PMs owning their own books. And, separately, **Citadel Securities**, the market maker, which hires differently. Both are titans; know which one a given job posting is for.

**Two Sigma** (founded 2001, New York). A systematic fund with roughly \$70B in assets under management and about 2,000 staff, leaning heavily on AI/ML and distributed computing, with a research-scientist culture and longer-horizon signals. A natural home for someone like Wei.

**D.E. Shaw** (founded 1988 by David E. Shaw, the original "quant king"). About \$65B in assets, historically a math-models-and-anomaly-hunting shop, more recently adding discretionary strategies. Famously rigorous and famously secretive about its methods.

**WorldQuant.** A systematic firm built on a distinctive **alpha-factory** model: a global network of research consultants who each build many small formulaic signals ("alphas") on the firm's **BRAIN / WebSim** platform. Where Two Sigma wants a few deep researchers, WorldQuant industrializes the search for many shallow ones.

Beyond these, a deep ecosystem: **DRW, Tower Research, Five Rings, Akuna Capital, Old Mission, XTX Markets, Radix**, plus the systematic desks inside banks. To anchor the scale, Five Rings and Jane Street have led H1B visa base-salary disclosures at around \$300k, with Citadel Securities around \$257k — public filings that give a rare hard floor on what these firms pay before any bonus.

That is the club. It is small — a few dozen firms genuinely matter — which is both intimidating (everyone is aiming at the same doors) and clarifying (you can actually learn all of them). The companion post [how quant firms actually make money](/blog/trading/quant-careers/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money) goes under the hood of the business models that pay for all this.

## The honest reality: selectivity, survivorship, and the bonus that doesn't repeat

Now the part the recruiting glossy brochures leave out. Yes, the comp is real and it is large. No, it is not what the screenshots imply for the median person who walks in the door. Three honest truths, with numbers.

**Truth one: the selectivity is brutal.** Top intern and new-grad programs at these firms are commonly described as accepting on the order of a *low single-digit percent* of applicants — comparable to, or tighter than, the admit rates of the most selective universities. Thousands of strong applicants chase each seat. This is widely reported and approximate; no firm publishes an exact acceptance rate, and the precise number varies by year, role, and office. But the order of magnitude is not in dispute: you are competing against a global pool of mathematically gifted people, and most of them — most of *you*, statistically — will not get the specific seat they want on the first try. That is not a verdict on your worth. It is arithmetic, and arithmetic is exactly what you should bring to it (which is what the funnel section does).

**Truth two: the comp stories are survivorship-biased.** When someone says "everyone makes \$600k by year five," they are quoting the people who *survived* — who cleared the up-or-out filter, kept their seat, and had a decent run. The industry runs largely on **up-or-out**: if your P&L contribution or your research output doesn't justify the seat, the seat goes to someone else. Many people who start at these firms wash out within a few years, switch firms laterally, or land in roles that pay far below the headline. The \$600k figure is conditional on having survived a filter that removed a meaningful fraction of the people who started beside you. Figure 3 shows what this looks like: three outcome bands — below-target, standard, and strong — fanning out as tenure grows.

![A line chart of total compensation in USD millions by years of experience, showing three diverging bands for below-target, standard, and strong outcomes from new grad to the eight-year mark, annotated to note these are survivors](/imgs/blogs/breaking-into-quant-the-map-and-the-mission-3.png)

Read the chart carefully. At the new-grad stage the bands are close together — \$350k to \$650k covers most of the spread, because everyone is on a similar starting package. By year five the standard band sits around \$800k–\$1.2M, the weak band around \$475k–\$625k, and the strong outliers reach \$2M–\$4M. By year eight the strong outliers can exceed \$8M while the weak band is still around \$0.9M–\$1.8M. The fan-out is the story. Two people who started identically can be a 5x apart by year eight, driven entirely by which seat they landed, how their P&L went, and whether they survived. And remember: every point on that chart is a *survivor*. The people who left are not plotted, which is precisely what makes the averages look so good. All figures are reported ranges as of 2025–2026 from levels.fyi, Glassdoor, efinancialcareers, and the "Young & Calculated" 2026 quant-pay survey, and are illustrative band midpoints, not guarantees.

**Truth three: the base is flat and the bonus is the lever — and the bonus doesn't repeat.** This is the single most misunderstood fact about quant comp. Your base salary is roughly fixed: a top new grad might have a base around \$250k–\$375k, and even a senior trader's base might be \$200k–\$250k. Base barely moves with tenure. What moves is the **bonus**, which is tied to your P&L contribution or your team's performance — and it does *not* carry forward automatically. A trader who earns a \$1.3M bonus in a strong year can earn \$300k the next year if the seat changes, the strategy decays, or the market regime shifts. Figure 6 shows this directly.

![A stacked bar chart of base salary versus bonus for a new grad and a five-year survivor, showing base barely changing while the bonus grows to dominate total compensation](/imgs/blogs/breaking-into-quant-the-map-and-the-mission-6.png)

For a new grad in the standard band, the package might be roughly \$300k base plus a \$250k bonus, totaling around \$550k. For a five-year survivor in the standard band, the base might *fall* to around \$200k while the bonus grows to around \$800k, totaling about \$1.0M. The base did not do the work — the bonus did. And the bonus is the variable, risky, doesn't-repeat part. Internalize this now: a single great year is not your new baseline, and planning your life around a bonus that hasn't been earned yet is the financial equivalent of betting your whole stack on one hand. (For how a working trader thinks about sizing bets so a bad run doesn't ruin them, see the interview series on [the Kelly criterion and sequential betting](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/expected-value-techniques-quant-interviews).)

Let's make the year-1-to-year-2 reality concrete, because it trips up smart people every year.

#### Worked example: the comp math when the bonus doesn't repeat

Wei lands a research-scientist seat. Year one: base \$250k, sign-on bonus \$100k (paid once, for joining), performance bonus \$200k. First-year total: `250 + 100 + 200 = \$550k`. Wei sees that number, mentally annualizes it, and starts shopping for an apartment that assumes \$550k forever.

Now year two. The sign-on does not repeat — it was a one-time payment to get Wei in the door, so that \$100k is gone. The base ticks up slightly to \$260k. The performance bonus depends entirely on how Wei's research contributed; suppose it was a quiet year and the bonus is \$150k. Year-two total: `260 + 150 = \$410k`. Wei's comp *fell* by \$140k, or about 25%, despite getting a raise on base and doing perfectly competent work. Nothing went wrong — the sign-on simply wasn't recurring and the bonus is volatile by design.

Run the strong case instead: Wei's signal contributes meaningfully to the fund's P&L, and the year-two performance bonus is \$700k. Total: `260 + 700 = \$960k`, nearly double year one. Same person, same firm, wildly different outcomes — and the *only* honest way to plan around this is to treat the base plus a conservative bonus estimate as your real income and treat the upside as a windfall. *A bonus is a payment for a result that already happened; it is not a promise about the next one, and the quants who stay solvent are the ones who spend like the median year, not the best one.*

This is why the EV mindset matters for your own finances, not just your trading. The senior quant has internalized "judge the process, expect variance in the outcome" in markets *and* in their own paycheck.

## The internship is the real front door

There is a structural fact about quant hiring that most outsiders miss entirely, and it changes everything about how you should approach the funnel: **the full-time seats are mostly filled by converting interns.** At the top firms, the internship is not a summer of fetching coffee and shadowing — it *is* the interview, stretched over ten to twelve weeks, with real work and a real evaluation at the end. Strong programs convert most of their interns into full-time return offers, which means the genuinely competitive moment is getting the internship, not the full-time role. If you are a student, this reframes your timeline: the seat you want for after graduation is largely won the *summer before*, sometimes two summers before. Recruit the internship first, aggressively, because that is where the doors actually are.

The pay during the internship is, frankly, surreal — and it tells you how seriously these firms take the audition. As of the 2026 cycle, Jane Street interns earn roughly an annualized \$300k, which works out to about \$4,500–\$6,000 per week. Citadel and Citadel Securities pay interns \$4,300–\$5,800 per week (roughly \$47k–\$64k over an eleven-week summer) plus a \$15k–\$25k sign-on and corporate housing worth around \$5k a month. IMC interns can earn up to about \$5,800 per week, and their conversion packages run around \$425k in total comp. Optiver's PhD interns earn roughly \$80k–\$90k for an eight-week placement, and XTX's AI-research interns can earn up to about \$35k a month. These figures are reported on firm pages and the "Young & Calculated" 2026 internship survey and are illustrative of the cycle, not contractual guarantees. The point is not the exact number — it's the *signal*. A firm that pays an undergraduate a \$300k-annualized rate to spend a summer with it is making a serious bet, and it expects a serious return: it is using the summer to decide whether to bet on you for a decade.

This reshapes Maya's strategy completely. Rather than treating the full-time funnel as her main shot, she should be aiming at internships a year earlier than feels natural, because intern conversion is a higher-probability path than cold full-time recruiting. The funnel math from earlier still applies, but the prize at the end of the intern funnel is not just a summer paycheck — it's a near-direct line to a full-time seat that bypasses the most brutal stage of the full-time competition. *If you remember one tactical thing from this whole post, it is this: the internship is the real front door, and the people who walk through it casually as if it were a normal summer job are the ones who later wonder why the full-time process felt impossible.*

## What the interview loop actually tests

Before we lay out the map, it's worth demystifying the loop itself, because "quant interview" means very different things at different firms, and preparing for the wrong one is the most common avoidable mistake. The loop generally runs in stages, and each stage is filtering for a specific quality.

The first stage is almost always an **online screen**. For market makers — Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, IMC, Akuna, Citadel Securities — this is the timed **mental-math test**: roughly 60 to 80 arithmetic questions in about 8 minutes, no calculator and no scratch paper, with a passing bar commonly around 70–85% correct. It is not testing whether you *can* do arithmetic; it is testing whether you can do it fast, accurately, and under a clock that induces exactly the kind of pressure a live market does. For developer-leaning roles and firms like IMC and Citadel Securities, the screen is instead a HackerRank or Codility coding assessment. Either way, this first stage culls the largest absolute number of candidates, which — recalling Figure 5 — is precisely why it deserves the most prep per expected payoff.

Pass the screen and you reach **phone rounds**, typically a mix of probability and brainteasers: expected value, conditional probability, fair-value and no-arbitrage reasoning, and a couple of classic puzzles per round. The classic puzzles are not trivia; they're calibration tests dressed as riddles, and the interviewer cares as much about how you reason aloud and update as about the final answer. Then come the **trading games** — the market-making simulations and dice, betting, and war games that Jane Street and SIG are famous for. These test EV thinking, calibration, the willingness to update on new information, and grace under pressure. They are deliberately *not* solvable by memorizing finance facts; they reward the temperament described all the way back in Figure 2.

For coding and research roles the loop branches. **Coding rounds** for quant developers and software engineers run from competitive-programming-adjacent algorithm problems up to, at the low-latency firms, a deep **C++ round** (the memory model, undefined behavior, templates, lock-free programming, cache behavior) and a **systems-design round** (kernel bypass, real-time constraints). HRT, Jump, and Citadel Securities lean hardest here. **Research cases** for quant researchers are a signal or backtest take-home or live exercise: the test is how you frame the problem, avoid overfitting, maintain out-of-sample discipline, write up the result, and — most tellingly — whether you can *kill your own idea* when the evidence says you should.

The whole thing typically assembles as: online screen, then one or two phone screens, then a **superday** or on-site of four to six 45-to-60-minute rounds with different people, mixing math, coding, games, research, and a light behavioral conversation. Jane Street tends to run two to three rounds per stage. Knowing which qualities each stage filters for lets you prepare deliberately — and the sibling series breaks each one down problem by problem in [the quant interview process and how to prepare](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/quant-interview-process-strategy-how-to-prepare). The backgrounds that tend to clear this gauntlet are math, CS, physics, statistics, electrical engineering, and operations research, and a competition signal — IMO, IOI, Putnam, ICPC, Kaggle, or IMC Prosperity — is a strong tailwind on the resume that lifts the very first screen's pass probability.

Now let's do the EV math that Track C is built on, because it directly answers the question every reader has: how many firms should I apply to, and is preparation worth it?

#### Worked example: the funnel math — how many offers should you expect?

Maya is deciding her application strategy. Suppose, without serious preparation, her probability of converting any single top-firm application into an offer is `p = 0.03` (3% — roughly the brutal base rate). She applies to `N = 10` firms. Treating the applications as roughly independent (a simplification — strong firms correlate, but it's close enough for intuition), the expected number of offers is `N × p = 10 × 0.03 = 0.3`. That's less than one expected offer. The probability she gets *at least one* offer is `1 − (1 − p)^N = 1 − 0.97^10 ≈ 1 − 0.737 = 0.263`, about 26%. So unprepared, applying to 10 firms, Maya has roughly a one-in-four chance of landing anywhere. Not great.

Two levers change this. The first is `N`: apply to more firms. At `N = 25` firms and the same `p = 0.03`, her chance of at least one offer is `1 − 0.97^25 ≈ 1 − 0.467 = 0.533`, about 53%. Just by widening the funnel from 10 to 25 well-targeted firms, she goes from a 26% to a 53% chance of an offer — that's the risk-control row from Figure 2 in action: a portfolio of bets beats a single bet.

The second lever is far more powerful: `p` itself. The whole point of preparation — mental-math drills, hundreds of probability puzzles, trading-game reps, mock interviews — is to lift your per-application offer probability. Suppose six months of serious prep raises Maya's `p` from 0.03 to 0.08 (still under one in ten — these firms are hard even when you're ready). Now at `N = 10`, her chance of at least one offer is `1 − 0.92^10 ≈ 1 − 0.434 = 0.566`, about 57%, from just ten applications. At `N = 25`, it's `1 − 0.92^25 ≈ 1 − 0.124 = 0.876`, about 88%. *Widening the funnel helps linearly, but lifting your per-shot probability through preparation helps exponentially — which is exactly why the single highest-EV thing a candidate can do is get genuinely better, not just apply to more places.* And the place to get better is the [quant interview process and strategy](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/quant-interview-process-strategy-how-to-prepare) guide and the drills it links to.

Figure 5 zooms into the funnel itself, so you can see where the drop-off happens and therefore where preparation pays.

![A six-stage pipeline of the hiring funnel from thousands of applicants through the online screen, phone rounds, superday loop, offers, and intern conversion, with drop-off percentages labeled at each stage](/imgs/blogs/breaking-into-quant-the-map-and-the-mission-5.png)

The drop-offs are illustrative but representative of the shape. Roughly 3,000 applicants per seat get cut to about 450 at the online screen (the mental-math or coding assessment), to about 120 through phone rounds of probability and puzzles, to about 30 through the superday loop of four to six live rounds, to about 15 offers — a low single-digit percent from the top. The biggest absolute cull is the very first screen, which is why a timed mental-math test or a HackerRank assessment is often the difference between getting a human conversation and getting auto-rejected. Prepare for the stage that's eliminating the most people, not the stage you find most interesting.

#### Worked example: the EV of spending six months preparing

Wei, the PhD, is choosing whether to spend six months preparing seriously or to just apply with his existing background and hope his research speaks for itself. Let's put dollars on the decision.

Assume a successful new-grad offer is worth, conservatively, an extra \$300k per year over Wei's next-best option (an industry ML job), and that he'd capture that delta for at least three years before things diverge — call the lifetime value of landing a quant seat \$900k in present-value-ish terms (rounded, illustrative). Without prep, say Wei's chance of landing *somewhere* good this cycle is 30%. With six months of focused prep, suppose it rises to 65%. The prep lifts his probability of the \$900k prize by `0.65 − 0.30 = 0.35`. The expected value of preparing is therefore `0.35 × \$900k = \$315k`.

What does the prep cost? Six months of Wei's time. If he values that time at the \$150k/year he could have earned working, six months is \$75k of opportunity cost, plus maybe \$2k for books and mock interviews. So the cost is about \$77k, and the expected benefit is about \$315k. The EV of preparing is `315 − 77 ≈ +\$238k`, and that ignores the option value of *also* applying again next cycle with the skills already built. Even under pessimistic assumptions — halve the prize, halve the probability lift — the prep stays solidly positive-EV. *Preparation is not a chore you grudgingly do before applying; it is, on the numbers, one of the highest-expected-value investments a strong technical person can make with six months of their life — provided they prepare for the right things, which is what Track D and the sibling series are for.*

Notice that all four worked examples were the same algorithm — estimate probabilities, weigh payoffs, control downside — applied to a trade, a paycheck, an application portfolio, and a time investment. That sameness is the thesis. Learn to think this way and you will be better at both halves of the journey.

## Common misconceptions

The quant world is wrapped in folklore, and the folklore actively hurts good candidates. Here are the five myths I most want to kill, named and corrected.

**Myth 1: "You need a PhD."** False for most roles. Trading and many developer roles are dominated by people with strong bachelor's or master's degrees in math, CS, physics, statistics, or engineering — Maya's path. A PhD is *common but not mandatory* for research-scientist roles at the most research-heavy funds (Two Sigma, D.E. Shaw), and it can help there. But plenty of senior traders and quant developers at Jane Street, Optiver, HRT, and Jump never wrote a dissertation. If you have a PhD, like Wei, it's an asset for research seats; if you don't, the trading and dev doors are wide open. Don't talk yourself out of the industry over a credential it mostly doesn't require.

**Myth 2: "It's all luck."** The opposite of the survivorship myth, and equally wrong. Yes, any single trade, any single year, any single interview has a large random component — that's why we judge process over outcome. But over thousands of trades and many years, skill dominates: the firms that consistently profit do so because their edge is real and repeatable, and the people who consistently get hired and promoted do so because their preparation and judgment are real. Luck is the *variance* around a skill-determined *mean*. Mistaking the variance for the whole story is exactly the calibration error the job is designed to filter out.

**Myth 3: "Everyone clears a million."** Survivorship bias, addressed at length above. The million-plus figures describe survivors in standard-or-better seats several years in. The new-grad band is more like \$450k–\$650k on target, the weak band stays well under a million for years, and a meaningful fraction of starters don't make it to year five at the same firm at all. The comp is genuinely excellent — among the best in the world for a technical career — but quoting the 90th percentile as if it's the median is how people end up bitter or broke.

**Myth 4: "The math is the hard part."** Usually false. The math required for most quant roles, while substantial, is learnable — it's the content of an entire 50-post sibling series, and it's finite. The genuinely hard parts are: *speed and calibration under pressure* (doing correct arithmetic and probability fast, while being watched, without panicking), *honest self-assessment* (killing your own bad ideas before the market does), and *resilience through variance* (staying disciplined through losing streaks and rejection streaks). Smart people who fail usually fail on temperament and calibration, not on whether they can integrate. Prepare your mindset as deliberately as your math.

**Myth 5: "Interviews are just LeetCode."** Partly true for some developer roles, but misleading overall. Yes, low-latency firms like HRT, Jump, and Citadel Securities lean hard on algorithms, C++ depth (memory model, undefined behavior, templates, lock-free structures), and systems design — for those, [the C++ for low-latency interview](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/coding-interview-quant-data-structures-algorithms) prep is essential. But market-maker interviews (Jane Street, Optiver, SIG, IMC) are built around *mental math, probability, trading games, and EV reasoning*, not data-structure puzzles. And research interviews (Two Sigma, D.E. Shaw, WorldQuant) center on a signal or backtest case where the test is whether you can avoid overfitting and kill your own idea. Preparing only LeetCode for a Jane Street trading interview is preparing for the wrong exam.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you decide quant is a real target rather than a daydream — which, if you've read this far, it probably is. The single takeaway to carry into every later post is the thesis: *the job is a probabilistic edge, and so is getting it.* Approach the firms, the funnel, the comp, and your own career the way a good trader approaches a market — as expected-value decisions under uncertainty, judged by process and protected by risk control. Do that and the brutal selectivity stops being a wall and becomes what it actually is: an arithmetic problem you can tilt in your favor by widening your funnel and, far more powerfully, by getting genuinely better.
