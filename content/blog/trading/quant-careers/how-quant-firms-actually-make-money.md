> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Every quant paycheck is funded by one of four engines, and each engine earns a tiny edge an enormous number of times.
>
> - **Market making** captures the bid-ask spread on millions of fills a day; a fifth of a cent per trade becomes \$200,000 a day at 100 million trades. Its enemy is adverse selection.
> - **Statistical arbitrage** stacks hundreds of weakly-correlated 51-percent bets so the noise cancels and the small edge compounds into a high Sharpe. Its enemy is crowding and decay.
> - **Latency arbitrage** wins races measured in microseconds; the edge is being first, and it is an unending technology arms race.
> - **Systematic alpha** holds predictive signals for days to weeks; the edge is forecasting, and every signal decays as capital and competitors crowd in. Capacity caps how much money it can hold.
> - **The number to remember:** the edge is always tiny. A quant firm is a machine for turning a microscopic, statistical advantage into a fortune by repeating it at industrial scale — and the hardest part is that the edge keeps trying to disappear.

A market maker at Citadel Securities or Jane Street will, on a busy day, trade tens of millions of times. On the overwhelming majority of those trades it earns less than a single cent. Not a dollar. A fraction of a cent. If you watched any one of those trades, you would conclude the firm was wasting its time — the edge is invisible, swamped by fees and randomness and the occasional trade that goes badly wrong. And yet that same firm reports billions of dollars in annual trading revenue, pays new graduates more than senior software engineers at the largest tech companies, and is one of the most profitable businesses per employee on earth.

That gap — between the absurd smallness of the edge on any one trade and the enormousness of the aggregate profit — is the single most important thing to understand about this industry. It explains the comp. It explains why the firms are secretive. It explains why they hire people who can do mental math in their sleep and write lock-free C++ in their other sleep. And it explains the career: your job, whatever your title, is to find, sharpen, and defend one of these tiny edges, and to keep it alive against an entire industry of brilliant people trying to compete it away.

This post is the business-model layer of the series. We will not derive any pricing models or solve any interview problems — those live in the [math](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/probability-spaces-random-variables-math-for-quants) and [interview](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) sibling series, and we will link out as we go. Here we answer a more basic question that almost nobody explains honestly: **where does the money actually come from?** There are four main engines, and Figure 1 is the map.

![A six-column comparison grid of the four engines — market making, statistical arbitrage, latency arbitrage, systematic alpha — against edge source, holding period, daily volume, main risk, and the archetype firm that runs each](/imgs/blogs/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money-1.png)

Read that grid top to bottom and you can already see the shape of the industry. Market making earns the spread on a torrent of short-lived quotes and lives in fear of adverse selection. Statistical arbitrage earns mean reversion on thousands of small bets and lives in fear of crowding. Latency arbitrage earns the right to be first and lives in a permanent technology arms race. Systematic alpha earns a forecasting edge held for days and lives in fear of capacity limits and overfitting. Same underlying logic — tiny edge, huge repetition — four very different businesses, four very different kinds of people, four very different ways to lose.

Two characters will help us keep score across the post. **Maya** is a math undergrad who wants to be a trader; she thinks in expected value and probability. **Wei** is a CS PhD who wants to be a researcher; he thinks in signals, data, and code. By the end you will understand not just how each engine works, but which one is the right fit for a Maya and which for a Wei — because the engine a firm runs dictates the people it hires.

## Foundations: spread, edge, volume, and the law of large numbers

Before the engines, the vocabulary. If you are new to markets, build these definitions slowly — every dollar figure later in the post is assembled from these primitives.

**The bid, the ask, and the spread.** At any moment, for any tradable thing — a stock, an option, a Treasury future — there is a highest price someone is willing to *buy* at (the **bid**) and a lowest price someone is willing to *sell* at (the **ask**, or offer). The ask is always higher than the bid; the difference is the **bid-ask spread**. If a stock is bid \$99.99 and offered at \$100.01, the spread is two cents. If you want to buy *right now*, you pay the ask (\$100.01). If you want to sell right now, you receive the bid (\$99.99). The spread is the toll for the convenience of trading immediately.

**A market maker** is the entity quoting both of those prices at once. It stands ready to buy at \$99.99 and to sell at \$100.01, simultaneously, all day. If one person sells to it at \$99.99 and another buys from it at \$100.01 a moment later, the market maker has bought low and sold high without taking a view on where the price is going. It pocketed the two-cent spread — minus costs — for providing liquidity to both sides. That spread, captured over and over, is the first engine.

**Edge per trade.** "Edge" is the expected profit on a single transaction, measured *before* you know how it turns out. A market maker's gross edge is roughly half the spread (it captures the full spread only when it gets both sides; on average it captures about half). A statistical-arbitrage bet's edge is the small expected return of a position that is, say, 51 percent likely to move your way. Edge is always an *expectation* — the average over many repetitions — never a guarantee on any one trade. This is the single most important reframing in the whole industry: a quant does not predict outcomes, a quant has a positive expected value and the patience to let it play out.

**Turnover and volume.** **Volume** is how many trades, or how much notional, you do in a period. **Turnover** is how fast you cycle your capital — a market maker might turn its entire book over hundreds of times a day; a systematic fund might hold a position for two weeks. High edge per trade plus low volume can make the same money as tiny edge per trade plus colossal volume. The four engines sit at different points on that trade-off, and that point determines almost everything else about the business.

**P&L.** Profit and loss — the money the strategy actually made. For one trade, P&L is the realized outcome (which has randomness). Over many trades, **expected P&L ≈ edge per trade × number of trades**. That product is the heartbeat of every quant business, and the entire industry is a contest over its two factors: push the edge up, push the volume up, and keep both from collapsing.

**Sharpe ratio.** The single most-used number in quant trading. The **Sharpe ratio** is your average return divided by the standard deviation (the "wobble") of that return, annualized. It measures return *per unit of risk*. A strategy that makes 10 percent a year with wild swings is worse than one that makes 8 percent smoothly. A Sharpe of 1 is decent; a Sharpe of 2 is very good; the best market-making and stat-arb books run Sharpes of 4, 6, even higher — not because any single trade is reliable, but because they do so many weakly-related trades that the noise averages out. We explore exactly how to compute and trust this in [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research); here, just hold the intuition: **Sharpe rises with the number of independent bets.**

**The law of large numbers.** This is the mathematical engine under all four business models. If you flip a slightly biased coin once, the result is mostly luck. Flip it ten thousand times and the *average* converges to the bias with almost no wobble. A quant firm is a machine for flipping a slightly biased coin millions of times. The bias is the edge; the flips are the volume; the convergence is why a firm with a real edge makes money with boring, almost mechanical, regularity even though any single trade is a coin flip. Every engine below is a different way to manufacture a biased coin and flip it at scale. (The formal version — random variables, variance, and convergence — is in [probability spaces and random variables](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/probability-spaces-random-variables-math-for-quants).)

**Capacity.** The amount of money a strategy can deploy before its own size eats its edge. A great signal on \$10 million might be a worthless signal on \$10 billion, because moving that much capital pushes prices against you and because, at scale, others notice and copy you. Capacity is the ceiling on how big each engine can get, and it is why a fund's *return* and its *assets under management* (AUM) pull in opposite directions.

**Adverse selection.** The reason market making is hard rather than free money. When you quote a price and someone trades against it, *they chose to*. Sometimes they trade for innocent reasons (they need cash, they are rebalancing). But sometimes they trade because they *know something you do not* — they have faster data, or news, or a better model — and they only hit your quote when it is stale and about to move against you. You are "adversely selected": filled exactly when you least want to be. Managing adverse selection is the core skill of market making, and we will give it its own figure later.

With that vocabulary, the four engines are just four different answers to one question: *what is your biased coin, and how do you flip it without getting picked off, crowded out, or scaled into the ground?*

## Engine 1: Market making — capturing the spread at scale

![A five-stage pipeline of the market-making loop: quote both sides, get filled, manage inventory, skew the quote, re-quote, looping back to quote](/imgs/blogs/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money-2.png)

The market maker's job is to quote a bid and an ask on a huge number of instruments, continuously, and to capture the spread between them. Figure 2 shows the loop. You post a bid at \$99.99 and an ask at \$100.01. Someone hits your bid — now you own a share you did not want, bought at \$99.99, and you are "long inventory." You manage that inventory (hedge it, or skew your quotes to attract a seller-balancing buyer), then you re-quote and the loop spins again. Round trip after round trip, you collect roughly half the spread on each fill, net of the trades that go against you.

The genius and the difficulty both live in inventory. A market maker does **not** want to bet on direction. The moment a fill leaves it long or short, it is exposed to the price moving — which is exactly the risk it is trying to avoid. So the entire craft is getting flat again cheaply: hedging in a correlated instrument, or **skewing** the quote (shading both prices down a touch to make a buyer more likely and a seller less likely) so the next fills push you back toward zero. A good market maker is rarely holding a big position; it is constantly cycling through small ones and harvesting the spread on the cycle.

**Where the edge comes from.** The spread exists because liquidity has value. A pension fund that needs to buy a million shares *now* cannot wait around for a natural seller; it pays the spread for immediacy, and the market maker that supplies that immediacy earns it. The edge is not a prediction about price — it is a fee for a service (providing a continuous two-sided market) plus a rebate the exchanges sometimes pay to firms that post liquidity. This is genuinely productive: tighter spreads from competitive market makers lower trading costs for everyone.

**Who runs it.** The biggest pure market makers are **Citadel Securities** and **Jane Street** in the United States, and **Optiver**, **IMC**, **SIG (Susquehanna)**, and **Virtu** with deep roots in listed options and European derivatives. Jane Street is famous for ETF and options market making and for an interview style built around exactly the EV-under-pressure thinking the job requires — the [market-making games](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) you will face in their loop are a direct simulation of the desk. Optiver and IMC are famous for the [mental-math gauntlet](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund) because a market maker has to re-price hundreds of instruments in their head as the market moves.

#### Worked example: a market maker's daily unit economics

Maya wants to know how a fraction of a cent becomes a real business, so she builds the unit economics from scratch. The firm makes a market in a liquid stock, quoting a two-cent spread. On a typical fill it captures about half the spread gross — call it **one cent per trade**. But it gets picked off by faster, informed traders on some fills, and it pays exchange fees and clearing costs. Suppose adverse selection plus costs eat **0.8 cents** of that gross cent on average. The **net captured edge is 0.2 cents per trade** — a fifth of a cent.

A fifth of a cent is a rounding error. So the firm scales the volume:

- At **100,000 trades a day**: \$0.002 × 100,000 = **\$200 a day.** A hobby.
- At **1 million trades a day**: **\$2,000 a day.** A small desk's lunch money.
- At **10 million trades a day**: **\$20,000 a day.**
- At **50 million trades a day**: **\$100,000 a day.**
- At **100 million trades a day**: \$0.002 × 100,000,000 = **\$200,000 a day** — roughly **\$50 million a year** from one liquid name, on an edge of a fifth of a cent.

Figure 3 plots exactly this. The orange line — the edge per trade — never moves; it sits flat at 0.2 cents. The blue bars — daily P&L — climb in lockstep with volume. The entire business is contained in that picture: **you do not make the edge bigger, you make the coin flip more often.**

![A bar chart with a flat orange edge line at 0.2 cents and rising blue daily P&L bars from 200 dollars at 100K trades to 200,000 dollars at 100 million trades](/imgs/blogs/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money-3.png)

*The market maker's profit is volume × a microscopic edge, so the whole business is an exercise in doing a tiny, reliable thing an industrial number of times without letting the edge erode.*

That is why market makers obsess over volume, latency, breadth (more instruments = more flips), and above all over keeping that 0.2-cent net edge from sliding to zero as adverse selection grows. Notice how fragile it is: if costs and pick-off rose from 0.8 to 1.0 cents, the net edge would be *zero* and the \$50-million-a-year name would make nothing. The edge is not a comfortable margin; it is a knife's edge defended by speed and skill.

## Engine 2: Statistical arbitrage — many small mean-reverting bets

If market making earns a service fee, statistical arbitrage earns a *statistical tendency*. The classic version is **pairs trading**: two similar stocks — say two large banks — usually move together. When they temporarily diverge (one drifts up relative to the other for no fundamental reason), you bet they will converge: short the rich one, buy the cheap one, and profit when the gap closes. Any single such bet is barely better than a coin flip — maybe 51 percent likely to work. But you run *hundreds* of them, across pairs, sectors, factors, and events, and the math of aggregation does the rest.

![A stacked figure showing four small noisy 51-percent bets and a buried single bet at the bottom, all averaging up into one smooth high-Sharpe aggregate book at the top](/imgs/blogs/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money-4.png)

Figure 4 is the whole idea in one picture. At the bottom, a single bet: its edge is real but buried in variance — you genuinely cannot tell, from one trade, whether you have an edge or are just gambling. Stack hundreds of *weakly-correlated* bets, though, and the idiosyncratic noise of each one partially cancels against the others. What survives the cancellation is the small shared edge, now riding on top of a vastly smaller aggregate wobble. The result, at the top, is a smooth, high-Sharpe return stream built entirely out of bets that were individually unreliable.

**Where the edge comes from.** Mean reversion and relative value. Prices overshoot, liquidity demands push things temporarily out of line, index rebalances force mechanical flows, and the statistical-arbitrage book bets on the snap-back. The edge per bet is tiny and the win rate is barely above half, but the bets are numerous and diversified, so by the law of large numbers the aggregate is reliable. Crucially, the bets must be **weakly correlated** — if all your "independent" bets are really the same bet in disguise (all long tech, say), the noise does not cancel and your Sharpe collapses. Finding genuinely diversifying signals is the researcher's core job, and it is the subject of [building an alpha signal](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research).

**Who runs it.** The multi-strategy pod shops — **Citadel**, **Millennium**, **Point72**, **Balyasny** — are the cathedral of stat arb. A "pod" is a portfolio manager with their own book, their own researchers, and a tight risk budget; the firm runs dozens or hundreds of pods, each capturing different small edges, and diversifies *across pods* exactly as a pod diversifies across bets. We dig into the pod structure in [the firm archetypes](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund). This is Wei's natural habitat: it is a research business, not a speed business.

#### Worked example: 100 bets at 51 percent and the Sharpe it buys

Wei wants to see why a barely-better-than-a-coin edge is worth building a firm around. Take one bet: it wins **\$1** with probability **0.51** and loses **\$1** with probability **0.49**. The expected value per bet is:

\$1 × 0.51 − \$1 × 0.49 = **\$0.02** — two cents of edge on a one-dollar bet.

The *risk* on one bet is the standard deviation of the outcome. With a roughly even win/lose of ±\$1, the standard deviation is about **\$1** (the variance is approximately 1 − 0.02² ≈ 1). So the Sharpe of one bet — edge divided by wobble — is about **0.02 / 1 = 0.02**. Hopeless. You could not tell that edge from zero in a lifetime of single bets.

Now run **100 weakly-correlated bets** at once. If they were perfectly independent, the *total* expected profit scales with the number of bets (100 × \$0.02 = **\$2.00**), but the *total* wobble scales with the square root of the number of bets (√100 × \$1 = **\$10**). The Sharpe of the portfolio of 100 bets is:

\$2.00 / \$10 = **0.20** — ten times the single-bet Sharpe.

Push to **2,500 weakly-correlated bets**: edge ≈ 2,500 × \$0.02 = **\$50**, wobble ≈ √2,500 × \$1 = **\$50**, Sharpe ≈ **1.0** per round of bets. Stack several such rounds across a year and you reach the Sharpe-of-several territory the best stat-arb books actually run at. The magic number is the **√N** in the denominator: profit grows like N, risk grows only like √N, so **Sharpe grows like √N**. Double your number of *independent* bets and your risk-adjusted return rises by about 41 percent — for free, with no improvement to any individual bet.

*A 51-percent edge is worthless once and a fortune ten thousand times, because the edge adds up linearly while the noise only grows with the square root — which is the entire reason stat-arb firms hire armies of researchers to find more uncorrelated bets rather than one better bet.*

The catch, foreshadowing the "where it breaks" section: that √N magic assumes the bets stay *weakly correlated*. In a crisis, correlations snap to one — every "diversified" bet becomes the same bet (everyone is forced to sell) — and the √N denominator collapses back toward N. That is precisely when stat-arb books blow up, and it is why risk management, not signal generation, is what separates the survivors.

## Engine 3: Latency arbitrage and HFT — being first

The third engine does not earn a service fee or a statistical tendency. It earns the right to **act before anyone else can**. When a price changes on one exchange, there is a brief window — now measured in nanoseconds to microseconds — before that information has propagated everywhere and everyone has updated. The firm that sees the change first and reacts first can trade against quotes that are, for an instant, stale. That is **latency arbitrage**, and the broader family of speed-driven strategies is **high-frequency trading** (HFT).

The canonical example is the **trade race**. A large buy order lands on Exchange A and lifts the price there. On Exchange B, there are still resting sell orders at the old, lower price — but only for a few microseconds, until those sellers (or the firms providing those quotes) react to the move on Exchange A and cancel. Dozens of HFT firms now race to either grab those stale offers on B (a taker) or cancel their own stale quotes before they get picked off (a maker defending itself). The race is won by whoever's signal-to-decision-to-order path is shortest, and it is decided by physics: the speed of light in fiber, the length of the cable, the latency of the network card, the cycles in the matching logic.

This is why HFT firms spend fortunes on **co-location** (renting rack space inside the exchange's own data center, so your server is feet rather than miles from the matching engine), **microwave and millimeter-wave networks** (radio through air is faster than light through glass fiber, so firms build line-of-sight towers between Chicago and New Jersey), **FPGAs and custom hardware** (so the trading logic runs in silicon, not software), and **kernel-bypass networking** (so packets skip the operating system). The edge is purely relative: you do not need to be fast, you need to be *faster than the next firm*. It is a permanent arms race with no finish line.

**Who runs it.** **Jump Trading**, **Hudson River Trading (HRT)**, **Virtu**, **Tower Research**, and **DRW** are the names most associated with the deepest low-latency work, along with the HFT arms of Citadel Securities and others. These firms are, structurally, technology companies that happen to trade — "engineering excellence drives everything," as HRT puts it. They hire systems engineers, hardware specialists, and C++ experts who can reason about the CPU cache and the network stack, which is why their loops lean hard on [C++ for low-latency](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) depth and systems design rather than on derivatives math. This is the one engine where neither Maya's EV instincts nor Wei's research instincts are the headline skill — it is an engineer's game.

#### Worked example: the dollar value of being one microsecond faster

Maya wants to know whether shaving a microsecond is worth the millions a firm spends to do it. Consider a recurring trade race: a profitable opportunity (a stale quote worth, on average, **\$50** of edge if you win it) appears **20,000 times a day**. Several firms race for it. Whoever is fastest takes it.

Suppose that today your firm and one rival are evenly matched, so you each win **50 percent** of the races. Your daily take from this race is:

0.50 × 20,000 × \$50 = **\$500,000 a day.**

Now you invest in a faster network path and a tuned FPGA, and it makes you reliably **one microsecond faster** than the rival. In a race that is decided by who arrives first, one microsecond is often decisive — say it lifts your win rate from 50 percent to **80 percent**. Your daily take becomes:

0.80 × 20,000 × \$50 = **\$800,000 a day** — an extra **\$300,000 a day**, roughly **\$75 million a year**, bought by a single microsecond.

That is why a firm will happily spend tens of millions of dollars and years of engineering on infrastructure that, to an outsider, sounds insane — a dedicated microwave tower to save a few hundred microseconds between two cities. The payoff is not linear in speed; it is a **step function around the win threshold.** Being second by a microsecond pays nothing. Being first by a microsecond pays everything in that race. The whole economics of HFT is the brutal, winner-take-most nature of that step.

*Speed is not a luxury in this engine, it is the entire edge: the firm that is one microsecond slower does not earn a slightly smaller profit, it earns roughly nothing, because in a race there is no prize for second place.*

The flip side — and the honest part — is that this arms race is deflationary. As more firms get faster, the win margins shrink, the races get more crowded, and the edge per race falls. HFT revenue across the industry has compressed over the last decade precisely because the arms race works: everyone gets fast, so being fast stops being special, and the firms keep spending just to stand still.

## Engine 4: Systematic alpha — predictive signals held longer

The fourth engine is the one most people picture when they imagine "quants": building a **predictive signal** — a model that forecasts which assets will outperform over the next days or weeks — and trading on the forecast. Unlike market making (which takes no view) and unlike pure stat arb (which bets on short-term reversion), systematic alpha takes a genuine *directional view* derived from data: fundamentals, alternative data, price patterns, supply-chain signals, satellite imagery, anything that carries information about future returns.

The unit of work here is the **alpha** — a signal whose predictions are, on average, weakly correlated with future returns. The strength of that correlation is measured by the **information coefficient (IC)**: the correlation between what the signal forecast and what actually happened. A *good* equity alpha has an IC of maybe 0.03 to 0.05 — meaning its forecasts explain a *tiny* sliver of what stocks actually do. That sounds pathetic, and on any one stock it is. But applied across hundreds of names and held for the right horizon, a 0.05 IC is a real, tradable edge — once again, the law of large numbers turning a faint signal into a reliable return. We unpack IC, Sharpe, and turnover together in [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research).

**Where the edge comes from.** Information that the rest of the market has not yet fully priced. A systematic fund's research process — hypothesize, get data, [backtest carefully](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/building-an-alpha-signal-quant-research), guard against overfitting, paper-trade, go live, monitor — is a factory for finding such signals and combining many of them into a portfolio. The holding period (days to weeks) is much longer than the other engines, which means less dependence on raw speed and more dependence on the *quality of the prediction* and the discipline of the research.

**Who runs it.** **Two Sigma** (about \$70 billion AUM as of recent reports), **D.E. Shaw** (about \$65 billion), **AQR**, **WorldQuant** (the [alpha-factory model](/blog/trading/quant-careers/what-is-a-quant-really-the-taxonomy-of-roles)), and the systematic arms of the big multi-strats. These are research-scientist cultures, heavy on machine learning, distributed computing, and publish-and-rigor norms. This is Wei's deepest fit — a place where a PhD's research instincts and a long feedback loop are the whole point.

#### Worked example: alpha decay and the capacity that caps your AUM

Wei has found a signal. It launches with an information coefficient of **0.05** — genuinely good. He wants to know two things every researcher must: how long will it last, and how much money can it hold?

**Decay.** No alpha lives forever. As the firm trades on it, the firm's own buying nudges prices toward fair value (eroding the mispricing the signal exploited), and other firms discover the same edge and pile in. Suppose the signal's live IC has a **half-life of 12 months** — it loses half its strength every year. The signal only pays as long as its IC stays above the **cost floor**: the level of predictive power below which the profit no longer covers the trading costs of harvesting it. Suppose that floor is an IC of about **0.012**. Then the signal is alive only until:

IC(t) = 0.05 × (1/2)^(t / 12) = 0.012 → t ≈ **25 months.**

After roughly two years, this signal is *dead* — its IC has decayed to the cost floor and it earns nothing net of costs. Figure 5 draws exactly this curve: the blue IC line decaying from 0.05, the red cost floor at 0.012, and the amber marker where they cross at about 25 months. The green region is the signal's profitable life; the red region is where it has become a money-loser you must turn off.

![A decay curve showing information coefficient falling from 0.05 with a 12-month half-life, crossing a red cost floor at 0.012 around 25 months, with the profitable region shaded green and the dead region red](/imgs/blogs/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money-5.png)

*Every alpha is a melting ice cube: it earns from the day you discover it until the day crowding and your own footprint push its predictive power down to the cost of trading it, so a systematic firm must constantly discover new signals just to replace the ones decaying to zero.*

**Capacity.** Now the second question. The signal's edge is fixed, but trading *more* money on it costs *more* in market impact — pushing prices against yourself as you build the position. Past a certain size, the extra impact cost exceeds the extra edge, and your *net* return per dollar starts falling. That size is the **capacity**. Concretely: if a signal nets, say, 8 percent on \$200 million but only 3 percent on \$2 billion (because the impact of moving \$2 billion eats most of the edge), then the *dollar* profit is \$16 million versus \$60 million — more total money, but a far worse return, and beyond some point even the *dollar* profit peaks and then falls. Worse, crowding *lowers* capacity over time: as Figure 5's annotation notes, more AUM and more competitors steepen the decay, so the same signal holds less money next year than this year.

This is the central tension of a systematic fund and the reason their returns and their size fight each other: **the more money you raise, the lower your return per dollar, and the faster your signals decay.** It is why the very best systematic shops sometimes *return capital* to investors rather than dilute their edge — a move that looks insane to outsiders and is perfectly rational once you understand capacity.

## Why the engine dictates the firm and the hire

![A holding-period timeline from microseconds to weeks, mapping latency arb (Jump, HRT, Virtu), market making (Citadel Securities, Jane Street), statistical arbitrage (Citadel, Millennium), and systematic alpha (Two Sigma, DE Shaw) onto their time horizons](/imgs/blogs/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money-6.png)

Stand back and one variable organizes the entire industry: **holding period.** Figure 6 lays the four engines on a single time axis, from microseconds on the left to weeks on the right, and the firms sort themselves cleanly along it. The faster the engine, the more it lives or dies on *technology and the spread*; the slower the engine, the more it lives or dies on *prediction and research*. That single fact dictates the org chart and the hire.

**Microsecond engines (latency arb / HFT) hire engineers.** When your edge is being first, your bottleneck is the network card, the FPGA, the cache line, the matching-engine round trip. Jump and HRT are, organizationally, elite systems-engineering shops; the trader and the researcher exist, but the hardware and software engineers are the spine. The [four paths](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund) post details how different "quant developer" means here versus elsewhere. For Maya, this is not the lane; for an engineering-minded candidate, it is the deepest technical bar in finance.

**Second-to-minute engines (market making) hire fast, calibrated risk-takers.** When your edge is the spread and your enemy is adverse selection and inventory, you need people who price hundreds of instruments in real time, size risk under pressure, and stay calibrated when the market moves. That is why Jane Street, Optiver, and SIG interview with [market-making games](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews), poker, and mental-math sprints — they are auditioning for the exact reflexes the desk demands. This is Maya's home: it rewards the EV-under-uncertainty instinct directly.

**Hours-to-days engines (statistical arbitrage) hire researchers under tight risk.** When your edge is the aggregate of thousands of weak bets, you need an army of researchers finding *uncorrelated* signals and a fierce risk apparatus keeping the book diversified and within drawdown limits. The pod structure at Citadel and Millennium is purpose-built for this: many small books, each a researcher-plus-PM, diversified at the firm level. The hire is a quantitative researcher who can find a real signal and, just as importantly, *kill a fake one* — the skill we cover in [evaluating alpha signals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research).

**Days-to-weeks engines (systematic alpha) hire research scientists.** When your edge is prediction held over a longer horizon, the bottleneck is the quality and originality of the signal and the rigor of the research that validates it. Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw hire PhDs into research-scientist roles with long feedback loops, heavy ML, and publish-grade rigor. This is Wei's deepest fit. Notice that a PhD is *common but not required* on the faster engines and *common and often expected* here — the engine, not the industry, sets the credential.

The practical upshot for your career: **do not ask "how do I get into quant," ask "which engine fits how I think."** A market-making desk and a systematic-research seat are as different as a fighter pilot and a research chemist, even though both are "quant." Match the engine to your temperament — speed and calibration, or breadth and risk, or systems engineering, or deep research — and you will both interview better and be happier once in. The [taxonomy of roles](/blog/trading/quant-careers/what-is-a-quant-really-the-taxonomy-of-roles) maps each engine to the day-to-day job.

## Where it breaks: adverse selection, crowding, decay, capacity

Every engine has a built-in failure mode, and the senior version of understanding this business is understanding how each one dies.

**Market making breaks on adverse selection.** Recall the definition: you get filled exactly when you least want to be, by someone who knows more than you. Figure 7 makes this concrete. Two kinds of flow hit your quote. **Uninformed flow** — retail, index rebalancers, people trading for liquidity reasons unrelated to your fair value — pays you the spread, because they are as likely to be right as wrong. **Informed flow** — a faster firm, someone with news, a better model — only trades against you when your price is stale and about to move *against* you. From the uninformed you keep the half-spread; from the informed you take a loss that can dwarf many spreads' worth of profit.

![A four-node branching graph showing uninformed flow and informed flow both hitting a market maker's quote, splitting into the maker winning the spread from uninformed flow and losing to informed flow that picks it off](/imgs/blogs/how-quant-firms-actually-make-money-7.png)

The whole game of market making is managing that mix. If the informed fraction of your flow rises — because you are too slow, your model is stale, or a faster competitor is in front of you — your net edge slides from 0.2 cents toward zero and then negative. This is why market makers *widen their spreads* (charge more) when volatility spikes or news is imminent: they are demanding more compensation for a moment when more of the flow is likely to be informed. It is also why **speed is defensive as well as offensive** — being fast lets you cancel a stale quote before the informed trader picks it off. The failure mode is not exotic; it is the everyday tax that turns a one-cent gross edge into a fifth-of-a-cent net edge, and it is one bad day from turning the net edge negative.

**Statistical arbitrage breaks on crowding and correlation snap.** The √N magic that makes the engine work assumes the bets stay weakly correlated. They do not, in two situations. First, **crowding**: when many firms discover and trade the same signals, the mispricings get arbitraged away faster, the edge per bet shrinks, and the "diversified" bets all start being the *same* crowded bet. Second, the **correlation snap** in a crisis: when everyone is forced to deleverage at once (think the August 2007 "quant quake," when a sudden unwind of similar stat-arb books caused them all to lose together for several days before snapping back), every supposedly-independent bet becomes one bet — *get me out* — and the diversification that justified the leverage evaporates exactly when you need it. The √N denominator collapses toward N and the book takes a loss far larger than the model said was possible.

**Latency arbitrage breaks on the arms race itself.** The edge is purely relative, so it is self-eroding: every firm's investment in speed devalues every other firm's. The races get tighter, the win margins thinner, and the capital expenditure to stay competitive keeps rising while the revenue per race keeps falling. The failure mode is structural — you can do everything right and still watch your edge compress because the whole field got faster. The honest reality, reflected in the broad decline of industry HFT revenues over the past decade, is that this is a treadmill: you spend more each year to earn the same or less.

**Systematic alpha breaks on decay and capacity.** As the worked example showed, every signal melts, and your own size accelerates the melt. A systematic firm is in a permanent race to discover new alpha faster than its existing alpha decays — a kind of treadmill of ideas rather than of hardware. And capacity caps how large the whole enterprise can be: raise too much money and your returns degrade and your decay speeds up, which is why the discipline of *killing dead signals* and even *returning capital* is a survival skill, not a luxury.

The unifying lesson: **the edge is never safe.** Every one of these businesses is being actively competed against by other brilliant, well-capitalized firms, and the natural state of any edge is to shrink toward zero. The job — every quant's job, at every level — is not to find an edge once. It is to find edges faster than the market destroys them, and to manage risk so that the day the diversification fails does not end the firm. That is the real content of "the job is a probabilistic edge, and so is keeping it."

## Common misconceptions

**"It's just gambling."** A casino and a quant firm are opposites in one decisive way: the casino *is* the house and the quant firm is *trying to be* the house. A gambler bets with negative expected value and hopes variance is kind. A quant firm bets with *positive* expected value — a real edge — and uses the law of large numbers to convert that edge into near-certain profit *in aggregate*, while ruthlessly managing the variance so no single loss is fatal. The resemblance is superficial (both involve odds and money); the substance is opposite (one has the edge against it, the other has built a machine to harvest a tiny edge in its favor millions of times). If anything, a well-run quant firm is *less* exposed to luck on any given year than a discretionary stock-picker, precisely because it diversifies across so many independent bets.

**"They can predict the market."** No engine here predicts *the market* in the sense of knowing where the S&P 500 will close. Market making takes *no* directional view at all — it is deliberately agnostic about direction and just harvests the spread. Latency arb does not predict; it *reacts* faster to information that already exists. Stat arb predicts only short-term *relative* reversion with a 51-percent edge, not absolute direction. Even systematic alpha, the most prediction-like engine, runs on an information coefficient of 0.05 — its forecasts are *barely* correlated with outcomes, and it makes money only by being slightly right across hundreds of names, not by being confidently right about any one. The fantasy of a quant who "knows" where the market is going is exactly backwards: the entire industry is built on having a *weak* edge and the scale and discipline to monetize it.

**"Spreads are free money."** Figure 7 is the rebuttal. The spread is gross revenue, not net profit. Adverse selection — getting picked off by informed flow — plus exchange fees, clearing costs, and the cost of hedging inventory all eat into it, and on a bad name or a volatile day they can eat all of it. The one-cent gross spread in our worked example netted a fifth of a cent *after* adverse selection and costs, and that fifth of a cent is what the firm fights to defend with speed, breadth, and risk control. Market making looks like free money only to people who have never been on the wrong side of an informed trade.

**"More data always wins."** Data has sharply diminishing returns, and more data is also more opportunity to *overfit* — to find patterns in the noise that look like signals in a backtest and vanish in live trading. A systematic researcher's hardest skill is not acquiring data; it is *not fooling themselves* with it. The discipline of out-of-sample testing, purged cross-validation, and deflating your Sharpe for the number of things you tried — covered in [overfitting and deflated Sharpe](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/evaluating-alpha-signals-ic-sharpe-turnover-quant-research) — exists precisely because raw data plus a flexible model plus a researcher who *wants* a result is a reliable recipe for a beautiful backtest that loses money live. The firms that win are not the ones with the most data; they are the ones most ruthless about killing their own ideas.

**"One genius with one great idea built the firm."** The engines are all law-of-large-numbers businesses, which means they are *portfolios* of edges, not single bets. No single signal, no single trader, no single strategy is the firm. Stat-arb shops run hundreds of pods; systematic funds run hundreds of decaying signals; market makers quote thousands of instruments. The robustness comes from breadth and diversification, not from one irreplaceable insight — which is also, incidentally, why these firms can survive the departure of star individuals far better than outsiders expect.

## How it plays out at real firms

Map the four engines onto the real landscape and the industry stops looking like a monolith.

**The pure market makers.** **Citadel Securities** and **Jane Street** are the two giants of US market making; **Optiver** (founded 1986, Amsterdam, about 2,100 employees), **IMC** (founded 1989), **SIG / Susquehanna** (founded 1987, 3,000-plus employees), and **Virtu** round out the field, with deep strength in listed options and ETFs. Their interviews — mental-math sprints, market-making games, poker at SIG — are direct simulations of the spread-capture-under-pressure job. Comp reflects the productivity: as reported on levels.fyi in 2025, a Jane Street quant trader's base is around \$300,000 with averages above that and 90th-percentile figures past \$500,000, and new-grad on-target totals across top market makers commonly land in the \$450,000–\$650,000 range. Remember the honesty mandate: those are the survivors' numbers, the bonus is the variable lever, and a strong year is not the median.

**The HFT / low-latency shops.** **Jump Trading** (founded 1999, Chicago), **Hudson River Trading** (founded 2002, NYC), **Virtu**, **Tower Research**, and **DRW** are the latency-arbitrage spine of the industry. Structurally they are technology companies — HRT's "engineering excellence drives everything" is the literal pitch — and their interviews lean hardest on C++ depth, systems design, and an understanding of hardware and networks. Five Rings and Jane Street lead H1B base disclosures at around \$300,000; Citadel Securities sits near \$257,000. The work is secretive and the comp is high because the edge — being first — is worth a microsecond's fortune, as the race worked example showed.

**The multi-strat pod shops.** **Citadel** (founded 1990 by Ken Griffin), **Millennium**, **Point72**, and **Balyasny** run the statistical-arbitrage engine at scale through the pod model: a portfolio manager owns a book, has a tight risk budget, and is funded as long as they perform — a sharp-elbowed, high-accountability culture. Note the deliberate split inside Citadel: the **pod hedge fund** (Citadel) and the **market maker** (Citadel Securities) are *different businesses with different hiring*, running different engines (stat arb versus spread capture). Mid-level pod-shop QR comp around four years of experience has been reported near \$575,000 (roughly \$175,000 base plus \$400,000 bonus), with the explicit caveat that the bonus tracks P&L and does not repeat automatically.

**The systematic research funds.** **Two Sigma** (founded 2001, about \$70 billion AUM, roughly 2,000 staff), **D.E. Shaw** (founded 1988, about \$65 billion AUM), **AQR**, and **WorldQuant** run the systematic-alpha engine. These are research-scientist cultures — heavy ML, distributed computing, long feedback loops, publish-grade rigor — where a PhD is common and the hire is judged on the originality and rigor of their research. WorldQuant's distinctive twist is the **alpha-factory** model: a distributed network of researchers each producing formulaic alphas on the firm's BRAIN platform, a literal industrialization of the "many small edges" idea.

**The wider ecosystem.** Beyond the headline names sit DRW, Tower, Five Rings, Akuna, Old Mission, Radix, XTX Markets, and the systematic desks inside the big banks — most running some blend of the four engines. When you read a firm's name, ask the diagnostic question: *which engine pays its bills?* That single answer tells you the holding period, the dominant risk, the kind of person it hires, and the shape of its interview. A firm that earns the spread will test your speed and calibration; a firm that earns predictive alpha will test your research judgment; a firm that earns the race will test your systems engineering. The engine is the firm.

#### Worked example: pricing your own seat against the engine

Maya has two offers and wants to value them honestly, using the engine logic. Offer A is a market-making seat at a top firm: first-year on-target total around **\$500,000** (base \$300,000 plus a bonus targeted at \$200,000), where the bonus tracks her desk's P&L. Offer B is a systematic-research seat at a quant fund: first-year total around **\$450,000**, more salary-weighted, with a longer feedback loop before her work shows up in comp.

The headline says A wins by \$50,000. But the engine logic reframes it. In the market-making seat, Maya's comp is tied to a *fast* feedback loop — her desk's spread capture this year — so a strong year can push the bonus well above \$200,000 and a weak year well below it; her variance is high and her edge shows up *now*. In the research seat, Maya's signals may take a year or more to reach production and P&L, so her year-one comp is more guaranteed but her *upside* is back-loaded — a successful signal compounds into future bonuses. If Maya is a fast, calibrated EV thinker who will thrive on a market-making desk, Offer A's higher expected value *and* better fit make it clearly +EV for her. If she is secretly a Wei — happier on a long research loop — the nominally lower Offer B may be higher EV *for her* once you account for the probability she succeeds in each seat.

*The right way to price a quant seat is not the headline number but expected value conditioned on fit: the engine you are best suited to run is the one where your personal probability of capturing the edge is highest, and that probability, not the offer letter, is what compounds over a career.*

We treat the full comp picture — base, bonus, sign-on, deferred, and why the headline is not the number — in [the firm archetypes](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund), and the position-sizing intuition behind risking capital on an edge in [the Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews). The same EV-under-uncertainty lens that prices a trade prices a career.

## When this matters / Further reading

Understanding the four engines is the foundation for almost every other decision in a quant career. It tells you which firms to target (match the engine to how you think). It tells you what each interview is really testing (speed and calibration for the spread, research judgment for alpha, systems depth for the race). It tells you why the comp is what it is (the engines are absurdly profitable per employee because they monetize a tiny edge at industrial scale). And it tells you what your actual job will be at every level: finding, sharpening, and defending one of these edges against an industry trying to compete it away.

The thread that runs through all four engines — and through this whole series — is that **the job is a probabilistic edge, and the edge is never safe.** A fifth of a cent, a 51-percent bet, one microsecond, an information coefficient of 0.05: every engine is a way of taking a microscopic, fragile advantage and repeating it enough times that the law of large numbers turns it into a fortune, while managing risk so the inevitable bad day is survivable. Internalize that and the rest of the industry — the secrecy, the comp, the hiring bar, the obsession with risk — stops looking mysterious and starts looking inevitable.
