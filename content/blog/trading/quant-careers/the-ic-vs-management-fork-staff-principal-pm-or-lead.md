> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Every senior quant eventually hits a fork: keep going deep as an *individual contributor* (staff/principal researcher, a senior trader carrying ever-bigger risk, a principal engineer) or move into *management* (team lead, head of desk, a portfolio manager running others' capital). The thesis of this post: in quant, the deep IC track is a *first-class, often higher-paying* path — choose it deliberately, do not get defaulted onto the wrong one.
>
> - **There are two ladders, not one.** The career runs junior to senior as a shared trunk, then forks. The IC prong (staff, then principal) and the management prong (lead, then head/director) are *peers in level and comp* — not a ladder where you must climb into management to be senior.
> - **In quant, the IC track can out-earn management** — the opposite of big tech. Comp tracks P&L contribution and impact, not headcount managed; a staff trader or principal researcher in a strong seat can match or beat a head of desk at the same level. The deep path is not the consolation prize.
> - **The day jobs are genuinely different.** An IC defends long blocks of deep work; a manager lives in people-time, meetings, and interrupts; a portfolio manager (PM) is a hybrid who owns a book *and* a team and splits the day in two. Pick the *day* you want, not just the title.
> - **The one trap to avoid:** being promoted into management *because you were a good IC*. That can halve your deep-work time, let your edge rust, and — in this industry — sometimes *lower* your pay. The fork is a choice you make on purpose, re-examined yearly, not a default the firm picks for you.

Maya gets the email on a Tuesday. Her head of desk wants fifteen minutes; the calendar invite is titled, ominously, "your growth." She has spent four years becoming one of the two or three best researchers on the floor — the person whose backtests other people trust, the one who found the regime filter that saved the flagship strategy a brutal quarter last year. The meeting, it turns out, is an offer: the desk is growing, they want to create a new pod, and they want Maya to *lead* it. Three reports to start, maybe six within a year. It is, everyone agrees, a promotion. Her boss says the word "trajectory" twice.

And Maya, to her own surprise, does not want it. She loves the work — the hours where she is alone with the data and a hard question and the world goes quiet. She has watched the leads on her floor spend their days in 1:1s and hiring loops and cross-team meetings, and the thought of that being *her* day fills her with a low dread. But she cannot shake a second fear, the one that actually keeps her up: *if I say no to this, am I telling them I have no ambition? Does turning down management cap my career here? Is "no" a career-limiting move?*

It is not. That is the whole point of this post, and it is the single most expensive misconception in the industry. Down the same hallway sits Wei — the same cohort, the same raw talent — who has quietly realized the *opposite*: that what energizes him is precisely the part Maya dreads. He likes unblocking people. He likes that a good architectural decision he makes ripples into ten other people's systems. He is, although nobody has said it yet, a manager who hasn't been given a team. Two excellent quants, two different forks, and the firm's job — and theirs — is to get each of them onto the *right* ladder rather than the default one. Here is the picture that contains the whole argument.

![The IC ladder and the management ladder shown side by side across day, compensation, skills rewarded, lifestyle, and ceiling, with the deep IC track able to out-earn most managers](/imgs/blogs/the-ic-vs-management-fork-staff-principal-pm-or-lead-1.png)

Look at the two columns and notice what is *not* there: there is no arrow from the left column to the right one. They are not stages. They are not a hierarchy. They are two parallel ladders that branch from the same trunk and climb to the same heights — different days, different skills, different lifestyles, comparable comp. In most of the corporate world the right column quietly sits above the left, and "individual contributor" is where you wait until you are allowed to manage. Quant is one of the few industries where that is genuinely false, because of *how the money is made*. The rest of this post earns that claim: it defines both ladders from zero, walks the day-to-day and the comp and the lifestyle of each, shows the PM as a hybrid that carries both, gives you a self-assessment to find your own fork, and — most importantly — shows you how not to get shoved onto the wrong one by a well-meaning boss who confuses "good at the job" with "should manage."

## Foundations: the two ladders and why quant is different

Before we can talk about choosing, we have to define the words, because almost every bad career decision at this fork comes from leaving them undefined. Five ideas carry the whole post: **individual contributor (IC)**, **management**, the **dual ladder**, the **level equivalence** between the two, and the **portfolio manager (PM)** as a hybrid. Define them cleanly and the choice becomes legible.

**Individual contributor (IC).** An individual contributor is someone whose value comes primarily from work they do *themselves* — the research they produce, the risk they manage, the systems they build — rather than from directing other people. The word "individual" is misleading: senior ICs collaborate constantly, mentor heavily, and set technical direction for whole teams. What makes them an IC is that *they are not on the hook for managing people's careers, performance, and headcount* — they are on the hook for an outcome they primarily drive with their own hands and judgment. A senior researcher who owns the firm's flagship strategy, a trader carrying a nine-figure book, a principal engineer who owns the trading path: all ICs, none of them managers, all of them senior.

**Management.** A manager's value comes from the *output of the people they are responsible for*. A manager is accountable for a team's results, but they achieve those results indirectly — through hiring, coaching, prioritizing, removing obstacles, resolving conflicts, and making the calls that span more than one person's work. The manager's own hands are off the keyboard more and more as they climb; their leverage is entirely in other people. At a quant firm the management titles run roughly **team lead → head of desk → director / head of trading or research**, and the higher you go the more the job is people and strategy and the less it is the craft you came in doing.

**The dual ladder.** A dual ladder (sometimes "dual-track" or "the IC track") is a career structure in which a firm offers *two parallel ladders of advancement* — one for ICs, one for managers — that reach the same levels of seniority, influence, and pay. The point of a dual ladder is to let a brilliant practitioner keep advancing *without* being forced to manage. The IC ladder typically runs **senior → staff → principal** (sometimes with a "distinguished" or "fellow" tier above that at the largest firms); the management ladder runs **lead → head → director → and up**. The two are explicitly designed to be *peers*: a staff IC and a team lead are the same "level"; a principal and a head of desk are the same level. The level equivalence is the part that gets forgotten, so let us make it concrete.

![The dual ladder shown as a shared junior to senior trunk that forks into an IC prong of staff then principal and a management prong of lead then head, with the portfolio manager as a bridge that carries both](/imgs/blogs/the-ic-vs-management-fork-staff-principal-pm-or-lead-2.png)

**Level equivalence.** This is the map that defuses Maya's fear. Junior and senior are a *shared trunk* — everyone climbs it the same way, by going from "solves the problem given" to "finds and owns the problem worth solving," which is exactly the move the [companion post on what senior actually means](/blog/trading/quant-careers/what-senior-actually-means-at-a-quant-firm) is about. At the senior line, the ladder forks. Go up the IC prong and the rungs are **staff** (you own a major area deeply and others build on your work) and then **principal** (your judgment shapes the firm's direction in your domain, you are one of a handful of people whose technical call settles an argument). Go up the management prong and the rungs are **team lead** (your first reports, you are accountable for a small group's output) and then **head / director** (you run a desk or a function, you own headcount and budget). A staff IC ≈ a team lead in level and comp. A principal ≈ a head of desk in level and comp. *You do not give up rank or money by staying IC* — you give up managing people, which for many of the best quants is a feature, not a cost.

**The portfolio manager (PM) as a hybrid.** Sitting across the fork is a role that confuses everyone because it is genuinely both: the portfolio manager. A PM — most prominently at multi-strategy pod shops like [Citadel](/blog/trading/quant-careers/owning-pnl-and-owning-research-the-accountability-ladder), Millennium, Point72, Balyasny — *runs a book of capital* (a deeply IC act: the P&L is theirs, the trades are their call) *and usually runs a small team* of researchers and traders supporting that book (a management act). The PM is not "a senior trader with a bigger title" and it is not "a manager who used to trade." It is its own thing: an owner of risk who also leads the people who help them deploy it. We will give the PM its own section, because the hybrid is where a lot of the highest comp in the industry lives, and also where a lot of people burn out.

Now the part that makes quant *different* from almost every other knowledge industry, and the reason this post exists at all.

**Why the deep IC path can out-earn management here.** In most of big tech, a manager out-earns an IC at the "same" level once you get senior enough, because the org chart is the value chain: the bigger your span of control, the more value flows through you, and comp follows span. Quant inverts this for a simple structural reason: **value is measured in P&L and in research impact, and both are directly attributable to individuals.** A trader's book makes money or it does not, and the firm can see exactly whose book it was. A researcher's signal lifts the strategy's Sharpe or it does not, and the firm can attribute the lift. When value is *individually attributable*, the firm can — and to retain its best people, *must* — pay an IC for the value they personally generate, even if they manage nobody. A principal researcher whose signal makes the firm forty million dollars a year is worth a fraction of that to the firm whether or not they have a single report. A head of desk is worth what their *desk* makes, which can be more or can be less. The result, reported consistently across levels.fyi, Glassdoor, and the 2026 "Young & Calculated" quant-pay survey, is that **at top quant firms the senior IC and senior management bands overlap heavily, and the strongest individual seats — a top trader, a flagship researcher, a star PM — sit at the very top, above most managers.** That is the structural fact Maya needs to internalize before her Tuesday meeting. Saying "no" to management is not stepping off the up-escalator. In quant there are two up-escalators, and one of them does not require her to stop doing the thing she is best at.

With the words defined, let us walk each track as it actually feels day to day — because the comp overlap means the *real* decision is about the day, the skills, and the lifestyle, not the money.

## The IC track: staff, principal, and the senior who carries more

Start with the track Maya wants and does not yet know she is allowed to want. The IC track at a quant firm has three faces, depending on which of [the four paths](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer) you came up through: the **staff / principal researcher**, the **senior trader carrying ever-bigger risk**, and the **principal engineer**. They differ in what they own, but the shape is the same: you go *deeper*, your bets get *bigger*, and your influence spreads through your *work* rather than through reports.

### The staff / principal researcher

A staff researcher owns a research area the way a senior trader owns a book. They are not handed questions; they decide which questions are worth the team's scarcest resource, which is research time. They own a strategy or a family of signals from idea through to live P&L, and the firm's results move with their judgment about what to pursue and — just as importantly — what to kill. The principal researcher is the next rung: their methodological standards become the team's standards, their call on "is this edge real or is it an overfit ghost" settles arguments, and they are one of a handful of people whose research taste the firm is, in effect, buying. This is exactly the discipline the research series develops in [killing your own ideas](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/quant-research-writeup-killing-ideas) and in honest out-of-sample testing — at the principal level it becomes the standard the whole desk inherits, which is leverage without a single direct report.

**What you do all day:** read, frame hypotheses, wrangle data, build signals, run honest backtests, review other researchers' work and kill the doomed ideas cheaply, and defend or retire your own. The day is long stretches of deep, quiet, skeptical work, punctuated by the research-review meetings where ideas live or die.

**Comp:** tied to the impact of the research you drive — the P&L your signals contribute, weighted by how attributable that lift is to you. In a strong seat at a top systematic fund, a principal researcher's total comp sits squarely in the senior bands we will chart below; the very best, whose work underpins a flagship, are paid like the top of the firm, manager or not.

**Lifestyle:** the most protected deep-work life of any senior role in the industry. You guard your focus blocks; meetings are kept to a minimum; your worst interruption is usually a research review you chose to attend. The cost is that your influence is bounded by what you and the people who voluntarily adopt your standards can do — you cannot *direct* anyone.

### The senior trader carrying ever-bigger risk

On the trading path, the IC track is not a different title so much as a *bigger limit*. A junior trader manages a small slice under tight supervision; a senior IC trader carries a real book, owns a meaningful risk limit, and the firm's monthly P&L moves with their sizing decisions. Going "deeper" as an IC trader means being trusted with *more risk* — a larger limit, more products, more discretion — because the firm has watched your judgment and decided to put more of its money behind its estimate of you. This is the [accountability ladder](/blog/trading/quant-careers/owning-pnl-and-owning-research-the-accountability-ladder) made literal: rising as an IC trader *is* being trusted with a larger consequence.

**What you do all day:** watch the screens, manage the book, decide how big to be, when to add, when to cut, when to do nothing while everyone panics. The judgment that defines you is the risk feel — sizing to the regime, holding a good trade through noise, the discipline not to revenge-trade a loss.

**Comp:** the most directly P&L-linked of any role. A senior IC trader's bonus is a function of the book's contribution. In a strong year and a strong seat, this is where the eye-watering numbers in the industry come from — and, honestly, where they vanish in a weak year, because the bonus does not repeat automatically.

**Lifestyle:** intense during market hours, with real adrenaline and real stress, but with a clean boundary: when the close comes, the book's risk is what it is. You are not carrying twelve people's career anxieties home.

### The principal engineer

On the developer path, the IC track runs senior → staff → principal engineer, and the senior-most engineers at firms like [Jump and HRT](/blog/trading/quant-careers/jump-and-hrt-playbook-the-low-latency-systems-bar) frequently manage nobody while owning the systems the entire firm depends on — the trading path, the research platform, the risk system, the latency budget. A principal engineer's call on an architectural decision is the decision; ten developers build on top of it for years. At firms where "engineering excellence drives everything," the principal engineer is paid like — and respected like — the most senior trader, because a millisecond shaved off the critical path or an outage prevented is worth as much as a good trade.

**What you do all day:** design systems, make the trade-off calls that compound across years and across many users, review the changes that touch production, and set the patterns the team reuses. Less typing than a junior, more deciding.

**Comp:** at the top low-latency and systematic firms, principal engineers sit in the senior bands alongside traders and researchers — engineering is not a support function here, it is the edge, and it is paid like the edge.

**Lifestyle:** deep-work-heavy, with the specific stress of being the person whose system *cannot* go down during market hours. On-call is real; the 3 a.m. page exists. But the day is still mostly building, not managing.

The common thread across all three IC faces: **your hands stay on the craft, your bets get bigger, and you influence others through your work and your standards rather than through authority over their careers.** For a lot of the best quants, that is precisely the deal they want. Now the other ladder.

## The management track: lead, head of desk, and the people business

Wei's ladder. The management track is a genuinely different *job*, not a more senior version of the same one — and that is the thing most people who default into it never quite grasp until they are six months in and quietly miserable or, occasionally, delighted to discover they love it.

### The team lead: your first reports

The first rung is team lead: you go from being responsible for *your* output to being responsible for a *small group's* output — typically three to six people. Overnight, your success metric changes. It is no longer "did I produce great work"; it is "did my people produce great work, and did I make that more likely." You run 1:1s, you set priorities, you remove blockers, you give feedback, you mediate the friction that any group of ambitious people generates, and you start participating in hiring loops and performance reviews. The single biggest shock for a new lead from an IC background is that **the work that used to feel like the job now has to happen in the scraps of time between people-obligations** — and a lot of new leads grieve the loss of their deep-work blocks before they make peace with the trade or decide it was a mistake.

**What you do all day:** people. 1:1s, coaching, unblocking, prioritizing, cross-team coordination, hiring. The deep technical work shrinks to maybe an eighth of the day, often in fragments.

**Comp:** tied to the team's total output, not your personal P&L. At the lead level this is roughly *peer* to a staff IC — sometimes a touch more, sometimes a touch less, rarely a step-change up. The myth that "manager = more money" is, at this rung in quant, simply false.

**Lifestyle:** you become the queue. Your calendar is other people's needs. The interruptions are the job, not a distraction from it. If context-switching drains you, this is a hard adjustment; if you find energy in other people's progress, it is the best job you have ever had.

### The head of desk / director: running the business

The next rung up is head of desk or director: you now run a desk or a function — multiple teams, a budget, headcount decisions, the desk's strategy, and accountability for its aggregate results to the firm's leadership. The craft is now almost entirely behind you; your value is in the quality of your hiring, the clarity of your strategy, the strength of the people you have built, and your judgment about where to point the desk's resources. You are managing managers, or managing a large group of senior people, and your wins are measured in quarters and in the careers you have grown, not in a backtest or a day's P&L.

**What you do all day:** strategy, hiring at scale, managing managers, representing the desk to the firm, making the resource-allocation calls. Almost no hands-on craft.

**Comp:** scales with the size and success of what you run. A head of a large, profitable desk can be paid at the very top of the firm — but note the *conditionality*: that comp is tied to the desk's results, which depend on many people, and it scales with *span*, so it grows by growing the org. This is the one place where the big-tech intuition partly returns: bigger org, bigger comp. But it does not dominate the top IC seats the way it does in tech.

**Lifestyle:** the most meeting-dense, most political, most context-switched life in the building — and, for the right person, the most rewarding, because the leverage is enormous. One good head of desk makes a hundred people more effective.

The honest summary of the management track: **you trade the craft for leverage through people, your day becomes other people's needs, and your comp tracks the team you build rather than the work you personally do.** Whether that is a promotion or a punishment depends entirely on who you are — which is exactly why the fork must be a choice. Before we get to choosing, the hybrid.

## The PM hybrid: running a book and a team at once

The portfolio manager deserves its own section because it is the role that breaks the clean IC-vs-management split, and because it is where a remarkable amount of the industry's highest comp concentrates — along with a remarkable amount of its burnout.

A PM at a multi-strategy pod shop owns a *book of capital*: a risk allocation from the fund that they deploy according to their strategy, and whose P&L is theirs, cleanly attributable, marked daily. That is the most IC thing in finance — the trades are the PM's call, the drawdown is the PM's drawdown, the up-or-out is brutally direct (a pod that breaches its drawdown limit is often shut down, the way the [pod-shop accountability model](/blog/trading/quant-careers/owning-pnl-and-owning-research-the-accountability-ladder) makes explicit). *And* the PM usually runs a small team — a few researchers and traders and maybe a dedicated developer — who help them find and deploy the edge. That is management: hiring those people, directing them, owning their growth and their friction.

So the PM is genuinely both, and the day reflects it. Roughly: a large chunk on the book itself — sizing, risk, the actual trading decisions — and a meaningful chunk on the team — what to research next, who to hire, how to keep the analysts productive. During market hours the PM is in IC mode, risk-on, screens up; around the edges they are in manager mode. The split is the job.

**Comp:** the PM is paid on a *payout* of their book's net P&L — a percentage, after costs and the fund's cut. In a strong year on a large, well-performing book this is the highest comp in the industry, full stop, and it scales with the book, not with headcount. In a weak year it can be zero or negative-trending-toward-shutdown. The PM seat is the highest-variance, highest-ceiling comp on the map, and it sits *across* the fork because it pays you as an owner of risk (IC) while requiring you to lead a team (management).

**Lifestyle:** intense, high-pressure, high-variance. You carry the book's risk *and* the team's needs. The PM seat is not a softer version of either track; it is the demanding union of both, which is why it pays what it pays and why it burns out the people who took it without wanting both halves.

The reason the PM matters to *this* post is that it is the natural destination for the person whose honest answer to "do you want to go deep or lead people" is *both* — and the trap is taking it when your honest answer is really one or the other. A pure-IC trader who is pushed into a PM seat to "build a pod" can find that managing the team is the part they resent. A natural manager who takes a PM seat for the comp can find they hate carrying the personal P&L risk. The PM is a first-class destination, not a default — same as the other two. Now let us put the comp claim on a chart, because the numbers are the thing that frees Maya to choose.

## How the comp actually compares across tracks

Everything above asserts that the IC track can match or beat management in quant. Here is that claim as a picture, using the reported bands from the series data appendix — flagged, as always, as illustrative midpoints of wide, bonus-driven, survivorship-biased ranges.

![Grouped bars comparing total compensation by track at the senior five-year mark and the principal eight-year mark, showing the deep IC track matching or beating management with the PM hybrid highest and widest](/imgs/blogs/the-ic-vs-management-fork-staff-principal-pm-or-lead-3.png)

Read the chart at the level of its *shape*, not its exact heights — the error bars are wide on purpose, because the honest truth is that comp here is enormously seat- and year-dependent. At the senior / five-year mark, the staff IC, the team lead, and the PM cluster together around the ~1M USD-midpoint band that survivors reach; the IC bar is *level with or slightly above* the manager bar, and the PM's is a touch higher and much wider. At the principal / eight-year mark the same pattern holds and widens: the principal IC sits in the same band as the head of desk, and the PM's bar stretches highest of all because a strong book pays like nothing else. The annotation says the load-bearing thing: **IC matches or beats management at the same level.** That is the structural fact, and it is the opposite of what the big-tech-trained reader expects. Let us make it concrete with the first worked example.

#### Worked example: comp on each track at the same level

Three quants are all at the same firm, all at the senior / five-year mark, all strong performers. Use illustrative midpoints from the reported bands (levels.fyi / Glassdoor / "Young & Calculated" 2026, as of 2026):

- **Anita, staff researcher (IC).** Base \$250,000, bonus \$750,000, total **\~\$1.0M**. Her bonus is a function of the research impact attributable to her — the Sharpe lift her signals gave the strategy. She manages nobody.
- **Ben, team lead (management).** Base \$275,000, bonus \$675,000, total **\~\$0.95M**. His bonus tracks his team's aggregate output. He has four reports and spends most of his week on them.
- **Carlos, PM (hybrid).** Base \$200,000, plus a payout on his book's net P&L. In *this* year his book made the fund \$22M net, and his payout works out to a bonus of \$900,000, total **\~\$1.1M**. He runs a book *and* a three-person pod.

Notice what is true and what is not. It is *not* true that Ben, the manager, out-earns Anita, the IC — they are within a rounding error of each other, and in a strong research year Anita could clear Ben easily. It *is* true that Carlos, the PM, edges both — but his number is the *most* conditional: had his book lost money, his payout could be zero and his seat at risk, while Anita's and Ben's downside in a flat year is "a smaller bonus," not "shut down." The shape to remember: **at the same level, IC and management comp overlap almost completely, and the PM trades a higher ceiling for a higher variance.** *In quant, choosing to stay IC does not cost you money — so the choice should be made on the day and the work, not on a pay gap that, unlike in big tech, mostly is not there.*

That is the fact that should set Maya free. Now the part that should make her *careful* — because while the comp won't punish her choice, a *default* into the wrong track absolutely can.

## How to tell which fork fits you

The comp overlap means the decision reduces to a single honest question: **which day do you want?** Not which title sounds more impressive, not which one your parents will understand, not which one the org chart implies is "higher." Which set of activities makes the hours vanish, and which set drains you? The cleanest way to surface that is to look at how the three tracks actually spend a day, side by side.

![A grid comparing how an IC, a manager, and a PM spend a typical day across deep work, people time, meetings, interrupts, and what success feels like, with the IC mostly deep work the manager mostly people and the PM split](/imgs/blogs/the-ic-vs-management-fork-staff-principal-pm-or-lead-4.png)

Read down the columns and the choice stops being abstract. The IC spends roughly seventy percent of the day in deep-work blocks they actively defend, ten percent on ad-hoc mentoring, ten percent in meetings, and lives with *low* interruption because they guard their focus — and success *feels like* a signal or a system they built with their own hands. The manager inverts every row: deep work collapses to maybe fifteen percent and arrives in scraps, people-time becomes the *core forty-five percent of the job*, meetings and hiring eat thirty percent, interruption is constant because they *are* the queue — and success feels like their people shipping wins. The PM splits the difference, with real deep work on the book *and* real people-time on the team, and the specific stress of high interruption during market hours.

There is no right column here. There is only *your* column. If reading the IC column made you exhale and reading the manager column tightened your chest, you have your answer — and vice versa. The test is not "which sounds prestigious," it is "which day would I choose for the next five years of my life." Most people know within a few seconds of an honest read; the ones who do not are usually candidates for the PM hybrid, or for trying a small management responsibility on for size before committing. Here is the decision as a flow.

![A pipeline showing the decision flowing from what energizes you to the matching track to the deliberate signal that keeps you off the wrong path with a yearly re-check because the fork is not permanent](/imgs/blogs/the-ic-vs-management-fork-staff-principal-pm-or-lead-5.png)

The flow starts where it must: with the honest question of what work makes the hours vanish. If the answer is *depth* — solving the hard problem yourself, the quiet focus — it points to the IC track, and the action is to aim for staff and then principal and to *grow your scope*: ask for the bigger book, the harder mandate, the area nobody else can own. If the answer is *people* — others winning because of you, the energy of unblocking a team — it points to the management track, and the action is to ask to lead a project, then a small team. The crucial node is the last one before the loop: **signal your choice deliberately.** A firm that does not hear you want the IC track will, by default, assume your good work means you want to manage — and that default is the trap. And the final node closes the loop: re-check yearly, because the fork is *not* permanent. People move from IC to management when they discover they love leading; people move from management back to IC when they realize they miss the craft, and in quant that move is not a demotion or a pay cut. Let us look hard at the trap, because it is the most expensive mistake at this fork.

#### Worked example: a self-assessment that scores you toward IC or management

Score yourself honestly on each of these, one point to whichever column your gut picks. No fence-sitting — if you genuinely cannot pick, that row is a point for "PM hybrid."

1. My best day at work was the day I **(a)** cracked a brutal problem alone / **(b)** got the whole team unblocked.
2. Meetings feel like **(a)** a tax on my real work / **(b)** where a lot of the real work happens.
3. I get energy from **(a)** depth and uninterrupted focus / **(b)** people and constant context-switching.
4. I want my name on **(a)** the signal or system itself / **(b)** the team that shipped it.
5. The hardest skill I *want* to build next is **(a)** deeper technical edge and judgment / **(b)** hiring, coaching, leadership.
6. Five years out I picture myself as **(a)** a principal owning a flagship / **(b)** a head running a desk.

Suppose Wei scores it: 1(b), 2(b), 3(b), 4(b), 5(b), 6(b) — a clean **6 toward management**. There is no ambiguity; Wei should be a lead, and the firm not giving him the chance is the waste. Suppose Maya scores it: 1(a), 2(a), 3(a), 4(a), 5(a), 6(a) — a clean **6 toward IC**. Her dread in the Tuesday meeting was *data*, not cowardice. Now suppose a third person, Devi, scores 1(a), 2(b), 3(a), 4(b), 5(a), 6(b) — a genuine **3-3 split**: she loves the craft *and* loves leading. Devi is the PM-hybrid candidate, the person for whom the book-plus-team role is not a compromise but the actual fit. *A clear majority is your fork; a true tie is itself an answer, and it usually points at the hybrid rather than at "flip a coin."* The score is not destiny — it is a mirror, and most people are startled by how lopsided their honest answers are.

## Avoiding the default-promotion trap

Here is the single most important idea in this post, and the reason it had to be written. The most common way a great quant ends up on the wrong ladder is not a deliberate bad choice. It is a *default*: they were excellent at the IC job, so their boss — out of genuine goodwill, and out of a corporate reflex inherited from industries where management is the only way up — offers them a management role as the reward for being good. The promotion *feels* like recognition. And for the person whose honest self-assessment leans IC, accepting it is one of the most expensive mistakes they will make in their career. The trap has a name in the wider industry — the "Peter Principle," promoting people to the level of their incompetence — but in quant it has a specific, measurable cost. Here it is in one picture.

![A before and after comparison contrasting the default promotion trap where a strong IC is pushed into management and loses deep work with a deliberate choice that keeps the person on the track that fits](/imgs/blogs/the-ic-vs-management-fork-staff-principal-pm-or-lead-6.png)

The left column is the trap, traced through its consequences. The *reason* for the promotion is "you were the best IC, so you should manage" — which is a non-sequitur, because being a great researcher and being a great manager of researchers are nearly unrelated skills. The first consequence is mechanical: deep work falls from roughly seventy percent of the day to roughly fifteen percent, because the manager day *is* people-time. The second is slower and worse: the edge that made you valuable — the research taste, the risk feel, the systems intuition — *atrophies*, because it was a perishable skill maintained by daily practice, and you have stopped practicing. The third is the one nobody warns you about: your comp can *fall*, because you have traded a P&L-linked or impact-linked IC bonus for a team-output-linked management bonus, and at the lead level those are at best peers and at worst a step *down* if your IC seat was a strong one. And the final consequence is the cruelest: the firm gets a *worse manager* (because you did not want the job and were not selected for the skill) *and loses a great IC* (because that seat is now empty and your edge is rusting). It is lose-lose, dressed up as a promotion.

The right column is the deliberate choice. The reason is "you chose the work you love." Deep work is protected if you stay IC. Your edge *compounds* instead of rusting. Your comp tracks the impact you actually have, on whichever track fits. And the result is the right person on the right ladder — which is the only outcome that is good for both you and the firm. The difference between the columns is not talent or luck; it is whether the choice was *made* or *defaulted*.

#### Worked example: the cost of taking a lead role that halves your deep-work time

Put numbers on the trap. Maya, the star researcher, is offered the lead role. Suppose she takes it against her instincts. Before: as a staff researcher she spent roughly 70% of a ~50-hour week — about **35 hours** — in deep research work, and that work was directly responsible for, say, **\$3.5M/year** of attributable strategy P&L (her regime filter and the signals she maintains). After the promotion to lead: deep-work time falls to ~15% of the week, about **7.5 hours** — a *fifth* of what it was. Her personal research output does not fall by 80% immediately, but over a year, as her edge rusts and her attention fragments, her *personal* attributable P&L realistically drops toward **\$1.0M/year** — a loss of about **\$2.5M/year** in the thing she was uniquely good at.

Against that, what does the firm gain from her management? She now leads four researchers. If she were a *great* manager, lifting each of their outputs by, say, 15%, and each generates ~\$2M/year, that is `4 × \$2M × 0.15 = \$1.2M/year` of added team output. But she is an *untested, reluctant* manager who took the job by default — assume she lifts the team by a more realistic 5%, or `4 × \$2M × 0.05 = \$0.4M/year`. Net effect of the default promotion: the firm *loses* roughly `\$2.5M − \$0.4M = \$2.1M/year` of value, and Maya spends her days doing work that drains her. Now compare the deliberate alternative: keep Maya as a principal-track IC and give the lead role to **Wei**, whose honest self-assessment screamed management. Wei, *selected for the skill and wanting it*, plausibly lifts his team by the full 15% — `+\$1.2M/year` — while Maya's \$3.5M of IC value stays intact and grows. The deliberate allocation is worth on the order of **\$3.3M/year more** than the default one. *The default-promotion trap is not a soft "culture" problem — it is a multi-million-dollar misallocation, every year, hiding inside a kind gesture.* (Figures illustrative — round numbers to show the shape of the cost, not a quote from any desk.)

So how do you *not* fall into it? Three concrete moves. **First, signal early and explicitly.** Long before the management offer comes, tell your manager — in a growth conversation, in a review — "my goal is the staff/principal IC track; I want to go deeper, not into management." A firm with a real dual ladder will respect that and route your growth accordingly; a firm that *cannot* hear it is telling you something important about whether it actually values ICs. **Second, when the offer comes, you are allowed to say no — and ask for the IC equivalent instead.** "Thank you, but I want to keep going deep. Can we talk about the staff/principal path and a bigger mandate instead?" In quant, with the dual ladder real and the comp overlapping, that is a *legitimate, ambition-signaling* answer, not a white flag. **Third, if you are genuinely unsure, try before you commit.** Ask to lead a project, or mentor formally, or run a small initiative — a reversible taste of management — before you accept an irreversible-feeling org change. Many people discover in a low-stakes trial that they love it, or hate it, and either discovery is worth far more than guessing. The next figure is the self-assessment as a rubric you can actually run on yourself.

![A matrix rubric scoring six traits each toward a leans IC column or a leans management column with a near even split itself signaling the PM bridge](/imgs/blogs/the-ic-vs-management-fork-staff-principal-pm-or-lead-7.png)

Run the rubric honestly and the lean is usually obvious within a minute. The point of putting it in a grid is to force a *per-trait* answer rather than a vague overall feeling — because the overall feeling is exactly where status and other people's expectations sneak in and corrupt the choice. Score each row to the column your gut actually picks, count, and trust the majority. And remember the meta-rule the flow figure ended on: this is a yearly re-check, not a tattoo.

## How to choose your fork

Pull the threads together into a procedure you can actually follow, because "know thyself" is true but useless without steps.

**Step one: separate the comp question from the fit question, and then ignore the comp question.** We have established it: in quant the IC and management bands overlap, and the PM trades ceiling for variance. So comp does *not* break the tie. Take it off the table entirely and decide on fit. The number of people who chose management "for the money" and found there was no extra money *and* a job they disliked is the saddest cohort in this industry.

**Step two: read the day.** Look again at the typical-day grid. Picture your next Monday on each track in concrete detail — the calendar, the interruptions, what you touch with your own hands, what "a good day" would mean. Your body usually answers before your résumé does.

**Step three: run the rubric.** Six traits, one point each, count the lean. A clear majority is your fork. A genuine tie points at the PM hybrid or at a low-stakes trial.

**Step four: signal it, deliberately and early.** Tell your manager which ladder you want *before* a default offer forces the question. Make your ambition legible on the track you actually want. For an IC, that means asking for scope — a bigger book, a harder mandate, the area no one else can own — so that "ambitious" and "IC" are visibly the same thing in your manager's mind. For a would-be manager, it means raising your hand to lead.

**Step five: re-examine yearly.** You are not deciding once forever. The fork is reversible in both directions, and in quant — uniquely — moving from management back to deep IC is not a demotion and not a pay cut. People grow into wanting to lead; people grow back into wanting the craft. Check in with yourself each year: *is this still the day I want?* If the answer changes, the ladder you are on can change with it.

#### Worked example: Maya chooses IC, Wei chooses management

Back to the Tuesday meeting. Armed with all of the above, Maya runs the procedure. The comp question: she checks the bands and confirms that staying IC, on the principal track, pays her in the same league as the lead role — there is no money on the table to chase. The day: she pictures the lead's Monday (four 1:1s, two hiring loops, a cross-team sync, research squeezed into the gaps) and her chest tightens; she pictures the principal's Monday (a long morning alone with a hard regime question, an afternoon research review where she kills two weak ideas and sharpens a good one) and she exhales. The rubric: 6 toward IC. So she walks into the meeting and says: "Thank you — genuinely. But the work I want to do is deeper, not broader-across-people. I want the principal track. Can we talk about a bigger mandate — owning the whole volatility research direction — instead of a team?" Her head of desk, who runs a real dual ladder, says yes, because a principal who owns the firm's flagship research direction is worth *more* to the desk than a reluctant lead of four. Maya's edge keeps compounding; her comp keeps tracking her impact; her "no" cost her nothing.

Wei runs the same procedure and gets the mirror image. Comp: a wash, same as Maya. The day: the lead's Monday energizes him — he *likes* the 1:1s, he has been informally unblocking people for a year and it is the most satisfying part of his week. The rubric: 6 toward management. So when the same desk needs a lead for the new pod, Wei raises his hand for it — and is *selected for the skill he actually wants to build*, not defaulted into it because he was a good IC. A year later the new pod is humming under Wei, Maya owns the volatility research direction as a principal, and the desk has two people doing the job each was built for. *The fork is not about who is more senior or who earns more — Maya and Wei are peers on both counts. It is about which day each of them wanted, made explicit and chosen on purpose rather than assigned by default.* That deliberate split — the same self-knowledge applied to a career that a good quant applies to a trade — is the senior move this whole track is about.

## Common misconceptions

The fork is surrounded by folklore imported from industries where it works differently. Name the myths and they lose their grip.

**Myth 1: "Management is the only way up."** This is the big-tech and general-corporate intuition, and in quant it is simply false. The dual ladder is real here, and the IC prong (staff → principal) reaches the same levels, the same influence, and the same comp as the management prong (lead → head). At the very top, the strongest IC seats — a flagship researcher, a top trader, a star PM — sit *above* most managers. "Up" has two staircases, and one of them never requires you to manage a soul. Believing this myth is what turns a star IC's reasonable "no" into a panic about a capped career.

**Myth 2: "The IC track caps out — managers have no ceiling."** The reverse of myth 1, and equally wrong. Management comp scales with *span* (the size of the org you run), which feels uncapped, but IC comp scales with *impact* (the P&L or research value attributable to you), which in quant is *also* effectively uncapped — a principal researcher whose work underpins a strategy that makes the firm hundreds of millions is paid accordingly. The PM seat, the most IC-flavored role of all, has the *highest* ceiling in the industry. The IC track does not cap out; it just caps out *differently* — on the size of the bet and the impact you are trusted with, rather than on headcount.

**Myth 3: "A PM is just a senior trader with a bigger title."** No — the PM is a structural hybrid, and missing the management half is how people walk into a role they will resent. A PM owns a book (the IC half) *and* runs a team that supports the book (the management half). A senior IC trader who carries a big book but manages no one is *not* a PM; a PM who stopped trading and only ran the team would *not* be a PM either. The defining feature is carrying personal P&L risk *while* leading people — both at once. If you want one without the other, the PM seat is the wrong fit even though it pays the most.

**Myth 4: "You have to decide once, forever."** The fork is reversible, in both directions, and in quant the reverse direction is not punished. Plenty of people try management, find they miss the craft, and return to a senior IC seat with no loss of level or pay — something that is hard to do gracefully in industries where management is the only "up." Plenty go the other way after years as an IC. The right model is a yearly check-in, not a one-time irreversible vow. Treating it as permanent is what makes the choice feel terrifying when it should feel like a reversible experiment.

**Myth 5: "If you're good, they'll put you on the right track automatically."** The opposite is the default. Left alone, a firm's reflex is to reward a good IC with a *management* offer, because that reflex is baked into how most of the corporate world works. The right track is the one you *signal* and *ask for*. Silence gets you the default, and the default is the trap. Being good is necessary; being *legible about which ladder you want* is what actually routes you correctly.

**Myth 6: "Saying no to management hurts your reputation."** In a firm with a real dual ladder, asking for the IC track instead of management reads as *clarity and ambition*, not as a lack of drive — especially when you pair the "no" with "and I want a bigger IC mandate instead." The only place this myth is true is at a firm with no real IC ladder, in which case the myth is doing you a favor by revealing that the firm does not actually value ICs, which is something you want to know.

## How it plays out in the real world

Strip away the parable and here is what the fork looks like at named firms, with the honest conditionality the [series insists on](/blog/trading/quant-careers/what-senior-actually-means-at-a-quant-firm).

At a **systematic research powerhouse like Two Sigma or D.E. Shaw**, the IC research ladder is deep and prestigious: senior researcher → staff → principal, and the principal researchers whose work underpins flagship strategies are among the highest-paid and most respected people in the building, frequently with zero reports. The management ladder exists in parallel — team leads and heads of research — and is a *peer* track, explicitly not a superior one. A researcher who wants to go deep can spend a whole career going deeper, and the firm is structured to pay them for it because research impact is individually attributable. This is the cleanest dual ladder in the industry.

At a **prop / market-making firm like Jane Street, Optiver, or SIG**, the IC trading ladder is "bigger book, more products, more discretion," and the senior-most traders carry enormous risk while managing few or no people. Engineering at these firms — and especially at the **low-latency houses, Jump and HRT** — has a genuine principal-engineer track where the systems people are paid like the traders, because [the systems *are* the edge](/blog/trading/quant-careers/jump-and-hrt-playbook-the-low-latency-systems-bar). Management exists (heads of desk, engineering leads) but is one option among peers, not the apex. Jane Street's collaborative, anti-hierarchical culture in particular makes the deep-IC path a fully respected default rather than a holding pattern.

At a **multi-strategy pod shop like Citadel, Millennium, Point72, or Balyasny**, the apex role is the **PM** — the hybrid we dissected — and the comp at the top of a strong book is the highest in the industry, with the highest variance and the most brutal up-or-out (breach your drawdown limit and the pod can be shut). Supporting the PMs are deep IC researchers and traders within each pod *and* a management layer that runs the platform, risk, and the business. Here the fork is most vivid: you can be a researcher who never wants their own book (IC), a PM who runs a book and a small team (hybrid), or a business/risk leader who runs the platform (management) — three first-class destinations, three different days.

The conditionality, stated plainly because the [honesty mandate demands it](/blog/trading/quant-careers/owning-pnl-and-owning-research-the-accountability-ladder): every comp figure in this post is a reported, illustrative midpoint of a wide, bonus-driven, survivorship-biased band (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, efinancialcareers, the 2026 "Young & Calculated" quant-pay survey, as of 2026). The people quoting top-of-band IC *and* top-of-band management numbers are the survivors of a severe filter; the bonus that makes a number eye-watering does not repeat automatically, and a strong year is not the median. What *does* hold across all the conditionality is the structural fact this post is built on: **in quant, the deep IC track is a first-class path that pays in the same league as management at the same level, and at the top exceeds it.** That fact is robust to the noise in any individual number, and it is the fact that should free you to choose the fork on fit rather than on a pay gap that mostly is not there.

One more real-world note, on *timing*. The fork usually presents itself somewhere around the senior line — three to six years in for fast movers, depending on the firm and the path. You do not have to have an answer before then; in fact, having strong opinions about management before you have ever led anything is a bit like having strong opinions about a strategy you have never traded. What you *should* do early is keep the option legible: do enough informal mentoring and project-leading to know whether the people-work energizes or drains you, and keep your IC edge sharp enough that staying deep is always a live option. The worst position is to arrive at the fork having quietly defaulted toward management for years — taking every coordination task, drifting away from the craft — and then discover you are on a track you never chose. Arrive at the fork with both doors genuinely open, and the choice is yours to make instead of the org chart's to make for you.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you are good enough that someone offers you a team — and it matters *before* that, in the years when you are quietly accumulating either deep-IC signal or management signal by what you say yes to. The decision is not "manage or stall." It is "two first-class ladders, comparable pay, genuinely different days — which day do you want, and have you said so out loud?" Get that right and the rest of a senior quant career is the [same probabilistic self-knowledge](/blog/trading/quant-careers/what-senior-actually-means-at-a-quant-firm) you would apply to a trade, turned on your own life: read the situation honestly, size your bet on fit not on status, own the outcome, and revisit when the regime changes.

The thesis, one more time: **in quant the deep IC path is first-class and often higher-paying — so choose your fork deliberately, signal it early, and never let a kind boss default you onto the wrong ladder because you happened to be good at the last one.** Maya kept her edge and her ambition by saying a clear "no." Wei got the job he was built for by raising his hand. Same level, same comp, opposite forks — both right, because both *chose*.
