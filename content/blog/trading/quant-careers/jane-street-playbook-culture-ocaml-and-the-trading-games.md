It is a Tuesday afternoon on a video call, and **Maya** — a third-year math undergraduate who has spent two months drilling arithmetic — is playing a game she has never played before. The interviewer says: "I'm thinking of a number. It's the sum of the digits of a random page I'll open in a 600-page book. You're the market maker. Make me a market." Maya's stomach drops, because there is no formula for this. She thinks for four seconds: a page number is roughly uniform on 1 to 600, its digits sum to somewhere around 8 or 9 on average, the spread of outcomes is maybe 3 to 18. She says, "I'm **8 at 10**" — meaning she will *buy* at 8 and *sell* at 10. The interviewer says, "I'll buy 5 from you at 10." Maya now owns a short position: she has promised to deliver 5 units of a number she expects to be about 9. She has just been told something — the interviewer was willing to pay her *ask* — and the only thing being graded is what she does next. Does she freeze and keep quoting 8 at 10, or does she lift her whole market because the flow just told her the truth is probably *higher* than she thought?

That single moment is Jane Street's entire interview philosophy in miniature. The firm is not checking whether Maya knows what an option is. It is watching whether she can put a number on her uncertainty, take a position, read information out of a trade, and update — calmly, out loud, with the clock running. Multiply that by a dozen rounds and you have the loop. This post is the complete candidate's playbook for *that* loop and *this* firm: what Jane Street is, why it interviews the way it does, what its culture actually feels like, the process stage by stage, the trading games with worked expected-value math, the compensation with its honest caveats, and exactly how to prepare. ## Foundations: what Jane Street is and why it interviews the way it does

Before any of the games make sense, you need a small vocabulary and one structural fact about the business. None of it is hard, but it is load-bearing for everything below, so let us build it from zero.

**Jane Street is a proprietary trading firm and a market maker.** Founded in **2000** and headquartered in New York City, it trades its *own* capital — its partners' money and its accumulated profits — with no outside investors and no clients whose money it manages. That single fact ("own capital") shapes the culture, and we will use it repeatedly. The firm is enormous in **ETFs** (exchange-traded funds) and **options**, two markets where its core business is providing liquidity.

**What a market maker does.** A market maker stands ready, all day, to *both* buy and sell an instrument. It posts a **bid** — the price at which it will buy — and an **ask** (or *offer*) — the price at which it will sell. The gap between them is the **spread**. When one trader sells to the firm at its bid and, moments later, another buys from the firm at its ask, the firm has bought low and sold high and captured the spread — *without ever needing a view on which direction the price will go*. Do that across millions of instruments and fills a day and the tiny per-trade edge compounds into serious money. This is why the job is fundamentally about *pricing and risk*, not *forecasting*. A market maker is largely direction-agnostic; if you walk into a Jane Street superday with a thesis about where rates are headed, you have misread the job.

**ETFs and options, briefly, because they explain the skill set.** An **ETF** is a fund whose shares trade on an exchange like a stock, but whose value is tied to a basket of underlying assets (say, 500 stocks). A market maker in ETFs must constantly answer: *what is this basket really worth right now*, given that its components are all moving? That is an arbitrage-and-pricing problem under uncertainty. An **option** is a contract giving the right to buy or sell something at a set price by a set date; its fair value depends on volatility, time, and probability. Both products reward exactly the skill the interview tests: **putting a defensible number on an uncertain quantity, fast.** That is not a coincidence — the games are a stripped-down simulation of the actual job.

**Expected value (EV) — the spine of everything.** The **expected value** of an uncertain payoff is the probability-weighted average of all its outcomes. If a bet pays \$10 with probability 0.5 and loses \$4 with probability 0.5, its EV is `0.5 × 10 + 0.5 × (−4) = \$3`. A trader's entire job, and the entire interview, is to act when EV is positive and the size is right, and to decline when it is not. "Make a market" is just "tell me a price at which both buying and selling have non-negative EV for you, given what you know." When you hear Jane Street described as having an "EV culture," this is the literal meaning. If you want the technique drilled cold, the sibling post on [expected-value techniques for quant interviews](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/expected-value-techniques-quant-interviews) is the place; here we care about why *this firm* built its whole filter around it.

**Calibration.** To be **calibrated** is for your stated confidence to match reality: of all the times you say "I'm 70% sure," you are right about 70% of the time. A calibrated person who says "the answer is between 8 and 10, and I'd bet at those prices" is far more valuable to a market maker than a brilliant person who is overconfident, because an overconfident quote gets *picked off* — traded against precisely when it is wrong. Calibration is the trait the games are secretly measuring. A wide, honest quote beats a narrow, wrong one.

**Edge, P&L, and the spread, in one breath.** A firm's **edge** is the durable reason it makes money — for a market maker, the spread captured at scale, net of *adverse selection* (the risk that whoever trades against you knows more than you do). **P&L** is profit and loss, the dollars a desk or trader makes or loses. Almost every compensation number in this industry is ultimately a function of P&L. Keep these three words handy; they recur in every section.

**Adverse selection — the thing the games quietly test.** This term deserves its own paragraph, because the whole skill of updating after a fill is built on it. When a market maker posts a two-sided quote, two kinds of counterparties trade against it: *uninformed* flow (a pension fund rebalancing, a retail order, someone who just needs liquidity) and *informed* flow (a faster player who has read the news, or who knows the basket is mispriced). Uninformed flow pays the maker the spread and is the bread-and-butter profit. Informed flow only trades when it is *right* — which means the maker gets filled precisely when the price is about to move against the position. A naive maker who never updates gets *adversely selected*: filled disproportionately by the people who know better, and bled dry one fill at a time. The defense is to treat every fill as information and re-price — which is exactly the "update after being hit" skill the trading game grades. When you understand adverse selection, the game stops looking like a math puzzle and starts looking like the actual job: *the counterparty's willingness to trade is itself the most important data point you have.*

**Why a wide quote can beat a narrow one.** New candidates instinctively quote tight markets to look confident. That is backwards. A tight quote that is *miscentered* is a gift to informed flow — it offers a great price on the wrong side. A wider quote sacrifices some volume of profitable uninformed flow but buys protection against being picked off. The right width depends on how uncertain you are and how informed you expect the counterparty to be: against a knowledgeable interviewer who trades only when they have an edge, you should quote *wider* than you would against random flow. The interviewer is watching whether you understand this tradeoff — a candidate who quotes recklessly tight markets reveals they do not yet grasp adverse selection, regardless of how fast their arithmetic is.

So *why* does Jane Street interview with estimation puzzles, betting games, and market-making simulations rather than finance questions or pure LeetCode? Because the job is, at its core, *repeatedly pricing uncertain things and updating when the market pushes back*, and the cheapest high-fidelity test of that skill is a game where you must quote a price on something you cannot possibly know exactly. Finance trivia is learnable in a weekend and tells the firm nothing about how you *think*. A market-making game tells them everything in ten minutes. The firm has gone to real trouble to build a process that strips away pedigree and trivia and exposes how you reason. Your prep should respect that.

## The culture: collaboration, OCaml, puzzles, and training

Jane Street's culture is genuinely distinctive, and understanding it does two things for you: it tells you whether you would *like* the job, and it tells you what the interviewers are quietly checking for beyond raw skill. Figure 5 (reached in the comp section, where it sets up *why* the pay is shaped as it is) stacks the four pillars; let us take them one at a time here.

**Collaboration, and why "own capital" causes it.** Recall the structural fact: Jane Street trades its own money as essentially *one shared book*. There is no setup where each trader runs an independent portfolio and gets fired the instant their personal P&L draws down — that is the *pod-shop* model (Citadel, Millennium), and it produces a sharp-elbowed culture by design. (The sibling post on [the firm archetypes](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund) derives why whose-money-it-is predicts the culture.) Because the *firm's* P&L is the thing being optimized, your incentive is to make the *desk* better, not to hoard an edge. People openly debate ideas, share what they have learned, and carry a teammate through a quiet quarter. This is not a marketing claim; it falls directly out of the capital structure. In the interview, this means the firm is also screening for *whether you are someone people will want to think alongside*. A candidate who is brilliant but combative, or who cannot explain their reasoning, is a worse hire here than at a firm where you trade alone.

**OCaml and the functional-programming culture.** Jane Street famously writes essentially its entire trading system in **OCaml**, a statically-typed *functional* programming language. Most of the industry runs on C++ (for speed) and Python (for research); Jane Street's bet is different. Functional programming emphasizes *immutability* (data that does not change in place) and a powerful *type system* that catches whole classes of bugs at compile time, before the code ever touches a live market. For a firm whose worst-case outcome is a software bug sending bad orders into a real exchange, "the compiler proves my code can't do X" is worth more than shaving the last microsecond — Jane Street is a fast market maker, but it is not a latency-arms-race HFT shop where nanoseconds are the entire edge. The cultural consequence is that *correctness and clear thinking* are prized over clever hacks, and a shared, well-typed codebase lets one carefully-written library serve the whole firm. **You do not need to know OCaml to be hired** — we will demolish that myth explicitly later — but you should understand that the firm's engineering values are "clear, correct, composable," and that a dev or quant-research candidate who writes clean, readable, tested code is speaking the firm's language even in another syntax.

**The puzzle habit.** Jane Street publishes monthly puzzles, sprinkles its recruiting with brain-teasers, and treats estimation and EV games as daily mental reps. This is not whimsy; it is the same muscle the desk uses. A culture that does puzzles for fun is a culture that has internalized "put a number on the uncertain thing and reason carefully," which is exactly the job. For a candidate, the practical takeaway is that *the games are not a hazing ritual you survive and then never see again* — they are a thin slice of the actual daily work, which is reassuring: if you enjoy the interview, you will probably enjoy the job, and if you hate it, that is real information.

**Heavy formal training.** New hires — especially traders — go through months of structured teaching before they own meaningful risk. The firm would rather invest in turning a calibrated, clear-thinking generalist into a trader than hire someone who already "knows finance." This is *why* the interview can afford to ignore finance knowledge: the firm will teach you the finance. It is selecting for the raw substrate — EV reasoning, calibration, clarity, the ability to learn fast — and supplying the domain knowledge itself. Understanding this should change your prep: stop trying to look like a finance person, and start *being* a calibrated, clear, fast probabilistic thinker.

**How the four pillars reinforce each other.** These are not four separate facts; they are one self-consistent system, and seeing the loop helps you read the firm. Own capital removes the incentive to hoard an edge, which *enables* collaboration. Collaboration only scales if everyone can read and safely modify everyone else's code, which is *why* a strongly-typed functional codebase like OCaml is worth more than raw speed — the type system lets a new hire touch a shared library without silently breaking it. A shared codebase and a collaborative desk both reward people who think clearly and explain themselves, which is *why* the puzzle habit and the games-based interview select for exactly that trait. And because the firm hires for the substrate rather than the domain, *heavy training* is the necessary final piece — you cannot hire generalists and then expect them to know finance on day one, so you teach it. Pull any one pillar and the others wobble. For a candidate, the practical consequence is that the firm is internally coherent: the thing that gets you hired (clear EV reasoning, explained out loud) is the same thing that makes you successful on the desk, which is rarer than it sounds and is a genuine reason the firm is a good place to grow rather than just a good place to get paid.

**A word on the lifestyle reality, honestly.** The collaborative, train-you-up culture is real, but it is not a country club. The hours are demanding, especially in the first years; the bar is high and sustained; and the calm-relative-to-a-pod-shop framing is *relative* — you are still in a high-stakes, high-performance environment where underperformance over a long stretch ends seats, just without the month-to-month drawdown-stop-out terror of an independent book. The right way to read "lowest risk of the archetypes" is *structurally lower month-to-month volatility in your job security*, not *low pressure*. If you want the contrast spelled out against the pod-shop and HFT lifestyles, [the firm archetypes](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund) draws the lines.

#### Worked example: a Jane Street market-making game, end to end

Let us make the opening hook concrete with real numbers, because the EV math is the whole point. The interviewer says: *"I'm thinking of the number you get by rolling two fair six-sided dice and summing them. You are the market maker — make me a tight market on the sum, and I'll trade against you."*

First, Maya computes the **true expected value**. The sum of two dice has EV `= 7` (each die averages 3.5). Its distribution is the familiar triangle peaking at 7, ranging 2 to 12. So fair value is **7**.

Maya quotes a market *around* fair value with a spread to protect herself: she says **"6 at 8"** — she will buy at 6, sell at 8. The spread (8 − 6 = 2) is her cushion against being wrong and against the interviewer knowing more than she does. Now the interviewer trades. Two cases:

- **Case A — the interviewer buys 1 unit from Maya at 8.** Maya is now *short* one unit of the sum: she has received \$8 and must pay out the realized sum. Her P&L is `8 − (realized sum)`. Her *expected* P&L on this single trade is `8 − E[sum] = 8 − 7 = +\$1`. She captured one full point of edge by selling above fair value. Good trade.
- **Case B — the interviewer sells 1 unit to Maya at 6.** Maya is now *long*: she paid \$6 and will receive the realized sum. Expected P&L is `E[sum] − 6 = 7 − 6 = +\$1`. Also good — she bought below fair value.

Either way, because her market straddled the true EV of 7 with a symmetric spread, *both sides of her quote had positive expected value*. That is what "make a market" means: quote prices at which you are happy to be hit on *either* side. The grading is not "did you nail 7" — it is "was your market centered near fair value, was your spread sane (not absurdly wide, not recklessly tight), and did you reason out loud?"

Now the test escalates. The interviewer, who is not betting randomly, **lifts Maya's ask** (buys at 8) and then says, "Make me a market again." A weak player re-quotes 6 at 8. Maya does not. She reasons aloud: *"You were willing to pay my offer of 8, which is above fair value of 7 — that is mild evidence you think the number is high, or that you are testing whether I update. I will shade my market up and keep a cushion: I'm now **7 at 9**."* She has treated the trade as information and moved her quote. The expected-value lesson: *in a real market the counterparty's willingness to trade is itself a signal, and the trait being graded is not the arithmetic of EV but the discipline of updating on it.*

The mechanics of these games — how to set spread width, how aggressively to skew, how to size — are drilled in depth in the sibling post on [market-making games in quant interviews](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews). What matters for *this firm* is the meta-point: the game is a faithful miniature of the desk, and Jane Street built its loop around it on purpose.

## The interview loop, decoded stage by stage

Now the process itself. Jane Street's loop, like most market-maker loops, is a funnel: a wide, cheap automated screen at the top, then progressively more expensive human rounds. Figure 1 (the cover) is the map; here is what each stage actually contains and what it is filtering for.

**Stage 0 — application.** A resume and, ideally, a referral or a strong competition signal (USACO/IOI, Putnam, ICPC, Kaggle, the IMC Prosperity trading competition). Crucially, **you should aim at the internship, not the full-time role**, if you are a student — more on why in the comp section. The application is a coarse filter: it gets you *into* the loop, and then your pedigree stops mattering.

**Stage 1 — the mental-math / OA screen.** Like the other market makers (Optiver, SIG, IMC), Jane Street uses a timed mental-arithmetic screen as an early gate. The format is brutal by design: on the order of **60–80 questions in about 8 minutes, no calculator and no scratch paper**, with a pass bar widely reported around **70–85% correct** (figures as of 2026, approximate, and varying by year and role). This is not testing whether you can do arithmetic — everyone reading this can. It is testing whether you can do it *fast and accurately under time pressure*, which is a proxy for staying calibrated when the clock is running and the stakes are real. The technique (estimation, anchoring, dropping precision intelligently) lives in the [mental-math and arithmetic-speed](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) prep; the point here is that it is a *speed-and-composure* filter, not an intelligence test, and it is eminently trainable. Figure 6 shows what clearing the bar looks like.

![An illustrative bar chart of four candidates' mental-math screen scores as a percentage of about eighty questions, with the seventy to eighty-five percent pass band shaded in amber](/imgs/blogs/jane-street-playbook-culture-ocaml-and-the-trading-games-6.png)

The chart is illustrative — those are composite candidates, not real scores — but the shape is the lesson: an under-prepped strong student often lands in the low 50s percent (failing) on a first attempt, and two months of timed drilling routinely moves the *same person* into the pass band. The screen rewards reps, not genius.

**Stage 2 — phone games (probability + EV + a market-making sim).** If you clear the screen, you get one or more phone or video rounds of roughly 45 minutes, each with a different person. Expect a mix of: probability and expected-value problems (conditional probability, fair-value/no-arbitrage reasoning, a couple of classic brain-teasers), and at least one *live market-making or betting game* like the dice example above. The interviewer is listening for your *process*: do you state assumptions, put a number on the uncertain thing, reason out loud, and update gracefully when challenged? A correct answer delivered silently is worth less here than a slightly-off answer with transparent, calibrated reasoning — because the firm is hiring the *reasoning*, which it can refine, not the one answer.

**Stage 3 — the final / superday.** The on-site (or virtual on-site) is the main event: typically **two to three rounds**, each 45–60 minutes, with different interviewers, mixing harder trading games, estimation/Fermi questions, probability, and light behavioral/fit conversation. For dev and quant-research candidates the mix shifts toward coding and a research case respectively (see below). The superday is where the *collaborative-culture* screen happens implicitly: interviewers are asking themselves "would I want to sit next to this person and think through a hard problem?" Arrogance, defensiveness, or an inability to take a hint and update will sink an otherwise strong candidate.

**Role variants.** The brief's "trading games" loop is the **quant-trader** path. If you are interviewing for:
- **Software development / engineering**, expect algorithmic/data-structures coding (competitive-programming-adjacent), and — because of the OCaml culture — an emphasis on *clean, correct, well-reasoned* code over raw micro-optimization. You typically are *not* grilled on kernel-bypass and lock-free latency the way you would be at Jump or HRT; Jane Street values correctness and composability. The general coding-prep map is in [the four paths: trader, researcher, developer, engineer](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer).
- **Quant research / ML**, expect probability and statistics depth plus a research-style case: framing a problem, avoiding overfitting, out-of-sample discipline, and being able to *kill your own idea* honestly.

Across all variants, the through-line is the same: **EV reasoning, calibration, clarity, and (for technical roles) clean code** — exactly the four rows of Figure 2.

**What each round is *really* measuring, beneath the surface problem.** It helps to read the loop as a sequence of increasingly expensive measurements of the same underlying traits, each round adding a dimension the previous one could not see:
- The **mental-math screen** measures *speed and accuracy under time pressure* — a cheap, automatable proxy for "does this person stay precise when the clock is running?" It cannot see your reasoning, so it is deliberately a coarse first gate.
- The **phone games** add *reasoning and updating* — now a human can hear you decompose a problem, put a number on uncertainty, and react to a fill. This is the first round that can tell a calibrated thinker from a fast-but-overconfident one.
- The **superday** adds *collaboration and judgment under sustained pressure across multiple people* — can you stay clear and pleasant to think with for three hours, take a hint, change your mind without ego, and handle a problem that has no clean answer? This is the dimension no automated screen can capture, which is why the expensive on-site exists at all.

Seen this way, the loop is not arbitrary hazing; it is a measurement instrument that spends more money the deeper you go, precisely because the traits it most cares about (calibration, judgment, collaboration) are the hardest and most expensive to observe. Your prep should match: drill the cheap, gate-keeping skill (mental math) to the point of reliability, then invest the bulk of your remaining effort in the expensive traits (game-play, out-loud reasoning, calibration) that decide the rounds where offers are actually won or lost.

**A note on behavioral and "fit."** Jane Street's behavioral component is light relative to a bank's, but it is not absent, and candidates underrate it. The interviewers are forming a judgment about whether you are someone the desk wants in the room — curious, honest about what you do not know, quick to update, not territorial. You demonstrate this not through rehearsed answers but through *how you behave during the technical rounds*: a candidate who says "good point, let me redo that" after being challenged is showing exactly the trait a collaborative shared-book firm needs. Treat every round as partly behavioral.

#### Worked example: an estimation / Fermi question, worked end to end

Estimation questions are a superday staple because they test the same muscle as a trading game: decompose an unknowable quantity into knowable pieces, attach numbers, and *quote a range you would bet on*. Suppose the interviewer asks Maya: **"How many golf balls would fit inside a typical commercial airplane?"** The interviewer does not want a number; they want a *method* and a *calibrated range*.

Maya decomposes. **Step 1 — the cabin volume.** A single-aisle jet's cabin is roughly a cylinder: about 4 feet of usable radius (call the cross-section a 7-foot-wide, 7-foot-tall ellipse, so a cross-sectional area of maybe `π × 3.5 × 3.5 ≈ 38` square feet) and about 100 feet of cabin length. Volume `≈ 38 × 100 = 3,800` cubic feet. **Step 2 — packing in consistent units.** A cubic foot is `12 × 12 × 12 = 1,728` cubic inches. So the cabin is about `3,800 × 1,728 ≈ 6.6` million cubic inches. **Step 3 — the volume of a golf ball.** A golf ball is about 1.68 inches in diameter, radius ~0.84 inch; its volume is `(4/3) × π × 0.84³ ≈ 2.5` cubic inches. **Step 4 — packing efficiency.** Spheres do not fill space; random packing is about **64%** efficient. **Step 5 — assemble.** `6.6 million × 0.64 / 2.5 ≈ 1.7 million` golf balls.

Maya then *quotes a range*, because that is the calibrated move: "I'd say roughly **1 to 2.5 million**, centered near 1.7 million, and I'm least sure about the cabin volume and the seats-and-cargo I ignored." The grade is not the number — *no one knows the number*. The grade is: did she decompose cleanly, keep units honest, use a sane packing factor, and *bound her own uncertainty*? That last move — naming what she is unsure about — is calibration, and it is the same trait the dice game tested.

The full toolkit for these (which constants to memorize, how to bound error, how to sanity-check) is in [estimation and Fermi problems for quant interviews](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/estimation-fermi-problems-quant-interviews). For Jane Street specifically, the lesson is: *an estimation question is a market-making game wearing a different costume — both ask you to price the unpriceable and own your uncertainty.*

## The trading games up close: a good update versus a bad one

The single most decision-relevant thing in the whole loop is **how you update after a trade**. Two candidates can quote the identical opening market and diverge completely on the next move, and that divergence is most of your score. Figure 4 puts the two players side by side on the same fill.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting a weak player who keeps the same quote after being hit against a strong player who lifts the midpoint and re-skews the spread](/imgs/blogs/jane-street-playbook-culture-ocaml-and-the-trading-games-4.png)

Trace the right-hand column. The strong player quotes **50 bid / 54 ask** on some unknown quantity (a tight, symmetric market around a fair value near 52). They get *lifted on the 54 ask* — the interviewer bought at the offer. A strong player reads that as *evidence the true value is probably higher than 52, or that the counterparty has a buying lean*, and re-skews to roughly **53 bid / 57 ask**: the midpoint moves up (because the flow suggested "high"), and the spread may widen slightly to guard against an informed counterparty. The weak player, in the left column, ignores the information entirely and re-quotes 50/54 — and gets picked off again, bleeding expected value trade after trade. The arithmetic of EV is trivial; the *discipline of updating on order flow* is the whole skill. This is also, not coincidentally, exactly what an ETF or options market maker does all day: every fill is a tiny Bayesian update on fair value and on how informed the flow is.

#### Worked example: a betting game — compute fair value, then size with EV

The phone screen sometimes drops the market-making frame and asks a clean betting game, because it isolates EV and *sizing*. Suppose the interviewer offers Maya this: *"I'll roll a fair die. If it comes up 6, I pay you \$5. Otherwise, you pay me \$1. Do you want this bet, and how much would you scale it if you could?"*

**Step 1 — fair value (the EV).** Probability of a 6 is `1/6`; otherwise `5/6`. EV per unit `= (1/6) × (+5) + (5/6) × (−1) = 5/6 − 5/6 = \$0`. The bet is *exactly fair* — zero edge. A naive candidate says "sure, it's fair, I'll take it." But a fair bet has zero expected profit and positive variance, so taking it adds risk for no reward. The calibrated answer is: *"At those terms I'm indifferent; I have no edge, so I wouldn't pay anything to play and I'd size it at zero."*

**Step 2 — now negotiate the edge.** The interviewer sweetens it: "Fine — if it's a 6 I pay you **\$8**, otherwise you pay \$1." Recompute: EV `= (1/6) × 8 + (5/6) × (−1) = 8/6 − 5/6 = 3/6 = +\$0.50` per unit. *Now* there is real edge.

**Step 3 — size it.** With positive EV, how big do you go? This is where *Kelly intuition* enters: bet more when the edge is larger and the variance smaller, but never so much that a bad run ruins you. Maya does not need the full Kelly formula in the room; she needs to say, "Positive EV of \$0.50 per unit, but the variance is high — I lose 5 times out of 6 — so I'd size it meaningfully but not bet the farm; I'd scale up roughly with my edge and down with my variance." That sentence demonstrates she understands that *EV decides direction and sizing decides survival*. The lesson: *a quant's job is two questions, not one — is the EV positive, and how much should I risk given the variance — and the games test both.* (The mechanics of sizing live in the [Kelly-criterion and sequential-betting](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/market-making-games-quant-interviews) interview material.)

## Compensation and the intern pipeline

Now the numbers everyone actually clicked for — presented honestly, with their conditionality, because the headline figures are survivorship-biased and the bonus is the variable that nobody controls. Figure 5 first sets up the culture pillars that explain *why* the comp is shaped the way it is, and then we do the money.

![A four-layer stack of Jane Street culture pillars showing collaboration on top, a functional OCaml codebase, a puzzle habit, and heavy formal training as the base](/imgs/blogs/jane-street-playbook-culture-ocaml-and-the-trading-games-5.png)

**The base is flat and famous.** Jane Street quotes an *"annualized equivalent of about \$300k"* base across its quant-trader, quant-research, ML, and FPGA roles — strikingly uniform across functions, which fits the collaborative, "we hire generalists and train them" ethos. On levels.fyi (reported 2025), Jane Street quant-trader base sits around **\$300k**, with the average near **\$309k**, the **75th percentile near \$407k**, and the **90th percentile near \$512k** in total comp. (In H1B disclosures, Jane Street and Five Rings lead the field with base figures around **\$300k**, ahead of Citadel Securities around \$257k.) Figure 3 puts the comp checkpoints on one axis.

![A stacked bar chart of Jane Street compensation checkpoints showing a flat three-hundred-thousand-dollar base across the intern annualized rate, the intern conversion offer near four hundred twenty-five thousand, and quant-trader percentiles up to about five hundred twelve thousand](/imgs/blogs/jane-street-playbook-culture-ocaml-and-the-trading-games-3.png)

**The internship is the headline — and the strategy.** Jane Street interns are paid at roughly an **annualized \$300k** rate (on the order of \$4,500–\$6,000 per week), and — this is the part that should reorganize your whole plan — the internship *is the real interview*. Strong programs convert most of their interns to full-time return offers, and the firm fills the large majority of its full-time seats from that converted pool rather than from cold full-time applications. The reported conversion package lands near **\$425k** in first-year total comp. So the dominant strategy for a student is not "apply for the full-time job" — it is "win the internship, then convert it." The intern seat is both easier to get (a wider funnel) and the actual gateway to the full-time role.

**The honest caveats, which matter more than the headlines.**
1. **The bonus does not repeat automatically.** Base is flat and contractual; the variable pay is tied to P&L contribution and the firm's year. A great year is not the median, and last year's number is *not* an annuity. This is the single most-misunderstood fact about quant comp.
2. **These are reported ranges, not your offer.** levels.fyi and Glassdoor numbers are self-reported by people who *got and kept* the seat — they are survivorship-biased upward. The median new grad is not the 90th percentile.
3. **Selectivity is real.** Getting any of these numbers requires clearing one of the tightest filters in any industry (more on that below).

#### Worked example: the intern conversion comp math, ~425k first-year

Let us build the \$425k figure from its parts so it stops being a magic number. Maya converts her internship into a first-year quant-trader seat. Her package, illustratively (consistent with the DATA APPENDIX and levels.fyi 2025, as of June 2026):

- **Base salary:** about **\$300k**, flat and contractual — she gets this regardless of how the year goes.
- **Sign-on / first-year guarantee and bonus:** roughly **\$125k** on top, combining a signing bonus and a (often partially guaranteed) first-year performance bonus.
- **First-year total:** `300k + 125k ≈ \$425k`.

Now the year-2 reality check, which is the *real* lesson. Suppose her first-year guarantee evaporates and her year-2 bonus is purely P&L-driven. If she has a *standard* year, her bonus might be, say, \$200k, for a year-2 total near `300k + 200k = \$500k` — a raise. But if she has a *quiet* year, the bonus could be \$75k, for a total near `300k + 75k = \$375k` — *below* her first-year number, even though nothing went wrong with her base. The base is the floor; the bonus is the lever, and the lever moves with P&L and the firm's year, not with tenure. *The intuition: read every quant comp number as that year's outcome, never as a salary you will collect on repeat — the only contractual part is the base.*

For the full cross-firm comp picture — how Jane Street's shape compares to a pod shop's binary stop-out payout or a systematic fund's fee-pool bonus — see [the firm archetypes](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-firm-archetypes-prop-vs-hft-vs-pod-shop-vs-systematic-fund), which derives each engine's pay dollar by dollar.

One more piece of comp literacy worth carrying into any offer conversation: large bonuses at top firms are frequently *partially deferred*, meaning a chunk of a headline number is paid out over the following years and is often *forfeited if you leave* before it vests. Deferred comp is a retention tool — it raises the cost of quitting and lets the firm align your incentives with staying. So when you read a number like "\$425k first-year total," ask what fraction is cash now versus deferred, and remember that the multi-year figures people quote (the \$1M+ years a few years in, as a strong-seat prop trader) are *that year's* number, conditional on P&L, and not an annuity you collect on repeat. The reliable part of your pay is the flat base; everything above it is a function of how you and the firm performed, paid partly on a delay. Plan your life around the base, treat the bonus as upside, and you will never be surprised by a down year.

## How to prepare for Jane Street specifically

General quant prep is necessary but not sufficient; this firm rewards a *specific* shape of preparation. Figure 7 is your fit-and-prep checklist; here is the reasoning behind each line.

![A five-row matrix showing whether Jane Street is a good fit for you in the left column and a concrete prep checklist in the right column, covering temperament, mental math, trading games, communication, and the internship](/imgs/blogs/jane-street-playbook-culture-ocaml-and-the-trading-games-7.png)

1. **Drill mental math to the pass bar, on a timer.** The screen is on the order of 60–80 questions in 8 minutes; you should practice *at that pace* until you are reliably in the 70–85% band, not just "good at arithmetic." Use approximation, anchoring, and intelligent precision-dropping. This is the most trainable part of the entire loop and the cheapest point to fail at, so it deserves the most reps.

2. **Play betting and market-making games until updating is reflexive.** Find a partner and run mock market-making sims: one person quotes a market on an unknown quantity, the other trades, and the quoter must *re-quote with a justified skew*. The trait you are building is not arithmetic — it is the instinct to treat every fill as information and update calmly. Re-read Figure 4 until the strong-player column is your default.

3. **Build a calibration habit — and log it.** Whenever you make a prediction (about anything), state a probability or a range, then track whether reality lands inside it. Over a few weeks you will discover your systematic overconfidence and learn to widen your quotes. Calibrated people quote ranges they would bet on; that is precisely what the games reward.

4. **Practice thinking out loud.** The single most common way strong candidates fail is by going quiet, computing in their head, and stating only the answer. The firm is grading the *reasoning*. Narrate every assumption and every EV calculation out loud, even when it feels slow. Treat silence as a scoring penalty.

5. **For dev/QR roles: write clean, correct, tested code, and learn what functional thinking is.** You do not need OCaml, but you should be comfortable with the *values* it encodes — immutability, strong typing, composability, correctness over cleverness. If you have a free weekend, working through a little OCaml or another functional language (Haskell, F#) is a genuine signal of culture fit and will make the interview's coding style feel native. For QR, practice framing a research problem and *killing your own idea* with out-of-sample discipline.

6. **Aim at the internship and recruit early.** Because conversion is the dominant path, a student's optimal strategy is to land the *summer* seat, ideally a year or more before graduation, and then convert it. The full-time funnel is narrower and largely fed by interns. Apply early in the cycle; these seats fill fast.

7. **Skip the finance cramming.** Do *not* spend your prep memorizing option Greeks, accounting, or macro. The firm will teach you the finance; it is selecting for the substrate. Time spent on finance trivia is time stolen from mental math, games, and calibration — the things actually being graded.

#### Worked example: the application-funnel math, and how prep moves it

Let us put EV on the *career* decision itself, because the meta-thesis of this series is that getting the job is also a probabilistic edge. Suppose Maya applies to **6** top market-maker internship programs, and her unconditioned per-firm probability of converting an application into an offer is a sobering **p = 3%** (these programs are widely reported as low-single-digit-percent selective). Treating the firms as roughly independent, her expected number of offers is `6 × 0.03 = 0.18`, and her probability of *at least one* offer is `1 − (1 − 0.03)^6 = 1 − 0.97^6 ≈ 1 − 0.833 = 16.7%`.

Now she does the prep above: timed mental-math drilling, mock games, calibration logging, out-loud reasoning. Say that lifts her per-firm probability to **p = 8%** (still humble, but the prep genuinely moves the screen-pass and game-performance odds). Expected offers become `6 × 0.08 = 0.48`, and probability of at least one offer is `1 − 0.92^6 ≈ 1 − 0.606 = 39.4%` — she has more than *doubled* her odds of breaking in, from ~17% to ~39%, purely by preparing for the specific filter. Add two more firms (8 total) at p = 8% and it climbs to `1 − 0.92^8 ≈ 49%`. *The intuition: you cannot control the brutal base rate, but prep and breadth are the two levers you fully own, and both compound — a few points of per-firm probability across a handful of firms is the difference between a coin-flip and a long shot.*

(The general version of this funnel math — how many firms to target, how prep lifts p, when to widen versus deepen — is the spine of [the four paths](/blog/trading/quant-careers/the-four-paths-trader-researcher-developer-engineer) and the broader process strategy.)

## Common misconceptions

Four myths sink more Jane Street candidates than any skill gap. Name them and correct them.

**Myth 1: "You have to know OCaml to get hired."** False, and it is the most common deterrent. The firm hires people who have never seen OCaml all the time and teaches it on the job; its training program exists precisely to bring strong generalists up to speed. Knowing a functional language is a mild *plus* (it signals culture fit and makes the coding rounds feel native), but it is not a requirement for *any* role, and for trading roles it is essentially irrelevant. Do not let the language scare you off. What the firm actually wants is clear, correct thinking — OCaml is the expression of that value, not a prerequisite for being hired.

**Myth 2: "The interview is finance trivia, so I need to cram options theory and accounting."** The opposite is true. The loop deliberately avoids finance knowledge because it is learnable in a weekend and reveals nothing about how you reason. Every minute you spend memorizing Black-Scholes is a minute stolen from the mental math, games, and calibration that are actually graded. The firm *will teach you the finance*; it is hiring the substrate underneath it.

**Myth 3: "You need a target school to get in."** Pedigree may help you get *screened in* at the resume stage — that is real — but it is *invisible* the moment a game starts. The entire architecture of the loop (timed math, live games, estimation) exists to strip away pedigree and trivia and expose raw reasoning, precisely so that a brilliant calibrated thinker from a non-target school can outperform a credentialed one who freezes in a market-making sim. The competition-math signal (Putnam, IOI, ICPC, Kaggle, IMC Prosperity) is a far stronger tailwind than a school name, and it is available to anyone.

**Myth 4: "The trading games are basically luck."** This is the most dangerous misconception because it discourages preparation. The games have *random outcomes*, but they grade your *process*, not the realized roll. Whether the die comes up 6 is luck; whether you quoted a market centered on the true EV of 7, sized sanely, reasoned out loud, and updated correctly when hit is entirely skill — and it is exactly reproducible across many rounds, which is why the firm can distinguish a strong player from a weak one despite the randomness. A calibrated player wins *in expectation* over the loop even though any single game is noisy. Luck is the surface; calibration is what is measured.

**A bonus myth worth flagging: "Everyone at Jane Street makes \$600k+."** No — that figure describes the *survivors* of an extraordinarily tight filter, reported by the people who got and kept the seat. Many strong applicants never get an offer; some who do underperform, see their bonus shrink, or move on. The headline numbers are real *and* survivorship-biased, and both things are true at once. Plan on the median, hope for the tail.

## How it plays out in the real world

Here is the honest, end-to-end arc, stitched from the numbers and facts above into one realistic path — and one realistic miss.

**The hit.** Maya, the math undergraduate, spends the spring of her junior year drilling mental math on a timer until she is reliably in the high-70s percent, runs twice-weekly mock market-making games with a friend until updating on a fill is reflexive, and keeps a calibration journal that cures her of systematic overconfidence. She applies *early* to six market-maker internship programs aiming at the *summer* seat, not the full-time role. She clears Jane Street's screen, handles the phone games by reasoning out loud and re-skewing her quotes when hit, and at the superday she comes across as someone the interviewers would happily think alongside. She gets the internship at roughly a \$300k annualized rate, has a strong summer, and converts to a first-year seat near **\$425k** total. Year two, with the guarantee gone, her comp is purely a function of her P&L and the firm's year — it could be \$375k or \$500k, and she has internalized that the base is the floor and the bonus is the lever. This is the textbook path, and it is reproducible: it is built almost entirely from the controllable levers (prep, breadth, recruiting the intern seat first), riding a brutal but real base rate.

**The miss.** A second candidate, equally brilliant, spends two months memorizing option Greeks and accounting, treats the games as luck and barely practices them, applies *only* to the full-time role late in the cycle, and goes quiet in the superday — computing in his head and stating bare answers. He fails the mental-math screen at one firm (he never practiced *at pace*), and at the firm where he clears it, he freezes when an interviewer lifts his market and he keeps re-quoting the same numbers. He misreads the entire job: he prepared to *look like a finance person* instead of *being a calibrated, clear, fast probabilistic thinker*, and he aimed at the narrow full-time funnel instead of the wide intern one. Nothing about his raw ability was the problem; everything about his *model of the firm* was.

The difference between the two is not talent. It is whether they understood that **Jane Street selects for EV reasoning, calibration, clarity, and clean code — delivered out loud, through games, with the internship as the real door.** Everything in this post is downstream of that one sentence.

A note on the wider ecosystem, for calibration: Jane Street is one of several elite market makers, and its games-and-math loop is a cousin of the screens at Optiver, SIG, and IMC (the mental-math gauntlet is most extreme at Optiver). If you are casting a net — and the funnel math above says you should — prepare the *shared* substrate (mental math, EV games, calibration) once and apply it across all of them, then tune for each firm's flavor (Jane Street's collaboration-and-OCaml ethos, SIG's poker-and-decision-theory culture, Optiver's pure-speed screen). The companion firm playbooks in this series cover those flavors firm by firm.
