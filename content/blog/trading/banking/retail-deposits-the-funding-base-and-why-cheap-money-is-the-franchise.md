> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The most valuable thing a bank owns is not its loans or its building; it is the right to hold your everyday money almost for free. A base of cheap, sticky, insured retail deposits is the real franchise — it is what lets a bank fund a 6.5% loan with money that costs it 0.5%, and the gap is where almost all of a normal bank's profit comes from.
>
> - Deposits come in three kinds — checking (current), savings, and term (CDs) — and they get more expensive and less sticky as you go down the list. The cheap, sticky part is the prize.
> - The **CASA ratio** (current-and-savings as a share of total deposits) is the single best one-number gauge of how cheap a bank's funding is. A high CASA ratio means a low cost of funds, and a low cost of funds means a fat margin.
> - **Deposit beta** measures how much of a central-bank rate hike a bank has to pass on to depositors. A low beta is a superpower: it means rates can rise and the bank's funding stays cheap. US deposit beta rose from about 0.10 early in 2022 to roughly 0.55 by mid-2024.
> - The one number to remember: deposits fund about **71%** of a typical large bank, and the cheapest slice of those deposits is worth more than the loan book it supports. Lose it — to a run or to a competitor — and the bank has nothing.

Why does your bank pay you almost nothing on your checking account? You hand it your salary, it sits there, you spend some, more arrives next month — and for the privilege of holding the most useful money you own, the bank pays you 0%, or close to it. It feels like a slightly insulting arrangement. It is, in fact, the most profitable arrangement in all of finance, and it runs entirely in the bank's favor.

Here is the trick laid bare. In 2023, the average US bank was paying its depositors an effective rate well under 2% while collecting more than 5% on its assets. On the roughly \$18 trillion of deposits sitting in the US banking system, even a one-percentage-point difference in what banks pay is worth on the order of \$180 billion a year. That money does not come from being clever in markets or from fancy products. It comes from the simple fact that hundreds of millions of people and businesses leave their everyday cash in accounts that pay almost nothing — and leave it there, month after month, year after year, no matter what interest rates do. That pile of patient, cheap, loyal money is what bankers quietly call *the franchise*. Everything else a bank does is built on top of it.

The figure below is the mental model for the whole post: the three rungs of the deposit ladder, ranked by what they cost the bank and how long the money stays. The top rung — free, sticky checking money — is the gold. The bottom rung — expensive term deposits that walk the moment a better rate appears — is the consolation prize. The art of running a bank's funding is climbing as high up this ladder as you can.

![Matrix of checking savings and term deposits ranked by rate paid and stickiness](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-1.png)

This connects straight to the spine of the whole series. A bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine: it borrows short and lends long, earns the spread between the two, and survives only as long as depositors trust it. Retail deposits are *both* halves of that sentence at once. They are the "borrow short" — the cheap funding that makes the spread possible. And they are the "confidence-funded" — the trust that, if it ever breaks, ends the bank in days. Understand deposits and you understand why a bank is, simultaneously, the most profitable kind of business and the most fragile.

## Foundations: what a deposit actually is, and the three kinds

Let's build every piece from zero. By the end of this section you will know exactly what a deposit is from the bank's point of view, why there are three flavors of it, and what makes one flavor worth ten times more to the bank than another.

### A deposit is a loan you make to the bank

Start with the single idea that flips everything around. When you put \$1,000 into your bank account, you are not "storing" your money in a vault somewhere with your name on it. You are *lending* the bank \$1,000. The account balance is the bank's IOU to you — a promise to give your money back, on demand, whenever you ask. On the bank's books, your \$1,000 is a *liability* (something it owes), and the cash itself is now the bank's to use. It will lend most of it out to someone else.

This is the inversion at the heart of banking, and it trips up nearly everyone the first time. Your bank account is an *asset* to you and a *liability* to the bank. The mortgage is an *asset* to the bank and a *liability* to you. The bank's whole business is taking in liabilities (deposits) that are cheap and turning them into assets (loans) that pay more. A *liability* is simply money you owe; an *asset* is money or value you own. We will use those two words constantly, so lock them in now.

Because a deposit is a loan to the bank, the bank pays you interest on it — sometimes. How much it pays, and how reliably the money stays, depends on what kind of deposit it is. There are three.

### The three kinds of deposit

**Current accounts (also called checking, or demand deposits).** This is the everyday-spending money: the account your salary lands in, that you pay rent and buy groceries from. The defining feature is that you can withdraw it *on demand*, instantly, with no notice and no penalty — that is why it is also called a *demand deposit*. Because it is so liquid and convenient, the bank can usually get away with paying you almost nothing for it: often 0%, sometimes 0.01%. (*Liquidity* means how quickly something can be turned into spendable cash without loss; a current account is the most liquid thing there is.) From the bank's side, current-account money is the cheapest funding on Earth.

**Savings accounts.** This is money you have set aside but want to keep accessible — an emergency fund, savings for a holiday. You can still get at it easily, but you are not touching it every day. Banks pay a bit more on savings than on checking — perhaps 0.3% to 1% in normal times — but still far below market interest rates. Crucially, savings rates are *sticky upward*: when the central bank raises rates from 0% to 5%, banks raise savings rates slowly and grudgingly, often to nowhere near 5%, because most savers do not move their money in response.

**Term deposits (also called certificates of deposit, or CDs; in some countries, fixed deposits).** Here you agree to lock your money away for a fixed period — three months, one year, five years — in exchange for a higher rate. If you take it out early, you pay a penalty. Because you give up access, the bank pays you much more, often close to the market rate: 4% to 5% when policy rates are high. Term deposits are the *expensive* end of the ladder. They are also a double-edged sword for stickiness, as we will see: locked in for now, but liable to walk the moment the term ends and a competitor is offering more.

### CASA: the one ratio that captures the whole game

Put the cheapest two rungs together — **C**urrent **A**nd **S**avings **A**ccounts — and you get the term every bank analyst lives by: the **CASA ratio**. It is the share of a bank's total deposits that sits in cheap current and savings accounts, as opposed to expensive term deposits.

$$\text{CASA ratio} = \frac{\text{Current deposits} + \text{Savings deposits}}{\text{Total deposits}}$$

A bank with a CASA ratio of 75% funds three-quarters of its deposit base with money that costs almost nothing. A bank with a CASA ratio of 35% is relying on expensive CDs for most of its funding and is paying dearly for it. We will compute exactly how much that difference is worth in dollars shortly — it is the difference between a thriving bank and a struggling one. For now, hold this: **CASA ratio up, cost of funds down, margin up.** It is the most important chain of cause and effect in retail banking.

### Core vs non-core, operational vs excess

Two more distinctions sharpen the analysis, because not all CASA money is created equal either. The first is **core versus non-core** deposits. Core deposits are the stable, relationship-driven balances a bank can count on through thick and thin — your salary account, a small business's working cash. Non-core deposits are the opportunistic, rate-chasing balances that showed up for a promotion and will leave for the next one: brokered deposits (gathered through middlemen who shop them around for the best rate), large time deposits, and hot money from rate-comparison sites. Two banks can report the same total deposits, but the one with mostly core deposits has a far better, safer franchise. Regulators watch the core-versus-non-core split closely; a bank whose growth is driven by brokered, non-core money is flashing a warning light.

The second distinction is **operational versus excess** balances, which matters most for business deposits. Operational balances are the money a customer *must* keep at the bank to run its day-to-day life — the float in a company's payments account, the cash a business needs to make payroll. This money is genuinely sticky because the customer cannot function without it sitting there. Excess balances are everything above that — money the customer is merely *parking* at the bank because it has not found a better home yet. Excess balances are flighty; they leave the instant a money-market fund or a Treasury bill offers a better deal. A deposit base heavy in operational balances is a fortress; one heavy in excess balances is a flight risk dressed up as a deposit. The art of deposit gathering is maximizing the core, operational money and not mistaking parked excess cash for franchise.

### Why deposits are the bulk of how a bank is funded

A bank has to fund its assets somehow. It can take deposits, it can borrow from other banks or from markets (this is called *wholesale funding* — large, professional, rate-sensitive money), it can issue bonds, and it has a thin sliver of its own shareholders' money (*equity*). For a typical large bank, the breakdown looks like the figure below.

![Stacked bar showing deposits at 71 percent of bank funding with wholesale debt equity above](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-2.png)

Deposits are about **71%** of total funding. Wholesale and repo borrowing is about 10%, long-term debt about 7%, other liabilities about 4%, and equity — the shareholders' loss-absorbing cushion — is about 8%. (That 8% equity, the thin slice on top, is why a bank is so leveraged; it owns about \$12.50 of assets for every \$1 of its own money. The rest is other people's money, and most of it is yours.) The dominance of that blue block is the whole story: a bank lives or dies on its deposit base. The other funding sources are more expensive, less stable, and they vanish in a crisis exactly when you need them. Deposits, done right, are cheap, plentiful, and loyal. That is the franchise.

For the system-level view of how this money is actually created when banks lend — how a deposit and a loan spring into existence on the same day — link out to [how money is created](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier). Here we are inside one bank, looking at why its slice of that money is so precious.

## The cost of funds: why cheap deposits beat everything

Now we can put numbers on the prize. The reason a deposit base is worth more than the loans it funds comes down to one calculation that every banker does in their sleep: the **cost of funds**.

### What the cost of funds is

The cost of funds is the blended interest rate a bank pays across all its funding, weighted by how much of each kind it has. It is the price of the raw material — money — that the bank then re-sells as loans. If a bank's cost of funds is 1.5% and it lends at 6.5%, its raw spread is 5 percentage points. If a competitor's cost of funds is 3.5% because it relies on expensive term deposits and wholesale borrowing, that competitor earns only 3 percentage points on the same 6.5% loan. Same loan, same risk — but one bank makes two-thirds more on it, purely because of how cheaply it funds itself.

This is why the funding side, not the lending side, is where most banks actually compete and win. Almost any bank can lend at 6.5%. The bank that *funds* at 1.5% instead of 3.5% wins.

#### Worked example: a blended cost of funds

Let's compute one from scratch. Take a simplified bank with \$100 of funding, split like a real one:

- \$40 in current accounts paying **0%**
- \$25 in savings accounts paying **0.5%**
- \$20 in term deposits (CDs) paying **4.5%**
- \$15 in wholesale and bond funding paying **5.0%**

The interest the bank pays each year is the rate times the amount, summed up:

- Current: \$40 × 0% = \$0.00
- Savings: \$25 × 0.5% = \$0.125
- Term: \$20 × 4.5% = \$0.90
- Wholesale: \$15 × 5.0% = \$0.75

Total interest paid = \$0.00 + \$0.125 + \$0.90 + \$0.75 = **\$1.775** on \$100 of funding. So the blended cost of funds is **1.775%**.

Now notice where almost all the cost came from. The \$65 of CASA money (current plus savings) contributed only \$0.125 — about 7% of the total interest bill — even though it is 65% of the funding. The \$35 of term and wholesale money, just 35% of the funding, produced 93% of the cost. **The cheap money does the work for free; the expensive money does the damage.** That is the entire argument for a high CASA ratio in one calculation.

### CASA mix and the blended cost of funds

The relationship between the CASA ratio and the cost of funds is almost mechanical. The more of your deposits sit in free current and cheap savings accounts, the lower your blended cost. The chart below makes it concrete: holding the cheap rate at 0.5% and the term rate at 4.5%, watch what happens to the blended cost of deposits as the CASA share climbs from 30% to 90%.

![Line chart of blended cost of funds falling as the CASA share of deposits rises](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-5.png)

A bank with only a 30% CASA ratio pays a blended **3.3%** on its deposits. Push the CASA ratio to 75% and the blended cost falls to **1.5%**. Push it to 90% and you are paying just **0.9%**. That is a swing of 2.4 percentage points on the entire deposit base, achieved without lending a single dollar differently — purely by gathering cheaper money.

#### Worked example: CASA mix lowering funding cost

Make it real with dollars. Take two banks, each with \$200 billion of deposits, each lending at the same 6.0% yield. The only difference is their CASA ratio.

**Bank A** has a 75% CASA ratio. Its \$150 billion of CASA money costs 0.5%, and its \$50 billion of term deposits costs 4.5%.

- CASA cost: \$150bn × 0.5% = \$0.75bn
- Term cost: \$50bn × 4.5% = \$2.25bn
- Total deposit interest = \$3.0bn, so cost of deposits = \$3.0bn ÷ \$200bn = **1.5%**

**Bank B** has a 40% CASA ratio. Its \$80 billion of CASA money costs 0.5%, and its \$120 billion of term deposits costs 4.5%.

- CASA cost: \$80bn × 0.5% = \$0.40bn
- Term cost: \$120bn × 4.5% = \$5.40bn
- Total deposit interest = \$5.80bn, so cost of deposits = \$5.80bn ÷ \$200bn = **2.9%**

Now the punchline. Both banks earn 6.0% on \$200 billion of loans = \$12.0bn of interest income. But Bank A pays \$3.0bn for its deposits and Bank B pays \$5.8bn. Bank A's net interest income from this is \$9.0bn; Bank B's is \$6.2bn. **Same loans, same size, same yields — and Bank A makes \$2.8 billion more per year, every year, just because it funds itself with cheaper deposits.** Capitalize that gap and Bank A is worth tens of billions more. The CASA ratio is not an accounting detail; it is the difference between two completely different companies. The intuition: in banking, you win on what you pay for money, not just on what you charge for it.

This is the deepest reason the spread business works at all. For the full mechanics of how that spread becomes the bank's headline margin, link out to the dedicated post on [net interest margin and the spread business](/blog/trading/banking/net-interest-margin-and-the-spread-business-explained); here, the point is that the *funding* leg is where the cheap-deposit franchise pays off.

## Deposit beta: the superpower of not having to pay up

So far we have treated deposit rates as fixed. They are not. When the central bank raises rates, every bank faces a question: how much of that hike do I have to pass on to my depositors to keep them from leaving? The answer to that question — the fraction of a rate move a bank passes through to its deposit rates — is called **deposit beta**, and it is one of the most important and least understood numbers in banking.

### What deposit beta means

Suppose the central bank raises its policy rate by 2 percentage points. A bank with a deposit beta of 0.4 raises the rate it pays depositors by 0.4 × 2 = 0.8 percentage points. A bank with a beta of 0.9 raises it by 1.8 points. The lower your beta, the more of the rate hike you get to *keep* as extra margin rather than handing it to depositors.

Why does a low beta matter so enormously? Because the asset side of a bank reprices toward the higher rate fairly quickly — new loans get written at higher rates, floating-rate loans reset, maturing bonds get reinvested at the new yield. If your assets are earning more but your deposits still cost almost nothing, the spread explodes in your favor. A low deposit beta is, quite literally, a license to print money in a rising-rate environment. It is the reason banks' profits often *jump* when rates rise — for a while.

#### Worked example: a deposit beta of 0.4 on a 2-point hike

Take a bank with \$100 billion of deposits and a deposit beta of 0.4. The central bank hikes 2 percentage points. The bank's asset yield rises by roughly the full 2 points (assume its assets are fairly rate-sensitive). What happens to its profit from this?

- Deposit cost rises by: 0.4 × 2.0% = **0.8 percentage points**
- Asset yield rises by: roughly **2.0 percentage points**
- So the spread *widens* by: 2.0 − 0.8 = **1.2 percentage points**

On \$100 billion of deposits funding \$100 billion of assets, 1.2 percentage points of extra spread is \$100bn × 1.2% = **\$1.2 billion a year of extra profit** — created out of thin air by the simple fact that the bank did not have to pass the full hike to depositors. Had its beta been 0.9 instead of 0.4, the deposit cost would have risen 1.8 points, the spread would have widened only 0.2 points, and the windfall would have been just \$200 million. The intuition: deposit beta is the dial that decides how much of a rate cycle a bank gets to keep for itself.

### Beta rises over a cycle — and that is the catch

Deposit beta is not constant. Early in a hiking cycle, banks barely move their deposit rates — depositors are slow to notice, slow to act, and used to getting nothing. Beta is low, and profits soar. But as the cycle wears on, depositors wake up. They notice that money-market funds and Treasury bills are paying 5% while their savings account pays 0.5%. They start to move money. To slow the bleed, banks are forced to raise deposit rates — to *pay up*. Beta climbs. The early windfall narrows.

The chart below shows exactly this pattern in the most recent US hiking cycle. Cumulative deposit beta — the total share of the Fed's hikes that banks had passed on — was only about 0.10 in mid-2022, when the Fed had just started. By the end of 2023 it had reached roughly 0.50, and by mid-2024 about 0.55.

![Step chart of US deposit beta rising from 0.10 in 2022 to 0.55 in 2024](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-3.png)

The shape of that line is the story of every rate cycle. Banks feast early, when beta is low, and the feast slowly fades as beta creeps up and depositors demand their share. The banks with the *stickiest* depositors — the ones with the highest-quality retail franchise — keep their beta low the longest. That is why a sticky, low-beta deposit base is worth so much: it is not just cheap today, it *stays* cheap even when rates rise, capturing more of the cycle than a flightier competitor ever could.

### The asymmetry: deposit rates rise slowly and fall fast

There is a beautiful asymmetry in deposit beta that works in the bank's favor on both sides of a cycle, and it is worth understanding precisely. When the central bank *raises* rates, banks raise deposit rates slowly and reluctantly — *upward beta* is low. But when the central bank *cuts* rates, banks cut deposit rates quickly and eagerly — *downward beta* is high. The bank pays up grudgingly and pays down promptly.

Think about why. When rates rise, the bank wants to keep paying you as little as possible, so it drags its feet, hoping inertia keeps you from leaving. When rates fall, the bank wants to cut its costs immediately, so it slashes your deposit rate the moment it can — and you, the depositor, have nowhere better to go anyway, because everyone else's rates are falling too. The result is that over a full cycle, the bank's deposit cost tends to lag rates on the way up and lead them on the way down, capturing extra margin in both directions. This asymmetry is a structural gift to banks with sticky deposits, and it is invisible in any single quarter — you only see it across a whole cycle.

The flip side is the trap that this lulls weaker banks into. A bank that enjoyed a low upward beta early in a cycle can convince itself its deposits are stickier than they are. Then, halfway through, depositors wake up, beta spikes, and the bank discovers its funding was rate-sensitive all along — just slow to react. The 2022–2023 cycle did exactly this to banks that had grown their deposit bases cheaply in the zero-rate era: the money that flooded in for convenience when rates were zero turned out to be rate-shopping money the moment Treasury bills paid 5%. A franchise is not proven by a low beta early in a cycle; it is proven by a low beta *late* in one.

## Stickiness: why deposits stay (until suddenly they don't)

We keep using the word *sticky*. It is time to define it precisely, because stickiness is the second pillar of the franchise — and the one that, when it fails, kills the bank.

### What makes a deposit sticky

A deposit is **sticky** if the depositor leaves it in the account even when a better deal is available elsewhere. Stickiness is not loyalty in any sentimental sense; it is the product of friction, habit, and trust:

- **Friction.** Moving your salary direct-deposit, your standing orders, your linked bills, and your debit-card subscriptions to a new bank is a genuine hassle. Most people will not do it to chase an extra 0.5%.
- **Habit and inertia.** People bank where they have always banked. The average current-account relationship lasts more than a decade. Inertia is the bank's best friend.
- **Operational dependence.** A business that runs its payroll, collections, and supplier payments through a bank cannot easily move; the deposits that sit alongside those services are extremely sticky. This is why *transaction banking* — running a company's day-to-day money — generates such valuable deposits.
- **Trust and insurance.** If you believe your money is safe — backed by the bank's solidity and by government deposit insurance — you have no reason to move it in a panic. Trust is the foundation of stickiness, and its collapse is the foundation of a run.

The first three forces make deposits sticky in *normal* times — that is what keeps the cost of funds low and the beta low. The fourth force — trust — is what keeps them sticky in *abnormal* times, in a scare. And the two behave completely differently.

### The two faces of stickiness: normal times and a scare

In normal times, even uninsured, rate-sensitive deposits are reasonably sticky — friction and inertia hold them in place. The danger is that this lulls a bank into thinking *all* its deposits are sticky. They are not. In a scare, friction and inertia evaporate in an instant; the only thing that holds money in place is trust and insurance. And the deposits held by people and businesses with *too much to lose* — balances above the insurance limit — are the first out the door.

The figure below contrasts the two kinds of base under the exact same scare: a bank funded mostly by large uninsured balances versus a bank funded by small insured retail deposits.

![Before and after comparison of an uninsured deposit base fleeing versus an insured base staying in a scare](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-4.png)

The left column is the nightmare: a base concentrated in a few large, uninsured, professionally managed balances. When the bad headline hits, those depositors — who have millions above the insured limit and a fiduciary duty to protect it — move first and move fast. The right column is the franchise: millions of small everyday accounts, almost all under the insurance limit, whose owners have no reason to run because their money is guaranteed safe. Same headline, completely different outcome. The insured retail base barely twitches; the uninsured wholesale base empties out in a day or two.

This is not theoretical. It is exactly what separated the banks that died in 2023 from the ones that did not. We will walk through the cases shortly.

### Insurance is what makes retail deposits the gold standard

Government **deposit insurance** is the single most important reason small retail deposits are so sticky and so valuable. In the US, the FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation) guarantees deposits up to **\$250,000** per depositor, per bank. In the EU, the guarantee is €100,000; in the UK, £85,000. If the bank fails, the government makes insured depositors whole — usually within days.

Think about what that does to behavior. If your \$40,000 is fully insured, you have *literally no financial reason* to join a bank run, even if you are certain the bank will fail — you will get your money back regardless. Insured depositors are the calmest money in the system. They do not run, which means the bank that holds mostly insured retail money has the most stable funding base in existence. Insurance converts trust — a fragile, psychological thing — into a hard government guarantee. That is why a deposit base full of insured retail money is the gold standard, and why banks pay so little for it: it is both cheap *and* it will not run.

There is a deeper, almost paradoxical point here. Insurance is most valuable precisely when it is *never used*. The government rarely has to pay out, because the mere existence of the guarantee stops the run before it starts — there is no point fleeing a bank if your money is safe whether it lives or dies. This is the same logic as a fire-sprinkler system that earns its keep by preventing the fire, not by putting it out. The insured retail deposit is so stable not because depositors trust the bank, but because they no longer *need* to. Trust in the individual bank is replaced by trust in the system, which is far harder to shake. That substitution — bank-level trust swapped for system-level insurance — is the quiet innovation that made modern retail banking the stable, profitable machine it is. Before deposit insurance (introduced in the US in 1933, after thousands of banks failed in runs), even a healthy bank could be killed by a rumor. After it, the small-saver run essentially vanished from the developed world. Every dollar of insured retail funding a bank holds is a dollar that inherited that stability for free.

### The deposit-insurance fund is not free, and not infinite

One nuance worth carrying: deposit insurance is funded by the banks themselves, through premiums paid into a fund (in the US, the Deposit Insurance Fund). The system is mutualized — sound banks effectively pre-pay for the failures of unsound ones. This is a cost of the franchise, but a tiny one relative to what the guarantee is worth: the premium a bank pays for insurance is a rounding error next to the funding-cost saving the insured deposits deliver. The bank gives up a few basis points in premium and gets back a run-proof, low-beta funding base. It is one of the best trades in finance, which is exactly why banks are willing to be regulated as heavily as they are to keep access to it. The guarantee and the regulation are a package: the price of cheap, stable, insured deposits is supervision.

## The dark side: when deposits flee, the run math is brutal

We have celebrated the franchise. Now look at what happens when it breaks, because the same features that make deposits so valuable — that they are short-term and withdrawable on demand — are exactly what makes a bank fragile. The franchise and the fragility are the same coin.

### Why a maturity-transforming bank can never survive a full run

Recall the core trade: the bank takes in deposits (withdrawable today) and lends them out long (a 30-year mortgage, a 5-year business loan). This is *maturity transformation*, and it is the source of both the spread and the danger. On any normal day, only a small fraction of depositors want their money back, and fresh deposits arrive to replace the outflows, so the bank only needs to keep a thin layer of cash on hand. But the loans cannot be called back on a moment's notice. If enough depositors demand their money at once, the bank cannot turn its long loans into cash fast enough to pay them. It is not that the bank is necessarily *insolvent* (assets worth less than liabilities); it can be perfectly solvent and still fail purely because it cannot raise cash quickly enough. This is the distinction between *solvency* (do my assets exceed my liabilities?) and *liquidity* (can I meet my cash demands right now?). A run is a liquidity death, and it can kill a solvent bank.

#### Worked example: the run math on a fragile deposit base

Take a bank with \$100 billion of deposits, of which 60% (\$60 billion) is uninsured. It holds \$15 billion in cash and easily-sellable assets — a 15% liquidity buffer, which is healthy by normal standards. Then a scare hits.

- Day 1: uninsured depositors panic. If just one-third of them flee, that is \$60bn × 33% = **\$20 billion** out the door — more than the bank's entire \$15 billion buffer, in a single day.
- The bank is now \$5 billion short and the run is only accelerating. It must fire-sell long-term loans and bonds at a loss to raise cash, which crystallizes losses, which scares depositors further, which accelerates the run.

Compare a bank with the *same* \$15 billion buffer but only 10% uninsured deposits (\$10 billion). Even if every single uninsured depositor flees on day one, that is \$10 billion — comfortably inside the \$15 billion buffer. The bank survives the same scare untouched. The two banks have identical size, identical buffers, and identical loan books. The only difference is the *composition* of the deposits — and it is the entire difference between surviving and failing. The intuition: a liquidity buffer is sized against your flightiest deposits, not your average ones, and uninsured money is the flightiest of all.

This is why regulators force banks to hold a **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)** — enough high-quality liquid assets to survive 30 days of a modeled stress run. The LCR explicitly weights deposits by how likely they are to flee: insured retail deposits get a low assumed run-off (a few percent), while uninsured and wholesale deposits get punishingly high run-off assumptions. The regulation is, in effect, the deposit-quality map of this post written into law. A bank with a flightier base is *forced* to hold more idle liquidity, which drags on its returns — another way that a low-quality deposit base costs a bank, even before any run actually happens.

### The digital run changed the speed, not the logic

The classic image of a bank run — depositors queuing around the block — is now obsolete. In 2023, SVB's depositors did not queue; they moved \$42 billion with a few taps on their phones, coordinated by group chats and venture-capital email blasts, in a single day. The old runs took weeks because withdrawing money was slow and physical; the new runs take hours because money moves at the speed of an app. The underlying logic is identical — uninsured depositors fleeing a bank they no longer trust — but the *speed* has collapsed from days to hours. This makes the quality of the deposit base matter more, not less: a bank no longer has the luxury of a slow run during which regulators and rescuers can arrange a bailout. The franchise has to be run-proof *by construction* — cheap, sticky, insured — because there is no longer time to fix a fragile base once the run begins.

## From deposit to loan to spread: the franchise in motion

We now have all the pieces. Let's watch them work together — the cheap, sticky deposit becoming the bank's profit.

The figure below traces the journey of a single deposit. A customer deposits money the bank pays 0.5% on. The bank uses that money to fund a loan it earns 6.5% on. The difference — 6.0 percentage points — is the spread the bank keeps. Repeat that across the entire balance sheet, leverage it roughly 12 times over the thin equity base, and you have the engine of bank profit.

![Pipeline showing a deposit funding a loan and producing a 6 point spread](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-6.png)

The reason this works — the reason the bank can pay 0.5% and lend at 6.5% — is everything we have built up: the deposit is cheap because it is mostly CASA money, it stays cheap through a rate cycle because the deposit beta is low, and it does not run because it is sticky and insured. Strip away any one of those properties and the spread collapses. A bank with expensive deposits has a thin spread. A bank with a high beta loses the spread as soon as rates rise. A bank with flighty deposits loses the *funding itself* in a scare and dies. The franchise is the rare combination of all three: cheap, stable through the cycle, and run-proof.

#### Worked example: the franchise value of a cheap deposit base

How much is a great deposit base actually worth? Let's value one directly. Suppose a bank has **\$100 billion** of cheap, sticky retail deposits that cost it 1.0%. If it did not have those deposits, it would have to fund those same assets in the wholesale market at, say, 4.5%. So the deposits save the bank 4.5% − 1.0% = **3.5 percentage points** a year on \$100 billion.

- Annual funding saving = \$100bn × 3.5% = **\$3.5 billion a year**

That saving is not a one-off; it recurs every year, and because the base is sticky, it is durable and predictable — exactly the kind of cash flow that deserves a high value. To turn a recurring annual cash flow into a present value, you divide by a discount rate (a *discount rate* is the annual return investors demand; for a durable, low-risk stream, a 10% cost of equity is a reasonable figure). So:

- Franchise value = \$3.5bn ÷ 0.10 = **\$35 billion**

![Bar chart of the franchise value of cheap sticky deposits versus market funding cost](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-9.png)

Look at that number again. A \$100 billion deposit base, sitting in unglamorous checking and savings accounts paying almost nothing, is worth on the order of **\$35 billion** — a third of the deposits themselves — just from the funding cost it saves. And that is *before* counting the fees, the cross-sold loans, the cards, and the relationships those deposit customers also bring. This is why, when a bank fails, the buyers fight over the *deposits*, not the loans. When First Republic failed in 2023, JPMorgan did not want it for its mortgage book; it wanted the deposit franchise. The intuition: the deposit base is the asset; the loans are just what the bank happens to do with it.

## How cheap funding drives the whole margin

Step back and watch the cheap-deposit franchise show up in the one number that summarizes a bank's spread business: the **net interest margin**, or NIM — net interest income divided by the bank's earning assets. NIM is the spread expressed as a percentage of the assets that earn it.

The chart below traces US bank NIM across a full rate cycle. Watch what cheap funding does to it.

![Line chart of US bank net interest margin from 2010 to 2024 showing the 2021 trough and 2023 rebound](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-7.png)

In 2021, with the Fed's policy rate pinned near zero, NIM bottomed at about **2.56%** — the lowest in the modern record. There was no spread to be had: assets earned almost nothing, so even free deposits could not produce much margin. Then the Fed hiked more than five percentage points in eighteen months. Banks' assets repriced upward fast, while their deposit costs — thanks to low early-cycle beta and sticky cheap deposits — rose far more slowly. By 2023 the industry NIM had jumped to roughly **3.30%**.

That swing of about three-quarters of a percentage point was driven *almost entirely by the funding side staying cheap while assets repriced*. A bank with a flighty, high-beta deposit base could not have captured that jump — it would have had to pay up to keep its deposits, surrendering most of the windfall. The banks that profited most from the hiking cycle were precisely the ones with the best retail deposit franchises: cheap, sticky, low-beta money that let them keep the spread.

#### Worked example: the same hike, two deposit bases

Take two banks with identical \$50 billion asset books, both of which see their asset yield rise from 3.0% to 5.5% as rates climb — a 2.5-point increase.

**Bank Sticky** has a deposit beta of 0.3. Its deposit cost rises by 0.3 × 2.5% = 0.75 points, from 0.4% to 1.15%.

- New spread = 5.5% − 1.15% = 4.35%; old spread = 3.0% − 0.4% = 2.6%
- Spread widened by 1.75 points → extra income = \$50bn × 1.75% = **\$875 million a year**

**Bank Flighty** has a deposit beta of 0.8. Its deposit cost rises by 0.8 × 2.5% = 2.0 points, from 0.4% to 2.4%.

- New spread = 5.5% − 2.4% = 3.1%; old spread = 2.6%
- Spread widened by only 0.5 points → extra income = \$50bn × 0.5% = **\$250 million a year**

Same hike, same assets — and Bank Sticky captures \$875 million while Bank Flighty captures only \$250 million. The \$625 million difference is the cash value of a sticky, low-beta deposit franchise in a single year of a rising-rate cycle. The intuition: when rates rise, your deposit base decides whether you feast or merely nibble.

## Common misconceptions

**"A bank makes its money by lending; deposits are just the raw material."** This gets the franchise exactly backwards. Almost any institution can lend money — fintechs, funds, and shadow banks do it constantly. What makes a *bank* special, and durably profitable, is its access to cheap, sticky, insured deposits. The deposit base is the scarce, valuable asset; lending is the commodity activity built on top of it. That is why, in our worked example, two banks with identical loan books differed by \$2.8 billion a year purely on funding cost. The money is made on the liability side.

**"Higher interest rates are always good for banks."** Only for a while, and only if your deposits are sticky. Rising rates help a bank *as long as its deposit beta stays low* — its assets reprice up faster than its funding cost. But as a cycle matures, beta climbs (it went from 0.10 to 0.55 in the last US cycle), depositors demand their share, and the early windfall fades. A bank with a flighty, high-beta base can actually be *squeezed* by rising rates as it is forced to pay up to keep its funding. Rates are a tailwind early and a test later.

**"Deposit insurance means no bank can ever have a run."** Insurance stops *insured* depositors from running — and that is enormously stabilizing. But it does nothing for *uninsured* deposits above the \$250,000 limit. A bank funded heavily by large uninsured balances is just as run-prone as a bank in 1929, because the people with millions at stake have every reason to flee at the first sign of trouble. Silicon Valley Bank, with 94% of its deposits uninsured, proved this in 36 hours in 2023. Insurance protects the small saver; it does not protect a bank that built its funding on big, nervous money.

**"All deposits are equally good funding."** A dollar of free, insured checking money and a dollar of brokered term deposit are wildly different to a bank. The checking dollar costs nothing, stays for years, and will not run; the brokered dollar costs near the market rate, leaves the moment its term ends and a better rate appears, and is the first thing to flee in a scare. This is why the *composition* of deposits — the CASA ratio, the insured share, the retail-versus-wholesale mix — matters far more than the headline total. Two banks with the same deposit total can have funding bases worth completely different amounts.

**"A bank that pays more for deposits is being generous to customers."** Usually it is a warning sign. A bank that has to offer above-market deposit rates is typically a bank that *cannot* attract cheap, sticky money on its reputation and convenience alone — so it is buying funding with rate. That higher-cost, rate-shopped money is also the flightiest: it came for the rate and will leave for a better one. Chasing deposits with price is often the tell of a weakening franchise, not a strengthening one. Watch for a bank whose cost of deposits is rising faster than its peers'.

## How it shows up in real banks

### Silicon Valley Bank, March 2023: the wrong kind of deposit base

SVB is the textbook case of a deposit base that was cheap but neither sticky nor insured. The bank had grown explosively by banking venture-capital-backed tech startups, who parked enormous balances — far above the \$250,000 insurance limit — from their funding rounds. By early 2023, an astonishing **94%** of SVB's deposits were *uninsured*. The base looked great in good times: large, low-cost balances. But it had two fatal flaws. It was concentrated in a single, tightly networked community (one VC's panicked email could reach the whole client base in minutes), and it was overwhelmingly uninsured, so every depositor had a powerful reason to run at the first hint of trouble.

When word spread on March 9, 2023, that the bank had taken losses on its bond portfolio and needed capital, depositors tried to pull **\$42 billion in a single day** — about a quarter of the bank's \$175 billion deposit base. A further \$100 billion was queued to leave the next morning before regulators seized the bank. There was no slow, friction-driven run; this was a digital, networked, uninsured run that emptied the bank in roughly 36 hours. The lesson is precise and brutal: cheap deposits are worthless if they are not sticky, and uninsured, concentrated deposits are the opposite of sticky. SVB had a funding base that was an asset in calm weather and a fatal liability in a storm. (For the full anatomy of this collapse and its Credit Suisse counterpart, see [the SVB and Credit Suisse 2023 bank runs](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs).)

### First Republic, 2023: when the buyers fight over the deposits

A few weeks after SVB, First Republic Bank failed too — at \$229 billion in assets, the second-largest US bank failure in history. First Republic had a high-touch, high-net-worth client base and, like SVB, a large share of uninsured deposits that fled once confidence broke. But the telling part is what happened in the auction. When the FDIC sold the failed bank, the prize that JPMorgan and others competed for was not First Republic's loan book — much of it was low-yielding jumbo mortgages written when rates were low, actually worth *less* than face value in a high-rate world. The prize was the deposit relationships and the franchise. JPMorgan paid for the right to keep banking those customers. It is the clearest possible demonstration that, in a bank, the deposit franchise *is* the value, and the loans are sometimes a liability you take on to get it.

### The 2022–2023 deposit-beta squeeze across the industry

Beyond the failures, the whole US banking industry lived through the deposit-beta story in real time during the 2022–2023 hiking cycle. In 2022, as the Fed began raising rates, banks barely moved deposit rates — beta near 0.10 — and reported soaring net interest income; their funding stayed cheap while loans repriced up. By 2023, depositors had woken up. Money-market funds offering 5% pulled hundreds of billions out of low-paying bank accounts. To stem the outflow, banks were forced to raise deposit rates, and cumulative beta climbed past 0.50. The banks with the stickiest retail bases — large diversified banks with millions of small insured accounts — held their beta lowest and kept the most margin. The banks with rate-sensitive, commercial-heavy, or uninsured bases saw their funding costs balloon and their margins squeezed. Same rate environment, completely different outcomes, all decided by the quality of the deposit franchise.

### Northern Rock, 2007: what happens with too few deposits

The mirror image of the cheap-deposit franchise is a bank that does not have one. Northern Rock, a UK mortgage lender, funded only a minority of its lending with retail deposits; the rest came from short-term wholesale money markets. When those markets froze in the summer of 2007, Northern Rock could not roll over its funding. It had no deep, sticky deposit cushion to fall back on, and the resulting panic produced the first run on a British bank since 1866 — images of depositors queuing around the block. The bank was nationalized. The lesson is the franchise stated in the negative: a bank that skimps on the slow, expensive work of gathering a real retail deposit base, and leans instead on cheap-looking wholesale funding, is building on sand. The deposit franchise is hard to build precisely because it is so valuable — and the banks that take the shortcut around it are the ones that fail when funding markets seize.

### The transaction-banking deposit machine

A quieter, happier real-world case: the corporate transaction-banking businesses inside the large banks. When a big bank runs a corporation's payroll, collections, supplier payments, and cash management, the corporation must keep large operating balances in accounts at that bank. Those balances are deposits — and they are extraordinarily sticky, because the company cannot easily move its entire payments operation. They are also cheap, because the company holds them for operational convenience, not for yield. This is why banks fight so hard for corporate transaction-banking mandates that, on the surface, earn modest fees: the real prize is the river of cheap, sticky operating deposits that comes with the relationship. It is the same franchise logic — cheap and sticky money — applied to the corporate side. (The mechanics of how those loans the deposits fund get underwritten in the first place are covered in [the lending business, end to end](/blog/trading/banking/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end).)

## A map of deposit quality

Pulling the threads together, we can draw the map every bank analyst carries in their head: which deposits are the franchise and which are the danger. It runs along two axes — who owns the money (retail, business, or wholesale) and whether it is insured.

![Matrix mapping insured versus uninsured and retail versus wholesale deposit stickiness](/imgs/blogs/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise-8.png)

The top-left corner — small, insured, retail deposits — is the gold standard: low beta, stays in a scare, the bedrock of the franchise. The bottom-right corner — uninsured wholesale and brokered money — is the danger zone: it flees on any bad news and is the funding that has killed bank after bank. Most real banks sit somewhere in between, and the whole art of treasury management is shifting the balance toward that top-left corner: more insured retail, less uninsured wholesale, a higher CASA ratio, a lower deposit beta. A bank's funding *quality* is not one number; it is its position on this map. Two banks with identical deposit totals can sit in completely different corners — and be worth completely different amounts.

#### Worked example: scoring two banks' funding quality

Put the map to work. Two banks each have \$200 billion of deposits.

**Bank Prime:** 80% retail, 90% of balances insured, CASA ratio 70%, estimated through-cycle deposit beta 0.30. Its blended cost of deposits in a high-rate environment lands around 1.4%, and in a scare you would expect maybe 5–10% of deposits to leave — a stress it can easily survive with normal liquidity buffers.

**Bank Fragile:** 30% retail, only 40% of balances insured, CASA ratio 40%, estimated deposit beta 0.70. Its blended cost of deposits runs around 2.9% — more than double Bank Prime's — and in a scare, with 60% of its deposits uninsured, it could plausibly lose 30–40% of its base in days, the kind of outflow that no liquidity buffer survives.

The annual cost difference alone — 2.9% versus 1.4% on \$200 billion — is \$200bn × 1.5% = **\$3 billion a year**. But the deeper difference is survival: Bank Prime's base is run-proof and Bank Fragile's is not. When you analyze a bank, you are really scoring where its deposits sit on this map, on both dimensions at once: how cheap (cost and beta) and how safe (insured and retail). The intuition: the deposit base is the first thing to read and the last thing that protects the bank.

## The takeaway: how to use this

If you take one idea from this entire post, make it this: **a bank's franchise is its deposit base, not its loan book.** Everything else — the lending, the fees, the markets businesses — is built on top of cheap, sticky, insured retail money, and is only as durable as that foundation. The bank that funds itself at 1% while its competitor funds at 3% has already won, before either of them makes a single loan.

So when you look at any bank — as an investor, a customer, a curious reader, or someone trying to spot the next failure — read the funding side first, and read it along three dimensions:

- **How cheap?** Look at the CASA ratio and the cost of deposits. A high CASA ratio (say, above 60%) and a low, peer-beating cost of funds mean the franchise is strong. A bank paying up for deposits is a bank without one.
- **How durable through a cycle?** Look at deposit beta. A bank that holds its beta low while rates rise has sticky, high-quality money and will capture the spread when others cannot. A bank whose deposit costs balloon as rates climb is being squeezed by its own funding base.
- **How safe in a scare?** Look at the insured share and the retail-versus-wholesale mix. A base of small, insured, retail deposits is run-proof. A base of large, uninsured, concentrated, or wholesale money is a March-2023 collapse waiting for a headline. SVB's 94% uninsured share was the number that mattered, and almost no one was looking at it until it was too late.

Those three readings — cheap, durable, safe — are the whole diagnosis of a deposit franchise, and through it, of the bank. They tie directly back to the spine of this series: a bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine, and the deposit base is *both* the cheap funding that makes the maturity-transformation profitable *and* the confidence that, if it ever breaks, ends the machine. Cheap money is the franchise. Sticky money is survival. Insured money is the difference between a slow rainy day and a 36-hour death. Read a bank's deposits and you have read the bank — its profit, its resilience, and its risk of dying — all at once.

The educational point, not advice: the next time your bank pays you 0.1% on your checking account and you feel mildly cheated, remember what you are actually doing. You are handing the most valuable raw material in finance to an institution that will turn it into a third of its own value — and you are, in a small way, a shareholder in the most profitable and most fragile trade in the economy.
