> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A loan is not a moment of approval; it is a manufactured asset with a seven-stage life. The bank finds a borrower (*origination*), checks whether they can and will repay (*underwriting*), decides yes/no/yes-with-strings (*credit decision*), signs and funds it (*documentation and disbursement*), then watches and collects on it for years (*servicing, monitoring, collection*). Every stage exists to make sure the interest the bank earns is more than the losses it eventually eats.
>
> - **Underwriting is the whole game.** A loan is priced and approved once, but it lives for years; the bank cannot reprice it after the borrower's life falls apart. The quality of the decision at stage 2 determines whether the asset earns or destroys money.
> - **Two numbers do most of the deciding:** the *debt-service coverage ratio* (can the cash flow cover the payment, with cushion?) and the *loan-to-value ratio* (how much equity sits below the bank's claim if it has to seize collateral?).
> - **A bank prices every loan to beat its own expected loss.** *Expected loss = PD × LGD × EAD* — the chance of default, times the fraction lost if it defaults, times the amount owed at that moment. The interest spread has to cover that *before* it earns a profit.
> - **Loans are the dominant earning asset on a bank's balance sheet** — about half of total assets at a typical universal bank. The lending business is, quite literally, most of what a bank *is*.
> - The number to remember: on a one-million-dollar BBB-grade loan, the bank's expected loss is roughly **\$1,080 a year** — small in any single year, but the lifeblood of the business is keeping it small while charging enough spread to cover it many times over.

In the spring of 2007, two American banks made the same kind of loan to the same kind of borrower: a self-employed buyer with a thin file, a stated income nobody checked, and a property worth not much more than the mortgage. One bank booked it at a *loan-to-value* of 95% — meaning the loan was 95% of the home's value, so the buyer had almost no equity in it. The other declined that structure and would lend only at 80%, demanding the buyer put real money down. Both loans looked identical on the day they were signed. Both paid interest on schedule for a year. Both were, on paper, performing assets earning a healthy spread.

Then house prices fell. The 95% loan was instantly *underwater* — the borrower owed more than the house was worth — and when the borrower's income wobbled, walking away cost them nothing. The 80% loan still had a 20% equity cushion beneath the bank's claim; the borrower had skin in the game and kept paying, and even the ones that defaulted left enough collateral to repay the bank in full. The difference between those two outcomes was not luck, and it was not the economy. It was a decision made years earlier, in an invisible back-office process called *underwriting*, that nobody outside the bank ever saw.

This is the strange truth about lending: a bank earns its money in tiny, steady increments over many years, but it makes the decision that determines whether it earns or loses that money in a single moment at the start. A loan is the bank's core *earning asset* — the thing on the left side of its balance sheet that actually generates income. But it is an asset the bank *manufactures*, one application at a time, through a production line that turns a phone call into a booked, monitored, collectible promise to repay. This post walks that production line end to end.

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: a loan moves left to right through seven stages, from finding a borrower to either retiring the loan cleanly or chasing the money when it goes bad. The blue stages build the asset, the green stage is the asset earning its keep, and the red stages are where a bad decision at stage 2 comes home to roost. We will walk each box in order. By the end you will be able to read a loan the way a credit officer does — not as a number on a statement, but as a living asset with a history, a structure, and a set of ways it can quietly kill the bank that made it.

## Foundations: the language of lending, from zero

Before we follow a single loan, we need a shared vocabulary. A bank, at its heart, is a *maturity-transformation machine*: it borrows money short-term — your deposit, which you can withdraw any time — and lends it long-term, like a five-year business loan or a thirty-year mortgage, and it keeps the difference between what it earns on the loan and what it pays on the deposit. That difference is the *spread*, and the spread is the whole business. (For the deep version of why this trade is both profitable and structurally fragile, see [what a bank actually does](/blog/trading/banking/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread).) Lending is the half of that trade where the money goes *out* and, with luck, comes back with interest. Let me define every term you will meet, in plain English, before any of it appears in a formula.

**Origination.** This is the act of *creating* a loan: finding a borrower, taking in their application, and starting the file. A loan is "originated" when it enters the bank's pipeline as a real request, not when it is approved. Origination is sales and intake — the front door of the lending factory. Banks originate loans through branches, loan officers, websites, mortgage brokers, car dealers, and corporate relationship managers; the channel shapes the cost and the quality of what comes in.

**Underwriting.** This is the assessment: the bank's judgment of whether the borrower *can* repay (do they have enough income or cash flow?) and *will* repay (do they have the history and the incentive?), and on what terms. The word comes from old marine insurance, where a person who agreed to bear a risk "wrote their name under" the contract — they *under-wrote* it. To underwrite a loan is to decide, on the bank's behalf, that the risk is acceptable at a given price. It is the single most important stage in the whole lifecycle, and most of this post lives inside it.

**Credit risk.** This is the risk that the borrower fails to pay back what they owe — the core risk of any loan. It is not the only risk a bank runs (there is also interest-rate risk, liquidity risk, and operational risk), but it is the one the lending business is built to manage. Everything in underwriting exists to measure, price, and limit credit risk. (For the broader system view of how credit risk is priced in the bond market, see [credit spreads: pricing the probability of default](/blog/trading/fixed-income/credit-spreads-pricing-the-probability-of-default).)

**Default.** This is the event the bank is afraid of: the borrower stops paying as agreed. In practice a loan is usually declared in default after a set period of missed payments — commonly *90 days past due* for many loan types — at which point the bank must treat it as a problem asset rather than a performing one. Default does not always mean the bank loses everything; what it loses depends on the collateral and the recovery process, which we will get to.

**The credit memo.** This is the written case for a loan — the document an underwriter prepares that lays out who the borrower is, what they want, how they will repay, what backs the loan, and what could go wrong. For a meaningful loan, a human (or a committee of humans) reads the memo and approves or rejects it. The memo is where the bank's judgment is recorded; it is the paper trail that says "we looked, and here is why we said yes."

**The credit committee.** This is the group of senior people who approve loans above a certain size. Small loans are decided automatically or by a single officer; large loans go to a committee, because a single bad large loan can do real damage to the bank's thin equity cushion. The committee is a control: it forces a second, third, and fourth set of eyes onto the biggest risks, and it spreads the accountability so that no one person can quietly book a disaster.

**Servicing.** This is everything that happens *after* the money goes out: collecting the payments, sending the statements, holding the collateral, tracking whether the borrower is keeping their promises, and handling the loan when something goes wrong. A loan can be sold to another institution while a *servicer* continues to collect on it; servicing is a business in its own right. If underwriting is the decision, servicing is the long, quiet job of keeping the asset alive and the cash flowing.

**Collateral and the loan-to-value ratio.** *Collateral* is something of value the borrower pledges that the bank can seize and sell if the loan goes bad — a house, a car, equipment, inventory. The *loan-to-value ratio*, or LTV, is the loan amount divided by the value of the collateral, expressed as a percent. A \$80,000 loan against a \$100,000 house is an 80% LTV; the remaining 20% is the borrower's equity and the bank's safety margin. Lower LTV means more cushion below the bank's claim.

**The debt-service coverage ratio.** *Debt service* is the periodic payment a borrower owes — principal plus interest. The *debt-service coverage ratio*, or DSCR, is the borrower's available cash flow divided by that required payment. A DSCR of 1.40 means the borrower generates \$1.40 of cash for every \$1.00 of payment they owe — a 40% cushion. A DSCR of 1.00 means no cushion at all: any dip in income and the borrower can't pay. DSCR is the single best measure of whether a borrower can actually *afford* the loan.

With those nine terms in hand, we can follow a loan through every stage. We will use a running example: a small business owner, "Mai," who walks into a bank wanting to borrow \$1,000,000 to buy a commercial property she will rent out. We will underwrite her, decide on her, fund her, monitor her, and — in one version of the story — collect on her. Everything else is detail layered on top of that spine.

## Stage 1 — Origination: finding the borrower and taking the application

A loan starts not with money but with a *want*. Mai wants \$1,000,000. The bank's job at origination is to capture that want, turn it into a structured application, and decide whether it is even worth underwriting before spending money analyzing it.

Origination is, before anything else, a *sales and channel* problem. Where do borrowers come from? A retail bank originates mortgages through its branches, its website, and independent mortgage brokers; it originates car loans through dealerships (the dealer arranges the financing on the showroom floor); it originates credit cards through mailings and apps; and it originates business loans through *relationship managers* — bankers whose job is to know the local business owners and bring their borrowing to the bank. Each channel has a different *cost of acquisition* and, crucially, a different *quality of borrower*. A loan that walks in through a long banking relationship is, on average, a better risk than one bought from a broker who is paid per loan closed and bears none of the consequences if it goes bad. The 2008 mortgage crisis was, in large part, a story of origination channels — broker-originated, "originate-to-distribute" loans — where the people making the loans had no stake in whether they were ever repaid.

At origination, the bank collects the raw material of the decision: who the borrower is, how much they want, what for, for how long, and what they will pledge. It runs a fast, cheap *pre-screen* — often an automated check of a credit score and a few ratios — to weed out applications that are obviously too risky before a human spends hours on them. This is why a credit-card application can be approved in seconds: the bank has pre-computed the decision rules and the application either clears the bar or it doesn't.

The deeper point is that *origination quality caps loan quality*. No amount of careful underwriting downstream can fix a pipeline full of bad applicants; the bank can only decline them, which wastes the cost of originating them. Banks that grow their loan book fastest are often the ones loosening their origination standards to keep the pipeline full — which is exactly why *rapid loan growth* is one of the classic early-warning signs of a bank heading for trouble. When you see a bank's loans growing far faster than its peers', the question to ask is: where are all these new borrowers coming from, and what did the bank stop checking to find them?

#### Worked example: the cost of a thin pipeline

Suppose a bank originates 100 business-loan applications at a cost of \$2,000 each in loan-officer time, credit reports, and processing — \$200,000 spent at the front door. If its underwriting standards are sound, perhaps 40 of those clear and become loans; the \$200,000 origination cost is spread across 40 booked loans, or \$5,000 per loan. Now suppose the bank, hungry for growth, loosens the pre-screen so 70 of the 100 clear. The cost per booked loan falls to about \$2,857 — origination looks *more* efficient. But the 30 extra loans it approved are, by construction, the weaker 30 it used to reject. If even a third of them default and the bank loses \$300,000 each, that is \$3,000,000 of credit losses chasing a \$60,000 saving in origination cost. **The lesson: cheap origination that lowers the bar is the most expensive thing a bank can do, because the bill arrives years later as credit losses, not upfront as processing cost.**

## Stage 2 — Underwriting: can they repay, and will they?

This is the heart of the lending business, and it splits into two questions that have been asked, in one form or another, for as long as people have lent money: *can* the borrower repay, and *will* they? Capacity and character. Everything in underwriting is an attempt to answer those two questions with evidence rather than hope.

The framework most banks use to organize this is the **Five Cs of credit**: Character (will they repay — their history and reputation), Capacity (can they afford it — their cash flow), Capital (do they have skin in the game — their own money at risk), Collateral (what backs the loan if it fails), and Conditions (what the loan is for and what the economy is doing). The Five Cs are not a formula; they are a checklist for thinking, a way of making sure the underwriter looks at the borrower from every angle before deciding. The deep dive on how the Five Cs map to a scoring model and an approval lives in [credit analysis: the five Cs and how a loan gets approved](/blog/trading/banking/credit-analysis-the-five-cs-and-how-a-loan-gets-approved); here we focus on the two C's that carry the most weight in the actual decision, because they reduce to two numbers a bank can compute and compare across every loan it makes.

![Underwriting decision tree routing one application through capacity collateral and character tests into approve approve with conditions or decline](/imgs/blogs/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end-2.png)

The decision tree above shows the shape of the thing: one application enters, runs through the capacity test, the collateral test, and the character test, and exits as one of three answers — approve, approve with conditions, or decline. Real underwriting is messier than three clean branches, but the logic is exactly this: each test can knock the loan down a level, and a loan that fails the right test fails outright.

### The capacity test: the debt-service coverage ratio

The single most important question in underwriting is whether the borrower's *cash flow* can cover the *payment*. Not their net worth, not their assets — their cash flow, because loans are repaid out of cash, and a borrower who is rich on paper but cash-poor can still miss a payment. The tool is the *debt-service coverage ratio* (DSCR): the cash available to service debt, divided by the debt payment required.

For Mai's commercial property, the cash flow is the net rental income the property will throw off after operating costs, and the debt service is the annual mortgage payment. A bank does not want a DSCR of 1.00 — that means the rent exactly equals the mortgage, with zero room for a vacancy, a repair, or a rent cut. It wants a *cushion*. For income-property loans, banks commonly require a DSCR of at least 1.20 to 1.25; for stronger structures they look for 1.35 to 1.50. The cushion is the whole point: it is the margin by which the borrower's income can fall before they can no longer pay.

#### Worked example: a DSCR that decides the loan

Mai's property will generate \$95,000 a year in net operating income (rent, minus taxes, insurance, and maintenance). She wants to borrow \$1,000,000 at 7% over 25 years, which works out to an annual debt-service payment of roughly \$84,800. Her DSCR is:

$$\text{DSCR} = \frac{\text{net operating income}}{\text{annual debt service}} = \frac{\$95{,}000}{\$84{,}800} \approx 1.12$$

A DSCR of 1.12 means Mai's property covers its mortgage with only a 12% cushion. If rents fall 12% — one tenant leaves, or the market softens — she is at break-even, and any further dip and she cannot pay. Most banks would *decline this structure*, not because Mai is a bad person, but because 12% is not enough room. The underwriter has two moves: shrink the loan, or demand more cushion. If the bank lends Mai \$850,000 instead of \$1,000,000, the annual debt service falls to about \$72,100, and her DSCR rises to \$95,000 / \$72,100 ≈ 1.32 — comfortably above the 1.25 floor. **The lesson: the DSCR doesn't just say yes or no; it tells the bank the largest loan it can safely make to this borrower, which is why underwriting so often ends in "approve, but for less than you asked."**

### The collateral test: the loan-to-value ratio

The second decisive number is the *loan-to-value ratio* (LTV): the loan amount divided by the value of whatever backs it. Capacity tells you whether the borrower can pay; collateral tells you what the bank recovers if they *can't*. The two are complements — a strong borrower needs little collateral, weak collateral demands a stronger borrower — and a good loan usually has both.

For Mai's \$850,000 loan against a property appraised at \$1,180,000, the LTV is:

$$\text{LTV} = \frac{\text{loan amount}}{\text{collateral value}} = \frac{\$850{,}000}{\$1{,}180{,}000} \approx 72\%$$

That 72% means there is 28% equity — about \$330,000 — sitting *below* the bank's claim. If Mai defaults and the bank has to seize and sell the property, prices would have to fall more than 28% before the bank failed to recover its loan in full. LTV is the bank's *margin of safety against a fall in collateral value*, just as DSCR is its margin of safety against a fall in income.

#### Worked example: how LTV turns a default into a loss — or doesn't

Take two versions of Mai's loan. In version A, the bank lends \$850,000 against the \$1,180,000 property: 72% LTV, \$330,000 of equity below the loan. In version B, a more aggressive bank lends \$1,121,000 against the same property: 95% LTV, only \$59,000 of equity below the loan. Now suppose a recession knocks the property's value down 25%, to \$885,000, and Mai defaults.

- **Version A (72% LTV):** the bank seizes a property now worth \$885,000 and is owed \$850,000. After selling costs of, say, 8% (\$70,800), it nets \$814,200 against an \$850,000 claim — a loss of about \$35,800, or roughly 4% of the loan. Painful, but survivable.
- **Version B (95% LTV):** the bank seizes the same \$885,000 property but is owed \$1,121,000. After the same 8% selling cost it nets \$814,200 against a \$1,121,000 claim — a loss of about \$306,800, or roughly 27% of the loan.

Same borrower, same property, same recession. The *only* difference is the LTV chosen at underwriting, and it turns a 4% loss into a 27% loss. **The lesson: collateral is not a yes/no — it is a dial, and where the bank sets the LTV dial at underwriting determines how much of a downturn it can absorb without bleeding.**

### Character, capital, and conditions: the rest of the case

Capacity and collateral are the two numbers, but a loan is not approved on numbers alone. *Character* — the borrower's track record, captured for individuals in a credit score and for companies in their payment history and reputation — answers "will they pay even when it's hard?" *Capital* — the borrower's own money in the deal — answers "do they have something to lose?" A borrower who put 30% down behaves very differently in a downturn than one who put 5% down, even at the same LTV, because they have more to walk away from. *Conditions* covers the purpose of the loan and the state of the world: a loan to expand a restaurant means something different in a boom than in a recession, and an underwriter who ignores the macro backdrop is underwriting yesterday's economy.

The underwriter weaves all five C's into a single document — the credit memo — that we will look at in detail when we reach the approval stage. The output of underwriting is not just "yes" or "no" but a *structure*: this much money, at this rate, for this term, with these conditions, backed by this collateral. Underwriting is design, not just judgment.

## How a bank prices the loan: the spread must beat the expected loss

Before the loan can be approved, it has to be *priced* — and pricing is where the lending business reveals its true nature. A bank does not charge a rate because the borrower can afford it; it charges a rate that, across thousands of loans, covers everything the lending business costs *and* leaves a margin. The deepest of those costs is the one most beginners overlook: the bank *expects* to lose money on lending, every year, as a normal cost of doing business. The art is making sure the interest more than covers that expected loss.

The tool for measuring it is the most important equation in credit, and it has three parts:

$$\text{Expected loss} = \text{PD} \times \text{LGD} \times \text{EAD}$$

- **PD** is the *probability of default* — the chance, over a year, that the borrower stops paying. A pristine borrower might have a PD of 0.06%; a shaky one, 5% or more.
- **LGD** is the *loss given default* — the fraction of the loan the bank actually loses *if* default happens, after recovering collateral. A senior secured loan might lose 25%; an unsecured one, 45% or more. LGD is where collateral and LTV pay off: better security means lower LGD.
- **EAD** is the *exposure at default* — how much the borrower owes at the moment they default. For a term loan that is roughly the outstanding balance; for a credit line it includes the amount they are likely to have drawn.

Multiply the three and you get the *expected loss*: the average amount the bank loses per year on this loan, blending the cases where it pays fine (most of them) with the rare cases where it defaults. The deep mechanics of PD, LGD, and EAD — how banks estimate them, how they shift over the credit cycle — live in the risk track; here we use them to do the one thing that matters at underwriting: make sure the price beats the loss.

![Bar chart of one year default probability rising steeply from AAA to CCC on a log scale](/imgs/blogs/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end-3.png)

The chart above shows the first ingredient — the probability of default — across internal credit ratings, on a log scale. The jump is enormous: a AAA borrower defaults about once in 10,000 in a year (0.01%), while a CCC borrower defaults about once in four (26%). Default risk is not linear in quality; it explodes as you go down the scale. This is why a bank cannot simply "charge a bit more" for a weak borrower and call it covered — the extra risk it is taking on is not a bit more, it is orders of magnitude more.

![Horizontal bar chart of loss given default by seniority and security from senior secured to subordinated](/imgs/blogs/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end-4.png)

The second ingredient, loss given default, is shown above. A senior secured loan — first in line, backed by good collateral — loses about 25% in a default, because the collateral covers most of the claim. An unsecured retail loan loses about 55%, and a subordinated claim about 65%, because there is little or nothing to seize. This is the dollar payoff of all that work on collateral and LTV at underwriting: every point of LGD the bank shaves off through good security flows straight into a lower expected loss and a lower required rate.

#### Worked example: expected loss on Mai's loan

Mai's restructured loan is \$850,000, secured by a commercial property at a comfortable 72% LTV. Suppose her business and the structure put her at an internal rating of BBB, with a one-year PD of 0.24%, and the good security gives an LGD of 35% (better than the 45% unsecured baseline because the collateral is solid). Her exposure at default is the full \$850,000. The expected loss is:

$$\text{EL} = \text{PD} \times \text{LGD} \times \text{EAD} = 0.0024 \times 0.35 \times \$850{,}000 \approx \$714 \text{ per year}$$

\$714 a year. That is the average annual cost of Mai's credit risk — tiny against an \$850,000 loan, because most years she simply pays. **The lesson: expected loss is small in any single year, which is exactly the trap — it tempts a bank to underprice credit risk, right up until a recession arrives and a year's worth of defaults all land at once.**

![Bar chart of expected loss in dollars per year on a one million dollar loan rising from AAA to CCC on a log scale](/imgs/blogs/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end-5.png)

The chart above puts the whole expected-loss equation together on a standard \$1,000,000 loan at a 45% LGD, across the rating scale. The numbers are stark: a AAA loan carries an expected loss of \$45 a year; a BBB loan, \$1,080 a year; a B loan, \$24,750; a CCC loan, \$117,000 a year. The same dollar of credit can be almost free or ruinously expensive depending on who borrows it — and a bank that charges every borrower the same rate is, in effect, having its good borrowers subsidize its bad ones until the bad ones leave and the book rots.

#### Worked example: the spread versus the expected loss

Now we close the loop — this is how a loan is actually priced. The interest rate Mai pays has to cover four things: the bank's *cost of funds* (what it pays depositors and lenders for the money it hands her), its *expected loss*, its *operating cost* of making and servicing the loan, and a *capital charge plus profit margin* for putting its thin equity at risk. Suppose the bank's all-in cost of funds is 4.0%. On Mai's \$850,000 loan:

- Cost of funds: 4.00% × \$850,000 = \$34,000/yr
- Expected loss: \$714/yr (from above), which is 0.08% of the loan
- Operating + servicing cost: about 0.50% × \$850,000 = \$4,250/yr
- Capital charge + target margin: about 2.00% × \$850,000 = \$17,000/yr

Add them: \$34,000 + \$714 + \$4,250 + \$17,000 = \$55,964, which on an \$850,000 loan is a required rate of about **6.58%**. The bank quotes Mai 7%, giving itself a little extra. Notice the expected loss — the credit risk everyone worries about — is the *smallest* line, \$714 against a \$55,964 total. That is true *on average*, in a normal year. The reason the bank still obsesses over it is that expected loss is the line that *moves* in a recession: cost of funds and operating cost are stable, but a downturn can multiply the expected loss tenfold as PDs jump across the whole book at once. **The lesson: a loan's price is built so the spread covers the expected loss several times over — the cushion isn't there for the average year, it's there for the bad one, when every loan's PD rises together.**

## Stage 3 — The credit decision: the memo and the committee

Once a loan is underwritten and priced, someone has to *decide*. For a small, standardized loan — a credit card, a small personal loan, a conforming mortgage — the decision is made by a model: the application's numbers go into a scoring engine, and if they clear pre-set thresholds, it is approved automatically. Speed and consistency are the goal; a human would only add cost and bias. But for a loan large enough to hurt the bank, the decision goes to people, and the instrument of that decision is the *credit memo*.

The credit memo is the written case for the loan. It is the underwriter's argument, in a standard format the decision-makers can read quickly: who the borrower is, what they want and why, how they will repay (the DSCR analysis), what backs the loan (the collateral and LTV), the risk rating and expected loss, the proposed structure and price, and — crucially — *what could go wrong and how the bank is protected if it does*. A good memo does not hide the risks; it surfaces them and shows how the structure mitigates each one. The memo is also the bank's *record*: if the loan later goes bad, the memo is what regulators and the bank's own auditors read to judge whether the decision was reasonable at the time.

![Credit memo matrix mapping the five Cs to the question the evidence and the deciding number](/imgs/blogs/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end-7.png)

The matrix above is the skeleton of a credit memo: each of the Five Cs becomes a row, and each row pairs a question with the evidence that answers it and the single number that settles it. Character → the credit score. Capacity → the DSCR. Capital → the borrower's equity. Collateral → the LTV. Conditions → the covenants and limits the loan will carry. A reader can scan that grid and know in thirty seconds whether the loan hangs together.

For loans above a size threshold, the memo goes to the *credit committee* — a group of senior credit officers who approve or reject it, sometimes after sending it back for more information or a tighter structure. Why a committee? Because of the spine of this whole series: a bank runs on a *thin equity cushion*, and a single large bad loan can eat a meaningful slice of it. (For why that cushion is so thin and so load-bearing, see [bank capital and leverage: why equity is the thin cushion](/blog/trading/banking/bank-capital-and-leverage-why-equity-is-the-thin-cushion).) The committee exists to make sure no individual — no matter how senior or how eager to hit a growth target — can single-handedly book a loan big enough to damage the bank. It is a *control*, in the same family as the requirement that the people who originate loans are different from the people who approve them, who are different again from the people who fund and monitor them. Separating those roles is how a bank stops its own salespeople from approving their own deals.

The committee's three possible answers are the three leaves of our decision tree. *Approve*: book the loan as proposed. *Decline*: the risk is unacceptable at any price the borrower would accept. And the most interesting one, *approve with conditions*: yes, but only if the borrower accepts a smaller amount, a higher rate, a personal guarantee, an extra piece of collateral, or a *covenant* — a binding promise to maintain some financial condition (say, "keep DSCR above 1.25" or "do not take on more debt"). Conditions are how a bank turns a "no" into a "yes" by re-engineering the risk until it fits. A huge fraction of real-world credit decisions land here: not rejection, but reshaping.

## Stage 4 — Documentation and disbursement: turning a yes into a booked asset

A "yes" from the committee is not a loan. Between approval and money changing hands sits a stage that beginners underestimate and lawyers obsess over: *documentation and disbursement*. This is where the decision becomes a legally enforceable, properly secured asset on the bank's balance sheet — and where sloppiness can quietly destroy the value of an otherwise good loan.

Three things have to happen. First, the *loan agreement* is drafted and signed: the legal contract specifying the amount, rate, term, repayment schedule, covenants, and what happens on default. This is the document the bank will rely on in court if it ever has to enforce the loan. Second — and this is the part that gets skipped at everyone's peril — the bank must *perfect its security interest* in the collateral. "Perfecting" means taking the legal steps that make the bank's claim on the collateral enforceable against the world and senior to other claimants: registering a lien, filing the right public notice, recording the mortgage. An unperfected security interest is collateral on paper only; in a bankruptcy, the bank can find itself standing behind other creditors on an asset it thought it owned first. The entire LGD calculation — the recovery the bank counted on — depends on this paperwork being done right.

Third, the money is *disbursed*: funds are released to the borrower, and the loan is *booked* — recorded as an asset on the bank's balance sheet at the moment the obligation becomes real. From this instant, the loan is an *earning asset*: it accrues interest, it consumes a slice of the bank's capital (because regulators require capital to be held against it as a buffer), and it joins the population of loans the bank must monitor. The funding for that disbursement comes from the bank's deposit base — the cheap, sticky money that makes lending profitable in the first place. (For why a low-cost deposit base is the whole franchise, see [retail deposits: the funding base and why cheap money is the franchise](/blog/trading/banking/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise).)

The deep point of this stage is that *a loan's value is only as good as its documentation*. A perfectly underwritten loan with a flawed lien is a worse asset than a mediocre loan that is airtight. The 2010-2011 US "robo-signing" scandal — where banks foreclosing on mortgages were found to have signed and filed legal documents without proper review — was, at root, a documentation failure: the asset (the mortgage) was real, but the paperwork that made it enforceable was not, and it cost the banks billions and stalled foreclosures for years. Disbursement is the least glamorous stage and one of the most expensive to get wrong.

## Stage 5 — Servicing: the long, quiet life of an earning asset

The money is out. Now begins the part that lasts years: *servicing*. This is the operational machinery of being owed money — sending statements, collecting payments, applying them correctly to principal and interest, holding and tracking the collateral, handling the borrower's questions, and producing the data the bank needs to know whether the loan is still healthy. Servicing is unglamorous and enormous; for a bank with millions of loans, it is a major operation in its own right, and it is often outsourced or sold separately from the loan itself.

![Asset mix bar chart showing loans as the largest share of total bank assets](/imgs/blogs/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end-8.png)

The chart above shows why servicing matters so much: at a typical universal bank, *loans are about half of all assets* — the single largest category, larger than securities, cash, and everything else combined. The lending business is not a line item; it is the core of what a bank owns. Every one of those loans is being serviced, every day, and the quality of that servicing — whether problems are caught early, whether payments are applied right, whether collateral values are tracked — directly determines how much the bank actually collects on its biggest asset.

During servicing, a performing loan throws off the spread the bank priced for. Mai pays her mortgage every month; the bank books interest income, applies part of the payment to principal (slowly shrinking the exposure at default), and the loan quietly does its job as an earning asset. Most loans, most of the time, live their entire lives here and never leave — they amortize down to zero and are retired cleanly, and the bank's only job was to collect. The boring outcome is the profitable one. But the bank cannot *assume* every loan stays boring, which is why servicing shades into the next stage: monitoring.

## Stage 6 — Monitoring: watching for the loan that is about to turn

A bank that only finds out a loan has gone bad when the payment stops has waited too long. By then the borrower's situation has usually been deteriorating for months, and the bank has lost the chance to act while there was still something to do. *Monitoring* is the discipline of watching a loan for *early warning signs* of trouble — the tells that come before a default, while the bank can still restructure, demand more collateral, or quietly reduce its exposure.

What does a bank watch? For a business loan, it tracks the borrower's financial statements against the covenants in the loan agreement: is the DSCR still above the required floor? Is the company taking on more debt than it promised? Are sales falling? It watches for *behavioral* tells: a borrower who starts paying late, or who suddenly draws down a credit line to the limit, is often a borrower in distress. For a whole portfolio, the bank watches concentrations — too many loans to one industry, one region, one type of collateral — because a single shock can turn many individual loans bad at once. And it re-rates loans periodically: a borrower who was BBB at origination might be downgraded to BB as their business weakens, which raises their estimated PD and, through the expected-loss equation, raises the provisions the bank must set aside against them.

This is also where the accounting catches up to the credit risk. Under modern rules — *IFRS 9* internationally and *CECL* in the United States — a bank must recognize *expected credit losses* on its loans and update that estimate as conditions change, building reserves *before* losses are realized rather than waiting for the default. The mechanics of that staging and provisioning are deep enough to deserve their own treatment; see [collateral, security, and loan-loss provisioning: IFRS 9 and CECL](/blog/trading/banking/collateral-security-and-loan-loss-provisioning-ifrs9-and-cecl). For our purposes the point is that monitoring is not passive watching — it directly drives the numbers in the bank's accounts, moving loans between "healthy" and "watch" and "impaired" and forcing the bank to take the hit on paper as the risk rises.

![Two loans through a downturn comparing thin underwriting that defaults against disciplined underwriting that performs](/imgs/blogs/the-lending-business-how-a-bank-underwrites-a-loan-end-to-end-6.png)

The before-and-after above is the lesson of monitoring and underwriting combined. Two loans enter a downturn. The thinly-underwritten one — 1.05 DSCR, 95% LTV, stated income — has no cushion: when cash flow falls 20%, its DSCR drops below 1, it defaults, and with little equity below the loan the bank takes a real loss. The disciplined one — 1.40 DSCR, 70% LTV, verified income and a guarantee — absorbs the same 20% shock and keeps paying, and even if it slips, the equity below it absorbs the loss. *The downturn is identical.* The only difference was the structure set years earlier at underwriting and the diligence of monitoring in between. This is the spine of the post in one picture: the loss a bank takes in a recession was largely decided in the boom, one underwriting decision at a time.

## Stage 7 — Collection and recovery: when the loan goes bad

Despite everything, some loans default. The borrower stops paying, the covenants are breached, the early-warning signs were missed or the shock was too big. Now the loan enters its final stage: *collection and recovery*, the process of getting back as much of the money as possible. This is where LGD — that abstract number from the pricing stage — becomes a concrete, painful reality.

Collection runs along a spectrum from gentle to terminal. At the soft end, the bank's collections team contacts the late borrower, works out a temporary payment plan, or *restructures* the loan — extending the term, lowering the payment, capitalizing missed payments — to get the borrower back on track. A restructured loan that keeps performing is far more valuable than a seized asset; banks generally prefer to keep a borrower paying than to foreclose, because foreclosure is slow, expensive, and recovers less. This is why so much of collection is, paradoxically, about *keeping the loan alive* rather than killing it.

When restructuring fails, the bank moves to *recovery*: it enforces its claim. For a secured loan, this means seizing and selling the collateral — foreclosing on the property, repossessing the car, liquidating the equipment. This is the moment all that documentation work pays off (or doesn't): a properly perfected lien lets the bank take the collateral ahead of other creditors; a flawed one leaves it fighting in line. The bank's recovery is the sale price, minus the costs of seizing and selling, minus the time value of the months or years the process takes. Whatever it *doesn't* recover is the realized loss — the LGD made real. For an unsecured loan there is no collateral to seize, so recovery means pursuing the borrower's other assets or selling the defaulted debt to a collections agency for cents on the dollar.

Finally, when a loan is deemed uncollectible, the bank *writes it off*: it removes the loan from its assets and recognizes the loss against the reserves it built up during monitoring. A write-off is not a surprise loss if the bank did its job — it should have been provisioned for in advance, so the actual write-off just confirms the reserve. The full machinery of classifying, restructuring, recovering, and writing off problem loans — the *workout* process — is involved enough that it gets its own treatment in the non-performing-loans post of this series. The takeaway here is that collection is the bank *eating the cost it priced for years earlier*. If underwriting was good, the recovery is decent and the loss is small; if underwriting was bad, the recovery is poor and the loss is the kind that, multiplied across a book full of bad loans, sinks banks.

## Common misconceptions

**"A bank makes money by approving as many loans as possible."** No — a bank makes money by approving the *right* loans and pricing them so the spread beats the expected loss. Loan volume with bad underwriting is the fastest route to failure: the income from a loan is a thin spread earned over years, while the loss from a default can be a large chunk of principal lost at once. A bank that grows its book 30% a year by lowering its standards is not building a franchise; it is accumulating losses that haven't been recognized yet. Recall the worked example: a \$60,000 saving on origination led to \$3,000,000 of credit losses. Volume without discipline is a liability dressed as growth.

**"Collateral means the loan is safe."** Collateral lowers the *loss given default*, not the *probability of default*, and only if it is properly valued, properly perfected, and holds its value in the exact downturn that would also cause the borrower to default. The trouble is that collateral values tend to fall *at the same time* borrowers default — a recession hits both — so the bank is seizing depreciated assets from defaulting borrowers precisely when it most needs the recovery. A 95% LTV loan is risky no matter how nice the collateral, because the cushion is too thin to survive the correlated downturn. Collateral is a second line of defense, not a substitute for a borrower who can pay.

**"The interest rate is the bank's profit."** Most of the interest rate is *cost*, not profit. Recall Mai's loan: of the roughly 6.58% required rate, 4.00% was the bank's own cost of funds, and the rest covered expected loss, operating cost, and a capital charge before any margin. The spread the bank actually keeps — net of its funding, its losses, and its costs — is a fraction of the headline rate, often one to two percentage points. A loan that looks like it earns 7% might net the bank far less once everything it has to cover is subtracted.

**"Underwriting is just running a credit score."** A credit score is one input — it mostly informs the *character* C. It says nothing about whether the borrower's cash flow covers the payment (capacity/DSCR), how much equity sits below the loan (collateral/LTV), or whether the loan's purpose makes sense in the current economy (conditions). A borrower with a perfect score can still be a terrible loan if the DSCR is 1.02 and the LTV is 98%. Reducing underwriting to a score is exactly the kind of shortcut that fills a pipeline with loans that look fine and behave badly.

**"Once a loan is approved, the bank's job is done."** The decision is made once, but the asset lives for years, and most of the bank's actual work — servicing, monitoring, collecting — happens *after* approval. A loan that is approved well but serviced badly (payments misapplied, warning signs missed, collateral untracked) can still turn into a loss. The lending business is not a series of approvals; it is the long-term management of a population of earning assets, and the approval is just the moment they are born.

## How it shows up in real banks

**The 2008 mortgage machine — origination without underwriting.** In the years before 2008, large parts of the US mortgage industry ran on an *originate-to-distribute* model: loans were originated by brokers, sold to banks, packaged into securities, and sold on to investors. (The mechanics of that packaging are covered in [securitization: ABS and turning loans into bonds](/blog/trading/fixed-income/securitization-abs-and-turning-loans-into-bonds).) Because the originator did not keep the loan, it had little reason to underwrite it carefully — the entire incentive structure of the lifecycle was broken at stage 1. "Stated income" loans (no verification of capacity), 100%-LTV loans (no equity cushion), and teaser-rate loans whose payments would jump later all became common. When house prices fell, the loans that had been originated without real underwriting defaulted in waves, and the LGD on high-LTV loans against falling collateral was brutal. The crisis was, at its root, a failure of the lending lifecycle: every control that this post describes had been bypassed in the name of volume.

**Wells Fargo and the limits of the spread.** A bank's lending discipline is only as good as the incentives of the people running the lifecycle. The Wells Fargo fake-accounts scandal — though it was about accounts, not loans — showed what happens when sales targets override controls: employees gamed the system to hit numbers. The same dynamic, applied to lending, produces a book full of loans booked to hit growth targets rather than because they were good credits. The lesson the underwriting committee structure is built to enforce is that *the people who are paid to grow the book cannot be the only ones who decide what goes into it.* When that separation breaks, the lifecycle's controls become theater.

**The Vietnamese and Asian property-loan cycles.** Across many fast-growing economies, banks have repeatedly run into trouble by lending heavily against property at high LTVs during booms, only to find — when prices stall — that the collateral they counted on is worth far less than the loans against it, and that developers' cash flows (the DSCR) never supported the debt in the first place. The pattern is always the same: rapid loan growth concentrated in one collateral type, underwriting that leaned on rising collateral values instead of borrower cash flow, and a monitoring function that noticed the deterioration too late. It is the before-and-after figure of this post, played out at the scale of an entire banking system.

**Credit cards — underwriting at the speed of a swipe.** At the other end of the spectrum from Mai's bespoke commercial loan sits the credit card, where the entire seven-stage lifecycle runs in milliseconds and in bulk. Origination is an app or a mailing; underwriting is a scoring model; the decision is automatic; "disbursement" is a credit line the borrower draws as they spend; servicing is a monthly statement; and collection, when it fails, is a charge-off and a sale to a debt collector. The economics work because the law of large numbers makes the *expected loss* predictable across millions of accounts — the bank doesn't need to be right about any one cardholder, just right on average. But the same lifecycle logic applies: when card issuers loosen underwriting to chase growth, charge-offs rise a year or two later, exactly as the model predicts.

**The 2023 commercial-real-estate squeeze.** When interest rates rose sharply in 2022-2023, a wave of commercial-property loans came up for refinancing into a world of much higher rates and, in some sectors, much lower property values. Loans underwritten in the cheap-money era at aggressive DSCRs and LTVs suddenly didn't cover their new, higher payments, and the collateral behind them — especially office buildings — had fallen in value, raising LGD. Banks with concentrations in this sector found the early-warning signs flashing across whole portfolios at once. It was a live demonstration that a loan's risk is not fixed at origination: the *conditions* C — the macro environment — can turn a well-behaved loan into a problem years later, which is precisely why monitoring never stops until the loan is repaid.

## The takeaway: a loan is a decision that ages

The thing to carry away from all of this is that a loan is not a transaction; it is a *decision that ages*. The bank commits in a single moment — at underwriting and approval — to a stream of cash flows that will play out over years, through an economy it cannot predict, with a borrower whose life it cannot control. It earns its money slowly, in a thin spread, and it can lose that money quickly, in a single default. The entire seven-stage lifecycle is built to make that asymmetric bet survivable: origination to find the right borrowers, underwriting and pricing to make sure the spread beats the expected loss, the memo and committee to stop any one person from booking a disaster, documentation to make the claim enforceable, and servicing, monitoring, and collection to manage the asset all the way to its grave.

This is why, when you look at a bank, the lending business is the place to look first. Loans are about half of what a bank owns; they are the core earning asset, and they are the asset most able to destroy the bank's thin equity cushion. So when you read a bank's results, do not just look at the interest income — look at the *quality* of the loan book: how fast it grew (fast growth is a yellow flag), how concentrated it is (in one sector, one collateral type), how its underwriting standards have moved, and how its provisions are trending. A bank that is growing its loans quickly and easing its standards is building tomorrow's losses today; a bank that is lending carefully into a downturn, with discipline on DSCR and LTV, is building the asset that will carry it through.

The lending business connects straight back to the spine of this whole series. A bank borrows short and lends long, earns the spread, and survives only as long as its thin equity cushion can absorb losses faster than they arrive. The loan is where those losses are born — and underwriting is the moment the bank decides how big they will be. Everything else in lending is just the long process of living with that decision.

*This is educational, not financial advice — it explains how banks make and manage loans, not what you should borrow or how a particular bank is run.*
