> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A bank used to be one bundled thing: it held the charter, ran the ledger, reached the payment rails, issued the cards, did the lending, ran compliance, and owned you, the customer. The last decade has pried those functions apart into separate, API-callable building blocks. **Open banking** lets an app read your data and push a payment from your account. **Banking-as-a-Service** lets a non-bank rent a licensed bank's charter and rails through an API. **Embedded finance** puts that rented banking *inside* an app that has nothing to do with banking. The bank still holds the money and the license — but the app owns the customer, and when the middleware in the middle fails, it turns out almost nobody knows whose money is whose.
>
> - **Open banking** (the EU's PSD2 rules made it law) splits the bank into two new services: **account information** (read my balances and transactions) and **payment initiation** (push money straight from my account, no card).
> - **Banking-as-a-Service** is a licensed **sponsor bank** renting its charter, ledger, and rails to a fintech through an API; the fintech gets to "be a bank" without a banking license, and the bank earns fees on someone else's customers.
> - **Embedded finance** is a non-bank — a shopping platform, a payroll app, a ride-hailing company — offering banking *inside* its own product, so the customer never knowingly touches a bank at all.
> - The one number to remember: in the **2024 Synapse collapse**, a BaaS middleware company failed and roughly **\$85 million** of end-customer money could not be located against the bank's records, freezing more than **100,000 people** out of their accounts — because the only ledger that mapped customers to dollars was the broken middleware's, not the bank's.

In the spring of 2024 a piece of software almost nobody had heard of stopped working, and tens of thousands of ordinary Americans suddenly could not get to their own money. They had downloaded slick fintech apps — savings apps, teen-banking apps, paycheck-advance apps — deposited their wages, and watched a clean balance tick up. What they did not know was that their app was not a bank. It was a brand sitting on top of a "Banking-as-a-Service" middleware company called Synapse, which itself sat on top of a small licensed bank in Tennessee. When Synapse went bankrupt, the money was still, mostly, *somewhere* — sitting in big pooled accounts at the sponsor banks. The problem was that the only detailed record of which customer owned which slice of that pool lived inside Synapse's systems, and that record no longer reconciled. The bank held the cash but could no longer trust the map. As of the bankruptcy filings, somewhere around **\$85 million** of customer money was unaccounted for, and over **100,000 people** were locked out of their balances for months.

That episode is the dark side of the most important structural change in modern banking: the **unbundling** of the bank. For a curious reader with no banking background, the easiest way in is to notice that "a bank" is not one thing. It is a stack of separate jobs that happened, for a century, to live inside one regulated company. Software has been quietly pulling those jobs apart and selling them one at a time — as **APIs**, the digital plumbing that lets one company's software call another's. This post is about how that happens, who ends up owning the customer, and where the money and the risk really sit when the wiring is invisible.

![Unbundling a bank from one bundled institution into separate API-callable building blocks](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole article. On the left is the old, bundled bank: one regulated entity that does everything from holding the license to owning the customer relationship. On the right is the same set of functions, but now each one is a separate service that can be rented over an API — the charter rented to fintechs, the ledger exposed through a Banking-as-a-Service interface, payments offered through an open-banking interface, and crucially, the *customer relationship* captured by whatever app the person actually opens. Keep this picture in your head: the functions did not disappear, they got pulled apart and re-sold, and the question that decides who wins (and who is on the hook when it breaks) is which layer ends up owning the customer.

This connects directly to the spine that runs through this whole banking series: **a bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine that borrows short, lends long, and survives only as long as depositors trust it.** Unbundling does not repeal that. The deposits still fund loans; the thin equity cushion still has to absorb losses faster than they arrive. What unbundling changes is *who the depositor thinks they are dealing with* — and that turns out to matter enormously, because trust and the customer relationship are the franchise. A bank that rents out its rails but keeps the customer is selling a commodity. A bank that loses the customer to an app on top of it has quietly become a back-end utility, paid a thin fee to carry someone else's risk. Whether that is a good trade is one of the central questions of the next decade of banking.

## Foundations: the words people use loosely, defined precisely

Before we go anywhere, we need a small, exact vocabulary, because the marketing around fintech mashes all of these terms together and they mean genuinely different things. Read this section slowly; everything below is just these ideas interacting.

**An API is a way for one program to ask another to do something.** *API* stands for *application programming interface*, but forget the words — it is a **menu of requests** one piece of software exposes so other software can use it without knowing how it works inside. When your weather app shows the forecast, it does not run a weather model; it calls a weather service's API ("give me today's forecast for this zip code") and gets back a tidy answer. A banking API is the same idea: a menu of requests like "create an account", "show this account's balance", "move \$50 from account A to account B", "issue a card". The whole story of this post is what happens when the things a bank does become items on a menu that any developer can call.

**A bank charter is a government license to be a bank.** This is the thing that makes a bank a bank, legally. A *charter* (or banking license) is permission, granted by a regulator, to take deposits from the public and to access the core banking infrastructure — the central-bank reserve account, the payment networks, deposit insurance. It comes with heavy obligations: you must hold capital, follow anti-money-laundering rules, file reports, and submit to examination. Getting a charter is slow, expensive, and rare. That scarcity is the whole reason Banking-as-a-Service exists: if you cannot easily *get* a charter, you rent one.

**Open banking is the rule that your bank must share your data and let others move your money — with your permission.** *Open banking* is the principle that the data and payment capabilities of *your* accounts belong, in effect, to *you*, and that your bank must — when you consent — let trusted third parties access them through secure APIs. It turns the bank from a walled garden into something other apps can plug into. In Europe this stopped being a nice idea and became law under a regulation called **PSD2**.

**PSD2 is the European law that forced open banking into existence.** *PSD2* stands for the *Second Payment Services Directive*, an EU law that took full effect around 2018–2019. Its key move was to require banks to open two specific doors, under strict security, to licensed third parties that the customer has authorized. Those two doors are the next two definitions, and they are the heart of open banking.

**Account information (AIS) is the read-only door: see my balances and transactions.** An *account information service* (AIS) is a licensed third party that, with your consent, can *read* your account data across all your banks — balances, transaction history — and show it to you in one place. A budgeting app that pulls in all your accounts to show your total net worth is an AIS. It can look; it cannot move money.

**Payment initiation (PIS) is the write door: push money straight from my account.** A *payment initiation service* (PIS) is a licensed third party that, with your consent, can instruct *your* bank to send a payment directly from your account — no card, no card network in the middle. When a checkout offers "pay directly from your bank account" and you approve it by logging into your own bank, a PIS made that happen. It cannot read your data beyond what it needs; it can *push* a payment.

**Banking-as-a-Service (BaaS) is a licensed bank renting its charter and rails to a non-bank through an API.** *Banking-as-a-Service* is the arrangement where a fully licensed bank — the **sponsor bank** — packages its regulated capabilities (holding deposits, issuing cards, moving money on the rails) and offers them to other companies as an API. A startup with no banking license can call that API and offer "a bank account" or "a debit card" to its users, while the actual deposits, the actual license, and the actual regulatory responsibility stay with the sponsor bank. The startup gets to look like a bank; the sponsor bank gets fees and deposits.

**A sponsor bank is the licensed bank that stands behind a fintech.** The *sponsor bank* (sometimes "partner bank") is the chartered institution whose license the fintech is borrowing. It is typically a small or mid-sized community bank that has decided BaaS is a growth business. Legally and to the regulators, the sponsor bank *is* the bank — it owns the compliance duty, holds the deposits, and answers for what its fintech partners do. That asymmetry — the small bank carries the risk while the fast-moving fintech carries the brand — is the source of nearly every BaaS disaster.

**Embedded finance is banking offered inside a non-bank's app, where the user never knowingly touches a bank.** *Embedded finance* is what you get when a company whose core business is *not* banking — a shopping platform, an accounting app, a ride-hailing service — uses BaaS to put financial products directly inside its own product. The driver who gets paid instantly into a wallet inside the ride-hailing app, the small merchant who takes a loan offered right inside its e-commerce dashboard: that is embedded finance. The bank is there, holding the money, but it is invisible to the user, who simply trusts the app.

**Unbundling is splitting the bank's functions apart; rebundling is an app reassembling them under one brand.** *Unbundling* is the trend this whole post describes: each function of a bank gets pulled out and offered separately. *Rebundling* is the counter-trend: a fintech that started by doing one thing well (say, payments) gradually adds savings, cards, lending, and investing until it looks like a full bank again — but a bank it assembled out of rented parts and a brand customers like. The pendulum swings both ways at once.

**The customer-ownership question is: whose app does the person actually open, and whose name do they trust?** This is the quiet question that decides who captures the value. The licensed bank always holds the money. But the company whose logo is on the app, whose support line the customer calls, whose brand the customer trusts — *that* company "owns the customer", earns the loyalty, captures the data, and keeps most of the fee. In traditional banking the bank owns the customer. In BaaS and embedded finance, the app does. The bank can end up as a low-paid utility carrying the risk for a relationship it does not own.

That is the whole dictionary. Now let's watch these ideas do real work.

## Open banking: turning the bank into something other apps can plug into

Start with the gentler of the three forces, because it is the one most regulators actively pushed. For most of banking history, your account was a walled garden. The only software that could see your balance or move your money was your own bank's app or website. If a budgeting startup wanted to show you all your accounts in one screen, it had to resort to "screen scraping" — literally logging in as you with your password and reading the page like a robot. That was fragile, insecure, and the banks hated it.

Open banking replaced that hack with a front door. The idea is simple and, once you see it, obviously fair: *your* financial data and *your* ability to pay are yours, not the bank's property, so you should be able to grant a trusted app access to them through a secure, standardized API — and the bank must build that door and keep it open. Europe made it mandatory with PSD2; the UK built a tightly standardized version; other places (Brazil, Australia, India's account-aggregator framework) followed with their own flavors. The United States moved more by market force than by a single law, though a federal open-banking rule has been working its way through the system.

The two doors PSD2 opened map exactly to the two things you can do with an account: look at it, and pay from it.

The **account information service (AIS)** is the read door. With your one-time consent, an AIS can fetch your balances and transactions from every bank you use and present them together. This is the technology under every "see all your money in one app" experience. The value is convenience and insight, not movement — an AIS is a window, not a wire.

The **payment initiation service (PIS)** is the more radical door, because it threatens a very profitable business: cards. A PIS can instruct your bank to push a payment directly from your account to a merchant. You approve it by authenticating at your *own* bank (PSD2 requires **strong customer authentication** — typically two factors, like a password plus a fingerprint or a one-time code). No card number is entered anywhere. No card network sits in the middle taking a cut. The money goes account-to-account.

![Open-banking payment initiation flow from checkout through consent and strong authentication to a direct bank push](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-3.png)

Walk the figure left to right. At checkout you choose "pay by bank". A licensed PIS asks you to approve a specific payment — \$100 to this merchant, once. You are bounced to your own bank's login and complete strong authentication. The PIS then calls your bank's payment-initiation API, and the bank pushes the money as an instant account-to-account credit transfer. The merchant is paid with final money and no card fee was charged. Notice what is *not* in the picture: a card network. That absence is the entire economic point, and it is worth a worked example.

#### Worked example: a payment initiation vs card rails cost

You run an online store and sell a \$100 item. Compare what you pay to get paid.

On **card rails**, the merchant pays a *merchant discount rate* — the total cut taken out of each card sale. Using the representative US split from this series' data, that rate breaks down into **interchange** (the fee paid to the customer's card-issuing bank) of about **1.75%**, a **network fee** (Visa/Mastercard) of about **0.13%**, and the **acquirer's markup** (your own payment processor's cut) of about **0.42%**. Add them up: 1.75% + 0.13% + 0.42% = **2.30%**. On a \$100 sale, that is **\$2.30** gone. On \$1,000,000 of annual sales, **\$23,000**.

On an **open-banking push**, there is no interchange and no network fee, because no card and no card network are involved. The PIS charges a small, often flat, per-payment fee — call it **0.20%** on this basket, or **\$0.20** on the \$100 sale. On \$1,000,000 of sales that is about **\$2,000**.

The difference: roughly **\$21,000 a year saved** on a million dollars of sales, an order-of-magnitude cut in payment cost.

The intuition: the card networks' profit is the toll you pay for a payment to hop across a four-party system; open banking lets the money go straight from one bank account to another, so the toll mostly disappears.

![Merchant cost of a card payment versus an open-banking account-to-account push](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-9.png)

The chart makes the gap visceral: a card sale costs the merchant about 2.30% — most of it the interchange that flows to the customer's bank — while an account-to-account push costs a fraction of that. That is why open-banking payments are growing fastest exactly where card fees bite hardest: large bills, recurring payments, account top-ups, and any flow where the merchant, not the consumer, is price-sensitive. (If you want the full mechanics of how money actually crosses between banks once it has been initiated, this series covers it in [the payments business](/blog/trading/banking/the-payments-business-how-money-actually-moves-between-banks).)

### Consent, security, and why the door is narrow

It is tempting to hear "the bank must share your data" and assume it means a free-for-all where anyone can rummage through your account. The reality is the opposite: the open-banking door is deliberately narrow, and the narrowness is the point. Three guardrails make it work.

The first is **consent, scoped and revocable.** A third party gets access only to the specific things you approve — read your current account but not your savings, or initiate this one payment but not pull money again next month — and only for a limited time, after which it must ask again. You can revoke the access from your own bank at any time, the way you can revoke an app's access to your phone's camera. The bank, not the third party, controls the door.

The second is **licensing.** An AIS or a PIS is not just any app. To call a bank's open-banking APIs under PSD2, the third party must itself be authorized and supervised by a financial regulator. That is what makes "let a trusted third party move my money" tolerable: the third parties are not anonymous; they are licensed entities the regulator can punish.

The third is **strong customer authentication** — the two-factor login at your own bank that we met in the flow diagram. The PIS never sees your banking password; you authenticate directly with your bank, and only the *result* (an authorized payment) flows back. The sensitive credential never leaves the bank's control.

These guardrails explain why open banking has spread without producing a wave of account-draining fraud, and why it took a *law* to make it happen. Banks had every incentive to keep the garden walled; only a regulation forcing standardized, secure APIs could pry the door open while keeping it safe. The friction that remains is mostly technical and commercial — every bank's API behaves a little differently, so aggregators spend real effort smoothing over the inconsistencies — but the security model itself has held up.

But — and this is where the spine reasserts itself — open banking does **not** unbundle the deposit. The money still sits in your bank account, funding your bank's loan book, right up until the instant it is pushed. Your bank is still the licensed deposit-taker. Open banking pries off the *interface* and the *payment movement*, not the balance sheet. It is the mildest form of unbundling: the bank stays a bank and merely loses its monopoly on the front-end. The other two forces go much deeper.

## Banking-as-a-Service: renting the charter

Now the deep cut. Open banking lets an app *read your bank* and *push from your bank*. Banking-as-a-Service lets an app *be a bank* — without being one.

Here is the problem BaaS solves. Suppose you have a brilliant idea for a financial app: a debit card for teenagers, a savings app for gig workers, a business account for freelancers. To offer any of it, you legally need to take deposits, issue cards, and move money on the rails — and to do *those* things you need a banking charter, which takes years and millions of dollars to obtain, if a regulator will grant you one at all. For a startup, that is a non-starter. The charter is the moat, and the moat is the point.

BaaS turns that moat into a rental property. A licensed bank — the sponsor — builds (or buys) an API layer over its regulated capabilities and rents them out. You, the fintech, call the API to open accounts, issue cards, and move money. The deposits land on the *sponsor bank's* balance sheet. The license stays the sponsor's. The compliance duty stays the sponsor's. You provide the brand, the app, the user experience, and the customer-acquisition machine. To your users, you look exactly like a bank. Behind the screen, you are a thin layer of software calling someone else's charter.

Most BaaS relationships have a third party in the middle, and it is the one that caused all the trouble: the **middleware** (also called a "BaaS platform" or "program manager"). The sponsor bank's own systems are usually old, slow, and not built for ten startups to plug into at once. So a software company sits between the bank and the fintechs, translating the bank's clunky core into one clean, modern API and handling much of the day-to-day operations — including, fatefully, a **sub-ledger** that tracks which end customer owns which dollars. Remember that sub-ledger; it is the time bomb.

![The Banking-as-a-Service stack from sponsor bank through middleware and fintech app to the end customer](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-2.png)

The figure shows the full stack. At the bottom, the **sponsor bank** holds the charter, the FDIC insurance, the real deposit accounts, the payment rails, and — critically — the legal compliance liability. The **BaaS middleware** wraps the bank's rails and ledger in a clean API and keeps its own sub-ledger of who owns what. The **fintech app** sits on top with the brand, the slick onboarding, and the growth engine — and it takes the largest share of the economics. At the very top is the **end customer**, who sees only the app and, in the typical case, has no idea which bank is holding their money. Trace the value: the bank does the heavy regulated lifting and gets paid the least; the app does the marketing and gets paid the most. That inversion is exactly why BaaS is attractive to fintechs and seductive — and dangerous — to the small banks chasing the fee income.

#### Worked example: the BaaS revenue split between sponsor, middleware, and fintech

Let's put numbers on who keeps what. Take a debit-card program built on BaaS. Say a customer spends **\$10,000** a year on the card. The program earns money mainly from **interchange** — the fee merchants pay every time the card is swiped, which flows back to the card-issuing side. For a small-bank debit program (which, under the US "Durbin" rules, keeps the higher unregulated interchange), assume a blended **1.0%** of spend ends up as program revenue. On \$10,000 of spend that is **\$100** of economics to divide.

Now the split. In a typical arrangement, the **sponsor bank** — which holds the license and the deposits and carries the compliance risk — keeps the *smallest* slice, say **\$20** (it also earns separately on the deposits it now funds cheaply, which we will get to). The **middleware** that provides the API and operations takes roughly **\$25**. The **fintech app** that owns the customer and drove the spend keeps the largest share, around **\$55**.

So of the \$100, the customer-facing app keeps **55%**, the middleware **25%**, and the bank that is legally on the hook for everything keeps just **20%**.

The intuition: in BaaS, the party that owns the customer captures most of the money, and the party that owns the license and the risk captures the least — which tells you exactly why a small bank doing this needs the *volume* of many fintechs to make the math work, and why that hunger for volume is what later tempts it to onboard partners it cannot properly supervise.

![Illustrative split of one hundred dollars of program revenue across the fintech app, the middleware, and the sponsor bank](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-5.png)

The chart shows the inversion plainly: the fintech app keeps more than half of each \$100, the middleware a quarter, and the sponsor bank — the only licensed, FDIC-insured, examined entity in the whole stack — keeps a fifth. Now, the bank is not doing this purely for that fifth. There is a second, quieter prize, and it is the one that ties BaaS straight back to the spine of this series.

### Why a small bank does it anyway: the deposit prize

Recall the spine: a bank lives on cheap, stable funding. Its whole business is borrowing at a low rate (deposits) and lending at a higher one (loans), earning the spread. The scarcest, most valuable raw material in banking is a **cheap deposit base**.

BaaS hands a small bank a firehose of deposits it could never have gathered on its own. A community bank in Tennessee with twelve branches has no way to attract a million tech-savvy gig workers. But by powering ten fintech apps, those customers' balances all land on *its* balance sheet — often paying little or no interest, because the fintech, not the bank, sets the customer rate. To the sponsor bank, BaaS deposits can be the cheapest funding it has ever seen, and it can lend that money out at a healthy spread. That is the real prize. The interchange fee is the appetizer; the cheap deposits are the meal.

Put a number on it. Suppose BaaS brings a sponsor bank **\$500 million** in fintech deposits that pay an average customer rate of **0.5%**, so the bank's funding cost on them is \$2.5 million a year. The bank invests that money in safe assets and loans yielding, say, **4.5%**, earning \$22.5 million. The spread is 4.5% − 0.5% = **4.0%**, or **\$20 million a year** on funding the bank acquired through software instead of branches. That is a serious earnings line for a small bank — and it is exactly why the temptation to keep adding fintech partners is so strong, even past the point where the bank can properly supervise them. The deposit prize is real; the supervision bill that comes with it is what banks keep underestimating.

This is also the hidden fragility. Those deposits are *not* sticky in the way a branch customer's checking account is. The customer has no relationship with the bank — they have a relationship with an app. If the app fails, or switches sponsor banks, or its users panic, those deposits can leave en masse, fast, exactly like a digital bank run. The sponsor bank has funded loans with money that belongs, emotionally and operationally, to someone else's customers. (This is the same maturity-transformation trap, just with a more skittish funding base — the mechanism that turns rate risk and deposit flight into a crisis is the one this series dissects in the [interest-rate-risk and duration-gap post](/blog/trading/banking/interest-rate-risk-in-the-banking-book-irrbb-and-the-duration-gap).)

## Embedded finance: when banking disappears into someone else's app

Banking-as-a-Service is the *plumbing*. Embedded finance is what people *build* with it. If BaaS is the question "can a non-bank rent a charter?", embedded finance is the answer "...so that financial products can show up anywhere, inside any app, exactly when you need them."

The defining feature of embedded finance is *contextual invisibility*. The user is not trying to do banking. They are trying to do something else — buy a sofa, get paid for a delivery, run their small business — and a financial product appears, perfectly timed, inside that flow. They tap "pay in 4 installments" at a furniture checkout and a loan is originated in two seconds. A delivery driver finishes a shift and the money is in a wallet *inside the delivery app* instantly, with a debit card they can spend from. A small merchant on an e-commerce platform sees "you're eligible for a \$15,000 working-capital loan" right in the dashboard where they manage orders. In none of these moments does the user think "I am now using a bank." The bank has dissolved into the background.

This is the most complete form of unbundling, because it separates banking not just from the *interface* (open banking) or the *charter* (BaaS) but from the very *idea* that you are dealing with a financial company at all. The brand the customer trusts is the furniture store, the delivery app, the software platform. The bank is a back-end supplier, like the company that makes the store's payment terminals.

It helps to see embedded finance as the *product layer* and BaaS as the *infrastructure layer* of the same stack. BaaS is the wholesale capability — a charter and rails, packaged behind an API. Embedded finance is the retail expression of that capability — the loan button, the in-app wallet, the instant payout — placed at the exact moment in a non-financial journey where a customer needs it. You almost never get one without the other: embedded finance is what BaaS is *for*, and BaaS is what makes embedded finance possible. When a commentator says "embedded finance is eating banking", what they usually mean is that the moment of need — checkout, payday, the order dashboard — is being captured by whoever owns that moment, and that owner is rarely the bank.

Why do non-banks want to do this? Two reasons, and the second is the powerful one.

First, **convenience sells the core product.** A "buy now, pay later" button increases checkout conversion; instant pay keeps delivery drivers loyal; an embedded loan keeps a merchant on the platform. The financial product is a feature that makes the main product stickier.

Second, and more importantly, **financial products carry a far higher take-rate than most core products.** The *take-rate* is the share of a transaction a platform gets to keep. Selling software on subscription might net a platform 1% of the money flowing through it. Embedding payments can lift that to several percent; embedding lending, with its fat margins, higher still. For a platform with millions of transactions, that difference is the whole profit story.

#### Worked example: an embedded-finance take-rate vs a standalone product

Take a software platform for small e-commerce sellers — \$2 billion of merchandise flows through its sellers' stores each year. Compare three ways it can make money on that flow.

On its **core software subscription**, suppose it effectively keeps about **1.0%** of the gross merchandise value as software fees: 1.0% × \$2 billion = **\$20 million** a year. Solid, but capped.

Now it **embeds payments** — processing the card and bank payments for its sellers using a BaaS/payments partner. After paying the sponsor and the rails, it keeps an effective take of about **2.8%** on the payment volume it captures. If it captures even half the flow, \$1 billion, that is 2.8% × \$1 billion = **\$28 million** — already more than the entire software business.

Now it **embeds lending** — offering working-capital advances to sellers, originated by a sponsor bank but underwritten with the platform's unique data on each seller's sales. Lending take-rates are higher still; say an effective **5.5%** net spread on the loan book. On a modest \$300 million of advances, that is 5.5% × \$300 million = **\$16.5 million**.

Stack them: \$20m + \$28m + \$16.5m = **\$64.5 million**, more than triple the software-only business — built on the same merchant relationships, just monetized through embedded banking.

The intuition: the platform's edge is not that it is a better bank; it is that it already owns the customer and the data, so embedding finance lets it earn a banking take-rate on a relationship it acquired for free — which is precisely the relationship the sponsor bank does *not* own.

![Effective platform take-rate for core software versus embedded payments and embedded lending](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-7.png)

The chart shows why every software company with a payment flow is now scheming to embed finance: the take-rate on embedded payments and especially embedded lending dwarfs the thin margin on the core product. But notice the words "after paying the sponsor and the rails" — that margin is shared with the licensed bank underneath. Which brings us back to the question that decides who actually wins.

### Rebundling: the pendulum swings back

Unbundling is only half the story, and the other half confuses people. The same decade that pulled the bank apart has also seen fintechs frantically gluing the pieces back together — a trend the Foundations called **rebundling.** A startup launches doing one thing brilliantly: a money-transfer app, a "buy now, pay later" button, a stock-trading app. Then it adds a debit card. Then a savings balance. Then a credit product, then bill pay, then a brokerage, then crypto. Step by step, the focused fintech reassembles the full menu of a bank — all of it stitched together out of *rented* parts (a sponsor bank's charter, a card issuer's API, a brokerage's API) under one brand the customer already loves.

Why does this happen? Two forces pull in the same direction. The first is economics: a single financial product, sold alone, often barely pays for the cost of acquiring the customer. The profit comes from *the second and third product* sold to a customer you already have. A money-transfer app that adds a card and a savings account multiplies its revenue per user without paying to acquire the user again. The second is stickiness: a customer with one product is easy to leave; a customer whose paycheck, card, savings, and bills all live in your app is not. Rebundling is how a fintech converts a cheap front-end into a durable franchise.

The irony is exact. The unbundlers set out to disrupt the one-stop-shop bank, and the successful ones are racing to become a one-stop-shop bank — just one built on APIs and a beloved brand instead of branches and a charter. The bundle keeps reforming because the *economics* of owning the whole customer relationship are too strong to resist. That is the same economic gravity that built the original banks; software just changed who gets to ride it.

## Who owns the customer — and why the bank can lose even when it holds the money

We now have three models — open banking, BaaS, embedded finance — and one question that cuts across all of them: **who owns the customer?** Because in every single one of these arrangements, the licensed bank holds the money. That never changes; only a chartered bank can take insured deposits. What changes is who the customer thinks they are dealing with, and that is where the value goes.

![Who owns the customer relationship in traditional banking, Banking-as-a-Service, and embedded finance](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-6.png)

The figure makes the split explicit. In all three models, the bank holds the money — it is the licensed deposit-taker underneath everything. But the *trust* flows to whoever's face is on the app. In **traditional banking**, that face is the bank itself: it owns the customer, the data, and the loyalty. In **BaaS**, the face is the fintech, so the fintech owns the customer while the bank is invisible plumbing. In **embedded finance**, the face is a non-bank brand the customer was already using, so an even more removed company owns the relationship. The bank, in the bottom two cases, has rented out the most valuable thing it has — the customer relationship — in exchange for fees and deposits.

This is a profound and slightly alarming inversion of the spine. The whole reason a bank's deposit base is the franchise is that customers *trust the bank* and stick around. Strip away the trust and the relationship, and what is left is a balance sheet for rent — a regulated utility carrying the risk for relationships it does not own and cannot see. A bank that does BaaS at scale has, in a real sense, sold its franchise to its own partners while keeping all the liability.

The four ways the three models differ are worth a single comparison view, because the marketing constantly blurs them.

![Comparison of open banking, Banking-as-a-Service, and embedded finance across what is shared, who is licensed, who holds the money, who owns the customer, and the bank's main risk](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-4.png)

Read the matrix column by column. **Open banking** shares only data and payment initiation; the bank stays the bank, the customer's own bank holds the money, and the bank still owns the customer — its main risk is data misuse and consent abuse. **Banking-as-a-Service** rents out the charter, ledger, and rails; only the sponsor bank is licensed, it holds the money (in pooled "for-benefit-of" accounts, which we will define in a moment), but the fintech app owns the customer — and the bank's main risk is compliance failures and ledger gaps. **Embedded finance** shares a full banking product wrapped inside a non-bank brand; again only the sponsor bank is licensed and holds the money, but now an even-further-removed brand owns the customer, and the bank's deepest risk is being *blind* to the actual end customers it is legally responsible for. That last cell — the bank being legally responsible for customers it cannot see — is where the Synapse disaster lived.

## The compliance and ledger risk: where the money and the danger actually sit

Now we reach the heart of the matter, the part that turns an elegant unbundling story into a cautionary one. When a fintech "offers a bank account" via BaaS, what is that account, really? Almost always, it is **not** an individual account at the bank with the customer's name on it. Instead, the sponsor bank holds one or a few giant pooled accounts called **for-benefit-of accounts** (FBO accounts). All the fintech's customers' money sits commingled in that pool. The detailed record of *which customer owns which dollars within the pool* is kept in a **sub-ledger** — and that sub-ledger is very often maintained by the middleware or the fintech, **not** the bank.

Sit with that for a second, because it is the whole risk in one sentence: **the bank holds the cash, but it may not hold the map of whose cash it is.**

#### Worked example: reconciling a for-benefit-of account

Here is what "reconciliation" means in this world, with round numbers. A sponsor bank holds one FBO account for a fintech with three customers. The bank's books show the FBO account contains **\$1,000,000** — that is the bank's truth: it knows exactly how much real money is in the pool.

The middleware's sub-ledger claims the \$1,000,000 belongs to the customers as: Alice **\$400,000**, Bob **\$350,000**, and Carol **\$250,000**. Add those: 400,000 + 350,000 + 250,000 = **\$1,000,000**. Everything *reconciles* — the sum of the sub-ledger balances equals the cash in the FBO account. The map matches the territory. This check is supposed to run **every single day**.

Now suppose, through sloppy bookkeeping across multiple banks and middleware errors, the sub-ledger one day claims the customers are owed **\$1,000,000** but the FBO account actually holds only **\$915,000**. There is an **\$85,000 shortfall** — money the records say exists but the bank cannot find in the pool. Who is short? Alice, Bob, or Carol? The bank cannot tell, because it never kept the per-customer map; it only ever saw the pooled total. Until the sub-ledger is rebuilt and reconciled, **nobody can be paid**, because paying anyone risks paying them with someone else's money.

The intuition: in pooled FBO banking, the daily reconciliation between the bank's cash and the middleware's customer map is the *only* thing standing between "your balance is real" and "your balance is a number on a broken spreadsheet" — and when that reconciliation is neglected, a solvent pool of cash becomes an unpayable mess.

That \$85,000 in the toy example is not an arbitrary number. Scale it up by a factor of a thousand and you have the **Synapse collapse** of 2024, where the shortfall between what fintech customers were told they had and what could be reconciled against the banks' records ran to roughly **\$85 million**.

![Who holds the deposits and the ledger when a Banking-as-a-Service middleware fails](/imgs/blogs/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance-8.png)

The before-and-after figure shows the failure mode exactly. While the middleware is *working* (left), the bank holds one FBO account, the middleware's sub-ledger names each customer's share, the app shows everyone their live balance, and customers can move their money freely. When the middleware *fails* (right), the bank still holds the pooled cash — the money did not vanish — but the sub-ledger is frozen, its totals no longer match the cash, the app goes dark, and customers are locked out for months while administrators try to rebuild who owns what. The cash being "safe" at the bank is cold comfort to a customer who cannot get to it and may not get all of it back.

There are two more risks layered on top of the ledger problem, and both trace to the same root: **the bank is legally responsible for things it cannot see.**

The first is **compliance risk.** The sponsor bank owns the duty to know its customers (KYC), to watch for money laundering (AML), and to follow consumer-protection rules — for *every* end customer of *every* fintech it powers, even though those customers onboarded through an app the bank does not run. If a fintech does sloppy identity checks, or onboards fraudsters, or lets sanctioned parties open accounts, the *bank* is the one the regulator punishes. Through 2023 and 2024, US regulators issued a wave of enforcement actions against BaaS sponsor banks precisely for failing to supervise their fintech partners' compliance — consent orders demanding the banks fix their oversight or exit the business. The fast-moving fintech took the growth; the slow-moving bank took the fine.

The second is **concentration and funding risk**, the spine again. A small bank that has built a large share of its balance sheet on BaaS deposits from a handful of fintechs has tied its funding to relationships it does not control. If one big fintech partner fails or leaves, a large chunk of the bank's deposits can walk out the door at once — a classic funding shock, on a base far more skittish than branch deposits, against a loan book that cannot be liquidated overnight. It is the maturity-transformation trap wearing a hoodie.

## Common misconceptions

**"My fintech app is a bank, so my money is FDIC-insured."** Not exactly, and the gap is where people got hurt. The *sponsor bank* is FDIC-insured, and FDIC insurance covers a bank *failing*. But in the Synapse case the bank did *not* fail — the *middleware* did. FDIC insurance does not cover a non-bank middleware's bankruptcy or a botched sub-ledger; it covers up to **\$250,000** per depositor if the insured *bank* goes under. When the failure is in the software layer between you and the bank, deposit insurance may simply not apply, and customers can lose money that "felt" insured. The lesson regulators drew is that "FDIC-insured" on a fintech app can be dangerously misleading when the deposits sit in a pooled account and the failure point is the program manager, not the bank.

**"Open banking and Banking-as-a-Service are the same thing."** They are nearly opposites in depth. Open banking shares *data and payment initiation* while the bank stays fully the bank and keeps the customer. BaaS rents out the *entire charter and balance sheet* so a non-bank can hold deposits under the bank's license. One pries off the front-end; the other rents out the whole machine. Conflating them is like confusing "letting a friend read your bank statement" with "letting a stranger open accounts in your bank's name."

**"Embedded finance means the tech company became a bank."** No — that is the entire trick. The tech company is *not* a bank and does not want to be; it rents a sponsor bank's charter through BaaS. It captures the customer and most of the economics precisely *because* it avoided the slow, capital-heavy, regulated work of being a bank. The bank underneath is doing the regulated heavy lifting and getting paid a thin fee. The non-bank wins by *not* becoming a bank.

**"The fintech app holds my deposits."** Almost never. The fintech holds your *attention*; the sponsor bank holds your *money*, usually in a pooled for-benefit-of account, with a sub-ledger somewhere mapping you to your share. The number on your app screen is a claim against a slice of a pool, and it is only as reliable as the daily reconciliation between that sub-ledger and the bank's actual cash. When that reconciliation is sound, the number is real money. When it is neglected, it is a promise on a spreadsheet.

**"Unbundling will kill the banks."** The evidence so far says the opposite of the simple version. The licensed charter turned out to be the one thing the unbundlers *could not* replicate — so they rent it. The bank does not disappear; it risks being demoted from a customer-owning franchise to a low-margin, high-liability utility. The threat to a bank is not extinction but *commoditization*: holding all the risk while owning none of the relationship. Whether a given bank ends up the winner or the utility depends entirely on whether it keeps owning customers.

**"BaaS is just outsourcing — banks have always used vendors."** This is the misconception that lulled some sponsor banks into trouble. Ordinary outsourcing hands a *task* to a vendor while the bank keeps the customer and the controls: a bank that outsources its data center still knows every customer and runs its own compliance. BaaS is different in kind, not degree, because the bank hands over the *customer-facing relationship and the day-to-day operations*, often including the sub-ledger, while keeping the legal liability. The bank is responsible for customers it never onboarded, watching transactions it does not directly control, through a partner whose incentives (grow fast) and the bank's (stay safe) are not aligned. Treating that like routine vendor management — light-touch oversight, an annual audit — is precisely the mistake the 2023–2024 enforcement wave punished. BaaS is not outsourcing a task; it is renting out the franchise while keeping the blame.

## How it shows up in real banks

**The Synapse collapse (2024).** This is the case study that taught the whole industry what the ledger risk really means. Synapse was a BaaS middleware connecting consumer fintech apps (savings, teen-banking, and paycheck-advance brands) to several small sponsor banks. When Synapse entered bankruptcy in 2024, the reconciliation between what its sub-ledger said customers were owed and what could be matched against the banks' actual FBO balances broke down. Estimates of the shortfall reached roughly **\$85 million**; more than **100,000 end customers** were frozen out of their balances for months, some never made whole. The money had not been "stolen" in the simple sense — much of it was sitting in pooled accounts at solvent banks. The catastrophe was that *no single party held a reliable map* of whose money was whose, because the map lived in the failed middleware. It is the cleanest possible illustration of the post's central lesson: in unbundled banking, holding the cash is not the same as knowing whose cash it is.

**The 2023–2024 sponsor-bank enforcement wave.** As BaaS grew, US regulators (the Federal Reserve, the FDIC, and the OCC) issued a string of consent orders against community banks that had built fast-growing BaaS businesses, citing weak oversight of their fintech partners' compliance and unsafe concentrations of fintech deposits. Several banks were ordered to strengthen their controls or wind down partnerships. The pattern was consistent: the small bank chased the deposit-and-fee growth, onboarded more fintech partners than it could supervise, and discovered that the regulator holds *the bank*, not the app, responsible for every end customer. Banking-as-a-Service turned out to require *more* compliance muscle than ordinary banking, not less, precisely because the bank is liable for customers it never meets.

**Goldman Sachs and the Apple Card.** A blue-chip example of the customer-ownership problem running in reverse. Goldman, a giant licensed bank, provided the actual banking behind the Apple Card and the Apple savings account — it was the sponsor. But Apple owned the customer completely: the brand, the app, the experience, the loyalty. Goldman took the credit risk and the regulatory weight while Apple owned the relationship. When the economics and the compliance burden proved painful, it was Goldman that wanted out, reportedly absorbing significant losses on the consumer push. Even a bank as powerful as Goldman discovered that being the back-end to someone else's beloved brand is a hard place to make money — exactly the commoditization risk the matrix warned about.

**European open banking and "pay by bank".** On the gentler end, PSD2's payment-initiation door has quietly produced real volume. Across Europe and the UK, "pay by bank" account-to-account options now appear at checkouts, for tax payments, and for account top-ups — chosen by merchants who would rather pay a few cents than the ~2%+ card fee from this post's worked example. Variable recurring payments (a PSD2-enabled way to authorize ongoing pulls directly from your bank, like a smarter direct debit) are extending this into subscriptions. It is unbundling at its most benign: the bank keeps the deposit and the customer, and merely loses its grip on the payment toll — a structural nick to card economics rather than a threat to the bank itself.

**Embedded finance inside platforms (Shopify, Uber, and the BNPL wave).** The clearest embedded-finance success stories live inside platforms that already owned millions of relationships. E-commerce platforms offering merchant accounts, working-capital advances, and card-issuing to their sellers; ride-hailing and delivery apps paying drivers instantly into in-app wallets with attached debit cards; and the "buy now, pay later" providers wedging an embedded loan into the checkout of stores everywhere. In each case a sponsor bank (often via a BaaS middleware) holds the money and the license, and the platform captures the relationship and the higher take-rate from the worked example. These are the businesses living the upside of unbundling — and, after Synapse, living under sharply increased regulatory attention on exactly where the deposits sit and who keeps the ledger.

## The takeaway / How to use this

If you take one idea away, let it be this: **a bank is not one thing, it is a stack of functions, and software has learned to rent them out one at a time — but the one function that cannot be unbundled is the licensed balance sheet, and the one that decides who wins is owning the customer.**

So when you look at any modern "fintech" or any "embedded" financial feature, train yourself to ask the three questions this post is built around, in order:

First, **who is actually licensed?** Find the sponsor bank. There always is one, because only a chartered bank can hold insured deposits and reach the rails. The app is a brand; the bank is the bank. If you cannot find the bank, you do not yet understand the product.

Second, **where does the money actually sit, and who holds the ledger?** Is it an individual insured account in your name at the bank, or a slice of a pooled for-benefit-of account whose map lives in a middleware? That distinction is the difference between Synapse-safe and Synapse-exposed. The cash being at a solvent bank is necessary but not sufficient; what protects you is a reliable, daily-reconciled record of whose money is whose.

Third, **who owns the customer?** Whoever's app you open and whose brand you trust captures the value — the data, the loyalty, the fat take-rate. In traditional banking that is the bank; in BaaS and embedded finance it is the app, and the bank is left holding the risk for a relationship it does not own. That is why the most important strategic question for any bank in the next decade is not "should we build APIs?" but "are we still the brand the customer trusts, or have we quietly become a utility renting out our charter to the companies that are?"

And for the spine that ties this whole series together: unbundling does not repeal the fundamental trade. The deposits still fund the loans; the thin equity cushion still has to absorb losses faster than they arrive; confidence is still the fuel. What changes is that the *confidence* — the trust that makes deposits sticky and cheap — increasingly belongs to an app, not to the bank that holds the money. A bank that rents out its charter for fees while keeping the customer relationship has found a clever new line of business. A bank that rents it out and *loses* the customer has sold its franchise and kept only the liability. The unbundled bank survives. The question is whether it survives as the franchise or as the utility — and that is decided not by who holds the money, but by whose name is on the app.
