> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Islamic banking runs the same maturity-transformation machine as any bank — borrow short, fund long, earn a spread — but it cannot earn that spread from lending money at interest, so it re-routes every transaction through a real asset it buys, owns, leases, or partners in.
>
> - The ban is on *riba* (a guaranteed return on lent money) and *gharar* (excessive uncertainty), so a bank earns instead from **selling** an asset (murabaha), **leasing** it (ijara), or **sharing the profit** of a venture (musharaka, mudaraba).
> - A "mortgage" becomes a cost-plus sale: the bank buys the \$250,000 house and sells it back to you for \$480,000 payable over ten years — economically close to a 6% loan, but legally a sale of an asset, not a loan of money.
> - Deposits are not guaranteed-interest accounts. They are **profit-sharing investment accounts** (PSIAs): your money buys a share of the bank's asset pool, and your return floats with the pool's profit instead of a promised rate.
> - The whole industry holds roughly **\$3.9 trillion** in assets (2024 estimates), about three-quarters of it in banking, with a sukuk market near \$900 billion — small next to global banking, but the fastest-growing corner of finance for two decades.

In March 2009, in the depths of the financial crisis, a striking claim went around: Islamic banks had sailed through the storm better than their conventional neighbours. The reasoning was tidy — a bank that cannot deal in interest cannot stuff its balance sheet with subprime mortgage-backed securities, cannot lever 30-to-1 on a portfolio of synthetic credit, cannot earn a yield on a pile of pure debt it never inspected. If your contracts must attach to a real asset you actually buy and own, the argument went, you are insulated from the casino.

The truth was more nuanced. Islamic banks held fewer toxic securities, yes — but they also held a lot of real estate, and when property prices fell in Dubai in 2009, those banks took the hit directly because they *owned the buildings*. The crisis did not prove that interest-free banking is safe. It proved something more interesting: that a bank built on a ban on interest is still a bank. It still borrows short from depositors and funds long-dated assets. It still lives or dies on confidence and a thin equity cushion. It has simply changed the *legal object* of every transaction — from a loan of money to a sale, a lease, or a partnership in something real.

That is the whole subject of this post. We will build Islamic banking from zero — define the prohibition, the contracts, the board that enforces them, and the deposit accounts that fund it all — and we will keep asking the question that runs through this entire series: *where does the risk and the return actually sit?* The diagram below is the mental model. On the left, a conventional mortgage: the bank lends you money and charges interest on it. On the right, a murabaha: the bank buys the house and sells it to you at a markup. The monthly cash can be almost identical. What changes is what the bank legally *did* — and, as we will see, that legal difference is not cosmetic. It moves real risk onto the bank's books.

## Foundations: riba, real assets, and the contracts that replace a loan

Before any mechanics, we need the vocabulary. Islamic banking is governed by *Shariah* — Islamic law derived from the Qur'an and the practice of the Prophet Muhammad — as interpreted for finance by scholars. Four ideas do almost all the work.

**Riba.** This is the headline prohibition, usually translated as "interest" or "usury", though it means something more precise: an unjustified, guaranteed increase on a loan of money. If you lend \$100 and contract to get back \$105 simply for the passage of time, the extra \$5 is *riba* and is forbidden. The objection is not to profit — profit is encouraged — but to earning a guaranteed return on money itself, divorced from any real economic activity or risk. Money, in this view, is a medium of exchange, not a commodity that should breed more of itself. That single rule is why an Islamic bank cannot do the most basic thing a conventional bank does: take a deposit at 2% and lend it out at 6%, pocketing the 4% spread as interest.

**Gharar.** The second prohibition is on *gharar* — excessive uncertainty or ambiguity in a contract. A contract where the price, the asset, or the delivery is unknown or speculative is invalid. This is why conventional derivatives, short selling, and most insurance (where you pay a premium for an uncertain payout) sit uneasily with Shariah, and why Islamic finance built its own alternatives. *Gharar* pushes the whole system toward concrete, fully-specified deals: a known asset, a known price, a known date.

**The asset-ownership principle.** Put riba and gharar together and you get the spine of the entire field: **money must be tied to a real asset and to real risk.** You cannot earn a return on a pile of money; you can earn a return on a *thing* you own, sell, lease, or build. To make a financing deal valid, the bank usually has to take genuine ownership of an asset — for a moment or for years — and bear the risk that comes with owning it. This is the principle that turns a loan into a sale, a lease, or a partnership.

There is a deeper economic logic underneath the religious rule, and it is worth stating plainly because it is what makes the system coherent rather than arbitrary. The riba prohibition is, in effect, a rule that says *return must be earned by bearing risk*. A pure lender at fixed interest bears almost no risk — they get their money back plus a guaranteed increment regardless of whether the borrower's project succeeds or fails — and yet they extract a return. Shariah treats that as unjust: the lender has separated reward from risk. By forcing the financier to own, sell, lease, or partner, the system re-attaches the two. The bank that sells you a house bore the risk of owning it; the bank that leases you a car bears the risk of it being destroyed; the bank that partners in your business bears the risk of the business failing. The return is the *price of that risk*, not the price of time alone. Whether the industry lives up to that ideal is exactly the debate we will reach later — but the ideal is internally consistent, and it is close to the logic that says equity investors deserve a higher return than bondholders precisely because they bear more risk.

The modern industry is younger than most people assume. While the prohibition on riba is ancient, *commercial* Islamic banking dates only to the 1960s and 1970s: a small savings experiment in Mit Ghamr, Egypt, in 1963; the Dubai Islamic Bank, the first full commercial Islamic bank, in 1975; the Islamic Development Bank, a multilateral, the same year. The Gulf oil boom supplied the capital, a wave of religious revival supplied the demand, and by the 2000s the industry had professionalised into the multi-trillion-dollar field we see today, complete with international standard-setters and sovereign issuers. It is, in other words, a contemporary financial system built on an old principle — which is why it has had to *engineer* its compliance, contract by contract, rather than inherit a ready-made playbook.

From this principle flow the core contracts. You do not need to memorise the Arabic, but you do need the shapes:

- **Murabaha** — a cost-plus *sale*. The bank buys an asset and sells it to you at a disclosed markup, payable in instalments. The most common contract in the entire industry.
- **Ijara** — a *lease*. The bank buys an asset and rents it to you; often a lease-to-own, where title transfers at the end. It behaves like an operating or finance lease.
- **Musharaka** — a *partnership* where both you and the bank put in capital and share profit by agreement and loss by capital share. Equity-like.
- **Mudaraba** — a *partnership* where one side (the bank) provides the capital and the other (you, or the entrepreneur) provides the labour and management; profit is shared, but a pure financial loss falls on the capital provider. The contract that, flipped around, also defines how deposits work.
- **Sukuk** — often called "Islamic bonds", but more accurately *certificates of ownership* in a pool of real assets. Holders receive the income those assets generate (rent, profit) rather than interest on a loan.

And two institutions you must know:

- **The Shariah board** — a committee of qualified scholars that sits inside (or alongside) the bank, reviews every product for compliance, issues *fatwas* (rulings) approving them, and audits the bank's actual practice. No product ships without their sign-off. They are, in effect, a second risk-and-compliance function layered on top of the usual one.
- **The profit-sharing investment account (PSIA)** — the deposit product that replaces the interest-bearing savings account. Instead of a promised rate, your money is invested in the bank's asset pool and you receive a share of the realised profit. We will spend a whole section on it, because it is where the "borrow short, fund long" trade gets genuinely strange.

The matrix below lays out the five contracts side by side — what each one is, and who carries the risk. Keep coming back to the right-hand column. That is the column this whole series cares about.

If you prefer the same information as a table, here is the conventional analogue of each piece, which is the fastest way for a reader from a conventional-banking background to orient:

| Islamic contract | Conventional analogue | What the bank's return is | Where the loss falls |
|---|---|---|---|
| Murabaha | Fixed-rate instalment loan | Markup on a sale | Bank, briefly, while it owns the asset |
| Ijara | Finance / operating lease | Rent on a leased asset | Bank, which owns and insures the asset |
| Musharaka | Joint-venture equity | Share of venture profit | Both partners, by capital share |
| Mudaraba | Managed investment / silent partnership | Share of venture profit | Capital provider (the bank, or the depositor) |

| PSIA (deposit) | Savings / time deposit | Pays the saver a profit share | Saver, in theory; bank, in practice |

The right-most two columns are the ones that separate this from a relabelling exercise. In a conventional loan, the lender's return is detached from any specific asset and the loss falls almost entirely on the borrower. In every Islamic contract, the return is welded to a real asset or venture, and some genuine slice of the loss sits with the bank. How big that slice is — trivial in murabaha, total in mudaraba — is the dial that runs through the rest of this post.

One framing to carry through everything that follows: an Islamic bank is the *same machine* as a conventional one — a leveraged, confidence-funded engine that takes short-dated money from savers and turns it into long-dated assets, living on the gap between what it earns on assets and what it pays for funding. It has not abolished the spread. It has changed the *form* the spread takes, from interest on a loan to profit on an asset. Whether that change is real or merely cosmetic is the debate we will end on. First, the mechanics.

## Murabaha: how a bank earns from a sale, not a loan

Murabaha is the workhorse — by most estimates the majority of Islamic-bank financing assets are murabaha or murabaha-like. The idea is simple enough to state in one line: instead of lending you money to buy a thing, the bank buys the thing and sells it to you at a higher, agreed price, which you pay over time.

The mechanics matter because the *order of operations* is what makes it valid. Watch the steps in the figure: you identify the asset and ask the bank to finance it; the bank buys it from the seller; the bank takes genuine ownership; the bank sells it to you at cost plus a disclosed markup; you pay in instalments. The crucial bit is step three — the bank must *actually own* the asset, even if only for an instant, before it can sell it on. Selling something you do not own is not a valid sale. That moment of ownership is where the bank assumes real risk, and it is the legal hinge that turns this from a disguised loan into a trade.

![Pipeline of a murabaha showing bank buys the asset takes title sells at a markup and collects instalments](/imgs/blogs/islamic-banking-how-banking-works-without-interest-2.png)

#### Worked example: a murabaha home purchase vs a conventional mortgage

You want a \$250,000 house. Let us run it both ways.

*The conventional mortgage.* The bank lends you \$250,000 at 6% fixed over 10 years. The monthly payment on a 10-year, 6% loan is about \$2,776. Over 120 months you pay roughly \$2,776 × 120 = \$333,000 — except that is the wrong way to think about a long mortgage. The cleaner comparison is a longer horizon; but to keep the arithmetic friendly, stretch the principal and rate so the total is round: across the life of the loan you repay your \$250,000 principal *plus* about \$230,500 of interest, for a total near **\$480,500**. The bank's gain is the \$230,500 of interest — a return on money lent.

*The murabaha.* The bank buys the same house for \$250,000. It then sells it to you for a fixed price of **\$480,000**, payable as roughly \$4,000 per month for ten years. The bank's gain is the \$230,000 markup — a profit on an asset sold. Notice the punchline: \$480,000 versus \$480,500. The cash you part with is *almost identical*. The markup was, in practice, calculated by reference to a benchmark interest rate (often a published rate plus a margin) precisely so the bank stays competitive with the conventional lender down the road.

The one-sentence intuition: a murabaha and a fixed-rate mortgage can net to nearly the same dollars, because the markup is reverse-engineered from a rate — but the bank reached that number by buying and selling a real house, not by lending money, and that legal route is what makes it permissible.

The chart makes the near-equivalence visible. Two bars, almost the same height: the conventional mortgage's principal-plus-interest and the murabaha's cost-plus-markup land within a rounding error of each other.

![Bar chart comparing total cost of a conventional mortgage and a murabaha showing they net similar](/imgs/blogs/islamic-banking-how-banking-works-without-interest-4.png)

So far this looks like interest with extra paperwork — and critics say exactly that. But the legal form is not free. Three real differences fall out of it:

1. **The price is fixed at signing and cannot float.** Because the sale price is set the day you sign, a pure murabaha cannot have a floating rate. Once the bank sells you the house for \$480,000, that is the price, full stop. If market rates rise to 9% next year, the bank is stuck earning its original markup — it cannot reprice you. (Banks engineer around this with shorter-tenor or rolling structures, but the base contract is genuinely fixed.)
2. **Late payments cannot generate more profit.** If you pay late, the bank cannot charge compounding penalty interest, because that would be riba on the overdue amount. It can charge a fixed administrative fee or require a charitable donation, but it cannot profit from your delinquency the way a credit card does. That removes one of conventional banking's most reliable revenue lines.
3. **For that instant of ownership, the bank carries asset risk.** If the house burns down between the bank buying it and selling it to you, that is the bank's loss. In practice this window is milliseconds of paperwork, so the risk is tiny — but it is real, and Shariah requires it to be real. The bank is not a pure lender; it is, however briefly, an owner.

That last point is the recurring theme: every Islamic contract drags a sliver of genuine asset or business risk onto the bank's balance sheet that a conventional loan would not carry. Sometimes the sliver is trivial. Sometimes, as in musharaka, it is the whole point.

There is also a workhorse cousin of murabaha that you should know, because it is the most contested structure in the entire field: *tawarruq*, sometimes called "commodity murabaha". Suppose you do not want a house — you just want cash, the way you would take a personal loan. A pure cash loan at a markup would be riba. So the bank runs a multi-step trade: it buys a quantity of a freely-traded commodity (often metals on the London Metal Exchange), sells it to you on deferred-payment terms at cost plus markup, and then — on your behalf, or via a broker — immediately sells that same commodity back into the market for spot cash. You end up holding cash now and a debt to the bank later, exactly as if you had taken a loan; the commodity was a pass-through that existed only to make the transaction a "sale". Stricter scholars regard this as *hila* — a legal device that obeys the letter while defeating the spirit of the prohibition — and some Malaysian and South Asian authorities discourage or ban "organised tawarruq". Gulf banks use it heavily for personal finance, credit-card-equivalents, and interbank liquidity. It is the sharpest example of the form-over-substance tension that the Shariah board exists to police, and we will return to it.

## Ijara: financing by leasing the asset

If murabaha is a sale, *ijara* is a lease. The bank buys the asset — a car, a machine, an aircraft, a building — and rents it to you for a fixed period at an agreed rent. Because the bank *owns* the asset throughout, the rent it collects is permissible income: it is renting out a thing it owns, not charging interest on money.

Most consumer ijara is *ijara wa iqtina* — lease-to-own. You pay rent for the term, and at the end, title transfers to you (sometimes via a token final sale, sometimes as a promised gift). Functionally it is a finance lease, and the steps are clean: the bank buys the car, owns and insures it, you pay monthly rent, the bank books that rent as income, and when the lease ends the title passes to you.

![Pipeline of an ijara lease to own showing bank buys the car rents it collects rent and transfers title](/imgs/blogs/islamic-banking-how-banking-works-without-interest-8.png)

The ownership requirement here is not a formality — it bites continuously, not just for an instant:

- **The bank, as owner, must bear the ownership risks.** It is responsible for major maintenance and for insuring the asset (using *takaful*, the Shariah-compliant cooperative insurance that replaces conventional insurance). If the asset is destroyed by no fault of yours, the loss is the bank's, and your obligation to pay rent stops because there is nothing left to rent. A conventional auto loan keeps you on the hook for the full balance even after the car is totalled; an ijara, in principle, does not.
- **Rent can be re-set periodically.** Unlike murabaha's locked-in price, an ijara's rent can be tied to a benchmark and reviewed at intervals — which is precisely how Islamic banks build floating-rate-like products without violating the fixed-price rule. The rent for each period is re-agreed, not pre-fixed for all time.

#### Worked example: an ijara car lease

You want a \$30,000 car. The bank buys it and leases it to you for 5 years at \$600 per month. Over 60 months you pay \$600 × 60 = \$36,000, and then take title. The bank's gross return is \$36,000 − \$30,000 = **\$6,000** over five years, or about \$1,200 a year on \$30,000 — roughly a 4% annual yield, in the ballpark of a conventional auto loan.

But now suppose the car is written off in a flood in year three, through no fault of yours. You have paid \$600 × 36 = \$21,600 in rent. Under the ijara, the bank owned the car, the bank's takaful cover pays out, and your rent obligation *ends* — you do not owe the remaining \$14,400 of payments. Under a conventional loan, the lender would still want the outstanding principal, and your own insurance (if any) would have to cover the gap.

The one-sentence intuition: an ijara earns the bank a lease yield close to loan interest, but because the bank genuinely owns the asset, the destruction risk sits with the bank and its insurer, not with you — which is a materially different risk allocation from a loan.

This is the cleanest illustration of the asset-ownership principle paying for itself in risk terms. The bank is not pretending to own the car. It owns the car, insures the car, and eats the loss if the car disappears. That ownership is what makes the rent halal, and it is also a real liability the bank's risk managers must price.

## Musharaka and mudaraba: when the bank actually shares the risk

Murabaha and ijara are debt-like: the customer ends up owing a fixed or rent-defined stream, and the bank's downside is limited. The *partnership* contracts are different in kind. Here the bank's return genuinely depends on how a venture performs, and the bank can lose money if the venture loses money. This is where Islamic finance is most distinct from conventional lending — and, not coincidentally, where it is used least, because banks dislike equity risk.

**Musharaka** is a joint venture. You and the bank both contribute capital to a project or a purchase. Profits are split by a pre-agreed ratio (not necessarily in proportion to capital — the working partner can take a larger profit share for effort). Crucially, *losses* are shared strictly in proportion to capital contributed. If the bank put in 60% of the money, it eats 60% of any loss. There is no fixed return; the bank is an equity partner.

A common consumer variant is *diminishing musharaka*, used for home finance: the bank and you co-own the house, you pay rent on the bank's share, and you gradually buy out the bank's stake until you own 100%. It blends partnership and lease, and it is widely seen as more authentically Shariah-compliant than murabaha for housing.

**Mudaraba** is a labour-and-capital partnership. One party — the *rabb al-mal* — provides all the capital; the other — the *mudarib* — provides the work and expertise. Profits are shared by agreement. But the loss rule is asymmetric and important: a pure *financial* loss falls entirely on the capital provider; the working partner simply loses their time and effort (and earns nothing). The working partner only bears financial loss if they were negligent or broke the contract.

#### Worked example: a mudaraba profit split

The bank provides \$100,000 of capital to a trader (the mudarib) to run an import business for a year. They agree a profit split of 70% to the bank, 30% to the trader.

*Good year.* The business earns a \$20,000 profit. The bank takes 70% = **\$14,000**; the trader takes 30% = \$6,000. The bank's return on its \$100,000 is 14% — well above a loan rate, which is the upside for taking equity risk.

*Bad year.* The business loses \$15,000 (a genuine market loss, no negligence). The bank, as sole capital provider, bears the entire \$15,000 loss — its capital is now worth \$85,000. The trader earns nothing for a year of work but owes the bank nothing. There is no "you still owe me my money plus interest". The downside is real and it lands on the bank.

The one-sentence intuition: in a mudaraba the bank is an investor, not a lender — it can earn 14% in a good year and lose 15% of its capital in a bad one, which is exactly the risk-sharing the riba prohibition is meant to encourage, and exactly the risk most banks try hard to avoid.

Diminishing musharaka deserves a closer look, because it is the partnership contract that most directly competes with murabaha for the home-finance market and is often held up as the more authentic option. Here is how it works for a house. You and the bank co-purchase the property — say you put in 20% and the bank 80%, so you jointly own it 20/80. The bank's 80% is split into units. You then do two things every month: you *pay rent* on the share of the house you do not yet own (the bank's portion), and you *buy back* a slice of the bank's units. As you buy units, your ownership share rises, the bank's falls, and the rent you owe shrinks because you are renting a smaller and smaller portion. Over the term you ratchet from 20% owner to 100% owner, the bank exits, and the house is yours.

The reason scholars prefer it is that the bank is a genuine *co-owner* the whole way through, not merely a financier who briefly touched the asset. If the house needs major structural repair, the co-owner bank arguably shares that cost in proportion to its stake; if the property is destroyed, the loss is shared by ownership share, not dumped entirely on you. In practice banks draft the contracts to minimise their exposure, and the rent is still benchmarked to interest rates, so the economics again converge toward a conventional mortgage — but the risk-sharing is structurally more real than in a murabaha, and it is the direction the industry's own scholars keep pushing.

This asymmetry — the bank can lose principal — is why true profit-and-loss-sharing finance is a small slice of most Islamic banks' assets. Banks are leveraged institutions funded by depositors who expect their money back; they cannot put much of that money into ventures that might lose it. So the industry leans heavily on the debt-like contracts (murabaha, ijara) for its asset side and reserves the partnership contracts for special cases. The mudaraba's real importance, as we will now see, is on the *other* side of the balance sheet — it is the legal engine behind the deposit.

## How deposits work: the profit-sharing investment account

Here is where Islamic banking gets genuinely strange, and where it touches the spine of this series most directly. A conventional bank promises depositors a rate: put in \$10,000, earn 2%, and the bank owes you \$10,200 a year later regardless of how its loans perform. That promise *is* riba — a guaranteed return on money — so an Islamic bank cannot make it.

Instead, the bank takes most savings as a **mudaraba** with the roles reversed: *you* are the capital provider (rabb al-mal) and the *bank* is the manager (mudarib). Your deposit goes into a pool that the bank invests in its murabaha, ijara, and sukuk assets. At period end, the pool's realised profit is shared between you and the bank by an agreed ratio. Your return is not promised; it floats with the pool's performance. This is the **profit-sharing investment account**, or PSIA.

![Graph showing a deposit entering a profit sharing pool that funds bank assets then splitting profit between saver and bank](/imgs/blogs/islamic-banking-how-banking-works-without-interest-5.png)

The figure traces it: your \$10,000 enters the PSIA pool (shared with other savers); the pool funds the bank's murabaha sales, ijara leases, and sukuk holdings; those assets earn a profit; and the profit is split — a share to you, a management cut to the bank. Banks usually also offer a separate *current account* taken as *qard* (an interest-free loan to the bank or a safekeeping arrangement) — that money is guaranteed and repayable on demand but earns nothing, exactly like a conventional non-interest checking account.

#### Worked example: a PSIA return vs a savings account

You deposit \$10,000 in a PSIA. Over the year, the bank's asset pool earns a 5% gross profit. The mudaraba ratio says the bank, as manager, keeps 20% of the profit and passes 80% to depositors.

Pool profit on your slice: \$10,000 × 5% = \$500. Your 80% share: **\$400**. The bank's 20% management share on your slice: \$100. So you earn an effective 4% — and your statement may even quote it as a "4% expected profit rate" to look familiar. But it was not promised. If the pool had earned only 3%, your 80% share would be \$240 — an effective 2.4% — and you would have no contractual complaint.

In a bad year the difference becomes stark. Suppose the pool *loses* money. In principle, as the capital provider in a mudaraba, *you* bear the loss — your \$10,000 could come back as \$9,700. A conventional depositor never faces that; their \$10,000 is \$10,000 plus interest.

The one-sentence intuition: a PSIA makes the depositor a junior investor in the bank's assets, so the saver theoretically shares the bank's losses — which is more honest about where bank risk lives, and also far more fragile, because a depositor who can lose principal has every reason to run at the first bad rumour.

That fragility is not theoretical, and it collides head-on with the maturity-transformation spine of this series. A bank funded by PSIAs is *still* borrowing short and lending long — your "investment account" is redeemable on short notice, while the murabaha and ijara assets it funds run for years. If depositors believe the pool is losing money and might haircut their balances, they have a sharper incentive to withdraw than a conventional insured depositor does. So Islamic banks face the same run risk as everyone else, with an extra twist.

What do they do about it? In practice, two things, and both quietly undermine the purity of the model:

- **Profit-equalisation reserves.** Banks hold back a slice of good-year profits in a reserve and release it in bad years, so the rate paid to depositors is smoothed and rarely (if ever) goes negative. This is called "displaced commercial risk" by regulators — the bank effectively subsidises depositor returns out of its own pocket to stop them fleeing, because in reality almost no Islamic bank has ever passed a *loss* to retail depositors.
- **The implicit guarantee.** Because passing losses to retail savers would trigger a run, most Islamic banks treat PSIA balances as if they were guaranteed, even though the contract says otherwise. Many jurisdictions now extend conventional-style deposit insurance to Islamic deposits. The legal form says "investment that can lose"; the lived reality is "deposit that won't".

So the deposit base that is supposed to be the cleanest expression of risk-sharing turns out, under the pressure of run risk, to behave almost exactly like a conventional deposit. Hold that thought — it is the heart of the "is it really different?" debate.

It is worth dwelling on why this matters so much for stability, because it is a clean illustration of the series' spine and a genuine, unresolved tension in the model. The riba prohibition's *intent* is that depositors share risk — that they are investors who participate in the bank's fortunes, not creditors guaranteed their money back. A system where savers truly absorbed losses would be remarkably stable in one sense: the bank could never be technically insolvent to its depositors, because their claims would simply shrink with the assets, the way a mutual fund's value falls without the fund "failing". Some economists have argued this makes Islamic banking inherently more shock-absorbing than conventional banking, where fixed deposit claims and falling asset values collide to produce insolvency.

But the run-risk reality flips the argument. A depositor who can *lose principal* has a far stronger incentive to withdraw at the first whiff of trouble than a conventional insured depositor, who knows their first \$250,000 is safe no matter what. If savers believe the asset pool is souring and their "investment" might be haircut, the rational move is to redeem now, before the loss is recognised — which is precisely the self-fulfilling logic of a bank run. So the very feature meant to make the system stable (risk-sharing depositors) would, if actually enforced, make it *more* run-prone. Islamic banks resolve the contradiction by not enforcing it: equalisation reserves, displaced commercial risk absorbed by the bank's own equity, and increasingly formal deposit insurance all conspire to make the PSIA behave like a guaranteed deposit. The principle says "investor"; the plumbing says "depositor". And the plumbing wins, because the alternative is a chronically fragile funding base. This is the single sharpest place where the operating model bends back toward the conventional one under the gravity of maturity transformation.

## Sukuk: ownership certificates, not bonds

On the funding and investment side, the conventional bond has a Shariah problem: a bond is a loan, and its coupon is interest. So Islamic finance built the *sukuk* (singular *sakk*, the root of the English word "cheque"). A sukuk is best understood not as a bond but as a **certificate of co-ownership in a pool of real assets**. The holder owns a fractional, tradable share of those assets and receives the income they generate — rent from a leased building, profit from a venture — rather than interest on a loan.

The before-and-after figure makes the contrast concrete. A conventional bond: you lend the issuer \$1,000, you hold a debt claim (an IOU), you receive a fixed coupon, your return is interest on the loan, and at default you rank as a creditor. A sukuk: you buy a share of a real asset, you hold an ownership certificate, you receive rent or profit, your return is the asset's cash flow, and you have a claim on the asset itself.

![Before and after diagram contrasting a conventional bond as a debt claim with a sukuk as asset ownership](/imgs/blogs/islamic-banking-how-banking-works-without-interest-6.png)

The most common structure is a *sukuk al-ijara*. An issuer (say, a government or a corporate) sets up a special-purpose vehicle, sells it an asset — a building, a toll road, a slice of an airport — and the SPV issues sukuk to investors to fund the purchase. The SPV leases the asset back to the issuer, collects rent, and passes that rent to sukuk holders as their "coupon". At maturity, the issuer buys the asset back at a pre-agreed price, redeeming the sukuk. The investor's cash flows look almost exactly like a bond's — periodic payments plus principal back at the end — but every payment is sourced from a real lease on a real asset.

#### Worked example: a sukuk return vs a bond coupon

A government issues \$1 billion of 5-year sukuk al-ijara backed by a government office building, with a 5% annual distribution. You buy \$1,000 of it.

Each year, the building's rent flows through the SPV to you: \$1,000 × 5% = **\$50**. Over five years you collect \$250 in distributions, and at maturity the government repurchases its building from the SPV, so you get your \$1,000 back. Total received: \$1,250. A conventional 5-year, 5% bond would pay you exactly the same: \$50 a year, \$1,000 at maturity, \$1,250 total.

So why bother? Because the legal structure changes who holds what. In a "true sale" sukuk, you own a piece of the building, so if the government defaults you have a claim on a real asset, which can mean better recovery. In practice, most sukuk are "asset-based" rather than "asset-backed" — the investor relies on the issuer's promise to repurchase, not on a genuine claim to the building — which makes them function, in a default, much like an unsecured bond. The 2009 Nakheel sukuk near-default in Dubai turned precisely on this question: did holders own the land, or just a promise?

The one-sentence intuition: a sukuk can deliver bond-identical cash flows of \$50 a year and \$1,000 back, but whether it gives you genuine asset ownership or just a dressed-up corporate promise depends on the fine print — and that distinction only matters, but matters enormously, on the day the issuer cannot pay.

Ijara is only the most common wrapper; the sukuk family is broader, and the structure determines the risk:

- **Sukuk al-ijara** (lease-based, as above) — the holder's income is rent on a leased asset; the most common and the most bond-like.
- **Sukuk al-murabaha** — backed by a cost-plus sale; the income is the deferred sale receivable. These are generally *not* tradable on the secondary market in stricter interpretations, because you would be trading a pure debt at a price other than face value, which reintroduces riba. That non-tradability is a real liquidity drawback.
- **Sukuk al-musharaka / al-mudaraba** — backed by a partnership; the income is a share of venture profit, so the return genuinely varies and the holder takes equity-like risk. Rarer, and the most authentically risk-sharing.
- **Hybrid and wakala sukuk** — pools mixing several asset types managed by an agent (*wakil*), now common for sovereign and bank issuance because they offer structuring flexibility.

The sukuk market is the most visible, most-traded part of Islamic finance — roughly \$900 billion to \$1 trillion outstanding, with Malaysia and the Gulf states as the dominant issuers, and a growing band of conventional and even non-Muslim-majority issuers (the UK, Hong Kong, Luxembourg, South Africa, and several blue-chip corporates) tapping it to reach Gulf and Asian Islamic investors. The UK became the first Western sovereign to issue sukuk in 2014, a £200 million deal that was oversubscribed many times over — a signal that the buyer base extends well beyond religiously-motivated investors to anyone wanting exposure to Gulf liquidity. For a bank, sukuk are both an asset to hold (Shariah-compliant high-quality liquid assets are scarce, and sukuk help fill the liquidity buffer that liquidity rules demand) and a tool to raise term funding without issuing interest-bearing bonds. That scarcity of compliant liquid assets is itself a structural headache: Islamic banks have a harder time meeting liquidity-coverage requirements than conventional ones, because the menu of safe, liquid, Shariah-compliant instruments is thin, and they cannot simply park excess cash in interest-bearing government bills the way a conventional bank does.

## The Shariah board: a second compliance machine

Every Islamic bank runs a *Shariah Supervisory Board* — typically three to seven qualified scholars expert in both Islamic jurisprudence and finance. Nothing reaches a customer without their approval. Their job has three layers: **ex-ante**, they review and approve (or reject) every new product and issue a *fatwa* documenting why it complies; **ongoing**, they advise on edge cases and novel structures; and **ex-post**, they conduct a Shariah audit of what the bank actually did, checking that the murabaha really involved ownership, that the sukuk's assets really exist, that any tainted income (say, an accidental interest receipt) is purified by donating it to charity.

This is a genuine second risk function bolted on top of the conventional one. It has real teeth — a board can refuse to certify a product, killing a revenue line — and real costs and frictions:

- **Scholar scarcity and conflicts.** There are relatively few scholars with deep finance expertise, so a handful sit on dozens of boards at once. That raises both capacity limits and conflict-of-interest concerns: a scholar paid by the bank to approve its products is not a fully independent referee, much as an auditor paid by the company they audit is not.
- **Interpretive divergence.** Shariah is interpreted, not codified, and schools differ. A structure deemed compliant by a Gulf board (which tends to be more permissive on murabaha and "organised tawarruq", a multi-step cash-generating sale) may be frowned on by a stricter Malaysian or South Asian board. There is no single global rulebook, though standard-setters like AAOIFI (the Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) and the IFSB (Islamic Financial Services Board) have pushed toward harmonisation.
- **Form-over-substance risk.** The board's hardest job is policing the line between a genuinely different transaction and a conventional one in Islamic costume. *Tawarruq* — where a customer buys a commodity on credit from the bank and immediately sells it for cash, ending up with cash and a debt, exactly like a personal loan — is the most contested example. Some boards permit it; stricter scholars call it a legal trick (*hila*) that defeats the purpose of the prohibition.

A vivid illustration of how high the stakes can get came in 2017, when Dana Gas, a UAE energy company, declared that its own \$700 million sukuk had become "unlawful" under UAE law and was therefore unenforceable — effectively trying to avoid repaying investors by arguing the instrument was no longer Shariah-compliant. The market was shaken: if an issuer could escape its obligations by retroactively challenging the compliance of a structure its own scholars had once approved, the legal certainty underpinning the entire sukuk market was in doubt. The dispute wound through English and UAE courts and was eventually settled, but it crystallised a real risk that pure bonds do not carry — *Shariah risk*, the risk that a structure blessed at issuance is later ruled non-compliant, with financial consequences. It is a reminder that the second compliance layer is not only a cost; it is also a distinct source of legal uncertainty.

The governance lesson connects to the scandals track of this series: a control function that is paid by the entity it polices, scarce enough that the same few people sit everywhere, and operating on interpretation rather than bright-line rules, is a control function under pressure. It is not corrupt — most Shariah scholars take their duty extremely seriously, and the reputational cost of certifying something later deemed non-compliant is severe — but it has the same structural vulnerability as any gatekeeper whose independence is compromised by who signs the cheque. The industry's response has been to push board members toward greater independence (limiting how many boards one scholar can serve, separating the audit role from the approval role) and toward codified standards from AAOIFI and the IFSB, so that "compliant" means something more uniform than one committee's opinion. It is the same arc every governance function in banking has travelled: from trusted individuals to documented, independent, standardised process — because trust alone does not survive a billion-dollar incentive to bend.

## Where the risk actually sits: same machine, rearranged

Step back and ask the series' question across all the contracts. In a conventional bank, almost all the credit and rate risk sits in one place: the loan book. The bank lends money, and it is exposed to the borrower not repaying (credit risk) and to rates moving against its fixed-rate book (rate risk). Where does that risk go in an Islamic bank?

It gets *redistributed across the contract types*, and in two of them it changes character entirely:

- **Murabaha** carries credit risk almost identical to a loan — the customer might not pay the instalments — plus a flash of asset-price risk during the moment of ownership. Net: very loan-like.
- **Ijara** carries credit risk (you might stop paying rent) *plus continuous ownership risk* — the bank insures and maintains the asset and eats its destruction. More risk on the bank than a loan.
- **Musharaka and mudaraba** carry *equity risk* — the bank can lose principal if the venture fails. Far more risk than a loan, which is why they are used sparingly.
- **PSIA funding** pushes risk the other way — toward the depositor, who is theoretically a loss-sharing investor. Except, as we saw, run risk forces banks to absorb that risk back via equalisation reserves, so it boomerangs onto the bank's equity.

The honest summary: an Islamic bank often ends up with risk that is *economically similar* to a conventional bank's, but *structured around real assets and ownership* rather than around pure debt claims. The benchmark rate still drives pricing (murabaha markups and ijara rents are set off published rates). The maturity transformation is still there (short PSIAs funding long murabaha and ijara). The thin equity cushion still has to absorb losses faster than they arrive. What is genuinely different is at the margins: the ban on selling pure debt, on compounding penalty interest, and on certain speculative instruments does keep some of the worst conventional excesses off the balance sheet, and the ownership requirement does drag real-economy risk onto the bank in places a loan would not. The machine is the same; its plumbing has been re-routed through things you can touch.

## Is it really different? The central debate

No discussion of Islamic banking is honest without this argument, because thoughtful Muslims make it too. The critique runs: if a murabaha nets to the same cash as a 6% loan, if its markup is *calculated from* a 6% benchmark rate, if late payment is handled by a fee that looks like a penalty, if the deposit pays a "profit rate" that tracks conventional deposit rates and never actually loses money — then is this anything more than conventional banking with Arabic labels and extra documents? The pejorative term for this is *Shariah-compliant* versus *Shariah-based*: technically within the rules, but missing their spirit.

The defenders answer on three levels:

1. **Form is substance in contract law.** The legal object genuinely differs, and that difference produces real consequences in real cases: the bank cannot float a murabaha's price, cannot compound penalties, must own and insure an ijara asset, and must bear equity loss in a true partnership. Those are not cosmetic — they bind in the edge cases where it counts.
2. **The prohibited extremes really are excluded.** You will not find an Islamic bank that securitised subprime debt into synthetic CDOs, ran a naked credit-default-swap book, or earned its living on payday-loan compounding. The riba and gharar prohibitions wall off a chunk of the conventional toolkit, including some of the parts that blew up in 2008.
3. **Direction of travel.** The industry's own scholars push toward more *Shariah-based* products — diminishing musharaka for homes instead of murabaha, more genuine risk-sharing — even as commercial pressure pulls toward the convenient, loan-like contracts. It is an unfinished project, not a finished disguise.

The fairest verdict, and the one consistent with everything in this series: the *operating model* is meaningfully different in its contracts, constraints, and excluded risks, but the *economic core* — leveraged maturity transformation funded by skittish short-term money — is identical, and so the fundamental fragility is identical too. An Islamic bank can fail exactly the way a conventional bank fails: a run on its funding, a hole in its asset book, an equity cushion too thin to absorb the loss in time. The riba prohibition reshapes what is on the balance sheet. It does not repeal the laws of banking.

## Common misconceptions

**"Islamic banks don't charge interest, so financing is cheaper or free."** Wrong by construction. The whole point of murabaha and ijara is to earn a return economically equivalent to interest through a permissible legal route. The markup or rent is *calculated from* prevailing interest-rate benchmarks so the bank stays competitive. A \$250,000 murabaha home purchase costs you about \$480,000 — essentially the same total as a 6% mortgage, give or take. The price of money is still the price of money; only the contract changes.

**"A profit-sharing deposit means I can lose my savings any time."** In strict theory, yes — a PSIA makes you a loss-sharing investor. In practice, almost no Islamic bank has ever imposed a loss on retail depositors. Banks hold profit-equalisation reserves and absorb "displaced commercial risk" out of their own equity to keep depositor returns positive, because a bank that haircut savers would trigger an instant run. Many countries also extend deposit insurance to Islamic accounts. The legal form says "investment"; the lived experience is "deposit".

**"Sukuk are just Islamic bonds — same thing, different name."** The cash flows can be identical, but the legal claim is not. A bond is a debt claim with a coupon of interest. A sukuk is, in principle, a share of ownership in real assets paying you their rent or profit. The distinction is invisible until default, when it determines whether you have a claim on a building or merely on a corporate promise to repurchase. Most sukuk are "asset-based" (relying on the issuer's promise), not "asset-backed" (a true claim on the asset) — which is exactly why the Nakheel and other Gulf sukuk crises turned on a fine-print fight over what holders actually owned.

**"Islamic banks are inherently safer because they can't speculate."** Partly true, partly dangerous. The prohibitions do exclude pure debt-trading, naked derivatives, and compounding consumer debt — real sources of conventional fragility. But Islamic banks concentrate in real estate and trade finance, carry asset-ownership risk a lender would not, and run the same maturity transformation that breaks any bank. In Dubai's 2009 property crash, Islamic banks took direct hits *because* they owned the assets. Different risks, not absent risks.

**"This is a niche religious product with no relevance to mainstream finance."** It is a roughly \$3.9 trillion industry growing faster than conventional banking for two decades, with sovereign sukuk issued by the UK, Hong Kong, and Luxembourg, and Islamic windows run by HSBC, Standard Chartered, and Citi. It is the dominant or co-dominant model in Saudi Arabia, the Gulf, Malaysia, and a fast-growing share of Indonesia, Pakistan, and Turkey. Niche by global share, perhaps; mainstream in a billion-plus-person market.

## How it shows up in real banks

**The size of the industry.** Total global Islamic-finance assets — banking, sukuk, funds, and takaful combined — reached roughly **\$3.9 trillion** by 2024 on industry estimates, up from about \$1.6 trillion in 2012. That is a compound growth rate well above conventional banking's. The chart shows the climb: a steady doubling-plus over a decade, driven by oil-wealthy Gulf economies, Malaysia's deliberate hub-building, and rising demand across Indonesia, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Turkey.

![Bar chart of global Islamic finance assets growing from about 1.6 trillion to 3.9 trillion dollars between 2012 and 2024](/imgs/blogs/islamic-banking-how-banking-works-without-interest-7.png)

**Where the assets are.** Islamic *banking* dominates the total — roughly \$2.9 trillion, about three-quarters of the industry — with the sukuk market the next-largest block near \$900 billion, then Islamic funds and a small takaful (insurance) sector. The breakdown matters because it tells you the industry is, overwhelmingly, *banks doing banking* — taking deposits and funding assets — not some exotic capital-markets phenomenon. The bar chart makes the concentration plain.

![Horizontal bar chart showing Islamic banking far larger than sukuk funds and takaful](/imgs/blogs/islamic-banking-how-banking-works-without-interest-9.png)

**The Gulf giants.** Saudi Arabia is the largest single market. Al Rajhi Bank, headquartered in Riyadh, is one of the world's largest fully Shariah-compliant banks by assets (well over \$200 billion), funded overwhelmingly by low-cost current and savings accounts — the same cheap-deposit franchise advantage that makes any retail bank profitable. Saudi National Bank, Kuwait Finance House, Dubai Islamic Bank (the world's first commercial Islamic bank, founded 1975), and Abu Dhabi Islamic Bank are the other heavyweights. These are not boutiques; they are systemically important national champions running full-service retail and corporate banking entirely on murabaha, ijara, and partnership contracts.

**Malaysia, the deliberate hub.** Malaysia built its position by design over three decades, with a central-bank-backed Shariah Advisory Council providing national-level rulings (reducing the interpretive fragmentation that dogs the Gulf), a deep domestic sukuk market, and a dual system where Islamic and conventional banks compete side by side. Malaysia and the Gulf together account for the bulk of global sukuk issuance, and Malaysia's ringgit sukuk market is among the world's most liquid.

**The conventional banks' Islamic windows.** You do not have to be a standalone Islamic bank to play. HSBC Amanah, Standard Chartered Saadiq, and Citi's Islamic operations run "windows" — ring-fenced Shariah-compliant units inside a conventional group, with their own Shariah boards and segregated funds, so the Islamic business does not commingle with interest-based money. This is how global banks serve Gulf and Southeast Asian clients who require compliant products, and it is a reminder that the contracts, not the institution, are what must be compliant.

**The sukuk that tested the model.** The 2009 Nakheel sukuk — \$3.5 billion issued by a Dubai property developer — nearly defaulted as Dubai's real-estate bubble burst, and the episode became the field's stress test. It exposed the asset-based-versus-asset-backed problem (did holders own the land or just a promise?), forced a last-minute Abu Dhabi bailout of Dubai, and taught the market that a sukuk is only as safe as its legal claim on the underlying asset. Later defaults (Dana Gas in 2017 disputed whether its sukuk were even Shariah-compliant) kept the lesson alive: the structure is novel, but the credit risk is utterly conventional.

## The takeaway / How to use this

The reason Islamic banking is worth a careful look is not piety — it is that it runs a controlled experiment on the question this whole series asks. Take a bank, forbid it the single most basic move (earn interest on lent money), and watch what survives. What survives is everything that matters: the borrow-short-lend-long trade, the spread (now earned as markup and rent), the thin equity cushion, the dependence on depositor confidence, the run risk. What changes is the legal skin on every transaction and the exclusion of a few of conventional banking's worst habits.

So when you read about an Islamic bank, read it the same way you read any other bank, with three adjustments. First, translate the vocabulary: "financing assets" are its loan book (mostly murabaha and ijara), "investment accounts" are its deposits (PSIAs), "sukuk" are its bonds. Second, find where the risk-sharing is real and where it is nominal — check whether the bank has ever actually passed a loss to PSIA depositors (almost none have), and whether its partnership-contract book is a meaningful share of assets or a rounding error (usually the latter). Third, look at the asset concentration the contracts create: an institution that must own real things tends to be heavy in real estate and trade, with the cyclical risk that brings.

And keep the spine in view. The riba prohibition is a serious moral and legal constraint that genuinely reshapes a balance sheet — but it does not exempt a bank from being a bank. An Islamic bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine, the same as the one in your neighbourhood. It has simply chosen to earn its spread by owning, selling, and leasing real assets instead of by lending money at interest. That is a different operating model wrapped around an identical, and identically fragile, core. (This is educational, not financial or religious advice — for a ruling on what is permissible, ask a qualified scholar, not a finance blog.)
