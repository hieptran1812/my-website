> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Securitization pools many illiquid loans, sells them to a separate company (an SPV), and reissues the pooled cash flows as tradable bonds sliced into tranches — turning loans a bank could never sell one-by-one into securities the whole world will buy.
>
> - **The trick is tranching.** Cut one pool into a senior slice (paid first, loses last), a mezzanine slice, and an equity slice (paid last, loses first). The senior slice can earn a AAA rating even when every underlying loan is mediocre, because the slices below it absorb the first losses.
> - **The fuel is originate-to-distribute.** Once a bank can sell the loan within weeks, it stops eating the default and starts earning a fee on volume. That fixes the bank's funding and capital — and quietly poisons its incentive to underwrite well.
> - **2008 was this machine taken to its breaking point.** Subprime mortgages became MBS, the unsellable BBB slices were repackaged into CDOs, rating agencies stamped roughly 80% of those CDOs AAA, the world bought them, and when house prices fell everywhere at once the "safe" tranches were not safe.
> - **The one number to remember:** a senior tranche sized at 80% of a pool can be rated AAA only because the 20% beneath it is engineered to take the first losses. When that 20% turns out to be too thin, the whole rating is a fiction.

In the summer of 2007, two hedge funds run by Bear Stearns — funds with the comforting names *High-Grade Structured Credit Strategies* and its *Enhanced Leverage* sibling — went from billions of dollars to essentially zero in a matter of weeks. They held bonds that were stamped investment-grade by Moody's and Standard & Poor's. They held bonds that, on paper, almost could not lose money. They were, the marketing said, *high-grade*.

What they actually held were slices of repackaged subprime mortgages — securities built on top of securities built on top of home loans made to borrowers who, in many cases, had not documented their income. When American house prices stopped rising and then started falling, the carefully engineered "safety" of those slices evaporated. By March 2008 Bear Stearns itself, an 85-year-old investment bank, was sold to JPMorgan in a weekend fire sale for \$2 a share. Six months after that, Lehman Brothers — which had ridden the same machine — filed the largest bankruptcy in history with \$639 billion of assets and roughly 30 times leverage.

The machine that did all of this has a dull, technical name: **securitization**. It is one of the most important financial inventions of the last fifty years, and one of the most misunderstood. It is not, in itself, a scam. It is the plumbing that funds a huge share of the world's mortgages, car loans, and corporate debt, and on most days it works quietly and well. But it is also a machine for transforming risk — for taking risk apart, relabeling the pieces, and moving them somewhere else — and any machine that can hide risk can also be used to launder it. The diagram below is the mental model for everything that follows: a bank's loans go in one end, and tradable bonds come out the other, with a separate legal entity and a slicing step in between.

![Securitization pipeline loans to SPV to pooled cash flows to tranches sold to investors](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-1.png)

To understand both the genius and the danger, we have to build the whole thing from the ground up — starting from a problem that every bank has, and that securitization was invented to solve.

## Foundations: from one illiquid loan to a tradable bond

Recall the spine of this whole series: a bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine. It borrows short — your deposits, which you can withdraw any day — and it lends long — a 30-year mortgage, a 5-year car loan, a 7-year corporate term loan. It earns the *spread* between what it pays depositors and what it charges borrowers. (A *spread* is just the gap between two interest rates.) That trade is the bank's reason to exist, and it has two structural headaches that securitization was built to relieve.

**Headache one: loans are illiquid.** *Illiquid* means hard to sell quickly for a fair price. If I lend you \$250,000 to buy a house, I now hold a one-of-a-kind contract: a promise from *you specifically*, secured by *your specific house*, to pay me back over 30 years. There is no deep market where I can sell that single loan tomorrow at a clean price, the way I could sell 100 shares of Apple. To value it, a buyer would have to investigate you, your job, your house, your neighborhood. That investigation is expensive, so single loans trade rarely and at a discount. They sit on the bank's balance sheet, frozen, for years.

**Headache two: loans tie up scarce capital.** Every loan a bank holds must be backed by a sliver of the bank's own *equity* — the shareholders' money that absorbs losses before depositors are touched. Across this series we use a realistic base case of about 8% equity, which means a bank runs roughly 12.5 times leverage (1 ÷ 0.08 ≈ 12.5). Hold a loan, and you must hold capital against it. Capital is finite. So the number of loans a bank can make is capped by how much equity it has — unless it can get loans *off* its balance sheet.

Securitization solves both headaches at once. Here are the pieces, defined from zero.

**Securitization** is the process of pooling many illiquid loans together and turning the pool into securities — bonds — that can be bought and sold in the open market. The verb *to securitize* means "to turn into a security." A *security* is just a tradable financial instrument: a stock, a bond, anything that trades on a market with a price.

The first move is to pool. One mortgage is a unique, scary, unsellable thing. But a thousand mortgages, taken together, are statistical. You cannot predict whether *you* will default, but across a thousand borrowers you can estimate that, say, 2% will default in a normal year. Pooling converts a collection of individual unknowns into an aggregate with a knowable average — the same logic that lets an insurance company that cannot predict your house fire confidently insure a million houses. This is the single most important idea in the whole subject, so hold onto it: **securitization sells the average, not the individual.**

**An SPV** — a *special purpose vehicle*, also called a *special purpose entity* or, when it is structured as a trust, simply *the trust* — is the legal heart of the deal. It is a shell company created for one job: to buy the pool of loans from the bank and to issue the bonds backed by them. The bank sells its loans to the SPV in what lawyers call a *true sale*: the loans legally leave the bank and become the SPV's property. The SPV is deliberately **bankruptcy-remote**, meaning it is structured so that if the originating bank goes bust, the bank's creditors cannot claw back the loans sitting inside the SPV. The investors who bought the SPV's bonds are protected from the bank's troubles, and — crucially — the bank gets the loans off its own books.

**ABS, MBS, CLO, CDO** are the products that come out the other end. They are all securitizations; they differ only in what loans sit in the pool. *MBS* (mortgage-backed securities) are backed by home mortgages. *ABS* (asset-backed securities) is the catch-all term, but in practice usually means securities backed by *other* consumer debt — credit-card balances, car loans, student loans. *CLOs* (collateralized loan obligations) are backed by corporate leveraged loans. And *CDOs* (collateralized debt obligations) are the dangerous cousin: they are backed not by loans at all but by *tranches of other securitizations* — securities built on securities. We will return to that distinction again and again, because the difference between a CLO and a CDO is, roughly, the difference between the part of structured finance that survived 2008 and the part that detonated it.

**A tranche** (from the French for "slice") is the key innovation. Instead of selling the pool as one uniform bond, the SPV slices the pool's cash flows into layers of different risk. The French word matters because the whole point is that you do not buy *the pool* — you buy *a slice* of it, and which slice you buy determines whether you are taking almost no risk or almost all of it.

**The waterfall** is the rule that determines who in the tranche stack gets paid first and who absorbs losses first. Cash from the borrowers flows *down* the stack — the senior tranche is paid first, then the mezzanine, then the equity at the bottom gets whatever is left. Losses climb *up* the stack from the bottom — the equity tranche is wiped out first, then the mezzanine, and only after both are gone does the senior tranche take a dollar of loss. We will spend a whole section on this because it is where the magic — and the deception — lives.

**Credit enhancement** is the umbrella term for everything that makes the senior tranche safer than the raw pool: the cushion of junior tranches below it (the most important form), plus extras like *overcollateralization* (putting \$105 of loans behind \$100 of bonds), *excess spread* (the gap between what the loans pay and what the bonds owe), and sometimes a third-party guarantee.

**Originate-to-distribute** is the business model securitization makes possible. The traditional bank *originated* a loan and *held* it to maturity, eating any default. In the originate-to-distribute model, the bank originates the loan and then *distributes* it — sells it into a securitization within weeks — keeping a fee and passing the credit risk to whoever buys the bonds. This sounds like efficient division of labor. It is also, as we will see, an incentive time bomb.

With those nine terms in hand, the rest of this post is just watching them interact.

## How the machine actually works, step by step

Let us run one deal all the way through with round numbers, so the mechanics are concrete before we get clever about the risk.

A bank has originated 1,000 home mortgages, each for \$250,000, for a total pool of \$250 million. These loans pay an average of 6% interest. On the bank's balance sheet, they are \$250 million of frozen, capital-hungry, illiquid assets. The bank would like its capital and its cash back so it can lend again.

**Step one — pool and sell.** The bank bundles all 1,000 mortgages into one pool and sells the pool to an SPV it has set up for exactly this purpose. The SPV pays the bank \$250 million (we will see in a moment where the SPV gets that cash). The sale is structured as a true sale: the mortgages now legally belong to the SPV, not the bank.

**Step two — the SPV issues bonds.** The SPV needs \$250 million to pay the bank, and it raises it by issuing \$250 million of bonds to investors. The bonds are *backed by* the mortgage pool: every month, as the 1,000 homeowners make their payments, that cash flows into the SPV and out to the bondholders. The bank's loans have become the world's bonds.

**Step three — slice into tranches.** Here is the clever part. Rather than issue one homogeneous \$250 million bond, the SPV issues three classes:

- A **senior tranche** of \$200 million (80% of the pool), which has first claim on all the cash and last exposure to losses. Because it is so well protected, a rating agency will rate it AAA, and it can be sold to the most conservative buyers — pension funds, insurers, foreign central banks — at a low yield, say 4%.
- A **mezzanine tranche** of \$37.5 million (15% of the pool), which gets paid only after the senior tranche is satisfied and absorbs losses only after the equity is gone. It is riskier, rated around BBB, and pays a higher yield, say 7%.
- An **equity tranche** (also called the *residual* or *first-loss* piece) of \$12.5 million (5% of the pool), which gets paid last — only whatever is left after senior and mezzanine — and absorbs the *first* dollar of any loss. It is unrated, and it pays a very high return when losses are low (often 15% or more) or nothing at all when losses are high.

Notice what just happened. Out of a pool of ordinary mortgages — none of which is individually AAA-quality — the structurers manufactured a \$200 million AAA bond. That is the central, almost magical claim of securitization, and the next section is devoted entirely to *why it can be true* and *when it becomes a lie*.

**Step four — the bank pockets the fee and walks away.** The bank sold \$250 million of loans for \$250 million of cash (plus, often, a small premium), it earned origination and servicing fees, it got its capital back, and — if it sold every tranche, including the equity — it no longer bears the credit risk of those 1,000 mortgages at all. It can now use the recovered capital to originate another 1,000 mortgages and do the whole thing again. This is the engine that lets a bank with a fixed amount of equity originate many multiples of that equity in loans over a year.

That is securitization in one pass: pool, sell to an SPV, slice into tranches, distribute the slices, recycle the capital. Everything difficult about the subject comes from one step — the slicing — so we go there next.

## The cast of characters and the life of a deal

The four steps above hide a small ecosystem of parties, each with a defined job and a defined fee. Knowing who they are matters, because in 2008 the breakdown happened in the *gaps between* these parties — each one assumed someone else was checking the loans.

The **originator** is the bank or lender that made the loans in the first place. The **arranger** (or *sponsor*, usually an investment bank) designs the deal: it decides the tranche sizes, negotiates with the rating agencies, and finds the investors. The **SPV / issuer** is the bankruptcy-remote shell that legally owns the pool and issues the bonds. The **servicer** is the party that collects the monthly payments from borrowers, chases the late ones, handles foreclosures, and passes the cash up to the trust — often the originator keeps this role, because the servicing fee (typically around 0.25% of the pool balance a year) is steady, capital-light income. The **trustee** is an independent administrator who holds the legal documents, enforces the waterfall rules, and distributes cash to the right tranches in the right order. And the **rating agencies** rate the tranches so that investors who cannot inspect the collateral themselves have a shorthand for its risk.

Here is the subtle failure mode this structure invites: notice that *no single party in the chain has both the information and the incentive to police loan quality.* The originator has the information but sells the loan and keeps a fee. The arranger wants the deal to close. The servicer is paid to collect, not to second-guess underwriting. The trustee enforces mechanical rules, not credit judgment. The rating agency relies on the data the arranger hands it. The investor relies on the rating. Responsibility for "are these loans actually good?" falls through every crack in the chain. That diffusion of responsibility is not a bug that appeared in 2008 — it is a structural property of originate-to-distribute, and it is why the model demands either retained risk (skin in the game) or genuinely independent verification to be safe.

There is one more thing the simple snapshot misses: a securitization is not static. Over its life, the pool *amortizes* — borrowers pay down principal every month — and it experiences *prepayment*, when borrowers pay off early (by refinancing or selling the house). Prepayment is its own risk, and it falls unevenly across the tranches. Most mortgage deals pay principal *sequentially*: the senior tranche is paid down first, so it gets shorter and safer over time, while the junior tranches wait. When rates fall and everyone refinances at once, the bonds get repaid far faster than expected; when rates rise and refinancing stops, they stretch out. This sensitivity — getting your money back at exactly the wrong time — is the *negative convexity* that makes mortgage securities behave unlike ordinary bonds, and it is explored in depth in the fixed-income companion to this post. For our purposes, the point is simply that the cash flowing through the waterfall is *lumpy and uncertain in timing*, not a clean fixed coupon — which is one more reason the slices behave so differently from one another.

## The waterfall: paid top-down, lose bottom-up

The tranche structure only makes sense once you internalize a single asymmetry: **cash flows down the stack, and losses climb up it.** The senior tranche sits at the top of the cash waterfall (paid first) but at the top of the *loss* protection (hit last). The equity tranche sits at the bottom of both. The figure below captures the whole idea in one diagram.

![Tranche waterfall stack senior mezzanine equity loss absorption order](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-2.png)

Read it from the top. Every dollar a borrower pays flows first to the senior tranche until its 4% coupon and principal are covered. Only then does cash spill down to the mezzanine, and only then to the equity. Now flip it: when a borrower defaults and the pool loses money, that loss is charged *first* to the equity tranche. The equity holders' \$12.5 million is gone before the mezzanine loses a cent; the mezzanine's \$37.5 million is gone before the senior loses a cent. The equity is the *first-loss* piece; the senior is the *last-loss* piece.

This is why the senior tranche can be rated AAA even when the underlying loans are mediocre. The senior bondholder does not lose a dollar until cumulative pool losses exceed 20% — the combined thickness of the equity (5%) plus the mezzanine (15%) beneath it. In a pool where you expect maybe 2-3% of loans to default and recover some value, a 20% buffer looks enormous. The senior tranche is, in the language of the trade, *credit-enhanced* by the junior tranches that absorb the first losses on its behalf.

#### Worked example: a tranche waterfall absorbing losses

Take our \$250 million pool: senior \$200m, mezzanine \$37.5m, equity \$12.5m. Suppose the housing market sours and the pool ultimately loses **8%** of its value — \$20 million of the \$250 million never comes back.

Walk the waterfall from the bottom. The first \$12.5 million of loss is charged entirely to the **equity** tranche: it is wiped out, a 100% loss. That leaves \$20m − \$12.5m = \$7.5 million of loss still to allocate. That \$7.5 million climbs to the **mezzanine** tranche, which started at \$37.5 million. The mezzanine loses \$7.5m of \$37.5m, a 20% loss — painful, but the tranche survives with \$30m intact. And the **senior** tranche? It loses **nothing**. The entire \$20 million loss was absorbed below it.

So an 8% loss on the pool translates into a 100% loss for the equity holder, a 20% loss for the mezzanine holder, and a 0% loss for the senior holder. The intuition: the same pool delivers wildly different outcomes depending on which slice you bought — tranching does not reduce the pool's total risk, it *redistributes* it, concentrating it at the bottom so the top can be sold as safe.

The chart below generalizes that example across a range of pool losses, so you can see the losses literally climb the stack.

![Stacked bar showing losses absorbed by equity then mezzanine then senior tranche](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-3.png)

At a 2% pool loss, only part of the equity is gone. At 5%, the equity is exactly wiped and the mezzanine is about to start taking hits. At 12%, the equity is gone and the mezzanine is eating the next 7%. Not until cumulative losses pass 20% — the entire equity-plus-mezzanine cushion — does a single dollar of loss reach the senior tranche (the green that appears only in the rightmost bar). The senior holder's entire safety rests on one bet: *that pool losses will stay below 20%.* Hold that thought; it is the exact bet that failed in 2008.

#### Worked example: how a BBB pool becomes a AAA senior tranche

This is the step that sounds like alchemy, so let us make it concrete. Suppose every loan in the pool, taken on its own, would be rated BBB — solidly investment-grade but nowhere near AAA. How can a bond *backed by BBB loans* be rated AAA?

The answer is *subordination*. A rating is, at bottom, an estimate of the probability that the bondholder loses money. A BBB loan might have, say, a 4% chance of producing a loss over its life. But the senior *tranche* does not lose money when one loan defaults — it loses money only when *cumulative pool losses breach the 20% attachment point* beneath it. For that to happen, you would need not one BBB borrower to default but a fifth of the entire pool to default *and* recover little.

If you (heroically, and wrongly, as we will see) assume the defaults are roughly independent — that one homeowner's default tells you little about the next — then the probability that 20% of a 1,000-loan pool defaults at once is astronomically smaller than the 4% chance that any single loan defaults. Run the binomial math on independent 4%-default loans and the chance of a 20% pool-wide loss is essentially zero. *That* near-zero loss probability is what earns the senior tranche its AAA. The figure below shows the transformation: a pool that is 100% BBB in quality becomes, after tranching, 80% AAA.

![Horizontal bar showing a BBB pool becoming 80 percent AAA senior 15 percent BBB mezzanine 5 percent equity](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-7.png)

The intuition, and the entire fault line of 2008 in one sentence: **the AAA rating is real only if the defaults really are roughly independent — and in a nationwide housing bust, they are not.** When house prices fall everywhere at once, the loans default *together*, the 20% buffer that looked impossibly thick gets blown through, and the AAA was never AAA.

To feel why correlation is so deadly, push the two extreme cases. Suppose every loan has a 5% chance of defaulting (and losing everything, for simplicity). In the **fully independent** world, the *expected* loss on the pool is 5%, and — by the law of large numbers — the *actual* loss in any given pool clusters tightly around 5%. A 20% pool loss would require an outcome so far from the mean that it essentially never happens; the senior tranche, attaching at 20%, is genuinely bombproof. Now flip to the **fully correlated** world, where the loans rise and fall together because they all depend on the same thing — national house prices. Here the pool no longer behaves like a thousand coin flips; it behaves like *one* coin flip scaled up. With 95% probability you lose nothing, and with 5% probability the *entire pool* defaults at once. The expected loss is still 5% — the average has not moved — but the *distribution* is now barbell-shaped, and in that 5% bad state the loss is 100%, which obliterates not just the equity and mezzanine but the senior tranche too. Same loans, same average default rate, completely different tranche outcomes. **Tranching's safety is entirely a bet about correlation, and the rating agencies' models set that correlation far too low.** That is the single technical error at the center of the crisis.

## Credit enhancement: every trick that makes the top look safe

The junior-tranche cushion is the main form of credit enhancement, but structurers stack several others on top. It is worth knowing them, because each one is a lever that can be tuned to make the senior tranche *look* safer without the underlying loans being any better.

**Subordination** (the tranche cushion) we have covered: the 20% of junior capital that takes losses first.

**Overcollateralization** means putting more loans into the pool than the bonds you issue against it. Put \$105 million of loans behind \$100 million of bonds, and you have a \$5 million buffer of extra collateral before bondholders lose anything. The extra \$5 million is, in effect, more first-loss protection.

**Excess spread** is the gap between the interest the pool earns and the interest the bonds owe. If the mortgages pay 6% and the weighted-average coupon on the bonds is 4.5%, there is 1.5% of "excess spread" each year. That spread can be trapped in a reserve account and used to cover early losses — a self-funding cushion that exists as long as the loans keep paying.

**A reserve account** is simply cash set aside at the deal's start (or built up from excess spread) to cover shortfalls.

**Third-party guarantees** were the most dangerous form. A *monoline insurer* — a company like Ambac or MBIA whose only business was insuring bonds — would, for a fee, guarantee a tranche, lending its own AAA rating to the bond. In 2008 this created a deadly chain: the bonds were "safe" because the monolines guaranteed them, and the monolines were "safe" because... they had AAA ratings of their own. When the bonds went bad, the monolines could not pay, and their own AAA ratings collapsed, taking everything they had wrapped down with them.

To see how these stack into real protection, put numbers on a deal. Take a \$100 million pool with three layers of enhancement: \$5 million of overcollateralization (the SPV bought \$105m of loans but issued only \$100m of bonds), a \$2 million reserve account funded at closing, and 1.5% of excess spread on \$100m — about \$1.5 million a year that the deal traps to cover early losses. Before a single tranche takes a hit, the deal can absorb \$5m + \$2m + (a few years of) \$1.5m ≈ \$10 million of losses from these cushions *alone*, on top of the equity tranche beneath the bonds. That is roughly 10% of the pool consumed before the rated bonds notice. The point is that credit enhancement is *layered*: each form buys a little more distance between the loans and the senior bondholder. The danger is that every one of these cushions can be modeled, tuned, and marketed — so a deal can be made to *look* heavily enhanced while the loans inside it are quietly deteriorating.

#### Worked example: the equity tranche wiped first

The equity tranche is where the credit enhancement actually lives, so it deserves its own walk-through — because the people who buy it are making a leveraged bet on the pool, whether they say so or not.

Our equity tranche is \$12.5 million (5% of the \$250 million pool). The equity holder is promised everything left over after the senior (4% on \$200m = \$8m a year) and mezzanine (7% on \$37.5m = \$2.625m a year) are paid. The pool earns 6% on \$250m = \$15 million a year. So in a good year the equity holder collects \$15m − \$8m − \$2.625m = \$4.375 million on a \$12.5 million investment — a 35% return. That is the seduction of the equity tranche: when losses are low, the returns are spectacular.

Now let losses arrive. Recall the 8% pool-loss scenario: the equity's entire \$12.5 million is gone — a 100% loss — before the mezzanine loses its first dollar. The equity holder went from a 35% yield to a total wipeout, while a pool loss of only 8% would have barely dented a plain unsliced bond. The intuition: **the equity tranche is the pool's risk, concentrated and levered.** It pays like a venture bet because it *is* one — it exists precisely so the senior tranche above it can be sold as boring and safe.

## Originate-to-distribute: the incentive that quietly rots

So far securitization looks like an unambiguous good: it frees the bank's capital, it lets more credit flow, it gives investors a menu of risk-return choices, and it spreads risk away from the banking system toward investors who choose to hold it. On a good day, all of that is true. The problem is what it does to the bank's incentive to lend carefully.

Under the old model — *originate-to-hold* — the bank that made the loan kept the loan. If the borrower defaulted, the bank ate the loss. That gave the bank a brutally direct reason to underwrite well: to check the borrower's income, verify the appraisal, decline the loans that smelled wrong. The bank's money was on the line for 30 years.

Under *originate-to-distribute*, the bank sells the loan within weeks. The default, when it comes years later, lands on whoever bought the bonds — a pension fund in Norway, an insurer in Tokyo, a German Landesbank. The bank keeps the origination fee no matter what happens to the loan afterward. The figure below contrasts the two worlds.

![Before after comparison originate to hold versus originate to distribute incentives](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-4.png)

Read the left column as the world where the bank's incentive is to lend *well*, and the right column as the world where its incentive is to lend *much*. When you are paid a fee per loan originated, and you bear none of the default, the rational thing is to originate as many loans as you can move — and the way to move more loans is to lower your standards. This is the **moral hazard** at the center of securitization: the party that knows the most about the loan's quality (the originator) has the least reason to care about it once the loan is sold.

This is not a theoretical worry. In the run-up to 2008, originate-to-distribute lending produced an explosion of mortgage products that exist only when nobody plans to hold the loan: *no-doc loans* (no income documentation), *NINJA loans* (No Income, No Job or Assets), *teaser-rate* adjustable mortgages that started at 2% and reset to 9% after two years. No bank that intended to *keep* these loans would have made them. They were made precisely because they could be packaged and sold before they blew up.

#### Worked example: the originate-to-distribute fee versus holding the loan

Put numbers on the temptation. A bank makes a \$250,000 mortgage. Compare two strategies.

**Hold it.** The bank keeps the loan and earns the spread: say it lends at 6% and funds at 2%, a 4% net spread on \$250,000 = \$10,000 a year. But it must hold capital against the loan and it bears the default risk: if there is a 1% annual chance of a default that loses 30% of the loan (\$75,000), the *expected* annual loss is 1% × \$75,000 = \$750. Net, the bank earns roughly \$10,000 − \$750 = \$9,250 a year — and it has \$250,000 tied up and capital reserved for 30 years.

**Originate and distribute.** The bank charges 1% in origination and processing fees on the \$250,000 = \$2,500, sells the loan into a securitization within 60 days for face value, and recovers the entire \$250,000 plus its capital — *immediately*. It bears zero default risk going forward. If it can recycle that \$250,000 into a new loan every two months, the same capital originates six loans a year, earning 6 × \$2,500 = \$15,000 in fees with *no* credit risk retained.

The bank that holds earns \$9,250 a year and carries 30 years of default risk. The bank that distributes earns \$15,000 a year and carries *none*. The intuition: **once a loan can be sold, the fee-per-origination model pays the bank to maximize volume, not quality — and the faster it can distribute, the less any single loan's eventual default matters to it.** That is the moral hazard, expressed in dollars, and it is why originate-to-distribute, left unchecked, bends a banking system toward making loans that should never be made.

## The securitization family: ABS, MBS, CLO, CDO

All securitizations share the machine we have built — pool, SPV, tranche, distribute. They differ in one thing: what sits in the pool. That single difference separates the boring, useful corners of structured finance from the corner that blew up the world. The matrix below lays out the family.

![Matrix comparing MBS ABS CLO and CDO by collateral pool and significance](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-6.png)

**MBS — mortgage-backed securities.** The pool is home mortgages. This is the largest securitization market on earth; in the United States it is dominated by *agency MBS*, issued or guaranteed by Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and Ginnie Mae, where the government effectively backstops the credit risk. Agency MBS are about as safe as a security gets. The trouble in 2008 came from *private-label* (non-agency) MBS — pools of mortgages too risky for the agencies to touch, securitized by Wall Street banks with no government guarantee. We cover the price behavior of MBS — and their notorious *negative convexity* — in depth in the fixed-income series; here the point is simply that mortgages are the canonical securitization collateral.

**ABS — asset-backed securities.** The pool is some other kind of consumer debt: credit-card receivables, auto loans, student loans, equipment leases. ABS is the workhorse of consumer-credit funding. When you take out a car loan, there is a good chance it ends up in an auto-ABS pool funding the lender; when you carry a credit-card balance, it may sit inside a card-ABS trust. These markets are large, mature, and generally well-behaved, because the collateral is diversified across millions of small borrowers and the data on default behavior is decades deep.

**CLO — collateralized loan obligations.** The pool is *corporate* loans — specifically *leveraged loans*, the floating-rate loans banks make to companies that already carry a lot of debt (often companies owned by private-equity firms). A typical CLO holds 100 to 300 different corporate loans and tranches them exactly like an MBS: senior, mezzanine, equity. CLOs are important to understand because they are the part of structured finance that *survived* 2008 largely intact — even through the crisis, AAA CLO tranches paid out in full. We will see why in the final section, because the contrast with CDOs is the whole lesson.

**CDO — collateralized debt obligations.** Here is the dangerous one. A CDO's pool is not loans at all — it is *tranches of other securitizations*, most infamously the leftover BBB mezzanine slices of subprime MBS deals. Those mezzanine slices were hard to sell on their own (who wants the second-loss piece of a subprime pool?), so banks gathered them up, poured them into a new SPV, and *re-tranched them*. The result was a security whose collateral was itself the risky middle of other securitizations — a bet on a bet. And when the same trick was applied again — a CDO whose collateral was tranches of *other CDOs* — you got the **CDO-squared**, a security so abstracted from the underlying homeowners that essentially no one could trace what it actually owned.

The distinction between a CLO and a CDO is the most important one in this section. A CLO is backed by *actual diversified corporate loans*. A 2000s-vintage subprime CDO was backed by *the worst slices of other mortgage deals*, all exposed to the same single risk — US house prices. One was diversification; the other was concentration dressed up as diversification. We turn now to how that concentration, stamped AAA, became the engine of the worst financial crisis in eighty years.

## Common misconceptions

**"Securitization creates risk out of thin air."** No — and this matters. Securitization does not manufacture or destroy the total risk in a pool of loans; it *redistributes* it. If 1,000 mortgages will collectively lose \$20 million, that \$20 million of loss exists whether the loans sit on a bank's balance sheet or are sliced into tranches. Tranching only changes *who bears* the loss and in *what order*. The equity holder volunteers to take the first \$12.5 million; the senior holder is paid to be last in line. What securitization *can* do is *hide* risk — make a pile of risk look safer than it is by relabeling its top slice AAA — and hiding risk is not the same as removing it. The 2008 failure was not that risk was created; it was that risk was *mispriced and mislabeled*, so it ended up held by people who did not understand they held it.

**"AAA means it cannot lose money."** A AAA rating is an opinion about the *probability* of loss, not a guarantee. For a senior tranche, that probability was computed under specific assumptions — chiefly that defaults across the pool would be roughly independent. Change the assumption (assume defaults are highly correlated, as they are in a nationwide housing bust) and the same tranche's loss probability skyrockets. In 2008, AAA-rated subprime CDO tranches lost the majority of their value. The rating was not a lie about the math; it was a lie about the assumptions the math rested on.

**"The bank that makes your loan is the one that holds it."** Increasingly false. A large share of US mortgages are securitized within weeks of origination; the institution that underwrote your loan often has no economic interest in it within a quarter. The entity you mail your payment to (the *servicer*) may be different again. This separation of originating, owning, and servicing is exactly the fragmentation that originate-to-distribute creates — and it is why, in the foreclosure mess after 2008, it was sometimes genuinely unclear *who* even had the legal standing to foreclose.

**"Securitization is inherently predatory or fraudulent."** No. On its merits it is a genuine improvement to how credit is funded: it lets banks lend more against the same capital, gives savers access to diversified pools of credit, and matches long-dated assets (mortgages) with long-dated investors (pension funds and insurers) instead of forcing them onto banks funded by overnight deposits — which is itself a reduction of the maturity-mismatch risk at the heart of this series. Most securitizations, most years, do exactly this and nothing goes wrong. The machine is not evil; it is *powerful*, and power without underwriting discipline and honest ratings is what turns useful plumbing into a weapon.

**"CLOs are just the CDOs of 2008 with a new name."** A common and lazy claim. CLOs and 2008-vintage subprime CDOs share the tranche structure but differ in the one thing that matters — the collateral. A CLO holds a diversified book of actual corporate loans across many industries; a subprime CDO held the concentrated, correlated mezzanine slices of mortgage deals all exposed to one factor. Through 2008-09, AAA and even AA CLO tranches did not take principal losses, while AAA subprime CDO tranches were largely wiped out. Same skeleton, very different body.

**"If the loans are sold to investors, the risk has left the banking system."** This was the comforting story before 2008, and it was wrong twice over. First, banks did not actually distribute all the risk — they kept enormous holdings of their own AAA tranches (they yielded more than Treasuries and counted as "safe" for capital purposes) and ran off-balance-sheet vehicles, funded with short-term paper, that warehoused the stuff. When those tranches went bad, the losses were squarely back inside the banks. Second, even risk that genuinely left the banks landed on insurers, pension funds, and money-market funds whose distress fed straight back into the banking system through funding markets and counterparty exposures. Risk that is "distributed" into a tightly interconnected financial system has not left the building; it has just moved to a room with a thinner door.

## How it shows up in real banks: the 2008 collapse and CLOs today

This is where the abstractions become history. The 2008 financial crisis was, in large part, the securitization machine running past its design limits — and the cleanest way to understand the crisis is to follow one chain link by link.

### The chain from a subprime loan to a global collapse

The figure below traces the path that turned a marginal mortgage in, say, Stockton, California into a write-off on a balance sheet in Düsseldorf.

![Graph of the 2008 chain subprime loans to MBS to CDO to ratings to collapse](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-8.png)

Start at the left. **Subprime mortgages** — loans to borrowers with weak credit, often with teaser rates that would reset sharply upward — were originated in enormous volume between roughly 2004 and 2007, precisely because they could be sold. They were **pooled into subprime MBS** and sliced into the familiar tranches. The **senior MBS tranches**, stamped AAA, sold easily to banks and money-market funds worldwide. But the **mezzanine MBS tranches** — the BBB middle — were hard to sell; nobody is eager to buy the second-loss piece of a pool of NINJA loans.

So Wall Street applied the machine to itself. It gathered those unsellable BBB mezzanine slices, poured them into a new SPV, and re-tranched them into a **CDO**. And here is the part that should make you wince: the rating agencies, using models that assumed low correlation among the underlying mortgages, stamped roughly **80% of a CDO built entirely from BBB collateral as AAA.** A pile of second-loss subprime slices went in; a majority-AAA security came out. **Global investors** — banks, pensions, insurers, and especially the off-balance-sheet vehicles banks themselves ran — loaded up, because AAA paper that yielded more than Treasuries looked like free money.

Then the assumption broke. When US house prices peaked in 2006 and began falling *nationwide*, the subprime borrowers defaulted not one-by-one but *together* — the correlation that the models had assumed away turned out to be nearly total, because every one of these loans was a bet on the same thing. The 20% buffer beneath the senior tranches, which had looked impossibly thick under the independence assumption, was blown straight through. The chart below shows the brutal gap between what each tranche was *priced* to lose and what it *actually* lost.

![Grouped bar chart subprime tranche losses expected versus realized in 2008](/imgs/blogs/securitization-how-banks-turn-loans-into-securities-5.png)

The equity was priced for heavy losses and delivered a total wipeout. The BBB mezzanine, priced for a modest 5% loss, lost most of its value. And the AAA senior tranches — priced to lose essentially nothing — took real, substantial losses, especially in the CDOs, where the AAA was built on already-risky collateral. **AAA bonds collapsed.** Banks that held them, or that had guaranteed them, or that had funded their purchase with short-term borrowing, faced losses that vaporized their thin equity cushions. The interbank market froze because no bank could be sure which other bank was sitting on a hole. That freeze is what took down Bear Stearns, Lehman Brothers, and very nearly the entire system.

### The ratings failure

It is worth dwelling on the role of the rating agencies, because their failure was the linchpin. A pension fund in Norway cannot investigate 5,000 individual subprime mortgages; it relies on the AAA stamp from Moody's or S&P. Those stamps were the bridge that let toxic collateral cross into the portfolios of the world's most conservative investors. Two structural flaws made the stamps unreliable. First, the **issuer-pays model**: the bank creating the security pays the agency to rate it, and an agency that hands out tough ratings loses business to a more generous competitor — a conflict of interest baked into the business model. Second, the **correlation assumption**: the agencies' models assumed mortgage defaults were largely uncorrelated, which made the senior tranches look near-bulletproof; that single assumption was the load-bearing wall, and it was wrong. When agencies later downgraded thousands of tranches at once in 2007-08, the downgrades themselves triggered forced selling and margin calls, accelerating the collapse. We cover the agencies' business model and their post-crisis reckoning in detail in a dedicated piece; the lesson for reading any structured product is blunt: *a rating is only as good as the assumptions inside the model that produced it, and you must ask what those assumptions are.*

### Synthetic CDOs: betting on the loans without owning them

The story has one more turn that made 2008 worse than the underlying mortgages alone could have. By the mid-2000s, demand for mortgage exposure outran the supply of actual mortgages, so the machine found a way to manufacture more exposure without making a single new loan: the **synthetic CDO**. Instead of owning mortgage bonds, a synthetic CDO held *credit default swaps* (CDS) referencing them — insurance-like contracts that pay out if a named bond defaults. The CDO sold protection (took the risk) and collected premiums; investors got the same cash flows and tranches as a normal CDO, but the "collateral" was a *bet* on mortgages, not the mortgages themselves.

The consequence was that the same pool of subprime bonds could be referenced by many synthetic CDOs at once. A few billion dollars of actual bad mortgages could anchor tens of billions of dollars of synthetic exposure, multiplying the system's losses far beyond the size of the real loans. And on the other side of those swaps sat firms that had sold protection in enormous size — most famously AIG's financial-products unit, which had written roughly \$440 billion of credit protection, much of it on supposedly AAA mortgage risk, with essentially no capital reserved against it because the protection was "never going to be needed." When the mortgages went bad, AIG owed collateral it did not have; its near-collapse forced an \$182 billion government rescue, because AIG was the counterparty standing behind a huge share of the banking system's "hedges." The lesson is severe: securitization plus derivatives let the financial system take a *larger* bet on subprime mortgages than the entire stock of subprime mortgages — risk was not just mislabeled, it was *cloned*.

### Washington Mutual and the originate-to-distribute mortgage banks

The crisis also killed the originators themselves. Washington Mutual — at \$307 billion in assets, the largest bank failure in US history — had built a mortgage machine optimized for volume and distribution, heavy in exactly the option-ARM and subprime products that only make sense if you plan to sell them. When the secondary market for private-label MBS froze in 2007-08, the distribution end of originate-to-distribute simply stopped working: the bank was suddenly *holding* loans it had always intended to sell, the losses came home, and depositors fled. IndyMac, another thrift built on the same model, failed the same year. The deeper banking lesson — explored in the failure case studies later in this series — is that originate-to-distribute does not eliminate the originator's risk; it converts it into *pipeline risk* and *funding risk*, which sit dormant until the moment the market for the product closes, and then arrive all at once.

### CLOs today: the machine that learned (mostly)

After 2008 it would have been reasonable to expect securitization to die. It did not. The agency-MBS market never stopped functioning, and the corporate-loan corner — CLOs — has grown to well over a trillion dollars in the United States and is a routine part of how leveraged corporate lending is funded. The reason CLOs survived where subprime CDOs detonated comes straight from this post: a CLO's collateral is a *genuinely diversified* book of corporate loans across many industries, actively managed, with real first-loss equity (often 8-10% of the deal) held by managers with their own money at stake — meaningful credit enhancement and aligned incentives, rather than the paper-thin, correlated-collateral structures of the worst CDOs. Through the 2020 COVID shock and the 2022-23 rate spike, CLO senior tranches again paid in full.

That is not a clean bill of health. CLOs concentrate exposure to highly indebted companies, the leveraged-loan market has weakened lender protections (the rise of *covenant-lite* loans), and a deep corporate-default cycle has not yet tested the post-2008 vintages the way the housing bust tested subprime. The honest summary is that the *structure* works when the *collateral* is real and diversified and the *first-loss piece* is thick and held by someone with skin in the game — and fails when any of those three is faked. Securitization did not need to be abolished; it needed honest underwriting, honest ratings, and real subordination. Where it has those, it is useful plumbing. Where it lacks them, it is 2008 waiting to happen again.

### The regulatory fix: putting skin back in the game

The single biggest lesson regulators drew from 2008 was the one this post keeps returning to: the originate-to-distribute model breaks when the originator keeps none of the risk. So the headline post-crisis reform attacked exactly that. Under the Dodd-Frank Act in the United States and parallel rules in Europe, a sponsor of most securitizations must now **retain at least 5% of the credit risk of the deal** — the so-called *risk-retention* or "skin-in-the-game" rule. The idea is elegant: if you have to keep a slice of every deal you create, you will care about the quality of the loans inside it, because your own money rides on them. A bank can satisfy the rule by holding 5% of every tranche (a *vertical* slice), or by holding a 5% first-loss *horizontal* slice (the equity), or a mix.

Risk retention is a real improvement, but it is a partial one, and it is worth being clear-eyed about its limits. Five percent is a thin tether; an originator earning fat fees on volume can still find that 5% worth eating. The rules carved out major exemptions — notably qualifying residential mortgages and, after a 2018 court ruling, open-market CLO managers in the United States. And rules do not fix incentives that route *around* them: the synthetic-CDO trick, which created exposure without making loans at all, sidesteps the spirit of risk retention entirely. The honest verdict is that the reforms made the worst abuses harder and the system meaningfully safer, but they did not repeal the underlying tension. Securitization will always pull the originator toward volume and away from quality; regulation can lean against that pull, but only retained risk, honest verification, and investors who actually read what they buy can hold the line. The machine is only ever as safe as the discipline imposed on it.

## The takeaway: how to read any securitization

Step back to the spine of this series. A bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine that lives or dies on two things: whether its funding stays, and whether its thin equity cushion absorbs losses faster than they arrive. Securitization is, at heart, a tool for managing *both* — it converts frozen long-dated loans into cash and frees the capital that backs them, letting the same equity support far more lending. Used with discipline, it makes the maturity-transformation trade safer, not riskier, by moving long-dated credit risk off the fragile deposit-funded balance sheet and onto investors who actually want to hold it.

The danger is never the machine; it is what the machine lets people stop caring about. The moment a bank can sell a loan, it stops bearing the loan's default — and unless something forces it to keep skin in the game, its incentive shifts from lending *well* to lending *much*. The moment a tranche can be stamped AAA, the people who buy it stop investigating what is inside — and unless the rating rests on honest assumptions, the stamp is a bridge that carries hidden risk into the safest portfolios in the world. Every failure of securitization traces to one of those two abdications of attention.

So when you encounter any structured product — an MBS, an ABS, a CLO, or something with a more exotic name — ask the four questions this post was built to let you answer. *What is actually in the pool?* (Real diversified loans, or the correlated leftovers of other deals?) *How thick is the first-loss cushion beneath the senior tranche, and who holds it?* (Is there real subordination, and does the holder have skin in the game?) *What assumption is the rating resting on?* (Independence? A house-price floor? Ask, because that assumption is the load-bearing wall.) And *who eats the loss if the loans go bad, and do they understand that they will?* If you can answer those four, you can tell the difference between the securitization that funds the world's mortgages quietly and well, and the kind that takes an 85-year-old investment bank to \$2 a share in a weekend. The machine does not decide which one you are looking at. The collateral, the cushion, the assumptions, and the incentives do.
