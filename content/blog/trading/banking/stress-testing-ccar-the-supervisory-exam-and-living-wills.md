> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A supervisory stress test hands every large bank the same imaginary recession, runs the bank's actual loan and trading book through it, and checks whether enough capital survives. It is the regulator's answer to one question: if a deep slump arrives, does this bank stay above its capital floor or run out of cushion?
>
> - A stress test has three moving parts: a **scenario** (a recession written down as numbers — unemployment, GDP, house prices), a **loss projection** (what those numbers do to the bank's portfolio over nine quarters), and a **post-stress capital ratio** (where capital ends up after the losses).
> - In the US the exercise is **CCAR/DFAST**, run by the Federal Reserve; in the EU it is the **EBA stress test**. Passing means the bank keeps a CET1 ratio above the 4.5% minimum even in the severely-adverse scenario — and earns the right to pay dividends.
> - On top of the math, human examiners grade a bank on six dimensions known as **CAMELS** (Capital, Assets, Management, Earnings, Liquidity, Sensitivity). A 1 is healthy; a 5 is on the brink.
> - Banks must also write a **living will** — a resolution plan for how they can fail without taking the system down. The goal is a **bail-in** and orderly wind-down instead of a Lehman-style freeze.
> - The number to remember: in the 2010 wave, **157 US banks failed in a single year**. Stress testing exists so that number never returns.

In February 2009, with the financial system still smoldering, the US Treasury and the Federal Reserve did something that had never been done at scale. They took the nineteen largest American banks, wrote down a hypothetical recession deeper than the one already underway, and ran every bank's balance sheet through it to see how much money each would lose and whether its capital would hold. They called it the Supervisory Capital Assessment Program — SCAP for short — and the results were published openly in May 2009. Ten of the nineteen banks were told to raise a combined \$75 billion of new capital. The shocking part was not that ten banks were short. It was that publishing the numbers *calmed* the panic instead of deepening it. For the first time, the market could see which banks were solid and which were not, and the fog of fear lifted.

That exercise became the template for everything modern bank supervision does today. The annual ritual it spawned in the United States is called CCAR — the Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review — and its quieter cousin DFAST, the Dodd-Frank Act Stress Test. Europe runs its own version through the European Banking Authority. They all do the same thing: they invent a bad-enough future, push the bank through it on paper, and ask whether the thin sliver of equity that funds the bank survives the journey. This post is about how that machine works, why it exists, and where it has both saved the system and quietly let a disaster slip through.

![How a supervisory stress test works from scenario to capital actions](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model for the whole post. A stress test is a five-step pipeline: the regulator writes a recession as a scenario, projects the losses that scenario inflicts on the bank's book, subtracts those losses to get a post-stress capital ratio, checks that ratio against a hard floor to decide pass or fail, and uses the result to allow or block the bank from paying out capital. Everything else — CAMELS, living wills, the EBA test — hangs off this skeleton. Let us build it from zero.

## Foundations: stress tests, scenarios, capital ratios, and the words you need

Before any of this makes sense, we have to define the vocabulary. If you have read other posts in this series, some of this will be review; if not, this section gives you everything you need to follow the rest.

Start with the thing being protected: **capital**. A bank funds itself mostly with other people's money. Of every dollar of assets a typical large bank holds, roughly 71 cents is funded by deposits, about 10 cents by short-term wholesale borrowing, about 7 cents by long-term bonds, a few cents by other liabilities, and only about **8 cents by the bank's own equity** — the money put in by shareholders plus profits it never paid out. That 8 cents is the *capital*. It is the only money that belongs to the bank itself, and it is the only money that can absorb a loss without breaking a promise to a depositor or a lender. When a bank loses money, the loss eats into capital first. Run out of capital and the bank is insolvent: it owes more than it owns.

A **capital ratio** measures how thick that cushion is, relative to how risky the bank's assets are. The headline ratio is **CET1** — Common Equity Tier 1 — the purest, most loss-absorbing form of capital (essentially common shares plus retained earnings), divided by the bank's **risk-weighted assets** (RWA). RWA is the asset base scaled up or down by how risky each asset is: a government bond gets a low weight, a risky corporate loan a high one. So a CET1 ratio of 12% means the bank holds twelve dollars of pristine equity for every hundred dollars of risk-weighted exposure. The regulatory *minimum* CET1 ratio is **4.5%**; with the standard capital-conservation buffer on top, banks are really expected to keep at least **7%**. Fall below 4.5% and you have failed the most basic test of solvency. (For the full mechanics of how RWA and these ratios are built, see [risk-weighted assets and how capital ratios really work](/blog/trading/banking/bank-capital-and-leverage-why-equity-is-the-thin-cushion) — capital and leverage are covered in depth there.)

A **stress test** is a forward-looking experiment. Instead of asking "is the bank healthy today?" it asks "would the bank still be healthy *after* a severe but plausible shock?" You take the bank's current portfolio, apply a scenario, and project how capital evolves over a defined horizon. In CCAR that horizon is **nine quarters** — a little over two years.

A **scenario** is that shock, written down as numbers. It is not a story; it is a precise path for a set of macroeconomic and financial variables: the unemployment rate, real GDP, house prices, commercial real estate prices, stock prices, the level of interest rates, credit spreads, and so on. The Federal Reserve publishes three scenarios each year: a **baseline** (roughly the consensus economic forecast), an **adverse** (a moderate downturn), and a **severely adverse** (a deep recession). The severely-adverse scenario is the binding one — the exam everyone studies for.

**CCAR** (Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review) is the Fed's annual program for the largest US bank holding companies. It combines the quantitative stress test with a qualitative review of the bank's capital plan — its proposed dividends and buybacks. **DFAST** (Dodd-Frank Act Stress Test) is the closely related, more mechanical version mandated by the 2010 Dodd-Frank law; the two share the same scenarios and loss models. In practice people use "the Fed's stress test" to mean the whole apparatus.

The **post-stress capital ratio** is the punchline of the whole exercise: the bank's CET1 ratio at its *lowest point* over the nine-quarter horizon, after all projected losses are subtracted. If that minimum stays above the 4.5% floor (plus, since 2020, an extra requirement we will get to called the stress capital buffer), the bank passes.

**CAMELS** is a separate, older tool: a supervisory rating system that human examiners use to grade a bank from 1 (best) to 5 (worst) on six components — **C**apital adequacy, **A**sset quality, **M**anagement, **E**arnings, **L**iquidity, and **S**ensitivity to market risk. CAMELS is the on-the-ground judgment that surrounds the math.

A **resolution plan** — colloquially a **living will** — is a document each large bank must write describing exactly how it could be wound down in bankruptcy without disrupting the broader financial system or requiring a taxpayer bailout. A **bail-in** is the mechanism at the heart of modern resolution: instead of the government injecting money (a *bail-out*), the bank's own shareholders and certain bondholders absorb the losses — their equity is wiped out and their bonds are converted to equity or written down — so the bank can be recapitalized from within while its critical functions keep running.

With those eight terms defined, the rest of the post is just turning the crank.

## The scenario: writing a recession as a set of numbers

The first thing a stress test needs is a recession. Not a real one — an imagined one, specified precisely enough that every bank runs the same gauntlet and the results can be compared. This is the part that looks the least like banking and the most like macroeconomic forecasting in reverse.

Each January the Federal Reserve releases its scenarios for the year. The severely-adverse scenario is deliberately brutal. In a typical recent year it has assumed the US unemployment rate roughly *doubling* from around 4% to around 10%, real GDP falling by something like 8% peak to trough, house prices dropping by roughly 30%, commercial real estate prices falling by a similar amount, and equity prices crashing by about 50%. Layered on top are a flight to safety in government bonds, a spike in corporate credit spreads, and a separate "global market shock" component applied to the biggest trading banks — an instantaneous repricing of their trading positions as if markets had seized in a single day.

![Three Fed scenarios from baseline to severely adverse across four variables](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-3.png)

The matrix above lays the three scenarios side by side. The baseline is roughly business as usual: unemployment near 4%, GDP growing about 2% a year, housing calm, stocks drifting up. The adverse scenario is a mild-to-moderate downturn. The severely-adverse scenario is the one that matters — it is calibrated to be at least as bad as a serious post-war US recession, and in the variables that hurt banks most (unemployment, asset prices) it is roughly as severe as 2008. Importantly, the Fed designs the scenario to be *countercyclical*: when the economy is booming and risks are building, the hypothetical recession gets harsher, not gentler. The idea is to lean against the human tendency to assume good times will last.

Why specify *these* particular variables? Because each one maps to a way a bank loses money. Unemployment drives consumer loan defaults — people who lose jobs stop paying credit cards and mortgages. House prices drive mortgage losses through the value of the collateral: if a home is worth less than the loan, a default becomes a loss. Commercial real estate prices hit a bank's CRE book, often its riskiest large exposure. Equity and credit-spread shocks hit the trading book and any holdings of securities. Interest-rate paths drive both net interest income and the value of the bank's bond portfolio. The scenario is, in effect, a list of all the levers that can move a bank's profit and loss, set to their painful positions at once.

For the largest trading banks there is an extra, separate ingredient bolted onto the macro scenario: the **global market shock**. While the macro scenario plays out gradually over nine quarters, the global market shock is an *instantaneous* repricing of the bank's trading positions — credit spreads gap wider, equity and commodity prices crash, and volatility explodes, all assumed to happen in a single moment as if a 2008-style market seizure struck overnight. This component exists because a bank's trading book can lose enormous sums in days, long before a slow recession would register in its loan losses. A handful of the very biggest banks also face a **counterparty default** component, which assumes their single largest trading counterparty fails outright, leaving the bank to absorb whatever it was owed. The point of these add-ons is that the same recession that slowly poisons a loan book can also detonate a trading book instantly, and the test must capture both speeds of loss.

There is one more design choice worth understanding: the scenario is deliberately *not* a forecast. The Fed is not predicting that unemployment will hit 10%; it is asking what would happen if it did. This distinction matters because critics sometimes complain that the scenario "didn't predict" the crisis that actually arrived. That is not its job. A fire drill does not predict a fire; it tests whether the building can be evacuated if one happens. The severely-adverse scenario is a fire drill for the balance sheet — a stylized, severe, internally-consistent stress chosen to be hard enough that surviving it is meaningful, not a best guess at next year's economy. The trade-off, which we will return to, is that a drill only rehearses the exit it was designed around; a fire that starts somewhere unexpected can still catch the building off guard.

#### Worked example: reading the severely-adverse scenario

Suppose the Fed's severely-adverse scenario this year says: unemployment rises from 4.0% to 10.0% over the first year and stays there; house prices fall 28%; equity prices fall 50%; and BBB corporate spreads widen by 350 basis points (a *basis point* is one hundredth of a percent, so 350 bps is 3.5 percentage points). A bank with a \$200 billion mortgage book now knows two things at once: its borrowers are more likely to default (unemployment up) and, when they do, the loss per default is larger (collateral worth 28% less). A bank with a \$50 billion BBB corporate bond portfolio knows its bonds just repriced lower by roughly the spread move times duration — if those bonds have a duration of 5 years, a 3.5-point spread widening is roughly a \$50 billion × 5 × 3.5% = \$8.75 billion mark-to-market hit. The scenario is not abstract: every line of it translates into a number the bank must absorb. That is the intuition — a scenario is a recession converted into a list of losses waiting to happen.

## Projecting losses: turning the scenario into a number

Once you have the recession on paper, you have to run the bank's actual portfolio through it. This is the heaviest machinery in the whole exercise, and it is where supervisors and banks both pour enormous modeling effort.

In principle it is simple. Take every category of the bank's assets and ask: under this scenario, how much of this will I lose, and over what period? The Fed and the banks run models that translate the scenario variables into loss rates for each portfolio. A consumer-credit model takes the unemployment path and produces a projected charge-off rate on credit cards. A mortgage model takes unemployment plus house prices and produces a projected loss rate on the mortgage book. A commercial-loan model takes GDP and spreads and produces a default-and-loss path for corporate loans. The trading book gets the global market shock applied directly. And the income side gets projected too — pre-provision net revenue, the bank's earnings *before* loan losses, which partly offsets the damage because the bank keeps earning fees and interest even in a recession.

![Projected stressed loss rates by portfolio under the severe scenario](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-4.png)

The chart above shows the rough hierarchy of pain by portfolio under a severely-adverse run, expressed as a cumulative loss rate over the horizon. Credit cards are the worst — unsecured, lent to consumers who lose jobs, they routinely show stressed loss rates in the mid-to-high teens. Commercial real estate is next, because CRE values can fall hard and many loans are large and concentrated. Commercial-and-industrial (C&I) loans and other consumer loans sit in the middle. First-lien mortgages are surprisingly resilient — they are secured by a home and most borrowers keep paying — so even a 28% house-price fall produces a low single-digit loss rate. Trading and counterparty losses depend heavily on the bank's book. The ranking is the point: a bank heavy in credit cards and CRE will project far larger losses than one heavy in mortgages, even with identical assets on paper.

The reason this matters is that the stress test rewards a *diversified, well-collateralized* book and punishes concentration in the portfolios that bleed under stress. A bank cannot game the test simply by being big; it has to actually hold assets that survive a recession. That is the discipline the test imposes: it makes the cost of risky lending visible *before* the recession arrives, in the form of the extra capital the bank must hold against it.

Inside the machinery, the loss models lean on three parameters that come up throughout bank risk management — and they are worth knowing because they show exactly how a scenario becomes a dollar loss. The first is **PD**, the *probability of default*: the chance a borrower stops paying over a given period. The second is **LGD**, *loss given default*: of the amount owed, the fraction the bank actually loses after recovering whatever collateral it can — so an LGD of 45% means the bank loses 45 cents on the dollar and recovers 55. The third is **EAD**, *exposure at default*: how much the borrower owes at the moment of default. Multiply them and you get the *expected loss* on an exposure: expected loss = PD × LGD × EAD. A stress scenario works by *moving these parameters in the bad direction* — unemployment pushes PD up, falling collateral values push LGD up — so the same loan that carried a tiny expected loss in calm times carries a large one in the scenario. (This is the same machinery used in everyday loan pricing and provisioning; the stress test just runs it at recession settings.)

There is a second loss channel that surprises people: **provisioning**. Modern accounting (CECL in the US, IFRS 9 in much of the world) forces a bank to set aside reserves for *expected future* losses the moment the economic outlook darkens, not when loans actually go bad. So in the early quarters of a stress scenario, a bank takes a large up-front provision expense as it recognizes the coming losses — which is exactly why the CET1 path in the next chart falls so steeply at first and only recovers later, once those front-loaded reserves are built and earnings start outpacing new losses. The accounting rule shapes the *timing* of the capital trough, concentrating the damage early.

#### Worked example: PD, LGD, and EAD under stress

Take a single \$10 million commercial loan. In normal times the borrower is a solid BBB credit with a 1-year PD of about 0.24% and, because the loan is senior unsecured, an LGD of about 45%. Expected loss = 0.0024 × 0.45 × \$10,000,000 = about **\$10,800** a year — trivial. Now apply the severely-adverse scenario. Recession stress might lift this borrower's PD to 3% (a downgrade-and-distress jump) and push LGD to 60% as recovery values fall. Expected loss = 0.03 × 0.60 × \$10,000,000 = **\$180,000** — sixteen times larger. Multiply that kind of jump across an entire loan book and you see how a scenario that "only" assumes 10% unemployment converts into tens of billions of projected losses. The intuition: stress does not invent new loans to lose money on; it cranks up the probability and severity of loss on the loans the bank already holds.

#### Worked example: projecting loss on a portfolio

Take a \$100 billion credit-card portfolio and apply a 16.5% cumulative stressed loss rate over the nine quarters. The projected loss is \$100 billion × 0.165 = **\$16.5 billion**. Now take a \$200 billion first-lien mortgage book at a 2.5% loss rate: \$200 billion × 0.025 = **\$5 billion**. Notice that the smaller portfolio (cards) produces more than three times the loss of the larger one (mortgages), purely because cards are unsecured and consumers default hard when unemployment spikes. If this bank also has \$80 billion of CRE at a 9% loss rate, that adds \$80 billion × 0.09 = \$7.2 billion. Total projected credit losses so far: \$16.5 + \$5 + \$7.2 = **\$28.7 billion**. The intuition: the test forces a bank to confront not how big its book is, but how *fragile* its specific mix of loans is under one specified storm.

## From losses to a post-stress capital ratio

Now we connect the losses back to capital. This is the heart of the whole exercise, and the place where the abstract recession becomes a single pass-or-fail number.

The mechanics are an accounting walk forward, quarter by quarter. The bank starts with its current CET1 capital and its current RWA. Each quarter of the scenario, three things happen. Projected losses on loans and securities are subtracted from capital. Pre-provision net revenue — the bank's stressed earnings — is added back, partly offsetting the losses. And RWA may rise or fall as the scenario plays out. The CET1 ratio at the end of each quarter is the running CET1 capital divided by the running RWA. The test tracks this ratio across all nine quarters and finds its *minimum* — the worst point on the path. That minimum is the post-stress CET1 ratio.

![A bank CET1 ratio path falling to a trough then rebuilding under stress](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-2.png)

The chart above traces a typical CET1 path through a severe stress test. The bank starts at 12%. As losses pile up in the early quarters — the recession bites hardest at first — capital falls and the ratio drops to a trough around 8.3% in the middle of the horizon. Then, as the bank keeps earning and the worst losses are already booked, capital starts to rebuild and the ratio climbs back. The two horizontal lines are the thresholds that matter: the red dashed line at 4.5% is the hard CET1 minimum, and the amber dotted line at 7% is the level with the standard buffer. This bank's trough of 8.3% sits comfortably above both. It passes.

The shape of that curve — down, then up — is itself a lesson. A stress test is not asking "could the bank survive the worst single quarter?" It is asking "does the bank have enough capital to absorb the front-loaded losses of a recession *and* keep operating until earnings recover?" The trough is the moment of maximum danger. A bank passes if its trough never breaches the floor.

#### Worked example: a bank's capital ratio falling under the severe scenario

Start a bank with \$120 billion of CET1 capital against \$1,000 billion of RWA — a CET1 ratio of 120 / 1,000 = **12.0%**. Now apply our recession. Over the nine quarters, projected credit and trading losses total \$60 billion, while stressed pre-provision net revenue contributes \$25 billion back. Net hit to capital: \$60 − \$25 = \$35 billion. (Assume the bank pays no dividends during stress and RWA stays roughly flat for simplicity.) Ending CET1 capital: \$120 − \$35 = \$85 billion. Post-stress CET1 ratio: 85 / 1,000 = **8.5%**. The trough along the way dipped to about 8.3% before earnings pulled it back up. Compare 8.3% to the 4.5% minimum: the bank clears it by 3.8 percentage points, which on \$1,000 billion of RWA is \$38 billion of spare capital. The intuition: the post-stress ratio tells you how much of the cushion a recession would eat — here, the bank gives up a third of its capital but keeps two-thirds, and stays well above the line.

## Pass or fail, and what passing actually buys you

Here is the subtle part that trips up most people: in modern CCAR, "pass" and "fail" are not the whole story, because the stress test result *sets* the capital requirement rather than just checking against a fixed one.

In the early years (2009 to 2019), the logic was binary. The Fed could "object" to a bank's capital plan on either quantitative grounds (the post-stress ratio fell below the minimum) or qualitative grounds (the bank's internal risk and capital processes were not good enough). An objection meant the bank could not pay the dividends and buybacks it had requested. A pass meant it could.

Since 2020, the mechanism is more elegant. The stress test now produces a number called the **stress capital buffer** (SCB): essentially, the amount of CET1 the bank lost from peak to trough in the severely-adverse scenario, with a floor of 2.5%. That buffer is then *added on top of* the 4.5% minimum to set the bank's actual ongoing CET1 requirement. A bank that bleeds more capital under stress gets a bigger buffer and must hold more capital all the time. A bank that is more resilient gets a smaller buffer. The test no longer just grades you — it *prices* your risk into your everyday capital requirement.

![Pass versus fail and the capital actions each one unlocks](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-5.png)

The before-after figure above shows what is really at stake. The left column is a failing bank: its trough of 3.8% breaches the 4.5% floor, its capital plan is rejected, all distributions are frozen, and it must either raise fresh equity or shrink its balance sheet to rebuild the ratio before it can return a cent to shareholders. The right column is a passing bank: its stressed trough of 8.7% sits above the floor, its capital plan is not objected to, and it may pay dividends and buy back stock — constrained only by its stress capital buffer. This is why the stress test is, in practice, a *gate on payouts*. A bank's executives and shareholders care intensely about dividends and buybacks; the Fed knows it, and uses that lever to enforce capital discipline. Fail, and the spigot closes.

This is also why the test shapes behavior all year, not just on results day. A bank that wants to pay generous dividends must keep enough capital that even a severe recession would leave it above the floor *plus* its buffer. The test, in other words, quietly determines how much capital the entire large-bank system carries — and therefore how much it can lend, how much it returns to investors, and how much shock it can absorb before any of it touches a depositor.

#### Worked example: pass/fail versus the minimum

Two banks enter the same severely-adverse scenario. Bank A starts at 11.0% CET1 and troughs at 7.5%. Bank B starts at 9.0% and troughs at 3.8%. The minimum is 4.5%. Bank A's trough of 7.5% is 3.0 points above the floor — a clear pass — and its stress capital buffer is the peak-to-trough drop of 11.0 − 7.5 = 3.5 points (above the 2.5% floor, so the SCB is 3.5%), making its ongoing CET1 requirement 4.5% + 3.5% = 8.0%. Bank B's trough of 3.8% is *below* 4.5% — a fail. Its capital plan is rejected, distributions are frozen, and it must rebuild. The intuition: the same recession can be a routine pass for a well-capitalized bank and an existential event for a thin one, and the only difference is how much equity each chose to hold going in.

### The qualitative side: the capital plan and the review

There is a half of CCAR that does not appear in any chart, and for years it was where banks actually got tripped up: the **qualitative review**. Alongside the quantitative stress test, each bank submits a *capital plan* — a detailed proposal for how much it intends to pay in dividends and buybacks over the coming year, and the analysis and governance behind that decision. The Fed reviews not just the numbers but the *process*: how the bank identifies its risks, whether its internal models are sound, whether its board genuinely understands the capital decisions it is approving, and whether its data and controls are good enough to trust the outputs.

In the program's early years this qualitative objection had real teeth. Several large banks — including the US arms of some major foreign banks — *passed* the quantitative test with capital to spare but had their plans rejected on qualitative grounds, because their risk-management infrastructure was judged inadequate. The message was pointed: it is not enough to have the capital; you must be able to *prove you know why you have it and how it would behave*. The Fed has since folded most of the qualitative assessment into normal year-round supervision rather than a once-a-year public objection, but the principle endures. A stress test result is only as credible as the bank's ability to produce and defend it, which is why so much of the work happens in the governance and data plumbing that never shows up in the headline ratio.

## The CAMELS exam: the human judgment around the math

Stress testing is the quantitative side of supervision, but it is not the whole of it. Long before SCAP, and still today for banks of every size, regulators send human examiners into banks to grade them. The framework they use is **CAMELS**, and it is the qualitative backbone of bank supervision in the United States (with close analogues elsewhere). Where the stress test is a model run once a year on the biggest banks, CAMELS is an ongoing, judgment-based assessment that covers every insured bank, large or small.

CAMELS rates a bank from 1 to 5 on each of six components, and then assigns a composite 1-to-5 rating. A composite **1** is a sound bank with no significant concerns. A **2** is fundamentally sound with minor weaknesses. A **3** has weaknesses that warrant supervisory attention. A **4** has serious problems threatening viability. A **5** is critically deficient and likely to fail without immediate action. These ratings are confidential — banks do not disclose them — but they drive everything supervisors do, from how often they inspect a bank to whether they force it to raise capital or restrict its growth.

![The six CAMELS components and what a weak score in each means](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-6.png)

The matrix above breaks down the six letters. **C — Capital adequacy**: is the equity cushion big enough for the bank's risk profile? **A — Asset quality**: how good is the loan book; are loans going bad faster than reserves can cover? **M — Management**: are the people running the bank competent, with sound controls and risk governance? This is the most judgmental and often the most decisive component. **E — Earnings**: is the bank reliably profitable, or are earnings thin, volatile, or propped up by one-offs? **L — Liquidity**: can the bank meet deposit outflows and funding needs without fire-selling assets? **S — Sensitivity to market risk**: how exposed is the bank to moves in interest rates and other market factors — the duration-gap problem that destroyed Silicon Valley Bank lives here.

The crucial insight is that the six components interact, and a single weak letter can sink a bank that looks fine on the others. A bank can be well-capitalized (a strong C) and still fail because its management (M) made a catastrophic bet, or its liquidity (L) evaporated in a run, or its sensitivity (S) to rising rates blew a hole in its bond portfolio. CAMELS exists precisely because solvency on paper is not the same as soundness in practice. The stress test checks the *capital* arithmetic; CAMELS checks whether the rest of the bank — its people, its funding, its risk discipline — is sound enough to keep that capital from evaporating in the first place.

#### Worked example: scoring a CAMELS rating

Take a mid-size bank in early 2023. Its capital ratio is healthy at 12% (C: 1, strong). Its loan book is clean, mostly to solid borrowers (A: 2, good). But it has loaded up on long-dated bonds bought when rates were near zero, and as rates rose those bonds lost roughly 20% of their value — a sensitivity problem (S: 4, weak). Its funding is concentrated in large, uninsured, flighty deposits from a single industry (L: 4, weak). And management never hedged the rate risk or diversified the funding (M: 4, weak). Even though capital and assets look fine, three weak letters — S, L, and M — drag the composite to a **4**. The arithmetic of capital said "solvent"; CAMELS said "fragile." Within weeks the bank could be gone. The intuition: a bank does not fail on the average of its scores — it fails on its worst one, and CAMELS is built to catch the single weakness the capital ratio hides.

## Resolution planning: how a bank is supposed to fail safely

Stress testing and CAMELS are about keeping a bank *alive*. But regulators learned a harder lesson in 2008: some banks will fail anyway, and the way they fail matters as much as whether they fail. When Lehman Brothers collapsed on 15 September 2008, the problem was not only that it was insolvent — it was that *nobody knew how to wind it down*. Its assets froze in bankruptcy courts across dozens of jurisdictions, counterparties could not get their collateral back, and the uncertainty radiated outward until the entire short-term funding market seized. The failure of one firm became a failure of the system.

The response was **resolution planning**, mandated for large banks by Dodd-Frank and by parallel rules in Europe and the UK. Every systemically important bank must now write and maintain a **living will** — a detailed plan, submitted to regulators, describing exactly how it could be resolved in bankruptcy in an orderly way: which legal entities hold which assets, how its critical functions (payments, custody, deposit-taking) would keep running through a failure, how it could be sold or wound down over a weekend, and how losses would be allocated. Regulators review these plans and can reject them as "not credible," forcing the bank to simplify its structure until failure becomes manageable.

The financial engine inside a modern resolution is the **bail-in**. The old way to save a failing bank was a **bail-out**: the government injects taxpayer money to plug the hole. That is politically toxic and creates moral hazard — if banks know they will be rescued, they take more risk. The bail-in inverts it. Instead of the taxpayer paying, the bank's own investors pay, in strict order of seniority. Shareholders are wiped out first. Then a special layer of bonds — designed for exactly this purpose, called TLAC (Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity) or, in Europe, MREL — is written down or converted into new equity. The losses are absorbed *inside* the bank's own capital structure, recapitalizing it from within, while depositors are protected and the bank's critical functions never stop. The bank can then be restructured, sold, or wound down without a run and without a check from the government.

![Resolution paths from living will to bail-in versus a disorderly collapse](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-8.png)

The graph above contrasts the two roads out of a failure. When a bank's losses exceed its equity, it is insolvent — that is the top node. If a credible living will exists, the path goes down the left: a bail-in wipes out shareholders and imposes losses on bondholders, the bank is recapitalized, and an orderly wind-down keeps critical functions running and deposits safe. If no credible plan exists, the path goes down the right: a disorderly freeze locks up assets Lehman-style, which triggers contagion — runs spread, the funding system seizes, and a bailout becomes the only option. The whole apparatus of resolution planning exists to make the left path always available, so that no bank is ever "too big to fail" in the sense that the only choice is a taxpayer rescue.

#### Worked example: a bail-in absorbing a loss

A bank has \$80 billion of equity, \$40 billion of bail-in-able (TLAC) bonds, and \$880 billion of other liabilities including \$700 billion of deposits, against \$1,000 billion of assets. Now it discovers \$95 billion of losses — more than its equity. In a disorderly failure, those losses would cascade toward depositors and the panic would spread. In a bail-in, the order is mechanical: the \$80 billion of equity is wiped out first (absorbing the first \$80 billion of loss), then \$15 billion of the \$40 billion TLAC bonds is written down to cover the remaining loss. The bondholders who lose are the ones who knowingly bought risk-bearing debt. Depositors lose nothing. The bank, now recapitalized with the surviving \$25 billion of converted TLAC as fresh equity, keeps operating. The intuition: a bail-in routes a loss that would have caused a panic into the specific investors who were paid to bear it — equity and junior debt — and leaves the deposit franchise and the payment system untouched.

This is the deepest connection back to the spine of this whole series. A bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine; its entire existence depends on depositors trusting that their money is safe. Resolution planning is the regulator's promise that even when a bank dies, that trust is honored — the depositor is made whole and the system keeps running — so that one bank's death does not become every bank's run.

## The EBA test and how the global regime fits together

The United States runs CCAR and DFAST; the European Union runs its own exercise through the **European Banking Authority** (EBA), in coordination with the European Central Bank's Single Supervisory Mechanism and national regulators. The mechanics rhyme with the American version: the EBA publishes a common adverse scenario, banks project losses and capital across a multi-year horizon (the EU uses a three-year horizon), and the results feed into each bank's capital requirements. The EBA test is typically run every two years on the largest EU banks and, unlike the US test, has historically not had a single hard pass/fail threshold — instead the results inform the supervisory dialogue and the "Pillar 2" capital guidance each bank receives. The Bank of England runs its own annual cyclical scenario for major UK banks. The common thread across all of them is identical to the model we built: a scenario, a loss projection, a post-stress capital ratio, and a consequence.

There are real differences in philosophy worth knowing. The US CCAR is *bank-run* in the sense that each bank produces its own projections, but the Fed also runs its *own* independent models and, crucially, publishes the result for each named bank — radical transparency that lets the market reward the strong and pressure the weak. The EBA exercise is more *constrained bottom-up*: banks project losses using their own models under tight EBA-imposed methodology and caps, and the results feed a supervisory dialogue rather than a single hard pass/fail line. The EU also historically used a *static balance sheet* assumption — the bank is assumed not to shrink or change its book as the recession hits — which is more conservative in one sense (no credit for clever management) but less realistic in another. Different choices, same purpose: convert a hypothetical recession into a capital number that cannot be flattered away.

The reason every major jurisdiction now does this is the 2008 lesson, generalized. Capital ratios measured at a single point in time are backward-looking and easy to flatter. A bank can look strongly capitalized in calm markets and be desperately thin the moment a recession exposes the true riskiness of its assets. Worse, a bank's own incentives push toward *under*-stating risk: lower measured risk means lower required capital, which means more room to lend and pay dividends. The stress test is the one tool that forces the question "what would happen *if*?" and puts a number on the answer that the bank cannot wriggle out of — because the regulator writes the scenario, not the bank. For the regulator's-eye view of how Basel and the global capital framework set the rules these tests enforce, see the broader piece on [the BIS and Basel bank regulation](/blog/trading/finance/bis-and-basel-bank-regulation).

## Common misconceptions

**"Passing the stress test means the bank is safe."** No. Passing means the bank would stay above its capital floor in *one specific scenario the Fed wrote*. A real crisis can take a shape the scenario did not anticipate. The 2023 failures of Silicon Valley Bank and First Republic happened to banks that were not even subject to the full annual stress test, and the trigger — a rate-driven collapse in bond values combined with an ultra-fast digital deposit run — was not the kind of credit-loss recession the severely-adverse scenario centers on. A pass is evidence of resilience to a defined shock, not a guarantee of survival against all shocks.

**"Stress tests are just a paperwork exercise."** The opposite is true: the test directly sets how much capital a bank must hold (through the stress capital buffer) and whether it may pay dividends and buy back stock. In 2020, when COVID hit, the Fed used its stress-test authority to *freeze* big-bank buybacks and cap dividends, forcing the system to preserve capital. Tens of billions of dollars of shareholder payouts hinge on the result. That is not paperwork.

**"The bank's own losses are what the test measures."** Subtly wrong. The test does not measure what a bank *did* lose; it projects what it *would* lose under a hypothetical future, using models. The numbers are estimates, not history. This is a strength — it is forward-looking — and a weakness — the models can miss a risk, as the rate-risk blind spot at SVB-type banks showed. A stress test is only as good as the scenario it specifies and the models it uses.

**"A bail-in is the same as a bailout."** They are opposites. A bailout uses outside money (usually taxpayers') to rescue a bank, protecting its investors. A bail-in uses the bank's own investors' money — wiping out shareholders and writing down junior bondholders — to absorb the loss, protecting taxpayers and depositors. The 2023 wipeout of \$16 billion of Credit Suisse AT1 (Additional Tier 1) bonds was a real bail-in: the holders of those bonds bore the loss while the bank was absorbed by UBS, and no government check went to the bondholders.

**"CAMELS is obsolete now that we have stress tests."** Not at all. The stress test only applies to the largest banks and only models capital under a macro scenario. CAMELS covers every insured bank and grades dimensions the stress test barely touches — the quality of management, the soundness of liquidity, the reliability of earnings. The two are complementary. Indeed, the banks that failed in 2023 would have been flagged far more by their CAMELS weaknesses (sensitivity to rates, concentrated flighty funding) than by a credit-recession stress test.

**"If a bank has a living will, it can't trigger a crisis."** A living will is a plan, not a guarantee, and plans collide with reality. Credit Suisse had a full resolution plan and was still resolved through an emergency state-brokered takeover by UBS rather than the textbook process — because regulators feared that even an orderly resolution of a bank that size, executed for the first time in a panic, might itself spark contagion. The living will lowers the odds of a disorderly collapse and makes a bail-in possible; it does not make a giant bank's failure painless. The honest claim is narrower: a credible plan turns "no good options" into "at least one option that doesn't require a taxpayer check."

**"Banks with more capital always pass and banks with less always fail."** The level of capital matters, but so does its *resilience* — how much of it survives the scenario. A bank with a high starting ratio but a concentrated, fragile book can shed more capital under stress than a bank with a lower starting ratio and a diversified, well-collateralized book. The test measures the *trough*, not the *start*. This is why two banks with identical headline ratios can get very different stress capital buffers, and why the test rewards the shape of the book, not just the size of the cushion.

## How it shows up in real banks

**The 2009 SCAP — the test that ended the panic.** In the depths of the crisis, the Treasury and Fed ran the first modern stress test on the nineteen largest US bank holding companies, covering about two-thirds of the banking system's assets. They published the results openly in May 2009 — an act of radical transparency at a moment of maximum fear. The exercise found that ten of the nineteen banks needed to raise a combined roughly \$75 billion of additional capital to withstand a deeper recession. The market reaction was the opposite of what the doomsayers predicted: instead of triggering runs on the weak banks, the disclosure *removed* the uncertainty that had paralyzed everyone. Investors could finally tell solid banks from shaky ones, capital flowed back in, and the banking system stabilized within months. SCAP is the proof of concept for the entire modern apparatus: a credible, transparent stress test can be a circuit-breaker for a panic.

**The annual CCAR ritual.** Since 2011, the Fed has run CCAR every year on the largest US banks. The numbers are large: in a typical recent severely-adverse run, the Fed has projected aggregate losses across the tested banks of well over \$500 billion over the nine quarters — the bulk of it in loan losses, with tens of billions more from trading and counterparty shocks at the biggest dealers. Despite that, the system has consistently been found to hold enough capital to absorb the hit and stay above the minimums, with the aggregate CET1 ratio falling by several points to a trough still comfortably above 4.5%. The annual rhythm has institutionalized capital discipline — banks now plan their dividends and buybacks around the stress capital buffer the test will assign them, and the Fed periodically updates the scenario to lean against whatever risks are building (adding exploratory components for things like higher-for-longer rates or a private-credit shock). The ritual's quiet success is measured in the failures that did not happen: the 2010 peak of 157 bank failures in a single year has never returned for the large banks the test covers.

![FDIC insured bank failures per year from 2005 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills-7.png)

The chart above is the real-world stakes, drawn from the FDIC's failed-bank list. The 2009-2012 wave dwarfs everything: 140 failures in 2009 and 157 in 2010, the deluge that stress testing was invented to prevent from recurring. Then a long, quiet stretch — entire years with zero failures. And then the 2023 spike, which by *count* was tiny (just five banks) but by *assets* was the second-largest failure wave in US history, because three of those five were enormous: Silicon Valley Bank, Signature, and First Republic. That contrast is the most important thing on the chart. The stress-test regime tamed the *number* of failures. It did not, on its own, prevent a handful of very large, very fast failures driven by a risk the annual credit-recession scenario was not designed to catch.

**SVB's exemption from full stress testing.** This is the cautionary tale that ties the whole post together. Silicon Valley Bank had grown into the 16th-largest US bank, with about \$209 billion in assets, but it was *not* subject to the full annual CCAR/DFAST regime. A 2018 law (the Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act) raised the asset threshold for the strictest supervision from \$50 billion to \$250 billion and gave the Fed discretion below that line. Banks in SVB's size tier were moved to a lighter, less frequent stress-testing cadence. So the bank that most needed a hard look at its interest-rate sensitivity — it had piled roughly \$91 billion into long-dated held-to-maturity securities that carried about \$17 billion of unrealized losses as rates rose — was the one that escaped the annual exam. When the duration trap sprung and a digital run pulled \$42 billion in a single day (with another ~\$100 billion queued for the next morning), the bank was gone in roughly 36 hours. Even a standard stress test centered on credit losses might not have caught it; what would have caught it was a hard supervisory focus on the *S* and *L* of CAMELS — sensitivity to rates and liquidity of funding. SVB is the clearest evidence that the stress-test machine is necessary but not sufficient: it works on the risks it is pointed at, and a bank exempted from it, or stress-tested for the wrong recession, can still die. (The full anatomy of that collapse is in the piece on [SVB and Credit Suisse in 2023](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs).)

**Credit Suisse and the AT1 bail-in.** In March 2023, the 167-year-old Credit Suisse — a global systemically important bank with a living will and a full resolution plan — failed in slow motion as depositors pulled roughly CHF 110 billion in a single quarter and confidence collapsed. The Swiss authorities did not run the textbook resolution; they brokered an emergency takeover by UBS for about CHF 3 billion. But they *did* use the bail-in tool: roughly CHF 16 billion of Credit Suisse's AT1 (Additional Tier 1) bonds were written down to zero, imposing the loss on those bondholders rather than on the public purse. Controversially, AT1 holders were wiped out while shareholders received some value in the UBS deal — an inversion of the usual seniority that triggered lawsuits. The episode showed both the power and the messiness of the bail-in idea: the loss-absorbing layer did absorb a loss, sparing taxpayers, but the way it was applied taught the market a hard lesson about exactly what that layer is for.

## The takeaway / How to use this

If you remember one thing, make it this: a stress test is the regulator's attempt to *answer the spine of this entire series with a number*. A bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine, and its life hangs on whether its thin equity cushion can absorb losses faster than a recession can inflict them. The stress test invents the recession, inflicts the losses on paper, and measures whether the cushion holds. CAMELS surrounds that math with human judgment about the parts a model cannot capture — management, liquidity, the duration trap. And the living will is the admission that, despite all of it, some banks will die anyway, plus a plan to make sure they die without taking everyone else down.

So when you read about a bank — as an investor, a depositor, a citizen, or just a curious reader — you now have three lenses that regulators actually use. First, *would its capital survive a severe recession?* That is the stress-test question, and for big US banks the answer is public every June in the form of the stress capital buffer. Second, *is the bank sound beyond its capital ratio* — competent management, sticky funding, low sensitivity to rate moves? That is the CAMELS question, and the 2023 failures are the proof that it is the one most often missed. Third, *if it failed, could it fail safely?* That is the resolution question, answered by whether a credible living will and enough bail-in-able debt exist.

The deepest insight is that none of these tools makes a bank safe across the board. They make a bank safe *against the specific futures the regulator specified and the dimensions the examiner graded*. The system's failures — Lehman in 2008, SVB in 2023 — were always the risk that fell *outside* the specified scenario or *below* the supervised threshold. That is not an argument against stress testing; the 2010 wave of 157 failures, never repeated for the large banks, is the argument for it. It is an argument for humility about what any test can promise. The honest version of "this bank passed its stress test" is "this bank would survive the recession the regulator drew up." Whether it survives the one that actually comes is the question no test can fully close — which is exactly why the living will exists. A bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded machine; the entire supervisory apparatus, from the stress scenario to the bail-in clause, is one long attempt to make sure that when confidence is tested, the equity cushion and the resolution plan are already in place — because by the time the recession arrives, it is far too late to build either.
