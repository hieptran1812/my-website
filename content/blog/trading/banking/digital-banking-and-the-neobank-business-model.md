> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A neobank is a bank with the branch deleted: it can serve a customer for a few dollars a year instead of a few hundred, but it also tends to win only a thin slice of that customer's money, so revenue per user is a fraction of an incumbent's and most neobanks struggle to clear their own costs.
>
> - The cost side is genuinely revolutionary — no branches, no tellers, cloud-native — so the cost to serve a customer falls from \$300–\$500 a year to single-digit dollars, and viral app growth pulls customer-acquisition cost (CAC) down to \$10–\$40.
> - The revenue side is the trap. A neobank that is a "second account" earns maybe \$15–\$120 per user per year, mostly card interchange, against an incumbent primary account that earns several hundred — because the deposit spread scales with the balance, and the salary lives at the old bank.
> - The decisive variable is the **primary-account problem**: does the salary land in your app or not? If it does, deposits per user jump from a few hundred dollars to thousands, and net interest income — the real engine of banking — finally switches on.
> - Owning a banking licence lets a neobank keep the deposit spread but costs capital, compliance, and years; renting one through banking-as-a-service (BaaS) is fast but hands the best economics to the sponsor bank.
> - The one number to remember: a neobank only works when **lifetime value beats acquisition cost by roughly 3-to-1**, and for a thin user earning \$40 a year against a \$30 CAC, it barely does.

In early 2021, a digital bank in Brazil called Nubank was preparing to go public, and the headline number was staggering: more than 40 million customers, most of them acquired with almost no marketing spend, signed up because a friend had a purple card and said it was good. The cost to bring in each new customer was a small fraction of what a traditional bank pays — a few dollars where a branch bank might spend a hundred or more. For a moment it looked like the entire economics of banking had been rewritten. Acquire customers almost for free, run no branches, ride an exponential growth curve, and the profits would surely follow.

Many of them did not follow. Across the rest of the industry, the same playbook — beautiful app, viral referral, free account, zero branches — produced tens of millions of users and a river of losses. Some of the most celebrated names in European digital banking spent the better part of a decade reporting annual losses while their user counts climbed into the millions. The puzzle is sharp and worth sitting with: if you can acquire a customer for almost nothing and serve them for almost nothing, how do you lose money on them?

The answer is the whole subject of this post, and it traces straight back to the spine of this series. A bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine: it takes in deposits and lends them out, earning the spread, and it lives or dies on whether that spread covers its costs and its losses. A neobank slashes one side of that equation — the cost of running the machine — to almost zero. But it often fails to win the other side: the cheap, sticky deposits that make the spread worth earning in the first place. The figure below is the mental model for everything that follows.

The diagram above is the mental model: a neobank trades a heavy cost base for a thin revenue base. Whether that trade is a brilliant business or a slow bonfire depends entirely on the middle box — how much of the customer's actual money you end up holding. Let us build the whole thing from zero.

## Foundations: the words you need before anything makes sense

The reader of this post may have used a digital bank without ever knowing what one technically is, so we will define every term carefully and only once. A *bank*, at its core, is a licensed institution allowed to take *deposits* — money the public lends to it, usually repayable on demand — and to make *loans*. It earns the gap between what it pays depositors and what it charges borrowers; that gap is the *spread*, and the spread expressed as a percentage of the bank's interest-earning assets is the *net interest margin*, or NIM. If you want that engine in full, this series covers it in [retail deposits and why cheap money is the franchise](/blog/trading/banking/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise); here we only need the headline: deposits are the fuel, and the spread is the income.

A *neobank* — the terms *challenger bank* and *digital bank* are used almost interchangeably — is a bank that delivers all of this through an app and a website, with **no physical branches**. There is no marble lobby, no teller window, no drive-through. Everything happens on a phone. That single architectural choice changes the cost structure so profoundly that it deserves its own definition: a neobank is *digital-only*, meaning its entire customer-facing operation is software, and a customer can open an account, get a card, move money, and contact support without ever speaking to a human in a building.

Now the economic vocabulary. **Customer-acquisition cost (CAC)** is the average amount a company spends to bring in one new customer — total sales-and-marketing spend in a period divided by the number of customers gained. A traditional bank's CAC is high because branches, advertising, and account-opening bonuses are expensive; a neobank's CAC can be very low because users invite each other. **Lifetime value (LTV)** is the total profit (technically, the total *contribution margin* — revenue minus the variable cost to serve) a company expects to earn from one customer over the whole time they stay, discounted for the fact that future money is worth less than money today. The single most important ratio in any subscription-or-account business is **LTV divided by CAC**: if it is comfortably above one, each customer is worth more than they cost; the rough industry rule of thumb is that you want LTV/CAC of about 3 or higher to leave room for the fixed costs the per-user math ignores.

Next, the revenue lines. **Interchange** is a small fee, set by the card networks (Visa, Mastercard), that the merchant's bank pays the cardholder's bank every time the card is swiped — typically a fraction of a percent up to roughly 1.5–2% of the purchase in markets without caps. For a neobank whose main product is a debit or prepaid card, interchange is often the largest single revenue source. The mechanics of who pays whom on a card transaction are covered in detail in [the cards business and the interchange and MDR split](/blog/trading/banking/the-cards-business-issuing-acquiring-interchange-and-the-mdr-split); the point here is simply that interchange is real money that flows to the bank that issued your card every time you spend.

The **primary account** is the account where your salary is paid in and out of which your rent, your bills, and your everyday spending flow. It is the account you would be most reluctant to lose. The primary account holds large average balances and generates the most activity, which is exactly why it is the prize every bank fights for. A *secondary* account — a top-up card you load for holidays, a budgeting app you use for coffee — holds little money and generates little revenue.

**ARPU** stands for *average revenue per user*: total revenue divided by total active users, usually quoted per year. It is the number that tells you, in one figure, how much each customer is worth on the revenue line. And finally, the structural choice that quietly decides a neobank's economics: **owning a banking licence versus renting one through banking-as-a-service (BaaS)**. A licensed bank can hold deposits on its own balance sheet, lend them out, and keep the spread; a neobank without a licence partners with a *sponsor bank* that holds the deposits under its charter, and the neobank earns a thinner slice — typically a share of interchange and a fee — while the sponsor keeps the deposit spread. The full system view of open banking, BaaS, and embedded finance has its own post in this series at [open banking, banking-as-a-service, and embedded finance](/blog/trading/banking/open-banking-apis-banking-as-a-service-and-embedded-finance); here we focus on what the licence choice does to the unit economics.

Two more pieces of vocabulary close the foundation. **Cost to serve** is the annual operating cost a bank incurs to keep one customer — the share of branches, staff, technology, payments processing, fraud, and support attributable to a single account. It is the denominator against which all the revenue lines are measured: a customer is profitable when revenue per user exceeds cost to serve plus their share of fixed and credit costs. And **churn** is the rate at which customers leave — the share of users who stop being active in a given year. Churn is the silent killer of lifetime value, because LTV is contribution margin multiplied by the *number of years* a customer stays; double the churn and you roughly halve the LTV, which can flip a business into a bonfire without the per-year economics changing at all.

It is also worth being precise about the difference between *fixed* and *variable* costs, because the entire neobank debate turns on it. The cost to serve we just defined is largely *variable* — it scales up roughly in step with the number of customers. But a bank also carries large *fixed* costs that do not move with the next customer: the banking licence and the regulatory-capital it requires, the core engineering organisation, the compliance and financial-crime function, the senior management, the brand. A neobank's path to profit is fundamentally a race between rising total contribution margin (variable revenue minus variable cost, summed across all users) and these stubborn fixed costs. Hold that distinction; it is the spine of every worked example below.

With that vocabulary in hand, we can now look at the model itself.

## The digital-only model: deleting the most expensive thing a bank owns

To see why the neobank model is genuinely radical, start with what a branch *is* from an accounting point of view. A branch is a long-term lease on prime retail real estate, a team of salaried staff, cash-handling and security infrastructure, and a share of a regional and head-office overhead. Industry estimates have long put the all-in cost of a single bank branch in the hundreds of thousands of dollars a year, and a large bank runs hundreds or thousands of them. That cost is spread across the customers the branch network serves, and it is the dominant reason a traditional bank's cost to serve a retail customer lands somewhere in the range of \$300 to \$500 a year once you load in branches, call centres, legacy technology, and back-office staff.

A neobank deletes the branch entirely. Its cost to serve a customer is essentially the marginal cost of running software: a slice of cloud-computing bills, the card-processing and payment-rail fees, fraud and compliance screening, and a customer-support team that handles everyone in the country from one room (or, increasingly, with chatbots handling the first layer). Reasonable estimates for a well-run digital bank put the cost to serve an active customer in the single-digit to low-tens of dollars per year. That is not a 10% improvement; it is a roughly *fifty-fold* reduction on the cost-to-serve line.

This is the half of the story the early hype got right, and it is genuinely important. Because the cost to serve is so low, a neobank can profitably serve customers a traditional bank cannot. A customer with a \$400 balance who makes a few card payments a week is a money-loser for a branch bank — the cost to serve them exceeds anything they generate — but can be marginally profitable for a neobank whose cost to serve them is \$8. This is why neobanks did so well at first among the young, the underbanked, the gig-economy worker, and the traveller: customer segments the incumbents had quietly given up on because the branch model could not serve them at a profit.

#### Worked example: deposits per user, neobank versus a branch bank

Let us make the cost gap concrete and connect it to the engine of banking. Suppose a branch bank holds an average of \$18,000 per retail customer (a primary account with salary, savings, and a buffer). At a net interest margin of roughly 3% — the recent US industry figure runs around 3.2% — that balance throws off about \$18,000 × 3% = \$540 a year in net interest income, before any fees. Against a cost to serve of, say, \$400, that customer is comfortably profitable on the spread alone.

Now take a neobank whose typical user keeps it as a spending card, with an average balance of \$350. The same 3% spread on \$350 is just \$350 × 3% = \$10.50 a year. Even with a near-zero cost to serve of \$8, the deposit spread barely covers the cost of keeping that customer's lights on. The neobank cut its costs fifty-fold — but its deposit-driven revenue fell *fifty-fold too*, because the spread scales with the balance, and the balance is tiny. The intuition: deleting the branch fixes your cost base, but it does nothing, by itself, to win the deposits that make the spread worth earning.

That single example is the heart of the whole problem. But before leaving the cost side, it is worth being honest that the cost advantage, while real, is not infinite — and the places where it erodes are instructive. The cloud bill and the support team genuinely scale far better than branches ever could; a chatbot that resolves a balance query costs a fraction of a cent against a teller's minutes. But two cost lines refuse to shrink the way the optimists hoped. The first is **fraud and financial crime**: a digital-only, instantly-opened, no-face account is a magnet for fraudsters, money mules, and identity theft, so neobanks have to spend heavily on transaction monitoring, identity verification, and chargebacks — and when they underspend, regulators step in, as several have learned. The second is **compliance and regulatory capital**: the moment a neobank holds a licence, it inherits the full apparatus of capital rules, reporting, audits, and supervision, none of which gets cheaper per customer in the way the app does. So the honest version of the cost story is: a neobank's *customer-facing* cost is revolutionised, but its *risk-and-regulation* cost is a heavy fixed burden that does not melt away. The cost advantage is concentrated in the branch deletion, not spread evenly across the whole machine.

Now let us go deeper into the revenue side, because deposits are only one of three ways a neobank can make money.

## The unit economics: three thin revenue lines

A neobank's revenue per user comes from three sources, and understanding their relative size explains almost everything about the industry's struggles and its rare successes. The chart below breaks down the annual revenue per user for a typical neobank against an incumbent primary account.

![Annual revenue per user split into interchange interest and subscription for a neobank versus an incumbent](/imgs/blogs/digital-banking-and-the-neobank-business-model-2.png)

The three lines are interchange, net interest, and subscription-plus-fees, and they behave very differently.

**Interchange** is the foundation revenue for most neobanks, especially in their early years. Every time a customer pays with the neobank's debit card, the neobank (as the card issuer) collects a small interchange fee. The crucial detail is that interchange is a percentage of *spending*, not of *balance* — so it does not require the customer to keep much money in the account, only to use the card. This is why neobanks push so hard to get their card to the top of your phone's wallet and to be the card you tap for coffee. A user who spends \$1,500 a month, or \$18,000 a year, at an average interchange of, say, 0.4% in a capped market or 1.3% in an uncapped one, generates between roughly \$72 and \$234 a year in interchange. That is real, and for a thin user it can be the *largest* of the three lines.

But here is the catch that shapes the entire industry, and it is worth stating as a hard fact: **interchange is capped or under pressure in most large markets.** In the European Union, debit interchange is capped at 0.2% of the transaction and credit at 0.3% by regulation. In the United States, the *Durbin Amendment* (part of the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act) caps debit interchange at roughly 0.05% plus 21–22 cents per transaction — but, critically, only for banks with more than \$10 billion in assets. Smaller banks, and the neobanks that partner with them, are *exempt* and can earn the much higher unregulated debit interchange of around 1.2–1.5%. This single regulatory quirk is the financial backbone of the US neobank industry: by keeping their sponsor banks small, neobanks harvest "exempt" interchange that a JPMorgan-scale bank legally cannot. It is also a fragile foundation, because it depends on a regulatory carve-out that could narrow at any time.

**Net interest** is the deposit spread plus any lending income, and it is the line that separates a real bank from a card-with-an-app. As the worked example above showed, net interest is brutal for a thin user because it scales with the balance. But it is enormous for a primary account: the chart shows the incumbent's net-interest bar towering over everything else, because that is where the \$18,000 balance lives. The entire strategic battle of the neobank industry is the fight to grow this bar — to turn a spending card into the account where the money actually sits.

**Subscription and fees** is the third line: a monthly fee for a premium tier (metal cards, travel insurance, higher limits), foreign-exchange margins on overseas spending, overdraft fees, interchange on a "buy now, pay later" product, and so on. Some of the more successful neobanks lean heavily here — a subscription tier converts a free user with thin economics into a paying user with predictable recurring revenue, and it is one of the few levers that does not depend on the customer keeping a large balance. The risk is that you can only charge a subscription if the product is good enough that people will pay; most users stay on the free tier, so the paying minority has to subsidise the whole base.

#### Worked example: revenue per user = interchange + interest + subscription

Let us build a realistic thin-user ARPU from the three lines, using round numbers. Take a neobank user in an uncapped-interchange market who spends \$1,000 a month and keeps an average balance of \$350.

- **Interchange:** \$1,000 × 12 months = \$12,000 of annual spend, at roughly 0.4% net to the issuer after network and processing costs, gives about \$48 a year. (In a capped EU market this would be closer to \$24; we will use \$24 to match the figure's conservative split.)
- **Net interest:** \$350 average balance × a 3% spread = about \$10 a year.
- **Subscription and fees:** most users are on the free tier; blended across all users, the average is maybe \$6 a year (a few premium subscribers spread thin over many free ones).

Add them up: roughly \$24 + \$10 + \$6 = **\$40 per user per year**. That is the thin-user ARPU the rest of this post will use, and it matches the left-hand bar in the revenue chart. Now compare it to the incumbent primary account at roughly \$70 interchange + \$380 net interest + \$90 fees = **\$540 per user per year**. The intuition: a neobank's typical user is worth less than a tenth of an incumbent's primary account, and the gap is almost entirely the net-interest line — the deposit balance that the salary, not the spending, creates.

That \$40-versus-\$540 gap is why the cost savings, real as they are, do not automatically rescue the model. We can now look at the acquisition side and see whether \$40 a year is enough.

## CAC versus LTV: the same low cost, a business or a bonfire

Here is where the famously low acquisition cost meets the famously thin revenue, and where most of the industry's fate is decided. The chart below shows the deciding comparison: the cost to acquire one user against the lifetime value that user throws off, for two kinds of user.

![Customer acquisition cost compared with lifetime value for a thin user and a primary account user](/imgs/blogs/digital-banking-and-the-neobank-business-model-3.png)

Customer-acquisition cost is the neobank's great advantage. Because users refer each other, because the product is a free app one tap away in the store, and because there is no branch to staff, a well-executed neobank can acquire a customer for \$10 to \$40 — against a traditional bank that might spend \$150 to \$300 once you account for account-opening bonuses, advertising, and the share of branch cost attributable to acquisition. So the neobank starts with a CAC roughly *ten times lower*. That sounds decisive. It is not, on its own, because LTV is what CAC has to be measured against.

#### Worked example: CAC versus LTV for a thin user and a primary user

Take the thin user from the last example: ARPU of \$40 a year, cost to serve of \$8, so the annual *contribution margin* (revenue minus variable cost) is \$40 − \$8 = \$32. Suppose the average thin user stays for about 3 years before churning to another app. Ignoring discounting for simplicity, the lifetime value is roughly \$32 × 3 = \$96 — but neobank churn is high and engagement decays, so a more realistic figure, after a discount for time and for users who go dormant, lands closer to \$40. Against a CAC of \$30, the LTV/CAC ratio is about \$40 / \$30 = **1.3**.

That is the bonfire case. An LTV/CAC of 1.3 means each customer is worth only a little more than they cost to acquire — and that thin margin has to cover all the *fixed* costs the per-user math ignored: the banking licence or sponsor fee, the engineering team, the head office, the fraud losses, the regulatory capital. With a 1.3 ratio and large fixed costs, the more customers you acquire, the more money you lose, because every cohort barely pays for itself before the overhead even enters the picture.

Now take a primary-account user: ARPU of \$540, cost to serve of \$30 (more support, more product), contribution margin \$510 a year. Even with a discounted multi-year LTV that we will conservatively call \$360 (one year's margin, heavily haircut for churn and time), against the same \$30 CAC the ratio is \$360 / \$30 = **12**. That is the business case. An LTV/CAC of 12 leaves enormous room to cover fixed costs and still profit.

The takeaway is stark: *the same low acquisition cost is a business or a bonfire depending entirely on which kind of user you acquire.* The neobanks that struggle are not bad at acquisition — they are often brilliant at it. They are acquiring the \$40 user when the model only works with the \$540 user. The whole game, once you see this, is converting the former into the latter.

## Why profit is so hard: the unit-economics loop

It helps to see the whole dynamic as a loop, because the variables feed back into each other. The graph below traces it.

![Diagram of the neobank unit economics loop from cheap acquisition through thin revenue to scale or profit or burn](/imgs/blogs/digital-banking-and-the-neobank-business-model-4.png)

Read the loop from the top. **Viral app growth** brings users in cheaply — the great strength. But it delivers them at a **thin revenue per user**, because most new users start as spending cards or second accounts. Meanwhile the neobank carries a stack of **fixed costs**: the banking licence and the risk team (if it owns one), the engineering organisation, fraud and financial-crime screening, compliance, and the cloud bill. Those fixed costs do not shrink per user as you grow — they are spread over the user base, which means there are only two ways out of the trap, and the diagram shows both.

**Path A is scale.** If revenue per user is thin but positive at the margin, and fixed costs are fixed, then at some user count the total contribution margin finally exceeds total fixed cost and the firm tips into profit. This is the classic software logic: lose money while you are small, make money once you are huge. The problem for neobanks is that the per-user contribution is so thin that the break-even user count is enormous — often in the many millions — and getting there burns years of investor cash. Some never reach it before the funding runs out.

**Path B is to become the primary account.** Instead of waiting for sheer numbers to overwhelm the fixed cost, the neobank deepens the relationship so that each user is worth far more: the salary lands in the app, the balance grows, deposits-per-user climbs, and the net-interest line — the towering bar in the revenue chart — finally switches on. A neobank that converts even a modest fraction of its base into primary-account users transforms its economics, because it moves those users from the \$40 column to something approaching the \$540 column.

**Path C is the stall:** thin users, no scale, and a perpetual cash burn covered by raising more equity until investors lose patience. This is where a large share of the industry has lived. The chart's red boxes are not a moral failing; they are the default outcome of a model that nails acquisition and cost but never solves the revenue side.

#### Worked example: how many users to break even

Let us compute a break-even user count to see why scale alone is so punishing. Suppose a neobank has \$120 million a year of fixed costs — licence, engineering, compliance, head office — and a thin-user contribution margin of \$32 a year (the \$40 ARPU minus \$8 cost to serve from earlier). To break even on a pure-scale basis, it needs total contribution to equal fixed cost:

\$120,000,000 ÷ \$32 per user = **3,750,000 users.**

It needs roughly 3.75 million genuinely active, card-using customers just to cover its fixed costs — before earning a cent of profit and before any marketing spend to acquire those users. Now watch what happens if the firm instead lifts the average contribution per user to \$80 (by pushing subscriptions and a bit more interchange and balance): \$120,000,000 ÷ \$80 = 1,500,000 users. The same fixed cost is covered by less than half as many users. And if it converts users to primary accounts averaging \$300 of contribution: \$120,000,000 ÷ \$300 = 400,000 users. The intuition: raising ARPU does not just improve the margin — it slashes the scale you need to survive, which is why every serious neobank's strategy is ultimately about ARPU, not user count.

## The primary-account problem: the variable that decides everything

If you remember only one idea from this post, make it this one. The deepest reason most neobanks struggle is that they win the customer's *attention* but not the customer's *money*. People love the app, the instant notifications, the clean design, the fee-free spending abroad — and they keep their salary, their savings, and their emergency buffer at the boring old bank they have used for fifteen years. The neobank becomes the fun second card, not the financial home.

This matters because, as we have now seen from three angles, the single largest revenue line in banking is the deposit spread, and the deposit spread scales with the *balance*, which is governed by whether the account is primary. The chart below shows the gulf in deposits per user across account roles.

![Average deposit balance per user for a spending card neobank a maturing neobank and an incumbent](/imgs/blogs/digital-banking-and-the-neobank-business-model-5.png)

A spending-card neobank might hold \$350 per active user. A maturing neobank that has started winning salaries and savings might hold \$2,500. An incumbent retail bank holds something like \$18,000 — the full primary relationship. The whole strategic distance a neobank must travel is the gap between the first bar and the third.

Why is the primary account so sticky, so hard to dislodge? Three reasons, each worth naming. First, **inertia and trust**: people are reluctant to move the account that pays their rent, because a glitch there is a catastrophe, and trust in a bank you have used for years is hard to transfer to a two-year-old app. Second, **switching friction**: every direct debit, standing order, and automatic payment is wired to the primary account, and re-pointing them all is a chore even where regulators have built switching services to ease it. Third, the **deposit-insurance and "realness" question**: customers want to know their money is protected (the FDIC limit in the US is \$250,000 per depositor per bank, with €100,000 schemes in the EU and £85,000 in the UK), and they want to be sure the institution is a *real* bank, not a fintech that might vanish. A neobank renting a licence has to make that protection legible to a nervous customer, which is harder when the deposits technically sit at a sponsor bank the customer has never heard of.

#### A note on what "deposits per user" really buys

It is worth pausing on *why* the deposit balance, not the spending, is the prize — because it is counterintuitive that a quiet savings balance beats an active spender. The answer is leverage and duration. A primary account's balance is not just a source of spread today; it is *cheap, sticky funding* that lets the bank lend at a profit, and it tends to stay put through a rate cycle even when the bank pays little on it. That is the franchise the whole series keeps returning to — the cheap deposit base described in [retail deposits and the funding base](/blog/trading/banking/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise). A spender who keeps \$350 gives you interchange but no funding; a primary-account holder who keeps \$18,000 gives you both. The neobank that only ever wins the spender has, in a real sense, only won half a bank.

#### Worked example: what one converted primary account is worth

It is worth quantifying the prize directly, because the numbers are dramatic. Take a thin user worth \$40 a year. Convert them into a primary account: the balance rises from \$350 to \$2,500, so the net-interest line goes from \$350 × 3% = \$10.50 to \$2,500 × 3% = \$75; their card spending roughly doubles as they use the account for everything, lifting interchange from \$24 to about \$48; and they become far more likely to take a paid premium tier, adding perhaps \$30 of subscription. Their ARPU climbs from \$40 to roughly \$75 + \$48 + \$30 = \$153 — almost a *four-fold* increase from one behavioural change. Now spread that across a base: a neobank that converts just 20% of 5,000,000 users into primary accounts has added one million users worth \$153 instead of \$40, an extra \$113 per converted user, or about \$113 million of annual revenue — with no extra acquisition spend at all. The intuition: a conversion is worth far more than an acquisition, because it multiplies the value of a customer you already paid to win, which is why the smartest neobanks pour their energy into *deepening* rather than only *widening* the base.

The neobanks that have broken through are precisely the ones that solved this. They did it by becoming the place the salary lands — through good current-account features, early-salary access, attractive savings rates, and relentless nudging — and by then layering on lending, which we turn to next, because lending is the other half of becoming a real bank. It is no accident that the deposit balance and the lending opportunity are linked: a bank that holds your salary can see your income and your spending, which is exactly the information it needs to underwrite a loan to you safely. The primary account is not just the source of cheap funding; it is the source of the *data* that makes the lending profitable rather than reckless. Winning the salary, in other words, unlocks both halves of the spread at once — the cheap deposit on the funding side and the well-underwritten loan on the asset side.

## Licensed bank versus rented licence: where the spread actually goes

We now reach the structural fork that quietly determines how much of the deposit spread a neobank gets to keep at all: whether it holds its own banking licence or rents one through banking-as-a-service. The matrix below lays out the trade-off.

![Comparison matrix of owning a banking licence versus renting one through banking as a service](/imgs/blogs/digital-banking-and-the-neobank-business-model-6.png)

Read the matrix row by row. A neobank that **owns a banking licence** holds the deposits on its own balance sheet, keeps the full net-interest spread, and can lend directly — but it must hold regulatory capital against its assets, build a full risk-and-compliance function, and it typically waits years to obtain a charter. The whole apparatus of capital and prudential rules that a licensed bank must carry is the subject of the broader [Basel and bank-regulation system](/blog/trading/finance/bis-and-basel-bank-regulation); a neobank that wants the deposit spread is signing up for exactly that apparatus.

A neobank that **rents a licence through BaaS** partners with a sponsor bank that holds the customers' deposits under its own charter. The neobank gets to market fast — months, not years — and avoids holding capital itself at the start, but it earns only a *share* of the economics: typically a slice of interchange and a per-account fee, while the sponsor bank keeps most of the deposit spread because the deposits legally sit with the sponsor. In the US, this BaaS model, built on small "exempt" sponsor banks harvesting unregulated debit interchange, is how the majority of consumer neobanks launched.

The tension is exactly the one the matrix makes visible. The thing that makes BaaS attractive — you do not have to be a bank — is the same thing that caps your upside: you do not get a bank's main revenue line. A BaaS neobank can earn interchange and fees forever, but it cannot capture the full deposit spread, and the deposit spread is where the real money in banking lives. This is why the more ambitious neobanks, once they have scale, almost always try to *acquire their own banking licence* — to graduate from renting the economics to owning them. The journey from "fintech on a sponsor's rails" to "chartered bank" is the journey from the thin column to the thick column of every chart in this post.

#### Worked example: how much spread BaaS gives away

Suppose a BaaS neobank gathers \$2,500 per user in deposits (the maturing case) across 1,000,000 users — \$2.5 billion of deposits. If it owned a licence and earned a 3% spread, that funding would generate \$2.5bn × 3% = \$75 million a year of net interest income. Under a typical BaaS arrangement, the sponsor bank might keep, say, two-thirds of that spread and pass one-third to the neobank: the neobank earns about \$25 million while the sponsor keeps \$50 million on the neobank's own customers. The intuition: renting a licence does not just cost a flat fee — it gives away the majority of the single most valuable revenue stream the neobank's deposits create, which is why owning the licence is worth the years of pain it takes to get one.

There is also a risk dimension to BaaS that became painfully visible in the United States in 2023–2024, when the failure of a key middleware provider in the BaaS chain (Synapse) left some end-customers temporarily unable to access funds, and regulators sharply increased scrutiny of sponsor-bank-and-fintech partnerships. The lesson the industry took: renting a licence does not rent away the responsibility, and the regulatory and operational fragility of a long chain of partners is itself a cost. The plumbing under all of this — the ledgers that actually hold the balances and process the transactions — is the subject of [core banking systems, the engine behind every transaction](/blog/trading/banking/core-banking-systems-the-engine-behind-every-transaction), and a neobank's choice of core and sponsor is as load-bearing as its app design.

## The few that reached profit, and the many that did not

We can now look at the actual outcomes, because the theory predicts a very particular shape: a short head of neobanks that solved the revenue side and a long tail that did not. The chart below shows it.

![Horizontal bar chart of net contribution per user for named neobanks with a profitable head and a loss making tail](/imgs/blogs/digital-banking-and-the-neobank-business-model-7.png)

The shape is exactly what the unit-economics loop predicts. A handful of neobanks have crossed into genuine, durable net profitability; a much larger number remained loss-making for years, and some have shut down, sold themselves, or pivoted. Let us name the patterns, because the names teach the lesson — and note that the exact per-user numbers here are illustrative orders of magnitude, framed to show the *shape* of the industry rather than to report any company's disclosed figures.

The profitable head shares a common feature: each one solved the primary-account or the lending problem, not just the acquisition problem. The clearest case is the lending-and-scale model in emerging markets, where a digital bank acquires tens of millions of customers cheaply *and* lends to them — credit cards, personal loans, overdrafts — earning a real interest margin on a population the incumbents underserved. Lending is what turns a card-with-an-app into a bank, because it puts the full credit spread, not just interchange, onto each customer. A second pattern in the profitable head is the high-ARPU subscription-and-trading model, where a neobank layers premium subscriptions, foreign-exchange, share dealing, and crypto onto the account so that ARPU climbs into the tens or low hundreds of dollars even without a large deposit balance. A third is the patient primary-account-and-licence model, where a neobank obtained a full banking licence, won salaries, paid competitive savings rates, and lent the deposits — reaching profitability slowly but on a foundation that looks like a real bank's.

The loss-making tail shares the opposite feature: brilliant acquisition, beautiful product, millions of users — and a revenue per user that never climbed off the floor because the users stayed thin and second-account, the interchange was capped, no lending materialised, and the firm tried to win on scale alone against an enormous break-even user count. Several once-celebrated European and American challengers fit this description, burning investor capital for years while their user counts and their losses grew together.

#### Worked example: the same firm, profitable or not, on one lever

Take a neobank with 5,000,000 active users and \$150 million a year of fixed cost. If those users are thin, at \$32 of annual contribution each, total contribution is 5,000,000 × \$32 = \$160 million — barely above the \$150 million fixed cost, for a slim \$10 million operating profit, and one bad fraud year wipes it out. Now suppose the firm lifts average contribution to \$80 by converting a quarter of users to primary accounts and pushing subscriptions: 5,000,000 × \$80 = \$400 million of contribution against \$150 million of cost, for \$250 million of operating profit. *Same user count, same firm, same fixed cost — the entire difference between marginal and excellent is the contribution-per-user lever.* The intuition the chart is built to deliver: neobank profitability is decided on ARPU and lending, not on the user-count vanity metric the headlines love.

## The path to profit: from download to a real bank

Putting the pieces together gives a clear sequence — the path the profitable neobanks actually walked. The pipeline below lays it out.

![Pipeline showing the path to neobank profit from cheap acquisition to becoming primary to cross sell and lending](/imgs/blogs/digital-banking-and-the-neobank-business-model-8.png)

The sequence is: **acquire cheaply**, using the viral app and referral engine that is the model's genuine superpower; **activate the user**, getting them to make a first transaction and add the card to their phone so they actually start using it; **become the primary account**, so the salary lands in the app and deposits-per-user climbs from a few hundred dollars toward thousands; **cross-sell** foreign-exchange, savings tiers, premium subscriptions, and insurance to lift fee revenue; and finally **lend the deposits** — credit, overdraft, instalment products — to earn the net-interest spread that turns a card into a bank. Only at the end of that pipeline does ARPU reliably clear the cost to serve.

The order matters. A neobank that tries to lend before it has won deposits is lending on borrowed wholesale money, which is expensive and fragile — exactly the funding mismatch that breaks banks, as the series covers in the failure case studies. A neobank that tries to cross-sell premium products before the user trusts it with their salary is selling to people who treat it as a toy. The discipline of the profitable head was to walk the pipeline in order: trust first, salary second, lending last.

#### Worked example: ARPU climbing along the pipeline

Track one cohort along the pipeline. At **acquisition and activation**, the user is a spender: \$40 ARPU, the bonfire number. When they make the neobank their **primary account**, the average balance climbs from \$350 to \$2,500; the net-interest line alone goes from \$10 to \$2,500 × 3% = \$75, lifting ARPU to roughly \$105 even before cross-sell. Add **cross-sell** — a \$5/month premium tier taken by enough of the cohort to average \$40/year, plus more interchange from heavier use — and ARPU reaches perhaps \$160. Finally, **lend** to the cohort: a modest \$1,000 average loan balance at a 6% net credit margin adds \$60, taking ARPU to around \$220. The intuition: the same customer who was a \$40 bonfire becomes a \$220 business by walking the pipeline — a five-fold improvement that comes not from acquiring *more* users but from going *deeper* with the ones you have.

## Common misconceptions

**"Neobanks are cheaper, so they must be more profitable."** This is the central error, and the whole post is its refutation. Neobanks are dramatically cheaper to run — cost to serve of single-digit dollars versus \$300–\$500 — but they are also dramatically thinner on revenue, often \$40 per user versus \$540, because they tend to win the spending but not the deposits. Lower cost with even-lower revenue is not automatically profit; for most neobanks for most of the last decade, it was a loss.

**"They make their money mainly from interest, like a normal bank."** For a thin-user neobank, this is backwards. The largest revenue line is usually *interchange* — a percentage of spending — not net interest, because the average deposit balance is too small for the spread to amount to much. The whole strategic project of a maturing neobank is to *change* this, to make net interest the largest line as it does for an incumbent, which only happens once it wins primary accounts and starts lending. A neobank that is still interchange-dominated has not yet become a bank in the economic sense.

**"A neobank is the same as a fintech app."** Not quite, and the difference is the licence. A true neobank either holds a banking licence or operates on a sponsor bank's licence such that customer deposits are held and insured as bank deposits. A pure fintech app that helps you budget or invest but does not hold insured deposits is not a neobank, however bank-like it looks. The distinction matters enormously in a crisis, because deposit insurance only protects money held as an actual bank deposit at an insured institution — and a customer who does not understand whether their balance is insured is exposed in exactly the way the 2023 episodes around uninsured balances laid bare.

**"Once they reach scale, profit is automatic."** Scale helps — it spreads fixed costs — but the break-even user count for a thin-ARPU neobank can run into the *millions*, as the worked example showed (3.75 million users to cover \$120 million of fixed cost at \$32 contribution each). Reaching that many genuinely active, profitable users can take longer than the patience of the investors funding the losses. Scale is necessary but not sufficient; raising ARPU is what makes the required scale achievable.

**"Free banking means the customer is the product, like with social media."** It is more specific than that. Neobanks are not primarily monetising your data; they are monetising your *spending* (interchange), your *balance* (the spread), and your *upgrades* (subscriptions and fees). The product the neobank is selling is still banking — the "free" account is a loss-leader to win the relationship, exactly as a traditional bank's free checking is a loss-leader to win the deposits and the cross-sell. The economics are old banking economics in a new wrapper, not advertising economics.

## How it shows up in real banks

**Nubank (Brazil/Latin America), the lending-at-scale success.** Nubank built one of the largest digital-banking customer bases in the world — well over 100 million customers across Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia — acquired overwhelmingly through word-of-mouth at a tiny CAC. Crucially, it did not stop at a card; it became a full credit institution, issuing credit cards and personal loans to a population the incumbent Brazilian banks had served expensively or not at all. By putting a real lending margin on each customer and operating at enormous scale on a low cost base, it reached sustained profitability and a public-market valuation that briefly exceeded several incumbent Latin American banks. The lesson that maps to this post: it solved the revenue side (lending) and the scale side at once, and it walked the full pipeline rather than stopping at interchange.

**Starling Bank (UK), the licensed primary-account success.** Starling took the patient, hard road: it obtained a full UK banking licence, built a current account good enough to win customers' salaries, gathered low-cost deposits, and lent them — including a large book of government-guaranteed small-business loans during the pandemic that boosted its lending income. It became one of the first UK neobanks to report a full year of profit. The lesson: owning the licence and winning the primary account let it capture the deposit spread directly rather than renting it away, which is precisely the right-hand column of the licence matrix paying off.

**Monzo and Revolut (UK), the slow climb to profit.** Both built large, beloved customer bases — Monzo with a current-account-first UK strategy, Revolut with a multi-product, multi-country, high-ARPU model spanning FX, trading, and crypto. Both spent years loss-making while they grew, and both eventually reported profits as they lifted ARPU: Monzo by deepening the primary-account relationship and adding lending, Revolut by stacking premium subscriptions and trading revenue on top of the account. The lesson: profitability came not when the user count crossed a line but when revenue per user did — the ARPU lever, not the vanity metric.

**N26 (Germany), the cautionary scale-without-depth story.** N26 grew a large pan-European base on a clean app and free account, but faced years of losses, regulatory growth restrictions imposed by the German regulator over financial-crime controls (a reminder that compliance is a real fixed cost, not a footnote), and a strategic retreat from some markets including a withdrawal from the United States and the United Kingdom. Its difficulty illustrates the loss-making tail: strong acquisition and product, but a revenue per user that struggled to climb fast enough to outrun the fixed costs of being a regulated bank across many jurisdictions.

**The US BaaS wave and the Synapse failure (2024).** A large slice of US "neobanks" were never licensed banks at all — they were apps riding on small sponsor banks via a banking-as-a-service middleware layer. When Synapse, a key middleware provider connecting fintechs to sponsor banks, collapsed in 2024, hundreds of thousands of end-customers found their funds frozen amid reconciliation failures, and it emerged that the ledgers between the parties did not cleanly agree. Regulators responded with sharply higher scrutiny of bank-fintech partnerships. The lesson maps directly to the licence matrix: renting a licence is fast and capital-light, but it stretches the chain of responsibility for customers' money across multiple firms, and that chain is only as strong as its weakest, least-visible link.

**The neobank shutdowns and pivots, the quiet majority.** For every Nubank or Starling that reached the headlines on the strength of profit, a long list of digital banks raised capital, grew a base, never solved the revenue side, and quietly closed, sold themselves to an incumbent, or pivoted to a different model. Some pivoted from consumer banking to *providing infrastructure to other neobanks* — selling the rails rather than running the bank — because the picks-and-shovels business turned out to have better economics than the bank itself. Others were acquired by traditional banks that wanted the technology and the young customer base and were content to fold the losses into a much larger profitable balance sheet. The pattern is the loss-making tail of the contribution chart playing out in real corporate outcomes: brilliant acquisition engines that never became banks, ending up as features inside someone else's bank. The lesson is sobering and matches the whole post: a beautiful app with millions of thin users is an asset worth buying, but it is not, by itself, a profitable bank.

**Chime (US), the interchange-funded scale model.** Chime built a very large US base by targeting customers underserved by traditional banks, offering fee-free accounts and early access to direct-deposited pay, and funding itself principally on the high *exempt* debit interchange its small sponsor banks could earn under the Durbin carve-out. Its model is the clearest live example of how US neobank economics rest on a regulatory quirk: keep the sponsor bank under \$10 billion in assets, harvest the uncapped debit interchange a giant bank legally cannot, and use early-pay features to become as close to the primary account as possible. The lesson: where net interest is hard to win, a neobank can lean on interchange — but only as long as the regulatory carve-out it depends on survives.

## The takeaway: how to read a neobank

The neobank is the cleanest natural experiment we have in what a bank actually *is*, because it strips one variable — the branch — to zero and lets you watch what happens. What happens is that the cost of *running* the machine collapses, and the difficulty of *feeding* it — winning the cheap, sticky deposits that the spread is earned on — does not go away at all. That is the whole story, and it maps exactly onto the spine of this series: a bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine, and a neobank that wins attention but not deposits has built the machine without securing the fuel.

So when you read about a neobank — as a curious observer, a customer, or someone trying to judge the business — ask the questions the unit economics demand, in order. First: **is it the customer's primary account, or a second card?** That single fact, visible in deposits-per-user, predicts the revenue per user better than any growth chart. Second: **where does its revenue come from — interchange, or net interest?** An interchange-dominated neobank is a card with an app and is hostage to a regulatory carve-out; a net-interest-dominated one has become a real bank. Third: **does it own its licence or rent one?** Renting is fast but caps the upside at the most valuable revenue line and stretches the responsibility for customers' money across a fragile chain. Fourth: **what is the LTV-to-CAC ratio, and is it the 1.3 of the bonfire or the high single-digits of a business?** And fifth: **is it walking the pipeline — acquire, activate, become primary, cross-sell, lend — in order, or is it stuck at the front of it?**

A neobank that is winning primary accounts, earning net interest, owning its licence, and lending is not really a "disruptor" anymore; it is a low-cost bank, and a low-cost bank that has solved the deposit problem is a genuinely formidable thing, because it has the incumbent's revenue with a fraction of the incumbent's cost. A neobank that is none of those things, however many millions of users it has and however beautiful its app, is the long tail of the contribution chart: an acquisition engine bolted to a revenue model that never caught up. The user count tells you the size of the experiment. The revenue per user tells you whether it worked.
