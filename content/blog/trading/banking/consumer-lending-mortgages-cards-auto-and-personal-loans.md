> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The four big retail loans — mortgages, auto loans, personal loans and credit cards — are the same trade priced four different ways: the bank trades the yield it earns against the losses it eats, and the swing factor is whether there is collateral to seize. Cards charge the highest rate (about 22%) and suffer the highest losses (often 3%-10% a year); mortgages charge the least (about 6%-7%) and lose almost nothing (well under half a percent).
>
> - **Secured beats unsecured on losses, not on yield.** A mortgage or auto loan is backed by the house or the car, so when the borrower defaults the bank seizes and sells the collateral and recovers most of the money. A credit card or personal loan has nothing behind it, so a default is closer to a total loss.
> - **The card wins the economics anyway.** After you subtract losses and funding costs, the credit card still leaves the widest risk-adjusted margin — roughly 12% versus about 3% for a mortgage. The bank is paid handsomely to absorb that loss volatility.
> - **Each product behaves differently through a cycle.** In a downturn, credit cards crack first (charge-offs jump within months), auto loans follow, and mortgages move last and least — which is why card losses are the bank's early-warning system.
> - **The one number to remember:** a 30-year, \$300,000 mortgage at 6.5% costs the borrower \$1,896 a month and \$382,633 in total interest — more than the house itself — and in year one, \$1,625 of the very first \$1,896 payment is pure interest. That asymmetry between interest and principal is the whole shape of consumer lending.

In the second quarter of 2024, the largest US banks reported something that looked, at a glance, like a paradox. Their mortgage books — by far the biggest pile of consumer loans on the balance sheet — were earning a yield in the mid-6% range and losing almost nothing: net charge-offs on first-lien mortgages were running well under half a percent a year. Their credit-card books, a far smaller slice of the assets, were earning over 20% and *losing* between 3% and 4% of the balance every year, with some lenders pushing past 5%. One product looked safe and boring; the other looked dangerous and expensive. And yet, when you walked into the room where the bank decides where to put its next dollar, the danger­ous, expensive product was the one everyone wanted more of.

That is not a contradiction. It is the central fact of consumer lending, and once you see it, the whole retail asset side of a bank snaps into focus. A bank does not get paid for taking *no* risk; it gets paid for taking risk it can price. The credit card earns four times the mortgage's yield precisely because it loses ten times as much — and the gap between those two numbers, after the bank pays for its money, is wider on the card than on anything else it lends. The art of consumer lending is knowing exactly how much each product yields, how much it loses, how long the bank's money is tied up, and what stands behind the loan if the borrower walks away.

The diagram above is the mental model for this whole post: four products, ranked along four axes — yield, loss rate, duration, and security. Everything that follows is an elaboration of that single grid. We will build each term from scratch, then take the four products one at a time, then watch them behave through a real credit cycle, and end on what the whole pattern tells you about how a bank actually lives or dies.

## Foundations: secured, unsecured, amortizing, revolving, APR, loss rate, and duration

Before we can compare four products, we need a small vocabulary. Each of these words is doing real work, so we will define it from zero, with a plain-English picture first and the precise term second.

**A loan is the bank's asset.** This is the first thing to un-learn. To *you*, a loan is a debt — money you owe, a liability. To the *bank*, the loan is an asset: a promise to pay it a stream of money over time. The whole consumer-lending business is the bank buying those promises (by handing over cash today) and collecting on them. The recurring theme of this entire series — that a bank borrows short from depositors and lends long — is exactly this: the deposits are the bank's cheap, short-term funding, and these consumer loans are the long-term assets that funding buys.

**Secured versus unsecured.** A *secured loan* is one backed by a specific piece of property — the *collateral* — that the bank can seize and sell if the borrower stops paying. A mortgage is secured by the house; an auto loan is secured by the car. An *unsecured loan* has no such backing: if the borrower defaults, the bank has only a legal claim, no specific thing to grab. A credit card and most personal loans are unsecured. This single distinction is the most important variable in the whole post, because it decides how much the bank loses when a loan goes bad.

**Amortizing versus revolving.** An *amortizing loan* has a fixed schedule that pays it down to zero. Each payment is part interest, part principal, and the loan has a definite end date — 30 years for a mortgage, 5-7 for a car. *Amortize* literally means "to kill off" (from the Latin for death); the loan dies on schedule. A *revolving* loan has no schedule and no end date. A credit card lets you borrow, repay, and borrow again up to a *credit limit*, with a minimum payment due each month and the rest carried forward. The balance "revolves" — it can stay alive indefinitely as long as you keep paying the minimum. This distinction decides the *duration* of the bank's money and the *shape* of the interest.

**APR.** The *Annual Percentage Rate* is the yearly cost of borrowing, expressed as a percent of the balance. A 6.5% mortgage APR means roughly \$6.50 of interest per \$100 borrowed per year; a 22% card APR means \$22. APR is the headline price of the loan and, ignoring fees, it is close to the *gross yield* the bank earns on that asset before any losses. The first thing to notice is the spread: card APRs run three to four times mortgage APRs, and that gap is the engine of everything.

**Gross yield versus net yield.** The APR is the *gross* yield — what the loan earns before anything goes wrong. The *net* yield is what the bank keeps after subtracting the two big costs: the losses (borrowers who don't pay) and the funding cost (what the bank pays for the money it lent). A 22% card that loses 7% and is funded at 3% nets far less than 22% — but, as we'll see, still nets more than any other product. Throughout this post, when we compare products, the number that matters is never the headline APR; it is the *net* of yield minus loss minus funding. Confusing the two is the single most common mistake in reading a loan book.

**Loss rate and charge-off.** Some borrowers don't pay back. When a bank decides a loan is uncollectible, it *charges it off* — it removes the loan from its assets and books the loss. The *net charge-off rate* (charge-offs minus any later recoveries, divided by the average balance) is the bank's annual *loss rate*: the fraction of the book that simply vanishes each year. A mortgage book might charge off 0.1%-0.5% a year; a card book 3%-10%. The loss rate is the *cost* side of lending, and it is the mirror image of the yield: the products that yield most lose most.

**Loss given default (LGD).** When a single loan defaults, the bank rarely loses 100% of it. It recovers something — from selling the collateral, from collections, from a settlement. *Loss given default* is the share it *does* lose: 1 minus the recovery rate. A secured loan has a low LGD because the collateral pays most of it back; an unsecured loan has a high LGD because there is nothing to sell. LGD is the bridge between "did the borrower default?" and "how much did the bank actually lose?" — and it is where secured and unsecured loans part company most dramatically. (We will meet LGD's siblings — probability of default and exposure at default — when this series gets to [the credit-risk engine of PD, LGD and EAD](/blog/trading/banking/credit-risk-management-pd-lgd-ead-and-expected-loss).)

**Duration.** In everyday terms, duration is *how long the bank's money is tied up* in the loan. A 30-year mortgage locks the bank in for decades; an auto loan for a handful of years; a card balance can turn over in months. Duration matters for two reasons. First, the longer the loan, the more exposed the bank is to interest rates moving against it — a fixed-rate 30-year mortgage made at 3% is a painful asset to hold when funding costs rise to 5%, the exact trap we will return to. Second, long loans tie up the bank's scarce capital for longer, so the bank wants to be paid for that. (Duration as a precise rate-risk measure belongs to the bond world — see [interest rates and mortgages, the master variable](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable) for how this plays out for the borrower.)

With those seven words in hand — secured, unsecured, amortizing, revolving, APR, loss rate, LGD, duration — we can read the entire retail asset side. Let us take the four products in order of size and safety.

## Mortgages: the giant, low-yield, low-loss anchor

The mortgage is the largest single asset on most retail banks' balance sheets, and it is the safest. A *mortgage* is a long-term loan secured by real estate: the bank lends you most of the purchase price of a home, you repay it over 15 to 30 years in level monthly installments, and the house itself is the collateral. If you stop paying, the bank can *foreclose* — take the house and sell it to recover its money.

Three features define the mortgage's economics: it is **secured**, it is **amortizing**, and it is **very long-duration**.

Because it is secured by an asset that usually holds or grows in value, the mortgage has the lowest loss rate of any consumer loan. When a borrower defaults, the bank forecloses and sells the home; in normal times it recovers most or all of the loan. That is why mortgage charge-offs run in the 0.1%-0.5% range even though the loans are enormous. The single most important number in mortgage underwriting is therefore the *loan-to-value ratio* (LTV) — the loan divided by the home's value. A \$300,000 loan on a \$400,000 house is 75% LTV, which means the borrower has \$100,000 of their own equity sitting beneath the bank's claim. That equity cushion is the bank's protection: home prices have to fall more than 25% before the bank is even at risk of a loss on default. Mortgages with low LTVs are among the safest assets a bank can hold.

Because it is amortizing over decades, the mortgage has a peculiar payment shape that surprises most first-time borrowers: in the early years, almost every dollar you pay is interest, and almost none is principal. This is not a trick; it falls straight out of the math. Each month the bank charges interest on the *remaining balance*, and early on the balance is enormous, so the interest portion is enormous too.

#### Worked example: a 30-year mortgage payment and where it goes

You borrow \$300,000 to buy a home, at a 6.5% fixed APR, repaid over 30 years (360 monthly payments). The monthly interest rate is 6.5% ÷ 12 = 0.5417% per month. The level monthly payment that pays the loan to exactly zero in 360 months is:

$$\text{payment} = P \cdot \frac{r}{1 - (1+r)^{-n}} = 300{,}000 \cdot \frac{0.005417}{1 - (1.005417)^{-360}}$$

where $P$ is the loan amount, $r$ is the monthly rate, and $n$ is the number of months. That works out to **\$1,896.20 a month**.

Now watch the first payment split. Interest for month one is the balance times the monthly rate: \$300,000 × 0.005417 = **\$1,625.00**. The payment is \$1,896.20, so the principal portion is only \$1,896.20 − \$1,625.00 = **\$271.20**. Of your very first payment, 86% is pure interest to the bank and just 14% actually reduces what you owe.

Over a full first year, you pay about \$19,401 in interest and only \$3,353 in principal — after twelve months of \$1,896 payments, your \$300,000 balance has fallen to about \$296,647. After *five* years of payments (\$113,772 paid in), the balance is still \$280,833 — you have paid down barely \$19,000 of principal. Over the entire 30 years you pay \$1,896.20 × 360 = \$682,633, of which **\$382,633 is interest** — more than the original loan.

The intuition: a long amortizing loan is front-loaded with interest, because interest is charged on a balance that starts huge and shrinks slowly. For the bank, this is a beautiful asset — a low-risk, decades-long stream of interest, secured by a house — which is exactly why mortgages anchor the balance sheet.

![Stacked bar chart of a thirty year mortgage showing interest paid early and principal repaid later](/imgs/blogs/consumer-lending-mortgages-cards-auto-and-personal-loans-3.png)

The figure shows the same loan year by year. The red band — interest paid to the bank — towers over the green band — principal repaid — in the early years, and the two slowly cross over near year 20. The total area of the red band is that \$382,633 of interest.

But the mortgage's great virtue — its length — is also its great hidden danger to the bank, and it has nothing to do with the borrower defaulting. A 30-year fixed-rate mortgage locks the *bank* into a fixed yield for 30 years, while the deposits funding it can reprice overnight. A bank that filled its book with 3% mortgages in 2021 and then watched its deposit costs climb toward 4%-5% by 2023 was earning a *negative* spread on those loans — a slow bleed with no borrower at fault. This is *interest-rate risk in the banking book*, and it is precisely the mechanism that broke Silicon Valley Bank (which held long-duration securities rather than mortgages, but the trap is identical). We cover the full mechanism in [interest-rate risk in the banking book and the duration gap](/blog/trading/banking/interest-rate-risk-in-the-banking-book-irrbb-and-the-duration-gap); for now, hold the thought that the mortgage's low *credit* loss is partly paid for with high *rate* risk.

There is a second, subtler danger hiding inside the mortgage that runs the *opposite* direction, and it teaches something important about long loans: *prepayment risk*. A mortgage borrower has the right to pay the loan off early — by selling the house, or by *refinancing* (taking a new loan at a lower rate to repay the old one). When rates fall, borrowers refinance in droves: they hand the bank back its principal and go borrow elsewhere at the new low rate. So the bank's high-yielding mortgages disappear exactly when it would most want to keep them, and its low-yielding mortgages stay on the books exactly when it would most want them gone. This is sometimes called *negative convexity*: the bank gets the worst of both moves. When rates rise, the bank is stuck holding old low-rate loans (the duration trap); when rates fall, the good high-rate loans run off (prepayment). The mortgage gives the *borrower* the valuable options and leaves the *bank* short them — which is one more reason the headline mortgage yield, low as it already is, overstates what the bank actually keeps.

This is also why the *structure* of the mortgage matters as much as the rate. A **fixed-rate mortgage** locks the rate for the whole term and dumps all the interest-rate risk onto the bank. An **adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM)** resets the rate periodically to track market rates, which moves the rate risk back onto the *borrower* — the bank's yield rises with its funding costs, so the spread is protected. US banks lean heavily toward 30-year fixed mortgages (largely because the government-sponsored agencies will buy them, letting the bank offload the risk), while much of the rest of the world runs on ARMs precisely because banks won't hold 30 years of fixed-rate risk on their own books. The same \$300,000 loan is a very different asset depending on who is carrying the rate risk — and that, not the credit risk, is the real question a mortgage book poses.

So what does the mortgage tell us about the bank? It is the ballast. Big, safe, long, low-yielding, and rate-sensitive in two directions at once. It rarely makes the bank rich, but it rarely sinks it on credit losses — and it is the product where the bank's funding base (cheap, sticky retail deposits, the subject of [retail deposits and the funding base](/blog/trading/banking/retail-deposits-the-funding-base-and-why-cheap-money-is-the-franchise)) matters most, because the spread is thin and the borrower holds the options.

## Auto loans: the secured middle child

The auto loan sits squarely in the middle of every axis. It is *secured* like a mortgage, *amortizing* like a mortgage, but *short-duration* — typically 5 to 7 years — and it yields more, in the 7%-10% range. It is the secured product's compromise: more loss than a mortgage, less than anything unsecured, more yield than a mortgage, much less than a card.

The collateral here is the car, and that changes the recovery story in an instructive way. A house tends to hold or gain value over the life of a mortgage; a car *depreciates* — it loses value the moment it leaves the lot and keeps losing it. This creates a window of danger early in the loan: in the first year or two, the borrower may owe more than the car is worth (being "underwater" or having *negative equity*), so even after the bank repossesses and sells the car, it can still take a loss. That is why auto loss rates (1%-3% in normal times) sit above mortgage rates: the collateral is real, but it is a melting asset.

Auto lending also splits into two very different risk pools. *Prime* auto loans go to borrowers with strong credit and run loss rates near the bottom of that range. *Subprime* auto loans go to weaker borrowers at much higher APRs (sometimes 15%-20%) and carry far higher losses — subprime auto charge-offs can run 8%-12% in a downturn. The blended "auto loan" number hides a wide spread, and the subprime end behaves much more like an unsecured product than a secured one, because by the time the bank repossesses a worn, depreciated, possibly damaged car from a stressed borrower, the recovery can be thin.

#### Worked example: an auto loan's yield net of losses

You are the bank. You make a \$30,000 auto loan at 9% APR over 6 years. Gross, the loan yields 9%. Now subtract the costs. Suppose this is a near-prime book with an annual loss rate of 2% — that is, on average 2% of the balance charges off each year, net of what you recover by repossessing and selling cars. And suppose your *funding cost* — what you pay depositors and wholesale lenders for the money you lent out — is 3%.

Your *risk-adjusted yield* on this loan is:

$$9\% \text{ (gross yield)} - 2\% \text{ (losses)} - 3\% \text{ (funding)} = 4\% \text{ net before operating costs.}$$

On a \$30,000 loan, that 4% is about \$1,200 a year of margin before you pay the staff, the systems, and the capital charge. Compare that to the mortgage from the last section: a 6.5% mortgage, losing 0.3%, funded at 3%, leaves 6.5% − 0.3% − 3% = 3.2%. The auto loan's higher yield more than pays for its higher losses — it nets *more* margin than the mortgage, on a much shorter, faster-turning book.

The intuition: a secured loan's higher loss rate is not a bug if the higher yield over-compensates for it. The bank is happy to lose 2% a year if it is being paid 9% to do so.

There is also a structural quirk in how auto loans reach the bank that shapes their risk: most are *originated at the dealership*, not at a bank branch. You negotiate the loan with the dealer's finance office, and the dealer sells the loan to a bank (often marking up the rate and keeping the difference — *dealer reserve*). This *indirect* channel is efficient — it puts the bank's product at the point of sale — but it weakens the bank's grip on underwriting, because the dealer's incentive is to close the car sale, not to protect the bank's loss rate. Banks manage this by setting strict rules on what they'll buy and by pricing the channel's extra risk into the rate. It is a useful reminder that *how* a loan is originated, not just what backs it, feeds into the loss rate.

Because the auto loan is short, it also turns over fast: the bank's money comes back in 5-7 years (often sooner, as people trade cars in), and it can re-lend at current rates. That shorter duration means far less of the interest-rate risk that haunts the mortgage book — the auto book reprices itself naturally every few years just by maturing and being replaced. The auto loan is the workhorse: decent yield, manageable loss, quick repayment, real collateral, no 30-year rate trap. It tells you that *security plus short duration* is a comfortable place for a bank to sit — paid well, protected on the downside, and not locked into yesterday's rate.

## Personal loans: unsecured, fixed, and priced for the gap

A *personal loan* (also called an unsecured installment loan) is the first product on our list with **nothing behind it**. It is an amortizing loan, like a mortgage or auto loan — a fixed amount, a fixed rate, fixed monthly payments, a definite end date, usually 3 to 5 years — but it is *unsecured*. There is no house, no car, no collateral. The bank is lending purely on the borrower's promise and credit history.

That single change — removing the collateral — transforms the economics. Because there is nothing to seize, the *loss given default* leaps. When a secured loan defaults, the bank recovers most of it from the collateral (LGD around 25%). When an unsecured personal loan defaults, the bank recovers only what it can squeeze through collections — typically a small fraction — so LGD runs around 55%. The same default event costs the bank roughly twice as much on an unsecured loan as on a secured one.

To be paid for that, the bank charges much more. Personal-loan APRs commonly run 11%-16%, and loss rates run 3%-7% — both far above the auto loan, both far below the credit card. The personal loan is the unsecured product in its *tamest* form: it has a fixed schedule that forces the balance down (unlike a card, which can revolve forever), and it is underwritten up front as a single decision rather than an open-ended line. That structure keeps its losses below a card's, even though both are unsecured.

The thing to understand about personal loans is *why they exist at all*, given that a credit card is also unsecured and already in the borrower's pocket. The answer is the fixed schedule. A borrower carrying a revolving card balance at 22% and paying only the minimum will be in debt for years (we will see exactly how long in a moment). A personal loan at 13% with a fixed 4-year payoff *forces* discipline: the balance must hit zero on schedule. Borrowers use personal loans to *consolidate* card debt — refinancing a revolving 22% balance into a fixed 13% installment loan — which is better for them and, often, better for the bank, because a forced payoff schedule is a lower-loss structure than an open-ended revolve.

#### Worked example: consolidating a card balance into a personal loan

Take the same \$5,000. Carried on a card at 22% and paid at a flat \$150 a month, it takes 52 months and \$2,798 in interest (we will derive that number precisely in the card section). Now refinance it into a 3-year personal loan at 13% APR. The fixed monthly payment on a \$5,000, 36-month, 13% loan is about \$168 a month, and the total interest over the three years is roughly \$1,065. The borrower pays a slightly higher *monthly* amount but clears the debt in 3 years instead of 4-plus, and pays about \$1,733 *less* in total interest.

The intuition: the personal loan's lower rate and forced schedule turn a sticky, compounding revolving balance into a disciplined, shrinking one. For the borrower it's cheaper; for the bank that makes the personal loan, it's a structurally lower-loss asset than the card it replaced — which is exactly why the bank can charge 13% instead of 22% and still come out fine.

The personal loan is the bridge product: unsecured like a card, disciplined like a mortgage.

![Bar chart of loss given default rising from senior secured to unsecured retail](/imgs/blogs/consumer-lending-mortgages-cards-auto-and-personal-loans-5.png)

The figure above makes the collateral effect concrete. It plots *loss given default* — the share the bank loses when a loan actually defaults — by how the claim is secured. A senior secured claim loses about 25%; a senior unsecured claim about 45%; a subordinated claim about 65%; and unsecured retail credit (the card-and-personal-loan world) about 55%. The jump from the green secured bar to the red unsecured bars is the entire reason unsecured products charge so much more. The bank is not greedy; it is pricing the missing collateral.

## Credit cards: the highest yield, the highest loss, and the best business

The credit card is the strangest and most profitable asset on the retail balance sheet. It is **unsecured** (nothing behind it), **revolving** (no schedule, no end date), and it carries the **highest APR** of any mainstream consumer loan — typically 20%-25%. It also suffers the **highest loss rate**, often 3%-10% a year and far worse in a recession. By every safety measure it is the worst loan a bank can make. By the measure that actually matters — risk-adjusted margin — it is the best.

Start with the revolving mechanism, because it is what makes the card different from everything else. With an amortizing loan, the bank sets the schedule and the balance marches to zero. With a card, the *borrower* sets the pace: each month a *minimum payment* is due (often around 2% of the balance, or a small floor like \$25), and whatever isn't paid *revolves* — it carries forward and accrues interest, which is then added to the balance that next month's interest is charged on. This is compounding working against the borrower, and it is the source of both the card's danger to households and its profitability to the bank.

![Pipeline showing a revolving credit card balance compounding from spend to interest to minimum payment to carried balance](/imgs/blogs/consumer-lending-mortgages-cards-auto-and-personal-loans-6.png)

#### Worked example: the cost of revolving a card balance

You charge \$5,000 to a card at a 22% APR. The monthly rate is 22% ÷ 12 = 1.83% per month. The first month's interest is \$5,000 × 0.0183 = **\$91.67**. You pay the minimum — say \$150 this month. Of that \$150, the \$91.67 goes to interest and only \$58.33 actually reduces the balance, leaving you owing \$4,941.67. Next month, interest is charged on *that* balance. The interest you didn't pay off is effectively rolled into the principal — compounding.

Now play it forward. If you keep paying a flat \$150 every month, it takes **52 months — over four years — to clear the \$5,000**, and you pay about **\$2,798 in interest** along the way, more than half the original purchase, again. And that is the *disciplined* case. If you pay the true minimum of 2% of the balance (which shrinks as the balance shrinks, so the payment gets smaller and smaller), the payoff stretches to *decades* — on this math, well over 50 years — because the minimum barely outruns the interest.

The intuition: a revolving balance compounds against the borrower because the minimum payment is sized to keep the loan alive, not to kill it. The same compounding that makes the borrower's debt sticky makes the bank's asset persistent and high-yielding.

That persistence is the secret of the card business. A revolving balance is an asset that earns 22% and keeps re-earning it month after month, because the borrower never quite pays it off. The fraction of cardholders who carry a balance — *revolvers*, as opposed to *transactors* who pay in full and owe no interest — are where the card lender makes its money. (Transactors are not free customers; the bank earns *interchange* on their spending instead — the fee the merchant's bank pays the cardholder's bank on every swipe. That entire four-party economic engine — issuer, acquirer, network, interchange and the merchant-discount-rate split — is its own deep subject, covered in [the cards business: issuing, acquiring, interchange and the MDR split](/blog/trading/banking/the-cards-business-issuing-acquiring-interchange-and-the-mdr-split).)

One feature of the card deserves a closer look because it is what makes the *transactor* possible at all: the *grace period*. If you pay your full statement balance by the due date, you owe **no interest at all** on that month's purchases — the bank effectively lends you the money for free for up to ~50 days between the purchase and the due date. This is the *float*, and it is why a careful cardholder can use a credit card for years and never pay a cent of interest. The grace period is a deliberate design: it pulls in spending volume (which generates interchange) and it converts some transactors into revolvers when an unexpectedly large bill finally tips them into carrying a balance. The moment you carry a balance, the grace period usually vanishes — interest starts accruing on new purchases from the day you make them, not from the due date. So the card runs two completely different economics on the same plastic: a free-float, fee-funded product for the disciplined, and a high-interest revolving loan for everyone else, with the same customer able to slide from one to the other in a single bad month. No other consumer loan flips its own economics like this.

The card also has the loosest relationship between the *credit limit* and the *balance*. A bank might extend a \$15,000 limit but the customer carries only \$2,000 — the rest is an unused, off-balance-sheet promise the bank must still hold capital against and which can be drawn at any moment. In a stress event, two bad things happen together: existing balances go bad (rising charge-offs) *and* frightened or desperate borrowers draw down their unused limits (rising exposure) right before they default. This is *exposure at default* climbing exactly when *probability of default* climbs — the two risk factors moving in the same wrong direction. It is one more reason card losses can blow out faster than the headline charge-off rate suggests, and why card lenders aggressively cut unused limits at the first sign of a downturn.

Now the losses. With no collateral and an open-ended structure, a card default is close to a total loss — LGD around 55% for the whole unsecured-retail bucket, and in practice charge-off severities on cards are high because there is nothing to repossess and the balances are unsecured. Card loss rates run 3%-7% in normal times and can spike toward 10% or more in a recession, far above any other product. The card is, on the loss axis alone, the most dangerous loan a bank makes.

So why is it the *best* business? Because the yield doesn't just cover the loss — it buries it.

#### Worked example: risk-adjusted margin by product

This is the punchline of the entire post. For each product, compute what is actually left after the two big costs — losses and funding. Using representative mid-cycle figures, gross yield minus the annual loss rate minus a 3% funding cost (every product is funded from the same deposit base, so funding is the same for all):

| Product | Gross yield | Loss rate | Funding | Risk-adjusted margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mortgage | 6.5% | 0.3% | 3.0% | **3.2%** |
| Auto | 8.5% | 2.0% | 3.0% | **3.5%** |
| Personal | 13.0% | 5.0% | 3.0% | **5.0%** |
| Credit card | 22.0% | 7.0% | 3.0% | **12.0%** |

The credit card loses more than twenty times what the mortgage loses (7% versus 0.3%) — and still ends up with nearly *four times* the risk-adjusted margin (12.0% versus 3.2%). The high loss rate is a fact, but it is a *priced* fact: the 22% APR more than pays for it. This is the answer to the opening paradox. The product that looks the most dangerous is the most profitable, because the bank is overpaid for the danger.

The intuition: you do not judge a loan by its loss rate; you judge it by yield *minus* loss *minus* funding. On that ruler, the card wins, which is why every bank wants more cards even as they generate the worst-looking charge-off numbers.

![Stacked bar chart of risk adjusted margin by product after subtracting losses and funding cost](/imgs/blogs/consumer-lending-mortgages-cards-auto-and-personal-loans-7.png)

The figure stacks the costs against the yield for each product. The amber band is funding (3% for everyone), the red band is losses, and the green band on top is the risk-adjusted margin that survives. The card's green band dwarfs the others — that is the 12% — even though its red loss band is also the tallest. Mortgages and autos have thin green slivers; the card has a wall of it.

There is one more reason banks love cards that doesn't show up in this margin math: the card is also a *fee* engine (interchange, late fees, annual fees) and a *relationship* anchor that brings deposits and other products with it. The interest margin understates the card's full economics. But even on interest alone, it wins.

The margin table is not the bank's final word, though, because it leaves out one cost that the regulator forces into the picture: the *capital charge*. A bank must hold equity capital against every loan in proportion to how risky that loan is — measured by its *risk weight*. A residential mortgage carries a low risk weight (often around 35%-50% under standardized rules), so the bank needs relatively little equity behind it; an unsecured consumer loan or card carries a much higher risk weight (75%-100% or more), so it eats more of the bank's scarce capital. Because equity is expensive — shareholders demand a high return on it — a loan that ties up more capital must clear a higher hurdle to be worth making. The proper way to rank the products is therefore not raw margin but *return on the capital they consume* — a measure banks call *RAROC*, risk-adjusted return on capital, the subject of [loan pricing: cost of funds, risk premium and the capital charge](/blog/trading/banking/loan-pricing-cost-of-funds-risk-premium-and-the-capital-charge). The card's fat margin shrinks somewhat once you charge it for the extra capital it ties up, and the mortgage's thin margin improves somewhat because it ties up so little — but even after this adjustment, the card's spread is wide enough that it usually still comes out on top. The capital charge narrows the gap; it does not close it. And it explains why a bank can't simply load up on cards without limit: each one demands a slice of the equity cushion that is the only thing standing between the bank and insolvency.

## The yield-loss ladder, side by side

We have now met all four products. Step back and look at them together, because the pattern is almost perfectly ordered.

![Grouped bar chart of gross yield versus annual loss rate for mortgage auto personal and credit card](/imgs/blogs/consumer-lending-mortgages-cards-auto-and-personal-loans-2.png)

The figure plots gross yield (blue) against loss rate (red) for all four. Read it left to right and you see a clean ladder: as you move from mortgage to auto to personal to card, *both* bars rise together. Yield climbs from 6.5% to 22%; losses climb from 0.3% to 7%. They move in lockstep because they are two views of the same thing — risk. The bank is not pricing randomly; it is pricing the loss it expects, plus a margin for the loss it can't predict.

This is worth stating as a law of consumer lending: **yield and loss are positively correlated across products, by design.** A product that earned a high yield with a low loss rate would be an arbitrage — every bank would pile in, competition would crush the yield, and the gap would close. The reason the card sustains a 22% yield is that it genuinely loses 7%; the reason the mortgage is stuck at 6.5% is that it genuinely loses almost nothing and is therefore intensely competitive. The market sorts the products onto a line where, after accounting for losses and funding, the surviving margins compensate for the *uncertainty* of those losses — and that uncertainty is highest exactly where the loss rate is highest. The card's fat 12% margin is not free money; it is payment for the fact that 7% is an *average*, and in a bad year it could be 11%.

## How the four products behave through a credit cycle

A loss rate is not a constant. It breathes with the economy, and — crucially — the four products do not breathe in sync. Understanding the *sequence* of how they crack is one of the most useful things in banking, because it turns card losses into an early-warning system for the whole book.

![Graph of the consumer credit cycle from loose lending through rising charge offs back to tightening standards](/imgs/blogs/consumer-lending-mortgages-cards-auto-and-personal-loans-8.png)

The cycle runs like this. In a **boom**, funding is cheap and lenders compete for borrowers, so credit standards loosen — banks approve thinner credit scores, higher loan-to-values, bigger card limits. Balances grow, and the *marginal* borrower added at the top of the cycle is weaker than the average borrower already on the books. This is the seed of the next loss wave, planted in the good times. Then a **shock** arrives — job losses, a rate spike, an income squeeze — and stressed households start to miss payments.

Here is the sequence, and it is remarkably reliable. **Credit cards crack first.** A card is the easiest debt to stop paying — there is no house to lose, no car to repossess, and the minimum payment is the first bill a stressed household drops. Card delinquencies and charge-offs jump within a quarter or two of a downturn. **Auto loans follow.** People will keep paying the car loan longer than the card (they need the car to get to work), but as stress deepens, repossessions rise — and because cars depreciate, the recovery is poor. **Mortgages move last and least.** People will do almost anything to keep their home; the mortgage is the last bill they stop paying, and even when they default, the house usually covers most of the loan. Mortgage losses are slow to rise and small when they do, unless — as in 2008 — home prices themselves collapse and the collateral fails.

Then comes the bank's reaction. As charge-offs rise, banks **build reserves** — they set aside provisions for expected future losses, which hits current profit hard (this is the pro-cyclical earnings machine we explore in [the credit cycle and why bank earnings are pro-cyclical](/blog/trading/banking/the-credit-cycle-and-why-bank-earnings-are-pro-cyclical)). And they **tighten standards** — they stop approving the marginal borrower, raise the bar, shrink limits. Credit dries up, which slows the economy further, which... and the cycle is ready to run again from a lower base.

There is a second-order effect worth naming, because it makes bank earnings swing far more than the underlying losses do. Banks don't book a loss only when a loan actually charges off; under modern accounting (the expected-loss regimes that replaced the old "wait until it goes bad" approach), they must *provision* for losses they *expect* — set aside a reserve against the future. When the cycle turns down and expected losses jump, the bank takes a big *provision* charge against current profit, often well *before* the actual charge-offs arrive. When the cycle turns up and the feared losses don't materialize, the bank *releases* reserves back into profit. The result is that bank earnings overshoot in both directions: profits crater early in a downturn (provisions front-run the losses) and balloon late in a recovery (reserves are released). The unsecured consumer book, with its volatile loss rate, is the single biggest driver of this swing — a 1% move in expected card losses on a large book is a multi-billion-dollar provision. So the consumer asset side doesn't just generate the bank's steadiest income; it also generates its most violent earnings volatility. (The accounting machinery — IFRS 9 and CECL staging — is its own subject in [collateral, security and loan-loss provisioning](/blog/trading/banking/collateral-security-and-loan-loss-provisioning-ifrs9-and-cecl).)

The practical lesson for anyone reading a bank: **watch card charge-offs as the leading indicator.** When card losses start climbing while mortgage and auto losses are still flat, the consumer is stressing at the edges, and the rest of the book will follow on a lag. A bank whose card losses are rising fast is telling you something about the next year of the whole consumer book — and, through the provisioning machinery, about the next year of its earnings too.

## Before and after: what a recession does to a secured versus an unsecured loan

To feel the secured-versus-unsecured distinction in your gut, run the same default through both kinds of loan.

![Before and after comparison of a secured loan recovering collateral versus an unsecured loan losing the full balance](/imgs/blogs/consumer-lending-mortgages-cards-auto-and-personal-loans-4.png)

#### Worked example: the same default, secured versus unsecured

A borrower owes \$20,000 and defaults in a recession.

On the **secured** side (say an auto loan), the bank repossesses the car and sells it. Even in a weak market with a depreciated vehicle, it recovers, say, \$15,000. The loss is \$20,000 − \$15,000 = **\$5,000** — an LGD of 25%. The collateral did its job: it capped the loss at a quarter of the exposure.

On the **unsecured** side (a credit card), there is nothing to seize. The bank pursues the debt through collections and recovers maybe \$2,000 over time. The loss is \$20,000 − \$2,000 = **\$18,000** — an LGD around 90% on this single account, well above the through-the-cycle 55% average because recessions crush recoveries on unsecured debt exactly when defaults spike.

The intuition: collateral is not just a comfort — it is a hard cap on the loss. The secured loan loses \$5,000; the unsecured loan loses \$18,000 on the identical \$20,000 default. That 3.6× difference is why the card has to charge 22% and the auto loan can charge 9%, and it is why, in a severe recession, unsecured losses can blow out far faster than secured ones.

This is also why a recession reorders the risk-adjusted ranking. In good times the card's 12% margin looks untouchable. But in a deep downturn, when card losses spike to 11% and recoveries collapse, that margin can evaporate or go negative, while the mortgage book — protected by collateral and by households' fierce determination to keep their homes — keeps grinding out its thin but stable spread. The card is the highest-margin product in normal times and the most *volatile* product in bad ones. The bank is paid that fat margin precisely to absorb that volatility — and a bank that loaded up on cards and personal loans without enough capital to absorb a loss spike is a bank that turned a good business into a fatal one. This is the spine of the whole series: a bank survives only as long as its thin equity cushion absorbs losses faster than they arrive, and unsecured consumer losses are among the fastest-arriving losses there are. (How that cushion works is [bank capital and leverage: why equity is the thin cushion](/blog/trading/banking/bank-capital-and-leverage-why-equity-is-the-thin-cushion).)

## Common misconceptions

**"The credit card is a bad business because the losses are so high."** The opposite is true. The card has the highest loss rate *and* the highest risk-adjusted margin — roughly 12% after losses and funding, versus about 3% for a mortgage. A high loss rate is only a problem if the yield doesn't cover it; on a card, the 22% APR buries the 7% loss with room to spare. Judge a loan by yield minus loss minus funding, never by the loss rate alone.

**"A secured loan can't lose money because the bank can seize the collateral."** It can, on both ends. Collateral *caps* the loss; it doesn't eliminate it. An auto borrower can be underwater (owing more than the car is worth) early in the loan, so even after repossession the bank takes a hit. And in 2008, mortgage collateral itself collapsed — when home prices fell 30%, the houses no longer covered the loans, and the "safe" secured product produced catastrophic losses. Collateral protects you against the borrower failing; it does not protect you against the *collateral* failing.

**"Paying the minimum on a credit card slowly pays it off."** It barely does. On a \$5,000 balance at 22%, the true 2% minimum payment is sized to keep the loan alive — most of each payment is interest. Paying only the minimum can stretch the payoff to *decades* and cost more in interest than the original purchase. The minimum payment is designed for the lender's persistence, not the borrower's freedom.

**"Mortgages are the safest loan, so they're where the risk is lowest for the bank."** They are the lowest *credit* risk but they carry the highest *interest-rate* risk. A 30-year fixed mortgage locks the bank's yield for decades while its funding reprices in months; a book of 3% mortgages funded at 5% bleeds money with no borrower at fault. The mortgage trades credit risk for rate risk — it is not risk-free, just risky in a different way.

**"All consumer loans charge interest the same way."** They split into two species. Amortizing loans (mortgage, auto, personal) charge interest on a balance that marches to zero on a fixed schedule, front-loaded with interest. Revolving loans (cards) charge interest on a balance the borrower controls, which can compound indefinitely. The amortizing loan ends; the revolving loan can live forever, and that difference is most of why the card is the most profitable and the most dangerous product on the book.

## How it shows up in real banks

**The 2024 card-loss normalization.** After the pandemic, US households were flush with stimulus and savings, and card charge-offs fell to historic lows — under 2% in 2021-2022. As savings ran down and rates rose through 2023-2024, card charge-offs climbed back above 4% at major issuers, with some pushing toward 5%-6%, while mortgage and auto losses stayed comparatively contained. This is the cycle exactly as described: cards crack first as the consumer normalizes, and the rising card-loss line was the cleanest single read on consumer stress — visible quarters before it showed up elsewhere.

**Capital One's all-in card bet.** Capital One built itself around the highest-margin, highest-loss end of the consumer book — heavy in credit cards, including subprime. In good years this produces returns that crush a plain-vanilla mortgage lender's; in bad years its charge-offs spike far harder. Its 2024 move to acquire Discover was, in part, a bet to own more of the card-network economics (interchange) on top of the lending margin. Capital One is the living illustration that the card is the best business *and* the most cyclical one — a strategy that demands a thick capital cushion to ride out the loss spikes.

**The 2008 mortgage collapse.** The cautionary tale on the secured side. Mortgages were treated as nearly risk-free because the collateral — homes — only ever went up. When national home prices fell roughly 30% from 2006 to 2012, the collateral failed: borrowers were deeply underwater, defaults soared, and the "safe" secured product produced losses that took down Washington Mutual (the largest bank failure in US history) and others. The lesson burned into every credit committee since: a secured loan is only as safe as its collateral, and collateral can collapse. (The system-level story is in the finance one-pager on [the 2023 runs at SVB and Credit Suisse](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs); the mechanics here are the consumer-asset side of the same coin.)

**Subprime auto stress.** In 2023-2024, subprime auto delinquencies rose to multi-decade highs as the borrowers who bought cars at inflated 2021-2022 prices found themselves underwater on rapidly depreciating vehicles while higher rates squeezed their budgets. The blended "auto loan" number looked fine; the subprime slice inside it behaved almost like unsecured debt, with thin recoveries on repossessed cars. It is the clearest recent reminder that *who* you lend to matters as much as *what* the loan is secured by — a melting collateral plus a stressed borrower is barely secured at all.

**The personal-loan / BNPL surge.** Fintech lenders and "buy now, pay later" providers expanded unsecured personal credit aggressively in the low-rate years, often to thinner-credit borrowers, and many discovered the unsecured-product truth the hard way when losses normalized: with no collateral and an untested through-the-cycle loss history, charge-offs ran above projections as conditions tightened. The product economics are real — the personal loan's risk-adjusted margin is genuinely attractive — but only for a lender that priced the loss correctly and held capital against the chance it priced it wrong.

## The takeaway / How to use this

The four retail loans are not four different businesses. They are one business — buying a stream of promised payments — priced at four points along a single risk-reward line. Read in order, they teach the whole logic of consumer lending: as you move from the mortgage to the card, yield rises, loss rises, security falls, and the product gets shorter and more revolving — and *every one of those movements is the same movement*, the movement up the risk curve. The mortgage is the low-risk anchor; the card is the high-risk engine; auto and personal sit between them, secured-but-melting and unsecured-but-disciplined.

If you take three things from this post, take these. First, **judge a loan by its risk-adjusted margin, not its loss rate** — yield minus loss minus funding. On that ruler the card wins, which is the only reason banks chase the product with the scariest charge-off numbers. Second, **security is the swing factor on losses**: collateral caps the loss when a borrower defaults, which is the entire reason a mortgage can charge 6.5% and a card must charge 22% — but collateral can itself fail, as 2008 proved, so "secured" is a discipline, not a guarantee. Third, **the products crack in sequence through a cycle** — cards first, autos next, mortgages last — so card charge-offs are the consumer book's early-warning light; when they climb while the rest stays flat, the bank is telling you the next year of losses is already on its way.

For the reader who wants to understand a bank, this is where you start when you open its consumer-lending disclosures: ask what *mix* of these four products the bank holds, what each is yielding, what each is losing, and how much capital stands behind the unsecured slice that can spike fastest. A bank heavy in mortgages is a low-margin, rate-sensitive, slow-moving machine; a bank heavy in cards is a high-margin, high-volatility machine that lives or dies on whether its capital cushion can absorb the next loss spike. Both are the same fragile trade at the heart of every bank — borrow short, lend long, earn the spread, survive the losses — just dialed to different settings on the risk knob. The consumer asset side is where you can read, product by product, exactly how aggressively a bank has turned that knob.
