> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Continental Illinois was a giant bank funded almost entirely by other professionals' money, and when those professionals quietly pulled their cash in 1984 the government rescued every single one of them, inventing the doctrine we now call "too big to fail."
>
> - Continental was the 7th-largest US bank, about \$40 billion in assets, but an Illinois law that banned branch banking left it with almost no ordinary depositors. It funded itself with volatile wholesale and foreign money instead.
> - It grew fast by buying energy loans it never made — participations from a small Oklahoma bank, Penn Square — and when oil prices fell those loans went bad, leaving more than \$2 billion classified as troubled.
> - The death was a *silent run*: no queues outside branches, just wire transfers. Big lenders simply stopped rolling over their money, draining billions a day in May 1984.
> - To stop the panic spreading to the hundreds of small banks that had parked money at Continental, the FDIC protected **all** depositors and creditors, took roughly 80% ownership, and weeks later a regulator told Congress that the largest banks were "too big to fail."
> - The one number to remember: the FDIC guaranteed **100% of depositors**, far past the then-\$100,000 insurance limit — the first time the US openly said a bank's creditors would never be allowed to lose.

In May 1984 there was no crowd outside the marble headquarters of Continental Illinois on LaSalle Street in Chicago. No anxious savers clutching passbooks, no police managing a line around the block. The bank that was dying — the seventh-largest in the United States — was being killed in total silence, by people in suits sitting at desks in London, Tokyo, and across America, who did nothing more dramatic than decide, one by one, not to lend it money for another day.

A bank run usually looks like a movie scene: frightened people queuing to get their cash before the bank locks the doors. Continental's run was the opposite. Its money came not from millions of households but from a few hundred professional lenders — other banks, money-market funds, foreign institutions, large corporations — who held billions of dollars in short-term deposits that had to be renewed constantly, sometimes overnight. When the rumor spread that Continental was sitting on a mountain of bad oil loans, those lenders did the rational thing: they let their deposits mature and took their money elsewhere. No drama, no shouting. Just billions of dollars a day quietly walking out the door over a wire.

Within weeks the US government did something it had never done so openly before. It announced that it would protect **every** depositor and **every** general creditor of Continental Illinois — not just the small savers covered by deposit insurance, but the giant uninsured lenders and the foreign banks too. A few months later, in a Congressional hearing room, a Comptroller of the Currency was asked whether some banks were simply too important to be allowed to collapse, and the answer — and a congressman's blunt phrase for it — gave the world a permanent piece of vocabulary: *too big to fail*. The diagram above is the mental model for this whole story: aggressive growth funded by hot money, a loan loss, a silent run, and a rescue that changed the rules for every large bank that came after.

![Timeline of Continental Illinois from aggressive growth to the too big to fail rescue](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-1.png)

This is the original "too big to fail" case. Everything you have heard about that idea — the 2008 bailouts, the anger at banks "privatizing gains and socializing losses," the worry that giant banks get cheap funding *because* they are dangerous — traces back to this one Chicago bank and the choice regulators made about it in the summer of 1984. To understand it you do not need to know any banking. You only need to understand four plain ideas, which we will build from zero.

## Foundations: the words you need before the story

Before we can tell the story properly, we need five terms. Each one is simple once you strip the jargon away. We will define each with an everyday-money picture first, then name the industry term so you can recognize it later.

### What a bank actually is, in one sentence

Strip away the buildings and the logos and a bank is a very simple, very fragile machine. It takes in money that people can demand back at short notice (deposits) and lends that money out for a long time (loans). It earns the gap between the low rate it pays depositors and the higher rate it charges borrowers. That is the whole business. The danger is baked in: the money it owes can be demanded back *today*, but the money it is owed will not come back for *years*. A bank only survives as long as the people who lent it money trust it enough to leave their money there. This is the spine of the entire banking story — a bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded machine that borrows short and lends long — and Continental is what happens when the confidence evaporates faster than the loans can be turned back into cash. For the system-level view of how banks create money in the first place, see [how money is created](/blog/trading/finance/how-money-is-created-banks-central-banks-money-multiplier).

### Retail deposits vs wholesale and brokered funding

Picture two corner shops, each of which lends out the cash in its till.

The first shop's cash comes from a thousand regular customers, each leaving a small amount in a jar by the counter, mostly because the shop is around the corner and convenient. No single customer's jar is large. If one gets nervous and takes their jar home, the shop barely notices. This is **retail deposits**: money from ordinary households and small businesses, spread across enormous numbers of small accounts, sticky because moving it is a hassle and most of it is government-insured anyway. Retail deposits are the cheapest, calmest, most loyal funding a bank can have — they are, quite literally, the franchise.

The second shop has no regular customers. Instead it borrows its working cash each morning from three large money-lenders down the street, who lend to whichever shop offers the best rate that day and who can demand their money back any morning they like. This is **wholesale funding**: large, short-term deposits and loans from professional players — other banks, money-market funds, corporations, foreign institutions. A close cousin is **brokered deposits**, where a middleman gathers up large blocks of money chasing the highest rate and parks them at whichever bank pays most, with zero loyalty. Wholesale and brokered money is convenient and lets a bank grow fast, but it is *rate-chasing and flighty*: it leaves the instant a better rate appears or the first whiff of trouble arrives. We go deep on this whole spectrum in [the funding stack](/blog/trading/banking/the-funding-stack-deposits-wholesale-funding-bonds-and-covered-bonds).

The single most important fact about Continental Illinois is that it was the second shop. By law, it had to be.

### A loan participation

Say a small bank in an oil town makes a \$10 million loan to a drilling company, but the small bank itself is far too small to hold a loan that size safely. So it keeps \$1 million of the loan on its own books and *sells* the other \$9 million in slices to bigger banks elsewhere, who each hold a piece and earn a share of the interest. Each slice is a **loan participation**: a share of someone else's loan, bought to put your money to work. The catch is that the bank that *originated* the loan did the underwriting — the credit checking — and the banks that bought participations are trusting that work was done well. If the originator was sloppy or crooked, the participating banks have bought a slice of garbage without ever meeting the borrower. Hold this idea; it is the seed of Continental's destruction.

### The silent (institutional) run

We all know the picture of a **bank run**: a queue of small depositors trying to pull their cash before the bank fails, each person's fear making the next person's fear rational, until the bank cannot meet the demands and collapses. (The mechanics of that classic run — why it is self-fulfilling — are their own topic; see [the anatomy of a bank run](/blog/trading/banking/the-anatomy-of-a-bank-run-from-whisper-to-collapse).)

A **silent run** is the same dynamic with no queue. When a bank funds itself with wholesale money, the "run" is just professional lenders declining to renew their short-term deposits. No one lines up; no one shouts. Each lender simply lets its money mature and wires it somewhere safer. Because the sums are huge and the lenders are sophisticated and fast, a silent run is *faster and more lethal* than a retail one. A retail run is throttled by how fast tellers can count cash and by the fact that small deposits are insured, so most savers have no reason to flee. A silent run has no such brakes: an uninsured \$50 million deposit can be gone in a single keystroke. Continental died this way, in a matter of weeks, in 1984. The same mechanism, sped up by smartphones, killed Silicon Valley Bank in 36 hours in 2023.

### Too big to fail and systemic risk

Finally, the phrase itself. **Systemic risk** is the danger that one institution's failure does not stay contained but spreads, knocking over others like dominoes, until the whole financial system seizes up. A bank can be systemically important not because it is healthy but because so many other institutions are *connected* to it — they have lent it money, hold its paper, or rely on it for payments.

**Too big to fail** is the policy conclusion that follows: if letting a particular institution collapse would set off that domino chain, the government may decide it *cannot* let it collapse, and will instead rescue it — protecting even the creditors who, in a normal failure, would lose their money. The phrase carries a sting, because the moment everyone believes a bank will be rescued, the discipline that is supposed to keep it careful disappears. We will come back to that sting at the end. For now, just hold the definition: too big to fail means the state has decided some institutions are so connected that their creditors will be made whole no matter what.

Those five ideas — retail vs wholesale funding, loan participations, the silent run, systemic risk, and too big to fail — are the entire toolkit. Now the story.

## The growth: how Continental got big, and why it had to borrow to do it

In the 1970s, Continental Illinois National Bank and Trust Company of Chicago was a sleepy, conservative institution with a strong corporate-lending franchise. Then it decided to grow — aggressively, deliberately, and fast. Through the late 1970s and into the early 1980s its management set out to become one of the great lenders to American business, and by 1981 it had succeeded on paper: Continental was the seventh-largest bank in the United States, with roughly \$40 billion in assets, and it was the largest commercial and industrial lender in the country. It was, by the standard measures, a triumph.

But a bank cannot grow its loans without growing its funding, and here Continental ran into a wall built by Illinois law. At the time, Illinois had a strict **unit-banking law** — a rule that essentially forbade banks from opening branches. Where a bank in California could blanket the state with hundreds of branches, each one gathering small, sticky, insured retail deposits, Continental was effectively confined to a single banking location in Chicago. It had almost no way to gather the cheap household deposits that anchor a normal bank's funding.

So to fund its surging loan book, Continental did the only thing it could: it bought money on the wholesale market. It borrowed from other banks, from money-market funds, from large corporations, and — heavily — from foreign banks and investors who had no insurance and no loyalty. By the early 1980s a strikingly large share of its funding came from these volatile sources. The bank that looked so impressive on the asset side was, on the funding side, resting almost entirely on hot money that could leave at any time.

#### Worked example: just how dependent on wholesale money was Continental?

Let us make the funding fragility concrete with round numbers. A *healthy* large commercial bank funds itself in roughly these proportions: about 71% from deposits (mostly sticky retail), about 10% from wholesale funding and repo, about 7% from long-term bonds, about 4% other, and about 8% equity. Of that, the genuinely flighty part — wholesale and repo — is around 10% of the balance sheet.

Now picture Continental. Blocked from branches, suppose only about 20% of its \$40 billion came from stable insured retail deposits — call it \$8 billion. That leaves \$32 billion to be raised from large, uninsured, mostly professional and foreign sources: more than \$40 of every \$100 on its balance sheet was money that could leave in a day. Contemporary accounts put Continental's reliance on purchased and foreign funds at over 40% of its funding, far above peers.

So compare the run-risk. A normal bank facing a panic might have to replace 10% of its balance sheet — \$4 billion on a \$40 billion bank — and even then most depositors, being insured, have no reason to flee. Continental had to roll over more than \$30 billion of *uninsured* money continuously, and every dollar of it had a reason to flee at the first sign of trouble. The intuition: Continental had built a giant on a funding base that was structurally a single, enormous, hair-trigger run waiting to happen.

![Branch funded bank with sticky insured deposits versus Continental funded by hot wholesale money](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-2.png)

The figure above is the heart of the vulnerability. Both banks lend long. But the left-hand bank borrows from millions of small, insured, sticky savers, while Continental borrowed from a few hundred giant, uninsured, mobile lenders. The same loan book, funded two different ways, has two completely different risks of dying. Continental had built the fragile version.

It is worth pausing on the cruel irony. The Illinois branching law was meant to *protect* the banking system — by keeping banks small and local, lawmakers thought they were preventing dangerous concentrations of financial power. Instead, by denying a big bank access to stable retail funding, the law forced exactly the kind of fragile, wholesale-dependent structure that would prove catastrophic. A rule designed for safety helped build the most spectacular bank failure of its era.

There is a second, subtler driver worth naming, because it explains the *culture* that made Continental reach for risky assets. A bank funded by expensive wholesale money is under constant pressure to make its loans pay. Sticky retail deposits are cheap — a household leaves money in a checking account earning almost nothing. Wholesale money is dear — professional lenders demand a competitive rate every single day, or they leave. So a wholesale-funded bank pays more for its money than a branch-funded rival, which means it must earn more on its loans just to make the same profit. The temptation, then, is to chase higher-yielding (which is to say riskier) lending — exactly the energy loans that looked so lucrative in the oil boom. The funding structure did not just make Continental fragile in a panic; it pushed the bank toward the very assets that would blow up. Expensive funding and aggressive lending are two faces of the same coin, and Continental had both.

## Penn Square: the bad loans Continental bought but never made

Aggressive growth needs assets to grow into, and in the early 1980s the hottest lending market in America was energy. Oil prices had soared through the 1970s, and everyone — bankers especially — assumed they would keep rising forever. Lending money to drill for oil and gas looked like free money: the collateral (oil in the ground) only ever went up in value, so how could the loans go bad?

Continental wanted a large slice of this boom but did not have the origination machine to make all those oil loans itself. Enter Penn Square Bank, a small, freewheeling shopping-mall bank in Oklahoma City that had thrown itself into oil-patch lending with abandon. Penn Square made loans to drillers and wildcatters at a furious pace, far more than its own tiny balance sheet could hold — and then sold the slices on to bigger banks as **participations**. Continental became Penn Square's biggest customer, buying roughly \$1 billion of energy loan participations that Penn Square had originated.

The problem, which Continental's own controls should have caught and did not, was that Penn Square's underwriting was a disaster. Loans were made on a handshake, documentation was missing, collateral was overvalued or imaginary, and the whole operation ran on the assumption that oil prices could only rise. Continental, hungry for assets, bought slice after slice without doing the independent credit work that the situation demanded. It had outsourced its underwriting to the worst possible originator.

In July 1982, Penn Square failed. Oil prices had started to soften, the loans began to sour, and the little Oklahoma bank that had originated them collapsed under the weight of its own bad lending. When Penn Square went down, the participations it had sold did not vanish — they sat on the books of the banks that had bought them, Continental chief among them. Suddenly Continental was the proud owner of around \$1 billion of energy loans it had never properly examined, made by a failed bank, secured by collateral that was worth far less than claimed.

![Buying Penn Square energy participations leads through an oil bust and bad loans to a wholesale funding run](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-4.png)

The pipeline above is the chain of causation in six steps: buy the participations, watch oil fall, watch the loans go bad, watch the lenders notice, watch the wholesale money stop rolling, and finally the run. Notice that the *trigger* (a loan loss) and the *cause of death* (a funding run) are two different things. Plenty of banks survive a loan loss. Continental did not, because of how it was funded.

#### Worked example: did the energy losses actually exceed the bank's capital?

Here is a number that surprises people: the loan losses, by themselves, probably should *not* have killed Continental. Let us check with round figures.

A bank's **equity** is the thin cushion that absorbs losses before depositors are touched. On our 8%-equity rule of thumb, a \$40 billion bank holds roughly \$3.2 billion of equity (\$40bn × 0.08). Continental's bad energy book — the Penn Square participations plus other troubled energy and commodity loans — grew until more than \$2 billion of loans were classified as non-performing by 1984.

Now do the arithmetic. If the bank ultimately lost, say, 50% of \$2 billion of troubled loans, that is a \$1 billion loss against a roughly \$3.2 billion equity cushion. Painful — it wipes out about a third of the bank's capital — but *survivable*. The bank would be wounded, its profits gone for years, its stock crushed, but it would still be solvent: its assets would still exceed its deposits.

Here is the crucial lesson of this whole post: **Continental was not killed by insolvency; it was killed by illiquidity.** The loan losses were large but not fatal to its *solvency*. What was fatal was that the losses spooked its uninsured wholesale lenders, who then refused to roll over more than \$30 billion of funding that the bank could not replace. A bank can survive losing some of its capital. No bank can survive losing most of its funding overnight. Solvency is about whether your assets exceed your debts; liquidity is about whether you can pay what's due *today*. Continental had a solvency scare and a liquidity death.

## The funding fragility: why a survivable loss became a fatal run

To see why a manageable loan loss turned into an unstoppable run, you have to think like one of Continental's wholesale lenders in the spring of 1984.

You run a money-market fund, or the treasury desk of a Japanese bank, or a corporate cash desk. You have, say, \$50 million parked at Continental in a deposit that matures next week. It is *uninsured* — far above the \$100,000 federal insurance limit of the day — so if Continental fails before your deposit matures, you could lose a large chunk of \$50 million. Your job is not to be patriotic about Continental; your job is to not lose your firm's money.

Now you read in the newspaper that Continental is sitting on a billion dollars of bad Penn Square energy loans, and you hear whispers that things are worse than disclosed. What do you do? You do not need to *believe* Continental will fail. You only need to believe that *other lenders might pull out*, because if they do and you are the last one in, you are the one who eats the loss. So the rational move is to not renew your deposit — to take your \$50 million and put it somewhere boring and safe. Every single one of Continental's lenders is doing this same calculation at the same time. The result is a stampede in slow motion, executed by wire transfer.

This is the self-fulfilling logic of a run, and it is *worse* for a wholesale-funded bank than for a retail one. A small insured saver has no reason to run — their \$5,000 is guaranteed by the government, so they leave it alone. But a giant uninsured lender has every reason to run at the first hint of trouble, because they have everything to lose and the insurance backstop does not cover them. Continental had built itself almost entirely out of exactly the depositors most likely to flee.

![Two kinds of run a visible retail queue and a silent wholesale wire flood both drain the funding](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-5.png)

The graph above contrasts the two kinds of run. Fear triggers both. A retail run produces a *visible queue* but a *slow drain*, capped by how fast tellers work and by deposit insurance that keeps most savers calm. A silent run produces *no queue at all* but a *fast drain* — billions a day, no cap, every dollar uninsured. Both end at the same fatal place: the funding the bank lends out is gone. Continental took the right-hand path.

#### Worked example: how fast can a wholesale run drain a bank?

Let us quantify the speed, because it is the difference between a problem and a catastrophe.

Suppose a bank holds \$10 billion of genuinely liquid assets — cash, reserves at the central bank, and securities it can sell or pledge quickly. That is its buffer against deposits leaving. Now compare two run speeds.

A *retail* run, throttled by branch hours and by insured savers who mostly stay put, might drain \$500 million a day. At that pace the bank's \$10 billion buffer lasts 20 days — three full weeks for regulators to arrange a rescue or an orderly sale. There is time.

A *wholesale* run has no throttle. When Continental's lenders fled in May 1984, the outflows ran into the billions of dollars per day — call it \$4 billion a day at the peak. At that pace a \$10 billion buffer is gone in **two and a half days**. There is no time. The bank cannot sell loans fast enough, cannot raise new funding (who would lend into a run?), and cannot wait for a buyer. The intuition: the same buffer that buys three weeks against a retail run buys barely two days against a wholesale run, which is exactly why a silent institutional run is the most dangerous thing that can happen to a bank.

In reality Continental did not have anything like enough liquid assets to outlast its run. As the wholesale money fled in May 1984, it was forced to borrow ever-larger emergency amounts — first from a syndicate of other large banks that put together a multi-billion-dollar lifeline, then from the Federal Reserve's discount window, where it ended up borrowing several billion dollars just to keep the lights on day to day. None of it stemmed the outflow. Each emergency loan was a signal that the bank was in trouble, which made the next lender even more eager to leave. The bank was caught in the run's death spiral: borrowing emergency money to replace fleeing money, with the borrowing itself accelerating the fleeing.

## The 1984 run: a death by wire transfer

The trigger for the final collapse was, fittingly for a wholesale bank, a *rumor*. In early May 1984, news-wire reports — some of them inaccurate or exaggerated — suggested that Continental was facing serious difficulty and that a foreign bank or government might have to step in. The reports were quickly denied, but it did not matter. For lenders running the calculation above, even an unconfirmed rumor was reason enough to pull out, because the cost of being wrong (losing tens of millions of uninsured dollars) vastly outweighed the cost of being needlessly cautious (a few days' lost interest).

The run went global within hours. Foreign banks, which held a large share of Continental's deposits, were the first and fastest to leave — they had the least information, the most to lose, and no insurance whatsoever. Then the domestic money funds, the corporate treasurers, the correspondent banks. Over the following days Continental lost billions of dollars of funding, replaced only by ever-larger emergency borrowing. By mid-May the bank was effectively being kept alive on government and syndicate life support, hemorrhaging deposits it could not replace.

What made it terrifying for the regulators was not just the size of Continental but its *connectedness*. A large bank like Continental runs a **correspondent-banking** business: it holds accounts for, and provides services to, hundreds of smaller banks that are too small to maintain their own presence in the big money centers. Those small banks park their reserves and surplus cash at the big correspondent — which means the big bank holds *their* money. When Continental wobbled, every small bank that banked with it suddenly faced the prospect that its own deposits there — sometimes a large fraction of its entire capital — might be locked up or lost. Estimates at the time suggested that well over a thousand other banks had money on deposit at Continental, and that for a significant number of them the exposure was large enough relative to their own capital that a Continental failure could push them into failure too. This is **systemic risk** made concrete: Continental was not just a big bank, it was a *hub*, and hubs cannot be allowed to collapse without taking the spokes with them.

#### Worked example: how many small banks could one giant's failure topple?

Let us put numbers on the contagion the regulators feared, because that fear is what justified the unprecedented rescue. The trade-off the FDIC weighs is laid out in the matrix later in this post; here we size just the domino risk.

Suppose a small heartland bank has \$100 million of total assets and, on our 8%-equity rule, about \$8 million of equity — the cushion that stands between it and failure. Now suppose, like many of its peers, it keeps \$12 million of its cash on deposit at Continental, its big-city correspondent. That \$12 million is *uninsured* (it is far above the \$100,000 limit), so if Continental fails and creditors take a loss of, say, 40 cents on the dollar, the small bank loses \$12m × 0.40 = \$4.8 million.

Compare that loss to the small bank's \$8 million of equity. A \$4.8 million hit wipes out 60% of its entire capital base in a single stroke — enough to push it from healthy to severely undercapitalized, and possibly into failure itself. Now multiply: if even a few hundred of the thousand-plus correspondent banks had Continental exposures of this magnitude, letting Continental's creditors take losses could have triggered a *cascade* of small-bank failures across the Midwest. The intuition: Continental's danger was not its own \$40 billion size but the web of smaller banks whose survival depended on getting their money back — the failure of the hub threatened to drain the equity out of dozens or hundreds of spokes at once, which is precisely the chain reaction "too big to fail" exists to prevent.

By late May 1984 the question facing the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation and the federal banking authorities was no longer "can Continental survive on its own?" — it plainly could not. The question was "what happens if we let it die?" And the answer they kept arriving at was: a chain reaction through the correspondent network, a wave of small-bank failures across the heartland, and a general panic that uninsured deposits at *any* large bank could vanish — which would set off silent runs everywhere. That fear is what drove the unprecedented response.

## The FDIC full rescue: protecting everyone

On July 26, 1984, the federal authorities announced a rescue of Continental Illinois unlike anything that had come before. The package had several moving parts, but one feature was the historic one. The FDIC declared that it would guarantee **all** depositors and **all** general creditors of Continental Illinois — in full, regardless of the insurance limit. The small saver with \$5,000 and the foreign bank with \$500 million in uninsured wholesale deposits were both made completely whole. Nobody who had lent money to Continental would lose a cent.

This was extraordinary. The entire architecture of deposit insurance was built on a *limit* — at the time, \$100,000 per depositor. Above that line, the deal was supposed to be that you were on your own: lend to a bank above the insured limit and you accept the risk. The point of the limit was precisely to keep *some* market discipline alive, so that large, sophisticated lenders would watch their banks and pull back from reckless ones. By guaranteeing everyone at Continental, the government tore up that deal for this bank — and, everyone understood, signaled that it would do the same for any bank big enough to matter.

The mechanics of the rescue were equally aggressive. The FDIC injected new capital into the bank — around \$2 billion — and the Federal Reserve kept the discount window open to fund it. The FDIC also took over a large block of Continental's worst loans, removing several billion dollars of bad assets from the bank so it could survive. In exchange for the capital injection, the FDIC took ownership of the great majority of the bank — roughly 80% — effectively nationalizing it. Top management was removed. Continental survived as a functioning institution, but as a ward of the state, owned and run on behalf of the government until it could be cleaned up and eventually sold years later (it was finally absorbed by Bank of America in 1994).

It is worth being precise about *why* the full guarantee, and not a partial one, was judged necessary. The regulators had considered the obvious middle path: protect the small insured depositors, as the law required, and let the large uninsured wholesale lenders take a loss, as the law also intended. The problem was that any loss imposed on uninsured creditors at Continental would have instantly answered the question every uninsured lender at every *other* large bank was asking — "could this happen to me?" — with a resounding yes. The moment a wholesale lender at a giant bank saw that its peers at Continental had taken losses, it would conclude that uninsured money at any big bank was at risk, and the silent run would spread from Continental to the next-weakest giant, and the next. To stop the panic, the guarantee had to be *total and visible*: only by protecting everyone could the authorities convince wholesale lenders everywhere that there was no reason to run. The completeness of the rescue was not generosity; it was the only thing that could break the logic of the run.

![Biggest US bank failures by assets at failure with Continental Illinois highlighted](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-3.png)

The chart above places Continental among the biggest US bank failures by assets. At about \$40 billion it was, for a long time, the largest bank failure in American history — only surpassed decades later by the 2008 and 2023 collapses (Washington Mutual at \$307 billion, First Republic at \$229 billion, Silicon Valley Bank at \$209 billion). What made Continental the template was not its size alone but the *response*: it was the first time the government openly protected all creditors of a giant bank to stop a systemic chain reaction.

#### Worked example: what did the rescue cost, and who paid?

Let us tally the bill in round numbers, because "the cost of a bailout" is often discussed and rarely added up.

The FDIC's costs came in pieces. It injected roughly \$2 billion of fresh capital. It took over a pool of bad loans with a face value of several billion dollars — say \$4.5 billion of troubled assets — knowing it would recover only a fraction. If those bad loans ultimately recovered, say, 60 cents on the dollar, the loss on that pool alone is \$4.5bn × 0.40 = \$1.8 billion. Add other costs and offset by the value of the 80% equity stake the FDIC took (which had some recovery value as the bank stabilized), and the net cost to the FDIC of the Continental rescue is generally put at around \$1 billion or more.

Now the crucial question: who paid? Not the taxpayer directly, in the first instance — the FDIC is funded by *premiums paid by banks*. So the immediate bill was borne by the banking industry's insurance fund. But the deeper cost was not the dollar figure at all. It was the *precedent*: by making all of Continental's creditors whole, the government created an expectation that the creditors of every large bank would be similarly protected. That expectation, as we will see, is worth billions of dollars a year to the largest banks in cheaper funding — a subsidy paid by everyone, forever. The intuition: the cash cost of the rescue was about a billion dollars; the *true* cost was an open-ended guarantee that has shaped banking ever since.

## The doctrine is born: "too big to fail" enters the language

The phrase itself was coined a few weeks after the rescue, in a Congressional hearing. On September 19, 1984, the Comptroller of the Currency — the federal regulator of national banks, then C. Todd Conover — testified before the House Banking Committee about the Continental rescue. Pressed by skeptical congressmen on why the government had protected all of Continental's uninsured creditors, Conover acknowledged that the regulators considered the eleven largest US banks to be effectively in a special category: they would not be allowed to fail in a way that imposed losses on depositors, because the systemic consequences were judged too severe.

It was Representative Stewart McKinney of Connecticut who put the blunt label on it. Reacting to the testimony, he remarked that the government had created a new kind of bank — "too big to fail" — and the phrase, perfect in its plainness, stuck instantly. From that hearing onward, "too big to fail" became the shorthand for the entire problem: that some institutions are so large and so connected that the state cannot credibly threaten to let them collapse, and that everyone knows it.

The implications rippled out immediately. If the eleven largest banks were too big to fail, then their uninsured creditors were, in practice, insured — by the implicit promise of a government rescue. And if that was true, then those creditors no longer had any reason to charge those banks a higher interest rate for taking risks, because they were not going to bear the losses. The discipline that is supposed to keep big banks careful — the threat that reckless behavior raises your funding costs — had been quietly switched off for the giants.

![The regulators choice let Continental fail or bail it out and the signal each choice sends](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-7.png)

The matrix above lays out the choice the FDIC actually faced. Letting Continental fail would have made its uninsured lenders and bondholders take the hit — restoring market discipline — but at the risk of toppling the hundreds of small banks connected to it and igniting panic across the system. Bailing it out stopped that chain reaction, but at the cost of telling every large bank's creditors that they would be protected too. The regulators chose to stop the chain reaction. The price was the doctrine.

#### Worked example: why "too big to fail" is really a statement about creditor expectations

The clearest way to understand too big to fail is not as a statement about a bank's size, but as a statement about what its *creditors believe*. Let us price it.

Consider two banks, each wanting to borrow \$1 billion in the wholesale market for a year. Bank A is small and unprotected; lenders know that if it makes bad loans, they can lose money, so they demand a *risk premium* — a basis point is one hundredth of a percent (0.01%) — of, say, 50 basis points (0.50%) above the risk-free rate to compensate for the chance of loss. Bank B is "too big to fail"; lenders believe the government will rescue them if it gets into trouble, so they demand a much smaller premium — say 10 basis points (0.10%), reflecting only minor inconvenience risk.

The funding-cost gap is 40 basis points (0.50% − 0.10%). On \$1 billion that is \$1bn × 0.0040 = \$4 million a year of cheaper funding — purely because creditors expect a bailout. Scale that across the hundreds of billions a giant bank borrows, and the implicit guarantee is worth many hundreds of millions, even billions, of dollars a year. Studies of the largest banks have estimated exactly this kind of funding subsidy. The intuition: too big to fail is not a label a regulator stamps on a building; it is a *belief held by lenders*, and that belief is worth real money, because it lets the biggest banks borrow more cheaply precisely because everyone assumes they will be saved.

This is the heart of the moral-hazard problem. **Moral hazard** is the tendency to take more risk when you are insulated from the consequences. If a giant bank's creditors will be protected no matter what, then the creditors stop watching the bank, and the bank — facing cheaper funding and less scrutiny — is free to take more risk. The guarantee meant to stabilize the system can end up encouraging the very recklessness it was supposed to prevent. We treat the full mechanics of this trade-off — deposit insurance, the lender of last resort, and moral hazard — in [deposit insurance and the lender of last resort](/blog/trading/banking/deposit-insurance-the-lender-of-last-resort-and-moral-hazard).

![Without a guarantee lenders price risk but a too big to fail guarantee cuts funding costs and discipline](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-8.png)

The before-and-after above is the moral-hazard mechanism in one picture. Without the implicit guarantee, lenders price the bank's risk, funding costs more, and the market keeps the bank careful. With the guarantee, lenders feel safe, funding gets cheaper, and the discipline fades — the bank can take more risk almost for free. Continental's rescue did not invent moral hazard, but it made the implicit guarantee explicit and permanent for the largest banks.

## Common misconceptions

**"Continental failed because it made bad loans."** Only half true, and the half it gets wrong is the important half. The bad energy loans were the *trigger*, but as the worked example showed, the loan losses (perhaps \$1 billion against a \$3.2 billion equity cushion) were survivable on their own. What actually killed Continental was the *funding run*: it lost more than \$30 billion of uninsured wholesale money it could not replace. Plenty of banks survive a bad loan book; almost none survive a wholesale run. The loss was the spark; the funding structure was the gasoline.

**"It was a normal bank run with people lining up."** No. This is the misconception that makes Continental so instructive. There were no queues. The run was conducted entirely by professional lenders pulling wholesale deposits over the wire — a *silent* run, faster and more lethal than the queue-at-the-branch version precisely because the money was large, uninsured, and could move in a keystroke. If your mental image of Continental's death is a 1930s newsreel, you have the wrong image entirely; the real scene was a trading floor quietly deciding not to renew.

**"Too big to fail means the bank's shareholders get bailed out."** Not exactly, and the distinction matters. In Continental's case the *creditors and depositors* were made whole, but the existing *shareholders* were largely wiped out — the FDIC took roughly 80% of the bank, massively diluting them, and management was fired. Too big to fail protects the *people who lent the bank money*, not the people who *owned* it. The point was never to rescue the owners; it was to stop the creditors' panic from cascading through the system.

**"The bailout cost taxpayers tens of billions."** The direct cost was far smaller — on the order of about \$1 billion net to the FDIC, which is funded by bank premiums, not by general taxes in the first instance. The misconception conflates Continental with the much larger Savings & Loan cleanup and the 2008 bailouts. The genuinely enormous cost of Continental was not the cash bill but the *precedent*: the open-ended, permanent expectation that big banks' creditors would always be protected.

**"After 1984 they fixed too big to fail."** They did not, and arguably they could not. The Continental rescue created an expectation that proved impossible to walk back. Twenty-four years later, in 2008, the government faced the same choice at vastly greater scale and made essentially the same decision over and over. The 2010 Dodd-Frank reforms tried to create tools to wind down a giant bank without a bailout (resolution authority, living wills), but whether those tools would actually be used in a real panic — rather than another rescue — remains untested and widely doubted.

**"Continental was just badly run, so the lesson is to hire better managers."** This misses the structural point entirely. Yes, buying a billion dollars of energy participations from a reckless little Oklahoma bank without proper credit checks was a management failure. But the *fatal* feature was not a single bad decision — it was the funding structure that turned any bad decision into a death sentence. A branch-funded bank that made the exact same energy-loan mistake would have absorbed the loss, cut its dividend, and survived, because its insured retail depositors would have stayed put. Continental could not survive the same mistake because its money could flee. The lesson is not "hire better managers"; it is "watch the funding," because a fragile funding base converts ordinary, survivable mistakes into fatal ones. Better management lowers the *chance* of a trigger; sound funding determines whether a trigger is *survivable*.

## How it shows up in real banks

### The 2008 echo: the same choice at ten times the scale

In September 2008, the US government faced the Continental question again, but with institutions an order of magnitude larger and more interconnected. It let Lehman Brothers fail — and the result was exactly the systemic chain reaction the Continental regulators had feared, frozen credit markets and a global panic. Days later, it reversed course and rescued essentially everyone else: AIG, the money-market funds, the giant banks through the TARP program. The lesson policymakers took from Lehman was, in effect, that the Continental doctrine had been *right* — that letting a systemically connected institution fail was unbearable. The symmetry with 1984 is almost exact. In both cases the proximate killer was a wholesale-funding run, not a slow erosion of capital; in both cases the authorities concluded that the connections radiating out from the failing firm made an orderly death impossible; and in both cases the response was to stand behind the creditors. The difference was only one of scale and of how openly the guarantee was admitted. Continental was the prototype, run once and quietly; 2008 was the same machine run a dozen times at once and on the front page of every newspaper. Lehman's own death, like Continental's, was a wholesale-funding run (its short-term repo lenders fled), magnified by 30-to-1 leverage; we cover it in detail in the Lehman case study. The 2008 bailouts were the Continental playbook, run at a scale that made the original look quaint, and they hardened the too-big-to-fail expectation into something close to certainty.

### The funding subsidy that giants actually enjoy

The implicit guarantee Continental created is not abstract — economists have repeatedly tried to measure it. Studies in the years after 2008 estimated that the largest US and European banks borrowed at meaningfully lower rates than their standalone risk justified, with the funding advantage running into the tens of basis points and the implied annual subsidy into the tens of billions of dollars across the largest institutions. Credit-rating agencies made it explicit by assigning the biggest banks a "sovereign support uplift" — a ratings boost granted *specifically because* the government was expected to stand behind them. The Continental worked example above (40 basis points on \$1 billion equals \$4 million a year) is exactly this effect, scaled up to a multi-trillion-dollar balance sheet. The doctrine born in a 1984 hearing room shows up, decades later, as a line item in the cost of funds of every global bank.

### SVB 2023: the silent run at smartphone speed

In March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank died of precisely Continental's disease, updated for the digital age. SVB was funded overwhelmingly by uninsured wholesale-like deposits — venture-backed startups with balances far above the (by-then \$250,000) insurance limit; about 94% of its deposits were uninsured. When fear spread, those depositors ran exactly as Continental's wholesale lenders had — silently, electronically, and at terrifying speed. Roughly \$42 billion was pulled in a single day (March 9), with about \$100 billion more queued for the next morning before regulators seized the bank. And the resolution echoed Continental directly: to stop the panic spreading to other regional banks, the FDIC invoked a *systemic risk exception* and guaranteed **all** of SVB's deposits, insured and uninsured alike — the Continental move, made openly, 39 years later. The system-level account of that episode is in [the SVB and Credit Suisse 2023 bank runs](/blog/trading/finance/svb-credit-suisse-2023-bank-runs).

### The thousand-failure backdrop the rescue sat against

Continental fell at the leading edge of a brutal era for American banks. The Savings & Loan crisis was building through the 1980s and would ultimately see more than a thousand thrifts fail, and the broader bank-failure wave ran into the early 1990s. The chart below shows the *modern* failure record — the 2008-2012 wave that peaked at 157 failures in 2010, and the 2023 spike — which is the yardstick we now use for "how often banks fail." Continental's 1984 rescue established the principle that would be invoked again and again as these waves rolled through: that a *large, connected* failure was a different category of problem from a *small, isolated* one, and warranted a different response.

![FDIC insured bank failures per year showing the 2008 to 2012 wave and the 2023 spike](/imgs/blogs/continental-illinois-1984-and-the-birth-of-too-big-to-fail-6.png)

The distinction the chart makes visible is the one Continental crystallized. Hundreds of small banks can fail and be quietly resolved by the FDIC with barely a ripple — that is the system working as designed, the columns of the 2010 wave each representing a bank wound down without a systemic rescue. But a handful of giant, hub-like banks are treated as a wholly different animal, because the regulators decided in 1984 that they could not be allowed to fall without bringing others down with them.

### Northern Rock and the modern wholesale-run template

In 2007, the British mortgage lender Northern Rock became the first UK bank run in over a century — and it ran for the same structural reason as Continental. Northern Rock had funded a fast-growing mortgage book not with sticky retail deposits but with short-term wholesale money raised in the markets. When the 2007 credit crunch froze those markets, its funding evaporated, and unlike Continental its trouble *did* eventually produce the iconic queues of retail savers (after the wholesale run had already done the damage). The British authorities, facing their own Continental moment, ultimately guaranteed all of Northern Rock's deposits and nationalized it. Across four decades and two continents, the pattern repeats with eerie fidelity: aggressive growth, wholesale funding, a market shock, a run, and a state rescue that protects the creditors.

## The takeaway: read every big bank through Continental's lens

The lasting value of Continental Illinois is not as a piece of 1980s trivia. It is a lens — a permanent set of questions to ask about any large bank, in any era.

**First, always look past the assets to the funding.** Continental had an impressive loan book and died anyway, because the right question was never "are its loans good?" but "where does its money come from, and can that money leave in a hurry?" When you read about a bank in trouble, find the funding mix. A bank funded by millions of small insured deposits is a fundamentally different — and safer — animal than one funded by a few hundred giant uninsured lenders, *even if their loan books are identical*. The asset side gets the headlines; the liability side does the killing.

**Second, distinguish the trigger from the cause of death.** Continental's bad energy loans were the spark, but the loss was survivable. The cause of death was illiquidity — a funding run that no buffer could outlast. This distinction, solvency versus liquidity, is the single most useful idea in reading bank failures. A solvent bank can die of a liquidity run in days; an insolvent bank can limp along for years if its funding stays put. When you hear "the bank failed," ask which one it was — almost always, the proximate killer is the run, not the loss.

**Third, understand that too big to fail is a price, not a policy.** It is not something regulators choose to grant; it is a belief that lenders hold, and that belief shows up as a measurable funding subsidy for the largest banks. Every time you read about a giant bank borrowing cheaply, part of that cheapness is the market pricing in a rescue that has never been formally promised and can never be formally denied. Continental is where that belief was born — the moment the government, trying to stop one fire, lit a slow-burning one that has shaped the cost of banking ever since.

And the deepest takeaway loops back to the spine of this whole series: a bank is a confidence-funded machine that borrows short and lends long, and it lives or dies on whether its lenders trust it enough to leave their money. Continental had built a giant on the least trusting, most mobile money in the world. When the trust went, the money went — silently, by wire, faster than any rescue could catch — and the only way to stop the bleeding from killing the patient and everyone connected to it was for the government to step in and promise, for the first time so openly, that some banks would simply never be allowed to fall. That promise is Continental's true monument, and we are still paying for it.
