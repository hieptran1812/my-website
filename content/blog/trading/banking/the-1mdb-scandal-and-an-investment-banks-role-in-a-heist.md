> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An investment bank is paid to be a *gatekeeper* on big deals; in the 1MDB scandal that gate failed so completely that one of the world's most prestigious banks helped move billions out of a country's sovereign fund.
>
> - Goldman Sachs arranged about **\$6.5 billion** of bonds for Malaysia's 1MDB fund across 2012–2013 and kept roughly **\$600 million** in revenue — about **9%** of the money raised, when a normal bond fee is **0.2%–0.5%**.
> - Of that \$6.5 billion, about **\$4.5 billion** — roughly **two-thirds** — was diverted through shell companies and spent on art, property, a yacht, and even a Hollywood film.
> - The outsized fee was the loudest red flag of all: a fee that high on a plain bond is not a reward for skill, it is a signal that something about the deal is not normal.
> - The bill: a roughly **\$2.9 billion** US settlement and a roughly **\$3.9 billion** settlement with Malaysia (about **\$6.8 billion** together), plus **\$174 million** of executive pay clawed back and individual bankers charged. The one number to remember: **\$4.5 billion stolen from a fund a bank was paid to serve.**

In October 2020, the United States Department of Justice announced that a subsidiary of Goldman Sachs had pleaded guilty to a criminal charge of conspiring to violate American anti-bribery law. The parent firm agreed to pay billions of dollars. For a bank that had spent a century building a reputation as the smartest, most careful house on Wall Street, it was an extraordinary admission: that its bankers had helped raise money for a sovereign fund knowing — or recklessly ignoring — that the money was going to be looted.

The fund was called 1Malaysia Development Berhad, or 1MDB. It was set up by the Malaysian government in 2009 to invest in projects that would develop the country: power plants, real estate, jobs. Instead, it became the vehicle for one of the largest financial frauds in history. And the bank that arranged its bonds did not just stand on the sidelines. It built the deals, sold them to investors, and pocketed a fee so large that, in hindsight, it looks less like payment for a service and more like a cut of the take.

This post is about how that happened — not as a true-crime story, but as a *banking* story. Because the 1MDB scandal is the clearest case study you will ever find of what an investment bank is actually *for*, what it means when a bank acts as a gatekeeper, and what it costs everyone — investors, a country, the bank's own shareholders — when the gate is propped open for a fee. The diagram below is the mental model: money left ordinary bond investors as a clean-looking sovereign bond and ended up in private accounts, with only a sliver ever reaching a real project.

## Foundations: the words you need before the story makes sense

Before we can see *why* this was a banking failure and not just a theft, we need to build up six ideas from zero. If you already know what a sovereign wealth fund or a deferred prosecution agreement is, skim. If you don't, do not skip — the whole scandal lives in the gap between what these things are *supposed* to do and what they did here.

### A sovereign wealth fund is a country's investment pot

A *sovereign wealth fund* is an investment fund owned by a government rather than by private investors. The idea is simple: a country with extra money — from oil, from trade surpluses, from selling assets — puts that money into a fund and invests it, so that today's wealth keeps working for the country's future. Norway's fund (built from oil revenue) is the famous clean example: it now holds well over a trillion dollars and quietly owns slices of thousands of companies worldwide on behalf of Norwegian citizens.

The key feature is that the money belongs to *the public*. A sovereign fund is supposed to be run with even more care than a private one, because losing it means losing a nation's savings. 1MDB was set up to be Malaysia's version — a development fund that would borrow money, invest it in big projects, and earn returns for the country. The borrowing is where the bank comes in.

### Bond underwriting is how a fund borrows from the world

When a fund or a government wants to borrow a large sum, it doesn't go to a single lender. It issues a *bond* — a promise to pay back a fixed amount on a fixed date, with interest along the way — and sells that promise to many investors at once. But a fund cannot just walk into the global market and sell billions of dollars of bonds on its own. It hires an investment bank to do it.

That job is called *underwriting* (or *arranging*). The bank does several things: it structures the bond (size, maturity, interest rate), it markets the bond to investors, and — crucially — it often *buys the bonds itself first* and then resells them. By taking the bonds onto its own books for a short time, the bank guarantees the issuer gets its money even if not every investor shows up. That guarantee is real risk, and the bank is paid for taking it.

The pay is the *underwriting fee* (sometimes called the gross spread or arranging fee): the difference between what investors pay for the bonds and what the issuer receives. For a normal, investment-grade bond from a government-linked issuer, this fee is *small* — typically **0.2% to 0.5%** of the amount raised, and rarely above 1%–2% even on a tricky deal. Hold that range in your head. It is the single most important number in this whole story, because Goldman's fee on 1MDB was roughly **9%**.

#### Worked example: what a normal bond fee should have been

Let's anchor the scale. 1MDB raised about **\$6.5 billion** across three bond deals that Goldman arranged.

- At a *typical* sovereign-bond fee of **0.3%**, the bank's revenue would be: \$6,500,000,000 × 0.003 = **\$19.5 million**.
- At a *generous* 0.5%: \$6,500,000,000 × 0.005 = **\$32.5 million**.
- Even at an aggressive **2%** — the kind of fee you'd see on a genuinely hard, junk-rated, hard-to-place deal — it would be: \$6,500,000,000 × 0.02 = **\$130 million**.

Goldman actually kept about **\$600 million**. That is \$600m ÷ \$6,500m = **9.2%** of the money raised — roughly **18 times** the \$32.5 million a 0.5% deal would have paid, and nearly **5 times** even the aggressive 2% case.

The intuition: a fee that is an order of magnitude above the market rate is not pricing a service. On a deal like this, it is almost always paying for something the paperwork doesn't mention.

### Bribery and the FCPA: paying to get the deal

A *bribe* is a payment made to a government official (or someone close to power) to get them to do something they shouldn't — award a contract, sign off on a deal, look the other way. In the United States, paying bribes to foreign officials to win business is a federal crime under the **Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA)**, passed in 1977. The FCPA reaches far: if a company touches the US financial system, uses US dollars, or trades on a US exchange, the DOJ can prosecute it for bribes paid anywhere in the world.

This matters because the 1MDB bonds were arranged in part by paying bribes and kickbacks to officials in Malaysia and Abu Dhabi to win the mandates and to wave the deals through. When a Goldman unit pleaded guilty in 2020, the charge was conspiracy to violate the FCPA — bribery, stated plainly.

### The gatekeeper role: what a bank is really paid for

Here is the idea the whole post turns on. An investment bank is not just a sales desk that moves bonds. It is a *gatekeeper*. When a respected bank puts its name on a deal, it is telling the world's investors: *we have looked at this issuer, we have checked who we are dealing with, and we are willing to stake our reputation on it.* That stamp is worth real money — it lets an obscure fund borrow billions from cautious institutions that would never lend to it directly.

A gatekeeper has duties that come with the stamp. It must run *know-your-customer* (KYC) checks — verifying who the client is, who controls it, and where the money goes. It must run the deal past *compliance* (the internal team whose job is to stop the bank from breaking laws) and a *deal committee* (senior bankers who approve the firm's biggest, riskiest mandates). Each of these is a gate. The system is designed so that *any one* of them can say no and kill a bad deal. We will see that on 1MDB, every gate that should have closed was instead pushed open.

A homely comparison makes the gatekeeper's economic role clear. Take a small-town building inspector. When the inspector signs off on a house, a buyer who has never seen the foundations is willing to pay full price, and a bank is willing to lend against it — *because the inspector's signature is trusted*. The inspector's whole value is that signature, and it is valuable precisely because the inspector is believed to say no to bad houses. Now suppose the inspector quietly takes a fat envelope to sign off on a house with a cracked foundation. For one payment, the inspector has done two things: enabled a bad sale, and — far more damaging — taught the world that the signature can be bought. The signature is now worth nothing, which means *every* house the inspector ever signed is suddenly suspect. An investment bank's stamp on a deal is that signature. The 1MDB fee was the envelope.

### Reputational risk vs conduct risk

Two flavors of risk run through this story, and they are not the same thing.

- **Conduct risk** is the risk that the bank's own people *behave badly* — break a rule, mislead a client, pay a bribe, hide a red flag for a bonus. It is a risk that lives *inside* the firm.
- **Reputational risk** is the risk that *the outside world stops trusting you* — clients leave, regulators tighten the screws, your name becomes a punchline. It is the *consequence* that conduct risk creates.

The relationship is causal: bad conduct, once it surfaces, becomes reputational damage. 1MDB is a textbook chain — a few bankers' conduct metastasized into years of headlines, billions in fines, and a permanent asterisk next to one of finance's proudest names.

### DPA and clawback: how the system punishes a firm

Two final terms, because they are how the story *ends*.

A **deferred prosecution agreement (DPA)** is a deal between a company and prosecutors: the company admits wrongdoing, pays a large penalty, agrees to reforms and oversight, and in exchange the criminal charge against it is "deferred" — held in suspension and dropped later if the company behaves. It lets prosecutors punish a firm hard without putting it out of business (and taking thousands of innocent jobs with it). Goldman's US resolution involved a DPA at the parent level alongside a guilty plea by a Malaysian subsidiary.

A **clawback** is when a bank reclaims pay it already gave — or cancels pay it promised — to executives or bankers after the fact, usually because a deal they were responsible for turned out to be a disaster. It is the mechanism that makes *individuals*, not just the faceless firm, feel the consequences. Clawbacks exist because of a deep asymmetry in how banks pay people: bonuses are paid *now*, on this year's revenue, but the risk a deal created can take years to show up. Without a clawback, a banker can book a giant fee, collect the bonus, and be long gone (or simply keep the cash) by the time the deal blows up. The clawback reaches back through time to claw the reward back to where the harm finally landed. It is, in effect, the bank admitting that its annual bonus cycle is too short to measure whether a deal was actually good — and building a tool to correct the timing mismatch after the fact.

These two tools — the DPA and the clawback — answer two different questions. The DPA answers "how do we punish the firm hard without destroying it and the jobs of thousands of innocent employees?" The clawback answers "how do we make sure the specific people who profited actually feel it?" A resolution that uses only the first risks letting individuals walk away rich while shareholders pay; one that leans on both, as the 1MDB outcome eventually did, spreads the consequence to where the decisions were actually made.

With those seven ideas in hand — sovereign fund, underwriting fee, bribery/FCPA, the gatekeeper role, conduct vs reputational risk, DPA, clawback — the scandal stops being a blur of big numbers and becomes a clear sequence of decisions. Let's walk it.

## The bond deals: three trades that raised 6.5 billion dollars

Between 2012 and 2013, Goldman Sachs arranged three separate bond issues for 1MDB, raising about **\$6.5 billion** in total. The structure of *how* they were done is the first thing a banker would notice as odd.

A normal large bond is sold through a *syndicate* — a group of banks that together build a public order book, taking bids from dozens or hundreds of investors. The process is slow, transparent, and competitive, which keeps fees low and spreads the bonds widely. The 1MDB bonds were done the opposite way. Goldman *privately placed* them: instead of building a public book, the bank bought the bonds onto its own balance sheet and then sold them on. The deals were rushed, done quietly, and structured so that very few people saw the whole transaction at once.

To make bonds from a brand-new, untested Malaysian fund sellable, the deals leaned on *guarantees* — in some cases a guarantee from an Abu Dhabi sovereign entity, which made the bonds look safer than the underlying fund actually was. A guarantee is a third party promising to pay if the borrower can't. It is a legitimate tool. But here it was the wrapper that let weak paper be sold as strong paper — and, as later emerged, securing those arrangements involved bribes and kickbacks.

The three deals were not identical. The first two, done in 2012, each raised about \$1.75 billion and were notionally for buying power-generation assets; both leaned on the Abu Dhabi guarantee to get an investment-grade-style reception. The third, in 2013, raised about \$3 billion and was tied to a planned development project. Add them up — roughly \$1.75bn + \$1.75bn + \$3bn — and you get the **\$6.5 billion** total. What unites them is the *method*: each was a private placement that Goldman warehoused on its own balance sheet first, each was done at speed, and each carried a fee far outside the normal range. Three deals, one template, and the template was the problem.

#### Worked example: the underwriting risk Goldman actually took

A bank will tell you a fat fee compensates the risk of buying the bonds onto its own books before reselling them. Let's test that claim with numbers.

When Goldman privately placed a tranche, it might hold, say, \$1.75 billion of bonds for a short window — days to weeks — before selling them on. The genuine risk in that window is that interest rates move against the bank before it offloads. A *very* bad rate move in a short holding period might be 1%–2% of the bond's value:

- Worst-case mark-to-market hit at 2%: \$1,750,000,000 × 0.02 = **\$35 million** of risk on that tranche.
- The bank also reportedly resold some bonds at a *profit* on top of the fee, so the warehouse was, if anything, a money-maker rather than a pure risk.

Now weigh that against the fee. Across the three deals the fee was about **\$600 million**. Even the alarming \$35 million warehousing risk on a single tranche is a small fraction of the fee.

The intuition: the "we took balance-sheet risk" story does not survive contact with arithmetic. The fee was roughly an order of magnitude larger than any honest warehousing risk could justify. When the stated reason for a price is dwarfed by the price itself, the real reason is somewhere else.

### Why the structure itself was the warning

Step back and look at the shape of the deal, not the details. A trusted, careful issuer wants its bond sold *widely and openly*, because wide distribution gets the lowest borrowing cost. An issuer that wants the opposite — a small number of buyers, a fast private placement, minimal scrutiny, a guarantee papering over the real risk — is an issuer that does not want the deal examined too closely. A banker is trained to ask why. On 1MDB, the bankers who should have asked were instead the ones being paid to push it through.

![Comparison of a normal sovereign bond mandate against the 1MDB deal](/imgs/blogs/the-1mdb-scandal-and-an-investment-banks-role-in-a-heist-3.png)

The figure above lines up a clean mandate against the 1MDB version. Every single feature is inverted: open syndicate becomes secret placement, wide distribution becomes a fast flip, clear use of proceeds becomes money vanishing into "partner" companies, and a tiny fee becomes a gigantic one. When *every* dimension of a deal points the wrong way at once, the deal is not unusual — it is rotten. The structure was telling the truth before anyone confessed.

This is the deep lesson and it ties straight back to the series' spine. A bank is a confidence machine — it survives on the trust of the people who fund it and the integrity of the names it lends its stamp to. To learn how an investment bank is *supposed* to make money from deals like this, see [inside an investment bank: how they make money](/blog/trading/finance/inside-an-investment-bank-how-they-make-money). The 1MDB deals took the legitimate machinery of underwriting and ran it in reverse.

## The outsized fee as a red flag

Now to the number that should have stopped everything: the fee. Goldman's roughly **\$600 million** in revenue on \$6.5 billion of bonds is the single most diagnostic fact in the case. Let me explain *why* a banker — or a regulator, or a board — should have seen it as an alarm, not a triumph.

Fees in finance are not arbitrary. They are competed down. If one bank tries to charge 9% to arrange a plain bond, a rival will offer to do it for 1%, and the issuer will switch. The only ways a fee survives at 9% are: the deal is so risky and hard that no one else will touch it (possible, but then *why is a top bank touching it?*), or the high fee is buying something other than underwriting — silence, speed, a willingness not to ask questions, or a channel through which other payments can flow.

![Goldman fee on the 1MDB bonds compared to normal underwriting fees](/imgs/blogs/the-1mdb-scandal-and-an-investment-banks-role-in-a-heist-2.png)

The chart makes the gap visceral. The four grey-and-amber bars on the left are what a normal-to-aggressive bond fee would have produced on \$6.5 billion — from about \$13 million to \$130 million. The red bar on the right is what Goldman actually kept: about \$600 million. It is not a little high. It is in a different category.

#### Worked example: how far out of line the fee was

Let's quantify "out of line" precisely.

- Goldman's fee rate: \$600m ÷ \$6,500m = **9.2%**.
- A normal sovereign-bond fee: about **0.3%**.
- Ratio: 9.2% ÷ 0.3% = **about 31×**.

So the bank earned roughly **thirty times** the going rate. Put differently: at a normal 0.3% fee, Goldman would have made about \$19.5 million across the three deals. The extra revenue — \$600m − \$19.5m ≈ **\$580 million** — is the "abnormal" portion. There is no honest underwriting service that costs an extra \$580 million.

The intuition: a fee thirty times the market rate is not a price, it is evidence. In a well-run bank, a fee that size on a vanilla bond would itself trigger a control review *before the deal closed*, regardless of who the client was.

### Why fees are a control signal, not just revenue

In a healthy bank, a deal's economics are a *risk signal*, watched by the people whose job is to keep the firm out of trouble. An unusually fat fee should set off the same instinct a fraud analyst gets from a transaction that's ten times a customer's normal spend. The questions write themselves: *Why is the client paying so much? What does the fee actually compensate? Who decided it was acceptable, and did anyone independent check?* The whole point of having a [four-risks framework — credit, market, liquidity, and operational](/blog/trading/banking/the-four-risks-every-bank-runs-credit-market-liquidity-operational) — is that operational and conduct risk are supposed to be *monitored*, not celebrated. On 1MDB, the fee was treated as a win to be booked rather than a question to be answered.

There is a subtle psychology here that makes outsized fees especially dangerous. A normal fee is invisible — it slides through the system because it looks like everything else. An outsized fee is *exciting*: it makes a quarter, it makes careers, it makes the division's numbers. The very thing that should make it suspicious — its size — is the thing that makes everyone want it to be real. This is why a control that depends on a human noticing and objecting tends to fail exactly when the stakes are highest. The fix is to make the flag *mechanical*: a rule that says "any fee above X% of deal size routes automatically to an independent review that the deal team cannot waive". A rule does not get excited. A rule does not have a bonus riding on the answer.

It is worth being precise about what a "normal" fee actually buys, so the gap is unmistakable. On an ordinary bond, the underwriting fee pays for: structuring the deal, the legal and documentation work, the sales effort of finding investors, and the genuine balance-sheet risk of warehousing the bonds for a short window. All of that, on a clean investment-grade deal, comes to a fraction of a percent. There is no honest line item — no amount of structuring, selling, or warehousing — that adds up to nine percent on guaranteed paper. When the fee cannot be explained by the work, the fee is not paying for the work. That single sentence is the whole red flag, and it was visible on day one, in the term sheet, before a dollar was ever stolen.

## The siphoning: where 4.5 billion dollars actually went

Now we follow the money out the back. Of the roughly \$6.5 billion the bonds raised, US prosecutors alleged that about **\$4.5 billion** was misappropriated — diverted away from 1MDB and into private hands. That is the figure in our data and it is staggering: roughly **two-thirds** of everything the fund borrowed.

![Stacked bar showing 4.5 billion misappropriated out of 6.5 billion raised](/imgs/blogs/the-1mdb-scandal-and-an-investment-banks-role-in-a-heist-4.png)

The chart splits the \$6.5 billion into the part that was diverted (red, the bottom block) and the part that reached 1MDB (blue). The red block dwarfs the blue. When a fund borrows \$6.5 billion and only a fraction of it goes to its stated purpose, the money did not "underperform" — it was stolen.

The mechanics of the theft ran through a man named Jho Low, a Malaysian financier with no official position at 1MDB but enormous influence over it. The pattern was the same across the deals: bond proceeds that were supposed to fund power-plant acquisitions and development projects were instead routed to entities with names designed to *sound* like legitimate partners — companies whose names echoed real, reputable firms — but which were in fact shells controlled by Low and his associates. From those shells, the money fanned out: into real estate in New York and London, fine art, a superyacht, jewelry, and famously into financing the very Hollywood film about Wall Street excess.

To see why this worked, you have to understand a *shell company*. A shell company is a legal entity that exists only on paper — it has a name, a bank account, and a registered office, but no real business, no employees, no products. Shells are not inherently illegal; companies use them for legitimate reasons all the time (holding a single asset, structuring a joint venture). What makes them dangerous is that a shell *breaks the chain of visibility*. Money goes into a shell as "payment to a partner" and comes out as "an investment", and unless someone insists on looking *through* the shell to who actually controls it and where the cash ultimately lands, the trail goes cold by design.

The 1MDB scheme chained shells together — money moved from 1MDB to one entity, then to another, then to another, across multiple countries and currencies, each hop adding a layer of distance between the stolen funds and their origin. This is *layering*, the same technique used in money laundering: the more hops, the harder it is for any single bank or investigator to see the whole flow from where they sit. The defense against it is to look at the *whole* path, not one hop — which is exactly the discipline that controls are supposed to enforce and exactly the discipline that failed here.

A useful everyday parallel is a charity that collects donations to dig village wells. Donors give \$100. The charity's treasurer routes \$69 of every \$100 to a "drilling contractor" that is really his cousin's empty company, which buys the treasurer a sports car, while \$31 actually reaches a well. Every donor still believes they funded clean water; the books show "paid to contractor"; only someone who follows the cash all the way to the sports car sees the theft. 1MDB is that scheme at the scale of a nation's borrowing, with global investors as the donors and a sovereign fund as the charity.

#### Worked example: how much of every dollar was looted

Let's make the scale of the diversion concrete with simple fractions.

- Total raised: **\$6.5 billion**.
- Misappropriated: **\$4.5 billion**.
- Share stolen: \$4.5bn ÷ \$6.5bn = **0.692**, or about **69%**.
- Share that reached the fund for its intended use: \$6.5bn − \$4.5bn = **\$2.0 billion**, or about **31%**.

So for every **\$1.00** the fund borrowed and now owed to bondholders, only about **31 cents** ever went toward a real Malaysian asset; about **69 cents** was diverted. The fund — and ultimately Malaysian taxpayers — were left owing the full dollar plus interest.

The intuition: a bond is a debt the issuer must repay in full. When two-thirds of the proceeds are stolen, the country is left carrying the entire \$6.5 billion liability for assets it never received. The theft did not just take money; it loaded a nation with debt for nothing.

This is also why the scandal is a *banking* story and not only a fraud story. The bonds were real obligations sold to real investors, who expected to be repaid. The fund's ability to borrow at all rested on the bank's stamp. The looting was possible *because* the borrowing was made to look legitimate. For the broader anatomy of how dirty money moves through the financial system and why banks are supposed to catch it, the failures of bank controls in cases like this rhyme with the operational and fraud loss-events covered in [operational risk: fraud, cyber, and the loss events](/blog/trading/banking/operational-risk-fraud-cyber-and-the-loss-events).

## The control and gatekeeper failure: who should have stopped it

Here is the part that should keep bank executives awake. The 1MDB deals did not slip through because no controls existed. Goldman, like every major bank, had KYC procedures, a compliance function, and deal committees. The deals went through because those controls were *overridden* — and because the people positioned to override them were the ones who stood to profit.

![Diagram of the bank gatekeepers who should have halted the 1MDB deals](/imgs/blogs/the-1mdb-scandal-and-an-investment-banks-role-in-a-heist-5.png)

Walk the gates in the figure. There were at least three independent points where the deal should have died:

1. **Know-your-customer / client vetting.** The central question — *who is Jho Low, and why does a man with no formal role have this much sway over a sovereign fund's bond deals?* — was exactly the question KYC exists to force. Internal concerns about Low were raised. They were not allowed to stop the deal.
2. **Compliance review.** The fee alone (about 9%) and the private-placement structure should have prompted compliance to dig into the economics and the counterparties. The function that is supposed to be the bank's brakes did not stop the car.
3. **The deal committee.** Senior bankers approve the firm's biggest mandates. A deal this large, this unusual, and this lucrative would have been visible at the top. Approving it meant the people with the most authority chose to accept the most obvious risks.

The design intent of a control system is that gates are *independent* — so that even if one fails, another catches the problem. The failure mode 1MDB exposed is what happens when the gates are not truly independent: when the same incentive (a giant fee, big bonuses, career-defining league-table credit) pulls *everyone* in the same direction, every gate fails in the same way at the same time. This is the deep weakness of control systems — they assume someone in the chain is willing to say no.

There is a useful way to think about *why* shared incentives defeat layered controls. Engineers call it *common-mode failure*: if you put two safety systems in a building but power them from the same circuit, a single outage takes out both, and the "redundancy" was an illusion. Layered bank controls have the same trap. On paper there are three independent gates; in practice, if all three answer to the same profit-and-loss line and the same bonus pool, a deal lucrative enough to swamp everyone's judgment is a single failure that knocks out all three at once. The redundancy is only real if at least one gate is wired to a *different* incentive — a compliance officer whose bonus does not depend on the deal closing, a risk committee that loses nothing by saying no. 1MDB is the textbook case of redundancy that wasn't, because every gate was on the same circuit.

#### Worked example: the incentive gap that bent the gates

To see how a fee bends a control system, put rough numbers on the incentives.

- Deal fee to the firm: about **\$600 million**.
- A meaningful slice of that flows to the bankers and their division as bonus and league-table credit — say, conservatively, **10%** of revenue translating into compensation and franchise value the team cares about: \$600m × 0.10 = **\$60 million** of personal-and-team upside riding on the deal closing.
- A compliance officer's annual pay might be on the order of **\$0.3 million**. Blocking the deal risks their job and their standing; waving it through risks, in their mind at the time, nothing.

Line those up: \$60 million of upside pulling the deal *forward* versus an individual gatekeeper with \$0.3 million of pay and a career to protect being asked to stand in front of it alone. The ratio is about **200:1** in favor of the deal proceeding.

The intuition: a control is only as strong as the incentive backing the person who must enforce it. When the reward for "yes" is two hundred times the reward for "no", the gate is decorative — and fixing that means changing the incentives, not adding another procedure.

### The three-lines-of-defense, and how it broke

Banks describe their control architecture as *three lines of defense*. The first line is the business itself (the bankers doing the deal). The second line is risk and compliance (the people whose only job is to check the first line). The third line is internal audit (who check that the first two are working). The model only works if the second line is genuinely empowered to overrule the first.

On 1MDB, the first line — the deal team chasing the fee — captured the process. The second line's concerns were managed, sidelined, or simply not escalated. The lesson regulators drew is that controls are only as strong as the *culture* that lets them bite. A compliance officer who knows that blocking a \$600 million fee will end their career is not really a control at all. To see how supervisors try to force this culture from the outside, through stress tests and supervisory exams, see [stress testing, CCAR, the supervisory exam, and living wills](/blog/trading/banking/stress-testing-ccar-the-supervisory-exam-and-living-wills).

## The investigations: how the heist unraveled

A theft this large does not stay hidden, because the money has to *go* somewhere, and big movements leave tracks. The unraveling came from several directions at once between 2015 and 2018.

Journalists and Malaysian whistleblowers began documenting unexplained billions and 1MDB's mounting, unrepayable debts. Investigators in the United States, where much of the stolen money had bought property and assets, opened a sprawling probe. The US Department of Justice filed civil *asset-forfeiture* complaints — legal actions to seize property bought with stolen money — that read like an inventory of excess: luxury apartments, a private jet, a yacht, Picassos, the rights to a major film. Each seized asset was a thread that, pulled, led back to the diverted bond proceeds.

Then, in 2018, Malaysian politics turned. A new government took power explicitly campaigning against the 1MDB looting, and it began pursuing both the individuals and the bank that had enabled the deals. The former prime minister was eventually convicted and imprisoned in connection with the scandal. With the country that had been robbed now actively cooperating, the pressure on the bank that arranged the bonds became impossible to manage quietly.

It is worth pausing on *why* a fraud this large took years to surface rather than days. Three forces protected it. First, the *layering* through shells across many jurisdictions meant no single bank, regulator, or country could see the whole flow — each saw only its slice. Second, the deals carried the apparent blessing of a top-tier bank and a sovereign guarantee, so the early presumption was that everything was legitimate; reputation is a shield that cuts both ways. Third, the people in the best position to raise the alarm were either profiting or politically protected. A fraud does not need to be clever to last; it needs to sit in the blind spot between institutions that each assume someone else is watching.

The unraveling, when it came, followed the cash. Because stolen money has to be *spent* to be enjoyed, and spending leaves records — a property deed, an auction receipt, a wire to a film studio — the assets themselves became the evidence. This is the quiet truth of financial forensics: you rarely catch a fraud by reading the fraudster's mind; you catch it by reconstructing where the money physically went and noticing that it does not match where it was supposed to go.

#### Worked example: the debt left behind versus the assets received

Investigators kept returning to one ugly arithmetic, the gap between what 1MDB *owed* and what it *had*.

- Bond debt created: about **\$6.5 billion** (plus years of interest the fund had to service).
- Productive assets actually acquired with the proceeds: only the roughly **\$2.0 billion** that wasn't diverted, and even some of that was tied up in troubled deals.
- The shortfall the public ultimately had to cover: on the order of **\$4.5 billion** of value that simply was not there.

The intuition: forensic investigators don't need a confession to prove a fraud — they reconstruct it from the *gap* between liabilities and assets. When a fund owes \$6.5 billion and can point to perhaps \$2 billion of real value, the missing \$4.5 billion is the crime, written in the balance sheet itself.

## The settlements: 2.9 billion in the US, 3.9 billion to Malaysia

The resolution came in 2020. It had two main pieces, and our data gives the figures for both.

First, the **US settlement**: a resolution with the Department of Justice (and parallel actions by other US regulators) of about **\$2.9 billion**, tied to the FCPA bribery charges. As part of this, a Goldman subsidiary in Malaysia pleaded guilty to a criminal conspiracy charge while the parent firm entered a deferred prosecution agreement — the DPA we defined earlier. This was historic: it was the first time the main Goldman Sachs Group entity faced this kind of resolution and one of the largest FCPA penalties ever.

Second, the **Malaysia settlement**: a separate agreement with the Malaysian government of about **\$3.9 billion** (a cash payment plus a guarantee to help recover assets), to compensate the country that had been robbed.

![Horizontal bars of the 2.9 billion US settlement and 3.9 billion Malaysia settlement](/imgs/blogs/the-1mdb-scandal-and-an-investment-banks-role-in-a-heist-6.png)

The chart shows the two side by side: the US DOJ resolution at about \$2.9 billion and the Malaysia settlement at about \$3.9 billion. Together they form the headline bill.

#### Worked example: adding up what the bank paid

Let's total the two main settlements and see the bill in context.

- US resolution: **\$2.9 billion**.
- Malaysia settlement: **\$3.9 billion**.
- Combined: \$2.9bn + \$3.9bn = **\$6.8 billion**.

Now compare that to the fee that started it all: Goldman kept about **\$600 million** in revenue on the deals. The settlements came to \$6.8bn ÷ \$0.6bn ≈ **more than 11 times** the fee earned. And that is *before* legal costs, clawed-back pay, lost business, and years of damaged reputation.

The intuition: the economics of misconduct are brutal in hindsight. A fee that looked like a \$600 million win turned into an \$6.8 billion loss — the bank paid back more than eleven dollars for every dollar of fee it booked. There is no version of this deal where the conduct paid off for the firm.

The settlement figures here are consistent with the broader pattern of conduct fines that banks have paid over the past fifteen years. To see where 1MDB sits among the great banking scandals — LIBOR rigging, fake-account schemes, money-laundering failures — note that the global Goldman 1MDB resolution (around \$5 billion in fines and disgorgement before counting the separate Malaysia compensation) ranks among the very largest single-bank conduct penalties on record.

It helps to understand *why* the bill was so large relative to the fee, because the gap is not an accident — it is by design. Regulators set conduct penalties to be *deterrent*, which means they must exceed the profit from the misconduct by a wide margin. If a bank could pay back exactly what it earned plus a small markup, misconduct would be a rational bet: do ten such deals, get caught on three, still come out ahead. To kill that math, penalties are engineered so that *getting caught even once* wipes out the gains from many clean deals. The 1MDB outcome — paying back more than eleven times the fee — is the deterrent principle working as intended. The lesson a banker should draw is not "we were unlucky to get caught" but "the expected value of this kind of deal is deeply negative once you weight it by the penalty, and it was negative before we ever did it".

There is also a *disgorgement* component worth naming. Disgorgement means handing back ill-gotten gains — the regulator forces the firm to give up the profit it should never have made. Part of the US resolution functioned this way: not only a punitive fine, but a clawback of the abnormal fee itself. So the roughly \$580 million of "abnormal" fee we computed earlier did not just evaporate in reputational damage; a chunk of it was explicitly disgorged. The system's logic is to make sure the firm ends up *worse off* than if it had never touched the deal — otherwise the deterrent fails.

## The clawbacks: making individuals pay

A firm paying a fine is not, by itself, accountability — shareholders pay the fine, and many of them had nothing to do with the deal. Real accountability means the people who *did* the deal, and the leaders who oversaw them, feel it personally. This is where clawbacks come in.

Goldman moved to reclaim and withhold a meaningful chunk of executive pay tied to the period of the scandal — on the order of **\$174 million** in compensation reduced, forfeited, or clawed back. This reached the most senior level: the firm cut the realized pay of leaders associated with the era, and current top executives gave up a portion of their own compensation to share in the consequence.

![Pipeline of the clawback and accountability aftermath of the 1MDB scandal](/imgs/blogs/the-1mdb-scandal-and-an-investment-banks-role-in-a-heist-8.png)

The figure traces the aftermath as a chain: the settlements paid, then pay clawed back, then leaders' compensation cut, then individual bankers charged, and finally controls tightened. Crucially, the consequences did not stop at money. Individual bankers faced criminal cases: one senior banker pleaded guilty and cooperated, and another was convicted at trial in the United States and sentenced to prison.

#### Worked example: the clawback as a fraction of the fee

Let's size the clawback against the deal's economics.

- Pay reclaimed and withheld: about **\$174 million**.
- Fee the deals earned: about **\$600 million**.
- Clawback as a share of the fee: \$174m ÷ \$600m = **about 29%**.

So the firm reclaimed roughly **29 cents of every fee dollar** from the executives associated with the period. That is far from the whole fee, and a tiny fraction of the \$6.8 billion the firm paid in settlements — but it is the mechanism that put a personal price on the misconduct rather than letting it be a faceless corporate cost.

The intuition: a fine alone teaches a firm nothing if the people who made the decisions keep their bonuses. Clawbacks exist to break the asymmetry where bankers keep the upside of a bad deal and shareholders eat the downside. Reclaiming about a third of the fee is partial — but it is the difference between punishing "the bank" and punishing the bankers.

## Common misconceptions

**"Goldman was just the broker — it didn't steal the money, so it's barely involved."** Wrong, and this is the most important misconception to clear. The bank was the *gatekeeper*. Its stamp is what let an obscure fund borrow \$6.5 billion from cautious global investors. A Goldman subsidiary pleaded *guilty* to a criminal conspiracy, and the firm paid about \$2.9 billion in the US alone. You do not pay billions and plead guilty for being an innocent middleman. The bank's role was load-bearing: without the credible underwriter, the heist could not have been funded.

**"A 9% fee just means it was a hard, risky deal."** A fee can be high for legitimate reasons — but 9% on a *guaranteed* sovereign-linked bond is not in the same universe as a hard deal. Recall the math: that is about 31× a normal 0.3% fee. A genuinely risky junk deal might pay 2%–3%. A fee at 9% on paper that was *guaranteed* (made to look safe) is internally contradictory: if it were truly that risky, the guarantee wouldn't make sense; if it were that safe, the fee makes no sense. The contradiction *was* the warning.

**"The controls failed because the bank didn't have any."** No — the bank had KYC, compliance, and deal committees, like every major bank. The controls failed because they were *overridden* by people who profited from the deal, and because concerns raised by the second line were not allowed to stop the first line. The failure was of *culture and independence*, not of the existence of procedures. This is why "we have a compliance department" is never, by itself, evidence that a bank is safe.

**"The settlements made everyone whole, so the system worked."** Partly, and only partly. Malaysia recovered a great deal through the \$3.9 billion settlement and asset seizures, and individuals went to prison. But about \$4.5 billion was diverted, recovery is never complete, the country carried years of debt and political crisis, and a generation of trust was destroyed. "The system caught it eventually, after the money was spent" is a far weaker claim than "the system stopped it." The gatekeeper is supposed to prevent, not just to pay damages afterward.

**"This is a one-off — a rogue scheme that couldn't happen again."** The specific cast of characters was unusual, but the *structure* of the failure is utterly generic: an outsized fee, a rushed and opaque deal, concerns that were managed rather than escalated, and incentives that pulled every gate the same way. Those ingredients exist in every bank, every year. The 1MDB pattern is a permanent risk, not a historical curiosity — which is exactly why it's worth studying.

## How it shows up in real banks

The 1MDB scandal is one episode, but the mechanisms it exposed appear over and over. Here is how the lessons actually surface inside real banks and real deals.

**Why a fee that high should trip controls — automatically.** After 1MDB, "fee outlier" became a recognized control trigger. A well-run bank now treats a fee that is far above the market rate for a deal type the same way an AML system treats a transaction far above a customer's normal pattern: as something that must be *explained and independently approved* before it closes, not booked as a win. The principle is general — abnormal economics are a risk signal. If a corporate loan carries a spread three times the borrower's risk profile justifies, that is also a flag; the syndication and pricing discipline that governs ordinary lending is covered in [corporate and commercial lending: term loans, revolvers, and syndication](/blog/trading/banking/corporate-and-commercial-lending-term-loans-revolvers-and-syndication). The unifying idea: when the price is weird, ask why before you take the money.

**Gatekeeper accountability and the named, empowered owner.** A recurring fix after conduct scandals is to make a *specific senior person accountable* for each control gate, by name, with the power to stop a deal and the protection to do so without career suicide. The United Kingdom's Senior Managers Regime, introduced after a wave of scandals, is the clearest example: it pins regulatory responsibility on named individuals so that "the system failed" can no longer hide who failed. 1MDB is the case study that made boards take this seriously, because it showed that diffuse responsibility means *no* responsibility.

**Conduct, legal, and reputational risk arrive together.** The figure below maps how a single bad mandate lights up three different risks at once. The conduct breach (bankers paying bribes and hiding flags) became legal risk (FCPA charges, a guilty plea, about \$6.8 billion in settlements) which became reputational risk (years of headlines, lost mandates, tighter oversight). A bank that thinks of these as separate departments misses the point: they are three faces of one event.

![Matrix of conduct, legal, and reputational risk triggered by the 1MDB deal](/imgs/blogs/the-1mdb-scandal-and-an-investment-banks-role-in-a-heist-7.png)

**The "name-alike" counterparty trap.** Part of how the money was diverted was through entities deliberately named to resemble real, reputable firms — so a quick glance at a payment instruction looked legitimate. Real banks now train staff and tune systems to catch exactly this: payees whose names are *close to* a known entity but not the same. It is a small, concrete control that traces directly back to how 1MDB moved money.

**The cost of a reputation, quantified.** Goldman's experience put a number on reputational risk that boards had previously treated as vague. About \$6.8 billion in settlements, \$174 million in clawed-back pay, criminal cases against employees, and years as a byword for a heist — all stemming from deals that earned about \$600 million. After 1MDB, "is this fee worth the reputational risk?" became a question with a real, terrifying denominator. Studying the great failures — from [Lehman Brothers in 2008](/blog/trading/banking/lehman-brothers-2008-leverage-repo-105-and-the-run-on-an-investment-bank) to conduct scandals like this one — is how risk managers calibrate just how expensive a single bad decision can be.

**The "tone from the top" became a measurable thing.** Before 1MDB, "culture" and "tone from the top" were soft phrases that audit reports gestured at. After it, banks started building *hard* tests of whether the second line can actually win an argument with a star revenue producer: tracking how many deals compliance escalated, how many it stopped, whether escalations were ever quietly reversed, and whether the people who raised concerns were later promoted or sidelined. The thinking is simple — if no deal in three years was ever killed by compliance, that is not evidence of clean business; it is evidence the brakes are not connected. A control function that never says no to a big fee is, statistically, a control function that *can't*.

**Why "the client wanted it that way" is not a defense.** A recurring excuse in conduct cases is that the client requested the unusual structure — the private placement, the speed, the secrecy. But a gatekeeper's whole job is to apply the bank's own standards regardless of what the client wants; a client preferring opacity is a reason for *more* scrutiny, not less. Real banks now train staff that client pressure to skip steps is itself a red flag to be logged and escalated, not a reason to skip the steps. The 1MDB deals were exactly what a client wants when the client is being looted by the people arranging them.

## The takeaway: a bank's real product is trust, and 1MDB shows what it costs to spend it

Strip away the yacht and the Hollywood film and the political drama, and the 1MDB scandal teaches one durable thing about how a bank lives or dies: **an investment bank's most valuable asset is not its balance sheet, it is its credibility as a gatekeeper — and that asset can be spent for a fee, but it cannot be bought back at any price.**

Run the logic back through the series' spine. A bank survives on confidence — depositors' confidence that their money is safe, investors' confidence that the bank's stamp means something. The whole machine of underwriting works because the world believes that when a respected bank arranges a bond, it has *checked*. The moment that belief is false — the moment the stamp is for sale — the bank has quietly sold the one thing that makes it a bank rather than a sales desk. 1MDB is what it looks like when that sale becomes public: about \$600 million booked as revenue, about \$6.8 billion paid back, careers ended, a name permanently marked.

So here is how to *use* this, whether you are reading about a bank or running one. When you see an unusually large fee, do not read it as a sign of a great deal — read it as a question the bank should have answered. When you hear "we have robust controls," ask the only question that matters: *is the person at the second-line gate empowered and protected enough to kill a \$600 million deal?* If the answer is no, the controls are decoration. And when a deal's structure is secretive, rushed, and guaranteed all at once, trust the structure over the reassurances — the shape of a deal tells the truth before anyone confesses.

The deepest lesson is the cheapest to state and the hardest to live: the gatekeeper's job is to be willing to say no to money. A bank that cannot say no to a fee that is thirty times the market rate is not managing conduct risk — it is one deal away from being the next case study.

And notice how cleanly the whole episode collapses back into the series' one idea. A bank is a machine that runs on confidence: depositors trust it with their savings, investors trust its stamp on a deal, a country trusts it with its sovereign fund. Every function we have studied — capital, liquidity, risk controls, the three lines of defense — exists to protect that trust, because the moment it breaks, the machine seizes. 1MDB did not break Goldman the way a bank run broke Northern Rock or leverage broke Lehman; the firm was far too big and too well-capitalized to fail over \$6.8 billion. What it broke was something a balance sheet cannot show: the unquestioned belief that when this bank says a deal is sound, it is. That belief was the real product, the fee was the price of spending it, and the settlements were the cost of finding out that the price was far, far too low. Read every future scandal through that lens — *what trust was sold, for how much, and what did getting caught cost?* — and the headline numbers stop being shocking and start being instructive.
