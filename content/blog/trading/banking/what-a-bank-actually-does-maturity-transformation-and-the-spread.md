> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A bank is a machine that borrows money you can take back tomorrow and lends it out for years, pocketing the gap between the two interest rates. That gap is the whole business.
>
> - A bank **borrows short** (your deposit, repayable on demand) and **lends long** (a 30-year mortgage). The difference in maturities is the trick, and the difference in interest rates — the *spread* — is the pay.
> - This is genuinely useful: it lets society finance long, illiquid things (houses, factories) with short, liquid money, and it lets you keep cash that quietly earns interest.
> - It is also structurally fragile. A bank can never give every depositor their money back at once, because the money is out on long loans. It survives only on **confidence**.
> - The bank funds about \$92 of every \$100 of assets with other people's money and just \$8 with its own. An 8% loss on its assets wipes out the owners.
> - **The one number to remember:** the spread. In 2024, US banks earned a net interest margin of about **3.2%** on their assets — the average gap between what they charged borrowers and paid depositors. Every later topic in this series — capital, runs, regulation, the great failures — traces back to that one number and that one trade.

In March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank was, by most measures, a normal mid-sized American bank. It had taken in about \$175 billion of deposits, mostly from technology startups and venture funds, and parked a large chunk of that money in US government bonds — the safest assets on Earth. There was no fraud, no reckless lending, no exotic derivatives. And yet, over roughly 36 hours on March 9th and 10th, it died.

What happened was almost embarrassingly simple, and it is the entire subject of this post. The bank had borrowed *short* — its deposits could be withdrawn at any moment — and invested *long*, in bonds that would not mature for years. When interest rates rose sharply in 2022, those long bonds fell in value. Depositors, who talk to each other on Slack and Twitter far faster than any 1930s queue formed outside a branch, noticed. On March 9th, customers tried to pull out \$42 billion. A further \$100 billion or so was queued to leave the next morning. No bank on Earth keeps \$142 billion of cash lying around — because if it did, it would not be a bank, it would be a vault. The money was out on long-dated bonds and loans. By Friday afternoon, regulators had seized it.

SVB was not killed by a bad business. It was killed by the *normal* business, run in the open. To understand why a perfectly ordinary bank can vanish in a day and a half — and why, despite that fragility, we keep building banks and trusting them with our paychecks — you have to understand the single trade at the heart of every bank that has ever existed. It is called **maturity transformation**, and once you see it, you will never read a banking headline the same way again.

![Pipeline showing deposits flowing into a bank that pays a low rate, lends the money out at a higher rate, and keeps the difference as the spread](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for this entire series. Money comes in cheap and short (a deposit you can withdraw any day), goes out dear and long (a loan locked up for years), and the bank lives on the gap between the two rates. Every single thing a bank does — every product, every risk control, every regulator's rule, every famous collapse — is a consequence of, or a defense against, that one move. Let us build it up from absolute zero.

## Foundations: bank, deposit, loan, spread, and the words you need

Before we go anywhere, let us define every term we will use, starting from a position of knowing nothing about finance. If you already know these, skim; if you do not, none of the rest will make sense without them.

A **bank** is a business whose product is *other people's money*. Most businesses make a thing or provide a service and charge for it. A bank's raw material, its inventory, and its product are all the same substance — money — and its skill is in moving money through time and across people. Here is the concrete version. Take the corner shop on your street. Every evening the till is full of cash from the day's sales. Now suppose the shopkeeper, instead of locking that cash in a safe overnight, lends it to the bakery next door, which pays it back with a little extra in the morning. The shopkeeper has just done the core of banking: taken money that is sitting idle and put it to work earning a return, while still being ready to make change for customers. A real bank does this at industrial scale, with millions of "tills" — your savings account, your neighbor's, a company's payroll account — pooled together and lent out.

A **deposit** is money you give the bank that the bank owes back to you. This is the part beginners most often get backwards, so sit with it: when you "put money in the bank," you are not storing it in a box with your name on it. You are *lending it to the bank*. Your bank balance is the bank's IOU to you — a promise to pay you that amount whenever you ask. Legally and economically, a depositor is a lender to the bank, and an unusually generous one, because you typically lend at a very low interest rate and let the bank repay you the instant you demand it. To the bank, your deposit is a *liability* — something it owes.

A **loan** is money the bank gives someone else that they owe back to the bank, with interest. A mortgage, a car loan, a business credit line, the balance on your credit card — these are all loans the bank has made. To the bank, a loan is an *asset* — something owed *to* it, something it owns. The borrower pays interest on the loan, and that interest is where the bank's revenue comes from.

Now the key move. The **interest rate** is the price of money over time, quoted as a percentage per year. If you borrow \$100 at 5% for one year, you pay back \$105: the extra \$5 is the interest, the rent on the money. Banks pay one interest rate on the money they borrow (your deposits) and charge a *higher* interest rate on the money they lend (the loans). The gap between those two rates is the **spread**. A spread is usually measured in *basis points* — a basis point is one hundredth of one percent, written "bps," so 100 basis points equals 1%, and a spread of 5 percentage points is 500 basis points. The spread is the bank's gross margin on the core business. Squeeze it and the bank earns less; widen it and it earns more.

**Maturity** is how long until a financial promise comes due. A deposit you can withdraw today has a maturity of essentially zero — it is due *now*, on demand. A 30-year mortgage has a maturity of 30 years. **Maturity transformation** is the bank's signature act: it borrows at short maturity (deposits, due now) and lends at long maturity (loans, due in years). It *transforms* short-term money into long-term money. This is genuinely valuable to society. You want your savings instantly available, but the economy needs someone to finance a house that takes 30 years to pay off. The bank stands in the middle and reconciles those two incompatible wishes — but, as we will see, only by taking on a risk it can never fully eliminate.

Two more words, and they are the two that decide whether a bank lives or dies. **Liquidity** is the ability to get cash *right now* without taking a loss. Cash is perfectly liquid; a house is illiquid (you cannot sell it this afternoon at full price). **Solvency** is whether your assets are worth more than what you owe — whether, given enough time to sell everything calmly, you would have money left over. These are different problems. A bank can be perfectly solvent (its loans are good, worth more than its deposits) and still die for lack of liquidity (it cannot turn those good-but-long loans into cash fast enough to pay fleeing depositors). Holding these two ideas apart is the single most important habit in reading a bank. We will return to it constantly.

#### Worked example: the spread on one dollar of deposits

Suppose you deposit \$1,000 in a bank that pays you 1% per year on savings. After one year the bank owes you \$1,000 × 1.01 = \$1,010 — it has paid you \$10 in interest. The bank takes your \$1,000 and lends it as part of a mortgage charging 6% per year. That mortgage pays the bank \$1,000 × 0.06 = \$60 in interest over the year. The bank collected \$60, paid you \$10, and kept \$60 − \$10 = \$50. That \$50 is the spread — 6% minus 1% equals 5%, or 500 basis points, on your \$1,000. **The bank's core profit is simply the rent it charges borrowers minus the rent it pays savers, on every dollar that passes through it.**

## The trick: borrow short, lend long, keep the difference

Now that the vocabulary is in place, look again at the machine. A bank does three things in sequence, and the whole institution is built to do them at scale, safely, profitably, and forever.

First, it **gathers deposits** — the cheapest money in the world. Why so cheap? Because depositors are not really chasing yield; they want safety and convenience. You keep money in a checking account that pays almost nothing because you want to be able to pay rent, swipe a card, and trust that the balance will be there. The bank exploits this preference. It offers you safety and instant access, and in exchange you accept an interest rate far below what the bank can earn lending the money out. Cheap, sticky deposits are the entire franchise of a bank — the thing that makes one bank worth more than another. We will spend a whole post later in this series on why a low-cost deposit base is the prize every bank fights for.

Second, it **deploys that money into longer, higher-yielding assets** — mostly loans, plus some securities (bonds the bank buys, which are just loans to governments and companies in tradable form). These pay more interest precisely because they are longer and riskier. A 30-year mortgage pays more than an overnight deposit because the lender is locking money up for decades and bearing the risk the borrower stops paying.

Third, it **keeps the spread** and, after covering its costs and its loan losses, that spread is the profit. This is *net interest income* — the difference between all the interest a bank earns and all the interest it pays. For a typical commercial bank, net interest income is the largest single source of revenue, dwarfing fees. The whole apparatus of branches, apps, risk officers, and regulators exists to protect and optimize that one stream.

Here is the trick stated plainly: **the spread exists because the maturities differ.** Short money is cheap; long money is dear. By borrowing short and lending long, the bank captures the difference between short and long interest rates — what economists call the *term premium*. In normal times, longer-term interest rates are higher than short-term ones (a relationship called an *upward-sloping yield curve*), and the bank earns money simply by sitting in the middle of that curve. It is, in a sense, paid to be the bridge between savers who want their money now and borrowers who want it for years.

But — and this is the hinge of everything — the same feature that creates the profit creates the danger. Because the bank's funding (deposits) can leave faster than its assets (loans) can be turned into cash, there is a permanent, irreducible *mismatch*. The bank's promises to depositors come due before its claims on borrowers come due. On any normal day, this is invisible, because only a small fraction of depositors want their money at once and new deposits flow in to replace those that leave. But the mismatch never goes away. It is the crack in the foundation that every bank is built on, and it is why a bank is the only kind of business that can be killed by its own customers simply choosing, all at once, to take back what is theirs.

#### Worked example: the spread scaled to a real bank

Imagine a small bank with \$10 billion of assets, funded by \$9 billion of deposits, \$0.2 billion of other borrowing, and \$0.8 billion of its own equity. It earns 5.5% on its \$10 billion of loans and securities, which is \$550 million of interest income. It pays 1.2% on its \$9 billion of deposits and a bit more on its other borrowing, costing it roughly \$120 million in interest expense. Its net interest income is about \$550 million − \$130 million ≈ \$420 million. Spread that over \$10 billion of assets and you get a net interest margin of roughly 4.2%. Out of that \$420 million the bank must pay its staff, its buildings, its technology, and set aside money for loans that go bad — but the spread is where it all starts. **A bank's profit and loss statement begins with one line: the interest it earns minus the interest it pays, and everything else is subtraction from there.**

## Why the bank's balance sheet is the inverse of yours

To see how thin the margin for error is, we need to look at the bank's *balance sheet* — the snapshot of everything it owns and owes. A balance sheet has two sides that always equal each other: **assets** (what you own) on one side, and **liabilities** (what you owe) plus **equity** (the owners' stake) on the other. Assets = liabilities + equity, always, by construction. Equity is just the plug: it is what is left for the owners after you subtract everything owed from everything owned.

Here is the part that surprises everyone. Your personal balance sheet and the bank's are mirror images. To you, your savings account is an *asset* — money you own. To the bank, that exact same account is a *liability* — money it owes you. When you take out a mortgage, the loan is a *liability* to you (you owe it) but an *asset* to the bank (it owns the right to your payments). The bank's balance sheet is the photographic negative of its customers'. This is not a trick of accounting; it is the literal mechanism by which a bank stands between savers and borrowers. The bank's assets are the public's debts, and the bank's debts are the public's savings.

![Balance sheet matrix showing bank assets, liabilities, and a thin sliver of equity, with the note that a four percent asset fall wipes out the owners](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-2.png)

Now study the proportions in the figure, because they are the most important numbers in all of banking. A typical large bank funds itself roughly like this: about 71% deposits, 10% wholesale and repo borrowing, 7% long-term debt, 4% other liabilities, and — here it is — only about **8% equity**. The owners of the bank have put in just \$8 of every \$100 on the balance sheet. The other \$92 is borrowed: from depositors, from bondholders, from other banks. On the asset side, that \$100 is held as roughly 52% loans, 22% securities, 13% cash and reserves, and 13% trading and other assets.

The thinness of that equity sliver is the whole game. Equity is the bank's shock absorber — the buffer that takes the first losses before depositors and creditors are touched. If 8% of the bank's assets turn out to be worthless — a wave of loan defaults, a fall in the value of its bonds — the entire equity cushion is gone and the bank is *insolvent*: it now owes more than it owns. A business funded with 8% equity is a business that can be destroyed by an 8% mistake. Compare that to a normal company with, say, 50% equity, which could lose half its assets before its owners were wiped out. A bank operates with a margin of error most businesses would find suicidal — and it does so deliberately, because that thin equity is exactly what makes the returns on the spread attractive to shareholders. We will see how that math works in a moment.

![Two columns showing bank assets are long and illiquid while funding is short and callable, with equity a four percent green sliver](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-6.png)

The figure above puts the two sides next to each other so the central tension is visible at a glance. The left column (assets) is dominated by loans and securities — long-dated, illiquid, hard to sell quickly at full price. The right column (funding) is dominated by deposits — short-dated, callable, mostly payable on demand. The two columns are the same height — the balance sheet balances — but the *maturities* on the two sides are wildly mismatched. Long, sticky assets are funded by short, runnable money, and only an 8% green sliver of equity stands between the two. Burn that sliver and the bank is gone. Hold this image in your head; it is the skeleton under every chart and case study that follows.

#### Worked example: how an 8% loss wipes out the owners

Take a bank with \$100 billion of assets, \$92 billion of liabilities (mostly deposits), and \$8 billion of equity. Suppose a recession hits and 8% of its assets — \$8 billion worth of loans and bonds — become worthless or have to be sold at a loss. The asset side falls to \$92 billion. The liabilities are unchanged at \$92 billion: the bank still owes depositors and bondholders every cent. So equity = assets − liabilities = \$92 billion − \$92 billion = \$0. The owners have been completely wiped out by a loss of just 8 cents on the dollar. If the loss were 9%, the bank would owe \$1 billion *more* than it owns — it would be insolvent, and someone (depositors, the deposit-insurance fund, taxpayers) would eat the difference. **The flip side of a bank's high returns is that a small percentage loss, magnified by thin equity, can erase the owners entirely.**

## Leverage: the amplifier that makes 1% become 15%

Why on Earth would anyone run a business with only 8% equity, knowing an 8% mistake ends it? Because that same thinness is what turns a modest return on assets into an attractive return for shareholders. The mechanism is *leverage*, and it is worth slowing down to make it concrete, because leverage is the reason banking is profitable, the reason it is fragile, and the reason regulators spend their lives policing it.

**Leverage** is the use of borrowed money to amplify returns. If a bank holds \$100 of assets on \$8 of equity, its *leverage* is \$100 / \$8 = 12.5 times. (Banks and regulators often quote it the other way around, as an equity-to-assets ratio of 8%, or measure it on risk-adjusted assets, but the intuition is the same: a small base of own-money is supporting a large pile of assets.) Leverage cuts both ways with brutal symmetry. A 1% gain on \$100 of assets is \$1, which is a 12.5% return on the \$8 of equity. A 1% *loss* on \$100 of assets is also \$1 — a 12.5% loss on the equity. The same multiple that turns a thin asset return into a fat equity return turns a thin asset loss into a fat equity loss.

This is the deal the banking business model offers shareholders: take a business that earns only about 1% on its assets — banking is a low-margin business in asset terms — and lever it up roughly 10 to 12 times (on a risk-weighted basis) to produce a 10–15% return on equity. The famous rule of thumb is "**1% ROA, 15% ROE**": a well-run bank earns about one percent return on its *assets* but, because those assets are stacked on a thin layer of equity, that translates into a low-to-mid teens return on its *equity*. The leverage identity makes it explicit: return on equity = return on assets × leverage. Earn 1% on assets, multiply by leverage of 12, and you get a 12% return on equity. The whole appeal of bank shares is built on that multiplication — and so is the whole danger.

#### Worked example: leverage turning 1% into 12%

A bank has \$120 billion of assets funded by \$110 billion of liabilities and \$10 billion of equity, so its leverage is \$120 / \$10 = 12 times. In a good year it earns a 1% return on assets: 1% × \$120 billion = \$1.2 billion of profit. As a return on the \$10 billion of equity, that is \$1.2 / \$10 = 12%. Shareholders are delighted: the bank earned a thin 1% on what it manages, but they got 12% on what they put in, because the bank ran their \$10 billion as a \$120 billion book. Now run the bad year: a 1% *loss* on assets is \$1.2 billion, which is a 12% hit to equity, dropping it to \$8.8 billion. A 4% loss on assets — \$4.8 billion — would cut equity nearly in half and put the bank in regulatory danger. **Leverage is the engine that makes a low-margin business worth owning, and the fuse that makes a bad year an existential one.**

## Liquidity versus solvency: the two ways a bank dies

We now arrive at the distinction that explains nearly every bank failure in history, including SVB. A bank can die two different ways, and they feel the same from the outside but have opposite causes.

A bank dies of **insolvency** when its assets are genuinely worth less than its liabilities — its loans went bad, the equity cushion is exhausted, and even if you gave it all the time in the world to sell everything calmly, it could not repay what it owes. This is a problem of *value*. The bank made bad bets.

A bank dies of **illiquidity** when it cannot convert its assets into cash fast enough to meet withdrawals — even if those assets are perfectly good and worth more than its liabilities. This is a problem of *timing*. The bank made fine bets, but its money is locked up in long loans and bonds, and it cannot raise cash quickly enough when depositors all want out at once. The assets are good; they are just not available *today*.

Here is the cruel twist: illiquidity can *cause* insolvency. A bank facing a run is forced to sell its long assets immediately, at whatever price it can get — a *fire sale*. Bonds that would have paid back \$100 at maturity might fetch only \$85 if you must sell them this afternoon. So a liquidity problem (need cash now) forces losses (sell at a discount) that can turn a solvent bank into an insolvent one. The run does not just reveal weakness; it *manufactures* it. This is the mechanism by which a healthy bank can be talked into death by a panic. We will dedicate an entire later post to the formal model of this — the Diamond–Dybvig model — but the intuition is already in your hands: a bank's assets are long and its liabilities are short, so a sudden demand for cash forces it to sell long assets early, and selling long assets early loses money.

#### Worked example: a fire sale turning solvent into insolvent

A bank has \$100 of assets (worth \$100 if held to maturity), \$96 of deposits, and \$4 of equity. It is solvent: \$100 > \$96. Now a run begins, and \$30 of depositors demand cash today. The bank has only \$13 of cash on hand. To raise the missing \$17, it must sell long bonds *immediately* into a falling market, where they fetch only 85 cents on the dollar. To net \$17 of cash it must sell bonds with a face value of \$17 / 0.85 = \$20, taking a \$3 loss in the process. After the sale, assets have fallen by that \$3 loss to \$97, while equity has fallen to \$4 − \$3 = \$1. If the run continues and more fire sales follow, those losses keep eating the \$1 of remaining equity until it is gone — at which point the bank, which started the day solvent, is now insolvent purely because it was forced to sell good assets at bad prices. **A run does not wait for a bank to be insolvent; it can create the insolvency it fears.**

![Before and after comparison of a calm day with a trickle of withdrawals versus a run where everyone leaves at once and the bank must fire-sale long assets](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-3.png)

The before/after figure captures why the same balance sheet behaves so differently on a calm day versus a run day. On a calm day (left), only a few percent of deposits leave, new ones arrive to replace them, and the bank pays withdrawals out of its cash buffer without ever touching the long loans. The spread keeps flowing in; the machine works. On a run day (right), everyone tries to leave at once, the cash buffer runs out in hours, the bank is forced to dump long loans and bonds at fire-sale prices, and the resulting losses chew through the thin 4% cushion. The bank can be solvent on paper at breakfast and dead by Friday. Nothing about the assets changed between the two columns — only the *behavior of the depositors* changed. That is what it means to say a bank runs on confidence.

## Confidence is the real capital

Step back and notice what is actually holding the whole structure up. It is not the equity cushion — that is only 4%, and a serious run can blow through it in hours. It is not the cash on hand — that covers only a fraction of deposits. What keeps a bank alive day to day is the *belief* of its depositors that they could get their money if they wanted it, which means they don't all want it at once.

This is why banking is sometimes called a *confidence trick* — not in the fraudulent sense, but in the literal one: the trick works only as long as everyone has confidence. The moment enough depositors doubt the bank, they rush to withdraw; the rush forces fire sales; the fire sales create real losses; the losses confirm the doubt. The fear is self-fulfilling. A bank is therefore unique among businesses in that its viability depends on a *collective belief* about its viability. A restaurant with great food survives even if everyone fears it will close; a bank that everyone fears will close *will* close, no matter how good its loans are, because the fear itself triggers the run that breaks it.

This is also why the modern state wraps banks in a thick blanket of guarantees. **Deposit insurance** — in the US, the FDIC guarantees deposits up to \$250,000 per depositor per bank — exists to short-circuit the panic: if your money is guaranteed, you have no reason to run, so the run never starts. The **central bank as lender of last resort** stands ready to lend a solvent-but-illiquid bank cash against its good assets, so a temporary cash crunch doesn't force a fire sale. These are not generosities; they are structural patches on the fundamental fragility of maturity transformation. We have a whole post later in this series on deposit insurance and the lender of last resort, and another on the great central-bank rescues, but the reason they exist is already clear: a system built on confidence needs an institution whose job is to supply confidence when the private kind evaporates.

The SVB case is so instructive precisely because it shows confidence failing in the internet age. SVB's depositors were unusually concentrated (a handful of venture funds advising hundreds of startups) and unusually well-informed and connected. About 94% of its deposits were above the \$250,000 insurance limit, meaning the vast majority of its depositors had every rational reason to run the instant they smelled trouble — their money was *not* guaranteed. When word spread that the bank had taken losses on its bond portfolio, the run did not form as a slow queue; it formed as a wave of wire-transfer requests executed from phones in minutes. The old assumption that deposits are "sticky" — that withdrawals trickle out slowly enough for the bank to react — was shattered by the speed of a digital, networked, concentrated depositor base. The fragility was always there; technology just removed the friction that used to slow it down.

## The giants: the scale of the maturity-transformation machine

It would be easy to think of all this as a quaint, small-town affair. It is not. The maturity-transformation machine is one of the largest concentrations of economic power on the planet, and the biggest banks are bigger than most countries' entire economies.

![Horizontal bar chart of the largest banks by total assets, with Chinese state banks above five trillion dollars and the largest US bank near four trillion](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-4.png)

The figure ranks the world's largest banks by total assets. The four big Chinese state banks sit at the top — ICBC alone holds around \$6.3 trillion of assets — followed by the US giants: JPMorgan Chase at roughly \$4.0 trillion, Bank of America near \$3.3 trillion, then HSBC, BNP Paribas, Citigroup, and Wells Fargo. To put \$6.3 trillion in perspective, it is larger than the annual economic output of every country except the United States and China. And remember what those "assets" are: they are loans to households and companies, and bonds, funded overwhelmingly by deposits. Every one of these institutions is running the same trade we have been describing — borrow short, lend long, keep the spread — just at a scale where a stumble can shake the global financial system. That is precisely why the largest banks are designated *globally systemically important* and made to hold extra equity: an 8% cushion on \$4 trillion of assets is a very different societal risk than an 8% cushion on \$4 billion.

#### Worked example: the spread on a trillion-dollar book

Take a bank with \$4 trillion of assets earning a net interest margin of 3.2% — the recent US industry average. Net interest income alone is roughly 0.032 × \$4 trillion = \$128 billion in a single year, before fee income and before costs. Even a tiny change in the spread is enormous money: if competition or falling rates compress the margin by just 10 basis points (0.10%), that is 0.001 × \$4 trillion = \$4 billion of revenue gone. **At the scale of the giants, hundredths of a percent on the spread are billions of dollars, which is why a bank's net interest margin is watched the way a factory watches its profit margin per unit.**

## The spread breathes: how the margin moves through a rate cycle

The spread is not a constant. It expands and contracts as interest rates rise and fall, and watching it move teaches you more about banking than any textbook definition. The single most-watched measure of the spread is the **net interest margin (NIM)** — net interest income divided by the bank's interest-earning assets, expressed as a percentage. It is, roughly, the average spread the bank earns across its whole balance sheet.

![Line chart of US bank net interest margin from 2010 to 2024 showing a trough near two and a half percent in 2021 and a rebound above three percent after rate hikes](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-5.png)

The chart traces the US banking industry's net interest margin from 2010 to 2024. Read it as a story about rates. After the 2008 crisis the margin started around 3.76% and drifted down through years of ultra-low interest rates as loans repriced lower. It collapsed to a trough of about 2.56% in 2021, when the Federal Reserve had pinned short-term rates near zero — the spread was squeezed because the bank could not charge much on new loans while it was already paying close to nothing on deposits, leaving little room between the two. Then, as the Fed hiked aggressively in 2022 and 2023, the margin snapped back above 3%, reaching about 3.30% in 2023 and 3.23% in 2024.

Why does the margin behave this way? Because of *timing mismatches in repricing*. When rates rise, a bank's assets and liabilities do not reprice at the same speed. Loans tied to floating rates, and new loans, start charging more quickly. Deposits, meanwhile, are *sticky and slow to reprice* — banks raise deposit rates grudgingly and with a lag, because depositors are not very rate-sensitive. The fraction of a rate increase that a bank passes through to depositors is called the **deposit beta**, and in the early part of the 2022–23 hiking cycle it was low (around 10% at first), meaning banks captured most of the rate rise as wider spread. As the cycle matured and depositors woke up and demanded higher rates, the deposit beta climbed toward 50–55%, and the margin tailwind faded. The spread, in other words, is a living thing that breathes with the rate cycle and with how alert depositors are — and managing it is the daily job of a bank's treasury, a topic we devote a whole later post to.

There is a sting in the tail of this story, and it is the SVB sting. Rising rates *widen the spread* (good for the income statement) but *cut the value of the long bonds a bank already holds* (bad for the balance sheet). A bond paying 1.5% is worth less once new bonds pay 5%, because no one will buy your old low-yielding bond at full price. SVB had loaded up on long-dated bonds when rates were low; when rates rose, the same move that was fattening other banks' margins was quietly gutting the market value of SVB's bond portfolio. The spread giveth, and the duration of the bond book taketh away. That tension — earn more on the flow, lose more on the stock — is the exact mechanism of *interest-rate risk in the banking book*, which we will dissect in detail later in the series.

#### Worked example: NIM and the deposit-beta squeeze

A bank funds \$100 of loans (yielding 4%) with \$96 of deposits (paying 0.5%). Its net interest income is \$4.00 − \$0.48 = \$3.52, a NIM of about 3.5%. Now the central bank raises rates by 2 percentage points. The bank's loans reprice quickly, lifting the loan yield to 6%, so interest income rises to \$6.00. At first the bank holds deposit rates flat (deposit beta near zero), so deposit cost stays \$0.48 and net interest income jumps to \$6.00 − \$0.48 = \$5.52 — a NIM of 5.5%. Wonderful — for a while. But as depositors notice they can earn 4% in a money-market fund, the bank is forced to raise its deposit rate to, say, 2.5% (a deposit beta of roughly 50% of the 2-point move). Now deposit cost is 0.025 × \$96 = \$2.40, and net interest income falls back to \$6.00 − \$2.40 = \$3.60, a NIM of about 3.6%. **A bank's spread fattens fast when rates first rise and depositors are asleep, then thins again as depositors wake up and demand their cut — which is why the early innings of a hiking cycle are a bank's best quarters.**

## Every bank risk traces back to one trade

We have been circling a single idea: the one trade that defines a bank — borrow short, lend long, keep the spread — does not create one risk, it creates a whole family of them. Each of the later tracks in this series is really a deep dive into one branch of that family. It helps to see them all at once.

![Graph showing the one trade of borrowing short and lending long fanning out into credit, liquidity, interest-rate, and operational risk, all converging on the thin equity cushion](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-7.png)

The figure fans the risks out from the central trade. **Credit risk** is the chance the borrowers default — the loan asset turns out to be worth less than its face value. It comes directly from "lend long": the bank is exposed for years to whether the borrower keeps paying. **Liquidity risk** is the chance depositors leave faster than the bank can raise cash — the SVB risk. It comes directly from "borrow short": the funding can vanish overnight. **Interest-rate risk** is the chance that a change in rates hurts the bank — the value of its long bonds falls, or its short funding gets more expensive faster than its assets reprice. It comes directly from the *mismatch* between short funding and long assets. **Operational risk** is everything else that can go wrong in running a large, complex money machine: fraud, a rogue trader, a cyberattack, a broken settlement system, a control failure. It is the price of being a big, intricate institution handling enormous flows.

And crucially, every one of these risks converges on the same place: the thin equity cushion. A credit loss eats equity. A fire sale from a liquidity crunch eats equity. A rate-driven mark-down eats equity. A rogue trader's loss eats equity. The four arrows all point at the same 4% sliver, and when that sliver is gone, the bank is gone, regardless of which risk delivered the blow. This is why the entire edifice of banking regulation — capital rules, liquidity rules, stress tests, the whole Basel framework — is fundamentally about one question: *is the equity cushion thick enough, and the liquidity buffer deep enough, to absorb the losses these four risks can throw at it before depositors lose faith?* Every rule you will read about in this series is an answer to that question, and every failure is a case where the answer turned out to be no.

#### Worked example: which risk kills first

Consider a bank with \$4 of equity per \$100 of assets, holding \$52 of loans, \$22 of bonds, and \$13 of cash. Path one (credit risk): a recession pushes 8% of its loans into default with a 50% recovery, a loss of 0.08 × 0.50 × \$52 ≈ \$2.1 — over half the equity gone, the bank wounded but alive. Path two (interest-rate risk): rates jump and the bonds lose 15% of value, a mark-down of 0.15 × \$22 ≈ \$3.3 — most of the cushion gone, and now depositors are nervous. Path three (liquidity risk): the nervous depositors run, the \$13 of cash is exhausted, fire sales begin, and the remaining \$0.7 of equity evaporates within a day. **The same thin cushion sits at the bottom of every risk path, and it is usually the liquidity run — the fastest of the four — that delivers the final blow to a bank already weakened by one of the slower risks.**

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs are so widespread, and so wrong, that correcting them is the fastest way to upgrade how you read banks.

**Misconception 1: "The bank keeps my money in a vault, ready for me."** No. The instant your deposit lands, the bank lends most of it out. A bank typically holds only a small fraction of deposits as cash and reserves — in our representative balance sheet, about 13% of assets as cash, against deposits that are 74% of funding. The rest is out on loans and bonds. This is not a scandal; it is the entire point of a bank, and it is what lets your idle savings finance someone else's house. But it means the literal cash to pay every depositor at once does not exist, and never has, in any functioning bank.

**Misconception 2: "A bank fails because it ran out of money / made bad loans."** Sometimes — that is insolvency. But many famous failures were banks that died of *illiquidity* while still solvent. SVB's bonds were US Treasuries and agency securities; held to maturity they would have paid back nearly in full. The bank was not killed by bad assets; it was killed by needing cash faster than it could raise it without crystallizing losses. A bank with good assets can fail in a day. The number to remember: SVB faced roughly \$142 billion of withdrawals across two days against a \$175 billion deposit base — no amount of "good lending" saves you from that.

**Misconception 3: "More equity / a bigger cushion would just hurt returns, so thin capital is efficient."** It is true that thin equity boosts return on equity in good years — that is the leverage math. But the cost of thin equity is borne by everyone else when it fails: depositors, the insurance fund, taxpayers, the wider economy. A bank running at minimal equity privatizes the upside of leverage and socializes the downside. The post-2008 regulatory push to roughly double effective capital requirements — a globally systemic bank now faces an effective CET1 demand of around 8.5% to 10.5% of risk-weighted assets, not 4% — is precisely an attempt to make banks internalize more of that downside.

**Misconception 4: "Banks make their money on fees and charges."** Fees matter, and for some banks they are large. But for the typical commercial bank, the dominant source of revenue is net interest income — the spread — not fees. In recent years the US industry's net interest margin has run around 3.0% to 3.3% of assets, and on a multi-trillion-dollar balance sheet that spread dwarfs overdraft and account fees. When a bank's earnings disappoint, the headline is almost always about the margin, not the fees.

**Misconception 5: "A bank run is a relic of the 1930s; deposit insurance ended them."** Deposit insurance dramatically reduced runs by *small, insured* depositors — if your money is guaranteed, you have no reason to flee. But runs by *large, uninsured* depositors and by *wholesale* funders (other banks, money funds) are very much alive, and they are faster than ever. SVB in 2023, Credit Suisse in 2023, Lehman and Northern Rock in 2007–08 were all modern runs. The 2023 episode proved that a concentrated, uninsured, internet-connected depositor base can run a bank in hours, not days. Insurance changed *who* runs and *how fast*, not *whether* runs happen.

## How it shows up in real banks

Abstractions become unforgettable when you watch them play out with real names, dates, and numbers. Here are the episodes that put every idea in this post into the historical record.

**Silicon Valley Bank, March 2023 — illiquidity killing a solvent bank in 36 hours.** SVB had about \$209 billion of assets and \$175 billion of deposits, with roughly \$91 billion sitting in a held-to-maturity bond book and about \$17 billion of unrealized losses on its securities as rates rose. Critically, around 94% of its deposits exceeded the \$250,000 FDIC limit, so its depositors had every incentive to run. On March 9th, customers attempted to withdraw \$42 billion; an estimated \$100 billion more was queued for March 10th before regulators seized the bank. This is the purest possible illustration of the post: borrow short (uninsured deposits), lend long (Treasuries and agency bonds), get caught by rising rates that cut the value of the long book, and die of illiquidity when confidence breaks.

![Bar chart of the Silicon Valley Bank run showing total deposits of one hundred seventy-five billion against forty-two billion withdrawn on March 9 and one hundred billion queued for March 10](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-8.png)

The figure shows the arithmetic of the run starkly. Against a \$175 billion deposit base, the two-day demand for cash totaled roughly \$142 billion. No maturity-transforming institution on Earth can meet that, because the whole business model is that the money is *not* sitting there in cash. SVB was the second-largest bank failure in US history at the time, behind only Washington Mutual in 2008 — and it failed not from bad lending but from the textbook fragility of the trade itself, accelerated to internet speed.

**Credit Suisse, March 2023 — the slow death of trust.** Within the same week as SVB, the 167-year-old Credit Suisse collapsed into a state-arranged rescue by UBS for about CHF 3 billion. Here the story was not a sudden bad bet but a multi-year erosion of *confidence* after a string of scandals and losses. Clients had pulled roughly CHF 110 billion in a single quarter in late 2022. When the Swiss National Bank offered a CHF 100 billion liquidity line and it still wasn't enough to restore faith, the lesson was the same as SVB's in a different key: a bank is a confidence machine, and once confidence goes, the funding goes, and once the funding goes, the bank goes — even a 167-year-old global institution. The episode also delivered a brutal reminder about the capital stack: about CHF 16 billion of Credit Suisse's "Additional Tier 1" bonds were written to zero, a wipeout we will examine when we get to bank capital.

**Lehman Brothers, September 2008 — leverage and a wholesale run.** Lehman was an investment bank, not a deposit-taker, but the mechanism rhymes exactly. It funded a roughly \$639 billion balance sheet with leverage of about 30 times in 2007 — meaning equity of barely 3% of assets — and relied heavily on *short-term wholesale funding* (overnight repo borrowing) rather than deposits. When counterparties lost confidence in its mortgage assets, they refused to roll over that overnight funding. That is a run, just by professional lenders instead of retail depositors. With 30-times leverage, a small fall in asset values exhausted the thin equity, and a wholesale run finished the job. Lehman is the leverage worked-example from earlier in this post, made real and catastrophic.

**The Savings & Loan crisis, 1980s — the maturity mismatch in slow motion.** Before the digital runs, the same fragility played out across an entire industry over a decade. US savings-and-loan institutions had made long-term, fixed-rate mortgages funded by short-term deposits. When interest rates spiked in the early 1980s, their funding costs soared while their old fixed-rate mortgages kept paying the same low rate — the spread went negative. Roughly 1,043 thrifts failed between 1986 and 1995, and the cleanup cost taxpayers around \$124 billion. No fraud was required (though some occurred); the *structure* — short funding, long fixed-rate assets, rising rates — was enough to break a thousand institutions. It is the SVB story stretched across years instead of hours, and it is exactly why interest-rate risk in the banking book has its own post later in this series.

**The 2008–2012 failure wave — what a real banking crisis looks like.** Zoom out from any single name and the pattern is a *wave*. In the years after the 2008 crisis, FDIC-insured bank failures spiked to 140 in 2009 and 157 in 2010 before subsiding. Banks do not fail one at a time at random; they fail in clusters, because they share exposures (the same housing market, the same rate environment) and because failure is contagious through confidence. Compare 2023, when only 5 banks failed by count — but three of them (SVB, Signature, First Republic) were giants, and the combined assets dwarfed many of the small-bank years. The lesson: count the *assets* that failed, not just the number of banks.

![Bar chart of FDIC-insured bank failures per year showing a peak of one hundred fifty-seven in 2010 and only five in 2023](/imgs/blogs/what-a-bank-actually-does-maturity-transformation-and-the-spread-9.png)

The failure chart is the heartbeat of the banking system's stress. The towering red bars of 2009–2011 are the aftermath of the housing-and-leverage crisis; the long calm of 2014–2022 is a period of rebuilt capital and tighter rules; the small-but-heavy spike of 2023 is the rate shock finding the banks that had stretched their maturity transformation too far. Read this chart alongside the net-interest-margin chart and you have the two faces of the same trade: in good times it pays a steady spread; in bad times it fails in clusters. Both are consequences of borrowing short and lending long.

## The takeaway: read every bank as one trade under stress

If you remember nothing else from this post, remember this: **a bank is a leveraged, confidence-funded maturity-transformation machine.** It borrows short, lends long, earns the spread, and survives only as long as depositors trust it and its thin equity cushion absorbs losses faster than they arrive. That single sentence is the lens for everything that follows.

Once you hold that lens, banking stops being a fog of jargon and becomes a small number of repeating questions. When you look at any bank — to invest in it, to deposit with it, to regulate it, or just to understand a headline — you are really asking four things, and each maps to one branch of the risk tree:

First, **how cheap and sticky is its funding?** The whole profit engine starts with low-cost deposits that don't run. A bank with a fortress of insured retail deposits is a fundamentally safer machine than one funded by concentrated, uninsured, or wholesale money — that is the difference between a sleepy community bank and SVB.

Second, **how good and how long are its assets?** The spread is only real if the loans get paid back (credit risk) and the bonds don't crater when rates move (interest-rate risk). A fat margin earned by reaching for risky borrowers or long bonds is a margin borrowed from the future.

Third, **how thick is the cushion?** Equity is the shock absorber, and at 4% there is not much of it. The more of its own money a bank has at stake, the more loss it can eat before depositors are touched — which is why every capital ratio you will ever read about is just a measure of how much room the bank has to be wrong.

Fourth, **how deep is the liquidity buffer, and how fast could confidence break?** A solvent bank still dies if it can't meet withdrawals. In the internet age, the relevant question is not "will there be a queue" but "how concentrated and how connected are the people who could leave, and how much cash can the bank raise in an afternoon."

Every later post in this series is one of these four questions, examined in depth. Reading the balance sheet, the income statement, and capital ratios is question three made rigorous. The deposit franchise, lending, payments, and trade finance are the machinery of questions one and two. Treasury and asset-liability management is the art of running the maturity mismatch on purpose. The risk and regulation tracks are the formal defenses around the cushion. And the great failures — SVB, Credit Suisse, Lehman, the S&L crisis, Continental Illinois, the scandals — are the case files of what happens when one of the four answers turns out to be no.

The deepest takeaway is the one that should make you slightly uneasy, in a useful way: this fragility is not a bug to be fixed. It is the *function*. The reason a bank can be killed by its own depositors is the same reason a bank can finance a 30-year house with money you can withdraw tomorrow. Maturity transformation is essential precisely because it is dangerous — it reconciles two genuinely incompatible wishes (savers want liquidity, the economy wants long finance) by absorbing the contradiction onto a thin, confidence-backed balance sheet. We cannot have the benefit without the risk; we can only manage the risk with capital, liquidity buffers, insurance, and a lender of last resort. That is what a banking system *is*: a useful, fragile bargain, patched and policed, doing every day the one trade that is both the source of its value and the seed of its destruction. Keep that paradox in mind, and you will understand banks better than most people who work in them.

This is educational, not financial advice — nothing here is a recommendation to buy, sell, or deposit anywhere.
