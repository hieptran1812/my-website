> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — LIBOR, the interest rate that sat under roughly \$300 trillion of loans and derivatives, was not measured from real trades; it was a daily survey of a handful of banks asking each other, in effect, "what do you *think* you'd pay to borrow?" — and a self-reported number that everyone trusts is a number that can be quietly bent.
>
> - **It was an opinion, not a price.** Each morning a panel of banks submitted an *estimate* of their own borrowing cost; the survey threw out the highest and lowest quarter and averaged the rest — the *trimmed mean*. Nobody had to trade at the rate they reported.
> - **The submitting desk also held the bets.** A trader with an interest-rate swap could gain millions from a fraction of a *basis point* (one hundredth of one percent, 0.01%) move, and at many banks that trader could just walk over and ask the submitter for the number he needed.
> - **There were two distinct frauds.** From the mid-2000s, traders nudged submissions to profit derivatives positions; then in 2008 banks *lowballed* their submissions to hide how stressed they really were, because a high borrowing cost would advertise distress in a panic.
> - **The one number to remember:** the industry paid about **\$9 billion** in fines for LIBOR rigging, several traders went to prison, and the survey was replaced by **SOFR** — a rate built from roughly \$2 trillion a day of *actual* overnight transactions, leaving nothing left to nudge.

For nearly thirty years, the single most important number in finance was produced the way a small town decides whose pie is best at the county fair: a panel of judges each writes down a score, the organizer crosses off the highest and lowest, and averages what's left. The number that came out of that exercise — the London Interbank Offered Rate, LIBOR — set the interest on your mortgage, your student loan, your credit card, the float on a corporation's revolving credit line, and the cash flows on hundreds of trillions of dollars of derivatives. When LIBOR ticked up a hundredth of a percent, money moved across the entire planet.

And the judges, it turned out, were also betting on the pie.

That is the LIBOR scandal in one sentence: the banks that *reported* the rate also *traded* contracts whose value depended on the rate, and for years some of them simply reported the number that made their own trades pay off. When regulators finally pieced it together — partly from the traders' own chat logs, where they thanked each other for favors with offers of champagne and sushi — the world discovered that the keystone of the global financial system had been a survey of guesses, and a riggable one. The diagram below is the mental model for the whole post: LIBOR was never a market price you could look up. It was a poll, trimmed and averaged, and a poll can be gamed.

![Pipeline showing LIBOR set by surveying banks then trimming and averaging submissions](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-1.png)

This post is a conduct case, and it belongs to the part of our series about *how a bank breaks*. Most of the great banking failures we've covered — the duration trap at Silicon Valley Bank, the leverage at Lehman — were failures of *risk*: the bank made an honest bet and lost. LIBOR is different. It is a failure of *integrity at the seam* between two functions that should never have touched: the desk that reported a public benchmark and the desk that profited from it. The deep lesson is one our spine keeps returning to — **a bank runs on trust, and a benchmark runs on trust most of all.** When the number everyone relies on is something a bank gets to *say* rather than something the market *does*, the temptation to shade it is structural, not personal. Let's build the whole thing from zero.

## Foundations: what LIBOR was and why everything hung on it

Start with the problem LIBOR was invented to solve. Say you are a bank in 1985 and you want to make a five-year loan to a company, but you don't want to lock in a fixed interest rate — if rates rise, a fixed-rate loan would leave you earning too little. So you make a *floating-rate* loan: the rate resets every three months to "whatever short-term borrowing costs are, plus a margin." But what *is* "whatever short-term borrowing costs are"? You need a single, agreed, published number that everyone can point to, so that you and the borrower don't argue about it every quarter. That published number is a **reference rate** (also called a *benchmark*): a public figure that contracts reference instead of writing their own.

LIBOR was the dominant reference rate for the dollar, the pound, the euro, the yen, and the Swiss franc. Its full name tells you what it was supposed to measure: the **London Interbank Offered Rate** — the rate at which big banks could borrow *unsecured* (without posting collateral) from *each other* in *London*, for a set period. "Interbank" because the lenders and borrowers were banks; "offered" because it was the rate offered to a borrowing bank.

Here is the mechanism, and it is the single most important thing to understand. LIBOR was not computed from trades. Every London business morning, around 11:00 a.m., a **panel** of banks — 16 to 18 of the largest, depending on the currency — was asked one question for each maturity: *"At what rate could you borrow funds, were you to do so by asking for and then accepting interbank offers in a reasonable market size, just prior to 11 a.m.?"* Note the verb tense. The question is not "what rate did you pay?" It is "what rate *could* you pay?" It asked for an estimate of a hypothetical. Each bank's *submitter* — a person on the money-market desk — typed in a number. The organizer (for decades, the British Bankers' Association, working with Thomson Reuters) collected the 16-18 submissions, **trimmed** them — discarded the top quarter and the bottom quarter — and **averaged** the middle. That average, the *trimmed mean*, was published as the day's LIBOR.

A few terms to nail down, because the rest of the post uses them constantly:

- A **basis point (bp)** is one hundredth of one percent — 0.01%. A rate of 0.50% is fifty basis points. Benchmarks move in basis points, and as we'll see, a single basis point can be worth a fortune.
- **Notional** is the headline size a derivative is written on — the \$100 million "face" amount of a swap — even though no one hands over the \$100 million. The cash that actually changes hands is a small percentage of the notional.
- A **derivative** is a contract whose value derives from something else — here, from where LIBOR fixes. An **interest-rate swap** is the workhorse: two parties exchange a fixed interest stream for a floating one (the floating leg pays LIBOR plus a margin), so one side gains and the other loses every time LIBOR moves.
- A **tenor** (or maturity) is the length of the loan the rate refers to. LIBOR was published for several tenors — overnight, one week, one month, three months, six months, twelve months — and across multiple currencies (dollar, sterling, euro, yen, Swiss franc). The three-month dollar tenor was the single most-referenced rate in the world.
- A **panel bank** (or *contributor*) is one of the banks asked the survey question. The dollar panel had around 16-18 members; the smaller-currency panels had fewer, which mattered enormously, because the fewer the contributors, the more one nudged submission moves the trimmed mean.

The submission itself was a small daily ritual with no obvious gravity to it. A submitter — typically a junior-to-mid-level person on the money-market or treasury desk — opened a screen each morning, looked at whatever market color was available (recent interbank deals if any, the futures market, where overnight rates were, what brokers were quoting), and typed in a number for each tenor. There was no requirement to attach evidence, no audit of whether the bank could really have borrowed at the rate it reported, and — for most of LIBOR's life — no surveillance of whether the submitted number happened to line up suspiciously with the bank's own trading book. The submission was treated as a routine administrative chore, which is exactly why it was so easy to corrupt: nobody guarded a process nobody thought was dangerous.

Now the scale, because it explains why anyone bothered. At its peak, an estimated **\$300 trillion** of financial contracts referenced LIBOR. That is not a typo — three hundred trillion dollars, several times the size of the entire world economy. The overwhelming majority of it was *derivatives*: interest-rate swaps and futures whose payments reset off LIBOR. The rest was the everyday stuff — syndicated business loans, floating-rate bonds, mortgages, credit cards. The figure below shows the rough composition, and the point is the shape, not the decimals: the base was enormous and *derivative-heavy*, which is exactly why a hundredth of a percent mattered to the people setting the number.

![Horizontal bar of the contracts referencing LIBOR by product, derivatives the largest](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-8.png)

It helps to know where LIBOR came from, because the origin explains the flaw. The rate was formalized in 1986 by the British Bankers' Association to standardize the floating leg of the new syndicated loans and interest-rate swaps that London's banks were inventing. In 1986 the survey design was reasonable: the unsecured interbank market was deep, a few large banks really did lend to each other in size every day, and asking them their borrowing cost was a fair proxy for a market that was genuinely transacting. The design problem is that the *world* changed and the *benchmark* didn't. Over the following decades the contracts referencing LIBOR exploded from billions to hundreds of trillions — while the underlying interbank market it was supposed to measure *shrank*, as banks shifted to secured (collateralized) funding and, after 2008, largely stopped lending to each other unsecured for three months at all. By the late 2000s, LIBOR was a survey of a market that had quietly evaporated: the question still got asked, but the real trades that should have anchored the answer had mostly disappeared. A benchmark that once measured something real had become an estimate of a fiction.

So the foundation is this: a reference rate is supposed to be an honest measurement of the cost of money, and when it moves, payments on \$300 trillion of contracts move with it. LIBOR was that rate. But LIBOR was *measured by asking the banks*, and the banks had every reason to care which way it went. Hold that tension; it is the whole story.

#### Worked example: how few banks it took to move the number

Let's make the trimmed mean concrete, because people assume "trimming the extremes" makes a survey robust. Suppose the dollar panel has 16 banks, and for three-month LIBOR they submit rates clustered around 0.50%. The survey **discards the top 4 and the bottom 4** (the top and bottom quarters) and averages the **middle 8**.

Now ask: how many banks must collude to move the published rate? Naively you'd think you need to control the *whole* middle, but you don't. To raise the fix, a colluding bank simply needs its submission to land *inside* the kept middle eight, on the high side, rather than getting trimmed off. If one bank that would normally submit 0.50% instead submits 0.54%, and 0.54% is still below the four highest "real" submissions that get trimmed, then 0.54% survives into the average — and it replaces a 0.50% that *was* in the average. The mean of the middle eight shifts up by (0.54 − 0.50) / 8 = 0.04 / 8 = **0.005%**, half a basis point, from *one* bank.

Get two or three banks to nudge in the same direction — which is exactly what the chat logs show happened — and you can move the fix one to two basis points reliably. **The intuition:** trimming protects against one wild outlier, but it does nothing against several banks shading gently in the same direction, because gentle shading stays inside the kept band. A survey is only as honest as the median of its respondents, and the median was for sale.

## How the rate was actually set: a survey dressed as a measurement

Walk through a single morning at a panel bank, because the procedural detail is where the fraud lived. The submitter — call her the cash desk — has to answer the LIBOR question for each maturity by 11:00 a.m. In a calm, liquid market, this is almost mechanical: the bank really does borrow in the interbank market, the submitter knows what she paid an hour ago, and she types in something close to it. The number is an estimate, but it's an estimate anchored to real transactions she can see.

The trouble is that the interbank unsecured market — banks lending to each other for three months without collateral — was *thin* even in good times and nearly *vanished* in bad ones. For many maturities and currencies, on many days, there simply weren't enough actual term loans between banks to read a rate off. So the submitter was making a judgment call: *given what I know, what rate could I plausibly borrow at?* That judgment is the soft spot. A judgment is not a fact; it can be argued either way by a few basis points; and crucially, **no one could prove it wrong**, because the bank never had to actually borrow at the rate it reported. The submission was an opinion about a hypothetical loan, published as if it were a measured price.

This is the structural flaw. A benchmark built on *what banks say* rather than *what banks did* has a built-in deniability: any submission within a reasonable range looks defensible. And "a reasonable range" is exactly where you hide a nudge. The whole edifice of \$300 trillion rested on the professional honesty of a few dozen submitters, each of whom worked at an institution with enormous, opposing financial interests in the answer.

#### Worked example: what one basis point was worth on a real book

Why would a trader risk his career over a hundredth of a percent? Because of *notional*. The sensitivity of an interest-rate position to a one-basis-point move is called its **DV01** — the dollar value of one basis point. For a position that resets off LIBOR, a rough DV01 over a single reset period is:

$$\text{DV01} \approx \text{notional} \times 0.0001 \times \text{(years of the reset)}$$

Take a trader running a \$50 billion book of swaps that resets to three-month LIBOR. Even on a single annual reset, a one-basis-point move is worth:

$$\$50{,}000{,}000{,}000 \times 0.0001 \times 1 = \$5{,}000{,}000$$

Five million dollars, from one basis point, on one reset. The chart below shows the swing across book sizes — and \$50 billion was not an exotic number; large dealer banks ran swap books many times that.

![Bar chart of swap P&L from a one basis point move across notional sizes](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-3.png)

Now stack the resets. A trader who knows a big fixing is coming — say, a quarter-end when an unusually large slice of his book reprices on the *same day* — can have tens of millions riding on whether LIBOR fixes one basis point higher or lower. **The intuition:** the benchmark looks tiny (hundredths of a percent) and the notional looks abstract (you never see \$50 billion), but multiply them and you get a number worth committing fraud over. That multiplication is the entire motive.

## The trader-driven rigging: when the submitter takes requests

Here is the conflict of interest, drawn as plainly as it can be. At a universal bank, the people who *submit* LIBOR and the people who *trade* LIBOR-linked derivatives worked in the same building, often on the same floor, sometimes for the same boss. A trader who needed a high fixing could message the submitter — by chat, by email, by leaning over a desk — and ask for it. And because the submission was a defensible "judgment" anyway, the submitter could grant the favor by choosing a number at the high end of plausible. Nothing about the request *looked* like a crime; it looked like a colleague accommodating a colleague.

![Graph showing a derivatives trader and desk boss both pressuring the rate submitter feeding the survey and the published rate](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-2.png)

The evidence, when it surfaced, was almost comically explicit. Investigators recovered chat transcripts in which traders made flat requests — "we need a really low 3m fix tomorrow" — and submitters confirmed — "I am going to go 90 altho 91 is what I should be posting" — and the favors were repaid in the open: "When I retire and write a book about this business your name will be written in golden letters." One trader promised a submitter a bottle of Bollinger champagne for a helpful fix. At UBS, the Japanese-yen trader Tom Hayes — later the central criminal figure — ran a web of requests across his own bank, interdealer brokers, and other banks, treating the daily fix as a variable he could *set* to suit whichever way his enormous yen book leaned.

Two features made this work and made it durable. First, **collusion across banks** amplified a single submitter's nudge: if Hayes wanted a higher yen fix, he didn't just ask his own bank's submitter; he asked brokers to spread the word and asked counterparts at other panel banks to submit in the same direction, so that several of the kept-middle submissions moved together. Second, **the brokers in the middle** — the firms that arranged interbank deals — could lend false credibility by feeding panel banks a "suggested" run of rates, so that submitters who weren't even in on the scheme drifted toward the rigged level. The fraud scaled because the benchmark was a social process, and social processes can be coordinated.

It is worth slowing down on *how* the cross-bank coordination actually worked, because it reveals how a benchmark becomes a network fraud rather than a single bad apple. Consider the role of the **interdealer brokers** — firms like ICAP, RP Martin, and Tullett Prebon that sat between banks, arranging deals and, importantly, distributing a daily "run" of suggested rate levels that submitters used as a sanity check. A trader who wanted a higher yen fix could pay a broker (in cash, or in the currency that mattered most on a trading floor: extra business steered the broker's way) to publish a "run" skewed in his direction. Submitters at *other* banks — many of whom were entirely honest and had no idea they were being played — would then anchor their own guesses to the doctored run, drifting the whole panel toward the rigged level. The broker, in one infamous chat, was promised "a curry on me" for help; in another, the going rate for nudging a fix was discussed as casually as lunch.

This is why the LIBOR scheme was so resilient: it didn't require everyone to be in on it. It required a few traders, a few submitters, and a few brokers — and the *structure* of a survey, in which honest participants take cues from shared signals, did the rest of the work. A handful of corrupt nodes, plus a process designed to make everyone converge, equals a benchmark that leans wherever the corrupt nodes push it. Tom Hayes' yen operation reportedly involved requests to submitters at his own banks (UBS, then Citigroup), to brokers, and to traders at other panel banks, woven into a routine he ran day after day. To him, the daily fix wasn't a number to *forecast*; it was a variable to *set*, and he allegedly made his employers tens of millions doing it.

The trader-driven manipulation was *bidirectional* — sometimes a desk wanted LIBOR higher, sometimes lower, depending on whether its book was receiving or paying the floating leg that day. That's the tell that distinguishes it from the second fraud we'll come to: trader rigging followed the *book*, not the bank's health, so the requested direction flipped from day to day as positions changed. It was, in the regulators' language, "manipulation for profit."

#### Worked example: a quarter-end nudge that pays for itself

Put numbers on a single rigged morning. A trader runs a \$20 billion notional book that, on this particular quarter-end, resets entirely to three-month LIBOR, and the book is structured so it *receives* floating — it gains when LIBOR fixes higher. He arranges, across his own submitter and one friendly counterpart at another panel bank, to nudge the fix **2 basis points** higher than it would otherwise have set.

His gain on that reset:

$$\$20{,}000{,}000{,}000 \times 0.0002 \times 0.25 \;(\text{three months}) = \$1{,}000{,}000$$

One million dollars, from a two-basis-point nudge on a single reset, arranged over chat in a few minutes. The cost to him was a favor he'd repay later and the risk — which for years felt like zero — of being caught. **The intuition:** the manipulation wasn't a desperate gamble; on the day, it was nearly free money, because the benchmark was a number a colleague could simply *write down* differently. When the expected reward is a million dollars and the perceived risk is a bottle of champagne owed, you have designed a machine for fraud.

## The 2008 lowballing: when the rate became a confession

The second LIBOR fraud is the more disturbing one, because it wasn't a handful of traders skimming — it was banks lying to survive, and arguably with the tacit understanding of authorities. To see it, remember what LIBOR *measured*: a bank's own cost of borrowing unsecured. In normal times that's a sleepy number. But in the financial crisis of 2007-2008, as banks began to fear that *other* banks might fail, the cost of unsecured interbank borrowing became a real-time *credit thermometer*. A bank that had to pay a high rate to borrow was a bank the market thought might not pay it back.

Now put yourself in the seat of a stressed bank's submitter in September 2008, days after Lehman Brothers collapsed. Your bank's *true* unsecured borrowing cost has spiked, because lenders are terrified. If you answer the LIBOR question *honestly* — submitting the high rate you'd really have to pay — you publish, for the whole world to read in tomorrow's newspaper, a confession: *we are in trouble, lenders distrust us, we may be next.* That published number could trigger the very run it describes — counterparties pulling funding, depositors fleeing, the share price collapsing. So banks submitted *low*. They reported borrowing costs healthier than the ones they actually faced, to look stronger than they were.

![Pipeline showing a stressed bank in 2008 submitting a low rate to look healthy understating system stress](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-6.png)

This was a different crime with a different shape. Trader rigging was for *profit* and flipped direction with the book. Lowballing was for *survival* and was *systematically downward* across the panic — every stressed bank had the same incentive to shade the same way, which is why aggregate LIBOR understated the real stress in funding markets through late 2008. Barclays' submissions in this period became the centerpiece of the first big settlement, and the bank's defense was telling: it argued that everyone was lowballing, that submitting honestly would have left it looking like an outlier in distress, and that it understood regulators to be comfortable with — even to be nudging toward — lower submissions to calm the panic. Whether or not that defense holds, it captures the perverse logic: in a crisis, the honest answer to the LIBOR question was the dangerous one.

There is a further, uncomfortable wrinkle: the lowballing happened partly in the *open*, with at least some awareness among authorities. A 2008 phone call between a senior Barclays executive and a Bank of England official became central to the later inquiry; Barclays read it (rightly or wrongly) as a hint that its submissions were running higher than its peers' and that lower submissions would be tolerated to keep the market calm. The Bank of England denied instructing anyone to lowball. But the very fact that the conversation was *ambiguous* tells you how compromised the benchmark had become: in a crisis, the honest answer was so dangerous that even the guardians of financial stability had reason to prefer a soothing fiction over an accurate confession. When the truth itself can start a run, everyone — banks and regulators alike — develops an interest in a gentler number.

The systemic cost of the lowballing was larger than the trader skim, even though it produced fewer headlines. Because so many contracts reset off LIBOR, an artificially *low* LIBOR through 2008 meant that everyone holding a LIBOR-linked asset — pension funds, municipalities, savers in floating-rate products — received *less* interest than an honest rate would have paid, while borrowers paid less. The transfers ran into the billions and landed unevenly, which is why so much private litigation followed: counterparties who had received the floating leg argued, with arithmetic on their side, that they'd been shorted by a rate that banks had bent to protect themselves.

The lowballing also points at a deeper design failure we'll return to. A benchmark that doubles as a *health disclosure* is poisoned at the root: the moment your reported borrowing cost can sink your bank, you will never report it honestly under stress, and stress is exactly when an accurate rate matters most. The number was least trustworthy precisely when the world most needed it.

#### Worked example: the size of the lowballing transfer

Put a rough dollar figure on what a few basis points of understatement moved. Suppose, conservatively, that lowballing held three-month dollar LIBOR about **10 basis points** below an honest level for a stretch of the crisis. Apply that to even a fraction of the LIBOR-linked stock — say \$50 trillion of floating-rate assets actually resetting in the affected window:

$$\$50{,}000{,}000{,}000{,}000 \times 0.0010 \times 0.25\ (\text{a quarter's accrual}) = \$12{,}500{,}000{,}000$$

That is \$12.5 billion of interest, for a single quarter, shifted from those *receiving* the floating leg (lenders, savers, pension funds) to those *paying* it (borrowers) — purely from a 10-basis-point shading. Spread across multiple quarters and the transfer dwarfs the few hundred million the traders skimmed. **The intuition:** because LIBOR sat under such a vast notional base, even a "small, defensible" downward nudge redistributed billions — which is why the lowballing, though less cinematic than the chat-room rigging, was arguably the costlier of the two frauds.

#### Worked example: reading the lowballing signal

You can actually *see* lowballing in the data without a single chat log, by comparing LIBOR to a price that *couldn't* be surveyed away. During the crisis, analysts watched the gap between three-month dollar LIBOR and the *overnight index swap (OIS)* rate — a market-implied measure of expected overnight rates. This **LIBOR-OIS spread** is, put simply, the market's price of *term bank credit risk*: how much extra you demand to lend to a bank for three months versus rolling overnight.

In calm times the LIBOR-OIS spread sat around **10 basis points**. After Lehman failed in September 2008 it blew out to roughly **350 basis points** — a thirty-fold widening that screamed funding panic. Now here's the tell: individual banks' *submissions* clustered suspiciously close together and drifted *below* where their own credit spreads (visible in the price of insuring their debt) said they should be. If a bank's credit-default-swap spread implied it should be paying, say, 4.50% to borrow, but it submitted LIBOR-consistent rates implying 4.00%, that 50-basis-point gap was the lowball, hiding in plain sight. **The intuition:** you don't need a confession to spot a rigged survey — you compare the survey to an independent price it should track, and when the survey is suspiciously calmer than the market, someone is shading the answer.

## Why a self-reported benchmark was riggable by design

Step back from the two frauds and ask the structural question: *why was this possible at all?* The answer isn't "bad people" — bad people exist everywhere, and most benchmarks aren't routinely rigged. The answer is that LIBOR combined four features that, together, made manipulation low-cost, low-risk, and high-reward.

**First, the input was an opinion, not a transaction.** The submission asked what a bank *could* borrow at, not what it *did* borrow at. An opinion has a defensible range; a transaction is a fact. As long as a submitter's number sat inside the plausible band, it was unfalsifiable. You cannot rig a measurement of something that actually happened; you can always shade an estimate of something hypothetical.

**Second, the thin underlying market gave cover.** Even honest submitters often had little or no real interbank borrowing to anchor to, especially in stressed conditions or illiquid currencies and maturities. When there's no real trade to point at, *every* number is a judgment, and judgments are where nudges hide. The benchmark purported to measure a market that, much of the time, barely existed.

**Third, the submitter and the beneficiary were the same institution.** The bank reporting the rate held positions that profited from the rate. This is the cardinal sin of benchmark design — the people who set the number are the people who benefit from it — and at most panel banks there wasn't even a wall between the cash desk that submitted and the swaps desk that traded. The conflict wasn't a bug that crept in; it was wired into who got asked the question.

**Fourth, the reward dwarfed the apparent risk.** As the DV01 example showed, a basis point on a big book is worth hundreds of thousands of dollars; collude on a few resets and it's millions. Against that, the perceived downside — for years — was essentially nil, because no one was watching the submissions against the traders' books. A system where the payoff is large, the probability of detection feels tiny, and the act looks like a routine professional judgment is a system that *will* be abused. It would be surprising if it weren't.

Notice that none of these four features is about individual morality, and that is the point worth dwelling on. You could have staffed every submission desk with saints and the *lowballing* would still have happened in 2008, because the incentive to hide distress was institutional, not personal — a saintly submitter at a stressed bank who reported honestly might have *triggered the run* that killed his employer and his colleagues' jobs. And you could have policed individual traders hard and still missed the *cross-bank coordination*, because no single bank's surveillance could see the network of brokers and counterparts that moved the panel together. The scandal was an *emergent property* of a benchmark design that put the question to interested parties, gave them defensible latitude, and watched no one. Whenever you find a number set that way, you should assume the number is bent and ask only by how much. The right mental posture toward a self-reported benchmark is not trust-then-verify; it is suspicion-by-default, because the design itself supplies the motive, the means, and the cover.

![Before and after showing one nudged submission still moving the trimmed mean of a benchmark](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-4.png)

The figure above closes the loop on the trimmed-mean point: people defended LIBOR by saying the trim made it robust. But the trim only removes *outliers*. A submission nudged from honest to high — but kept *inside* the surviving middle — survives the trim and drags the average. The protection everyone trusted was a protection against the wrong threat. It guarded against one bank screaming a crazy number; it was helpless against several banks whispering a slightly wrong one.

#### Worked example: the trim that didn't protect

Make it arithmetic. A 16-bank panel submits three-month LIBOR. The honest submissions, sorted, are: 0.47, 0.48, 0.48, 0.49, **0.49, 0.50, 0.50, 0.50, 0.51, 0.51, 0.52, 0.53** (the kept middle 12 after trimming the lowest 2 and highest 2 — using a 1/8 trim for the illustration), with 0.46, 0.46 trimmed at the bottom and 0.54, 0.55 trimmed at the top. The honest average of the kept set works out to about **0.499%**.

Now one bank that honestly belongs at 0.50 instead submits 0.53 — a 3-basis-point nudge that's still *below* the 0.54/0.55 that get trimmed, so it survives. Replacing a 0.50 in the kept set with a 0.53 raises the average by 0.03 / 12 = 0.0025%, a quarter of a basis point, from one bank. Add two colluding banks doing the same and you're up to roughly **three-quarters of a basis point** — and on a large book that's the difference, from the earlier example, of several hundred thousand dollars. **The intuition:** the trimmed mean is robust to *noise* and useless against *bias*. If respondents shade in the same direction, trimming the extremes just throws away the honest outliers and keeps the rigged middle.

## The investigations and the fines: roughly nine billion dollars

The unraveling started, as these things often do, with a data anomaly. In 2008, *Wall Street Journal* analysis and academic work flagged that banks' LIBOR submissions looked too low and too clustered relative to their independently visible credit risk — the lowballing signal from the worked example above. Regulators on both sides of the Atlantic — the UK's Financial Services Authority (later split into the FCA), the US Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) and Department of Justice, and others — opened investigations. What turned suspicion into prosecution was the *chat logs*: once investigators subpoenaed the messaging archives, the requests and favors were right there in writing, dated and signed.

The first domino fell in **June 2012**, when **Barclays** settled for about **\$450 million** across the FSA, CFTC, and DOJ, and the scandal became front-page news. The chief executive resigned within days under political pressure. After Barclays, the settlements rolled out for years: **UBS** paid about **\$1.5 billion** in December 2012 (with its Japanese unit pleading guilty); **Royal Bank of Scotland** about **\$610 million**; **Rabobank**, **Deutsche Bank** (which paid roughly **\$2.5 billion** in 2015, one of the largest single settlements), and interdealer brokers like ICAP all settled. Across the whole episode, the industry paid on the order of **\$9 billion** in fines and settlements — and that's before the private litigation, in which cities, pension funds, and counterparties sued banks for the losses from rigged fixings.

![Horizontal bar comparing LIBOR fines to other banking conduct cases](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-5.png)

As the chart shows, LIBOR was the largest conduct-fine episode in our banking series — bigger than the Wells Fargo fake-accounts penalties, bigger than the Goldman 1MDB settlement, dwarfing the Barings collapse loss. And unlike most of those, LIBOR produced *criminal convictions of individuals*, not just corporate fines. **Tom Hayes**, the UBS and Citigroup yen trader, was convicted in a London court in 2015 and sentenced to 14 years (later reduced to 11); several other traders and brokers were convicted in the UK and the US, though a number of those convictions were later overturned on appeal, and in 2024 the UK Supreme Court quashed Hayes' conviction on a point of legal direction to the jury — a reminder that the line between "aggressive submission within the rules" and "criminal manipulation" was genuinely contested in court.

That legal contest is more than a footnote, because it goes to *what exactly was illegal*. The defense traders ran — and the one the Supreme Court ultimately found had been improperly taken away from juries — was roughly this: the LIBOR question asked for a *range* of rates the bank could borrow at, the bank could legitimately pick *any* point in that range, and choosing the point that happened to help the trading book was therefore picking a *genuine* rate, not a false one. Prosecutors argued the opposite: that submitting with the *purpose* of advantaging a trading position corrupted the answer regardless of whether the number sat in a defensible range. Reasonable lawyers disagreed for over a decade. The ambiguity itself is the indictment of the benchmark's design: a number whose manipulation is genuinely *hard to define as a crime* is a number whose rules were never tight enough to protect it. You cannot have that argument about SOFR, because SOFR has no submission to characterize — there is only a median of trades.

The other accountability was institutional and cultural. Beyond the fines, banks fired or disciplined dozens of staff, clawed back bonuses, and rebuilt their benchmark-submission processes from scratch. Regulators imposed personal-accountability regimes (the UK's Senior Managers Regime grew partly out of this era) so that named executives, not a faceless "bank," owned conduct failures. The scandal became a case study taught in every compliance department: the canonical example of what happens when a control gap sits, unguarded, at the seam between two desks with opposing interests.

#### Worked example: adding up the price tag

It's worth assembling the \$9 billion from its pieces so the number isn't a vague "billions." Take the headline regulatory settlements, in round figures: Barclays ~\$0.45bn + UBS ~\$1.5bn + RBS ~\$0.6bn + Deutsche Bank ~\$2.5bn + Rabobank ~\$1.1bn + a cluster of others (Société Générale, Citigroup, JPMorgan, Lloyds, brokers) summing to several billion more. Add them and you clear **\$9 billion** in fines and settlements before counting private lawsuits.

Now compare that to the *profit* the rigging generated. The trader-driven manipulation, across all banks and all the rigged fixings, plausibly produced gains in the *hundreds of millions* — meaningful, but a fraction of the fines. **The intuition:** the fines weren't priced to claw back the profits; they were priced to punish the *breach of trust* in a number the whole system relied on. When a bank corrupts a public benchmark, the damage isn't the few hundred million it skimmed — it's the credibility of the \$300 trillion edifice built on top, and regulators charged for *that*.

## The fix: from a survey of opinions to a record of trades

If the disease was "the benchmark is an opinion the banks get to state," the cure is obvious in hindsight: build the benchmark from *transactions the banks actually did*, so there's no opinion left to shade. That is exactly the design of the replacement rates, and in the United States it is **SOFR** — the Secured Overnight Financing Rate.

SOFR measures the cost of borrowing cash *overnight* secured by US Treasury collateral — the *repo* market (which we cover in depth in [the repo market and how banks fund overnight](/blog/trading/banking/the-repo-market-and-how-banks-fund-overnight)). The crucial difference is volume and verifiability. The overnight Treasury repo market trades on the order of **\$2 trillion every single day** across thousands of real transactions. SOFR is computed by taking those actual trades and publishing a volume-weighted median. There is no survey, no panel of submitters, no "what rate *could* you borrow at." There is only "here is the rate at which roughly two trillion dollars *actually changed hands* overnight." You cannot nudge a number that is the median of two trillion dollars of trades you didn't make.

![Before and after contrasting survey-based LIBOR with transaction-based SOFR](/imgs/blogs/the-libor-scandal-rigging-the-worlds-most-important-number-7.png)

The transition was a genuinely enormous operation, because \$300 trillion of contracts had LIBOR written into them. New contracts moved to SOFR through the late 2010s; legacy contracts needed *fallback* language specifying what they'd reference once LIBOR stopped publishing. The UK's FCA announced that most LIBOR settings would cease at the end of **2021**, with the most-used US dollar settings running until **June 2023** to give legacy contracts time to mature or be amended. By mid-2023, the world's most important number — the one rigged for years — simply stopped existing.

The mechanics of the switch were harder than they sound, and the difficulty illuminates how deeply a benchmark embeds itself in the system. A loan or a swap doesn't just say "use LIBOR"; it says "use three-month LIBOR set two days before each reset, add this margin, on this day-count basis." SOFR, being an *overnight* rate, doesn't natively produce a three-month forward number. The industry solved this two ways. For derivatives and many loans, term contracts use **SOFR compounded in arrears**: you take the daily SOFR over the period and compound it, so the "three-month rate" is known only at the *end* of the period rather than the start — a real operational change for treasurers used to knowing their coupon in advance. For markets that genuinely needed a forward-looking number, a **Term SOFR** was constructed from SOFR *futures* prices (themselves built on the underlying transaction-based rate). To make legacy contracts work, an industry body (ISDA) published standardized fallbacks that added a fixed **credit-spread adjustment** to SOFR — the historical average gap between LIBOR and SOFR — so that flipping a contract from LIBOR to SOFR didn't silently transfer value from one party to the other. Each of these is a small engineering choice; together they are what it takes to extract a riggable opinion from the plumbing of the entire financial system and replace it with an observable fact.

SOFR isn't a free lunch, and it's worth being honest about the trade-offs. LIBOR was *forward-looking* and embedded *bank credit risk* (it was an unsecured rate, so it rose when banks looked shaky); SOFR is *backward-looking* (an overnight rate, compounded in arrears for term contracts) and *nearly credit-risk-free* (it's secured by Treasuries). That means SOFR behaves differently in a crisis: when bank credit risk spikes, LIBOR would jump but SOFR can actually *fall* (as panicked money floods into Treasury-backed lending), which is the opposite of what a bank's funding cost does. Markets have built credit-sensitive add-ons and term-SOFR variants to bridge the gap. But the core win is non-negotiable: the benchmark is now an *observation*, not an *assertion*, and the conflict that powered the scandal — submitters who profited from their own submissions — has no place to live.

#### Worked example: why you can't rig a transaction rate

Suppose a desk wanted to push SOFR up by one basis point to help its book, the way it once pushed LIBOR. To move a *trimmed survey*, it nudged one submission inside the kept middle — cheap and deniable. To move SOFR, which is the volume-weighted median of ~\$2 trillion of overnight repo, the desk would have to *transact enough real repo at off-market rates to shift the median of two trillion dollars*. Even moving the median by a single basis point would require executing many tens of billions of dollars of repo at a deliberately bad price — a trade that *loses* money directly, is visible to every counterparty, and is dwarfed by the honest market around it.

So the cost-benefit that made LIBOR rigging "nearly free money" inverts completely: with SOFR, manipulating the benchmark costs more than the position could ever gain, and it's observable. **The intuition:** you defeat benchmark manipulation not by adding rules to a survey, but by removing the survey — anchor the number to a deep, real, observable market, and the act of rigging becomes self-defeating arithmetic.

## Common misconceptions

**"LIBOR was a real interest rate the banks were paying."** No — and this is the misconception that hides the whole scandal. LIBOR was the *trimmed mean of banks' estimates* of what they *could* borrow at, not a record of what they did pay. On many days, for many tenors, there were few or no actual interbank loans to anchor the estimate. The number looked like a market price; it was a survey of opinions. That gap between "looks measured" and "is actually asserted" is precisely the soft spot the riggers exploited.

**"The trimmed mean made LIBOR manipulation-proof."** The trim removed the highest and lowest quarter of submissions, which protects against a single wild outlier. It does nothing against *coordinated, gentle* shading: a submission nudged a few basis points but kept inside the surviving middle survives the trim and moves the average. As the worked examples showed, two or three banks shading in the same direction could move the fix one to two basis points reliably. Trimming defends against noise, not bias.

**"It was a few rogue traders, like the rogue-trader cases."** Two things make LIBOR broader. First, the manipulation spanned *many* banks and was *coordinated* across them and through interdealer brokers — it was a network, not a lone gambler. Second, the 2008 *lowballing* wasn't traders at all; it was institutions systematically understating their borrowing costs to hide distress, arguably as a matter of corporate self-preservation. Comparing it to a single rogue trader (which we cover in [the Barings collapse](/blog/trading/banking/barings-and-nick-leeson-1995-how-one-trader-broke-a-300-year-old-bank)) understates how structural it was.

**"The \$9 billion in fines means the banks lost money on the scheme."** The fines (about \$9 billion across the industry) vastly exceeded the *profits* from the rigging, which were plausibly in the hundreds of millions. The penalty wasn't calibrated to disgorge the skim; it was calibrated to punish the corruption of a benchmark the entire financial system relied on. The crime priced was the breach of trust, not the trading gain.

**"Switching to SOFR was just a name change."** It was a fundamental redesign. LIBOR was a forward-looking, unsecured, *survey-based* rate that embedded bank credit risk; SOFR is a backward-looking, secured, *transaction-based* rate that's nearly credit-risk-free and built from ~\$2 trillion a day of real repo. They behave differently in a crisis and required rewriting fallback language in hundreds of trillions of dollars of contracts. The point of the change was to make the number un-riggable by making it an observation rather than an opinion.

## How it shows up in real banks: benchmark conduct beyond LIBOR

LIBOR was the largest benchmark scandal, but it wasn't the only one — the same structural flaw (a number set by interested participants rather than measured from arm's-length trades) showed up wherever a benchmark relied on submissions or on a thin, manipulable fixing window. The pattern repeats, and so does the fix.

**The FX "fixing" scandal (2013-2015).** Major banks were found to have manipulated foreign-exchange benchmark rates — especially the 4 p.m. London "WM/Reuters fix" used to value huge portfolios. Traders coordinated in chat rooms with names like "The Cartel" and "The Mafia," sharing client order information and timing their own trades to push the rate during the brief fixing window so their books and clients' executions landed favorably. The settlements ran to roughly **\$10 billion** across banks including Citigroup, JPMorgan, Barclays, RBS, UBS, and Bank of America, with several pleading guilty to criminal charges. The mechanism differed from LIBOR — here they manipulated *real trades* in a thin window rather than submissions — but the lesson is identical: a benchmark fixed at a moment that interested parties can crowd is a benchmark that will be crowded. The remedy was to *widen the fixing window* (from one minute to five) so no single bank's trades could dominate it.

**Gold and silver fixings.** The century-old London gold and silver "fixes" were set by a small group of banks on a private call. Regulators fined Barclays after a trader was found to have manipulated the gold fix to avoid paying out on a client option, and the silver and gold fixes were replaced (in 2014-2015) with *electronic auctions* with more participants and an audit trail — the same move from a clubby submission process to a transparent, transaction-based one.

**ISDAfix / swap-rate benchmarks.** A benchmark for interest-rate swap rates, set partly by dealer submissions, was also found to have been manipulated, with banks settling for hundreds of millions. Again: dealer submissions, dealer positions, same conflict, same fix — the benchmark moved toward transaction-based methodologies.

**Euribor and Tibor.** LIBOR's siblings — the euro-area Euribor and the Tokyo Tibor — were caught up in the same rigging, since many of the same banks and traders (Hayes traded yen, which touched Tibor) were involved across currencies. Euribor reformed its methodology to lean more on real transactions; the family of "-ibor" rates either reformed toward transactions or was retired in favor of new risk-free rates (SONIA in the UK, €STR in the euro area, SOFR in the US, TONA in Japan).

The through-line for how a *bank* should read all of this: benchmark submission is now treated as a serious **conduct risk**, governed like any other control failure. After LIBOR, banks built firewalls between submission desks and trading desks, recorded and surveilled communications, required transaction-based justification for any submission, and reported benchmark contributions up through the same three-lines-of-defense structure that governs other operational failures — the machinery we describe in [operational risk: fraud, cyber, and the loss events](/blog/trading/banking/operational-risk-fraud-cyber-and-the-loss-events). The deeper change is regulatory: contributing to a benchmark, and administering one, became *regulated activities* under EU and UK benchmark rules, with criminal liability for manipulation. The era in which a colleague could lean over and ask for a number is over by design.

What SOFR and its global cousins fix, concretely, is the *source* of the number. Every "-ibor" replacement rate is built the same way: take a deep, real, overnight market (Treasury repo for SOFR, sterling overnight deposits for SONIA), publish a volume-weighted statistic of *actual trades*, and compound it in arrears for term contracts. No panel. No submitters. No "could you borrow at." The benchmark becomes a record of what the market did, administered by an authority (the New York Fed publishes SOFR) with no position in the outcome. The conflict that powered the scandal — interested submitters setting a number they traded — is engineered out of existence.

## The takeaway: a benchmark is only as honest as its source

Here is the durable lesson, and it generalizes far past LIBOR. **The integrity of a number depends entirely on where the number comes from.** A benchmark sourced from *what interested parties say* will, sooner or later, be shaded by those parties — not because bankers are uniquely dishonest, but because the design hands them a defensible way to profit and a near-zero chance of being caught. A benchmark sourced from *what a deep market actually did* is robust by construction, because shading it means transacting against yourself in full view. LIBOR was the first kind; SOFR is the second. Everything else — the fines, the convictions, the multi-year transition — flows from that one difference in design.

For our series spine, LIBOR is the conduct chapter of the same story as every failure we've studied: **a bank runs on trust.** Usually we mean depositors' trust that their money is safe. Here it's the *market's* trust that the keystone number isn't being bent. When that trust breaks, the cost isn't measured in the few hundred million the riggers skimmed; it's measured in the \$9 billion of fines, the criminal cases, and the wholesale replacement of the benchmark — because the system cannot function on a number it can't believe. A bank that corrupts a public benchmark isn't just cheating a counterparty; it's degrading the shared infrastructure it itself depends on, the way a town poisons its own well.

So how do you *use* this, whether you're reading a bank, working at one, or just understanding the financial plumbing around you? Ask of any benchmark, rate, index, or "official" number: **is this measured from real transactions, or asserted by parties who benefit from it?** Who computes it, and do they have a position in the answer? How deep is the underlying market — could a few participants move it, or would moving it cost more than it's worth? Those three questions — source, conflict, depth — are the LIBOR scandal distilled into a checklist. They are how you tell a number you can trust from a number someone gets to say. And in finance, that distinction is worth, quite literally, \$300 trillion.
