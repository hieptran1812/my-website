> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The forty-year drive to build the *cheapest* possible global supply chain is reversing into a *security-first* reorganization, and that reversal is a durable repricing event, not a headline you fade.
>
> - The cost-first model (one cheapest supplier, thin inventory, "just-in-time") is being replaced by a security-first model (multiple suppliers in friendly countries, buffer stock, "just-in-case") — driven by the COVID shock, geopolitics, tariffs, and the weaponization of minerals.
> - The real chokepoint in critical minerals is **processing, not mining.** China mines a modest share of the world's rare earths but refines roughly **90%** of them, and refines **~98% of gallium** — so it can squeeze the chain without owning the mines.
> - Resilience costs money. Carrying more inventory and a second or third supplier raises a firm's working capital and lowers its return on capital — across the economy that is a **structural inflation floor**, not a transitory blip.
> - The one number to remember: China processes **~90% of rare earths and ~98% of gallium.** That gap between *mining* share and *processing* share is the entire game.

On 3 July 2023, China's Ministry of Commerce announced that, effective 1 August, exporters of **gallium** and **germanium** — two metals most investors had never heard of — would need a license to ship them abroad. Gallium goes into radar, 5G base stations, and the power chips that make electric vehicles and data centers efficient. Germanium goes into fiber-optic cable, infrared optics, and night-vision gear. China refines the overwhelming majority of both. Within weeks, spot gallium prices in Europe roughly doubled, defense and semiconductor procurement teams scrambled, and Western governments accelerated stockpiling plans they had been debating for years.

The curbs were not about the metals' dollar value — the global gallium market is tiny, a few hundred million dollars a year. They were a demonstration. Beijing was answering a years-long campaign of US and allied **export controls** on advanced semiconductors (covered in [Export Controls and the Chip War](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war)) by showing it held chokepoints of its own. The message landed: in a world where supply chains are weapons, the cheapest source is no longer automatically the right source.

This post is about that reversal — what practitioners now call the **great unbundling.** For four decades, globalization optimized one variable: cost. Firms chased the lowest landed price, concentrated production in whoever made things cheapest (overwhelmingly China), and ran inventories down to almost nothing. That model is being rewound, deliberately, by both companies and governments, in favor of *security of supply.* The reorganization is creating durable sector winners and losers, a scramble for the minerals that sit at the chokepoints, and — quietly but importantly — a higher floor under inflation. Let us build the whole thing from zero, then turn it into a way to read and position around the most important structural shift in global trade since China joined the WTO in 2001.

## Foundations: how supply chains became a security problem

Start with the model that ruled global business from roughly 1990 to 2018, because the whole story is a reaction against it.

**The cost-first supply chain.** A modern product — a phone, a car, a wind turbine — is assembled from hundreds of components that are themselves made from dozens of materials, sourced from all over the world. Under the cost-first model, a firm picks, for each input, the single cheapest reliable supplier on the planet, wherever that supplier sits. Because China spent thirty years building unmatched manufacturing scale, cheap labor, dense supplier networks, and subsidized infrastructure, "the single cheapest supplier" was very often Chinese. The result was a supply chain optimized like a finely tuned engine: maximally efficient, minimally redundant, and — as it turned out — maximally fragile.

**Why globalization was efficient in the first place.** It is worth being precise about *why* the cost-first model won, because that logic is exactly what de-globalization is now sacrificing. The intellectual foundation is the two-hundred-year-old idea of **comparative advantage:** if each country specializes in what it produces relatively most efficiently and trades for the rest, total world output is higher than if every country tries to make everything itself. Layer on top of that the modern realities of *economies of scale* (a factory making 100 million units per year has far lower unit costs than one making 1 million) and *agglomeration* (suppliers cluster near each other, so a dense manufacturing hub like China's Pearl River Delta can source any component within a day's drive), and you get a powerful gravitational pull toward concentrating production wherever it is cheapest at scale. For three decades that logic delivered falling real prices for manufactured goods, year after year — the *disinflation dividend* of globalization. The crucial point for an investor is that abandoning the cheapest-source model means giving up that dividend: de-globalization is, by construction, the partial *un-doing* of comparative advantage, scale, and agglomeration, and you pay for un-doing them in higher costs. The reorganization is not free lunch reversed; it is paid lunch.

**Just-in-time (JIT).** The inventory discipline that goes with the cost-first model. Pioneered by Toyota and adopted across global manufacturing, JIT means you hold as little inventory as possible — parts arrive *just in time* to go onto the assembly line, not weeks early to sit in a warehouse. Inventory is dead capital: a part sitting in a warehouse is cash you spent that earns nothing until the product sells. So firms drove inventories to razor-thin levels — sometimes a few days of stock — to free up that cash and lift returns. JIT works beautifully right up until a link in the chain breaks, at which point you have no buffer and the line stops.

**Just-in-case (JIC).** The opposite discipline, now ascendant. You hold *more* inventory than you strictly need, and you qualify a *second* or *third* supplier for critical inputs, so that a disruption — a port closure, a factory fire, a war, an export ban — doesn't halt production. JIC trades efficiency for resilience. It costs money (the extra inventory and the redundant suppliers are not free), and that cost is the hinge of this entire post.

> [!note]
> **The one tradeoff to anchor everything on.** A supply chain can be optimized for *cost* or hardened for *resilience*, but not maximally both at once. Cost-first means single-source and thin inventory; resilience means multi-source and buffer stock. The reorganization of the 2020s is the world collectively moving the dial from cost toward resilience — and paying for it.

### Why the cost-first model is reversing

Three forces, stacking on top of each other, flipped the calculus. None of them is partisan; they are structural facts a portfolio has to price.

**1. The COVID shock (2020–2022).** When the pandemic shut factories and ports, the fragility of single-source, zero-buffer chains became impossible to ignore. The most famous casualty was semiconductors: automakers, who had cancelled chip orders early in the pandemic, found themselves unable to get them back when demand snapped higher. Plants idled for *want of a chip that costs a dollar.* Ford and GM each lost billions in production. The lesson seared into every boardroom: a supply chain optimized purely for cost is a supply chain with no shock absorbers.

**2. Geopolitics and tariffs.** Starting in 2018, the United States imposed tariffs — taxes on imports — on a large swath of Chinese goods, and China retaliated. We cover the episode in depth in [The 2018–19 US–China Trade War](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war); here the relevant fact is the *magnitude and persistence* of the tariff wall, because that is what changed firms' location math.

![A step chart of the average US tariff on Chinese imports rising from about three percent to over nineteen percent between 2018 and 2020, and China's retaliatory tariff on US goods rising similarly](/imgs/blogs/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals-2.png)

The US average tariff on Chinese imports went from **3.1% in early 2018 to about 19.3% by 2020**, and it stayed there — through a change of administration and into 2024. Against a roughly 3% tariff the US charges the rest of the world, a near-20% wall on China is a permanent ~16-percentage-point penalty for sourcing from one specific country. Once a cost penalty looks *permanent* rather than temporary, it stops being a line item to absorb and becomes a reason to move the factory.

**3. The weaponization of supply.** The third force is the newest and, for this post, the most important: governments learned to use chokepoints in the supply chain as instruments of pressure. The US restricts the chips and chip-making tools China can buy; China restricts the minerals the West needs. When the inputs to your products can be switched off by a foreign government for reasons that have nothing to do with commerce, "cheapest source" is no longer a complete sentence — "cheapest source *that won't be cut off*" is.

### The three responses: reshoring, nearshoring, friend-shoring

Faced with those forces, firms and governments are relocating production. The jargon distinguishes three flavors, by *how far* the production moves:

- **Reshoring (or onshoring).** Bringing production *all the way home* — back to your own country. A US company moving a factory from China to Arizona is reshoring. It maximizes security and political goodwill but is the most expensive option, because you're now manufacturing in a high-wage economy.
- **Nearshoring.** Moving production to a *nearby* country rather than all the way home. For the US, that means Mexico. You get a shorter, more controllable supply line and lower labor costs than at home, while keeping the factory in the same time zone and trade bloc.
- **Friend-shoring (or ally-shoring).** Moving production to *politically aligned* countries, wherever they sit geographically. The logic is not distance but *trust*: source from countries unlikely to weaponize the chain against you. For the US, friend-shoring favors Vietnam, India, Mexico, Poland, and similar partners.

Sitting under all three is the corporate strategy known as **"China+1."** Most firms are not abandoning China — its scale and supplier ecosystem are irreplaceable in the near term. Instead they *keep* their China operations and *add* a second base elsewhere, so that no single country (and no single government's whims) can halt their production. China+1 is why the friend-shoring beneficiaries are absorbing the *marginal new factory* even as China keeps the existing ones.

A more refined version of the same idea, increasingly common in boardrooms, is the **"China-for-China, world-for-the-world"** split. Here a multinational runs *two parallel supply chains*: one inside China to serve the vast Chinese domestic market (insulated from Western tariffs and built to survive a decoupling), and a separate one outside China — in friend-shored hubs — to serve the US and Europe. The two chains are deliberately *de-coupled* so a shock to one (a tariff, a sanction, a conflict) does not take down the other. This split is more expensive than a single global chain (you duplicate engineering, supplier qualification, and overhead), and it is precisely the structural cost that feeds the inflation floor — but for a firm whose largest two markets are on opposite sides of a geopolitical fault line, running two chains is cheaper than betting the whole company on the fault line never moving. For an investor, the firms that have *already* built this redundancy trade at a quality premium, while those still running one undiversified global chain carry a latent decoupling risk the market is steadily pricing in.

### Critical minerals and rare earths

Now the material at the heart of the story. **Critical minerals** are the metals and elements that modern technology and defense *cannot function without* and whose supply is concentrated and vulnerable. The list varies by government but reliably includes the **rare-earth elements**, lithium, cobalt, graphite, gallium, germanium, and a handful of others.

**Rare-earth elements (REEs)** are a group of seventeen metals (the lanthanides plus scandium and yttrium). Despite the name they are not geologically rare — they are reasonably abundant in the Earth's crust. What is rare is finding them in concentrations worth mining and, far more importantly, the willingness and capability to *process* them, because separating one rare earth from another is chemically nasty, capital-intensive, and environmentally dirty. The most economically vital are **neodymium and praseodymium (NdPr)**, which go into the ultra-strong permanent magnets inside EV motors, wind turbines, hard drives, and precision-guided weapons. A modern EV drive motor and a guided missile both depend on a NdPr magnet, and there is no cheap substitute.

This is the single most important distinction in the entire field, so the next section gives it its own heading.

## Why processing, not mining, is the real chokepoint

The popular framing — "China controls the world's rare earths" — is *imprecise in a way that matters for your portfolio.* China does not control the world's rare-earth *ore.* It controls the world's rare-earth *processing.* Those are different stages of the chain, and confusing them leads to bad trades.

A critical-mineral supply chain has four stages: you **mine** the ore, you **process and separate** it into purified individual elements, you turn those elements into a **refined input** (a magnet, a battery-grade chemical, a chip-grade wafer), and you build that input into a **component and a final product.** China's grip is concentrated almost entirely on the second stage.

![A pipeline showing the critical-mineral chain from mining ore through processing and separation, where China holds sixty to ninety percent of capacity, then to making magnets or chip inputs, building components, and the final product](/imgs/blogs/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals-4.png)

The numbers tell the story cleanly. China mines roughly **60% of the world's rare earths** — a large share, but not a monopoly; the US, Australia, Myanmar, and others mine meaningful amounts. But China **processes and separates roughly 90% of the world's rare earths.** For some specific links the concentration is even more extreme: China refines an estimated **~98% of the world's gallium and ~60% of germanium**, and dominates the processing of **graphite (~90%)** and the refining of **lithium (~65%)** even though it mines only about 10% of the world's lithium ore. The matrix below lays the mining-versus-processing gap out element by element.

![A dependency matrix of five critical minerals showing China's mining share is modest while its processing share is dominant, around ninety to ninety-eight percent for rare earths, gallium, germanium, and graphite](/imgs/blogs/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals-5.png)

Why does processing concentrate when mining doesn't? Three reasons, all structural:

1. **Chemistry and capital.** Separating rare earths from each other requires hundreds of stages of solvent extraction, enormous capital, deep technical know-how built over decades, and tolerance for serious pollution. China spent forty years and accepted the environmental cost to build that capability at scale; rebuilding it elsewhere takes a decade and a willingness to permit dirty chemical plants that Western communities resist.
2. **Economics of by-products.** Gallium and germanium are not mined on purpose — they are *by-products* of aluminum (bauxite) and zinc refining. Because China dominates aluminum and zinc smelting, it incidentally dominates the gallium and germanium that fall out of those processes. You cannot easily "open a gallium mine"; you would have to rebuild an aluminum industry.
3. **Deliberate industrial policy.** China explicitly targeted midstream processing as strategic, subsidizing it for decades while the West offshored the "dirty" middle of the chain and kept the "clean" ends (design, branding). The West optimized for cost and environmental optics; China optimized for control of the chokepoint.

The investment implication is sharp: **funding a new mine outside China does not, by itself, break China's leverage.** If the ore still has to be shipped to China to be refined, the chokepoint is intact. The bottleneck — and therefore the value, the policy support, and the strategic risk — sits at the *processing* stage. A reshoring or "secure supply" thesis that funds mining without funding processing is solving the wrong problem.

This is the same chokepoint logic that governs the semiconductor war, just running in the opposite direction. In chips, the West holds the chokepoint: the most advanced logic chips can only be fabricated by a handful of firms (overwhelmingly Taiwan's TSMC) using lithography machines that only one company (ASML in the Netherlands) can build, and the US uses control of that chokepoint to restrict what China can buy — the subject of [Export Controls and the Chip War](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war). In critical minerals, China holds the chokepoint and answers in kind. The symmetry is the whole point: both sides have located a single irreplaceable stage of a long supply chain — advanced fabrication on one side, mineral processing on the other — and both have learned to squeeze it. For the investor, the lesson generalizes beyond either commodity: *find the one stage of any strategic supply chain that cannot be quickly substituted, and that is where the leverage, the policy money, and the durable repricing live.* Mapping chokepoints is the single most transferable skill this whole theme teaches.

#### Worked example: the cost of a gallium export curb to a manufacturer

Take a defense-electronics manufacturer that needs **2,000 kilograms of high-purity gallium per year** for radar and power-amplifier chips. Before China's July 2023 curbs, it bought gallium at roughly **\$300 per kilogram**, an annual gallium bill of `2,000 × $300 = $600,000`. After the curbs, with ~98% of refining controlled by the restricting country and licenses now required, the European spot price roughly doubled to about **\$600 per kilogram.** The new annual bill is `2,000 × $600 = $1,200,000` — an extra **\$600,000 a year** for the *same* quantity of the *same* metal. If the firm can't pass that through, and its product line generates \$20 million of revenue at a 10% operating margin (\$2 million of operating profit), the \$600,000 hit erases **30% of operating profit** from one small input. The core idea: a chokepoint metal that is a rounding error on the *cost sheet* can be a wrecking ball on the *margin* the moment it is restricted, because there is no second supplier to switch to.

## China's mineral leverage in practice: the 2010 episode and the 2023–24 curbs

This is not theoretical, and it is not new. China has used its mineral chokepoint as leverage before, and the historical record is the single best guide to how the current curbs will play out.

**The 2010 Japan episode.** In September 2010, a Chinese fishing trawler collided with Japanese coast-guard vessels near the disputed Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands, and Japan detained the captain. Within days, rare-earth shipments from China to Japan effectively halted. Japan's electronics and auto industries — utterly dependent on Chinese rare earths for magnets — faced a supply crisis. Rare-earth prices spiked many-fold over the following months (some oxides rose roughly *tenfold* through 2011). The episode was a wake-up call: it spurred Japan to fund alternative supply (notably backing Australia's Lynas, the largest rare-earth processor outside China), to recycle, to engineer magnets that use less rare earth, and to stockpile. The crucial lesson for traders: the *initial* price spike was enormous, but it *overshot* and then faded as buyers found workarounds, demand was destroyed, and new supply came online. The leverage was real but its price impact was front-loaded and partly self-correcting.

**The 2023–24 curbs.** Beijing has been systematically reasserting that leverage in response to Western chip controls:

- **July 2023:** export licenses required for gallium and germanium.
- **December 2023:** a ban on exporting rare-earth *extraction and separation technology* — protecting not just the material but the *know-how* that is the true moat.
- **December 2023:** export controls on certain graphite products (the dominant anode material in EV batteries).
- **December 2024:** an outright ban on exporting gallium, germanium, and antimony to the United States, plus tighter graphite scrutiny — a sharp escalation from licensing to prohibition for one destination.

The pattern is a tit-for-tat escalation ladder: each Western tightening of chip controls is met by a Chinese tightening of mineral controls. For an investor, the takeaway is that mineral export curbs are now a *recurring policy instrument*, not a one-off — which means the beneficiaries (non-China processors, stockpilers, recyclers) have a *structural* tailwind, not a one-headline pop.

**Strategic stockpiles.** Governments respond to this leverage with **strategic stockpiles** — government-held reserves of critical materials, held precisely so that a sudden export cut-off doesn't immediately halt defense production or the energy transition. The US National Defense Stockpile, Japan's rare-earth reserves, and the EU's plans under its Critical Raw Materials Act all aim to hold enough buffer to ride out a disruption while alternative supply spins up. Stockpiling is the national-scale version of "just-in-case," and it is itself a source of structural demand for the very minerals at the chokepoints.

**Why the leverage is asymmetric — and self-limiting.** It is worth being clear-eyed about both sides of the chokepoint, because the leverage is real but not unlimited. On the one hand, the asymmetry is stark: a curb on a chokepoint mineral can halt a high-value end product — a fighter jet, an EV, a data-center chip — for the want of a few kilograms of refined material that costs almost nothing relative to the finished good. That is enormous leverage per dollar of trade restricted, which is exactly why it is used as a *signaling* tool rather than for the trade revenue. On the other hand, the leverage is self-limiting in two ways. First, *using* it teaches every customer that the supply is unreliable, which accelerates the very diversification the dominant supplier would prefer to delay — every curb is an advertisement for building capacity elsewhere, and the 2010 Japan episode kick-started a decade of ex-China rare-earth investment. Second, the dominant supplier often *also* depends on the customer (China sells the West minerals but buys the West's machinery, chips, and end markets), so a full weaponization risks retaliation up the chain. The practical result is a calibrated escalation ladder — targeted curbs, licensing regimes, and destination-specific bans — rather than an all-out cutoff, and a steady, policy-supported build-out of alternative processing capacity that is the durable investment opportunity underneath the headlines.

## The resilience-versus-cost tradeoff and the inflation floor

Here is where supply-chain geopolitics meets the macro question every investor cares about: inflation. The mechanism is simple once you see it, and it is the most underappreciated investment implication of the whole reorganization.

Resilience is not free. Every step toward security-first costs money in a specific, measurable way:

- **More inventory.** Moving from ~10 days of stock to ~45 days means tying up far more cash in goods sitting in warehouses — cash that earns nothing and must be financed.
- **Redundant suppliers.** Qualifying and maintaining a second or third supplier means you forfeit the volume discounts of single-sourcing and you carry the overhead of managing multiple relationships.
- **Higher-cost geographies.** Reshoring to the US or friend-shoring to Mexico or Vietnam means manufacturing where labor, land, energy, and compliance cost more than they did in the cheapest-source location.
- **Duplicated capacity.** Building a second factory "just in case" the first is disrupted means running two plants at lower utilization than one optimized plant.

Each of those raises the cost of producing the same good. When the *entire economy* shifts from cost-first to security-first at once, the aggregate effect is a higher cost of supply that does not go away when the latest shock fades. That is what practitioners mean by a **structural inflation floor:** globalization spent thirty years exporting *disinflation* (cheaper goods every year as production moved to the cheapest source), and de-globalization runs that tape in reverse, putting a floor under how low goods inflation can go. It does not mean runaway inflation; it means the *baseline* is higher than the 2010s taught a generation of investors to expect. (For the policy side of how inflation feeds back into rates, see [2021–2023 inflation and the fastest hiking cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle).)

#### Worked example: the cost of resilience as a hit to return on capital

Take a manufacturer with **\$500 million of annual revenue** running a lean, cost-first chain. It holds 10 days of inventory. Annual cost of goods sold (COGS) is \$400 million, so 10 days of inventory is about `$400m × (10 / 365) ≈ $11 million` of stock on the balance sheet. Suppose its total invested capital (working capital plus fixed assets) is **\$250 million**, and it earns \$30 million of after-tax operating profit, for a return on invested capital (ROIC) of `$30m / $250m = 12%`.

Now it goes just-in-case: inventory rises to 45 days, or about `$400m × (45 / 365) ≈ $49 million` — an extra **\$38 million** of capital tied up in stock. It also builds a second supplier relationship and a modest buffer warehouse, adding say \$12 million more in capital, for **\$50 million of additional invested capital.** Invested capital is now \$300 million. If the extra resilience does not raise profit (it is insurance, not growth), ROIC falls to `$30m / $300m = 10%`. The firm just gave up **2 full points of ROIC** — from 12% to 10% — to buy resilience. Put plainly: resilience shows up not as a dramatic loss but as a quiet, permanent drag on the return the same business earns on each dollar of capital, which is exactly why markets re-rate the whole sector's valuation multiple down a notch.

## The second-order effects: where the money actually moves

The first-order story — more inventory, redundant suppliers, higher-cost geographies — is the obvious part. The durable trades sit in the *second-order* effects, the consequences that ripple out once an economy commits to security-first. Four matter most.

**Capital expenditure supercycle.** Reshoring and friend-shoring mean building things: fabs, battery plants, processing facilities, warehouses, ports, power lines, and the second and third factories that redundancy requires. Each is a multi-billion-dollar capital project, and the world is committing to thousands of them at once. That is a structural tailwind for the entire *capital-goods complex* — the makers of factory equipment, electrical gear, industrial automation, construction materials, and the engineering firms that design and build the plants. Unlike a consumer trend that can reverse in a quarter, a capex supercycle has multi-year order books: once a fab breaks ground, the spending is locked in for years. This is why "picks and shovels" beats "gold miners" as a way to play the theme — the suppliers to the build-out have more visible, more durable revenue than any single end-project.

**Energy and grid strain.** Manufacturing — especially semiconductor fabs, mineral processing, and battery production — is staggeringly energy-intensive. Re-concentrating that production in the US, Europe, and friendly Asia, on top of the electricity demand from EVs and data centers, strains power grids that were not built for it. The second-order beneficiaries are utilities, grid-equipment makers (transformers, switchgear, cabling), and the firms that supply the power. The second-order *cost* is that energy-hungry reshored production runs into higher and more volatile electricity prices than it enjoyed in the cheapest-source location — another brick in the inflation floor. A processing plant that needs cheap, reliable, abundant power cannot simply be wished into a grid-constrained geography; the energy constraint is one of the hardest limits on how fast the reorganization can actually proceed.

**Working-capital financing in a higher-rate world.** The extra inventory that JIC requires has to be financed, and it is being financed at the higher interest rates of the post-2021 world rather than the near-zero rates of the 2010s. A firm that carries an extra \$38 million of inventory (as in the worked example above) and finances it at 6% is paying an extra `$38m × 6% ≈ $2.3 million` a year in carrying cost that a zero-rate world would have made nearly free. The reorganization and the higher-rate regime reinforce each other: resilience is most expensive precisely when money is most expensive, which sharpens the penalty on captive, balance-sheet-heavy firms and the premium on asset-light ones.

**Geographic dispersion of risk — and new concentration.** The headline goal of friend-shoring is to *spread* risk across multiple countries. But the second-order reality is that the reorganization often just *moves* the concentration rather than eliminating it: production leaves China and re-concentrates in a few favored hubs (Mexico, Vietnam, India), and the critical-mineral chokepoint stays exactly where it was until processing capacity is rebuilt elsewhere — which takes a decade. So the *resilience* the reorganization buys is partial and uneven: an investor who assumes "diversified supply chain" automatically means "low risk" will be surprised when a shock to the new hub, or a fresh mineral curb, reveals that the chokepoints simply relocated rather than dissolved. Real diversification of a chokepoint is the hardest, slowest, most valuable part of the whole project — which is exactly why it commands the policy money in the next section.

## The subsidy-driven reshoring: CHIPS, the IRA, and the beneficiaries

Governments are not leaving the reorganization to firms alone — they are paying for it. Two US laws in 2022 turned reshoring from a corporate cost into a subsidized opportunity, and they define a large share of the winners.

**The CHIPS and Science Act (2022)** put roughly **\$52 billion** of federal money — direct grants plus a 25% investment tax credit — behind building semiconductor fabrication ("fabs") on US soil, reversing decades of offshoring chip production to Taiwan and South Korea. (Why chips specifically are *the* strategic supply chain is the subject of the companion post [Taiwan Semiconductors and the Most Important Supply Chain on Earth](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/taiwan-semiconductors-and-the-most-important-supply-chain-on-earth).) The beneficiaries are the firms building those fabs and the long tail of equipment and construction suppliers around them.

**The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA, 2022)** put several hundred billion dollars of clean-energy subsidies into the economy, many of them *explicitly conditioned on domestic or friendly-country content.* The EV tax credit, for example, requires that a rising percentage of battery minerals be extracted or processed in the US or a free-trade partner, and that battery components be made in North America. The IRA is, in effect, a friend-shoring law dressed as a climate law: it pays you to build the battery and mineral-processing supply chain outside China. (We cover the IRA's energy-transition angle in [Energy and Climate Regulation: Carbon Pricing and the IRA](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira).)

The structural point is that subsidies *change the return math* on reshoring. A factory that would never pencil out at US cost levels can clear its hurdle rate once a grant covers part of the capital and a tax credit lifts the after-tax return. That is why a subsidy announcement is a genuine repricing catalyst for the named beneficiaries — it moves a project from "uneconomic" to "funded."

#### Worked example: a reshoring beneficiary's revenue and margin gain

Take a US-based maker of factory-automation equipment — robots, conveyors, controls. When firms reshore or friend-shore, they build *new* factories, and new factories in high-wage countries automate heavily to offset labor costs. Say the reorganization adds **\$200 million per year** of incremental orders to this firm over a multi-year build-out, on top of its existing \$800 million base, lifting revenue to **\$1.0 billion (+25%).** Because the incremental volume runs through fixed factory and engineering overhead the firm already pays, it carries a *higher* incremental margin — say 30% versus a 20% company average. The extra \$200 million of revenue therefore drops `$200m × 30% = $60 million` of incremental operating profit onto a base of `$800m × 20% = $160 million`, lifting operating profit to \$220 million, a **+37.5%** jump. Earnings growing faster than revenue is exactly the "operating leverage" pattern that earns a stock a higher multiple — so the market often re-rates the *valuation* on top of the *earnings* gain. The core idea: the picks-and-shovels suppliers to reshoring (automation, electrical equipment, construction, industrial real estate) can capture the trend with less single-name risk than betting on which specific factory gets built where.

## The friend-shoring map: who absorbs the relocated factories

If production is leaving the single cheapest source, where does it go? The pattern is not random — it concentrates in a handful of geographies that combine political acceptability to the US with the labor, scale, or proximity to host real manufacturing.

![A map of China-plus-one diversification fanning out to Mexico, Vietnam, and India as friend-shoring winners, with a tail-risk node flagging transshipment and origin and tariff scrutiny](/imgs/blogs/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals-7.png)

- **Mexico** is the nearshoring winner. It shares a border and a free-trade agreement (USMCA) with the US, sits in the same time zone, and has a deep automotive supply base. It has been gaining factory investment in autos, appliances, and electronics as firms shorten their North American supply lines. Mexico overtook China as the United States' largest source of imports in 2023 — a milestone that captures the whole shift in one statistic.
- **Vietnam** is the electronics and apparel winner. It offers low wages, a stable government keen to attract manufacturing, proximity to China's supplier ecosystem, and a web of free-trade agreements. It became the textbook "+1" for electronics assembly. The friend-shoring boom shows up most clearly as *foreign direct investment* (FDI) — money foreign firms commit to build plants in the country.

![A grouped bar chart of Vietnam's annual foreign direct investment showing registered commitments and realized disbursements both rising to record levels by 2024](/imgs/blogs/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals-6.png)

Vietnam's *registered* FDI (new commitments) and *disbursed* FDI (money actually deployed into the ground) both climbed to records — disbursed FDI reaching roughly **\$25 billion in 2024**, the highest on record — as global firms built the "+1" base there. We give Vietnam its own deep treatment in [Vietnam in the US–China Squeeze](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze), because it is simultaneously the clearest beneficiary and the carrier of a specific tail risk.

- **India** is the scale winner. With a labor force rivaling China's and a government courting manufacturers (notably through production-linked incentive schemes), it has attracted phone assembly and pharmaceutical production. Its draw is sheer size and a large domestic market, balanced against weaker infrastructure and tougher logistics than China's.

**The tail risk to respect: transshipment and rules of origin.** Friend-shoring is not always *real* relocation. Sometimes a Chinese firm simply ships near-finished goods to Vietnam or Mexico, does minimal final assembly, slaps on a new "Made in Vietnam" label, and exports to the US to dodge the China tariff. This is **transshipment**, and customs authorities police it through **rules of origin** — legal tests of how much value must be added in a country for the goods to *count* as made there. When the US tightens origin rules or accuses a friend-shoring hub of being a transshipment back door, the hub's exporters face sudden tariff and compliance risk. So the beneficiary thesis carries a built-in fragility: the more a hub looks like a relabeling station rather than a genuine factory base, the more exposed it is to a regulatory crackdown.

## Inventory strategy shifts: the corporate balance-sheet footprint

The reorganization leaves a visible fingerprint on corporate financial statements, which is where a careful investor can *see* the trend rather than just talk about it. The single clearest metric is the **inventory-to-sales ratio** (or its inverse, inventory *turns*) — how much stock a firm carries relative to what it sells.

Through the cost-first decades, manufacturers drove inventory turns *up* (less stock per dollar of sales) as JIT spread. Since 2020, the trend has reversed: many manufacturers are running structurally higher inventory-to-sales ratios than their pre-2020 norms, deliberately holding buffer stock against disruption. That extra inventory is the JIC discipline showing up as a balance-sheet line. It is also a drag on cash flow and ROIC, exactly as the resilience-cost section described.

For the investor this cuts two ways. A *rising* inventory ratio can mean either prudent resilience (good, durable) or unsold goods piling up because demand softened (bad, a warning). Distinguishing them requires reading whether management is *choosing* the inventory (a strategic buffer, flagged in the filings) or *stuck* with it (a demand miss). The reorganization makes the first reading more common than it used to be — but it does not make the second go away, so the metric must be read in context, not mechanically.

#### Worked example: a friend-shoring host country's earnings and multiple re-rating

Take a Vietnamese industrial-park operator — a company that buys land, builds factory shells and infrastructure, and leases them to the foreign manufacturers pouring in under China+1. Say it earns **\$40 million** of net profit and trades at 12× earnings, a market value of `$40m × 12 = $480 million`. Now the friend-shoring wave lifts factory demand: occupancy rises, the operator develops more land, and net profit grows 15% a year for three years — `$40m × 1.15³ ≈ $61 million`, a +52% earnings gain. But the *bigger* move is often the multiple. As investors recognize a multi-year FDI tailwind (disbursed FDI into Vietnam reaching a record ~\$25 billion in 2024 is the macro evidence), they re-rate the stock from a sleepy 12× to a growth-stock 18×. Market value becomes `$61m × 18 = $1,098 million` — a **+129%** total return, of which the earnings did `+52%` and the *re-rating* did the rest. The lesson: in a friend-shoring host market, the durable winners get a double boost — faster earnings *and* a higher multiple — but that same multiple is what collapses fastest if a transshipment crackdown or a tariff threat (the tail risk flagged earlier) turns the FDI narrative sour, so the position must be sized for a sharp de-rating, not just a smooth compounding.

## Common misconceptions

The supply-chain reorganization is wrapped in slogans that mislead investors. Four corrections, each with the number that matters.

**Misconception 1: "Reshoring is fast and cheap — declare it and it's done."** Reshoring is slow and expensive. A leading-edge semiconductor fab takes roughly **3–5 years** to build and costs **\$10–20 billion**; a rare-earth processing plant takes the better part of a decade to permit and ramp because of the environmental approvals and the technical learning curve. The US average tariff on China has sat near 19% since 2020, yet China remained a top-two US import source years later, because you cannot rebuild thirty years of supplier ecosystems in a few quarters. The trade implication: reshoring is a *multi-year* theme that rewards patience and punishes investors who expect a one-year payoff. The capital expenditure is front-loaded; the earnings come later.

**Misconception 2: "Mining is the bottleneck — fund a mine and the dependency is solved."** As the entire processing section showed, the chokepoint is *processing*, not mining. China mines ~60% of rare earths but processes ~90%, and refines ~98% of gallium it barely mines (gallium is an aluminum by-product). A new mine that ships its ore to China for refining leaves the leverage exactly where it was. The dependency breaks only when *processing and separation capacity* is built outside China — which is the harder, dirtier, more capital-intensive half of the chain, and therefore where the policy money and the durable value concentrate. Funding upstream while ignoring midstream is the most common mistake in the "critical minerals" trade.

**Misconception 3: "Supply-chain risk is transitory — it'll normalize like the post-COVID bottlenecks did."** The 2021–22 shipping and chip bottlenecks *were* largely transitory, and they did normalize. But the post-COVID congestion is a different animal from the *deliberate, policy-driven* reorganization underway now. Tariffs near 19% have persisted for over half a decade across administrations; export-control regimes are being formalized into law on both sides; the CHIPS Act and IRA are multi-year subsidy programs. These are *structural* changes to the rules of trade, not a queue at a port that clears. Treating the reorganization as transitory leads you to fade a trend that is, in fact, a regime change — and to under-price the inflation floor it puts in.

**Misconception 4: "Friend-shoring fully de-risks the supply chain — buy the beneficiaries and ignore China."** Friend-shoring spreads risk; it rarely eliminates it. Two facts puncture the slogan. First, many "friend-shored" supply chains still depend on Chinese *inputs*: a phone assembled in India or Vietnam is often built from Chinese components and minerals, so a Chinese export curb still bites — the *final-assembly* flag moved, the *upstream* dependency did not. Second, the friend-shoring hubs carry their own concentrated risks: a single transshipment crackdown, a currency-manipulator label, or a tariff threat can hit a hub like Vietnam in a single headline (its equity market fell roughly 40% from its January 2022 peak to its November 2022 trough on a domestic policy shock, showing how quickly a hot frontier market can reprice). The correction: friend-shoring is a *partial* de-risking that often just relocates the concentration, and the beneficiaries are higher-beta, not lower-risk — size them as the volatile growth exposure they are, with the China dependency mapped, not assumed away.

## How it shows up in real markets

Four repricing patterns recur. Learn to recognize them and you can position before the move completes rather than chasing it.

**1. The critical-mineral export-curb spike.** When a dominant processor restricts exports of a chokepoint mineral, the spot price of that mineral spikes hard and fast, and the equities of *ex-China* producers and processors of that mineral re-rate sharply higher on the read-through to pricing power and strategic value. The 2010 Japan episode saw some rare-earth oxide prices rise roughly tenfold; the July 2023 gallium curbs roughly doubled European gallium prices within weeks. The pattern: a violent initial spike (the chokepoint is real and there is no instant substitute), followed — crucially — by a *partial fade* as buyers destroy demand, substitute, draw down stockpiles, and bring alternative supply online (the 2010 spike substantially reversed over the following two years). The trade is to position in non-China processors *before* the next curb (the escalation ladder makes more curbs likely), and to respect that the initial commodity spike usually overshoots.

**2. The reshoring-beneficiary re-rating.** When a major subsidy is announced or a flagship reshoring project is funded, the named beneficiaries — chip-equipment makers, automation and electrical-equipment suppliers, industrial real estate (warehouses and factory sites) near the new hubs, and the friend-shoring countries' equity markets — re-rate higher, often *before* the revenue actually arrives, because markets discount the multi-year order book into today's price. Vietnam's equity market and its FDI inflows climbing to records is the country-level version of this re-rating. The catalyst is policy (a subsidy law, a flagship plant announcement); the position is the picks-and-shovels suppliers and the host-country exposure; the risk is that the announced project slips or shrinks.

**3. The resilience-cost margin hit.** The mirror image: firms *stuck* in the costly middle — those with a high-cost, hard-to-relocate China-centric supply chain, thin pricing power, and rising inventory they're financing at higher rates — see margins compress and multiples de-rate. The market increasingly distinguishes between firms that *own* a chokepoint or a friend-shored alternative (premium) and firms that are *captive* to a vulnerable single source (discount). This dispersion — winners re-rated up, captives de-rated down — is the cleanest expression of the reorganization inside a sector, and it is a relative-value (long-winner, short-captive) opportunity more than an outright directional one. (For how such structural dispersion plays out across the cycle, see [Sector Rotation Through the Cycle](/blog/trading/cross-asset/sector-rotation-through-the-cycle).)

**4. The dual-use / defense read-through.** Critical minerals sit at the intersection of the energy transition *and* national defense — the same NdPr magnet that turns an EV motor steers a guided missile, and gallium goes into both 5G base stations and military radar. So a mineral chokepoint scare reprices not just the miners but the *defense* complex that depends on secure supply, which is simultaneously enjoying a structural spending tailwind from the same elevated geopolitical risk. The aerospace-and-defense sector re-rated meaningfully higher after the February 2022 Ukraine invasion (a rebased defense index rose roughly 38% over the following three years), and a critical-minerals curb adds a second leg to that thesis: defense primes now have to secure their own mineral and magnet supply, pulling demand toward ex-China processors and toward stockpiling. The position is the secure-supply chain *into* defense — the processors, magnet makers, and qualified suppliers — rather than the broad sector alone; the catalyst is the next curb or the next conflict; the risk is that a détente deflates both the spending and the scarcity premium at once.

Underneath all three sits the macro backdrop: elevated geopolitical risk is the *condition* that keeps the reorganization going. When the geopolitical risk index is high and rising, the security-first impulse strengthens and the beneficiaries' tailwind blows harder.

![A line chart of the geopolitical risk index from 2001 to 2024 showing spikes around major shocks and an elevated level after 2022 relative to its long-run average of about one hundred](/imgs/blogs/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals-8.png)

The geopolitical risk index spiked above 500 after 9/11 and sat well above its long-run average of ~100 through the 2022 Ukraine invasion and the 2023 Middle East conflict. Sustained elevated geopolitical risk is the macro signature of a world that has decided security beats cost — and it is the regime in which the reorganization compounds rather than reverses.

#### Worked example: the inflation-floor math from a reshoring cost premium

Quantify the inflation floor with a single product. Take a household appliance that costs **\$300** at retail, of which **\$180** is the imported cost of goods (the rest is shipping, retail markup, and margin). Under the cost-first model the \$180 came from the cheapest global source. Reshoring or friend-shoring that production raises the manufacturing cost by, say, **15%** — a representative premium for moving from the lowest-cost geography to a friendly higher-cost one. The \$180 input becomes `$180 × 1.15 = $207`, an extra **\$27** per unit. If the retailer passes it through proportionally, the shelf price rises from \$300 to about \$327 — a **9% price increase** baked into that one good, permanently, not as a one-time spike but as a higher base.

Now scale the logic: if goods that make up, say, 20% of a consumer's basket carry a 15% reshoring cost premium passed through in full, the *direct* contribution to the price level is roughly `20% × 15% = 3%` spread over the multi-year relocation, on top of whatever else is happening. That is the inflation floor in arithmetic: de-globalization adds a few points to goods prices that the cost-first era was *subtracting* each year. Put plainly: you don't need a crisis for the reorganization to matter to inflation — the steady, deliberate substitution of resilience for cost is itself mildly inflationary, which is why "structurally higher baseline inflation" is a core implication of the whole theme.

## The playbook: how to read and trade the great unbundling

Pull it together into something you can act on. The reorganization is a multi-year structural theme; the playbook is about positioning for a regime, then using events to add and trim.

**Step 1 — Map the chokepoints to your exposure.** For any portfolio holding or candidate, ask: *where in its supply chain does it depend on a single country or a single processor that could be cut off?* The high-value chokepoints to watch are rare-earth/gallium/germanium/graphite *processing* (China-dominated), advanced-chip fabrication (Taiwan-concentrated, see the companion chip-war post), and any input where one government controls the refining stage. A holding that depends on a chokepoint it cannot quickly substitute is carrying an unpriced tail risk; a holding that *owns* a chokepoint or a credible alternative is carrying an unpriced option.

**Step 2 — Position in the beneficiaries, on the right side of the chain.** The durable winners cluster in four buckets:
- *Non-China mineral processors and recyclers* — the firms building separation and refining capacity outside China (this is where the policy money and the chokepoint value sit — favor processing over pure mining).
- *Reshoring picks-and-shovels* — chip-equipment makers, factory automation, electrical equipment, engineering and construction, and industrial real estate near the new hubs. This captures the theme with less single-project risk than betting on one fab.
- *Friend-shoring host countries* — Mexico, Vietnam, India equity and FDI exposure, sized for their specific risks (see Step 4).
- *Stockpile and defense demand* — the structural buyers of chokepoint minerals.

**Step 3 — Price the inflation floor into the macro book.** If goods disinflation has a higher floor than the 2010s, then the regime favors *real* assets and pricing-power businesses over long-duration assets that need ever-falling inflation to justify their valuation. The reorganization is a reason to expect a structurally higher *baseline* for rates and inflation than the pre-2020 world — a tilt toward commodities, real assets, and companies that can pass through costs, and away from assets priced for permanent disinflation. (How a higher inflation regime reshapes cross-asset correlations is covered in [Correlation by Regime: Growth and Inflation](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-by-regime-growth-and-inflation).)

**Step 4 — Add and trim on the recurring events.** Use the predictable catalysts:
- *A new export curb* → the named mineral's price spikes and ex-China processors re-rate. Respect the overshoot — the initial commodity spike usually fades, so favor the durable equity beneficiaries over chasing the spot spike, and remember the escalation ladder makes the *next* curb likely.
- *A subsidy announcement or flagship project* → the named beneficiaries re-rate, often before revenue. Position ahead of, not after, the policy.
- *A transshipment/origin crackdown* → the implicated friend-shoring hub's exporters de-rate. This is the tail risk that invalidates the naive "Vietnam wins everything" thesis.

**Step 5 — Know what invalidates the view.** The reorganization thesis weakens or reverses if: tariffs are durably rolled back and the China cost penalty shrinks toward the rest-of-world level; a genuine geopolitical détente lowers the geopolitical risk index back toward its long-run average and persistently; China removes its mineral curbs as a goodwill gesture (collapsing the chokepoint premium); or a recession destroys enough demand that excess capacity, not security of supply, becomes the binding problem. Watch the tariff level, the geopolitical risk index, the cadence of export curbs, and the inventory-to-sales trend. If those roll over together and *stay* rolled over, the security-first regime is fading and the beneficiaries lose their tailwind. Absent that, the base case is that the great unbundling keeps compounding — and so do its winners, its captives, and its inflation floor.

A note on *how* to express the view, because the right instrument matters as much as the right direction. This is a slow, multi-year structural trade, not a fast event trade, which argues for *owning* the beneficiaries through the build-out rather than trying to scalp each headline. Three practical refinements. First, prefer the *relative-value* expression — long the secure-supply winners (ex-China processors, picks-and-shovels capital goods, friend-shore hosts) against short or underweight the captives (single-source, China-dependent, thin-margin importers) — because the dispersion *between* winners and captives is more reliable than the direction of the whole market, which is buffeted by rates, growth, and everything else. Second, size the friend-shore-host exposure (Vietnam, frontier markets) as the high-beta growth sleeve it is, with a hard stop on the transshipment/origin tail risk, rather than as a core holding. Third, because the mineral-curb spikes overshoot and fade, treat a spot-commodity spike as a *signal to trim*, not chase — the durable money is in the equity beneficiaries that compound across many curbs, not in the metal price that round-trips after each one. Match the instrument to the horizon and the trade survives the inevitable headfakes along a decade-long theme.

The deeper point, and the one that ties this post to the rest of the series: a supply chain is a set of *rules* about who supplies what to whom, and those rules are now written as much in trade law, export-control regimes, and industrial-policy statutes as in cost spreadsheets. When the rules of supply change, prices reorganize around the new rules — and the investor who maps the chokepoints early, sizes the repricing, and knows what would invalidate the view is positioned for the most durable structural trade of the decade. (For the master framework of how a rule becomes a price, see [How Law Moves Markets: The Transmission Chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain).)
