> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The dollar's grip on the world's reserves, trade invoicing and funding gives America an "exorbitant privilege" — cheaper borrowing and a sanctions superweapon — but using that weapon in 2022 gave the rest of the world a reason to look for the exits; the catch is that there is nowhere to go yet.
>
> - The dollar is roughly **58% of global FX reserves**, the invoice currency for about **half of world trade**, and one side of nearly **88% of all FX trades** — three overlapping monopolies, not one.
> - That privilege is worth real money: foreign demand for Treasuries shaves an estimated **0.5–1 percentage point** off US borrowing costs, a saving of **\$130–260bn a year** on a ~\$26T marketable debt stock.
> - The 2022 freeze of **\$300bn of Russia's \$643bn reserves** — 47% switched off overnight — is the trigger of the modern de-dollarization debate. It made every reserve manager ask: *could that be me?*
> - De-dollarization needs three things at once — a deep safe asset, open payment rails, and crisis liquidity. **No rival clears all three.** So the dollar's store-of-value role leaks slowly (mostly into gold), while its trade and FX roles stay nearly intact.
> - The one number to remember: the dollar's reserve share has drifted down only about **one percentage point a year** for two decades. That is a slow leak, not a burst dam.

On 26 February 2022, two days after Russian tanks crossed into Ukraine, the G7 did something that had never been done to a major economy: it froze roughly half of a large central bank's foreign reserves with the stroke of a regulatory pen. About **\$300bn** of Russia's roughly **\$643bn** war chest — money held as dollars, euros, yen and pounds in accounts at Western central banks and custodians — simply stopped being usable. No physical seizure, no court case, no warning. The assets were still "there"; Russia just could no longer touch them.

That single act answered a question most people had never thought to ask: *what does it actually mean to "have" reserves if another government holds the keys?* For decades the world had treated dollar reserves as the safest, most liquid money on earth. In one afternoon, every finance minister and central-bank governor outside the Western bloc learned that "safe" had a political asterisk. The freeze did not just punish Russia; it advertised, to anyone who might one day fall out with Washington, exactly how the financial weapon works. And so the modern de-dollarization debate was born — not from an economic theory, but from a legal action.

This post is about how much of that threat is real. It is fashionable to say the dollar is "about to collapse" and equally fashionable to say nothing is changing at all. Both are wrong, and the truth is more interesting: the dollar's dominance is built on three separate monopolies that erode at completely different speeds, and reading the difference is the whole trade. We will define what a reserve currency is from zero, build the network-effect moat that makes the dollar so sticky, weigh each would-be rival against what it would actually take to replace the dollar, and finish with how a practitioner separates the loud narrative from the quiet flow.

That split — what a foreign government can freeze versus what it cannot — is the seed of everything that follows.

## Foundations: what a reserve currency is and why the dollar won

Start with the thing itself. A **reserve currency** is money that governments and central banks outside the issuing country choose to *hold* as their official savings, and that businesses everywhere choose to *use* for cross-border deals even when neither side is American. It is the money the world defaults to when it does not want to think about which money to use. There is no law that crowns a reserve currency; it is a market outcome, the way English became the language of air-traffic control — convenient because everyone else already uses it.

A currency earns global-reserve status by doing four distinct jobs at once. Keep them separate in your head, because they break apart at different speeds and that is the key to the whole de-dollarization question.

**Role one: the reserve asset.** When a central bank runs a trade surplus or wants a rainy-day fund, it parks the proceeds in safe foreign assets — overwhelmingly short-term government debt. The dollar's share of these official reserves, tracked by the IMF's COFER survey, sits near **58%**, down from about 71% in 2000 but still more than triple the euro's roughly 20%. (COFER is the standard source for reserve shares; this series' data module does not carry the COFER time series, so the share figures here are drawn from the published IMF survey and labeled as such.)

**Role two: the invoicing and trade unit.** When a Korean firm buys oil from Saudi Arabia or a Brazilian exporter sells soybeans to China, the contract is very often written in dollars even though no American is involved. Roughly **half of all world trade** is invoiced in dollars — a share several times larger than the US share of world trade itself. Pricing a deal in dollars means both sides quote, hedge and settle in the same deep currency instead of a thin local one.

**Role three: the funding and debt-issuance currency.** Companies and governments around the world borrow in dollars because that is where the deepest pool of lenders sits. The stock of dollar-denominated debt owed by borrowers *outside* the US runs well past **\$13 trillion**. When global stress hits, those borrowers all scramble for dollars to service that debt at once — which is why the dollar paradoxically *rises* in a crisis that starts in America.

**Role four: FX-market turnover.** In the daily foreign-exchange market — about **\$7.5 trillion changing hands every day** — the dollar is on one side of nearly **88%** of all trades (out of 200%, since every trade has two sides). Even to swap Thai baht for Swedish krona, the cheapest route is usually baht → dollars → krona, because the direct baht/krona market is too thin.

Those four roles are not a list; they are a machine, each gear turning the others. That mutual reinforcement is exactly what the cover figure above lays out, and it is why no single reform can dislodge the dollar — you would have to break all four gears at once.

The gears interlock in a specific direction. Because the dollar is the reserve asset, central banks hold Treasuries, which deepens the Treasury market; because that market is the deepest, it is the cheapest place in the world to borrow, which pulls in the funding role; because so much is borrowed in dollars, trade gets invoiced in dollars to match the debt; because trade is invoiced in dollars, the FX market needed to settle it is overwhelmingly dollar-based; and because the FX market is dollar-based, the safest reserve to hold is — once more — the dollar. Follow the loop and you arrive back where you started, one turn stronger. A reform that attacks any single gear (say, a new payment rail that dents invoicing) leaves the other three spinning, and they drag the attacked gear back into line. This is why de-dollarization advocates who win one battle — a bilateral oil deal here, a new clearing system there — keep finding the war unchanged: the machine has redundancy built into its very structure.

### Why the dollar, specifically?

Three structural facts explain why these roles concentrated in *this* currency rather than splitting across several.

First, the **deepest, most liquid safe-asset market on earth**: the US Treasury market, roughly **\$28 trillion** of marketable debt that trades all day, every day, in size, with razor-thin spreads. A reserve is only useful if you can sell a lot of it fast without moving the price. No other government bond market comes close to that combination of size and liquidity.

Second, **rule of law and a (mostly) hands-off attitude toward foreign holders**. For seventy years the implicit deal was: park your money in dollars and the US will not expropriate it, freeze it, or block you from spending it. That promise — credible because America had never broken it at scale — is precisely what 2022 dented.

Third, **no rival of scale**. The eurozone is large but has no single common bond — there is no "euro-Treasury," just nineteen separate national bond markets of varying quality, from rock-solid German Bunds to far riskier peripheral debt. A reserve manager who wants to hold euros must choose *which* eurozone government to lend to, and there is no single, homogeneous, trillion-scale safe asset to default to. China is large but keeps its capital account closed. Japan is rich but small, and the Bank of Japan owns so much of its own bond market that the free float is thin. Gold has no issuer to freeze it but pays no yield and cannot move at the speed of modern payments. Every alternative fails a different essential test, which is the entire reason the dollar endures.

It helps to ground this in the one time it actually happened. The British pound sterling was the world's reserve currency through the nineteenth century and into the twentieth — the gold standard ran through London, sterling invoiced global trade, and the Bank of England set the rhythm of world finance. The dollar overtook it across roughly **1914 to 1945**. Note how long that took, and what it took: two world wars that bankrupted Britain and made it a net debtor; the rise of the United States as the largest creditor and manufacturing economy on earth; and the deliberate construction at Bretton Woods in 1944 of a new dollar-centered monetary order with a rival standing ready. Even then, sterling lingered as a major reserve currency into the 1950s. The lesson is not that reserve currencies are eternal — they are not — but that they turn over on a timescale of *decades*, triggered by the incumbent's catastrophic self-harm and only when a credible successor is already built. That is the historical base rate against which every "imminent collapse" headline should be measured.

### The exorbitant privilege

Put the four roles together and you get what a French finance minister in the 1960s, Valéry Giscard d'Estaing, sourly called America's **"exorbitant privilege."** Because the world *needs* dollars — to hold as reserves, to invoice trade, to service debt — there is permanent, price-insensitive demand for dollar assets, especially Treasuries. That standing bid does two things for the United States.

It lets America **borrow more cheaply than its fundamentals alone would justify.** Foreign central banks buy Treasuries because they need a safe dollar asset, not because they are chasing yield, so they push Treasury prices up and yields down regardless of the US fiscal position. And it hands Washington a **financial weapon**: because so much of the world's trade and finance must touch a US bank or a dollar-clearing system at some point, the US can cut a target off from the dollar and thereby from the global economy. The same dominance that lowers borrowing costs is also the lever behind every sanction. The privilege and the weapon are the same fact seen from two sides.

#### Worked example: the exorbitant privilege in dollars

Take the US marketable debt stock at roughly **\$26 trillion** (the figure has since climbed past \$28T; use \$26T as a round, conservative base). Empirical estimates of the "convenience yield" — the extra price investors pay for the safety and liquidity of Treasuries, much of it driven by foreign reserve demand — cluster around **0.5 to 1.0 percentage point** of yield. Take the conservative end, **0.5%** (50 basis points).

The annual interest saving is the spread times the stock:

- `0.50% × $26,000bn = $130bn per year`

At the upper estimate of **1.0%**:

- `1.00% × $26,000bn = $260bn per year`

So reserve-currency status is worth on the order of **\$130–260bn a year** in lower interest costs to the US Treasury — roughly the entire annual budget of a mid-sized federal department, handed over every year because the world needs somewhere safe to park dollars. That is the privilege in cash terms, and it is why even a slow erosion matters: each tenth of a percentage point of lost convenience yield is ~\$26bn a year of higher US borrowing costs.

The weapon side of the privilege deserves the same precision, because it is the part that 2022 changed. The mechanism is the **dollar-clearing chokepoint**. Almost every cross-border dollar payment, no matter who is on either end, ultimately settles through a US bank or through CHIPS (the Clearing House Interbank Payments System), which is supervised under US law. The Treasury's sanctions arm, OFAC, can therefore order US banks not to process payments touching a named target — and because there is no practical way to make a large dollar payment that *avoids* the US banking system entirely, the target is cut off not just from America but from the dollar-denominated half of the world economy. A "secondary sanction" extends the threat: a foreign bank that keeps dealing with the target risks losing *its own* dollar access, which for a global bank is corporate death. That is why sanctions bite far beyond US borders — the chokepoint is the dollar's plumbing, not America's geography. The freeze of Russia's reserves was the same lever applied to a sovereign's savings rather than a firm's payments, and it is the demonstration of that lever — not any economic shift — that put de-dollarization back on the table. (For the legal machinery of a single designation, see [sanctions law, OFAC, the SDN list and secondary sanctions](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sanctions-law-ofac-the-sdn-list-and-secondary-sanctions).)

### The petrodollar and the Triffin dilemma

Two pieces of plumbing finish the foundation. The first is the **petrodollar**: the long-standing convention, rooted in 1970s US–Saudi arrangements, that oil is priced and settled in dollars worldwide. Because every country needs oil and oil costs dollars, every country needs a dollar reserve — which recycled oil-export surpluses straight back into Treasuries. The petrodollar is often overstated as a *cause* of dollar dominance; it is better understood as one large, self-reinforcing application of the invoicing role. (For the full mechanism, see [the petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance).)

The second is the **Triffin dilemma**, named for economist Robert Triffin, who diagnosed it in the early 1960s while the world was still on the Bretton Woods dollar-gold peg. To supply the world with the dollars it wants to hold as reserves, the US must run persistent deficits — it must send more dollars out than it takes in. But running deficits forever eventually erodes confidence in the very currency everyone is hoarding. The reserve issuer is trapped: supply the world's reserve asset *and* undermine your own currency's long-run credibility, or defend the currency and starve the world of reserves. Triffin predicted this tension would eventually break the gold peg, and it did — in 1971, when President Nixon closed the gold window because foreign dollar claims had grown far larger than US gold holdings. The dilemma did not destroy the dollar; it forced the system to evolve from gold-backed to confidence-backed. That history is the template for de-dollarization: the dilemma is real but slow, and it tends to reshape the dollar system rather than end it. It sets the outer boundary of how long any reserve regime can last, not next quarter's exchange rate.

### What de-dollarization would actually require

Now define the thing whose threat we are weighing. **De-dollarization** means the world meaningfully reducing its reliance on the dollar across those four roles. For it to happen at scale, a rival must offer three things *simultaneously*:

1. **A deep, safe store of value** — a large pool of low-risk assets a reserve manager can hold and sell in size, the equivalent of the Treasury market.
2. **Open, trusted payment rails** — a way to move and settle large sums across borders that the rest of the world will accept, the equivalent of dollar clearing through CHIPS and the SWIFT messaging layer.
3. **Crisis liquidity** — the ability to convert the asset into spendable money *fast, in size, exactly when you need it most*, which is the whole point of holding a reserve.

A reserve currency is only as good as its worst day. Hold something you cannot sell in a panic and you do not have a reserve; you have a souvenir. As we will see, every candidate to replace the dollar nails one or two of these requirements and fails at least one — which is why the threat is real at the margin and overstated at the headline.

It is worth being explicit about why all three must hold *together*, because most de-dollarization arguments quietly assume that solving one solves the problem. A deep safe asset with no rails is a vault you cannot wire money out of. Open rails with no deep safe asset is a fast pipe connected to a shallow pool — you can move money quickly but there is nowhere large and safe to put it. And both of those with no crisis liquidity is a system that works beautifully right up until the day you actually need it, then fails. The dollar's dominance is not that it is best at any *one* of these; it is the only currency that clears all three at once and at scale. A challenger that wins on a single axis — gold on safety, CIPS on rails, the euro on openness — is competing on one leg of a three-legged stool. That framing is what the rest of this post applies to each rival in turn.

## The network-effect moat: why incumbency is so sticky

The deepest reason the dollar endures is not American power or oil; it is a **network effect**. A network effect means a thing becomes more useful to each user precisely *because* other users already use it — the way a telephone is worthless if you are the only one who owns one and indispensable once everyone does. Money is the purest network good there is: a currency is valuable to *you* only to the extent that the *next* person will accept it.

![Diagram of the reserve currency network effect where each dollar user makes the dollar deeper and cheaper for the next](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization-2.png)

Trace the loop in the figure above. Because everyone already holds dollars, the Treasury market is the deepest on earth and global trade is priced in dollars. Depth and ubiquity make the dollar the cheapest, most liquid currency to actually use — tightest spreads, most counterparties, easiest hedging. That cheapness pulls in the *next* user, who picks the dollar for the same reason everyone before them did, which deepens the market further. The loop feeds itself. And the flip side is brutal for any challenger: a rival currency has no scale, so it is expensive and illiquid to use, so nobody picks it, so it never gets scale. The incumbent is defended not by anyone's decision but by everyone's.

This is why de-dollarization cannot happen the way people picture a bank run — a sudden stampede for the exits. To switch, a critical mass of users would have to move *at the same time*, each one accepting the high cost of using a thin currency in the hope that enough others do too. That is a coordination problem of staggering difficulty. Network monopolies do fall — sterling lost reserve primacy to the dollar across roughly the first half of the twentieth century — but they fall over *decades*, usually only after the incumbent issuer suffers a catastrophic, self-inflicted loss of credibility (two world wars and bankruptcy, in Britain's case), and only when a credible rival of equal scale is already standing ready (the US, in that case). Today there is no such rival standing ready, which is the single most important fact in this entire debate.

The economics of the switch are worth spelling out, because they explain why even a country that *wants* to leave the dollar struggles to. Take a single exporter weighing whether to invoice its next contract in dollars or in a regional rival. Invoicing in dollars costs almost nothing — every bank quotes it, every counterparty accepts it, hedging is cheap. Invoicing in the rival costs more on every dimension: a wider spread, fewer banks that will handle it, harder hedging, and the risk of being stuck holding a currency nobody else wants. The *private* cost of switching is borne entirely by the exporter, while the *benefit* (a more multipolar currency system) is a diffuse public good shared by everyone. Whenever private cost is concentrated and the benefit is diffuse, the rational individual choice is to keep using the dollar and free-ride on the hope that others switch first. Multiply that across millions of firms and you get a system that nobody loves and everybody uses — the textbook signature of a coordination trap. Governments can try to override it by *mandating* local-currency settlement, and some do, but a mandate that forces firms to bear a real cost runs into evasion, gray markets, and the simple fact that the foreign counterparty still wants dollars. The moat is not a wall someone built; it is the sum of millions of individually rational choices, which is exactly why it is so hard to breach.

There is a second, subtler reason incumbency sticks: **denomination matching**. A firm or country that has borrowed in dollars must earn or hold dollars to repay that debt — otherwise a move in the exchange rate can wipe it out. Because there is \$13 trillion-plus of dollar debt outside the US, there is a vast standing population of borrowers who *need* dollars regardless of what any government prefers, and who therefore keep trade invoiced in dollars and reserves held in dollars to match their liabilities. De-dollarizing the *asset* side (reserves) while the *liability* side (debt) stays in dollars would leave a country dangerously mismatched. So the debt role drags the reserve and invoicing roles along behind it. To truly de-dollarize, the world would have to stop *borrowing* in dollars first — and that is the gear that turns slowest of all, because the dollar bond market is where the cheapest, deepest lending lives.

#### Worked example: the liquidity cost of invoicing in a thin currency

Put the moat in dollars. Take a commodity exporter who must convert **\$500m** of sale proceeds from a foreign buyer's local currency back into something usable. Compare two routes.

**Dollar rail.** The buyer pays in dollars. The exporter sells \$500m in the deep FX market at a bid-offer spread of, say, **2 basis points** (0.02%), typical for a major dollar pair:

- `0.02% × $500,000,000 = $100,000` in spread cost.

**Bypass rail, thin currency.** The buyer insists on paying in its own soft, lightly-traded currency. To convert \$500m-equivalent in a market that thin, the exporter faces a spread of perhaps **80 basis points** (0.80%) and meaningful slippage moving that size:

- `0.80% × $500,000,000 = $4,000,000` in spread cost.

The bypass route costs **\$3.9m more on a single \$500m flow** — a 40x penalty — purely because the thin currency lacks the dollar's liquidity. Multiply that across a year of trade and you have the exporter's entire profit margin. *This is the network moat as a cash cost: the dollar is not cheaper because of politics; it is cheaper because it is deep, and depth is exactly what a challenger cannot conjure overnight.*

## The challengers and their limits

With the moat clear, weigh each candidate against the three requirements. The verdict, laid out in the matrix below, is that every rival flunks at least one.

![Matrix comparing the dollar euro yuan gold and a BRICS unit against three reserve currency requirements](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization-4.png)

### The yuan and the BRICS ambition

The most-hyped challenger is China's renminbi (the yuan). The ambition is genuine: China is the world's largest trading nation and second-largest economy, it has built its own cross-border payment system (**CIPS**, the Cross-Border Interbank Payment System), and the **BRICS** bloc periodically floats the idea of a shared trading currency to escape the dollar.

But the yuan runs straight into a wall its own government built: **capital controls**. China does not allow money to flow freely in and out of its borders. A foreign central bank that holds a large yuan reserve cannot freely move that money out in a crisis — the very moment it most needs to. And a reserve you cannot exit on your worst day is not a reserve. This is not an accident China can fix with a payment system; it is structural. Beijing keeps the capital account closed precisely to control its exchange rate and domestic credit, and opening it would mean surrendering that control. So the yuan is stuck: it cannot be a true reserve currency *and* stay behind capital controls, and China will not give up the controls. (For the full legal mechanics of why a closed capital account caps reserve status, see [capital controls and the legal limits on money flows](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/capital-controls-and-the-legal-limits-on-money-flows).)

The yuan's share of global reserves reflects this: roughly **2%**, and it has actually *fallen* recently as reserve managers grew wary of the same custody risk that the dollar now carries — yuan reserves can in principle be frozen by Beijing just as dollar reserves were frozen by Washington. Swapping one political custodian for another is not diversification; it is trading a custodian you understand for one you trust less.

CIPS itself is smaller and more dependent than the headlines suggest. It processes a tiny fraction of the volume that flows through the dollar-clearing system, and — crucially — most CIPS transactions still rely on the SWIFT messaging network for the instructions that ride alongside the settlement. SWIFT is a Belgium-based cooperative, not American, but it operates within reach of Western pressure, as the 2022 expulsion of major Russian banks demonstrated. A payment rail that still leans on the incumbent messaging layer has not truly escaped it. Russia's own answer, the SPFS messaging system, exists but is used almost entirely within Russia and a handful of partners — a parallel network with almost no one on it, which is the network effect working in reverse: a messaging system is worthless until everyone joins, and everyone has not joined. The rails are being built, but a rail with little traffic is not yet a route, and building the traffic runs straight back into the coordination trap described above.

A shared **BRICS currency** is even further off. It would need a common safe asset (none exists), shared payment rails the members trust (they do not — India and China are rivals), and a way to settle imbalances among economies with wildly different inflation and credit (no mechanism). The euro took forty years of legal and institutional integration among friendly democracies and still lacks a common bond. A BRICS unit among rivals with closed capital accounts is, for now, a narrative — useful at summits, absent from balance sheets.

### The euro: the credible runner-up that cannot finish

The euro is the only currency that clears two of the three requirements outright: it has open capital flows and deep crisis liquidity, and it is held in size — roughly **20%** of global reserves, comfortably the world's number two. It is a real, credible reserve currency, used and trusted across the world. So why has it never threatened the dollar's primacy in the twenty-five years since its launch?

The answer is the missing safe asset. There is no "euro-Treasury." Instead there are separate national bond markets — German Bunds, French OATs, Italian BTPs, and so on — each with its own credit risk. A reserve manager who wants to hold euros must decide which government to lend to, and the high-quality, genuinely safe slice (chiefly German Bunds) is far too small to absorb global reserve demand. The eurozone debt crisis of 2010–2012 made the danger vivid: peripheral government bonds that everyone had treated as safe suddenly were not, and the euro itself nearly fractured. Until the eurozone issues a large, jointly-guaranteed common bond — politically blocked for decades because creditor nations refuse to backstop debtor nations — the euro is a currency without a unified safe asset at Treasury scale. It can be a strong number two forever and never become number one. The euro is the proof that *openness and liquidity are necessary but not sufficient*: without a deep, homogeneous safe asset, even a wealthy, trusted, freely-traded currency stalls out at a fifth of reserves.

### Gold: the neutral reserve

The one genuine winner from the weaponization of the dollar is **gold**, and the reason is precise: gold is the only major reserve asset with **no issuer to freeze it**. A dollar is a claim on the US system; a euro is a claim on the ECB system; even yuan reserves sit inside China's system. Gold held in your own domestic vault is a claim on no one — no foreign government can switch it off with a regulatory pen. After 2022, that property became suddenly, urgently valuable.

The flow is visible in the data: central banks have been net buyers of gold at a pace above **1,000 tonnes a year** for several years running — a multi-decade high, led by emerging-market central banks (China, Turkey, India, Poland) that are precisely the ones most exposed to the custody risk the freeze exposed. (This series' data module does not carry the official-sector gold-purchase time series; the ~1,000-tonnes-a-year figure is drawn from the World Gold Council's published Central Bank Gold surveys and is labeled conceptual here.)

But gold solves only *one* of the three requirements. It is an excellent store of value with no custody risk — and it is useless as a medium of exchange. You cannot wire gold to settle the day's \$7.5 trillion of FX turnover; it pays no yield; it costs money to store, insure, and transport; and selling it in size during a panic moves the price against you. So gold absorbs the *store-of-value* anxiety the freeze created without touching the dollar's *transactional* roles at all. The gold bid is the single most honest signal of real de-dollarization — and it is a signal about exactly one of the four roles.

#### Worked example: a central bank shifting reserves from dollars to gold

Put the diversification in dollars and tonnes. Take a central bank holding **\$200bn** of reserves that decides — post-freeze — to move **5%** into gold over a year:

- `5% × $200,000,000,000 = $10bn` rotated into gold.

At a gold price of roughly **\$2,000/oz** (≈ \$64.3m per tonne, since one tonne ≈ 32,150 troy ounces):

- `$10,000,000,000 ÷ $64,300,000 per tonne ≈ 156 tonnes` bought.

Now scale it up. If the official sector as a whole buys **1,000 tonnes** in a year, that is roughly **\$64bn** of demand — meaningful against annual mine supply of about **3,600 tonnes** (it absorbs nearly a *fifth* of new supply) but a rounding error against the **\$12-trillion-plus** stock of global FX reserves. *That asymmetry is the whole story: central-bank gold buying is large enough to move the gold price and bid it to records, yet far too small to dent the dollar's transactional dominance — a big trade in a small tank.*

### Alternative rails: CIPS and mBridge

The transactional challenge is being attacked from a different angle: **payment rails** that route around the dollar-clearing system. China's CIPS lets banks settle yuan payments without touching a US correspondent bank. **mBridge** is a multi-central-bank project (China, Hong Kong, Thailand, the UAE and others) to settle cross-border payments directly in digital central-bank money, skipping the dollar leg entirely. And bilateral local-currency trade deals — India paying for Russian oil in rupees, Brazil and China settling some trade in yuan and reais — chip at invoicing at the margin.

These are real and they matter, but the figure below shows the trade they make.

![Before and after diagram contrasting the deep dollar payment rail with a thin bypass rail in local currency](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization-7.png)

The dollar rail clears against the deepest market on earth, so spreads are tiny and the currency you end up holding is universally usable. The bypass rail escapes the chokepoint — no US bank can block it — but it dumps you into a thin currency you cannot easily redeploy. India discovered this concretely: it ended up with billions of rupees sitting in Russian accounts that Russia struggled to spend, because rupees buy little outside India. Escaping the dollar's *reach* means accepting the soft currency's *thinness*. The rails reduce the sanctions chokepoint; they do not reduce the dollar's liquidity advantage, which is why they grow at the margin of trade rather than replacing the core.

## The slow-grind vs sudden-break scenarios

Pull the threads together and there are exactly two ways the dollar could lose primacy. The scenario tree below separates them, and the separation is the whole point.

![Tree diagram contrasting a slow decades long grind path with a sudden crisis break path for the dollar](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization-5.png)

The **slow-grind** branch is what the data actually shows. The dollar's reserve share drips down by roughly one percentage point a year — into the euro, into a handful of smaller currencies (the Canadian and Australian dollars, the yuan at the margin), and most visibly into gold. This is real de-dollarization, but it is a *diversification within the system*, not an exit from it. At one percentage point a year, getting from 58% to, say, 50% takes the better part of a decade, and the dollar is still the dominant reserve currency the entire time.

The **sudden-break** branch is the one that fills headlines and almost never happens. A fast break would require either a catastrophic loss of faith in the dollar — a US default, runaway inflation, a constitutional crisis that calls Treasury repayment into question — *and* a credible, liquid rival standing ready to absorb the flight. Both conditions, together. The first is a tail risk the US can mostly control through its own policy. The second simply does not exist today: there is no rival with a Treasury-sized safe-asset market and an open capital account. A break with nowhere to break *to* is not a break; it is a panic that ends back in dollars, which is exactly what every modern crisis — 2008, 2020, 2022 — has produced.

So the honest framing is the one the sibling post on the 2022 sanctions used: take the dollar system as **three tanks, not one**. The store-of-value tank is leaking visibly into gold and at the margin into other currencies. The medium-of-exchange and unit-of-account tanks are nearly intact, defended by the network effect rather than by any promise of safety. Anyone trading "de-dollarization" must know which tank they are betting on. (For the four-weapons view of how the freeze itself worked, see [the 2022 Russia sanctions and the weaponization of finance](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance).)

### What it would mean if the privilege eroded

Suppose the slow grind accelerates — say foreign reserve demand for Treasuries falls meaningfully. What breaks? The exorbitant privilege runs in reverse: with less standing, price-insensitive demand for Treasuries, yields would have to rise to attract price-*sensitive* buyers (yield-chasers and US domestic investors), raising US borrowing costs and, through the discount rate, weighing on every dollar asset. A weaker structural bid for the dollar would also let it drift lower against other majors, which is inflationary for the US (imports cost more). None of this is a collapse; it is a gradual tightening of the financial conditions America has enjoyed for free.

Trace the chain one link further, because it is the whole reason this matters to a portfolio rather than just to the Treasury. The 10-year Treasury yield is not just America's borrowing cost; it is the **risk-free rate that anchors the price of nearly every financial asset on earth.** Equities are valued by discounting future earnings at a rate built on the 10-year. Corporate bonds price as the Treasury yield plus a credit spread. Mortgages, emerging-market debt, even private-equity hurdle rates all sit on top of it. So a structural rise in Treasury yields driven by fading reserve demand does not stay contained in Washington's interest bill — it lifts the floor under *every* discount rate, compressing valuations across stocks, credit, and real estate simultaneously. This is the second-order effect the collapse-mongers miss in one direction and the nothing-is-happening crowd miss in the other: the privilege eroding is not a dramatic dollar crash, but a slow, broad, grinding repricing of the entire global cost of capital. It is the kind of regime shift that does not announce itself on any single day, which is precisely why it is dangerous to a portfolio positioned for the world that came before it.

There is also a fiscal feedback loop worth naming. Higher borrowing costs raise the US deficit (more interest to pay), a larger deficit means more Treasury issuance, more issuance with less foreign demand means still-higher yields — a self-reinforcing spiral that, if it ever took hold, would be the financial-market expression of the Triffin dilemma finally biting. The US is nowhere near that today; the foreign bid is intact and the dollar still rallies in every crisis. But it is the mechanism every serious de-dollarization analyst is actually watching for, and it is why the *rate of change* of the reserve share — not its level — is the number that matters.

#### Worked example: the rate impact if reserve demand for Treasuries fell

Put a number on the reverse privilege. Suppose a chunk of foreign reserve demand evaporates and the convenience yield Treasuries enjoy shrinks by **25 basis points** (0.25%) — a plausible slow-grind increment, not a crash. On the ~\$26 trillion marketable debt stock, as that debt rolls over at the higher yield:

- `0.25% × $26,000bn = $65bn per year` of additional interest cost, building up as maturing debt is refinanced.

Now feel it as an investor. A 25bp rise in the structural level of Treasury yields drops the price of a 10-year note with a duration of about **8.5** by:

- `−8.5 × 0.25% = −2.1%` price decline on that bond.

And because the 10-year Treasury yield is the discount rate under every risk asset, a higher *structural* yield compresses equity valuations too: a market on a 20x earnings multiple (a 5% earnings yield) re-rating to demand an extra 25bp of return would see its fair multiple fall by roughly `0.25 / 5.25 ≈ 5%`, all else equal. *The privilege eroding is not a dollar crash you trade in a week; it is a slow grind higher in the world's discount rate that you position for over years — which is precisely why it is so easy to ignore and so expensive to ignore.*

## Common misconceptions

**"The dollar is about to collapse."** The collapse thesis sells newsletters and gold coins, but the share data refuses to cooperate. The dollar's reserve share has fallen from about 71% in 2000 to about 58% today — a decline of roughly **13 percentage points over 24 years**, or about **half a point a year**. That is the pace of a glacier, not an avalanche. And critically, the dollar's *transactional* dominance (88% of FX turnover, half of trade invoicing) has barely moved at all, because those roles are defended by the network effect rather than by trust in US policy. "Collapse" requires a stampede; the data shows a stroll.

**"A BRICS currency will replace the dollar."** This confuses an announcement with an instrument. A reserve currency needs a deep safe asset, open capital flows, and crisis liquidity, and a BRICS unit has **none of the three** — no common bond market, no shared rails the members trust, capital accounts that are mostly closed. The euro, built among friendly democracies over forty years of deep legal integration, *still* lacks a single common safe asset at Treasury scale and tops out near **20%** of reserves. A currency union among geopolitical rivals with closed accounts is not on the same planet as that. The headline is a summit communiqué; the balance sheets do not show it.

**"De-dollarization isn't happening at all."** The opposite error. It *is* happening — just slowly and in one specific tank. Central banks have bought gold at over **1,000 tonnes a year**, a multi-decade record, and the dollar's reserve share has genuinely drifted down. Dismissing this entirely means missing the single best real-money signal in the whole debate: the official-sector gold bid is a measurable vote of reduced confidence in dollar *custody*, even as confidence in dollar *liquidity* stays intact. The truth is in the middle, and the middle is where the trade lives.

## How it shows up in real markets

The de-dollarization story is mostly narrative, but it leaves real fingerprints in flows and prices. Four show up reliably.

**The central-bank gold bid.** This is the cleanest signal. Official-sector gold buying running above 1,000 tonnes a year, concentrated in emerging-market central banks, is the store-of-value tank leaking in real time. It is a slow, structural, price-supportive force under gold — not a catalyst that spikes on a given day, but a persistent bid that helps explain why gold made repeated record highs even as real US yields rose (a combination that historically would have *capped* gold). When the textbook relationship between gold and real yields breaks, the official bid is usually the reason. (For how the safe-haven bid behaves across regimes, see the [gold](/blog/trading/gold) coverage.)

What makes the gold bid such a high-quality signal is that central banks are the opposite of momentum traders. They buy slowly, on policy mandates set in advance, and they do not chase price or panic-sell — which means their demand is unusually *price-insensitive* and *persistent*. A hedge fund's gold position can reverse in a week; a central bank's reserve-diversification mandate plays out over years. So when you see the official sector adding gold quarter after quarter, you are watching a structural shift in *who wants the metal and why*, not a speculative wave that will unwind. That is why the post-2022 gold story is genuinely different from previous rallies driven by inflation fear or falling real yields: the marginal buyer changed identity, from a yield-chasing speculator to a sovereign hedging political-custody risk. The price can still be volatile, but the floor under it is being raised by buyers who are not going to leave.

**Marginal reserve diversification.** The IMF's COFER data shows the slow drift — not into one big rival but into a basket of "nontraditional" reserve currencies (Canadian and Australian dollars, Nordic currencies, the yuan) plus gold. The signal to watch is the *rate of change* of the dollar share, not the level: an acceleration from half a point a year toward a full point or more would be the first genuine evidence that the grind is speeding up.

**The dollar's resilience in stress.** The most counterintuitive fingerprint is the one that *disproves* imminent collapse: every modern crisis sends the dollar *up*, not down. In the 2008 crash, the 2020 COVID dash-for-cash, and the 2022 invasion, the dollar rallied as the world scrambled for the safe, liquid asset — Treasuries — to cover dollar debts. The very moment de-dollarization "should" show up (a global panic) is the moment the dollar's transactional moat proves deepest. Watch the dollar in the *next* crisis; if it finally falls when fear spikes, *that* is the regime change, and it has not happened yet.

**Emerging-market local-currency moves.** At the margin, the bilateral-trade rails show up in EM balance of payments — more trade settled in yuan or rupees, more local-currency bond issuance by EMs reducing their dollar-debt fragility. This is the healthiest form of de-dollarization (EMs becoming less dollar-dependent reduces the crisis-amplification channel) and the least threatening to the dollar's core, because it shrinks dollar *borrowing* at the periphery without touching the reserve or FX core.

A fifth fingerprint is worth a mention because it is the one that *confirms* the slow-grind reading rather than the collapse one: **the dollar's share of newly-issued debt and trade invoicing has barely moved.** Even as central banks diversified reserves into gold, the volume of new dollar bonds issued by foreign borrowers and the share of trade contracts written in dollars stayed essentially flat. That divergence — reserves drifting while issuance and invoicing hold — is the clearest possible evidence that it is the *store-of-value* tank leaking and the *transactional* tanks intact. A reader who tracks only the reserve-share headline sees "de-dollarization" and panics; a reader who also tracks issuance and invoicing sees a system rebalancing one of its functions while keeping the other three, and stays calm. The full read requires all the gauges, not the loudest one.

These four (and the fifth) are not equally fast. The freeze that started the debate registered as a real but contained geopolitical shock — the kind that bids gold and adds a small political risk premium to dollar assets without triggering a stampede. The figure below puts that shock in proportion against history.

![Geopolitical risk index spiked to 277 in February 2022 well below the 9 11 reading of 512](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization-6.png)

The reserve freeze pushed the geopolitical-risk index to **277** in February 2022 — elevated, clearly a shock, but well under half the **512** reading after 9/11, and it faded back toward **140** by 2024. That proportion matters for the trade: the freeze was big enough to start a multi-decade structural shift in *store-of-value* behavior (the gold bid) but not a financial-system rupture. The market priced it as the former, not the latter.

And the freeze did not happen in isolation — it was the largest of a rising tide of dollar-based sanctions. Every one of those designations runs through dollar plumbing, and each is a small new incentive for someone to build an alternative.

![Bar chart of new OFAC sanctions designations per year hitting a record around 2549 in 2022](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization-8.png)

New OFAC listings hit a record near **2,549** in 2022 — the freeze year. This is the use-it-and-lose-it tension made visible: the dollar weapon works *because* the dollar is central, and using it harder gives the rest of the world a stronger reason to construct the rails that, over decades, erode that centrality. The sanctions count is therefore both a measure of US financial power *today* and a leading indicator of the slow-grind pressure *tomorrow*. (For how a single designation bites, see [sanctions law, OFAC, the SDN list and secondary sanctions](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sanctions-law-ofac-the-sdn-list-and-secondary-sanctions).)

## The playbook: trading the dollar's slow grind

The discipline here is simple to state and hard to follow: **separate the narrative from the flow, and position for the slow grind rather than the collapse.** Here is how a practitioner reads and trades it.

**Track the two real signals, ignore the noise.** The narrative ("the dollar is dying," "BRICS will replace it") is almost pure noise — loud at summits, absent from balance sheets. The flow is the signal, and there are exactly two flows worth a dashboard slot. First, the **dollar's COFER reserve share** and, more importantly, its *rate of change*: a stable ~58% share with no acceleration in the decline means the collapse trade has no catalyst. Second, **central-bank gold buying**: the World Gold Council's quarterly official-sector data is the cleanest read on the store-of-value tank leaking. If you are going to watch two numbers, watch those two.

**Know which tank you are betting on.** The gold bid is a real, ownable trade on the *store-of-value* tank — a structural, patient long in gold (and gold miners, and the central banks' own behavior) that does not depend on the dollar collapsing, only on the political-custody premium persisting. Betting that the dollar loses *reserve-currency status* outright is a bet on all three tanks draining at once, which on the evidence is a multi-decade proposition with no near-term catalyst. Never confuse the two: one is a positionable trade, the other is a thesis you can be right about and still lose money waiting for.

**Position for the grind, not the break.** The slow-grind view has three concrete expressions. *Long gold* as the structural beneficiary of custody anxiety — sized as a patient allocation, not a momentum trade. *A modestly higher term premium in long-dated Treasuries* over a multi-year horizon, as the standing foreign bid slowly thins (this is a glacial drift, so it is a strategic tilt, not a tactical short). And *EM local-currency assets* in countries reducing dollar dependence, which structurally lowers their crisis fragility. (For the rates expression, see [macro-trading](/blog/trading/macro-trading); for how a higher discount rate transmits to risk assets, the same lens applies across asset classes.)

**The fast trade is a loser's trade.** Shorting the dollar outright on a "collapse" thesis bleeds carry — you pay to be short the highest-yielding major while you wait for a catalyst that, on the data, is decades away if it comes at all. The leveraged "short the dollar now" bet is the version that goes to zero in a crisis, because the dollar *rises* in crises. The patient gold hedge stays on; the leveraged dollar short gets carried out.

**Mind the carry, because it is the whole reason timing matters.** A structural view that is *directionally* right can still lose money if it is expressed with an instrument that bleeds while you wait. Being short the dollar means being long a lower-yielding currency against a higher-yielding one — you pay the interest-rate differential every day the trade is on. Over the decade-plus horizon de-dollarization actually plays out on, that negative carry compounds into a fortune lost waiting to be right. The lesson generalizes: the correct way to express a slow, structural, uncertain-timing thesis is with a *positive-or-zero-carry* instrument (owning gold, owning the EM local-currency assets that benefit) rather than a *negative-carry* one (a leveraged dollar short, or out-of-the-money options that decay). The trade and the thesis must have matching time horizons, or the clock kills you before the thesis pays.

**Size it as a tail hedge, not a core bet.** Because the central case is "the dollar stays dominant for a long time," a de-dollarization position should be sized like insurance: a standing allocation to gold of a few percent of a portfolio, large enough to matter if the grind accelerates, small enough that you can hold it through years of the dollar refusing to die. The mistake is treating a multi-decade structural drift as a tactical conviction trade and sizing it like one — that is how investors who were *right about the direction* still blew up on the timing. Patience is not a personality trait here; it is the position structure.

**What invalidates the view.** Be precise about disproof, because this is a thesis you can hold for years. The *slow-grind* view is invalidated by an **acceleration** in the reserve-share decline — say from half a point a year toward two points a year, sustained — which would mean the grind has tipped into something faster and the gold/term-premium tilts should be sized up hard. The *anti-collapse* view is invalidated by the one observation that has never yet occurred: **the dollar falling in a genuine global panic.** If the next crisis sends the dollar *down* while fear spikes, the transactional moat has finally cracked, and that — not any summit communiqué — is the signal to take de-dollarization seriously as a fast trade. Until then, read the rule change for its repricing, size the slow grind, and write down in advance that a rising-fear dollar selloff is what would prove you wrong. (For the full cross-asset version of this discipline, see [the law, policy and geopolitics playbook](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-law-policy-and-geopolitics-playbook).)

The whole post reduces to one discipline: **count the tanks, then bet on the one that is actually leaking.** The dollar's store-of-value role is genuinely draining, slowly, into gold and a diversified basket — that is a real, ownable, positive-carry trade with a multi-year horizon. Its medium-of-exchange and unit-of-account roles are defended by a network effect that no rival can clear without a deep safe asset, open rails, and crisis liquidity *all at once* — and none has all three. So the honest answer to "how real is the threat?" is: real enough to own gold and watch the reserve share, not real enough to short the dollar. The investors who lose money on de-dollarization are almost never wrong about the direction; they are wrong about the speed, the instrument, and the carry. Get those three right and the slow grind is a position you can hold for a decade. Get them wrong and you are correct, broke, and early — the worst three words in markets.
