> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The Vietnamese state still owns large stakes in many of the country's biggest listed companies, and the legal process of selling those stakes down is both a recurring supply overhang and one of the most powerful catalysts in the market.
>
> - "Equitization" is Vietnam's word for partial privatization: a wholly state-owned firm is valued, turned into a joint-stock company, sold in slices, listed, and then slowly divested — and that leftover state stake is what you trade around.
> - A known future state sale acts as an overhang: buyers will not pay up for shares when they know a large block is coming to market, so the price sits at a discount until the block clears.
> - The same stake flips into a catalyst the moment it sells to a strategic buyer at a control premium — the Sabeco and Vinamilk divestments are the templates, where the state collected billions and the listed shares re-rated.
> - The one number to remember: a heavily state-controlled name can trade at a **governance discount of roughly 30 percent** to a comparable private peer, and that discount is exactly what closes when the state finally lets go.

On 18 December 2017, a single auction in Ho Chi Minh City moved more money than most entire trading days on the Vietnamese exchange. The state was selling a 53.59 percent stake in Sabeco — the Saigon Beer, Alcohol and Beverage Corporation, the company behind the green bottles in every bia hoi on every street corner in the south. The government had set a floor price that already looked rich: 320,000 dong a share, against a market that had been trading the stock around 230,000 only weeks earlier. Almost nobody expected a contested auction. There was, in the end, essentially one serious bidder — a vehicle backed by ThaiBev, the Thai brewing giant controlled by billionaire Charoen Sirivadhanabhakdi — and it paid the floor. The total came to roughly 110 trillion dong, on the order of \$4.8 billion at the exchange rates of the day. In one afternoon, a single piece of the state's portfolio raised more than Vietnam's entire equitization program had netted in some prior years combined.

That auction is the whole story of this post in miniature. For years before it, Sabeco had been a state-controlled company: profitable, dominant, but trading at a discount the market explicitly attached to "the government runs it and the government still owns most of it." The pending sale was an overhang — everyone knew a giant block was eventually coming, and that ceiling sat on the price. Then the block cleared, at a premium, to a strategic buyer who wanted the distribution network and the brand, not just the dividend. The overhang vanished, a new owner with commercial incentives took the wheel, and the legal status of the company changed from "state asset" to "private asset that happens to be listed."

Multiply that pattern across hundreds of companies and you have one of the defining features of investing in Vietnam. The state is not a passive minority shareholder dotted around the index — it is the controlling owner of a large slice of the listed market, and the legal machinery for selling those stakes down is a slow, political, stop-start process. Reading that machinery — where the overhangs sit, which divestments are real catalysts, and how big the governance discount is — is one of the highest-value skills a foreign investor in Vietnam can develop. This post builds the whole thing from zero.

The frame to keep in mind throughout is the series spine: a legal or policy change — here, the rules governing how and when the state may sell its stakes — reshapes the rules of the game, and the market discounts that change into prices before it fully takes effect. (For the master version of that mechanism, see [how law moves markets](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain).) A divestment decree, an auction date, a foreign-ownership approval, a valuation-rule reform — each is a legal event that flows through to flows and then to prices. The investor's job is to read the legal event early, size the repricing it implies, and know exactly what would invalidate the view. By the end you will have a concrete process for doing that with Vietnamese state-controlled names.

## Foundations: SOEs, equitization, and the institutions that sell the state's stakes

Start with the plainest possible version. A state-owned enterprise — an **SOE** — is a company the government owns. In a fully state-owned firm there are no tradable shares at all; the state is the single member, the way a person might be the sole owner of a corner shop. The firm earns money, employs people, and the profits ultimately belong to the state budget. In Vietnam, as in China, the former Soviet bloc, and much of the developing world, the state built and ran the commanding heights of the economy this way for decades: the breweries, the dairy, the airlines, the ports, the power utilities, the banks, the oil and gas firm, the rubber plantations.

The problem every such economy eventually runs into is that state-run firms tend to be less efficient, less profitable, and slower to invest than firms exposed to private capital and competition. So governments look for a way to inject private ownership, private discipline, and private money — without necessarily giving up control all at once, and without the political baggage of the word "privatization."

### What "equitization" actually means

Vietnam's word for this is **equitization** (*co phan hoa* in Vietnamese, which translates literally as "turning into shares"). It is deliberately not called privatization, because for a long time the state intended to remain the controlling shareholder of the equitized firm. The everyday version: take the corner shop that one owner holds outright, divide it into a thousand shares, sell some of those shares to the staff, sell some to an outside investor, sell some to the public — but keep, say, 600 of the 1,000 shares for yourself. The shop now has outside owners and outside money, it can list its shares, but you still control it.

That is equitization. Legally, the wholly state-owned enterprise is converted into a **joint-stock company** — a company with transferable shares and, eventually, a stock-market listing — while the state retains a defined ownership percentage. The size of that retained stake is the entire game. If the state keeps 65 percent, it controls everything and the company is an SOE in all but name. If it keeps 36 percent, it loses day-to-day control but retains a blocking minority. If it sells down below 36 percent, or out entirely, the company becomes a genuinely private, market-governed firm.

It helps to be precise about the ownership thresholds, because Vietnamese company law — like most company law — gives different rights at different levels, and the market prices each threshold differently. A holder above **50 percent** has ordinary majority control: it elects the board and passes ordinary resolutions, so it runs the company day to day. A holder above roughly **65 percent** has near-total control, comfortably above the supermajority needed for the biggest decisions. The threshold that catches people out is the **35-to-36-percent blocking level**: because major corporate actions — changing the charter, issuing large amounts of new stock, selling core assets, merging — require a supermajority of votes, a shareholder holding just over a third can *block* those actions even without majority control. So the state retaining "only" 36 percent is not the same as the state stepping back; it keeps a veto. And a holder below 36 percent is, for the first time, a true minority that cannot single-handedly block anything. Each step down — through 65, through 50, through 36 — releases a layer of the governance discount, which is why partial divestments can move a stock even when the state has not fully exited.

The steps of the process matter as much as the thresholds. When an SOE is equitized, the shares are typically sold in a few distinct slices: a slice to **employees** (often at a preferential price, to buy internal support for the reform); a slice to one or more **strategic investors** chosen for their industry expertise and capital, usually sold by negotiation or restricted auction rather than to the open public; a slice to the **public** through an initial public offering; and the large **residual** kept by the state. The strategic-investor slice is the one to watch — it is where a serious commercial owner first gets a foothold, and a well-chosen strategic investor at the equitization stage often becomes the eventual buyer of the state's residual stake years later.

### The institutions: SCIC and CMSC

Two acronyms do most of the work here, and you should learn them cold.

**SCIC** — the **State Capital Investment Corporation** — is the government's investment fund, founded in 2005, that holds and manages the state's equity stakes in equitized companies and is tasked with divesting many of them over time. Put plainly, it is a sovereign holding company: SCIC is, roughly, Vietnam's answer to Singapore's Temasek, though far less developed. When the state owns 36 percent of a listed dairy company and the official plan is to eventually sell that stake, it is usually SCIC that holds the shares and SCIC that will run the auction. Vinamilk — the dairy giant — was SCIC's flagship holding for years.

**CMSC** — the **Commission for the Management of State Capital at Enterprises**, established in 2018 — is the "super-committee" that was created to consolidate oversight of the nineteen largest SOEs and corporations directly under it, pulling them out from under the individual line ministries that used to run them. The idea was to separate the state's role as *owner* of these firms from the state's role as *regulator* of their industries — a conflict of interest that had hobbled reform for years. CMSC oversees the giants: the national oil and gas group, the electricity utility, the railways, the airlines holding, the telecoms.

The practical point for an investor: when you read that "the state owns X percent of company Y," you should ask *which arm of the state* holds it and *what its mandate is*. An SCIC stake earmarked for divestment is a future catalyst. A stake held by a line ministry with no divestment plan, or by CMSC inside a strategic industry the state will never relinquish, is closer to permanent. The label on the owner tells you whether the overhang is a coiled spring or a brick wall.

### State-ownership caps and the "golden share"

Some industries have a legal **state-ownership floor** — a minimum the state must keep — set by decree. The state will, by law, retain a controlling or blocking stake in sectors it deems strategic: defense, certain utilities, parts of banking and energy, key infrastructure. In those names there is no real divestment catalyst to trade, because the law forbids the sale that would unlock it.

A related concept is the **golden share** — a single share, or a small special class, that carries outsized rights: the power to veto certain decisions (a change of control, a sale of core assets, a foreign takeover) regardless of the ordinary economic stake. Vietnam has used golden-share-like arrangements and special state rights in sensitive privatizations to keep a hand on the wheel even after selling most of the economics. For a trader, a golden share is a permanent governance overhang: it means the state can block the very corporate actions — a takeover, a strategic sale — that would otherwise re-rate the stock.

### The divestment program

Putting it together: Vietnam runs a rolling, multi-year **divestment program** — a published list of SOEs the state intends to equitize and stakes it intends to sell, with targets attached. Decisions like Decision 1232 (2017) and later replacements named hundreds of companies and the percentages the state planned to sell by a given year. In practice, the program has chronically missed its targets — more on why below — but the *list itself* is a tradeable document. A name moving onto the active divestment list, or getting a concrete auction date, is a catalyst. A name that has been on the list for five years with no auction is an overhang that never springs.

### How equitization built the market you trade

It is worth pausing on a fact that is easy to miss if you arrived at the Vietnamese market recently: equitization is not a side activity bolted onto a private market — it is *how the market itself was built*. Vietnam had no stock exchange at all until the Ho Chi Minh City trading centre opened in July 2000 with a handful of listed names. The market that exists today, with hundreds of listed companies and a capitalization in the hundreds of billions of dollars, was populated overwhelmingly by equitized SOEs coming to market over the following two decades.

The biggest, most liquid names that anchor the index are, to a striking degree, former SOEs: the dairy champion, the brewers, the big commercial banks, the gas and fertilizer producers, the airlines, the rubber and mining groups, parts of the petroleum complex. When you buy "the Vietnamese market" through an index or an ETF, a large share of what you own is companies that were once wholly state-owned and were brought public through equitization — many of which the state still partly owns. This is the structural reason the topic matters so much: the legal overhang and the governance discount are not niche concerns about a few odd names; they sit on a meaningful fraction of the entire investable market.

It also explains a quirk of the market's plumbing. Many equitized firms first traded not on the main HOSE or HNX exchanges but on **UPCoM** — the Unlisted Public Company Market, a lighter-touch board that serves as a holding pen for public companies that are not yet fully listed. A common path is: equitize, register on UPCoM, then graduate to a full HOSE listing once the company meets the standards. For an investor, a planned move *up* from UPCoM to HOSE is itself a minor catalyst — it widens the eligible buyer base, often precedes index inclusion, and frequently coincides with the state preparing to sell down. Tracking the UPCoM-to-HOSE pipeline is part of reading where the next divestment-driven re-ratings will come from.

## The governance discount: why state control costs the share price

Here is the first deep mechanism. Markets do not value a dong of earnings the same way at every company. They pay more for earnings they trust will be managed for shareholders, and less for earnings they fear will be managed for someone else. A company that the state controls trades at a **governance discount** — a lower valuation multiple than a comparable private company with the same earnings — because minority shareholders price in a set of specific, rational fears.

What are they afraid of? Concretely: that the state-appointed board will prioritize policy goals (employment, social stability, supplying a sister SOE at a sweet price) over profit; that capital allocation will be politicized — empire-building, vanity projects, lending to connected parties; that related-party transactions will quietly transfer value out to other state entities; that minority holders have little real say because the controlling shareholder holds a permanent majority; and that dividends and buybacks are subordinated to the state budget's needs rather than to total shareholder return. None of these is certain in any given name, but as a class, state-controlled firms display enough of them that the market demands a haircut.

Several of these fears are not vague reputational worries but concrete, recurring patterns. Take **related-party transactions**: an SOE selling its output to a sister state company below market, or buying inputs from one above market, transfers value out of the listed entity and into the broader state portfolio — perfectly legal, often justified on policy grounds, and directly damaging to a minority holder whose claim is only on *this* company. Take **dividend policy**: a state owner that needs cash for the budget may push for high payouts that starve the company of reinvestment, or, conversely, a state owner pursuing a national-development mandate may force the company into low-return strategic projects that depress the cash available to all shareholders. Take **management selection**: when senior leaders are appointed for their standing in the party-state apparatus rather than purely for commercial track record, the bench of operating talent can be thinner than at a private competitor. And take **the speed of decisions**: an SOE that must route major moves through ministries and committees is structurally slower than a nimble private rival, a real cost in a fast-moving market. The governance discount is the market's aggregate, dollar-denominated estimate of all of these — a single number standing in for a basket of agency problems.

There is a subtlety worth flagging: the discount is partly *justified* and partly *excessive*, and telling the two apart is where alpha hides. Some state-controlled firms are genuinely well run, pay reliable dividends, and treat minorities fairly — yet still carry the class-wide discount simply because they wear the SOE label. Those are the names where the discount can compress even *without* a divestment, as the market gradually recognizes the quality. The pure divestment trade and the "unfairly tarred good SOE" trade are cousins: both are bets that a discount attached to control will narrow.

That haircut shows up as a lower price-to-earnings or price-to-book multiple. The figure below maps the relationship: as the state's ownership rises, the multiple the market is willing to pay falls — gently at low control, steeply once the state crosses the threshold where it can ignore minorities.

![Governance discount curve where the fair P/E multiple falls from about 14x to about 8x as state ownership rises from 0 to 65 percent](/imgs/blogs/state-owned-enterprises-equitization-and-the-legal-overhang-6.png)

The slope is not linear, and the kinks matter. Below roughly 36 percent the state cannot block special resolutions by itself, so the discount is modest. At a **blocking minority** — broadly 36 percent or more under Vietnam's company law, which requires a supermajority for major decisions — the state can veto things even without majority control, so the discount steepens. At outright **majority** control the state runs the company, and the discount is deepest. The single most important consequence is that the discount is *reversible*: it is attached to control, not to the business, so when control passes to a commercial owner, the discount lifts and the multiple expands. That is the re-rating the whole strategy is built on.

#### Worked example: the dollars the governance discount suppresses

Take a state-controlled industrial firm earning \$120 million a year, with 300 million shares outstanding, so earnings per share are \$0.40. A clean private peer in the same industry trades at 13 times earnings. If our firm were valued like that peer, it would trade at 13 × \$0.40 = \$5.20 a share, for a market value of \$1.56 billion.

But the market applies a governance discount because the state owns 60 percent and runs the board. Suppose the discount is 30 percent on the multiple, so the firm trades at roughly 9 times earnings instead of 13. That puts the share at 9 × \$0.40 = \$3.60, and the market value at \$1.08 billion. The discount has erased \$5.20 − \$3.60 = \$1.60 per share, or \$480 million of market value, purely because of who is in control. That suppressed \$480 million is not lost forever; it is the prize that sits on the table waiting for the day the state sells. The governance discount is a measurable, dollar-denominated bet on whether — and when — control changes hands.

## The overhang: how a known future sale caps the price

The second deep mechanism is the mirror image of the catalyst, and it is the one investors most often get wrong. A pending state divestment is, before it happens, a **supply overhang** — a known quantity of stock that will come to market in the future, which depresses the price today.

The logic is straightforward once you trace it. Suppose the state owns 40 percent of a listed company and has announced it will sell that entire stake. Every potential buyer of the shares now knows that a block equal to 40 percent of the company is coming. Why would anyone pay full price today, when they can wait and likely buy cheaper from the state's auction — or simply wait for the selling pressure to push the price down? The rational response is to hold off, or to demand a discount for taking shares ahead of the flood. The known future supply becomes a ceiling. The figure below shows the same stake wearing both faces.

![Before and after comparison where a pending state sale caps the price as an overhang and the cleared sale becomes a re-rating catalyst](/imgs/blogs/state-owned-enterprises-equitization-and-the-legal-overhang-3.png)

The size of the overhang's drag depends on three things: how big the block is relative to the free float, how soon it is expected, and how it will be sold. A block that is large relative to the existing float is a heavy weight — it can double or triple the tradeable supply overnight. A sale expected next quarter weighs more than one vaguely planned "someday." And the *method* matters enormously: a single negotiated sale to a strategic buyer barely touches the open-market price (the buyer takes the whole block off-market, and may even pay a premium), whereas a drip-feed of shares dumped into the order book over months is a persistent headwind that suppresses the price the entire time it lasts.

It is worth being concrete about the **sale methods**, because they are the single best predictor of whether a divestment will be a catalyst or a slow bleed. Vietnam's state sellers use a few channels. An **open auction** on the exchange, where the block is offered with a floor price and bidders compete: clean if it attracts a strategic buyer, a damp squib if only financial buyers show up or if the floor is too high and the auction fails. A **book-build or competitive tender** for a strategic stake, negotiated with pre-qualified bidders: this is the channel that produced the Sabeco premium, because it pits serious strategic buyers against each other for control. And the worst case for the open-market price, a **gradual sell-down** where the state instructs its holder to sell shares into the market over time: this is pure, persistent supply that caps the stock for as long as it continues, with no premium and no clean re-rating moment. When you read a divestment plan, the first question is always *which method* — the answer tells you whether to position for a catalyst or to stand aside from a name that will be capped for months.

The overhang also has a *time signature* that traders exploit. In the run-up to a credible auction date, the stock often drifts lower or sideways as buyers wait for the cheaper block. The drift can reverse sharply on the *result*: if a strategic buyer wins at a premium, the overhang is gone in a single print and the stock gaps up; if the auction fails, the overhang persists and the disappointment can push the stock lower still. So the same event — the auction — is a coin with two very different faces, and sizing the trade is really about estimating the probability that a strategic buyer turns up versus the probability the auction is empty.

#### Worked example: pricing the overhang discount on a state block

Take a company with 200 million shares, trading at \$3.00, so a market value of \$600 million. The free float — the shares actually available to trade — is just 30 percent, or 60 million shares, because the state holds the other 70 percent. Now the state announces it will sell a 25-percentage-point stake: 50 million shares, almost as many as the entire current float.

A block that nearly doubles the tradeable supply will not clear at the screen price. Say the market prices in a 12 percent overhang discount while the sale is pending — buyers refuse to pay up knowing the flood is coming — so the stock drifts to \$3.00 × 0.88 = \$2.64. On the 50 million shares the state is selling, that 36-cent-per-share discount is \$18 million of value the overhang is shaving off the state's own proceeds, and it caps the price for every other holder too. Now flip it: if the state instead sells the whole 50-million-share block in one negotiated deal to a strategic buyer, off-market and at a premium, the open-market overhang never materializes — the price can hold or rise. The lesson is that an overhang is not destiny; *how* the block is sold decides whether it is a slow bleed or a clean catalyst.

## Divestment as catalyst: the Vinamilk and Sabeco template

Now the upside. When a divestment is executed well — sold as a clean block to a buyer who wants the company — it is one of the most reliable catalysts in the Vietnamese market, because it does four good things at once.

First, it **removes the overhang**: the known future supply is now in firm hands, and the ceiling lifts. Second, it can deliver a **control premium**: a strategic buyer pays *above* the market price to secure control, which drags the listed shares up toward the deal price. Third, it **improves governance**: a commercial owner with skin in the game tends to run the company for profit, which justifies a higher multiple — the governance discount starts to close. Fourth, it **boosts the float and liquidity**: more shares in public hands means a more liquid, more investable stock, often with a higher index weight, which pulls in passive and institutional money. (This float-and-index mechanism is the same one that drives Vietnam's broader [foreign-ownership and index-upgrade story](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/foreign-ownership-limits-room-and-the-msci-ftse-upgrade-path).)

The two canonical cases are Vinamilk and Sabeco. **Vinamilk** — Vietnam Dairy Products — was SCIC's crown jewel. Through 2016 and 2017 SCIC sold tranches of its stake, and foreign strategic and financial buyers (including Singapore's Jardine-linked F&N and others) competed for the shares. The stock was already a market darling, and the steady, credible divestment combined with strong fundamentals turned Vinamilk into the heaviest weight in the index and a magnet for foreign money. The divestment did not so much create the re-rating as *confirm* it: each sale validated the price and added float.

**Sabeco** was the louder, cleaner catalyst. As described at the top, the state sold a 53.59 percent controlling stake to ThaiBev in December 2017 at 320,000 dong a share — a price well above where the stock had traded before the auction process began. ThaiBev was a *strategic* buyer: it wanted Sabeco's dominant share of the Vietnamese beer market, its distribution, and its brands, and it was willing to pay a control premium to get them. The figure below shows why a strategic buyer pays up where a purely financial buyer would not.

![Strategic buyer premium mechanism where a trade buyer pays a control premium and synergy value above a financial buyer, re-pricing listed shares](/imgs/blogs/state-owned-enterprises-equitization-and-the-legal-overhang-7.png)

The structure of that figure is the heart of the strategic-sale trade. A financial buyer — a fund — values the company on its standalone cash flows and will bid around fair value; it has no reason to overpay. A strategic buyer values the company on *its own* combined economics: the target's market share plus the buyer's distribution, the cost synergies, the ability to cross-sell, the strategic value of owning the dominant brand in a fast-growing market it could not otherwise enter. Those synergies are worth real money to the strategic buyer and to no one else, which is why it can rationally pay a premium that a financial bidder would consider insane. When the state runs an auction that attracts a strategic buyer, the seller captures part of that synergy value as a higher price — and the listed shares re-price toward it.

The re-rating itself can be decomposed into its drivers, which is useful both for understanding what you are buying and for setting a price target. The chart below walks a single state-controlled share from its discounted multiple up to its re-rated one, attributing the lift to each mechanism in turn: the overhang clearing, the float and liquidity improvement, and the governance fix. The numbers are an illustrative computation, not a market series, but they show how the pieces stack.

![Illustrative re-rating waterfall lifting a state-controlled share from a discounted 9x to a re-rated 13x P/E in USD steps](/imgs/blogs/state-owned-enterprises-equitization-and-the-legal-overhang-5.png)

Read left to right: the share starts at a discounted 9 times earnings (\$3.60 on \$0.40 of EPS), gains roughly one turn of multiple as the overhang clears, another turn as float and liquidity improve, and two more turns as a commercial owner fixes the governance — landing at a re-rated 13 times, or \$5.20. The total move from \$3.60 to \$5.20 is a 44 percent re-rating, and not one cent of it required the company's earnings to grow. That is the signature of the divestment catalyst: it re-prices the *multiple*, not the business.

#### Worked example: the strategic-investor premium and the re-rating

Take a state-controlled consumer company trading at \$8.00 a share before any divestment talk, on 200 million shares — a market value of \$1.6 billion. The state owns 54 percent. It runs an auction, and a strategic foreign buyer wins the 54-percent stake at \$11.20 a share — a 40 percent control premium over the pre-deal price, because the buyer values the brand and the distribution network at far more than a passive investor would.

Two things happen to the listed shares. The overhang is gone — the giant state block is now in committed strategic hands, not hanging over the market. And the market re-rates the remaining free float toward the deal economics and the improved governance: say the stock settles at \$10.50, partway between the old \$8.00 and the \$11.20 strategic price. For a minority holder who owned 1,000 shares at \$8.00 (a \$8,000 position), the holding is now worth \$10,500 — a \$2,500 gain, up 31 percent — without the company's underlying earnings changing at all. The entire move is the divestment catalyst: overhang removed, control premium revealed, governance discount closing. That is what you are trying to own ahead of.

## The foreign-strategic-investor angle

The strategic buyer in the biggest Vietnamese divestments is very often *foreign*, and that is not an accident — it is structural, and it has its own set of legal frictions worth understanding because they shape which deals can happen at all.

Why foreign? Because the buyers with both the deep pockets and the strategic motive to pay a control premium for a dominant Vietnamese consumer or industrial franchise are frequently regional or global players seeking entry into a fast-growing, 100-million-person market they cannot easily build into from scratch. ThaiBev wanted Sabeco's lock on the southern beer market. Korean, Japanese, Thai, and Singaporean strategics have repeatedly been the marginal buyers of Vietnamese banks, consumer companies, and industrials, because owning the local champion is faster and surer than competing with it. A domestic financial buyer rarely has the synergy case to outbid a foreign strategic for control, which is precisely why the state can extract a premium when it invites foreign strategics into the auction.

But foreign ownership runs straight into Vietnam's **foreign-ownership limit (FOL)**, the legal cap on how much of a given company foreigners may collectively own. The general cap is 49 percent for many sectors, with tighter limits in restricted ones — notably 30 percent for banks. The available headroom under that cap is called the **room**, and when a popular stock's room is full, foreigners cannot buy more on the open market at all, which is why room-constrained names often trade at a premium in off-market block deals. (The full mechanics of FOL, room, and the workarounds are in the [foreign-ownership and upgrade-path post](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/foreign-ownership-limits-room-and-the-msci-ftse-upgrade-path).)

The interaction between FOL and divestment is where it gets interesting for the trader. If the state wants a foreign strategic buyer to take a controlling stake — say 54 percent, as with Sabeco — but the FOL caps foreigners at 49 percent, something has to give. In practice these deals are structured around the limit: through special government approval for the specific transaction, through holding structures, through sectors where the cap is higher or absent, or through the foreign buyer taking shares that the FOL counts differently. The Sabeco deal itself was famously routed through a Vietnamese-incorporated vehicle to navigate the ownership rules. The practical lesson: a divestment that *requires* foreign control is only as real as the legal path to let a foreigner hold that much. When you screen a name as a strategic-sale candidate, you must check whether the FOL would even permit the control transfer — if it would not, and no special approval is forthcoming, the catalyst is blocked before it starts.

#### Worked example: the foreign strategic premium against a full-room constraint

Take a listed company at \$5.00 a share, 49 percent foreign-owned already — the room is full. A foreign strategic wants control and is willing to pay \$7.50 for the state's 51-percent block, a 50 percent premium, because the synergies are worth it. On the open market, foreigners cannot add a single share; the room is exhausted, so the screen price is held down by the lack of foreign buying. If the state's block sale to the foreign strategic is approved — lifting the effective foreign holding well past 49 percent via special clearance — two things happen at once: the control premium is revealed at \$7.50, and the supply-demand picture for the remaining shares shifts as the dominant new owner signals long-term commitment. A minority holder of 1,000 shares at \$5.00 (a \$5,000 position) sees the stock re-rate toward, say, \$6.50 — a \$1,500 gain, up 30 percent. But none of it happens if the FOL approval is denied; then the foreign strategic walks, no premium is paid, and the stock sits at \$5.00 with full room and no catalyst. The premium and the legal permission are inseparable.

## Why the pipeline stalls — and what accelerates it

If divestment is such a reliable catalyst, why is so much of the state's portfolio still unsold years after it was put on the list? Because equitization and divestment are slow, and understanding *why* they are slow tells you which catalysts are real and which are mirages.

The first brake is **valuation disputes, especially over land**. Many SOEs sit on enormously valuable land-use rights — old factories on prime urban plots, ports, sprawling campuses. Valuing those assets fairly is hard, politically fraught, and a magnet for accusations that state assets were sold too cheaply. The fear of being blamed for "losing state capital" makes every official involved cautious to the point of paralysis. After several high-profile corruption cases tied to undervalued land in privatizations, the whole apparatus slowed down further — nobody wants to be the signature on a deal later deemed a giveaway.

The second brake is **political and bureaucratic risk aversion**. An official who pushes a divestment through and gets a good price is rarely rewarded; an official who pushes one through and is later accused of underpricing can be prosecuted. The asymmetric incentive is to delay. Vietnam's broader anti-corruption drive — the "blazing furnace" campaign — while necessary, further chilled deal-making because signing off on a state-asset sale became a career risk.

The third brake is **market conditions and pricing rigidity**. The state often sets auction floor prices by formula or by a number it can defend politically, not by what the market will actually pay. When the floor is above the market clearing price — which happens often — the auction simply fails: no bidders, or only enough to take a sliver. Failed auctions are the norm rather than the exception in weak markets. And the state, sensibly, does not want to dump stakes into a falling market like the one Vietnam saw in 2022.

That 2022 episode is worth grounding in numbers, because it shows how the whole environment for divestment moves with the market and with policy. The chart below traces the VN-Index through the year of the bond-and-property crackdown, when liquidity drained, the index nearly halved from its peak, and any thought of selling state stakes into that tape was shelved.

![VN-Index chart falling from a peak of 1,528 in January 2022 to a trough of 874 in November 2022 then recovering](/imgs/blogs/state-owned-enterprises-equitization-and-the-legal-overhang-4.png)

When the index is collapsing, as it did from 1,528 to 874 across 2022, divestments stop — you cannot run a successful auction for a state stake when the whole market is in free fall and buyers have vanished. The corollary is the trader's edge: divestment activity is *pro-cyclical*. It picks up in strong, liquid, rising markets and freezes in weak ones. (The 2022 crackdown that drove that collapse is its own story, covered in the [bond-and-property crackdown post](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts); the SBV's credit and liquidity levers behind it are in the [Vietnam monetary-policy post](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).)

So what *accelerates* the pipeline? A few concrete catalysts to watch for. A new prime-ministerial decision or decree that re-lists divestment targets with fresh deadlines and, crucially, political cover for the officials executing them. A reformed valuation methodology that lets land be priced more realistically and shields signatories from later blame. A strong, rising market that makes auctions clearable above their floors. The need for budget revenue — divestment proceeds go to the state budget, so a fiscal squeeze can light a fire under sales. And the looming **emerging-market upgrade**: the prospect of billions in passive inflows gives the state a powerful reason to increase the free float of its biggest companies by selling down, since higher float means higher index weight and more of that passive money. That upgrade dynamic ties this whole topic to [Vietnam's regulatory-reform and upgrade catalysts](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts).

The budget driver deserves more weight than investors usually give it, because it is the most reliable forcing function. Vietnam, like most governments, funds part of its spending and infrastructure ambitions from non-tax sources, and divestment proceeds are a recognized line. When the state sets an annual collection target for divestment receipts and the year is running short, pressure builds to get deals done — even deals that had been stalled for years can suddenly find a signature when the treasury needs the cash. So a useful tell is to watch the public budget: a year with an ambitious infrastructure program, a widening deficit, or an explicit divestment-revenue target is a year in which the political will to actually execute sales rises. The catalyst is not just "the company is on the list"; it is "the company is on the list *and* the state needs the money this year."

A second accelerant is **precedent and momentum**. The reason Sabeco mattered beyond its own price was that it proved the model: a clean, high-profile, premium-priced sale to a strategic buyer that the apparatus could point to as a success rather than a scandal. Successful, defensible deals lower the perceived career risk for the next official, and a string of them can thaw a frozen pipeline. Conversely, a single high-profile deal later tarred as underpriced can re-freeze everything. So the divestment pipeline has a behavioral, reflexive quality: success breeds more sales, scandal breeds paralysis, and the trader who reads the *political temperature* around recent deals has an edge on timing the next ones.

#### Worked example: divestment proceeds, float, and the index-weight boost

Take a company with 500 million shares, trading at \$4.00, a market value of \$2.0 billion. The state owns 50 percent (250 million shares), so the free float is the other 250 million shares, worth \$1.0 billion. Index providers weight a stock by its *float-adjusted* market value, not its total value, so this company's index weight reflects only that \$1.0 billion of investable float.

Now the state divests a 30-percentage-point stake — 150 million shares — into the market and to new investors. The free float jumps from 250 million to 400 million shares: from \$1.0 billion to \$1.6 billion of investable value, a 60 percent increase. Even if the share price did not move at all, the company's weight in a float-based index rises by roughly that 60 percent, mechanically pulling in passive money proportional to the new weight. The state, meanwhile, collected 150 million × \$4.00 = \$600 million for its budget. Higher float, more liquidity, a bigger index weight, and cash for the treasury — all from one sale. To put the proceeds in local terms, at a USD/VND rate of about 25,000 that \$600 million is roughly 15 trillion dong, a meaningful line in the state budget and exactly the kind of number that motivates a finance ministry to push a deal through.

## Common misconceptions

**"State-owned means safe and government-backed, so it should trade at a premium."** The opposite is usually true for the equity. Implicit government backing can lower a state firm's *borrowing* cost — its bonds may price tight — but for *shareholders* state control is a discount, not a premium, because the state's interests routinely diverge from minority holders'. In the worked example above, the governance discount erased \$480 million of equity value, around 30 percent. Being government-linked protects creditors far more than it protects common shareholders.

**"A divestment announcement is automatically bullish — buy the headline."** Not in the near term, and often the reverse. An announcement that the state *will* sell a large stake creates an overhang before it creates a catalyst. The stock can drift lower for months while the block waits to clear, especially if the sale will be a drip-feed into the open market rather than a clean strategic block. In the overhang example, the pending sale knocked the stock from \$3.00 to \$2.64 — a 12 percent fall — before any catalyst arrived. The catalyst is the *execution* at a premium to a strategic buyer, not the announcement of intent.

**"The state always gets a great price because it sets the floor."** Empirically, the program has chronically missed its targets precisely because floors are set too high for the market and auctions fail. In weak years, a large share of planned divestments simply do not happen — bidders stay home rather than pay the state's number. The state's pricing power is real only when a strategic buyer wants the asset badly enough to pay up, as ThaiBev did for Sabeco at 320,000 dong against a ~230,000 market. Absent that strategic demand, "the state sets the floor" often just means "the auction is empty."

**"Equitization is the same as full privatization."** No — and the gap is the whole point. Equitization deliberately keeps the state as a large, often controlling, shareholder. A company can be "equitized" and listed for a decade while the state still owns 60 percent of it and runs the board. The governance discount and the overhang both persist for as long as that residual state stake does. Full divestment — the state selling out — is a separate, later, often-delayed event. Confusing the two leads investors to assume the discount has already closed when it has not.

## How it shows up in real markets

The pattern repeats with enough regularity that you can build a watchlist around it. A few of the ways it surfaces:

**The flagship strategic sale.** Sabeco (ThaiBev, December 2017, ~\$4.8 billion, 53.59 percent at 320,000 dong) is the textbook case: a clean controlling block to a strategic buyer at a premium, removing the overhang and re-rating the name. Vinamilk's staged SCIC divestments through 2016–17 are the slower-burn version, where credible, repeated selling plus strong fundamentals made the stock the index's heaviest weight and a foreign-money magnet. These are the cases every Vietnam fund studies, because they define what a "good" divestment looks like.

The two cases also illustrate two *different* divestment styles, and the contrast is instructive. Vinamilk was sold in **tranches** — SCIC offered slices of its stake over time rather than one controlling block, which let the market absorb the supply gradually and let foreign strategic and financial buyers build positions without a single dramatic control transfer. Because Vinamilk was already a high-quality, well-governed, dividend-paying business, the staged selling did not have to *fix* the company; it simply added float and validated the price, and the stock's strength came as much from its fundamentals as from the divestment mechanics. Sabeco, by contrast, was a **single controlling-block** sale where the entire catalyst landed in one auction: the overhang cleared, the premium was revealed, and a new commercial owner took the wheel all at once. The trader's takeaway is that "divestment" is not one trade but two: the tranche style is a slow, fundamentals-plus-float story you can hold patiently, while the controlling-block style is an event-driven, binary catalyst you position for around a specific date.

**The permanent overhang.** Plenty of large listed SOEs sit in the opposite state: the government owns a controlling stake, there is a vague long-term plan to divest, but no auction date and no strategic buyer in sight. Big state banks and several CMSC-supervised giants fall here. The stocks carry the governance discount year after year, and the overhang never springs because the political and valuation brakes never release. Trading these as "divestment plays" on every fresh headline is a recurring way to lose money — the catalyst keeps not arriving.

**The pro-cyclical freeze.** The 2022 drawdown shown above — VN-Index from 1,528 to 874 — froze the divestment pipeline entirely, as it does in every weak market. Divestment proceeds and new equitizations cluster in strong, liquid years and vanish in stressed ones. An investor watching for catalysts has to read the market regime first: the pipeline only flows downhill, and a falling market shuts the tap.

**The upgrade-driven push.** As Vietnam pursues an emerging-market reclassification, the incentive to increase the free float of the biggest companies — by selling down state stakes — grows, because float drives index weight and index weight drives passive inflows. The clearest tell that divestment is about to accelerate is when it becomes the path to billions of passive dollars, aligning the state's budget incentive with the upgrade incentive. Watch the largest, most liquid state-controlled names for divestment news as the upgrade approaches.

**The second-order ripples.** A big divestment does not only move its own stock; it sends signals through the sector. When the state sells the dominant brewer or dairy to a foreign strategic at a fat premium, it re-rates *perceptions* of what other state-controlled franchises in the same space might be worth in a sale, and it tells foreign strategics that Vietnam is open for control deals. So a flagship divestment can lift comparable state-controlled names on the read-across, and it can compress the governance discount sector-wide as the market raises its estimate of the probability that *these* stakes, too, eventually change hands. The alert investor reads a single high-profile sale not just as one catalyst but as new information about the whole cohort's option value — and positions in the cheaper, lower-profile names that have not yet moved but sit in the same line of fire for the next strategic buyer.

## How to trade it: the playbook

Pull the mechanisms together into a process. The core distinction is always the same one the screening matrix below draws: is this name a **divestment candidate** with a real, dated catalyst, or an **overhang name** where a big block hangs over the price with no clearing date? You buy the first and fade the second.

![Screening matrix comparing a divestment candidate versus an overhang name across sale timeline, likely buyer, float effect and how to trade it](/imgs/blogs/state-owned-enterprises-equitization-and-the-legal-overhang-8.png)

**Screen for genuine divestment candidates.** The criteria that separate a real catalyst from a perennial story: a stake held by SCIC (whose mandate is to divest) rather than by a line ministry or by CMSC inside a strategic sector with a legal state-ownership floor; a concrete auction date or an approved divestment decision, not a five-year-old line on a list; a quality underlying business that a strategic buyer would actually want; and a market regime that is strong enough for an auction to clear above its floor. When several of these line up — SCIC holder, scheduled auction, strategic-buyer-worthy asset, rising market — you have a candidate worth owning ahead of the catalyst.

**Price the governance discount before you buy.** Estimate the multiple a clean private peer earns, apply your estimate of the discount the state-controlled name carries, and you have both the current fair value *and* the upside if the discount closes. From the worked example, a 30 percent discount on a name earning \$0.40 was \$1.60 a share of suppressed value. That number is your target if control changes hands — and zero if it never does. Size the position by your probability that the catalyst actually fires, not by the full upside.

The probability weighting is the discipline that separates this trade from a hope. Suppose your work says the divestment, if it happens, is worth \$1.60 a share of upside on a \$3.60 stock — a 44 percent re-rating. If you judge the probability the auction actually clears at a premium this year to be, say, 40 percent, the probability-weighted upside is 0.40 × \$1.60 = \$0.64 a share, or about 18 percent — still attractive, but a very different number from the headline 44 percent, and one that tells you how much to size and how patient to be. Crucially, you must also weight the *downside* of the catalyst slipping: if a postponement would knock the overhang-discounted stock down a further few percent, your expected value has to net that out. Running the divestment thesis as an explicit probability-weighted expected value — upside times probability of firing, minus downside times probability of slipping — is what keeps you from overpaying for a catalyst that perpetually lives one year in the future.

**Buy the strategic-sale setup, not the drip-feed.** The biggest, cleanest re-ratings come from controlling blocks sold to strategic buyers at a premium — Sabeco, not a slow open-market dribble. So weight toward names where the likely buyer is a trade buyer who wants the business, the stake on offer is a control block, and the sale will be a single negotiated transaction. Fade or avoid names where the state plans to drip shares into the order book over months: that is persistent supply, an overhang that suppresses the price the whole time it lasts.

**Check the legal ceiling before anything else.** The fastest way to waste a season on a "divestment play" is to discover, too late, that the law forbids the sale that would unlock it. Before you build a thesis, confirm three legal facts. First, is there a **state-ownership floor** for this sector that keeps the state above a controlling or blocking level no matter what? If the decree says the state must keep 65 percent of a strategic utility, there is no control-transfer catalyst to trade, full stop. Second, is there a **golden share** or special state right that lets the state veto a change of control even after selling the economics? If so, a strategic buyer cannot get real control, and the premium never materializes. Third, does the **foreign-ownership limit** permit the buyer the deal needs? A control sale to a foreign strategic in a 49-percent-capped sector requires special clearance; without it, the catalyst is legally blocked. Only after those three boxes are checked is the rest of the analysis worth doing.

**Know the invalidation.** This trade is wrong, and you should be out, when: the auction is postponed again with no new date (the catalyst evaporates back into an overhang); the only bidders are financial buyers near market price, so there is no control premium to capture; the market regime turns down hard, as in 2022, and the whole pipeline freezes; a legal state-ownership floor or a golden share turns out to cap the divestment below the level needed to change control; or a fresh corruption or valuation scandal freezes signatures across the apparatus. Any one of these flips a candidate back into an overhang name. The discipline is to treat the catalyst as a dated event with a real probability — and to fade the name the moment the date slips and the probability collapses.

The deeper point that runs under all of it: the Vietnamese state's leftover stakes are a structural feature of this market, not a footnote. A large slice of the listed value carries a governance discount that is, in effect, a standing option on the state letting go. Most of those options expire worthless, killed by political caution and failed auctions. A handful pay off enormously when a strategic buyer arrives and the overhang flips to catalyst. Reading which is which — owner mandate, auction date, buyer type, market regime — is the edge.
