> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A new rule (a statute, a regulator's rule, a court ruling, a tariff, a sanction, or a geopolitical shock) reprices assets through a chain: **Law / Geopolitics → Economic Policy → Macro & Flows → Asset Prices → the Trade** — and markets discount the *expected* rule long before it fully bites.
>
> - Price is the present value of expected cash flows at a risk-adjusted rate. A rule moves price *today* if it changes either the **expected cash flows** or the **discount rate**.
> - The repricing concentrates on the **rumor and proposal**, not on the final-rule headline. By the time a law "passes," most of the move is usually behind you.
> - Distinguish **level-shift** rules (reset the number once) from **regime-change** rules (bend the growth slope, repricing for years), and **reversible** rules (a tariff, lifted by a deal) from **sticky** ones (a statute, needs a new law).
> - The one number to remember: after the 2017 US tax cut took the corporate rate from **35% to 21%**, S&P 500 gross buybacks jumped from **\$519bn in 2017 to \$806bn in 2018** — a rule firing straight through the chain into prices.

On the morning of December 20, 2017, the US Congress passed the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act. The headline was historic: the statutory corporate tax rate would fall from 35% to 21%, the largest cut in a generation. A naive reader watching CNBC that morning might have rushed to buy stocks — surely a giant corporate tax cut is bullish, and surely *today* is the day to act.

But the S&P 500 had already climbed roughly 20% over 2017. The market had spent the entire year *pricing in* the growing odds of a tax cut: every poll, every committee vote, every leaked draft nudged the expected rate down and the expected after-tax earnings up. By the time the gavel fell, the bill was the most telegraphed corporate windfall in modern history. The biggest single-day moves had already happened months earlier — on the surprise election result, on the first framework, on the committee passage. The "news" was old.

Contrast that with a very different morning. On February 24, 2022, Russian forces crossed into Ukraine. There was no year-long debate, no committee vote, no leaked draft to price against — a geopolitical shock arrived in hours. Brent crude leapt from about \$97 to \$99 that day and on toward \$128 within two weeks; European natural gas began a climb that would peak at more than thirteen times its prior-year level; defense and energy-security stocks began a multi-year re-rating. Here the market had *almost nothing* priced in, so the repricing was violent and immediate — the mirror image of the slow, fully-anticipated tax cut. The same underlying machinery was running in both cases; what differed was only *how much warning the market had*.

This contrast — a fully-telegraphed statute versus an out-of-nowhere geopolitical shock — is the single most important thing to understand about how rules move markets, and it is the thesis of this entire series: **a rule-change reprices assets through a chain, and markets discount the expected rule before it fully bites.** A statute, a regulation, a court ruling, a tariff, a sanction, or a geopolitical shock changes the *rules of the game* or the *expected path of policy*. That change flows through economic policy, into the macro economy and capital flows, and finally into the prices of stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities. How *much* of the move you can still catch depends entirely on where you are standing in the chain when you read the headline. The practitioner's job is to read the rule-change early, map it onto the chain, size the repricing, and know what would prove the view wrong. This post teaches you to do exactly that for *any* legal or geopolitical headline.

![Pipeline diagram of the transmission chain from a rule or shock through policy, macro and flows, asset repricing, to the trade](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model you will use for the rest of the series. Read it left to right, like a relay race: a baton (the change in expectations) gets passed from one runner to the next. The rule fires; it shifts the incentives policymakers and firms respond to; those responses change growth, inflation, rates, and where capital flows; that reprices assets; and the repricing implies a trade with a defined entry, size, and invalidation. Every legal or geopolitical headline in this series — securities rules, antitrust suits, tariffs, sanctions, central-bank law, court rulings — is a specific instance of this one chain firing. Learn the chain once and you can read all of them.

## Foundations: what counts as a rule, and how markets price it

Before we can trace the chain, we need two foundations from scratch: (1) what "the rules" actually *are*, in legal terms, and (2) the one mechanism by which any of them moves a price. No prior law or finance background is assumed. We will build both from zero and then never have to re-derive them.

### The five kinds of rules (and why the differences matter)

When practitioners say "the rules," they bundle together several legally distinct things that behave very differently in markets. Getting the distinctions right is the difference between trading a headline well and trading it badly.

**A statute** is a *law* — the highest-authority rule, written by a legislature (the US Congress, a national parliament, a state legislature). The 2017 tax cut was a statute. Statutes are slow: they require debate, votes in multiple chambers, and a signature. That slowness is a gift to investors, because it means the market gets months of warning. Statutes are also *sticky*: once passed, undoing one requires passing a whole new law, which is hard. A tax rate set by statute tends to stay put for years.

**A regulation** (also called a *rule*) is written by an *agency* — an executive body the legislature has delegated authority to. In the US, that means the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) for markets, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) for competition, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for pollution, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for drugs, the Federal Reserve for banks. Regulations fill in the operational detail a statute leaves blank. They move at medium speed: an agency proposes a rule, takes public comment, then issues a final rule — a process that takes months and is itself fully observable.

**A court ruling** is a decision by a judge or a panel of judges interpreting what an existing law *means* or whether a rule is *legal*. Rulings are the wild card: they are *fast and binary*. A court either strikes down a rule or upholds it, finds a company liable or not. There is no "comment period," and the outcome is often genuinely uncertain until the decision drops. The repricing is therefore concentrated into a single moment — ruling day — which makes rulings the most event-like of all rules.

**A tariff** is a tax on imported goods, usually set by the executive branch (a president's order, a trade ministry). Tariffs are *fast* — frequently a single order — and crucially they are *reversible*: a trade deal can lift them overnight. That reversibility changes how you trade them, because the market knows the rule could be undone by a phone call between two leaders.

**A sanction** is a legal restriction on doing business with a designated person, company, or country, usually imposed by an executive body (in the US, the Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control, or OFAC; also the UN and EU). Sanctions are *immediate* — a designation takes effect the day it is published — and they bite hard, because the penalty for violating them is severe. They are reversible in principle but slow to lift in practice.

There are two more categories worth naming, because they sit *between* the legislature and the agency and they move markets quietly but powerfully. **Guidance** is sub-regulatory: an agency publishes its interpretation of how it intends to apply a rule (a "no-action letter," an interpretive bulletin, a tax notice). Guidance carries less legal force than a regulation, but markets treat it as a near-certain signal of enforcement intent, and it can move prices the day it drops. **An executive order** is a directive from the head of the executive branch that has the force of law within the powers the executive already holds — fast, like a tariff, and reversible by the next administration's pen. Tariffs and many sanctions are *delivered* through executive orders, which is exactly why they share the "fast and reversible" profile.

Why do these distinctions matter so much for a trade? Because the *legal authority* behind a rule tells you how durable the repricing should be and how the rule can be undone. A repricing driven by a statute should be held in the price as near-permanent; a repricing driven by an executive order should be held only at the probability the order survives the next election or court challenge. Treating an executive order as if it were a statute — pricing it as permanent — is one of the most common and costly analytical errors in rule-driven investing. The market that learns a tariff can be lifted by a single deal will not pay full freight for the damage; the market that learns a tax rate is locked in statute will.

![Comparison matrix of statute, regulation, court ruling, tariff, and sanction by who writes it, speed to price, and reversibility](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-4.png)

The matrix above is your taxonomy. The three columns — *who writes it, speed to price, reversibility* — are exactly the three questions you should ask of any rule-change headline before doing anything else. A slow, sticky statute and a fast, reversible tariff demand completely different trades even if they move the same stock by the same amount, because the *timing* and *durability* of the move differ. We will return to this matrix constantly.

> [!note]
> Throughout this series we are scrupulously **neutral and analytical**. We describe *how* laws and policies move markets — the mechanism, the magnitude, the timing — and we never argue that a party, ideology, or policy is good or bad. A tax cut is "bullish for after-tax earnings" as a matter of arithmetic; whether it is good public policy is a question this series does not touch.

### The one mechanism: price is the present value of expected cash flows

Now the finance foundation. There is exactly one mechanism by which a rule moves a price, and once you internalize it, every case study in this series becomes a variation on a theme.

Any financial asset is worth the cash it is expected to pay you in the future, with *future cash discounted because a dollar later is worth less than a dollar now*. A bond pays coupons and principal. A stock pays (or will eventually pay) earnings to its owners. A currency, a commodity, a piece of real estate — all are worth the discounted stream of what holding them is expected to deliver. We write this as:

```
Price = sum over all future periods of:  Expected cash flow in that period
                                          --------------------------------
                                          (1 + discount rate) ^ period
```

Two inputs, then, determine every price: the **expected future cash flows** (the numerator) and the **discount rate** (the denominator). The discount rate is itself two things added together: the risk-free interest rate (what you could earn safely, e.g. on a government bond) plus a *risk premium* (extra return you demand for bearing uncertainty).

It is worth dwelling on the risk premium, because it is the part of the formula that legal and political risk acts on most directly. When you buy a stock with a pending lawsuit or a looming regulatory decision hanging over it, you are accepting a *wider range of outcomes*: it might be fine, or it might be forced to break up, pay a huge fine, or lose a product line. Investors do not like that uncertainty, so they demand to be paid for it — they will only hold the stock at a *lower price*, which is the same thing as demanding a *higher* expected return. That extra required return is the risk premium, and it goes straight into the denominator. The mechanical consequence is severe: because the denominator is raised to a power that grows with each future year, even a small increase in the discount rate compounds into a large drop in present value for a long-lived asset. A two-percentage-point rise in the discount rate can erase a quarter of a stock's value without changing a single dollar of this year's earnings — we will compute exactly that below. This is why "the lawsuit hasn't even cost them anything yet" is no defense against a falling price: the price is not about this year's cost, it is about the *uncertainty* the rule injects into every future year.

Here is the punchline. **A rule moves a price today if, and only if, it changes the market's expectation of either the future cash flows or the discount rate.** That is the entire transmission mechanism, distilled.

![Graph diagram showing a rule changing expected cash flows or the discount rate, both feeding present value and setting the price today](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-3.png)

The figure above splits the mechanism into its two channels. A tax cut raises *expected cash flows* (the left channel): each future year, the firm keeps more of its profit. A pending antitrust suit raises the *discount rate* (the right channel): investors demand a bigger risk premium to hold a stock that might be forced to break up. A tariff can hit *both* — it lowers a manufacturer's expected margins (cash-flow channel) *and* raises the uncertainty premium investors attach to its supply chain (discount-rate channel). When you read any rule-change headline, your first analytical move is to ask: *which channel does this hit, and by how much?*

#### Worked example: repricing a stock when a rule cuts its operating margin

Let us make the cash-flow channel concrete. Suppose a company, "MidCap Manufacturing," earns \$200 million in operating profit this year and the market expects that profit to grow 4% per year forever. Investors discount its cash flows at 9%. Using the standard growing-perpetuity formula — value equals next year's cash flow divided by (discount rate minus growth rate) — the firm is worth:

Next year's profit is \$200m × 1.04 = \$208m. So value = \$208m ÷ (0.09 − 0.04) = \$208m ÷ 0.05 = **\$4,160 million**, or \$4.16 billion.

Now a proposed regulation — say, a new safety standard — will permanently raise the firm's costs, cutting operating profit by 15%. Expected profit this year drops to \$200m × 0.85 = \$170m; next year's becomes \$170m × 1.04 = \$176.8m. The new value is \$176.8m ÷ 0.05 = **\$3,536 million**, or \$3.54 billion.

The repricing is \$4,160m − \$3,536m = **\$624 million wiped off the value**, a 15% drop — exactly proportional to the margin hit, because we only touched the cash-flow channel and left growth and the discount rate unchanged. *Intuition: when a rule scales down every future dollar of profit by the same fraction, it scales down the whole company's value by that same fraction — the price is just the discounted sum of those dollars.*

### Efficient markets and "priced-in": the trap that catches everyone

The last foundation is the one that catches almost every beginner. Markets are forward-looking. Prices reflect not what *has* happened but what participants *expect* to happen, weighted by probability. The phrase practitioners use is **"priced in"**: if everyone already expects a rule and has traded on that expectation, the rule is "priced in" and its arrival moves nothing.

This is what the *efficient markets* idea captures: at any moment, the price already embeds all widely known information and expectations. You do not get paid for knowing what everyone knows. You get paid — if at all — for one of three things: (1) seeing the rule-change *earlier* than the crowd, (2) sizing its impact *more accurately*, or (3) correctly judging the *probability* that an uncertain rule actually happens. Simply reading a headline that a law passed, after it has been debated for a year, is none of these.

There is an important nuance here, because real markets are not perfectly efficient and the "priced-in" idea can be overstated. Information about rules diffuses *unevenly*. A specialist who reads the actual text of a proposed rule, talks to the affected industry, and models the cash-flow impact genuinely can be ahead of a market that has only skimmed the headline. Complex rules — a 300-page financial regulation, an arcane tax provision, a technical export-control list — are routinely *mis*-priced for weeks because most participants have not done the work to understand them. The edge in rule-driven investing is precisely this: the rules are public, but *understanding their second- and third-order consequences is hard*, so the diligent analyst is paid for depth, not for speed. Efficiency is a tendency, not a law of physics; it is strongest for simple, widely-followed rules and weakest for complex, technical ones.

We will quantify "priced in" precisely below — it is one of the most useful calculations in the practitioner's toolkit. For now, hold the intuition: **the market trades the expectation, not the event.** When the expectation forms is when the money is made.

## The chain, link by link, with real mini-cases

With both foundations in hand, let us walk each link of the transmission chain and attach a real example so the abstraction stays grounded.

### Link 1 → 2: the rule changes economic policy and incentives

The first link is where a legal change becomes an *economic* change. A rule does not move prices by existing on paper; it moves prices by changing what firms, households, and policymakers are *incentivized to do*.

Take the 2017 tax cut again. The statute (link 1) cut the corporate rate. The *policy* effect (link 2) was that after-tax profit on every dollar of pre-tax income jumped: at 35%, a firm keeps 65 cents; at 21%, it keeps 79 cents — a 21.5% increase in retained profit per dollar earned. The statute also imposed a one-time "transition tax" that let companies bring home (repatriate) cash they had parked overseas. The incentive to hold cash abroad collapsed. These are *policy* changes — shifts in the after-tax economics firms face — not yet anything visible in a stock price.

### Link 2 → 3: policy reshapes macro and capital flows

The second link carries the policy change into the broader economy: growth, inflation, interest rates, and — critically — *where capital flows*. This is the link beginners most often skip, and it is where a lot of the actual repricing originates.

After the 2017 tax cut, companies suddenly had far more after-tax cash and a one-time incentive to repatriate. According to US Bureau of Economic Analysis data, repatriated dividends jumped from about \$155bn in 2017 to roughly **\$1,064bn in 2018** — a flood of cash coming home. What did firms do with it? A large share went to buying back their own shares.

![Bar chart of S&P 500 gross buybacks per year showing a jump from 519 billion dollars in 2017 to 806 billion in 2018 after the tax cut](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-7.png)

The chart makes the macro-and-flows link visible. Gray bars are the pre-cut years; blue bars are post-cut. S&P 500 gross buybacks ran around \$519–536bn in 2016–2017, then jumped to a then-record **\$806bn in 2018**, the first full year of the lower rate. Buybacks shrink the share count, so the same total earnings are spread over fewer shares, mechanically raising earnings *per share*. That flow — corporate cash converted into demand for the companies' own stock — is a macro/flows force that pushes prices up independent of any change in the underlying business.

Notice what just happened analytically, because it is the lesson of the whole link. The *first-order* effect of a tax cut is "after-tax profit is higher" — that is the obvious read. The *flows* effect — companies converting that profit into share repurchases, which compress the share count and lift per-share earnings further — is a second push in the same direction that a naive analyst, stopping at the first-order read, would miss. Most of the durable equity repricing after 2017 came not from any single quarter's earnings beat but from this *flow* operating year after year: buybacks stayed elevated (\$729bn in 2019, then \$882bn in 2021 and \$923bn in 2022 as the policy and the cash persisted). A rule that changes the *incentive to deploy cash* sets a flow in motion, and flows, unlike one-time earnings jumps, keep pushing prices for years. The practitioner who traced the chain all the way to "where does the freed-up cash go?" caught a multi-year tailwind; the one who stopped at "earnings are higher" caught only the first day of it.

#### Worked example: how a tax cut flows through to earnings per share

Suppose a firm earns \$1,000m in pre-tax profit, has 1,000m shares outstanding, and trades at a price-to-earnings (P/E) multiple of 18. At a 35% tax rate, after-tax profit is \$1,000m × (1 − 0.35) = \$650m, so earnings per share (EPS) = \$650m ÷ 1,000m shares = **\$0.65**, and the price is 18 × \$0.65 = **\$11.70**.

Now the rate drops to 21%. After-tax profit becomes \$1,000m × (1 − 0.21) = \$790m. Before any buyback, EPS = \$790m ÷ 1,000m = **\$0.79**. If the multiple holds at 18, the price is 18 × \$0.79 = **\$14.22** — a 21.5% gain purely from the tax change.

Now add the flows link. Say the firm spends \$500m of its windfall buying back stock at \$14.22, retiring about 35.2m shares (\$500m ÷ \$14.22), leaving roughly 964.8m shares. EPS rises further to \$790m ÷ 964.8m = **\$0.819**, and at an 18 multiple the price is **\$14.74**. *Intuition: the rule lifted earnings through the cash-flow channel, and the buyback flow then concentrated those earnings onto fewer shares — two links of the chain stacking on top of each other.*

### Link 3 → 4: macro and flows reprice the assets

The third link is where the macro and flow changes hit the present-value formula and actually move prices. Higher expected after-tax earnings raise the numerator; buyback flows raise per-share earnings further; and if the rule reduces uncertainty, the discount rate can fall too, lifting the multiple. All three push the same direction in the tax-cut case, which is why the equity repricing was large and durable.

But the same link can run the *other* way. When the Federal Reserve raises interest rates, the risk-free rate in the denominator rises, every future cash flow is discounted harder, and prices fall — especially for "long-duration" assets whose cash flows are far in the future (high-growth tech, long bonds). The macro link is bidirectional: a rule that lowers rates lifts prices; one that raises them depresses prices. (For the precise mechanics of how the central bank sets that rate, see the cross-link at the end on how the Fed sets interest rates.)

### Link 4 → 5: the repricing implies a trade

The final link converts an analyzed repricing into an actual position with a size and an exit. This is the payoff of the whole exercise and the subject of the playbook section below. A repricing you have correctly identified but cannot *act on* — because it is already priced in, or because you cannot size it or define what would prove you wrong — is an academic observation, not a trade. The discipline of the chain is that it forces you all the way to link 5 every time.

The form the trade takes depends on the rule's profile from the matrix. A *sticky, level-shift* rule (the tax cut) is best expressed as a position you put on early, during the rumor-and-proposal window, and hold through the re-rate — a "get there before the crowd and wait" trade. A *fast, reversible* rule (a tariff) is better suited to a recurring, fade-the-extreme posture, because the probability of the rule surviving keeps oscillating and the price oscillates with it. A *binary, ruling-day* event is a true event trade: you size for a defined gap in either direction and accept that you will know the answer in a single moment. And a *regime-change* shift (defense after 2022, the chip-supply realignment) is a multi-year *theme* you ride with a structural position, re-checking the thesis each quarter rather than trading the daily noise. One chain, four trade shapes — and the matrix tells you which one you are looking at before you commit a dollar.

## The lead-lag: where, in time, the repricing actually happens

We now confront the timing question directly, because it is where most money is won and lost. A rule does not arrive as a single event; it arrives as a *sequence* of escalating signals, and the market reprices a little more at each stage as the probability of the final rule climbs toward certainty.

![Timeline of four stages from rumor to proposal to final rule to enforcement, marking where the repricing concentrates](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-2.png)

The timeline shows the four stages. **Stage 1, the rumor or leak:** a news report, a politician's speech, a leaked draft. The probability of the rule jumps from, say, 10% to 35%, and the price begins to move. **Stage 2, the formal proposal:** the bill is introduced, the agency publishes its proposed rule, the case goes to trial. Probability jumps again, often to 60–80%, and this is frequently the *largest* single repricing because the move from "maybe" to "likely" covers the most probability. **Stage 3, the final rule passes:** the law is signed, the rule is finalized, the verdict is read. If the outcome was expected, the price barely moves — it was priced in over stages 1 and 2. **Stage 4, enforcement:** the rule actually bites, and the price drifts as the real cash-flow effects show up in earnings reports quarter after quarter.

The red stages on the timeline — rumor and proposal — are where the bulk of the repricing concentrates. The blue final-rule stage, the one that makes the front page, is usually the *quietest* for prices. This is the single most counterintuitive fact in the whole subject, and it explains why the naive CNBC-watcher who buys on the day a law passes is systematically late.

Why is the *proposal* stage so often the largest single move, rather than the final rule? Because the price is the probability-weighted value, and a move in probability translates into a move in price one-for-one *times the size of the impact*. Going from "10% likely" to "60% likely" — the typical jump when a vague rumor becomes a formal proposal — shifts the probability by 50 percentage points, and that 50-point shift gets multiplied by the full dollar impact of the rule. Going from "60% likely" to "100% certain" at the final-rule stage shifts probability by only 40 points, and often by far less, because by then the market has usually pushed the odds well above 60% in anticipation. The largest *change in expectation* tends to land in the middle of the sequence, not at the end — and price tracks the change in expectation, not the arrival of the event. There is one important exception: a *surprise* final rule (a court ruling no one expected, a sanction with no warning) compresses the entire repricing into a single moment because none of it was priced over the earlier stages. That is exactly why rulings and sanctions — the fast, binary rules from the matrix — produce the most violent single-day moves: there were no rumor and proposal stages to bleed the repricing out gradually.

#### Worked example: how much of an expected tariff is already in the price

Suppose a stock would be worth \$100 if no tariff is imposed and \$70 if a tariff is imposed (a 30% hit to its value). The market's job is to hold the *probability-weighted* value. If traders judge the tariff 50% likely, the price should be:

Price = 0.50 × \$100 + 0.50 × \$70 = \$50 + \$35 = **\$85**.

The stock has already fallen \$15 from its no-tariff value of \$100. The *full* tariff impact is \$30 (from \$100 down to \$70). So the fraction already priced in is \$15 ÷ \$30 = **50%** — exactly the probability the market assigns. If the tariff is then confirmed, the price falls the remaining \$15 (from \$85 to \$70); if it is called off, the price rises \$15 (from \$85 back to \$100).

The trading lesson is sharp: at a 50% probability you are *not* short the full \$30 move by waiting for confirmation — half of it is gone. The asymmetric, high-reward time to act was earlier, when the probability was 10% and only \$3 was priced in. *Intuition: the price already embeds the market's odds, so the money is in correctly forecasting a change in the odds before the crowd, not in reacting to the confirmed outcome.*

## Two ways rules differ: level-shift vs regime-change, reversible vs sticky

Not all rule-changes reprice the same way. Two distinctions govern *how big* and *how durable* the move is, and the matrix earlier already flagged one of them (reversibility). Here we develop both, because they directly determine the trade.

### Level-shift rules vs regime-change rules

A **level-shift rule** resets a number once. The tax cut is the canonical example: it dropped the cost base by a fixed amount, earnings jumped to a new plateau, and then growth continued at roughly the old underlying pace. The repricing is a one-time step up; after the market sets the new level, the rule stops being a tailwind.

A **regime-change rule** bends the *slope* of the future, not just its level. A rule that bans a company's main product line, or opens a vast new market to it, or fundamentally alters who is allowed to compete, changes the *trajectory* of earnings — and because a present value compounds growth over many years, a slope change reprices far more, and keeps repricing as the new regime plays out.

![Before-and-after diagram contrasting a level-shift rule that resets earnings once with a regime-change rule that bends the growth slope](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-5.png)

The figure contrasts them. On the left (the level-shift), each box describes a one-time reset: the rate steps down, earnings jump once, the multiple barely moves because growth is unchanged, and the market reprices once and moves on. On the right (the regime-change), the slope bends: the growth path itself changes, the multiple *re-rates* because higher growth earns a higher P/E, and the repricing runs for years. The practitioner's question — *is this a level-shift or a regime-change?* — tells you whether you are trading a quick re-rate or a multi-year theme.

The arithmetic behind the difference is worth seeing clearly, because it explains why regime-change rules reprice so much more. A present value compounds the growth rate over every future year, so changing the *level* of this year's earnings scales the whole value up by that one-time factor and nothing more. But changing the *growth rate* changes the value's sensitivity in the denominator of the growing-perpetuity formula — value equals next year's cash flow divided by (discount rate minus growth) — and because that denominator is a small number (the gap between the discount rate and growth), a small change in growth produces an outsized change in value. Lift sustainable growth from 3% to 5% at an 8% discount rate and the multiple goes from 1 ÷ (0.08 − 0.03) = 20× to 1 ÷ (0.08 − 0.05) = 33×, a 65% re-rate from the growth change alone — far more than any plausible one-time level shift. This is the mathematical reason a rule that opens a new market (a growth-slope event) reprices a stock more, and for longer, than a rule that simply hands it a one-time cost saving. When you classify a rule, ask whether it touches the *level* or the *slope*; the slope is where the big, durable money is.

#### Worked example: a discount-rate haircut from regulatory risk lowering a P/E

Here we isolate the *discount-rate* channel — the right-hand channel from the mechanism figure — which a regime-change risk often triggers. Suppose a stock earns \$5.00 per share and the market discounts its cash flows at 8%, with earnings growing 3% forever. A simple way to express the P/E it deserves is one divided by (discount rate minus growth): P/E = 1 ÷ (0.08 − 0.03) = 1 ÷ 0.05 = **20×**. The price is 20 × \$5.00 = **\$100**.

Now a serious antitrust investigation opens. Nothing about this year's earnings changes — but investors now demand a higher risk premium for the possibility of a forced breakup, raising the discount rate from 8% to 10%. The new P/E is 1 ÷ (0.10 − 0.03) = 1 ÷ 0.07 = **14.3×**, and the price falls to 14.3 × \$5.00 = **\$71.50**.

The stock dropped **28.5%** without a single dollar of current earnings changing — the entire move came through the discount-rate channel. *Intuition: regulatory and legal risk does not have to lower this year's profit to crush the stock; raising the rate at which all future profits are discounted is enough, and for a long-lived business that haircut is large.*

### Reversible rules vs sticky rules

The second distinction is durability, straight from the matrix. A **sticky rule** — a statute, a court precedent — is hard to undo, so the market reprices toward it as if permanent. A **reversible rule** — a tariff that a trade deal can lift, a sanction that diplomacy can ease — is held in the price only at the *probability it persists*. This is why tariff-exposed stocks are so volatile: every hint of a deal swings the probability that the rule survives, and the price swings with it.

The trading consequence is that reversible rules create *recurring* opportunities (the rule can come and go, and the market re-handicaps it repeatedly), while sticky rules tend to be priced once and then fade as a driver. Knowing which you face tells you whether to treat the situation as a one-time re-rate or an ongoing tradable theme.

## Second-order effects: substitution and who is on the other side

A first-rate analysis of a rule-change does not stop at the directly affected company. Two second-order effects routinely flip the naive conclusion.

**Substitution.** When a rule hurts one firm, business does not vanish — it moves. A tariff on imported steel hurts steel *importers* and steel-*using* manufacturers, but it *helps* domestic steel producers, who now face less competition and can raise prices. A rule that bans a product creates a windfall for the maker of the substitute. The complete trade is rarely just "short the loser"; it is often "short the loser, long the substitute," which also hedges out the broader market move.

**Who is on the other side.** Every repricing has a counterparty. When a rule is widely expected to hurt a stock and everyone has sold, the marginal seller is exhausted — and a *less-bad-than-feared* outcome can rip the stock higher even though the news is nominally negative. The most reliable edge in rule-driven trading is often not "what will the rule be?" but "what is *already priced*, and who is positioned for it?" If a rule is feared and a stock has already fallen 40%, the asymmetric trade may be the *long* into the confirmation, not the short.

There is a third second-order effect that deserves a name: **the regulatory moat.** When a rule raises the *fixed cost* of operating in an industry — compliance staff, capital requirements, licensing, testing — it falls hardest on small players and falls *proportionally lighter* on the giants who can spread that cost over a huge revenue base. A \$50m compliance bill is fatal to a firm with \$200m of revenue and a rounding error to one with \$50bn. So a rule that the headlines call "a crackdown on the industry" is frequently a *gift to the largest incumbent*, who watches smaller competitors exit and picks up their market share. This is why, again and again, the stock that rises on a regulatory crackdown is the biggest, most-compliant company in the targeted sector. The complete second-order analysis therefore asks three questions, not one: who is the direct loser, who is the *substitute beneficiary*, and who is the *incumbent* that the new fixed cost protects? Trading only the first leaves the other two — often the better trades — on the table.

A disciplined way to capture all three at once is the *pair trade*: go long the beneficiary and short the loser in matched dollar amounts. This hedges out the overall market direction (if the whole market rises or falls, both legs move together and cancel) and isolates the *relative* effect of the rule — which is exactly what your analysis predicted. A rule that you expect to shift share from importers to domestic producers is best expressed as long-domestic / short-importer, not as a naked bet on either one, because the pair pays off on the *spread* you actually have a view on while neutralizing the market move you do not.

#### Worked example: an event-study abnormal return around a court ruling

Practitioners measure the market's reaction to an event by computing the **abnormal return** — the stock's move minus what it "should" have done given the overall market. Suppose a pharmaceutical company awaits a court ruling on a key patent. On ruling day, the broad market index rises 1.0%, and historically this stock moves about 1.2× the market (its "beta"). So its *expected* return that day is 1.2 × 1.0% = **1.2%**.

The ruling goes against the company and the stock actually *falls* 14.0% on the day. The abnormal return is the actual minus the expected: −14.0% − (+1.2%) = **−15.2%**. That −15.2% is the market's estimate of the ruling's pure impact on the company, stripped of the day's general market move.

If the company's market value was \$8.0bn the day before, the ruling destroyed roughly 0.152 × \$8.0bn = **\$1.22bn** of value attributable to the legal outcome alone. *Intuition: subtracting the expected market-driven move isolates the rule's own footprint, which is the number you actually want when judging how hard a ruling hit.*

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs sound reasonable and are wrong. Each has cost investors real money.

**Misconception 1: "The law passed, so the move is over — or it's just starting."** Both halves are usually wrong. As the 2017 tax cut showed, the S&P had already risen about 20% in the year *before* passage as the probability climbed; the passage day itself was an anticlimax. The repricing happened across the rumor and proposal stages, months earlier. Conversely, people who waited to "buy the news" on passage day bought near a local top. The rule is the *culmination* of a repricing, not its trigger. **The number to remember: the biggest moves cluster on the surprises that change the probability, not on the confirmation that the rule everyone expected actually arrived.**

**Misconception 2: "Regulation is always bad for the stock."** Often the opposite. Heavy regulation raises the cost of *entering* an industry, which protects the *incumbents* who already comply. When the US imposed sweeping bank regulation after 2010, the largest banks — which could afford armies of compliance staff — gained share against smaller rivals that could not. Tobacco companies trade at high, stable cash-flow multiples precisely *because* advertising bans and litigation made the industry impossible for newcomers to enter. A rule that looks like a tax on an industry is frequently a *moat* for its biggest players. **Ask not "does this rule hurt the sector?" but "does it hurt this company *relative to its competitors*?"**

**Misconception 3: "Geopolitics doesn't matter for long-term investors."** It matters, but it matters *differently* than headlines suggest — and the data shows why.

![Area chart of the Caldara-Iacoviello geopolitical risk index spiking on shocks like 9/11 at 512 then fading toward the long-run mean of 100](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-8.png)

The Geopolitical Risk index above (a count of newspaper mentions of geopolitical tension, scaled so 100 is the 1985–2019 average) tells the real story: geopolitical shocks *spike* — 9/11 hit 512, more than five times normal — and then *mostly fade* back toward 100. For the *broad market*, most geopolitical shocks are transient: the index reverts, and a diversified long-term portfolio that panic-sold the spike usually regretted it. So the misconception has a kernel of truth for index investors. **But** geopolitics matters enormously for *specific* assets and *durable* shifts: it permanently re-rated European energy after 2022, lifted defense stocks for years, and is reshaping the semiconductor supply chain. The correct statement is: *transient* geopolitical shocks rarely change the long-run value of a diversified portfolio, but *regime-changing* geopolitical shifts permanently reprice the specific sectors they touch. Distinguishing the two is the whole game.

## How it shows up in real markets

Let us watch the chain fire end to end in three cases of different types, so the pattern becomes unmistakable.

### Case 1: the 2017 tax cut (a sticky, level-shift statute)

We have used this throughout, so here is the chain in one line. *Link 1:* a statute cut the corporate rate 35% → 21%. *Link 2:* after-tax profit per dollar rose 21.5%, and a transition tax pulled overseas cash home — repatriated dividends jumped from roughly \$155bn in 2017 to about \$1,064bn in 2018. *Link 3:* the cash flowed into buybacks, which leapt from \$519bn (2017) to \$806bn (2018). *Link 4:* higher after-tax earnings plus fewer shares lifted EPS, and equities repriced up. *Link 5:* the trade — owning the high-tax-rate domestic companies that gained the most — had to be put on *during 2017*, as the probability climbed, not on passage day. This is a textbook *sticky, level-shift* rule: priced once, durable, and largely complete by the time it was law.

The most instructive feature of this case is *who gained the most*. The headline "corporate tax cut" sounds like it should lift every company equally, but it did not. A company that had already been paying a low effective rate — because it earned most of its income abroad in low-tax jurisdictions, or used heavy tax shields — had little to gain; its rate was not really 35% to begin with. The biggest winners were *high-effective-rate, domestically-focused* companies (retailers, regional banks, domestic industrials) that had genuinely been paying close to 35% and now paid 21%. The chain analysis that stopped at "tax cut is bullish for stocks" was almost useless for picking a position; the analysis that traced link 2 down to *whose* after-tax cash flow actually changed identified the specific names that re-rated most. This is the recurring lesson of the foundations section made concrete: the trade lives in the *differential* impact across companies, not in the rule's headline direction.

### Case 2: the 2018–2019 trade war (a fast, reversible tariff)

The trade war is the cleanest example of a *rule firing into prices* in real time.

![Step chart of the US average tariff on Chinese imports rising from 3.1 percent in 2018 to 19.3 percent by 2020 and holding there](/imgs/blogs/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain-6.png)

The step chart shows the US average tariff on Chinese imports climbing from **3.1% in early 2018 to 19.3% by 2020**, holding there through 2024 — while the tariff on the rest of the world stayed flat at ~3% (the dashed line). Each step is a discrete rule firing. *Link 1:* executive orders raised tariffs in jumps. *Link 2:* the cost of imported inputs rose for US manufacturers; Chinese exporters faced shrinking US demand. *Link 3:* supply chains began shifting to Vietnam, Mexico, and India; input-cost inflation crept up. *Link 4:* import-dependent companies de-rated, domestic-substitute producers and "China+1" beneficiaries re-rated. *Link 5:* because tariffs are *reversible*, the trade was a recurring one — every rumor of a deal swung the affected stocks, and the playbook was to fade extremes in either direction as the probability of a deal oscillated. Note the contrast with Case 1: same chain, but a *fast, reversible* rule produced a choppy, recurring trade rather than a one-time re-rate.

### Case 3: the 2022 Russia sanctions (an immediate, regime-changing shock)

In February 2022, following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, Western governments imposed the most sweeping sanctions in modern history. *Link 1:* OFAC and allied bodies designated Russian banks, companies, and individuals, and — unprecedentedly — *immobilized* roughly **\$300bn of Russia's ~\$643bn in foreign-exchange reserves**. *Link 2:* Russian commerce with the West was severed; European buyers scrambled for non-Russian energy. *Link 3:* the macro shock was violent — Brent crude jumped from ~\$97 to ~\$128 within two weeks; European natural gas (TTF) eventually spiked to about €339/MWh, more than thirteen times its 2021 level. *Link 4:* energy producers and defense contractors re-rated up (the iShares aerospace and defense index rose from a rebased 100 at the invasion to 138 by end-2024), while European energy-intensive industry re-rated down. *Link 5:* the durable trade was the *regime-change* — long defense and energy security — not the transient panic. The GPR index spiked to 277 and faded to 140 by 2024, but the *structural* repricing of European energy and global defense persisted. This is the geopolitical analog of a regime-change rule: the headline shock faded, the structural shift did not.

### Case 4: the chip war (an export control as a regime-change rule)

A fourth case shows the chain firing through *export controls* — rules that restrict which technologies can be sold to whom. Starting in 2022, the US progressively barred the sale of the most advanced semiconductors and the equipment to make them to certain buyers. *Link 1:* the rule was a set of export-control regulations (delivered partly through executive action), fast and reversible in form but aimed at a *structural* outcome. *Link 2:* the policy split the chip world into two — buyers who could access the leading edge and those who could not — and incentivized a global scramble to build domestic capacity. *Link 3:* this collided with an already-concentrated supply chain. By revenue, the contract chip-making (foundry) market is dominated by a single company holding roughly **64% global share**, with about **90% of the most advanced (sub-7-nanometer) capacity** — a chokepoint a rule could weaponize. *Link 4:* equipment makers, leading-edge foundries, and domestic-fab beneficiaries re-rated up on the prospect of subsidized capacity and reduced competition; firms dependent on selling advanced chips to restricted buyers re-rated down on lost revenue. *Link 5:* because the *intent* of the rule was a durable realignment of the supply chain — not a one-time price — the trade was a regime-change theme (own the chokepoint and the domestic-capacity beneficiaries) rather than a quick reaction. This case is the clearest illustration of the matrix's subtlety: a rule that is *fast and reversible in form* can still be *regime-changing in effect* when it is aimed at a structural chokepoint. Classify by intent, not just by legal form.

#### Worked example: a sanction's revenue hit and the repricing it implies

Suppose an industrial company earns \$3,000m in annual revenue, of which **20%** comes from a country that is suddenly placed under comprehensive sanctions. Its net profit margin is 10%, so it earns \$300m of profit on that \$3,000m of revenue, and it trades at a P/E of 15, giving a market value of 15 × \$300m = **\$4,500m**.

The sanction makes the \$600m of sanctioned-country revenue (20% × \$3,000m) immediately uncollectable. Assume the associated costs cannot be cut as fast as the revenue disappears, so that segment, which had been contributing about 0.20 × \$300m = \$60m of profit, goes to roughly zero. Profit falls to \$240m. Worse, the market now attaches *uncertainty* to the rest of the business — could more sanctions follow? — and trims the multiple from 15 to 13. The new value is 13 × \$240m = **\$3,120m**.

The repricing is \$4,500m − \$3,120m = **\$1,380m wiped out, a 30.7% drop** — far larger than the 20% revenue exposure alone, because the *cash-flow* channel (lost profit) and the *discount-rate* channel (a lower multiple from added uncertainty) hit *together*. *Intuition: a sanction rarely costs only the revenue it directly blocks; it also raises the risk premium on everything that remains, and the two channels compound into a hit bigger than the headline exposure.*

## How to trade it: the five-step playbook

Everything above converges on one repeatable method. When a legal or geopolitical headline crosses your screen, run these five steps in order. They turn a headline into a position with a defined size and a defined exit.

**Step 1 — Classify the rule.** Use the taxonomy matrix. *Which* of the five is it (statute, regulation, ruling, tariff, sanction)? Answer the three questions: who writes it, how fast does it bite, is it reversible or sticky? This tells you the *shape* of the trade before you touch a number — a fast, reversible tariff is a recurring oscillation; a slow, sticky statute is a one-time re-rate.

**Step 2 — Trace the chain and find the channel.** Walk Law → Policy → Macro & Flows → Prices. Identify *which* present-value channel the rule hits: expected cash flows, the discount rate, or both. Name the specific companies, sectors, or assets at link 4, and — critically — name the *substitutes* and *beneficiaries*, not just the obvious loser.

**Step 3 — Locate yourself on the lead-lag timeline.** Which stage are we in — rumor, proposal, final, enforcement? Estimate how much is *already priced in* using the probability calculation. If the rule is 80% priced, the asymmetric move is largely gone; if it is a genuine surprise at 10%, the reward-to-risk is at its best. This step is what separates a good entry from a late one.

**Step 4 — Size the repricing and the position.** Do the present-value math (the worked examples above are the templates): how many dollars or what percentage should the asset move if the rule arrives? Combine that with the probability to get an expected value, and size the position so that being *wrong* costs an amount you can survive. The next worked example shows the calculation.

**Step 5 — Define the invalidation.** Write down, *before* entering, exactly what would prove the thesis wrong: a deal that lifts the tariff, a court ruling that goes the other way, a probability that moves against you, a price level that says the market disagrees. A trade without a pre-defined invalidation is a hope, not a position. When the invalidation triggers, you are out — no renegotiation with yourself.

A few execution disciplines separate practitioners who *use* this playbook profitably from those who merely understand it. First, **timing risk is real even when the thesis is right.** A rule you correctly expect can take far longer to arrive than you think, and a position that bleeds while you wait can force you out before the payoff — so for slow statutes, prefer instruments and sizes that let you hold for months, not days. Second, **mind the path, not just the destination.** A reversible tariff can swing your position violently in both directions before resolving where you expected; size so the *path* does not stop you out. Third, **respect the priced-in fraction.** The single most common way to lose money on a correct legal call is to put the trade on *after* the move — buying the winner once the rule has passed, when the asymmetric reward is already gone. The probability calculation in Step 3 exists precisely to keep you honest about how much is left. Fourth, **separate the legal forecast from the market reaction.** You can be exactly right about what the rule will be and still lose, because the market had priced in something worse and rallies on the "less bad than feared" outcome. Always ask what is *already in the price* before you ask what the rule will be.

These four mistakes — being early and bleeding out, getting stopped on the path, chasing a priced-in move, and confusing the legal outcome with the market reaction — account for most of the losses in rule-driven investing. The five-step loop is built to surface each of them *before* you commit capital. Classify tells you the trade shape and the timing risk; trace tells you the channel and the substitutes; locate tells you the priced-in fraction; size builds in the path tolerance; invalidate decides in advance when to admit you were wrong.

#### Worked example: converting a policy probability into expected value for a position

You judge that a proposed regulation will be *blocked* by a court. The stock currently trades at \$60. If the rule is blocked, you estimate it is worth \$80 (the overhang lifts); if the rule is upheld, it is worth \$45 (the rule bites). The ruling is two months away, and you put the probability of a block at 65%.

First, the market's implied view. The current \$60 price implies a probability *p* of a block such that p × \$80 + (1 − p) × \$45 = \$60. Solving: \$45 + p × \$35 = \$60, so p × \$35 = \$15, giving p = **43%**. The market thinks a block is only 43% likely; you think it is 65%. You have an edge *if your estimate is better than the market's*.

Now your expected value at your own probability: 0.65 × \$80 + 0.35 × \$45 = \$52.00 + \$15.75 = **\$67.75**. The trade's expected gain is \$67.75 − \$60.00 = **+\$7.75 per share**, about +12.9%. Your downside if you are wrong and the rule is upheld is \$60 − \$45 = **−\$15** (−25%); your upside if right is \$80 − \$60 = **+\$20** (+33%).

So you risk \$15 to make \$20, with a 65%-judged probability of the win. The expected value is positive (+\$7.75), the reward-to-risk is favorable (\$20 to \$15), and your invalidation is concrete: if news pushes the *market's* implied probability above your 65% (price above roughly \$67.75), your edge is gone and you exit. *Intuition: a trade is the product of an edge in probability and a favorable payoff ratio — quantifying both turns a legal opinion into a sized, disciplined position with a built-in exit.*

This five-step loop — classify, trace, locate, size, invalidate — is the engine of the entire series. Every subsequent post (on securities law, antitrust, banking rules, tariffs, sanctions, central-bank policy, court rulings, and the Vietnam-specific cases) is an application of this same chain to a specific kind of rule. Master it here and the rest of the series is practice.
