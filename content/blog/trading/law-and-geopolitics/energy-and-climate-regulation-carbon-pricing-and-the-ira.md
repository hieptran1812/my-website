> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Climate policy is now one of the largest capital-allocation forces in markets: a carbon price makes emitting cost money, and subsidies (the US Inflation Reduction Act) make clean energy cheap to build, and together they redirect hundreds of billions of dollars and create clear sector winners, losers, and stranded-asset risk.
>
> - A **carbon price** internalizes the cost of emissions. The EU's Emissions Trading System set its allowance (EUA) price from under €6/tonne in 2017 to over €80/tonne by 2022–23 — a real, rising cost line that hits steel, cement, and utilities.
> - The **Inflation Reduction Act** is not a spending bill in the usual sense: its roughly **\$370bn+** headline is mostly *uncapped tax credits*, so the true cost scales with how much clean capacity gets built — independent estimates run far higher.
> - The two tools push in opposite directions on the same goal — one taxes the dirty, one subsidizes the clean — and the trade is to map each sector onto the resulting winners/losers grid.
> - The biggest second-order risks are **stranded assets** (fossil reserves that become uneconomic) and **policy reversal** (credits as a political football, which markets price as an overhang).

On the morning of 23 February 2022, a tonne of carbon emitted inside the European Union cost a power plant or a steelmaker roughly €90 — a price that, five years earlier, had been under €6. Nothing about the chemistry of burning coal had changed. What changed was a *rule*: the EU had tightened the supply of emission allowances, and a market mechanism had repriced the right to pollute by more than fifteen-fold. That single number — the price of an EU carbon allowance — now sits inside the cost stack of every heavy industrial in Europe, and it moves the relative value of every utility, steelmaker, cement producer, and clean-energy developer on the continent.

Six months later, on the other side of the Atlantic, a different rule did the opposite. The US Inflation Reduction Act, signed in August 2022, did not tax carbon at all. Instead it offered to *pay down the build cost* of clean energy through tax credits — a production credit for every megawatt-hour of clean electricity, an investment credit worth roughly 30% of a project's capital cost, a credit of up to \$3 per kilogram for clean hydrogen, and a manufacturing credit for every battery cell made on US soil. Within eighteen months, companies had announced well over \$100bn of new clean-manufacturing investment chasing those credits.

These two laws — a carbon price in Europe and a subsidy program in America — are the two halves of how climate policy moves markets. One makes the dirty path more expensive; the other makes the clean path cheaper. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: an unpriced externality gets a price (and a subsidy), capital reallocates, sectors sort into winners and losers, and the practitioner reads the rule early to size the repricing.

![Five-step flow from the unpriced carbon externality to a carbon price plus IRA subsidies to capital reallocation to sector winners and losers to the trade](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-1.png)

This is a politically charged subject, so a note on stance: this post is analytical, not advocacy. The goal is to describe *how* these rules redirect capital and reprice assets — who pays, who benefits, what the second-order risks are, and how an investor reads the signal — not to argue whether any particular policy is good or wise. Markets do not care about the politics; they care about the cash flows the rule changes.

## Foundations: the externality, the price, and the subsidy

Start from the problem the whole field exists to solve, because every mechanism below is a different answer to it.

### Why carbon gets a price: the externality problem

An **externality** is a cost (or benefit) of an activity that falls on someone other than the person doing it, and which the market price does not capture. When a factory emits a tonne of carbon dioxide, the damage from that emission — its contribution to a warming climate, with the floods, heat, and crop losses that follow — is borne by society at large, across decades, not by the factory in its electricity bill. Because the emitter does not pay for the damage, the private cost of emitting is lower than the true social cost. Standard economics says that when private cost is below social cost, you get *too much* of the activity: firms emit more than they would if they faced the full bill.

The textbook fix, dating to the economist Arthur Pigou in the 1920s, is to **put a price on the externality** — to make the emitter pay something per tonne so that the private cost rises toward the social cost. That price is the **carbon price**. Once a tonne of CO2 costs money, every decision that produces emissions — what fuel to burn, what process to run, what plant to build — now carries that cost, and firms have a financial reason to emit less. The carbon price is the single most important number in this entire post: it is the cost line that turns climate from a moral question into a balance-sheet item.

How high *should* the price be? Economists estimate the **social cost of carbon** — the present value of the future damage from emitting one extra tonne of CO2. Official US estimates have ranged from around \$50/tonne to recent figures near \$190/tonne, depending on the discount rate and the damages modeled; the EU's market price has spent recent years in the €60–90 band. The gap between the *theoretical* social cost and the *actual* market price is itself an investment signal: where the market price is far below the estimated social cost, the policy direction is upward, and the assets that benefit from a rising price are structurally under-priced. The carbon price is not a fixed law of nature — it is a policy variable on a trajectory, and the trajectory is the trade.

### Two ways to set a carbon price: a tax versus cap-and-trade

There are two fundamentally different ways to put a price on carbon, and the distinction matters enormously for how the price *behaves* — and therefore for how you trade around it.

A **carbon tax** fixes the *price*. The government declares that every tonne of CO2 costs, say, \$50, and firms pay that on each tonne they emit. The price is certain and stable; what is uncertain is the *quantity* of emissions, because firms will emit however much still makes sense at \$50/tonne. A tax is administratively simple and gives businesses a predictable cost to plan around.

**Cap-and-trade** does the opposite: it fixes the *quantity*. The government sets a total ceiling — a **cap** — on how many tonnes may be emitted across the covered economy, then issues that many **allowances** (each one a permit to emit one tonne). Firms must hold an allowance for every tonne they emit. They can buy and sell allowances, so a *market* forms, and the **price of an allowance is discovered by trading**, not set by decree. As the government tightens the cap over time, allowances get scarcer and the price rises. The quantity of emissions is certain (it equals the cap); the price is uncertain and can swing hard.

![Side-by-side comparison of a carbon tax that fixes the price and lets quantity adjust versus cap-and-trade that fixes the quantity and lets the market discover a volatile price](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-2.png)

The trading implication is direct. A carbon tax gives you a known cost; there is little to trade in the carbon price itself because it does not move much. Cap-and-trade gives you a *traded asset* — the allowance — whose price moves with policy, weather, fuel switching, and the economic cycle. That moving allowance price is what re-rates whole sectors, and it is the heart of the EU system.

### The EU Emissions Trading System and the EUA price

The **EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)**, launched in 2005, is the world's largest cap-and-trade market. It covers roughly 40% of EU emissions — power generation, heavy industry (steel, cement, refining, chemicals), and aviation within Europe. The tradable unit is the **EU Allowance (EUA)**, each one a permit to emit one tonne of CO2. The EU sets a shrinking annual cap, auctions and allocates the allowances, and lets firms trade them on exchanges like the European Energy Exchange (EEX) and ICE.

For its first decade the EU ETS was, frankly, a weak signal: over-allocation left a glut of allowances, and the EUA price languished in the single digits — too low to change any real decision. That changed when the EU introduced the **Market Stability Reserve** in 2019, a mechanism that soaks up the surplus and tightens supply, and again when the bloc adopted more ambitious emissions targets. The price responded.

![Line chart of the EU carbon allowance EUA price rising from about 6 euros per tonne in 2017 to over 80 euros per tonne in 2022 and 2023](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-3.png)

The path is the story: roughly €6/tonne in 2017, €25 by 2019–20, €53 in 2021, and a peak band around €81–84 in 2022–23 before easing to about €65 in 2024. A fifteen-fold rise in five years is not noise — it is a structural repricing of the right to emit, and it now sits in the cost stack of every European industrial.

### The carbon border adjustment (CBAM)

A domestic carbon price has an obvious leak. If European steel must pay €90/tonne of carbon but imported Chinese or Turkish steel pays nothing, then the carbon price simply pushes production *offshore* — emissions do not fall, they relocate, and European producers lose share. This is **carbon leakage**, and it is the single biggest political vulnerability of any one-region carbon price.

The EU's answer is the **Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)**, phasing in from 2023 (reporting) with full financial obligations from 2026. CBAM requires importers of carbon-intensive goods — initially steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizer, electricity, and hydrogen — to buy certificates covering the **embedded carbon** in what they import, priced off the EUA. In effect, foreign producers must pay the same carbon cost as European ones to sell into Europe. CBAM is where climate policy becomes *trade* policy: it is a carbon tariff, and it links directly to the law of global trade.

### The US Inflation Reduction Act

The **Inflation Reduction Act (IRA)** of August 2022 is the United States' flagship climate law, but it takes the *opposite* approach to the EU. It does not price carbon. Instead it spends — or more precisely, it *forgoes tax revenue* — to make clean energy cheaper to build. The headline figure is roughly **\$370bn+** of climate and clean-energy provisions over a decade, delivered mainly through the tax code:

- The **Production Tax Credit (PTC)**: a credit (roughly \$26 per megawatt-hour, inflation-adjusted, when labor conditions are met) for each MWh of clean electricity generated, for the first ten years of a project.
- The **Investment Tax Credit (ITC)**: a credit worth roughly **30%** of a project's capital cost, claimed up front, as an alternative to the PTC.
- The **45X advanced manufacturing credit**: per-unit credits for making solar wafers, battery cells, and other components domestically.
- The **45V clean hydrogen credit**: up to **\$3 per kilogram** for hydrogen produced with low lifecycle emissions.
- Consumer credits for EVs and home efficiency, plus credits for carbon capture and nuclear.

The defining feature — the one that makes the IRA so much bigger than its headline — is that most of these credits are **uncapped**. There is no fixed pot of money that runs out. If more solar, more batteries, and more hydrogen get built, the government pays out more in credits. The fiscal cost is *demand-determined*, not appropriated. Several independent estimates (Goldman Sachs, the Penn Wharton Budget Model, Credit Suisse) put the true ten-year cost well above the \$370bn headline — some above \$800bn, some near \$1.2 trillion — precisely because the build-out has run ahead of expectations.

![Branching diagram of the IRA channeling about 370 billion dollars of tax credits through the production credit, investment credit, and hydrogen credit into solar, battery, and electrolyzer firms](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-4.png)

### Stranded assets and the green premium

Two more terms complete the toolkit.

A **stranded asset** is an asset that loses value far earlier than expected because of a change in the rules or the economics around it — most relevantly, a fossil-fuel reserve (oil, gas, coal) that can no longer be produced and sold at a profit. Reserves are booked on an oil company's balance sheet as if every barrel will eventually be sold at a market price. A rising carbon price, falling demand, or a regulatory ban can flip the marginal barrel from profitable to unprofitable, forcing a **writedown** — the reserve is "stranded" because it cannot be economically burned.

The **green premium** is the extra cost (per unit) of doing something the clean way instead of the dirty way — the gap between the price of green steel and ordinary steel, or green hydrogen and grey hydrogen. The entire policy apparatus is aimed at *shrinking the green premium*: a carbon price raises the cost of the dirty option, and a subsidy lowers the cost of the clean one, until the premium disappears and the clean choice wins on price alone.

### The two playbooks side by side

It is worth pinning down the contrast before the deep sections, because almost everything that follows is an elaboration of these two columns. The EU's "stick" and the US's "carrot" are not just different policies — they create different *kinds* of market signal, and they reward different positioning.

| Dimension | EU carbon price (the stick) | US IRA subsidy (the carrot) |
|---|---|---|
| Core mechanism | Make emitting cost money (EUA price) | Make clean energy cheap to build (tax credits) |
| Who feels it first | Emitters: steel, cement, power | Builders: solar, battery, hydrogen developers |
| The traded number | EUA allowance price (€/tonne) | Credit value (PTC \$/MWh, ITC % of cost) |
| Price behavior | Market-discovered, volatile | Statutory, stable until law changes |
| Fiscal cost | Raises revenue for the government | Forgoes revenue (uncapped, demand-driven) |
| Main second-order risk | Carbon leakage (fixed by CBAM) | Policy reversal / repeal |
| The investor's edge | Long carbon-light vs short carbon-heavy | Long the subsidized beneficiary chain |

Most of the world's emissions are still *not* priced at all: by recent counts, formal carbon pricing covers roughly a quarter of global emissions, at prices that range from a few dollars a tonne to the EU's €60–90 band. The unpriced majority is the running theme — the gap between where the carbon price is and where the science implies it "should" be is itself the asset, because closing that gap is what reprices sectors.

## How a rising EUA price changes the economics of heavy industry

The cleanest way to see a carbon price bite is to follow it into a single tonne of steel. Steelmaking via the traditional blast-furnace route emits roughly **1.8 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of steel** — it is one of the hardest sectors to decarbonize because the carbon is chemically bound up in the process (coke reduces iron ore), not just burned for heat. So every euro on the EUA price lands almost two-to-one on a tonne of steel.

#### Worked example: a steelmaker's carbon cost as the EUA repriced

Take a mid-sized European integrated steelmaker producing **10 million tonnes of steel a year** via blast furnace, emitting **1.8 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of steel**, so **18 million tonnes of CO2 a year**.

When the EUA traded at **€25/tonne** (roughly 2019–20):

- Carbon cost = 18,000,000 tonnes CO2 × €25 = **€450 million per year**.

When the EUA repriced to **€90/tonne** (early 2022):

- Carbon cost = 18,000,000 × €90 = **€1,620 million per year**.

The increase is €1,620m − €450m = **€1,170 million a year** — well over a billion euros of new annual cost, on the *same* tonnes of steel, from a rule change alone. (In practice, much of EU industry still receives a shrinking allocation of *free* allowances to limit leakage, so the cash hit is smaller than the gross figure — but free allocation is being phased out as CBAM phases in, and the *marginal* tonne is priced at the full EUA, which is what drives decisions.)

To put the cost per tonne of steel in perspective: at €90/tonne of CO2 and 1.8 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of steel, the carbon cost is about **€162 per tonne of steel**. With European hot-rolled coil often selling around €600–700/tonne, the carbon cost is on the order of a quarter of the product price — large enough to swing the plant between profit and loss. *The intuition: a carbon price does not nibble at the edges of a heavy industrial — it can move a quarter of the cost base, which is why the EUA is now a primary swing factor for European steel, cement, and power.*

The same arithmetic applies, with different intensities, to cement (roughly 0.6 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of cement, again mostly process emissions from calcining limestone), to refining, and to coal-fired power. And it compounds with energy prices: the EUA cost rides on top of fuel cost, and when European gas spiked in 2022, the *combined* cost of running a carbon-priced economy multiplied.

![Bar chart of EU TTF natural gas price spiking to 339 euros per megawatt hour in August 2022 before easing](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-8.png)

The 2022 gas shock — TTF natural gas hit roughly €339/MWh in August 2022, up from around €25 a year earlier — is the reason the carbon-cost story and the energy-security story became inseparable in Europe. A factory faced a soaring fuel bill *and* a soaring carbon bill at the same time, which is exactly when demand destruction (plants idling), fuel switching, and the political pressure to relax the carbon price all peak. We cover the energy-security half of that shock in [energy geopolitics, OPEC, and the European gas shock](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock).

### Why the carbon price reorders the merit order

The most direct place a carbon price changes behavior is in electricity, through something called the **merit order** — the sequence in which power plants are dispatched to meet demand, cheapest first. Each generator has a **short-run marginal cost**: the cost to produce one more megawatt-hour, which for a fossil plant is fuel plus the carbon cost. The grid operator stacks plants from cheapest to most expensive and runs them in that order until demand is met; the most expensive plant still needed sets the **clearing price** that everyone gets paid.

A carbon price lifts the marginal cost of fossil plants in proportion to how dirty they are, and coal is far dirtier than gas. Burning coal emits roughly **0.9 tonnes of CO2 per MWh** of electricity, while a modern gas plant emits roughly **0.35 tonnes per MWh**. So a rising EUA price loads more cost onto coal than gas, and at a high enough carbon price the order *flips*: gas becomes cheaper to run than coal even when the raw fuel cost of coal is lower. This is **fuel switching**, and it is the carbon price doing exactly what it was designed to do — pushing the dirtier plant down the merit order and out of the money.

#### Worked example: the carbon price that flips coal and gas

Take a coal plant and a gas plant competing to supply the next MWh. Use round numbers:

- **Coal:** fuel cost \$30/MWh, emits 0.9 tCO2/MWh.
- **Gas:** fuel cost \$45/MWh, emits 0.35 tCO2/MWh.

With **no carbon price**, coal is cheaper to run: \$30 versus \$45. Coal sets the price and runs; gas sits idle.

Now add a carbon price of **€50/tonne** (treat €1 ≈ \$1 for the arithmetic):

- Coal all-in = \$30 + (0.9 × \$50) = \$30 + \$45 = **\$75/MWh**.
- Gas all-in = \$45 + (0.35 × \$50) = \$45 + \$17.50 = **\$62.50/MWh**.

Now gas is cheaper, by \$12.50/MWh. The carbon price has reordered the merit order: gas runs, coal idles, and emissions fall without anyone banning coal. Find the exact tipping point by setting the two equal: \$30 + 0.9c = \$45 + 0.35c gives 0.55c = \$15, so **c ≈ \$27/tonne**. *The takeaway: a carbon price does not need to be punitive to work — once it crosses the switching threshold (here ~\$27/tonne), it silently rewires which plants run, which is why the EUA's climb past €25 in 2019 was the moment it started to bite on European power.*

### Hard-to-abate sectors and the green premium

Power is the easy case, because clean substitutes (gas, then renewables) already exist and the switch is a dispatch decision. The hard cases are the **hard-to-abate** sectors — steel, cement, chemicals, aviation, shipping — where the emissions are bound into the chemistry of the process and there is no cheap clean substitute yet. For these, the carbon price does not trigger a switch; it creates a standing cost that can only be removed by inventing and building a new, more expensive process. The gap between the clean process and the dirty one is the **green premium**.

#### Worked example: the green premium on a tonne of steel and where it closes

Take green steel made with hydrogen-based direct reduction (using clean hydrogen instead of coke) versus conventional blast-furnace steel:

- **Blast-furnace steel:** production cost ~\$500/tonne, emits 1.8 tCO2/tonne.
- **Green (hydrogen) steel:** production cost ~\$650/tonne today, near-zero emissions.

The green premium is \$650 − \$500 = **\$150/tonne** — green steel costs about 30% more, so on cost alone, nobody buys it. Now ask: at what carbon price does the premium vanish? The carbon price has to make blast-furnace steel's *carbon cost* equal the \$150 premium. Carbon cost = 1.8 × carbon price, so 1.8 × c = \$150 gives **c ≈ \$83/tonne**.

At a carbon price around \$83/tonne, conventional steel's carbon cost (\$150) lifts its total to \$650/tonne — the same as green steel — and the premium is gone. Above that, green steel is *cheaper*. *The lesson: the green premium plus the carbon price are two sides of one equation, and the whole policy design is a race to push the carbon price up and the green cost down until they cross — at which point the clean choice wins on price, no mandate required.* The EUA's 2022 peak around €90/tonne sat right in the neighborhood of the steel switching threshold, which is why green-steel projects suddenly attracted serious capital.

## The IRA as a subsidy magnet

Where the EU raises the cost of the dirty path, the IRA lowers the cost of the clean one — and because the credits are uncapped and transferable, the law behaves like a magnet for capital. The mechanism is best seen through what a credit does to a project's return.

A clean-energy project — a solar farm, a wind farm, a battery factory — is a large up-front capital cost (the build) followed by years of revenue. Investors evaluate it on its **internal rate of return (IRR)**: the discount rate at which the project's future cash flows just cover its build cost. Anything that lowers the build cost or raises the cash flows lifts the IRR, and a higher IRR pulls in more capital. The IRA does both: the ITC slashes the up-front build cost by ~30%, and the PTC adds a per-MWh revenue stream for ten years.

#### Worked example: the ITC's effect on a solar project's economics

Take a utility-scale solar project with a build cost of **\$100 million**, expected to generate cash flows (after operating costs) of **\$11 million per year** for 25 years.

Without any credit, the simple cash-on-cash yield is \$11m ÷ \$100m = **11% per year**, and the project's IRR (accounting for the 25-year life) is roughly **10%**.

Now apply the **30% Investment Tax Credit**. The developer claims a credit of 0.30 × \$100m = **\$30 million**, which directly offsets tax owed (and under the IRA can be *sold* to a third party for cash if the developer cannot use it). The effective net build cost falls to \$100m − \$30m = **\$70 million**.

- New cash-on-cash yield = \$11m ÷ \$70m = **15.7% per year**.
- The IRR rises from roughly 10% to roughly **15%** — a jump of about **5 percentage points** of return from a single tax provision.

A 5-point IRR lift is the difference between a project that struggles to clear an investor's hurdle rate and one that is comfortably financeable. *The takeaway: the ITC does not just trim cost — it can re-rate a marginal project into an attractive one, which is exactly how a subsidy "magnetizes" hundreds of billions of private capital.*

#### Worked example: the PTC on a battery / clean-power project over its credit life

Take the same developer choosing the **Production Tax Credit** instead, on a clean-power project generating **500,000 MWh per year**, at a PTC of **\$26/MWh** for ten years.

- Annual credit = 500,000 MWh × \$26 = **\$13 million per year**.
- Over the ten-year credit window = \$13m × 10 = **\$130 million** of credits.

If the project's electricity otherwise sells for around \$40/MWh, the \$26/MWh credit is **65% on top of the energy price** for a decade — it can more than double the effective revenue per MWh in the early years. That is why so much new wind and utility-scale solar opted for the PTC, and why the 45X manufacturing credit (per battery cell, per solar wafer) triggered a wave of US factory announcements: the credit is paid *per unit produced*, so the more you build and run, the more you collect. *The lesson: an uncapped, per-unit credit turns volume into subsidy, which is precisely the incentive that built a domestic manufacturing boom.*

The beneficiaries fall into recognizable buckets: solar and wind developers and the utilities that own them; battery and EV manufacturers (and their lithium, cathode, and cell suppliers); electrolyzer makers and the nascent clean-hydrogen industry; and the engineering and equipment firms that build all of it. When the IRA passed, the market re-rated these names — not on current earnings, but on a credibly larger and cheaper-to-finance addressable market for a decade.

### Why transferability and direct pay made the IRA bigger than it looks

Two technical features of the IRA matter more than their dryness suggests, because they widened the pool of capital that can chase the credits.

The first is **transferability**. Before the IRA, a developer who could not use a tax credit (because it did not owe enough tax) had to find a "tax-equity" partner — usually a big bank — through a complex, expensive structured deal, and only a handful of institutions had the appetite. The IRA let developers simply *sell* most credits for cash to any taxpayer. A small solar developer can now monetize its ITC by selling it to a profitable corporation that wants to cut its tax bill, at perhaps 90–95 cents on the dollar. That turns a clumsy, bank-gated market into a near-cash subsidy and dramatically lowers the cost of capital for smaller players.

The second is **direct pay** (elective payment): tax-exempt entities — municipal utilities, rural electric cooperatives, governments — that owe no tax can receive the credit as a *cash payment* from the Treasury. Before, these entities got nothing from a tax credit; now they get the full value, which is why public power and co-ops became active clean-energy builders almost overnight.

#### Worked example: monetizing an ITC through transferability

Take the same \$100m solar project with a 30% ITC = **\$30m** credit, built by a developer with little taxable income (so it cannot use the credit itself).

Under the old regime, the developer needed a tax-equity deal, which typically left it capturing only **\~70–80%** of the credit's value after fees and the partner's required return — call it \$22m of effective benefit, and only if a tax-equity provider was available at all.

Under transferability, the developer **sells** the \$30m credit to a profitable corporation at, say, **93 cents on the dollar**: it receives 0.93 × \$30m = **\$27.9 million in cash**, with far lower transaction cost and no need to find a specialized partner. *The takeaway: transferability did not change the headline credit, but by converting it from an illiquid tax shield into near-cash, it raised the realized value of every credit and pulled a far wider set of developers and financiers into the build-out.*

### A global subsidy race, not a one-country story

The IRA did not happen in isolation; it kicked off a **subsidy arms race**. The EU responded with its Green Deal Industrial Plan and the Net-Zero Industry Act, loosening state-aid rules so member states could match US incentives. Canada, Japan, and others rolled out their own clean-manufacturing supports. The competitive dynamic matters for investors because it widens the set of beneficiaries beyond US borders — and because it raises the stakes of *where* a factory locates. The IRA's domestic-content requirements (extra credit for using US-made components, US-extracted minerals, and assembly in North America) are explicitly designed to pull supply chains onshore, which reshapes the geography of who wins. We treat the reshoring dynamic in the supply-chain context of the broader series; the point here is that climate subsidy is now an instrument of industrial *and* trade policy, not just environmental policy.

## CBAM as a trade-policy bridge

A carbon price that stops at the border is half a policy. CBAM is the bridge that carries the EU's carbon price *across* the border, and in doing so it turns an environmental rule into an instrument of trade law.

The mechanics: an EU importer of covered goods (steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizer, hydrogen, electricity to start) must, from 2026, surrender CBAM certificates equal to the embedded carbon in the imported goods, priced at the prevailing EUA level. If the exporting country already charged its own carbon price, that is deducted. The effect is to **equalize the carbon cost** between a tonne of European steel (which paid the EUA) and a tonne of imported steel (which now pays the CBAM certificate).

![Flow showing how an EU producer paying the EUA price faces carbon leakage from cheaper unpriced imports, which CBAM fixes by making importers buy certificates to level the field](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-6.png)

#### Worked example: a CBAM levy on a carbon-intensive import

Take a European buyer importing **50,000 tonnes of steel** from a country with no carbon price. The embedded carbon is **1.8 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of steel**, and the EUA (the CBAM reference price) is **€85/tonne**.

- Embedded carbon = 50,000 tonnes steel × 1.8 = **90,000 tonnes of CO2**.
- CBAM cost = 90,000 × €85 = **€7,650,000** of certificates.

On a 50,000-tonne shipment, that is **€153 per tonne of imported steel** — added to the import price. If the foreign steel was landing at, say, €550/tonne and European steel cost €620/tonne, the €153 CBAM levy erases the foreign cost advantage and then some. *The intuition: CBAM is a carbon tariff that converts an emissions gap into a price gap at the border, which is why it sits at the intersection of climate and trade policy.*

CBAM is consequential for three reasons. First, it protects EU industry from leakage, which makes the high EUA price *politically sustainable* — a carbon price that destroyed domestic industry would be repealed. Second, it is a de facto **export of EU climate policy**: trading partners now have an incentive to price their own carbon (so the revenue stays home rather than going to Brussels), which is how the EU's rule ripples outward. Third, it is a live **trade-dispute risk** — affected exporters argue it is a disguised tariff that may run against WTO rules. We unpack the tariff-authority and WTO mechanics in [the legal architecture of global trade](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade).

There is a fourth, subtler consequence: CBAM creates a **diplomatic incentive structure**. Because a CBAM certificate's cost is *reduced* by any carbon price the exporting country already charges, every trading partner now faces a choice — let the revenue flow to the EU as a CBAM levy, or set up its own carbon price and keep that revenue at home. Either way the carbon gets priced; the only question is who collects. This is why CBAM is sometimes described as a mechanism that "drags" the rest of the world toward carbon pricing without negotiating a single global treaty. For an investor, the signal is forward-looking: as more countries stand up their own carbon prices in response, the set of regions where emitters face a cost-per-tonne keeps widening, and the carbon-light-versus-carbon-heavy spread becomes a global trade rather than a European one.

## Stranded-asset risk for fossil producers

The mirror image of the clean-energy re-rating is the fossil-producer de-rating, and its sharpest expression is the stranded-asset problem. An oil or gas company's market value rests heavily on its **proven reserves** — the barrels it has the right to extract — valued as if all of them will be produced and sold at a healthy margin. A carbon price plus structurally falling demand attacks that assumption from both ends: it raises the cost of every produced barrel and lowers the price the barrel can fetch.

#### Worked example: a stranded-asset writedown at a carbon price

Take a producer holding **1 billion barrels** of proven reserves. Assume:

- Market oil price (with softening demand): **\$60/barrel**.
- All-in production cost (lifting, transport, overhead): **\$40/barrel**.
- A carbon cost imposed on the produced/consumed barrel: **\$25/barrel** (roughly a \$60/tonne-CO2 price applied at ~0.43 tonnes of CO2 per barrel of crude).

Without a carbon price, the margin is \$60 − \$40 = **\$20/barrel**, so the reserve is worth on the order of 1,000,000,000 × \$20 = **\$20 billion** of gross margin (before discounting).

Add the \$25/barrel carbon cost. The effective cost becomes \$40 + \$25 = **\$65/barrel**, which is now **above** the \$60 realizable price. The marginal barrel loses \$5. Those barrels are **uneconomic**: producing them destroys value. They cannot be booked at a positive margin, so the reserve value collapses toward zero and the company books a **writedown** — billions of dollars of "assets" that turn out to be liabilities to produce.

*The lesson: a stranded asset is not a barrel that disappears — it is a barrel whose cost, once carbon is priced in, rises above what anyone will pay, so it sits in the ground as a writedown rather than a sale.* This is not hypothetical: major oil companies took tens of billions of dollars of asset impairments in 2020 partly on lowered long-run price and demand assumptions, and the concept of a "carbon budget" — the finite tonnes that can still be emitted to hold warming to a target — implies that a large share of known fossil reserves is, by definition, unburnable.

![Before-and-after comparison of a fossil reserve worth 20 billion dollars at a 20 dollar margin becoming a writedown once a carbon cost pushes the effective cost above the realizable price](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-7.png)

The market does not wait for the writedown. It applies a **stranded-asset discount** to fossil producers *in advance* — a lower multiple on current earnings to reflect the risk that the long tail of reserves never gets monetized. That discount is the fossil-sector equivalent of the regulatory risk premium we discuss in [regulatory risk as an asset-pricing factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/regulatory-risk-as-an-asset-pricing-factor): a permanently lower valuation imposed by the *expectation* of a rule, long before the rule fully bites.

## The policy-reversal risk: subsidies as a political football

Every mechanism above assumes the rule stays in place. The single largest risk to a climate-policy trade is that the rule *changes* — and climate policy is unusually exposed to reversal because it is fiercely partisan and delivered through instruments (tax credits, agency rules, allowance caps) that a future government can tighten, loosen, or repeal.

On the subsidy side, the IRA's tax credits are creatures of statute, which means a future Congress can shorten their life, narrow eligibility, or repeal them. Even the threat does damage: a clean-energy developer financing a 20-year project on the assumption of a ten-year PTC must price in the probability that the credit is curtailed. That probability shows up as a **valuation overhang** — clean-energy equities trade at a discount to where the credits alone would justify, because the market is haircutting the credits for political risk. (A partial counterweight, often underappreciated: a large share of IRA-driven manufacturing investment has landed in districts whose representatives opposed the bill, which creates a local constituency for keeping the credits — repeal is rarely as clean as a campaign promise.)

On the carbon-price side, the reversal risk is a *loosening* of the cap or a flood of free allowances under industrial or political pressure — exactly what kept the EUA price near zero for a decade. A trader long EUAs is implicitly betting that the EU keeps tightening; a political decision to ease the cap would crush the price.

The practical consequence is that climate-policy assets carry a **two-sided policy beta**: they rally when policy tightens (a higher carbon price, richer credits, CBAM expansion) and sell off when policy is threatened (repeal talk, a softer cap, a court challenge). Reading the political calendar — elections, agency rule-makings, court dockets — is therefore as important as reading the carbon price itself. We treat the calendar discipline in the series spine, [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain).

There is also an asymmetry worth pricing. Repeal of a tax credit is legislatively hard — it requires new law, it would strand investment already committed (including in districts whose representatives might otherwise vote to repeal), and it tends to grandfather projects already underway. So the *base case* is durability with risk at the edges (narrowed eligibility, shortened windows, tighter domestic-content rules), not a clean wipeout. The market's overhang often over-weights the tail-risk of full repeal, which can create opportunity when the political noise is loudest and the cash flows are least affected. The discipline is to separate the *headline risk* (which moves the stock) from the *cash-flow risk* (which moves the value) — they are frequently not the same size.

#### Worked example: sizing the policy-reversal overhang

Take a clean-energy developer whose projects, with the IRA credits fully intact, are worth **\$50 per share** on a discounted-cash-flow basis. The market is pricing the stock at **\$38**, a 24% discount.

Decompose the discount. Suppose analysts judge the probability of a *materially* damaging credit curtailment at **30%**, and that in that scenario the fair value drops to **\$32** (credits narrowed, not erased, since committed projects are grandfathered). The probability-weighted value is:

- 0.70 × \$50 + 0.30 × \$32 = \$35 + \$9.60 = **\$44.60**.

If the fundamentals-plus-policy value is \$44.60 and the stock trades at \$38, the market is pricing *more* reversal risk than your scenario implies. Back out the market's implied bad-case probability by solving for the weight that makes the blended value equal the price: x × \$32 + (1 − x) × \$50 = \$38, which gives −\$18x = −\$12, so **x ≈ 67%**. The market is charging a 67% chance of the damaging case versus your 30% estimate. *The takeaway: the policy-reversal overhang is quantifiable — write down the scenarios, weight them, and back out the probability the market is charging; the trade is the gap between the market's 67% and your 30%.*

## Common misconceptions

**"Carbon pricing just kills corporate profits."** It redistributes them. A carbon price is a cost to emitters but a *tailwind* to their low-carbon competitors and to clean-energy suppliers. When the EUA repriced from €25 to €90, it cost our example steelmaker over €1bn a year — but it simultaneously improved the relative economics of electric-arc-furnace steel (which uses scrap and electricity, not coke), of renewables that displace fossil power, and of any firm selling abatement technology. The error is to look only at the cost side. Across a portfolio, a carbon price is roughly a transfer from the carbon-intensive to the carbon-light, not a uniform tax on "business." The trade is the *spread*, not the level.

**"The IRA is just \$370bn of government spending."** This understates it badly, in two ways. First, the credits are mostly **uncapped tax credits**, not a fixed appropriation — the fiscal cost scales with how much gets built, and independent estimates put the true ten-year figure far above \$370bn, with several above \$800bn. Second, the *private* capital the credits mobilize dwarfs the public cost: a 30% ITC means the government funds roughly a third of a project while private investors fund the other two-thirds, so each \$1 of credit can pull in \$2–3 of private capital. The headline number is the *seed*, not the total.

**"Clean energy is subsidy-dependent forever."** The data argue otherwise. The point of a subsidy is to buy scale, and scale crushes cost. The levelized cost of solar electricity fell by roughly **85–90%** over the 2010s, and onshore wind by more than half, largely through the volume that early subsidies financed. In many regions, new solar and wind are now the cheapest source of new electricity *unsubsidized*. The IRA's job is to do the same for the next wave — batteries, hydrogen, domestic manufacturing — not to prop up mature solar forever. The misconception confuses a *transition* subsidy (temporary, scale-buying) with a *permanent* one.

**"A high EUA price means European industry is doomed."** It would be, if the carbon price stopped at the border — but CBAM is specifically designed to prevent that, by making imports pay the same carbon cost. The combination of a high internal price *and* a border adjustment is what lets the EU keep tightening without simply exporting its industry. The risk is real (CBAM is complex, partially phased, and contested at the WTO), but "high carbon price = dead industry" ignores the second half of the policy design.

**"A carbon price and a subsidy are basically the same thing."** They aim at the same target but behave very differently as market signals, and the difference is tradeable. A carbon price is *market-discovered and volatile* — the EUA swung from €25 to €90 and back to €65, so it is itself a position with cyclical risk. A subsidy is *statutory and stable* until the law changes — the PTC is \$26/MWh whether the economy is hot or cold — so the risk is binary (repeal) rather than cyclical. A carbon price *raises government revenue*; an uncapped credit *forgoes* it, which is why the fiscal politics differ. And they hit opposite ends of the chain: the carbon price lands first on emitters, the subsidy first on builders. Treating them as interchangeable blurs exactly the distinctions that decide which side of a trade you want to be on.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The lost decade of the EU ETS (2008–2018).** Before the EU ETS became a force, it was a cautionary tale. Launched in 2005 and over-allocated from the start, the system was hit by the 2008 financial crisis — recession cut emissions, so allowances piled up unused, and the EUA price collapsed toward €5 and stayed there for nearly a decade. At that level the carbon price changed *nothing*: it was too small to flip the merit order or to make any abatement investment pay. The lesson, expensive and real, is that a cap-and-trade system is only as strong as its cap — a glut of allowances renders the price meaningless. It took the 2019 Market Stability Reserve and tighter targets to drain the surplus and let the price do its job. For an investor, the episode is a permanent reminder that the carbon price is a *policy* output, and a poorly designed or politically loosened scheme can leave it stranded near zero for years.

**The EUA repricing hits European industrials (2021–2022).** As the EUA climbed from the low €20s to the €80s through 2021 into early 2022, the cost stack of every covered industrial rose with it. European utilities with coal-heavy fleets saw the spark/dark spreads (the margin of power over fuel-plus-carbon) compress, while gas and renewables-heavy generators fared better; steel and cement names carried a visibly rising carbon line in their cost guidance. The repricing was not a one-day event — it was a year-long *drift* as the market digested the tighter cap and the Market Stability Reserve, which is the textbook pattern of a rule getting priced in gradually rather than in a single jump.

**The IRA beneficiary re-rating (August 2022).** When the IRA passed in August 2022, clean-energy equities, solar names, battery and EV supply-chain stocks, and hydrogen plays re-rated sharply higher on the news, then again as the scale of announced manufacturing investment became clear over the following year. The re-rating was about the *future* addressable market and its newly lower cost of capital, not current earnings — which is exactly why some of it later faded when rising interest rates raised financing costs and policy-reversal risk rose into the 2024 US election. A subsidy lifts the level of expected cash flows; rates and political risk move the discount rate applied to them, and both matter.

**The stranded-asset discount on fossil producers.** Through the 2020s, major integrated oil companies have traded at low single-digit to high single-digit earnings multiples even in periods of strong cash flow — a persistent discount that partly reflects the market's reluctance to capitalize the long tail of reserves at full value. The 2020 wave of impairments (tens of billions of dollars written off by several majors as they cut long-run price assumptions) made the abstract "stranded asset" concrete: reserves booked as assets became writedowns when the long-run economics were marked down.

**CBAM and the 2026 trade flashpoint.** As CBAM's financial obligations phase in from 2026, carbon-intensive exporters into the EU — steel and aluminum producers in particular — face a real cost at the border, and several governments have signaled WTO challenges or retaliatory measures. This is climate policy operating as trade policy in real time: an emissions rule reshaping trade flows and creating a cross-border dispute, exactly the kind of rule-change a practitioner reads ahead of the deadline.

**The US manufacturing-announcement wave (2022–2024).** In the eighteen months after the IRA, companies announced well over \$100bn of new US clean-manufacturing investment — battery gigafactories across the Southeast, solar module lines, and EV plants — a visible, trackable response to the 45X and ITC credits. For an investor, the announcement flow itself was a tradable signal: each new gigafactory is a multi-year order book for the equipment makers, the engineering firms, and the materials suppliers that feed it, and the cluster of plants in a few states created regional beneficiaries (local utilities, industrial real estate, construction). The lesson is that an uncapped per-unit subsidy converts into a physical capital-spending cycle, and capital-spending cycles have long, readable supply chains.

**The 2023 clean-energy de-rating on rates and policy doubt.** The mirror to the 2022 re-rating came in 2023, when many clean-energy and solar names fell sharply even though the IRA credits were fully intact. Two forces did it. First, rising real interest rates raised the discount rate on long-duration, capital-intensive projects — a solar farm's cash flows stretch 25 years, so they are acutely rate-sensitive, and higher rates cut their present value regardless of the subsidy. Second, as the 2024 US election approached, the market began pricing a probability of credit curtailment, widening the policy-reversal overhang. The episode is the cleanest illustration that a subsidy lifts the *numerator* (expected cash flows) while rates and political risk move the *denominator* (the discount rate), and that a beneficiary trade can lose money even when its subsidy is untouched.

**The EUA's cyclical wobble (2022–2024).** After peaking near €100 intraday in early 2023, the EUA price eased toward the €60s into 2024 — not because the cap loosened, but because a warm winter, weak industrial demand in a soft European economy, and high renewable output cut emissions, so fewer allowances were needed. This is the EUA behaving like the cyclical, demand-sensitive asset it is: recessions and mild weather are bearish for the carbon price, cold snaps and strong growth are bullish. A trader long the EUA on a structural-tightening thesis still has to survive the cycle, which is why the carbon price is a *position*, not a one-way bet.

## How to trade it: the playbook

The whole apparatus collapses into a single discipline: **map the carbon price and the subsidies onto the sectors, identify the winners and losers, and position around the rule-change before it fully bites.** Here is the structure.

**1. Map the two levers onto a winners/losers grid.** A rising carbon price punishes high-emission, hard-to-abate sectors (steel, cement, coal power, oil and gas) and rewards low-emission competitors and abatement suppliers. Subsidies reward whatever the credits target (solar, wind, batteries, hydrogen, domestic manufacturing). Build the grid: carbon-price exposure on one axis, subsidy exposure on the other, and read the net direction for each sector.

![Matrix of sectors against carbon-price exposure and subsidy exposure showing solar and battery as winners, utilities mixed, and steel cement and oil and gas as losers](/imgs/blogs/energy-and-climate-regulation-carbon-pricing-and-the-ira-5.png)

The clearest expression of the trade is rarely an outright bet on the sector; it is the **spread** — long the policy-advantaged side, short or underweight the policy-disadvantaged side, which strips out the broad market and isolates the climate-policy signal. Long clean-power developers versus short coal-heavy generators; long electric-arc-furnace steel versus short blast-furnace steel; long the abatement-technology supplier versus the unabated emitter.

The regulated-utility case deserves a separate note because it is the most misread. A utility is neither a clean winner nor a fossil loser by default — its position depends entirely on its **generation mix** and its **rate structure**. A coal-heavy regulated utility carries a rising carbon cost; a utility pivoting hard to subsidized renewables uses the IRA to build a *rate-based* asset (it earns a regulated return on the capital it deploys, and the credits cut the cost it must recover from customers). The same policy framework can make one utility a loser and its neighbor a winner, so the analysis is bottom-up: read the fleet, read the capex plan, and read how much of the IRA benefit the regulator lets the utility keep versus pass to ratepayers. The mistake is to treat "utilities" as a single bucket; the carbon price and the subsidy *split* the sector, and the split is the alpha.

**2. Trade the carbon price as a traded asset.** The EUA itself trades, and its path is a function of the cap trajectory, fuel-switching economics, weather (cold winters lift power demand and the price), the economic cycle (recessions cut emissions and the price), and policy decisions on the cap and free allocation. A view that the EU keeps tightening is a long-EUA view; a view that political pressure forces a looser cap is a short. The EUA is also a direct cost input for industrials — a rising EUA is a margin headwind for emitters and a relative tailwind for the carbon-light.

**3. Play the IRA through the beneficiary chain, and size the policy-reversal overhang.** The credits flow through identifiable buckets (PTC/ITC to developers and utilities, 45X to manufacturers, 45V to hydrogen). The re-rating is about a larger, cheaper-to-finance addressable market — but discount it for the credit's political durability and for the interest-rate sensitivity of long-dated, capital-intensive cash flows. A rising-rate environment is a headwind even when the subsidy is intact, because it raises the discount rate on the project's distant cash flows.

**4. Use the CBAM trade angle.** As CBAM phases in, the relative winners are EU producers who already pay the EUA (their import competition now pays too) and exporters who price their own carbon (they keep the revenue at home). The losers are carbon-intensive exporters into the EU with no domestic carbon price. CBAM is also a trade-dispute catalyst — a WTO challenge or retaliation is a headline risk to monitor on the calendar.

**5. Apply the stranded-asset lens to fossil producers.** Separate the *cash machine* (near-term production at today's prices) from the *reserve tail* (long-dated barrels at risk of being unburnable). The market already applies a discount; the question is whether it is too large (a value opportunity if demand proves sticky) or too small (a trap if the carbon price and demand decline accelerate). Capital discipline matters more than reserve size in this world — a producer returning cash rather than chasing new long-cycle reserves is de-risking its own stranded-asset exposure.

**What invalidates the view.** The climate-policy trade breaks in four ways, and each is a defined signal to watch:

- **Policy reversal.** Repeal or curtailment of the IRA credits, a looser EU cap, or a flood of free allowances. This is the dominant risk — watch the legislative and electoral calendar and agency rule-makings.
- **A carbon-price collapse.** A deep recession or a political decision that crushes the EUA removes the cost line that drives the winners/losers spread.
- **A rates regime that overwhelms the subsidy.** Clean-energy projects are long-duration, capital-intensive cash flows; a sustained jump in real rates can sink their valuations even with the credits fully intact.
- **A demand path that keeps fossil reserves economic.** If oil and gas demand proves stickier than the transition narrative assumes, the stranded-asset discount is too large and the de-rating reverses.

The discipline is the same one that runs through this whole series: a rule changes the rules of the game, markets discount the change before it fully arrives, and the practitioner's job is to read the rule early, size the repricing across the winners and losers, and know precisely what would prove the thesis wrong.
