> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A legal and geopolitical risk dashboard is a four-layer system that tracks *what is changing in the rules of the game* and maps it to *what that change does to your book*; without the exposure layer, a monitor is just a news feed.
>
> - The four layers, bottom to top: a **catalyst calendar** of known dates, a set of **live-risk monitors** (GPR, EPU, VIX, the OFAC feed, the Federal Register), an **exposure map** scoring each holding against each risk factor, and **action triggers** that pre-commit you to a hedge or trim at a defined level.
> - **Monitoring** answers "what changed?"; **exposure** answers "what does it do to me?" The same headline is a 4-percent gain for one book and an 18-percent drawdown for another — only the exposure map knows which.
> - Score each holding a **risk-beta** of 0 to 3 per factor (revenue exposure times price sensitivity). Multiply by position size and the expected repricing to get dollars at risk.
> - The number to remember: a trigger-based hedge that fires roughly **once a year** can cost a fraction of always-on protection — in the worked example below, **\$45,000 a year** versus **\$300,000** for continuous puts on the same book — while still catching the regime-shift moves that matter.

On a Friday afternoon in October 2022, a portfolio manager running a long-short equity book got a one-line alert from a homemade script: the US Bureau of Industry and Security had just published a sweeping new set of export controls on advanced semiconductors and the equipment that makes them. The manager did not have to read the 139-page rule to act. The dashboard had already tagged three holdings — a US chip-equipment maker, a fabless designer with heavy China revenue, and a Chinese-listed foundry ADR — as carrying a high "export-control beta." By the time the broader market digested the rule the following Monday and the chip-equipment names gapped down double digits, the position had already been trimmed and partially hedged. The repricing was caught not because the manager was smart about semiconductors on that particular Friday, but because the *system* knew which names were exposed and was watching the one docket that mattered.

This post is the synthesis of the entire series. Every prior post taught one mechanism — how a tariff prices in, how a sanction bites, how a court ruling repriced a sector, how an election platform moves a portfolio. Each was a lesson in *reading* one kind of rule-change. The job now is to turn all of that reading into a **process**: a dashboard that runs every day whether or not you are paying attention, that knows your holdings, and that tells you not just "the world changed" but "the world changed in a way that costs *you* a measurable number of dollars, and here is the level at which you do something about it."

A dashboard is the difference between an investor who *reacts* to headlines after they have already moved prices, and one who has pre-mapped the exposures and pre-committed the responses. The first is always late and always emotional. The second is early and mechanical. The whole point of building the system is to push every decision you can out of the panic of the moment and into the calm of the design phase. Let us build it from the components the series introduced.

## Foundations: what a risk dashboard actually is

Start from zero. A **risk dashboard** is a single, regularly updated view that pulls together (a) the things that could change the rules or the geopolitical backdrop your portfolio lives in, and (b) a measure of how much each of your holdings would be hurt or helped if those things did change. It is not a stock screen, and it is not a news app. A stock screen tells you what is cheap; a news app tells you what happened. A risk dashboard tells you *what is at stake for you* and *when*.

Two words do all the work in that definition, and they are easy to confuse, so define them sharply:

- **Monitoring** is measuring *the world*. It is the set of feeds that tell you what is changing in law and geopolitics: a court is hearing a case, a regulator posted a proposed rule, the geopolitical-risk index jumped, a name was added to the sanctions list. Monitoring is outward-facing. It does not know or care what you own.
- **Exposure** is measuring *your book*. It is the map from each risk factor to the dollars in your portfolio that would move if that factor changed. Exposure is inward-facing. It is specific to your holdings, your sizes, and your time horizon.

The reason to insist on the distinction is that a monitor alone is worthless and even dangerous. A feed of every geopolitical headline produces a steady drip of alarming news, almost none of which touches your specific holdings — and an investor who tries to trade every monitor reading will churn the book, pay spreads and taxes, and end up worse than one who never looked. The monitor only becomes useful the moment it is joined to an exposure map: *this* change, run through *your* holdings, equals *this many dollars at risk*. That join is the entire game.

![Two columns showing three monitor readings on the left and the very different book impacts they produce on the right](/imgs/blogs/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard-8.png)

The figure makes the asymmetry concrete. The Geopolitical Risk index jumping from 140 to 250 is the same headline for everyone; what it *means* depends entirely on the book. For a portfolio heavy in aerospace and defense, that jump historically coincides with a gain — defense order books swell when conflict risk rises. For a book heavy in airlines and consumer discretionary, the same jump is a loss, because higher oil and weaker demand follow. A new sanctions listing might hit nothing you own, in which case it is pure noise and the correct response is to do nothing. The dashboard's job is to route every monitor reading through the exposure map and surface only the ones that produce a real number.

### The data sources: where the monitoring feeds come from

The monitoring layer is built from public, mostly free data. The series has referenced all of these; here they are gathered as the dashboard's input feeds:

- **The Federal Register and the Unified Agenda.** The Federal Register is the US government's daily journal of proposed and final rules; the Unified Agenda is the semiannual list every agency publishes of the rules it plans to issue, with target dates. Together they are the master schedule of US rule-making. Tracking the Unified Agenda is how you learn months ahead that, say, the EPA intends to finalize a power-plant rule in the third quarter. (The full mechanics of the rule-making clock are covered in [the regulatory calendar](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock).)
- **regulations.gov dockets.** Every proposed federal rule has a docket where the public comments are posted. The comments — especially the ones filed by the industry's own lawyers and lobbyists — are a remarkably candid preview of how a rule will land and who it will hurt. Reading the docket is legal front-running of the public record.
- **Calendars: SCOTUS, FDA, elections, central banks.** The Supreme Court's term runs October to late June, and its decisions can reprice whole sectors. The FDA publishes PDUFA dates — the deadlines by which it must rule on a drug application — which are binary catalysts for biotech. Election dates are fixed years in advance. Central-bank meeting dates are published a year ahead. All of these are *known-date* risks.
- **The GPR and EPU indices.** The **Geopolitical Risk (GPR) index** of Caldara and Iacoviello counts the share of newspaper articles about geopolitical tension; it spikes around wars and attacks, with a long-run mean set to 100. The **Economic Policy Uncertainty (EPU) index** of Baker, Bloom, and Davis does the same for policy uncertainty. Both are continuous, daily-ish gauges of the *level* of risk in the air.
- **OFAC SDN feeds.** The Office of Foreign Assets Control publishes the **Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list** — the names US persons are barred from transacting with. OFAC posts machine-readable updates; a holding (or its key customer or supplier) appearing on the list is a discrete, often catastrophic event. (See [the OFAC mechanics post](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sanctions-law-ofac-the-sdn-list-and-secondary-sanctions).)
- **Lobbying-disclosure databases.** US lobbying filings (and similar registries abroad) reveal which firms are spending to shape which rules. A surge in lobbying spend on a specific bill is a signal that the rule is real and imminent and that the affected companies think it matters.

None of these is exotic. The skill is not in finding secret data; it is in *wiring the public data to your holdings* so that the relevant subset surfaces automatically.

It is worth dwelling on *why* the public data is enough, because the instinct of most investors is to assume the edge must live in something proprietary and expensive. The opposite is true for legal and geopolitical risk. The whole machinery of rule-making is, by design, transparent: a US agency cannot simply impose a rule by surprise. It must announce its intention (the Unified Agenda), publish a formal proposal (the Notice of Proposed Rule-making in the Federal Register), accept and respond to public comments (the docket on regulations.gov), and then publish a final rule with a stated effective date — usually 30 or 60 days out — before it binds anyone. Each of those steps is a public, dated event. The transparency that the law requires *for fairness* is the same transparency that lets a dashboard see the rule coming. The investor who loses to a rule-change almost never lost because the information was hidden; they lost because they were not looking at the place it was published in plain sight.

The same logic holds for the geopolitical feeds. The GPR and EPU indices are built entirely from public newspaper text and are published free by their academic authors. The OFAC SDN list is a legal requirement — a sanction is meaningless unless the public knows who is on it, so the list *must* be published. The lobbying registries exist because disclosure is mandated by law. In every case, the data is free and public *because the system depends on it being public*, and the dashboard simply harvests what the rules already require to be in the open. The cost of building the monitoring layer is measured in hours of wiring, not dollars of data fees.

A practical note on how to wire it: each feed should be reduced to a small number of *machine-checkable conditions* rather than a stream of prose to read. The OFAC feed becomes a daily set-membership test ("is any new SDN name in my watchlist?"). The GPR feed becomes a threshold test ("is the index above 200 and higher than last week?"). The Federal Register feed becomes a keyword filter ("any new entry tagged to my sectors?"). The calendar becomes a date proximity test ("any tagged catalyst inside two weeks?"). Reducing each feed to a yes/no condition is what keeps the daily read down to two minutes instead of an hour of doom-scrolling, and it is the single most important design choice in the whole build.

## The four-layer dashboard

A working dashboard has four layers, and they stack in a specific order because each depends on the one below it. From the base up: the catalyst calendar, the live-risk monitors, the exposure map, and the action triggers. The first figure showed the stack; now walk each layer in turn.

### Layer 1 — the catalyst calendar (known dates)

The bottom layer is the cheapest and the most underrated: a calendar of dates you already know. Roughly half the legal and geopolitical risk a portfolio faces in a given year arrives on a *scheduled* date — a court decision, a rule's effective date, an FDA deadline, an election, a central-bank meeting, an OPEC gathering. None of these is a surprise. The only question is whether you have written them down against your holdings.

![Timeline across twelve months marking an FDA decision, a final-rule effective date, a Supreme Court ruling, an OPEC meeting, an election, and a drug-price list](/imgs/blogs/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard-4.png)

The discipline is to build a single calendar each January (and refresh it quarterly) with every known-date catalyst that could touch a holding, tagged with which holding it touches and how large the position is. A biotech name with a PDUFA date in March goes on the calendar in January. The Supreme Court's pending case on agency authority, due by late June, goes on the calendar the moment the Court grants certiorari. The point is not to trade every date; most pass quietly. The point is that no scheduled catalyst should ever arrive as a surprise on a book you manage, because a surprise is the one thing you had the information to avoid.

A calendar catalyst is also where the cheapest edge lives, because the *date* is public but the *positioning* is sloppy. Markets routinely under-prepare for known dates — implied volatility often fails to fully reflect a binary event until the last few sessions, and many investors simply forget the date is coming. (The drift-and-fade dynamics around these events are the subject of [how to trade a regulatory event](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-to-trade-a-regulatory-event).)

There is a useful taxonomy inside the calendar layer, because not all known dates are the same shape. A **hard date** is fixed and immovable: an election is on its statutory day, a central-bank meeting is on its announced day, a final rule's effective date is printed in the rule. A **soft date** is a deadline the outcome must arrive *by* but might arrive earlier: an FDA PDUFA date is the latest the agency may decide, but it often decides weeks ahead; a Supreme Court term ends in late June, but a specific ruling lands on an unannounced day within the term. The distinction matters for positioning, because a hard date lets you concentrate a hedge precisely on one session, while a soft date forces you to carry the position — or the hedge — across a window, which is more expensive. The dashboard should tag each calendar entry as hard or soft so the cost of waiting is explicit.

A second refinement is to record, next to each calendar entry, the *expected size of the move* and the *direction risk*. A PDUFA date for a small biotech where the drug is the whole company is a different animal from a PDUFA date for a diversified pharma giant where the drug is 3 percent of revenue: the first can gap 60 percent, the second perhaps 4 percent. Writing the expected move on the calendar in January is what lets the February decision be quantitative — trim to a size where a 60-percent gap is survivable, rather than discovering on the morning of the decision that the position is far too large to hold through the binary. The calendar layer, done well, is not a list of dates; it is a list of *dated, sized, directional* risks against named holdings.

One more discipline belongs here: the calendar must include the catalysts that are *scheduled but whose outcome reshapes the rest of the year*. An election is the obvious example — it is one date, but its result re-tags half the exposure map, because a new administration changes the expected path of dozens of rules at once. The dashboard should treat such super-catalysts specially, with a pre-written branch for each plausible outcome, so that the morning after the result the exposure map can be re-scored in minutes rather than reconstructed from scratch over weeks.

### Layer 2 — the live-risk monitors (continuous gauges)

The second layer is the set of *continuous* feeds that move every day: the GPR index, the EPU index, the VIX, the OFAC SDN feed, and the stream of new Federal Register entries. These are not scheduled; they tell you the *level* of risk in the air and flag the unscheduled shocks the calendar can never anticipate — the surprise invasion, the surprise sanction, the surprise rule.

![Area chart of the GPR index over time with a dashed trigger line at 200 and a reference line at the long-run mean of 100](/imgs/blogs/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard-5.png)

The GPR index is the workhorse here. Its long-run mean is 100. It sat near 512 after the September 2001 attacks, near 277 around the February 2022 invasion of Ukraine, and faded back to roughly 140 by mid-2024. The pattern is the key to using it: geopolitical shocks spike the index hard and then *fade* — most of the time. The dashboard's job is not to react to every wiggle but to flag when the index crosses a threshold (a level of, say, 200) *and* is still rising, which is the configuration that historically precedes a genuine regime shift rather than a one-day scare. (Why most shocks fade and which do not is the core of [geopolitics as a market factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/geopolitics-as-a-market-factor-the-risk-premium-channel).)

The VIX — the options market's 30-day expected volatility on the S&P 500 — is the second continuous gauge, and it complements the GPR. The GPR measures how much geopolitical tension is being *talked about*; the VIX measures how much the market is *paying* for protection. The two often move together but not always, and the gap between them is itself information: a high GPR with a calm VIX means the news is loud but the market is unworried, which is either complacency or a sign the shock is already priced.

![Bar chart of the VIX at six named stress events from the 2010 flash crash to the 2024 yen-carry unwind with a dashed trigger line at 30](/imgs/blogs/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard-7.png)

At the named stress events the VIX has reached 33 (2010 flash crash), 37 (2018 Volmageddon), a record 83 (the March 2020 COVID crash), 30 (the 2022 Ukraine invasion), 26 (the 2023 SVB failure), and 39 (the 2024 yen-carry unwind). A dashboard trigger set at VIX 30 would have fired on five of those six — useful, but also a reminder that the VIX fires on *all* stress, financial and geopolitical alike. The SVB shock topped out at 26, just under a 30 trigger, which is the right lesson: any single threshold will miss some real events and catch some false ones. That is why the dashboard layers multiple monitors rather than betting on one.

The **Economic Policy Uncertainty (EPU) index** is the domestic-policy counterpart to the GPR. Where the GPR counts geopolitical tension, the EPU counts how much newspapers are writing about *uncertainty in economic policy* — taxes, regulation, spending, monetary policy. It spikes around debt-ceiling standoffs, contested elections, and major regulatory overhauls. For a book whose risk is more legal than geopolitical — heavy in regulated sectors like banks, pharma, or utilities — the EPU is often the more relevant continuous monitor of the two, because it captures the *rule-change* uncertainty that the GPR, focused on conflict, largely misses. A complete dashboard runs both and watches which one is doing the talking: a high EPU with a calm GPR says the risk is domestic and regulatory; the reverse says it is foreign and geopolitical, and the two call for different hedges (regulated-sector trims versus oil-and-defense and gold).

The OFAC SDN feed is different in kind from any of the index monitors. It is not a continuous level; it is a discrete event stream. Each update either contains a name you care about or it does not. The monitoring task is a simple set-membership check run daily: does any newly listed entity match a holding, a holding's major customer, or a holding's major supplier? If yes, the dashboard escalates immediately, because sanctions designations are among the few legal events that can take a position to near-zero overnight.

The subtlety in the SDN monitor is the *second-order* check. A direct hit — your holding is itself sanctioned — is rare and obvious. The more common and more missed case is a hit on a name your holding *depends on*: a major customer that suddenly cannot pay, a key supplier you can no longer buy from, a joint-venture partner now off-limits. When the 2022 Russia sanctions immobilized roughly \$300 billion of a \$643 billion reserve stock and cut major banks off from dollar clearing, the direct hits were a handful of Russian entities, but the *exposure* rippled into every Western company with Russian revenue, Russian supply, or Russian counterparties. A dashboard whose SDN check only matches your direct holdings would have flagged almost nothing; a dashboard that matches your holdings' top customers and suppliers would have lit up across consumer staples, energy services, and industrials. Building that second-order map — knowing your holdings' key counterparties — is more work, but it is where the sanctions monitor earns its keep. (The full chokepoint mechanics are in [the 2022 Russia sanctions post](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance).)

### Layer 3 — the exposure map (holding by risk factor)

The third layer is where monitoring becomes *yours*. The exposure map is a matrix: every holding is a row, every risk factor is a column, and every cell is a score — how exposed *this* holding is to *that* factor. This is the layer that turns a generic news feed into a portfolio-specific risk number, and it is the layer most investors skip, which is exactly why it is the source of most of the edge.

![Heatmap matrix of six portfolio holdings scored zero to three against six legal and geopolitical risk factors with darker cells marking higher exposure](/imgs/blogs/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard-3.png)

The heatmap shows a six-holding book scored against six factors on a 0-to-3 scale, where 0 means the factor is irrelevant to the holding and 3 means the factor sits at the core of the holding's revenue or valuation. Read it column by column and the structure of your risk jumps out. The big-pharma name carries a 3 on drug-pricing law — its whole business is at stake there — but a 0 on bank-capital rules. The regional bank is the mirror image: a 3 on bank capital, a 0 on drug pricing. The defense name and the oil major both carry a 3 on the sanctions-and-war (GPR) factor, but with opposite sign — defense gains from conflict, the oil major's exposure cuts both ways through the price of crude.

The visual payoff is that a *column* with many dark cells is a **concentration**: a single rule or shock that would hit a big slice of the book at once. If the sanctions-and-war column is dark across four of six holdings, your portfolio has a hidden geopolitical concentration that no single-stock analysis would reveal — because each holding looks fine on its own, and only the matrix shows that one shock moves them together.

Reading the matrix the *other* way — row by row — is equally useful but answers a different question. A row with many dark cells is a holding that is *broadly* exposed to legal and geopolitical risk: the EM ADR in the heatmap carries non-zero betas across five of six factors, because an emerging-market name catches sanctions risk, tariff risk, currency risk, and political risk all at once. A holding like that deserves a smaller position than its expected return alone would suggest, because it is carrying a stack of rule-and-geopolitics risks that do not show up in a conventional valuation model. The column view finds your *portfolio's* concentrations; the row view finds your *individual* land mines. A good dashboard surfaces both, and the discipline is to act on the column concentrations with portfolio-level hedges and on the row land mines with position-size limits.

There is a deeper reason the matrix beats every shortcut, and it is worth stating plainly: legal and geopolitical risk is *factor* risk, not *sector* risk, and factors cut across the lines that conventional portfolio tools draw. The standard risk report a brokerage hands you breaks the book into sectors, regions, and market-cap bands. None of those buckets contains "drug-pricing law" or "export controls" or "GPR shock" as a line item, so none of them can tell you that 29 percent of your book is exposed to one drug-pricing bill or that one geopolitical shock moves a third of your holdings together. The exposure map is, at bottom, a *custom factor model* for the risks that the off-the-shelf factor models ignore. Building it is the price of seeing the risks that everyone else is structurally blind to.

#### Scoring a holding's risk-beta

The cell score is what I call a holding's **risk-beta** to a factor, borrowing the term from the market beta you already know. Market beta tells you how much a stock moves for a 1-percent move in the index; risk-beta tells you how much a holding moves for a given rule or geopolitical shock. It is built from two pieces:

1. **Revenue exposure (0 to 3):** how much of the company's revenue or cash flow is directly governed by the factor. A pharma company whose top three drugs are all on a price-negotiation list scores 3 on drug-pricing law. A company with one minor product line affected scores 1.
2. **Price sensitivity (a multiplier):** how much the *stock* has historically moved per unit of the factor — measured, where possible, with an event study of past episodes (the abnormal return around prior rulings, tariffs, or designations). A name that fell 12 percent on the last adverse ruling has a higher price sensitivity than one that fell 3 percent.

![Five-stage pipeline from picking the factor through rating revenue exposure and price sensitivity to a combined risk-beta and dollars at risk](/imgs/blogs/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard-2.png)

You combine the two into a single 0-to-3 risk-beta per cell. The scoring is deliberately coarse — three is plenty of resolution for a risk map, and false precision (a 2.37) would imply a confidence you do not have. The output of the whole pipeline is the only number that ultimately matters: **dollars at risk** = position size × risk-beta × the expected percentage repricing if the factor fires. That is the number the action layer keys off.

The event-study step deserves a word, because it is where the risk-beta gets its empirical teeth. An **event study** is a simple, decades-old technique: take the dates of past episodes of the factor (every prior adverse drug-pricing headline, every prior tariff round, every prior designation in the sector), measure the stock's return over the window around each date, and subtract the market's return over the same window to isolate the *abnormal* return attributable to the event. Average those abnormal returns and you have a data-grounded estimate of how the stock moves when the factor fires. A name that has fallen an average of 12 percent on the last five adverse rulings has a measured price sensitivity; one that shrugged off the same rulings with a 2-percent wobble does not. This is the difference between a risk-beta that is a *guess* and one that is a *measurement* — and the dashboard should carry, next to each high score, the handful of historical dates that justify it. (The full event-study toolkit applied to legal events is in [how a rule becomes a price](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing).)

The reason to keep the score coarse despite the event-study rigor is that risk-betas are *unstable*. The relationship between a rule and a stock's reaction shifts as the company's business mix changes, as the market's attention waxes and wanes, and as the rule itself evolves. A risk-beta is not a constant like a physical measurement; it is a working estimate that you revisit every quarter. Pretending to two decimal places of precision on a number that genuinely drifts by a full point year to year is the kind of false rigor that gets risk managers in trouble. The honest stance is: this holding is *roughly* a 3 on this factor, here are the past episodes that support it, and I will re-score it when the next episode or the next earnings restructuring tells me something new.

### Layer 4 — the action triggers (what level moves you)

The top layer is the one that separates a dashboard from a diary. A **trigger** is a pre-committed rule: *if a monitor crosses this level, take this action on this exposure.* The triggers are written in advance, in calm conditions, precisely so that the decision is not being made in the middle of a shock when judgment is worst.

![Decision tree from a daily monitor read branching to GPR, VIX, and SDN conditions, each routing to a trim, hedge, exit, or hold action](/imgs/blogs/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard-6.png)

A simple, defensible trigger set looks like this:

- **GPR above 200 and rising** → trim equity beta by 1 percent of the book and add a gold or long-volatility sleeve.
- **VIX above 30 on a spike day** → buy index puts sized to the geopolitical exposure of the book.
- **An SDN hit on a portfolio name (or its key counterparty)** → exit the name within 24 hours, before secondary effects compound.
- **All monitors below threshold** → hold, and log the daily read so the baseline is documented.

The decision tree makes the logic explicit: a daily read fans out into the conditions, each condition routes to one pre-defined action, and the default — by far the most common branch — is "hold and log." A good trigger set is mostly "do nothing," because most days nothing crosses a threshold. The triggers exist for the handful of days a year when something does, and on those days they replace a frightened guess with a rule you already agreed to.

The art of the trigger layer is in the *thresholds*. Set them too tight and the dashboard whipsaws you into constant trading; set them too loose and it never fires until the damage is done. The right level is calibrated to your book's tolerance and to the base rates: if the GPR has historically needed to clear 200 to mark a regime shift, set the trim there, not at 150.

Triggers come in two flavors, and a mature dashboard uses both. A **level trigger** fires when a monitor crosses an absolute threshold (GPR above 200, VIX above 30). A **change trigger** fires on the *rate of change* regardless of the level (the GPR rising 50 points in a week, the VIX doubling in two sessions). The two catch different things. A level trigger catches a slow build that grinds past the line; a change trigger catches the violent, fast shock that may not have cleared the absolute threshold yet but is moving so fast it will. The 2020 COVID crash and the 2024 yen-carry unwind were both change-trigger events — the speed was the signal, and a dashboard waiting only for an absolute level would have been a day or two late on the fastest legs. Pairing a level trigger with a change trigger on the same monitor closes that gap.

A second design principle is that triggers should be *graduated*, not binary. A single all-or-nothing "hedge everything when GPR hits 200" trigger is brittle: it produces a giant one-time trade at one arbitrary level and nothing on either side of it. A graduated set is smoother and more honest — trim 0.5 percent of beta at GPR 180, another 1 percent at 220, another 1.5 percent at 260 — so the hedge scales with the severity of the signal. Graduation also dampens the whipsaw problem: a small first tranche at a modest level costs little if the signal turns out to be a scare, while the larger tranches only commit real capital once the signal has proven itself by pushing further. The dashboard's trigger table, in practice, is less a single line in the sand than a staircase.

Finally, every trigger needs a written *un-trigger*: the condition under which you take the hedge back off. A hedge with no exit rule tends to become permanent through inertia, quietly bleeding carry long after the risk has passed. The un-trigger for a GPR-driven hedge might be "the index falls back below 150 and holds for two weeks"; for a VIX-driven hedge, "the VIX closes below 22 for three sessions." Writing the exit at the same time as the entry is what keeps a trigger-based program from silently degrading into the expensive always-on hedging it was designed to avoid.

## How to score and size exposure: four worked examples

The four layers are the skeleton. The flesh is the arithmetic, because a dashboard that does not produce dollar numbers is just a mood ring. Here are four worked examples that take the system from a score to a sized trade. Every figure in this section uses simple, explicit math you can reproduce in a spreadsheet.

#### Worked example: scoring a portfolio's aggregate regulatory and geopolitical exposure

Take a \$10,000,000 long-only equity book with the six holdings from the heatmap, equally weighted at roughly \$1,666,667 each. Focus on the sanctions-and-war (GPR) factor, and suppose the dashboard's expected repricing for a genuine GPR regime shift (the index clearing 200 and holding) is a 15-percent adverse move on a holding with a risk-beta of 3, scaling linearly with the beta.

Take each holding's GPR risk-beta from the heatmap and compute its dollars at risk as *weight × (beta ÷ 3) × 15%*:

- Pharma, beta 1: \$1,666,667 × (1÷3) × 15% = \$83,333
- Defense, beta 3, but *positive* (it gains): +\$1,666,667 × (3÷3) × 15% = +\$250,000
- Tech, beta 2: −\$1,666,667 × (2÷3) × 15% = −\$166,667
- Regional bank, beta 1: −\$1,666,667 × (1÷3) × 15% = −\$83,333
- Oil major, beta 3: −\$1,666,667 × (3÷3) × 15% = −\$250,000
- EM ADR, beta 3: −\$1,666,667 × (3÷3) × 15% = −\$250,000

Sum the signed numbers: −83,333 + 250,000 − 166,667 − 83,333 − 250,000 − 250,000 = **−\$583,333**. So a 15-percent GPR shock would cost this book about \$583,000, or **5.8 percent** — *net* of the \$250,000 the defense holding gains as an offset. The single most important output is that offset: without the defense position, the gross hit would be \$833,000, so one holding is absorbing 30 percent of the geopolitical pain. The lesson is that aggregate exposure is a sum of signed bets, and a dashboard that ignores the natural hedges already in the book will systematically over-hedge.

#### Worked example: an exposure-concentration check on a single rule

Now switch factors to a specific, concrete rule: a drug-pricing law that would force price cuts on a set of high-revenue medicines. The dashboard's exposure map flags which holdings have drug-pricing revenue at stake. Suppose, in a \$20,000,000 book, the holdings with a non-zero drug-pricing risk-beta are: the big-pharma name at \$3,600,000 (beta 3), a healthcare-conglomerate name at \$1,400,000 (beta 2), and a med-tech name at \$800,000 (beta 1).

The dashboard's "dollars exposed to this single rule" is the position-weighted sum scaled by beta. First, the raw dollars *touched* by the rule: \$3,600,000 + \$1,400,000 + \$800,000 = \$5,800,000, which is **29 percent of the book**. That is the concentration headline — nearly a third of the portfolio sits in names a single law can touch.

But raw dollars overstate it, because the betas differ. Scale each by beta ÷ 3: \$3,600,000 × 1.0 + \$1,400,000 × 0.667 + \$800,000 × 0.333 = \$3,600,000 + \$933,333 + \$266,667 = **\$4,800,000 of beta-weighted exposure**. If the law's expected repricing on a beta-3 name is a 10-percent drop, the book's expected hit is \$4,800,000 × 10% = **\$480,000**, or 2.4 percent of the portfolio. The concentration check earns its place because the *headline* 29-percent number would have scared an investor into dumping the whole sleeve, while the *beta-weighted* number shows the real expected damage is a manageable 2.4 percent — and that the med-tech name, with a beta of 1, barely belongs in the worry at all.

#### Worked example: a trigger-based hedge sized to the exposure

The GPR index crosses 200 and is still rising — the trigger fires. The dashboard's net geopolitical exposure on the \$10,000,000 book was −\$583,333 (5.8 percent) from the first worked example. The trigger calls for buying index puts sized to that exposure.

Suppose three-month put options on the relevant equity index, struck 5 percent out of the money, cost 1.5 percent of notional. To hedge the full \$583,333 of expected loss, you need enough notional that the puts pay roughly that much in the shock scenario. If a 15-percent index decline produces about a 10-percent payoff on the 5-percent-OTM puts (the difference between the 15-percent fall and the 5-percent strike buffer), then notional × 10% = \$583,333 implies **\$5,833,330 of put notional**. The premium is \$5,833,330 × 1.5% = **\$87,500**.

So the trigger-based hedge costs \$87,500 to protect against a \$583,333 loss — a cost of about 15 cents per dollar of protection, for three months. The decision the dashboard forces is explicit: you are paying \$87,500 now, with high confidence the GPR signal precedes a real move, against a loss you have *measured* rather than guessed at. The single insight is that because the exposure was pre-computed, the hedge is sized to a number, not to fear — you buy exactly enough protection, not the round-number "buy some puts" that leaves you over- or under-covered.

#### Worked example: continuous hedging versus trigger-based hedging

Is it worth running the dashboard and triggers at all, versus just hedging all the time? Compare the costs on the same \$10,000,000 book over one year.

**Continuous hedging.** Holding the 5-percent-OTM index puts at 1.5 percent per three months, rolled four times a year, on enough notional to cover the \$583,333 exposure (\$5,833,330 notional): cost = \$5,833,330 × 1.5% × 4 = **\$350,000 a year**, or 3.5 percent of the book — a brutal, permanent drag whether or not a shock ever comes.

**Trigger-based hedging.** Suppose the GPR-above-200 trigger fires, on average, about once a year and the hedge is held for three months each time: cost = \$87,500 per firing × 1 = **\$87,500 a year**, or 0.9 percent. Even if the trigger fires *twice* in a bad year, the cost is \$175,000 — still half the continuous bill.

The tradeoff is real, not free: the continuous hedge is always on, so it protects against the shock the dashboard *misses* — the SVB-style event that topped out at VIX 26, just under the trigger. The trigger-based hedge is cheaper precisely because it accepts that gap. The honest framing is that you are trading \$262,500 a year of saved premium for the risk of being unhedged in the events your triggers do not catch. For most books, with a defensible trigger set and a natural hedge already in the portfolio (the defense holding), the trigger-based approach wins — but only because the dashboard makes the exposure, the trigger, and the gap all explicit numbers rather than vibes. (Position sizing and the cost of carry on a hedge are treated in depth in the [hedge-funds risk-management post](/blog/trading/hedge-funds/risk-management-as-a-business-function).)

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs sink more dashboards than any technical mistake. Each is corrected with a number.

### "You can't systematize geopolitics — it's too unpredictable"

This is the most common objection and it confuses *predicting* with *measuring*. You cannot predict whether a war breaks out next month. But you can absolutely *measure* the level of geopolitical tension and your book's sensitivity to it. The GPR index is a real, published, decades-long series with a long-run mean of 100 that spiked to 512 after September 2001 and to 277 in February 2022 — it is not noise, it is a quantified gauge with a clear base-rate distribution. The systematization is not "forecast the shock"; it is "know your exposure and pre-commit your response so that when the unforecastable shock arrives, you act in seconds rather than days." The October 2022 export-control example at the top of this post is the proof: nobody predicted the exact rule, but the system knew which three names it would hit, and that was enough to be early instead of late.

### "More monitors equals a better dashboard"

The opposite is true past a point. A dashboard with 50 feeds produces a wall of alerts, almost all of them noise relative to your specific holdings, and the human watching it learns to ignore all of them — which means it misses the one that mattered. Take the VIX-30 trigger: across six major stress events it would have fired on five and missed one (SVB at 26). Adding a dozen more volatility sub-indices would not have caught the SVB event any earlier; it would only have added a dozen more false alarms on the days nothing happened. The value of a monitor is not in its count but in whether it is *joined to your exposure map*. Five well-chosen feeds wired to a clean exposure matrix beats fifty feeds floating free. The discipline is to add a monitor only when you can name the holding it protects and the trigger it feeds.

The deeper failure mode behind monitor-hoarding is **alert fatigue**, and it is a documented human problem, not a personal weakness. When a system cries wolf often enough, the operator stops responding — and the cruel arithmetic is that a noisier dashboard has a *lower* hit rate on the alerts that matter, because attention is finite and every false alarm spends a little of it. A dashboard with five feeds firing a handful of times a year keeps the operator sharp; the same operator drowning in fifty feeds firing daily will, by the time the real signal comes, have trained themselves to swipe it away with the rest. The correct mental model is that each monitor has a *cost* measured in attention, and a feed that fires often without ever changing a decision is pure cost. Prune ruthlessly: if a monitor has never once flipped a trigger or changed a position over a full year, it is decoration, and decoration on a risk dashboard is worse than nothing because it dilutes the signals that work.

### "Exposure is just sector — own defensives and you're fine"

Sector is a crude proxy that misses the cross-cutting risks that actually concentrate a book. Two "healthcare" names can have wildly different drug-pricing betas: a hospital operator scores near 0 on drug-pricing law while a branded-pharma name scores 3, yet a sector lens treats them identically. Worse, the dangerous concentrations run *across* sectors. In the aggregate-exposure worked example, the sanctions-and-war factor hit the oil major, the tech name, and the EM ADR all at once — three different sectors, one shared risk factor, a combined \$666,667 of gross exposure that a sector breakdown would have scattered into "energy," "technology," and "emerging markets" and never summed. The exposure map exists precisely because the real concentrations are factor concentrations, not sector concentrations, and only a holding-by-factor matrix surfaces them.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three patterns recur often enough to be worth pre-wiring into any dashboard.

**A monitor that front-ran a repricing.** Return to the October 2022 export controls. The Bureau of Industry and Security published the rule on a Friday; a Federal-Register monitor wired to a "semiconductors / export controls" tag flagged it within hours. The exposure map already carried high export-control betas on chip-equipment makers (their China sales were a large revenue slice) and on a Chinese foundry ADR (its access to advanced tools was about to be cut). The dashboard's value was not insight into chips — it was *speed and pre-mapping*: the names were already tagged, so the trim happened before the Monday gap rather than after. The defense-index data in this series shows the mirror-image trade: the aerospace-and-defense index, rebased to 100 at the eve of the February 2022 invasion, rose to 110 within five weeks and to 138 by end-2024, exactly the kind of positive geopolitical beta a dashboard would have flagged on the GPR spike.

**An exposure concentration that hurt.** The 2023 regional-banking stress is the cautionary tale. Many investors held several regional banks and thought they were diversified because the names were different companies. But every regional bank carried a near-identical risk-beta to one factor: the sensitivity of held-to-maturity bond portfolios to the sharpest Fed hiking cycle in 40 years (the fed funds upper bound went from 0.50 percent in March 2022 to 5.50 percent by July 2023). A dashboard with an interest-rate-and-bank-capital column would have lit up that entire sleeve as a single concentrated bet, not a diversified one. SVB's specific failure — 94 percent of deposits above the \$250,000 FDIC cap, \$42 billion ordered out in a single day — was the trigger, but the *exposure* was a factor concentration that a sector lens (just "financials") completely hid. (The legal mechanics of that run are in [the deposit-insurance and bank-run post](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/deposit-insurance-lender-of-last-resort-and-the-anatomy-of-a-bank-run).)

**A calendar catalyst caught early.** The cleanest dashboard wins are the boring ones. A biotech holding with a PDUFA date on the calendar months ahead lets you decide *in advance* how to handle the binary: trim to a size you can stomach through a possible 40-percent gap, or buy a defined-risk options structure, or simply mark the date and accept the swing. The investor who calendared the date in January makes a calm choice in February; the one who forgot finds out the morning of the decision and either freezes or panics. The drug-price-negotiation list is the same shape at the portfolio level — a known publication date that reprices a whole sleeve of pharma names, fully foreseeable to anyone running the calendar layer.

**A slow-burn rule that the calendar saw coming for two years.** The 2018-19 US-China trade war is the canonical example of a rule-change that was neither a surprise nor a one-day event, and a dashboard would have tracked it the whole way. The US average tariff on Chinese imports climbed from 3.1 percent in early 2018 to 12 percent by that September, to 18.3 percent by mid-2019, and to roughly 19.3 percent by early 2020 — a staircase, not a cliff. Each step was announced in advance through the Section 301 process, published in the Federal Register, and preceded by comment periods. An investor running the monitoring layer saw each escalation coming; an investor running the exposure map knew which holdings carried tariff betas — the retailers importing finished goods, the semiconductor names with China revenue, the agricultural exporters facing China's retaliatory tariffs that rose in lockstep from 8 percent to 21 percent. The trade war is the clearest proof that legal risk is often *slow*, and that slowness is the dashboard's friend: a staircase gives you the whole climb to position, if you are watching the steps. (The full case is in [the 2018-19 trade-war post](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war).)

## The playbook: building and running the dashboard

Pull it together into a build sequence and an operating cadence.

**The build (sources → monitors → exposure map → triggers).** Build from the bottom of the stack up, because each layer feeds the one above it.

1. **Assemble the calendar.** Each January, list every known-date catalyst for the year — SCOTUS term, FDA PDUFA dates for biotech holdings, Unified Agenda target dates for rules that touch your sectors, election dates, central-bank and OPEC meetings — and tag each with the holding and position size it affects. This is a one-day task that prevents the most avoidable losses.
2. **Wire the monitors.** Subscribe to the GPR and EPU index updates, the VIX, the OFAC SDN machine-readable feed, and a keyword-filtered Federal Register stream tagged to your sectors. Keep the count small and every feed joined to a holding.
3. **Build the exposure map.** For each holding, score a risk-beta (0 to 3) against each factor using revenue exposure times historical price sensitivity. Render it as a heatmap so concentrations are visible at a glance. This is the layer that takes real work and delivers most of the edge — budget a few hours per holding for the first build, then maintain it as positions change.
4. **Set the triggers.** For each monitor, write the threshold and the pre-committed action, calibrated to base rates and your book's tolerance. Write them down where you cannot quietly ignore them.

**The review cadence.** A dashboard is a living system, not a one-time build.

- **Daily:** a two-minute read of the live monitors. Did any cross a trigger? If not, log the read and move on. The log matters because it documents the baseline and keeps you honest about when a level was actually breached.
- **Weekly:** scan the new Federal Register entries and regulations.gov dockets for your tagged sectors; check upcoming calendar catalysts inside a two-week window and pre-decide the handling.
- **Quarterly:** rebuild the exposure map as positions have changed; refresh the calendar; re-examine the trigger thresholds against the quarter's actual volatility.

**What invalidates a signal.** A signal is not a fact; it is a probability that can be wrong, and the discipline is to write down in advance what would make you stand down.

- A GPR spike that *fades* within a few sessions without a follow-through in the VIX or in the actual asset prices was a scare, not a regime shift — unwind the hedge and bank the small cost.
- An exposure score is invalidated when the underlying business changes: a company that divests the division that gave it a drug-pricing beta of 3 now scores a 1, and the cell must be updated or the dashboard lies to you.
- A trigger is invalidated when its base rate breaks: if the GPR clears 200 routinely without any market repricing for a sustained stretch, the threshold has lost its information content and must be re-calibrated, not blindly obeyed. A dashboard you follow mechanically *after* its signals have stopped working is worse than no dashboard, because it manufactures false confidence.

The single habit that holds the whole system together is the **decision log** — a dated record of every trigger that fired, what you did about it, and what happened next. The log is not bureaucracy; it is the only way the dashboard learns. Without it, memory does what memory always does: it remembers the trigger that saved you from a crash and forgets the five that whipsawed you out of nothing, leaving you with a falsely flattering sense of the system's accuracy. With the log, you can compute the real hit rate of each trigger, the average cost of a false alarm, and the average benefit of a true one — which is exactly the data the cost-benefit comparison between trigger-based and continuous hedging needs. A dashboard without a decision log is a system that cannot tell you whether it is working, and a risk system you cannot evaluate is a risk in itself. Over a few years, the log is what lets you retire the triggers that never earned their keep and tighten the ones that did, so the dashboard gets *better* with age rather than ossifying around thresholds chosen on the first day with no evidence behind them.

A last word on temperament. The hardest part of running a risk dashboard is not building it; it is obeying it on the days it tells you to do something uncomfortable, and ignoring it on the days your gut screams louder than the monitors. The whole architecture exists to move the decision out of the moment and into the design phase precisely because the moment is when judgment is worst — when the headline is terrifying, when the screen is red, when every instinct says to either freeze or to sell everything. A pre-committed trigger, sized to a pre-computed exposure, written down in calm, is a promise your rational self made to your panicked self. The dashboard's real product is not the numbers; it is the discipline of keeping that promise.

The dashboard is the whole series made operational. The transmission chain — law and geopolitics to policy to macro to prices to the trade — is the *theory*; the dashboard is the *machine* that runs the theory every day on your actual book. (For the full theory it implements, see [the capstone playbook](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-law-policy-and-geopolitics-playbook) and the spine post on [the regulatory calendar](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock).) Build it once, maintain it lightly, and it converts you from an investor who reads the news into one who has already priced it. Most days it tells you to do nothing, and that is the point: it exists so that on the handful of days when a rule or a shock actually moves your book, you are early, you are sized to a number, and your decision was made before the panic — in the calm of the design phase, where every good risk decision is made.
