> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A currency is not just a price; it is a policy choice, and governments routinely push it weaker to win exports or stronger to fight inflation, using legal tools that move the biggest market on earth.
>
> - Weakening a currency is an export subsidy and an import tax at the same time — it lifts growth and lifts inflation together, which is why every devaluation is a trade-off, never a free win.
> - The exchange-rate regime tells you how the game is played: a free float lets the market decide, a managed float or peg has an official target the central bank defends with reserves, and a currency board fixes the rate in law.
> - A managed currency offers a tradeable tension between the official target (the fix, the band, the intervention level) and where the market wants the rate to go — you trade toward defended levels and size the devaluation tail against the carry.
> - The one number to remember: in February 2022 roughly **\$300bn of Russia's \$643bn in reserves** — its ammunition to defend the ruble — was frozen overnight, the cleanest proof that reserves are only as good as your counterparties let them be.

On a Friday afternoon in September 2010, Brazil's finance minister Guido Mantega used a phrase that stuck to a decade of monetary policy. Speaking in São Paulo, with the US Federal Reserve about to launch a second round of bond-buying that markets expected to flood the world with cheap dollars, Mantega said the world was in "uma guerra cambial internacional" — an international currency war. He meant that the big economies were each trying to hold their own currencies down to grab a larger slice of global demand, and that the cost was being exported to countries like Brazil, whose real was shooting up as that cheap money poured in.

He was not describing a turn of phrase. He was describing a mechanism. When the Fed buys bonds, it pushes US interest rates down and dollars out into the world; some of those dollars chase higher returns in places like Brazil; that buying drives the real higher; a stronger real makes Brazilian coffee and steel more expensive abroad and Chinese imports cheaper at home. Brazil's exporters lose, its factories shrink, and the Brazilian central bank faces a choice between cutting its own rates (importing inflation) or letting the currency keep climbing (importing recession). Multiply that across every open economy and you have the strategic tangle Mantega named.

This post is about that mechanism and how to trade it. We will build up the regimes that govern exchange rates, the legal tools a government uses to push its currency around, the logic of competitive devaluation, and the specific tensions a managed currency creates between an official target and market forces — the yuan's daily fix, Japan's yen interventions, the way a peg cracks under a speculative attack. The aim is neutral and analytical: every government here is using legal policy levers, and our job is to read them early and price the repricing, not to cheer or boo.

## Foundations: what an exchange rate actually is, and who controls it

Start with the thing itself. An **exchange rate** is the price of one currency in terms of another — how many Vietnamese dong, or Japanese yen, or Brazilian reais it takes to buy one US dollar. Like any price, it is set by supply and demand: people who want to buy a country's goods, assets, or bonds must first buy its currency, pushing it up; people selling out of that country push it down. The foreign-exchange (FX) market where this happens is the largest market in the world, turning over more than \$7 trillion a day — far bigger than all the world's stock markets combined.

Here is the part beginners miss: for most large economies, the exchange rate is **also a policy variable**. A government, acting through its central bank, can choose how much it lets the market set the rate versus how much it steers the rate toward a target it prefers. That choice is called the **exchange-rate regime**, and it sits on a spectrum from "hands off" to "fixed by law."

### The regime spectrum

- A **free float** means the central bank does not target a level at all. The market sets it minute by minute. The US dollar, the euro, the British pound, and the Japanese yen are floats — though, as we will see, "float" never means "never intervene."
- A **managed float** (sometimes called a "dirty float") means the rate mostly trades on supply and demand, but the central bank leans against moves it dislikes — buying or selling currency, or posting a daily reference rate the market is nudged toward. The Chinese yuan, the Indian rupee, and the Vietnamese dong are managed.
- A **peg** fixes the currency to an anchor — usually the dollar — at a chosen rate, and the central bank commits to defending it by buying or selling its own currency whenever the market pushes it away. The Hong Kong dollar (pegged near 7.80 per USD) and the Saudi riyal (3.75 per USD) are pegs.
- A **currency board** is the hardest version: the rate is fixed in law and every unit of local currency in circulation must be fully backed by foreign reserves, so the central bank gives up the power to print freely. Hong Kong runs a currency board; so did Argentina from 1991 to 2002, with consequences we will return to.

![Matrix comparing free float, managed float, hard peg and currency board on flexibility, who sets the rate, reserves needed and examples](/imgs/blogs/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation-2.png)

The single most important thing the spectrum encodes is the trade-off in its rightmost column. The more tightly you fix the rate, the more **foreign-exchange reserves** you must hold to defend it, and the more violent the break if your defence fails. Reserves are a country's stockpile of foreign currency and gold — its ammunition. A floater needs none; a hard peg needs a mountain. We will spend the rest of this post on what happens at the managed-and-pegged end of that spectrum, because that is where the tradeable tensions live.

### The instruments: how a country pushes its currency around

A government has three broad legal toolkits to move the rate, and a serious trader watches all three.

**Interest rates.** This is the biggest lever. Cutting policy rates makes a currency less attractive to hold — investors earn less for parking money in it — so capital flows out and the currency weakens. Raising rates does the reverse. This is why a rate decision is, among other things, a currency decision; we cover the mechanics in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates). When the Fed cut rates to zero and bought trillions in bonds after 2008, the dollar's "carry" — the yield you earn for holding it — collapsed, and that is precisely the channel Mantega was complaining about.

**Direct FX intervention.** The central bank simply trades the currency itself. To weaken its own currency, it sells the currency (printing more of it) and buys foreign assets — usually US Treasuries — which *grows* its reserves. To strengthen its currency, it does the opposite: it sells reserves to buy back its own currency, which *shrinks* reserves. This asymmetry matters enormously and we will keep returning to it: a country can weaken its currency forever (it can always print more of its own money), but it can only strengthen it until the reserves run out.

**Capital-flow tools.** A government can change the *rules* about money crossing its borders — taxing inflows, requiring banks to hold reserves against foreign deposits, limiting how much currency residents can take out, or banning certain trades outright. These are capital controls, and they are the legal lever that lets a country partly escape the trade-offs the others impose. We treat them in full in [capital controls and the legal limits on money flows](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/capital-controls-and-the-legal-limits-on-money-flows); here, just hold the thought that the rules of money flow are themselves a policy choice.

### Two more terms you need before we go deep: reserves and the carry

**Foreign-exchange reserves** are the stockpile a central bank holds in currencies other than its own — overwhelmingly US dollars, plus euros, yen, sterling, and gold. They are held mostly as foreign-government bonds (US Treasuries above all) and bank deposits, because those are liquid and pay some interest. Reserves accumulate almost mechanically when a country runs a trade surplus and wants to stop its currency rising: foreign buyers hand over dollars for the country's exports, and if the central bank does not want those dollars bidding up its own currency, it buys the dollars (printing local currency to do so) and parks them as reserves. That is how China's reserves climbed past \$3 trillion in the 2000s and 2010s — the by-product of years of keeping the yuan from appreciating. Reserves are the defensive ammunition of the FX world: a country spends them to hold its currency up and accumulates them to hold its currency down.

**The carry trade** is the strategy that ties the whole FX game to global capital flows, so define it early. To "carry" a currency is to borrow money in a currency with a low interest rate and use it to hold a currency with a high interest rate, pocketing the difference. Borrow yen at near-zero, convert to a currency yielding 8%, and you earn roughly the 8% gap for as long as the exchange rate between them holds still. The carry trade is enormous — trillions of dollars chase yield this way — and it is the channel through which a country's interest-rate policy pulls in or pushes out foreign capital. A high-yielding currency attracts carry inflows that prop it up; when those flows reverse, the currency can fall hard and fast. We return to the carry's payoff profile in detail later, because it is where currency wars hurt a portfolio most directly. For now, hold two facts: carry inflows make a currency stronger than its trade fundamentals alone would, and a carry unwind makes it weaker just as fast.

## Why a weaker currency is an export subsidy and an import tax

Now the core economics, because everything else hangs off it. When a country's currency falls, two things happen at once, and they pull in opposite directions.

Take an exporter. Suppose a Vietnamese furniture maker sells a sofa for 25 million dong, and the rate is 25,000 dong per US dollar. To an American buyer, that sofa costs \$1,000. Now the dong weakens to 27,500 per dollar — a 10% devaluation. The Vietnamese maker still wants 25 million dong, but to the American that is now only **\$909**. Without the maker changing anything, its product just got 9% cheaper to every foreign customer. That is an **export subsidy** delivered through the exchange rate: a weaker currency makes your whole economy's output cheaper abroad, all at once, with no budget line and no law to pass.

Now take an importer in the same country. A Vietnamese company buys \$1,000 of imported steel. Before the move, that cost 25 million dong. After the 10% devaluation, the same \$1,000 of steel costs **27.5 million dong**. Nothing about the steel changed; the company simply pays 2.5 million dong more because its money buys less abroad. That is an **import tax** delivered through the exchange rate, and because so much of what an economy consumes is imported or priced in dollars (oil, chips, machinery, even food), it shows up quickly as **inflation** — rising prices that squeeze real wages at home.

#### Worked example: a 10% devaluation hits the exporter and the importer in opposite directions

Hold both sides in one frame. Say the rate moves from 25,000 to 27,500 dong per dollar (a 10% weaker dong).

- **Exporter, dong revenue fixed.** A sofa priced at 25,000,000 dong was \$1,000.00 (= 25,000,000 / 25,000). After the move it is \$909.09 (= 25,000,000 / 27,500). The foreign price fell 9.1%, so the exporter can either undercut rivals by 9% or hold the foreign price and pocket a fatter dong margin. Either way, competitiveness improved.
- **Importer, dollar cost fixed.** \$1,000 of steel cost 25,000,000 dong; it now costs 27,500,000 dong — a 2,500,000 dong (10%) jump. The factory either eats the hit or passes it on as higher prices.
- **Net for the country.** Exports get a tailwind, imports get a headwind, and the trade balance tends to improve — but the *price level* rises because imported inputs cost more. Growth up, inflation up.

The core idea to carry forward: devaluation does not create value, it *redistributes* it — from importers and consumers (who lose) to exporters and the tradable-goods sector (who win), with inflation as the bill that lands on everyone. That single fact is the reason "currency war" is the right name. If one country devalues to grab demand, its trading partners suffer the mirror image — their exporters lose, their imports get cheaper, their factories shrink. They are tempted to devalue back. This is the **beggar-thy-neighbor** logic: each country tries to improve its own position at its neighbors' expense, and if everyone plays, the relative exchange rates can end up roughly where they started while every country has imported more inflation. The "war" is a coordination failure, not a battle anyone wins.

#### Worked example: a devaluation as a market-share weapon between two exporters

Competitive devaluation is competitive because it is relative. Take two countries that both export the same gadget into the US market at \$100 each, splitting it 50/50. Country A's currency devalues 10% while Country B's holds.

- Country A's exporter can now cut its US price to about \$91 and still earn the same amount in its own currency (the 10% weaker currency offsets the 9% lower dollar price).
- Country B's gadget still costs \$100. Buyers shift toward the cheaper Country A product; say Country A's market share rises from 50% to 65%.
- Country B's exporters have lost a sixth of their US sales without doing anything wrong — their cost structure did not change, a foreign government's currency policy did. Their rational response is to lobby their own central bank to devalue back.

The takeaway: a devaluation steals market share from trading partners, which is exactly why it provokes retaliation — the "war" is the chain of mutual responses to one country reaching for this lever.

#### Worked example: the inflation cost of a weaker currency on an import bill

Put a number on the bill. Say a country imports \$120bn a year of goods priced in dollars, and its currency weakens 10%. The pass-through to local prices is rarely the full 10% — some sellers absorb it, some goods are sourced locally — so assume a 50% pass-through, a common empirical figure.

- The dollar import bill is unchanged at \$120bn, but in local-currency terms it costs 10% more to buy those same dollars.
- Pass-through of 50% means roughly **5%** of that 10% feeds into the prices consumers actually pay on imported goods.
- If imported goods are 30% of the consumption basket, the contribution to headline inflation is about 0.30 × 5% = **1.5 percentage points** of extra inflation from the currency move alone.

So a 10% devaluation in this economy adds on the order of 1.5 points to inflation — enough to force a central bank that was about to cut rates to hold or even hike instead, which is exactly the bind a currency war creates.

## The 2010s currency war: when easy money was devaluation by another name

Mantega's phrase caught on because the 2010s really did feature round after round of policy that weakened currencies — but almost none of it was *called* devaluation, because it was dressed as domestic monetary policy.

When a central bank does **quantitative easing** (QE) — creating new money to buy bonds, covered in [quantitative easing explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money) — its stated goal is to lower domestic borrowing costs and lift its own economy. But a side effect, every time, is a weaker currency: more of the currency exists, and its yield falls, so it becomes less attractive to hold. The Fed never announced "we are devaluing the dollar." It announced it was supporting US employment. The dollar fell anyway, and other countries felt the export-tax-on-them, import-subsidy-into-them side of the same coin. QE *is* devaluation when viewed from across the border — which is why a domestic monetary tool became an international flashpoint.

![Timeline of modern currency-war milestones from Mantega 2010 through Abenomics, the 2015 yuan devaluation, the 2019 manipulator label and Japan yen intervention](/imgs/blogs/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation-8.png)

The clearest single case was Japan. From late 2012, the "Abenomics" program — named for prime minister Shinzo Abe — combined massive bond-buying with a pledge to push inflation back up. The yen fell from around 80 per dollar to roughly 120 over about two years, a one-third devaluation. Japanese exporters' profits surged when translated back into yen, and Japanese stocks soared. Trading partners grumbled, but Japan had a domestic-policy cover story (deflation it genuinely needed to beat), and the move stood.

The lesson for a trader is that the *driver* of a currency war is rarely an explicit FX target. It is the differential path of monetary policy. When one big central bank eases far harder than another, the easier currency falls — and that flow is tradeable months before anyone uses the words "currency war."

## The legal and institutional rules of the game

Currency wars sound lawless, but they are fought inside a framework of rules and institutions, and a serious reader needs to know what those rules actually do — and do not — compel.

At the center sits the **International Monetary Fund** (IMF), created at Bretton Woods in 1944. Its founding charter, the Articles of Agreement, includes Article IV, under which members agree to "avoid manipulating exchange rates… to gain an unfair competitive advantage over other members." That sounds like a hard prohibition on competitive devaluation. In practice it is a soft one. The IMF conducts annual "Article IV consultations" — health checks on each member's economy and exchange-rate policy — and publishes its assessment of whether a currency is "overvalued," "broadly in line," or "undervalued." But the IMF has no power to fine a country, force a revaluation, or punish manipulation. Its leverage is reputational and, for countries that need IMF loans, conditional: a borrower can be required to change FX policy as a condition of a bailout. For a large surplus country that needs nothing from the Fund, an Article IV finding of undervaluation is a strongly-worded letter. The rule exists; the enforcement does not.

The United States runs its own parallel surveillance through the Treasury's semi-annual **report on the foreign-exchange policies of major trading partners**, mandated by US trade law (the 1988 and 2015 trade acts). Treasury applies three quantitative thresholds to decide whether to put a country on a "monitoring list" or formally label it a manipulator: a large bilateral trade surplus with the US (the 2015-act threshold was \$20bn, later raised), a large overall current-account surplus (around 3% of GDP), and persistent one-sided intervention to weaken the currency (net purchases above roughly 2% of GDP over a year). Meeting all three can trigger the label; meeting two lands a country on the watch list. The thresholds are public, which means a country can manage its way around them — and many do, calibrating intervention to stay just inside the lines. We unpack what the label does and does not do under misconceptions; the key point here is that the "rules" are mostly disclosure and pressure, not enforcement.

#### Worked example: the manipulator thresholds as a binary catalyst

Treat the Treasury report as a scheduled event with a binary outcome, the way you would an earnings date. Say a country runs a \$30bn bilateral surplus with the US, a 4% current-account surplus, and has bought dollars worth 3% of GDP over the year — clearing all three thresholds.

- Base case (most likely): the country is named a manipulator, which legally requires it to enter bilateral negotiations. No automatic tariff. Expect a one-day headline-risk move — a spike in that currency's volatility and a risk-off wobble — that often fades within days, as it did when China was labelled in August 2019 and de-labelled in January 2020.
- Tail case: the label becomes the on-ramp to actual tariffs or sanctions. *That* is the move worth positioning for, because the teeth are in the follow-on policy, not the label.
- The trade: buy cheap short-dated volatility into the report date if a country is clearly over the thresholds, because the *uncertainty* is real even though the *direct* consequence is small. Sell the volatility back after the headline if no tariff follows.

The takeaway: the manipulator label is a calendar catalyst for volatility, not a forced currency move — price the headline risk, not a fundamental shift.

History also records the rare opposite of a currency war: a coordinated agreement to move currencies *together*. In September 1985, the US, Japan, West Germany, France, and the UK signed the **Plaza Accord** at New York's Plaza Hotel, agreeing to jointly intervene to weaken an over-strong dollar that had hammered US exporters. It worked dramatically — the dollar fell roughly 25–30% over the following two years against the yen and mark. By February 1987 the same group signed the **Louvre Accord** to stop the dollar's slide, having decided it had fallen far enough. The Plaza–Louvre episode is the proof that exchange rates are policy: five governments sat down and moved the world's reserve currency by a quarter, on purpose, by agreement. It is also a reminder that coordinated intervention works when the big players agree — and that the absence of such agreement is precisely what makes a currency war a war.

## The yuan's managed regime: the fix, the band, and the offshore tell

No currency rewards close reading more than the Chinese yuan, because it is the world's most important *managed* currency — neither freely floating like the dollar nor hard-pegged like the Hong Kong dollar, but steered every single morning.

Here is the machinery. Each trading day at 9:15 a.m. Beijing time, the People's Bank of China (PBoC) publishes a **daily fix** (officially the "central parity rate") — the reference level for the yuan against the dollar. The onshore yuan, traded inside mainland China and called **CNY**, is then allowed to move only **±2%** away from that fix during the day. That band is the leash. If the PBoC wants a weaker yuan, it sets a weaker fix; if it wants stability, it sets the fix close to the prior close and steps in if the market pushes toward the band's edge.

![The yuan daily fix at the center with a plus or minus two percent band, the 7 per dollar level marked, and onshore CNY versus offshore CNH](/imgs/blogs/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation-5.png)

Three features make this tradeable. First, the fix is a **signal**: a fix set weaker than the model-implied level tells the market the PBoC is tolerating, or wanting, depreciation. Traders parse the gap between the actual fix and the level consensus models expected — a persistently weaker-than-expected fix is a green light to be short the yuan. Second, there is the famous **7 level**. For years, 7 yuan per dollar was a psychological line in the sand; when the yuan finally broke through 7 in August 2019, it made global headlines and triggered a sharp risk-off move, because it signalled Beijing was willing to let the currency weaken in response to US tariffs. The number itself has no special economic meaning — but markets had built a story around it, so the *break* was the event. Third, there is the **offshore yuan, called CNH**, traded freely in Hong Kong with no band. Because CNH is not leashed, it moves to wherever the market truly wants the yuan to be. The **gap between CNH and CNY** is therefore a pressure gauge: when CNH trades weaker than CNY, the market is leaning for more depreciation than the onshore band allows, and that gap predicts where the PBoC will eventually have to let the fix drift.

#### Worked example: reading the CNH–CNY gap as a positioning signal

Suppose the onshore CNY is pinned near the weak edge of its band at 7.28 per dollar, while offshore CNH trades at 7.34. The gap is 0.06 yuan, or about **0.8%** — large for this pair. What does it tell you?

- The offshore market, free of the band, is pricing the yuan roughly 0.8% weaker than the onshore fix allows. That is real-money demand for depreciation the PBoC is currently suppressing.
- Holding a level the market disagrees with costs reserves: the PBoC must sell dollars to keep CNY from gapping to where CNH already is.
- The trade: a persistent, widening CNH–CNY gap is a setup to be short the yuan (long USD/CNH), because either the PBoC eventually lets the fix drift weaker — closing the gap by moving CNY toward CNH — or it spends reserves to hold a level the market keeps testing. The gap closing the other way (CNH richening toward CNY) is what invalidates the short.

Put plainly: the band hides the market's true opinion onshore, but the offshore rate leaks it, and the gap between them is the cleanest single read on which way a managed currency is straining.

The yuan's regime is also a moving target, which is what makes it a live trade rather than a static fact. In **August 2015**, the PBoC abruptly changed how it set the fix, announcing it would make the daily reference rate more "market-determined" by anchoring it to the previous day's close. In isolation a technical reform — but because the market wanted the yuan weaker, the immediate effect was a fix set nearly 2% weaker on the first day and a roughly 3% devaluation across three days, the largest in two decades. Global markets read it as China joining the currency war and reacted violently: commodity currencies and emerging-market assets sold off, the S&P 500 fell sharply over the following weeks, and capital began fleeing China fast enough that the PBoC had to burn through hundreds of billions of dollars of reserves over the next year to stop the yuan from collapsing — the strengthen-side countdown in action. A reform meant to add flexibility instead demonstrated, in real time, that letting a managed currency find its market level can unleash exactly the depreciation pressure the band was suppressing. For a trader, the 2015 episode is the template: a change in the *mechanism* of a managed currency is a bigger event than any single fix, because it re-prices where the whole regime can go.

## Japan and the limits of intervention: defending a falling yen

China's problem is usually a currency the market wants *weaker* than policy allows. Japan in 2022–2024 had the opposite problem: a yen the market wanted far weaker than the government was comfortable with, and a fascinating split inside the state about what to do.

In Japan, the decision to intervene in FX markets legally belongs to the **Ministry of Finance** (MoF), not the Bank of Japan (BoJ); the BoJ merely executes the order as the MoF's agent. That matters because the two had opposite instincts. The BoJ was holding interest rates near zero to support the economy — which, with US rates above 5%, meant a yawning yield gap that pushed money out of yen and the currency down toward 150, then past 160 per dollar, its weakest in over three decades. The MoF, watching imported energy and food inflation bite households, wanted to slow the fall. So the BoJ's own ultra-easy policy was *pushing the yen down* while the MoF was *spending reserves to prop it up* — the left hand and the right hand pulling against each other, a textbook illustration of how a currency move is a policy outcome, not just a market one.

To strengthen the yen, the MoF sold US dollars from Japan's reserves and bought yen. Across several episodes in 2022 and 2024, Japan spent well over \$60bn doing this — among the largest interventions in its history. And here is the hard lesson: intervention to *strengthen* a currency is finite. Every dollar you sell to buy your own currency shrinks the reserves you have left, and unless you also change the underlying driver (the yield gap), you are pushing against a tide with a finite bucket. Japan's interventions slowed the yen's fall and punished the most aggressive shorts, but they could not reverse the trend while the BoJ kept rates at zero. The fundamental only turned when the yield gap itself began to narrow.

#### Worked example: how a yen-intervention spike punishes a crowded short

Take the positioning. A hedge fund is short ¥10bn of yen against the dollar at 158.00, betting the yen keeps falling. The MoF intervenes, and the yen snaps from 158 to 153 in a few hours — a 3.2% move against the short.

- The fund's loss on the FX move is roughly 3.2% of its ¥10bn notional, about ¥320m, or near **\$2.0m** at the new rate.
- Worse, the move triggers stop-losses across the crowded short, forcing more buying of yen, which feeds the spike — a short squeeze layered on top of the official buying.
- But the fund's thesis (zero BoJ rates, 5%+ US rates) is unchanged. If it survives the spike, the carry that drew it in keeps paying, and the yen often drifts back toward the trend within weeks — which is exactly why traders treat intervention as a level to fade *after* the spike, not a regime change.

The core idea: intervention against the fundamental moves the price violently but temporarily; it transfers money from the most leveraged shorts to the government's account, without changing the gravity that set the trend.

## How a peg breaks: the speculative attack and the trilemma

The most dramatic event in FX is a peg breaking, and it follows a script worth knowing cold, because it is the highest-payoff trade in the whole domain.

A peg is a promise: the central bank will defend a fixed rate by buying its own currency whenever the market sells it. The defence works only as long as the bank has reserves to sell and the will to keep rates high enough to make holding the currency attractive. A **speculative attack** is a bet that one or both will run out.

The script: markets come to doubt the peg — maybe inflation has made the fixed rate too strong for the real economy, maybe reserves look thin, maybe a political shock raises the odds of a break. Speculators borrow the local currency and sell it for dollars (going "short" the peg). The central bank must buy that flood of local currency with its reserves, and raise interest rates to make holding the currency more rewarding. Every day of defence burns reserves. If the bank's reserves and resolve outlast the attack, the peg holds and the speculators lose their borrowing costs. If the reserves run low first, the bank capitulates, the currency devalues sharply, and the shorts pay off enormously — because they sold at the old strong rate and buy back at the new weak one.

![Flow from markets doubting the peg through speculators selling and the central bank defending and draining reserves to either the peg holding or breaking](/imgs/blogs/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation-3.png)

The canonical case is **Black Wednesday, 16 September 1992**, when George Soros's fund and others broke the British pound out of the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM), a system that pegged European currencies in narrow bands. The pound had been forced into the ERM at a rate many thought too strong for Britain's economy. As pressure built, the Bank of England spent billions of reserves and hiked its base rate from 10% to 12%, then announced a further hike to 15% — all in a single day — trying to make holding the pound irresistible. It did not work; the selling overwhelmed the defence. By evening Britain withdrew from the ERM, the pound fell sharply, and Soros's fund reportedly made on the order of \$1bn. The trade was not magic. It was a clear-eyed read that the peg's fundamentals were untenable and its reserves and political will were finite.

A currency board — the hardest peg, where the rate is fixed in law and every unit of local money is fully backed by foreign reserves — is supposed to be unbreakable, because there is no discretion to abandon. Argentina proved otherwise. From 1991 it ran a currency board pegging one peso to one US dollar, which crushed hyperinflation and was, by design, beyond the central bank's power to weaken. But a currency board removes monetary independence entirely: Argentina had to import US monetary conditions even when its own economy needed the opposite. Through the late 1990s a strong dollar dragged the pegged peso up with it, gutting Argentine exporters, while the government could not devalue and could not print. Recession deepened, debt mounted, and in 2001–2002 the system collapsed under bank runs and default. The peg that "could not break" broke, the peso fell from 1 to nearly 4 per dollar, and savers who had trusted the law lost more than two-thirds of their dollar wealth. The lesson is that *no* fixed-rate promise is truly unbreakable; it is only as durable as the political willingness to bear the domestic pain the trilemma imposes — and that willingness has a price the market can probe.

Underneath every peg break sits a piece of iron logic economists call the **impossible trinity**, or trilemma: a country can have at most two of these three things — a fixed exchange rate, free movement of capital across its borders, and an independent monetary policy (the ability to set its own interest rates for domestic goals). It cannot have all three. Britain in 1992 wanted a fixed rate (the ERM) and free capital flows, which meant it had to set its interest rates to defend the peg — surrendering monetary independence. When defending the peg required rates so high they would have crushed a recessionary economy, the political will to keep them there evaporated, and the peg went. A country that wants to keep both a fixed rate and monetary independence must give up the third leg — free capital flow — which is exactly why pegs and capital controls so often travel together, the subject of [capital controls and the legal limits on money flows](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/capital-controls-and-the-legal-limits-on-money-flows).

#### Worked example: the reserve burn to defend a peg

Make the trilemma concrete with arithmetic, because the whole trade is a stopwatch on reserves. Say a central bank pegs its currency and holds \$50bn of reserves. A speculative attack hits, and the bank is forced to buy \$5bn of its own currency per day to hold the line.

- At \$5bn a day, the \$50bn stock lasts **10 days** of full-intensity defence — and markets can do that math too, so the attack often intensifies as the deadline nears.
- The bank's other weapon is rates: hiking from, say, 8% to 20% makes shorting the currency expensive (the speculator pays that rate to borrow it). But a 20% policy rate may be unbearable for a weak domestic economy — that is the trilemma biting.
- The trade for an attacker: estimate the reserve runway (stock ÷ daily burn) against the political pain of high rates. If the runway is short and the economy too fragile for punitive rates, the peg is a sell. The break, when it comes, can be a 20–40% overnight devaluation — a payoff many multiples of the borrowing cost carried while waiting.

The core idea: defending a peg is spending a finite stock of dollars to rent time; the speculator's edge is simply counting whether the stock runs out before the resolve does.

## Reserves as ammunition — and the new risk that they can be frozen

We keep saying reserves are ammunition. Two facts sharpen that.

First, the **asymmetry**. A country can resist its currency *rising* forever — to keep it down, it sells its own currency (which it can always print) and accumulates foreign reserves; that pile grows without limit, which is how China built up over \$3 trillion in reserves while keeping the yuan from appreciating in the 2000s. But a country can only resist its currency *falling* until the reserves run out, because propping it up means selling foreign assets you have a finite stock of. This is why "the central bank can hold any level" is false in one direction and true in the other: holding a currency down is sustainable; holding it up is a countdown.

Second, and newer, reserves are only as good as your counterparties allow. Most reserves are not gold in a vault; they are foreign-government bonds and bank deposits held *in* other countries' financial systems. That means a sufficiently coordinated set of foreign governments can freeze them.

![Stacked bar showing Russia's roughly 643 billion dollar reserves split into usable and frozen portions in February 2022](/imgs/blogs/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation-7.png)

In February 2022, that moved from theory to practice. Russia held roughly **\$643bn** in reserves — a war chest built precisely so it could defend the ruble and weather sanctions. But because well over half of it was held as Western-government bonds and deposits, the US, EU, and allies **immobilized about \$300bn** of it almost overnight. Russia still owned the assets on paper, but it could not use them to defend the ruble. The ruble crashed; Russia had to slam on capital controls and hike rates to 20% to stabilize it. The episode rewired how every reserve manager on earth thinks about safety, and it is a major driver of the de-dollarization debate we cover in [the dollar system and de-dollarization](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization). The trading takeaway is blunt: when sizing a peg or a managed-currency defence, the relevant number is not headline reserves but *usable* reserves — the slice your counterparties will not, or cannot, freeze.

#### Worked example: a 10% devaluation tail against the carry on a borrow-low-hold-high trade

Now bring the threads together into the trade most exposed to all of this: the **carry trade**. The idea is simple — borrow in a low-yielding currency and hold a high-yielding one, pocketing the interest-rate difference (the "carry"). It is one of the oldest trades in FX, and its profile is the reason currency wars matter to a portfolio.

Say you borrow yen at 0.5% and hold a high-yield emerging-market currency paying 8%. Put \$10m to work.

- The **carry** is the yield gap: 8% − 0.5% = 7.5% per year, about **\$750,000** on the \$10m position, paid steadily as long as the exchange rate holds.
- The **risk** is a devaluation of the high-yield currency. If it drops 10% against your funding currency, you lose 10% on the \$10m — **\$1,000,000** — wiping out more than a full year of carry in one move.
- And devaluations rarely stop at 10%: a peg break or a forced float can be 20–40%, turning a year of patient carry into a multi-year loss in a single session.

This is why traders describe the carry trade as "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller." The carry accrues in a thin, steady line; the tail loss arrives all at once. The currency-war lens is what tells you *when* the steamroller is coming: a managed currency straining against its band, thin usable reserves, a widening offshore gap, or a central bank easing into a defence it cannot afford.

## Common misconceptions

**"A strong currency is always good."** It feels patriotic, but it is half the story. A strong currency makes imports cheaper and travel abroad cheaper, and it signals confidence — but it makes a country's exporters uncompetitive and can hollow out manufacturing. When the yen fell a third under Abenomics, Japanese exporters and the Nikkei boomed; a strong yen had been strangling them for years. When the Swiss franc surged after Switzerland abandoned its euro cap in January 2015 — jumping nearly 20% in minutes — Swiss exporters and tourism took an immediate, brutal hit, and the central bank had spent years and hundreds of billions of francs *fighting* that strength. "Strong = good" ignores that an exchange rate is a relative price with winners and losers on both sides of every move.

**"A central bank can hold any level it wants."** True for holding a currency *down*, false for holding it *up*. Holding a currency down means printing your own currency to buy foreign assets — you can do that without limit, which is how reserve piles reach the trillions. Holding a currency *up* means selling a finite reserve stock; markets can count it. The Thai baht in 1997 is the cleanest case: Thailand had pegged the baht near 25 per dollar and defended it with reserves and high rates as speculators piled in, even resorting to undisclosed forward sales that quietly committed reserves it appeared to still have. When the truth emerged that the usable reserves were nearly gone, the defence collapsed in July 1997, the baht was floated and fell more than 50% within months, and the shock cascaded across Indonesia, South Korea, and Malaysia into the Asian financial crisis. The pound in 1992 and the Argentine peso in 2002 told the same story: each was a central bank that believed it could hold a level it could not, and the gap between belief and *usable* reserves was the trade. The headline reserve number is the bluff; the usable number is the truth, and a peg break is the market calling the bluff.

**"The currency-manipulator label has teeth."** The US Treasury publishes a semi-annual report and can formally name a country a "currency manipulator" if it meets thresholds on trade surplus, current-account surplus, and persistent one-sided FX intervention. The label sounds severe. In practice its direct legal consequence is mainly a requirement to enter negotiations; it carries no automatic tariff or penalty. When the US labelled China a manipulator in August 2019 (after the yuan broke 7), the designation was dropped a few months later as part of a trade deal, with little independent market impact beyond the headline-day risk-off. The label is a *signal* of political pressure — useful to track as a catalyst for volatility — but traders who expected it to force a currency move by itself were disappointed. The teeth, when there are any, come from the tariffs or sanctions that may follow, not the label.

## How it shows up in real markets

**A yuan-fix surprise.** On 5 August 2019, the PBoC set the daily fix weaker than the symbolic 7-per-dollar level for the first time in over a decade, and let CNY break 7. Global equities sold off hard, the S&P 500 fell about 3% that day, safe-haven bonds and gold rallied, and the US named China a manipulator within hours. Nothing about China's economy had changed overnight — a *number* and the *story around it* had broken, and that was enough to move every risk asset on the planet. The repeatable pattern: a managed-currency authority lets a watched level go, and the cross-asset move is bigger than the FX move itself.

**A yen-intervention spike.** On 22 September 2022, with the yen near 146 per dollar, Japan intervened to buy yen for the first time since 1998; the yen snapped several big figures stronger within minutes, then drifted back over the following weeks as the BoJ–US rate gap reasserted itself. Traders who faded the spike — selling yen back into the strength once the official buying ebbed — were rewarded, because the fundamental was unchanged. Intervention against a trend is a level, not a turn.

**A peg break.** Black Wednesday 1992 (the pound) and the Thai baht's float in July 1997 are the textbook cases, but the structure recurs whenever a fixed or heavily managed rate gets too far from fundamentals and reserves thin out. The tell is always the same: rising defence costs (reserve drawdowns, emergency rate hikes), a widening gap between the official and offshore/black-market rate, and a political economy that cannot bear the rates needed to defend the line.

**The carry-trade unwind.** On 5 August 2024, a sudden strengthening of the yen — after the BoJ hiked and the rate gap began to close — triggered a violent unwind of yen-funded carry trades. As the funding currency strengthened, leveraged carry positions hit losses and stops, forcing buying of yen and selling of the high-yield assets the carry had funded; the VIX spiked to nearly 39, and global equities sold off sharply for a few sessions. The carry's thin steady gains had quietly funded a crowded, leveraged position, and when the steamroller arrived it arrived for everyone at once. The episode is the purest demonstration that a currency move is never just an FX event: a 5% jump in one funding currency forced selling across stocks, credit, and emerging markets worldwide, because the leverage built on cheap yen had wormed into every corner of the risk complex.

**A managed currency drifting on purpose.** Not every managed-currency story is a crisis. Vietnam's dong is steered by the State Bank of Vietnam through a daily central reference rate and a trading band around it, and the policy through the 2020s was a deliberate, gentle weakening against the dollar — letting the dong slide a few percent a year to keep exports competitive while avoiding the import-inflation shock of a sharp devaluation. The reference rate drifted from roughly 23,200 dong per dollar in 2019 toward 24,600 by 2024, a controlled depreciation rather than a break.

![USD per VND reference rate drifting weaker from 2019 to 2024 as a managed-band currency](/imgs/blogs/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation-4.png)

The signal for a trader watching a currency like this is the *pace*, not the level: a steady, pre-announced drift tells you the authority is comfortable and in control; an acceleration in that pace, or a sudden defence of a level the band had been letting go, is the regime telling you pressure has built. A managed currency that moves smoothly is boring by design — and a sudden change in that smoothness is the trade. The dong's specific legal machinery, including the credit-quota system and gold-import rules that interact with the band, is the subject of [SBV monetary and banking law: credit quotas and the dong](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong).

## How to trade a managed currency: the playbook

Pull it together into a repeatable process. The edge in FX is not predicting the rate; it is reading the *tension* between the official target and market forces, and sizing the asymmetric payoff correctly.

Before the steps, internalize the shape of the opportunity, because it dictates how you trade it. A managed or pegged currency offers two very different payoff profiles depending on which side you take. If you trade *with* the authority — earning carry inside a band the central bank is comfortably defending — you collect a small, steady return punctuated by a rare, large loss when the regime finally cracks. That is a short-volatility profile: it pays like selling insurance until the day it does not. If you trade *against* an unsustainable level — positioning for a peg break or a forced devaluation — you pay a small, steady cost (the carry you forgo, the borrowing rate, the option premium) in exchange for a rare, large payoff when the level gives way. That is a long-volatility profile: it bleeds until it pays. Knowing which profile you are running tells you everything about position size, holding period, and what kills you. The fatal error is running the short-volatility carry while believing you have the long-volatility break — collecting nickels while telling yourself you would see the steamroller coming. You usually do not; the whole point of a managed regime is that the break is sudden by design.

**1. Read the regime first.** Before anything, classify the currency on the spectrum — free float, managed float, peg, currency board. The regime tells you what the official target is (a fix? a band? a hard rate?) and therefore what you are trading against. A peg is a binary: it holds (you earn carry) or it breaks (you make a fortune short). A managed float is a gradient: you trade toward or away from defended levels.

**2. Find the official target and the market's true level.** For the yuan, that is the daily fix versus the model-implied level, and CNY versus offshore CNH. For a peg, it is the fixed rate versus the offshore or black-market rate. A persistent, widening gap between the leashed onshore rate and the free offshore rate is the single best signal of which way the currency is straining and which way the authority will eventually have to move.

**3. Count the reserves — the usable ones.** Reserves ÷ daily defence cost is the runway for holding a currency up. Adjust headline reserves down for the slice that could be frozen by counterparties (the Russia lesson) and for short-term external debt that has first call on them. Thin usable reserves plus a strong-than-fundamental rate is the setup for a break.

**4. Trade toward intervention, against unsustainable defence.** When an authority is defending a level it *can* afford and *wants* to hold — strong reserves, a level consistent with fundamentals — fade moves away from it; the defence is a free option backing your trade. When an authority is defending a level it *cannot* afford — thin reserves, a rate the economy cannot bear, a fundamental that has moved — position for the break. The Soros trade was simply the second case read clearly.

**5. Size the devaluation tail against the carry.** If you are earning carry in a managed or pegged high-yielder, treat the carry as small and the tail as large: a year of 7% carry is one 10% gap from being erased, and a peg break can be 30%. Size the position so the tail loss is survivable, and weigh paying for cheap out-of-the-money options on the devaluation rather than running the full risk naked. The carry is the nickels; budget explicitly for the steamroller. A practical way to structure this: cap the carry position at a size where the worst plausible devaluation costs you no more than a few months of carry across the rest of the book, and buy a small allocation of cheap downside protection so a peg break is a bad week rather than a blown-up fund. The trade that ruins people is not being wrong about direction; it is being right for two years and then losing four years of gains in a session because the position was sized for the carry, not the tail.

**6. Watch the catalysts.** The events that move managed currencies are scheduled or semi-scheduled: central-bank meetings (the rate path is the deepest currency driver), the daily fix for the yuan, US Treasury FX reports (the manipulator label as a volatility catalyst), reserve-data releases, and inflation prints that change the easing-versus-defending calculus. A managed currency that has been drifting weaker on a steady official path — like the dong's gentle, deliberate slide against the dollar — is telling you the authority is comfortable; a sudden change in that pace is the signal. The single most important recurring catalyst is the policy-rate path of the funding and target currencies, because the rate differential is what drives the carry that, in turn, drives the capital flows that strain a managed currency. Watch the central-bank calendar before the FX calendar.

**What invalidates the view.** For a short against a managed currency: the offshore gap closing the other way, a surprise reserve build, a hawkish policy turn that defends the level credibly, or a fundamental shift (a trade surplus surge) that justifies the rate. For a peg-break trade: reserves proving deeper or more usable than estimated, political will hardening (rates held punitively high and tolerated), or external support (a swap line, an IMF package) that refills the ammunition. When the thing that made the level unsustainable reverses, the trade is over — get out, because the carry you were fighting now works against you.

The deeper point, true across this whole series, is that a currency is the price of a country's money, and money's price is set by policy as much as by markets. Read the policy — the regime, the target, the reserves, the political constraints — and the currency stops being a random walk and starts being a tension you can position around. The biggest market on earth is, at bottom, a slow-motion negotiation between what a government wants its currency to do and what the market will let it do, and the trade lives in the gap between the two.
