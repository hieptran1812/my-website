On June 18, 2025, a portfolio manager reads a one-line headline: a national-security agency has added a mid-cap industrial supplier to a restricted-export list, effective immediately. The stock is down 4% pre-market. The naive reaction is to either panic-sell or to "buy the dip." The trained reaction is to run the headline down the spine you have learned across this series. *Which body acted* — an agency, by order, so it is fast and only semi-reversible. *What does it change* — not this quarter's earnings (a small fraction of revenue), but the *uncertainty premium* on every future year, because the firm's entire China-facing supply chain is now in question. *Where in the chain are we standing* — at the very first repricing tick, before the sell-side has modeled the second-order customer losses. *What is the trade* — and *what would prove it wrong*. Within fifteen minutes, the trained manager has a position, a size, a catalyst calendar, and an invalidation level. The panicker has a loss; the dip-buyer has a falling knife.

That fifteen-minute process is what this entire series has been building toward. We started by establishing that a rule-change reprices assets through a chain, and that markets discount the *expected* rule before it fully bites. We then spent fifty-seven posts instancing that one idea across securities law, banking and capital rules, tax and antitrust, trade and sanctions, geopolitics, sector-specific regulation, and the Vietnam market — and four more posts turning it into an operating process. ## Foundations: the spine and the one mechanism, restated

Before the ten rules, two foundations — both built from zero in the opening posts of the series — must be fully in hand. The first is the spine itself. The second is the single mechanism by which any rule moves any price. Everything downstream is a special case of these two ideas, so we restate them tightly here and then never re-derive them.

### The spine: five links, one baton

The spine has five links. **Law and geopolitics** is the input: a statute, an agency regulation, a court ruling, a tariff, a sanction, an executive order, or an out-of-nowhere geopolitical shock. **Economic policy** is the first translation: the rule changes interest rates, taxes, capital requirements, trade barriers, or energy supply. **Macro and flows** is the second translation: policy moves growth, inflation, the dollar, and the direction capital travels. **Asset prices** is where the repricing lands: the change hits either expected cash flows or the discount rate, and the asset re-rates. **The trade** is the payoff: a position with an entry, a size, a catalyst that resolves it, and a level that proves it wrong.

The whole series is taught from the master post, [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain), which builds the spine link by link, and from [who writes the rules: legislatures, regulators, central banks, courts](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts), which catalogs the institutions that sit at the input. If you read only two posts in the series, read those two — this capstone assumes both.

### The one mechanism: price is the present value of expected cash flows

There is exactly one mechanism by which a rule moves a price. Any financial asset is worth the cash it is expected to pay you in the future, with future cash discounted because a dollar later is worth less than a dollar now:

```
Price = sum over all future periods of:  Expected cash flow in that period
                                          --------------------------------
                                          (1 + discount rate) ^ period
```

Two inputs determine every price: the **expected future cash flows** (the numerator) and the **discount rate** (the denominator, which is the risk-free rate plus a risk premium). A rule moves a price today if, and only if, it changes the market's expectation of one of those two. A tax cut raises expected cash flows. A pending antitrust suit raises the discount rate by widening the range of outcomes. A tariff can hit both. This is the engine behind every case study in the series, formalized in [regulatory risk as an asset-pricing factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/regulatory-risk-as-an-asset-pricing-factor), which treats legal and political risk as a priceable factor exactly like size or value.

The discount-rate channel is the one most people underweight, because it moves a price *without any change to this year's earnings* — which is exactly why "the lawsuit hasn't cost them a dollar yet" is no defense against a falling stock.

#### Worked example: a legal overhang repricing a stock through the discount rate

Take a firm with a stable, perpetual cash flow of \$500 million a year, discounted at 8%. As a simple perpetuity its value is cash flow divided by the discount rate: \$500m ÷ 0.08 = **\$6,250 million**, or \$6.25 billion. Now a regulator opens an investigation. Nothing about this year's \$500m changes — but investors now demand an extra two percentage points of risk premium to hold a name with an uncertain legal future, lifting the discount rate to 10%. The new value is \$500m ÷ 0.10 = **\$5,000 million**, a drop of \$1.25 billion, or **20%**, with not one dollar of earnings lost. The repricing is entirely in the denominator. When the investigation later closes with no action, the premium unwinds and the 20% returns — which is the relief-rally trade of Rule 1.

The takeaway: legal uncertainty reprices a stock through the discount rate, so a falling price can be pure risk premium, fully reversible the day the overhang lifts.

The reason *timing* dominates everything is that markets do not wait for the cash flow to change — they move the moment the *expectation* changes. The expected rule drifts into the price over the rumor, the draft, the vote, and the court date, a process developed in [how a rule becomes a price: expectations, drift, and repricing](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing). Put plainly, by the time the gavel falls, the price already holds the expected outcome, and only the gap between expectation and reality is left to trade.

![Graph diagram of priced-in expectation branching into harsher as-priced and softer outcomes and their trades](/imgs/blogs/the-law-policy-and-geopolitics-playbook-2.png)

The figure above is the discounting mechanism that makes the first rule work. A rule is coming; the market prices the expected outcome (probability times impact); then the outcome lands harsher than priced (price falls further), as priced (little move — the news was old), or softer than priced (a relief rally). Your edge is never the headline. It is your estimate of the outcome distribution versus the one already in the price. The scheduled-event machinery behind this — the rulemaking clock, comment periods, court terms — is mapped in [the regulatory calendar: trading the rulemaking clock](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock), and the reality that the rules themselves are shaped by interested parties is covered in [lobbying, rulemaking, and regulatory capture](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture).

With the spine and the mechanism in hand, here are the ten rules. The first five teach you to *read* a rule-change; the second five teach you to *trade* it.

## Rule 1 — Trade the surprise, not the news

The most expensive mistake in rule-driven investing is buying the headline. By the time a statute passes or a court rules, the price already holds the *expected* outcome, drifted in over months of rumor, draft, vote, and hearing. What is left to trade is only the **surprise**: the gap between the outcome and what was priced.

The mechanism is worth stating precisely, because it governs *where* on the timeline the money is made. At each step toward the outcome — a leaked draft, a committee vote, a favorable hearing — the market updates its probability of the rule and moves the price by the *change* in probability times the impact. If a sector stands to gain \$10 of value under a favorable rule and the probability of that rule rises from 40% to 70% on a key vote, the price moves by (0.70 − 0.40) × \$10 = \$3 *on that day*, not on the eventual passage. By the time the rule is certain (probability 100%), the remaining move from the day before is tiny, because almost all the probability mass was already in. This is why the biggest single-day moves cluster on the *surprise steps* — the unexpected election result, the first framework, the shock committee passage — and why passage day is so often a non-event. You want to be positioned *before* the surprise step you can forecast, not buying the confirmation everyone already sees.

This is why the 2017 tax cut was a non-event on signing day even though it was the largest corporate windfall in a generation — the S&P 500 had spent the whole year pricing it in. And it is why the February 2022 invasion was violent — almost nothing was priced. The mechanism, the drift, and the surprise are developed across [how a rule becomes a price](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing), [the regulatory calendar](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock), and the worked TCJA case in [the 2017 TCJA and the repatriation trade](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2017-tcja-and-the-repatriation-trade).

#### Worked example: extracting the priced-in probability from a merger spread

Take a target trading at \$92 after announcing a deal to be acquired for \$100 cash. If regulators block it, the stock falls to a standalone value of \$70. The spread tells you the market's implied probability of the deal closing. Let *p* be that probability. Then the market price equals the probability-weighted payoff:

\$92 = *p* × \$100 + (1 − *p*) × \$70.

Solving: \$92 = \$70 + *p* × \$30, so *p* × \$30 = \$22, and *p* = 22 ÷ 30 = **0.733**, or about a 73% implied chance of closing. If your own read of the antitrust risk says the deal is 90% likely to close, the market is too pessimistic and the long-the-spread trade has positive expected value: your fair price is 0.90 × \$100 + 0.10 × \$70 = \$97, versus \$92 in the market — a \$5 edge. If instead you think it is only 50% likely, the fair price is \$85 and you should be *short*. The trade is never "the deal was announced"; it is "the priced probability is wrong." The full mechanics of reading these spreads live in [merger arbitrage: trading regulatory deal risk](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/merger-arbitrage-trading-regulatory-deal-risk).

The takeaway: the spread *is* a probability quote, and your only job is to disagree with it for a reason.

## Rule 2 — Know which body acts; it sets speed and reversibility

Before you do anything with a rule-change, ask the three questions of the taxonomy: *who writes it, how fast does it price, and how reversible is it?* A statute is slow and sticky (it needs a whole new law to undo). An agency regulation is medium-speed and observable (propose, comment, finalize). A court ruling is fast and binary. A tariff or executive order is fast and reversible (a deal or a new administration can lift it overnight). A sanction is immediate and bites hard.

This single distinction determines how *durably* you should hold the repricing in the price. Treating a reversible executive order as if it were a permanent statute is one of the most common and costly errors in the discipline. The institutional map is built in [who writes the rules](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts), and the speed-and-reversibility taxonomy is the spine of [how law moves markets](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain). The body that matters most for the whole economy — the central bank — gets its own legal treatment in [the legal mandate of a central bank](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-mandate-of-a-central-bank), and you should pair it with the mechanism post on [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).

The securities-law machinery that defines *what must be disclosed and when* — the trigger for most rule-driven repricing in equities — is built across [securities law 101: the '33 and '34 Acts and the SEC](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/securities-law-101-the-33-and-34-acts-and-the-sec), [insider trading, Reg FD, and what is actually illegal](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/insider-trading-reg-fd-and-what-is-actually-illegal), and [disclosure and accounting law: SOX, IFRS vs GAAP](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/disclosure-and-accounting-law-sox-ifrs-vs-gaap). The plumbing that decides how a price *can* move on a chaotic day — halts, limits, short-sale rules — is in [market structure law: Reg NMS, PFOF, and short-selling rules](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/market-structure-law-reg-nms-pfof-and-short-selling-rules), [circuit breakers, halts, and the legal plumbing of a crash](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/circuit-breakers-halts-and-the-legal-plumbing-of-a-crash), and the post-2008 and European rulebooks [Dodd-Frank: the post-2008 rulebook](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/dodd-frank-the-post-2008-rulebook) and [MiFID II and the EU market-structure regime](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/mifid-ii-and-the-eu-market-structure-regime).

## Rule 3 — Regulation often entrenches incumbents

The instinctive read of "more regulation" is "bad for the industry." The trained read is often the opposite: a thick new rulebook raises the *fixed cost of compliance*, and a fixed cost is a smaller fraction of a big firm's revenue than a small firm's. Heavy regulation can therefore *widen* the moat of the largest incumbents — a compliance department of two hundred lawyers is a barrier the upstart cannot afford. The question is never "is this regulated?" but "who can afford the rulebook?"

There is a second, sharper version of this rule: incumbents do not merely *absorb* rules better than challengers — they often *shape the rules in the first place*. The firms with the most at stake fund the most lobbying, staff the most comment letters during the rulemaking window, and supply the technical experts agencies lean on to write the detail. The result is a rulebook that, intentionally or not, tends to codify the incumbent's existing practices as the standard everyone must meet. This is the dynamic of [lobbying, rulemaking, and regulatory capture](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture), and it is why a headline like "tougher rules coming for the sector" so often resolves into "tougher rules that the largest players already comply with." The practitioner watches *who is at the table* during the comment period, not just the final rule.

You see this most clearly in banking, where capital rules are themselves a competitive weapon. The framework is built in [Basel III/IV: bank capital rules and the price of credit](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/basel-iii-iv-bank-capital-rules-and-the-price-of-credit), with the safety net that lets big banks fund cheaply in [deposit insurance, lender of last resort, and the anatomy of a bank run](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/deposit-insurance-lender-of-last-resort-and-the-anatomy-of-a-bank-run) and the shadow-bank perimeter that escapes those rules in [money market funds and shadow-bank regulation](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/money-market-funds-and-shadow-bank-regulation) — pair it with [shadow banking and the repo market](/blog/trading/finance/shadow-banking-and-the-repo-market). The same incumbency dynamic runs through data and privacy law, where compliance cost favors the platforms in [data and privacy law: GDPR and the tech business model](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/data-and-privacy-law-gdpr-and-the-tech-business-model), and through drug approval, where the regulatory gauntlet is itself the moat in [pharma regulation: FDA approvals and drug pricing](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/pharma-regulation-fda-approvals-and-drug-pricing).

#### Worked example: a compliance cost as an incumbent's moat

Suppose a new rule imposes a fixed annual compliance cost of \$50 million on every firm in a sector. A large incumbent with \$10 billion of revenue absorbs that as a 0.5% margin hit: \$50m ÷ \$10,000m = 0.5%. A challenger with \$400 million of revenue absorbs the *same* \$50 million as a 12.5% margin hit: \$50m ÷ \$400m = 12.5%. If the challenger's operating margin was 10%, the rule makes it *unprofitable* (10% − 12.5% = −2.5%), while the incumbent merely sees margins slip from, say, 30% to 29.5%. The "more regulation is bad for the sector" trade is wrong; the right trade is *long the incumbent, short the marginal challenger* on the rule's announcement.

The takeaway: a fixed cost is a regressive tax on size, and markets that price it as a uniform headwind misjudge the winners.

## Rule 4 — The fine is small; the forced change to the business model is large

When an enforcement action or an antitrust ruling lands, the headline number is the fine. The fine is almost always the *least* important part. A one-time \$5 billion penalty is a single subtraction from book value; the market shrugs it off in a day if that is all there is. What matters is whether the action forces a *permanent change to the business model* — a forced breakup, a banned practice, a capped price, a lost data stream. That is a hit to the *recurring cash-flow stream*, capitalized over every future year, and it can be ten or fifty times the fine.

This is the core lesson of the antitrust track: [antitrust 101: Sherman, Clayton, and merger review](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/antitrust-101-sherman-clayton-and-merger-review) builds the law, and [big tech antitrust: the new Brandeisians](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/big-tech-antitrust-the-new-brandeisians) shows why a remedy that touches the ad-targeting engine or the app-store toll is far more dangerous to a platform than any fine. The same logic governs corporate-governance fights, where an activist forcing a strategic change matters more than any single board vote — [corporate governance: fiduciary duty and shareholder activism](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/corporate-governance-fiduciary-duty-and-shareholder-activism) — and bankruptcy, where the legal restructuring rewrites the entire capital stack rather than imposing a one-time cost, in [bankruptcy and restructuring law: Chapter 11 and the creditor stack](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/bankruptcy-and-restructuring-law-chapter-11-and-the-creditor-stack).

#### Worked example: capitalizing the multiple, not the fine

A platform earns \$8 billion a year from a practice an antitrust remedy might ban. The market discounts its cash flows at 10% with 3% perpetual growth. The *recurring* value of that practice, as a growing perpetuity, is next year's cash flow divided by (discount rate minus growth): (\$8bn × 1.03) ÷ (0.10 − 0.03) = \$8.24bn ÷ 0.07 = **\$117.7 billion**. Now suppose the same case also carries a one-time \$5 billion fine. The fine costs \$5bn; the *banned practice* costs \$117.7bn — about **23 times** the fine. A trader who sells on the fine headline and a trader who sells on the remedy risk are making opposite-sized bets. When the ruling specifies a *behavioral* remedy (banning the practice), the repricing is the \$117.7bn figure; when it specifies only a fine, it is the \$5bn figure.

The takeaway: a fine subtracts once from book value, but a forced model change subtracts from every future year — capitalize the recurring loss, not the headline.

## Rule 5 — Tax and capital rules quietly steer trillions

The flashiest rules — sanctions, antitrust suits, court rulings — get the headlines. The rules that move the *most capital* are the quiet ones: the tax code and the capital-and-flow regime. A change in the corporate tax rate, the treatment of buybacks, the deductibility of interest, or the rules on moving money across borders redirects trillions of dollars of after-tax cash, slowly and durably. Follow the after-tax cash, not the headline.

The canonical case is the 2017 US tax cut, which took the corporate rate from 35% to 21% and triggered a record wave of buybacks and repatriation — built in [tax law as a market force](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/tax-law-as-a-market-force) and worked end to end in [the 2017 TCJA and the repatriation trade](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2017-tcja-and-the-repatriation-trade). The capital-and-flow side — the legal limits on where money can go — is built in [capital controls and the legal limits on money flows](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/capital-controls-and-the-legal-limits-on-money-flows), and the way that screening can block cross-border capital on national-security grounds is in [CFIUS and national security investment screening](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/cfius-and-national-security-investment-screening).

What makes tax and capital rules so powerful — and so easy to underweight — is that they act on the *denominator of the whole market's after-tax return*, quietly and at scale, rather than on a single name with a headline. A small change in the deductibility of interest reshapes how every leveraged company finances itself. A change in the tax treatment of dividends versus buybacks redirects how trillions of dollars of corporate cash is returned to shareholders. A new rule on whether foreign investors can repatriate profits, or whether a pension fund can hold a given asset class, redirects entire pools of capital. None of these makes a dramatic headline, yet each one moves more money than most of the sanctions and rulings that dominate the news. The practitioner's discipline under Rule 5 is to read the *plumbing* changes — the tax notices, the capital-flow rules, the fund-eligibility tweaks — with the same attention everyone else reserves for the dramatic ones, because that is where the durable, un-crowded repricing hides.

#### Worked example: a tax cut flowing straight into buybacks

After the 2017 cut, a firm with \$1 billion of pre-tax profit kept (1 − 0.21) × \$1bn = \$790m after tax, versus (1 − 0.35) × \$1bn = \$650m under the old 35% rate — an extra **\$140 million** of after-tax cash per \$1 billion of profit, every year. Scaled across the S&P 500, that arithmetic is exactly why gross buybacks jumped from \$519bn in 2017 to **\$806bn in 2018**, a record. If a firm uses that windfall to retire shares, earnings per share rise mechanically even with flat profit: shrinking the share count by, say, 3% lifts EPS by roughly 3% with no change to the business. The trade after a corporate tax cut is therefore *long the highest-buyback, highest-margin names*, because they convert the windfall most directly into per-share value.

The takeaway: a tax rate is a recurring claim on every future dollar of profit, so a cut is a perpetual cash-flow upgrade, not a one-time gift.

## Rule 6 — Sanctions weaponize the dollar and the supply chain; map the chokepoint

Sanctions and export controls are the sharpest tools in the rule-maker's kit because they exploit *chokepoints* — single points through which a flow must pass. The dollar clearing system is a chokepoint: almost all global trade settles through it, so a designation can cut a target off from world commerce overnight. Advanced-chip manufacturing equipment is a chokepoint: a handful of firms make the machines, so a control on them can freeze an adversary's technology. The trade is never "sanctions are bad for the target"; it is "which firms sit *on* the chokepoint, and which depend on it?"

The dollar chokepoint deserves its own attention because it is the master one. When two firms in different countries trade, the payment almost always passes through a correspondent bank with a US dollar account, which puts the transaction inside US jurisdiction. *Secondary* sanctions exploit this: the US does not need to sanction a foreign bank directly — it can simply threaten to cut that bank off from the dollar system if it deals with a sanctioned party. Faced with losing access to the world's reserve currency, the foreign bank complies, and the sanction's reach extends far beyond US borders without a single foreign court being involved. This is the "weaponization of finance," and it is why the 2022 freezing of \$300 billion of a major central bank's reserves was such a watershed: it demonstrated that even sovereign reserves held in dollars and euros are revocable. That demonstration is itself the slow driver behind [the dollar system and de-dollarization](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization) — every state now prices the option value of *not* being inside a chokepoint someone else controls.

The legal machinery is built in [sanctions law: OFAC, the SDN list, and secondary sanctions](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sanctions-law-ofac-the-sdn-list-and-secondary-sanctions), with enforcement and the compliance burden it creates in [sanctions evasion, enforcement, and compliance risk](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sanctions-evasion-enforcement-and-compliance-risk). The supply-chain chokepoint is built across [export controls and the chip war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war) and [Taiwan, semiconductors, and the most important supply chain on earth](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/taiwan-semiconductors-and-the-most-important-supply-chain-on-earth), with the reshoring response in [supply-chain geopolitics: reshoring, friendshoring, and critical minerals](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals). The trade-law backdrop that all of this sits inside is in [the legal architecture of global trade](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade) and the worked tariff case in [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war). The dollar's role as the master chokepoint — and the slow effort to route around it — is in [the dollar system and de-dollarization](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization), best paired with the mechanism post [the petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance).

#### Worked example: sizing a sanction's revenue hit

A semiconductor-equipment maker books \$18 billion of annual revenue, of which 25% — \$4.5 billion — comes from customers in a country newly placed under export controls. Suppose 80% of that exposed revenue is now blocked: 0.80 × \$4.5bn = \$3.6 billion of revenue gone, or 20% of the total top line. If the firm earns a 30% operating margin on that lost revenue, operating profit falls by 0.30 × \$3.6bn = \$1.08 billion. At a 20× earnings multiple, that recurring profit loss capitalizes to roughly 20 × \$1.08bn = **\$21.6 billion** of market value — far more than any one-year revenue number suggests, because the loss is recurring. The trade is to short the equipment maker *and* go long the domestic champions the control is designed to protect, who now face less foreign competition.

The takeaway: a chokepoint control reroutes recurring revenue, so capitalize the lost stream, and remember every sanction has a beneficiary on the other side.

## Rule 7 — Energy is the channel geopolitics hits hardest

Most geopolitical events reach markets weakly and briefly. Energy is the exception. Because oil and gas are inputs to nearly everything and their short-run supply is inelastic, a geopolitical shock to energy supply transmits straight into inflation, central-bank policy, and the relative performance of entire sectors. When a geopolitical headline crosses, your first question is always: *does this threaten energy supply?* If yes, treat it as macro, not a one-day risk-off. If no, it will probably fade.

The reason energy is special is the *inelasticity* of both supply and demand in the short run. You cannot drill a new oilfield, build a new pipeline, or commission a new liquefied-gas terminal in a month, so when supply is cut, output cannot rise to meet demand for years. On the other side, you cannot stop heating your home or running your factory next week, so demand cannot fall much either. With both curves nearly vertical in the short run, even a modest physical shortfall forces a *large* price move to clear the market — which is why a few percent of lost supply can double a price. That price move then flows straight through the spine: higher energy prices raise headline inflation, which forces central banks to keep policy tighter for longer (the rate channel of [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates)), which reprices every long-duration asset through the discount rate. A single energy shock therefore fires the cash-flow channel (energy producers' revenue), the discount-rate channel (higher rates), and the macro channel (inflation) all at once — which is precisely why it does not fade like an ordinary geopolitical scare.

The risk-premium channel through which geopolitics reaches all assets is built in [geopolitics as a market factor: the risk-premium channel](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/geopolitics-as-a-market-factor-the-risk-premium-channel), and the energy channel specifically in [energy geopolitics: OPEC, the oil weapon, and the European gas shock](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock). How conflict itself prices into assets — and which sectors re-rate structurally rather than briefly — is in [war and markets: how conflict prices into assets](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/war-and-markets-how-conflict-prices-into-assets).

![Dual axis chart of Brent crude spiking and fading around the 2022 invasion against the US defense index re-rating](/imgs/blogs/the-law-policy-and-geopolitics-playbook-8.png)

The chart above is the energy channel made concrete. Brent crude leapt from about \$79 in early January 2022 to a \$128 peak after the February 24 invasion, then *faded* back toward \$86 by year-end as supply rerouted — the oil spike is real but often temporary. The defense index, by contrast, kept climbing from a rebased 100 to 138 over the following two years: a *structural* re-rating, not a fade. Same shock, two opposite trade profiles — which is exactly why Rule 7 and Rule 8 must be read together.

#### Worked example: an oil spike's drag on a fuel-heavy earnings line

An airline burns 4 billion gallons of jet fuel a year. Crude jumps from \$80 to \$120 a barrel — a \$40, or 50%, increase — and jet fuel, which tracks crude, rises about \$1.00 per gallon as a result. The added fuel cost is 4bn gallons × \$1.00 = **\$4 billion** a year. If the airline earned \$2 billion of pre-tax profit before the spike, the unhedged fuel hit *more than wipes out all of it* — the firm swings to a \$2 billion loss. Meanwhile an integrated oil major with 2 billion barrels of annual production sees roughly 2bn × \$40 = \$80 billion of extra gross revenue on the upstream side. The energy shock is not "risk-off for stocks"; it is a massive *transfer* from energy consumers to energy producers, and the trade is long the producer, short the heavy consumer.

The takeaway: energy is an input cost for most of the economy and a revenue line for a few, so an oil shock redistributes profit rather than destroying it uniformly.

## Rule 8 — Most shocks fade; fade the panic on base rates, but respect the oil exception

Here is the rule that pays for itself over a career: the *base rate* for a geopolitical shock is that markets overreact and then mean-revert within weeks. The Geopolitical Risk index spikes hard — to 512 after the 9/11 attacks, to 277 around the 2022 invasion — and then reverts toward its long-run mean of 100. The disciplined move on a non-energy, non-regime-change shock is to *fade the panic*: sell volatility, buy the dip in quality, and wait for the reversion. But the rule has a hard exception — when the shock genuinely threatens energy supply or a major power's regime, it does *not* fade, and fading it is how you blow up.

![Area chart of the geopolitical risk index spiking at major events and reverting toward its long run mean of 100](/imgs/blogs/the-law-policy-and-geopolitics-playbook-7.png)

The chart above is the empirical backbone of this rule: every major geopolitical episode is a spike that reverts toward the dashed mean line at 100. The base-rate discipline that makes fading work is built in [geopolitics as a market factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/geopolitics-as-a-market-factor-the-risk-premium-channel), the political-calendar version (most election scares fade) in [elections and political risk: trading the political calendar](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar), and the currency dimension — competitive devaluation as a slow-burn rather than a one-day event — in [currency wars and competitive devaluation](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation). The exception (the shocks that do *not* fade) is exactly the energy and conflict cases of Rule 7.

#### Worked example: the expected value of a base-rate fade

Take a non-energy geopolitical scare. History says that in 80% of such episodes the market fully recovers within a month (a fade you capture, worth +5% on a dip-bought quality basket) and in 20% it does not (the shock escalates, and you lose −15% before your stop). The expected value of the fade is 0.80 × (+5%) + 0.20 × (−15%) = 4% − 3% = **+1%** per episode. That positive but thin edge is *why the fade works on average and why sizing matters*: the 20% tail is large enough to ruin you if you bet too big. Now change the inputs for an *energy* shock — say only 40% fade and the bad case is −30% — and the expected value flips to 0.40 × (+5%) + 0.60 × (−30%) = 2% − 18% = **−16%**. Same instinct, opposite answer. The base-rate fade is a good trade only when you have correctly excluded the energy and regime-change exceptions first.

The takeaway: fading panic is a positive-expectancy bet *on the right events*, and the whole skill is in screening out the few shocks that genuinely change the world.

## Rule 9 — Size for fat tails; hedge with convexity

Legal and geopolitical outcomes are *binary and fat-tailed*: a court rules one way or the other, a deal closes or breaks, a war starts or it does not. The distribution of outcomes has heavy tails, and the single biggest source of ruin in this discipline is not being wrong about direction — it is being *right but too large*, so a fat-tail outcome wipes you out before the thesis plays. The fix is to size small relative to your worst case and to buy *convexity* — options that pay off non-linearly — so that the rare large move pays for the many small premiums.

Why are these outcomes *fat-tailed* rather than the gentle bell curve that ordinary daily returns roughly follow? Because they are *jump processes*, not diffusion. A normal trading day is a thousand small buys and sells that average into a small move. A ruling, a designation, or a declaration of war is a single discrete event that resolves a large chunk of uncertainty in one instant, so the price gaps rather than drifts. A stock can fall 30% in a second on a court loss with no chance to sell in between — there is no orderly exit at intermediate prices. This is exactly the failure mode the daily-returns rule of thumb misses: position sizing tuned to "normal" volatility is wildly too large for an asset whose next move is a coin-flip gap. The whole craft of [position sizing for tail and political risk](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/position-sizing-for-tail-and-political-risk) is sizing for the gap you cannot trade through, not the volatility you can.

Convexity is the structural answer to a gap you cannot trade through. A put option's loss is capped at the premium while its payoff grows as the underlying falls — so you can hold a position through a binary event knowing your worst case is bounded in advance, no orderly exit required. The cost is the premium you pay on the many events that resolve benignly; the benefit is survival (and a large payoff) on the rare event that gaps. When the standing monitor in [building a legal and geopolitical risk dashboard](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard) shows the tail fattening, that is the cue to pre-buy convexity while it is still cheap, before the event everyone can see coming bids up the options.

The full treatment of sizing for these tails is in [position sizing for tail and political risk](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/position-sizing-for-tail-and-political-risk), and the standing-monitor approach that tells you *when* the tail is fattening (so you can pre-buy cheap convexity) is in [building a legal and geopolitical risk dashboard](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard). When the tail event is a system-wide rates-and-liquidity shock rather than a single-name event, the macro hedge is the relevant tool — see the cross-asset and macro mechanism posts on policy and liquidity transmission, [the macro-trading section](/blog/trading/macro-trading) and [the cross-asset section](/blog/trading/cross-asset), and the safe-haven role of duration and gold during a flight to quality.

#### Worked example: sizing a binary legal bet against ruin

You have a 60% read that a court ruling goes your way, paying +40% on the position, versus a 40% chance it goes against you, costing −50%. The edge is positive: expected return = 0.60 × 40% − 0.40 × 50% = 24% − 20% = **+4%**. But the Kelly criterion for a binary bet — bet fraction = edge ÷ odds, or more precisely *f* = (p × b − q) ÷ b, where p = 0.60, q = 0.40, and b = the win/loss payoff ratio = 40 ÷ 50 = 0.8 — gives *f* = (0.60 × 0.8 − 0.40) ÷ 0.8 = (0.48 − 0.40) ÷ 0.8 = 0.08 ÷ 0.8 = **0.10**. So even with a real edge, the math says risk only about 10% of capital on this single binary — and most practitioners bet a *fraction* of Kelly (half-Kelly ≈ 5%) because the input probabilities are themselves uncertain. The trader who bets 50% of capital on a "60% sure thing" is one bad ruling from a 25% drawdown; the disciplined trader bets 5% and survives to compound.

The takeaway: a positive edge tells you which way to bet, but only your worst case tells you how much — and on fat-tailed legal outcomes, the worst case is the binding constraint.

## Rule 10 — War-game the branches; pre-plan the trade for each

The final rule is a process, not an insight. Because rule-driven outcomes are branching and uncertain, you should not wait for the outcome to decide what to do — you should map the branches *in advance*, attach a probability and a pre-planned trade to each, and then simply execute when the branch resolves. The decision is made in calm, not in the chaos of the announcement, which is precisely when judgment is worst.

The full scenario-analysis method is in [scenario analysis and war-gaming geopolitical events](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/scenario-analysis-and-war-gaming-geopolitical-events), and the event-day execution discipline — how to actually trade the moment a regulatory or legal catalyst resolves — is in [how to trade a regulatory event](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-to-trade-a-regulatory-event). Pair both with the event-reaction mechanics in [the event-trading section](/blog/trading/event-trading), which catalogs how different assets react to scheduled catalysts.

#### Worked example: a probability-weighted scenario tree

Suppose a regulator will rule on a sector in three ways. Branch A (40% probability): full approval, sector +12%. Branch B (35%): approval with conditions, sector +2%. Branch C (25%): rejection, sector −20%. The pre-event expected move is 0.40 × 12% + 0.35 × 2% + 0.25 × (−20%) = 4.8% + 0.7% − 5.0% = **+0.5%**, essentially flat — which tells you the *outright* directional bet is a coin flip and a poor use of risk. The information is in the *branches*: you can pre-plan to buy aggressively on Branch A, do nothing on Branch B, and have a short or a put already in place for Branch C. Each branch has its trade written before the ruling; on ruling day you simply read which world you are in and execute the plan. The work is done in advance, when you are calm.

The takeaway: a flat expected value can still hide a tradable structure, and war-gaming converts a chaotic announcement into a calm lookup of a pre-written plan.

## Putting it together: one event, several rules

The rules are not used one at a time. A real event fires several at once. Return to the June 2025 export-control headline from the opening. Here is the full chain, with the rules in play.

**Rule 2 (which body)**: an agency acted by order — fast, only semi-reversible — so the repricing should be held at the probability the control survives, not as permanent. **Rule 6 (chokepoint)**: the firm sits *downstream* of a controlled technology, so map who depends on it and who benefits; the domestic champion the control protects is a long candidate. **Rule 4 (the multiple)**: the headline is "4% of revenue restricted," but the danger is whether the firm's *whole* China-facing model is now impaired — capitalize the recurring loss, not the one quarter. **Rule 1 (the surprise)**: the stock is down 4% pre-market, so part of the move is already priced; your edge is your estimate of the *eventual* revenue and margin hit versus the ~4% the market has taken. **Rule 9 (sizing)**: the outcome is binary and fat-tailed (the control could broaden or be negotiated away), so size small and look at buying puts rather than shorting outright. **Rule 10 (war-game)**: write the trade for each branch — broadened control (add to the short), carve-out granted (cover and flip long), status quo (the priced 4% was roughly fair) — before the next agency update.

Six of the ten rules fire on a single mid-cap headline. That is the point of the framework: the spine routes the event, and the rules tell you what to do at each link. The TCJA case study in [the 2017 TCJA and the repatriation trade](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2017-tcja-and-the-repatriation-trade) is the same exercise for a statute; [the 2022 Russia sanctions](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance) is the same exercise for a shock.

## What invalidates each rule

A rule you cannot break is a dogma, not a tool. The mark of a senior practitioner is knowing the precise conditions under which each rule *flips*, so you can recognize the exception before it costs you. Here is the failure condition for each of the ten — the question that, if answered "yes," means the default rule does not apply.

**Rule 1 (trade the surprise) fails when nothing was priced.** A genuine bolt-from-the-blue — an unscheduled invasion, a surprise emergency rule, a flash court injunction — has no drift behind it, so the *whole* move is still available and "the news is old" is exactly wrong. The test: was this event on anyone's calendar? If not, the surprise *is* the news.

**Rule 2 (which body) fails when the bodies act in sequence.** A court can strike down an agency rule, which sends it back to the agency, which can re-issue it differently, which a legislature can then codify or override. When a rule is in this multi-body relay, treating any single step as final misreads its durability. The test: is another body able to reverse or entrench this within the holding period?

**Rule 3 (regulation entrenches incumbents) fails when the rule is a structural breakup.** A rule designed to *dismantle* the incumbent's scale advantage — a forced divestiture, an interoperability mandate, an open-access requirement — does the opposite of a compliance-cost moat: it hands the challenger the very advantage the incumbent built. The test: does the rule raise everyone's costs, or specifically attack the incumbent's scale?

**Rule 4 (the multiple, not the fine) fails when the remedy is genuinely cosmetic.** Some settlements really are just a fine plus a face-saving promise that changes nothing operationally. Capitalizing a "model change" that the firm routes around in a quarter overstates the damage. The test: does the remedy bind a *recurring* revenue stream, or is it a one-time payment dressed up as reform?

**Rule 5 (tax and capital rules steer trillions) fails when the change is already fully phased in.** A tax change announced years ahead with a long phase-in may be entirely in estimates before it takes effect, so the "effective date" is a non-event. The test: has the market had years to discount this, or is the magnitude itself a surprise?

**Rule 6 (map the chokepoint) fails when a substitute exists.** A control on a chokepoint only bites if the target cannot route around it. If a second supplier, a domestic alternative, or a gray-market channel exists, the chokepoint leaks and the revenue-loss math overstates the hit. The test: is this truly a single point of failure, or merely the cheapest of several paths?

**Rule 7 (energy is the channel) fails when supply is not actually threatened.** Plenty of frightening geopolitical headlines do not touch a barrel of oil or a cubic meter of gas. A coup in a country that produces neither is a Rule-8 fade, not a Rule-7 macro event. The test: trace the headline to a physical supply curve — if it does not move, energy is not the channel.

**Rule 8 (fade the panic) fails on the events that change the world.** This is the most dangerous exception, because it is rare and catastrophic. An energy shock, a great-power war, a regime change in a major producer, or a systemic financial rupture does *not* mean-revert toward 100 — it sets a new regime. Fading it is the classic blow-up. The test, again: energy or regime change involved? If yes, do not fade.

**Rule 9 (size for fat tails) fails by being *too* timid.** Over-hedging every benign event bleeds premium until the strategy cannot earn its keep. Convexity is insurance, and insurance bought on everything is a guaranteed slow loss. The test: is the tail genuinely fat *and* mispriced, or are you paying up for protection you do not need?

**Rule 10 (war-game the branches) fails when the branch set is wrong.** A scenario tree is only as good as its branches; the outcome that ruins you is the one you did not draw. The discipline is to always include a "something we did not model" branch with a non-trivial weight. The test: does your tree have a residual for the unknown, or does it sum to 100% across only the outcomes you thought of?

Knowing these ten failure conditions is what separates running the playbook mechanically from running it well. The rules are defaults; the exceptions are where the real money — and the real losses — live.

## Common misconceptions

**"The law just passed, so now is the time to trade it."** Usually wrong. Rule 1: the price already holds the expected outcome, drifted in over months. The 2017 tax cut was a non-event on signing day after a 20% pre-positioning rally. By the time you read the passage headline, most of the move is behind you — unless the *outcome surprised* the priced expectation. The drift mechanism is built in [how a rule becomes a price](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing).

**"More regulation is bad for the whole industry."** Often backwards. Rule 3: a thick rulebook is a regressive tax on size that *entrenches incumbents*. The worked example above showed a \$50m fixed cost as a 0.5% margin hit for the giant and a fatal 12.5% hit for the challenger. The right trade is frequently long the incumbent, as developed in [Basel III/IV](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/basel-iii-iv-bank-capital-rules-and-the-price-of-credit) and [data and privacy law](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/data-and-privacy-law-gdpr-and-the-tech-business-model).

**"The fine is the cost of the lawsuit."** Almost never. Rule 4: the fine is a one-time subtraction; the *forced model change* is a recurring loss capitalized over every future year. The worked example put a banned practice at 23 times its fine. The antitrust track — [antitrust 101](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/antitrust-101-sherman-clayton-and-merger-review) and [big tech antitrust](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/big-tech-antitrust-the-new-brandeisians) — is built around this distinction.

**"Geopolitical shocks crash the market."** Usually only briefly. Rule 8: the base rate is overreaction then mean-reversion, with the GPR index reverting toward 100. Fading the panic is positive-expectancy *except* for the energy and regime-change exceptions of Rule 7. Confusing the two — fading an oil shock — is a classic blow-up, as the worked expected-value flip showed.

**"A reversible tariff and a permanent statute that move a stock the same amount are the same trade."** No. Rule 2: a statute is sticky (held as near-permanent in the price); a tariff is reversible by a single deal (held only at the probability it survives). The 2018-19 trade war priced exactly this reversibility, which is why the market never paid full freight for the damage — see [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war).

## The playbook: the ten rules as a checklist

Run this checklist on any legal, regulatory, or geopolitical headline. The first five read the rule; the last five size the trade.

1. **Trade the surprise, not the news.** Estimate the outcome distribution versus what is already priced. If the outcome will land as priced, there is no trade. *(Develops: [how a rule becomes a price](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing), [the regulatory calendar](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock).)*

2. **Know which body acts.** Statute (slow, sticky), agency (medium, observable), court (fast, binary), tariff/order (fast, reversible), sanction (immediate, hard). It sets how durably to hold the repricing. *(Develops: [who writes the rules](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts).)*

3. **Regulation often entrenches incumbents.** A fixed compliance cost is a regressive tax on size; check who can afford the rulebook before shorting the sector. *(Develops: [lobbying and regulatory capture](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture).)*

4. **The fine is small; the model change is large.** Capitalize the recurring loss from a forced change, not the one-time headline penalty. *(Develops: [big tech antitrust](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/big-tech-antitrust-the-new-brandeisians).)*

5. **Tax and capital rules quietly steer trillions.** Follow the after-tax cash and the legal limits on where money can move. *(Develops: [tax law as a market force](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/tax-law-as-a-market-force).)*

6. **Sanctions weaponize the dollar and the supply chain — map the chokepoint.** Find who sits on the chokepoint and who depends on it; every sanction has a beneficiary. *(Develops: [sanctions law](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sanctions-law-ofac-the-sdn-list-and-secondary-sanctions), [export controls](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war).)*

7. **Energy is the channel geopolitics hits hardest.** Ask "does this threaten energy supply?" If yes, treat it as macro; an oil shock transfers profit from consumers to producers. *(Develops: [energy geopolitics](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock).)*

8. **Most shocks fade — fade the panic on base rates, but respect the oil and regime-change exception.** The GPR index reverts toward 100; fading is positive-expectancy except for the events that truly change the world. *(Develops: [geopolitics as a market factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/geopolitics-as-a-market-factor-the-risk-premium-channel).)*

9. **Size for fat tails; hedge with convexity.** Binary outcomes ruin the right-but-too-large trader; size by the worst case (fraction-of-Kelly) and buy options. *(Develops: [position sizing for tail and political risk](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/position-sizing-for-tail-and-political-risk).)*

10. **War-game the branches; pre-plan the trade for each.** Write the trade for every outcome before the event, then execute calmly when the branch resolves. *(Develops: [scenario analysis and war-gaming](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/scenario-analysis-and-war-gaming-geopolitical-events), [how to trade a regulatory event](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-to-trade-a-regulatory-event).)*

The discipline that ties all ten together is the spine: route every headline from law and geopolitics, through policy, through macro and flows, into prices, and out the far side as a sized trade with a defined invalidation. Read the rule with Rules 1 through 5; size the trade with Rules 6 through 10. Do that consistently and you will catch the repricing the headline-traders miss, and survive the fat tails that ruin the over-sized.
