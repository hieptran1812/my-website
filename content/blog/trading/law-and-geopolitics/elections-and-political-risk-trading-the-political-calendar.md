> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An election is *scheduled uncertainty*: the date is fixed and on the calendar months in advance, but the outcome and the policy platform that comes with it are not, so markets reprice volatility and sector exposure in predictable ways even though the winner is close to a coin flip.
>
> - The edge is **not** in predicting the winner. It is in the *platform repricing* (each outcome carries a different tax / regulation / spending / trade path that helps some sectors and hurts others) and in the *volatility around a known date* (vol gets bid into the date and crushed the morning after).
> - The single binary "who wins" is the wrong frame. Split it into the *binary* (the coin flip) and the *continuous* (the policy platforms behind each outcome). You trade the continuous, hedged across the binary.
> - The "partisan portfolio" — owning the stocks one party is supposedly good for — is a myth: long-run equity returns are statistically similar across administrations, so timing the index on an election is a coin flip with costs attached.
> - The one number to remember: the average S&P 500 calendar-year return has been within a few percentage points of its long-run ~10% under both major US parties since 1945. The differences are dwarfed by the noise. **The platform moves sectors; it does not reliably move the index.**

On the evening of **November 8, 2016**, US equity-index futures did something that surprised almost everyone who had positioned for the result. As the vote count turned away from the consensus that polls and prediction markets had priced, S&P 500 futures fell sharply — at one point the overnight limit-down move was about 5% — on the simple logic that an unexpected outcome meant an unknown policy path. Then, over the following hours and into the cash session, the market did the opposite of what the panic implied: it rallied, and it kept rallying for weeks, led by banks, small-caps, and domestic industrials, while bond yields jumped. Nothing in the economy had changed overnight. No law had passed. A *probability distribution over future policy* had been repriced, twice, in the space of a single night — first toward fear of the unknown, then toward the specific platform the result implied.

That whipsaw is the entire subject of this post. Elections are the cleanest recurring example of a market event where the *timing is known and the content is not*. Earnings have a date and a number. A central-bank meeting has a date and a decision. An election has a date and a *fork in the policy road*. The practitioner's job is not to guess which fork the country takes — you cannot reliably out-forecast a market that has already aggregated every poll, model, and prediction-market price into the tape. The job is to read what each fork would do to volatility and to specific sectors, and to position for the repricing while staying hedged across the coin flip itself.

We will stay rigorously, deliberately neutral. Whether a given platform is good policy, whether a party "helps the economy," whether a result is desirable — those are political questions, and they are not ours. Our job is the practitioner's job: read the calendar, separate the binary from the continuous, size the volatility, position the platform-sensitive sectors hedged, and budget for the tail where the result is contested. Every sector effect below is described in *both directions*, because the trade is the spread, not a side.

![Election binary outcome forks into two priced policy platforms and a vol bid then crush](/imgs/blogs/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one picture. A *known date* (blue) creates *scheduled uncertainty* (amber). That uncertainty forks into two outcomes — each roughly a coin flip — and each outcome carries a *policy platform* (blue) that reprices the same set of sectors in opposite directions (green). Meanwhile the uncertainty itself, independent of which way it breaks, bids up *event volatility* into the date and crushes it after (amber). Hold that fork in your head; every section below is one branch of it.

## Foundations: why a scheduled vote moves markets

Before we touch a single chart, we need to build five things from zero: why elections matter to markets at all, what "scheduled uncertainty" and "event volatility" actually mean, the crucial difference between the *binary* (who wins) and the *continuous* (the policy platforms), why prediction markets and polls are a probability source rather than a forecast, and the special case of emerging-market political risk. If you already trade event vol, skim. If you are new, this is the load-bearing section — everything later assumes it.

### Why an election matters to markets at all

A company's stock price is, at bottom, the present value of the cash flows it is expected to generate, discounted back at a rate that reflects how risky those cash flows are. Anything that changes *expected future cash flows* or the *discount rate* changes the price. An election can change both, because the winner gets to steer policy — and policy is one of the largest inputs into corporate cash flows that sits entirely outside any company's control.

Policy steers cash flows through four main channels, and it is worth naming them because they map directly onto the sectors we will trade:

- **Tax.** The corporate tax rate, the capital-gains rate, the treatment of buybacks and dividends, depreciation rules. A change in the corporate rate flows straight to after-tax earnings — we walk through the dollar mechanics in the companion post on [tax law as a market force](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/tax-law-as-a-market-force).
- **Regulation.** Drilling permits, drug-price rules, bank capital requirements, antitrust enforcement, emissions standards. Regulation is a cost and a gate; tightening it raises costs or blocks revenue, loosening it does the reverse.
- **Spending.** Defense procurement, infrastructure outlays, healthcare subsidies, clean-energy credits. Government spending is direct revenue for the firms that win the contracts.
- **Trade.** Tariffs, sanctions, export controls, the posture toward a major trading partner. These reroute whole supply chains — see the case study in [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war).

The key point for a trader: the *current* law is already priced. What an election changes is the *expected path* of these four levers. Markets discount the expected path the moment new information arrives — they do not wait for a bill to pass. This is the same mechanism that governs every rule-change in this series, laid out in detail in [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain). An election is just a *dated, scheduled* node on that chain.

### Scheduled uncertainty and event volatility

Here is the property that makes elections tradeable in a way that most geopolitical shocks are not. **A war, a coup, a terror attack, a surprise sanction — these are *unscheduled*. They hit the tape on the news itself.** You cannot position in advance because you do not know the date. An election is the opposite: the date is set, often years ahead, by law. The market knows exactly when the uncertainty will resolve. That single fact reorganizes how the risk gets priced.

When the market knows that a chunk of uncertainty will resolve on a specific date, it bids up the cost of protection that spans that date. The cleanest place to see this is **implied volatility** — the market's expectation of how much an asset will move, embedded in the price of its options. An option's value rises with the amount of movement it might capture, so options that cover an uncertain event cost more. As the election date approaches and the uncertainty concentrates, the implied vol of options expiring just after the date gets *bid*. Then, the morning after the result, the uncertainty is gone — and that election premium *crushes* to zero. We will quantify this exactly. For the general mechanism of how event premium decays, see the event-trading companion on [event volatility: implied vs realized and the vol crush](/blog/trading/event-trading/event-volatility-implied-vs-realized-and-the-vol-crush).

![Implied vol rises into a known election date and crushes the day after the result](/imgs/blogs/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar-3.png)

The curve above is the signature of every scheduled binary event. Take a base level of implied vol around 16% in the quiet weeks before. As the date nears, the *event premium* gets stacked on top, pushing implied vol toward a peak — say 28% — the day before the vote. The dotted red line is election day. The moment the result is known, the premium that was paying for "we don't know who wins" evaporates, and implied vol gaps down toward a new, slightly-elevated-then-normal level — say 17%. The amber band is the *vol bid*; the green band is the *vol crush*. Notice the asymmetry: the rise is gradual (premium accumulates over weeks), the fall is a cliff (the unknown resolves in hours).

It helps to contrast this with how *unscheduled* shocks register. The chart below shows where the VIX — the headline 30-day implied-vol index on the S&P 500 — actually spiked at past stress events. Those spikes happened *on the news*: the flash crash, COVID, the Ukraine invasion. An election is different in kind: its vol is *anticipated* and bid in advance, then crushed on resolution, rather than spiking on a surprise. That distinction is the source of the whole trade.

![VIX close at six past stress events versus a calm-market level near 16](/imgs/blogs/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar-4.png)

Note something important in that data: even the largest *unscheduled* shocks resolve into a VIX that is high but finite — COVID at about 83 was the modern extreme, Ukraine at about 30 was a moderate spike. Elections sit at the lower end of that range in implied terms (often a high-20s peak for a major contested race), because the market knows the *date* and one of a small set of outcomes *will* happen. The uncertainty is bounded. That boundedness is exactly why the premium is sellable.

### The binary and the continuous

This is the single most important distinction in the post, and most retail commentary gets it backwards. An election presents the market with two very different objects bundled together:

- **The binary.** Who wins. This is, in a competitive race, close to a coin flip — and crucially, the market has already aggregated polls, models, and money into a price for it. You have almost no edge here. Trying to "call the election" is trying to beat a consensus that has eaten more information than you have.
- **The continuous.** The *policy platform* that comes with each outcome — the specific tax / regulation / spending / trade path — and how that path reprices each sector's expected cash flows. This is where the analyzable, tradeable content lives, because it is about *mechanism* (this rule helps this sector's margins by this much), not about *forecasting* (who will win).

Put plainly: you do not bet on the coin. You map out what each face of the coin does to the things you can value, and you position to capture the *repricing* of those things while staying hedged across which face comes up. The binary is the source of the vol (which you can trade as vol); the continuous is the source of the relative-value sector trades (which you can hedge to be outcome-neutral). Conflating them is how traders lose money "trading the election" — they take a directional index bet on the coin flip and call it analysis.

There is a deeper reason the split matters, and it is about where information actually lives. The *binary* is an efficiently-priced object: thousands of participants, dozens of pollsters, and real money in prediction markets have all poured their information into the probability, so the consensus on "who wins" is hard to beat — your forecast is competing against the aggregate of everyone's. The *continuous* is a far less efficiently-priced object, because pricing it correctly requires *two* hard steps that most participants skip: estimating each platform's effect on a sector's cash flows (real fundamental work), *and* weighting those effects by the right probability. Markets are demonstrably good at the first object and frequently sloppy at the second — they over-react to a vivid platform headline and under-react to a boring-but-larger one, they anchor sector moves to the last election rather than this one's specifics, and they conflate "the candidate said X" with "X will become law" (it must still pass a legislature, survive courts, and clear agencies). The continuous is where a careful analyst's edge can survive, precisely because it is harder and less crowded. The whole argument for trading the platform and the vol rather than the winner is an argument about *which object is mispriced*.

### Prediction markets and polls as a probability source

If you are not forecasting the winner, why care about polls and prediction markets at all? Because they give you a *probability*, and a probability is what you need to weight the platform scenarios and to know whether the vol is cheap or rich.

A **poll** is a sample-based estimate of voter intention, with a margin of error — a "52% plus or minus 3" headline means the true value is *probably* (usually 95% confidence) within 49–55%. Polls are noisy, they have systematic biases, and a single poll is nearly meaningless; the value is in the *average* across many polls over time. A **prediction market** is a venue where people trade contracts that pay \$1 if an outcome happens and \$0 if it does not. The *price* of such a contract is, by no-arbitrage logic, the market's implied probability of the outcome: a contract trading at \$0.60 implies a 60% chance. Prediction markets aggregate polls *plus* money *plus* every other signal traders can find, which is why their prices often lead and tighten faster than poll averages.

The trap — and we will give it a full myth-busting later — is believing "the polls are the price." They are not. **The market price already contains the polls.** Your edge, if you have one, is only the *gap between your own well-reasoned probability and the priced probability*, after the fees and spreads of actually trading it.

![Polls and a prediction-market price become an implied probability and then a tradeable edge](/imgs/blogs/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar-5.png)

The pipeline above is how a probability becomes a trade. Polls feed (among other things) a prediction market; the market's \$0.60 contract price *is* a 60% implied probability; you compare that to your own estimate (say 55%); the *edge* is the gap (here negative — the market is more confident than you, so a "buy" has negative edge); and the decision is to fade or pass, sized for the tail. The number you act on is never the poll. It is the gap.

### Emerging-market political risk: when the currency is the trade

In a developed market, an election rarely threatens the *credibility of the whole system* — institutions, the central bank's independence, the rule of law, the willingness to honor debt are taken as given, so the election mostly reprices *sectors*. In an **emerging market (EM)** — a developing economy with less entrenched institutions and a higher cost of capital — an election can reprice the *entire country*. The variable that absorbs that repricing is usually the **currency** and the **local-currency bond yield**, because they are the cleanest read on the market's confidence in the country's policy credibility.

A "market-friendly" EM result — a candidate or coalition the market reads as fiscally disciplined, respectful of central-bank independence, and predictable — typically *strengthens* the currency and *lowers* local yields, because the country's risk premium falls. A "market-skeptic" result — read as fiscally loose, interventionist, or likely to pressure the central bank — typically *weakens* the currency and *raises* yields. These moves can be several percent in a session, far larger than the equity-index wobble a developed-market election produces. We will work a dollar-sized example of exactly this, anchored through a USD/local-currency rate.

Why is the EM reaction so much larger? Because the EM election reprices three things a developed-market election usually takes as given. The first is *fiscal credibility* — whether the government will run sustainable deficits or borrow until it cannot. The second is *central-bank independence* — whether the monetary authority will be free to fight inflation or be pressed to finance the deficit, which is the road to currency debasement. The third, in the harder cases, is the *rule of law and the willingness to honor debt* — whether contracts and bond claims will be respected. A developed-market election rarely puts any of these on the ballot; an EM election can put all three on it at once. When the result is read as threatening any of them, foreign capital — which financed the local bonds and the currency — heads for the exit simultaneously, and the currency, which sits at the intersection of all three, absorbs the blow. This is why an EM election is, for a foreign investor, primarily a *currency and rates* event and only secondarily an *equity* event, and why the hedge is built in FX and rates instruments rather than stock-index options.

With those five foundations — why policy moves cash flows, scheduled uncertainty and event vol, the binary-vs-continuous split, prediction markets as a probability source, and EM currency sensitivity — we can now go deep on each mechanism and then build the playbook.

## The policy-platform to sector map

The heart of the analyzable, tradeable content in any election is the map from *policy platform* to *sector earnings*. This is where you do real work, and it is where neutrality matters most: every sensitive sector has a platform under which it gets a tailwind and a platform under which it gets a headwind. The map is a *mirror image*, not a partisan ranking. Your trade is the *spread* between the two columns, not an endorsement of either.

![Policy platform to sector map showing mirror-image tailwinds and headwinds across five sectors](/imgs/blogs/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar-2.png)

Read the matrix above one row at a time, and notice that every row is symmetric:

- **Energy (fossil fuels).** One platform eases drilling permits, opens federal land and offshore leasing, and slows the pace of emissions rules — a tailwind for oil and gas producers' volumes and capital plans. The other curbs new drilling, tightens methane and emissions rules, and slows pipeline approvals — a headwind. Same companies, opposite signs.
- **Health (pharma and managed care).** One platform pursues aggressive drug-price negotiation and tighter rules on managed-care margins — a headwind for pharma pricing power and insurer margins. The other lightens price-setting and negotiation — a tailwind. (The mechanism of a drug-pricing regime is its own deep topic; the point here is only the *direction* flips with the platform.)
- **Financials (banks).** One platform leans toward lighter bank-capital and supervisory rules, freeing balance-sheet capacity and buybacks — a tailwind for bank return on equity. The other tightens capital requirements and supervision — a headwind. The capital-rules mechanism is covered in [Basel III/IV bank capital rules and the price of credit](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/basel-iii-iv-bank-capital-rules-and-the-price-of-credit).
- **Defense (aerospace and defense contractors).** One platform raises defense procurement and modernization outlays — direct revenue for prime contractors. The other holds outlays flat or reorients them — a relative headwind to order books.
- **Renewables (clean energy).** One platform expands subsidies, tax credits, and mandates for solar, wind, storage, and EVs — a tailwind. The other rolls them back — a headwind. Note this row points *opposite* to the energy row, which is exactly why an "energy vs renewables" pair trade is a clean way to express a platform view while hedging market direction.

The discipline the matrix enforces is this: **for every name you would buy on outcome A, there is a name you would buy on outcome B.** That symmetry is what lets you build an *outcome-neutral* book — long the A-platform winners and short the A-platform losers (which are the B-platform winners) — so that you profit from the *repricing of the platform spread* regardless of which way the coin lands, as long as the market has under- or over-priced the sensitivity. The directional bet on who wins is hedged away; the relative-value bet on platform sensitivity is what you keep.

#### Worked example: a policy-platform sector trade, probability-weighted

Take a hypothetical large managed-care insurer, "HealthCo," earning **\$8.00 per share** in the base case with the stock at **\$240** (a 30× price-to-earnings multiple — investors are paying 30 dollars for each dollar of annual earnings). Two platforms are on the ballot, each priced by the market at a **50%** probability:

- **Platform A (price-negotiation):** tighter rules cut HealthCo's earnings power by **15%**, to \$6.80 per share. If the multiple holds at 30×, fair value is 30 × \$6.80 = **\$204**.
- **Platform B (lighter rules):** margins expand, lifting earnings by **8%**, to \$8.64 per share. At 30×, fair value is 30 × \$8.64 = **\$259.20**.

The probability-weighted fair value is 0.50 × \$204 + 0.50 × \$259.20 = \$102 + \$129.60 = **\$231.60**. The stock at \$240 is therefore trading about **\$8.40 (≈3.5%) rich** to the blended scenario — the market is leaning slightly toward the lighter-rules outcome being more likely, or more valuable, than a straight coin flip implies. That \$8.40 of richness is itself information: it is the market's *implied tilt* toward Platform B, and you can back out an implied probability from it. If the true coin is 50/50, the fair price is \$231.60; for the price to be \$240 the implied probability of Platform B must be roughly 65% rather than 50% (solve 204 + p × 55.20 = 240, giving p ≈ 0.65). So the \$240 tag is quietly telling you the market assigns about a 65% chance to the lighter-rules platform — a far more useful number than the headline poll.

The *spread* between the two outcomes is \$259.20 − \$204 = \$55.20, or about **23% of the stock price**. That is the size of the repricing that lands on the result. Here is the trade: rather than bet on which platform wins, pair HealthCo against a *defense contractor* whose platform sensitivity points the *opposite* way (defense outlays rise under the platform that also tightens drug pricing, in this stylized setup). Sizing the pair so the two legs have equal dollar exposure to broad-market moves (equal "dollar beta"), you are now long the *platform spread* and roughly flat the index. If you put \$1,000,000 long HealthCo and \$1,000,000 short the defense name, a 1% rally in the whole market lifts both legs about \$10,000 and nets to roughly zero — but a platform-driven divergence of, say, 10% between the two sectors earns you about \$200,000. You make money if the realized repricing on the result is larger than the market priced into the pair today — and you do not care who wins.

The intuition: the tradeable object is not "will Platform A win" but "is the 23% platform spread correctly priced into the pair." You harvested a relative-value mispricing, hedged across the binary.

## The volatility term structure into an election

We saw the shape of the vol bid and crush in the foundations. Now we make it precise, because the vol trade is the most *reliable* election trade — more reliable than any sector view, because it does not depend on which outcome occurs at all.

The relevant object is the **volatility term structure**: implied vol plotted against option expiry. In normal times this curve is gently upward-sloping (longer-dated options imply slightly more vol because more can happen over a longer horizon). Into an election, the curve develops a *kink* or *hump* at the expiry that captures the event: options expiring just after the date carry an extra premium — the *event premium* — for the jump the result might cause. Options expiring just *before* the date do not, because they will be settled before the result is known.

You can decompose the post-event-expiry implied vol into two pieces: the *baseline* vol that would exist with no event, and the *event premium* that prices the expected jump. The premium is, in effect, the market's price for the straddle's exposure to the binary. The moment the result prints, the jump either happens or does not, the uncertainty is resolved, and the premium has nothing left to pay for — so it collapses. This collapse is the **vol crush**, and it is the same phenomenon that hits a single stock the morning after earnings, scaled up to an index and an entire macro policy path.

There is a subtlety in the *shape* of the crush that the term structure reveals and that separates a careful seller from a careless one. The event premium does not live only in the at-the-money straddle; it also fattens the *wings* — the out-of-the-money puts and calls that pay off only on a large move. Going into a contested election the *skew* (the relative richness of downside puts) often steepens, because the market is paying up specifically for protection against a left-tail result. That means two things for the practitioner. First, the cheapest way to *be long* the event is sometimes a call spread or put spread rather than the full straddle, because the wings carry their own premium that also decays. Second, when you *sell* the event premium, selling the at-the-money straddle leaves you exposed to exactly the wing moves the market is most worried about — which is why the defined-risk structures (selling the body, owning the wings) exist. The crush is reliable on the *body*; the *tail* is precisely where it can fail to crush, and where an unhedged seller gets carried out.

A second subtlety: the crush is a property of *implied* vol, not *realized* vol, and confusing the two is a classic error. Realized volatility — how much the index *actually* moved — can be high on the result (a 2% gap is a big realized move) while implied volatility *collapses* at the same moment, because the implied number was pricing the *unknown* and the unknown is now resolved. You can lose money being long realized-vol-via-gamma into the result and *still* see the vol crush wipe out your long-vega straddle. The two legs of "vol" point in opposite directions on the night, and the trade you put on must specify which one you are actually long.

The practitioner reads the term structure to answer one question: **is the event premium cheap or rich relative to the move the event is likely to produce?** If the straddle is pricing a larger move than you think the result will deliver, you sell the premium (and hedge the gap risk). If it is pricing a smaller move than the platforms' spread implies, you buy it. The skill is calibrating "the move the event will produce" — and the policy-platform sector map is exactly the tool that calibrates it, because a wide platform spread justifies a wide expected move.

#### Worked example: the option-implied move and the post-event vol crush

Suppose an index is at **\$5,000** and a one-week straddle (a long call plus a long put, both struck at the \$5,000 spot — a position that profits from a move in *either* direction) expiring the day after the election costs **\$200** in total premium (\$100 for the call plus \$100 for the put). A straddle's cost as a fraction of spot is the market's estimate of the *implied move* — the size of move that would let the position break even: \$200 ÷ \$5,000 = **4.0%**. So the options market is pricing roughly a **±4% move** over election week, in or out of the money, regardless of direction.

Now decompose that 4.0% into baseline and event premium. Say baseline weekly vol with no event on the calendar would price the same straddle at about **\$120** — an implied move of about 2.4%, the index's ordinary weekly fidget. The *event premium* is therefore \$200 − \$120 = **\$80**. That \$80 is what you are paying purely for the election's binary: the market's price for the jump the result might cause. It is the entire object of the vol trade.

Trade the crush. Sell the \$200 straddle the day before the vote. The result comes in cleanly and the index settles at **\$5,090** — a **1.8% move**, smaller than the 4.0% the straddle priced. At expiry the call is worth \$5,090 − \$5,000 = **\$90** and the put expires worthless at \$0, so the straddle you sold pays out only \$90. Your profit is \$200 received − \$90 paid out = **\$110 per straddle**. On 10 straddles that is **\$1,100**, earned because the realized move (1.8%) came in below the implied move (4.0%) and the \$80 event premium decayed to nothing the instant the result was known. You did not predict the winner; you sold over-priced uncertainty and let the resolution collapse it.

The risk is the mirror image, and it is exactly the contested-result tail. If the result is a shock — a disputed count, a market-skeptic surprise — and the index moves **6%** (to \$5,300 or \$4,700), the straddle pays \$300, and you *lose* \$300 − \$200 = **\$100 per straddle**, or \$1,000 on the ten. That asymmetry — a capped \$110 gain against an uncapped loss that grows with the move — is why you never sell election vol naked. You sell it as a defined-risk *iron condor* (sell the near straddle, buy further-out wings to cap the loss), or you simply buy back protection above a threshold. The intuition: selling election vol is selling insurance against a big surprise; you collect the premium when the result is ordinary, and the whole game is structuring the position so you survive the rare result that is not.

## How the priced-in probability works — and where your edge is

We established that a prediction-market price *is* an implied probability. The practitioner uses this in two ways: to *weight the platform scenarios* (as in the HealthCo example, where the 50/50 split came from the priced probability) and to *find a vol or directional edge* when your probability differs from the market's.

The mechanics of a binary contract are worth nailing down because they make the edge computation exact. A contract that pays **\$1** if an outcome occurs and **\$0** otherwise, trading at price *p*, has an expected value equal to your true probability times the payoff: if you believe the true probability is *p_true*, the contract is worth *p_true* dollars to you. You have positive expected value buying it when *p_true* exceeds *p* (you think the outcome is more likely than the price implies) and selling it when *p_true* is below *p*. The *edge per contract* is therefore (*p_true* − *p*) dollars before costs — and costs (the platform fee, the bid-ask spread, the cost of capital tied up until resolution) are the entire difference between a paper edge and a real one. A 4-cent paper edge that looks like free money on a spreadsheet is often a break-even trade once frictions land, which is why prediction-market "arbitrage" is far rarer than retail commentary assumes.

This is identical in spirit to sizing any probabilistic bet — the Kelly framework for how *much* to stake given an edge and odds is laid out in [the Kelly criterion and sequential betting](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews). The election-specific point is that your `p_true` has to *beat a market that already ate the polls*. That is a high bar, which is exactly why most of the edge in elections is in the *vol and the platform spread*, not in out-forecasting the binary.

#### Worked example: the priced-in probability and the edge if your estimate differs

A prediction-market contract on "Outcome A wins" trades at **\$0.62**, implying a **62%** probability. After your own analysis — averaging the polls, then adjusting for a turnout factor you believe the market is underweighting — you estimate the true probability at **68%**.

Your edge from buying the contract at \$0.62 is (\$0.68 − \$0.62) = **\$0.06** per contract, or **6 cents on a 62-cent contract — about a 9.7% expected return** if you are right about your probability. Buy 1,000 contracts for 1,000 × \$0.62 = **\$620** of capital at risk. The expected gross edge is 1,000 × \$0.06 = **\$60** before costs. So far this looks like an easy yes.

Now subtract costs, which is where most of these "edges" die. Say the platform charges a **2% fee** on net winnings and the bid-ask spread costs you another **1 cent** per contract on entry. The spread alone is 1,000 × \$0.01 = **\$10** off the top. The fee bites your gross winnings in the win state, and the cost of capital — \$620 tied up for weeks until resolution, unable to earn the risk-free rate elsewhere — trims more. A \$60 paper edge can easily shrink to \$40 or less after frictions. Still positive, but now ask the real question: is \$40 of expected value on \$620 at risk — with the *full \$620 lost if you are wrong* — worth the variance of a one-shot bet? The distribution is brutal: you make about 38 cents per winning contract or lose 62 cents per losing one, on a single binary outcome with no chance to average over many trials. Sizing this with a *fractional*-Kelly stake — never full Kelly, because your estimate of *p_true* itself carries error, and an over-estimated edge on a one-shot bet is how accounts blow up — is the disciplined answer. A common rule is to stake a quarter or less of the Kelly fraction precisely to absorb that estimation error.

The intuition: the edge is real only to the extent your probability genuinely differs from the price *and* survives fees, spread, and the cost of locked-up capital — and on a one-shot binary you must size tiny, because a wrong *p_true* is not a drawdown you can grind back over many trades, it is a clean total loss of the stake.

#### Worked example: an EM currency move on a market-friendly vs market-skeptic result

Take an emerging market whose currency trades at **25,000 local units per US dollar** going into a presidential election, with two outcomes the market reads very differently. Anchor everything in USD so the sizes are legible.

- **Market-friendly result:** the currency *strengthens* about **5%** to **23,800 per USD** as the country's risk premium falls — fewer local units buy a dollar, so the currency is worth more.
- **Market-skeptic result:** the currency *weakens* about **10%** to **27,500 per USD** as the risk premium rises.

Say you hold **\$1,000,000** of the country's local-currency government bonds, which means you are implicitly long the currency: to get your dollars back you must eventually convert local units to USD, so a weaker currency hurts you. Convert the exposure: at 25,000 per USD, \$1,000,000 buys 1,000,000 × 25,000 = **25.0 billion local units** of bond face. Hold the bond price fixed for a moment and watch only the currency leg.

- On the **friendly** result, those 25.0 billion local units convert at the stronger rate: 25,000,000,000 ÷ 23,800 = **\$1,050,420** — a gain of about **\$50,420** purely from the currency, *before* the bond price rises further as local yields fall (a second, reinforcing tailwind).
- On the **skeptic** result, the same 25.0 billion local units convert at the weaker rate: 25,000,000,000 ÷ 27,500 = **\$909,091** — a loss of about **\$90,909** from the currency alone, *compounded* by the bond price falling as local yields jump (a second, reinforcing headwind).

Notice the asymmetry, because it is the entire lesson. The skeptic-result loss (≈\$90.9k) is nearly **double** the friendly-result gain (≈\$50.4k) — both because the skeptic move is bigger (10% vs 5%) and because, on the bad outcome, the currency and bond losses *stack* in the same direction. That asymmetry is the whole reason to hedge rather than simply hold. Suppose the market prices the friendly outcome at 60%. Your expected currency profit-and-loss is 0.60 × \$50,420 − 0.40 × \$90,909 = \$30,252 − \$36,364 = **−\$6,112** — *negative* expected value even though the *more-likely* outcome is the good one, because the bad outcome hurts so much more per unit of probability. This is the trap that catches investors who reason "the good result is more likely, so I'm fine": probability-of-being-right is not the same as expected-value, and fat-tailed asymmetry can flip the sign. The rational response is to buy currency protection — an FX put on the local currency, or a partial forward hedge that locks part of the conversion rate — sized to the skeptic-result loss, accepting a known premium to cap the fat-tailed downside. The currency-management mechanics behind these managed-rate moves are covered in the macro series on [Vietnam's monetary policy and the dong](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).

The intuition: in an EM election the currency *is* the trade, the downside is fatter than the upside, and a positive most-likely outcome can still carry a negative expected value once you weight the asymmetric tail — so you hedge the tail, you do not hope it away.

## Why broad-index timing on elections usually fails

Everything above is *relative-value* and *vol* — deliberately, because the one thing that reliably does **not** work is timing the broad equity index on an election. The reasons are structural, and they are worth stating plainly because the temptation to "go to cash before the election" or "buy the market on the result" is enormous and almost always wrong.

First, the index outcome is dominated by the binary, which is a coin flip the market has already priced. You have no edge on the coin, and the index move on the result is the *surprise* relative to what was priced — which by construction you cannot forecast. Second, the platform effects that *are* analyzable largely *net out at the index level*: the energy tailwind under one platform is offset by the renewables headwind, the bank tailwind by the pharma headwind, and so on. The platform moves the *composition* of the index far more than its *level*. Third, the index reflects the long-run earnings power of the economy, which is shaped far more by the business cycle, productivity, and rates than by which party holds office — a point the partisan-portfolio data makes brutally.

The geopolitical-risk record reinforces the same lesson from a different angle. Most political and geopolitical risk spikes *mean-revert*: the index sells off on the fear and recovers as the fear fails to translate into a permanent change in cash flows.

![Geopolitical risk index spikes at major events and reverts toward its long-run mean of 100](/imgs/blogs/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar-8.png)

The chart shows the Caldara-Iacoviello Geopolitical Risk index, normalized so that 100 is the 1985–2019 average. Notice the pattern: huge spikes (512 around September 2001) collapse back toward the mean within quarters; even the 2022 invasion spike (277) faded to 140 by mid-2024. Political risk is overwhelmingly *transient* in its market impact. The few episodes that *persist* are the ones that change cash flows for real — a lasting tariff regime, a war that reroutes energy supply, a contested transfer of power that impairs the rule of law. For an election, that means: the *vol* around the date is real and tradeable, but the *directional index move* is usually noise that reverts, and betting the index on it is paying costs to flip a coin.

## Common misconceptions

Three myths cause more election-trading losses than any others. Each is corrected with a number or a specific mechanism.

### Myth 1: "One party is better for stocks"

This is the most expensive belief in election trading, and the data does not support it. Since 1945, the S&P 500's average calendar-year total return has been within a few percentage points of its long-run average of roughly **10%** under *both* major US parties — and the gap between them is statistically indistinguishable from noise once you account for the small number of administrations and the dominance of the business cycle. The single best predictor of equity returns over a presidential term is not the party; it is the *starting valuation and the path of the economy and rates*, neither of which any party controls cleanly.

Put concretely: if owning "the right party's portfolio" added even **2% per year** reliably, it would compound to a fortune over decades — and yet no systematic study finds a robust, tradeable partisan edge in *index* returns. What *does* differ is sector *composition* (energy vs renewables, banks vs utilities), which is why the platform-spread relative-value trade works while the "buy stocks because my side won" trade does not. The myth costs money because it tempts you into an undiversified, directional, leveraged-with-conviction index bet on a coin flip.

### Myth 2: "You should go to cash before an election"

The instinct to de-risk into a known uncertainty feels prudent, but for a long-term portfolio it is usually a wealth-destroying market-timing decision in disguise. Two costs bite. First, you give up *expected return* during the time you sit in cash — and elections cluster near year-end, a historically strong seasonal window, so the opportunity cost is real. Second, you face the *re-entry problem*: after the result, you have to decide when to get back in, and the post-result move is frequently a sharp relief rally (as in November 2016) that you miss while waiting for "clarity."

Quantify it. If equities return about **0.8% per month** on average and you sit in cash for the two months around an election "to be safe," you have forgone roughly **1.6%** of expected return for the *certainty* of avoiding a move you could not forecast anyway — and if the post-result rally is 5%, your timing decision cost you a multiple of what the protection was worth. The disciplined alternative is not to *sell the portfolio* but to *hedge the tail*: buy a defined amount of downside protection (puts or put spreads) sized to your risk budget, keep the core invested, and let the vol crush pay for part of the hedge by selling the over-priced near-dated premium against your longer-dated protection.

### Myth 3: "The polls are the price"

Traders see a poll showing 52% and a market priced at 60% and conclude the market is "wrong" — a free 8 points. It almost never is. **The market price already contains the polls, plus money, plus models, plus information you do not have.** Polls are a *noisy input* into the price, not the price itself, and they carry systematic biases (sampling, turnout assumptions, non-response) that the market has learned to adjust for. If a market consistently traded at the naive poll number, it would have been arbitraged to the more informative price long ago.

The number to remember: a prediction-market price is an *implied probability that has already eaten the polls*. Your edge is the gap between *your* well-reasoned probability — which must incorporate something the market is genuinely underweighting — and the *price*, net of fees and spread, as we computed in the worked example (a 6-cent gross edge that frictions can cut to 4 cents or less). Treating the poll as the fair value and the market as the misprice is exactly backwards, and it leads you to buy expensive contracts on the theory that they are cheap.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three patterns recur across elections, and recognizing them is most of the practitioner's read.

**The pre-election vol bid and post-election crush.** This is the most reliable pattern. In the weeks before a major contested election, the implied vol of options spanning the date climbs as the event premium accumulates — the term structure develops the hump we described, with the post-date expiry richest. The morning after a clean result, that premium collapses: implied vol on the relevant index can fall several points overnight as the binary resolves. The 2016 and 2020 US elections both showed pronounced near-dated vol bids into the date; the crush after a clean count is the mirror image. The trade is to be *short* the over-priced event premium going in (hedged), and to expect the crush to do the work on resolution.

**A policy-sensitive sector rotation on the result.** The index may barely move while *beneath the surface* the sectors rotate hard along the platform map. After the November 2016 US result, the market rallied but the *leadership* told the policy story: banks (lighter-regulation expectation), small-caps and domestic industrials (tax-cut and domestic-spending expectation), and defense outperformed, while bond-proxy and rate-sensitive sectors lagged as yields jumped. The level move was modest and arguable; the *rotation* was decisive and tradeable, and it is exactly what the platform-spread pair trade is built to capture.

**An EM currency move on the result.** In emerging markets the cleanest reaction is in the currency and local bonds. A result the market reads as fiscally credible and central-bank-respecting tends to rally the currency and compress local yields; a result read as the opposite weakens the currency and steepens or lifts yields. These moves are frequently several percent in a session — far larger than the developed-market equity wobble — because the result reprices the entire country's risk premium, not just a sector. The asymmetry we computed (the skeptic-result loss roughly double the friendly-result gain) is why EM election hedging is done through the currency and rates, not the equity index.

**The vol crush even when the index gaps.** Worth re-stating as its own pattern because it traps so many: the morning after a clean result the index can *gap* — a 1% or 2% open is common — and yet near-dated implied vol *falls hard at the same time*. A trader who bought the straddle "for protection into the election" and held it through the open frequently watches the position lose money on the vol crush even though the index moved, because the implied vol they paid for (the unknown) collapsed faster than the realized move (the gap) paid them back. The lesson is to be explicit about whether you are long the *event premium* (short it, and the crush pays you) or long *realized gamma* (own it only if you think the actual move will exceed what you paid, and plan to monetize it *into* the move, not after). Most retail "election hedges" are unwittingly the first dressed up as the second.

**The peaceful-transfer premium is usually invisible — until it isn't.** In a stable institutional setting, the assumption that power transfers peacefully and the rule of law holds is so ingrained that it carries *no visible premium* — it is priced at essentially 100% certainty, so it costs nothing and you never see it. That is exactly why a contested result is so violent when it appears: the market is not re-pricing from 90% to 80%, it is being forced to put a number on something it had treated as a certainty, and even a small probability on "the transfer is in doubt" can move the rule-of-law premium that underpins *every* asset in the jurisdiction. The 2000 US presidential result, resolved by the courts five weeks after the vote, is the developed-market reminder that the resolution date is not always election night; a string of disputed EM results are the harder reminders. You budget for this not by forecasting it — it is genuinely rare — but by never structuring a book that *requires* a clean, on-the-night resolution to survive.

## The playbook: trading the political calendar

Here is the payoff — the concrete process, the signals, the positions, and the invalidation. It is built entirely on the binary-vs-continuous split: trade the vol and the platform spread, never the coin.

**1. Build the election calendar.** Maintain a forward calendar of every relevant election with its *date* (the resolution point), the *outcomes* on the ballot, and the *platform spread* on the four policy levers (tax, regulation, spending, trade). Mark which of your holdings and which liquid sectors are platform-sensitive, and in which direction. This is the political-calendar analog of the [regulatory calendar](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock) — the same discipline of reading scheduled policy events like an earnings calendar, extended to elections and the unscheduled-shock overlay covered in [geopolitics, elections, and unscheduled shocks](/blog/trading/event-trading/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks).

**2. Trade the vol, not the direction.** Read the term structure into the date. If the event premium is rich relative to the platform spread's implied move, *sell* near-dated event vol as a defined-risk structure (an iron condor or a put-spread/call-spread combination), and let the crush pay you on resolution. If it is cheap relative to a wide platform spread, *buy* the straddle. Never sell election vol naked — the rare shock result is precisely the move that ruins an unhedged short-vol book. For the full mechanics of structuring these, see [options theory](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/options-theory).

**3. Position the platform spread, hedged.** Build the relative-value book from the sector map: long the names a given platform helps, short the names it hurts (which are the other platform's winners), sized to equal dollar beta so the pair is roughly index-neutral. You are now long the *repricing of the platform spread* and flat the binary. Energy-vs-renewables and banks-vs-bond-proxies are the cleanest expressions because their platform sensitivities point in opposite directions.

**4. Size for the contested-result tail.** Most elections resolve cleanly the night of the vote — concession by morning, vol crush, on with life. But a small probability of a *contested or delayed* result is the fat left tail that keeps vol bid past the date and, in the extreme, threatens the peaceful transfer of power and with it the rule-of-law premium baked into a country's assets.

![Contested or delayed election result is the fat tail that keeps volatility bid past the date](/imgs/blogs/elections-and-political-risk-trading-the-political-calendar-6.png)

The tree above is the scenario you must budget for. The base case — a clean result, a quick concession, a peaceful transfer — leads to the vol crush and a fading of regime risk (green). The tail — a contested count, recounts, court challenges, a transfer in genuine doubt — keeps vol *bid* and, in an EM or a fragile institutional setting, opens a currency and credit tail (red). You size for this by *not* selling all your event vol (keep some long convexity through the date), by holding the EM currency hedge through the *full* resolution window rather than lifting it on election night, and by capping any short-vol structure so the tail cannot blow up the book. The 2000 US result (resolved by the courts weeks later) and various EM disputed results are the historical reminders that "the night of the vote" is not always when the uncertainty ends.

**5. Know what invalidates the view.** The platform-spread trade is invalidated if the *spread itself* turns out to be priced correctly — if the post-result rotation matches what the pair already implied, you make nothing and pay the costs, so the entry requires a genuine mispricing of sensitivity, not just a directional hunch. The short-event-vol trade is invalidated by a *shock result or a contested count* that delivers a move larger than the premium you sold — which is why it must be defined-risk. The EM currency hedge is invalidated (in the happy sense) by a clean market-friendly result, in which case you lose the hedge premium and keep the bond gains — an acceptable cost of insurance. And the entire edifice is invalidated if you drift from *trading the continuous* into *betting the binary*: the moment you find yourself with a large, directional, unhedged position whose P&L depends on who wins, you have left the analyzable trade and started flipping a coin with leverage.

The through-line, one more time: the date is known, the outcome is a coin flip, and the policy platforms behind each outcome are analyzable. Sell the over-priced uncertainty around the known date, position the analyzable platform spread hedged across the coin, budget honestly for the contested-result tail, and never confuse the vol-and-spread trade — where the edge lives — with the directional index bet on the winner, where it does not. For how this election-risk module plugs into a whole portfolio's tail budget, see [position sizing for tail and political risk](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/position-sizing-for-tail-and-political-risk), and for the broader channel through which all geopolitical risk prices into a risk premium, see [geopolitics as a market factor: the risk-premium channel](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/geopolitics-as-a-market-factor-the-risk-premium-channel).
