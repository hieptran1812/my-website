> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Different rule-makers produce different *instruments* — a statute, an agency rule, a piece of guidance, a court ruling, an executive order, a tariff, a sanction — and each instrument hits markets on its own *timeline*, with its own *reversibility*, and its own *durability*. Reading **which body acts** tells you how fast, how big, and how reversible the repricing will be.
>
> - A **statute** is slow (1–3 years) but enormous and durable; an **executive order** or a **sanction** can move a price the *same day* but is just as easily undone.
> - In 2024 the Supreme Court ended **Chevron deference** (the *Loper Bright* decision), shifting the power to read ambiguous laws from agencies to judges — which raised litigation risk for every contested agency rule.
> - The single fact to remember: a **court vacating a rule** can re-rate a whole sector overnight, while the same rule took the agency 9–18 months to write. The repricing speed is set by the body, not the topic.

On the morning of **June 28, 2024**, traders who follow the Supreme Court were watching one docket entry. The Court handed down *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, and in a single opinion it overturned a 40-year-old doctrine called *Chevron deference* — the rule that, when a law was ambiguous, courts should defer to the federal agency's reasonable interpretation. Overnight, the legal ground under thousands of agency regulations shifted. The companies most exposed to contested rules — drug makers facing FDA pricing rules, banks facing capital rules, energy firms facing EPA rules — were suddenly easier to challenge in court. No statute had changed. No agency had acted. A *court* had spoken, and that was enough to move the risk premium on entire sectors.

Three days earlier, a different kind of rule-maker had moved a different kind of market. The **Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC)** — a small office inside the U.S. Treasury — added a batch of names to its sanctions list. For the firms named, the effect was instant: U.S. counterparties had to stop dealing with them by end of day, dollar-clearing froze, and any publicly traded exposure gapped lower before lunch. One office, one list, one afternoon.

Same week, two market-moving rule-changes — and they could not have looked more different. One came from the highest court in the country and took years of litigation to arrive; the other came from a Treasury sub-office and took an afternoon. If you want to read law and policy as a market force, the first thing you have to learn is *who is allowed to write which kind of rule* — because the body that acts is the single best predictor of how fast the price will move, how big the move will be, and how likely it is to reverse. That is the map this post draws.

## Foundations: the institutional map and the instruments it produces

Let's build this from absolute zero, assuming you've never opened a law book or a regulatory filing. Everything that "moves markets through the rules" comes from one of **four** kinds of rule-maker, and each produces a characteristic *instrument*. An instrument is just the formal document or action that carries the rule. Think of the rule-maker as a factory and the instrument as the product that rolls off its line.

**1. Legislatures — the people who pass laws.** In the United States this is **Congress** (the House plus the Senate); in other countries it's the parliament, the National Assembly, the Diet. Their product is a **statute** — an actual law, passed by a vote and signed by the head of state. Statutes are the heaviest instrument in the system: they create agencies, set tax rates, authorize spending, and define what is and isn't a crime. The legislature also holds the **power of the purse** — the authority to tax and to appropriate (allocate) government money. When you hear "Congress passed a \$370 billion clean-energy bill," that's a statute exercising the spending power, and it redirects real capital.

The catch: statutes are *slow*. A bill has to pass two chambers, survive committee, clear procedural hurdles (in the U.S. Senate, often a 60-vote threshold to end debate), and get signed. Major laws routinely take one to three years from first draft to enactment, and many die. So a statute is rare, huge, and — once passed — extremely hard to undo, because undoing it requires another statute.

**2. Agencies — the specialists who write the detailed rules.** Congress can't possibly write every detail of how the stock market, the banking system, or the drug-approval process should work. So it creates **agencies** and delegates authority to them. The alphabet soup you've seen — **SEC** (Securities and Exchange Commission, polices stocks and disclosure), the **Fed** (Federal Reserve, the central bank and a bank regulator), **FTC** and **DOJ Antitrust** (competition), **OFAC** (sanctions), **FDA** (drugs and food), **CFTC** (commodities and derivatives), **EPA** (environment), **CFPB** (consumer finance) — these are all agencies. Their product is an **agency rule** (also called a *regulation*), and they make it through a defined process called **notice-and-comment rulemaking**:

1. The agency publishes a **Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM)** — "here's the rule we're thinking about."
2. A **comment period** (often 30–60 days) opens; anyone — companies, lobbyists, the public — can submit written feedback.
3. The agency reads the comments and publishes a **final rule** with an **effective date**.
4. Affected parties can **sue** to block or overturn the rule in court.

This whole cycle usually takes 9–18 months from proposal to final rule. So an agency rule is *faster than a statute but slower than a court ruling*, and — crucially — it sits on top of a statute. The agency can only do what its **enabling statute** ("the law that created it and told it what it may regulate") allows. That dependency is the seed of the *Chevron* fight we'll get to.

It's worth knowing that "agency" is not one thing — different agencies wield different *tools*, and the tool determines the speed. Some agency power is *slow rulemaking* (the SEC writing a disclosure rule, the EPA writing an emissions standard); some is *enforcement* (the SEC or DOJ filing a lawsuit against a specific firm, which can reprice that firm in a day); and some is a *list* (OFAC adding a name to the sanctions list, effective the moment it posts). The same letterhead can produce a glacial rule and a same-day list. So when you read "the SEC acted," your first question is *which power* — a proposed rule (slow, sector-wide, two-sided) or an enforcement action (fast, firm-specific, often a one-way hit). The brief institutional vocabulary you need:

- **SEC** — disclosure, registration, insider-trading enforcement, market-structure rules. Tools: rulemaking *and* enforcement suits.
- **Fed** — bank supervision, capital and liquidity rules, the policy rate, emergency lending. Tools: rulemaking, supervisory guidance, the discount window.
- **FTC and DOJ Antitrust** — competition. The FTC is an independent agency; DOJ Antitrust is part of the executive's Justice Department. Tools: merger review, lawsuits to block deals, consent decrees.
- **OFAC** — sanctions, inside Treasury. Tool: the SDN list (same-day designations).
- **FDA** — drug and device approval. Tool: the approval decision (a scheduled binary catalyst on a known date).
- **CFTC** — commodities and derivatives. Tools: rulemaking, position limits, fraud enforcement.
- **EPA** — environment. Tool: emissions and pollution rules (slow, litigation-heavy).
- **CFPB** — consumer finance. Tool: rulemaking and enforcement on lending and fees.

There is a second legislative power that quietly moves markets and that beginners miss: the **budget and appropriations power**. Even when an agency *can* legally act, it can only act with money and authority that Congress appropriates. Congress can shape — or starve — a policy not by passing a new statute but by *funding* or *defunding* an agency's enforcement. A rider in an appropriations bill that bars an agency from "using any funds to enforce rule X" is a back-door repeal that never touches the rule itself. So when you map a theme to a body, remember that the legislature holds *two* levers: writing the statute (the rule) and writing the check (whether anyone enforces it).

**3. Central banks — the rule-makers for the price of money.** A central bank like the **Federal Reserve** is a special kind of agency. Its job is set by a statute (in the U.S., the *Federal Reserve Act*) that gives it a **mandate** — the legal goals it must pursue. The Fed's mandate is *dual*: maximum employment and stable prices. The instrument it produces day-to-day is a **policy decision** (raise or cut the policy interest rate), and in a crisis it can reach for **emergency powers** — Section 13(3) of the Federal Reserve Act lets it lend in "unusual and exigent circumstances." The central bank's legal independence — its insulation from the politicians who'd love to cut rates before an election — is what makes its signals credible. We won't re-derive monetary mechanics here; for that, see the deep dive on [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates). The point for this post is narrower: the central bank's *legal mandate* constrains what policy it can deliver, and reading that mandate tells you the boundary of what it will do.

**4. Courts — the rule-makers who decide what the rules mean.** Courts don't write laws; they *interpret* them and decide whether an action was lawful. In the U.S. federal system, a case starts in a **district court** (the trial court), can be appealed to a **circuit court** (the courts of appeals, organized into geographic circuits), and finally to the **Supreme Court (SCOTUS)**, which takes only a tiny fraction of cases and whose word is final. The court's product is a **ruling** — a decision that can *uphold* a rule, *strike it down* (often phrased as **vacate** — wipe it off the books), or reinterpret a statute so that it means something different than everyone assumed. A ruling is binding precedent: it doesn't just decide the case in front of the judge, it changes the rules for everyone in similar situations.

**5. The executive — the head of government, acting alone.** This is the President (or prime minister). The executive produces two market-relevant instruments. An **executive order (EO)** is a directive to the federal government — "agencies, do X" — that the President can sign alone, with no vote. It's fast (same-day) but fragile: the next President can revoke it with another EO. And under specific delegated statutes, the executive can wield **emergency economic powers** — most importantly the **International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)**, which (after the President declares a national emergency) authorizes broad sanctions, asset freezes, and, increasingly, tariffs. A **tariff proclamation** (a tax on imports) and a **sanction designation** (adding a person/entity to a blocked list) are the executive's sharpest market weapons because they can hit the *same day*.

The reason the executive can move so fast on tariffs and sanctions — when a *tax* (which is also "money the government takes") requires Congress and years — is a quirk worth understanding, because it explains the whole speed asymmetry. The Constitution gives the *taxing* and *tariff* power to Congress. But over the 20th century Congress passed statutes *delegating* slices of that power to the President for specific purposes: **Section 232** of the Trade Expansion Act (tariffs for national-security reasons), **Section 301** of the Trade Act (tariffs to retaliate against unfair foreign trade practices), and **IEEPA** (broad economic measures during a declared emergency). Each delegation comes with conditions, but once those conditions are met, the President can act *alone and immediately*. So the same economic effect — a tax on a sector — can travel two completely different roads: the slow road (Congress writes a statute) or the fast road (the President invokes a delegated trade or emergency power). The road determines the speed, the reversibility, and how vulnerable the action is to a court challenge. A tariff under a delegated statute is fast but can be litigated (does the situation actually meet the statute's "national security" or "emergency" condition?); a tax in a statute is slow but bulletproof once passed. When you see a tariff headline, your first question is *which authority* — because that tells you both how fast it bites and how easily a court could unwind it.

This is also why **sanctions** are the single fastest instrument in the system. A designation under IEEPA doesn't need a rule, a vote, or a comment period — Treasury adds a name to the SDN list, and the legal effect (U.S. persons must freeze and stop dealing) is immediate. The chokepoint that makes it bite is the **dollar-clearing system**: because most global trade settles in dollars and dollar payments route through U.S. banks, an OFAC designation can cut a target off from the global financial plumbing in an afternoon. Same-day power, global reach, no process — that combination is unique to the sanctions tool.

Here is the vocabulary distilled, because the rest of the post depends on you keeping these straight:

- **Statute** — a law, passed by the legislature. Slow, big, durable.
- **Agency rule (regulation)** — detailed rules written by an agency under a statute, via notice-and-comment. Medium speed, medium durability.
- **Guidance** — an agency's informal statement of how it interprets a rule (a bulletin, an FAQ, a no-action letter). Fast and soft; it isn't binding law, but markets watch it because it signals enforcement intent.
- **Ruling** — a court decision. Can be overnight; binding precedent; durable.
- **Executive order** — a Presidential directive. Same-day; easily reversed by the next President.
- **Tariff / sanction** — executive actions (often under IEEPA or trade statutes). Same-day; the fastest instruments in the system.

Once you have those six instruments straight, the whole game reduces to a single question: *which factory made this rule?* Because the factory determines the product's speed, reversibility, and durability — and those three properties determine how the market reprices.

## The three properties that matter: speed, reversibility, durability

Every market-relevant rule has three dimensions a trader cares about, and they trade off against each other in a way that, once you see it, you can't unsee.

- **Speed to market** — how long from "the body decides" to "the rule takes effect." A sanction is same-day; a statute is years.
- **Reversibility** — how easily the rule can be undone. An executive order dies with the next election; a statute requires a whole new statute to repeal.
- **Durability** — how long the rule, once in place, actually lasts. A court precedent or a statute can shape an industry for decades; a piece of guidance can vanish in a memo.

The deep pattern is that **speed and durability usually trade off**. The instruments that hit fastest — EOs, sanctions, guidance — are precisely the ones that are easiest to reverse and least durable. The instruments that take longest — statutes, hard-fought court precedents — are the ones that last. This isn't a coincidence; it's baked into the design. The framers of most legal systems made it *hard* to pass a law precisely so that, once passed, it would be *stable*. Fast power is rented; slow power is owned.

![Matrix of speed, reversibility, and durability for six legal instruments](/imgs/blogs/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts-2.png)

Why does a trader care about all three and not just speed? Because they answer three different trading questions:

- **Speed** answers *when do I need to be positioned?* If the instrument is a same-day sanction, you can't wait for the announcement — you have to be positioned for the *risk* in advance, because there's no time to react. If it's an 18-month rule, you have a long runway to position into the drift.
- **Reversibility** answers *how much should I size, and how long should I hold?* A repricing driven by an easily reversed EO is a *rentable* move — you might fade it, because the next administration can undo it. A repricing driven by a statute is a *structural* move you can hold.
- **Durability** answers *is this a trade or an investment?* A durable rule changes the long-run earnings power of a company; a soft, reversible one changes only the near-term headline.

Keep that triple in mind. It's the analytic core of everything below.

## How an instrument actually becomes a price

Here's the part beginners skip: a rule-change doesn't move a stock by magic. It moves it through a *finite chain* of cause and effect, and the chain is the same regardless of which body acted. Once you can name each link, you can estimate the size of the move.

A stock (or bond, or currency) is worth the present value of its expected future cash flows, discounted at a rate that reflects risk. Anything that changes either the **expected cash flows** or the **discount rate (risk premium)** changes the price. A rule-change feeds into *both* legs:

![How a rule-makers action flows through expectations and cash flows to reprice an asset](/imgs/blogs/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts-3.png)

1. **A body acts** — files an NPRM, casts a vote, signs an EO, hands down a ruling, publishes a designation.
2. **The market reads the signal.** Sophisticated investors don't wait for the rule to *bite*; they update on the *information* the action reveals about the rule's eventual probability and shape.
3. **The expected path of the rule shifts.** A proposed rule that the market thinks is 60% likely to become final, and 70% likely to survive litigation, has an *expected* impact of roughly 0.6 × 0.7 = 42% of its full impact — and the price already reflects that fraction *before the rule exists*.
4. **Expected cash flows or margins move.** A tariff raises a cost; a drug-pricing rule lowers a revenue line; a capital rule forces a bank to hold more equity, lowering its return on equity.
5. **The discount rate / risk premium moves.** Even if the central estimate of cash flows is unchanged, *uncertainty* about the rules raises the risk premium investors demand — regulated names trade at a discount precisely because the rules can change under them.
6. **The asset reprices — usually before the rule bites.** This is the punchline: markets discount the *expected* rule into the price ahead of time, so by the time the rule takes effect, much of the move has already happened. The trade is in the *anticipation*, not the event.

This chain is the spine of the whole series — see [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain) for the master version. For now, the key insight is that the *speed* of step 6 is governed by the *speed* of step 1, which is governed by *which body acted*. A same-day sanction collapses the whole chain into an afternoon. An 18-month rule stretches it across a year and a half of tradeable drift.

#### Worked example: a statute, an agency rule, and an executive order hit the same sector

Imagine three different rule-changes, each landing on the same hypothetical regulated utility, **Acme Power**, trading at \$100 with annual earnings of \$8 per share (a P/E of 12.5). Each change is designed to cut earnings by the same \$1 per share (a 12.5% earnings hit) — but they arrive via different bodies, so the market prices them very differently.

- **Path A — a statute.** Congress passes a law (after an 18-month fight) that imposes a \$1/share carbon levy on Acme, permanently. Because it's a durable, hard-to-reverse statute, the market treats the \$1 hit as *permanent* and re-rates the full earnings power. New fair value ≈ 12.5 × (\$8 − \$1) = **\$87.50**, a 12.5% drop. The market also *front-runs* it: as passage looks 80% likely, the stock has already drifted to roughly \$100 − 0.8 × \$12.50 = **\$90** before the vote.
- **Path B — an agency rule.** The EPA proposes a regulation with the same \$1/share effect, but it faces a 50% chance of surviving litigation under the post-*Chevron* legal regime. Expected impact = 0.5 × \$12.50 = \$6.25. The stock trades down to about **\$93.75** on the proposal and stays in limbo until the court decides — a wide, slow, two-sided bet.
- **Path C — an executive order.** The President signs an EO directing agencies to penalize Acme's emissions, same \$1/share effect. But it's an EO — reversible by the next President in ~3 years. The market discounts the hit only over the expected ~3-year life and assigns, say, a 40% chance it survives politically. Expected impact ≈ 0.4 × \$12.50 = \$5.00, landing the stock near **\$95** — and traders may *fade* it, betting on reversal.

Same \$1 earnings hit; three different prices (\$87.50 vs \$93.75 vs \$95) — because the *durability and reversibility* of the instrument, set by which body acted, scale the repricing.

## The signal and the bite: why the rule moves the price before it exists

The single most important — and least intuitive — idea in this whole subject is the gap between when a rule *signals* and when it *bites*. The **bite** is the moment the rule actually costs a company money: the tariff is collected, the tax is paid, the capital is held, the drug price is capped. The **signal** is everything *before* that moment that tells the market the bite is coming: a politician's speech, a draft bill, an NPRM, a court granting certiorari, an agency hiring enforcement staff. Markets reprice on the *signal*, not the bite, because the price of a stock is the present value of *expected* future cash flows — and expectations move the instant the information arrives.

This is why a beginner watching the news is always late. The headline "new rule takes effect today" is the *bite*, and by then the market has been discounting the rule for months. The professional is trading the *signal* path: estimating the probability that the rule arrives and survives, multiplying by the full impact, and positioning before the crowd. The whole edge lives in being earlier and more accurate on the *probability*, not in reacting faster to the event.

There's a useful way to make this precise. Treat the eventual rule as having a **full impact** \$F (the price move if the rule arrives at full force and is permanent), and assign a **probability** p that it arrives and survives in roughly that form. The price *today* reflects p × \$F. As new signals arrive, p moves, and the price moves with it — even though \$F (the rule's ultimate effect) hasn't changed at all. So three things move a regulated stock: a change in \$F (the rule got bigger or smaller), a change in p (it got more or less likely), or a change in the *risk premium* (the market got more or less willing to hold the uncertainty). A skilled reader of the institutional map is constantly updating p, because p is where most of the action is and where the body-watching pays off — an NPRM raises p off zero; a supportive comment period nudges it up; a stay from a court knocks it down; a final ruling collapses it to 0 or 1.

#### Worked example: positioning for the comment-to-final-rule lag with a probability update

Put numbers on it. A bank, **TrustBank**, trades at \$40 with \$3.20/share of earnings (P/E 12.5). The Fed proposes a capital rule that, if finalized at full strength, would force TrustBank to hold more equity and cut earnings by \$0.40/share — a full impact of \$F = 12.5 × \$0.40 = **\$5.00** of price.

On the day of the NPRM, the market guesses the rule arrives at full strength with probability p = 0.50, so it prices in 0.50 × \$5.00 = \$2.50 of damage: TrustBank trades to **\$37.50**. You read the comment docket and the political signals and conclude the Fed is likely to *soften* the rule in finalization (heavy bank pushback, a sympathetic political environment), cutting both \$F and p. You re-estimate \$F at \$3.00 (a weaker rule) and p at 0.40, so expected damage = 0.40 × \$3.00 = \$1.20, implying fair value ≈ **\$38.80**.

Your edge is the **\$38.80 − \$37.50 = \$1.30/share** between your estimate and the market's, captured as the final rule confirms the softening over the ~12-month lag. Note what you traded: not the rule, but the *trajectory of p and \$F* that the public process let you read early. The body (the Fed) told you which docket to watch; the process told you the timeline; the comments told you the probability.

## A single court ruling can reprice a whole sector overnight

The most dramatic version of "the body sets the speed" is a court. An agency might spend two years writing a rule; a court can erase it in a morning. And because a court ruling is *binding precedent*, it doesn't reprice one company — it reprices every company that relied on, or feared, that rule.

The mechanism is worth slowing down on. When a court **vacates** a rule, three things happen at once. First, the direct cost or revenue impact the rule would have imposed simply vanishes from the affected companies' forward earnings. Second, the *uncertainty* that had been weighing on the sector lifts — so the risk premium falls and the multiple expands. Third, every *other* contested rule in that legal family becomes more vulnerable, because the ruling sets precedent. So a single decision can re-rate not just the companies in the case but a whole cluster of regulated industries.

This is why legal-event-driven funds staff lawyers next to traders. They're not reading the law for its own sake; they're estimating, *before the decision*, the probability the court rules each way and the price impact of each outcome — exactly the binary-event math we'll do below.

Two pieces of court mechanics make these events especially sharp, and you should know them because they change the *timing* of the trade. The first is **standing and ripeness** — a court won't even hear a challenge unless the right party (someone actually harmed) sues at the right time (when the rule is real enough to cause harm). That's why the litigation clock often starts only *after* a rule is finalized, not when it's proposed: until the rule exists, there's nothing concrete to challenge. The practical effect is that the *biggest* legal-uncertainty discount on a stock often sits in the window *between* the final rule and the court's decision — a window you can see coming on the calendar.

The second is **nationwide vacatur** and the **stay**. When a court vacates a rule, it usually wipes it off the books *for everyone*, not just the plaintiff — which is why one circuit-court panel can reprice an entire industry. And before the final decision, a court can issue a **stay** (a temporary pause) or a **preliminary injunction** (a temporary block) that suspends the rule while the case proceeds. A stay is itself a tradeable event: it doesn't decide the case, but it signals the court thinks the challenger is likely to win, so the market reprices toward the rule being struck down well before the final ruling. Reading the *intermediate* court actions — the stay, the injunction, the oral-argument questioning — is how event traders update their probability estimate before the gavel falls.

#### Worked example: a court vacates a rule and re-rates the affected stocks

Suppose the FDA finalizes a drug-pricing rule that the market expects will knock \$2 per share off **PharmaCo**, currently trading at \$120 with \$10/share of earnings (P/E 12). At the moment the rule is final, the market prices in a 50% chance the rule survives an industry lawsuit, so the expected hit is 0.5 × (12 × \$2) = 0.5 × \$24 = \$12 — the stock sits at about **\$108** (\$120 − \$12) while the case is pending.

Now the circuit court **vacates** the rule. Two things move:

1. The \$2/share hit disappears from forward earnings — fair value on cash flows alone returns toward 12 × \$10 = **\$120**.
2. The lifted uncertainty lets the multiple expand modestly, say from 12 to 12.3 as the risk premium falls — adding 0.3 × \$10 = **\$3**.

The stock gaps from \$108 to roughly **\$123** — a **+14%** move in a morning, none of which required a single dollar of actual earnings to change yet. The whole move is the market re-rating *expected* cash flows and the *risk premium*. And the broader pharma group, which had been discounting similar rules, ticks up in sympathy because the precedent makes the next pricing rule easier to challenge.

The lesson: a court is the only body that can deliver a *large, durable* repricing at *same-day speed*. Statutes are large and durable but slow; EOs are fast but small and reversible. A vacated rule is the rare combination — which is exactly why these events are so violent.

## The Chevron earthquake: who gets to decide what an ambiguous law means

To understand why 2024 was a genuine regime change for regulated sectors, you need one more concept: what happens when a statute is *ambiguous*.

Congress writes laws in general language ("the agency shall ensure adequate competition," "the agency may regulate pollutants"). Inevitably the words don't settle every case. So who decides what the ambiguous words *mean* when an agency writes a detailed rule based on them — the agency, or the courts? For 40 years, the answer was **Chevron deference**, named after the 1984 case *Chevron v. NRDC*. The doctrine had two steps: (1) if the statute clearly answers the question, that controls; (2) if the statute is *ambiguous*, the court must **defer** to the agency's *reasonable* interpretation, even if the judge would have read it differently. In practice this tilted the field toward agencies — if a regulation was a reasonable reading of an ambiguous law, courts usually let it stand.

In **June 2024**, *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* overturned *Chevron*. Now, when a statute is ambiguous, *courts* decide the best reading themselves; the agency's view gets respect but not automatic deference. The practical effect: agency rules built on ambiguous statutory authority became substantially easier to challenge and overturn in court.

![Before and after Loper Bright, who resolves an ambiguous statute shifts from agency to court](/imgs/blogs/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts-7.png)

Two related doctrines reinforce this. The **major questions doctrine** (sharpened in *West Virginia v. EPA*, 2022) says that for decisions of vast economic or political significance, an agency needs *clear* congressional authorization — it can't infer huge new powers from a vague old statute. Together, *Loper Bright* plus major questions mean agencies now operate on a shorter leash: a contested rule of real economic weight faces a higher chance of being struck down.

For investors, this matters in a precise way: it **raised the litigation risk on every economically significant agency rule**, which raises the discount applied to companies that *benefit* from such rules and lowers the discount on companies that are *threatened* by them (because the threat is now more likely to be litigated away). The instrument's *durability* — that middle column of our matrix — got marked down across the board for agency rules. It didn't touch statutes (Congress's own words) or sanctions (a different legal track), which is exactly why knowing the instrument matters: the *Chevron* change repriced the *agency-rule* row of the matrix and left the others alone.

#### Worked example: timeline arbitrage on the comment-to-final-rule lag

Here's a trade the institutional map makes visible. The notice-and-comment process is *public and dated*: you can see the NPRM, count the comment days, and estimate the final-rule date. That predictable lag is tradeable.

The CFTC proposes a rule (NPRM published) that, if finalized, would *help* **ClearCo**, an exchange operator at \$50/share with \$4/share earnings (P/E 12.5). The full benefit if finalized and upheld: +\$1/share of earnings, worth 12.5 × \$1 = **\$12.50** in fair value. On the day of the NPRM, the market prices in a modest 30% chance of the rule arriving and surviving: expected lift = 0.30 × \$12.50 = \$3.75, so ClearCo trades to about **\$53.75**.

You judge that the comment period is overwhelmingly supportive (you read the public docket — most major comments back the rule) and that, post-*Loper Bright*, this particular rule rests on *clear* statutory authority, so litigation risk is low. You re-estimate the probability of arrival-and-survival at 70%. Expected lift = 0.70 × \$12.50 = \$8.75, implying fair value ≈ **\$58.75**.

Your edge is the **\$58.75 − \$53.75 = \$5/share** gap between your probability estimate and the market's, captured over the ~12-month comment-to-final lag. Position size scales with your confidence in the probability *and* in the durability (a clear-authority rule is more durable post-*Chevron*, so you can hold it). You're not trading the rule — you're trading the *clock and the probability* that the institutional process makes legible.

## Jurisdiction overlap and turf wars: when two bodies claim the same asset

The map gets messier — and more tradeable — when *more than one* body claims authority over the same thing. The cleanest live example is crypto.

Is a given crypto token a **security** (in which case the SEC regulates it, and it must register and disclose) or a **commodity** (in which case the CFTC regulates it, with a lighter touch)? The SEC applies the **Howey test** — a Supreme Court standard from 1946 that asks, roughly, whether buyers invested money in a common enterprise expecting profits from others' efforts — and has brought enforcement actions arguing many tokens are unregistered securities. The CFTC, meanwhile, has long called Bitcoin and Ether commodities. The same token can also trip **FinCEN** (anti-money-laundering registration) and the **IRS** (taxed as property). Four bodies, one asset, no settled answer.

![One crypto token claimed by SEC CFTC FinCEN and IRS creating overlapping legal risk](/imgs/blogs/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts-8.png)

That unresolved overlap is *itself* a priced risk. When jurisdiction is contested, every market participant has to provision for the possibility that the *strictest* claimant wins — so the asset trades at a discount for legal uncertainty, and a single clarifying event (a court ruling on whether a token is a security, a statute that assigns jurisdiction, an agency dropping a case) can release that discount in a jump. We'll spend a whole post on the [crypto SEC-vs-CFTC turf war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/crypto-regulation-securities-vs-commodities-and-the-turf-war); the point here is structural. Turf wars are common — SEC vs CFTC on crypto and on certain derivatives, FTC vs DOJ on which antitrust cases to take, federal vs state regulators on insurance and banking. Wherever two bodies overlap, watch for the event that *resolves* the overlap, because that's where the discount unwinds.

## When bodies hand off to each other: the relay

So far we've treated each rule-maker in isolation, but in practice they form a *relay*, and the handoffs are where some of the cleanest trades live. A theme rarely stays with one body. Watch how a single policy can pass through all four:

A legislature passes a *statute* that creates a new program and tells an agency to "implement it by rule." The agency spends a year writing the *rule* through notice-and-comment, issuing some *guidance* along the way to signal its thinking. Affected companies *sue*, and a *court* eventually decides whether the rule fit the statute. Separately, a new President may issue an *executive order* directing the agency to reinterpret or pause the rule. The same underlying policy bounced from Congress → agency → courts → executive — and at each handoff, the *speed and reversibility* of the controlling instrument changed, which means the *character* of the trade changed too.

The practical skill is to ask, at any moment, *which body currently holds the pen* — because that's the body whose next action will move the price. When a rule is in the comment period, the *agency* holds the pen; watch the docket. When it's been finalized and sued, the *court* holds the pen; watch the litigation calendar. When a new administration takes office, the *executive* may grab the pen back via EO; watch the transition. A position that made sense while the agency held the pen (trading the comment-period drift) may need to be restructured the moment the court takes over (now it's a binary). Mis-reading the handoff — continuing to trade the drift after the case has gone to a court that will resolve it overnight — is a classic way to be on the wrong side of a gap.

This relay also explains why *reversal risk* is not constant. An EO can be undone the day a new President takes office; a rule can be rewritten by the next agency head; a piece of guidance can be withdrawn in a memo. But a statute requires a whole new statute, and a Supreme Court precedent requires either a new statute that changes the underlying law or the Court itself reversing course — both rare. So when you size a position on a rule-driven move, the durability question is really "how many bodies, and how much process, stand between today's rule and its reversal?" The more bodies and the more process, the more durable the move and the more you can hold it as an investment rather than rent it as a trade.

## How to know which body to watch for a given theme

This is the practical skill the whole post builds toward. Given an investment theme, *which rule-maker holds the pen?* Get this right and you know which document to read, which calendar to watch, and how fast the move will come. Get it wrong and you watch the wrong feed while the price moves on a different signal.

The mapping is surprisingly stable:

- **Disclosure, insider trading, market structure, stock listings** → the **SEC**. Watch its rulemaking docket and enforcement actions.
- **Interest rates, bank liquidity, money-market plumbing** → the **Fed** and other bank regulators. Watch FOMC meetings and supervisory guidance.
- **Mergers and big-tech competition** → **DOJ Antitrust** and the **FTC**. Watch HSR filings, complaints, and consent decrees.
- **Tariffs and trade** → the **executive** (under Section 301/232/IEEPA trade statutes) and, for the architecture, the **WTO**. Watch proclamations and USTR actions.
- **Sanctions** → **OFAC**. Watch the SDN list updates — they post same-day.
- **Drug approvals and pricing** → the **FDA** (approvals, a binary catalyst) and the rules implementing drug-pricing statutes.
- **Tax** → **Congress** (the rate) and the **IRS / Treasury** (the implementing regulations).
- **The meaning of any contested rule** → the **courts**. Watch circuit-court and Supreme Court dockets for the relevant case.

Notice the pattern: for *fast* themes (sanctions, tariffs, EOs) you watch a feed and pre-position for *risk*, because there's no reaction time. For *slow* themes (statutes, agency rules) you watch a calendar and trade the *drift*. For *court* themes you watch a docket and price a *binary*. The body tells you which playbook to run.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "A law gets passed and then the market reacts."** Backwards. Markets react to the *expected* law long before it passes, and often the biggest moves happen on *changes in the probability* of passage, not on the passage itself. Recall the worked example: as a statute's passage went from uncertain to 80% likely, the stock had already moved most of the way (\$100 → \$90) *before the vote*. By the time the law is signed, the easy money is gone. The famous market adage "buy the rumor, sell the news" is just this mechanism in plain English — the *rumor* (rising probability) is where the repricing lives.

**Misconception 2: "Agencies basically write their own laws, so an agency rule is as good as a statute."** This was *closer* to true under *Chevron*, and it became materially *less* true in June 2024. After *Loper Bright*, an agency rule built on ambiguous statutory authority is meaningfully more likely to be struck down — which is why the durability of the agency-rule row in our matrix got marked down while the statute row didn't. Treating a contested agency rule as if it were a permanent statute will systematically overestimate its durability and mis-size the trade.

**Misconception 3: "Sanctions and tariffs are slow, bureaucratic, geopolitical things that don't move fast."** The opposite. They're the *fastest* instruments in the entire system. An OFAC designation takes effect the day it's posted; a tariff proclamation can be effective within days. The 2022 Russia sanctions wave illustrates the scale: OFAC added roughly **2,549** new designations that year — more than triple a typical pre-2022 year — and froze about **\$300 billion** of Russia's roughly \$643 billion in foreign reserves, essentially overnight. Anyone who assumed they'd have time to react after the announcement was already too late.

![New OFAC sanctions designations per year with a 2022 Russia spike](/imgs/blogs/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts-4.png)

**Misconception 4: "Guidance is just paperwork, ignore it."** Guidance isn't binding law, but it's a *high-information signal* of where enforcement is heading — and enforcement is what actually costs money. When an agency issues guidance saying "we interpret the rule to cover X," the firms doing X reprice immediately, even though no binding rule changed, because the guidance reveals the agency's intent to act. Guidance is fast and soft, but markets treat it as a leading indicator of the harder instruments to come.

**Misconception 5: "Central banks can do whatever they want."** No — a central bank is constrained by its *statutory mandate*. The Fed must pursue maximum employment and stable prices; it can't legally pursue, say, a stock-market target. Its emergency lending powers (Section 13(3)) require "unusual and exigent circumstances" and specific procedures. Reading the legal mandate tells you the *boundary* of what the central bank will do — which is why mandate-reading is part of macro analysis, not a legal footnote.

## How it shows up in real markets

Let's ground the framework in three real episodes, one per instrument type, to show the speed/durability contrast in live data.

**A statute moves slowly but durably — the 2017 corporate tax cut.** The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) cut the U.S. federal corporate tax rate from **35% to 21%**, effective January 1, 2018. Look at the shape of the change: one cliff, then flat for years.

![US federal statutory corporate tax rate stepping from 35 percent to 21 percent in 2018](/imgs/blogs/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts-5.png)

That single-step-then-flat profile is the *signature of a statute* — a rare, large, durable change. A lower tax rate raises after-tax earnings mechanically, and because a statute is durable, the market re-rated it as *permanent*: S&P 500 companies' after-tax profits jumped, and gross buybacks surged to a then-record **\$806 billion** in 2018 (up from \$519 billion in 2017) as companies returned the windfall. The repricing built over the *months* the bill moved through Congress in late 2017 — slow to arrive, but a structural shift in earnings power once it landed.

The statute also triggered a one-time flow worth watching as a case study in durability. TCJA imposed a one-time "transition tax" that effectively freed companies to bring home cash they'd parked overseas, and the repatriated dividends spiked from about **\$155 billion** in 2017 to roughly **\$1,064 billion** in 2018 before fading back toward \$553 billion in 2019 and \$295 billion in 2020. That decay is itself instructive: the *rate cut* (the durable part of the statute) re-rated earnings *permanently*, while the *repatriation* (a one-time provision) produced a *transient* flow that faded within two years. Same statute, two different durabilities — which is why "read the instrument" sometimes means reading *which provision* of the instrument, not just which body wrote it. A trader who treated the 2018 buyback surge as a permanent run-rate would have been disappointed; one who recognized the repatriation pulse as a one-off and the rate cut as structural sized the two correctly.

**An agency rule grinds through a calendar.** Contrast the tax cliff with how an SEC or EPA rule arrives: an NPRM, a comment period, a final rule 9–18 months later, then often a lawsuit. Each stage is a separate catalyst with its own price drift, and the *whole* thing can be undone by the court at the end. The agency-rule path is a *slow, two-sided, litigation-contingent* bet — the polar opposite of the tax statute's clean one-time step. This is why a regulatory calendar is as useful to a legal-event trader as an earnings calendar is to an equity trader; the dates are knowable in advance. (We devote a whole post to building one: [the regulatory calendar — trading the rulemaking clock](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock).)

![The rulemaking clock from proposed rule through comment final rule effective date and litigation](/imgs/blogs/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts-6.png)

**An agency designation hits the same day — OFAC sanctions.** The OFAC chart above is the speed extreme. When the 2022 designations landed, named entities lost dollar access and any traded exposure gapped within hours. There was no comment period, no 18-month lag, no warning. This is the purest illustration of the thesis: the *same kind of body* (an agency) produces a *slow* instrument (a notice-and-comment rule) and a *fast* one (a same-day designation) — and you have to know *which power* it's exercising to know how fast to be positioned.

**A central bank's legal mandate sets the boundary of what it can do.** The 2023 banking stress is the cleanest recent example of legal authority shaping a market outcome in real time. When Silicon Valley Bank failed in March 2023, the question that gripped markets over a weekend wasn't economic — it was *legal*: what could the authorities actually do, and how fast? The answer ran through three distinct legal tools, each from a different body. The **FDIC** (a deposit-insurance agency) could seize the bank and guarantee deposits, but the statutory insurance cap was \$250,000 — and SVB's deposits were ~94% *above* that cap, so a literal reading left most depositors exposed. The regulators invoked a **systemic-risk exception** to guarantee uninsured deposits, a legal switch that exists precisely for this case. Meanwhile the **Fed** stood up a new emergency lending facility (the Bank Term Funding Program) under its statutory crisis powers, letting banks borrow against bonds at par. Each of these was a *legal* instrument with its own speed and reversibility, and the market repriced regional banks tick-by-tick as it learned *which authority* the officials would use. The lesson generalizes: a central bank can't do "whatever it wants" — it acts inside a statutory mandate and a defined set of emergency powers, and reading those legal boundaries tells you the *ceiling* on the rescue, which is exactly what the market is trying to price during a crisis weekend. (The monetary mechanics of how the Fed's rate decisions then transmit to the broader economy are covered in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).)

## How to trade it: the playbook

The whole point of the institutional map is to turn "a rule changed" into a concrete decision. Here's the lookup table — given a theme, it tells you which body holds the pen, which document to read, the timeline you're working with, and what invalidates the view.

| Theme | Body to watch | Document / signal | Timeline | What invalidates |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sector tax change | Congress (rate); IRS (rules) | Bill text, committee markup, then regs | 1–3 yrs to pass; durable | Bill stalls; sunset clause; rate reversed by new law |
| Stock disclosure / market structure | SEC | NPRM, comment docket, final rule | 9–18 mo; then litigation | Court vacates; agency withdraws |
| Bank capital / liquidity | Fed + bank regulators | Proposed rule, supervisory guidance | Months to phase-in | Rule softened in finalization; rollback |
| Merger approval | DOJ Antitrust / FTC | HSR filing, complaint, consent decree | 6–18 mo per deal | Regulators clear it; remedy accepted |
| Tariffs | Executive (301/232/IEEPA) | Proclamation, USTR notice | Days to weeks | Reversed by next administration; carve-outs |
| Sanctions | OFAC | SDN list update | Same day | De-listing; license/waiver granted |
| Drug approval | FDA | PDUFA date, advisory-committee vote | Set in advance; binary | Approval denied; label restricted |
| Meaning of a contested rule | Courts (district→circuit→SCOTUS) | Docket, oral-argument date, opinion | Months–years; overnight at decision | Higher court reverses; case settles |
| Crypto classification | SEC vs CFTC (turf war) | Enforcement action, court ruling, statute | Years; unresolved | Jurisdiction assigned; case dropped |

Three operating rules fall out of the table:

1. **Match your reaction speed to the instrument's speed.** For same-day instruments (sanctions, tariffs, EOs), you cannot trade the announcement — you must pre-position for the *risk* and size it as a tail. For slow instruments (statutes, agency rules), you trade the *drift* across the public calendar. For court events, you price the *binary* in advance and let the decision resolve it.

2. **Match your holding period to the instrument's reversibility.** A move driven by a reversible EO is *rentable* — consider fading it or buying optionality on reversal. A move driven by a statute or a hard court precedent is *structural* — you can hold it as an investment, not just a trade.

3. **Hunt for the event that resolves uncertainty.** The biggest, cleanest repricings come not from the rule arriving but from the *uncertainty lifting* — a court vacating a rule, a turf war being settled, a probability snapping from 50/50 to near-certain. Map your portfolio's exposure to *which body* could deliver that event, and you'll know where your fat-tailed risk and your fat-tailed opportunity live.

#### Worked example: sizing exposure to a fast designation power (sanctions)

Suppose you hold \$1,000,000 in a basket of emerging-market industrial names, and you judge there's a **10%** chance over the next year that OFAC adds a key supplier in the basket to the SDN list — a same-day event you *cannot* react to in time. If it happens, you estimate the basket gaps down **25%** in a day (lost contracts, frozen counterparties, forced unwinds), a \$250,000 loss.

Your expected loss from this single tail is 0.10 × \$250,000 = **\$25,000**, or 2.5% of the position. Because it's a *same-day* event, you can't stop out into it — so you have to *pre-hedge*. You buy 1-year out-of-the-money put options on the basket (or a proxy index) that pay off in the crash scenario, costing, say, **\$15,000** in premium (1.5% of the position). If the designation hits, the puts return roughly \$200,000, cutting your net loss from \$250,000 to about \$50,000. If it doesn't hit (the 90% case), you're out the \$15,000 premium — a carrying cost you accept precisely *because the instrument is too fast to react to*.

Compare this with hedging exposure to a *slow* statute: there you'd have months of drift to scale out, so you wouldn't pay for standing option protection — you'd manage it with the cash position. The hedge structure is dictated by the *instrument's speed*: fast designation power demands pre-bought convexity; slow statutory risk can be managed in the cash book. Sizing the hedge to the body's speed is the discipline that keeps a same-day designation from being a portfolio-ending event.

To turn this into a repeatable process — mapping a portfolio's exposures to bodies, calendars, and dockets, then sizing each — see [building a macro thesis: from data to a trade](/blog/trading/macro-trading/building-a-macro-thesis-from-data-to-a-trade), which applies the same data-to-position discipline to the macro layer this series feeds into.

The whole post compresses into one habit. When any rule-change crosses your screen, run the four questions in order before you do anything else. *Which body acted?* — that fixes the instrument. *What's the instrument's speed?* — that tells you whether you trade the drift, price the binary, or pre-hedge the tail. *What's its reversibility?* — that tells you whether to hold or fade. *What's its durability?* — that tells you whether this is a trade or an investment. Answer those four and you've converted a vague "the rules changed" headline into a concrete decision with a size and a holding period. Get them in the wrong order — reacting to the bite instead of the signal, treating a reversible EO as a permanent statute, or trading the drift after a court has taken over the binary — and you'll be late, mis-sized, and on the wrong side of the gap. The body writes the rule; the body also tells you how to trade it.
