> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Rule-making and policy run on a **predictable, public schedule**, so you can treat known dates like an earnings calendar: position around binary risk you can actually see coming.
>
> - The dates are not secret. Comment-period deadlines, rule effective dates, the Unified Agenda, the Supreme Court term, FDA decision dates, FOMC meetings and election dates are all published months ahead.
> - The hard split is **binary vs continuous**: an FDA decision, a court ruling or an election resolves on a single date (a gap); a comment period or an agenda item reprices gradually (a drift). They call for different trades.
> - Markets price the *expected* outcome into options before the date. Implied volatility gets bid up into a known catalyst and **crushes** the morning after — so a long-vol bet is only "cheap" if the realized move beats what you paid for.
> - The one number to remember: a near-dated **at-the-money straddle implies an expected move of roughly 0.85 × its own price** — the market's published odds on how far the stock travels by the date.

On the last Monday of June, the U.S. Supreme Court does something the rest of the year never sees: it dumps a stack of its biggest decisions onto the public in a span of days. Traders who cover regulated industries — health insurers, for-profit colleges, payment networks, energy utilities — have that window circled on a calendar months in advance. They do not know *what* the Court will rule. But they know *when*. And on those mornings, single stocks gap 5, 10, sometimes 20 percent in the first minute of trading on a decision whose date was never in doubt.

That is the whole idea of this post. A statute, a regulator's rule, a court ruling, a drug approval, a rate decision — each of these changes the rules of the game or the expected path of policy, and markets discount that change into prices. We covered *how* that repricing works in [how a rule becomes a price](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing). Here we focus on the part everyone underrates: the **timing is knowable**. The legal and policy machine runs on a clock. Comment periods close on dated deadlines. Rules take effect on dated deadlines. The Supreme Court hears arguments October through April and hands down opinions that cluster in June. The FDA commits to a decision date when it accepts a drug application. The Federal Reserve publishes its meeting schedule a year ahead.

If earnings season has taught a generation of traders to build a calendar, mark the binary dates, check the implied move and decide whether to bet on direction or on volatility — then the rule-making clock deserves exactly the same treatment. The dates carry known risk. You can position around them. And the market, surprisingly often, mis-times the repricing.

The contrast with how most people *think* about regulation is stark. Ask an investor about "regulatory risk" and you will usually hear a shrug: politics is unpredictable, you cannot know what Congress or the courts will do, so you either avoid regulated names or accept the chaos. That instinct is half right and half catastrophically wrong. The *outcomes* are genuinely hard to forecast. But the *timing* of when those outcomes arrive is, for an enormous share of consequential events, printed on a public schedule months ahead. Conflating "I can't predict the outcome" with "I can't predict anything" is the single most expensive mistake in this corner of markets — it leaves traders perpetually *surprised* by events whose dates they could have circled, walking into a known binary with no plan because they told themselves it was unknowable. This post is about the half that is knowable and what to do with it.

## Foundations: how the rule-making clock actually works

Before any trade, you need the vocabulary. None of this is finance jargon — it is the public plumbing of how laws and rules get made, and every term below has a date attached to it. Let us build the calendar from zero.

### The Federal Register and the NPRM clock

In the United States, federal agencies — the SEC, the EPA, the FDA, bank regulators, the Department of Transportation, and dozens more — cannot simply decide a rule and impose it. The law (the Administrative Procedure Act of 1946) forces them through a public, dated process called **notice-and-comment rule-making**. It runs in stages, and each stage is published in the *Federal Register*, the government's official daily journal.

- **Notice of Proposed Rule-making (NPRM).** The agency publishes the proposed rule and says, in effect, "here is what we intend to do." This is the moment a vague threat becomes concrete text. Markets often react *more* to the NPRM than to the final rule, because the NPRM is where the surprise lives.
- **Comment period.** The agency must give the public time to respond — usually 30 to 60 days, and the closing date is printed in the NPRM itself. This is a *dated deadline*, but the period is **continuous**: the rule is not decided on the closing day, so the repricing tends to drift rather than gap.
- **Final rule.** After reading comments, the agency publishes the final rule. This is the binary: the rule is now real (or, occasionally, withdrawn). Its publication date is the one to circle.
- **Effective date.** A final rule usually does not bite immediately. By statute, "major" rules must wait at least 60 days after publication before taking effect. So the *legal* deadline and the *economic* deadline are different — and, as we will see, the market has usually priced the rule long before the effective date arrives.
- **Litigation and stays.** Almost any consequential rule gets sued. A court can issue a **stay** that freezes the rule before it takes effect. That re-opens a binary you thought had closed — which is exactly why the calendar never really ends.

![Pipeline showing a federal rule moving from Unified Agenda through NPRM, comment, final rule, effective date and litigation](/imgs/blogs/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock-2.png)

### The Unified Agenda — the regulator's earnings guidance

Twice a year (spring and fall), the U.S. government publishes the **Unified Agenda of Regulatory and Deregulatory Actions** — a master list of every rule each agency *plans* to work on, with a rough target date for each stage. Think of it as forward guidance from the regulators. It is not binding, and dates slip constantly, but it tells you which rules are "active," which are "long-term," and roughly when an NPRM might land. A name moving from the long-term list to the active list is itself a signal, months before any rule text exists.

### PDUFA — the FDA's promise of a decision date

When a drug company submits a new drug application, the FDA does not approve or reject it on an open-ended timeline. Under the **Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA)**, the agency commits to a **PDUFA date** — a target decision date, typically 10 months after a standard filing (6 months for a priority review). That date is public the moment the FDA accepts the application. For a small biotech whose entire value rests on one drug, the PDUFA date is the single most important number in the company's existence: a true binary, approve or reject, on a known day.

### The Supreme Court term — October to June

The U.S. Supreme Court runs on an annual **term** that opens the first Monday in October and runs through late June or early July. The Court hears **oral arguments** October through April, then issues written **opinions**. Crucially, the biggest, most contested opinions almost always come last — they **cluster in June**. So the calendar has a known shape: arguments in the fall and winter (when a case's odds can be read from the Justices' questions), and a dense decision window in June (when sector-moving rulings drop). A ruling that strikes down an agency's authority, or upholds a tax, or reshapes liability for an entire industry, lands on a morning you could have circled the previous October.

### The FOMC schedule, appropriations and elections

Three more public clocks complete the picture:

- **The FOMC.** The Federal Reserve's Federal Open Market Committee meets **eight times a year** on dates published a year ahead, and four of those meetings come with a *Summary of Economic Projections* (the "dot plot"). Rate decisions are the most-watched scheduled catalysts in all of markets. For the mechanism of how the Fed actually moves rates, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).
- **The appropriations / debt-ceiling calendar.** The U.S. fiscal year ends **September 30**. If Congress has not funded the government by then, you get a shutdown threat — a recurring, dated standoff. The debt-ceiling deadline (the "X-date") is less precise but is estimated publicly months ahead.
- **Elections.** U.S. federal elections are on the Tuesday after the first Monday in November, even years. Around the world, election dates are fixed or announced well ahead. An election is a clean binary on a known date with a market-moving policy platform attached to each outcome.

Two more dated processes round out a serious calendar. **Sanctions and trade reviews** run on statutory clocks: the U.S. Treasury and the Office of Foreign Assets Control conduct periodic reviews, and tools like the annual currency-manipulation report or the renewal deadlines on specific sanctions programs land on dates you can anticipate. A name's addition to — or removal from — a sanctions list is a binary with enormous consequence for the affected entity, and the *review* dates telegraph when those decisions cluster. **WTO and trade-dispute timelines** are slower but equally dateable: a dispute panel takes months to years, but its report and any appellate decision arrive in a window the parties know, and the affected tariffs, commodities and currencies reprice when the ruling lands. These sit at the geopolitical end of the spectrum, but they obey the same logic — a known date, an unknown outcome, a repricing you can position around.

Put all of these on one page and you have a **catalyst calendar**: a list of dated events, each carrying known risk, that you can position around exactly the way an equity trader positions around earnings.

### The calendar is not only American

Everything above has a counterpart in every major jurisdiction, and a global book has to track all of them. The European Union runs its own rule-making clock: the European Commission proposes, the Parliament and Council co-decide, and major files (think the AI Act, MiFID II, or the Digital Markets Act) move through dated trilogue and vote stages that are published well ahead. The European Court of Justice and national constitutional courts hand down rulings on scheduled days. The European Central Bank, the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan and the People's Bank of China all publish meeting calendars. The World Trade Organization's dispute panels run on multi-year but dateable timelines — a panel report or an appellate decision lands in a window you can anticipate. Vietnam's State Securities Commission and State Bank publish regulatory roadmaps and quarterly credit-quota decisions; the SBV's credit-ceiling mechanism, covered in [Vietnam's monetary policy and the dong](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling), is a dated policy lever in its own right. The point generalizes: wherever there is a rule-making body, a court, or a central bank, there is a public schedule, and the schedule is tradeable.

What differs across jurisdictions is the *cleanliness* of the binary and the speed of the repricing. U.S. equity markets are deep and liquid enough that a SCOTUS opinion gets discounted in seconds. A WTO ruling on a tariff dispute may take days to filter into the affected commodity and currency, because the affected market is thinner and the legal implications take longer to parse. A trader builds the same calendar everywhere but adjusts the *expected reaction speed* to the liquidity and sophistication of the market doing the pricing.

### What a single calendar entry actually contains

It is worth being concrete about the fields you record for each dated catalyst, because a vague "FDA thing in July" is useless and a precise entry is a trade. A usable calendar entry carries: the **exact date** (or window, for SCOTUS June); the **affected names and the dollar exposure** in your book; the **binary-vs-continuous classification**; whether the **outcome can leak early** (advisory committee, oral-argument signal, NPRM); the **option-implied move** if the name has a liquid options market; your **own estimate** of the realized move and outcome odds; and the **invalidation** — what tells you the thesis is wrong. Fill those fields for every dated catalyst touching the portfolio and you have converted a wall of legal jargon into a ranked list of trades and hedges.

## Reading the docket: the calendar as a public information feed

A point that surprises people new to this: almost everything you need to build the calendar is *free and public*, posted by the government itself. The *Federal Register* publishes every NPRM and final rule, with dates. The regulations dot gov docket shows every comment letter filed on a rule — so you can literally read what the affected industry is arguing and count the weight of opposition. The Supreme Court posts its argument calendar and its list of pending cases. The FDA's filing acceptances and advisory-committee schedules are public. The Fed's meeting calendar is published a year ahead. Congressional committee hearing schedules are public. This is the opposite of inside information: it is the regulator broadcasting its own clock.

The edge, then, is not access — everyone can access it — but *attention and synthesis*. Most market participants do not read dockets. They react to the headline when the final rule drops, by which point the information date has passed. The trader who reads the NPRM the week it publishes, scores the comment letters, and reads the oral-argument transcript the day of the hearing is operating on the *information* timeline, not the *headline* timeline. That gap — between when the information is legally public and when the crowd notices — is the whole opportunity. It is entirely legal to front-run a rule on the public docket; what is illegal is trading on *non*-public information, and the docket is the definition of public.

Lobbying disclosures add a second, slower signal. Firms must report what they spend lobbying on which issues. A sudden surge of lobbying spend against a proposed rule tells you the affected industry takes the threat seriously and is fighting hard — useful color on both the odds and the magnitude of the eventual repricing. None of this predicts the outcome with certainty, but it sharpens the probability you assign, which is exactly the input the sizing math below demands.

It is worth dwelling on *why* the headline timeline lags the information timeline so reliably, because that lag is the whole edge and it is not going away. Most market participants are not staffed to read dockets. A portfolio manager covering forty names cannot read every NPRM that touches them, score every comment letter, and parse every oral-argument transcript — so they outsource attention to the headline, and the headline only fires when the final rule or the decision drops. The information, meanwhile, has been public for weeks or months. This is a structural inefficiency of *attention*, not of *access*, and attention is scarce in a way access is not. A small desk that specializes — that reads the dockets in one sector deeply rather than skimming forty sectors shallowly — can systematically operate ahead of the crowd on the legal calendar. The barrier is effort and focus, which is exactly why so few do it and why the lag persists. The calendar, in other words, is not a secret; it is simply ignored, and ignored information is the cheapest edge there is.

## The hard split: which dates are binaries and which are drifts

The single most useful thing you can do with a catalyst calendar is sort every entry into one of two buckets, because the bucket decides the trade.

A **binary** resolves on a single date. The FDA approves or rejects on the PDUFA date. The Supreme Court rules one way or the other on an opinion day. An election picks a winner overnight. There is no in-between: the morning after, the stock has either gapped up or gapped down, and the uncertainty that was priced into the option premium *vanishes*. Binaries produce **gaps**.

A **continuous** event reprices gradually. A comment period has a deadline, but the rule is not decided on that day — the market chews through the implications over weeks. A Unified Agenda update is a signal, not a resolution. Continuous events produce **drifts**: slow, grinding repricing that you trade by positioning early and fading the move, not by buying a one-day lottery ticket.

![Matrix sorting FDA, SCOTUS, election, comment period and Unified Agenda into binary or continuous catalysts](/imgs/blogs/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock-3.png)

The distinction matters because it determines whether you are trading **direction** or **volatility**. On a binary, if you have a genuine view on the outcome (you think the drug gets approved, you think the Court upholds the rule), you can bet direction. If you have no edge on the outcome but you think the *move* will be bigger than the market expects, you bet volatility — you buy options and hope the gap beats the premium. On a continuous event, neither pure bet works well; you position early in the direction of the likely drift and trim as it plays out.

There is a subtle trap here, and it is worth naming early. A binary is only a *clean* binary if there is no information leak before the date. The Supreme Court is famously leak-resistant; the decision genuinely arrives on opinion day. An election result genuinely arrives on election night. But many "binary" regulatory events leak: an FDA advisory committee meets weeks before the PDUFA date and votes publicly, often telegraphing the agency's lean; a rule's direction is usually obvious from the NPRM long before the final. The cleaner the binary, the more the option market has to price *pure* uncertainty into the date — and the bigger the volatility crush afterward.

### Trading the continuous drift

Continuous events get less attention than binaries because they are less dramatic, but they are arguably easier to trade because they leak by construction. When an NPRM is published, the market does not know whether the final rule will be tougher, softer, or withdrawn — but it can read the proposed text, count the comment letters from industry, watch the litigation posture, and gradually update. The price grinds in the direction of the most likely final outcome over the weeks of the comment period and the months to the final rule. There is no single gap day; there is a *trend* you can lean into.

The art of trading a drift is to position *early*, when the consensus is still forming, and *fade* the move as it matures and the outcome becomes obvious. By the time the final rule is published, the repricing is largely done — the easy money was made in the weeks after the NPRM, not on the final-rule date. This is the opposite of the binary playbook, where the move happens *on* the date. A common mistake is to wait for the "event" (the final rule) on a continuous catalyst, only to find the market already discounted it. The signal on a continuous event is the *information flow* during the comment-and-litigation window, not a single date.

A second feature of continuous events is that they rarely justify paying for elevated option vol, because there is no concentrated jump to price. You express a drift view with the *underlying* or with longer-dated, cheaper options, not with the expensive near-dated contracts you would buy for a binary. Spending binary-style option premium on a continuous drift is one of the most common ways to bleed money on a correct directional view: you pay for a jump that never comes.

## How the priced-in move shows up in options

Here is the mechanism that turns a date on a calendar into a tradeable number. Options are insurance on price moves, and their price embeds the market's expectation of how far the underlying will travel before they expire. When a known binary date sits inside an option's life, the market bids up the **implied volatility** of options that span that date — because everyone knows a big move is *possible* on a known day. Then, the morning after the event resolves, that uncertainty is gone, and the implied volatility **crushes** lower, often violently.

![Timeline of implied volatility rising into a binary date and crushing the morning after](/imgs/blogs/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock-8.png)

This is the most important and most misunderstood pattern in event trading. The vol bid is not a free lunch; it is the market's *fair price* for the uncertainty you can see coming. If you buy that elevated vol and the actual move is smaller than what you paid for, you lose — even if you correctly predicted the direction — because the premium you paid was set for a bigger move. The mechanics of vol bid-and-crush around scheduled events are the bread and butter of [building an event-day trading plan](/blog/trading/event-trading/building-an-event-day-trading-plan), and the options machinery itself is laid out in the [quantitative finance options series](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/options-greeks-and-implied-volatility).

The practical handle on all of this is the **option-implied expected move**. The price of a near-dated, at-the-money straddle — buying both a call and a put at the current price — is the market's published bet on how far the stock will travel by expiry. A useful rule of thumb is that the *one-standard-deviation expected move* is roughly **0.85 times the at-the-money straddle price**. So if a stock trades at \$100 and the straddle expiring just after a PDUFA date costs \$10.00, the market is implying an expected move of about \$8.50, or 8.5 percent, by that date. That is the number you compare your own view against.

![Long-straddle payoff diagram with strike at 100, premium 8.50 and breakevens at 91.50 and 108.50](/imgs/blogs/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock-4.png)

### Why the vol bid concentrates on the date: term structure

There is a deeper layer worth understanding, because it explains *which* options get expensive and is the basis for the calendar-spread trade later. Implied volatility is not a single number; it varies by expiry, and the curve of implied vol across expiries is called the **term structure**. In calm conditions, the term structure is gently upward-sloping — longer options imply a bit more annualized vol because more can happen over a longer horizon. A known binary date *bends* that curve. The specific expiries that *span* the event carry an extra lump of implied vol — the market is pricing the one-day jump risk into exactly the contracts that will be alive when the jump happens — while expiries that end *before* the event stay cheap.

You can decompose it cleanly. Total expected variance over an option's life is the sum of the "ordinary" day-to-day variance plus the variance of the one-day event jump. Two options that differ only in whether they include the event date will price very differently per day of life: the one spanning the event has the jump variance baked in. This is why a 2-week option spanning a PDUFA date can imply, say, 90 percent annualized vol while a 6-week option spanning the same event implies only 55 percent — the event jump is a fixed lump spread over more days in the longer option, so it dilutes. The near option is "all event"; the far option is "mostly ordinary vol plus a sliver of event." That structural fact is precisely what a calendar spread monetizes: sell the near expiry that is dense with event vol, own the far expiry that is cheap per day.

The morning after the event, the jump variance is *realized and gone*. The term-structure bend snaps back flat. The near option's implied vol — which was carrying the whole event lump — collapses the hardest. That collapse is the **vol crush**, and it is mechanical: it would happen even if the stock did not move at all, simply because the calendar uncertainty the premium was paying for no longer exists. A trader who is long that near option needs the realized move to beat not just the implied move but the crush; a trader short it collects the crush as the lump it was pricing evaporates.

#### Worked example: the implied expected move into a known binary date

A small biotech, BioNova, trades at \$100 a share. Its lead drug has a PDUFA decision date three weeks out — a clean binary. You look at the options expiring the Friday after the PDUFA date. The at-the-money call (strike \$100) is priced at \$5.20 and the at-the-money put (strike \$100) at \$4.80. The straddle costs:

```
straddle = call + put = 5.20 + 4.80 = 10.00
```

The quick estimate of the expected move is the straddle price scaled by the rule-of-thumb factor:

```
expected move ~= 0.85 x straddle = 0.85 x 10.00 = 8.50
```

So the market is implying a one-standard-deviation move of about **\$8.50, or 8.5 percent**, by expiry. The straddle buyer's breakevens are simply the strike plus and minus the full premium: \$100 − \$10.00 = \$90.00 on the downside and \$100 + \$10.00 = \$110.00 on the upside. (The 0.85× figure estimates the *typical* move; the breakeven uses the *full* premium because that is what you must overcome to profit.) The lesson: the option market has already told you, in dollars, how big a move it is bracing for — your job is to decide whether the real move will be bigger or smaller than that.

## The "known unknown": you know the date, not the outcome

This is the philosophical core of trading the regulatory calendar, and it is worth stating plainly because it cuts against intuition. The calendar gives you *certainty about timing* and *zero certainty about outcome*. A PDUFA date is a known unknown: you know exactly when the answer arrives and have no idea what it will be.

That asymmetry is precisely what makes these events tradeable — and dangerous. Because the timing is certain, the option market can concentrate its uncertainty pricing into the dates around the event, which is why implied vol bids up so cleanly. Because the outcome is uncertain, the realized move is a genuine coin-flip relative to your information. Most retail blow-ups around scheduled events come from confusing the two: people feel they "know" a date is coming (true) and slide into feeling they "know" what will happen (false), then buy a lottery ticket at a price set by a market that priced the same coin-flip more carefully than they did.

The professional framing is the reverse. You treat the date as a fact and the outcome as a probability distribution, and you ask one question: **is the option market's priced-in move too rich, too cheap, or fair, relative to my honest estimate?** If the market is pricing an 8.5 percent move and you genuinely think the realized move will be 15 percent (because, say, this binary is unusually all-or-nothing), you are a buyer of vol. If you think the realized move will be 4 percent (because the outcome leaked at the advisory committee weeks ago and the suspense is gone), you are a seller of vol. Direction is a separate, additional bet you make only when you have real edge on the outcome.

This framing also explains why the same calendar entry is a *different* trade for different participants. A specialist biotech fund that has read every clinical trial and modeled the drug's mechanism may genuinely have edge on the *outcome* of a PDUFA date — for them, a directional bet makes sense. A generalist with no clinical edge has no business betting the direction, but may still have a defensible view on the *move size* relative to implied — for them, the vol trade is the only honest expression. The calendar is neutral; it hands everyone the same date. Your edge, if you have one, comes from knowing *which* of the two questions you can actually answer better than the market, and trading only that one. Pretending to have edge on the outcome when you do not is how a generalist turns a knowable date into a coin-flip with house odds against them.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs about the regulatory calendar are widespread, intuitive, and wrong. Each costs traders money.

### Misconception 1: "You can't predict regulation"

You cannot predict the *content* of a rule with confidence. But you can predict the *dates* with remarkable precision, and the dates are where the tradeable risk lives. The Supreme Court has published opinions in late June essentially every year for decades. The FDA tells you the PDUFA date the day it accepts a filing. The FOMC publishes its meeting calendar a full year ahead — eight meetings, fixed dates. The fiscal year ends September 30 every year. Confusing "I can't predict the outcome" with "I can't predict anything" is the error. You can build a calendar of dated binary risk for an entire portfolio without forecasting a single outcome — and that calendar alone changes how you size and hedge. A trader who simply *knew* that a holding had a PDUFA date next Thursday, and trimmed or hedged accordingly, would have avoided a great many ugly Friday mornings.

### Misconception 2: "Buying vol into a known date is a free lunch"

It feels obvious: a big move is coming on a known day, so just buy options and collect. The flaw is that *everyone* can see the same date, so the move is already priced into the premium. The implied vol is bid up precisely *because* the date is known. To make money buying vol into an event, the realized move must exceed the *implied* move — and on average it does not, because the market sets the implied move at roughly its fair expectation. Empirically, owning straddles into scheduled binary events is, on average, a slightly losing strategy after the post-event vol crush, which is why so many desks prefer to be *sellers* of event vol and collect the premium.

Consider the magnitudes. VIX, the market's broad volatility gauge, has a long-run average around 19–20 and spikes to the 30s and 80s only on genuine shocks: it printed 82.7 at the COVID low in March 2020, 38.6 in the August 2024 yen-carry unwind, 37.3 in the February 2018 "Volmageddon," and 32.8 in the 2010 flash crash. Notice the pattern in the chart below — those extreme prints cluster on *surprises*, not on scheduled dates. The scheduled catalysts you can put on a calendar rarely move the *whole market*; they move *specific names and sectors*, and the option vol on those names is bid up in advance. So the straddle buyer is not buying a cheap lottery ticket into a known date — they are buying insurance whose price already reflects the very event they are betting on. Run the arithmetic on a single name: if the implied move is 8.5 percent and the realized move averages, over many such events, also about 8.5 percent, then the straddle buyer breaks even *before* costs and loses *after* the vol crush and the bid-ask spread. That negative drift, repeated across dozens of events, is why systematic event-vol *selling* — collecting the rich premium and surviving the occasional large gap — is a more common professional posture than event-vol buying. The next worked example shows exactly how the crush eats a winning directional view.

### Misconception 3: "The Supreme Court term is irrelevant to markets"

Many investors file the Court under "politics, not markets." That is a mistake worth real money in specific sectors. A single ruling can reprice an entire industry in a morning. When the Court upheld the constitutionality of the Affordable Care Act in June 2012, hospital and insurer stocks moved sharply on the decision day. When it has narrowed or expanded federal agencies' regulatory authority, it has reset the risk premium on every company that lives or dies by those agencies' rules — utilities under the EPA, fintechs under the consumer-protection regime, employers under labor rules. The June opinion window is a dense cluster of *dated binary risk* for regulated sectors, and the cases that will be decided were argued — and their odds debated — the previous fall. Treating the term as irrelevant means walking into June blind in exactly the names most exposed to it.

### Misconception 4: "The effective date is the date that matters"

Newcomers circle the *effective date* of a rule — the day it legally takes force — as the market event. Usually it is not. Markets are forward-looking: they price the rule when its outcome becomes *known*, which is at the final-rule publication (or even at the NPRM, if the proposal is a surprise). By the effective date, 60-plus days later, the repricing is old news. The trade is around the *information* date, not the *legal* date. The exception is when implementation itself is uncertain — a rule that might be stayed by a court before it takes effect re-creates a binary around the effective date and any litigation deadline.

## How it shows up in real markets

The calendar is not a theory. It plays out, dated event by dated event, across every regulated sector. Here are four recognizable shapes.

### A PDUFA-date binary

A clinical-stage biotech with one important drug is the purest case. The PDUFA date is public months ahead. Implied vol on the options spanning that date climbs steadily as it approaches; the stock itself may barely move while the market simply marks up the *insurance*. Then the decision drops — approval or "complete response letter" (a rejection) — and the stock gaps, frequently 30 to 80 percent in a single session. The options that cost a fortune the day before are worth either a windfall or nothing by the open, and the surviving options' implied vol collapses as the suspense ends. This is the cleanest binary in public markets and the textbook case for the long-vol-vs-short-vol decision we work through below.

The PDUFA case also illustrates the leak problem precisely. Many drug applications go before an **advisory committee** — an outside panel that reviews the data and votes publicly weeks before the PDUFA date. That vote is not binding, but a strong "yes" or "no" telegraphs the agency's likely lean and lets the air out of the suspense. A stock can gap on the *advisory-committee* day and then barely move on the PDUFA date, because the real surprise already happened. So the calendar entry for a drug with an advisory committee has *two* dated catalysts, and the earlier, leakier one often carries the bigger move. A trader who circles only the PDUFA date and ignores the committee date is positioned for the wrong day. The lesson generalizes far beyond pharma: always ask whether a binary has an earlier, leakier checkpoint, and treat that checkpoint as a catalyst in its own right.

### A Supreme Court June decision repricing a sector

Picture a case argued the previous November that turns on whether a federal agency can keep enforcing a rule that underpins an industry's economics. From the oral-argument questions, analysts handicap the odds — say, 60/40 that the agency loses. The case then sits on the docket until late June, when the opinion drops on a morning everyone has circled. If the agency loses, the affected names gap on the open as the market reprices their regulatory burden; if the agency wins, the relief rally is just as fast. The date was knowable from the previous October; only the outcome was a coin-flip weighted by the argument.

The SCOTUS case has a feature that makes it especially tradeable for the patient: there is a long, quiet gap between the *argument* (when the odds become readable) and the *decision* (when the gap happens). A trader who reads the oral-argument transcript in November and disagrees with the consensus odds has months to build a position before the June crowd shows up. And because the Court releases opinions on a handful of pre-announced "opinion days" each June, you can narrow the window further — the market knows the candidate days and bids vol into each one. The cleanliness is unusual: there is essentially no leak, so the option market must price genuine, undiluted uncertainty into the June window, and the post-decision vol crush in the affected names is correspondingly sharp. Few catalysts offer such a clean separation of *when the odds are knowable* from *when the answer arrives*.

### An FOMC decision

The Fed meets on a published schedule, eight times a year. In a tightening or easing cycle, each meeting is a scheduled catalyst with a binary-ish core (hike, hold or cut) and a continuous tail (the statement language and the chair's press conference, which the market chews through for hours). Across the 2022–24 cycle the FOMC took its target range from near zero to a peak of 5.25–5.50 percent and back down — a sequence of dated decisions, each one a known catalyst that bond and equity vol bid into and crushed out of. The *mechanism* of those moves is covered in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) and, for the historical extreme, [the Volcker rate shock](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation); here the point is simply that the dates were on the calendar a year ahead.

The FOMC also shows how a binary can be *partly leaked* in real time. By the morning of a meeting, futures markets price the probability of each rate outcome with high precision — a hike might be "85 percent priced" — so the *level* decision is often a small surprise. The unleaked part is the *guidance*: the statement wording and the chair's press conference, which reset the expected path of future meetings. That is why equity and rate vol often crushes only *partly* on the decision and then moves again during the press conference. The calendar entry for an FOMC date therefore really contains two sub-catalysts: the 2:00 p.m. decision and the 2:30 p.m. presser. A trader sizing for an FOMC day who ignores the presser is positioned for the smaller of the two events.

### An election-week vol spike

An election is a clean binary on a known date with two distinct policy platforms attached. Index and single-name implied vol typically bid up into election week — the options market pricing the uncertainty of the outcome and the policy repricing that follows — then crush once the result is known and the platform becomes a fact to be discounted rather than a coin-flip. The shape is identical to a PDUFA date, just at the index level: a vol bid into a dated known unknown, and a crush after. The reaction mechanics across asset classes are the focus of the [event-trading series](/blog/trading/event-trading/building-an-event-day-trading-plan).

The election case has its own wrinkle: the binary may not resolve on election night. A close or contested race can leave the outcome unknown for days, which means the vol does *not* crush on the scheduled date — the calendar told you *when the count starts*, not *when the answer arrives*. Term-structure traders handle this by spreading their vol exposure across a window of expiries rather than betting it all on the single election-night expiry, because the resolution date is itself uncertain. It is a useful reminder that "known date" and "known resolution" are not always the same thing, and the gap between them is its own risk. The other election-specific subtlety is that the policy repricing is *sector-specific*: a given outcome is bullish for some industries (defense, traditional energy, financials under lighter regulation) and bearish for others (renewables under subsidy risk, healthcare under pricing pressure). The clean index vol bid masks a set of *relative* trades underneath — long the winners' sectors against the losers' — that often carry better risk-reward than the index straddle, because the relative move can be large even when the index barely nets out.

![Line chart of the Geopolitical Risk index spiking on shocks rather than on the calendar](/imgs/blogs/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock-6.png)

One honest caveat the calendar cannot fix: not every market mover is on a schedule. The Geopolitical Risk index above spikes on *shocks* — an invasion, a terrorist attack — that arrive without a date. The calendar disciplines the *scheduled* risk you can position around; it does nothing for the unscheduled tail, which you manage with standing hedges, not with a date on a page. Knowing which of your risks are calendar risks and which are shock risks is itself part of the discipline.

## How to trade it: the playbook

Everything above converges on a repeatable process. This is how a desk turns the rule-making clock into positions.

![Decision graph routing a known calendar date through binary or continuous branches to a vol or directional trade](/imgs/blogs/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock-7.png)

**Step 1 — Build the calendar.** For every name and sector in the book, list the dated catalysts: PDUFA dates, FOMC meetings, the June SCOTUS window for exposed sectors, comment-period deadlines and rule effective dates from the Unified Agenda, the September 30 appropriations cliff, and election dates. Pull these from public sources — the *Federal Register*, agency dockets, the Supreme Court's argument calendar, the Fed's schedule. This is the same discipline as [building a legal and geopolitical risk dashboard](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/building-a-legal-and-geopolitical-risk-dashboard), scoped to dates.

**Step 2 — Mark the binaries.** Sort each entry into binary (single-date resolution → gap) or continuous (gradual repricing → drift). For binaries, note whether the outcome can leak early (FDA advisory committees, NPRM signals) — a leaky binary has a smaller surprise and a smaller crush.

**Step 3 — Check the implied move.** For a binary, price the near-dated at-the-money straddle and compute the implied move (≈ 0.85 × straddle). This is the market's published bet. Compare it against your own honest estimate of the realized move. The gap between the two is your edge — or your warning that there is no edge.

**Step 4 — Decide direction vs vol.** If you have a real view on the *outcome*, take a directional position (and consider expressing it with options to cap the downside). If you have no outcome edge but think the realized move will beat the implied move, go long vol; if you think it will fall short — especially on a leaky or over-hyped binary — go short vol and collect the crush. On a continuous event, position early in the direction of the likely drift.

**Step 5 — Size for the gap.** A binary can gap far past your stop; a stop-loss does not protect you overnight. Size the position so the *worst-case gap* is survivable, and prefer defined-risk option structures where the most you can lose is the premium. This is where the [position-sizing-for-tail-risk discipline](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/position-sizing-for-tail-and-political-risk) earns its keep.

**Step 6 — Set the invalidation.** Decide in advance what tells you the thesis is wrong and where you exit. For a directional binary bet, the invalidation is simply the adverse outcome — so the position must be sized to survive it. For a continuous drift, the invalidation is the move stalling or reversing before the deadline.

A few hard-won refinements turn this from a checklist into an edge:

- **Enter the vol trade early, exit the day before.** If you are short event vol to collect the crush, you want the position on *before* the bulk of the vol bid arrives, and you want to be flat the underlying directional risk by the time the gap can hit you. The crush you are harvesting happens *at* the event; the directional gamble you are not paid for happens *over* it. Structure so you own the crush and not the coin-flip — that is what the calendar spread does.
- **Beware the date that slips.** Regulatory dates move. The FDA can extend a PDUFA date by three months if the company submits new data. A rule's final-rule date routinely slips past the Unified Agenda target. A court can reschedule. A slipped date does not just delay your catalyst; it can *deflate* the vol you were short or *bleed* the long-vol position you were holding, because the event you priced moved out of your option's window. Build the possibility of a slip into the trade — prefer expiries with a buffer, and re-check the date as it approaches.
- **Separate the two bets explicitly.** Every event trade is, at most, two distinct bets: a bet on the *outcome* (direction) and a bet on the *move size* relative to implied (vol). Write down which one you are making and why you have edge on it. If you cannot articulate an edge on either, the honest position is *smaller*, or a hedge rather than a bet. The calendar's job is to make sure you are never *surprised* by a dated event; it does not, by itself, give you an edge on the outcome.
- **Map the whole portfolio's calendar, not just one name.** The real value shows up at the book level. If three holdings all have catalysts clustered in the same June opinion week, your portfolio carries concentrated dated risk you might never have noticed name-by-name. Aggregating the calendar across the book reveals when the *portfolio* is most exposed — and tells you when to carry a standing index hedge against a cluster of binaries you cannot individually handicap.

#### Worked example: long vol vs short vol into a date, and the crush P&L

Return to BioNova at \$100 with the \$10.00 straddle (implied move ≈ \$8.50) into the PDUFA date.

**The long-vol case.** Suppose you believe this is an unusually all-or-nothing drug: approval sends the stock to \$130, rejection sends it to \$70 — a \$30 move either way, far above the \$8.50 implied. You buy the straddle for \$10.00. After the binary resolves, say the stock lands at \$130:

```
call payoff = 130 - 100 = 30.00      put payoff = 0
gross = 30.00      cost = 10.00      net P/L = +20.00 per share (+200%)
```

You win because the realized \$30 move beat the \$10 you paid.

**The short-vol case and the crush.** Now suppose instead the outcome leaked at the advisory committee and the real move is only \$4.00 (stock to \$104). The straddle *seller* who took your \$10.00 keeps most of it:

```
call payoff owed = 104 - 100 = 4.00      put payoff owed = 0
seller keeps = 10.00 - 4.00 = +6.00 per share
```

The straddle *buyer* loses \$6.00 even though they correctly called the direction (up). The realized \$4 move fell short of the \$8.50 implied, and the post-event vol crush did the rest. The lesson: into a known date, the bet is the *size* of the move versus the implied move, not merely the direction.

#### Worked example: sizing a binary with an estimated win probability (Kelly-lite)

You have a directional view on a SCOTUS-exposed name into the June opinion window. You estimate a 60 percent chance the ruling is favorable (stock +25 percent) and a 40 percent chance it is unfavorable (stock −20 percent). How much of a \$1,000,000 book should you risk?

First, the expected value of the trade per dollar risked:

```
EV = 0.60 x (+0.25) + 0.40 x (-0.20)
   = 0.150 - 0.080 = +0.070  (a +7% edge per dollar of exposure)
```

The full-Kelly fraction for a binary with payoff b on a win, probability p of winning and q of losing is f = (b·p − q) / b. With a win of +0.25 (so b = 0.25 relative to the −0.20 loss... use the simpler odds form b = win/loss = 0.25 / 0.20 = 1.25):

```
f = (b x p - q) / b = (1.25 x 0.60 - 0.40) / 1.25
  = (0.750 - 0.400) / 1.25 = 0.350 / 1.25 = 0.28
```

Full Kelly says risk 28 percent of the loss-defining capital — wildly aggressive for a binary you can be flat wrong on. Professionals use a fraction of Kelly. At quarter-Kelly:

```
sized fraction = 0.28 / 4 = 0.07  ->  7% of the book
position risk = 0.07 x 1,000,000 = 70,000 of capital at risk
```

Because the downside is a −20 percent gap, the *notional* you can hold is the risk budget divided by the gap: \$70,000 / 0.20 = \$350,000 of exposure. The lesson: a positive-EV binary still demands a fractional-Kelly haircut, because the gap can hand you the full loss overnight with no stop to save you.

#### Worked example: a calendar spread around a known catalyst

Sometimes the cleanest expression of "vol will be high into the date and crush after" is a **calendar spread**: sell the near-dated option that spans the event (rich with event vol) and buy a longer-dated option (cheaper per day of vol). Suppose a stock is \$100 with a PDUFA date in two weeks.

```
sell the 2-week 100 call (spans the event):   collect 4.00
buy  the 8-week 100 call (event is small share of its life):  pay 6.00
net debit = 6.00 - 4.00 = 2.00 paid
```

If the event passes with only a modest move, the 2-week call you sold expires near worthless (its event vol crushed), while the 8-week call you own retains most of its value:

```
2-week call you sold expires ~ 0.20 (small move, vol crushed)
8-week call you own now worth ~ 3.40 (lost the near event premium + some theta)
position value ~ 3.40 - 0.20 = 3.20    vs    2.00 paid
net P/L ~ +1.20 per spread (+60% on the debit)
```

You profited from the *vol crush*, not from direction: the near option you were short lost its event premium fast, while the longer option you were long kept most of its value. The lesson: a calendar spread is the structured way to be short the event's specific vol bump while staying long vol in general — a precise bet on the crush.

#### Worked example: positioning a continuous drift into a final rule

Now the continuous case, where there is no gap to trade and the money is in the drift. A regulated utility, GridCo, trades at \$50. An NPRM proposes a rule that, if finalized as written, would cut GridCo's allowed return and shave an estimated \$4 off fair value (an 8 percent hit). You read the docket: industry comment letters are heavy, and the agency's recent posture suggests it will soften the rule, so you estimate only a 50 percent chance the harsh version survives. The expected fair-value impact is:

```
expected hit = 0.50 x (-4.00) + 0.50 x (0.00) = -2.00 per share  (-4%)
```

The day the NPRM publishes, the stock is still at \$50 — the market has not yet digested it. Over the next three weeks, as analysts work through the docket, the price drifts toward the expected impact:

```
fair value now ~ 50.00 - 2.00 = 48.00
```

A short position entered at \$50 the week of the NPRM, covered at \$48 as the drift matures, captures:

```
gain per share = 50.00 - 48.00 = 2.00  (+4% on the short)
```

There was no single date to trade and no expensive option vol to buy — you leaned on the *underlying* into the most likely final outcome and covered as the drift played out, long before the final rule made it official. The lesson: on a continuous event, the trade is the weeks-long repricing after the NPRM, not a gap on the final-rule date.

A final caution on the whole playbook: the calendar tells you *when*, never *what*. Treat every binary as a coin-flip weighted by your honest odds, size so the adverse gap is survivable, and remember that the elevated option premium is the market's fair price for the very uncertainty you are trying to monetize — not a gift. The edge is in superior estimation of the *move size* and the *outcome odds*, married to disciplined sizing, not in the mere knowledge that a date exists. But knowing the date is the price of admission, and most of the field never even builds the calendar.
