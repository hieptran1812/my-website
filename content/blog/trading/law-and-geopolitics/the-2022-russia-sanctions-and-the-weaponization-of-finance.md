> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In 2022 the G7 turned the plumbing of global finance into a weapon against a major economy for the first time, and the repricing it caused is still rolling through markets years later.
>
> - The toolkit had four distinct parts: cutting major Russian banks off **SWIFT** (the bank-messaging network), **freezing ~\$300bn of central-bank reserves**, an **oil price cap** that gates Western shipping and insurance, and a **corporate exodus** of 1,000+ firms.
> - The immediate price reaction was an **energy shock** — Brent spiked to ~\$128/bbl and European gas (TTF) blew out ~13x to **€339/MWh** — not a Russian collapse. Russia's economy adapted; the barrels rerouted to India and China at a discount.
> - The deepest, slowest effect is a **debate about the dollar's safety as a reserve asset**: freezing another sovereign's reserves was unprecedented, and it gave every reserve manager on earth a reason to diversify. The evidence so far is a slow drift (and a strong central-bank gold bid), not a collapse.
> - The one number to remember: **\$300bn of \$643bn** — roughly half of Russia's reserve war chest was switched off overnight, which is what made this the most sweeping use of financial power ever aimed at a large economy.

On the morning of **24 February 2022**, two markets started moving before most of the world was awake. Brent crude, which had opened the year near \$79 a barrel, gapped through \$99 and kept climbing. And in Frankfurt, a contract most people have never heard of — Dutch TTF natural gas, the European gas benchmark — began a months-long blowout that would peak at an almost unbelievable **€339 per megawatt-hour**, more than thirteen times its normal level.

Neither move was about a shortage that day. No pipeline had been cut yet; no tanker had been seized. What the market was pricing was a *rule change*: the dawning realization that the United States, the European Union, the United Kingdom, Japan, and their partners were about to point the machinery of global finance — the messaging network banks use to talk to each other, the foreign reserves a central bank keeps abroad, the insurance that lets oil tankers sail — directly at the world's eleventh-largest economy and its largest energy exporter. Within days, major Russian banks were cut off from SWIFT, roughly \$300bn of the Russian central bank's reserves were frozen, and over a thousand Western companies began announcing their exit.

This was new. Sanctions are ancient; the United States alone maintains thousands of them. But sanctions of this *scale*, aimed at an economy of this *size*, hitting the *central bank* itself — that had no real precedent. This post is about what that toolkit actually does, why each piece was unprecedented, and — because this is a markets blog, not an op-ed — how the whole episode repriced energy, rewired commodity flows, re-rated defense stocks, and reopened a question that quietly haunts the dollar: *if reserves can be frozen, are they really money?* Throughout, the lens is analytical and politically neutral. The goal is to understand how the rules moved the prices, not to argue about whether they should have.

## Foundations: the building blocks of financial warfare

Before any of the price action makes sense, you need to understand the four instruments, because each one works through a completely different channel. Treat this section as the toolbox; the rest of the post is about what each tool broke.

### What SWIFT is, and what cutting a bank off actually does

Start with the most misunderstood piece. **SWIFT** — the Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication — is *not* a bank, a payment system, or a pile of money. It is a **messaging network**, a secure cooperative based in Belgium that lets more than 11,000 financial institutions in 200-plus countries send each other standardized instructions: "pay \$10 million from our account at your bank to this beneficiary." Think of it as the email system for banks — the envelope and the trusted address book, not the cash inside.

Here is the crucial distinction. The money itself moves through **correspondent banking** relationships and central-bank settlement systems; SWIFT just carries the *instructions*. So cutting a bank off SWIFT does not literally seize a single dollar. What it does is make the bank **operationally deaf and mute** to most of the global financial system. Its counterparties can no longer easily, cheaply, and verifiably receive its payment instructions. Transactions that used to take seconds now require phone calls, telexes, or workarounds that most reputable banks refuse to touch for fear of secondary penalties.

The effect is friction, not confiscation. A bank cut off SWIFT can still move money — through non-Western channels, through banks willing to handle it manually, through Russia's own domestic messaging system (SPFS) or China's CIPS. But every transaction gets slower, more expensive, and more visible. For a country that exports oil and gas and imports machinery and chips, that friction is a tax on the entire economy. In 2022 the West cut off a *selected list* of major Russian banks rather than the whole country — a deliberate choice that left a channel open for energy payments Europe still needed to make.

To make the analogy precise: imagine every bank in the world shares one trusted instant-messaging system. When a bank is expelled, it does not lose its money or its building — it loses the *ability to be trusted at speed*. A counterparty in Singapore that gets a SWIFT message from a known bank can act on it in seconds, because the network authenticates the sender and the format is standardized. Replace that with a phone call from an unfamiliar Russian institution, and the Singapore bank now has to manually verify identity, worry about whether processing the payment violates its own country's rules, and accept the reputational risk of being seen to facilitate a sanctioned entity. Most banks simply decline — a phenomenon called **de-risking**, where institutions cut off entire categories of counterparties because the compliance cost and legal risk outweigh the fee. De-risking is why a "selective" SWIFT cutoff bleeds far wider than its named targets: banks that were never sanctioned still refuse Russian business to stay clean. That over-compliance — the chilling effect — is a large part of what makes the tool bite.

There is also a strategic cost to *using* the tool, which is why the West held SWIFT expulsion back for years before 2022. Every time a country is cut off, it builds an incentive for the rest of the world to construct alternatives — SPFS, CIPS, bilateral arrangements that bypass the Western network entirely. A messaging monopoly is powerful precisely because everyone uses it; weaponize it too often and you accelerate the construction of the competitors that erode the monopoly. This tension — the tool works because it is central, and using it makes it less central — runs through every part of the 2022 toolkit and is the seed of the de-dollarization debate later in this post.

### Central-bank reserves, and why freezing them was unprecedented

This is the part that genuinely broke new ground. A country's **central bank** holds **foreign-exchange reserves** — a war chest of other countries' currencies and assets (US Treasuries, euro-denominated bonds, gold, yuan) — to defend its own currency, pay for imports in a crisis, and reassure markets. Crucially, most of these reserves are not stored in a vault at home. They are held *abroad*, as bookkeeping entries in accounts at the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, and the Bank of England, or as securities sitting in Western custodians and clearinghouses.

That custody arrangement is the vulnerability. In the days after the invasion, the G7 **froze** — immobilized — the Russian central bank's assets held within their jurisdictions. The accounts still exist on paper, but the money cannot be moved, sold, or used. Of an estimated **\$643bn** in total reserves, roughly **\$300bn** was switched off this way (more on the exact arithmetic below).

Why was this unprecedented? Sanctioning a private bank, an oligarch, or even a state-owned company has decades of precedent. But a **central bank** is the financial heart of a sovereign state, and its reserves are the assets it relies on to be solvent in a crisis. Freezing them is the financial equivalent of disarming a country's lender of last resort. It had been done to small, isolated states (Iran, Afghanistan, Venezuela), but never to a G20 economy with a globally integrated financial system. The act demonstrated, in public, that reserves held in another sovereign's currency are ultimately held *at that sovereign's pleasure* — a fact every reserve manager understood in theory and now had to confront in practice. (For the mechanics of how a central bank manages reserves and sets policy, see [the legal mandate of a central bank](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-mandate-of-a-central-bank).)

It helps to be concrete about *what a reserve actually is*, because the word makes people picture a vault of cash. When a central bank "holds \$100bn in US Treasuries," it does not have paper certificates in Moscow. It has an account — at the New York Fed, or at a custodian like Euroclear or a Western bank — and that account shows a balance and a list of securities. The securities are ultimately registered in systems that sit inside US, EU, and UK jurisdiction. A "freeze" is simply an order to those custodians and settlement systems: *do not let this account holder transact*. No money physically moves; the entries are flagged as untouchable. This is why the freeze was both instant and total for the affected portion — it required no seizure of physical assets, just an instruction to the institutions that maintain the ledgers.

There is a deep distinction lurking here between **freezing** and **seizing**. Freezing immobilizes the assets but leaves legal title with the owner — the money is still Russia's on paper, just unusable. Seizing (or *confiscating*) would transfer ownership outright, for example to fund reconstruction. In 2022 the G7 froze; the question of whether frozen reserves can lawfully be *confiscated* became one of the thorniest legal debates of the following years, because outright confiscation of a central bank's assets would be an even more dramatic break with the principle of sovereign immunity — and an even louder signal to every other reserve holder. For the market, the distinction matters: a freeze is reversible (assets could be released in a settlement), while confiscation is permanent and would raise the political risk premium on reserves another notch.

The strategic irony repeats the SWIFT lesson. Russia had spent years *de-risking its own reserves* after the milder 2014 sanctions — cutting US Treasury holdings, buying gold, shifting into yuan — precisely to reduce its exposure to exactly this move. That preparation is why "only" 47% was freezable rather than nearly all of it. And the visible success of the freeze taught every other large reserve holder to do the same de-risking that Russia had done, which is the mechanism by which weaponizing reserves slowly erodes the very leverage it depends on.

### The oil price cap, and why it is a clever piece of plumbing

The third tool is the strangest and the most misread. The G7's **oil price cap**, in force from December 2022, did *not* ban anyone from buying Russian oil. Banning it outright would have removed millions of barrels a day from the market and sent prices to the moon — punishing the buyers (and inflation everywhere) more than the seller.

Instead the cap works through a **chokepoint**: the maritime services that nearly all seaborne oil depends on. About 90% of the world's tanker insurance, and a large share of tanker financing and reinsurance, runs through London, the EU, and allied jurisdictions. The rule says: Western shippers, insurers, and financiers may handle Russian crude **only if it was sold at or below a price cap** (initially \$60 a barrel). Sell above the cap and you lose access to G7 shipping and insurance. The buyer or trader signs an **attestation** confirming the price; the insurer relies on it.

The genius — and the limitation — is that it keeps the oil flowing while squeezing the *revenue* per barrel. Russia can still sell all it wants, but to use the cheap, reliable Western shipping ecosystem it has to accept a capped price. To sell above the cap, it must build a parallel system — the now-famous **shadow fleet** of aging tankers with opaque ownership and non-Western (or no real) insurance. That parallel system has real costs and real risk, which is itself a tax on the seller.

### Sanctioning a company vs. sanctioning a state

A quick but load-bearing distinction. **Sanctioning a company** (or a person) — adding it to OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals list, for example — makes it toxic to deal with: US persons must freeze its assets and cut ties, and foreign firms risk **secondary sanctions** if they keep doing business with it. That is a scalpel. (For the legal machinery of the SDN list and secondary sanctions, see [sanctions law, OFAC, the SDN list and secondary sanctions](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sanctions-law-ofac-the-sdn-list-and-secondary-sanctions).)

**Sanctioning a state** at the scale of 2022 is a different beast. It combines hundreds of those scalpel cuts (designating banks, defense firms, officials) with **system-level** measures aimed at the state's financial infrastructure itself — the SWIFT cutoff and the reserve freeze. The whole-of-economy version is what raises the de-dollarization question, because it targets not a bad actor inside the system but a *member* of the system, using assets the system holds in trust.

### Energy as leverage: the symmetry of dependence

The last building block is not a sanction at all — it is the constraint that shaped every sanction. In 2021, the EU imported roughly **40% of its natural gas** and a large share of its crude from Russia. That dependence ran both ways: Russia needed the export revenue; Europe needed the molecules to heat homes and run industry. This **mutual dependence** is why the West designed sanctions to keep energy partly flowing (carve-outs for energy payments, the price cap instead of an embargo) and why Russia could retaliate by throttling gas. The energy market became the battlefield precisely because both sides had leverage there. (For the deeper mechanics of energy as a geopolitical weapon, see [energy geopolitics, OPEC, the oil weapon and the European gas shock](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock).)

## The timeline: how the shock unfolded

Sanctions of this scale are not a single event; they are a *sequence*, and markets repriced at each step. Walking the timeline is the cleanest way to see how a legal/geopolitical decision propagated into prices.

**The invasion (24 Feb 2022).** Russian forces crossed into Ukraine. Markets had partially priced the risk — Brent was already up from \$79 to \$97 over February as troops massed — but the confirmation triggered the classic risk-off cascade: equities down, the VIX (the stock market's "fear gauge") up to ~30, oil and gas up, gold bid.

**The SWIFT cutoff and reserve freeze (26 Feb–2 Mar 2022).** Over a single weekend the G7 announced two things at once: selected Russian banks would be removed from SWIFT, and the Russian central bank's reserves held in the West would be frozen. The ruble cratered (briefly past 130 to the dollar, from ~80 pre-war), and Russia responded with emergency **capital controls** and a doubling of its policy rate to 20% to stop the bleeding. (On the legal limits and effects of capital controls, see [capital controls and the legal limits on money flows](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/capital-controls-and-the-legal-limits-on-money-flows).)

**The energy scramble (Mar–Aug 2022).** With Russian supply suddenly carrying political risk and Russia throttling pipeline gas to Europe in retaliation, the energy complex went vertical. Brent peaked at ~\$128 on 8 March; European gas marched up through the summer to that €339 peak on 26 August as Europe raced to fill storage before winter without Russian flows.

**The corporate exodus (Mar 2022 onward).** Western firms — from energy majors to consumer brands — announced withdrawals, suspensions, and write-downs. Some moves were legally compelled; many were reputational or precautionary. The financial result was billions in one-time impairments on Russian assets across Western balance sheets.

**The oil price cap (Dec 2022).** The slowest-moving tool came last, deliberately. By the time the \$60 cap took effect, the acute panic had passed, oil had faded to the mid-\$80s, and Russia had begun building the shadow fleet and redirecting barrels east. The cap was designed to keep oil flowing while compressing Russian revenue — a fundamentally different goal from the February measures, which were about shock and deterrence.

The key takeaway from the sequence: **the biggest price moves came from the energy channel, not from the financial-system measures themselves.** The reserve freeze was the most unprecedented act, but it did not spike a tradable price the way the gas blowout did. The two effects operate on completely different clocks — one is a slow structural question, the other was an acute supply scare.

It is worth dwelling on *why* the sequencing mattered so much for prices, because it is the single most common thing traders get wrong about sanctions. A sanction is not one event with one reaction; it is a chain of events, each of which the market prices *as it learns the design*. On the weekend of the SWIFT cutoff and reserve freeze, the market did not know how Russia would respond, how much energy would actually be cut, or how the carve-outs would work. So it priced the *worst plausible case* — a wholesale loss of Russian energy — which is what drove Brent to \$128 and gas toward its peak. As the actual design became clear (energy carve-outs, a price cap rather than an embargo, barrels rerouting east), the market un-priced the worst case and oil faded. The lesson: the first reaction to a sanctions shock is usually an *overshoot* priced off the worst-case interpretation, and the durable trade is often the *fade* as the real, more limited design becomes visible. The exception is the captive commodity (European gas) where there genuinely was no quick substitute, so the high price persisted.

This overshoot-then-clarify pattern is also why **the Geopolitical Risk index spiked and then receded**. The GPR index — a measure built from how often major newspapers mention war, geopolitical tension, and related terms — jumped sharply in February–March 2022 as the invasion dominated headlines (a reading of 277 against a long-run mean of 100), then drifted back down over the following two years even though the war and the sanctions persisted. Geopolitical-risk gauges measure *attention and surprise*, not the ongoing level of conflict; they spike on the shock and fade as the situation becomes the new normal, which is exactly why they are useful for timing the *initial* risk-off move but useless for timing the *structural* trades (defense, gold) that play out over years.

![Geopolitical risk index spiked to 277 on the invasion below the 9 11 reading of 512 and faded to 140 by 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance-9.png)

Notice in the figure that even the invasion's reading of 277 sat well *below* the 512 of 9/11 — a useful calibration. The 2022 shock was severe but not the most extreme geopolitical event of the century by this measure, and it faded faster than the persistence of the war would suggest, because markets habituate. For the practitioner this is the difference between a *catalyst* and a *regime*: the GPR spike is a catalyst (trade the first move, expect it to fade), while the reset in defense budgets and the slow erosion of reserve trust are regimes (position for years, ignore the noise).

## The energy repricing: where the money actually moved

If you traded one thing in 2022, you traded energy. The financial measures grabbed the headlines; the energy market grabbed the P&L.

Brent crude is the clearest picture. It entered the year near \$79, sat at ~\$97 on the eve of the invasion (already pricing the risk), gapped to \$99 on the day, and peaked at **\$127.98 on 8 March 2022** as the market feared a wholesale removal of Russian barrels. Then — and this is the part most people forget — it *faded*, settling back into the mid-\$80s by year-end as it became clear the barrels were rerouting rather than disappearing.

![Brent crude spiked to about 128 dollars per barrel after the invasion then faded to the mid 80s by year end](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance-2.png)

European natural gas was a far more violent and consequential move, because gas is much harder to ship around the world than oil. Oil is fungible and travels on tankers everywhere; pipeline gas is captive to its pipes, and the LNG (liquefied natural gas) needed to replace it requires expensive terminals and ships that take years to build. So when Russia throttled pipeline flows, Europe had no quick substitute and had to *bid up* the global LNG market against Asia. The benchmark Dutch TTF contract went from a normal ~€25/MWh in mid-2021 to **€339/MWh on 26 August 2022** — a roughly **13x** move that translated directly into crippling electricity bills, shuttered fertilizer and aluminum plants, and tens of billions in government subsidies to cap household bills.

![European natural gas TTF blew out about 13 times to 339 euros per megawatt hour in 2022](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance-4.png)

#### Worked example: a German utility's gas cost when TTF goes from €25 to €339

Take a mid-sized industrial gas buyer that consumes **1 million MWh** of gas a year (roughly the appetite of a large chemical or fertilizer plant). At the "normal" pre-crisis price of **€25/MWh**, the annual gas bill is:

```
1,000,000 MWh x EUR 25/MWh = EUR 25,000,000  (EUR 25m)
```

At the August 2022 peak of **€339/MWh**, the same volume costs:

```
1,000,000 MWh x EUR 339/MWh = EUR 339,000,000  (EUR 339m)
```

That is a **€314m increase** — the bill went up by **13.6x**, an extra €314 million for the identical molecules. No business with a normal margin can absorb a cost line jumping 13-fold, which is exactly why European heavy industry idled plants, why governments stepped in with price caps, and why "demand destruction" (using less gas because it became unaffordable) was the main thing that eventually brought the price back down. The intuition: when a critical input has no fast substitute, its price is set not by its cost but by what the most desperate buyer will pay before going dark.

The repricing went beyond the front-month price. The whole forward curve, electricity prices (gas sets the marginal power price in much of Europe), carbon, fertilizer, and shipping all moved together. And it fed straight into inflation — the European energy spike was a major reason the European Central Bank ended up hiking rates aggressively, which then repriced bonds, the euro, and rate-sensitive equities. A geopolitical shock had become a monetary-policy story. (For how rate decisions transmit into every other asset, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).)

This transmission chain — geopolitics to energy to inflation to interest rates to every other asset — is the single most important thing to internalize about the 2022 episode, because it is why a war in Eastern Europe ended up repricing a homeowner's mortgage in Ohio. Energy is an input to almost everything: it moves goods, powers factories, makes fertilizer (which sets food prices), and heats homes. When its price multiplies, that cost works its way into the price of nearly every other good with a lag of a few months. Central banks, whose job is price stability, respond by raising rates to cool demand. Higher rates then reprice bonds (yields up, prices down), compress the valuations of long-duration growth stocks (whose value depends on distant cash flows discounted at a now-higher rate), and strengthen or weaken currencies depending on who hikes fastest. So the practitioner reading a sanctions-driven energy shock should immediately ask the *next* question: how much of this energy inflation will force central banks to tighten, and what does that tightening do to my bond, equity, and currency books? The energy spike is the first domino; the rate response is the one that touches everything.

## The commodity-flow rerouting: the barrels did not stop, they turned east

Here is the single most important lesson of the whole episode for anyone who trades commodities: **sanctions on a fungible commodity reroute it; they rarely remove it.** Oil is the most fungible thing in the world. If Europe stops buying Russian crude, that crude does not evaporate — it sails to whoever will buy it, at whatever discount clears the market.

And that is exactly what happened. Pre-war, Europe took roughly half of Russia's crude exports, carried on Western tankers with Western insurance and paid in dollars and euros through SWIFT. Within a year, the map had been redrawn: the dominant buyers became **India, China, and Turkey**; the barrels moved on a **shadow fleet** of hundreds of older tankers with opaque ownership and non-G7 insurance; the price was a steep discount (Russia's Urals grade traded **\$20–30 below Brent** at the widest); and payment shifted toward **rupees, yuan, and dirhams** to sidestep the dollar plumbing.

![Before and after comparison of Russian oil flows showing rerouting to Asia at a discount](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance-5.png)

This rerouting is why the "sanctions will collapse Russia's oil revenue" thesis was only half right. Russia's *volumes* held up remarkably well; what it lost was the *margin* — the discount it had to grant new buyers, plus the higher cost of running a shadow fleet and arranging non-standard payment. The price cap was specifically designed to widen and entrench that discount.

#### Worked example: the Urals discount and a refiner's pickup on one cargo

Consider an Indian refiner buying a standard crude cargo of **1 million barrels** when Brent is **\$85/bbl** and Russian Urals is trading at a **\$25/bbl discount**, i.e. **\$60/bbl**. Compare the landed cost to a non-discounted alternative:

```
Brent-priced cargo:  1,000,000 bbl x USD 85 = USD 85,000,000
Urals-priced cargo:  1,000,000 bbl x USD 60 = USD 60,000,000
Discount captured:   USD 25,000,000 per cargo
```

On a single cargo the refiner pockets **\$25 million** in raw input savings. If it imports, say, 30 such cargoes a year, that is **\$750m** of annual margin uplift — a windfall that flowed straight to the bottom line of Indian and Chinese refiners, who then often re-exported refined products (diesel, jet fuel) back into Western markets at full price. The intuition: a sanction that creates a forced seller hands a structural discount to whichever buyers are willing to take the political and logistical risk — the discount is the price of looking the other way.

This is also why on-chain-style "follow the flows" analysis became a cottage industry in commodities: tracking dark tankers (ships that switch off their transponders), reading bills of lading, and watching which ports the shadow fleet calls at. The sanction created a giant arbitrage, and arbitrage attracts both traders and enforcement.

The **shadow fleet** deserves a closer look, because it is the physical embodiment of what happens when you try to gate a fungible commodity. Before 2022, a Russian crude cargo moved on a modern tanker, insured by a London or Norwegian protection-and-indemnity (P&I) club, financed by a Western trade bank, and tracked openly by its transponder. To sell above the price cap without Western services, an entirely parallel logistics system had to be assembled almost overnight: hundreds of older tankers (many near the end of their working lives), bought up through opaque shell ownership, insured by unknown or state-backed entities of dubious credibility, often going "dark" by switching off their automatic identification system, sometimes transferring oil ship-to-ship in the open ocean to disguise its origin. This fleet is estimated to number well over 600 vessels.

Every layer of that workaround is a cost. Older ships command higher charter rates and carry higher accident and spill risk. Murky insurance means that if one of these tankers runs aground, there may be no credible insurer to pay for the cleanup — a genuine environmental hazard that has prompted coastal states to start checking insurance documents in chokepoints like the Danish Straits. Ship-to-ship transfers and dark voyages add days and fuel. All of that friction is, in effect, a *second* tax on the seller stacked on top of the price discount: Russia keeps selling, but a larger slice of each barrel's value is eaten by the cost of evading the system. The price cap's designers understood this — the goal was never to stop the oil but to force as much of the revenue as possible into either the capped (low-price) channel or the expensive (shadow) channel, squeezing the seller's net take from both sides.

For an investor, the shadow fleet is also a *signal source*. The clearest real-time read on whether sanctions are tightening or leaking is not a government press release — it is the data: the Urals-to-Brent discount (narrowing means leakage, widening means enforcement is biting), tanker tracking that shows how many vessels are going dark and how long voyages have become, and the freight rates for the aging ships the shadow fleet relies on. When enforcement tightens — new designations of specific tankers, secondary-sanction threats against ports and insurers — the discount widens and the shadow-fleet freight market tightens. When it leaks, the discount narrows. Trading the sanction means trading those second-order data series, not the headline.

## The corporate exodus and the write-downs

The fourth tool was less a government action than a stampede. Within months of the invasion, the running count of Western companies curtailing operations in Russia exceeded **1,000**. The reasons were a mix: some were legally forced out by sanctions, some feared secondary sanctions, many acted on reputational pressure and the simple operational impossibility of doing business when payments, logistics, and staff safety had all broken down.

For markets, the visible effect was a wave of **write-downs** — one-time, non-cash charges as companies marked the value of their Russian assets down toward zero. Energy majors took the largest hits, writing off stakes in Russian joint ventures and oilfields, in some cases more than \$20bn for a single company. Consumer, industrial, and financial firms followed with smaller charges. These impairments dented reported earnings in 2022 but, because they were one-time and non-cash, markets generally looked through them — the share-price reaction was usually muted once the size of the charge was known and bounded. The bigger long-run effect was strategic: a generation of corporate boards learned that a single geopolitical event could strand an entire country's worth of assets overnight, which permanently raised the risk premium on operating in geopolitically exposed markets. (For how that kind of regulatory and geopolitical exposure becomes a priced risk factor, see [regulatory risk as an asset pricing factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/regulatory-risk-as-an-asset-pricing-factor).)

There is a subtle but important market lesson in *why the share-price reaction was muted*. A write-down is an accounting event, not a cash event: the company is acknowledging that an asset it already owned is worth less than its books said. The cash was spent years ago; marking it to zero today does not drain the bank account. Sophisticated investors had already discounted most Russian exposure the moment the invasion made it un-monetizable, so by the time the formal impairment hit the quarterly report, the news was stale. This is a recurring pattern with geopolitical write-downs: the *price* adjusts when the asset becomes impaired in reality, while the *reported earnings* adjust later when accounting catches up. The trap for a naive investor is to react to the headline impairment as if it were new information; the experienced one recognizes the market priced the loss months earlier and looks instead at whether the *ongoing* business — stripped of the lost geography — is healthier or weaker than the headline suggests. The durable signal from the exodus was not the one-time charges but the permanent upward reset in how much extra return investors now demand to own assets in any country that could plausibly become the next sanctions target.

## The reserve freeze and the de-dollarization debate

Now the deep question — the one with the longest tail. Freezing \$300bn of a major central bank's reserves was the most novel act of 2022, and its consequences are measured not in days but in decades.

#### Worked example: how much of the war chest was switched off

Russia entered the war with roughly **\$643bn** in total reserves — one of the largest war chests on earth, deliberately built up after 2014 precisely to sanction-proof the economy. The G7 froze the portion held in their jurisdictions:

```
Total reserves:            USD 643bn
Frozen (immobilized):      USD 300bn
Share frozen:              300 / 643 = 46.7%  (~ half)
Still usable:              643 - 300 = USD 343bn
```

Nearly **47%** of the war chest was switched off overnight. What survived was the part Russia had deliberately moved *out* of Western reach: **gold** held in domestic vaults (which no foreign sovereign can freeze) and reserves held in **Chinese yuan**. The lesson Russia's own pre-war strategy taught the rest of the world is the punchline of this whole post: *the only reserves you truly control are the ones no other sovereign holds in custody.* That single fact is the seed of the de-dollarization debate.

![Russia foreign exchange reserves of 643 billion dollars with 300 billion or 47 percent frozen by the G7](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance-6.png)

The logic runs like this. The dollar's role as the world's reserve currency rests on a promise that has never been seriously tested for a large economy: that dollar assets are *safe* — politically neutral, always available, never confiscated. Freezing Russia's reserves demonstrated that the promise has an asterisk: dollar (and euro, and sterling) assets are safe *unless the issuing government decides you are an adversary*. Every reserve manager from Beijing to Riyadh to Brasília watched this happen and had to update their risk model, even if their own relationship with the West is friendly. The question is no longer "are these assets safe from default?" but "are these assets safe from *politics*?"

![Diagram of the de-dollarization debate from the reserve freeze through diversification to the evidence so far](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance-7.png)

So did the dollar collapse? **No — and this is critical to get right.** The evidence so far is a slow drift, not an exit. The dollar's share of global central-bank reserves has been edging down for two decades, from the low-70s percent in the early 2000s toward roughly **59%** today, and 2022 did not produce a visible step-change in that trend. The dollar is still overwhelmingly the currency of trade invoicing, debt issuance, and FX trading, for the simple reason that there is no ready substitute: the euro has no unified safe asset at scale, the yuan is not freely convertible and sits behind capital controls, and gold cannot settle a trillion dollars of daily trade. (For why the dollar's dominance is so structurally sticky, see [petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance).)

Where the effect *is* visible is at the margin, and most clearly in **gold**. Central banks — led by China, Turkey, India, and others — bought gold at the fastest pace in decades after 2022, exceeding **1,000 tonnes a year** in 2022, 2023, and 2024. Gold is the one reserve asset with no issuer, no counterparty, and no government that can freeze it. The surge in official gold buying is the cleanest market fingerprint of the reserve freeze: it is what diversification *looks like* when you cannot yet replace the dollar but want an asset no sovereign can switch off. (For how gold behaves as the ultimate apolitical reserve, see [fear and the safe-haven trade: how gold behaves in a crisis](/blog/trading/gold/fear-and-the-safe-haven-trade-how-gold-behaves-in-a-crisis).)

The honest summary: the reserve freeze did not kill the dollar, and it probably will not soon. But it added a permanent **political risk premium** to dollar-bloc reserves for non-aligned countries, accelerated a pre-existing diversification trend at the margin, and turbocharged official gold demand. The tail risk — a multi-decade erosion of dollar dominance — is real but slow, and betting on it requires enormous patience.

To understand why the dollar is so hard to dislodge, it helps to separate the three jobs a reserve currency does, because the freeze only weakened one of them. The dollar is (1) a **store of value** — what central banks park reserves in; (2) a **medium of exchange** — what cross-border trade is invoiced and settled in; and (3) a **unit of account** — what commodities and global debt are priced in. The reserve freeze attacked job (1): it made dollar reserves feel less safe for non-aligned states, which is exactly why those states are diversifying into gold. But it barely touched jobs (2) and (3), and those are held in place by **network effects** that no single decision can break. Oil is priced in dollars because everyone prices oil in dollars; debt is issued in dollars because that is where the deepest, most liquid pool of lenders sits; trade is invoiced in dollars because your counterparty's bank, and their counterparty's bank, all clear in dollars. Network effects are sticky precisely because no individual actor benefits from being the first to switch. A country can move its *reserves* into gold unilaterally; it cannot unilaterally re-denominate the global oil trade.

This is why the de-dollarization story is best understood as **a slow leak in one of three tanks, not a burst dam.** The "store of value" tank is leaking — visibly, into gold and at the margin into yuan and other currencies. The "medium of exchange" and "unit of account" tanks are essentially intact, because they are defended by network effects rather than by any promise of safety. Anyone trading "de-dollarization" needs to know which tank they are betting on. The gold bid is a real, observable trade on the leaking tank. "The dollar loses reserve-currency status" is a bet on all three tanks draining, which on the evidence is a multi-decade proposition at best.

It is also worth being precise about what the alternatives can and cannot do, because the absence of a substitute is the whole reason the dollar endures. The euro is a credible reserve currency but lacks a single, unified, deep safe asset at the scale of US Treasuries — the eurozone has no common bond market on that scale, which caps how much reserve money can sit in euros. The yuan is the currency of the world's second-largest economy, but it is not freely convertible; it sits behind capital controls, which means a reserve manager holding yuan cannot freely move large sums out when it needs to — and the entire point of a reserve is that you can use it in a crisis. Gold has no issuer to freeze it, which is its great strength, but it pays no yield, costs money to store and insure, and cannot settle the roughly seven trillion dollars of daily global FX turnover. Each candidate fails a different essential test, which is why diversification happens *at the margin* rather than as a wholesale switch. The freeze did not change those structural facts; it only raised the political cost of *not* diversifying within those constraints. (For the full structural argument, see [the dollar system and de-dollarization](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-dollar-system-and-de-dollarization).)

## The defense-spending re-rating

One more durable repricing. The invasion did not just spike energy; it permanently reset the *expected path of defense spending* across NATO and beyond. Countries that had spent years below NATO's 2%-of-GDP target announced sharp increases; Germany announced a one-time €100bn special fund and a commitment to sustained higher spending. Higher and more durable defense budgets flow directly to defense contractors' order books.

Markets re-rated defense stocks accordingly — and crucially, this was a **structural re-rating, not a spike.** A pure event spike would have faded like the oil price did. Instead, US aerospace-and-defense rose steadily and *kept* rising: an index rebased to 100 at the eve of the invasion was up roughly **38% by the end of 2024**. The market was pricing not a one-quarter bump but a multi-year regime of higher budgets.

![US aerospace and defense index rebased to 100 at the invasion eve rose about 38 percent by end 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-russia-sanctions-and-the-weaponization-of-finance-8.png)

#### Worked example: the math of a defense-stock re-rating

Re-ratings happen through two channels: higher expected earnings *and* a higher multiple the market is willing to pay for those earnings. Take a defense contractor earning **\$5.00 per share** that the market valued at a **15x** price-to-earnings multiple before the war:

```
Pre-war price:   USD 5.00 EPS x 15.0  = USD 75.00
```

Now suppose the war does two things. Backlogs swell, so the market expects earnings to grow — say to **\$5.75 EPS**. And because the higher-budget regime looks durable, investors pay a higher multiple — say **18x** — for the same earnings stream:

```
Post-war price:  USD 5.75 EPS x 18.0  = USD 103.50
Total re-rating: (103.50 - 75.00) / 75.00 = +38.0%
```

The stock is up **38%**, and you can decompose it: earnings growth (5.75/5.00 = +15%) and multiple expansion (18/15 = +20%) compound to roughly +38%. The intuition: the most powerful re-ratings happen when a geopolitical shift raises *both* how much a company will earn *and* how confident the market is that the earnings will keep coming — multiple expansion on top of earnings growth is what turns a good year into a structural re-rating. (For how to decompose price moves into earnings vs. multiple, see [valuation and the building blocks of equity research](/blog/trading/equity-research/discounted-cash-flow-dcf-valuation-from-first-principles).)

## Common misconceptions

The 2022 episode generated more confident wrong takes than almost any market event of the decade. Here are the three that cost people money, each corrected with numbers.

### Misconception 1: "The sanctions quickly collapsed the Russian economy"

The ruble's crash to 130/\$ in early March looked like a knockout blow, and many positioned for a Russian economic collapse. It did not happen on that timeline. Capital controls and a 20% policy rate stabilized the ruble within weeks (it actually *strengthened* past pre-war levels by mid-2022). And the energy revenue kept flowing: with oil near \$100+ for much of 2022, Russia's *energy export earnings actually rose* that year even as volumes shifted east, because the price gain initially outran the discount. The economy contracted only modestly in 2022, far less than the double-digit crash many predicted.

The correction: sanctions on a large, resource-rich, fungible-export economy work *slowly and at the margin*, by raising costs and compressing margins over years — not by triggering an overnight collapse. Russia adapted: it rerouted oil, built a shadow fleet, found non-dollar payment, and substituted imports through third countries. The barrels found buyers. Anyone short "Russian collapse" in 2022 was early at best and wrong at worst.

### Misconception 2: "The reserve freeze killed the dollar"

The freeze was genuinely unprecedented, and the "this is the end of dollar hegemony" takes were everywhere. Years later, the dollar's reserve share is ~59% — down a touch, consistent with a two-decade gentle drift, with **no visible step-change** attributable to 2022. The dollar's share of trade invoicing, FX volume, and global debt remains overwhelming because there is no scalable substitute.

The correction: the freeze added a *political risk premium* and accelerated diversification *at the margin* — most visibly in the 1,000+-tonnes-a-year central-bank gold bid — but it did not produce an exodus, because reserve managers have nowhere big and liquid enough to go. De-dollarization is a real, slow, marginal trend, not a 2022 event. Trading it as an event was a category error.

### Misconception 3: "The oil price cap failed"

Critics on one side said the cap failed because Russian oil kept flowing (true, by design); critics on the other said it failed because Russia found ways around it (also partly true). Both judge it against the wrong goal. The cap was never meant to stop the oil — that would have spiked global prices. It was meant to **keep the oil flowing while compressing the price Russia receives**, and on that metric the record is *mixed*, not a failure: Urals traded at a **\$20–30 discount** to Brent at the widest, which is real lost revenue, even though enforcement leaked as the shadow fleet grew and the discount narrowed over time.

The correction: judge a tool by its actual objective. The cap's goal was revenue compression without a supply shock, and it delivered a meaningful (if eroding) discount while keeping barrels on the water. "Failed" and "succeeded" are both too simple; "partially worked, with leakage" is the accurate verdict.

## How it shows up in real markets

Step back from the specific event and the 2022 episode becomes a *template* for how a financial-warfare shock reprices markets. Four fingerprints recur:

**The energy spike.** A geopolitical shock to a major energy exporter spikes the relevant energy benchmark first and fastest — Brent to \$128, TTF to €339 — because energy is the channel with the least substitutability on short notice. The spike is often *transitory* if the commodity is fungible (oil faded) and *persistent* if it is captive (gas stayed elevated longer because Europe could not quickly replace pipeline volumes).

**The commodity-flow discount.** Sanctions on a fungible export create a *forced seller* and hand a structural discount to risk-tolerant buyers. The trade is the discount (Urals \$20–30 under Brent) and the secondary beneficiaries (Indian/Chinese refiners, the shadow-fleet shipping market). This is durable while sanctions hold.

**The defense re-rating.** A shift in the geopolitical regime that raises *expected defense budgets* re-rates defense contractors structurally (+38% over 2022–24), via both higher earnings and higher multiples. Unlike the energy spike, this is a slow-burn, multi-year theme, not an event trade.

**The safe-haven / de-dollarization bid.** Weaponizing reserves adds a political risk premium to the dollar bloc and bids up the one apolitical reserve asset: gold (1,000+ tonnes/year of official buying). This is the slowest, most marginal, and most patient of the four trades — a tail hedge, not a catalyst trade.

The connective tissue across all four: **read the rule change for its second-order effect, not its first-order headline.** The headline was "the West sanctioned Russia." The tradable consequences were a gas blowout in Germany, a discount for an Indian refiner, a multiple expansion for a US defense contractor, and a gold bid in Beijing — none of which is obvious from the headline. (For the general framework of how a rule becomes a price, see [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain).)

## How to trade it: the sanctions-shock playbook

The point of all this is to be ready *the next time* a major financial-warfare shock hits — and there will be a next time, because 2022 proved the tools work and lowered the threshold for using them. Here is the practitioner's playbook, organized by the four fingerprints, with the catalysts to watch and what invalidates each leg.

### Leg 1 — Energy: be long the squeezed benchmark, but know the half-life

**The position.** On a confirmed supply shock to a major energy exporter, the first and highest-conviction trade is long the *most captive* energy benchmark. In 2022 that was European gas, not oil, because gas had no fast substitute while oil could reroute. Distinguish fungible (oil — spikes then fades) from captive (pipeline gas, regional power — spikes and *stays*).

**The signal.** Watch the forward curve shape. A spike that is steeply *backwardated* (front prices far above later ones) says the market expects the disruption to be temporary; a curve that shifts up *in parallel* says the market is pricing a persistent regime. The 2022 gas curve shifted up persistently — that was the tell to stay long longer.

**What invalidates it.** Demand destruction (the price gets so high that buyers simply use less), a fast substitute coming online (LNG terminals, alternative pipelines), or a mild winter. European gas ultimately fell because all three arrived: industry idled, LNG imports surged, and storage filled. When you see demand destruction in the data, the long is done.

### Leg 2 — Commodities: own the discount and the reroute beneficiaries

**The position.** Sanctions on a fungible export create a discount and a set of beneficiaries. You generally cannot trade the sanctioned barrel directly, so trade the *consequences*: the refiners and economies that get the cheap input (the Indian and Chinese refining complex captured a \$25m-per-cargo windfall), and the shipping/insurance markets that price the rerouting (tanker rates, the shadow-fleet economy).

**The signal.** The width and persistence of the discount (Urals-to-Brent spread), tanker tracking data (dark fleet activity, voyage lengths — rerouting east lengthens voyages and tightens tanker supply), and import statistics from the new buyer countries.

**What invalidates it.** Enforcement tightening that closes the discount, the sanctioned producer cutting volumes (which would shrink the available discount), or a political settlement that lifts sanctions and lets flows snap back to their old, cheaper routes.

### Leg 3 — Defense: a structural, multi-year re-rating

**The position.** A regime shift that durably raises defense budgets is a *long* in defense contractors and the broader military-industrial supply chain — but sized as a multi-year theme, not an event trade. The re-rating comes from both earnings growth (swelling backlogs) and multiple expansion (the market believing the higher-budget regime is durable).

**The signal.** Announced multi-year budget commitments (Germany's €100bn fund, NATO 2%+ pledges), order backlog growth in the quarterly reports, and the durability of the political consensus behind higher spending.

**What invalidates it.** A credible peace that collapses the threat premium, a fiscal crunch that forces budget cuts, or valuations that have already fully priced the higher-budget regime (when the multiple has expanded as far as the new normal justifies, the easy re-rating is over).

### Leg 4 — The de-dollarization tail: a patient gold hedge, not a catalyst trade

**The position.** Weaponizing reserves is structurally bullish for the one apolitical reserve asset. The cleanest expression is *gold* — held as a long-horizon hedge against the slow erosion of dollar-bloc trust, with the central-bank bid as the structural buyer underneath. This is a tail hedge: small, patient, and not expected to pay off on any particular catalyst.

**The signal.** Official-sector gold buying (the 1,000+-tonnes-a-year pace), the dollar's reserve share in the quarterly IMF COFER data, and the buildout of non-dollar payment rails (CIPS volumes, bilateral local-currency trade agreements).

**What invalidates the *fast* version.** The thesis is *slow* by nature — so what invalidates a *de-dollarization event trade* (as opposed to the patient hedge) is simply the data: a stable ~59% dollar reserve share with no step-change means the "dollar collapse" trade has no catalyst and bleeds carry while you wait. Trade it as a hedge, never as a near-term catalyst. The patient gold hedge stays on; the leveraged "short the dollar now" bet is the loser's version.

### Putting the legs together

The master move is *sequencing*. In the first days of a financial-warfare shock, the energy and safe-haven legs pay first and fastest. Over weeks, the commodity-discount and reroute trades develop. Over months and years, the defense re-rating compounds and the de-dollarization tail slowly thickens. A practitioner does not hold all four with equal size — they rotate weight from the fast, fading legs (the energy spike) toward the slow, structural ones (defense, gold) as the shock matures. And across all of them, the discipline is the same one this whole series preaches: **read the rule change for its repricing, size it, and write down in advance exactly what would prove you wrong.** (For the full cross-asset version of this discipline, see [the law, policy and geopolitics playbook](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-law-policy-and-geopolitics-playbook).)

The largest lesson of 2022 sits above any individual trade, and it is the one to carry forward. The episode proved that the plumbing of global finance — the messaging network, the reserve-custody system, the maritime-insurance market — is not neutral infrastructure but a set of *chokepoints* that the states which control them can switch on and off. That is a permanent feature of the world now, not a one-time event, because the tools worked and the threshold for using them has fallen. For an investor, the implication is that geopolitics has moved from a tail-risk footnote to a front-line pricing factor: the location of an asset, the currency of a reserve, the route of a cargo, and the citizenship of a counterparty all now carry a measurable, repriceable risk premium. The practitioners who did well out of 2022 were not the ones who predicted the war; they were the ones who, the moment the rules changed, asked the right second-order question — *which price does this chokepoint move, on what clock, and what would tell me I am wrong?* — and sized their answer with discipline rather than conviction.
