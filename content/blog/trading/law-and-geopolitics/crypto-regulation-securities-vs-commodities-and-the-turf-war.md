> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A crypto token's price is driven as much by its *legal status* as by its technology: whether a regulator calls it a "security" or a "commodity," whether a spot ETF is approved, and how its stablecoins are backed decide what can be listed, custodied, and held by institutions — and each ruling reprices the whole asset class.
>
> - The **Howey test** decides if a token is a "security." If yes, the SEC claims it, and registration rules can force exchanges to delist it — a liquidity event worth tens of percent. If no, it is usually a "commodity" the CFTC oversees, and it trades freely.
> - The **US spot-bitcoin-ETF approval** in January 2024 was a structural *demand unlock*: a spot ETF must buy and hold real coin, so inflows pull supply off the market. Bitcoin ran from roughly \$27k in October 2023 to ~\$73k by March 2024 around that event — with a textbook "sell the news" dip first.
> - **Stablecoins are the systemic node.** A fiat-backed coin holds cash and T-bills; an algorithmic one holds only a promise. When Terra's UST broke its peg in May 2022, ~\$40bn evaporated in days and dragged the whole market down — that is the contagion regulators now write rules to prevent.
> - **The one number to remember:** the 2024 US spot-bitcoin ETFs pulled in roughly **\$35bn of net inflows in their first year** — real, regulation-enabled demand that did not exist before the rule changed.

On the morning of January 10, 2024, the US Securities and Exchange Commission did something it had refused to do for more than a decade: it approved eleven spot-bitcoin exchange-traded funds. For ten years the agency had rejected every application, arguing the underlying market was too easy to manipulate. Nothing about bitcoin's code changed that day. The blockchain produced the same block every ten minutes it always had. What changed was a *rule* — and bitcoin, which had traded near \$27,000 three months earlier, was on its way to \$73,000 by March.

That is the whole thesis of this post in one event. In most asset classes you analyze the cash flows, the supply and demand, the macro backdrop. In crypto you do all of that *and* you analyze the law, because the law decides who is even allowed to buy. A token that an exchange must delist loses its buyers overnight. A token wrapped in an ETF gains an army of new ones — pension funds, registered investment advisors, retirement accounts — who were legally or operationally barred from touching it the day before. The rule is not background noise. The rule is the demand curve.

This is the opening post of the series' sector track, and crypto is the purest case in the whole series of the spine we keep returning to: *law changes the rules of the game, markets discount that change into prices, and the practitioner who reads the rule-change early sizes the repricing*. We will build every term from zero — what a "security" even is, why the SEC and the CFTC are fighting over crypto, what a spot ETF actually does mechanically, and why one stablecoin's failure can take down the others. Then we will turn each mechanism into a trade.

## Foundations: what a "security" is, and why the label is the whole game

Start with the single most important word in this entire post: **security**. In everyday English it means safety. In financial law it means something completely different and specific: a tradable financial instrument that represents an *investment* — most importantly, an investment where you hand money to someone else and expect *them* to make it grow for you. A share of stock is a security. A bond is a security. An ownership stake in a startup is a security.

Why does it matter so much whether something is legally a "security"? Because the moment an instrument is a security in the United States, an enormous body of law switches on. The issuer must **register** it with the SEC and publish detailed disclosures — audited financials, risk factors, who runs the company. The venues that trade it must be **registered exchanges** with their own rulebooks. The firms that hold it for you must be **registered custodians**. None of that is optional. Selling an unregistered security is itself a violation, regardless of whether anyone was harmed.

Take the practical consequence for a crypto token. If a token is declared a security, a US exchange that lists it without registering as a securities exchange is breaking the law. So the exchange does the only safe thing: it **delists** the token, or geofences it out of the US. Delisting is not a paperwork nuisance — it is a *liquidity event*. The buyers vanish, the bid-ask spread blows out, and the price gaps down. The technology is unchanged; the access is destroyed. That is why, in crypto, the security-or-not question is worth more than almost any technical roadmap.

It helps to separate the three distinct rules that the "security" label triggers, because each one cuts off a different channel of demand. The first is **registration**: a security must be registered with the SEC (or sold under a narrow exemption), and registration means publishing a detailed prospectus and ongoing financial reports. Most crypto projects never did this — they sold tokens to the public without registering — so the moment the token is deemed a security, those past sales become alleged violations and the project is on the defensive. The second is **the exchange rule**: a venue that matches buyers and sellers of securities must register as a national securities exchange or operate as an alternative trading system, with surveillance, recordkeeping, and conflict-of-interest rules. Crypto exchanges were not built to those standards, so they cannot legally list a token once it is a security — hence the delisting. The third is **custody**: a registered investment advisor must hold client securities with a *qualified custodian*, and for years there was no clear qualified custodian for crypto. No custody means the largest pools of professional money — the advisors and funds that manage trillions — simply cannot hold the asset for clients even if they want to.

Stack those three together and you see why the label is so violent for price. A security designation does not merely add compliance cost; it severs the token from registered exchanges (where the deepest US liquidity lives), from registered advisors (who control the largest client pools), and from qualified custody (without which institutions are barred). Each severed channel is lost demand, and lost demand on a fixed or slowly growing supply is a lower price. The commodity designation cuts none of these channels — a commodity can list on a far lighter regime, be held without a securities custodian, and trade freely — which is exactly why projects fight so hard to *not* be securities.

### The Howey test: four questions that draw the line

So how does anyone decide whether a token is a security? The answer comes from a 1946 Supreme Court case, *SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.*, about — of all things — Florida orange groves. A company sold plots of an orange grove to buyers and then offered to manage the groves and split the profit. The buyers were not orange farmers; they were investors hoping the Howey company would make them money. The Court ruled that this arrangement, despite being a land sale on paper, was an **investment contract** — and an investment contract is a security.

From that case comes the **Howey test**, four questions that are still the law today. An arrangement is a security if it involves *all four* of:

1. **An investment of money** — you put capital in.
2. **In a common enterprise** — your fortune is pooled with others and tied to a shared venture.
3. **With an expectation of profit** — you are buying it to make money, not to use it.
4. **Derived from the efforts of others** — someone *else's* work (a team, a promoter, a developer) is what is supposed to make it valuable.

That fourth prong is the crypto battleground. When a startup sells a brand-new token to fund its own development team and promises that team will build something valuable, that looks exactly like prong four — profits from the efforts of others. Most of the 2017 "ICO" (initial coin offering) tokens fit all four prongs cleanly and the SEC treated them as unregistered securities. Bitcoin, by contrast, has no company, no team you are betting on, no central promoter; its value comes from a decentralized network, not someone's "efforts." It fails prong four, and that is precisely why even the SEC treats bitcoin as *not* a security.

The genuinely hard cases live in the middle, and they are where most of the value at risk sits. A token may *start* its life looking like a security — sold by a small team to fund development, with buyers clearly betting on that team's efforts — and then, as the network grows and decentralizes, arguably *stop* being one, because no single team's efforts drive the value anymore. This is the "sufficiently decentralized" idea: the same token could be a security at issuance and a commodity years later, which is a deeply uncomfortable place for the law to be, because an asset's legal nature is not supposed to drift with time. There is no bright-line test for when decentralization is "sufficient," and that vagueness is the source of much of the litigation and much of the priced uncertainty. A second hard case is the *secondary-market* question: even if the *original sale* of a token was an investment contract, is a later trade between two strangers on an exchange — neither of whom is dealing with the issuer — still a securities transaction? Courts have split on exactly this, and the answer determines whether an exchange can keep listing a token long after its initial sale. These are not academic puzzles; they are the open questions whose resolution reprices billions of dollars of tokens.

#### Worked example: pricing the delisting risk a "security" label creates

Suppose a mid-cap token — call it TOKN — trades at \$4.00 with \$200m of daily volume across US exchanges, and 60% of that volume is on US venues. An SEC complaint names TOKN as an unregistered security. The US exchanges, to stay compliant, announce they will delist it in 30 days for US customers.

Walk the repricing through. Roughly 60% of liquidity — \$120m of daily volume — is about to disappear from the deepest venues. Thin order books mean larger price impact per dollar sold, and the *expectation* of forced selling by US holders front-runs the actual delisting. A reasonable haircut for a forced-delisting of a token of this profile, based on past cases, is **30% to 50%**. Take 40%: TOKN reprices from \$4.00 toward \$2.40. If you held \$50,000 of TOKN, that is a \$20,000 paper loss — none of which has anything to do with whether the underlying protocol still works. The takeaway: the security label does not change what the token *does*; it changes *who is allowed to hold it*, and a forced exit of US buyers is what gets priced.

### The SEC versus the CFTC: one asset, two regulators, an unsettled border

Here is where it gets genuinely messy, and where a lot of the price volatility comes from. The United States does not have one crypto regulator. It has (at least) two, and they have overlapping, contested claims.

The **SEC** — the Securities and Exchange Commission — is the *securities* cop. Its authority comes from the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 (the two foundational statutes covered in our [securities-law primer](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/securities-law-101-the-33-and-34-acts-and-the-sec)). Its job is investor protection, and it claims any token that passes the Howey test.

The **CFTC** — the Commodity Futures Trading Commission — is the *derivatives and commodities* cop. Its authority comes from the Commodity Exchange Act. A **commodity** in this legal sense is not just corn and crude oil; the definition is broad enough that US courts have repeatedly held that bitcoin and ether are commodities. The CFTC oversees the *futures* markets on those commodities and polices fraud in the underlying spot markets, but it does not require a commodity itself to be "registered" the way a security must be. If your token is a commodity, you can list it, hold it, and trade it far more freely.

So the entire fight reduces to a single border: **is this token a security (SEC, heavy rules, delisting risk) or a commodity (CFTC, light rules, freely tradable)?** And the painful truth is that for the thousands of tokens between bitcoin and an obvious ICO scam, *nobody has a definitive answer*. There is no clean statute that says "spot tokens are commodities." The agencies assert overlapping claims; the courts decide case by case; and that unresolved gap is a permanent pricing overhang on the whole sector.

![SEC and CFTC split crypto along the security commodity border](/imgs/blogs/crypto-regulation-securities-vs-commodities-and-the-turf-war-3.png)

This jurisdictional fog is itself a *priced* risk, in exactly the way we describe in [regulatory risk as an asset-pricing factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/regulatory-risk-as-an-asset-pricing-factor). When you cannot know whether your token will be deemed a security next year, you demand a higher return to hold it — which is just another way of saying you pay a *lower* price for it today. Clarity, when it finally arrives, releases that discount.

## The enforcement-by-litigation era: when uncertainty is the overhang

For most of crypto's history in the United States, there was no comprehensive crypto statute. Congress had not passed a law that says "here is how tokens are classified and traded." Into that vacuum stepped the SEC, and the tool it used was not rulemaking — it was *litigation*. This is the **enforcement-by-litigation** era, and understanding it is essential because the uncertainty it created was, for years, the single biggest weight on token prices.

The mechanic is simple to describe and brutal in practice. Rather than publishing a clear rule that tells every project in advance "do X and you are compliant," the SEC would file lawsuits against specific exchanges and token issuers, alleging that *this particular token* was an unregistered security. Each suit was a coin flip with the whole project's US access on the line. Win, and you keep your listings. Lose, and US exchanges delist you to avoid being the next defendant. Even *being named* in a complaint — before any ruling — was enough to trigger preemptive delistings, because the exchanges did not want the legal exposure.

Why does this depress prices across the board, not just for the named token? Because the legal status of *most* tokens is genuinely unknown, and a suit against one token tells the market "the next one could be yours." The whole asset class trades with a discount baked in for the risk that any given holding gets labeled a security and loses its US liquidity. Academic finance has a name for this: a *risk premium*. Crypto's regulatory risk premium was enormous precisely because the rule was being written one lawsuit at a time, in public, with no warning.

#### Worked example: the regulatory discount embedded in a token's price

Put a number on that overhang. Say a basket of mid-cap "altcoins" has fundamentals — usage, fees, growth — that, in a world of legal certainty, would justify a price of \$1.00 per unit. The market assigns a 30% probability that, within a year, an SEC action gets the token delisted from US venues, and estimates the delisting would cut the price to \$0.50 (a 50% haircut). 

The expected value with the overhang is: 70% × \$1.00 (no action) + 30% × \$0.50 (delisted) = \$0.70 + \$0.15 = **\$0.85**. The token trades at \$0.85, not \$1.00 — a **15% discount** that is pure regulatory risk, unrelated to anything the protocol does. Now suppose a court ruling or a new statute *removes* the delisting threat. The 30% probability collapses toward zero, and the token re-rates from \$0.85 back toward \$1.00 — an 18% gain from clarity alone. The takeaway: a chunk of every uncertain token's price is a discount for the chance the law turns against it, and that discount is what a clarity catalyst pays out.

There is a subtle reason enforcement-by-litigation is *worse* for prices than an equally strict but *clear* rule. A clear rule, even a harsh one, lets a market price the outcome and move on — the discount settles to a known level and stops moving. Litigation, by contrast, keeps the outcome a live coin flip indefinitely: every new complaint, every court filing, every leaked Wells notice (the SEC's warning that charges may be coming) re-rolls the dice and re-shocks the price. The market cannot settle on a discount because the rule itself is still being written, in public, one case at a time. Volatility is the price of an unwritten rule, and crypto's regulatory volatility in this era was extreme precisely because no statute pinned the rule down.

The asymmetry matters for how the catalysts trade, too. Because the *threat* was the baseline, *adverse* news (a new suit, an unfavorable ruling) often moved prices less than *favorable* news (a dismissal, a court loss for the regulator, a clarity statute) — the bad case was already partly discounted, while the good case was a surprise that released the accumulated overhang. This is why some of the sharpest crypto rallies in the era came not from new technology but from the *regulator losing in court*, an outcome the market had not priced.

The most important single case in this era was about an ETF, not a suit, and it cracked the dam. For years the SEC had rejected spot-bitcoin-ETF applications. One applicant, Grayscale, sued the SEC over the rejection of its proposed conversion to a spot ETF. In August 2023 a federal appeals court ruled against the SEC, calling its rejection "arbitrary and capricious" — essentially saying the agency had approved *futures*-based bitcoin ETFs while rejecting *spot* ones on logic that did not hold together. That court loss is what forced the SEC's hand, and five months later the spot ETFs were approved. A *court ruling* — not a vote of Congress, not a change in bitcoin — repriced the asset. It is the cleanest demonstration in the whole series that the *judiciary*, not just the legislature or the regulator, is a market-moving branch: a court can overturn a regulator's stance and reprice an entire asset class in an afternoon.

## The spot-ETF approval as a structural demand unlock

To understand why the January 2024 approval mattered so much, you have to understand what a spot ETF *mechanically does* versus the futures ETFs that already existed — and why one removes coin from the market while the other does not.

An **ETF** (exchange-traded fund) is a fund whose shares trade on a stock exchange like a normal stock. You buy it in an ordinary brokerage or retirement account. Crucially, what the fund *holds* determines whether your money touches the real asset.

A **futures ETF**, which the SEC had allowed since 2021, does not hold any bitcoin at all. It holds bitcoin *futures contracts* — paper agreements to buy bitcoin at a set price on a future date, traded on a regulated derivatives exchange (the CME). Because each contract expires, the fund must continually "roll" — sell the expiring contract and buy a later-dated one. When later-dated contracts cost more (a condition called *contango*), that roll bleeds money, often 5%–10% a year. And here is the key point: a futures ETF buys *no real coin*. No bitcoin is removed from the market. It is a bet on the price, not a purchase of the asset.

A **spot ETF** is different in the one way that matters for supply and demand. "Spot" means the actual asset at today's price. A spot-bitcoin ETF must hold *real bitcoin*, custodied with a qualified custodian. When investors put a dollar into the ETF, the fund's authorized participants must go buy actual bitcoin to back the new shares. Every dollar of net inflow becomes a dollar of *real demand* for coin — and bitcoin's supply is fixed by its code, with no central party who can mint more to meet the demand. Inflows pull coin off the float and into long-term custody.

![Spot ETF buys real bitcoin while futures ETF holds paper](/imgs/blogs/crypto-regulation-securities-vs-commodities-and-the-turf-war-4.png)

There is one more mechanical detail worth knowing, because it determines whether inflows truly become coin demand. ETFs grow and shrink through *creation and redemption*: when demand for shares exceeds supply, authorized participants create new shares; when it falls, they redeem them. The US spot-bitcoin ETFs launched with *cash* creation — the participant hands the fund cash, and the fund buys the bitcoin — rather than *in-kind* creation, where the participant hands over coin directly. Either way, net new money into the ETF ends up as net new bitcoin purchased and custodied, so the supply-absorption logic holds; the cash-versus-in-kind distinction mostly affects tax efficiency and operational friction, not the core fact that inflows pull coin off the float.

This is the demand unlock. Before the spot ETF, an enormous pool of capital was effectively *barred* from bitcoin. A financial advisor at a large brokerage often cannot buy a token directly for a client — there is no approved custody, no ticker on the platform, compliance says no. A pension fund's mandate may forbid holding an asset with no qualified custodian. A spot ETF dissolves all of those barriers at once: now bitcoin is a ticker symbol the advisor can buy in the same account as Apple stock, with the same custody and reporting as any other fund. Capital that was structurally locked out becomes structurally able to flow in.

The flows confirmed the theory. The 2024 US spot-bitcoin ETFs pulled in on the order of **\$35bn of net inflows in their first year** — among the most successful ETF launches in history. That is real demand that simply did not exist the week before the rule changed. For the broader institutional-access story, see [bitcoin ETFs and the TradFi bridge](/blog/trading/crypto/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge).

#### Worked example: sizing the spot-ETF flow unlock against fixed supply

Quantify how a flow of new money translates into price when supply cannot expand. Suppose the spot ETFs attract \$35bn of net inflows over a year, and the issuers must buy real bitcoin with all of it. The hard part is that bitcoin's *available* float — coin actually for sale near the current price — is far smaller than its \$1tn-plus market cap, because a large share is held by long-term holders who will not sell at current levels.

Take a simplified but instructive model. Say the *thin, liquid* float responsive to new demand over the year is around \$100bn worth of coin. A demand shock of \$35bn against a \$100bn responsive float is enormous — it is asking the market to absorb roughly 35% more demand against the supply that is actually willing to move. Even with a conservative price sensitivity where each \$1 of net inflow lifts market value by, say, \$3–\$5 (a common finding in ETF and flow studies, because buying drives price, which draws more buying), \$35bn of inflow maps to \$105bn–\$175bn of added market value. On a ~\$1tn starting cap that is a **+10% to +18%** structural lift, *layered on top of* whatever the rest of the market is doing. The core idea: when you bolt a large new demand pipe onto an asset whose supply cannot grow, the price has to rise until enough reluctant holders are coaxed into selling.

### The path: buy the rumor, sell the news, then the real rally

Markets do not wait for a catalyst to arrive — they *discount* it in advance, exactly as we describe in [how a rule becomes a price](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing). The spot-ETF story is a textbook example of the pattern traders call "buy the rumor, sell the news."

In the months before approval, as the Grayscale court win made approval look inevitable, bitcoin rallied hard on the *anticipation* — from roughly \$27,000 in mid-October 2023 to about \$46,600 by the approval date on January 10, 2024. The good news was already in the price. So when the approval actually landed — the rumor became fact — the speculative longs who had bought the anticipation took profits, and bitcoin *fell* about 15% over the next two weeks, to around \$39,500 by January 23. This is "sell the news": the event everyone positioned for is, paradoxically, a selling trigger once it is confirmed and there is nothing left to anticipate.

But the spot ETF was not only an anticipation event — it was a *structural* one, because the inflows kept coming after approval. As the real ETF demand showed up week after week, bitcoin recovered and then ran to a new high near \$73,000 by mid-March 2024. The chart below shows the whole path: the anticipation run-up, the sell-the-news dip, and the inflow-driven rally.

![Bitcoin price path around the January 2024 US spot-ETF approval](/imgs/blogs/crypto-regulation-securities-vs-commodities-and-the-turf-war-5.png)

#### Worked example: the buy-the-rumor, sell-the-news trade and its P&L

Trade the pattern with real numbers from that path. Suppose on December 1, 2023, with bitcoin at \$38,680 and approval looking likely, you buy 1 BTC to ride the anticipation. You set a rule: *sell into the approval headline*, because the event is when the crowd's good news peaks.

You sell on the approval print of January 10 at \$46,640. Your gain on the anticipation leg is \$46,640 − \$38,680 = **+\$7,960 per BTC, about +20.6%** in roughly six weeks. Now watch what your discipline saved you: a trader who instead held *through* the event rode the sell-the-news dip to \$39,530 by January 23 — giving back nearly the entire gain, down to +\$850 from the entry. 

The second leg is the structural trade. Seeing the dip is a *flow*-driven event (profit-taking, not a change in the inflow thesis), you re-buy on January 23 at \$39,530 and hold for the structural inflow rally. By March 13 bitcoin is \$73,080, so the second leg gains \$73,080 − \$39,530 = **+\$33,550, about +85%**. Combined, splitting the trade into "sell the rumor's peak, re-buy the news dip, hold the structural flow" captured roughly +\$41,500 per BTC where buy-and-hold from December 1 captured +\$34,400 — and with far less drawdown stress. The takeaway: an anticipation rally and a structural unlock are *two different trades* stapled together by one event, and recognizing the seam is the edge.

## MiCA as a clarity template: how a written rulebook re-rates a market

While the United States was writing crypto law one lawsuit at a time, the European Union did the opposite: it wrote a comprehensive statute. **MiCA** — the Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation — is the EU's single framework, phased in through 2024–2025, that classifies crypto-assets, licenses the firms that issue and trade them, and sets reserve rules for stablecoins. It is the clearest example in the world of what *regulatory clarity* looks like, and of how clarity itself can re-rate an asset class.

The mechanism is the mirror image of the enforcement-by-litigation overhang. Under MiCA, a project does not have to guess whether it is a security and wait to be sued. The categories are written down: there are *asset-referenced tokens*, *e-money tokens* (the EU's term for fiat-backed stablecoins), and other crypto-assets, each with defined rules. A firm that wants to issue or trade them applies for a license, meets the requirements, and operates legally across all 27 member states with one authorization (a "passport"). The uncertainty discount — the chunk of every token's price that compensated holders for not knowing the rules — shrinks, because now the rules are knowable in advance.

![Clarity re-rates a market that uncertainty had discounted](/imgs/blogs/crypto-regulation-securities-vs-commodities-and-the-turf-war-8.png)

This is why "clarity is bullish" is not a slogan but a pricing argument. Institutions do not allocate to assets whose legal status could reverse overnight; the career risk and the compliance risk are too high. A clear framework is what lets a regulated European bank custody crypto for clients, lets a fund add it to a mandate, and lets an exchange list it without fear of retroactive enforcement. Each of those unlocks demand — the same demand-unlock logic as the spot ETF, but applied to the whole market rather than one wrapper. The United States is moving the same direction with proposed market-structure legislation that would split SEC/CFTC jurisdiction explicitly and with stablecoin statutes (discussed next); each step toward a written rule is, on this logic, a re-rating catalyst.

#### Worked example: the re-rating when an overhang lifts

Quantify a clarity re-rating directly. Take a regional crypto exchange's token that trades at \$8.00 in a jurisdiction with no crypto law, where the market prices a 25% chance the exchange gets shut down or crippled by an unpredictable enforcement action within a year, and estimates that outcome would cut the token to \$2.00.

Expected value today: 75% × \$8.00 (survives) + 25% × \$2.00 (crippled) = \$6.00 + \$0.50 = \$6.50. But notice — the market is *already* pricing the overhang, so the \$8.00 quote in a stress-free world would actually be higher. Reframe: the "clean" value absent legal risk is \$8.00, and the live price is the expected value \$6.50. Now the jurisdiction adopts a MiCA-style licensing regime and the exchange gets licensed. The shutdown probability collapses from 25% toward ~3% (residual operational risk). New expected value: 97% × \$8.00 + 3% × \$2.00 = \$7.76 + \$0.06 = **\$7.82**. The token re-rates from \$6.50 to \$7.82 — a **+20%** move driven entirely by a written rulebook replacing an unknown one. Put plainly: clarity does not add cash flows; it removes a discount, and removing a discount is a gain.

### Clarity versus threat: how the *direction* of a rule-change moves price

It is worth stating the general principle that ties the enforcement era and the clarity era together, because it is the lever you actually trade. A regulatory event moves a token's price through two channels at once: it changes the *level* of the rule (more permissive or more restrictive) and it changes the *uncertainty* about the rule (the range of possible outcomes widens or narrows). The price reacts to both, and they do not always point the same way.

Sort the four cases. A **permissive, clarifying** event — a spot-ETF approval, a favorable court ruling, a MiCA-style framework — is doubly bullish: it both opens a pipe (level) and shrinks the unknown (uncertainty). This is the strongest up-catalyst, and it is why the biggest crypto rallies cluster around clarity wins. A **restrictive, clarifying** event — a clear law that *bans* a use, say an outright prohibition on algorithmic stablecoins for payments — is mixed: it is bad for the banned thing (level down) but it can be *good* for the survivors, because the uncertainty that hung over the whole category is resolved in their favor. A **permissive but vague** signal — a regulator's friendly speech with no rule behind it — is a weak, fade-prone rally, because nothing structural changed. And a **restrictive, threatening** event — a new enforcement suit — is bearish but, as we saw, often *less* bearish than it looks, because the threat was already the baseline.

The practical reading is that *uncertainty resolution* is frequently the larger force, and its direction is not always obvious from whether the headline "sounds" pro- or anti-crypto. A harsh-sounding statute that finally draws a clear line can re-rate the compliant survivors *up*, because the market can finally price them without a tail risk of arbitrary enforcement. Conversely, a friendly-sounding but vague gesture rarely sticks, because it does not change the plumbing. When you read a crypto regulatory headline, separate the two questions — *did the level move, and did the uncertainty move?* — and weight the uncertainty channel heavily, because in a market that traded for years on an unwritten rule, the resolution of the rule is the main event.

## Stablecoins as the systemic node: the reserve question and the depeg tail

Of all the pieces in crypto regulation, stablecoins are the one regulators worry about most — because they are the *plumbing*. A **stablecoin** is a token designed to hold a fixed value, almost always one US dollar. It is the cash leg of crypto: traders park value in it between trades, it is the dollar side of most trading pairs, it settles DeFi loans, and it moves dollars across borders. When the stablecoins work, the whole machine runs. When one breaks, the failure radiates outward through everything that used it as money.

The all-important question for any stablecoin is: **what actually backs the promise of \$1?** This is the *reserve* question, and the answer sorts stablecoins into categories that fail in completely different ways.

A **fiat-backed** stablecoin (USDC, USDT) claims to hold one real dollar — as cash and short-term US Treasury bills — for every token in circulation. If everyone redeems, there is supposedly real money to pay them. The risk here is not the design; it is whether the reserves are *actually there and actually liquid*. USDC briefly broke its peg in March 2023 — falling to about \$0.88 — not because its design failed but because a chunk of its cash reserves sat in Silicon Valley Bank, which had just failed; once the deposits were guaranteed, USDC snapped back to \$1.00 within days. A fiat-backed coin's depeg is usually a *liquidity scare*, and it heals when redemption is restored.

A **crypto-collateralized** stablecoin (DAI) holds *crypto* as collateral — but more than 100% of the coin's value, an *over-collateralization* of, say, 150%, to absorb the collateral's own volatility. If ETH crashes and the cushion thins, the system liquidates collateral to defend the peg. It is more robust than it sounds, but its safety depends on the collateral not falling faster than the system can liquidate.

An **algorithmic** stablecoin holds *nothing real*. It tries to maintain the peg with a mechanism — typically a mint-and-burn loop tied to a second, free-floating "sister" token. When the stablecoin is above \$1, the system mints more; when below, it lets holders burn the stablecoin to mint the sister token, supposedly soaking up the excess supply. The fatal flaw is *reflexivity*: the stablecoin is backed by a token whose value depends on confidence in the stablecoin. When confidence cracks, both fall together.

### The death spiral and the contagion

The clearest case of an algorithmic stablecoin failing — and the reason regulators around the world now treat stablecoins as a systemic priority — is Terra's UST in May 2022. UST was an algorithmic stablecoin paired with a sister token called LUNA. The mechanism: you could always burn \$1 of UST to mint \$1 of LUNA, and vice versa. As long as people believed it, the arbitrage held the peg.

When a wave of large redemptions hit and UST slipped below \$1, the mechanism did exactly what it was designed to do — and that is what destroyed it. Holders burned UST to mint LUNA, which dumped huge new supplies of LUNA onto the market. LUNA's price collapsed, which meant burning \$1 of UST now minted far *less* than \$1 of value, which meant the peg could not be defended, which sent more holders fleeing, which minted even more LUNA. This is the **death spiral**: a self-reinforcing loop where the defense mechanism accelerates the collapse. Within days, UST went from \$1 to near zero and LUNA from tens of dollars to fractions of a cent. Roughly **\$40bn of value evaporated**. (For the full mechanics, see [the Terra-LUNA 2022 collapse](/blog/trading/crypto/terra-luna-2022-collapse).)

The collapse did not stay contained, and *that* is the systemic part. UST and LUNA were used as collateral across crypto lending platforms and held by leveraged funds. When they went to zero, the lenders and funds that held them took catastrophic losses, were forced to sell *other* assets to cover, and dragged bitcoin and ether down with them in a cross-asset fire sale. A hedge fund that had borrowed against crypto positions blew up; the lenders it owed froze withdrawals; their customers panicked. One stablecoin's failure became a market-wide deleveraging.

![A stablecoin depeg propagates through collateral into a system-wide contagion](/imgs/blogs/crypto-regulation-securities-vs-commodities-and-the-turf-war-7.png)

This is exactly the contagion that the new wave of stablecoin regulation is written to prevent. The EU's MiCA imposes hard reserve and redemption rules on fiat-backed stablecoins. In the United States, **GENIUS-style stablecoin legislation** — federal frameworks that would require payment stablecoins to be fully backed by high-quality liquid assets (cash and short-term Treasuries), audited, and redeemable at par — aims to make the fiat-backed model safe and to effectively outlaw the algorithmic model for payment use. The regulatory logic is plain: a stablecoin that is truly money needs to be backed like money. For how these flows show up on-chain as a market signal, see [stablecoin flows as the dry-powder metric](/blog/trading/onchain/stablecoin-flows-the-dry-powder-metric).

## The venue and custody plumbing: how a rule reaches the price

There is a step in the chain that beginners tend to skip and professionals obsess over: *the rule does not move the price directly — it moves the price by changing the plumbing*. The plumbing is the set of venues that can list an asset, the custodians that can hold it, and the wrappers (like an ETF) that let particular pools of money buy it. A rule-change is bullish or bearish only to the extent that it widens or narrows that plumbing, because the plumbing *is* the demand curve.

Walk the pipes one at a time. A **listing venue** is where a buyer meets a seller. For crypto, US listing venues split into two worlds: the lightly regulated crypto exchanges (where most spot trading happens) and the heavily regulated securities exchanges (where stocks and ETFs trade). A token deemed a commodity can live on the first; a token deemed a security must move to the second — and there is essentially no securities exchange that lists raw tokens, so a security designation effectively pushes the token off US venues entirely. That is why the listing question and the security question are the same question wearing different clothes.

A **custodian** is whoever holds the asset for you. Retail self-custody (your own wallet) is fine for individuals, but it is a non-starter for a pension fund or a registered advisor, whose rules demand a *qualified custodian* — a regulated entity that segregates client assets, is audited, and can prove the assets exist. For years, the absence of clear qualified crypto custody was a silent ceiling on institutional demand: even an institution that wanted bitcoin often could not hold it in a compliant way. The slow arrival of regulated custodians — and the spot ETF, which outsources the custody problem to a qualified custodian the fund hires — is what lifted that ceiling.

A **wrapper** is the legal package the asset travels in. The same bitcoin can be a raw token (most pools of money can't touch it), a futures contract (a derivatives bet, no real coin), or a spot-ETF share (a securities-regulated product that any brokerage account can buy). The wrapper decides which pool of capital is allowed in. This is the whole reason the spot ETF mattered so much: it did not change bitcoin, it changed the *wrapper*, and the new wrapper was one that trillions of dollars of advised and retirement money could finally hold.

So when you evaluate a crypto rule-change, do not ask "is this good or bad for crypto" in the abstract. Ask the plumbing question: *which venues, custodians, or wrappers does this rule open or close, and how much capital sits behind each newly opened or closed pipe?* A rule that opens a pipe to a large, previously-barred pool of money (a spot ETF, bank custody approval, a clear listing standard) is a demand unlock and bullish. A rule that closes a pipe (a security designation that forces delisting, a custody ban) is a demand cutoff and bearish. The size of the move scales with the size of the pool behind the pipe — which is why the spot ETF, opening the door to advised and retirement money, was a larger structural event than almost any single token's enforcement case.

#### Worked example: the reserve gap when a stablecoin depegs

Put the reserve question in dollars. A fiat-backed stablecoin called USDX has **5 billion tokens** outstanding, each promising \$1.00, so it owes \$5.0bn on full redemption. Its published reserves are \$5.0bn — but a closer look shows the composition is \$3.5bn in cash and short-dated T-bills (instantly liquid) and \$1.5bn in *commercial paper and longer assets* that cannot be sold instantly without a loss.

Now a confidence shock hits and holders rush to redeem \$2.0bn in a single day. The \$3.5bn liquid bucket covers that — barely — so the peg holds, *this time*. But suppose the shock is \$4.0bn (80% of supply) in two days. The liquid bucket of \$3.5bn is exhausted; to pay the next \$0.5bn the issuer must dump illiquid assets at, say, 90 cents on the dollar, realizing a loss and revealing that not every token can be redeemed at \$1.00. The market sees the gap and USDX trades to where the *realizable* reserves imply: if true liquidation value is \$4.7bn against 5bn tokens, the floor is about **\$0.94** — a 6-cent depeg, a 6% loss for anyone forced to sell. Against an *algorithmic* coin with \$0 real reserves, the same run has no floor at all and goes toward zero. The takeaway: a stablecoin is only as stable as the *liquid* portion of its reserves under a run — the peg is a promise that is tested precisely when everyone tries to collect at once.

## Common misconceptions

**"Crypto is unregulated."** This is the most persistent myth, and it is false. Crypto is heavily regulated — it is just regulated by *enforcement and existing law* rather than by a single bespoke statute (in the US) or by a comprehensive new one (in the EU's MiCA). US exchanges register as money-services businesses, comply with anti-money-laundering and know-your-customer rules, and live under the constant threat of SEC and CFTC actions. The thousands of ICO-era tokens the SEC pursued were prosecuted under *1933* securities law. The accurate statement is not "crypto is unregulated" but "crypto's regulation was, for years, unclear and applied retroactively" — which is worse for prices than clear regulation, because uncertainty is itself a discount, as the worked examples above showed (a 15%–20% overhang in plausible cases).

**"A spot ETF changes the technology."** It changes nothing about bitcoin's code, supply schedule, or security. What the spot ETF changed was *access and flows*: it wrapped bitcoin in a vehicle that pension funds, advisors, and retirement accounts could legally and operationally buy, and it forced real coin purchases against a fixed supply. The ~\$35bn of first-year inflows were not a vote on the technology; they were capital that was previously locked out by custody, compliance, and mandate rules and was suddenly let in. Confusing "more demand was unlocked" with "the asset got better" leads to bad analysis — the demand is real, but it is a *plumbing* change, not a *protocol* change.

**"All stablecoins are the same."** They share a \$1 target and nothing else that matters. A fiat-backed coin holds cash and T-bills and depegs only in a liquidity scare (USDC's \$0.88 dip in March 2023, fully recovered). An over-collateralized coin holds 150%+ in crypto and fails only if collateral crashes faster than it can liquidate. An algorithmic coin holds *nothing* and is one confidence shock away from a death spiral to zero (UST, ~\$40bn gone in May 2022). Treating "stablecoin" as one thing is like treating "a checking account at a solvent bank" and "an IOU from a stranger" as the same dollar. The reserve composition is the entire story, and a regulation that mandates full, liquid, audited backing is precisely an attempt to force every "stablecoin" to actually be the first kind.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three episodes, each a clean instance of a *rule* (or a rule's failure) repricing the asset class.

**The January 2024 spot-ETF approval — anticipation, sell-the-news, then the structural rally.** As covered above and shown in the price chart, bitcoin ran from ~\$27,000 (October 2023) to ~\$46,600 by the approval date (January 10, 2024) on anticipation, dipped ~15% to ~\$39,500 by January 23 as the news was sold, then rallied to ~\$73,080 by mid-March 2024 as the real ETF inflows (~\$35bn over the first year) showed up. The lesson for a trader is that one event contained *two* distinct trades — the anticipation rally and the structural flow rally — separated by a sell-the-news dip that punished anyone who could not tell the difference between profit-taking and a thesis breaking.

**A token dropping when labeled a security.** The enforcement-by-litigation era produced a recurring pattern: when the SEC named specific tokens as alleged unregistered securities in complaints against major exchanges, those tokens routinely fell sharply — often 20%–40% in days — as US exchanges moved to restrict or delist them and holders front-ran the lost liquidity. The drop was not about the protocols' code; it was the market repricing the loss of US access and the now-higher probability that the security label would stick. The token that survives such a scare and later gets clarity (a favorable ruling, a settlement, a new statute) can re-rate just as sharply in the other direction — the overhang lifting.

**A stablecoin depeg.** UST's collapse in May 2022 is the canonical case: \$1 to near zero in days, ~\$40bn destroyed, and a contagion that forced cross-asset selling and toppled leveraged lenders and funds. The milder, instructive counter-case is USDC's March 2023 wobble to \$0.88 — a *fiat-backed* coin depegging on a liquidity scare (reserves stuck at a failed bank), which healed within days once redemption was assured. Same word, "depeg," two completely different events: one structural and terminal, one a liquidity scare that the reserves ultimately covered. A trader who knew the reserve structure knew which was which in real time.

**The MiCA phase-in as a slow-motion clarity event.** Not every rule-change is a single-day shock; some arrive as a scheduled, telegraphed phase-in, and those reprice differently. MiCA's rules — especially its stablecoin requirements, which took effect in mid-2024, ahead of the broader regime in late 2024 — were known years in advance, so the market discounted them gradually rather than in one gap. The visible effect was *compositional*: stablecoin issuers that could meet MiCA's reserve and licensing rules gained access to the EU market, while those that could not faced restrictions or delisting from EU venues. The lesson for a trader is that a long-telegraphed framework rarely produces a clean event-day trade; instead it slowly re-rates the *compliant* names higher relative to the non-compliant ones over the run-up, as the market handicaps who will and will not make the cut. The trade is relative (long compliant, underweight non-compliant), not a single-date directional bet.

## How to trade it: the playbook

Everything above converges on a single operating principle: **in crypto, the rule is a tradable catalyst, and the legal label is a pricing input you can underwrite.** Here is how to act on it.

**1. Trade regulatory-clarity catalysts as the dominant event class.** The biggest, most reliable repricings come from rule-changes, not roadmaps. Build a calendar of the catalysts that matter: ETF approval or denial dates, court rulings (the Grayscale loss is the template — a court can force a regulator's hand), and legislation milestones (a stablecoin statute passing committee, a market-structure bill that splits SEC/CFTC jurisdiction, a MiCA phase going live). For each, ask the three spine questions: *what rule is changing, what repricing does it trigger, and is it already in the price?* The catalysts that pay are the ones the market has *not* fully discounted — which is why a court loss for the SEC (unexpected) moved more than a long-telegraphed approval.

**2. Price the security-label overhang explicitly, and bet on its resolution.** For any token that is *not* obviously a commodity (i.e., not bitcoin or ether), estimate the discount its uncertain legal status is imposing — the worked examples put it at 15%–20% in plausible cases. That discount is your payout if clarity arrives favorably and your loss if the label sticks. The asymmetric trades are: (a) *long* a token where a clarity catalyst is approaching and the overhang is large (the discount can release fast), and (b) *avoid or short* a token that is squarely in the SEC's crosshairs ahead of a likely complaint, where delisting risk (a 30%–50% haircut) is mispriced as low.

**3. Underwrite the stablecoin-depeg tail before it bites, not after.** Treat every stablecoin you touch — directly or as the cash leg of a trade — as a credit instrument and read its reserves. Fiat-backed with audited, fully-liquid reserves: a depeg is a liquidity scare and likely a *buying* opportunity near the floor (USDC at \$0.88 was a high-probability snap-back). Algorithmic or thinly-reserved: the tail is a death spiral to zero, and the correct position size is *small or none*. The depeg trade — buying a fiat-backed coin's brief dip below \$1 — is one of the cleaner edges in crypto *if and only if* you have done the reserve work first. The contagion lesson is the risk-management corollary: a major stablecoin failure is a market-wide deleveraging signal, so a depeg in *any* large stablecoin is a reason to cut leverage everywhere, not just in that coin.

**4. Map the venue and custody plumbing, because access is the demand curve.** A rule's price impact runs through *who can list, custody, and hold* the asset. When a token gets a clarity unlock, the question is whether regulated venues and qualified custodians can now serve institutions — that is the pipe through which demand flows. Watch for the ETF wrapper (the spot-ETF demand unlock), bank custody approvals (institutions cannot hold what they cannot custody), and exchange listings on regulated venues. The structural bull case for the asset class is the steady widening of that plumbing as clarity arrives jurisdiction by jurisdiction.

**What invalidates the view.** Be honest about when the regulatory thesis is wrong. A *clarity* trade is invalidated if the framework arrives but is *hostile* — clarity that bans or cripples the asset is bearish, not bullish; "clarity is good" only holds when the clear rule is *permissive*. A *spot-ETF flow* thesis is invalidated if inflows stall or reverse to *outflows* — the structural demand can dry up or unwind, and ETF flows are public daily, so there is no excuse for being surprised. A *depeg-buy* on a fiat-backed coin is invalidated the moment the reserve story is worse than you thought (the dip is not a liquidity scare but genuine insolvency), so the floor is the realizable reserve value, not \$1. And the whole "regulation drives price" framework is *less* powerful in the rare windows when a macro shock (a liquidity crunch, a rate spike) dwarfs every rule-change — in those moments crypto trades as the longest-duration risk asset and the law takes a back seat to the [Fed's liquidity dial](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money). Know which regime you are in before you size the trade.

The throughline of this series holds with unusual force in crypto: the law writes the rules of the game, the market prices the rule-change before it fully bites, and the practitioner who reads the security-or-commodity label, the ETF wrapper, and the stablecoin reserve sheet *early* sizes the repricing while everyone else is still arguing about the technology. For how this same transmission logic runs across every asset class in the series, see [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain) and the synthesis in [the law, policy, and geopolitics playbook](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-law-policy-and-geopolitics-playbook).
