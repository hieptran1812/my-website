> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Before you buy a single Vietnamese stock, you have to learn the legal map: the State Securities Commission (SSC) governs the securities, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) governs the money and the foreign exchange, and the three exchanges (HOSE, HNX, UPCoM) are where they meet. This legal architecture is the gate every dollar of foreign capital must pass through, and it explains every quirk that confuses newcomers.
>
> - Vietnam runs **two regulators, not one**: the SSC (a market regulator under the Ministry of Finance) and the SBV (the central bank). A foreign trade needs both to clear — the security side and the money side.
> - The market's three boards are three different liquidity worlds. **HOSE** is the deep main board, **HNX** is smaller caps plus the bond market, and **UPCoM** is a lightly regulated board for unlisted public companies.
> - The biggest friction is **pre-funding**: a foreign investor must have 100 percent of the cash in the account *before* placing a buy order. That idle cash is a real, measurable drag on returns.
> - Vietnam is still classified as a **frontier market**. The single number to remember: an upgrade to emerging-market status could pull in **several billion dollars** of passive inflows and re-rate the whole market's valuation.

On 6 January 2022 the VN-Index, Vietnam's main stock benchmark, closed at an all-time high of 1,528 points. Ten months later, on 15 November 2022, it closed at 874 — a drop of roughly 43 percent. No global crash caused it. No recession hit Vietnam's economy, which grew over 8 percent that year. What moved the market was *law and enforcement*: a corporate-bond decree, a high-profile fraud arrest, and a sudden freeze in the bond market that cascaded into property developers and then into everything.

For a foreign investor, that episode is the whole thesis of this post in miniature. Vietnam is one of the most exciting growth stories on earth — a young, fast-industrializing country pulling in tens of billions of dollars of foreign factory investment every year. But the market that sits on top of that economy is not a free-for-all you can wire money into and start trading. It is a tightly governed legal structure with its own regulators, its own access rules, its own settlement mechanics, and its own classification in the eyes of the global index providers. If you do not learn that structure first, you will misread the quirks — the pre-funding, the foreign-ownership ceilings, the settlement cycle — as bugs, when they are actually the load-bearing walls of the system.

This post draws the legal map. We will define every institution and rule from zero, in plain English, and then go genuinely deep on the mechanics that matter to your returns. The goal is simple: by the end you should be able to look at any Vietnamese stock and know which board it trades on, which regulator governs it, how a foreign dollar reaches it, and what reform would change its price.

## Foundations: the institutions and the rules, from zero

Let us build the map one piece at a time. Every term here is defined on first use, and we romanize all Vietnamese names (no diacritics) so they read cleanly in English.

**The Securities Law.** A securities law is the statute that says who may issue shares to the public, how they must disclose information, where shares may trade, and who polices all of it. Vietnam's current Securities Law was passed by the National Assembly (the country's legislature) in 2019 and took effect on 1 January 2021. It replaced an older 2006 law. This statute is the top of the legal pyramid — everything below it (the regulators, the exchanges, the access rules) derives its authority from it. When you hear that Vietnam is "amending the Securities Law" in 2024 to enable reforms, that is the legislature changing the rules at the very top, which then cascades down.

Below the statute sit *decrees* (issued by the Government) and *circulars* (issued by ministries and regulators). This is the standard civil-law hierarchy: law > decree > circular. A decree fills in the operating detail the law leaves open; a circular fills in the detail the decree leaves open. The 2022 bond crisis, as we will see, turned on a *decree* (Decree 65 on corporate bonds), not on the Securities Law itself. For a trader, the practical point is that the rules that move the market most often arrive as decrees and circulars, which move faster and with less warning than a full statute.

**The State Securities Commission (SSC).** The SSC is Vietnam's stock-market regulator. It is the rough equivalent of the SEC in the United States — but with one crucial difference: the SSC is *not* an independent agency. It sits *under the Ministry of Finance (MOF)*. That subordination matters. It means securities policy is closely tied to fiscal and macro policy, and that major decisions can involve the Ministry and the Government, not just the regulator. The SSC licenses brokers and fund managers, approves listings, sets disclosure rules, supervises the exchanges, and enforces against market abuse. Every foreign investor first meets the SSC indirectly, because the SSC is who you register with (through your broker) to get the code that lets you trade at all.

**The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV).** The SBV is Vietnam's central bank. It is *not* a stock-market regulator — it governs money, banks, credit, and foreign exchange. So why does a stock investor need to know it? Because in Vietnam the *money side* of every trade is the SBV's domain. To buy a Vietnamese stock with US dollars, you must convert those dollars into Vietnamese dong (VND) through a regulated capital account, and the SBV governs that conversion, the dong's managed exchange-rate band, and the banking system that holds your cash. The SBV also runs the credit-growth quota system that drives how much liquidity flows through the whole economy — and liquidity is the tide that lifts or sinks the VN-Index. In short: the SSC clears your *security*; the SBV clears your *money*.

![SSC governs the security and SBV governs the money on the two sides of a foreign trade](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges-8.png)

**The exchanges.** Vietnam has three trading venues, now organized under a single holding company, the Vietnam Exchange (VNX), but operationally distinct:

- **HOSE** — the Ho Chi Minh City Stock Exchange. This is the main board, where the largest and most liquid companies list. The VN-Index tracks HOSE. When a foreign fund says it owns "Vietnamese equities", it overwhelmingly means HOSE names. Listing standards here are the strictest.
- **HNX** — the Hanoi Stock Exchange. This is the smaller-cap equity board *and* the home of the government and corporate bond markets. The HNX-Index tracks its equities. Liquidity is thinner than HOSE.
- **UPCoM** — the Unlisted Public Company Market, run by HNX. This is the lightly regulated board for public companies that are not (or not yet) formally listed. Put plainly, it is a halfway house: a company that has sold shares to the public but has not met or chosen full listing standards trades here. Disclosure is lighter, liquidity is often near zero, and governance quality varies wildly.

**The VSDC.** The Vietnam Securities Depository and Clearing Corporation (formerly the VSD) is the depository and clearing house. When you buy a share, the VSDC is the institution that records that you now own it and ensures the cash and the shares change hands. It is the plumbing that makes settlement real. Foreign custody — the legal holding of your shares — runs through the VSDC and your custodian bank.

**The VN-Index.** This is the headline benchmark: a capitalization-weighted index of all stocks listed on HOSE. When people quote "the Vietnam market is up 12 percent this year", they almost always mean the VN-Index. It is dominated by banks, real estate, and a handful of large industrials and consumer names.

**Foreign access mechanics.** To trade as a foreigner you need three things, in order: (1) a **securities trading code (STC)**, a unique identifier the SSC issues to each foreign investor; (2) an **indirect-investment capital account (IICA)** at a licensed Vietnamese bank, the regulated channel through which your foreign currency comes in, converts to dong, and later leaves; and (3) a **custody account** to hold your shares. Without all three, you cannot place a single order.

**T+ settlement.** "T+2" means a trade executed on day T settles (cash and shares finally exchange) two business days later. Vietnam moved to a T+2 cycle, but with a crucial twist for foreigners that we cover below: the cash often has to be in place *before* the trade, not after.

**The frontier classification.** The big global index providers — MSCI and FTSE Russell — sort the world's stock markets into tiers: developed, emerging, and frontier. Vietnam is still in the *frontier* tier in MSCI's classification (FTSE has it on a watchlist for upgrade to *secondary emerging*). Frontier is the smallest, least-tracked tier. The size of the global pool of money that automatically buys a market is vastly larger for emerging than for frontier. This single classification is the most important catalyst hanging over the entire market, and we devote a full section to it.

That is the whole cast of characters. Now we go deep on the parts that actually move prices.

## A short history: how a market was built from nothing

It helps to know that this is a *young* market, deliberately constructed. Vietnam had no stock exchange at all until the Ho Chi Minh City Securities Trading Center opened in July 2000 with exactly two listed companies and a tiny pool of capital. Hanoi followed in 2005. The market was an instrument of policy from day one: the state needed a venue to partially privatize state-owned enterprises (a process called *equitization* in Vietnam, by which a wholly state-owned company sells part of itself to outside investors) and to channel domestic savings into productive capital. The exchanges were not a spontaneous private invention; they were built by the government to serve a development goal.

That origin shapes everything downstream. Because the market was state-built and is state-supervised, the regulators retain a degree of direct control that a developed-market investor will find unfamiliar — daily price limits that cap how far a stock can move in a session, the credit-quota lever on liquidity, the managed currency, the foreign-ownership ceilings, and the willingness of the authorities to intervene forcefully (as in 2022) when they judge the system is at risk. None of this is hidden; it is the explicit design. The practitioner's job is not to wish the design away but to price it.

The market also grew up *retail-first*. Domestic individual investors, not institutions, dominate daily turnover on HOSE — frequently 80 to 90 percent of volume in active periods. That matters because retail flows are sentiment-driven and heavily margin-financed, which makes the index more volatile and more sensitive to the credit cycle than a market dominated by long-horizon institutions. When the SBV tightens credit, retail margin contracts and the index can fall hard even with no change in corporate earnings. A foreign investor stepping in is, in effect, a patient minority among a fast-moving retail majority.

The trajectory since 2000 has been one of steady institutionalization: more listings, the move to electronic continuous matching, the creation of a depository, the 2006 and then 2019 Securities Laws, the opening to foreign investors, and now the reform push toward an emerging-market upgrade. Each step has been a legal one. The market does not modernize on its own — it modernizes when the National Assembly, the Government, the SSC, and the SBV change a rule.

## What the 2019 Securities Law actually changed

The Securities Law of 2019 (effective 2021) is worth understanding on its own terms, because amendments to it are now a live catalyst. The 2019 law was a significant upgrade over the 2006 version in several ways that bear directly on foreign investors and market quality.

First, it strengthened the SSC's enforcement powers and raised disclosure standards, tightening the rules on what listed companies must report and when, and stiffening penalties for violations. Better disclosure is the foundation of price discovery — you cannot value what you cannot see — so this was a quiet but real improvement in market quality. Second, it formalized and clarified the foreign-ownership framework, providing a clearer legal basis for the foreign-room rules that gate how much of a company foreigners may own. Third, it reorganized the market's institutional structure, paving the way for the single Vietnam Exchange holding company over HOSE and HNX, and for the modern depository and clearing arrangement.

The reason this matters *now* is that Vietnam has been amending the law and its decrees specifically to clear the accessibility hurdles the index providers cite. Changes that enable non-pre-funding, that smooth foreign access to information in English, and that modernize settlement all flow through the legal layer. So when you read that "Vietnam is amending the Securities Law" or issuing "a new decree on foreign investment", translate it concretely: the legislature and regulators are editing the top of the pyramid to unlock the upgrade. These are not abstract legal housekeeping; they are the precise catalysts the whole reform thesis rests on.

A useful way to hold the hierarchy in your head: the **Securities Law** sets the constitution of the market; **decrees** from the Government operationalize it (and can move fast — Decree 65 on corporate bonds in 2022 is the cautionary example); **circulars** from the SSC and SBV fill in the day-to-day mechanics; and **the exchanges' own rules** set the trading microstructure. A rule change at any level can move prices, but the *speed* and *warning* differ sharply by level. Statutes are slow and well-telegraphed; decrees and circulars can land suddenly. The 2022 shock was a decree-and-enforcement event precisely because that level moves fast.

## The SSC versus the SBV: a two-regulator market

The single most common error a newcomer makes is to treat Vietnam like a one-regulator market. In the United States you can roughly think "the SEC governs my stock trade" and move on, because the dollar is the domestic currency and capital flows freely. In Vietnam, *two* legal domains gate every foreign trade, and they answer to two different institutions with two different mandates.

The SSC's mandate is the *securities side*. It decides what may list, on which board, under what disclosure rules. It sets the trading rules — price limits, lot sizes, the matching mechanism. It supervises the exchanges and the VSDC. It enforces against insider trading, manipulation, and disclosure violations. And, critically for us, it administers the foreign-ownership framework: the rules on how much of a company foreigners may collectively own (the "foreign room", covered in the next post in this series).

The SBV's mandate is the *money side*. It governs the dong, which is a *managed* currency — the SBV sets a daily central reference rate and allows the market rate to float within a band around it. It governs the capital account, which is *not* fully open: money cannot move in and out of Vietnam freely the way it can in a developed market. It runs the credit-growth quota system, an unusual tool by which the SBV tells each commercial bank the maximum percentage by which its loan book may grow that year — a direct lever on systemwide liquidity. And it supervises the banks, including the custodian banks that hold foreign investors' cash and shares.

Why does this two-headed structure matter so much in practice? Because a rule change from *either* regulator can move the market, and the two do not always move together. An SSC rule on foreign room is a securities-side event. An SBV move on the credit quota or the dong band is a money-side event. A sophisticated reader watches both calendars. The 2022 crash was triggered on the securities/legal side (a bond decree and fraud enforcement) but was amplified on the money side (tight credit and a defensive SBV raising rates to protect the dong). The two domains compounded each other.

This is also why the reform that everyone is waiting for — the frontier-to-emerging upgrade — requires *both* regulators to act. The SSC has to fix the settlement and pre-funding mechanics on the securities side; the SBV has to smooth the foreign-exchange and capital-account frictions on the money side. Neither alone is sufficient.

#### Worked example: the all-in cost of being a foreigner

Take a foreign fund putting \$100,000 to work in Vietnamese equities for one year. The two-regulator structure imposes costs on *both* sides, and they stack:

- **FX conversion (SBV side):** converting USD to VND on the way in and back to USD on the way out, at a typical round-trip spread of about 0.5 percent, costs roughly \$500 on \$100,000.
- **Custody and account fees (SSC/bank side):** a foreign custodian typically charges on the order of 0.15 percent per year on assets held, or about \$150 on \$100,000.
- **Pre-funding cash drag (the friction we cover next):** holding cash idle to satisfy pre-funding, say an average 10 percent of the portfolio parked in non-yielding dong while US cash earns about 4.5 percent, costs roughly 0.10 × 0.045 × \$100,000 = \$450 in forgone yield.

Add them: \$500 + \$150 + \$450 ≈ \$1,100, or about **1.1 percent per year** before you have made a single good or bad stock pick. For a developed-market index fund the equivalent friction is a few basis points. The legal architecture, not the companies, creates most of that gap — and it is the architecture, not the economy, that reform would fix. The lesson: in a frontier market, the *structure* is a return drag you must underwrite before you underwrite any stock.

## The pre-funding friction: the rule that defines the market

If you remember one operational quirk of Vietnam, make it pre-funding. It is the rule that most distinguishes the market from a developed one, the one global index providers cite most often as a barrier, and the one whose reform would most directly re-rate the market.

Here is the rule. In a developed market, when an institution places a buy order, the cash is delivered *at settlement*, two days after the trade. The broker extends short-term credit in between; this is normal and frictionless. In Vietnam, by contrast, a foreign investor has historically had to have **100 percent of the purchase cash sitting in the account before the order is placed**. You cannot buy on the expectation that you will fund it in two days. The cash goes in first; only then can you trade.

![How a foreign dollar reaches a Vietnamese stock through six legal and operational gates](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges-3.png)

Why does this exist? It is a risk-control choice. Pre-funding guarantees that no trade can fail for lack of cash — the money is provably there before matching. For a market that is still building institutional trust and that lacks the deep, regulated broker-credit infrastructure of a developed market, requiring cash upfront is a conservative way to eliminate settlement-failure risk. It is prudent from the regulator's seat. But it is expensive from the investor's seat, and the expense is the point.

The cost is *idle cash carry*. To trade actively, a foreign fund must keep a cash buffer in dong, in the account, earning little or nothing, ready to fire. Every dollar parked in that buffer is a dollar not invested and not earning the yield it could earn elsewhere. For a passively managed fund that must replicate an index — buying many names, rebalancing on a schedule — the buffer requirement is even more painful, because index replication needs cash available to buy exactly when the index changes.

![The pre-funding cash drag day by day from wiring cash in to T plus two settlement](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges-7.png)

The strategic significance is enormous. Pre-funding is one of the specific criteria MSCI and FTSE flag as an obstacle to upgrading Vietnam. A large passive fund tracking an emerging-market index cannot easily hold the idle-cash buffers that Vietnam's pre-funding demands across dozens of frontier names. So pre-funding is not just an annoyance — it is a structural cap on *who can buy Vietnam at all*. Reform it, and you widen the buyer pool.

Vietnam has been working on a partial fix called *non-pre-funding* (sometimes "NPS"), which lets certain securities companies extend the short-term credit so that qualified foreign institutions no longer have to pre-fund every trade. The rollout of this mechanism in late 2024 and 2025 was one of the concrete reforms cited in FTSE's upgrade considerations. The direction of travel is clear: pre-funding is being chipped away precisely because it is the gate that keeps the big money out.

#### Worked example: the pre-funding idle-cash drag

Suppose a foreign fund runs a \$2,000,000 Vietnam portfolio and, to trade actively under pre-funding, must keep an average **15 percent** of it — \$300,000 — sitting in idle dong cash. At the USD/VND anchor of about 25,000 dong per dollar, that is roughly 7.5 billion dong parked in the account.

Now compare two worlds for that idle cash:

- In a developed market, that same buffer cash could sit in a money-market fund earning, say, **4.5 percent** a year: 0.045 × \$300,000 = **\$13,500** of annual income.
- Under pre-funding in Vietnam, the buffer earns essentially nothing (a token deposit rate at best).

The annual drag is about **\$13,500 on a \$2,000,000 portfolio, or 0.675 percent** of total assets — every year, forever, just to keep the market tradable. Across a five-year holding period that compounds to well over 3 percent of the portfolio surrendered to a settlement rule. The intuition: pre-funding does not tax your trades; it taxes your *cash*, continuously, which is why removing it is worth real basis points to every foreign holder.

## Three boards, three liquidity worlds

A frequent mistake is to treat "the Vietnam market" as one thing. It is three, and the board a stock sits on tells you most of what you need to know about its quality, liquidity, and investability before you read a single financial statement.

![HOSE HNX and UPCoM compared by listing bar liquidity foreign access and governance](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges-4.png)

**HOSE** is where the serious money lives. The listing bar is the highest — minimum capital, profitability history, public float, and governance requirements. The largest banks, the biggest real-estate developers, the major industrials and consumer names list here. Liquidity is deep: daily turnover on HOSE dwarfs the other boards combined. The VN-Index is built from HOSE. If you are a foreign investor building a core Vietnam position, you are overwhelmingly buying HOSE names, because they are the only ones with enough liquidity to get in and out of at scale, and the only ones in the indices the big funds track.

**HNX** is the second tier for equities and the *primary venue for bonds*. The equity listing bar is lower than HOSE; the companies are smaller; spreads are wider and depth is thinner. The bond market — both government bonds and (before 2022) the booming corporate-bond market — trades through HNX infrastructure. For an equity investor, HNX is a place to find smaller, sometimes faster-growing names, but with materially higher liquidity risk: a position that is easy to build can be hard to exit.

**UPCoM** is the wild frontier within the frontier. It exists so that public companies that are not formally listed still have a regulated place to trade — for example, a state-owned enterprise that has been partially privatized but has not completed a full HOSE or HNX listing. Disclosure requirements are the lightest of the three boards. Many UPCoM stocks trade rarely or not at all on a given day. Governance quality ranges from "essentially a listed company waiting to graduate" to "near-impossible to analyze". The opportunity is that occasionally a genuinely good company sits on UPCoM at a discount because few can or will trade it; the risk is that you buy something you cannot sell.

The **liquidity gap** between the boards is the practical heart of this. It is not a smooth gradient — it is a cliff. A blue-chip on HOSE might trade tens of millions of dollars a day; a typical UPCoM name might trade a few thousand dollars a day, if that. For a fund of any size, that cliff determines the entire investable universe. You can have a brilliant thesis on a UPCoM company and be completely unable to act on it, because building a meaningful position would move the price against you and exiting would be worse.

#### Worked example: the liquidity cliff in dollars

Take two companies with the same business and the same \$200,000,000 market capitalization, one on HOSE and one on UPCoM.

- The **HOSE** name trades, say, **0.5 percent** of its market cap a day: 0.005 × \$200m = **\$1,000,000** of daily turnover.
- The **UPCoM** name trades **0.02 percent** of its market cap a day: 0.0002 × \$200m = **\$40,000** of daily turnover.

Now suppose a fund wants to build a \$2,000,000 position without being more than 20 percent of daily volume (a standard discipline to avoid moving the price). On HOSE that takes \$2,000,000 ÷ (0.20 × \$1,000,000) = **10 trading days**. On UPCoM it takes \$2,000,000 ÷ (0.20 × \$40,000) = **250 trading days** — an entire year of patient accumulation. The core idea: identical companies are *not* identical investments once you account for the board, because liquidity, not the business, sets how much capital you can actually deploy and recover.

## The foreign-room system: a preview

We will devote the next post in this series entirely to the foreign-ownership framework, but it is impossible to understand the legal architecture without a working sketch of it here, because it is the second great access constraint after pre-funding.

The rule is this. For most Vietnamese companies, the law caps the *total* percentage of shares that foreigners may collectively own. The general ceiling is 49 percent for most sectors, with stricter limits in sensitive ones — notably banks, where the foreign cap has historically been 30 percent. The unused portion of that cap is the *foreign room*. When a desirable bank or blue-chip has its foreign room fully used up, a new foreign buyer simply cannot buy shares on the open market at the normal price, no matter how much they want to — there is no room left for foreign ownership.

The consequence is a *foreign-room premium*. When room is scarce in a name foreigners want, those shares can change hands between foreigners at a premium to the local price, because the right to own the stock as a foreigner is itself scarce. The local price and the price a foreigner must pay can diverge. Various workarounds have grown up around this — including instruments that give foreigners economic exposure without legal ownership — and reform proposals (such as non-voting depositary receipts) aim to relieve the constraint. The full mechanics, including how to find and trade the room, are the subject of the next post.

For the legal map, the point is structural and stacks with everything above: even after you have your trading code, your custody account, and your pre-funded cash, you can still be *legally barred* from buying the exact stock you want because the foreign room is full. The architecture gates not just *how* you buy but *whether* you can buy a given name at all.

#### Worked example: the foreign-room premium

Take a Vietnamese bank trading at a local price of **40,000 dong per share** with its 30 percent foreign cap fully used. A foreign fund that must own it can only buy from another foreigner exiting, and in a tight market those shares trade at, say, a **7 percent** premium to the local board price.

- Local price: 40,000 dong, which at the 25,000 dong-per-dollar anchor is about **\$1.60** per share.
- Foreign price with the room premium: 40,000 × 1.07 = 42,800 dong, about **\$1.71** per share.

On a \$1,000,000 position the premium costs an extra 7 percent, or **\$70,000**, just for the legal right to hold the stock as a foreigner. And the premium is not static — it widens when foreign demand rises against a fixed cap and collapses if the cap is raised or the demand fades. The core idea: in a room-constrained name, part of what a foreigner pays is not for the business at all but for *legal access to it*, and that access premium is itself a tradable, reform-sensitive variable.

## The frontier discount and what an upgrade unlocks

Now we come to the catalyst that hangs over everything: Vietnam's classification as a frontier market, and what would change if it were upgraded to emerging.

Start with what classification *is*. MSCI and FTSE Russell each maintain a hierarchy — developed, emerging, frontier — and they assign every country a tier based on economic development, market size and liquidity, and *market accessibility* (can foreigners actually get money in and out and trade efficiently). The classification is not academic. Trillions of dollars of passive money — index funds and ETFs — automatically buy the constituents of these tiers in the weights the index assigns. The pool of money tracking *emerging-market* indices is vastly larger than the pool tracking *frontier* indices. Vietnam sits in frontier today; it is one of the largest, most liquid markets in that tier, which is exactly why an upgrade would be so consequential.

![The frontier discount versus what an emerging market upgrade unlocks for Vietnam](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges-6.png)

The *frontier discount* is the gap in valuation that results from this restricted buyer pool. With fewer eligible buyers — mostly specialist active frontier funds rather than the giant passive emerging-market machine — Vietnamese stocks trade at lower price-to-earnings (P/E) multiples than comparable emerging-market peers. The discount is partly about risk (frontier markets are perceived as riskier) and partly, mechanically, about *demand*: fewer buyers chasing the same earnings means lower prices for those earnings.

What an upgrade unlocks is twofold. First, *passive inflows*: when an index provider moves Vietnam from frontier to emerging, every fund tracking the emerging-market index must buy Vietnam in its new index weight, on a schedule, regardless of view. Estimates for the one-time and sustained inflows from an upgrade have run into the **several billions of dollars**, against a market whose investable free float for foreigners is itself limited — a large flow into a constrained pool. Second, *re-rating*: a wider, deeper buyer pool lifts the multiple the market is willing to pay for the same earnings. The two reinforce each other.

But — and this is the practitioner's point — the upgrade is *conditional on the legal architecture we have been describing*. The index providers do not upgrade a market because its economy is strong. They upgrade it when *accessibility* clears their bar: pre-funding addressed, settlement modernized, foreign room and information access improved, capital movement smoothed. This is why the reforms (non-pre-funding, the new trading system, the Securities Law amendments) are the real catalysts to watch. The economy has been ready for years. The legal plumbing is what gates the upgrade.

#### Worked example: a frontier-to-emerging re-rating

Suppose Vietnam's market trades at a frontier P/E of **11x** earnings while comparable emerging-market peers trade at **15x**. Take a representative HOSE company earning \$50,000,000 a year.

- At the frontier multiple: 11 × \$50m = **\$550,000,000** market cap.
- At the emerging multiple, if an upgrade closes the gap: 15 × \$50m = **\$750,000,000** market cap.

That is a **\$200,000,000 increase, or +36 percent**, from the re-rating alone — no change in the company's earnings, only a change in who is allowed to buy it and at what multiple. Now scale that across the whole market: even a *partial* closing of the discount over hundreds of names is an enormous repricing. The core idea: a classification upgrade is one of the rare events where the legal and accessibility wrapper around a company, not the company itself, is the entire trade.

## The KRX trading system: the plumbing reform

For years Vietnam's market ran on an aging trading system inherited, in part, from a partnership with the Thai exchange. Its limitations were a quiet but real constraint: it struggled to support the modern features a developed market takes for granted, and on heavy days it could cap throughput. A market cannot offer intraday short selling, same-day trading, or sophisticated order types if its core matching engine cannot handle them.

The fix is a new system built with the Korea Exchange (KRX), generally called the *KRX system* or *KRX go-live*. After years of delays, it went live in 2025. The significance is that a modern matching engine is the *technical precondition* for the market-structure reforms that the upgrade and the local industry both want: shorter and more flexible settlement, the operational backbone for non-pre-funding, central-counterparty (CCP) clearing down the road, and eventually products like covered short selling and intraday trading.

Why does a foreign investor care about a piece of exchange technology? Because it is the enabling layer beneath the catalysts. Pre-funding reform, faster settlement, and the index upgrade all assume a trading and clearing system that can support them. The KRX go-live does not by itself re-rate the market — but it removes the technical excuse, and it is a milestone the index providers and reform-watchers track closely. When you read that "the KRX system is live", translate it as "the technical blocker to the next wave of accessibility reforms is cleared".

The deeper prize the new system unlocks is *central-counterparty clearing* (a CCP). A CCP is an institution that steps between the two sides of every trade and guarantees settlement — if your counterparty fails, the CCP makes you whole. This is the standard plumbing of developed markets, and it is the technical answer to the exact risk that pre-funding was a blunt instrument to manage. Once a CCP stands behind trades, the system no longer needs every foreign buyer to pre-fund 100 percent of cash, because the CCP, not the cash buffer, guarantees the trade completes. That is why the sequence matters: the KRX system enables a CCP, the CCP enables non-pre-funding at scale, and non-pre-funding removes the single barrier the index providers cite most. A foreign investor reading the reform calendar should watch that chain — system, then CCP, then the broad end of pre-funding — because each link is a discrete, observable milestone on the road to an upgrade.

A note of realism: this reform path has slipped repeatedly. The KRX system was years late; pre-funding fixes have rolled out in partial, qualified forms; and index reviews have deferred more than once. The lesson for positioning is that the *direction* is high-conviction but the *timing* is not. Reform-driven trades in Vietnam reward patience and punish leverage, because the catalysts are real but arrive on the government's schedule, not the market's.

## Liquidity, credit, and the dong: the SBV's hand on the market

We have spent most of this post on the securities side, but a foreign investor who ignores the money side will be repeatedly surprised. The SBV's tools are not stock-market tools, yet they move the stock market through liquidity.

The central tool is the **credit-growth quota**. Rather than steering the economy purely through an interest rate the way the Federal Reserve does, the SBV historically set an explicit annual ceiling on how fast each bank's loan book may grow — a system-wide credit target in the low-to-mid teens percent in recent years. When the SBV loosens the quota, banks lend more, money sloshes into the economy and often into asset markets, and the VN-Index tends to lift. When it tightens — as it did defensively in 2022 to protect the dong and contain the bond fallout — credit dries up, margin lending to retail investors contracts, and the index falls. Retail investors dominate daily turnover on HOSE, and much of their buying is on borrowed money, so the credit cycle and the index cycle are tightly linked.

The quota is unusual enough to be worth dwelling on. Most central banks set a price for money (an interest rate) and let the *quantity* of credit adjust. The SBV does the reverse: it sets a *quantity* (each bank's permitted loan-book growth) more directly than most peers. In practice the SBV announces a system-wide credit-growth target for the year and parcels it out to individual banks, sometimes revising the allocations mid-year as conditions change. The recent targets have run in the low-to-mid teens — 14.0 percent in 2019 and 2022, 14.5 percent in 2023, and 15.0 percent in 2024 — and the *timing* of when banks are allowed to use their room can itself be a market event. When the SBV grants extra room late in the year, banks lend, liquidity improves, and equities often catch a bid; when room is exhausted, lending stalls. A foreign investor who tracks only the policy interest rate will miss this lever entirely, because the binding constraint is frequently the *quota*, not the rate.

The **dong** itself is a constraint. Because the SBV manages the exchange rate within a band, a foreign investor's returns have a currency leg that the SBV partly controls. The mechanism is a daily central reference rate set by the SBV, around which the market rate may move within a permitted band. The reference rate has drifted weaker over the years — roughly 23,200 dong per dollar in 2019 and 2020, about 23,700 in 2022, 24,200 in 2023, and 24,600 in 2024 — and the market rate sits above that. For a dollar-based investor, a 5 percent depreciation of the dong is a straight 5 percent haircut to dollar returns, layered on top of whatever the stocks did in local terms. If the SBV is forced to let the dong weaken to defend reserves — as pressure mounted in 2022 and again in later tightening cycles — a foreign holder's dollar returns take a hit even if the stocks rose in dong terms. The capital account is not fully open, so you cannot always hedge this cleanly or move money instantly. The dong band is, in effect, a slow-moving risk factor sitting under every foreign position, and it is a *legal* construct — the SBV chooses the reference rate and the band width.

There is a third money-side lever worth naming because it shows how broad the SBV's reach is: **gold-import and gold-market rules**. The SBV tightly controls the import and domestic trade of gold, which historically created a large premium between the local gold price and the world price and which is a pressure-relief valve for the dong. This sits outside the equity market directly, but it is part of the same money-side toolkit — when the SBV manages gold, it is managing demand for dollars and the pressure on the dong, which loops back into the liquidity backdrop for stocks.

We treat the credit-quota system, the dong band, and the gold rules in depth in a dedicated post; here the point is structural. The SBV does not regulate your stock, but it regulates the *liquidity* that prices your stock and the *currency* that translates your return back into dollars. Reading the SBV's stance is part of reading the market — the next section shows how this played out for real.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Vietnam has one regulator, like the SEC."** It has two that both gate a foreign trade, plus the Ministry of Finance above the SSC. The SSC governs the security; the SBV governs the money and the FX; the MOF sits above the SSC. The 2022 drawdown — VN-Index from 1,528 to 874, about 43 percent — was a securities-side legal shock (a bond decree and fraud enforcement) amplified by a money-side tightening (the SBV defending the dong and squeezing credit). If you watch only one regulator, you will miss half the catalysts.

**Misconception 2: "Pre-funding is a minor settlement detail."** It is the single barrier most cited by index providers and a real, quantifiable return drag. On a \$2,000,000 portfolio keeping a 15 percent idle-cash buffer, the forgone yield is on the order of \$13,500 a year — roughly 0.675 percent of assets, every year. It is not a footnote; it is a structural tax on holding the market, and removing it is worth real basis points to every foreign holder.

**Misconception 3: "A stock is a stock — the board it trades on does not matter."** The board is most of the investability. Two identical \$200,000,000 companies, one on HOSE and one on UPCoM, can have a 25-to-1 difference in daily turnover (\$1,000,000 versus \$40,000 in the worked example), which turns a 10-day position build on HOSE into a 250-day slog on UPCoM. The board sets how much capital you can actually deploy and recover, regardless of the business.

**Misconception 4: "If the economy is strong, the upgrade will come."** Index upgrades are not awarded for GDP growth; they are awarded for *accessibility*. Vietnam's economy has grown 6 to 8 percent for years while it stayed in the frontier tier. The gating items are legal and operational — pre-funding, settlement, foreign room, information access — not economic. That is exactly why the reforms (non-pre-funding, the KRX system, the Securities Law amendments) are the catalysts, not the GDP print.

**Misconception 5: "Registered foreign investment numbers tell you what is actually invested."** Vietnam reports FDI as both *registered* (committed) and *disbursed* (actually deployed), and the two differ by a wide margin every year. Registered FDI ran around \$38 billion in 2024 while disbursed was about \$25 billion — a roughly \$13 billion gap. Reading the headline registered number as money-in-the-ground overstates reality by a third or more. Always check disbursed against registered.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2022 crackdown drawdown.** The clearest case study is the one we opened with. Through 2021 and early 2022, a corporate-bond boom (issuance hit roughly 658 trillion dong in 2021) and heavy retail margin lending pushed the VN-Index to a record 1,528 on 6 January 2022. Then the legal architecture asserted itself. Authorities arrested high-profile figures on fraud charges, tightened the corporate-bond decree, and the bond market seized up — issuance collapsed to roughly 270 trillion dong in 2022, less than half the prior year. Property developers who had funded themselves with bonds were squeezed. Simultaneously the SBV tightened to defend the dong as global rates rose. The index bottomed at 874 on 15 November 2022 — a 43 percent fall driven by law, enforcement, and liquidity, not by a recession in the real economy.

![VN-Index level from late 2021 to 2024 showing the 2022 crackdown drawdown and recovery](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges-2.png)

The recovery is as instructive as the fall. As the policy response stabilized the bond market and credit loosened, the index recovered to about 1,007 by end-2022, 1,130 by end-2023, and 1,267 by end-2024 — still below its 2022 peak years later. The episode is the spine of this whole series in one chart: a rule change (the bond decree plus enforcement) altered the rules of the game, the market repriced violently and *ahead* of any economic damage, and the path back tracked the policy and liquidity response.

**The FDI commitment-versus-reality gap.** The second real-market pattern is the persistent wedge between registered and disbursed FDI. Vietnam's growth story rests on foreign factory investment, and the headline registered numbers are genuinely strong: about \$38.0 billion in 2019, dipping to \$27.7 billion in 2022, recovering to \$36.6 billion in 2023 and \$38.2 billion in 2024. But disbursed FDI — the money actually deployed — ran far lower and far steadier: \$20.4 billion in 2019 rising gradually to \$25.4 billion in 2024.

![Vietnam FDI registered commitments versus disbursed realized in USD billion per year](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges-5.png)

That steadier disbursed line is the better read on the real economy: foreign manufacturers keep building, regardless of the stock market's mood swings. For an equity investor the takeaway is to separate the two narratives. The *FDI story* (factories, exports, the supply-chain shift) is real and durable and shows up in disbursed numbers. The *equity-market story* (the VN-Index, foreign portfolio flows) is governed by the legal architecture and is far more volatile. A great factory economy can sit on top of a frustrating, gated equity market — and for years, it has.

**The credit-quota liquidity cycle.** A quieter but recurring pattern is the way the SBV's credit-quota decisions ripple into the index. In years when the SBV granted banks additional lending room — often in the back half of the year as it judged inflation and the dong to be under control — domestic margin lending expanded, retail buying power rose, and the VN-Index tended to firm. In tightening years the reverse held: room ran out, margin contracted, and the index struggled even when corporate earnings were fine. Because retail margin is such a large share of HOSE turnover, the credit quota functions almost like a throttle on the index. A foreign investor watching the SBV's quota announcements is reading a leading indicator of the local liquidity tide, which is a different and often earlier signal than anything in the companies' results.

**The reform-driven re-rating attempts.** Each time the market has believed an upgrade was near — when non-pre-funding rolled out, when the KRX system approached go-live, when FTSE signaled progress on its watchlist — Vietnamese equities have shown a tendency to rally on the *anticipation*, exactly as the discounting mechanism predicts. The market prices the *expected* accessibility improvement before it fully arrives, then fades or confirms on the actual decision. This is the legal-event drift-and-repricing pattern, applied to a market-classification catalyst.

#### Worked example: VN-Index sensitivity to a foreign-flow shock

Foreign portfolio investors own only a slice of Vietnam's float, but at the margin their flows move the index because liquidity is shallow relative to a developed market. Suppose an upgrade triggers **\$2,000,000,000** of net passive buying into a foreign-investable HOSE float worth, say, **\$40,000,000,000**. That is incremental demand equal to **5 percent** of the available float arriving over a compressed window.

In a deep market, 5 percent of float absorbed gradually barely moves price. In a shallow one, demand that size, arriving on an index-rebalance schedule, can lift prices by a multiple of the naive percentage because sellers are scarce and the buying is price-insensitive. If even a conservative *price-impact* assumption says each 1 percent of float bought lifts the index roughly 2 to 3 percent in a thin market, a 5 percent-of-float inflow implies a **10 to 15 percent** index move from the flow alone — on top of any re-rating. The core idea: in a constrained, gated market, *who is allowed to buy* can matter more for the index than what the companies earn, because the supply of stock for sale is fixed and the demand is suddenly not.

## How to trade it: the playbook

This is the payoff. Here is how a foreign investor actually uses the legal architecture, rather than just admiring it.

**Read the structure before the stock.** Before you analyze a single Vietnamese company, answer three legal questions: Which board does it trade on (HOSE, HNX, or UPCoM)? Is there foreign room left, or is the foreign-ownership limit full (covered next post)? And what is its real daily liquidity in dollars? These three answers bound the entire opportunity. A brilliant company on UPCoM with no liquidity and full foreign room is, for a foreign fund, often un-investable — and that is a structural fact, not a judgment on the business.

**Underwrite the access frictions explicitly.** Build the all-in cost of being a foreigner into your return assumptions from the start: FX round-trip (roughly 0.5 percent), custody (roughly 0.15 percent a year), and pre-funding cash drag (variable, but on the order of 0.5 to 0.7 percent a year for an active book). Roughly 1 percent a year of structural friction must be cleared before your stock-picking adds value. If your edge is thin, the friction eats it.

**Watch both regulatory calendars, not one.** Build a catalyst calendar with two columns. On the securities side (SSC and the exchanges): listing-rule and disclosure changes, foreign-room adjustments, the KRX system's feature rollout, settlement-cycle changes, and the index providers' review dates. On the money side (SBV): the annual credit-quota announcement and any mid-year revisions, policy-rate moves, the central reference rate's trajectory for the dong, and reserve and gold actions that signal currency stress. The two columns interact. A securities-side reform landing while the SBV is loosening credit is a double tailwind; the same reform landing into an SBV tightening cycle and a weakening dong can be neutralized entirely. The 2022 episode was exactly the bad case — a securities-side shock compounded by money-side tightening. A reader who tracks only one calendar will be repeatedly blindsided by the other.

**Position for the reform catalysts, but separate them from the economy.** The catalysts to watch are specific and legal: the rollout and broadening of non-pre-funding; the KRX system's full feature enablement; the Securities Law amendments; and — the big one — MSCI and FTSE classification reviews and decisions. Each is a discrete, calendar-able event. The discounting mechanism says the market will price the *expected* upgrade ahead of the decision, so the trade is to accumulate into credible reform progress and be positioned *before* the index providers confirm, not after. Keep this separate from the FDI/factory story, which is a slower, steadier real-economy thesis best read through disbursed FDI, not the stock market.

#### Worked example: sizing the upgrade trade as a binary bet

Treat the upgrade as a binary catalyst and price it the way a merger-arbitrage trader prices a deal. Suppose you judge the probability of an emerging-market upgrade decision in the next review at **40 percent**. If it happens, a basket of foreign-favored HOSE names re-rates by, say, **25 percent** (combining the multiple expansion and the flow impact from earlier examples); if it fails, the basket fades **8 percent** as disappointed positioning unwinds.

The expected value of the catalyst is: 0.40 × (+25 percent) + 0.60 × (−8 percent) = +10.0 percent − 4.8 percent = **+5.2 percent**.

On a \$1,000,000 allocation that is an expected **\$52,000** of catalyst-driven return, with a skew that pays \$250,000 in the win case and costs \$80,000 in the loss case. Now subtract the structural friction we computed — roughly 1 percent a year all-in — and the carry while you wait. The position only makes sense if your probability estimate is better than the market's *and* you can hold through repeated deferrals without forced selling. The core idea: an upgrade is a probability-weighted legal event, so you size it on expected value and on your staying power through delay, not on conviction that it must happen.

**Know what invalidates the view.** The bull case on Vietnam's equity market is fundamentally a *reform-and-upgrade* case layered on a strong economy. It is invalidated by: reforms stalling or reversing (pre-funding reform that fails to attract real institutional uptake; an index review that defers the upgrade again); a renewed legal/enforcement shock like 2022 that freezes a funding channel; or an SBV forced into hard tightening and dong defense that drains liquidity and crushes the currency leg of foreign returns. The single cleanest invalidation signal is an MSCI or FTSE review that *defers* an expected upgrade with cited accessibility gaps — that tells you the legal plumbing the whole thesis depends on is not yet fixed.

**Size for the liquidity, not the conviction.** The final discipline is humility about exit. In a shallow, gated market, the position you can build is capped by what you can sell in a stressed tape. Size every position to the liquidity cliff, especially off HOSE, and assume that in a 2022-style event the exit door narrows for everyone at once. The legal architecture that gates entry also gates exit — and the exit is the part that matters when it goes wrong.

The through-line of this whole series applies cleanly here: a rule (the Securities Law and its decrees) and a regulator's stance (the SSC on access, the SBV on liquidity and the dong) change the rules of the game; the market discounts those changes into prices ahead of the economic effect; and the practitioner who has learned the legal map first is the one who can read the catalyst early, size the repricing, and know exactly what would prove the view wrong.
