> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — History gives clear base rates for how markets behave around war, and those base rates, not the headlines, are the edge: equities sell off then usually recover within months, oil and gold spike, safe-haven bonds and the dollar bid, and defense stocks re-rate durably.
>
> - The start of a conflict is usually **peak fear**, because the uncertainty is highest before the worst case is ruled out. That is why "buy the invasion" works more often than not: the fear was pre-priced into the selloff, and the resolution of uncertainty is what lets the market recover.
> - The reaction map is repeatable: **equities down, oil up, gold up, safe-haven bonds and USD and CHF up, defense up**. Each leg has a mechanism, and each leg has a typical size.
> - The one distinction that matters is **contained regional war vs systemic oil-supply war**. A contained war is a fade; a systemic shock that chokes a global supply line (1973, arguably 1990) leaves a lasting macro scar and is not a fade.
> - The one number to remember: across the textbook cases, the S&P 500 sold off a **median of roughly 5 to 8 percent** into a geopolitical shock and was back to its pre-event level within about **three to four weeks** in the contained cases. The defense re-rating, by contrast, is durable — US aerospace and defense rose about **38 percent** in the three years after the 2022 Ukraine invasion.

On the morning of February 24, 2022, Russian forces crossed into Ukraine. The S&P 500 futures had been sliding for weeks on the buildup, and in the first hours of the invasion the index opened down hard — the cash market traded down more than 2 percent intraday and the Nasdaq fell into a technical bear market. Brent crude vaulted above \$100 a barrel for the first time since 2014. Gold spiked. By the close that same day, the S&P 500 had reversed its entire intraday loss and finished **up** more than 1.5 percent. Anyone who sold the open and bought nothing was left staring at a green candle that made no emotional sense.

It made perfect sense as market mechanics. The headlines were at their most frightening exactly when the fear was already in the price. The repricing into the buildup had done the work; the actual invasion resolved the binary question — *will it happen?* — and a resolved uncertainty is worth more to a market than an unresolved one, even when the resolution is bad. This is the central, counterintuitive fact of war and markets, and it has a long track record behind it.

This post is about that track record. It is deliberately analytical and politically neutral: we are studying how the machine reprices conflict, not passing judgment on any conflict or any actor. The thesis is simple. War creates a measurable uncertainty premium; that premium spikes and then, in most cases, fades; the assets that move do so in a repeatable pattern with typical sizes; and an investor who knows the base rates can fade the panic, hedge the genuine tail, and tell the difference between the two. The figure below is the whole map on one page — the rest of the post fills it in.

## Foundations: why war creates uncertainty and how that uncertainty prices in

Start from zero. A market price is, at bottom, the present value of a stream of expected future cash flows, discounted at a rate that reflects how risky those cash flows are. Two things move a price: a change in the **expected cash flows** (will companies earn more or less?) and a change in the **discount rate** (how much extra return do investors demand to hold a risky asset right now?). War hits both, but the fast, violent move on day one is almost entirely the second one — the discount rate, or more precisely the **risk premium** embedded in it.

The **risk premium** is the extra expected return investors require to hold a risky asset instead of a safe one. When the future becomes harder to forecast, investors demand a bigger cushion, so they pay less today for the same expected cash flows. That is a higher discount rate, and a higher discount rate mechanically lowers prices even if no company's earnings have changed yet. War is a near-perfect generator of that effect because it widens the range of possible outcomes: it could stay local and end in weeks, or it could draw in more countries, choke an energy supply line, trigger a recession, or escalate to something far worse. The market cannot know which, so it prices a fatter tail — and a fatter tail means a higher premium and lower prices. We build out the mechanics of this premium channel in [geopolitics as a market factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/geopolitics-as-a-market-factor-the-risk-premium-channel); here we focus on war specifically.

It helps to separate the two ways war can hit a price, because they behave completely differently and the whole trade hangs on telling them apart. A simple way to write the value of an equity index is `value = expected earnings / (risk-free rate + risk premium)`. The denominator is the discount rate; the numerator is the cash flows. A **discount-rate shock** raises the risk premium in the denominator while leaving earnings untouched — the price falls, but nothing has actually happened to the underlying businesses, so the move reverses as soon as the premium normalizes. A **cash-flow shock** lowers the numerator — earnings genuinely fall (because input costs rose, or demand collapsed, or a recession arrived) — and *that* move does not reverse when sentiment calms, because the damage is real. The single most useful question you can ask about any war selloff is: *is this a discount-rate move or a cash-flow move?* Almost every contained-war selloff is a pure discount-rate move — fast, violent, and reversible. The rare systemic war (1973) is a cash-flow move — slower to fully arrive, but durable. The base-rate edge is mostly a bet that you are looking at the first kind when the crowd is treating it as the second.

To make that concrete: if an index trades at 5,000 on expected earnings of \$250 (a 20× multiple, or equivalently a 5 percent earnings yield), and a war scare pushes the demanded risk premium up by half a percentage point with earnings unchanged, the multiple compresses from 20× toward about 18.5× and the price falls to roughly 4,630 — a 7.4 percent drop with not one dollar of earnings lost. That entire 7.4 percent is "owed back" the moment the premium reverts. Contrast a 1973-style shock where the oil price quadruples, corporate margins are crushed, and expected earnings themselves fall 15 percent to \$212: now the price falls to `\$212 × 18.5 = ~3,920` even at the same elevated multiple, and the earnings piece of that loss does not come back when fear fades. Same headline ("war, market down"), opposite trade.

You can measure the premium spike directly. The **Geopolitical Risk (GPR) index**, built by economists Dario Caldara and Matteo Iacoviello, counts how often major newspapers mention war, terrorism, and military tension; it is normalized so that 100 is the long-run average. The index is not a market price — it is a measure of *attention* to geopolitical risk — but attention and the risk premium move together, and the GPR's spikes line up cleanly with the moments markets repriced conflict.

![Line chart of the Geopolitical Risk index spiking at 9 11 Iraq Crimea Ukraine and the Middle East then fading toward the long run average](/imgs/blogs/war-and-markets-how-conflict-prices-into-assets-2.png)

Two features of that chart are the whole game. First, every spike is real and large: 9/11 sent the index above 500, five times its average; Iraq 2003, the 2022 Ukraine invasion, and the 2023 Middle East escalation each more than doubled it. Second — and this is where the edge lives — **every spike fades**. The index does not stay elevated; it reverts toward 100 over months. The fear is acute and temporary. If the fear is temporary and the price drop tracks the fear, then the price drop is temporary too, *unless* the war does something durable to cash flows rather than just to sentiment. Sorting those two cases apart is the analytical core of trading war, and we will spend the back half of this post on it.

Why does the fear fade so reliably? Three forces pull it back toward baseline. The first is **habituation**: a shock is frightening in proportion to how novel it is, and as a conflict drags on without the feared escalation, each new headline carries less marginal information. The market stops reacting to the hundredth report of fighting the way it reacted to the first. The second is **resolution of the binary**: most of the fear in the buildup is about *whether* the worst case happens; once a few weeks pass without the supply line being cut, the nuclear threshold being crossed, or the conflict spreading, the market can prune those branches and reprice toward the (less bad) base case. The third is **adaptation of the real economy**: supply chains reroute, alternative suppliers ramp, central banks respond, and the feared economic damage often proves smaller than the first-day panic implied. Russian crude finding Indian and Chinese buyers in 2022 is the clean example — the feared global oil-supply gap simply did not materialize, so the premium that had priced it deflated.

The practical upshot is that geopolitical fear has a *half-life*, and it is short — typically measured in weeks, not years. That short half-life is precisely why the equity recovery is fast in the base-rate cases and why fading the panic has positive expectancy. The danger is mistaking a shock with a short half-life (a discount-rate move) for one with a long half-life (a cash-flow move). The 1973 embargo had a long half-life because it permanently raised an input cost; the 2022 Ukraine *equity* shock had a short half-life because the war, however terrible, did not durably damage US corporate earnings. The investor's job is to estimate the half-life before sizing, not after.

### The reaction map: which assets move, which way, and why

When the premium spikes, capital does not just sit in cash. It rotates along a well-worn path. Five legs move almost every time, and each has a mechanism you can name.

**Equities down.** Stocks are the highest-beta claim on an uncertain future, so they take the biggest hit from a rising risk premium. The selloff is a discount-rate move first (the premium widens) and only later, if at all, an earnings move (if the war actually dents demand or input costs). Because it is a premium move, it is fast and it is reversible — exactly the kind of move that snaps back when the premium normalizes. The dispersion *within* equities is its own signal: cyclicals, small caps, and anything exposed to the conflict region sell off hardest, while consumer staples, utilities, and other defensives hold up — a rotation that itself tells you the market is repricing risk appetite rather than a specific earnings hit. When the *whole* market falls roughly uniformly, that is a pure risk-premium move; when the selloff concentrates in oil-sensitive sectors (airlines, chemicals, autos), the market is starting to price a cash-flow channel, which is the early warning of a systemic case.

**Oil up.** Conflict, especially in or near an energy-producing region, raises the probability of a supply disruption. Oil prices in a *supply-risk premium*: the spot barrel may be flowing fine today, but the market pays up for the risk that it stops flowing tomorrow. This is the single most important leg, because oil is the one asset whose move can feed back into the real economy and convert a sentiment shock into a macro shock. The transmission is direct: oil is an input to almost everything, so a sustained price spike acts like a tax on global growth — it raises costs for producers and drains discretionary income from consumers at the pump. A rough rule of thumb practitioners use is that a sustained \$10-per-barrel rise in oil shaves on the order of 0.1 to 0.2 percentage points off the next year's US GDP growth and adds a few tenths to headline inflation. That is the channel through which a *war* becomes a *recession*, and it is why the oil leg is the one to watch above all others. We trace that channel in depth in [energy geopolitics and the oil weapon](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock).

**Gold up.** Gold is the oldest safe-haven asset — it has no counterparty, no default risk, and no government that can freeze it. When confidence in the system wobbles, gold catches a bid. But the move is usually concentrated in the initial spike; gold is a fear thermometer, not a war annuity, and it tends to give back gains as the fear fades. The reason gold so often round-trips is that its main competitor as a safe store of value is the *real* (inflation-adjusted) yield on Treasuries. Gold pays no income, so when a war scare prompts central banks to fight the resulting inflation with higher rates, real yields rise and the opportunity cost of holding gold goes up — which pulls gold back down even while the conflict continues. That is exactly what happened in 2022: the war bid faded as the Fed hiked. Gold wins durably in war only when the war is *also* a debasement story (rising inflation with central banks unable or unwilling to fight it), which is the minority case.

**Safe-haven bonds, the dollar, and the Swiss franc up.** The classic *flight to safety*. Money fleeing risky assets has to go somewhere, and it goes to the deepest, most liquid, most legally protected stores of value: US Treasuries, the US dollar, and the Swiss franc. Treasury yields fall (prices rise) as buyers pile in; the dollar strengthens because the world clears in dollars and panic is dollar-positive — a dynamic rooted in the dollar's reserve status (see [the petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance)). The Swiss franc and the Japanese yen have historically played the same haven role, backed by large external surpluses and political neutrality. But there is a crucial nuance that defines the systemic case: in a war that *also* spikes inflation through oil, bonds can sell off *instead* of rallying, because the inflation fear overwhelms the haven bid. A government bond that yields a fixed coupon is a terrible thing to hold into rising inflation, so the flight-to-safety into Treasuries only works cleanly in a *disinflationary* or neutral shock. When the war is an inflation shock, the haven moves *into* commodities and gold and *out of* nominal bonds. Hold that thought; it is the single sharpest tell that separates a contained shock from a systemic one.

**Defense stocks up.** This is the one leg with a *durable*, not transient, mechanism. A major conflict raises the expected path of government defense spending for years. That is not a sentiment move — it is a genuine upward revision to the future cash flows of defense contractors, and it shows up in two distinct ways: higher *earnings* as order books fill, and a higher *multiple* as the market decides those earnings are more durable and faster-growing than it previously assumed. Defense budgets are sticky on the way up — once a country lifts its spending target in response to a war, it rarely cuts back quickly, because the geopolitical anxiety that drove the increase persists long after the headlines fade. So while equities-down and gold-up tend to reverse, the defense re-rating tends to stick, which makes it the one leg you *hold* rather than fade.

That last distinction — transient sentiment moves vs durable cash-flow moves — is worth pausing on, because it is the difference between a trade you fade and a trade you hold. The first four legs (equities, oil, gold, the haven bid) are mostly discount-rate phenomena that mean-revert; the defense leg is a genuine cash-flow phenomenon that persists. Mixing them up — fading the defense re-rating, or holding the gold spike — is the most common way to be right about the map and wrong about the money.

### "War is bullish once it starts" — the pattern and the mechanism

Here is the line that sounds cynical and is actually just arithmetic: for equities, **the start of a war is often the bottom, not the top, of the selloff**. Put plainly, the worst moment for a stock index is usually the moment of maximum uncertainty, and that moment arrives *before* and *at* the invasion, not after it.

![Timeline of sell the buildup buy the war showing peak fear at the invasion and recovery within three months](/imgs/blogs/war-and-markets-how-conflict-prices-into-assets-3.png)

Walk the timeline. In the buildup, headlines escalate and the market grinds lower as the probability of conflict rises from "unlikely" toward "likely." At the moment of invasion, the probability snaps to "certain" — but crucially, the *range of remaining outcomes narrows*. Before the invasion the market was pricing a wide distribution: maybe nothing happens, maybe a limited incursion, maybe a full war, maybe a wider regional war. The day the tanks roll, several of those branches are pruned. The market now knows what kind of conflict it is dealing with, and it can start pricing the actual cash-flow impact rather than the full fan of fears. A narrower distribution is a lower risk premium, and a lower premium is a higher price. The fear was the trade; the resolution of the fear unwinds it.

This is why the disciplined framing is **"sell the buildup, buy the war."** You do not chase the selloff into the event; you fade the panic *at* the event, because the event itself is usually peak premium. The Gulf War in 1991 is the textbook case, and we will work through it in the case-study section. But the logic is general, and it is the reason a calm investor with the base rates in hand can act when everyone else is selling.

## The historical base rates: what actually happened

Anecdotes are not edges; base rates are. The value of studying war and markets is that we have a long sample of major conflicts, and the equity reaction is remarkably consistent across them. The single most cited study, by S&P Capital IQ's Sam Stovall and replicated by many others, looked at the S&P 500's reaction to the major geopolitical and military shocks since World War II. The headline result: across roughly two dozen shocks, the median peak-to-trough drawdown was about **5 to 8 percent**, and the median time to recover the pre-event level was about **three to four weeks**. The mean is dragged higher by a few systemic episodes, but the typical case is a shallow, fast-healing dip.

This is exactly the kind of repeated, well-documented event where a *base rate* beats a *forecast*. A base rate is just the historical frequency of an outcome across many similar cases — "in N past invasions, the market did X within Y weeks." It is the outside view: instead of building a story about *this* war (the inside view, which feels rigorous but is mostly narrative), you ask what happened the last twenty times something like this occurred. Decades of forecasting research show the outside view systematically beats the inside view for repeated events, because the inside view over-weights the vivid specifics of the case in front of you and under-weights the boring statistical regularity. War is the textbook setting for this: the specifics are always uniquely terrifying, which is precisely why the crowd over-reacts and the base-rate trader gets paid. The whole edge is choosing the outside view when everyone else is trapped in the inside one.

Run through the textbook cases, because the dispersion around that median is the whole story.

**Pearl Harbor and World War II (December 1941).** The Dow fell about 10 percent in the weeks after the attack on December 7, 1941, and the market stayed weak through the spring of 1942 as the war news was uniformly bad. But the bottom came in **April 1942** — long before the war's outcome was decided — and the Dow then rose more than 50 percent over the next two years *while the war was still being fought*. This is the deepest, longest war drawdown in the modern US sample, and even it bottomed less than five months in, with the war's resolution still years away. The market discounted *eventual* clarity, not the armistice.

**Korea (June 1950).** North Korea crossed the 38th parallel on June 25, 1950. The S&P 500 fell about 12 percent over the next few weeks — a sharp, real drop, and a useful counterexample to the idea that the *first* low is always the bottom: the Korean War also produced a second scare in late 1950 when China entered the conflict, jolting the market again. But each scare faded, the index recovered the loss within roughly **six months**, and it ended 1950 higher. A contained regional war with no disruption to the US economy is a fast fade even when it has multiple frightening chapters — the fades just come one chapter at a time. The lesson is that "fade the panic" can mean fading *several* panics over the life of a long conflict, each one a fresh discount-rate spike against an unchanged earnings base.

**The Cuban Missile Crisis (October 1962).** The closest the world came to nuclear war. The market had been weak all year, but during the thirteen days of the crisis itself the S&P fell only about 6 to 7 percent — and then ripped higher the moment the crisis resolved, gaining more than 10 percent in the following weeks and over 30 percent in the next year. The Cuba case teaches the most uncomfortable lesson in this whole field: the nuclear tail is the one risk a market genuinely cannot price, because a true nuclear exchange voids the entire valuation framework — there is no "discount rate" for the end of the world, and no portfolio positioning that helps. Faced with a risk it cannot price, the market does the only rational thing: it *ignores* it and prices the base case (the crisis is resolved short of catastrophe). This is not optimism; it is the logical conclusion that hedging an unhedgeable tail is pointless. The implication for the trader is stark and a little grim: you cannot, and should not, position for the nuclear scenario, because in that scenario the trade does not matter. You position for the base case, accept that the unpriceable tail exists, and recognize that when the base case resolves benignly — as it almost always has — the relief rally is enormous precisely because the market was holding its breath.

**The Gulf War (1990 to 1991).** The single cleanest illustration of "sell the buildup, buy the war," and we devote a full case study to it below.

**9/11 (September 2001).** The attacks closed US equity markets for four trading days — the longest closure since the Depression, and itself a reminder that the legal plumbing of a crash (halts, closures, and the decision of *when* to reopen) is part of the market mechanics; we cover that machinery in [circuit breakers, halts, and the legal plumbing of a crash](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/circuit-breakers-halts-and-the-legal-plumbing-of-a-crash). When markets reopened on September 17, the S&P fell about **12 percent** over the following week, bottoming on September 21. From that low it recovered the entire loss within **about one month**, trading back above its September 10 close by mid-October. A massive shock to confidence, a fast V-shaped recovery once the immediate panic passed. The 9/11 case is also a caution against the deepest version of the fade trade: the recovery was fast, but the attack landed on a market already sliding into the dot-com recession, so the post-recovery rally was short-lived and the broader bear market resumed. The base-rate trade fades the *geopolitical* shock; it does not override a separate, ongoing macro trend. Attribution matters — which is the lesson Ukraine 2022 drives home hardest.

**Iraq (March 2003).** Perhaps the purest "buy the invasion" signal in the modern record. The S&P had fallen through the buildup of late 2002 and early 2003. The invasion began March 19 to 20, 2003 — and the market **bottomed on March 11, a week before**, then rallied roughly 30 percent over the rest of the year. The fear was fully discounted before a shot was fired.

**Crimea (2014).** Russia's annexation of Crimea in late February and March 2014 barely registered in US equities — a dip of 2 percent or less, recovered within days. Contained, no supply disruption, no US economic channel. The GPR index ticked up modestly (to about 152) and faded. A non-event for global markets, which is itself instructive: not every geopolitical headline is a tradeable shock, and the discipline of *not* trading the contained-non-events is as valuable as fading the big ones. Crimea also seeded a slow-burn risk that took eight years to detonate — the same actors, the same region, a far larger 2022 conflict — a reminder that the *base rate* is about the market reaction, not about whether the underlying tension is truly resolved. Markets faded Crimea correctly even though the geopolitical problem was not actually solved; the two questions (will the market recover, is the conflict over) are genuinely separate, and only the first one is the trade.

**Ukraine (2022).** The case we opened with. The S&P had drifted down through the January and February buildup; on the February 24 invasion it traded down sharply intraday and closed *up*; it then made a final low about three weeks later (mid-March) before recovering. The attribution point is the whole lesson of 2022, and getting it wrong cost a lot of people a lot of money: the broader 2022 bear market — the S&P fell about 25 percent peak to trough that year — was driven by the **Federal Reserve's rate-hiking cycle against inflation**, not by the war directly. The Fed went from a near-zero policy rate in March 2022 to 4.5 percent by December, the fastest tightening cycle in forty years (see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates)). Investors who blamed the war for the 2022 decline kept waiting for a "war resolution" rally that never came, because the thing crushing valuations was the discount rate's *risk-free* component rising, not the war's premium. The war-specific equity shock was textbook (fast dip, fast recovery); the macro bear market that swamped it was a monetary-policy story. Disentangling the two — what is the war doing versus what is the central bank doing — is the hardest and most important attribution an investor makes around a wartime selloff. Oil, gold, and defense, meanwhile, reacted exactly per the map.

**The 2023 to 2024 Middle East episodes.** The October 2023 escalation and the subsequent 2024 direct exchanges drove the GPR index up (to about 235 in October 2023) and briefly bid oil and gold, but the S&P 500's reaction was muted — single-digit-percent wobbles, fast recoveries — because the conflict did not durably disrupt global oil flows. Markets repeatedly faded these spikes, which by 2024 had trained traders to fade them faster each time.

The pattern across all of these is the same: a sharp drawdown clustered at the event, a median dip in the mid-single-digits to low-double-digits, and a recovery measured in weeks to a few months — *with one category of exception*.

### The exceptions: oil-supply shocks that leave a macro scar

The cases that did *not* fade quickly share one feature: the war disrupted the supply of oil enough to inflict lasting damage on the real economy. When that happens, the move stops being a sentiment move and becomes an earnings-and-inflation move, and earnings-and-inflation moves do not snap back.

**1973 (the Arab oil embargo).** Following the October 1973 Arab-Israeli war, OPEC members embargoed shipments to the US and several allies and quadrupled the oil price — from about \$3 to about \$12 a barrel within months. This was not a fear spike; it was a permanent step-change in the price of the economy's most important input. The result was a genuine supply-driven recession and one of the worst equity bear markets of the postwar era: the S&P fell about 48 percent from its 1973 peak to its 1974 trough, and it did not recover for years. The war was the trigger, but the *oil supply shock* was the mechanism, and the mechanism was real, not sentimental.

**1990 (the Gulf War, arguably).** Iraq's August 1990 invasion of Kuwait took roughly 4 million barrels a day of combined Iraqi and Kuwaiti production off the market and doubled oil from about \$17 to over \$40 a barrel. That oil spike contributed to the 1990 to 1991 US recession — so the Gulf War sits in a gray zone: the equity drawdown was fade-able (and the war itself produced a textbook V-bottom, see below), but the oil-supply component had a genuine, if brief, macro effect. The distinction that saved traders was that the disruption proved *temporary* — Saudi Arabia and others raised output, and the lost barrels came back.

The rule that falls out of these exceptions: **a war is a fade unless it durably chokes a global supply line.** The 1973 embargo did; the 2022 Ukraine war's effect on global *oil* did not (Russian crude largely kept flowing to non-Western buyers, even as European *gas* was severely disrupted). That single test — does it choke a global supply line? — is the most important question you can ask about a conflict, and it deserves its own framework.

### Separating a market-systemic war from a contained one

Most conflicts are *contained*: regional, with no disruption to a globally critical commodity or financial chokepoint. The market reaction is a sentiment spike that fades — the base-rate trade. A small minority are *systemic*: they threaten the global supply of oil (the Strait of Hormuz, a major exporter), or advanced semiconductors (Taiwan — see [Taiwan and the most important supply chain on earth](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/taiwan-semiconductors-and-the-most-important-supply-chain-on-earth)), or the integrity of the financial system itself. Those are not fades; they carry a lasting macro effect and require a hedge, not a dip-buy.

![Decision tree showing contained war leads to fade the panic while systemic war leads to respect the shock and hedge](/imgs/blogs/war-and-markets-how-conflict-prices-into-assets-5.png)

The decision tree above is the practitioner's first cut. The branch point is one question — *does the conflict choke a global supply line?* — and the two branches lead to opposite trades. The "no" branch (contained) leads to fading the panic: buy the selloff, expect recovery in weeks to months. The "yes" branch (systemic) leads to respecting the shock: hedge the macro hit, expect lasting inflation and rising recession risk, and do *not* assume mean reversion.

The hard part is judging the branch correctly in real time, before the data confirms it. A few tells help you decide which branch you are on while the shock is still fresh:

- **Is a critical commodity or chokepoint directly involved?** A war in or near a major oil-exporting region, or one that threatens the Strait of Hormuz (through which roughly a fifth of the world's seaborne oil passes) or the Taiwan Strait (which carries the bulk of the world's advanced semiconductors), has a credible systemic path. A war between two countries with no globally critical export has almost none. Crimea 2014 had none; a Hormuz closure would have a clear one.
- **Is the commodity *actually* disrupted, or just spiking on fear?** This is the decisive distinction. Oil spiking while barrels keep flowing is a fear premium that fades. Oil spiking *because real barrels have left the market* (an embargo, a blockade, a destroyed export terminal) is a cash-flow shock that persists. In 2022, despite the price spike, Russian crude volumes largely kept flowing — to different buyers — so the global *supply* was not actually cut, and the price reverted. The 1973 embargo physically removed barrels, and the price did not revert for years.
- **Are nominal bonds rallying or selling off?** In a contained, disinflationary shock, Treasuries rally (the haven bid). In a systemic, inflationary shock, Treasuries *sell off* even as equities fall — the rare "everything down" tape — because the market is pricing higher inflation and a hawkish central bank. A bond market that refuses to rally during an equity selloff is screaming "this is an inflation shock, not a fear shock."
- **Where is the VIX?** A volatility spike into the 25 to 35 zone says "contained, tradeable wobble." A spike above 50 says "systemic, respect it." The level is a real-time crowd-sourced read on which branch the market itself believes it is on.

Get the branch right and the rest of the playbook follows mechanically. Get it wrong — fade a 1973, or hedge a Crimea — and you either take a real loss or pay for insurance you never needed. The next section quantifies how the legs actually behaved in the real cases, so you have the base rates to calibrate the branch in the moment.

## Common misconceptions

Four beliefs about war and markets are widely held, intuitive, and wrong in ways you can put numbers on.

**Misconception 1: "War crashes the market for a long time."** Usually false, and usually by a wide margin. The base-rate sample says the typical drawdown is **5 to 8 percent** and the typical recovery is **three to four weeks**, not the years people imagine. Even the deep cases bottomed early: Pearl Harbor's WWII low came in April 1942, less than five months after the attack and years before the war ended; 9/11's low came ten days after the attack and was fully recovered within a month; Iraq 2003 bottomed *before* the invasion. The genuine long crashes (1973) were oil-supply shocks, not war as such. If your model is "war means a multi-year bear market," the historical record will repeatedly take the other side of your trades.

**Misconception 2: "You should sell on the invasion."** Usually false — the invasion is often the bottom. Iraq 2003 bottomed March 11, eight days before the March 19 invasion. Ukraine 2022 closed *up* more than 1.5 percent on the February 24 invasion day after opening sharply lower. The Gulf War made its low the night the air campaign began (more below). Selling on the invasion means selling at maximum priced-in fear — the worst possible entry on the short side and, more often, the moment to buy. The fear is in the price *before* the event; the event resolves it.

**Misconception 3: "Gold always wins in war."** Mostly false beyond the initial spike. Gold reliably catches a bid in the first days of a shock — it is the purest fear asset — but it tends to give back much of that gain as the premium fades, *unless* the war coincides with a lasting inflation or currency-debasement story. After the 2022 Ukraine invasion, gold spiked toward \$2,070 in the first weeks and then *fell* back below \$1,650 by September 2022 as the Fed hiked rates and the dollar surged — a roughly 20 percent round-trip down from the spike. Gold is a thermometer, not an annuity; trading it as a war hedge means capturing the spike and respecting the mean reversion, not buying and holding through the conflict. We cover gold's safe-haven mechanics in depth in the [gold series](/blog/trading/gold).

**Misconception 4: "You can predict the conflict, so the edge is in the geopolitical call."** False, and it is the most expensive belief on this list. The edge in war trading is *not* forecasting whether or when a war starts, how it unfolds, or who wins — those are genuinely hard to predict, and the market is full of confident geopolitical forecasters who lose money. The edge is in the *reaction*: knowing the base-rate response and positioning for it regardless of the geopolitical outcome. The Iraq 2003 bottom came eight days *before* the invasion, which means you did not need to know the war's outcome to profit — you needed to know that the fear was fully priced and the resolution would unwind it. Conflate the two skills and you will spend your time on the unpredictable input (the geopolitics) instead of the predictable output (the market reaction), which is exactly backwards. The base rate is the edge; the forecast is a distraction.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three episodes show the map and the base rates in motion: the Gulf War V-bottom, the 2022 Ukraine multi-asset reaction, and the typical fade.

### The Gulf War V-bottom (1990 to 1991)

This is the case every desk teaches. Iraq invaded Kuwait on August 2, 1990. From the July peak, the S&P 500 fell about **17 percent** into its October low as oil doubled and a US-led intervention looked certain — a textbook "sell the buildup" leg, deepened by the oil-supply component. The market then chopped sideways through the autumn standoff, with the question hanging: would the coalition attack, and how badly would it go?

The answer came the night of **January 16 to 17, 1991**, when the air campaign ("Operation Desert Storm") began. And here is the punchline: the market **bottomed that night and gapped up the next morning**. The S&P rose about 3.7 percent on January 17 alone and never looked back, recovering its entire 17 percent drawdown within roughly two months and ending 1991 up about 26 percent.

Oil did the mirror image, and the oil move is the cleanest illustration of a fear premium evaporating that markets have ever produced. Crude had spiked to the low \$40s in anticipation of the war, pricing a scenario in which the conflict dragged on, Kuwaiti and Saudi oil fields were set ablaze, and supply was disrupted for an extended period. When the air campaign began and it became immediately clear that the coalition had total air supremacy — that this would be a fast, decisive win rather than a protracted quagmire — that entire feared scenario was priced out in hours. Oil *collapsed* more than \$10 a barrel in a single session, one of the largest one-day drops on record at the time, falling from the low \$40s toward the low \$30s and then continuing lower. The supply disruption the market had paid up to insure against was now obviously not going to happen, so the insurance premium was refunded all at once.

The two moves together — equities gapping up, oil collapsing, both on the same overnight news — are the same event viewed through two assets. The uncertainty that had been crushing equities and inflating oil was the identical uncertainty: *will there be a war, and will it be a quagmire that chokes oil supply?* The instant the bombs fell and the answer became "yes there is a war, but no it will not be a quagmire," both legs reversed simultaneously. Peak fear was the buildup; the war itself was the all-clear. An investor who understood that the air campaign would resolve the *quagmire* question — not the *whether* question, which was already answered — had the single best risk-reward entry of the decade handed to them by everyone else's fear.

### The 2022 Ukraine reaction: oil, gold, and defense

Ukraine 2022 is the cleanest modern demonstration of the multi-asset map, because the legs separated so neatly. Equities took the sentiment hit and recovered the war-specific portion within weeks (the larger 2022 decline was a Fed story). Oil and gold spiked and then faded. Defense re-rated and *stayed* re-rated.

![Area chart of Brent crude spiking above 120 dollars after the Ukraine invasion then round tripping back near pre war levels by year end](/imgs/blogs/war-and-markets-how-conflict-prices-into-assets-4.png)

Brent crude is the headline. It had been near \$79 at the start of January 2022, drifted to the high \$90s on the buildup, jumped above \$99 on the invasion day, and peaked near **\$128 on March 8, 2022** as Western sanctions on Russian energy were threatened and an EU embargo loomed. Then it *round-tripped*: by late September 2022 Brent was back near \$84, and it ended the year near \$86 — essentially where it sat in early February, before the invasion. The supply-risk premium spiked and then deflated as Russian crude found non-Western buyers and the feared global supply gap never materialized. (European *natural gas* was a different and genuinely systemic story — TTF gas spiked from about €25/MWh to a peak near €339/MWh in August 2022 — which is precisely why Europe suffered a real energy-cost recession while the US did not. The contained-vs-systemic test applied at the *regional* level: oil was contained globally, gas was systemic for Europe.)

Gold told the thermometer story: a spike toward \$2,070 in early March followed by a 20 percent slide into the autumn as the Fed hiked and the dollar surged. Defense told the durable-cash-flow story, and it is the leg that paid the most to *hold*.

![Line chart of a US aerospace and defense index rebased to 100 at the Ukraine invasion rising about 38 percent over three years](/imgs/blogs/war-and-markets-how-conflict-prices-into-assets-6.png)

The defense re-rating did not fade. A US aerospace and defense index, rebased to 100 on the eve of the invasion, was up about 10 percent within five weeks, 19 percent by year-end 2022, and roughly **38 percent by the end of 2024**. The mechanism is exactly the one from the foundations: the invasion durably raised the expected path of NATO and European defense budgets — Germany announced a €100 billion special defense fund within days, and NATO members lifted spending targets — and that is a genuine, multi-year upward revision to defense-contractor cash flows, not a sentiment blip. This is the single most important practical lesson of the 2022 episode: *the fade legs faded and the durable leg endured, exactly as the mechanism predicts.*

### The typical fade — and how the VIX tells you the size

Most war headlines are contained, and the market fades them fast. The clearest way to see how *mild* most geopolitical shocks are is to put their volatility spike next to genuinely systemic shocks.

![Horizontal bar chart of VIX at stress events with the Ukraine invasion at 30 well below COVID at 82 and other systemic shocks](/imgs/blogs/war-and-markets-how-conflict-prices-into-assets-8.png)

The VIX — the market's "fear gauge," derived from S&P 500 option prices — closed at about **30.3 on the 2022 Ukraine invasion day**. That is elevated (the long-run VIX average is around 19) but it is *modest*: it sat below the 2010 flash crash (32.8), the 2018 "Volmageddon" (37.3), the 2024 yen-carry unwind (38.6), and far, far below the COVID crash (82.7). A 30 VIX is a tradeable wobble, not a systemic panic. The lesson for sizing: the VIX level at the shock is a real-time read on whether the market itself is treating the conflict as contained (VIX in the 25 to 35 zone, a fade) or systemic (VIX above 50, respect it). For the event-reaction mechanics across asset classes, see the [event-trading series](/blog/trading/event-trading).

## The playbook: trading the base rates, not the headlines

Everything above lands here. The edge in war is not predicting the conflict — you cannot, and trying to is how you lose. The edge is knowing the base rates, reading which case you are in, and positioning so that the base-rate outcome pays you. Before the four moves, one framing rule that governs all of them: **act on the base rate, but size for the tail.** The base rate says fade; the tail says the rare systemic case can hurt you badly. A trade that respects both is small enough that the systemic tail does not blow you up and large enough that the base-rate win matters. That is the discipline that lets you keep taking the positive-expectancy fade trade across many conflicts without one bad draw ending the game. Four moves implement it.

**1. Use the base rates to fade the panic — or hedge the tail.** In a *contained* conflict, the base rate says equities dip mid-single-digits and recover in weeks. So fade the panic: buy the selloff at or just after the invasion (peak priced-in fear), size it to survive a deeper-than-median dip, and target recovery over one to three months. In a *systemic* conflict (the supply-line test fails), do the opposite: do not buy the dip; hedge the macro hit with oil/energy exposure, gold, defensives, and tail options, because the move will not mean-revert.

**2. Trade the asset reaction map, leg by leg.** Equities: fade the dip in contained cases. Oil: the spike is real but usually mean-reverts once the supply gap fails to materialize, so capture the spike and fade the extension. Gold: capture the initial spike, respect the fade. Bonds/USD/CHF: the haven bid is the cleanest contained-case trade, but flips if the war is an inflation/oil shock (then bonds *sell off*). Defense: the one leg to *hold* — the re-rating is durable because the cash flows genuinely change.

**3. Distinguish contained vs systemic before you size.** Run the one-question test: *does this choke a global supply line?* Check the real-time tells — is oil actually disrupted (not just spiking on fear), is the VIX in the fade zone (25 to 35) or the panic zone (50+), is a major exporter or a chokepoint (Hormuz, Taiwan) directly involved? Contained → fade. Systemic → hedge. War-game the systemic branches explicitly before they happen, as in [scenario analysis and war-gaming](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/scenario-analysis-and-war-gaming-geopolitical-events).

**4. Know what invalidates the view.** The fade trade is invalidated by a genuine, *durable* oil-supply disruption (a Hormuz closure, a major exporter knocked offline for months) or by nuclear escalation. An oil-supply shock converts the sentiment trade into a macro trade and breaks the mean-reversion thesis — that is your stop. Nuclear escalation voids the entire valuation framework and is the one tail you hedge but never fade. If either tail starts to price, you are no longer in the base-rate game; flip to the systemic playbook immediately.

Two practical cautions sit on top of these four moves. First, **mind the attribution.** A wartime selloff often overlaps with an unrelated macro trend — the dot-com unwind around 9/11, the Fed's tightening cycle around Ukraine 2022. The base-rate trade fades the *war* component; it does nothing for a separate bear market running underneath. Before you size a fade, ask what else is moving the tape, and do not blame on the war what the central bank is actually doing. Second, **the base rate is a distribution, not a guarantee.** "Recovers in three to four weeks" is a median across two dozen episodes; any single episode can be the deep tail. That is why the fade is a *probabilistic* trade sized to survive a worse-than-median outcome, never an all-in conviction bet. You are not predicting this war's path; you are betting that the population of wars behaves the way it has, and sizing so that the occasional outlier does not ruin you. The four worked examples below put real dollars on each move.

#### Worked example: the base-rate fade trade

Take a contained-conflict invasion that knocks the S&P 500 down 6 percent from 5,000 to 4,700, with the historical base rate saying recovery to the pre-event level within about one month. You buy \$100,000 of an S&P 500 index fund at 4,700.

The base-rate target is the pre-event 5,000 level, a recovery of `(5,000 − 4,700) / 4,700 = 6.38%`. On \$100,000 that is a gain of `0.0638 × \$100,000 = \$6,380` if the base case plays out. Now weight it by the historical hit rate. Across the contained cases, the market recovered the pre-event level within roughly a month in about 70 percent of episodes; in the other 30 percent, say the dip extended another 6 percent to 4,420 before recovering, a temporary mark-to-market loss of `(4,700 − 4,420) / 4,700 = −5.96%`, or about −\$5,960 at the trough. The expected value of the trade over a one-month horizon is `0.70 × \$6,380 + 0.30 × (−\$5,960) = \$4,466 − \$1,788 = \$2,678`, a positive expectancy of about +2.7 percent for stepping in at peak fear.

The takeaway: a positive-expectancy bet exists precisely because most people are selling at the moment the base rate says to buy.

#### Worked example: the oil and gold spike-and-fade

Suppose an invasion spikes Brent crude from \$80 to \$120 (the 2022 path, roughly) and the base rate says the supply gap fails to materialize and oil round-trips toward \$85 within six months. You do not chase the spike long; you wait for the extension to exhaust and fade it. You short one Brent futures contract (1,000 barrels) at \$115 as the rally stalls, with a stop at \$130 (above the spike high, the level that would signal a *real* supply disruption).

If oil round-trips to \$85, the gain is `(\$115 − \$85) × 1,000 = \$30,000` per contract. If instead the supply shock is real and oil runs to your \$130 stop, the loss is `(\$130 − \$115) × 1,000 = \$15,000`. Your reward-to-risk is `\$30,000 / \$15,000 = 2:1`. Run the same logic on gold: it spikes from \$1,850 to \$2,070, you fade the extension expecting a reversion toward \$1,750 as the Fed-and-dollar story reasserts; the \$320 reversion on a 100-ounce position is `\$320 × 100 = \$32,000`, against a stop above \$2,150 risking `(\$2,150 − \$2,000) × 100 = \$15,000` if you entered the short at \$2,000.

The takeaway: the spike is the market over-paying for a supply disruption that, in contained wars, usually never arrives — so the edge is in the fade, with the stop set at the level that would prove you wrong.

#### Worked example: the defense-stock re-rating from a higher budget

A defense contractor trades at \$200 with earnings per share of \$10, a price-to-earnings (P/E) multiple of `\$200 / \$10 = 20×`. A major conflict durably raises the expected path of defense spending. Two things can happen to the stock: earnings rise as order books fill, and the *multiple* expands because the market now values those earnings as more durable and faster-growing.

Say the multiple re-rates from 20× to 23× on the higher-for-longer spending outlook — a `(23 − 20) / 20 = 15%` multiple expansion. On unchanged \$10 earnings that alone lifts the price to `23 × \$10 = \$230`, a \$30 or 15 percent gain. Now layer in the earnings effect: a sustained order surge lifts EPS to \$11.50 over two years. The combined price becomes `23 × \$11.50 = \$264.50`, a `(\$264.50 − \$200) / \$200 = 32%` total gain — closely matching the ~38 percent the real defense index delivered over three years after 2022. On a \$50,000 position, that is `0.32 × \$50,000 = \$16,000` of gain, and unlike the equity-dip and oil legs, it is *not* expected to reverse.

The takeaway: defense is the one war leg where both the earnings and the multiple move *up and stay up*, because the cash flows genuinely change rather than the sentiment.

#### Worked example: sizing a fade-the-war-panic position with an oil-shock stop

You want to fade a war panic but bound your risk against the chance it is the systemic exception. Your portfolio is \$1,000,000 and you cap the loss on this trade at 2 percent of capital, or \$20,000. You assign a base-rate probability of 70 percent that the conflict is contained (the fade works) and 30 percent that it is systemic (an oil-supply shock — your invalidation).

You buy the equity index down 6 percent, with a hard stop if oil breaks decisively above its spike high (the real-time tell that the supply line is genuinely choking — the systemic branch). Position size is set by the stop: if your stop on the equity leg is an additional 8 percent below entry (the level that would coincide with a confirmed oil-supply shock and a broken thesis), then to risk only \$20,000 you size the position at `\$20,000 / 0.08 = \$250,000`. Expected value: `0.70 × (recovery gain) − 0.30 × (\$20,000 stop loss)`. With a base-case recovery of 6.4 percent on \$250,000 = `0.064 × \$250,000 = \$16,000`, the expectancy is `0.70 × \$16,000 − 0.30 × \$20,000 = \$11,200 − \$6,000 = \$5,200` — positive, and capped at a \$20,000 loss if the systemic tail hits.

The takeaway: the oil-supply break is both your invalidation signal and your stop, so the same line in the sand that tells you the thesis is wrong also caps the loss at a size your capital can absorb. For building portfolio-level hedges against this tail, see [position sizing for tail and political risk](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/position-sizing-for-tail-and-political-risk) and the [cross-asset series](/blog/trading/cross-asset) on regime-dependent correlations.
