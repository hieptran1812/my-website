> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnam is the single biggest winner of the US-China decoupling: factories and foreign capital relocate there to dodge tariffs and de-risk supply chains, but the very same position carries a legal tail — US scrutiny of transshipment, tightening origin rules, and a currency-manipulator-label history. Vietnam's geopolitical luck is real but legally conditional.
>
> - The boom is a *relocation* trade, not a growth-out-of-nowhere trade: capital and capacity move from China to Vietnam because the US tariff on Chinese goods sits near 19% while the tariff on Vietnamese goods sits near 3%. That gap is the whole engine.
> - There is a wide gap between *registered* FDI (commitments, ~38 USD billion in 2024) and *disbursed* FDI (realized, ~25 USD billion). The realized number is what builds factories and rents industrial parks; the headline number is a pipeline, not a fact.
> - The tail is structural: a large bilateral trade surplus with the US lights up the same Treasury currency-report criteria that put Vietnam on the manipulator list in December 2020 (removed in 2021), and any goods merely *routed* through Vietnam to dodge a China tariff risk an anti-circumvention duty at the full China rate.
> - The one number to remember: the US tariff gap of roughly **19% on China vs 3% on Vietnam** is both the reason the factories come and the reason Washington watches what crosses Vietnam's border.

On a single week in **December 2020**, the US Treasury Department published a report that, on paper, was a routine semi-annual exercise: its review of the foreign-exchange policies of major trading partners. Buried in it was a designation that briefly rattled every investor positioned for Vietnam's manufacturing boom. The Treasury labeled Vietnam a **currency manipulator** — the first time a small frontier-market economy had been put in the same category that, in 2019, had been applied to China. The label carried no immediate tariff, but it carried a threat: it opened a legal pathway to one. Within months, after negotiations, the designation was quietly removed. But the episode taught a lasting lesson to anyone trading the Vietnam story: the same export success that draws the factories also draws the scrutiny.

That tension is the whole subject of this post. Vietnam is the textbook beneficiary of a once-in-a-generation rewiring of global supply chains. As Washington and Beijing decouple — through tariffs, export controls, and a broad push to "de-risk" — companies that once concentrated everything in China are spreading production across other countries, and Vietnam has captured more of that shifted capacity than anywhere else. Foreign direct investment pours in, industrial parks fill up, exports to the US climb, and the country's stock market and currency carry a structural tailwind. That part is real and durable.

But the position is *legally conditional*. Vietnam's good fortune is not a gift; it is the by-product of a US strategy aimed at China, and that strategy has guardrails. Goods cannot simply be relabeled "Made in Vietnam" to escape a China tariff — US customs law has a test for that, and the penalty for failing it is severe. A trade surplus that grows too fast trips wires in US trade and currency law. We will stay rigorously neutral throughout: this is not a post about whether any of these policies are wise. It is a practitioner's map of *how the rules move the money* — where the upside is, how to size it, and exactly what would turn the tailwind into a headwind.

The upside and the tail are not separate stories. They are two branches of the same root. Hold that fork in your head; every section below traces one of its arrows.

## Foundations: how a supply-chain shift becomes a national windfall

Before any chart, we need to build five ideas from zero: what friend-shoring and "China+1" actually mean, why a tariff gap is the engine of relocation, the difference between registered and disbursed FDI, what an "export-led model" implies for a trade surplus, and how that surplus connects to US law. If you already follow Vietnam, skim. If this is new, this section is load-bearing — everything later assumes it.

### Friend-shoring, China+1, and de-risking

Start with the vocabulary, because the financial press uses three overlapping terms loosely and they matter.

**Offshoring** is the original move: a company in a high-cost country (say the US) shifts production to a low-cost one (historically China) to cut costs. For thirty years that was the dominant logic, and it concentrated an enormous share of the world's manufacturing in one country.

**Reshoring** is the reversal: bringing production *back* to the home country. It sounds clean but is expensive and slow, because the home country lost the skilled workforce, the supplier networks, and the cheap labor that made offshoring attractive in the first place.

**Friend-shoring** (sometimes "ally-shoring") is the middle path that actually dominates in practice: instead of bringing production home, move it to a country that is politically *aligned* or at least *non-adversarial*. The US does not need its phones built in Ohio; it needs them built somewhere that will not be cut off in a geopolitical conflict. Vietnam — a fast-growing economy that signed a **Comprehensive Strategic Partnership** with the US in September 2023 (the highest tier in Vietnam's diplomatic framework, a tier it shares with China and Russia) — fits that bill well.

**China+1** is the corporate-strategy version of the same idea, and it is the most important term for an investor. It means: do not abandon China (the costs and the scale are still unmatched for many products), but add *one more* country so you are not dependent on a single location. The "plus one" has most often been Vietnam. Critically, China+1 is *additive* — it does not require closing Chinese plants, only building new capacity elsewhere — which is why the shift shows up first as *new factories in Vietnam* rather than empty factories in China.

Put plainly, the engine here is corporate risk management meeting US policy. A company de-risks by spreading its eggs across baskets; a government de-risks by steering critical production toward friends. Vietnam sits exactly where those two incentives point. We cover the broader strategy in the companion post on [supply-chain geopolitics, reshoring, and friend-shoring](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/supply-chain-geopolitics-reshoring-friendshoring-and-critical-minerals); here we focus on what it means for the country that won the lottery.

### The tariff gap is the engine

The reason capacity moved to *Vietnam specifically*, and the reason it moved *when it did*, is a number. The 2018-19 US-China trade war — which we dissect in [the dedicated case study](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war) — pushed the average US tariff on Chinese imports from about 3% before the war to about 19% by 2020, where it has stayed for years. Meanwhile the average US tariff on the rest of the world, Vietnam included, stayed near 3%.

![US average tariff on Chinese imports stepped from 3 percent to 19 percent the push factor for relocation](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze-3.png)

The chart shows the push factor as a staircase: each step is a tariff list, and the dashed line at the bottom is the roughly 3% tariff the US applies to everyone else. The gap between the red staircase and that dashed line is the relocation incentive. A company importing a finished good from China pays the US government roughly 19% of its value at the border; the identical good imported from Vietnam pays roughly 3%. For a high-volume, thin-margin product, a 16-percentage-point tariff difference is not a rounding error — it is the difference between a profitable product line and a dead one. That single gap is why the maps redrew.

#### Worked example: the per-unit math that moves a factory

Take a contract manufacturer importing a consumer-electronics accessory into the US, declared customs value \$40 per unit, at a run-rate of 5 million units a year (so \$200 million of imports). Sourced from China, the unit pays the roughly 25% List-3 tariff that applies to its product code, or \$10 per unit — \$50 million a year in tariff. Sourced from Vietnam, the same unit pays the roughly 3% most-favored-nation rate, or \$1.20 per unit — \$6 million a year. The annual tariff saving from relocating is \$50M − \$6M = **\$44 million**, against a one-time cost of standing up a Vietnamese line that might run \$30-60 million. The relocation pays for itself inside the first one to two years, and every year after is pure margin. That payback math, repeated across thousands of firms, *is* the friend-shoring boom — the factory moves because the arithmetic moves first.

### Registered vs disbursed FDI: the pipeline and the fact

Now the single most misread number in the Vietnam story. **Foreign direct investment (FDI)** is money that foreigners put into building or buying productive assets — factories, equipment, stakes in local firms — as opposed to "portfolio" money that just buys tradable stocks and bonds. FDI is the *real economy* money; it builds the things.

Vietnam's statistics agency reports FDI in two flavors, and confusing them is the most common analytical error:

- **Registered FDI** is *committed* capital: the total value of projects that have been licensed and pledged. It is a forward-looking pipeline number. It is the headline you see in the news.
- **Disbursed FDI** is *realized* capital: money that has actually been spent on the ground — concrete poured, machines installed. It is the number that actually builds a factory and rents an industrial park.

The two differ by a lot, and the gap matters for trading.

![Vietnam FDI registered commitments run well above disbursed realized capital each year](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze-2.png)

The grouped bars (blue = registered commitments, green = realized disbursement) show the gap every year: registered FDI ran around 38 USD billion in 2024 while disbursed FDI ran around 25 USD billion. The registered number is bigger, lumpier (one mega-project can spike it), and more prone to cancellation or delay. The disbursed number is steadier and is what shows up as actual industrial-park occupancy, electricity demand, and port throughput. When you read "Vietnam attracted a record \$38 billion of FDI," the tradeable fact is the *disbursement trend* underneath it — and disbursement has climbed steadily, from about 20 USD billion in 2019 to about 25 USD billion in 2024. That steady realized climb, not the spiky headline, is the durable signal.

#### Worked example: turning disbursed FDI into industrial-park demand

Suppose disbursed FDI in a year is \$25 billion, and assume — as a rough industry rule of thumb — that manufacturing FDI spends about 20% of its project budget on land and built factory space, with the rest on machinery, working capital, and labor. That implies roughly \$5 billion of demand for industrial real estate in a single year. If serviced industrial land leases at an average of, say, \$150 per square meter per year (a representative northern-Vietnam rate), \$5 billion of annual property spend supports demand for a very large block of new leased space — enough to keep park developers' occupancy high and rents rising. The point is mechanical: *disbursed* FDI converts almost directly into industrial-property revenue, while *registered* FDI is only a promise to do so. An investor who anchors a park-landlord thesis on registered FDI is pricing a pipeline; one who anchors it on disbursement is pricing a fact.

### The export-led model and the trade surplus

Vietnam runs an **export-led growth model**: it imports components and raw materials, adds labor and assembly, and exports finished goods — especially to the US, its largest export market. When a country exports far more to a partner than it imports from that partner, it runs a **bilateral trade surplus** with that partner. Vietnam's surplus with the US has grown into one of the largest the US has with any country — a direct consequence of all those relocated factories now shipping their output to American shelves.

Here is the uncomfortable structural fact: *the friend-shoring boom mechanically manufactures the trade surplus that creates the political tail.* The factories move to Vietnam precisely so their output can enter the US at the 3% rate instead of the 19% rate. That output is, by construction, US-bound exports. So the more successful the relocation, the bigger Vietnam's surplus with the US — and the bigger the surplus, the more it shows up on the radar of US trade and currency law. The upside and the tail are the same coin.

### How the surplus connects to US law

Two legal mechanisms turn a trade surplus into a market risk for Vietnam, and an investor must know both by name.

First, **the currency-manipulator framework.** Under US law (the Trade Facilitation and Trade Enforcement Act of 2015 and earlier statutes), the Treasury must report twice a year on whether major trading partners are manipulating their exchange rates to gain an unfair trade advantage. Treasury applies three quantitative criteria: a large bilateral trade surplus with the US, a material global current-account surplus, and persistent one-sided intervention in the FX market (buying foreign currency to hold the home currency down). Meet all three and a country can be *designated* a manipulator — which triggers negotiations and, potentially, a path to tariffs or other penalties. Vietnam met the thresholds and was designated in December 2020, then removed in 2021 after reaching an understanding with the Treasury. The criteria did not go away; Vietnam simply moved back inside the lines.

Second, **Section 301 and anti-circumvention duties.** Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 is the same statute that authorized the China tariffs. It can be pointed at *any* country whose trade practices the US deems unfair — including, in principle, a country used to *circumvent* the China tariffs. Separately, US customs and the Commerce Department can impose **anti-circumvention duties** when they find that goods are merely being routed through a third country to disguise their Chinese origin. We turn to that mechanism in detail below, because it is the sharpest edge of the tail.

The throughline: Vietnam's success is conditional on staying inside a set of legal lines drawn by US policy. The next sections trace each line.

### The diplomatic frame: the comprehensive strategic partnership

There is one more piece of architecture that an investor should understand, because it changes the *base rate* of how the tail resolves. In September 2023 the US and Vietnam elevated their relationship to a **Comprehensive Strategic Partnership** — the top rung of Vietnam's three-tier diplomatic ladder. Vietnam reserves that tier for its most important relationships, and before 2023 it shared the tier with only a handful of countries including China, Russia, and India. Putting the US on the same rung was a deliberate signal in both capitals.

Why does a diplomatic label matter to a trade? Because it shifts the *politics* of every item in the tail. When two countries are formal strategic partners, the US has an incentive to *resolve* trade and currency frictions through negotiation rather than to escalate them into punitive tariffs — the relationship is itself an asset that both sides want to protect. The 2020 manipulator designation being removed in 2021, before the partnership was even formalized, already showed this pattern; the partnership cements it. It does not make the tail disappear — the legal criteria are still mechanical and can still trip — but it raises the probability that a tripped wire ends in a negotiated commitment (as in 2021) rather than a tariff. For an investor sizing the tail, the partnership is a reason to weight the "resolves through negotiation" branch more heavily than the "escalates to tariff" branch.

The counter-risk is that the partnership cuts both ways. Vietnam's careful "bamboo diplomacy" — bending with the wind, staying friendly with both Washington and Beijing rather than choosing a side — is part of what makes it an attractive friend-shoring destination. But it also means Vietnam cannot be seen as simply a back door for Chinese goods, because that would undermine the very relationship that protects it. That is precisely why Vietnam itself has an incentive to *enforce* origin rules and crack down on transshipment: its long-run access to the US market depends on being a genuine manufacturer, not a relabeling depot. The investable implication is subtle but important — the country's own self-interest is aligned with the green branch of the origin test, which makes genuine-value-add manufacturing the structurally favored outcome.

### Why Vietnam, and not somewhere else

It is worth pausing on why this capacity landed in Vietnam specifically rather than in any of the dozen other low-cost economies, because the answer tells you how durable the position is. Four factors compounded. First, *geography*: Vietnam shares a land border with China, so components can move from Chinese suppliers into Vietnamese final-assembly plants cheaply and quickly — the China+1 model works best when the "plus one" is next door to the "China." Second, *an existing manufacturing base*: Vietnam had spent two decades building textile, footwear, and basic electronics capacity, so the relocation wave landed on prepared ground rather than empty fields. Third, *demographics and cost*: a large, young, literate workforce at wages well below China's gave exporters the labor-cost gap that the tariff gap amplified. Fourth, *policy stability and openness*: a government that actively courted FDI with tax incentives, industrial zones, and a long string of trade agreements (Vietnam is party to more than a dozen free-trade agreements, including the CPTPP and the EU-Vietnam FTA).

The reason this matters for the *durability* of the trade is that the first three factors are sticky — geography does not change, a manufacturing base compounds, and a workforce takes a generation to build — while the fourth (policy openness) is the one the country controls and has every incentive to maintain. That stickiness is why the relocation, once started, tends to *deepen* rather than reverse: each new factory makes the supplier ecosystem a little richer, which lowers the cost for the next factory, which is the classic agglomeration flywheel. The risk is not that the factories suddenly leave; it is that the *next marginal* factory, at the margin, starts to find India or Indonesia cheaper as Vietnamese wages rise — a slow ceiling, not a cliff.

## The FDI relocation wave: electronics, textiles, furniture

The relocation did not hit every industry equally. It concentrated where three conditions all held: the product was a high-volume export to the US (so the tariff gap was large in absolute dollars), the manufacturing was labor-intensive and relatively low-tech (so it could be moved without rebuilding a deep supplier ecosystem), and the lead time to stand up capacity was short. Three sectors fit best.

![China plus one relocation moved final assembly from a China only footprint to a China plus Vietnam footprint](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze-4.png)

The before-and-after figure captures the move. On the left (red and amber, the costly "before") sits the old China-only footprint: low-cost assembly at full scale, but fully exposed to the roughly 19% US tariff and to single-country risk. On the right (green, the de-risked "after") sits the China+Vietnam footprint: final assembly shifts to Vietnam, which dodges the tariff gap (facing roughly 3% not 19%) and adds a second site that satisfies the de-risking and friend-shoring logic. Note what does *not* change: this is China+1, not China-exit. The Chinese plants often keep running for the domestic-China and non-US markets; what moves is the *US-bound* output.

**Electronics** led. Smartphones, laptops, earbuds, and components — assembly that is labor-intensive at the final stage and ships in huge US volumes — moved fastest. Vietnam went from a minor electronics exporter to one of the world's largest in barely a decade, and electronics is now its single biggest export category. The key nuance: much of this is *final assembly*, with high-value components still made in China, Korea, or Taiwan and imported into Vietnam. That nuance is exactly what the origin rules in the next section probe.

**Textiles and footwear** were a natural fit because the industry is mobile by design — apparel and shoe production has chased low labor costs around the globe for a century, and Vietnam already had a base. When US tariffs and brand de-risking pushed orders out of China, Vietnam absorbed a large share. This sector is also the most exposed to a *different* tail — anti-dumping and anti-circumvention duties on specific products — because the goods are simple enough that "routing" is technically easy and therefore legally suspect.

**Furniture and wood products** rounded out the wave. Wooden furniture carried steep US tariffs when sourced from China, and the assembly is again labor-intensive and low-tech. Vietnam became a dominant supplier of US furniture imports. This sector has already produced concrete anti-circumvention cases (on items like wooden cabinets and plywood), making it a live example rather than a hypothetical.

A useful way to rank these sectors for an investor is by what we can call *relocation depth* — how much of the value chain actually moved, not just the final step. Furniture sits at the shallow end (the assembly moved, but it is simple work and the wood inputs are commodity), which is why it draws the most circumvention scrutiny. Textiles sit in the middle (cut-and-sew is genuine work, and Vietnam built real fabric and trim supply over time). Electronics span the full range: a phone whose only Vietnamese content is the final click-together is shallow and fragile, while a producer that has localized circuit-board assembly, casing, and testing is deep and durable. The deeper the relocation, the more of the value is genuinely created in Vietnam, the safer the origin position, and the stickier the FDI — all three move together. When you screen Vietnamese exporters, relocation depth is the single variable that best predicts which ones survive both the origin test *and* the next wage cycle, because deep local value-add is hard to undo and hard to challenge.

#### Worked example: a manufacturer's dollars saved by relocating

Take a mid-size furniture maker importing \$120 million of wooden cabinets a year into the US. From China, the product faced a combined duty — the base tariff plus trade-war Section 301 tariffs — of roughly 25%, so \$30 million a year to US customs. The firm relocates final assembly to Vietnam, where the same finished cabinet enters at roughly the 3% rate, or \$3.6 million a year. The gross tariff saving is \$30M − \$3.6M = **\$26.4 million a year**. Even after paying for the new Vietnamese plant, higher logistics on imported inputs, and a learning-curve dip in productivity — call it \$8 million of extra annual cost in the first years — the firm is still roughly \$18 million a year better off. That spread is why the relocation happened despite real friction; the tariff gap simply swamped the switching cost.

## Transshipment risk: when "Made in Vietnam" is not really made in Vietnam

Here is the sharpest edge of the tail, and the one investors most often underestimate. If the only reason to move to Vietnam is to dodge a China tariff, the cheapest version of that move is *not* to build a real factory — it is to ship a nearly finished Chinese good to Vietnam, do trivial work on it (repackage, relabel, attach a part), and re-export it as "Made in Vietnam." That is **transshipment** for tariff evasion, and US law has a specific test designed to catch it.

### The substantial-transformation test

The legal line is the **substantial-transformation** rule. Under US customs law, the "country of origin" of a good — the country whose tariff rate applies — is the last country where the good underwent a *substantial transformation*: a process that changes its name, character, or use into a new and different article of commerce. Cutting fabric, assembling complex sub-components, and manufacturing that adds real value generally qualifies. Slapping a label on, repackaging, or doing minor finishing generally does *not*. The penalty for failing the test is not a slap on the wrist — it is the imposition of the *origin country's* duty (the full China rate) plus potential fraud penalties and back-duties.

![Transshipment routed through Vietnam passes the origin test only with a real factory otherwise it triggers a full China rate duty](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze-5.png)

The decision flow makes the fork explicit. China-made goods routed through Vietnam hit the US origin test: did a *substantial transformation* happen? If a real factory added genuine value, the good counts as Vietnamese origin and passes at the roughly 3% US tariff (green). If it was merely relabeled, that is circumvention, and the anti-circumvention duty applies at the full China rate (red). The entire investability of a Vietnam manufacturing thesis lives on the green branch — on whether the value-add is *real*. A factory that genuinely transforms inputs is a durable asset; a "screwdriver plant" that just assembles imported kits is a duty case waiting to be opened.

### Anti-circumvention cases in practice

This is not theoretical. The US has run multiple high-profile anti-circumvention investigations into goods routed through Vietnam:

- **Steel.** Corrosion-resistant and cold-rolled steel made in China, then sent to Vietnam for minor processing and re-exported, was found to be circumventing US anti-dumping and countervailing duties — and was hit with duties at the China rate.
- **Solar panels.** A 2023 Commerce Department determination found that some solar cells and modules assembled in Southeast Asian countries, Vietnam among them, used Chinese components in a way that circumvented duties on Chinese solar products — triggering duties on the affected producers.
- **Plywood and wooden cabinets.** Commerce has pursued circumvention findings on wood products routed through Vietnam, directly affecting that furniture-relocation wave.

The pattern is consistent: the simpler the product and the thinner the local value-add, the higher the circumvention risk. Complex electronics with genuine multi-stage assembly are harder to challenge; commodity steel that was merely passed through is the easiest target.

A second mechanism layers on top of substantial transformation, and an investor should know it by name because it is where many cases actually get decided: **regional value content** and component-origin rules. Even when a good is assembled in Vietnam, US authorities (and trade agreements generally) can ask what *fraction* of the good's value was added there versus imported from China. If a "Vietnamese" solar module is 90% Chinese cells with only the final lamination done locally, the value added in Vietnam is small, and that is exactly the fact pattern the 2023 solar determination turned on. The deeper the local content — the more of the bill of materials that is genuinely sourced or transformed in Vietnam — the safer the position. This is why the *maturity* of a sector matters: early-stage assembly (import a kit, screw it together) is fragile, while a sector that has built local component suppliers over years (so more of the value chain sits in-country) is durable. An investor evaluating an exporter should ask not just "is it assembled in Vietnam?" but "how much of the value is *created* in Vietnam?" — that single question separates the green branch from the red one better than any label on the box.

There is also an enforcement-direction nuance that affects timing. Anti-circumvention cases can be initiated by the Commerce Department on its own, but they are far more often triggered by a *petition* from a domestic US industry that is losing share to the imports — US steelmakers, US solar manufacturers, US furniture makers. That means the risk is not random: it concentrates in sectors where a politically organized US industry competes directly with the Vietnamese imports. A product with no domestic US producer to complain (because the US simply does not make it anymore) faces almost no circumvention risk; a product that competes head-to-head with a vocal US industry faces the most. Mapping your exposure to the *presence of a domestic US complainant* is one of the sharpest ways to handicap which Vietnamese exporters carry the tail.

#### Worked example: the revenue at risk from a circumvention duty

Take a producer exporting \$80 million a year of a steel product from Vietnam to the US, currently entering at the roughly 3% rate. Commerce opens a circumvention case and rules that the product is really of Chinese origin, applying the China anti-dumping duty — assume a combined rate of 200% (anti-dumping duties on Chinese steel have run into triple digits). The duty bill jumps from \$80M × 3% = \$2.4 million to \$80M × 200% = **\$160 million** — far more than the product's sale value, which simply ends the business overnight. Worse, the ruling can apply *retroactively* to goods already shipped, creating a back-duty liability. For an investor, the lesson is not "avoid Vietnam exporters" but "underwrite the value-add": a position in a genuine manufacturer is safe on the green branch, while a position in a thinly-disguised passthrough is sitting on the red one.

## The surplus, the label, and the tariff threat: the policy chain

Now the macro-legal tail — the chain that runs from export success to a tariff threat through US currency and trade law. This is the slower-moving but more systemic risk, because it scales with Vietnam's *aggregate* success rather than with any single product.

![A large US trade surplus plus a current account surplus and FX buying can trigger a US manipulator label and tariff threat](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze-6.png)

The figure traces the chain as a fan-in. An export boom to the US (green) produces three things at once: a large bilateral trade surplus, a wide global current-account surplus, and — if the central bank buys dollars to keep the currency from appreciating too fast — persistent FX buying (all amber, the friction). Those three feed into the US Treasury's semi-annual FX report review (lavender, the policy-maker), which checks them against its three criteria. Meet all three and the manipulator-label risk turns on (red), which opens the path to tariff and negotiation pressure (red). The chain is mechanical: each box is a quantitative threshold, not a judgment call, which is what makes it *trackable*.

### Why the currency is in the chain

A country running a big export surplus naturally sees its currency want to *appreciate* — foreign buyers need its currency to pay for its goods, bidding it up. But a stronger currency makes its exports more expensive and erodes the very advantage that drew the factories. So an export-led economy faces a temptation: lean against the appreciation by having the central bank buy foreign currency (mostly dollars) and accumulate reserves, holding the home currency artificially soft. That is exactly the behavior the third Treasury criterion targets. We unpack the general mechanics of managed currencies and the beggar-thy-neighbor logic in [currency wars and competitive devaluation](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation); the Vietnam-specific point is that the State Bank of Vietnam's reserve-management choices are *legally legible* to the US Treasury, and an investor should watch them as a leading indicator of label risk.

The USD/VND rate is managed within a band — the State Bank of Vietnam sets a daily central reference rate and allows trading within a tolerance around it, rather than letting the currency float freely. We cover the Vietnamese policy toolkit in [Vietnam's monetary policy: the State Bank, the dong, and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling). For this post, the key fact is that a *managed* currency plus a *large surplus* is precisely the combination the manipulator framework is built to flag — which is why the 2020 designation was not a surprise to anyone tracking the criteria.

The deeper bind is that Vietnam's central bank is genuinely caught between two failure modes, and a trader should understand both because they pull in opposite directions. If the State Bank lets the dong appreciate freely, exporters lose price competitiveness and the relocation flywheel slows — the country gives back part of the very advantage that drew the factories. If instead it buys dollars aggressively to hold the dong soft and pile up reserves, it ticks the third Treasury criterion (persistent one-sided intervention) and risks the manipulator label. The State Bank's real-world choice has been to thread the needle: allow gradual depreciation in line with fundamentals, intervene mainly to smooth volatility rather than to set a target, and communicate transparency about its operations — the exact commitments that got the 2020 label removed. For an investor, the *style* of intervention is the signal, not the spot rate: smoothing volatility is benign, but a sustained, one-directional accumulation of reserves while the surplus balloons is the configuration that lights up the criteria.

There is a second-order point that connects the currency to the rest of the macro backdrop. When the US Federal Reserve raises rates aggressively — as it did across 2022-24, taking the funds rate from near zero to above 5% — capital is pulled toward dollar assets, and emerging-market currencies like the dong come under depreciation pressure *regardless* of the central bank's wishes. In that environment the State Bank's problem inverts: rather than fighting *appreciation*, it must spend reserves to defend against *depreciation*, which paradoxically takes pressure off the manipulator criteria (you cannot be accused of holding your currency down while you are spending reserves to hold it up). This is why the manipulator-label tail is *state-dependent*: it is most acute when the dong wants to strengthen (big surplus, easy global money) and least acute when the dong is under pressure (Fed tightening, risk-off). A practitioner reads the tail through the lens of the global rate cycle, not in isolation — the same surplus is far more dangerous in an easing world than in a tightening one. We cover the mechanics of how the Fed's rate path ripples into emerging markets in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).

### What the label actually does (and does not do)

A crucial nuance for sizing the risk: the manipulator label is **not itself a tariff.** It is a designation that triggers *enhanced engagement* — formal negotiations between the US and the designated country to address the causes of the imbalance. It can lead to tariffs or to a country being denied certain US government financing, but the immediate, mechanical effect is diplomatic pressure, not a border tax. Vietnam's 2020 designation was removed in 2021 after the State Bank committed to greater exchange-rate flexibility and transparency, without any tariff being imposed in between. So the label is best read as a *yellow card*, not a red one: it raises the probability of a future tariff event and it forces a policy adjustment, but it is a warning shot with a negotiation window, not an instant penalty.

There is a related, lighter-touch status worth knowing because it appears in the headlines more often than the full designation: the **monitoring list**. Treasury places a country on the monitoring list when it meets *some* but not all of the criteria — for example, a large surplus and a current-account surplus but no clear evidence of one-sided intervention. The monitoring list carries no consequence beyond closer attention, and a country typically sits on it for at least two consecutive reports before either being escalated to a designation or dropped. For a trader, the monitoring list is the *early-warning* layer of the tail: a country moving onto it is the first sign the criteria are tightening, well before any designation, and it is the cheapest moment to put on a hedge because the market usually shrugs at a monitoring-list mention. By the time a full designation lands — as in December 2020 — the risk is already partly priced; the edge is in reading the *trajectory* through the monitoring list, not in reacting to the designation itself. Treat each semi-annual report as a scheduled checkpoint and watch which criteria Vietnam is closest to tripping.

This is also where the politics and the arithmetic interact in a way that is easy to miss. The criteria are quantitative, but the *decision* to designate — and the speed of removal — is made by an administration with broader trade and foreign-policy goals. A country that is strategically valuable (a friend-shoring partner, a counterweight in the region) gets more benefit of the doubt and a faster off-ramp than an adversary tripping the identical numbers. That is the practical meaning of the comprehensive strategic partnership for the tail: it does not change the arithmetic, but it changes the *posture* with which the arithmetic is read. An investor sizing the tail should therefore track two things in parallel — the hard criteria (surplus, current account, intervention) and the soft state of the bilateral relationship — because the second is what determines whether a tripped criterion becomes a negotiation or a tariff.

#### Worked example: pricing the expected cost of label-then-tariff risk

Suppose you are sizing the tail on a basket of Vietnam US-export names with, say, \$10 billion of combined US-bound revenue. You judge — from the surplus trend and FX-reserve behavior — a 25% chance over the next two years that the surplus growth triggers a fresh manipulator designation, and *conditional* on a designation, a 30% chance it escalates to an actual punitive tariff (most designations resolve through negotiation, as 2020 did). If a tariff did land, assume it adds 10 percentage points of cost on the affected revenue, with the firms able to pass roughly half through, leaving a net 5-point margin hit on \$10 billion, or \$500 million of annual earnings damage. The expected annual cost is 0.25 × 0.30 × \$500M = **\$37.5 million**. That is small relative to the upside the basket already enjoys — which is the honest conclusion: the tail is real but its probability-weighted cost is modest, so it is something to *hedge and monitor*, not to avoid. The mistake is treating the tail as either zero (complacent) or certain (paralyzed); the discipline is to price it.

## Playing the beneficiary while pricing the tail

We can now assemble the two halves — upside and tail — into a single sector map. The friend-shoring windfall does not accrue evenly; it concentrates in a handful of sectors, and each one carries a *specific* condition that can flip the trade.

![Beneficiary map of Vietnam friend-shoring upside by sector with the specific legal or capacity condition for each](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze-7.png)

The matrix lays out the four clearest beneficiary sectors against the two things that matter for each: the upside (green, why it wins) and the condition (red or amber, what flips it). **Electronics assembly** wins on US-bound phone and laptop volume but needs real value-add or it fails the origin test. **Industrial property** wins because park landlords rent to every new factory, but the condition is the registered-vs-disbursed gap — occupancy lags the headline FDI number. **Textiles and footwear** capture shifted apparel orders on the labor-cost gap, but face anti-dumping or transshipment duties on routed goods. **Ports and logistics** win because every relocated container moves through Vietnamese ports, but the binding condition is *physical*: power, road, and port capacity. That last category is the bridge to the final foundational constraint.

### The infrastructure and labor ceiling

The tail is not only legal. The most underrated near-term risk to the Vietnam thesis is *capacity* — the country's ability to physically absorb the FDI flooding in. Three constraints bind:

- **Power.** Manufacturing is electricity-hungry, and Vietnam's grid has at times struggled to keep up, with industrial-power shortages in the northern manufacturing hub during peak demand. A factory you cannot reliably power is a factory you cannot fully run.
- **Logistics.** Ports, roads, and warehousing must scale with export volume. Bottlenecks raise costs and lengthen lead times, eroding part of the labor-cost advantage that drew the factories.
- **Labor.** The supply of cheap labor is large but not infinite, and as demand rises, wages rise — which, over a long horizon, narrows the gap with China and pushes the *next* marginal factory to look at India, Indonesia, or Mexico instead.

These constraints do not invalidate the thesis; they *cap its slope*. They mean the boom is real but rate-limited, and they explain why disbursed FDI grows steadily rather than exploding — the country can only pour so much concrete and string so much wire per year. For an investor, the infrastructure names (power, ports, logistics) are a way to play the *enabling layer* of the boom rather than the volatile export names directly.

The infrastructure ceiling also reframes how to read the registered-vs-disbursed gap from the foundations. That gap is not pure noise or pure optimism — part of it is the country's physical absorption rate. When registered FDI spikes far above disbursement, it can mean two very different things: either the projects are genuinely in the pipeline and will convert as factories get built (bullish, just delayed), or the absorption capacity (power, permits, skilled labor) is the bottleneck and a chunk of the commitments will quietly lapse (a cap on the slope). Distinguishing the two is where the work is. The tell is whether the *enabling layer* is keeping pace: rising power generation, new port capacity coming online, and industrial-zone build-out signal that the pipeline will convert, while a flat enabling layer against a soaring registered number signals that the gap is a ceiling, not a delay. This is why the infrastructure data series — electricity output, port throughput, zone occupancy — are leading indicators for the *whole* thesis, not just for the infrastructure names themselves.

#### Worked example: an industrial-park landlord's revenue from the FDI wave

Take an industrial-park developer with 500 hectares of leasable land — 5 million square meters — in a northern province near the electronics cluster. As the FDI wave fills the park, occupancy rises from 60% to 85%, so leased area goes from 3.0 million to 4.25 million square meters. At an average lease of \$130 per square meter per year, annual leasing revenue rises from 3.0M × \$130 = \$390 million to 4.25M × \$130 = \$552.5 million — a jump of about **\$162.5 million a year**, before any rent escalation. Land that was bought years ago at a fraction of today's value throws off this cash with very high incremental margins, which is why park landlords are among the cleanest plays on *disbursed* (not registered) FDI. The condition from the matrix bites here too: if the FDI is registered but not disbursed, the tenants never arrive, and the occupancy gain never materializes — so the landlord thesis must be underwritten on realized money, not pledged money.

## Common misconceptions

**"Vietnam's manufacturing boom is organic growth, so it is immune to US-China politics."** It is the opposite: the boom is *caused by* US-China politics. The factories moved because of the tariff gap. That makes the boom highly sensitive to the same politics — a US-China détente that narrowed the tariff gap, or a US decision to extend China-style tariffs to Vietnam, would remove the engine. The 19% vs 3% gap is not a permanent feature of the world; it is a policy choice that can change.

**"Goods just get relabeled 'Made in Vietnam' to dodge tariffs, and it works."** Sometimes attempted, rarely durable. The substantial-transformation test exists precisely to catch this, and the enforcement record is real: the steel, solar, plywood, and cabinet cases imposed duties at the *full China rate*, retroactively. A pure relabeling scheme is not a clever loophole; it is a duty case with a delay. The genuine, investable relocation is the one with real value-add — and that is also the one that survives an origin audit.

**"The currency-manipulator label means an automatic tariff, so it is catastrophic."** No. The label is a designation that triggers *negotiations*, not an instant border tax. Vietnam was designated in December 2020 and removed in 2021 with no tariff imposed in between, after committing to greater exchange-rate flexibility. The label raises the *probability* of a future tariff and forces a policy adjustment; it is a yellow card, and pricing it as a certain catastrophe overstates the tail by an order of magnitude.

**"Record registered FDI means the factories are already here."** Registered FDI is a *pipeline*, not a fact. The gap between registered (~38 USD billion in 2024) and disbursed (~25 USD billion) is large and persistent, and projects can be delayed or cancelled. Industrial-park occupancy, power demand, and port throughput track *disbursement*, which climbs steadily rather than in the lumpy spikes of the headline number. An analyst pricing the boom off registered FDI is pricing a promise.

**"Vietnam can absorb unlimited relocation, so the boom has no ceiling."** Physical constraints bind. Power shortages in the northern manufacturing hub, port and road bottlenecks, and rising wages all cap how fast the country can absorb capital. The boom is real but rate-limited — which is *why* disbursed FDI grows steadily rather than exploding, and why the next marginal factory increasingly looks at India, Indonesia, or Mexico.

## How it shows up in real markets

The cleanest way to see the tension between the structural tailwind and the policy tail is to watch what *trades* on which signal.

![VN Index rose to a 2022 peak then fell to a trough before recovering showing the macro tailwind is not a straight line](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze-8.png)

The VN-Index chart makes the central caution vivid. The friend-shoring tailwind is a real, multi-year structural force — but the local stock index does *not* trade in a straight line off it. The index peaked near 1,528 in January 2022 and fell to a trough near 874 by November 2022, a drawdown of more than 40%, before recovering toward 1,267 by the end of 2024. That collapse had almost nothing to do with the FDI story and almost everything to do with *domestic* policy: a corporate-bond and property crackdown, a credit squeeze, and a major fraud case. The lesson for an investor is sharp: the FDI boom is a tailwind for *the economy and for specific export and property sectors*, but the *index* is dominated by domestic banks, real estate, and policy and flows. You can be completely right on friend-shoring and still lose money in the VN-Index if you ignore the domestic cycle. We cover that domestic cycle in [Vietnam's 2022 bond and property crackdown](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnams-2022-bond-and-property-crackdown) and the broader Vietnam equity landscape in the [Vietnam stocks](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks) section.

The currency tells the second half of the story. Through the 2022-24 tightening cycle the dong weakened against the dollar — the annual reference rate drifted from around 23,200 in 2021 toward 24,600 in 2024 — as the State Bank balanced supporting growth against the risk of importing inflation and the risk of looking like it was deliberately weakening the currency. That balancing act is the manipulator-label tail in live action: too much intervention to hold the dong down, and the FX-report criteria light up; too little, and exporters lose their edge. An investor watching for label risk watches the *reserve accumulation* and the *one-sidedness* of intervention, not just the spot rate.

The third signal is the enforcement docket. Each anti-circumvention determination — a Commerce ruling on steel, a solar-cell finding, a plywood case — is a discrete, dated event that reprices the affected exporters. These are the Vietnam-specific equivalents of the tariff-list events that drove the trade war: legal findings with a calendar, which means they can be positioned around. We treat the general skill of trading a scheduled legal event in [how a rule becomes a price](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-a-rule-becomes-a-price-expectations-drift-and-repricing); for Vietnam the catalysts are Commerce circumvention rulings and the semi-annual Treasury FX report.

There is a fourth, slower-burning signal that is the most positive of all: the **market-classification upgrade**. Vietnam's equity market is currently classified as a *frontier* market by the major index providers rather than an *emerging* market — a technical distinction with enormous flow consequences. Frontier markets attract a small pool of specialist capital; emerging markets sit in the benchmarks (like the MSCI Emerging Markets index) that trillions of dollars of passive and benchmark-aware money track. An upgrade would mechanically force a large slug of foreign passive money to buy Vietnamese equities to match the new benchmark weight. The index providers have kept Vietnam on watch, tying an upgrade to specific market-structure reforms (settlement mechanics, foreign-ownership access, disclosure). That makes the upgrade a *scheduled, criteria-based catalyst* — exactly the kind of rule-driven repricing this whole series is about — layered on top of the friend-shoring economic story. We cover the upgrade path and what unlocks it in the companion post on [Vietnam's regulatory reform and upgrade catalysts](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts). For now, the point is that the foreign-flow tailwind has a *legal* trigger sitting on the horizon, distinct from the FDI story but pointing the same way.

It is worth contrasting two kinds of foreign money to keep the signals straight. FDI is *sticky* — once a factory is built, it does not flee on a bad headline, which is why the friend-shoring boom is structurally durable. Portfolio flows (the money that buys VN-Index stocks) are *fast* — they arrive and leave on sentiment, rate cycles, and classification changes, which is why the index can fall 40% in a year even while factories keep opening. An investor who blurs these two will be perpetually confused; the discipline is to underwrite the *real-economy* thesis on sticky FDI and the *equity-index* thesis on fast portfolio flows, and to recognize that the upgrade catalyst is the one event that could turn the fast money structurally positive.

## The playbook: positioning the beneficiary, pricing the tail

This is the payoff. How do you actually trade Vietnam's place in the squeeze? The whole approach rests on one idea: the upside and the tail are *the same trade seen from two sides*, so the goal is never to choose between "bullish Vietnam" and "scared of the tail" — it is to own the structural upside while pricing and hedging the conditional downside. A practitioner who is purely bullish ignores the December 2020 lesson; one who is purely fearful misses a multi-year, sticky-FDI tailwind. The edge is in holding both views at once and sizing each correctly.

**Position in the beneficiaries — but choose by value-add, not by label.** The durable winners are firms with *genuine* Vietnamese value-add: real factories, real transformation, real local supply chains. They sit on the green branch of the origin test and survive a customs audit. Favor industrial-park landlords and logistics/ports as the cleanest *enabling-layer* plays on disbursed FDI, and favor electronics and apparel exporters with deep, demonstrable local manufacturing over thin assemblers. The screen is simple: if the company's edge is *only* the tariff arbitrage of routing Chinese goods, it is sitting on the red branch — avoid it.

**Anchor the thesis on disbursed FDI, not registered.** Track the realized-disbursement trend (steadily up, toward ~25 USD billion) and use it to underwrite industrial-property occupancy and export-capacity growth. Treat the spiky registered headline as sentiment, not substance. The leading indicators that confirm the boom is *real* are industrial-power demand, port throughput, and park occupancy — all downstream of disbursement.

**Price the US-policy tail as a probability, not a binary.** Build a simple expected-cost frame (as in the worked example above): probability of a fresh manipulator designation × conditional probability of escalation to a tariff × the margin damage if it lands. The honest answer is usually a modest probability-weighted cost — large enough to hedge, too small to avoid the whole trade. Hedge it where it is cheap (currency exposure, or options on the most US-surplus-exposed names) rather than abandoning the structural upside.

**Watch the surplus signal as your tail gauge.** The single best leading indicator of label risk is the trajectory of Vietnam's bilateral surplus with the US and the *one-sidedness* of FX intervention. A surplus growing rapidly *and* a central bank visibly accumulating reserves to hold the dong down is the configuration that lights up all three Treasury criteria. When both move together, raise your estimate of label risk and tighten your hedges. The semi-annual Treasury FX report is a scheduled catalyst — mark its dates.

**Trade the enforcement docket as discrete events.** Commerce circumvention rulings and anti-dumping determinations are dated, attributable findings that reprice specific exporters. Map your holdings to their product codes and known pending cases, exactly as a trader mapped companies to tariff lists in the trade war. A pending solar or steel circumvention case is a known catalyst with a calendar.

**What invalidates the view.** Be explicit about what would prove the friend-shoring thesis wrong, because that is how you avoid riding a dead trade. The thesis breaks if: (1) the US-China tariff gap *narrows* materially — a détente that pulls China tariffs back toward 3% removes the relocation engine; (2) the US extends China-style tariffs *to Vietnam* directly — turning the beneficiary into a target, which a fresh, escalating manipulator designation or a broad Section 301 action against Vietnam could do; (3) disbursed FDI *stalls* despite high registered figures — signaling that infrastructure, power, or labor constraints have become binding; or (4) wages and costs in Vietnam rise enough that the *next marginal factory* routes to India, Indonesia, or Mexico instead, capping the boom. Any one of these flips the slope of the trade. The discipline is to hold the structural upside while watching these four tripwires — Vietnam's luck is real, but it is legally and physically conditional, and a practitioner prices both halves.
