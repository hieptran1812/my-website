> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Trillions of dollars sit in money-market funds and other non-bank cash vehicles that look as safe as a bank deposit but are regulated far more lightly, and the rules governing them — SEC Rule 2a-7, the 2014 and 2023 reforms, gates and fees, and the Fed's reverse-repo facility — decide whether they are a stable cash haven or the next run.
>
> - A money fund promising a steady \$1.00 share price is *structurally* run-prone: the first investor out gets the full dollar, so when trouble hits, everyone races to be first. That is a bank run with a different uniform.
> - "Shadow banking" — credit intermediation outside the regulated, deposit-insured bank: repo, money funds, finance companies, and now private credit — is huge, legal, and where the rules are thinnest. It has no FDIC and no automatic lender of last resort.
> - Read the plumbing as a liquidity signal: money-fund assets and the Fed's overnight reverse-repo (ON RRP) balance tell you where the marginal dollar of cash is parked, and a widening prime-versus-government fund spread flags stress before it hits stocks.
> - The one number to remember: a money fund only has to reprice below \$1.00 when its "shadow" net asset value drops under **\$0.9950** — a half-cent of loss is the whole game.

In September 2008, the safest fund in America broke. The Reserve Primary Fund had been around since 1971 — its founder, Bruce Bent, *invented* the money-market fund — and it sold itself the way every money fund does: a place to park cash that always gives you back exactly one dollar for every dollar you put in, plus a little interest. On the morning of September 16, 2008, one day after Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy, the fund disclosed that it held \$785 million of Lehman commercial paper now worth essentially nothing. Its share price fell to \$0.97. In money-fund language, it had "broken the buck."

What happened next was a bank run in everything but name. Investors who had treated the fund like a checking account tried to pull \$40 billion in two days. The panic jumped to other prime money funds, then to the \$3.5 trillion commercial-paper market that funds American payrolls and inventories. Within a week the US Treasury had guaranteed the entire money-fund industry and the Federal Reserve had stood up an emergency facility to buy the paper nobody else would. A product marketed as boring, cash-equivalent, and safe had nearly taken down short-term funding for the whole economy.

That episode is the reason this corner of finance has a rulebook at all — and the reason the rulebook keeps getting rewritten after every crisis. This post is about the law and policy that governs the cash plumbing: what a money fund actually is, why a stable share price makes it fragile, the specific SEC rule (2a-7) that tries to keep it safe, the reforms layered on after 2008, 2020, and 2023, the Fed facilities that backstop or compete with it, and the much larger world of "shadow banking" that sits just outside the regulated bank. The thesis is simple and a little unsettling: the safest-looking cash in the system is held in vehicles where regulation is thinnest, and the rules — not the assets — decide whether they hold.

## Foundations: what a money fund is and what "shadow banking" means

Start with the everyday picture. You have cash you do not need this week but want to keep safe and earn a little on. A bank will take it as a deposit, pay you a small rate, and lend it out. But there is another option that looks almost identical from your seat: a **money-market fund** (MMF), a type of mutual fund that pools cash from many investors and buys a basket of very short-term, very safe IOUs — Treasury bills, repurchase agreements, bank certificates of deposit, and short-term corporate IOUs called commercial paper. The fund passes the interest on those IOUs back to you, minus a small fee.

The defining trick of a traditional money fund is that it aims to keep its share price fixed at exactly **\$1.00**. You put in \$1,000, you own 1,000 shares; each share is always worth a dollar; the interest shows up as more shares, not a higher price. This is called a **stable net asset value** (stable NAV). "Net asset value" just means the per-share value of what the fund owns: total assets minus liabilities, divided by shares outstanding. For a normal stock fund the NAV floats up and down every day. A money fund freezes it at a dollar so that, to you, it behaves exactly like a checking account with a yield.

It helps to know the actual IOUs a money fund buys, because the differences between them are where the risk lives. **Treasury bills** are short-term debt of the US government, sold at a discount and maturing in a year or less; they are the gold standard of safe, liquid, short paper. **Repurchase agreements** ("repo") are overnight secured loans: the fund hands cash to a dealer and receives securities (usually Treasuries) as collateral, with an agreement that the dealer buys them back tomorrow at a slightly higher price — the difference is the interest. Repo is how money funds lend cash overnight to the dealers who carry the bond market's inventory. **Commercial paper** (CP) is short-term unsecured corporate debt — a company borrowing for 30 or 90 days to fund payroll and inventory, backed only by its own promise to pay. **Certificates of deposit** (CDs) are short-term IOUs from banks. The fund's job is to assemble a basket of these that yields a bit more than cash while staying short and safe enough to give every investor back a dollar on demand. Treasuries and Treasury repo almost never disappoint; commercial paper is the piece that can default — which is exactly what happened to the Reserve Primary Fund's Lehman CP in 2008.

That stability is an accounting convention, not a law of nature. The fund's holdings *do* move in value — a Treasury bill ticks up and down, a piece of commercial paper can default. The fund is allowed to round to \$1.00 as long as the true, marked-to-market value of a share — its **shadow NAV** — stays within a half-cent of par, i.e. at or above **\$0.9950**. Drop below that floor and the fund can no longer honestly call itself a dollar; it must "break the buck" and let the price float to its real value. That single threshold, \$0.9950, is the hinge the whole structure turns on, and we will come back to it with numbers.

### Government versus prime: same dollar, different risk

Not all money funds are the same, and the difference is the most important thing a cash investor can know. There are two big families:

- A **government money fund** holds essentially only US Treasury securities and repurchase agreements collateralized by Treasuries. Its credit risk is the credit risk of the US government — about as low as a financial instrument gets. These funds keep the stable \$1.00 NAV.
- A **prime money fund** is allowed to hold private, non-government paper too: commercial paper issued by corporations, certificates of deposit from banks, and other short-term private IOUs. That paper yields more — but it can default, and in a panic it can become un-sellable. Prime funds reach for yield in exactly the instruments that froze in 2008 and 2020.

The names obscure the risk gap. "Prime" sounds like the safest tier; in practice it is the riskier one, because it lends to private borrowers rather than the Treasury. The Reserve Primary Fund was, as the name says, a prime fund. Government funds, by contrast, are where cash *flees to* when prime funds wobble.

### Why a stable dollar is run-prone

Here is the structural flaw, and it is worth slowing down on because it is the engine of every money-fund crisis. Suppose a prime fund's shadow NAV has quietly slipped to \$0.9920 because some of its commercial paper soured — but it is still *printing* \$1.0000 because it hasn't broken the buck yet. If you redeem today, you get a full dollar for a share that is really worth 99.2 cents. The fund pays you out of its most liquid, highest-quality assets — the cash and Treasuries — because those are easiest to sell. What's left behind for the investors who *didn't* run is a portfolio that is now even more concentrated in the bad paper. Their shadow NAV drops further.

This is the **first-mover advantage**, and it is the same logic as a classic bank run: there is a fixed promise (a dollar a share, or your deposit back at par) backed by assets that are worth less than the promise and cannot all be sold at once. Whoever redeems first is made whole at the expense of whoever redeems last. Once any investor suspects others might run, the rational move is to run *first* — even if you think the fund is probably fine. The promise of a fixed \$1.00 turns a small loss into a stampede.

![The money-fund run loop from a small loss through first-mover redemptions to breaking the buck](/imgs/blogs/money-market-funds-and-shadow-bank-regulation-2.png)

A floating-NAV fund does not have this problem in the same way. If the share price already reflects the loss — say it's printing \$0.9920 — then redeeming today gets you 99.2 cents, the same as waiting. There is no prize for being first, so there is no race. That insight is exactly what the post-2008 reforms tried to bottle, as we'll see.

### What "shadow banking" actually is

"Shadow banking" is a scary-sounding term for something mundane: **credit intermediation that happens outside the regulated, deposit-insured bank.** A bank takes short-term money (deposits) and makes longer-term loans; that maturity transformation is what makes banks both useful and fragile, which is why they get deposit insurance and a lender of last resort in exchange for heavy regulation. Shadow banks do the same maturity and credit transformation — borrow short, lend longer or riskier — but without the bank charter, without FDIC insurance, and historically without guaranteed access to the Fed's emergency lending.

The shadow-banking universe includes money funds, the repurchase ("repo") market where dealers fund their securities overnight, finance companies and business development companies (BDCs) that lend to consumers and mid-sized firms, securitization vehicles, and — the fastest-growing piece today — **private credit**, where investment funds lend directly to companies in deals that never touch a bank or a public market. The Financial Stability Board's broad measure of non-bank financial intermediation runs into the hundreds of trillions of dollars globally; the narrower "shadow banking" subset that does bank-like maturity transformation is itself tens of trillions. It is not a fringe. In many credit markets it is now larger than the banks.

The reason regulators lose sleep over it is precisely the safety net it lacks. A bank deposit up to \$250,000 is insured by the FDIC; a money-fund share is not insured by anyone. A bank in a liquidity crunch can borrow from the Fed's discount window; a private-credit fund cannot. When a shadow bank runs, there is no automatic firefighter — which is why the responses are improvised crisis facilities, built in a weekend, named with acronyms nobody had heard of the week before.

The repo market deserves a closer look, because it is the single largest piece of the shadow-bank plumbing and the most prone to sudden seizures. Every day, dealers finance trillions of dollars of securities by borrowing overnight in repo, and money funds are among the biggest cash lenders on the other side. The market normally runs so smoothly it is invisible — until it isn't. In September 2019, with no crisis in sight, the overnight repo rate suddenly spiked from around 2% to nearly 10% intraday because a confluence of corporate-tax payments and Treasury settlements drained cash from the system faster than the plumbing could refill it. The Fed had to inject tens of billions of dollars of cash, day after day, to push the rate back down — and ultimately stood up a permanent **Standing Repo Facility (SRF)** in 2021 so that eligible institutions could always borrow cash against Treasuries at a set rate, capping any future spike. The 2019 episode is the cleanest reminder that even the safest-collateralized corner of shadow banking can break on a quiet Tuesday, and that the fix, again, was a new Fed facility. For a fuller treatment of how repo works, see the cross-linked deep dive at the end.

## Rule 2a-7: the rulebook that keeps a fund near a dollar

If a stable-NAV fund is structurally fragile, why doesn't it break constantly? Because of a single SEC rule with an unglamorous name: **Rule 2a-7** under the Investment Company Act of 1940. It is the rulebook that lets a fund call itself a money-market fund and round its NAV to \$1.00, and in exchange it imposes tight limits designed to keep the shadow NAV from ever drifting far from par. Think of 2a-7 as four dials, all turned toward "short and liquid."

**Credit quality.** A money fund may only hold high-quality short-term paper. Issuers must be in the top credit tiers; the fund must do its own credit analysis rather than blindly trust ratings; and there are limits on how much it can hold from any single issuer (generally no more than 5% in any one non-government name). The point is that no single default should be able to push the shadow NAV through the floor.

**Maturity.** The fund's holdings must be short. Rule 2a-7 caps the **weighted-average maturity (WAM)** of the portfolio at **60 days** and the **weighted-average life (WAL)** at **120 days**. WAM controls how much the portfolio's value swings when interest rates move — short paper barely moves, long paper moves a lot. WAL is similar but uses each security's final maturity, capturing the credit and liquidity tail. Short maturities mean the fund is constantly getting cash back as paper matures, so it rarely has to *sell* anything to meet redemptions.

**Liquidity buffers.** This is the part the crises kept sharpening. The fund must hold a minimum of **daily liquid assets** — cash and securities maturing within one day — and **weekly liquid assets** — those maturing within one week (plus Treasuries, which are always treated as liquid). After the 2023 reforms these floors are **at least 25% daily** and **at least 50% weekly**. The logic: if a quarter of the fund can be turned into cash today and half within a week, it can meet a very large wave of redemptions without dumping its longer paper into a falling market.

![Matrix of Rule 2a-7 liquidity and maturity limits, what each controls, and the breach risk](/imgs/blogs/money-market-funds-and-shadow-bank-regulation-3.png)

The whole rulebook is one idea expressed four ways: hold paper short enough and liquid enough that you almost never have to sell at a loss to pay a redeemer. When 2a-7 works, the shadow NAV barely moves and the dollar holds. When a shock is big and fast enough to overwhelm the buffers — Lehman in 2008, COVID in March 2020 — the dial settings are not enough, and the structural fragility reasserts itself.

#### Worked example: breaking the buck on a half-cent of loss

Take a prime money fund with exactly \$10.000 billion of assets and 10.000 billion shares, each printing \$1.0000. Suppose 2% of its assets — \$200 million — are commercial paper from a single issuer that suddenly defaults, and the recovery on that paper is 75 cents on the dollar. The loss is \$200 million × (1 − 0.75) = **\$50 million**.

New asset value: \$10.000bn − \$0.050bn = \$9.950 billion. Shadow NAV per share = \$9.950bn ÷ 10.000bn shares = **\$0.9950**. That is *exactly* the rounding floor — the fund is right on the knife's edge and may still round to \$1.00.

Now make the default a little worse: recovery of only 70 cents. Loss = \$200m × 0.30 = \$60 million. New asset value = \$9.940 billion; shadow NAV = **\$0.9940**. That is below \$0.9950. The fund can no longer honestly round to a dollar; it must break the buck and reprice to \$0.9940. The intuition that should stick: it took a loss of just **0.60% of assets** — six-tenths of one cent per share — to break a fund everyone treated as cash, because the whole product is built on a half-cent of headroom.

![Shadow NAV falling from one dollar through the 0.9950 floor after a 1.5 percent loss](/imgs/blogs/money-market-funds-and-shadow-bank-regulation-8.png)

#### Worked example: the first-mover advantage in dollars

Stay with the fund the moment after the second default, when its shadow NAV is \$0.9940 but — for one more morning — it is still paying redemptions at \$1.0000 because it hasn't formally broken the buck. You hold 1,000,000 shares.

If you redeem *now*, you receive 1,000,000 × \$1.0000 = **\$1,000,000** for shares whose true value is 1,000,000 × \$0.9940 = \$994,000. You captured \$6,000 of value that, in fairness, belonged to the investors who stayed.

If you wait until tomorrow, after the fund repriced to its real \$0.9940 (and assume no further losses), you receive 1,000,000 × \$0.9940 = **\$994,000**. The fast redeemer is \$6,000 — 0.6% — better off than the slow one, purely for moving first.

Six-tenths of a percent does not sound like much. But scale it: an institutional treasurer parking \$2 billion captures \$2,000,000,000 × 0.006 = **\$12 million** by redeeming a day early. That is a career-making call for a cash manager and a career-ending one for whoever moved last. The intuition: the prize for running is small per share but enormous per dollar, and for big institutional holders it is more than enough to justify a panic. That is why prime funds, dominated by institutional cash, run first.

## The reforms: three crises, three rewrites

Money-fund regulation is reactive. A run happens, the SEC writes a rule that fixes the last problem, and the next run finds the gap the rule left. There have been three big rounds.

![Timeline of the 2008, 2014, and 2023 money-fund reforms after each crisis](/imgs/blogs/money-market-funds-and-shadow-bank-regulation-6.png)

### 2008 and the 2010/2014 reforms

It is worth dwelling on how fast 2008 moved, because the speed is the whole lesson. The Reserve Primary Fund disclosed its Lehman problem mid-morning on September 16. By that afternoon it had broken the buck. Within 24 hours, institutional investors had submitted redemption requests for roughly two-thirds of the fund's assets. The contagion was nearly instant: other prime funds saw their own institutional holders redeem, those funds stopped rolling over commercial paper to conserve cash, and the CP market — the short-term funding lifeline for blue-chip corporations — began to freeze within days. General Electric, which funded itself heavily in CP, later said the market simply stopped functioning. The US Treasury responded on September 19 by temporarily guaranteeing money-fund shares outright, and the Fed launched a facility to finance purchases of the CP and asset-backed paper funds were dumping. A product sold as a cash substitute had, in 72 hours, threatened the payroll of corporate America. That is the speed at which a stable-NAV run propagates, and it is why every reform since has been about *liquidity you can access in hours*, not credit quality you can assess over weeks.

After the Reserve Primary failure, the SEC moved in two steps. The 2010 amendments tightened 2a-7's basics — shorter maturities, first explicit liquidity minimums, and stress testing. The bigger structural change came in **2014**, aimed squarely at the run-prone stable dollar.

The 2014 reforms split the money-fund world. **Government funds** kept the stable \$1.00 NAV — Treasuries don't break the buck. But **institutional prime and institutional municipal funds** were forced to adopt a **floating NAV**: their share price now moves with the market value of their holdings, priced to four decimal places (\$1.0000, \$0.9998, and so on). The idea, straight from the run logic above, was to kill the first-mover advantage: if the price already reflects today's loss, there is no prize for redeeming first.

The 2014 package also introduced **liquidity fees and redemption gates**. If a fund's weekly liquid assets fell below a threshold (30% at the time), the board *could* impose a fee on redemptions or a temporary **gate** — a pause on withdrawals of up to 10 business days. The intent was to slow a run and protect remaining shareholders. As we'll see, the design backfired, and the 2023 reforms had to undo a large part of it.

### 2020: the dash for cash and the MMLF

The 2014 reforms had not yet faced a true test when COVID hit. In March 2020, as the pandemic shut down the economy, investors and corporations scrambled for cash all at once — a "dash for cash" — and prime money funds came under exactly the run pressure 2014 was meant to prevent. Over roughly two weeks, institutional prime funds lost about 30% of their assets as investors fled to government funds and Treasuries.

Crucially, the 2014 *gates* made it worse, not better. Because a gate could be triggered when weekly liquid assets fell below 30%, investors watched that buffer like a hawk and redeemed *preemptively* — pulling money out *before* the fund got close to the threshold, precisely to avoid being trapped behind a gate. The tool built to stop runs had given investors a fresh reason to run early. We'll quantify that perverse incentive in a moment.

With short-term funding markets seizing, the Fed once again improvised a rescue: the **Money Market Mutual Fund Liquidity Facility (MMLF)**, launched March 18, 2020. The MMLF lent to banks against assets they bought *from* money funds — effectively letting funds sell their commercial paper and CDs to banks, who pledged them to the Fed. It put a floor under the assets money funds were dumping and stopped the run. Like 2008's Treasury guarantee, it was a public backstop for a privately-run, lightly-regulated product — the safety net showing up after the fact.

### 2023: swing pricing and bigger buffers

The 2023 SEC reforms drew the obvious lesson from 2020. They **removed the tie between liquidity thresholds and gates** — the feature that had encouraged preemptive runs. Gates were eliminated for prime funds; a fund could no longer simply lock investors in.

In their place came two changes. First, the liquidity minimums were raised to the **25% daily / 50% weekly** floors described earlier — much thicker buffers, so a fund can absorb a far larger redemption wave before it has to sell illiquid paper. Second, institutional prime funds got **mandatory swing pricing**: on a day of heavy net redemptions, the fund adjusts (swings) its NAV downward by an estimate of the trading costs that redemptions impose, so the redeeming investors bear those costs rather than dumping them on the investors who stay. Swing pricing is the floating-NAV idea pushed further: it explicitly prices in the *cost of your exit* so that leaving in a panic is no longer free.

The elegance of swing pricing is that it directly attacks the first-mover advantage with a price signal rather than a gate. Under a gate, the choice an investor faces is binary and frightening — get out now or get locked in — which is precisely what triggers preemptive runs. Under swing pricing, the choice is continuous: you can always get out, but on a heavy-redemption day you pay the realistic cost of liquidating your slice of the portfolio, which is exactly the cost you would otherwise have shoved onto the people who stayed. If everyone is redeeming, the swing is large and redeeming is expensive, which *cools* the incentive to pile on rather than heating it. The 2023 reforms thus did something subtle: they replaced a tool that turned the liquidity buffer into a starting gun with a tool that makes running progressively more expensive as the run grows. Whether that holds under a true March-2020-scale shock is the open question, and the honest answer is that it has not yet been stress-tested at scale.

#### Worked example: how a gate perversely accelerates a run

Imagine a 2014-era prime fund with weekly liquid assets at **32%**, just above the 30% gate trigger, holding \$50 billion. You are a corporate treasurer with \$1 billion in the fund. The fund is fine today, but the market is jittery.

Your reasoning: if other treasurers redeem and pull the weekly liquid buffer below 30%, the board may slap on a gate and freeze my \$1 billion for up to 10 business days — two weeks during which I cannot make payroll or pay suppliers. The cost of being trapped (say I'd have to borrow \$1 billion at an emergency 8% annualized rate for two weeks) is roughly \$1,000,000,000 × 0.08 × (14/365) ≈ **\$3.07 million**, plus the sheer operational risk of not having my cash.

So you redeem the full \$1 billion *today*, while you still can. But your \$1 billion redemption itself drains the buffer: paying you out of liquid assets cuts weekly liquidity from 32% toward the trigger, making a gate *more* likely for everyone else — who then race to redeem for the same reason. The gate, designed to stop a run, manufactured one. The intuition that the 2023 reform encodes: a threatened gate converts a fund's liquidity buffer into a starting gun.

#### Worked example: swing pricing makes the runner pay their own exit cost

Take the same \$50 billion prime fund, now under the 2023 rules with mandatory swing pricing, and a heavy-redemption day. The board estimates that liquidating assets to meet net redemptions will cost **0.30%** of the redeemed amount in spreads and market impact, so it sets the swing factor at 0.30% and swings the NAV from \$1.0000 down to **\$0.9970** for the day.

You redeem your \$1 billion. Under the old stable-NAV regime you would have received \$1,000,000,000 × \$1.0000 = \$1,000,000,000, and the 0.30% trading cost — \$1,000,000,000 × 0.30% = **\$3,000,000** — would have been borne by the *remaining* shareholders, whose NAV would quietly drop. Under swing pricing, you instead receive \$1,000,000,000 × \$0.9970 = **\$997,000,000**: you eat your own \$3 million of exit cost, and the investors who stay are left whole at \$1.0000. The intuition: swing pricing converts the first-mover *advantage* into a first-mover *cost*, so on a panic day the early redeemer is no longer subsidized by the patient one — which removes the prize that makes a run rational.

## The Fed facilities: backstop and competitor

The rules that govern money funds are only half the story. The other half is the set of Federal Reserve facilities that sit underneath and alongside the cash plumbing — sometimes as a backstop that catches a falling market, sometimes as a competitor that pulls cash *toward* the Fed and away from banks. Three matter here.

**The MMLF (the backstop).** As described above, the Money Market Mutual Fund Liquidity Facility was the 2020 crisis tool that lent to banks against assets they bought from money funds. It is the modern descendant of the 2008 Treasury guarantee: a public balance sheet stepping in to buy what a run is dumping. The MMLF was temporary — it expired in 2021 — which is the crucial point. Money funds do *not* have a standing, automatic backstop the way banks have the discount window. They get rescued *if and when* a crisis is bad enough that the Fed and Treasury choose to act, which they have, twice. That conditional, discretionary safety net is exactly what makes shadow banking different from banking.

**The ON RRP (the floor and the competitor).** The overnight reverse-repo facility, which we will meet again in the markets section, lets eligible money funds lend cash to the Fed overnight against Treasury collateral at an administered rate. Its first job is to put a *floor* under short-term interest rates: no rational money fund lends to a private borrower for less than it can earn risk-free at the Fed, so the ON RRP rate becomes the minimum yield on cash. Its side effect, especially when rates are high, is to make the Fed a giant *competitor* for cash — a place that absorbs trillions of dollars that might otherwise sit as bank deposits. That is a deliberate monetary-policy tool, not an accident, and it reshapes where cash lives.

**The discount window and the SRF (the bank-side backstops).** For contrast, banks have a standing **discount window** where they can borrow from the Fed against collateral at any time, and since 2021 there is a **Standing Repo Facility** that lets eligible institutions borrow cash against Treasuries at a capped rate. These are permanent, rules-based, always-on. A money fund cannot walk up to the discount window; a private-credit fund certainly cannot. The asymmetry is the whole thesis in one picture: the more regulated you are, the more reliable your safety net; the deeper into the shadows you go, the more you depend on a rescue that may or may not come.

The practical takeaway is that the Fed's facilities are a load-bearing part of why government money funds feel safe today. Strip away the implicit understanding that the Fed will backstop a dash for cash, and a stable-NAV prime fund is exactly as fragile as it was in 2008. The rules and the facilities are two halves of the same safety apparatus, and a practitioner watches both — the rulebook for the buffers, the facilities for the backstop.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs about this corner of the market are widespread, comforting, and wrong. Each can cost real money.

**Misconception 1: "Money funds are as safe as bank deposits."** They are not, and the gap is precise. A US bank deposit is insured by the FDIC up to **\$250,000** per depositor per bank; if the bank fails, the government makes you whole up to that cap. A money-fund share carries **no** FDIC insurance — none, at any dollar amount. Its safety comes entirely from the quality of its holdings and the discipline of Rule 2a-7. In normal times a government money fund is arguably *safer* than an uninsured deposit above \$250,000, because it holds Treasuries directly rather than lending to one bank. But a prime fund can and has broken the buck. The Reserve Primary Fund's investors who didn't run early ultimately got back about **\$0.99** on the dollar — a 1% loss on "cash." Small in percentage, shocking for a product sold as a dollar.

**Misconception 2: "Shadow banking is small, illegal, or sinister."** It is none of these. It is enormous, entirely legal, and woven through the financial system you use every day. When you buy on margin, when a corporation rolls its commercial paper, when a fund lends directly to a private company, when a dealer funds Treasuries overnight in repo — that is shadow banking. The non-bank share of US business lending has grown so large that private-credit funds now compete head-to-head with banks for mid-market loans, with assets estimated well over \$1.5 trillion and climbing. "Shadow" refers to its position *outside the bank regulatory perimeter*, not to legality. The risk is not that it is criminal; it is that it is big, run-prone, and lightly watched.

**Misconception 3: "Gates and fees protect investors."** This is the one the regulators themselves got wrong in 2014 and reversed in 2023. The intuition sounds right — slow the run, protect the people who stay. But as the worked example showed and March 2020 proved, a *threatened* gate gives every investor a reason to redeem *before* the gate can slam, which drains liquidity faster and brings the gate (or the run) on sooner. A tool that turns a liquidity buffer into a tripwire amplifies the very panic it was meant to dampen. The 2023 reforms ripped the gate–threshold link out for exactly this reason. The deeper lesson generalizes to all of shadow banking: any "circuit breaker" that investors can see coming becomes a *reason* to act before it trips, so the design that works is the one that makes running continuously costly (swing pricing, floating NAV), not the one that threatens a sudden hard stop.

**Misconception 4: "A money fund holding only the safest paper can't lose money."** Even a fund that never holds a single defaulting bond can disappoint, because the risk that breaks money funds in a panic is usually *liquidity*, not credit. In March 2020 the commercial paper held by prime funds did not default — most of it paid in full. The problem was that, for two weeks, *nobody would buy it at a fair price*, so a fund needing cash to meet redemptions could only sell at a fire-sale discount. A 0.5% loss from selling perfectly good paper into a frozen market is just as capable of breaking the \$0.9950 floor as a 0.5% loss from a default. This is why Rule 2a-7 obsesses over *liquidity* buffers, not just credit quality: the asset can be money-good and still un-sellable in the exact moment you need to sell it. "Safe assets" and "safe in a panic" are not the same property.

## How it shows up in real markets

The plumbing is invisible until it isn't. Three episodes show how money-fund and shadow-bank rules move prices and flows.

### 2020: the dash for cash, in the data

In March 2020 the run logic played out in real time. Institutional prime funds shed roughly 30% of assets in about two weeks; government funds *gained* hundreds of billions as that cash fled to safety; and the commercial-paper market — which prime funds are a huge buyer of — seized up, threatening the short-term funding of real companies. The CP that prime funds were trying to sell was, for the most part, not defaulting; it was simply un-sellable because every potential buyer was hoarding cash at once. That is the liquidity-versus-credit distinction made concrete: good paper, no bid. The Fed's MMLF, announced March 18, stopped the bleed within days by buying the assets funds were dumping, and the Fed paired it with a Commercial Paper Funding Facility to backstop the CP market directly. Within roughly a week of the facilities being announced, prime-fund outflows slowed and CP rates came back down — a vivid demonstration that the run was a *liquidity* panic curable by a credible buyer of last resort, not a solvency problem.

The episode is the cleanest modern proof that a stable-NAV product, even a reformed one, reverts to its run-prone nature under a fast enough shock, and that the ultimate backstop is a public one improvised in a crisis. It also exposed the gap in the 2014 reforms — the gate trigger that encouraged preemptive runs — and so wrote the agenda for 2023. Note the recurring shape: the rule meant to prevent the last crisis (the 2014 gate) became a contributing cause of the next one (the 2020 preemptive run), which then produced the next rule (2023 swing pricing), which will be tested by the crisis after that. The plumbing's regulatory history is a loop, not a line.

### 2022–23: rate hikes pull cash out of banks

The next episode was not a panic but a migration, and it was driven by the rules of the Fed's own facilities. When the Federal Reserve raised interest rates aggressively in 2022–23 — lifting the target range from near zero to over 5% — money funds repriced almost immediately, because they hold paper that matures in days and rolls into the new higher rate. Bank deposits did *not* reprice. Banks, flush with deposits and slow to compete, kept paying savers close to nothing while money funds paid north of 5%. Cash noticed.

![Fed funds target upper bound stepping from 0.50 percent to 5.50 percent in 2022 to 2023](/imgs/blogs/money-market-funds-and-shadow-bank-regulation-5.png)

A critical piece of plumbing here is the Fed's **overnight reverse-repo facility (ON RRP)**. In a reverse repo, an eligible money fund lends cash to the Fed *overnight* and gets Treasury collateral in return, earning the Fed's administered ON RRP rate. The facility was built as a *floor* for short-term rates: no money fund will lend to a private borrower for less than it can earn risk-free at the Fed, so the ON RRP rate sets a hard minimum for cash yields. As rates rose, government money funds parked enormous balances at the ON RRP — peaking above \$2.2 trillion in 2022–23 — because it paid more than most alternatives with zero credit risk. The facility thereby became a giant *deposit competitor*: it gave money funds a place to absorb the cash fleeing low-paying bank deposits.

![Cash choosing between low-yielding bank deposits and the Fed-paid ON RRP and money funds](/imgs/blogs/money-market-funds-and-shadow-bank-regulation-7.png)

This deposit flight was not just an academic flow. It thinned the deposit base of banks that had grown complacent — and at the banks with the most rate-sensitive, uninsured depositors, it set the stage for the fastest runs in history in March 2023, when a regional bank lost tens of billions of deposits in a single day. The rules of where cash *can* earn the policy rate — money funds and the ON RRP versus a sleepy deposit — quietly redrew the funding map of the banking system.

### A prime-versus-government yield gap as a stress gauge

In calm markets, a prime money fund yields a little more than a government fund — call it 10 to 20 basis points (0.10–0.20%) — because it earns the extra spread on private commercial paper and CDs. That gap is the market's price for taking a sliver of credit and liquidity risk. When stress builds, the gap behaves in a telling way: as investors flee prime funds, the funds must hold more cash and the highest-quality paper, and the prime-minus-government yield advantage compresses or even inverts, while *new* prime issuance gets repriced wider. Watching that spread, and watching prime-fund assets fall while government-fund assets rise, is a real-time read on whether cash investors are getting nervous — usually before the nervousness reaches stocks.

#### Worked example: the yield pickup that pulled cash from deposits

Put numbers on the 2022–23 migration. By mid-2023 the Fed's target upper bound was **5.50%**, and a government money fund yielded roughly **5.0%** after fees. A typical big-bank savings deposit still paid about **0.50%**. You are a corporation sitting on \$500 million of operating cash.

Leave it in the bank: \$500,000,000 × 0.50% = **\$2.5 million** of interest per year.

Move it to a government money fund: \$500,000,000 × 5.0% = **\$25 million** per year.

The pickup is \$25m − \$2.5m = **\$22.5 million per year** for a money that is arguably *safer* — Treasuries and Treasury repo versus an uninsured deposit at one bank. No competent treasurer leaves \$22.5 million on the table. Multiply that calculus across thousands of corporations and millions of households and you get the trillion-dollar surge into money funds and the ON RRP that hollowed out lazy bank deposits in 2022–23. The intuition: when the policy rate jumps and deposits don't follow, the rules let cash vote with its feet, and it does so by the trillion.

#### Worked example: a prime-versus-government spread trade

Suppose you run a \$200 million corporate cash book and you can split it between a prime fund yielding **5.30%** and a government fund yielding **5.05%** — a 25-basis-point (0.25%) pickup for holding the prime fund's commercial-paper risk. The annual extra income from putting it all in prime is \$200,000,000 × 0.25% = **\$500,000**.

Now price the risk you're being paid for. If a 2008- or 2020-style event hits and your prime fund breaks the buck at, say, \$0.9900 — a 1% loss — your downside is \$200,000,000 × 1% = **\$2,000,000**, plus the operational nightmare of having your cash impaired or gated for a week. So you are collecting \$500,000 a year to risk a \$2,000,000 loss in a tail event. Whether that is a good trade depends on how likely the tail is *and* on whether you can read the warning signs (the spread compressing, prime assets falling, weekly liquidity dropping) in time to move to the government fund first. The intuition: the prime pickup is real income in calm times and a small premium against a fat, fast tail — a trade you only want on when you are watching the plumbing closely.

## Private credit: the new shadow-bank frontier

The reforms of 2014 and 2023 hardened money funds. But regulation has a way of squeezing risk out of one place and into another, and the place it has flowed is **private credit** — investment funds that lend directly to companies, bypassing both banks and public bond markets. After the post-2008 rules made it costlier for banks to hold risky mid-market loans, that lending migrated to private-credit funds, BDCs, and direct-lending vehicles. The asset class has grown from a niche to well over \$1.5 trillion, and it is now a primary lender to private-equity-owned companies.

Why this is the frontier of shadow-bank risk: private credit is lightly regulated, opaque, and hard to value. The loans are not traded, so their marks are estimates, not prices — which means losses can build quietly and surface in a lump. The funds often use leverage and promise investors periodic liquidity even though the underlying loans are illiquid, recreating the maturity-mismatch that makes any shadow bank run-prone. And because the loans sit outside the banking system, there is no FDIC, no discount window, and no Rule 2a-7 forcing short maturities or liquidity buffers. The lesson of money funds — that the rules, not the assets, determine whether a vehicle holds up — applies with even more force where there are almost no rules. Regulators now watch private-credit growth the way they once watched prime money funds: as a place where the next surprising run could start.

A particular structure to watch is the **semi-liquid** or "evergreen" private-credit fund — increasingly sold to wealthy individuals — that holds illiquid direct loans but offers investors quarterly redemptions, usually capped at a few percent of the fund per quarter (a "redemption gate" by another name). This is the classic maturity mismatch dressed in new clothes, and the cap is the tell. As long as redemptions stay below the cap, everything looks calm. The moment they exceed it, the fund gates, redemptions are pro-rated, and — exactly as with money-fund gates in 2020 — the threat of being pro-rated next quarter gives every remaining investor a reason to file their redemption *first*. The vehicle is structurally identical to a prime money fund circa 2014; it just hasn't had its 2008 yet.

#### Worked example: the private-credit maturity mismatch in numbers

Consider a \$5 billion semi-liquid private-credit fund that holds illiquid corporate loans yielding 11% and offers investors a quarterly redemption capped at **5% of net asset value**, or \$250 million per quarter. In calm times redemptions run \$100 million a quarter — comfortably under the cap — and everyone is happy collecting an 11% yield on what feels like a flexible investment.

Now a recession hits and investors want out. In one quarter, redemption requests arrive for \$600 million — 12% of the fund. The cap allows only \$250 million, so requests are pro-rated: each investor gets about 250/600 = **42%** of what they asked for, and the rest rolls to next quarter's queue. An investor who wanted their full \$10 million back receives only \$4.2 million now and joins the line for the rest. Seeing this, every other investor rationally files a *full* redemption immediately to maximize their pro-rata slice — which balloons next quarter's requests and guarantees the gate stays on. Meanwhile the fund, to raise even the \$250 million, may have to sell loans at a discount or draw on leverage, marking down the NAV for everyone. The intuition: a liquidity promise the underlying assets can't keep is a run waiting for a trigger, and the redemption cap that looks like protection is the same starting gun that money-fund gates were — now sitting in a market with no Rule 2a-7 and no MMLF behind it.

## Why the rules keep chasing the risk

Step back and a pattern emerges that is worth naming, because it is the master key to this whole domain. Every time regulators tighten the rules in one place, the risk does not disappear — it migrates to the least-regulated place that can still earn a return. This is **regulatory arbitrage**, and the history of shadow banking is essentially its history.

Money funds themselves were a regulatory-arbitrage product: they were invented in the 1970s to pay savers a market interest rate at a time when **Regulation Q** capped what banks could pay on deposits. Cash flowed out of capped bank deposits into uncapped money funds — exactly the deposit flight we saw in 2022–23, but driven by a different rule. When the post-2008 reforms made prime money funds less attractive, hundreds of billions migrated to government funds and, ultimately, the ON RRP. When Basel bank-capital rules (covered in the cross-linked post) made it expensive for banks to hold leveraged loans, that lending migrated to private credit. The risk is conserved; only its address changes.

This is why the cash plumbing is never "fixed." A rule that makes money funds safe pushes the marginal risk-taker into a vehicle the rule doesn't cover, and the next crisis emerges there. For an investor, the implication is liberating rather than depressing: you do not need to predict *which* vehicle will break next, only to follow the money to wherever the rules are thinnest and the growth is fastest. In the 1970s it was money funds; in 2008 it was prime funds and the CP market; in 2019 it was repo; in 2020 it was prime funds again; today the spotlight is on private credit and the semi-liquid funds that bring it to retail investors. The rules tell you where the risk *was*; the flows tell you where it *went*.

## How to trade it: reading the plumbing

This is the payoff. The cash plumbing is one of the most underrated sources of macro signal, because it tells you where the marginal dollar of safe money is going *before* that information shows up in risk assets. Here is how a practitioner reads and positions around it.

**Read money-fund flows and the ON RRP balance as a liquidity gauge.** Total money-fund assets, and the split between prime and government funds, are reported weekly. A surge into government funds and the ON RRP signals cash hoarding and risk-off — money looking for the highest *safe* yield, or fleeing something. A drain *out* of the ON RRP, as happened through 2024 as the Fed's balance sheet shrank, signals cash being pulled back into the financial system to fund Treasury issuance and bank reserves; watch it as a read on system liquidity. The signal: rising government-fund and ON RRP balances = defensive cash; falling = liquidity being deployed or drained.

**Watch prime-fund gate and fee risk — even post-2023.** Gates are gone for prime funds, but swing pricing and the liquidity floors are not. In a stress event, a prime fund hitting its liquidity minimums will swing its NAV down and may restrict flows operationally. If you hold institutional prime funds for the yield pickup, your invalidation signal is the prime-minus-government spread compressing and prime weekly-liquid-asset levels dropping toward the floor. When you see both, move to a government fund and give up the pickup — the small carry is not worth standing in front of a run.

**Monitor private-credit growth as a systemic-risk indicator.** You do not need to trade private credit to use it as a signal. Rapid growth in private-credit assets, rising leverage in BDCs, and reports of funds gating investor redemptions are early warnings that risk has migrated to a corner with no backstop. Historically, the place where leverage and opacity grow fastest in a cycle is where the next crisis surfaces. Treat accelerating private-credit growth and the first redemption restrictions as a reason to add hedges to credit-sensitive positions.

**Use the deposit-flight signal for bank equities.** The 2022–23 migration is repeatable: whenever the policy rate rises faster than banks reprice their deposits, cash drains from low-paying deposits into money funds and the ON RRP, and the banks most exposed are those with the most rate-sensitive, uninsured deposit bases. For an equity investor, this is a concrete screen. When you see the Fed hiking and money-fund assets climbing, look at which banks fund themselves with sticky insured retail deposits (resilient) versus hot uninsured commercial deposits (vulnerable), and price the funding-cost squeeze and run risk accordingly. The plumbing tells you *where* the deposit flight lands before the bank's next earnings call does. The collapse of a large regional bank in March 2023 was, mechanically, a deposit-flight story that the money-fund and ON RRP data had been narrating for a year.

**The position and the catalysts.** In calm regimes, the trade is simply to capture the cash yield efficiently: government funds and the ON RRP floor tell you the risk-free short rate, and a modest prime allocation harvests the spread if you are watching. The catalysts that flip the regime are the ones that overwhelm the buffers: a fast credit shock (a default in a name prime funds hold), a liquidity shock (a dash for cash like March 2020), or a sharp change in the policy rate that moves trillions between deposits, funds, and the ON RRP. Around those catalysts, the plumbing leads: prime outflows, ON RRP surges, and a compressing prime spread show up before equities react. The sequencing matters for a trader — the cash market is the *first* domino, because cash is the most risk-averse money in the system and moves at the first hint of trouble. By the time the stress reaches credit spreads and then equities, the money-fund and ON RRP data have already turned. That lead-time is the edge.

**What invalidates the view.** The bear case on the plumbing as a haven is that the 2023 reforms and the standing Fed facilities (the ON RRP as a floor, and the standing repo facility as a backstop) have genuinely made government money funds a robust cash haven — in which case there is no edge in fearing a run there, only in harvesting the yield. That view is *invalidated* if a stress event again forces an emergency facility like the MMLF, proving the buffers still aren't enough. The bullish-on-stability view is invalidated the day the Fed has to improvise another rescue; the bearish view is invalidated by a stress event the new rules absorb without a bailout. Either way, the tell is the same place you've been watching: the flows, the ON RRP, and the prime spread.

**A concrete watchlist.** If you want to operationalize all of this into a routine, four data series tell you almost everything: total money-fund assets and the prime-versus-government split (reported weekly), the ON RRP balance (reported daily by the New York Fed), the prime-minus-government fund yield spread, and the pace of private-credit and BDC asset growth (reported quarterly). Rising government-fund and ON RRP balances with a compressing prime spread is the defensive-cash signature — risk-off is building in the safest money first. A draining ON RRP with stable spreads is benign liquidity being redeployed. And accelerating private-credit growth with the first reports of redemption gating is the early-warning siren for the *next* venue, the one with no Rule 2a-7 behind it. None of these require inside information; they are all public, and they all lead the price action in risk assets because cash moves first.

The deeper point ties back to where we started. Trillions of dollars sit in vehicles that look like cash but are, underneath, lightly-regulated lenders running a maturity mismatch. Whether they are a haven or a hazard is not decided by how safe the assets *feel* — it is decided by the rules: Rule 2a-7's dials, the gate and swing-pricing reforms, the liquidity floors, and the Fed facilities that backstop or compete with them. The rules are reactive, the risk migrates, and the safety net is conditional the deeper into the shadows you go. Read the rules and the plumbing together — the rulebook for the buffers, the flows for where the marginal dollar is hiding, and the facilities for whether a backstop exists — and you can see the next run forming before it reaches your screen.
