> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The rules of global trade — the WTO system, free-trade agreements, and a handful of US domestic statutes (Sections 301, 232, IEEPA, and the antidumping process) — decide which goods flow, at what cost, and how fast a trade war can erupt; knowing the legal lever in play tells you how quickly and how far trade policy can move a market.
>
> - A tariff is a tax on an **import**, and despite the rhetoric the **importer pays it at the border** — most of the cost lands on the buyer's country, not the exporter's. In 2018-19 the pass-through to US prices was close to **100%**.
> - The WTO sets a global floor (the "most-favoured-nation," or MFN, tariff everyone gets), but **domestic statutes**, not the WTO, are what let a president raise tariffs. A Section 232 or IEEPA tariff can take effect in **days**; a WTO case to challenge it takes **years** — and since 2019 the WTO's top appeals court has had **no judges**.
> - The speed of the legal lever *is* the trade. The faster the statute, the faster the repricing; the slower the WTO clock, the longer the tariff stays on regardless of whether it was legal.
> - The one number to remember: in 2018 the average US tariff on Chinese goods went from **3.1% to ~19.3%**, and China's on US goods from **8.0% to ~21.1%** — a tit-for-tat ratchet that no court ever stopped.

On the morning of March 1, 2018, a US president stood in a cabinet meeting and said, almost offhandedly, "trade wars are good, and easy to win." Within hours the share prices of steel-consuming companies — carmakers, can manufacturers, appliance firms — fell, while a handful of US steel mills jumped. A 25% tariff on imported steel and a 10% tariff on aluminium had just been announced under a statute most investors had never heard of: Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962. No vote in Congress. No ruling from the World Trade Organization. A finding by the Commerce Department that steel imports threatened "national security," and a presidential signature, were enough.

That single sentence — and the legal machinery behind it — is what this post is about. Most people think of trade as something that just *happens*: ships move, goods arrive, prices are what they are. But every one of those flows runs through a thick layer of law. There are treaties that promise countries won't discriminate against each other's goods. There are free-trade agreements that carve out zero-tariff zones. And there are domestic statutes — quiet, decades-old laws — that hand a single official the power to tax an entire category of imports overnight. When one of those levers gets pulled, markets reprice before the first container is even taxed.

For an investor, the lesson is not "trade wars are bad" or "tariffs are good." It is far more practical: **the legal lever in play tells you how fast and how far the move can go.** A tariff that needs a year-long investigation moves slowly and is half-priced before it lands. A tariff a president can sign on a Friday night moves the tape on Monday's open. Knowing which statute is being used — and what could undo it — is the difference between trading the headline and being run over by it.

This is the spine of the whole post: a statute sets the lever, the lever sets the tariff, the tariff lands on the importer, and the cost then splits among consumers, margins, and exchange rates — which is what the market actually prices. We'll build every box in that chain from the ground up, then turn it into a playbook. If you want the general version of this mechanism — how *any* rule-change drifts into prices — see [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain). Here we do the trade-specific version in full detail.

## Foundations: trade, tariffs, and the rules that govern them

Before we can trade trade policy, we need five plain-English ideas: why countries trade at all, what a tariff actually is, who the WTO is, what "MFN" and "bound vs applied" mean, and what a free-trade agreement does. None of these require any economics background. I'll define each from zero.

### Why trade happens: comparative advantage

Start with the oldest idea in the field. In 1817 the economist David Ricardo pointed out something counter-intuitive: two countries are both better off trading even if one of them is *worse* at making everything. The reason is **comparative advantage** — what matters is not who is better in absolute terms, but who gives up the least to make a particular thing.

Here is the everyday version. Suppose you are a brilliant surgeon who also happens to type faster than any assistant in town. Should you do your own typing? No — because every hour you spend typing is an hour you *don't* spend in surgery, and surgery is worth far more. You "trade" with an assistant: they type, you operate, and you both end up richer than if each did everything alone. Countries work the same way. Vietnam might give up very little (in terms of other production) to assemble electronics; the United States gives up a lot of high-value work to do the same assembly. So Vietnam assembles, the US designs, and trade moves the goods between them. The total pie is bigger.

That is the *economic* case for free trade, and it is why, for seventy years, the default direction of policy was toward fewer barriers. But comparative advantage says nothing about *who* inside each country wins. The surgeon-and-typist story has a hidden loser: the town's other typists, who now compete with a cheaper one. In trade, the "other typists" are domestic workers and firms who compete with imports — and they vote, lobby, and sue. **The entire legal architecture of trade exists to manage that tension:** to capture the gains from trade while giving governments tools to protect (or appear to protect) the losers. Every statute we'll meet is a tool for slowing, taxing, or blocking a flow that comparative advantage would otherwise let run free.

This is worth holding onto because it explains the *politics* of every tariff, and politics is what drives the timing of trade-policy shocks. The gains from trade are large but *diffuse* — spread thinly across hundreds of millions of consumers who each save a few dollars on cheaper goods and never notice. The losses are smaller in total but *concentrated* — a specific town's steel mill, a specific industry's workers, who feel the full weight and organise to fight back. Concentrated losers out-lobby diffuse winners every time. That asymmetry is why protectionist measures are politically durable even when they make the country as a whole poorer, and why, as a trader, you should never bet that a tariff will come off "because it's economically irrational." It stays on because the people it helps are loud and the people it hurts are quiet. The legal levers exist precisely to give politicians a fast way to answer the loud, concentrated losers.

### A short history: from Smoot-Hawley to the WTO

The modern architecture is a direct reaction to a disaster, and knowing the arc helps you read the present. In June 1930, with the US economy already sliding into the Depression, Congress passed the **Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act**, raising average US tariffs on dutiable imports to roughly 45-60%. The intent was to protect American farmers and factories. The effect was catastrophic: trading partners retaliated with their own tariffs, world trade collapsed by about two-thirds between 1929 and 1934, and the downturn deepened into a global slump. Smoot-Hawley is the cautionary tale every trade negotiator carries — the proof that a tariff spiral has no winners.

The post-war response was deliberate. The 1947 GATT began a multi-decade project to *lower* tariffs through reciprocal negotiation, under a binding no-discrimination rule. Average tariffs in advanced economies fell from those Depression-era peaks to low single digits by the 1990s. The 1995 creation of the WTO added teeth — a binding dispute court — to what had been a gentleman's agreement. For two generations the direction of travel was one-way: lower barriers, more rules, deeper integration. The 2018-19 trade war, and the broader turn toward "national security" and "emergency" tariffs, is the first sustained reversal of that arc since 1947 — which is exactly why it caught so many markets off guard. A generation of investors had priced trade liberalisation as a near-constant. The statutes that enable the reversal were always on the books; they were simply dormant.

### What a tariff is, and the punchline about who pays

A **tariff** is simply a tax on an imported good, collected by the importing country's customs agency at the border. In the US that agency is Customs and Border Protection (CBP). If the US puts a 25% tariff on a \$1,000 imported washing machine, then when the machine crosses the border, the US company *importing* it writes a check to CBP for \$250.

Read that again, because it is the single most misunderstood fact in all of trade policy: **the importer pays the tariff, and the importer is in the buyer's country.** When a politician says "we are charging China 25%," what physically happens is that a US company — Walmart, Best Buy, an auto-parts distributor — pays the US Treasury \$250 per machine. China's government receives nothing. The Chinese exporter might *later* feel the pain if the US importer demands a lower price to compensate, but the legal incidence — who is on the hook to the government — is the importer. We will spend a whole section on what happens to that \$250 after it's paid, because that's where the market action is.

### The WTO and its predecessor, GATT

After World War II, the major economies wanted to avoid repeating the 1930s, when a spiral of retaliatory tariffs (kicked off by the US Smoot-Hawley Act of 1930) deepened the Great Depression. So in 1947 they signed the **General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT)** — a treaty whose core promise was: *we will lower tariffs together, and we will not discriminate.* In 1995 GATT was folded into a permanent institution, the **World Trade Organization (WTO)**, now with 166 member economies.

The WTO is best understood as three things bundled together:

1. **A rulebook.** Members agree to maximum tariff levels and rules against unfair practices (dumping, illegal subsidies, discrimination).
2. **A negotiating forum.** Periodic "rounds" where countries trade concessions ("I'll cut my tariff on your cars if you cut yours on my rice").
3. **A court.** A dispute-settlement system where one member can sue another for breaking the rules, win a ruling, and — if the loser won't comply — get authorization to retaliate.

The crucial thing for an investor is what the WTO *cannot* do. It cannot stop a country from imposing a tariff in the first place. It can only, after a multi-year legal process, declare the tariff illegal and authorize the wronged country to retaliate. The tariff stays on the whole time. As we'll see, that gap between "imposed instantly" and "judged years later" is the entire reason trade policy can move markets so violently.

### Most-favoured-nation, bound vs applied

Two pieces of jargon you must own, because every tariff headline turns on them.

**Most-favoured-nation (MFN) treatment** is the WTO's anti-discrimination rule. It says: whatever your *best* tariff offer is to any one country, you must give it to *all* WTO members. The name is misleading — "most favoured" actually means "treated the same as everyone else," i.e. no special penalties. If the US offers a 2.5% car tariff to Japan, MFN says it must offer 2.5% to Germany, Korea, and everyone else too. The exceptions to MFN are what make trade interesting: free-trade agreements (you *can* give a partner a better deal) and penalty tariffs aimed at one country (which require a specific legal basis to be WTO-compliant).

**Bound vs applied** rates. When a country joins the WTO, it commits to a maximum tariff for each product — its **bound rate**, a legal ceiling. The tariff it *actually* charges is the **applied rate**, which can be anywhere from zero up to the bound. The gap matters enormously for risk. If a country's applied tariff on a good is 3% but its bound rate is 30%, it can legally raise that tariff *ten-fold* without breaking any WTO commitment — there's a huge amount of "dry powder." Developed economies usually apply rates close to their bounds (little room to raise legally); many developing economies apply far below their bounds (lots of room). When you assess "how much worse can this get," the bound rate is your ceiling for the legal path.

The MFN rule also explains why a *trade war* is visible in the data as a sharp wedge. Under MFN, the US applies roughly a 3% average tariff to the rest of the world; that's the baseline everyone gets. When the US singled China out and pushed the average tariff on Chinese goods to nearly 20%, it created a six-fold gap between what China faced and what every other WTO member faced — a textbook violation of the spirit of MFN, made possible only because the Section 301 statute provided a legal hook. The chart below makes the discrimination concrete: each country's penalty tariff on the *other* dwarfs the modest MFN rate it applies to the rest of the world.

![Bar chart contrasting US and China tariffs on each other versus their tariffs on the rest of the world](/imgs/blogs/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade-6.png)

That wedge is exactly why production reroutes. If a good made in China faces a 20% US tariff but the same good made in Vietnam faces only the 3% MFN rate, a 17-percentage-point gap opens up that any rational supply chain will try to capture by moving assembly to Vietnam — even if China is genuinely the lower-cost producer. The legal structure of MFN-plus-penalty-tariff is what turns a political dispute into a multi-year reallocation of factories, and it's the single most durable trade in the whole episode (more on this in the playbook).

### Free-trade agreements and trade blocs

A **free-trade agreement (FTA)** is a treaty between two or more countries to charge each other little or no tariff — a legal exception to MFN. The members give each other a discount they don't have to give the rest of the world. The big ones you'll see in headlines:

- **USMCA** (the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement, which replaced NAFTA in 2020) — near-zero tariffs across North America, *if* a good meets the "rules of origin" (enough of it must be made within the bloc to qualify).
- **CPTPP** — an 11-country Pacific Rim pact, notable for *not* including the US (which withdrew in 2017) or China.
- **RCEP** — a 15-country Asia-Pacific pact anchored by China and including ASEAN, the world's largest trade bloc by population.
- **The EU single market** — the deepest form: not just zero internal tariffs but a customs union with a *common external tariff*, so the 27 members present one trade wall to the outside world.

![Comparison matrix of WTO, USMCA, CPTPP, RCEP and the EU single market by what each is, members, and internal tariffs](/imgs/blogs/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade-8.png)

The mental model is layers. The WTO sets a *floor* — the MFN rate everyone gets. On top of that, FTAs cut tariffs further among their own members. A good crossing a border faces whichever rate applies: the deep discount if it's inside an FTA and meets the rules of origin, the MFN applied rate if not, or a penalty rate if a specific statute has singled out its country of origin. Where a good is *made* — not just where it ships from — therefore becomes a legal and financial question worth billions, which is exactly why supply-chain rerouting is a core part of the trade-war playbook later.

## The US tariff statutes: four levers, four speeds

Here is the heart of the matter. The WTO and FTAs are the international layer. But a tariff is imposed under *domestic* law, and in the US there are four main statutes that let the executive branch raise tariffs without a fresh act of Congress. They differ in their legal basis, what triggers them, how broad they can be, and — most importantly for a trader — **how fast they bite.**

![Matrix of the four US tariff statutes: Section 301, Section 232, IEEPA, and AD CVD, by legal basis, trigger, speed, and decision-maker](/imgs/blogs/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade-3.png)

### Section 301 — unfair trade practices

**Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974** lets the US Trade Representative (USTR) investigate and respond to a foreign country's "unfair" trade practices — things like stealing intellectual property, forcing technology transfer, or subsidising in ways that hurt US firms. After an investigation (which can run several months) and a public-comment period, the USTR can recommend tariffs, and the president can impose them. This is the statute behind most of the 2018-19 China tariffs: the USTR's investigation concluded that China's IP and technology-transfer policies were unfair, and the tariffs followed.

Section 301 is *medium speed*. The investigation and comment period give it a multi-month lead time, which means markets can see it coming — there are public hearings, draft tariff lists, and comment deadlines that all telegraph the move. That telegraphing is a gift to a trader: you can position into the announcement and fade or add on the confirmation.

### Section 232 — national security

**Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962** lets the Commerce Department investigate whether imports of a product "threaten to impair the national security," and if so, the president can restrict them — including with tariffs. This is the steel-and-aluminium statute. The legal trigger ("national security") is vague and broad, which is precisely what makes it powerful: steel, aluminium, autos, and even uranium have all been investigated under it. Once Commerce delivers its report, the president has 90 days to act, and the tariff can land almost immediately after the decision.

Section 232 is *fast*. The investigation happens largely inside the executive branch, the standard is loose, and once the report is in, the tariff can hit within days. For a trader, a 232 investigation being *announced* on a sector is itself a tradable event — it signals a credible, fast tariff threat months before any tariff exists.

### IEEPA — emergency economic powers

**The International Emergency Economic Powers Act of 1977 (IEEPA)** is the broadest and fastest lever of all. It lets the president declare a national emergency with respect to an "unusual and extraordinary threat" originating abroad, and then regulate or prohibit a vast range of economic transactions — including, in recent interpretations, imposing tariffs. IEEPA was historically the basis for *sanctions* (freezing assets, blocking transactions), but it has been stretched to cover tariffs framed as responses to emergencies like fentanyl trafficking or migration.

IEEPA is *the overnight lever*. There is no investigation requirement, no comment period — a presidential declaration and an executive order, and tariffs can be in force the next morning. This is the maximum-speed, maximum-uncertainty case. It is also the most legally contested: courts have begun to question whether IEEPA's text — which never mentions the word "tariff" — actually authorizes them at all. That legal vulnerability is itself a trade: an IEEPA tariff carries reversal risk that a Section 301 tariff does not.

### AD/CVD — antidumping and countervailing duties

The fourth lever is different in character. **Antidumping (AD) and countervailing duties (CVD)** are the WTO-blessed, rules-based response to specific unfair pricing. *Dumping* is selling exports below the price charged at home (or below cost); a *countervailable subsidy* is a foreign government subsidy that lets exporters undercut domestic producers. Under the Tariff Act of 1930, a US industry can petition the Commerce Department and the International Trade Commission (ITC) to investigate. If Commerce finds dumping/subsidy *and* the ITC finds it injured a US industry, a duty is imposed to offset exactly that margin.

AD/CVD is *slow but durable*. A full investigation takes roughly 12 to 18 months. But because it follows the WTO's own rulebook, the resulting duty is legally solid and can stay on for years (subject to periodic reviews). For a trader, AD/CVD is the lever to watch for *narrow, product-specific* moves — solar panels, steel pipe, washing machines, shrimp — rather than economy-wide repricing. The timeline is long enough that the affected names usually drift toward the outcome well before the final duty.

> [!note]
> **The one-sentence summary of the four levers:** Section 232 and IEEPA are the *fast* levers a president can pull almost unilaterally and almost overnight; Section 301 is *medium* (a months-long investigation telegraphs it); AD/CVD is *slow but legally durable* (12-18 months, WTO-compliant, product-specific). The speed of the lever sets the speed of the repricing.

## Who actually pays a tariff: the incidence debate

We established that the importer writes the check. But that's just the *legal* incidence. The *economic* incidence — who ultimately bears the cost after everyone adjusts their prices — is the question that decides which stocks move. There are three places the \$250 from our washing-machine example can land.

![Flow showing a 250 dollar tariff paid by the importer then split among the consumer, the importer margin, and the exporter](/imgs/blogs/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade-4.png)

1. **The consumer** pays it, if the importer simply raises the shelf price. This is "pass-through" to prices.
2. **The importer** eats it, if competition forces the importer to hold its price and absorb the tariff in a thinner margin.
3. **The exporter** eats it, if the importer demands a lower wholesale price and the foreign exporter cuts to keep the business.

Which of these dominates is an *empirical* question, and the 2018-19 trade war became the largest natural experiment in modern history. The headline finding from multiple independent studies (Amiti, Redding & Weinstein 2019; Cavallo et al. 2021; Fajgelbaum et al.) was striking: the pass-through to US import prices was **close to complete**. In plain terms, the prices US importers paid for Chinese goods, tariff-inclusive, rose by almost the full amount of the tariff — meaning the cost fell overwhelmingly on **US importers and consumers, not on Chinese exporters.** The "China pays" framing was, in the aggregate, empirically false.

![Stacked bars showing how a 250 dollar tariff splits among exporter, importer, and consumer under three pass-through regimes](/imgs/blogs/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade-5.png)

That said, incidence varies enormously by product. For a commodity where the foreign exporter has many other buyers and the importer has many other suppliers (think generic components), the importer/consumer bears almost everything. For a product where the foreign exporter is desperate to keep US market share and has weak pricing power (and where the foreign currency falls), the exporter can absorb a chunk. The two offsets that shift cost back onto the exporter's side — a falling exporter currency and a glutted exporter facing nowhere else to sell — are exactly the second-order effects we'll quantify next.

#### Worked example: splitting a 25% tariff three ways

Let's make the incidence concrete with our \$1,000 imported good and a 25% tariff, so the tariff is \$250.

Take the **near-full pass-through** case that matched the 2018-19 data. Suppose the foreign exporter, with strong demand elsewhere, cuts its price by only \$20 (from \$1,000 to \$980). The importer, facing competitors who all got hit by the same tariff, passes almost all of the rest to the shelf. The arithmetic:

- Exporter price cut: \$20 absorbed by the exporter.
- Tariff base is now \$980, so the tariff is 25% × \$980 = \$245.
- The importer absorbs, say, \$25 of that in a slightly thinner margin to stay competitive.
- The consumer pays the remaining \$245 − \$25 = \$220 as a higher price, *plus* loses the \$20 the exporter would otherwise have passed on as a lower price — but in price terms, the shelf price rises by roughly \$200.

Of the roughly \$245 in tariff revenue, about \$20 (8%) came out of the exporter, ~\$25 (10%) out of the importer, and ~\$200 (82%) out of the US consumer. **The intuition: when every importer faces the same tariff and the exporter has other buyers, the cost flows almost entirely downstream to the domestic consumer — which is why a broad tariff reads as a tax hike and a hit to domestic spending power, not as a hit to the foreign country.**

#### Worked example: the gross-margin hit to a US retailer

Now flip to the importer's income statement, because that's what an equity trader actually models. Suppose a US discount retailer sells \$5 billion a year of goods sourced from China, at a gross margin of 30% — so cost of goods sold (COGS) is \$3.5 billion and gross profit is \$1.5 billion. A new 25% tariff lands on those \$3.5 billion of imports.

The raw tariff bill is 25% × \$3.5bn = **\$875 million**. The question is how much of that the retailer can pass to customers. Two scenarios:

- **80% pass-through to prices:** the retailer raises prices enough to recover \$700m, and absorbs \$175m in its own margin. Gross profit falls from \$1.5bn to about \$1.325bn — an **11.7% hit to gross profit**. If the retailer trades at 15× earnings and earnings move roughly with gross profit, that's a double-digit-percentage repricing of the stock, before any volume loss.
- **50% pass-through (fierce competition):** the retailer recovers only \$437m and absorbs \$438m. Gross profit falls to about \$1.06bn — a **29% hit**. This is the kind of number that takes a retailer's stock down 20-30% on a tariff headline.

**The intuition: the tariff rate is not the trade — the *pass-through* assumption is. A 25% tariff on a low-margin importer that can only pass half of it through can wipe out a third of gross profit, which is why import-heavy, thin-margin retailers are the most violent movers on a tariff announcement.** For the mechanics of how a margin hit flows into a fair-value estimate, see [profitability margins: gross, operating, net](/blog/trading/equity-research/profitability-margins-gross-operating-net).

## The currency offset: how FX neutralizes a tariff

Here is the second-order effect that surprises people. A tariff makes a country's exports more expensive in the destination market. But if the *exporter's currency falls* against the destination currency, that makes its goods cheaper — and the two effects can cancel out. This is why, during the 2018-19 trade war, every tariff escalation was watched alongside the Chinese yuan: a weaker yuan was, in effect, China's quiet counter-tariff.

The mechanism is arithmetic. A US importer buys a Chinese good priced in yuan, then converts dollars to yuan to pay for it. If the yuan weakens — more yuan per dollar — the dollar cost of the same yuan-priced good falls, partially offsetting the tariff the importer now owes. If the yuan falls by roughly the same percentage as the tariff, the all-in dollar landed cost is roughly unchanged, and the US consumer never feels the tariff at all. The cost has been shifted onto the *purchasing power of the exporter's economy* — Chinese workers and savers, whose yuan now buys fewer imports — rather than onto US buyers.

#### Worked example: how much yuan depreciation offsets a 10% tariff

Suppose a Chinese good is priced at 700 yuan, and the exchange rate is 7.0 yuan per dollar, so the good costs the US importer \$100. The US now imposes a **10% tariff**, raising the landed cost to \$110.

How much must the yuan fall to neutralize the tariff entirely — to get the all-in dollar cost back to \$100?

We need the post-tariff dollar price to fall back to \$100. With a 10% tariff, the pre-tariff dollar price must drop to \$100 / 1.10 = **\$90.91**. The good is still 700 yuan, so the new exchange rate must satisfy 700 yuan / (new rate) = \$90.91, giving a new rate of 700 / 90.91 = **7.70 yuan per dollar**. That's a move from 7.0 to 7.70 — a **10% depreciation of the yuan**.

So the rough rule is: **a depreciation of the exporter's currency equal to the tariff percentage fully offsets the tariff for the importing consumer.** In practice the yuan didn't move the full amount — between early 2018 and late 2019 it fell from about 6.3 to 7.18 per dollar, roughly 12-14%, which offset a meaningful slice of the average tariff but not all of it. **The intuition: a tariff and a currency are two prices on the same flow; watch them together, because a central bank that lets its currency slide is fighting your tariff trade in the background.** For the deeper mechanics of how currencies move on policy, see [trading the dollar: DXY, carry, and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile) and the cross-asset view in [FX currencies: the relative-value layer](/blog/trading/cross-asset/fx-currencies-the-relative-value-layer).

## The supply-chain offset: rerouting around the tariff

The second great offset, alongside currency, is the supply chain itself. A tariff on goods *from China* is not a tariff on the goods themselves — it's a tariff on their *origin*. So the most powerful long-run response is to change the origin: move final assembly to a country that isn't tariffed, and the goods sail in at the low MFN rate. This is the "China + 1" strategy, and it turns a tariff into a one-time relocation cost rather than a permanent tax.

How fast this works depends entirely on the product. The deciding variables are: how portable the production is (assembling a toy is portable; fabricating a leading-edge chip is not), how much capital the new facility needs, and how strict the *rules of origin* are. Rules of origin are the legal fine print that decides what counts as "made in" a country — typically a requirement that enough value be added locally, or that the product undergo a "substantial transformation." A tariff regime with loose rules of origin is easy to route around (just do final assembly elsewhere); one with strict rules forces real relocation of the supply chain, not just the last screw.

This creates a sharp split in how different tariffs behave. A tariff on **low-tech, portable** goods (apparel, furniture, simple electronics assembly) decays fast — within a quarter or two, production reroutes to Vietnam, Mexico, or India, the import-price hit fades, and any short on the "consumer pays" thesis decays with it. A tariff on **high-tech, locked-in** goods (advanced semiconductors, specialised chemicals, aerospace components) bites hard and lasts for years, because there is no Vietnam to reroute to — the capability simply doesn't exist elsewhere. This is why tariffs and export controls on semiconductors are a fundamentally different, more durable trade than tariffs on consumer goods, a distinction explored in depth in [export controls and the chip war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war). Before you put on a tariff trade, ask: *how portable is this supply chain?* The answer tells you whether your trade is a fast fade or a multi-year regime.

## The beneficiary: domestic producers gain pricing power

So far we've focused on the losers. But a tariff is a transfer, and someone is on the receiving end: the domestic producers who compete with the now-more-expensive imports. When a 25% tariff raises the price of imported steel, domestic steelmakers can raise *their* prices too — not by the full 25%, but enough to fatten margins. This is the "protection" the statute is nominally for, and it's a real, tradable long.

#### Worked example: a domestic steelmaker's pricing-power gain

Suppose imported steel lands at \$700 per tonne pre-tariff, and a domestic mill sells at \$720 (a small premium for shorter lead times and reliability). The mill produces 5 million tonnes a year.

Now a 25% Section 232 tariff lands on imports, raising the landed cost of imported steel to \$700 × 1.25 = \$875 per tonne. The domestic mill, no longer undercut, can raise its price toward the new import ceiling. Suppose it lifts its price to \$820 — still \$55 below the tariffed import price, so it keeps its customers, but \$100 above its old \$720.

The gain is \$100 per tonne × 5 million tonnes = **\$500 million of additional revenue**, almost all of which drops to operating profit because the mill's costs haven't changed. If the mill was earning \$300m in operating profit before, this more than doubles it. **The intuition: a tariff is a price umbrella — it lets domestic producers raise prices under its shade, and the smaller and more import-exposed the domestic industry, the bigger the per-unit windfall. The long side of a tariff trade is the protected domestic producer; the short side is the import-dependent buyer.** This is why, on the steel-tariff morning, US steel mills jumped while carmakers fell — the same headline, opposite trades.

## Retaliation: the trade war's second front

A tariff is rarely the end of the story, because the other country retaliates — and retaliation is *targeted*, not random. A country choosing what to hit back picks goods that maximise political pain on the other side while minimising pain at home. That makes retaliation eerily predictable, which is a gift to a trader.

When the US tariffed Chinese goods in 2018, China retaliated on US agricultural exports — above all **soybeans** — because farm states are politically sensitive and China could source soybeans elsewhere (Brazil). The result: US soybean exports to China collapsed, prices fell, and the US government ultimately paid farmers about **\$28 billion** in two rounds of bailout payments to offset the damage. The retaliation was as economically real as the original tariff, and the affected names (agricultural commodity traders, fertiliser firms, farm-equipment makers, the railroads that haul grain) repriced accordingly. Reading the *retaliation* correctly was a separate, parallel trade to reading the original tariff.

#### Worked example: the expected-value of a retaliation trade

Suppose a tariff escalation is brewing and you're weighing a short on a US soybean-exposed name whose stock is \$50. You judge there's a **60%** chance China retaliates on agriculture within the quarter (taking the stock to \$38, a \$12 fall) and a **40%** chance a deal is struck instead (taking it to \$56, a \$6 gain).

The expected value of being short one share is the probability-weighted payoff:

- Retaliation leg: 0.60 × (+\$12 to a short) = +\$7.20
- Deal leg: 0.40 × (−\$6 to a short) = −\$2.40
- **Expected value = \$7.20 − \$2.40 = +\$4.80 per share**, on a \$50 stock — about a 9.6% expected return.

The trade is positive-expectancy, but the 40% deal scenario is a real risk of a \$6 loss, so you'd size it small and define the deal headline as your stop. **The intuition: trade-policy events are binary catalysts, so the right framing is expected value across the branches, not a single point forecast — and because retaliation targets are politically predictable, the probabilities are more estimable here than in most event trades.** For the full machinery of binary-event and scenario trades, see [scenario analysis and war-gaming geopolitical events](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/scenario-analysis-and-war-gaming-geopolitical-events).

## The erosion of the rules-based order

We need to understand why, in the 2020s, the legal restraints on tariffs have grown so weak — because that weakness is itself a market force. The WTO's dispute system was designed as a two-step process: a *panel* hears a case and rules, and either side can *appeal* to a standing **Appellate Body** of seven judges, whose ruling is final. The system worked for two decades; tariffs that broke the rules got struck down, and countries mostly complied.

Then it broke. Starting in 2017, the US — under both parties — blocked the appointment of new Appellate Body judges, arguing the body had overstepped its mandate. Judges' terms expired and weren't replaced. By **December 2019 the Appellate Body fell below the three-judge minimum needed to hear a case, and it has had no functioning quorum since.** The practical consequence is profound: a losing country can now appeal a panel ruling "into the void" — into an appeals court that cannot convene — and the case simply never concludes. The tariff stays on indefinitely, regardless of its legality.

![Timeline contrasting a statutory tariff that bites in days with a WTO dispute that takes years and stalls at a defunct Appellate Body](/imgs/blogs/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade-7.png)

For an investor this changes the risk calculus completely. In the old world, a blatantly illegal tariff carried real reversal risk: the WTO would strike it down in a couple of years, and the affected trade flow would normalise. In today's world, **the WTO is no longer a meaningful brake on a determined government.** The only effective checks left are *domestic* — a US court ruling that a statute doesn't authorize the tariff (as has happened with some IEEPA tariffs), or a domestic political reversal. So when you assess "can this tariff be undone," the WTO is largely off the table; the real question is whether a domestic court or election can reverse it.

A subset of WTO members built a workaround called the **Multi-Party Interim Appeal Arbitration Arrangement (MPIA)** — a voluntary system that lets participating members appeal panel rulings to arbitrators instead of the dead Appellate Body. But it's optional and the world's largest economy isn't in it, so it doesn't bind the disputes that matter most for markets. The net effect is a two-tier system: rules-based discipline still works among the countries that *choose* to be bound, and is effectively absent for those that don't. For a trader, that means the credibility of any given tariff's legal challenge depends on *who* imposed it and *under which statute* — not on a uniform global rulebook. A tariff from a country inside the binding system carries more reversal risk than one from a country that has opted out of the only functioning appeals mechanism.

There's a deeper market signal in the erosion itself. When the rules-based order was strong, trade policy was *predictable* — a slow, negotiated, mean-reverting process — and so it carried a low risk premium. As the legal restraints weakened, trade policy became a source of sudden, discretionary, hard-to-hedge shocks, and the assets most exposed to it (globally diversified manufacturers, emerging-market exporters, the currencies of trade-surplus economies) began to carry a higher risk premium. The weakening of the WTO is, in effect, a structural increase in the volatility of one input to corporate earnings. That repricing of *trade-policy uncertainty* as a risk factor is subtle and slow, but it's real — and it's why globally integrated firms now trade with a discount they didn't carry in the 2000s.

## How fast each lever moves: the speed dimension

Let's pull together the single most important practical fact, because it governs how you trade every trade-policy headline: **the legal basis determines the speed, and the speed determines how the market prices it.**

![Step chart of US tariff on China rising to 19 percent and China tariff on US rising to 21 percent from 2018 to 2020](/imgs/blogs/the-legal-architecture-of-global-trade-2.png)

- An **IEEPA tariff** can be in force overnight. There's no lead time, so the market can't pre-price it — the move is a *gap*, a sudden repricing on the open after the announcement. You either had the position on or you didn't.
- A **Section 232 tariff** moves in days once the Commerce report lands, but the *investigation* is announced months ahead. So there are two tradable events: the slow drift as the investigation proceeds, and the fast gap when the tariff is confirmed.
- A **Section 301 tariff** is the most telegraphed: draft tariff lists, public hearings, and comment deadlines all give weeks of warning. The market does a lot of the repricing *before* the tariff exists, on the published draft list. By the time the tariff is final, much of the move is done — the classic "buy the rumour, sell the news" setup.
- An **AD/CVD duty** takes 12-18 months, so the affected names drift slowly toward the preliminary determination (often the bigger mover than the final). The repricing is gradual and tradable on the milestones.

The chart above shows the cumulative effect of these levers in 2018-19: the US average tariff on Chinese goods stepped up from 3.1% to about 19.3% in a series of distinct escalations, and China retaliated almost in lockstep, raising its average tariff on US goods from 8.0% to about 21.1%. Each step is a Section 301 (or in the steel case, 232) action and a Chinese counter-measure — a visible, tradable ratchet. Notice that both lines settled around 20% and stayed there for *years*, through a change of administration: tariffs, once on, are politically sticky and rarely come off quickly. That stickiness is itself a tradable fact — don't position for a fast reversal just because a tariff "should" be illegal.

## Non-tariff barriers: the rules that don't show up as a tax

Tariffs are the visible barrier, but governments have a whole arsenal of **non-tariff barriers (NTBs)** that restrict trade without ever charging a tax. They matter because they're harder to see, harder to challenge, and often more powerful:

- **Quotas** cap the *quantity* of a good that can be imported. A tariff-rate quota (TRQ) charges a low tariff up to a quota, then a punitive rate above it — common in agriculture (sugar, dairy).
- **Standards and regulations** — safety, sanitary, technical, or environmental rules — can block imports that don't comply. A rule that, say, a food product meet a specific (and conveniently domestic) standard can be a protectionist wall dressed as consumer protection.
- **Subsidies** work in reverse: instead of taxing imports, a government pays its own producers, letting them undercut foreign rivals. This is what AD/CVD's "countervailing" half targets.
- **Local-content and procurement rules** ("buy domestic") steer government and sometimes private spending toward home producers.

For a trader, NTBs are the slow-burn risk. They don't produce a single dramatic headline like a tariff, but a new standard or quota can quietly reroute a multi-billion-dollar flow, and the affected names reprice as the rule phases in. They're also the favoured tool when a country wants to retaliate *without* appearing to start a tariff war — China's informal "slow-walking" of customs clearance for a targeted country's goods is a classic example.

## Common misconceptions

Three myths dominate the trade-policy conversation, and each one, corrected, is a trading edge.

### Myth 1: "The exporting country pays the tariff"

This is the most common and the most expensive misconception. As we showed, the importer pays the tariff at the border, and the empirical evidence from 2018-19 is that the cost passed through almost completely to **US import prices and consumers** — studies put the pass-through near 100%. The exporting country bears the cost only to the extent its exporters cut prices or its currency falls, and in the China case those offsets covered only a fraction. **The trade:** when a broad tariff is announced, the cleaner short is often the import-dependent *domestic* buyer (the retailer, the manufacturer that needs the input), not the foreign exporter — because the buyer is the one whose margins actually get squeezed.

### Myth 2: "Tariffs are illegal without WTO approval"

Wrong on the law. The WTO does not pre-approve tariffs; it can only judge them *after the fact*, and even that channel is broken now that the Appellate Body has no quorum. Tariffs are imposed under *domestic* statutes — 301, 232, IEEPA, AD/CVD — and become law the moment the relevant official acts. A tariff can be simultaneously *in force* and *WTO-inconsistent* for years. **The trade:** don't assume a tariff is fragile because it looks illegal. Its durability depends on domestic law (can a US court strike it down?) and politics, not on Geneva. The 19% average US tariff on China has stood since 2019 despite being challenged.

### Myth 3: "Trade wars have clear winners"

The political framing is that a trade war is a contest one side wins. The economics says otherwise: tariffs are a transfer that creates domestic winners (protected producers, the Treasury collecting the duty) and domestic *losers* (consumers paying higher prices, exporters hit by retaliation, manufacturers paying more for inputs) within the *same* country. The US-China trade war reduced trade between the two, raised US consumer prices, hit US farmers (who needed \$28 billion in federal bailouts after China retaliated on soybeans), and helped third countries like Vietnam and Mexico capture rerouted production. **The trade:** stop thinking "who wins the war" and start thinking "which specific cost-bearers and beneficiaries get repriced" — that's where the alpha is, and it's almost never the country in the headline.

## How it shows up in real markets

Now the payoff: the three repricing patterns that recur on every major trade-policy headline, with the 2018-19 trade war as the worked case.

### The tariff-announcement repricing

The cleanest pattern. A tariff is announced (or a draft list published), and within minutes the market sorts every affected name into cost-bearers and beneficiaries. On the March 2018 steel-tariff morning, US steel producers (the protected beneficiaries) rallied while steel-*consuming* manufacturers — automakers, appliance makers, beverage-can producers — fell. When the September 2018 list of \$200bn in Chinese goods landed, import-heavy retailers and electronics firms gapped down. The speed of the repricing tracks the speed of the lever: the 232 steel tariff (fast) gapped on the morning; the 301 lists (telegraphed) had been partly priced over the preceding weeks of draft lists and hearings.

### The FX offset

As the tariffs escalated through 2018-19, the yuan slid from about 6.3 to 7.18 per dollar — past the symbolically important 7.0 level in August 2019, which the US briefly responded to by labelling China a "currency manipulator." Traders watched the yuan as a real-time barometer of how much China was offsetting the tariffs and of escalation risk. A sharp yuan move *was* the trade signal: a sudden depreciation meant China was absorbing the tariff via FX (bearish for the "China pays" thesis, and a risk-off signal for global markets that fear a currency war). For how this fits the broader dollar regime, see [the dollar system: why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) and [petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance).

### The sector rotation

The deepest pattern is the multi-year reallocation of capital. The trade war didn't just move stocks for a day; it set off a structural rotation: capital flowed *out* of firms with China-concentrated supply chains and *into* the beneficiaries of "China + 1" diversification — Vietnamese, Mexican, and Indian manufacturers, US domestic producers shielded by the tariffs, and the logistics and automation names that profit from supply-chain reshoring. Vietnam was the standout: its exports to the US surged as production rerouted, and foreign direct investment poured in — registered FDI commitments ran in the high \$30 billions a year through the period, and the country's trade surplus with the US ballooned. That's the durable trade hiding inside the daily noise: not "short the tariffed name for a day," but "own the rerouting beneficiary for years."

That rotation also carries its own tail risk, which is the kind of detail that separates a good trade-policy read from a naive one. As production rerouted to Vietnam, the US began scrutinising whether some of it was genuine relocation or merely **transshipment** — Chinese goods passing through Vietnam with minimal local value-add to dodge the tariff, which violates the rules of origin. That scrutiny is itself a future legal risk to the rerouting trade: a country that becomes too obviously a tariff-dodging conduit can find itself the next target. So the supply-chain rotation is a long with a known invalidator — an origin-fraud crackdown or a fresh tariff on the substitute country. This is exactly the squeeze a friend-shoring beneficiary lives in, covered in [Vietnam in the US-China squeeze](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze). That rotation is still playing out years later — which is the difference between trading the headline and trading the regime. For the regime lens, see [reading the regime in real time: the dashboard](/blog/trading/cross-asset/reading-the-regime-in-real-time-the-dashboard).

## How to trade it: the playbook

Every trade-policy event reduces to the same five questions. Run them in order and you'll know the position, the size, the catalyst, and what invalidates the view.

### Step 1 — Identify the legal lever (it sets the speed and scope)

The first question is always *which statute*. Read the announcement for the legal basis:

- **IEEPA** → overnight, no warning. Expect a *gap*, not a drift. You can't pre-position on the specifics; you can only carry a general hedge against the risk. Also flag reversal risk — IEEPA tariffs face the strongest domestic-court challenges.
- **Section 232** → an *investigation* announcement is the early signal (months of lead time); the *report + decision* is the fast confirmation. Two distinct trades.
- **Section 301** → the most telegraphed. Trade the draft list and the hearings; by the final tariff, much is priced. Classic buy-the-rumour-sell-the-news.
- **AD/CVD** → slow (12-18 months), narrow (product-specific). Trade the preliminary determination, not just the final.

The lever also tells you the *scope*. IEEPA and broad 301 actions can hit hundreds of billions of dollars of goods (economy-wide repricing); a 232 action hits one sector; an AD/CVD duty hits one product line. Size your trade to the scope.

### Step 2 — Map the cost-bearers vs the beneficiaries

Forget "which country wins." For the specific goods hit, list:

- **Shorts (cost-bearers):** import-dependent buyers with thin margins and weak pricing power — discount retailers, contract manufacturers, anyone whose COGS is concentrated in the tariffed origin. Use the gross-margin-hit math from the worked example to size the earnings damage at a realistic pass-through.
- **Longs (beneficiaries):** the protected domestic producers (the price-umbrella trade), the Treasury (irrelevant to stocks), and — crucially — the *third-country* substitutes who capture the rerouted flow. The biggest, slowest, most durable gains are often in the substitute geography, not the home country.

### Step 3 — Find the FX and supply-chain offset

Before you commit, ask what's working against you. Watch the exporter's currency: a depreciation equal to the tariff percentage neutralizes the tariff for the consumer and undercuts a "consumer pays" short. And ask how fast the supply chain can reroute: if production can shift to a non-tariffed country within a quarter or two, the import-price hit is temporary and the short decays. Tariffs on goods with easy substitution (low-tech assembly) get arbitraged away fast; tariffs on goods with locked-in supply chains (advanced semiconductors, specialised chemicals) bite hard and last. For the deeper version of this in the chip context, see [export controls and the chip war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war).

### Step 4 — Size the repricing and the catalyst

Translate the tariff into earnings, then into price. The chain is: tariff rate → pass-through assumption → gross-margin hit → earnings hit → multiple × earnings = price move (the worked examples above are the template). Then pin the *catalyst*: the announcement date, the comment deadline, the preliminary determination, the report delivery. The repricing clusters around those dates, so that's when you put the trade on or take it off.

### Step 5 — Define what invalidates the view

Three things commonly kill a trade-policy trade, and you should write them down before you enter:

1. **A deal or carve-out.** A "Phase One" deal, an exemption list, or a tariff pause reverses the repricing fast. Tariff trades are headline-driven and can whipsaw on a single tweet.
2. **A currency or supply-chain offset bigger than you modelled.** If the yuan falls 12% against a 15% tariff, most of your "consumer pays" thesis evaporates.
3. **A legal reversal.** A US court striking down an IEEPA tariff, or a change of administration unwinding one. (Note: WTO action is *not* on this list anymore — the Appellate Body can't act.)

The discipline is the same as any rule-driven trade: the legal lever tells you the speed and scope, the cost-bearer/beneficiary map tells you the names, the FX and supply-chain offsets tell you the hedge, and the catalyst tells you the timing. Read the statute first, and you'll know how fast the market can move before the first container is ever taxed. For the full transmission framework that this post is one application of, see [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain); for the two trade-war case studies that put this playbook to work, see [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war) and [export controls and the chip war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war).
